                                                         A BSTRACT
010
011
                   Event sequences, characterized by irregular sampling intervals and a mix of cate-
012                gorical and numerical features, are common data structures in various real-world
013                domains such as healthcare, finance, and user interaction logs. Despite advances in
014                temporal data modeling techniques, there is no standardized benchmarks for evalu-
015                ating their performance on event sequences. This complicates result comparison
016                across different papers due to varying evaluation protocols, potentially misleading
017                progress in this field. We introduce EBES, a comprehensive benchmarking tool
018                with standardized evaluation scenarios and protocols, focusing on regression and
019                classification problems with sequence-level targets. Our library 1 simplifies bench-
020
                   marking, dataset addition, and method integration through a unified interface. It
                   includes a novel synthetic dataset and provides preprocessed real-world datasets,
021
                   including the largest publicly available banking dataset. Our results provide an
022
                   in-depth analysis of datasets, identifying some as unsuitable for model comparison.
023                We investigate the importance of modeling temporal and sequential components,
024                as well as the robustness and scaling properties of the models. These findings
025                highlight potential directions for future research. Our benchmark aim is to facilitate
026                reproducible research, expediting progress and increasing real-world impacts.

029   1       I NTRODUCTION
030
      The world we live in is constantly changing (Laertius, 1925). We continuously collect and analyze
031   data to understand and navigate this dynamic environment. This ongoing data collection helps capture
032   the evolving nature of reality and can be captured in sequential datasets, which can be further analyzed
033   or used for modeling.
034
      Various types of sequential data are usually approached differently based on their characteristics. One
035
      prevalent form of sequential data is time series, regular measurements of some processes. The unifor-
036
      mity of these intervals enables researchers to apply a wide range of developed techniques (Eckner,
037   2012). Measurements of some processes that are taken or observed at non-uniform time intervals
038   lead to irregularly sampled time series (ISTS). Fewer methods exist specifically for ISTS (Eckner,
039   2012), and modeling them brings new challenges (Li & Marlin, 2020). However, modeling them has
040   a considerable importance since they naturally occur in many real-world areas: ecology (Clark &
041   Bjørnstad, 2004), astronomy (Scargle, 1982), climate (Schulz & Stattegger, 1997), biology (Eckner,
042   2012), medicine (Goldberger et al., 2000; Johnson et al., 2016; Reyna et al., 2020), geology (Fang
043   et al., 2023) and finance (Bazarova et al., 2024).
044   Another widely explored temporal data type is a stream of discrete events. Intervals between events
045   are random, and modeling the distribution of inter-event intervals is an essential task with many
046   applications. Temporal point process (TPP) model is commonly employed to model streams of
047   discrete events (Du et al., 2016; Mei & Eisner, 2017; Omi et al., 2019; Jia & Benson, 2019; Shchur
048   et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2020; Zuo et al., 2020; Zhuzhel et al., 2023; Song et al., 2024).
049   In this work, we focus on another type of sequential data, event sequences (EvS), which are sequences
050   of observations made at irregular times characterized by numerical and categorical features. EvS can
051   be viewed as a generalization of both ISTS and streams of discrete events. Examples of various types
052   of EvS are illustrated in Figure 1. Many modeling tasks naturally arise when dealing with sequential

072   or forecasting (De Brouwer et al., 2019), missing data imputation (Rubanova et al., 2019), point-wise

075   known given a raw dataset. For instance, predicting the time of the next event is not reasonable when
076

      the entire sequence regardless of the assumptions about the nature of the data.

078   As a task we consider the whole EvS classification and regression task, which we refer to EvS
079   assessment. We emphasize the crucial role of EvS classification and regression in medicine (Shukla
080   & Marlin, 2018), churn prediction (Jain et al., 2021), e-commerce (Zhao et al., 2023), fraud detec-
081   tion (Xie et al., 2022) and more.
082   Our contributions are as follows:
083
       • We introduce EBES, a comprehensive benchmarking framework designed for EvS assessment.
084
         EBES features unified interfaces for datasets, models, and experimental protocols, facilitating
085
         future research in EvS assessment. Our library is publicly available.
086
       • We design a benchmark protocol that considers both model and dataset analysis. Our evaluation
087
         includes various scenarios, including some specific to EvS, highlighting important properties of
088
         both the datasets and models.
089
       • Using EBES, we evaluated various methods on established datasets through a multi-phase evalua-
090
         tion protocol. This approach ensures a fair and consistent comparison across different methods.
091
         All results are tested for statistical significance. As a result of our analysis, we provide recommen-
092      dations for future research. These recommendations include possible pitfalls related to dataset
093      usage and model evaluation.

095   2    B ENCHMARK GOALS AND APPROACHES
096
      Numerous methods have been proposed for EvS modeling and related problems. However, most
097   of these methods lack rigorous evaluation, and there is currently no established benchmark for this
098   domain. Benchmarking machine learning algorithms involves two main components: benchmark
099   design and datasets, each presenting its challenges and goals. Below, we describe how we address
100   each challenge in the context of EvS.
101
102   2.1 DATASETS
103   We have chosen three commonly used datasets based on previous studies Shukla & Marlin (2021);
104   Udovichenko et al. (2024); Babaev et al. (2022); Moskvoretskii et al. (2024), one recent and one of
105   the largest event sequence datasets MBD Dzhambulat et al. (2024), two medical datasets, and one
106   synthetic pendulum dataset to validate the importance of time and how models capture the sequential
107   properties of the data. We present statistics for each dataset in Table 1, and a detailed description of
      each dataset can be found in Appendix C.

126   Data Quality One of the primary challenges in benchmarking is ensuring that the datasets used are
127   high quality and accurately represent the problem domain. Poor data quality can lead to misleading
128   benchmark results.
129   To address data quality, we employ two strategies:
130
131   • Synthetic Dataset Development: We create a synthetic Pendulum dataset, particularly useful for
132     evaluating time-sensitive methods; dataset creation is described in Appendix C.
133
      • Dataset Analysis: We analyze the correlation of model performance with Monte Carlo cross-
        validation. Specifically, we consider the relationship between metrics across various folds and the
134
        holdout test set.
135
136   Diversity of Datasets. Datasets with similar structures but different domains can vary greatly. For
137   example, financial transactions differ significantly from medical records. Additionally, datasets can
138   vary in complexity and difficulty. Our work includes a diverse range: two medical, three banking,
139   one retail, and one synthetic dataset.
140   Volume of Data. Large datasets enable models to capture the complexity and nuances of real-world
141   phenomena, leading to more accurate and reliable predictions. Moreover, different algorithms scale
142   differently as the data grows. To address this challenge, we included datasets of various sizes.
143
      Open Access to Data. It is crucial that data is available to researchers worldwide for reproducibility,
144
      collaboration, and innovation. While many event-sequence datasets exist, we focus on open-access
145   ones and welcome contributions from other domains to enhance our collection. For example,
146   astronomical observations (Carrasco-Davis et al., 2019) are event sequences but are not openly
147   accessible.

      2.2 B ENCHMARK D ESIGN
150
151   Creating effective benchmarks is a complex task, which involves designing tests that accurately reflect
152
      the capabilities of machine learning models across different scenarios:
153   Model evaluation. Hyperparameters are a fundamental aspect of machine learning that directly
154   impacts model performance. However, the procedure of hyperparameters tuning is rarely described.
155   Therefore, this becomes a source of non-reproducibility Arnold et al. (2023); Gundersen et al. (2022).
156   Moreover, manual hyperparameter tuning can lead to the leakage of the test set into the training
157   procedure and performance Lones (2021), and testing different hyperparameter values is necessary to
158
      find a model that generalizes well Gundersen et al. (2022).
159   In our procedure, we first conduct an extensive hyperparameter search. Randomness can destabilize
160   models, causing large variances in results across training runs. Ignoring this sensitivity can create a
161   false perception of research progress (Pecher et al., 2024). Therefore, after determining the optimal
      hyperparameters, we perform Monte Carlo cross-validation (MCCV) (Xu & Liang, 2001) with 20


                                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      seeds. At each MCCV step we train the model with the best hyperparameters and pick the checkpoint
163   based on the (randomly sampled) validation set. Finally, the checkpoint is evaluated on the held-out
164   test set. The mean test score across all seeds is reported as the model performance.
165
166   Scalability As datasets grow larger, machine learning algorithms scale differently. Large datasets
167   enable models to capture real-world nuances, improving prediction accuracy. To address this, we
168   study the scaling properties of various event sequence assessment algorithms.
169
170
      Importance of Time and Sequence Order It is possible to perform EvS assessment while disre-
      garding the temporal and sequential nature of the data. To evaluate the importance of each component,
171
      we designed two stress tests for event sequences: rearranging the sequence order and replacing time
172
      components with noise. This analysis provides significant insights and highlights future research
173   directions.
174
175   Model Granularity As AI systems grow more complex, assessing which components contribute
176   to success becomes challenging. In our work, we evaluate different components, such as various
177   aggregation approaches along the temporal dimension, batch normalization, and the impact of adding
178   time as a separate feature on overall model performance.
179
180   2.3 B ENCHMARK ACCESSIBILITY AND M AINTENANCE
181   The rapid evolution of machine learning makes keeping benchmarks up-to-date challenging. Bench-
182   marks must reflect the latest advancements, incorporate new data and algorithms, and be maintained
183   over time. Our work focuses on developing an easy-to-use plug-and-play codebase to facilitate col-
184   laboration and research. The library’s interface structure enforces the independence of implementing
185   new datasets, methods, and experiments, making adding and testing new components easy. We
186   are committed to maintaining this benchmark and encourage contributions from researchers and
187
      practitioners to support reproducible research.
188
189   2.4 M ODELS
190   We have carefully selected a diverse set of popular models and approaches that have been previously
191   applied for EvS assessment. Some of the models, such as MLP, are included as baseline solutions,
192   some are commonly used for sequential data GRU (Chung et al., 2014), Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023),
193
      Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017). The following models were explicitly designed to handle the
      unique challenges associated with EvS: mTAND (Shukla & Marlin, 2021), PrimeNet (Chowdhury
194
      et al., 2023) and CoLES (Babaev et al., 2022). Appendix B provides a detailed description of each
195
      model.

197   3    B ENCHMARKING M ETHODS
198
199   3.1 DATASET PREPOSSESSING
200   In our work we aim to perform as little preprocessing as possible to preserve the originality of the
201   data in order to prevent data preproccessing from affecting model evaluation. For ease of extensibility,
202
      we convert all datasets into a single format and release scripts that perform the conversion.
203   Our data preprocessing includes:
204
       • Applying a logarithm to fat-tailed variables, which are selected manually;
205
206    • Rescaling time points to make the time range of all sequences to fall in [0, 1];
207    • For missing values, we propagate them forward for the PhysioNet, MIMIC-III, and Pendulum
208      datasets based on results in Che et al. (2018), and impute with constants for others.
209    • We encode categorical features using embedding layer and treat missing values as additional
210      categories.
211
212   3.2 M ODEL EVALUATION AND HPO
213   Hyperparameter optimization (HPO) and Monte Carlo cross-validation are at the core of our bench-
214   mark design, as they enable us to evaluate numerous design choices and hyperparameters, and to
215   fairly compare models. Furthermore, we derive important insights from multiple HPO runs. Our
      evaluation procedure is twofold:

246   4.1 A SSESSMENT PERFORMANCE
247   In this section, we address the main question of the benchmark: Which model performs the best?
248   The results are presented in Table 2, where methods are sorted from top to least performing. Along
249   with the mean performance we report method’s rank as a superscript. We performed pairwise
250   Mann–Whitney U test (Mann & Whitney, 1947) with Benjamini–Hochberg correction (Benjamini
251   & Hochberg, 1995), methods with no significant performance difference (p > 0.01) share the same
252   superscript. All top three performing methods are based on GRU with different pre-training strategies.
253   CoLES improves metrics on tasks where the target is a characteristic of an observed sequence, such
254
      as Age, Pendulum, and Retail. However, on datasets where the target is somehow connected to
      future events, such as Taobao, MIMIC-III, MBD, and PhysioNet, the pretraining does not provide a
255
      significant boost. MLEM performs similarly to CoLES, likely due to its usage of pretrained CoLES
256
      components.
257
258   Transformer and Mamba comes next in rating, suggesting that this architectures are less suitable
259   for EvS assessment. mTAND (Shukla & Marlin, 2021) excelled on the Pendulum dataset due to its
260
      architecture tailored for modeling the time component, particularly suited for ISTS like Pendulum.
      However, its poor performance on other datasets indicates that ISTS methods may not be as effective
261
      for general event sequences.
262
263   The MLP performs relatively well, typically within 5% of the top-performing method on all real-world
264   datasets. This suggests that EvS assessment can be effectively carried out using aggregated statistics
265   along temporal dimensions, a practice commonly employed in industrial applications with boosting
266
      models (ABIDAR et al., 2023). The difference in performance between MLP and mTAND on the
      Pendulum dataset further supports this idea, since we can not apply such aggregation approach to this
267
      dataset.
268
269   We see that, all methods show close results on the PhysioNet2012 dataset, based on ranks. This raises
      questions about its suitability for evaluating models for EvS assessment task.

340   4.3 DATA S CALING R ESULTS
341
      To study the scaling properties of various models, we evaluated each model trained with different
342
      numbers of sequences. We focused on two biggest real-world dataset in our benchmark: Retail and
343   MBD. We sampled different subsets, each containing progressively more data. Each model was
344   trained from scratch on different-sized subsets with Monte Carlo cross-validation using three random
345   seeds.
346
      A common approach is to estimate model performance with a fixed data size. However, as seen in
347
      Figure 4, while all models improve with the growth of the data, their ranking does not stay the same,
348
      except for CoLES on the Retail Dataset, where it demonstrates superior performance. With some data
349   size, even MLP becomes a top performer. Most models, except for MLP, mTAND, and PrimeNet,
350   converge to similar performance on the MBD dataset given a large data size. It is worth noting that
351   for each dataset, we used the BHP found for each model when the dataset was at its full size.
352
      The standard deviation, decreases as the data size increases and models perform very differently with
353
      smaller subsets. This makes evaluating model performance on relatively small datasets more prone to
354
      misleading results.

358   Although our models exhibit a diverse range of architectures, there are several common design
359   choices among them. We evaluated the impact of these choices as part of our HPO procedure.
360   We observe that some design choices depend more on the dataset than on the method, highlighting the
361   importance of HPO for fair evaluation. First, we study the effect of different aggregation approaches
362   along the temporal dimension on overall performance. We focus on two approaches: mean across all
363   hidden states and the last sequence state. The best aggregation strategy depends more on the dataset
364   than on the method. Similarly, batch normalization for numerical features improves performance
365   for almost all methods and datasets, except for Pendulum. Finally, we evaluate the importance of
366   hyperparameters according to HPO. There is no clear winner except for the learning rate, which is
367   often the most important hyperparameter across all HPO runs. Results are presented in Tables 10, 9

      and 8 in the Appendix.

478   5     R ELATED W ORK
479
      Event Sequences is an important domain that encompasses a variety of tasks. There are several
480   distinct research directions that involve Event Sequences. Predictive Process Monitoring (PPM)
481   is a crucial branch of process mining focused on forecasting the future states of ongoing business
482   processes. It involves analyzing event logs to predict various outcomes, such as process completion
483   time, subsequent events, or final outcomes of process instances Teinemaa et al. (2019); Márquez-
484   Chamorro et al. (2017); Rama-Maneiro et al. (2021); Tax et al. (2020). One related to PPM but distinct
485   problem is Event detection Azib et al. (2023), accurately identifying specific events is vital for making
      informed decisions. Deep Learning-based Temporal Point Processes, which primarily aims at


                                                                                   9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      predicting the type and timing of the next event Xue et al. (2024). Event Sequence Analysis employs
487   visual methods for data analysis Zinat et al. (2024). This approach focuses on building libraries. In
488   our work, we focus on another important yet understudied task: Event Sequence assessment, which
489   primarily includes the classification or regression of entire sequences. Therefore, our research is
490   distinct from previously studied benchmarks on time series classification and other studies that also
491   address the domain of EvS.
492
      The UCR Time Series Archive, widely used for time-series classification, is limited to univariate time
493   series, offering 128 datasets for algorithm evaluation (Dau et al., 2019). Despite its extensive use,
494   this benchmark does not address the complexity of event sequence data, crucial for many real-world
495   applications. The torchtime package (Darke et al., 2022) extends the utility of UEA & UCR
496   datasets by providing reproducible implementations for PyTorch, simplifying data access and ensuring
497   fair model comparisons, it is still primarily focuses on time series classification. EasyTPP (Xue et al.,
498   2024) is a new benchmark targeting streams of discrete events, offering a centralized repository for
499   evaluating TPP models. It emphasizes reproducible research through a standardized benchmarking
500   framework and provides various research assets. However, EasyTPP cannot be extended to handle
501
      general EvS, as event sequences generally cannot be modeled using TPP. The sequence of card
      transactions made by a client is a good example of EvS. Each transaction is characterized by attributes
502
      such as transaction amount and merchant category code, making them unfit for time series or discrete
503
      event streams categories. Authors in Bazarova et al. (2024); Yugay & Zaytsev (2024); Babaev et al.
504   (2022) evaluate several representation learning approaches on event sequences. In Bazarova et al.
505   (2024), the authors propose a protocol for evaluating obtained representations on a set of downstream
506   tasks.
507
508   6    C ONCLUSION
509
      In this work, we presented EBES, an open and comprehensive benchmark for the standardized
510   and transparent comparison of event sequence models. The benchmark includes a diverse range of
511   datasets and models. Additionally, it provides a user-friendly interface and a rich library, allowing
512   for the easy integration of new datasets and the implementation of new models. With these features,
513   EBES has the potential to facilitate future research in event sequence modeling significantly.
514
      We emphasize the importance of HPO and cross-validation for fair model evaluation. Moreover, we
515
      recommend performing several runs to validate if the model performance is statistically significant,
516   especially on small datasets. This is also supported by scaling experiments, where model rankings
517   tend to change significantly on smaller data sizes and slowly converge to the same point as the data
518   size grows while the standard deviation decreases.
519
      Our analysis of datasets highlights two crucial points. We found that results on the PhysioNet2012
520
      dataset are not statistically distinguishable. Therefore, future researchers should be cautious when
521
      deriving conclusions for EvS assessment based on results obtained with this dataset. Another
522   observation is that out-of-time data splits naturally tend to have a distribution shift, and one should
523   account for it during model validation and HPO. For example, this appears in the low correlation
524   between the Taobao dataset’s validation and test metric values.
525
      We demonstrate that the importance of time and the sequential nature of the data varies for real-world
526
      datasets concerning EvS assessment. Similarly, different models capture these properties differently.
527
      Developing or testing models that inherently account for the time component on real-world data
528   could be a promising direction for future research.

751
752   GRU We have chosen to use the GRU as one of our base models due to its proven effectiveness
753   in encoding time-ordered sequences Babaev et al. (2022); Rubanova et al. (2019); Tonekaboni
754   et al. (2021); Yoon et al. (2019); Udovichenko et al. (2024). In recent study on neural architecture
755   search Udovichenko et al. (2024)), authors demonstrated that architectures with RNN blocks tend to
      exhibit higher performance on average on EvS assesment task.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      MLP The models applied 3 linear layers with the ReLU nonlinearity and dropouts in between to
757   the aggregated embeddings obtained right after the preprocessing block. So effectively the model is
758   just a basic MLP applied to aggregations. Models for EvS handles the sequential nature of data in a
759   special way, ofthen considering the exact time intervals between the events, so we were interested in
760   the performance of the model, that consciously discards the sequential nature of data.
761
762   Mamba Mamba Gu & Dao (2023) is a recent state-space model (SSM) that has been designed
763   for efficient handling of complex, long sequences. It incorporates selective state spaces to deliver
764   top-notch performance across different modalities, including language, audio, and genomics, outper-
765   forming Transformers in some scenarios. For the best of our knowledge Mamba has not been applied
766   to EvS assessment previously, however, we believe that type of models worth of investigating.
767
768   mTAND Authors in Shukla & Marlin (2021) proposed an architecture which learns an embedding
769   of continuous-time values and utilizes an attention mechanism to produce a fixed-length representation
770
      of a time series. This procedure is specifically designed to deal with ISTS and has been shown
      to outperform numerous ordinary differential equations-based models such as Latent ODE and
771
      ODE-RNN Rubanova et al. (2019).
772
773
      CoLES The contrastive pretraining method for sequential data was proposed by Babaev et al.
774
      (2022). We specifically focus on this method due to its superior performance compared to other
775   contrastive approaches demonstrated in the work. CoLES learns to encode a sequence into a latent
776   vector by bringing sub-sequences of the same sequence closer in the embedding space while pushing
777   sub-sequences from different sequences further apart.
778
779   PrimeNet The method proposed in Chowdhury et al. (2023) also, falls under the category of
780   self-supervised. It utilizes time-sensitive contrastive pretraining and enhances pretraining procedure
781   with data reconstruction tasks to facilitate the usage of unlabeled data. Authors modify mTAN
782   architecture by replacing an RNN block with Feature-Feature Attention.
783
784   MLEM The Multimodal Learning Event Model Moskvoretskii et al. (2024) is a recently proposed
785   method for Event Sequences that unifies contrastive learning with generative modeling. It treats
786   generative pre-training and contrastive learning as distinct modalities. First, a contrastive encoder
787   is trained, followed by an encoder-decoder that learns latent states using reconstruction loss while
788
      aligning with contrastive embeddings to enhance the embedding information.
