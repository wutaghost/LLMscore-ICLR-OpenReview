009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We study Leaky ResNets, which interpolate between ResNets (L̃ = 0) and Fully-
012            Connected nets (L̃ → ∞) depending on an ’effective depth’ hyper-parameter L̃.
013            In the infinite depth limit, we study ’representation geodesics’ Ap : continuous
014            paths in representation space (similar to NeuralODEs) from input p = 0 to output
015            p = 1 that minimize the parameter norm of the network. We give a Lagrangian
016            and Hamiltonian reformulation, which highlight the importance of two terms: a
017            kinetic energy which favors small layer derivatives ∂p Ap and a potential energy
018
               that favors low-dimensional representations, as measured by the ’Cost of Identity’.
               The balance between these two forces offers an intuitive understanding of feature
019
               learning in ResNets. We leverage this intuition to explain the emergence of a
020
               bottleneck structure, as observed in previous work: for large L̃ the potential energy
021
               dominates and leads to a separation of timescales, where the representation jumps
022            rapidly from the high dimensional inputs to a low-dimensional representation,
023            move slowly inside the space of low-dimensional representations, before jumping
024            back to the potentially high-dimensional outputs. Inspired by this phenomenon, we
025            train with an adaptive layer step-size to adapt to the separation of timescales.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Feature learning is generally considered to be at the center of the recent successes of deep neural
031   networks (DNNs), but it also remains one of the least understood aspects of DNN training.
032   There is a rich history of empirical analysis of the features learned by DNNs, for example the
033   appearance of local edge detections in CNNs with a striking similarity to the biological visual cortex
034   (Krizhevsky et al., 2012), feature arithmetic properties of word embeddings (Mikolov et al., 2013),
035   similarities between representations at different layers (Kornblith et al., 2019; Li & Papyan, 2024),
036   or properties such as Neural Collapse (Papyan et al., 2020) to name a few. While some of these
037   phenomena have been studied theoretically (Arora et al., 2016; Ethayarajh et al., 2018; Súkeník et al.,
038
      2024), a more general theory of feature learning in DNNs is still lacking.
039   For shallow networks, there is now strong evidence that the first weight matrix is able to recognize
040   a low-dimensional projection of the inputs that determines the output (assuming this structure is
041   present) (Bach, 2017; Abbe et al., 2021; 2022). A similar phenomenon appears in linear networks,
042   where the network is biased towards learning low-rank functions and low-dimensional representations
043   in its hidden layers (Gunasekar et al., 2018b; Li et al., 2020; Wang & Jacot, 2024). But in both cases
044
      the learned features are restricted to depend linearly on the inputs, and the feature learning happens
      in the very first weight matrix, whereas it has been observed that features increase in complexity
045
      throughout the layers (Zeiler & Fergus, 2014).
046
047   The linear feature learning ability of shallow networks has inspired a line of work that postulates that
048   the weight matrices learn to align themselves with the backward gradients and that by optimizing for
049   this alignment directly, one can achieve similar feature learning abilities even in deep nets (Beaglehole
050
      et al., 2023; Radhakrishnan et al., 2024).
051   For deep nonlinear networks, a theory that has garnered a lot of interest is the Information Bottleneck
052   (Tishby & Zaslavsky, 2015), which observed amongst other things that the inner representations
053   appear to maximize their mutual information with the outputs, while minimizing the mutual
      information with the inputs. A limitation of this theory is its reliance on the notion of mutual


                                                         1
054
      information which has no obvious definition for empirical distributions, which led to some criticism
055   (Saxe et al., 2018).
056
057   A recent theory that is similar to the Information Bottleneck but with a focus on the
058
      dimensionality/rank of the representations and weight matrices rather than the mutual information is
      the Bottleneck rank/Bottleneck structure (Jacot, 2023a;b; Wen & Jacot, 2024): which describes how,
059
      for large depths, most of the representations will have approximately the same low dimension, which
060
      equals the Bottleneck rank of the task (the minimal dimension that the inputs can be projected to while
061   still allowing for fitting the outputs). The intuitive explanation for this bias is that a smaller parameter
062   norm is required to (approximately) represent the identity on low-dimensional representations rather
063   than high dimensional ones. Some other types of low-rank bias have been observed in recent work
064   (Galanti et al., 2022; Guth et al., 2023).
065
      In this paper we will focus on describing the Bottleneck structure in ResNets, and formalize the
066
      notion of ‘cost of identity’ as a driving force for the bias towards low dimensional representation.
067   The ResNet setup allows us to consider the continuous paths in representation space from input to
068   output, similar to the NeuralODE (Chen et al., 2018), and by adding weight decay, we can analyze
069   representation geodesics, which are paths that minimize parameter norm, as already studied in
070   (Owhadi, 2020). The appearance of separation of timescales in the layers of ResNets with a modified
071   loss has been mentioned in Geshkovski & Zuazua (2022), under the name ‘turnpike principle’, but
072   the underlying mechanism for the separation of timescales/turnpike behavior are very different to
073   ours. One of the main differences is the absence of a low-dimensional bias, as we observe.
074

      2     B OTTLENECK S TRUCTURE IN R EPRESENTATION G EODESICS
354
      A recent line of work (Jacot, 2023a;b) studies the appearance of a so-called Bottleneck structure
355
      in large depth fully-connected networks, where the weight matrices and representations of ‘almost
356   all’ layers are approximately low-rank/low-dimensional as the depth grows. This dimension k is
357   consistent across layers, and can be interpreted as being equal to the so-called Bottleneck rank of the
358   learned function. This structure has been shown to extend to CNNs in (Wen & Jacot, 2024), and we
359   will observe a similar structure in our leaky ResNets, further showcasing its generality.
360

361

362   not) over FCNNs is that these geodesics can be approximated by continuous paths and are described
363   by differential equations (as described by the Hamiltonian reformulation).

      4    C ONCLUSION
504
505
      We have given a description of the representation geodesics Ap of Leaky ResNets. We have identified
506   an invariant, the Hamiltonian, which is the sum of the kinetic and potential energy, where the kinetic
507   energy measures the size of the derivative ∂p Ap , while the potential energy is inversely proportional
508   to the cost of identity, which is a measure of dimensionality of the representations. As the effective
509   depth of the network grows, the potential energy dominates and we observe a separation of timescales.
510   At layers with minimal dimensionality over the path, the kinetic energy (and thus the derivative ∂p Ap )
511   is finite. Conversely, at layers where the representation is higher-dimensional, the kinetic energy must
512   scale with L̃. This leads to a Bottleneck structure, with a short, high-dimensional jump from the input
513   representation to a low dimensional representation, followed by slow dynamics inside the space of
514   low-dimensional representations followed by a final high-dimensional jump to the high dimensional
515   outputs.

920
      Our experiments make use of synthetic data to train leaky ResNets so that the Bottleneck rank k ∗
921   is known for our experiments. The synthetic data is generated by teacher networks for a given true
922   rank k ∗ . To construct a bottleneck, the teacher network is a composition of networks for which the
923   the inner-dimension is k ∗ . For data, we sampled a thousand data points for training, and another
924   thousand for testing which are collectively augmented by demeaning and normalization.
925
      To train the leaky ResNets, it is important for them to be wide, usually wider than the input or output
926
      dimension, we opted for a width of 200. However, the width of the representation must be constant
927   to implement leaky residual connections, so we introduce a single linear mapping at the start, and
928   another at the end, of the forward pass to project the representations into a higher dimension for the
929   paths. These linear mappings can be either learned or fixed.
930
      To achieve a tight convergence in training, we train primarily using Adam using Mean Squared Error
931
      as a loss function, and our custom weight decay function. After training on Adam (we found 20000
932
      epochs to work well), we then train briefly (usually 10000 epochs) using SGD with a smaller learning
933   rate to tighten the convergence.
934
935   The bottleneck structure of a trained network, as seen in Figure 3, can be observed in the spectra of
936
      the weight matrices Wp at each layer. As long as the training is not over-regularized (λ too large)
      then the spectra reveals a clear separation between k ∗ number of large values as the rest decay. In our
937
      experiments, λ = 0.002 yielded good results. To facilitate the formation of the bottleneck structure,
938
      L should be large, for our experiments we used L = 50 and then a range from 4 to 22. Figure 2a
939   shows how larger L, which have better separation between large and small singular values, lead to
940   improved test performance.

      H is difficult due to the instability of the pseudo-inverse. Although the relaxation (Kp + γI) improves
943
      the stability, we also utilize the solve function to avoid computing a pseudo-inverse altogether. The
944
      stability of these computations rely on the boundedness of some additional properties: the path length
945     ||∂p Ap || dp, as well as the magnitudes of Bp , and Bp σ(Ap )T from the Hamiltonian reformulation.
