009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Robot manipulation in complex scenarios usually involves multiple successful ac-
012            tions, which requires generative models to estimate the distribution of various
013            successful actions. In recent years, the diffusion model has been widely studied
014            in many robot manipulation tasks. However, the diffusion model experiences in-
015            consistent noise supervision across various action labels and denoising timesteps,
016
               which compromises accurate action prediction. On the one hand, CIDM designs
               new noise supervision to avoid interference between different successful actions,
017
               leading to consistent denoising directions. On the other hand, CIDM unifies all
018
               denoising timesteps, avoiding inconsistent predictions of the diffusion model over
019            different timesteps. Moreover, we also designed a novel radial loss to make the
020            model focus on denoising results rather than iterative process routes. Our method
021            achieves a new state-of-the-art performance on RLBench with the highest success
022            rate of 82.3% on a multi-view setup and 83.9% on a single-view setup.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      As an important research field of embodied intelligence, robot arm manipulation has a wide range
      of real-world application scenarios and attracts widespread attention. Robot manipulation mainly
028
      involves two steps, acquiring effective scene representation and predicting correct actions. Due to
029
      the complexity of action strategies in challenging scenarios, there is an increasing interest in the
030   policy network, which predicts well-performed actions. Additionally, introducing more complex
031   tasks with a diverse set of successful actions puts an extra burden on action predicting (Jia et al.,
032   2024). Traditional regression models as policy networks can only predict a single action, making it
033   difficult to understand scenes with multiple successful actions. Benefiting from the ability to model
034   the distribution of multiple actions, generative models show superior performance in many robotic
035   manipulation tasks.
036   Among different generative models, the diffusion model achieves leading performance in many
037   visual generation tasks. So far, a series of works inspired by the visual generation, make progress
038   on robotic manipulation using the diffusion model. Diffusion Policy (Chi et al., 2023) finds that
039   diffusion formulation has a strong advantage of robust manipulation and exhibits impressive training
040   stability. Imitating Diffusion (Pearce et al., 2023) discover that diffusion models are suitable for
041   learning from sequential robotic demonstrations. READ (Oba et al., 2024) designs an asymmetric
042   denoising process motivated by Cold Diffusion (Bansal et al., 2024).
043   However, different from the high sampling density in the visual generation task, robot manipulation
044   has a high data acquisition cost (Cui et al., 2023). It is hard for the diffusion model to learn the
045   accurate probability distribution with inadequate training data. Specifically, the above difficulty of
046   learning an accurate diffusion model for action denoising mainly comes from two aspects:
047
      1) Difficulty in clarifying an accurate denoising direction. Since the diffusion model may pro-
048   duce the same noisy action over different successful actions (Ho et al., 2020), the diffusion model
049   will be confused about the denoising directions, leading to inaccurate noise prediction. Especially
050   in the initial denoising stage, the similar initial distributions of successful actions induce severe con-
051   fusion in the denoising direction. For the sake of clarity, assuming a simple scenario containing
052   two successful actions with equal prior probability, the denoising process of the diffusion model is
053   shown in Figure 1(a). For the noisy action (black point) sampled from the initial noisy distribution,

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Diffusion model. Through the iterative denoising process, early diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020;
111
      Song et al., 2021a) enable diverse and high-quality visual generation. Since the diffusion process
112   could be modeled as a stochastic differential equation (Song et al., 2021b), the continuous diffusion
113   models (Dockhorn et al., 2022; Jolicoeur-Martineau et al., 2021) achieve more efficient generation
114   with fewer steps. The widely-regarded stable diffusion adopts a latent space (Rombach et al., 2022;
115   Vahdat et al., 2021) to achieve computational efficiency, while the lower bound on the dimension
116   of the latent space is still limited by the need to decode images. In the last few years, more than
117   speeding up the denoising process, recent work has also provided a more in-depth analysis of diffu-
118   sion models. Cold Diffusion (Bansal et al., 2024) designs a more robust iteration to revert arbitrary
119   degradation. Inversion by Direct Iteration (Delbracio & Milanfar, 2023) pursues a simpler form
120
      to get rid of the limitations of traditional diffusion. Some recent works Lin et al. (2024); Zhang
      et al. (2024) have noticed and attempted to address the subtle differences in sampling distributions
121
      between training and inference, which were previously ignored. Research on a few samples (Wu
122
      et al., 2024b) is done through fine-tuning rather than complete retraining, which also reflects the
123   dependence of diffusion models on sufficient training data.
124
125   Diffusion model in robotic manipulation. In recent years, a series of works have verified the po-
126
      tential of diffusion models in robot manipulation. Different from using diffusion models to generate
      more visual scene information (Wu et al., 2024a), the potential of diffusion models to predict actions
127
      has also been explored. Diffusion Policy (Chi et al., 2023) successfully models the probability of
128
      trajectory sequences in different tasks. 3D Diffusion Policy (Ze et al., 2024) incorporates the power
129   of 3D visual representations into conditional diffusion models. DNActor (Yan et al., 2024) distill
130   2D semantic features from foundation models, such as Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) and
131   state representation on NeRF (Driess et al., 2022), to a 3D space in its pretrain phase. With the
132   continuous improvement of diffusion models in the field of visual generation, works are designing
133   new diffusion paradigms in robotic manipulation. Hierarchical Diffusion Policy (Ma et al., 2024)
134   adds a new robot kinematic constraint on the diffusion models. READ (Oba et al., 2024) preserves
135   the kinematic feasibility of the generated action via forward diffusion in a low-dimensional latent
136   space, while using cold diffusion to achieve high-resolution action via back diffusion in the original
137
      task space. These methods inspire us to enhance the diffusion model for generating actions that
      align with the characteristics of robot manipulation.

150   Robot manipulation. The key-frame robot manipulation is described by a sparse sequence of the
151   robot trajectory and corresponding scene information x, which contains multi-view RGB-D images,
152   the text instruction, and the current robot state. As parallel gripper robot arms interact with the
153   environment through the end effector, we use end-effort posture y to guide the action of the robot
154
      arm, which includes the translation, the rotation, and the binary opening state of the gripper. Due
      to the opening state containing little location information, only the translations and the rotations are
155
      input as noisy actions. In the successful demonstrations for training, each scene x corresponds to a
156
      label action ŷ, which is one of the successful actions {ŷ i }ki=1 .

257   3.3    C ONSISTENT D ENOISING F IELD
258
259   As CIDM relies on the denoising network ϵθ (Fx , y) for iterative denoising, it is very important to
260   learn from a reasonable denoising field ϵx (y). In an ideal time-invariant denoising field, arbitrary
261   actions y in the action space can reach a successful action within finite steps of denoising. Since
262
      iterative denoising results in different successful actions, we divide the action space into distinct
      regions, each region corresponding to a specific successful action. The reasonable denoising field
263
      ϵx (y) as described above complies with two conditions:
264
265   (1) Since the reasonable denoising field always makes noisy action closer to its target successful
266   action, there must be a neighborhood of ŷ where actions reach ŷ through a single-step denoising,
267   expressed as follows:
268
                                      ∃ c<0, ∀ ∥y − ŷ∥2 <c, ϵx (y) = y − ŷ.                           (12)
269   (2) On the boundaries that separate different regions in the action space, the noise prediction cannot
      point to either side. To achieve the best symmetry, we set ϵx (y) = 0 with y on the boundaries.

285   By training on all (x, ŷ) pairs, the denoising network ϵθ (Fx , y) learns to predict noise without de-

289   network ϵθ (Fx , y). Consequently, we achieve accurate iterative denoising during inference based on
290   better noise prediction.

293
294   As the design of the ϵx (y; ŷ) during training is only necessary but not sufficient to learn a reasonable
295   ϵx (y), we design the radial loss function L to optimize the denoising network ϵθ (Fx , y). Essentially,
296   the optimization of the denoising network on all training data is as follows:
297
                               θ = arg min Ep(y|ŷ)p(x,ŷ) L ϵθ (Fx , y), ϵx (y; ŷ) .                      (15)

301   the target denoising field ϵx (y) expressed as follows:
302

      Our radial loss function L should make ϵx (y) close to ϵx (y; ŷ j ) when y is close to ŷ j . In addition,
306   a small noise prediction error is acceptable for actions requiring multi-step denoising. Therefore,
307   when noisy action y gets closer to successful actions {ŷ i }ki=1 , the loss function should pay more
308   attention to it. Based on the above perceptions, we designed a radial loss function as follows:
309
310                              L(y, ŷ) = δ(∥y − ŷ∥2 ) L1 ϵθ (Fx , y), ϵx (y; ŷ) ,                       (17)

      where L1 denotes the L1 loss function and δ(·) denotes the radial weight as follows:
313

                                          δ(r) = min(1/ r, 10).                                             (18)

315   The δ(r) ensures that small radial distance r = ∥y − ŷ∥2 corresponds to a large weight in the overall
316   loss. We set an upper bound of 10 for δ to avoid excessive loss, which leads to unstable training.
317   Compared with L2 loss, the gradient of L1 loss does not increase with a larger prediction error. The
318   L1 loss makes the target denoising field ϵx (y) focus on specific successful action corresponding to
319   y, instead of being affected by all successful actions.
320   The target denoised field ϵx (y) obtains good properties through the radial loss function. On the
321   one hand, ϵx (y) can also accurately denoise in a single step when y is close to a successful action
322   ŷ (Section A.3 in Appendix). On the other hand, ϵx (y) enables the correct denoising of noisy
323   actions that are far from successful actions. Therefore, our radial loss helps to learn an accurate and

342   4.1     E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
343
344   Dataset and simulation. We evaluate our CIDM on a multi-task manipulation benchmark devel-
345   oped in RLBench (James et al., 2020). We use 18 distinct tasks, each comprising 150 demonstra-
346
      tions, with 14 of them illustrated in Figure 3. Each task includes various text instructions, which
      feature between 2 to 60 variations. These variations consist of several types, such as variations in
347
      position and color. The demonstrations are collected in a simulation environment built by Cop-
348
      peliaSim (Rohmer et al., 2013). The 256 × 256 RGB-D images in demonstrations are captured by
349   four noiseless cameras positioned at the front, left shoulder, right shoulder, and wrist of the robot.
350   In addition, we verify our performance in the simulation environment.
351
352
      Training and evaluation details. Our CIDM is trained on 4 NVIDIA 3090Ti 10GB GPUs for 60K
      steps with a cosine learning rate decay schedule. We adopt a batch size of 32 and initialize the
353
      learning rate to 10−4 . Among the 150 pre-generated demonstrations, 100 are used for training, 25
354
      for validation, and 25 for testing. During training, we prefer to sample points close to successful
355   actions. We evaluate CIDM in both multi-view and single-view settings. As the action planner in
356   the simulation environment has a certain degree of randomness, we evaluate each task four times
357   and take the average success probability as the performance metric.
358
      Baselines. In text-guided robot manipulation, we compare CIDM with the existing baselines, which
359
      have made significant advancements and achieved excellent performance. The following work has
360
      improved scene representation methods in robotic manipulation: PolarNet (Chen et al., 2023), Hive-
361   Former (Guhur et al., 2023), PerAct (Shridhar et al., 2023), Act3D (Gervet et al., 2023), RVT (Goyal
362   et al., 2023). Additionally, RVT2 (Goyal et al., 2024) using action-value graphs and 3D Diffuser Ac-
363   tor Ke et al. (2024) using the diffusion model make progress in modeling multiple successful actions.
364

      4.2     M AIN R ESULTS
366
367   Following the setting of PerAct (Shridhar et al., 2023), we conduct experiments on 18 tasks with
368   4 camera views. As shown in Table 1, our CIDM achieves the sota performance among existing
369   methods, boasting the highest average success rate of 82.3% and the best average ranking of 2.0
370   across all tasks. Specifically, our CIDM secures the best performance on 7 tasks and the suboptimal
371   performance on 7 tasks. Moreover, compared with the diffusion-based model 3D Diffuser Actor,
372   CIDM improves performance on tasks with multiple successful actions. Performance degradation
373   on some tasks is caused by multi-task training, where success rates fluctuate to some extent.
374   Following the setting of GNFactor (Ze et al., 2023), we also conduct experiments on 10 tasks (a
375   subset of the 18 tasks) with a single view. As shown in Table 2, our CIDM achieves the highest

478   5   C ONCLUSION
479
480   In this paper, we propose the consistent iterative denoising model (CIDM) for text-guided robot
481   manipulation. We build a more consistent denoising field than the diffusion model, by designing
482   noise supervision and unifying the timesteps during training. Moreover, utilizing the radial loss,
483   CIDM avoids interference from other successful actions and obtains accurate denoised actions. On
484   diverse simulated robot manipulation tasks, CIDM achieves state-of-the-art performance in both
485   multi-view and single-view settings. Ablation studies are conducted on various components within
      CIDM, providing further clarity on their efficiency.
