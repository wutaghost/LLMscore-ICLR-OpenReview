011                                                    A BSTRACT
012
013             Large language models (LLMs) excel in various tasks but often suffer from hal-
014             lucinations, providing incorrect information with high confidence. To address
015             this, we focus on detecting whether an LLM possesses enough knowledge for
016             the answer, a concept referred to as the “feeling of knowing” (FoK). We pro-
017             pose a novel approach called semantic compression by trying to answer in one-
018             word (SCAO), which enables efficient FoK before generating full sentences, with
019             only minimal computational cost. Additionally, we introduce a method to mea-
020
                sure the self-awareness of FoK methods precisely by filtering out distracting
                variables, an approximate question-dependency effect (AQE) test. Our exper-
021
                iments demonstrate that the feature fusion model of our SCAO and probing
022
                achieves enhanced performance in FoK in both factoid and open-ended ques-
023             tion answering involving entity recall. The code and the dataset are available online
024             (https://anonymous.4open.science/r/SCAO-2FF8).

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Large language models (LLMs) demonstrate impressive abilities in various applications (Ouyang
030   et al., 2022; OpenAI, 2023). However, even the state-of-the-art LLMs often suffer from hallucination
031   (Cohen et al., 2023). By providing just one wrong answer out of 10 correct answers, the reliability of
032
      the entire answer is undermined. While hallucination arises from various causes (failure of reading,
      reasoning, memory retrieval, etc), it is well-known that a major cause is when the model is asked
033
      questions on the knowledge not pre-trained (Tonmoy et al., 2024). In this sense, a simple strategy to
034
      significantly reduce hallucination is just to reject answering when it can not. Therefore, the task of
035   determining whether an LLM knows the knowledge or not is crucial. This task has been researched
036   recently, and we name this task as the feeling of knowing (FoK), which is a psychological term
037   (Nelson, 1990; Koriat, 1993) referring to “The self-judgment whether a human can recall certain
038   memory”1 . Similarly, the FoK of LLMs can be defined as the ability to be aware of whether the
039   model possesses specific knowledge. We introduce the term FoK to distinguish it from "hallucination
040   detection," as the two often have been regarded as identical (Zhang et al., 2024).
041   Numerous outstanding works have explored FoK of LLM; however, most of them have focused on a
042   scenario of “generating whole answer sentence and then detect” (Manakul et al., 2023; Chen et al.,
043   2024; Ren et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023; Kuhn et al., 2023). Though performing FoK prediction
044   with all information may increase the accuracy, this approach is highly time-consuming and costly.
045   This approach makes the user wait until the end of the answer to know whether it is reliable, which is
046   less useful in a real-world service. Moreover, recent cognitive neuroscience research suggests that
047   the human brain conducts FoK judgment in about 300 milliseconds at the unconscious level, right
048   after being given a query (Irak et al., 2019). Additionally, this process is related to a brain region of
049
      the prefrontal cortex, which is apart from the main region for language generation (Wernicke’s area)
      (Binder, 2015). This observation suggests that detecting whether a biological neural network holds
050
      a certain memory or not (FoK) can be partially achieved in a very short time, leveraging only the
051
      information provided before the verbalization of the answer. Moreover, one of the widely recognized

      theories of human FoK mechanism is the accessibility model (Koriat, 1993), which claims vividness
055   of memory serves as an important cue.
056
057   Believing that similar efficiency is achievable in artificial neural networks, we focus on exploring the
058
      possibility of FoK before answer generation. In particular, we highlight that token-level confidence
      can serve as one of the key sources of information, representing the vividness of memory. Previous
059
      research has also explored token-level confidence as a method for FoK in generated text (Fadeeva
060
      et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024), but there remains still room for further improvement. We propose a
061   perspective that LLMs are structurally similar to dense retrievers, as both conduct maximum inner
062   product search (MIPS) over knowledge space. As the confidence scores of retrieved documents
063   reflect whether the queried knowledge is contained in the vector database (Zhang et al., 2022), the
064   token-level confidence of LLM might reflect whether the queried knowledge is contained in the LLM,
065   serving as a FoK verifier. However, this concept will make sense only when a single token embedding
066   vector of LLM encapsulates a single piece of knowledge, just like a dense retriever. For this, the token
067   embedding requires semantic compression, which is nontrivial. However, we discovered this could be
068   achieved simply by using the instruction prompt “answer in only one word” without further parameter
069
      tuning. We term this method semantic compression via trying to answer in one word (SCAO). We
      propose a method of combining SCAO with probing (Azaria & Mitchell, 2023), achieving enhanced
070
      performance in the FoK task.
071
072   In addition, for a more precise evaluation of FoK, we provide a metric that can assess whether
073   a dataset is fit for evaluating FoK. As FoK is a fascinating topic that focuses on the LLM’s self-
074   awareness, it necessitates methods that can measure self-awareness. However, what we can explicitly
075
      measure is only whether the model answers incorrectly, which does not directly indicate the model’s
      lack of knowledge; It can also be due to the question itself, being too ambiguous or unanswerable.
076
      To measure and filter out this question-dependent portion, we devised a novel metric without any
077
      expensive human labor, called AQE (approximately measuring the question-dependency effect).
078   Through the AQE test, we prove that Mintaka2 and ParaRel OOD3 is relatively bias-free benchmarks.
079
080   We summarize our contributions: 1) By leveraging a perspective that LLMs and retrievers are
081
      structurally similar, we develop a FoK verification method SCAO, which needs near-to-zero additional
      resources. 2) We propose a metric to clearly measure the model-awareness of FoK methods. 3)
082
      Experimental results in a controlled environment show that the feature fusion model of SCAO and
083
      probing achieves enhanced performance in entity recall question answering, which demonstrates their
084   synergetic nature.

089   As the human FoK has been extensively explored in cognitive psychology and neuroscience, the
090
      concepts from this field can be leveraged to structure and categorize approaches on FoK in LLM.
091   FoK of Human: Cognitive Neuropsychology dd Observations of Koriat (1993); Irak et al. (2019);
092   Brown et al. (2017) suggest that human FoK is achieved through two major processes. 1) Unconscious
093   level: When a query is received, in the level not directly monitored by the conscious, the brain retrieves
094   related memories and determines whether each fits the temporal context. During this process, the
095   orbitofrontal cortex and prefrontal cortex are activated around 300-500 milliseconds (Schnider, 2001;
096
      Irak et al., 2019), which are distinct regions from the area responsible for verbal fluency, posterior
      temporal lobe (i.e., Wernicke area) (Binder, 2015). Koriat (1993) suggest that the stimuli (i.e., cue
097
      for the process) for FoK include the amount of information activated, ease of access, and vividness
098
      of each memory (Koriat, 1993). 2) Conscious level: Memories processed at the unconscious level
099   emerge at the conscious level and are further assessed with various meta-cognitive strategies. The
100   strategy of checking for logical and temporal consistencies between the retrieved memories is an
101   example. FoK of humans results from the ensemble of all these underlying processes.
102
      According to the dual-process theory of Kahneman (2011), the level of the main process can vary
103
      depending on the type of question or task. Immediate entity recall involves unconscious or implicit
104
      memory systems, while tasks that require more procedural thinking—such as solving mathematical
105   equations or logical puzzles—engage conscious cognitive resources.

      FoK of LLM: a part of Hallucination Detection dd The two verification procedures of humans
109   roughly align with the before-generation and after-generation approaches in hallucination detection
110   (Among this, FoK of LLM is specified to knowledge recall issue). Also, benchmarks for FoK are
111   categorized to correspond to each process. 1) Before-generation: Including our work, studies on
112   the method of hallucination detection before answer generation (Mallen et al., 2022) align with
113   the feature of the unconscious process of human FoK. Also, benchmarks with entity type question-
114   answer (Sen et al., 2022; Elazar et al., 2021b) primarily utilize immediate memory retrieval. 2)
115   After-generation: Conversely, studies that assume the scenario of generating full answers one or
116   multiple times, including methods of utilizing other tools like external retriever (Béchard & Ayala,
117
      2024), can be aligned with the feature of conscious level (Manakul et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024).
      Among benchmarks, mathematical problem solving such as MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021) is more
118
      closely associated with the conscious level, as it benefits more from deliberate strategies such as
119
      multi-step reasoning, rather than from vivid recall of knowledge. Additionally, answering abstract
120   questions that require multiple steps of reasoning (benchmarks such as TruthfulQA (Lin et al., 2022),
121   ELI5 (Fan et al., 2019a), and Natural Questions (Kwiatkowski et al., 2019)) are also more related to
122   conscious processes.
123
      While FoK takes a large portion of hallucinations, not all hallucinations directly engage FoK, which
124
      may raise confusion. For instance, hallucinations that arise in open-book tasks are more associated
125
      with issues in reading comprehension than possession of knowledge. Benchmarks mainly associated
126   with open-book tasks are SQuAD (Rajpurkar et al., 2016), FEVER (Thorne et al., 2018).
127
128   While various tasks and methods have been grouped under the same name of “hallucination”, they
129
      involve essentially different types of cognitive processes. Each process (e.g., memory retrieval,
      reasoning, reading comprehension) may require distinct optimal methods to address its specific
130
      challenges. Ultimately, it will be necessary to ensemble these different approaches. However, In our
131
      work, we focus on methods and benchmarks related to FoK on the knowledge retrieval question at
132   the before-generation phase.

149   The structural similarity between LLMs and dense retrievers provides an explanation for why
150   confidence is a suitable criterion for LLM’s FoK. Furthermore, this perspective provides insights for
151   improving method with confidence score.
152   First, the mechanism of a dense retriever is retrieving knowledge by measuring the relevance scores
153   or distances (e.g., inner product, cosine similarity) between the query vector and document vectors. A
154   basic LM-powered dense retriever such as DPR (Karpukhin et al., 2020) consists of a question encoder
155   and a vector database (DB). Embedding vector in the DB compressively represents a paragraph that
156   contains factual knowledge. When a query is input, scores between this question vector and all
157   the document vectors are computed. And the document vector with the highest score (or minimal
158
      distance) is retrieved, which is referred to as maximum inner product search (MIPS).
159   The distance can also be used to determine whether certain knowledge exists in the database. Previous
160   research on the dense retriever system such as Faiss (Douze et al., 2024) suggests range search,
161   which finds all the document vectors that are within some distance threshold. This concept can be
      interpreted in reverse that we can evaluate whether knowledge is within the vector DB, with a fixed


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      confidence threshold. For example, querying “Give me an explanation on Lincoln” to a vector DB
163   of natural sciences may return only a few documents with confidence scores above the threshold.
164   Querying “Give me an explanation on Newton” would likely yield more documents surpassing the
165   threshold, indicating greater alignment between the query and the knowledge in the DB.
166
      As an LLM is structurally analogous to a dense retriever, its confidence score can also be utilized to
167
      assess the containment of certain knowledge. Specifically, the transformer body of LLM corresponds
168
      to a question encoder, and its token vocabulary corresponds to a vector DB, if we assume each vocab
169   represents a piece of knowledge. When the LLM inputs a query, the final layer outputs a hidden state
170   (output embedding vector), which corresponds to the question embedding. The LM-head (decoder
171   linear layer of the LM) conducts MIPS between this output vector and all of the token vectors, thus
172   searching for the token with the highest confidence.
173
      For more structural analogy, the output embedding vector should contains densely compressed
174
      information. Unlike the dense retriever, the generative LLM often infers an output vector that holds
175   concept with low information density or focus only on the grammatical context, thus mapping to
176   tokens with minimal semantic significance, such as “a” or “It”. Therefore, we can consider that
177   LLM becomes structurally similar to a dense retriever, only if a single output vector of LLM is
178   semantically compressed: the output vector intensively aligns with vocab embeddings that contain
179   key concept of the answer. As an intuitive example of semantic compression, for the question “What
180   is the job of Abraham Lincoln?”, an answer “president” is more semantically compressed than an
181   answer such as “I know that Abraham Lincoln was a president”, as it carries essential information in
182   fewer expression. The semantic compression may be feasible to only a limited extent, because the
183
      vocab embedding vector is not of high density itself, containing only fragments of words (e.g., “Pr”,
      “Ch”). Though it is nontrivial, we provide the approximate compression method in the §4.3, which
184
      empirically proves enhancement in FoK performance.

189   4.1   TASK D EFINITION : F O K B EFORE A NSWERING
190
191   We define the FoK task as a binary classification to determine whether our target LLM θ possesses
192   the knowledge to correctly answer a given question q, based on the inner state of θ. In particular,
193   this process must be completed before answer generation. To test this task, we first need to create a
194   FoK dataset D, by letting the target LLM θ solve a question-answering benchmark. The benchmark
195   should consist of question q and the entity label z (e.g., “Lincoln”).
196   FoK dataset buildup dd To build the FoK dataset, we employ the setting of Zhang et al. (2024). The
197   θ is given q to generate answer a for the length of 50 tokens. It is then checked whether the entity
198   label z is contained in a, utilizing a string match with the normalized case. If z is present in a, the
199   FoK label y is annotated True (or 1), otherwise as False (or 0). As a result, we get the FoK dataset
200   D = {(q1 , y1 ), (q2 , y2 ), ..., (qn , yn ), }.
201   FoK task dd FoK module with learnable parameter, ϕ, is trained on D to input q and predict y. Any
202   method can be applied, including extracting the hidden state of θ seeing q and then training ϕ to
203   utilize this to predict y. ϕ can be simply a threshold or a deep neural network. In our work, we
204   assume a scenario in which the answer token generation step of θ must be ≤ 1.
205
      Real-world inference dd For a real-world scenario, we assume that when the system receives a
206
      question, it first performs a rapid FoK assessment. Based on this, the system decides whether to allow
207   the LLM to start generating detailed answers, decline to answer, or leverage tools such as a retriever.

316   5     AQE: A SSESSING Q UESTION - DEPENDENCY E FFECT OF F O K DATASET
317
318   For a more precise evaluation of FoK, we provide a metric that can assess whether a dataset is fit for
319   evaluating model-awareness. While FoK is the ability to be aware of whether LLM possesses specific
320
      knowledge, what
                  Pn we can explicitly measure is only the amount of incorrect answers (hallucinations,
      denote H = i=1 1(yi = False)), which is not enough to measure self-awareness.
321
322   Causes of incorrect answers can be naively divided into two: question-dependent and model-
323   dependent. (1) Question-dependent: This occurs when the question is difficult or unanswerable
      (denote Q). (2) Model-dependent: This happens when the model lacks the necessary knowledge to


                                                        6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      answer the question (denote M ). When H = Q + M , FoK refers to predicting the portion of M .
325   Since H is measurable, we should measure Q to accurately determine M . But measuring Q directly
326   is non-trivial, as it is for M .
327
328
      To address this, we propose a method to approximately measure the Q. Our approach starts from the
      definition of Q: the portion of incorrect answers (H) that can be predicted solely with the properties of
329
      the question, independent of θ. To fit this definition, we train and test a model ϕ to predict y = False
330
      (incorrect answer) case using only the question as input. The closer Q and H, the easier ϕ predict y.
331   This accuracy of ϕ is the AQE score. The closer AQE is to 1, the lower the model-dependency of the
332   dataset (paired with a certain model), making it unsuitable for measuring FoK.
333
334
      We observe that the dataset with a high AQE score contains questions nearly impossible to answer
      correctly. We analyze this arises from the failure to properly constrain the one-to-many mapping
335
      between question and answer, which can be considered misannotation. The SimpleQA, which
336
      recorded the highest AQE (82%), contains questions like “What is a Western genre on Netflix?”.
337   Though there are countless Western genre movies on Netflix, this dataset provides only one label
338   ("Rawhide"). Even if the LM possesses extensive knowledge about Netflix, any answer other than
339   "Rawhide" will be labeled as incorrect. As there are multiple similar types of questions (e.g., “What
340   is a romance genre on Netflix?”, “What is a action genre on Netflix?”), this can cause the bias that
341   any question on Netfilx is paired with only a negative FoK label. This bias makes the FoK dataset
342   question-dependent, raising the AQE score. Even if a FoK method achieves a high score on such a
343   dataset, it is unlikely to predict M well on other datasets, or even H.
344   In contrast, Mintaka, which has a lower AQE (60%), contains questions with detailed information to
345   ensure that each question has only one label (e.g., “Who was the first wife of Queen Elizabeth II’s
346   eldest son?”). Such questions may appear as detailed tail questions but help prevent the misannotation,
347   resulting in lower AQE. ParaRel contains questions vulnerable to misannotation, as ParaRel ID (in-
348   domain) shows a high AQE score. However, the separation of the out-of-domain test dataset seems to
349   control the possibility of finding question-dependent shortcuts, resulting in a low AQE score. We
350   describe further benchmark setting in §6.2.1

      6.1       BASELINE AND M ETRIC
393
      Our baseline should predict FoK label y from question q without letting the target LLM θ in-
394
      fer more than two steps. As this scenario has not been extensively explored, there are limited
395   methodologies available. We utilize LLaMA-34 (Meta-Llama-3-8B-Instruct), one of the
396   most advanced generative models. Additionally, we include experimental results from the larger
397   (Llama-2-13b-chat) model. Further details on the baselines are in Appendix D.2.
398
      Confidence-Based Methodsd The baselines based on the confidence are as described in §4.2. For
399
      notation, SCAOthre is a threshold-based method while SCAOdnn is DNN-based. SCAOprob is the
400
      feature fusion model described in §4.4. Somewordsthre is threshold-based, but utilizes normal
401   instruction rather than SCAO.
402
403   Probingdd We employ the linear probing method of Li et al. (2024); Azaria & Mitchell (2023);
404
      Mallen et al. (2022). As the LLM has H hidden layers, h ∈ {1, 2, ...H}, We train H number of a
      FoK module ϕh corresponding to each hth hidden state from the first token of answer. Each ϕh is
405
      trained to input hth hidden state to predict y. Then, only one ϕh with the highest accuracy on the
406
      validation dataset is used for the test session. For notation, Probe (Linear) indicates that the ϕh
407   is a linear regression, while Probe (DNN) indicates that ϕh is 3-layer DNN.
408
409   R-tuningdd R-tuning (Zhang et al., 2024) is a method to train LLM to tell by itself whether it knows
410
      certain knowledge in yes or no. While the original work conducted R-tuning with the data form
      of “question + answer + sure/unsure expression” (note as R-tuning), we also train with the form
411
      without answer “question + sure/unsure expression” (note as R-tuning (q only), as we assume a
412
      before-generation FoK scenario.
413
414   Metric: accuracy, AUROCddOur metric is measuring whether ϕ accurately predicts the FoK label y,
415   as the FoK label indicates whether the LLM θ properly answered the question. Previous hallucination
416
      detection studies (Chen et al., 2024; Ren et al., 2023) commonly use the AUROC metric to measure
      this, due to the binary nature of the task. This metric applies all possible thresholds to the probability
417
      score inferred by the model. However, as a common real-world setup allows only one fixed threshold,
418
      accuracy better reflects the actual performance experienced by users. Thus, we mainly focus on
419   accuracy while we still include the AUROC to keep consistency with previous works.

      A    C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
703
704   By leveraging the concept that LLMs and retrievers are structurally analogous, we suggest a hypothesis
705   that semantic compression enhances the utility of confidence of LLM for the FoK task. And we
706   demonstrate it through extensive experiments. Particularly, SCAO exhibited a clear performance
707   improvement in straightforward entity recall tasks, while the gains were relatively modest for
708   questions requiring multiple reasoning steps. Additionally, there remain limitations and future
709   research subjects on the following topics.
710
711   Semantic compression of vocab embedding vectors. As described in §3, the output embedding
712   vector is semantically compressed, while the vocab embedding vector is still of low density. This
713   property might limit the extent of compression, resulting in limited performance gain. If we find a way
714
      to compress the vocab embedding vector to represent knowledge more densely, we may anticipate
      further improvement in FoK performance.
715
716
      FoK on the temporally evolving knowledge. SCAO relies on LLM’s confidence to determine FoK,
717
      making it hard to handle temporally evolving knowledge. This is a common issue of all approaches
718   that utilize LLM’s inner state, including probing. We can gain insights from the neuro-cognitive
719   domain. According to Gündoğar & Demirci (2007), humans store time-related information alongside
720   knowledge memories. When retrieving memories, the temporal relevance of the information is
721   unconsciously evaluated. A similar idea is proposed in the dense retriever literature (Liska et al., 2022),
722   where document embeddings are encoded with temporal metadata and retrieved with consideration
723   of temporal context. Applying this concept, if a system is developed where LLMs store information
724   with temporal grounding, the confidence score could reflect the temporal relevance of the information.
725   Our work is significant as it contributes to this ultimate solution.

      Comparison with full sentence generation scenario. We assume that after-generation approaches
      are provided with more information, thus yielding better FoK performances. However, extensive
728
      experimentation is required to investigate the performance gap.

937   D.2.1    SCAO WITH FINETUNING
938
939   In this part, we describe the finetuning method to achieve SCAO, training θ with a focused an-
940   swering pattern. The process involves the following step: (1) We build a dataset DSCAO with a
941
      random 0.5 portion of the training dataset that consists of a sentence with the form of “question
      + instruction + one-word answer”. The one-word answer is the entity label, with the
942
      grammatical prefix (e.g., “The ”) removed. For example, “[Question]:What is the job of Lincoln?
943
      Answer in only one word. [Answer]:President.”. (2) We attach a LoRA adapter (Hu et al., 2021) π to
944   θ, train π on the DSCAO . (3) On the inference time, let π infer P with SCAO instruction again. π is
945   an adapter just for performing FoK, and the real answer exhibited on the service is generated by only
946   θ.
947
948   D.2.2    R- TUNING
949
950   Distinct from the original work, we train a separate LoRA adapter as a ϕ, then let ϕ predict the FoK
951
      label of the body LLM θ. The original work directly trains θ itself as a ϕ. This modification is to
      address the catastrophic forgetting problem (Jang et al., 2021) during training θ directly. We observe
952
      that the True rate decreases by 13.2%p after R-tuning, seriously undermining the justification of the
953
      method. We train for one epoch with a global batch size of 16 and a learning rate of 1e-5, as it is
954   reported that a small batch size is better for R-tuning.

1069
1070   Semantic Compression Compression of LLMs is currently explored, with most studies focusing
1071   on scenarios where a large knowledge is compressed into few or a single embedding vector (Ge et al.,
1072   2024; Cheng et al., 2024). This vector is not projected into the token space but is directly fed into
1073
       the LLM, functioning like an externally supplied hidden state vector. This vector is utilized as a
       replacement for real text documents in a scenario of retriever-augmented generation.
1074
1075   Our method shares a similar concept of compression, but assumes distinct scenarios and pipelines.
1076   In our work, the compressed vector is projected to the token embedding space, and the distances
1077   between the compressed vector and token embedding vectors become a key variable. This distance is
1078   utilized as a measure of FoK.
1079



                                                        20
