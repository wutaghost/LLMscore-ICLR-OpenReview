009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            While cryptographic algorithms such as the ubiquitous Advanced Encryption Stan-
012            dard (AES) are secure, physical implementations of these algorithms in hardware
013            inevitably вҖҳleakвҖҷ sensitive information such as cryptographic keys. A particularly
014            insidious form of leakage arises from the fact that hardwareвҖҷs power consumption
015            over time is statistically associated with the data it processes and the instructions
016            it executes. Supervised deep learning has emerged as a state-of-the-art tool for
017            carrying out power side-channel attacks, which exploit this leakage to break cryp-
018
               tographic implementations by learning to map power consumption measurements
               recorded during encryption to the secret key used for that encryption. In this work,
019
               we seek instead to develop a principled deep learning framework for defense against
020
               such attacks by understanding the relative leakage due to power measurements
021            recorded at different points in time. This information is invaluable to cryptographic
022            hardware designers for understanding why their hardware leaks and how they can
023            mitigate the leakage (e.g. by indicating that a particular section of code or electronic
024            component is responsible for leakage and should be revised). Towards this end,
025            we propose a novel deep learning algorithm by formulating an adversarial game
026            played between a classifier trained to estimate the conditional distribution of a
027            key given power measurements, and an вҖҳobfuscatorвҖҷ which probabilistically erases
028            individual power measurements and is trained to minimize the classifier-estimated
029
               log-likelihood of the correct key, subject to a penalty on erasure probability. We
               theoretically characterize the ideal output of our algorithm in terms of conditional
030
               mutual information quantities involving the key and individual power measure-
031
               ments. We then empirically demonstrate the efficacy of our algorithm on real
032            and synthetic datasets of power measurements from implementations of the AES
033            cryptographic standard. Our code can be found in the supplementary materials.

036   1    I NTRODUCTION
037
038   The Advanced Encryption Standard (AES) (Daemem & Rijmen, 1999; Daemen & Rijmen, 2013) is
039   widely used and trusted for protecting sensitive data. For example, it is approved by the United States
040   National Security Agency for protecting top secret information (Committee on National Security
041   Systems, 2003), it is a major component of the Transport Layer Security (TLS) protocol (Rescorla,
042
      2000) which underlies the security of HTTPS (Rescorla, 2000), and is used in payment card readers
      to secure card information before transmission to financial institutions (Bluefin Payment Systems,
043
      2023).
044
045   AES aims to keep data secret when it is transmitted over insecure channels that are accessible to
046   unknown and untrusted parties (e.g. via wireless transmissions which may be intercepted, or storage
047   on hard drives which may be accessed by untrusted individuals). Prior to transmission, the data is first
048
      encoded and partitioned into a sequence of fixed-length bitstrings called plaintexts. Each plaintext is
      then encrypted into a ciphertext by applying an invertible function from a family of functions indexed
049
      by an integer called a cryptographic key. This family of functions is designed so that if the key is
050
      sampled uniformly at random, then the plaintext and ciphertext are marginally independent. The key
051   is known to the sender and intended recipients of the transmission,1 and is kept secret from potential
052
         1
053        For example, the key may be exchanged and periodically updated using an asymmetric-key cryptographic
      algorithm such as RSA or Diffie-Hellman (Paar & Pelzl, 2010, ch. 6) Such algorithms do not require the sender


                                                           1
054
      eavesdroppers. Thus, the intended recipients can use the key to decrypt the ciphertext back into the
055   original plaintext, while eavesdroppers who possess the ciphertext but not the key learn nothing about
056   the plaintext.
057
058
      Clearly, such an algorithm is effective only if the cryptographic key remains outside of the hands
      of eavesdroppers. AES is believed to be вҖҳalgorithmically secureвҖҷ in the sense that given an AES
059
      implementation with a fixed key, it is not feasible to determine the key by encrypting a chosen
060
      sequence of plaintexts and observing the resulting ciphertexts (Mouha, 2021). For reference, to our
061   knowledge, the best known attack on the 128-bit version of AES under realistic conditions would
062   require about 2125 such encryptions on average to successfully determine the key (Tao & Wu, 2015),
063   compared to 2127 encryptions for a naive brute-force attack which randomly guesses and checks keys
064   until success.
065
      Despite the вҖҳalgorithmicвҖҷ security of AES and other cryptographic algorithms, physical implementa-
066
      tions of these algorithms in hardware inevitably вҖҳleakвҖҷ information about their cryptographic keys.
067   This phenomenon, called side-channel leakage, occurs because hardware produces measurable physi-
068   cal signals that are statistically associated with the data it processes and the instructions it executes.
069   In this work, we consider power side-channel leakage, i.e. statistical association between a deviceвҖҷs
070   cryptographic key and its power consumption over time while encrypting data, which is a major secu-
071   rity vulnerability for AES implementations (Kocher et al., 1999; Bronchain & Standaert, 2020). Note,
072   however, that hardware emits many diverse physical signals which cause side-channel leakage, such
073   as electromagnetic radiation (Quisquater & Samyde, 2001; Genkin et al., 2016), program/operation
074   execution time (Kocher, 1996; Lipp et al., 2018; Kocher et al., 2019), and sound due to vibration of
075
      electronic components (Genkin et al., 2014). Refer to appendix A for some simple intuition-building
      examples of side-channel leakage.
076
077   Cryptographic implementations can be circumvented by side-channel attacks, which exploit side-
078   channel leakage to learn the cryptographic key of some target device. In this work, we consider
079   profiling power side-channel attacks, in which the attacker is assumed to possess a clone of the
080   target device and can repeatedly measure its power consumption over time while encrypting arbitrary
081
      plaintexts using arbitrary keys. Measured power consumption during encryption is encoded as a real
      vector called a power trace, where each element encodes the power measurement at a fixed point in
082
      time relative to the start of encryption. Attackers can use the clone device to model the conditional
083
      distribution of the cryptographic key given the power trace, and can then collect power traces from
084   the target device and identify the key which maximizes the likelihood of the key and power traces
085   according to their model.
086
087
      Supervised deep learning has emerged as a state-of-the-art technique for carrying out profiled power
      side-channel attacks, achieving comparable or superior performance to prior approaches with far less
088
      data preprocessing and feature selection (Maghrebi et al., 2016; Benadjila et al., 2020; Zaid et al.,
089
      2020; Wouters et al., 2020; Bursztein et al., 2023). The limitations of classical (non-deep learning)
090   attacks include assuming specific forms for the conditional distribution of keys given traces (Chari
091   et al., 2003; Schindler et al., 2005; Hospodar et al., 2011), requiring feature selection or principal
092   component analysis to significantly reduce the dimensionality of power traces (Chari et al., 2003;
093   Archambeau et al., 2006), and limited ability to exploit n-th order leakage where n > 1, i.e. where
094   the key is dependent on a set of n measurements but independent of all of its subsets with cardinality
095   less than n (Messerges, 2000; Agrawal et al., 2005). In contrast, neural nets are asymptotically
096   universal function approximators, and hence can in principle represent nearly arbitrary conditional
097   distributions (Hornik et al., 1989). CNNs and transformers have proven capable of operating on raw
098
      power traces without feature selection or dimensionality reduction (Lu et al., 2021; Bursztein et al.,
      2023), and neural nets are effective against вҖҳmaskingвҖҷ countermeasures which exploit the difficulty of
099
      exploiting 2nd -order leakage with classical attacks (Benadjila et al., 2020; Zaid et al., 2020; Wouters
100
      et al., 2020). Thus, deep learning is a major threat to a wide assortment of security measures and
101   evaluations that were designed with the limitations of classical attacks in mind.
102
103   In this work, we seek to instead leverage deep learning to defend against side-channel attacks by
104
      identifying specific points in time at which power consumption is вҖҳusefulвҖҷ for predicting the key. Our
      intent is to enable the designers of implementations to understand why their implementations leak
105
      (e.g. which machine instructions are responsible, whether their countermeasures are effective), as

      exchange and to use AES for transmission of large quantities of data.

176

177   (conditional) mutual information (Shannon, 1948) between Xt and Y :

189   carries out the first SubBytes operation at time t, we expect to have I[Y ; Xt ] > 0.
190

      Unlike for our synthetic datasets, here we lack ground truth knowledge about which power measure-
508
      ments are leaking, and it is not established or obvious how to evaluate the fidelity of an attempt to
509
      localize leakage. Masure et al. (2019) and Hettwer et al. (2020) propose several metrics and heuristics
510   based on the intuition that the вҖҳleakinessвҖҷ assigned to a set of measurements should positively-correlate
511   with the performance of a side-channel attack carried out using only those measurements. We propose
512   our own metric (see appendix C.2.1) which is inspired by these but addresses shortcomings that they
513   have. The rough idea is to perform many Gaussian template attacks (Chari et al., 2003) on subsets
514   of available power measurements and compute the expected Kendall П„ rank correlation coefficient
515   (Kendall, 1938) between a set of points and its estimated leakage. A higher expected rank correlation
516   indicates that estimated-leakier points are indeed more exploitable to side-channel attackers, and thus
517   indicates higher fidelity of the leakage localization attempt.
518   In table 1 we report the performance of our method and considered baselines. Figure 4 contains plots
519   of the amount of leakage at each timestep as estimated by each method. We find qualitatively-similar
520   results for all methods apart from our random baseline, apart from the first-order statistical methods
521   on ASCADv1 which are unable to detect the second-order statistical associations which result from
522   Boolean masking. It appears that the deep learning-based methods assign significant leakage to non-
523   leaky timesteps, likely due to overfitting. Our synthetic dataset results suggest that this issue may be
524
      alleviated in settings where we have access to infinitely-large datasets (e.g. in the pre-manufacturing
      phase where we can simulate arbitrarily-many synthetic power traces for a design).

527   6      C ONCLUSION
528
529   We have proposed a novel, principled algorithm for learning to localize power side channel leakage
530   from cryptographic algorithms, and have demonstrated its efficacy on real and synthetic implementa-
531   tions of the ubiquitous AES cryptographic standard. Our algorithm is generic enough to be applicable
532   to other cryptographic standards as well. The growing assortment of deep learning-based side-channel
533
      attacks departs from classical attacks in that the DL-based attacks can largely treat cryptographic
      hardware as a black box to be characterized using data, whereas classical techniques required strong
534
      assumptions and knowledge about the particular device to be attacked. However, despite the demon-
535
      strated ability of deep learning to exploit side-channel leakage without a priori knowledge about its
536   existence or nature, little work thus far exists on leveraging this ability to understand why devices leak,
537   which is critical for designing countermeasures against side-channel attacks. Our work represents an
538   initial step towards using deep learning to uncover power side-channel leakage without relying on
539   human understanding of the complicated and non-ideal device physics by which the leakage happens.

843
844   A.1     C RYPTOGRAPHIC ALGORITHMS
845
846   Data is often transmitted over insecure channels which leave it accessible not only to intended
847   recipients, but also to unknown and untrusted parties. For example, when a signal is wirelessly
848   transmitted from one antenna to another, an eavesdropper could set up a third antenna between the
849   two and intercept the signal. Alternately, data stored on a hard drive by one user of a computer may
850   be accessed by a different user. Cryptographic algorithms aim to preserve the privacy of data under
851
      such circumstances by transforming it so that it is meaningful only in combination with additional
      data which is known to its intended recipients but not to the untrusted parties.
852
853   In this work we consider the advanced encryption standard (AES), which is a symmetric-key cryp-
854   tographic algorithm. See figure 5 for a diagram illustrating the important components of such
855   algorithms. The unencrypted data to be transmitted is encoded and partitioned into a sequence of
856   fixed-length bitstrings called plaintexts. The cryptographic algorithm encrypts each plaintext into a
857
      ciphertext by applying an invertible function from a set of functions indexed by an integer called the
      cryptographic key. This set of functions is designed so that of one were to sample a key and plaintext
858
      uniformly at random from the sets of all possible keys and plaintexts, then the plaintext and ciphertext
859
      would be marginally independent. Thus, such an algorithm may be used to securely transmit data by
860   ensuring that the sender and recipient of the data know a shared key,2 and that the key is kept secret
861   from all potential eavesdroppers on the data.
862

      A.2    SIDE - CHANNEL ATTACKS
865
866
      Many symmetric-key cryptographic algorithms are believed to be secure in the sense that it is not
867   feasible to determine their cryptographic key by encrypting known plaintexts and observing the
868   resulting ciphertexts. Any such algorithm with a finite number of possible keys is vulnerable to
869   вҖҳbrute-forceвҖҷ attacks based on arbitrarily guessing and checking keys until success, but doing so
870   requires checking half of all possible keys in the average case, which is unrealistic for algorithms
871   such as AES which has either 2128 , 2192 , or 2256 possible keys. To our knowledge the best known
872   such attack against AES reduces the required number of guesses by less than a factor of 8 compared
873   to a naive brute force attack (Mouha, 2021; Tao & Wu, 2015).
874   However, while algorithms may be secure when considering only their intended inputs and outputs,
875   hardware executing these algorithms will inevitably emit measurable physical signals which are statis-
876   tically associated with their intermediate variables and operations. Examples of such signals include
877   a deviceвҖҷs power consumption over time (Kocher et al., 1999), the amount of time it takes to execute
878   a program or instruction (Kocher, 1996; Lipp et al., 2018; Kocher et al., 2019), electromagnetic
879   radiation it emits (Quisquater & Samyde, 2001; Genkin et al., 2016), and sound due to vibrations of
880   its electronic components (Genkin et al., 2014). This phenomenon is called side-channel leakage,
881
      and can be exploited to determine sensitive data such as a cryptographic key through side-channel
      attacks.
882
883   As a simple example of side-channel leakage, consider the following Python function which checks
884   whether a password is correct:
885
886   def is_correct ( provided_password : str , correct_password : str ) -> bool :
887       if len ( provided_password ) != len ( correct_password ):
              return False
888
          for i in range ( len ( provided_password )):
889           if provided_password [i] != correct_password [i ]:
890                 return False
891       return True
892
893
      Suppose the password consists of n characters, each with c possible values. Consider an at-
894
      tacker seeking to determine the correct password by feeding various guessed passwords until
895   the function returns True. Naively, the attacker could simply guess and check all possible m-
896   length passwords for m = 1, . . . , n. This would require O(cn ) calls to the function, which
897   would be extremely costly for realistically-large c and n. However, an attacker with knowledge
898   of the functionвҖҷs implementation could dramatically reduce this cost by observing that the func-
899   tionвҖҷs execution time depends on correct_password. Because the function exits immediately if
900   len(provided_password) != len(correct_password), the attacker can determine the length of
901   correct_password in O(n) time by feeding increasing-length guesses to is_correct until its exe-
902   cution time increases. Next, because is_correct exits the first time it detects an incorrect character,
903
      the attacker can sequentially determine each of the characters of correct_password by checking all
      c possible values of each character and noting that the correct value leads to an increase in execution
904
      time. Thus, although is_correct secure against attackers which use only its intended inputs and
905
      outputs, it provides essentially no security against attackers which measure its execution time.

945   A.3     P OWER SIDE - CHANNEL ATTACKS ON AES IMPLEMENTATIONS
946
947   Side-channel attacks are techniques which exploit side-channel leakage to learn sensitive information
948   such as cryptographic keys. There are many categories of attacks, but in this work we focus on a cate-
949   gory called profiled side-channel attacks on symmetric-key cryptographic algorithms. These attacks
950   assume that the вҖҳattackerвҖҷ has access to a clone of the actual cryptographic device to be attacked,
951   and the ability to encrypt arbitrary plaintexts with arbitrary cryptographic keys, observe the resulting
952
      ciphertexts, and measure the side-channel leakage during encryption. In practice, these assumptions
      almost certainly overestimate the capabilities of attackers вҖ“ for example, while in some cases an
953
      attacker could plausibly identify the hardware and source code of a cryptographic implementation,
954
      purchase copies of this hardware, program them with the source code, and characterize these devices,
955   the nature of the side-channel leakage of these purchased copies would differ from those of the actual
956   device due to imperfect manufacturing processes. It has been demonstrated that profiled side-channel
957   attacks can be effective despite this, especially when numerous copies of the target hardware are used
958   for profiling (Das et al., 2019; Danial et al., 2021). Regardless, this type of attack provides an upper
959   bound on the vulnerability of a device to side-channel attacks, which is a useful metric for hardware
960   designers.
961   While there are diverse types of profiled side-channel attacks, at a high level the following steps
962   encompass the important elements of these attacks:
963
964          1. Select some вҖҳsensitiveвҖҷ intermediate variable of the cryptographic algorithm which reveals
965             the cryptographic key (or part of it).
966
             2. Compile a dataset of (side-channel leakage, intermediate variable) pairs by repeatedly
967
                randomly selecting a key and plaintext, encrypting the plaintext using the key and recording
968
                the resulting ciphertext and side-channel leakage during encryption, and computing the
969             intermediate variable based on knowledge of the cryptographic algorithm.
970
971          3. Use supervised learning to train a parametric function approximator to predict intermediate
                variables from recordings of side-channel leakage during encryption.


                                                         18
972
                  4. Measure side-channel leakage during encryptions by the actual target device. Use the
973                  trained predictor to predict sensitive variables from side-channel leakage. Potentially, these
974                  predictions can be combined to get a better estimate of the key.
975
976         In the case of power side-channel attacks on AES, it is generally infeasible to directly target the
977         cryptographic key because care is taken by hardware designers to prevent it from directly influencing
978         power consumption. Instead, it is common to target an intermediate variable called the SubBytes

1041

1042

       We base our synthetic power trace datasets on the Hamming weight leakage model of Mangard
1642
       et al. (2007, ch. 4). This model3 assumes that we have a device which executes a cryptographic
1643
       algorithm as a sequence of operations on data. As above, let X := (Xt : t = 1, . . . , T ) be a random
1644   vector with range RT which encodes power consumption. Let D := (Dt : t = 1, . . . , T ) and
1645   O := (Ot : t = 1, . . . , T ) be random vectors denoting the data and operations, respectively, where
1646   each Dt has range {0, 1}nbits (i.e. a sequence of nbits bits) and each Ot has range [1 .. nops ] for some
1647   nbits , nops вҲҲ Z++ . For each t вҲҲ [1 .. T ], we can decompose

1833   C.2.1    E VALUATING PERFORMANCE WITHOUT GROUND TRUTH KNOWLEDGE ABOUT LEAKAGE
1834
1835   For synthetic datasets it is easy to tell whether the output of our algorithm is reasonable because
       we know the points in time at which power consumption is directly affected by sensitive variables.


                                                             34
1836
       However, we lack this knowledge for real datasets and in our motivating setting, which is hardware
1837   designers seeking to understand why their cryptographic implementation leaks. Intuitively, a power
1838   measurement is вҖҳmore-leakyвҖҷ if it is more-useful for performing a side-channel attack. Based on
1839   this intuition, we propose a novel performance metric based on the extent to which the leakage
1840   value assigned to a power measurement positively correlates with the performance gain of a profiled
1841   side-channel attack on partial power traces which includes this measurement. This metric is similar
1842   to the evaluation methods of Masure et al. (2019) and Hettwer et al. (2020), while overcoming
1843   limitations of both.
1844   In Masure et al. (2019), the authors propose the Gradient Visualization techinque and evaluate its
1845   efficacy by performing a Gaussian template attack on the вҖҳmost-leakyвҖҷ points it has identified, with
1846   better template attack performance indicating that the identified points were вҖҳleakierвҖҷ. The limitation
1847   of this approach is that while it is sensitive to true-positive leakage detection, it is not sensitive to
1848   false-negatives.
1849   In Hettwer et al. (2020), the authors evaluate several existing neural network attribution techniques as
1850   ways to localize leakage. They propose several performance metrics which are all conceptually-similar
1851   to the following: 1) Train a deep neural network to perform a side-channel attack. 2) Successively
1852   replace individual timesteps with random noise in order of their estimated вҖҳleakinessвҖҷ. 3) Visually
1853   assess how the performance of the neural net changes as points are replaced. It is expected that
1854   performance will decrease monotonically as points are replaced, and a steeper negative slope early
1855   on indicates that вҖҳleakierвҖҷ points have been identified. The limitations of this approach are that it
1856   requires visual inspection of the curves, and that because the same style of neural net is used for
1857
       both leakage localization and evaluation of the leakage assessment, there is a risk that evaluations are
       biased towards types of leakage which happen to be useful to that style of neural net.
1858
1859   To evaluate the performance of a leakage assessment, we sort the timesteps based on their estimated
1860   leakage and partition the sorted points into sets of 10. We fit a Gaussian mixture model (similar to a
1861   Gaussian template attack) to each of these sets of 10 measurements and fit a Gaussian distribution
1862   to the rank of the correct key for each power trace in the conditional distribution returned by the
1863
       model. We then compute a вҖҳsoftenedвҖҷ version of the Kendall П„ rank correlation coefficient between
       the amount of leakage estimated for a set of points and the mean rank assigned to the correct key. It
1864
       is вҖҳsoftenedвҖҷ in the sense that we compute the expected value of the difference between concordant
1865
       and discordant pair counts. See algorithm 5 for details. We use the Kendall П„ correlation coefficient
1866   because it checks only the monotonicity of a curve, rather than its shape. We model the performance
1867   metrics as random variables because there are generally many performance evaluations which are
1868   statistically-indistinguishable but may differ due to random change, and expectation is a natural way
1869   to вҖҳdownweightвҖҷ the pairs of measurements for which this the case.
1870
