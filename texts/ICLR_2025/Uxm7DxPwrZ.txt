                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
012            Offline Reinforcement Learning (RL) has emerged as a powerful alternative to
013
               imitation learning for behavior modeling in various domains, particularly in com-
               plex navigation tasks. An existing challenge with Offline RL is the signal-to-noise
014
               ratio, i.e. how to mitigate incorrect policy updates due to errors in value estimates.
015
               Towards this, multiple works have demonstrated the advantage of hierarchical of-
016            fline RL methods, which decouples high-level path planning from low-level path
017            following. In this work, we present a novel hierarchical transformer-based ap-
018            proach leveraging a learned quantizer of the space. This quantization enables the
019            training of a simpler zone-conditioned low-level policy and simplifies planning,
020            which is reduced to discrete autoregressive prediction. Among other benefits,
021            zone-level reasoning in planning enables explicit trajectory stitching rather than
022            implicit stitching based on noisy value function estimates. By combining this
023            transformer-based planner with recent advancements in offline RL, our proposed
024
               approach achieves state-of-the-art results in complex long-distance navigation en-
               vironments.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Navigation and locomotion in complex, embodied environments is a long-standing challenge within
031   Machine Learning (Kaelbling et al., 1996; Sutton & Barto, 2018). Operating non-trivial agents in
032   complex spaces is critical in a wide range of real-world applications, such as in robotics or in the
033   video game industry. A core difficulty of navigation lies in solving long-horizon tasks that require
034   intricate path planning (Hoang et al., 2021; Park et al., 2024). In the Reinforcement Learning (RL)
035   setting (Sutton & Barto, 2018), traditional online Goal-Conditioned deep Reinforcement Learning
036
      (GCRL) methods often struggle with such long-horizon tasks because of the sparse nature of the
      reward signal, leading to hard exploration problems. Offline GCRL circumvents this exploration
037
      problem by leveraging large amounts of unlabeled and diverse demonstration data to learn policies
038
      through passive learning (Prudencio et al., 2023). A core advantage of offline RL compared to
039   other forms of behavior extraction from datasets – for instance imitation learning – is the ability to
040   improve over suboptimal datasets, e.g. by learning state value functions to bias the learned policy
041   towards rewarding actions (Kostrikov et al., 2021). However, offline RL is not trivial to apply for
042   long-horizon goal-reaching tasks, which often provide sparse reward signals, leading to a noisy value
043   function which consequently hinders the performance of the policy. Part of this issue comes from
044   low ”signal-to-noise” ratio to learn the value function (Park et al., 2024). Because a suboptimal
045   action can be corrected quickly in subsequent steps of a trajectory, its impact on the real value
046   for faraway goals can be covered by the value prediction noise, which can lead to the learning of
047
      suboptimal behaviors for the policy.
048   Hierarchical architectures have shown considerable advantages in goal-conditioned navigation to
049   solve such issues (Vezhnevets et al., 2017; Pertsch et al., 2021; Park et al., 2024). These approaches
050   effectively decompose the problem into two distinct components: high-level path planning and low-
051   level path following. Among these, Hierarchical Implicit Q-Learning (HIQL) (Park et al., 2024) has
052   recently emerged as the state-of-the-art method. HIQL leverages a hierarchical structure to learn
053
      both a high-level policy for generating subgoals and a low-level policy for achieving these subgoals,
      all within an offline reinforcement learning framework.

      2   R ELATED WORK
082
083
      VQ-VAEs for reinforcement learning Vector Quantized Variational Autoencoders (Van
084
      Den Oord et al., 2017) have demonstrated their utility in reinforcement learning., particularly for
085
      offline setups. Jiang et al. (2022) and Luo et al. (2023) use a VQ-VAE to discretize continuous ac-
086   tion spaces, mitigating the curse of dimensionality. In contrast, QPHIL uses a VQ-VAE to quantize
087   states, simplifying waypoint generation in long-horizon tasks. These two approaches are comple-
088   mentary: action quantization focuses on simplifying policy search, while state quantization helps in
089   task decomposition such as in Hamed et al. (2024) where a VQ-VAE is used to define landmarks as
090   states like in QPHIL but without a contrastive loss and in the online setting. Within goal-conditioned
091   reinforcement learning, several works used VQ-VAE to encode observations into discrete (sub)goals.
092   Lee et al. (2024a) learn quantized goals to simplify curriculum learning. Islam et al. (2022) also use
093   quantization to map (sub)goals into discrete and factorized representations to efficiently handle novel
094
      goals at test time. Both these works focus on online goal-conditioned reinforcement learning while
      QPHIL tackles the offline learning setting. Kujanpää et al. (2023) also uses VQ-VAE to generate
095
      discrete subgoals, but while they rely on a finite subgoal set derived from offline learning, QPHIL
096
      identifies subgoals directly through the VQ-VAE. To the best of our knowledge, VQ-VAE has never
097   been applied for discrete planning in offline GCRL settings.
098
099
      Hierarchical offline learning Leveraging a hierarchical structure to decompose and simplify se-
100
      quential decision-making problems is a long-standing idea and subject of research within machine
101
      learning (Schmidhuber, 1991; Sutton et al., 1999). Recently, multiple successful works renewed
102   the interest of the research community to these models, both for online (Vezhnevets et al., 2017;
103   Pertsch et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2021; Fang et al., 2022) and offline reinforcement learning (Nachum
104   et al., 2018b; Ajay et al., 2020; Rao et al., 2021; Rosete-Beas et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; Shin
105   & Kim, 2023). Among these, Hierarchical Implicit Q-Learning (HIQL) from Park et al. (2024) has
106   emerged as the state-of-the-art method for offline goal-conditioned RL. Most methods differ in how
107   they represent and use subgoals; for instance, Nachum et al. (2018a) compare different subgoal rep-
      resentations, while Ajay et al. (2020) focus on detecting and combining primitive behaviors. While

      all aforementioned works focus on continuous state representations, QPHIL leverages the advantage
109   of discrete state representations to simplify long-distance navigation.

111   Planning Hierarchical planning methods are a natural complement to RL. For instance, HIPS (Ku-
112   janpää et al., 2023) learns to segment trajectories, generating subgoals of varying lengths to facilitate
113   adaptive planning. HIPS-ε (Jiang et al., 2023; Kujanpää et al., 2024) introduces hybrid hierarchical
114   methods and offers completeness guarantees, but their method is only usable with a discrete state
115   space. Li et al. (2022) proposes a method combining a high-level planner, based on a VAE, with
116   a low-level offline RL policy. The VAE serves as the planner by leveraging the low-level policy’s
117   value function. QPHIL, on the other hand, utilizes a simpler subgoal planning approach directly tied
118   to a discrete state-space via VQ-VAE.

120
      Transformers for (hierarchical) offline RL Transformers have recently found application in hi-
      erarchical setups to solve RL problems. In Correia & Alexandre (2023), transformers are used for
121
      hierarchical subgoal sampling, where a high-level transformer generates subgoals for a low-level
122
      transformer responsible for action selection. Similarly, Badrinath et al. (2024) combine a Decision
123   Transformer (DT) from Chen et al. (2021) with a waypoint-generation network. Ma et al. (2024)
124   present a hierarchical transformer-based approach outperforming both aforementioned methods. Li
125   et al. (2022); Ma et al. (2024) are extensions of DT that allows stitching, a known issue with De-
126   cision Transformer (Fujimoto & Gu, 2021; Emmons et al., 2021; Kostrikov et al., 2021; Yamagata
127   et al., 2023; Xiao et al., 2023). Their approach, Goal-prompted Autotuned Decision Transformer
128   (G-ADT) (Ma et al., 2024), is able to efficiently integrate an offline RL learning scheme similar to
129   HIQL. G-ADT uses a low-level transformer and a simple high level subgoal policy, while we focus
130   on the opposite scenario: our transformer is used to generate a plan of subgoal, which are then fol-
131
      lowed by a fully connected neural network. G-ADT uses an offline reinforcement learning objective
      to train its high-level policy while we rely on a simpler imitation learning objective.
132
133
      Sequence generation Lee et al. (2024b) explore sequence generation using tokens learned via
134
      a VQ-VAE, much like QPHIL; but their work does not extend to reinforcement learning. In RL,
135   sequence models have been used for various components (Bakker, 2001; Heess et al., 2015; Chiappa
136   et al., 2017; Parisotto et al., 2020; Kumar et al., 2020). However, none of these approaches integrate
137   planning over a discrete representation, which is central to our method. Sequence generation also
138   plays a key role in offline RL, where it is helping to prevent out-of-distribution actions (Fujimoto
139   et al., 2019; Kumar et al., 2019; Ghasemipour et al., 2021). Trajectory Transformer (Janner et al.,
140   2021) completely treats offline RL as a sequence generation problem. They use a transformer to
141   perform planning using Beam-Search and an IQL-like value function. Contrary to QPHIL, they use
142   a simple dimension-wise uniform or quantile discretization.
143
144   Spatial zone learning and skill discovery Zone-based spatial navigation and skill discovery are
145
      additional techniques that overlap with hierarchical RL and QPHIL. For instance in the context
      of online RL, Kamienny et al. (2022) proposes to discover a set of ”easy-to-learn” short policies,
146
      each corresponding to a given skill defined as an area of the state space, which can be eventually
147
      composed for the task at hand. Using a VQ-VAE, Mazzaglia et al. (2022) define skills that are used
148   to influence exploration in online policies. In a closer context with a known state space, Gao et al.
149   (2023) segments space into zones and uses these segments to facilitate goal-reaching tasks, but their
150   method is restricted to vision environments. Similarly, Hausman et al. (2017) uses a multi-modal
151   imitation learning approach to discover and segment skills from unstructured demonstrations, which
152   is close to the discrete landmark generation in QPHIL. In skill decision transformer Sudhakaran &
153   Risi (2023), a transformer is used to predict actions from latent variables discovered by a VQ-VAE.
154   While their approach is close to QPHIL, it differs by not using contrastive loss and not planning
155   subgoals.

      Quantizing Planner for Hierarchial Implicit Learning (QPHIL) If HIQL shows significant
210   improvements in offline GCRL problems compared to previous flat-policy methods, its performance
211   depends on the right choice of the subgoal step k. A high k would improve the high policy’s signal-
212   to-noise ratio by querying more diverse subgoals but at the cost of decreasing the signal-to-noise
213   ratio of the low policy. Conversely, a low k would improve the low policy’s signal-to-noise ratio by
214   querying values for nearby goals but at the cost of the diversity of the high subgoals. Hence, HIQL
215   might struggle for longer term goal reaching tasks as the low level performance imposes the choice
      of a sufficiently low k, which leads to high frequency noisy high subgoal targets for the low policy

220   4     Q UANTIZING P LANNER FOR H IERARCHICAL I MPLICIT L EARNING
221
222   In this paper, we propose a new hierarchical goal conditionned offline RL algorithm: Quantizing
223
      Planner for Hierarchical Implicit Q-Learning (QPHIL). While current hierarchical methods rely
      heavily on continuous waypoint predictions, we propose to consider discrete subgoals, allowing
224
      to simplify the planning process. Instead of relying on precise coordinates, QPHIL identifies key
225
      landmarks to guide trajectory planning, much like how a road trip is described by cities or highways
226   rather than specific geographic points. The algorithm detects these landmarks, creates landmark-
227   based sequences, and formulates policies to navigate between them, ultimately reaching the target
228   destination.
229

      Antmaze We measure QPHIL performance through a set of AntMaze environments of increasing
379   sizes, providing challenging long-term navigation problems. In AntMaze the agent controls an 8-
380   DoF ant-shaped robot. Observations consist in a 29-dimension state vector (e.g. positions, torso
381   coordinates and velocity, angles between leg parts). For original AntMaze environments we use
382   datasets provided in the D4RL library (Fu et al., 2020) as well as two additional datasets for even
383   larger mazes, namely Antmaze-Ultra provided by Jiang et al. (2022) and Antmaze-Extreme which
384   we created (Appendix C.2). For each maze type (medium, large, ultra and extreme), we train on two
385   types of datasets: ”play” and ”diverse”, each containing 1000 trajectories of 1000 steps. The ”play”
386   variant has been generated using hand-picked locations for the goal and starting positions while the
387
      ”diverse” variant has been generated using random goal and starting positions.

      Baselines We compare QPHIL (open-loop planning version) to 8 previous methods ranging from
      model-free behavior cloning and offline RL methods as well as model-based methods. For model-
390
      free methods, we include in our baselines the flat Goal-Conditioned Behavior Cloning (GCBC)
391
      (Ghosh et al., 2019) and the Hierarchical Goal-Conditioned Behavior Cloning (HGCBC) (Gupta
392   et al., 2019). For offline RL, we include a goal-conditioned variant of Implicit Q-Learning (GC-
393   IQL) (Kostrikov et al., 2021), a goal-conditioned variant of Policy-Guided Imitation (GC-POR) (Xu
394   et al., 2022) as well as Hierarchical Implicit Q-Learning (HIQL) (Park et al., 2024). For model-based
395   methods, we include Trajectory Transformer (TT) (Janner et al., 2021) which leverages a trans-
396   former architecture to model the entire flattened state-action sequence, Trajectory Autoencoding
397   Planner (TAP) (Jiang et al., 2022) which performs model-based planning over discrete latent actions
398   quantized by a VQ-VAE as well as Goal-prompted Autotuned Decision Transformer (G-ADT) (Ma

400
      from the very recent Planning Transformer (PT) method (Clinton & Lieck, 2024), which leverages
      the use of a decision transformer as an agent, that consumes a sequence of imagined next states of
401
      the agent (i.e., a plan) as input. As HIQL baselines and QPHIL ran on 8 seeded runs, results are

      provided with the mean ± std format.

      6     C ONCLUSION
519
520
      We proposed QPHIL, a hierarchical offline goal-conditioned reinforcement learning method that
521
      leverages pre-recorded demonstration to learn a discrete representation and temporally consistent
522   representation of the state space. QPHIL utilizes those discrete state representations to plan sub-
523   goals in a discretized space and guide a low policy towards its final goal in a human-inspired man-
524   ner. QPHIL reaches as a result top performance in challenging long-term navigation benchmarks,
525   showing promising next steps for discretization and planning in continous offline RL settings.
526
527   Limitations and next directions QPHIL is a method aimed at navigation as it aims to solve tasks
528   where a low amount of landmarks is sufficient. An interesting direction would be to analyse how to
529   leverage such discrete representation in more intricate planning settings, such as multi-task robotics
530   scenarios. Also, while leveraging the planning capabilities of QPHIL for model predictive control
531   usecases could be an interesting follow up, enhancing the learning of the landmarks to adapt their
532
      size or spread regrading the uncertainty or the importance that characterize each area also constitute
      very promising next steps.

       B.3   DATA CLEANING
988
989
       Instability in some trajectories of the dataset or an indecisive tokenizer can lead to wobbliness in
990    sequences of the dataset. For instance, successive states that are close to two tokens could lead to
991    alternating tokens in the sequence. While this gives information, it would make training harder for
992    the sequence generator. To correct this, if a state has already been seen during the last for steps, it is
993    not taken into account. Cycles can appear in sequences, especially with data augmentation. Cycles
994    in the dataset create two problems: the model would learn to generate cycles which leads to indefinite
995    repetitions of cycles at inference; and cycles create longer episodes that tends to reduce the overall
996    performance of the model. To correct this, we remove cycles from the dataset. Finally, the finite
997    number of tokens leads to repeated sequences, a problem that is exacerbated by data augmentation.
998
       To prevent the model from learning to repeat the same sequences, we remove repeated sequences
       from the dataset. This is efficiently implemented using a hash table.

1136   AntMaze is a very popular benchmark in offline reinforcement learning, and it is part of the D4RL Fu
1137   et al. (2020) dataset suite, interfaced through the Gym library Brockman et al. (2016). It uses the
1138   Mujoco physics engine Todorov et al. (2012) for its simulation.
1139
                                                       The antmaze environment consist of a maze-like struc-
1140                                                   ture in which a simulated ant agent must navigate from
1141                                                   a starting position to a goal position. Contrary to what
1142                                                   one might expect, the agent is not controlled by sim-
1143                                                   ple directional commands. Instead, the ant is controlled
1144                                                   through an 8-dimensional continuous action space. Due
1145                                                   to the complex dynamics of the ant and the intricate
1146                                                   structure of the maze, this environment poses a signifi-
1147                                                   cant challenge for both exploration and planning. Each
1148
                                                       dimension of the action space corresponds to a torque
                                                       applied to one of the ant’s joints. Values in the ac-
1149
                                                       tion space are bounded between -1 and 1 and are in
1150
                                                       Nm. Hence, A = [−1, 1]9 . The observation space is a
1151                                                   29-dimensional continuous space corresponding to the
1152       Figure 12: Maze of Antmaze ultra
                                                       cartesian product of the x,y ant coordinates and the ant’s
1153                                                   configuration space Sant . This space is unbounded in
1154   all directions: Sant = R27 . The first dimension is the height in meter of the torso. The four fol-
1155   lowing dimensions correspond to respectively the x, y, z and w orientation in radian of the torso.
1156   The height next dimensions are the angles between different links, in radian. Then, x, y and z ve-
1157   locities in m.s−1 followed by their respective angular velocities and angular velocities of all links,
1158   in rad.s−1 . The goal space is a subspace of R2 : goals are given in x, y coordinates. The reward is
1159
       sparse: it is equal to 0 until the ant has reach the goal, where it is equal to 1. Hence, R = {0, 1}.
1160   There are three variations of this environment: medium, large and ultra; each
1161   one consisting of a different maze structure. The ultra variant is not part of the
1162   original dataset and has been introduced in Jiang et al. (2022). Each maze has
1163   a different datasets, each one composed of 1000 trajectories of 1000 steps, for
1164   a total of 106 steps. D4RL provides two variations datasets per: “play” and            Figure 13: Ant

       and the starting position whereas the latter is generated using a random goal

1169   area is approximately 166% larger than antmaze ultra and three times the size of antmaze large.

1270   QPHIL’s high policy allows full planning in an open-loop manner and shows great performance by
1271   doing so. In the experiments reported in the main body of this paper, we considered this open-loop
1272   version (as presented in figure 3), that plans the sequence of subgoals at the start of the episode and
1273

1274
       into an unexpected landmark, the initial can become obsolete, more optimal paths could be consider.
1275

1276   ing a landmark never seen for that situation during training. Then, one might wonder if replanning
1277   a new path from this new token in a closed-loop manner would help the policy to perform better.

1279   the agent. We tested on the AntMaze-Extreme variants the impact of replanning when the obtained
1280   token is different from the next planned subgoal. To replan, we sample a given number of plans with
1281   our sequence generator π plan , and finally select the shortest one among those successfully reach-
1282   ing the goal area. We see in table 9 that re-planning at out-of-path situations doesn’t significantly
1283   impact the overall performance of our models in the AntMaze-Extreme variants. Though, some
1284
       improvement is still observed with the best from 10-samples version (especially for extreme-play).

1285
       or MCTS decoding strategies), but this result is promising for the ability of QPHIL to deal with
1286
       more complex environments (with some distribution shifts of the dynamics or with stochasticity for
