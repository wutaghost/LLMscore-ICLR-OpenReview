009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011             In Deep Reinforcement Learning models trained using gradient-based techniques,
012             the choice of optimizer and its learning rate are crucial to achieving good per-
013             formance: higher learning rates can prevent the model from learning effec-
014             tively, while lower ones might slow convergence. Additionally, due to the non-
015             stationarity of the objective function, the best-performing learning rate can change
016             over the training steps. To adapt the learning rate, a standard technique consists
017             of using decay schedulers. However, these schedulers assume that the model is
018
                progressively approaching convergence, which may not always be true, leading to
                delayed or premature adjustments. In this work, we propose dynamic Learning
019
                Rate for deep Reinforcement Learning (LRRL), a meta-learning approach that se-
020
                lects the learning rate based on the agent’s performance during training. LRRL is
021             based on a multi-armed bandit algorithm, where each arm represents a different
022             learning rate, and the bandit feedback is provided by the cumulative returns of
023             the RL policy to update the arms’ probability distribution. Our empirical results
024             demonstrate that LRRL can substantially improve the performance of deep RL
025             algorithms.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Reinforcement Learning (RL), when combined with function approximators such as Artificial Neu-
031   ral Networks (ANNs), has shown success in learning policies that outperform humans in complex
032   games by leveraging extensive datasets (see, e.g., Silver et al., 2016; Lample & Chaplot, 2017;
033   Vinyals et al., 2019; Wurman et al., 2022). While ANNs were previously used as value function ap-
034   proximators (Riedmiller, 2005), the introduction of Deep Q-Networks (DQN) by Mnih et al. (2013;
035
      2015) marked a significant breakthrough by improving learning stability through two mechanisms:
      the target network and experience replay.
036
037   The experience replay (see Lin, 1992) stores the agent’s interactions within the environment, al-
038   lowing sampling of past interactions in a random way that disrupts their correlation. The target
039   network further stabilizes the learning process by periodically copying the parameters of the learn-
040   ing network. This strategy is crucial because the Bellman update —using estimations to update other
041
      estimations— would otherwise occur using the same network, potentially causing divergence. By
      leveraging the target network, gradient steps are directed towards a periodically fixed target, ensur-
042
      ing more stability in the learning process. Additionally, the learning rate hyperparameter controls
043
      the magnitude of these gradient steps in optimizers such as the stochastic gradient descent algorithm,
044   affecting the training convergence.
045
046   The learning rate is one of the most important hyperparameters, with previous work demonstrating
047
      that decreasing its value during policy finetuning can enhance performance by up to 25% in vanilla
      DQN (Agarwal et al., 2022). Determining the appropriate learning rate1 is essential for achieving
048
      good model performance: higher values can prevent the agent from learning, while lower values can
049
      lead to slow convergence (see Goodfellow et al., 2016; Blier et al., 2019; You et al., 2019). However,
050   finding a learning rate value that improves the model performance requires extensive and compu-
051   tationally expensive testing. In order to adapt its initial choice during training, optimizers such as

      Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015) and RMSProp (Tieleman & Hinton, 2012) employ an internal scheme
055   that dynamically adjusts the learning rate, considering, for instance, past gradient information. Nev-
056   ertheless, various learning rate scheduling strategies can be combined with the optimizer to decrease
057   the learning rate and improve the convergence over the training steps.
058
      Standard learning rate schedulers typically decrease the learning rate based on training progress
059
      using, e.g., linear or exponential decay strategies (Senior et al., 2013; You et al., 2019). In the
060
      context of RL, this approach can lead to premature or delayed learning rate adjustments, which may
061   hinder the agent’s ability to learn. Unlike supervised learning, RL usually involves generating data
062   by trading off between exploration (discovery of new states) and exploitation (refining of the agent’s
063   knowledge). As the policy improves, the data distribution encountered by the agent becomes more
064   concentrated, but this evolution occurs at a different pace than the overall training progress. For
065   instance, some environments require extensive exploration due to the sparseness of rewards, while
066   others need more exploitation to refine the policy to the complexity of the task. Consequently, a
067   more sophisticated decaying learning rate strategy that accounts for policy performance rather than
068   training steps can significantly enhance learning in deep RL.
069   In this work, we propose dynamic Learning Rate for deep Reinforcement Learning (LRRL), a
070   method to select the learning rate on the fly for deep RL. Our approach acknowledges that different
071   learning phases require different learning rates, and as such, instead of scheduling the learning
072   rate decay using some blanket approach, we dynamically choose the learning rate using a Multi-
073   Armed Bandit (MAB) algorithm, which accounts for the current policy’s performance. Our method
074   has the advantage of being algorithm-agnostic and applicable to any optimizer, although the results
075
      show that it works best when coupled with Adam. We conduct experiments on our approach using
      baselines provided in the Dopamine framework (see Castro et al., 2018). Our results focus on
076
      exploiting different settings for LRRL to illustrate its robustness under many possible configurations.
077
      Our main contributions are the following:
078
079          • We introduce LRRL, the first approach, to our knowledge, that leverages a multi-armed
080            bandit algorithm to select the learning rate dynamically in deep RL. Our results demon-
081            strate that LRRL achieves competitive performance with or superior to standard deep RL
082            algorithms using fixed baselines or traditional learning rate schedulers.
083
             • Our results show that LRRL significantly reduces the need for hyperparameter optimization
084
               by dynamically selecting from a set of possible learning rates using a multi-armed bandit
085            approach. This method mitigates the need for exhaustive techniques like grid search, as
086            it efficiently adapts the learning rate during training in a single run, rather than requiring
087            multiple runs to test each learning rate individually.
088
             • We assess the robustness of our method by employing the Adam and RMSProp optimizers
089
               with different sets of arms. We also compare the results using stochastic and adversarial
090            multi-armed bandit algorithms in Appendix A.2.

093   2   R ELATED W ORK
094
095   Multi-armed bandit for (hyper)parameter selection. Deep RL is known to be overly optimistic
096   in the face of uncertainty (Ostrovski et al., 2021), and many works have addressed this issue by
097   proposing conservative policy updates (van Hasselt et al., 2016; Fujimoto et al., 2019; Agarwal
098   et al., 2020). However, when the agent is able to interact with the environment, this optimism can
099
      encourage exploration, potentially leading to the discovery of higher returns. Building on this idea,
      Moskovitz et al. (2021) use an adversarial MAB algorithm to trade-off between pessimistic and
100
      optimistic policy updates based on the agent’s performance over the learning process. In order to
101
      select the learning rate for stochastic gradient MCMC, Coullon et al. (2023) employ an algorithm
102   based on Successive Halving (Karnin et al., 2013; Jamieson & Talwalkar, 2016), a MAB strategy that
103   promotes promising arms and prunes suboptimal ones over time. In the context of hyperparameter
104   optimization, Successive Halving has also been used in combination with infinite-arm bandits to
105   select hyperparameters for supervised learning (Li et al., 2018; Shang et al., 2019). A key difference
106   between these approaches to hyperparameter optimization for supervised learning and our work is
107   that we focus on selecting the best learning rate from a predefined set, rather than performing an
      extensive and computationally expensive search over the hyperparameter space. Close to our work,

      Liu et al. (2020) propose Adam with Bandit Sampling (ADAMBS), which employs the Exponential-
109   weight algorithm for Exploration and Exploitation (Auer et al., 2002) to enhance sample efficiency
110   by incorporating importance sampling within the Adam optimizer. While ADAMBS prioritizes
111   informative samples, our method focuses on RL tasks by dynamically adjusting the learning rate,
112   accelerating learning when the policy is far from optimal, and slowing it down as it converges.

114   Learning rate adapters/schedulers. Optimizers such as RMSProp (Tieleman & Hinton, 2012)
115   have an adaptive mechanism to update a set of parameters θ by normalizing past gradients, while
116   Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015) also incorporates momentum to smooth gradient steps. However,
117   despite their widespread adoption, these algorithms have inherent limitations in non-stationary en-
118   vironments since they do not adapt to changes in the objective function over time (see Degris et al.,

      2024). Increment-Delta-Bar-Delta (IDBD), introduced by Sutton (1992), has an adaptive mecha-

120
      has been extended to settings including RL (Young et al., 2019). Learning rate schedulers with
121
      time decay (Senior et al., 2013; You et al., 2019) are coupled with optimizers, assuming gradual
122   convergence to a good solution, but often require task-specific manual tuning. A meta-gradient re-
123   inforcement learning is proposed in Xu et al. (2020), composed of a two-level optimization process:
124   one that uses the agent’s objective and the other to learn meta-parameters of the objective function.
125   Our work differs from these methods by employing a multi-armed bandit approach to dynamically
126   select the learning rate over the training process, specifically targeting RL settings.

      3.2   M ULTI -A RMED BANDIT
170
171   Multi-Armed Bandits (MAB) provide an elegant framework for making sequential decisions under
172   uncertainty (see for instance Lattimore & Szepesvári, 2020). MAB can be viewed as a special case
173   of RL with a single state, where at each round n, the agent selects an arm kn ∈ {1, . . . , K} from
174   a set of K arms and receives a feedback (reward) fn (kn ) ∈ R. Like RL, MAB algorithms must
175   balance the trade-off between exploring arms that have been tried less frequently and exploiting
176   arms that have yielded higher rewards up to time n.
177   To account for the non-stationarity of the RL rewards, we will consider in this work the MAB setting
178   of adversarial bandits Auer et al. (2002). In this setting, at each round n, the agent selects an arm kn
179   according to some distribution pn while the environment (the adversary) arbitrarily (e.g., without
180   stationary constraints) determines the rewards fn (k) for all arms k ∈ K. MAB algorithms are
181   designed to minimize the pseudo-regret GN after N rounds defined by:

      4     DYNAMIC L EARNING R ATE FOR D EEP RL
195
196   In this section, we tackle the challenge of selecting the learning rate over the training steps by
197   introducing a dynamic Learning Rate for deep Reinforcement Learning (LRRL). LRRL is a meta-
198
      learning approach designed to dynamically select the learning rate in response to the agent’s per-
      formance. LRRL couples with stochastic gradient descent optimizers and adapts the learning rate
199
      based on the reward achieved by the policy πθ using an adversarial MAB algorithm. As the agent
200
      interacts with the environment, the average of observed rewards is used as bandit feedback to guide
201   the selection of the most appropriate learning rate throughout the training process.
202

461   6   C ONCLUSION
462
463
      In this work, we introduced dynamic Learning Rate for Deep Reinforcement Learning (LRRL),
      a meta-learning approach for selecting the optimizer’s learning rate on the fly. We demonstrated
464
      empirically that combining LRRL with the Adam optimizer could significantly enhance the perfor-
465
      mance of the value-based algorithm DQN, outperforming baselines and learning rate schedulers in
466   some tasks while remaining competitive in others. Furthermore, by employing a multi-armed ban-
467   dit algorithm, LRRL reduces the need for extensive hyperparameter tuning, as it explores a set of
468   learning rates in a single run with minimal extra computational overhead.
469
      While this work focused on dynamically selecting the best-performing learning rate, future investi-
470
      gations could extend LRRL ideas to other critical hyperparameters, such as mini-batch size, which
471
      also plays a key role in the model’s convergence. Moreover, although LRRL selects learning rates
472   based on policy performance, alternative feedback mechanisms could be explored, such as using
473   gradient information to select block-wise (e.g., per-layer) learning rates, extending these ideas to
474   supervised learning and applying them to other non-stationary objective functions, including those
475   encountered in Continual Learning (Rusu et al., 2016; Kirkpatrick et al., 2016; Abel et al., 2023).

      In this section, we describe the evaluation metrics that can be used to evaluate agent’s performance

788

789
      used in this work, as defined in (Taylor & Stone, 2009):
790
791          • Max average return: The highest average return obtained by an algorithm throughout
792            the learning process. It is calculated by averaging the outcomes across multiple individual
793            runs.
794          • Final performance: The performance of an algorithm after a predefined number of inter-
795            actions. While two algorithms may reach the same final performance, they might require
796            different amounts of data to do so. This metric captures the efficiency of an algorithm in
797            reaching a certain level of performance within a limited number of interactions. In Fig-
798            ure 7, the final performance overlaps with the max average return, represented by the black
799            dashed line.
800          • Jumpstart performance: The performance at the initial stages of training, starting from a
801            policy with randomized parameters θ. In Figure 7, Algorithm B exhibits better jumpstart
802            performance but ultimately achieves lower final performance than Algorithm A. A lower
803            jumpstart performance can result from factors such as a lower learning rate, although this
804
               work demonstrates that this does not necessarily lead to worse final performance.
