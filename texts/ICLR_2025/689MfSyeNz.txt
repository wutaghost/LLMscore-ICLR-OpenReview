011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent advances in vision-language models (VLMs) have led to impressive
014            progress in video understanding. However, despite their promising performance,
015
               existing state-of-the-art (SOTA) solutions require an excessive number of tokens
               (e.g., up to 6,272 tokens in the Llava-OneVision model) to represent input videos,
016
               leading to a non-negligible bottleneck in inference efficiency. Motivated by find-
017
               ings in human perception, where individuals first focus on high-level overviews
018            and then zoom into specific areas for detailed information, we hypothesize that a
019            similar approach can enhance the inference efficiency of VLMs by reducing the
020            number of tokens needed to represent videos. Based on this hypothesis, we propose
021            ZoomVLM, a tuning-free, plug-and-play efficient video processing framework
022            for video VLMs. ZoomVLM first generates an overview of the entire video and
023            then adaptively zooms in and out on different parts based on the content being
024            generated. Our key insight is that the attention distributions in the Large Language
025            Model (LLM) within the VLM can provide sensible guidance on where to focus
026
               (by allocating more tokens) and where to discard (by dropping tokens) during
               inference. Specifically, ZoomVLM integrates two key components: (1) a Video
027
               Overview Augmenter, which enables cost-effective high-level understanding by
028
               augmenting downsampled video overview with a few high-resolution keyframes;
029            and (2) an Adaptive Token Adjustment, which predicts the importance of different
030            video parts in the upcoming generation process and adjusts the number of tokens
031            allocated to each part according to their importance. Extensive experiments and
032            ablation studies across two challenging open-ended video understanding bench-
033            marks and four models validate that ZoomVLM effectively improves inference
034            efficiency by reducing the number of tokens and boosting throughput in terms
035            of the number of generated tokens per second without degradation in achievable
036            accuracy. Specifically, when applying ZoomVLM to Llava-Next-Video-7B-DPO,
037
               ZoomVLM achieves a 30% higher token generation rate with a 0.259 improvement
               in the Video Detail Description score.

      2.1   VLM S AND V IDEO VLM S
117
118   VLMs bridge visual and textual understanding by converting images and videos into tokens for joint
119   reasoning (Liu et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2024b; Lin et al., 2024). Image-based VLMs (Liu et al.,
120   2023b; Li et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023c) achieve impressive results using large-scale image-text
121   datasets. Extending to video data introduces challenges due to temporal complexity, leading to two
122
      main types of video VLMs. Type 1: Sequentially processing frames as tokens. Models like Liu
      et al. (2024a); Zhang et al. (2023a) adapt image-based VLMs by feeding multiple frames sequentially,
123
      treating frames as a large image. This achieves state-of-the-art video understanding but requires
124
      an enormous number of tokens—up to 6,272 (Li et al., 2024a)—leading to efficiency bottlenecks.
125   Type 2: Summarizing videos into a fixed number of tokens. These models use fewer tokens
126   before feeding them into LLMs. For example, Li et al. (2023); Lin et al. (2024) employ the Query
127   Transformer (Q-Former)(Li et al., 2023) with learnable query vectors to extract visual features. This
128   reduces computational overhead but can result in accuracy loss, as crucial details may be lost.
129
      Some works try to balance efficiency and accuracy but require drastic architectural changes, making
130
      them hard to apply to pretrained VLMs. For instance, Feichtenhofer et al. (2019); Xu et al. (2024a)
131   use “slow” and “fast” pathways, and Weng et al. (2024) introduce efficient attention mechanisms,
132   but both alter the VLM structure. In contrast, our proposed ZoomVLM offers a tuning-free, plug-
133   and-play framework that enhances efficiency without compromising accuracy, inspired by human
134   “zoom-then-focus” strategies.
135
136   2.2   ATTENTION PATTERNS IN T RANSFORMERS
137
      With the application of LLMs to various domains, understanding their attention mechanisms is
138
      evolving. Early studies (Clark et al., 2019; Vig, 2019; Sun & Lu, 2020) analyzed attention patterns
139
      in small-scale transformers to interpret model behavior. More recent works reveal how attention
140   distributions provide insights into LLM processing. For example,Xiao et al. (2023) observe attention
141   sinks, where certain tokens receive disproportionately large attention scores, affecting performance in
142   streaming applications. Similarly,Yu et al. (2024) find that not all attention sinks positively impact
143   accuracy. Additionally,Sun et al. (2024) identify massive activations leading to concentrated attention
144   probabilities and implicit biases. To address these issues,Zhang et al. (2023b) introduce a method to
145   steer LLM attention post-hoc, enhancing instruction-following by manipulating attention distributions
146   without altering model parameters.
147   However, these works primarily focus on attention in LLMs rather than VLMs. While attention is
148   crucial for aligning visual and textual modalities in VLMs, it remains underexplored. Our proposed
149   method, ZoomVLM, addresses this gap by leveraging attention patterns within the LLM component of
150   VLMs to guide efficient video processing. By analyzing attention distributions, ZoomVLM adaptively
151   allocates computational resources to different video segments, improving inference efficiency without
152   sacrificing accuracy.
153
154   2.3   KV C ACHE C OMPRESSION FOR L ONG S EQUENCES
155
      With the increasing demand for longer context inputs in LLMs, the large size of cached key-value
156   tokens (KV cache), which store attention keys and values during generation to prevent re-computation,
157   has become a major bottleneck in the generation stage in terms of latency and memory usage (Yuan
158   et al., 2024). To compress the KV cache and better handle long input sequences, two types of
159   compression solutions have emerged: (1) Token dropping: (Xiao et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024d;
160   Xiao et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024) propose dropping some tokens from the KV cache, similar to
161   pruning weights in neural networks; (2) Token quantization: (Sheng et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b)
      adopt a quantization approach for storing the KV cache, reducing data loading time, which is critical


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      for memory-bottlenecked LLM inference. Some recent works also explore combining these two
163   approaches (Zhang et al., 2024c).
164
165   However, these methods have two main drawbacks: (1) Their effectiveness has not been verified in
166
      VLMs, which are more complex than LLMs due to the requirement of understanding both visual and
      textual information; (2) They struggle to handle the dynamic information needs during generation
167
      based on input. In contrast, our proposed ZoomVLM pioneers the study of token cache compression
168
      in VLMs, while developing a compression strategy that dynamically adapts to the latest information
169   received during generation.

242   4.2   V IDEO OVERVIEW AUGMENTER
243
      Motivation and Goal. In the Video Overview Augmenter, our objective is to provide the VLM with
244
      a compact yet informative representation of the video, enabling effective understanding. However, as
245
      illustrated in Fig 1, simply reducing the number of tokens via common techniques such as spatial
246   pooling or temporal uniform sampling (Li et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2024b; Xu et al., 2024b) leads
247   to significant accuracy degradation. This drop in accuracy occurs due to the loss of critical detailed
248   information in the compressed video overview.
249
      To address this issue, in Video Overview Augmenter, we aim to design an approach that can effectively
250
      provide the compressed overview video with necessary detailed information. The key challenge
251
      is identifying an accurate and efficient mechanism to locate this critical information during the
252   generation process, thereby enhancing the video overview and improving the VLM’s understanding
253   and task performance. In the remainder of this section, we first present our analysis of existing works
254   to identify the source of potential mechanism we can leverage as the identifier, followed by a detailed
255   introduction of our proposed Video Overview Augmenter.
256
      Analysis of Existing Works on Attention Mechanism. Attention mechanism in large transformer
257
      models effectively reveal the model’s focus during inference. For example, in ViTs, excessive
258   attention to specific locations can indicate overfitting Yu et al. (2023), while in LLMs, attention
259   enhances instruction-following capabilities Zhang et al. (2023b). Building upon this interpretability,
260   we leverage attention to identify important parts of the video conditioned not only on the text prompt
261   (e.g., question), as in prior works Zhang et al. (2023b), but also on the content generated thus far.
262   This dual conditioning provides additional guidance to better assess the relevance of different video
263   segments during the generation process. By eliminating irrelevant information that is generally
264   considered important but unrelated to the generated content, we reduce redundancy in P as in Eq. 1
265   and enhance the model’s efficiency and performance.
266   Proposed Approach. The proposed Video Overview Augmenter leverages a condensed overview
267   of the video to identify important frames and augment the video’s representation with those frames,
268   enhancing text generation, as shown in Fig. 2. This process begins by generating a high-level summary
269   of the video through spatial pooling and temporal sampling, effectively reducing the video’s size
      and the number of tokens required for its representation. This condensed overview is then input into

306   4.3   A DAPTIVE T OKEN A DJUSTMENT
307
308   Motivation and Goal. Although the Video Overview Augmenter provides ZoomVLM with a
309
      cost-effective overview containing most of the necessary information for generation, we observe
      that answers to questions about a provided video typically adaptively focus on different locations
310
      within the video. This behavior aligns with human perception processes as indicated in (Sternberg
311
      & Sternberg, 2006; Lachter et al., 2004; Cherry, 1953). However, existing video VLMs (Li et al.,
312   2024a; Lin et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024c; Zhang et al., 2024b) and the augmented overview information
313   from the Video Overview Augmenter consider the video representation as static, creating a mismatch
314   between the LLM’s perception process and the generation process. To alleviate this issue, in Adaptive
315   Token Adjustment, we aim to develop a low-cost approach that adjusts the video representation
316   during the generation process. By emphasizing information critical for the current generation step
317   and mitigating the influence of unrelated information, Adaptive Token Adjustment aims to better
318   align the adjusted video representation at each generation step with the content being generated,
319   thereby improving generation accuracy.
320   Proposed Approach. To efficiently identify which parts of the video should be emphasized or ignored
321   with minimal computational overhead, we leverage attention as a low-cost identifier. Specifically,
322   following the Video Overview Augmenter, we periodically activate Adaptive Token Adjustment by
323   examining the attention distribution across L layers in the LLM. Tokens receiving high attention are
      emphasized by duplicating them, while tokens with low attention are discarded to reduce redundancy.

338   5.1   E VALUATION S ETTINGS
339   Models, Tasks, and Datasets. We evaluate ZoomVLM across four SOTA video VLMs including
340   Llava-Next-Video-7B-DPO (Li et al., 2024a), Llava-Next-Video-7B (Li et al., 2024a), Llava-Next-
341   Interleave-7B-DPO (Li et al., 2024c), and Llava-OneVision-0.5B-OV (Li et al., 2024a). The evaluation
342   is conducted on challenging video understanding tasks across three benchmarks: Video Detail De-
343   scription (VDD) (Zhang et al., 2024b) and Video-ChatGPT (Maaz et al., 2023). This comprehensive
344   assessment aims to evaluate ZoomVLM’s performance across diverse and complex settings.
345   Evaluation Settings. We conduct the evaluation primarily following the default settings and metrics
346   of each benchmark and model. Specifically, we adopt the settings from (Li et al., 2024b) for VDD
347   and Video-ChatGPT. During inference, we set the default video resolution to H = W = 336 with 32
348   frames. We construct L to include layers 5–20 for the Llava-Next series models and layers 10–25 for
349   Llava-OneVision, considering that their backbones have different numbers of layers. In the Video
350   Overview Augmenter, we set the initial generation step s = 20, the number of selected frames
351   k = 10, the temporal downsampling ratio r = 1.5, and the spatial resolution of the condensed video
352   Ĥ = Ŵ = 224. During Adaptive Token Adjustment, we perform adjustments periodically every
353   three sentences and set m = 10. All experiments are run on a single A100-80GB GPU with a batch
354   size of 1.
355   Evaluation Metrics. Following existing works, we use GPT models (Brown et al., 2020; OpenAI,
356   2023b) to score the generated responses to evaluate the generation quality. Specifically: (1) For
357   VDD, we employ GPT-3.5 to compare the differences between the generated responses and reference
358   descriptions. (2) For Video-ChatGPT, GPT-3.5 assesses the responses based on five aspects: Correct-
359   ness of Information (Correctness), Detail Orientation (Detail), Contextual Understanding (Context),
360   Temporal Understanding (Temporal), and Consistency. To evaluate the efficiency, we use the number
361
      of generated tokens per second (Token/sec) to evaluate the generation throughput and number of
      video tokens needed (# Video Tokens) to represent the theoretical improvement in representing the
362
      information in videos efficiently.

      In this paper, we introduced ZoomVLM, a tuning-free, plug-and-play efficient video processing
533
      framework for video VLMs. Inspired by human perceptual strategies, ZoomVLM first generates
534   a high-level overview of the entire video and then adaptively zooms in on specific parts based on
535   the content being generated. Our framework incorporates two key components: a Video Overview
536   Augmenter, which creates an informative summary by augmenting downsampled videos with high-
537   resolution keyframes, and an Adaptive Token Adjustment mechanism, which predicts the importance
538   of different video segments and adjusts token allocation accordingly during inference. Extensive
539   experiments demonstrate that ZoomVLM improves accuracy efficiency trade-offs by as much as 30%
      higher token generation rate a 0.259 improvement in the Video Detail Description score.

854   F.1   A BLATION ON N UMBER OF C OPY AND D ROPPING T OKENS IN E ACH A DJUSEMENT
855
      Copying and dropping a larger number of tokens during each Adaptive Token Adjustment allows
856
      for a more dynamic inference process and more aggressive manipulation of the LLM’s attention. To
857

858   and dropped in each adjustment. As shown in Table 11, we observe that increasing the number of
859   tokens copied and dropped does not necessarily lead to better performance. We attribute the inferior
860   performance to excessive changes in the token distribution, which may confuse the VLM and lead to
861   hallucinations. Furthermore, the superior accuracy achieved when fewer tokens are changed suggests
862   that, despite the thousands of tokens in the video representation, only a few are truly important.

915

917   size of 14 and a stride of 2, as specified in Li et al. (2024a). We systematically reduce the resolution
      to 224 and subsequently to 168, while maintaining all other parameters constant.
