011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Photosensitive Epilepsy (PE) is a neurological disorder characterized by seizures
014            triggered by harmful visual stimuli, such as flashing lights and high-contrast pat-
015            terns. The mechanisms underlying PE remain poorly understood, and to date,
016            no computational model has captured the phenomena associated with this con-
017            dition. Biologically detailed spiking networks trained for efficient prediction of
018            natural scenes have been shown to capture V1-like characteristics. Here, we
019            show that these models display seizure-like activity in response to harmful stimuli
020
               while retaining healthy responses to non-provocative stimuli when post-synaptic
               inhibitory connections are weakened. Notably, our adapted model resembles the
021
               motion tuning and contrast gain responses of excitatory V1 neurons in mice with
022
               optogenetically reduced inhibitory activity. We offer testable predictions under-
023            lying the pathophysiology of PE by exploring how reduced inhibition leads to
024            seizure-like activity. Finally, we show that artificially injecting pulsating input
025            current into the model units prevents seizure-like activity and restores baseline
026            function. In summary, we present a model of PE that offers new insights to under-
027            stand and treat this condition.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   In 1997, a Pokemon episode featuring rapid explosion scenes triggered seizures in over 600 children
034   with Photosensitive Epilepsy (PE) (Hermes et al., 2017). PE is characterised by abnormal sensory
035   processing in the primary visual cortex (V1) in response to specific visual stimuli, such as flickering
036   lights and high-contrast features (Fisher et al., 2022; Seshia & Carmant, 2005) (Fig. 1a). This
037   results in seizure-like activity, defined as excessive neuronal firing (Fig. 1b). Extensive research
038   suggests that one of the main causes underlying seizures is weakened GABA-mediated transmission
039
      at inhibitory synapses (Staley, 2015; Prince & Wilder, 1967; McCormick & Contreras, 2001) (Fig.
      1c), as supported by the efficacy of numerous anti-epileptic drugs that enhance GABAergic function
040
      (Shao et al., 2019). However, the exact mechanisms underlying PE remain unclear, partly due to
041
      the lack of an accurate animal or computational model that accurately captures the human condition
042   (Hermes et al., 2017; Cossart et al., 2005).
043
044   Most existing computational work in epilepsy has focused on identifying seizure-like activity
045
      (Kuhlmann et al., 2018) and seizure onset zones for surgical resection (Saggio & Jirsa, 2024). The
      few investigations that model seizure-like activity have fixated on emulating general seizure dynam-
046
      ics in non-visual areas like the hippocampus and widespread cortical areas (Jirsa et al., 2014; Liou
047
      et al., 2020) instead of capturing seizures induced by photic stimulation in the visual cortex. There
048   are several models of seizure dynamics using dynamic systems theory (Da Silva et al., 2003; Du
049   et al., 2019). However, these models do not emulate underlying biophysical processes, making it
050   difficult to link abstract model variables to biological mechanisms (Liou et al., 2020). An alternative
051   model (Liou et al., 2020) was proposed which does incorporate key biophysical elements (such as
052   recurrent projections), however, it lacks other biological characteristics, such as spontaneous seizure
053   initiation, relying instead on focal excitatory inputs to initiate seizure-like activity. To date, no bio-
      logically realistic model captures the response properties of PE.

072   A recent modelling study (Taylor et al., 2024b) developed a recurrently connected spiking network
073   of excitatory and inhibitory units, trained for efficient temporal prediction of natural movie clips
074   (Fig. 1d). This model captures simple and complex cell-like tuning characteristics and highlights
075   differences between excitatory and inhibitory V1 neurons. Given the model’s resemblance to V1,
076   and the weakened inhibitory transmission theory underlying epilepsy, we aimed to explore the fol-
077   lowing question: Do weakened inhibitory connections in the V1 spiking temporal-prediction
078   model result in response characteristics similar to those found in photosensitive epilepsy? Our
079   contributions are summarized as follows:
080
             • To the best of our knowledge, we establish the first computational model of PE. We demon-
081
               strate how the spiking temporal-prediction model (Taylor et al., 2024b), with weakened
082
               inhibitory synapses, captures seizure-like activity in response to known seizure-inducing
083            stimuli, while retaining normal V1-like activity to non-provocative stimuli.
084
085
             • We found our seizure model to capture V1-like spike statistics during non-seizure periods,
               with the model’s tuning properties and contrast responses resembling those of V1 neurons
086
               with weakened inhibitory input.
087
088          • We investigated mechanisms that trigger and sustain seizure-like activity in our model,
089            finding the weakened inhibitory synapses to increase response gain. As a result, the model
090
               was more susceptible to abnormally high firing rates in response to high-contrast stimuli,
               with seizure-like activity sustained by an imbalance in excitatory and inhibitory currents.
091
092          • Notably, we managed to prevent seizure-like activity by inputting pulsating currents into
093            model units, akin to the responsive neurostimulation (RNS) technique used in the treatment
094
               of epilepsy (Krauss et al., 2021). Our findings may be clinically relevant to optimizing
               neural stimulation protocols, and be a suitable in silico candidate for further exploring
095
               seizure mechanisms and clinical therapies.

      The V1 spiking temporal-prediction model with weakened inhibitory synapses We imple-
101
      mented the spiking V1 seizure model using the pre-trained spiking V1 temporal-prediction model
102   (Taylor et al., 2024b) with weakened inhibitory connections to reflect hypothesized GABAergic ab-
103   normalities in PE networks (see Appendix 5.1 for details). We used this model as it incorporates
104   important biological properties (spikes and Dale’s law); is trained using a biologically plausible
105   (Keller & Mrsic-Flogel, 2018) and unsupervised normative objective of efficient future-frame pre-
106   diction of natural movies (as also explored in other works (Singer et al., 2023; Taylor et al., 2024c));
107   and captures V1-like spike statistics, motion tuning and various key differences between excitatory
      and inhibitory neurons. In comparison, other models omit spikes (Echeveste et al., 2020); hard-code

      connectivity (Hennequin et al., 2018); operate in the spatial domain (Zylberberg & DeWeese, 2013);
109   or explore predictive objectives in non-visual brain regions like the hippocampus (Levenstein et al.,
110   2024). We report all results using the model with 80% weakened inhibitory connections. How-
111   ever, we found a range (50-100%) of inhibitory weight decreases to result in seizure-like activity in
112   response to provocative stimuli (Appendix Fig. 1).

114   Datasets We used three different visual datasets for model validation. 1. We collected a dataset of
115   various ∼ 10 second long movie scenes (Pokemon Shock clip, Kanye West’s All of the Lights music
116   video, a Citroen advert, the Incredibles 2 movie and The Weeknd’s Take My Breath music video)
117   which have been reported to induce seizures in patients with PE ((Concepcion, 2013; Sweney, 2012;
118   Svachula, 2018; Aswad, 2021)) to assess if our model also exhibits seizure-like activity; 2. We used
119   segments of the Star Wars movies (8000 patches of ∼ 2 second duration) for comparing model spike
120   responses to previously published monkey V1 data (Rasch et al., 2011); 3. We used a natural stimu-
121   lus dataset (2000 patches of ∼ 10 second duration) (Taylor, 2023) containing various natural scenes,
122
      which we used to analyse model contrast tuning and excitatory-inhibitory (EI) balance. To match
      the model’s training data (Taylor et al., 2024b), all validation stimuli were grayscaled; sampled at
123
      (or sampled to) 120Hz, and bandpass filtered to emulate the retinal and thalamic transformations;
124
      normalized; and clamped to range [−3.5, 3.5].

      Seizure identification in the model and classifying stimuli Seizures are usually identified
127
      through clinical observation (e.g. loss of consciousness or jerking movements). Patients with pho-
128
      tosensitive epilepsy (PE) are diagnosed with electroencephalogram (EEG) to identify abnormally
129   elevated activity in response to photic stimuli (Fisher et al., 2005). These diagnostic measures
130   are not directly possible in our model and hence we used the model’s spiking activity to identify
131   seizure-like activity. Prior works define seizure activity as an abnormally large increase in popula-
132   tion activity (Schevon et al., 2012). We confirmed if the model’s activity was abnormally high on
133   our collected dataset of known provocative movie stimuli (Appendix Fig. 1) and was not elevated
134   to non-provocative stimuli. We found a small portion of clips to evoke abnormally high population
135   activity to the natural stimulus dataset in the seizure model (and not in the control model) and we
136   classified these clips as provocative if the model’s mean population firing rate surpassed a threshold
137
      of 84Hz (Appendix Fig. 2). Note, due to ethical reasons we could not confirm if these clips induced
      seizures in PE patients. As additional comparisons, we calculated spectrograms from the local
138
      field potential (LFP) approximations of the model’s mean population spiking responses (Barbieri
139
      et al., 2014; Mazzoni et al., 2008), which represent the same biophysical process as EEG recordings
140   (Buzsáki et al., 2012). Furthermore, we also calculated the synchronicity of the model’s spiking
141   activity, where increased neural synchrony has been noted as a characteristic of seizures (Schevon
142   et al., 2012) (although this remains controversial (Truccolo et al., 2011)).
143

147   calculating the average correlation coefficient between the spike trains (binned at 25ms) of 400
148   randomly sampled unit pairs for varying temporal lag. We calculated the orientation selectivity

      Excitatory and inhibitory currents We quantified the global (i.e. how equal) and precise (i.e.
184   how coupled) EI balance of the model󰁓  units (Vogels et al., 2011; Taylor et al., 2024b). A unit’s global
185   EI balance was measured as the ratio t Ex[t]/|In[t]| between its excitatory (Ex) and inhibitory (In)

193   RNS-like stimulation in the model We replicated neural stimulation techniques for treating drug-
194
      resistant epilepsy by inputting pulsating current into the model units. Current stimulation in our
      model resembles RNS, where electrodes are implanted near the epileptic focus in the cortex or hip-
195
      pocampus (Krauss et al., 2021), unlike deep brain stimulation, which targets the thalamus (Foutz
196
      & Wong, 2022). RNS stimulation parameters range between 1-3mA with a pulsation frequency
197   up to 200Hz (Bigelow & Kouzani, 2019). We explored the space of stimulation frequencies loga-
198   rithmically spaced between 0-120Hz and amplitudes logarithmically spaced between 0-1.28a.u. to
199   determine the optimal values for reducing seizure-like activity in response to the natural stimulus
200   dataset.

254
255
      We examined the seizure model’s spike statistics and tuning properties, and found several V1-like
      characteristics to emerge. We analyzed the model’s spike activity in response to Star Wars movie
256
      clips (Rasch et al., 2011), classifying clips as provocative if they triggered seizure-like activity, or
257
      non-provocative, if they did not (see Methods). The seizure model displayed a firing rate distribu-
258   tion with an exponential fall-off (Fig. 3a) to the non-provocative stimuli as similarly found in the
259   control model (Taylor et al., 2024b) and V1 data (Rasch et al., 2011; Baddeley et al., 1997). Quanti-
260   fying the correlation statistics using spike train cross-correlograms (Rasch et al., 2011), revealed the
261   correlation between different unit spike trains to exhibit a low peak correlation that symmetrically
262   decays for increasing lag (Fig. 3b). This resembles the correlation statistics of the control model
263   and of anesthetized monkey V1 (Rasch et al., 2011). However, the seizure model’s spike statistics
264   substantially changed in response to the provocative stimuli. The firing rate distribution peaked at
265   ∼ 100Hz (Fig. 3a) and the peak correlation increased from 0.04 to 0.55 CC (Fig. 3b), reminiscent
266
      of seizure-like activity (Schevon et al., 2012). In comparison, the control model’s spike statistics
      remained largely unaltered.

347   V1 neurons exhibit a growing response curve shaped like the hyperbolic ratio equation (Albrecht
348   & Hamilton, 1982), in response to visual gratings of increasing contrast. Excitatory V1 neuron
349   contrast-response curves are reported to rise in amplitude as the influence of V1 inhibitory neurons
350   is optogenetically (Atallah et al., 2012) and pharmacologically (Katzner et al., 2011) reduced. The
351   contrast-response curves of the control and seizure model units appeared to be well-fitted by the
352
      hyperbolic ratio equation on visual inspection (Fig. 4d). Consistent with biology, the seizure model
      with weakened inhibitory synapses exhibited higher contrast responses than the control model (p =
353
      8.28 × 10−126 , t-test), across the population of excitatory units (Fig. 4d and e).
354
355   To understand how weakened inhibitory synapses in the seizure model led to increased responsive-
356   ness to high-contrast visual input, we inspected each model’s receptive fields (RFs). The seizure
357   model’s RF shapes were unaltered compared to the control model (CC = 0.99), and resembled the
358
      RFs of V1 simple cells (Hubel & Wiesel, 1959; 1968) (Fig. 4f), with varying numbers of excitatory
      and inhibitory subfields tuned for a particular spatial frequency and orientation (Jones & Palmer,
359
      1987; Ringach, 2002). However, the seizure model’s RF exhibited higher power compared to the
360
      control model (Fig 4g), where RF power quantifies the response sensitivity of a unit’s RF to visual
361   input (Taylor et al., 2024b). This indicates that the weakened inhibitory synapses in the seizure
362   model enhance the response gain of the units, rendering the model more susceptible to heightened
363   activity.

      3.4                               E XCITATION - INHIBITION IMBALANCE SUSTAIN SEIZURE - LIKE ACTIVITY
366
367   We explored whether the seizure model’s prolonged seizure-like activity was primarily sustained by
368   the provocative input stimulus, or rather by an imbalance in recurrent dynamics. We investigated
369   this by masking the provocative input stimuli following the onset of seizure-like activity (Fig. 5a).
370   The fraction of provocative clips only marginally reduced from 11.8% to 9.7% (Fig. 5b), implying
371   the seizure-like activity to be driven by an imbalance in recurrent dynamics.
372   The balance of excitatory and inhibitory (EI) synaptic currents received by cortical neurons (Okun
373   & Lampl, 2008; Wehr & Zador, 2003; Anderson et al., 2000; Monier et al., 2003; Xue et al., 2014)
374   is thought to be critical for maintaining stable neural activity (Sadeh & Clopath, 2021). We qualita-
375   tively observed the excitatory input currents to be more elevated in the seizure model compared to
376   the control model in response to provocative stimuli (Fig. 5c). The EI balance can be quantified us-
377   ing the EI ratio and correlation (see Methods), which, respectively, measure how equal and coupled
      excitatory and inhibitory currents are over time (Vogels et al., 2011; Taylor et al., 2024b).

      3.5      M INIMIZING SEIZURE OCCURRENCE THROUGH PULSATING INPUT CURRENTS
414
415   One treatment option for patients unresponsive to anti-epileptic drugs are neuronal stimulation tech-
416   niques such as RNS (Baud et al., 2018). Here, a multielectrode array is implanted in close proximity
417   to the seizure onset zone (Fig. 6a) (Baud et al., 2018). The electrode emits a pulsating stimulatory
418   current to prevent or terminate seizure-like activity. We explored if the seizure model’s seizure-like
419   activity could be reduced by inputting pulsating input current into the units (Fig. 6b).
420   Surprisingly, we were able to prevent seizure-like activity in response to the provocative seizure-
421   inducing Pokemon segment (Hermes et al., 2017) by inputting particular stimulatory currents (Fig.
422   6c). The spike raster of the stimulated seizure model resembled that of the control model with a
423   firing rate of ∼ 10Hz, significantly lower than the unstimulated seizure model’s firing rate of 88Hz.
424   Additionally, the stimulated seizure model’s predicted future frames qualitatively matched those
425   of the control model and did not contain the noisy artifacts seen in the unstimulated model. The
426   prediction accuracy of the stimulated seizure model was similar to the control model (∼ 0.63 MSE),
427   marking a significant improvement over the unstimulated seizure model (∼ 0.92 MSE).
428   We explored the space of stimulation frequencies (logarithmically spaced between 0 and 120Hz)
429   and amplitudes (logarithmically spaced between 0 and −1.28 a.u.) to determine the optimal values
430   for reducing seizure-like activity in response to the natural stimulus dataset. The percentage of
431   clips triggering seizure-like activity declined as a function of increasing stimulation amplitude and
      frequency (Fig. 6d). For the largest tested frequency and amplitude values, we found seizure-like

433   (Fig. 6e).

444   4   D ISCUSSION
445
446   Mechanism We investigated the theory that PE results from abnormal GABAergic function by
447   weakening inhibitory synapses in a spiking network model of V1. This led to abnormally elevated
448   firing rates, particularly in response to high-contrast stimuli (Fig. 4b), with persistent activity even
449   after stimulus removal (Fig. 5a). Thus, our findings support the notion of inhibition stabilizing net-
450
      work activity and preventing runaway excitation (Sadeh & Clopath, 2021; Adesnik, 2017). Notably,
      while the gain response is higher in the seizure model, tuning characteristics remain similar to the
451
      control model (Fig. 3), reflecting findings in biology (Atallah et al., 2012; Katzner et al., 2011).
452
      This may explain how altered excitatory and inhibitory dynamics in PE patients result in seizures,
453   but visual processing remains normal during interictal periods.
454
455
      Translation High-frequency current stimulation in the seizure model restored baseline firing ac-
456
      tivity and function as measured by the model’s prediction MSE (Fig. 6c). These results align with
457
      high-frequency RNS treatment in patients which effectively reduces seizures (Alcala-Zermeno et al.,
458   2023). High-frequency stimulation is proposed to inhibit targeted structures, yet its exact mecha-
459   nisms remain unknown (Bikson et al., 2001; Foutz & Wong, 2022). Interestingly, the most effective
460   neural stimulation frequency range (64-120Hz) used in our model overlaps with gamma oscillations
461   (30-70Hz) in human V1, which are implicated in seizures resulting from high-contrast gratings (Her-
462   mes et al., 2017). Thus, high-frequency stimulation may be disrupting these synchronous gamma
463   oscillations (Sadeh & Clopath, 2021) and preventing seizure onset. Our seizure model also extends
464   to computational theories of cortical function and dysfunction. Predictive processing is hypothesized
465   to be fundamental to healthy V1 functioning (Keller & Mrsic-Flogel, 2018). We computationally
466
      showed inhibitory synapses to be crucial for maintaining healthy predictive function in the spiking
      model, where seizure-like activity impaired the model’s predictive performance (Fig. 6c). These
467
      findings may extend to biological theories linking prediction errors to pathology (Horga et al., 2014;
468
      Van de Cruys et al., 2014).
469
470
471
      Limitations Defining seizure-like activity in our model is challenging due to the broad definition
      of seizures in humans (Truccolo et al., 2011) and the lack of a validated seizure-inducing dataset.
472
      We therefore validated our model using various clips reported to induce seizures. Another limitation
473
      is the validation of results against animal studies (mice and monkeys). Animal models have been
474   extremely valuable to understand epilepsy dynamics (Grone & Baraban, 2015). However, it is un-
475   known to what extent the findings we compare to animal models (Fig. 3a and Fig. 4d, e) translate to
476   human patients.
477
478
      Impact and Ethics Adolescents, who are already at a higher risk of developing PE (Fisher et al.,
479
      2005), face increased seizure risks with the surge of video consumption on platforms like TikTok.
480   As testing harmful content on patients with PE is unethical, our model could screen social media
481   content for provocative stimuli. Current warning systems screen for features such as ’high-contrast
482   stimuli’ and ’pulsating light stimulation’ (Fisher et al., 2022), and therefore may miss certain stimuli
483   or overly restrict content. Our model is not trained to identify particular stimuli and may be better
484   suited to identify harmful features that do not fall into these categories. However, it is important to
485   note that while our model offers a framework to test the underlying pathophysiology of PE, it does
      not fully replicate the complexity of human physiology.

      Input current The input current Ii [t] ∈ R (Eq. 1) to unit i at time step t is calculated from the
      input stimulus x ∈ RT ×H×W (of T = 42 frames, and spatial height H = 20px and spatial width
781
      W = 20px); model output spikes S[t−1] ∈ RN from the previous time step; and a bias term bin   i ∈ R.
782
      The feedforward connectivity W in ∈ RN ×TE ×H×W (of TE = 15 temporal frame span) maps the
783
      input stimulus into a feedforward current contribution and the recurrent connectivity W rec ∈ RN ×N
784   maps previous output spikes into a recurrent current contribution. The first 5 spatial frames of the
785   feedforward connectivity were zero-masked to emulate the transduction and transmission latency to
786   V1 (Land et al., 2013; Kirchberger et al., 2023) and Gaussian noise 󰂃p ∼ N (0, 0.22 ) was added to
787   the input stimulus to emulate the noisy photoreceptors in the retina (Baylor et al., 1980). 15% of
788   the model unit population were assigned to be inhibitory (NI = 90 inhibitory units and NE = 510
789   excitatory units) to mimic the biology (Sillito, 1975; Fitzpatrick et al., 1987; Hofer et al., 2011).
790   All outward connection weights from unit j are either positive (i.e. excitatory) or negative (i.e.
791   inhibitory), which were enforced by taking the absolute value of every weight in the unconstrained
792   recurrent weight matrix W̃ rec , and appropriately negating connection weights from unit j to i (Eq.
793   5). In this work, we introduced parameter α which we used to weaken the inhibitory connections in
794
      the model by multiplying their value by α (where α = 1 is a 100% weight decrease to zero).
795
      Spiking dynamics and predicted output All spiking units implement the normalized and dis-
796
      cretized leaky integrate-and-fire (LIF) model (Taylor et al., 2024a), which decays the membrane
797
      potential Vi [t] ∈ R of unit i at time step t by learnt factor βi (Eq. 2). The membrane was also
798   multiplicatively perturbed using Gaussian sampled noise 󰂃g ∼ N (0, 0.62 ) to emulate the synap-
799   tic noise of V1 neurons (Rusakov et al., 2020). In the event of a spike occurring in the preceding
800   time step (which happens when the membrane potential reaches the firing threshold equal to one),
801   the membrane potential resets to zero (Eq. 3). A prediction of the future spatial movie frame
802   ŷ[t] ∈ RH×W was generated (with ŷhw [t] ∈ R denoting the predicted pixel value) at every time
803   step t, by mapping a span of TD = 2 temporal frames of proceeding spike activity using weights
804   W out ∈ RN ×TD ×H×W , plus a bias bout ∈ R (Eq. 4).
