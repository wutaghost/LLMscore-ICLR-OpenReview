011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            We introduce Generalized Instruction Tuning (called GLAN), a general and scal-
014            able method for instruction tuning of Large Language Models (LLMs). Un-
015            like prior work that relies on seed examples or existing datasets to construct
016
               instruction-tuning data, GLAN exclusively utilizes a pre-curated taxonomy of
               human knowledge and capabilities as input and generates large-scale synthetic
017
               instruction data across all disciplines. Specifically, inspired by the systematic
018
               structure in human education system, we build the taxonomy by decomposing
019            human knowledge and capabilities to various fields, sub-fields and ultimately, dis-
020            tinct disciplines semi-automatically, facilitated by LLMs. Subsequently, we gen-
021            erate a comprehensive list of subjects for every discipline and proceed to design
022            a syllabus tailored to each subject, again utilizing LLMs. With the fine-grained
023            key concepts detailed in every class session of the syllabus, we are able to gen-
024            erate diverse instructions with a broad coverage across the entire spectrum of hu-
025            man knowledge and skills. Extensive experiments on large language models (e.g.,
026            Mistral) demonstrate that GLAN excels in multiple dimensions from mathemat-
027
               ical reasoning, coding, academic exams, logical reasoning to general instruction
               following without task-specific training data. In addition, GLAN allows for easy
028
               customization and new fields or skills can be added by simply incorporating a new
029
               node into our taxonomy.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Large Language Models (LLMs) have enabled unprecedented capabilities to understand and gener-
035
      ate text like humans. By scaling up model size and data size (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al.,
036   2022), LLMs are better at predicting next tokens and prompting to perform certain tasks with a few
037   demonstrations (Brown et al., 2020). However, these capabilities do not directly translate to bet-
038   ter human instruction-following ability (Ouyang et al., 2022). Instruction tuning (Wei et al., 2022)
039   bridges this gap by fine-tuning LLMs on instructions paired with human-preferred responses.
040
      Previous work has constructed instruction tuning data using seed examples or existing datasets (Xu
041
      et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023). For example, FLAN (Wei et al., 2022) aggregates traditional NLP
042   datasets into an instruction-following set. However, the availability of only a few thousand NLP
043   tasks (Wang et al., 2022; Longpre et al., 2023) restricts the generalization capabilities of LLMs
044   trained on FLAN (Xu et al., 2023a). Recently, the Self-instruct approach (Wang et al., 2023) has
045   generated synthetic instruction tuning datasets from a limited pool of human-written seed instruc-
046   tions. Evolve-Instruct (Xu et al., 2023a) further enhances this by augmenting existing instruction
047   tuning datasets through rewriting operations using LLMs. Despite these advancements, these strate-
048   gies primarily rely on data augmentation, meaning the range of domains or tasks covered by the
049   augmented datasets remains constrained by the original input datasets.
050   How to create a general instruction tuning dataset? We draw inspiration from the systematic struc-
051   ture in human education system. The structure of human education includes several levels, starting
052   from early childhood education up to higher education and beyond (Wikipedia contributors, 2023).
053   Within each level, a student acquires knowledge, skills, and values in a systematic process. The
      courses a student learns from primary school to college cover a broad range of knowledge and skills,

      In this paper, we introduce a generalized instruction tuning paradigm GLAN (shorthand for
      Generalized Instruction-Tuning for Large LANguage Models) to generate synthetic instruction tun-
083
      ing data almost from scratch. As shown in Figure 1, unlike existing work (Xu et al., 2023a; Luo
084
      et al., 2023b;a; Mukherjee et al., 2023), GLAN exclusively utilizes a pre-curated taxonomy of hu-
085   man knowledge and capabilities as input and generates large-scale instruction data systematically
086   and automatically across all disciplines. Specifically, inspired by the structure of the human educa-
087   tion system, the input taxonomy is constructed by decomposing human knowledge and capabilities
088   to various fields, sub-fields, and, ultimately, distinct disciplines semi-automatically, facilitated by
089   LLMs and human verification. The cost of human verification process is low due to the limited
090   number of disciplines in the taxonomy. As shown in Figure 1, we then further break down these
091   disciplines into even smaller units. We continue to generate a comprehensive list of subjects for ev-
092   ery discipline and proceed to design a syllabus tailored to each subject, again utilizing LLMs. With
093
      the fine-grained key concepts detailed in every class session of the syllabus, we can first sample
      from them and then generate diverse instructions with broad coverage across the entire spectrum of
094
      human knowledge and skills. The process described above mirrors the human educational system,
095
      where educators in each discipline craft a series of subjects for student learning. Instructors then
096   develop a syllabus for each subject, breaking down the content into specific class sessions. These
097   sessions are then further divided into core concepts that students must comprehend and internalize.
098   Based on these detailed core concepts outlined in the syllabus, teaching materials and exercises are
099   subsequently created, which are our instruction tuning data.
100
      GLAN is general, scalable and customizable. GLAN is a general method, which is task-agnostic
101
      and is capable of covering a wide range of domains. GLAN is scalable. Similar to Wang et al.
102   (2023); Xu et al. (2023a), GLAN generates instructions using LLMs, which can produce instruc-
103   tions on a massive scale. Moreover, the input of GLAN is a taxonomy, which is generated by
104   prompting an LLM and human verification, requiring minimal human effort. GLAN allows for
105   easy customization. New fields or skills can be added by simply incorporating a new node into our
106   taxonomy. Note that each node of the taxonomy can be expanded independently, which means that
107   we only need to apply our method to the newly added nodes without re-generating the entire dataset.
      Extensive experiments on large language models (e.g., Mistral) demonstrate that GLAN excels in

112   2     GLAN: G ENERALIZED I NSTRUCTION -T UNED L ANGUAGE M ODELS
113
114
      GLAN aims to create synthetic instruction data covering various domains of human knowledge and
115
      capabilities on a large scale. As shown in Algorithm 1, we first build a taxonomy of human knowl-
116   edge and capabilities using frontier LLMs (i.e., GPT-4) and human verification. The taxonomy
117   naturally breaks down human knowledge and capabilities to fields, sub-fields, and ultimately differ-
118   ent disciplines (see Section 2.1). The following steps are fully autonomously facilitated by GPT-4
119   (or GPT-3.5). Then for each discipline, we again instruct GPT-4 to further decompose it into a
120   list of subjects within this discipline (Section 2.2). Similar to an instructor, GPT-4 continues to
121   design a syllabus for each subject, which inherently breaks a subject into various class sessions with
122   key concepts that students need to master (Section 2.3). With the obtained class sessions and key
123   concepts, we are ready to construct synthetic instructions. We prompt GPT-4 to generate home-
124
      work questions based on randomly sampled class sessions and key concepts as well as the syllabus
      (Section 2.4). We recursively decompose human knowledge and capabilities into smaller units until
125
      atomic-level components (i.e., class sessions and key concepts). We expect to randomly combine
126
      these class sessions and key concepts to ensure the coverage and diversity of synthetic instructions.

      2.1    TAXONOMY OF H UMAN K NOWLEDGE AND C APABILITIES
147
      We build a taxonomy of human knowledge and capabilities to guide the generation of synthetic in-
148
      structions. Therefore, its coverage is important. On the other hand, it is also essential to make the
149
      taxonomy highly extensible, since the preferred capabilities of LLMs may change over time. In the
150   first step, we propose to generate the taxonomy by prompting GPT-4 with a set of different instruc-
151   tions (e.g., list all fields of human knowledge and capabilities). Then, we
152   do human post-editing to ensure its correctness and completeness. Due to the limited number of
153   fields, sub-fields, and disciplines in our taxonomy, the cost of human verification is reasonably low.
154   Another advantage of human post-editing is that we can easily add new fields or disciplines to the
155   taxonomy as needed.
156   Our taxonomy currently covers a diverse range of knowledge and capabilities in both academic
157   education and vocational training. The top level of the taxonomy contains fields such as Natural
158   Sciences, Humanities, or Services (vocational training). These fields branch out to various sub-fields
159   and/or disciplines such as Chemistry, Sociology or Retailing. We keep breaking down nodes of the
160   taxonomy until disciplines, and we leave the breaking down of disciplines to automatic methods
161   described in the following sections. By collecting the leaf nodes of the taxonomy, we obtain a list
      of disciplines D = {d1 , d2 , . . . , dM }.

      2.2   S UBJECT G ENERATOR
163
164   As in Algorithm 1, for each discipline d, we aim to extract the list of subjects in it through
165   prompt engineering. Specifically, we instruct GPT-4 to act as an education expert of
166   discipline d and design a list of subjects a student should learn.
167   The completion of GPT-4 contains a comprehensive list of subjects and their meta data (e.g., level,
168   introduction and subtopics of the subject) in unstructured text format, which can not be directly
169   used in subsequent steps. We therefore used another round of prompting to convert the completion
170
      to JSONL format:
171      Prompt
172      Transform the above to JSONL format so that it is easier for a computer to understand.
173      Enclose the JSONL output between two sets of triple backticks. For each JSONL object, use
         the keys “subject_name”, “level” and “subtopics”.
174
175   It is worth noting that generating a subject list in JSONL format using a single prompt is feasi-
176   ble. However, we refrain to do so, because we observe that incorporating additional formatting
177   instructions directly into the prompt can compromise the quality of the resulting subject list. These
178   extracted subjects (as well as their meta data) S = {s1 , s2 , . . . , sN } can be subsequently used in
179   next steps. For each s ∈ S, let s.name, s.level and s.subtopics denote the name, grade
180   level and subtopics of subject s, respectively. We can apply the above prompts multiple times to
181
      ensure better coverage of subjects within this discipline.

183   2.3   S YLLABUS G ENERATOR
184
      For each subject s, we have already extracted its name (s.name), grade level (s.level), and a
185   small set of included sub-topics (s.subtopics) in a structured format. In this section, we aim to
186   further segment each subject into smaller units, making them more suitable for creating homework
187   assignments. We consult GPT-4 to design a syllabus for this subject. We opt for syllabus generation
188   for the following reasons. Firstly, a syllabus essentially breaks down the main topic of a subject
189   into smaller segments in a hierarchical manner. Specifically, each subject comprises several class
190   sessions, and each session covers a variety of sub-topics and key concepts. Secondly, a syllabus
191   provides an introduction, objectives, and expected outcomes of a subject, which are inherently useful
192   for formulating homework questions. We instruct GPT-4 to 1) design a syllabus based on its meta
193
      data (s.level, s.name and s.subtopics); 2) break the subject into different class sessions;
      3) provide details for each class session with a description and detailed key concepts students need
194
      to master.
195
196   Let A denote the generated syllabus. The resulting syllabus A is in unstructured text format. How-
197   ever, class session names and key concepts of each class are required in the instruction generation
198   step (see Algorithm 1). Similar to the process of subject list extraction in Section 2.2, we again
199
      extract these meta data of each class session by prompting GPT-4. As a result, we obtain a list of
      class sessions C = {c1 , c2 , . . . , c|C| } and their corresponding key concepts K = {k1 , k2 , . . . , k|C| }.
200
      The detailed prompt for syllabus generation is in Appendix A.3.

      2.4   I NSTRUCTION G ENERATOR
203
204   Given a syllabus A as well as a list of its class sessions C and their associated key concepts K,
205   we are ready to generate homework questions and their answers. To generate diverse homework
206   questions, we first sample one or two class session names from C and one to five key concepts under
207   these selected class sessions. Let Ĉ denote the selected class session names and K̂ the selected
208   key concepts. Then we prompt GPT-4 (or GPT-3.5) to generate a homework question given
209   the selected class sessions Ĉ and key concepts K̂ as well as the syllabus A. We intend to give
210   GPT-4/3.5 more context (e.g., what students have already learned in previous sessions) when
211   creating assignments. Therefore, we additionally instruct GPT to consider that students have learned
212   up to class sessions Ĉ when crafting homework and try to leverage multiple key concepts across
213   different class sessions. See details of our prompt for instruction generation in Appendix A.4.
214

237
238   Taxonomy Creation By asking GPT-4 to create a taxonomy of human knowledge and capabili-
239   ties, we end up with a set of fields, sub-fields, and disciplines that cover a broad range of domains
240   in human knowledge and capabilities. Next, we ask human annotators to decide whether these el-
241   ements in the taxonomy should be kept or not in order to reduce the redundancy of the taxonomy
242   while maintaining its correctness. Note that if a field or sub-field is marked as remove, we remove
243
      its descendant as well. We kept 126 disciplines after majority voting (provided in supplementary
      materials). Note that it is feasible to manually add extra disciplines, sub-fields, or fields whenever
244
      necessary.
245
246
      Subject and Syllabus Generation During the subject list and syllabus generation, we prompt
247
      GPT-4 and employ nucleus sampling (Holtzman et al., 2020) with temperature T = 1.0 and top-
248
      p = 0.95 to encourage diversity. We do not use GPT-3.5-turbo since some subjects belong to
249   the long-tail distribution which may not be effectively modeled by GPT-3.5-turbo. To ensure
250   diversity and completeness of the generated subjects, we query GPT-4 10 times for each discipline
251   (Section 2.2). There are 100 to 200 subjects for each discipline on average. It is worth noting
252   that the same subjects may appear in different disciplines. For instance, the subject calculus is
253   both in physics and mathematics. We do not de-duplicate those subjects, since it may reflect their
254   importance in human knowledge. Given a subject in a specified discipline, we query GPT-4 for
255   only one time to design a syllabus (see details in section 2.3). The temperature and top-p are still set
256   to 1.0 and 0.95, respectively. The number of class sessions contained in each syllabus varies from
257
      10 to 30 and each class session contains around five key concepts.
258
259   Instruction Data Generation Each instruction data consists of a question and its answer. We
260   choose to generate questions and answers separately since we observed that separate generations
261
      lead to higher quality outputs. After question generation with GPT-4, each question is then an-
      swered by GPT-3.5-turbo with temperature T = 0.7, top-p = 0.95 (we use a lower temperature
262
      in order to make the resulting answers more accurate). We use GPT-3.5-turbo instead of GPT-4
263
      for answer generation, because GPT-3.5-turbo is significantly faster with reasonably good re-
264   sults. According to the calculation method outlined in Section 2.4, we have over 500 million unique
265   combinations of class sessions and key concepts, which guarantees the diversity of the generated
266   data. In this paper, we generate 10 million instruction-response pairs in total and then we do train-
267   ing data decontamination. Specifically, the training instruction-response pairs are decontaminated
268   by removing pairs that contain questions or input prompts from the test and training (if any) sets
269   of benchmarks we evaluate. We exclude the training set of benchmarks we evaluate to verify the
      generalization capability of our synthetic data.

288   Inference Cost The inference cost of GLAN is closely tied to the models used for data genera-
289   tion. Note that GLAN is not limited to GPT-4 or GPT-3.5; it can be applied to any open-source or
290   closed-source models. To best showcase GLAN’s performance, we selected the top available mod-
291   els at the time of writing—specifically, GPT-4 and GPT-3.5. We queried GPT-4 approximately
292   26,000 times for taxonomy, subject, and syllabus generation combined. For instruction generation,
293   we queried GPT-4 10 million times, and for answer generation, we queried GPT-3.5 also 10
294   million times. For more details, please refer to Appendix A.5.

296   3.2   M ODEL T RAINING
297
      We employ Mistral 7B (Jiang et al., 2023) as our base model. During training, we concatenate each
298
      instruction and response pair to a single sequence and only compute loss on response tokens. We
299   train our model for 3 epochs with a learning rate of 3e-6. The batch size is set to approximately 512
300   instruction-response pairs. We employ a dynamic batch size to ensure a constant total number of
301   tokens per batch. We use a cosine learning rate schedule and we start with a linear warm-up of 1000
302   steps and the final learning rate is reduced to 0. The training requires approximately 8 days using 32
303   A100 GPUs.

360   A Closer Look at Academic Exams ARC and MMLU are all multi-choice based benchmarks
361   on academic exams. However, we observe that improvements of GLAN over Mistral on ARC
362   are much larger than these on MMLU (see Table 1). By grouping the 57 subjects in MMLU into
363   four categories (i.e., STEM, Humanities, Social Sciences, and Other (business, health, misc.)), we
364   observe GLAN wildly improves on STEM in MMLU while not in other categories (Table 2). This
365
      is consistent with recent findings that Chain-of-Thought (CoT) primarily aids in symbolic reasoning
      problems rather than other types of questions (Sprague et al., 2024). Also note that ARC is composed
366
      of high school science problems, which are also STEM questions. GLAN is good at STEM subjects
367
      may be because responses of our dataset are from GPT-3.5-turbo, which by default generates
368   responses with CoT reasoning. Indeed, we observe that GLAN generates solutions with CoT for
369   multi-choice questions.

      3.5                 TASK - SPECIFIC T RAINING DATA
410
411   GLAN is a generalized method to create synthetic data for instruction tuning. In order to evaluate
412   the generalization capabilities of this synthetic data, we deliberately exclude task-specific training
413   sets from all benchmarks on which we conduct our assessments. Similar to Wei et al. (2023), we
414   explore whether models have been trained on task-specific in-domain data. We compute the training
415   loss Ltrain and test loss Ltest on ARC Challenge (ARC-C), GSM8K, and MATH for GLAN and
416   other models in comparison. We choose these datasets because among all benchmarks evaluated in
417   Section 3.3, these benchmarks contain training sets. Intuitively, the larger ∆ = Ltest − Ltrain is,
418   the more likely the training set is exposed. To make ∆ easier to interpret, we additionally compute
419
      the relative difference ∆(%) = (Ltest − Ltrain )/Ltest . Table 3 shows the losses of the training
      and test splits for GLAN are nearly identical (or ∆ is negative). This suggests that GLAN has not
420
      been exposed to in-domain data during training and tuning procedures. Please refer to the detailed
421
      losses of Ltrain and Ltest in Table 8 (in Appendix). Additionally, as shown in Table 8, we observe
422   that GLAN obtains higher losses on both test and training splits on GSM8K, MATH, and ARC
423   compared to other models, while performances of GLAN on these datasets are high (see Table 1).
424   This might imply that synthetic data generated by GLAN is diverse and our resulting model avoids
425   convergence to any specific domain or style present in existing benchmarks.

      4      R ELATED W ORK
478
479
      Recent literature has extensively explored the collection of various human-made resources for in-
480   struction tuning. An intuitive direction is to collect existing NLP datasets and corresponding task
481   descriptions (Sanh et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023a), typical LLMs such as
482   BLOOMZ (Muennighoff et al., 2023) and FLAN (Wei et al., 2022) are trained on this type of in-
483   struction tuning data. However, with only tens to thousands of existing datasets available, the scope
484   and diversity of instruction tuning are inevitably limited. Another common practice is to implement
485   instruction tuning with real-world human user prompts. For instance, InstructGPT (Ouyang et al.,
      2022) was trained on high-quality human prompts submitted by real-world users to OpenAI GPT


                                                             9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      APIs. Vicuna (Chiang et al., 2023) leverages user-shared prompts along with ChatGPT responses
487   for instruction tuning, and Dolly(Conover et al., 2023) was trained on simulated human-user inter-
488   actions written by over 5k employees. Nevertheless, acquiring instructional data from human users
489   typically involves high costs and involves privacy concerns.
490
      As LLM capabilities improve, instruction tuning with LLM-generated data exhibits better scala-
491
      bility and potential in addressing the super-alignment problem (Shen et al., 2023). Leveraging
492
      the in-context learning ability of LLMs, Unnatural instructions (Honovich et al., 2023) and Self-
493   instruct (Wang et al., 2023) sampled seed instructions as examples to elicit LLMs to generate new
494   instructions. Taking advantage of the rephrasing ability of LLMs, WizardLM (Xu et al., 2023a) and
495   WizardMath (Luo et al., 2023a) were trained using Evol-Instruct. Evol-Instruct iteratively employs
496   ChatGPT to rewrite seed instructions into increasingly complex instructions. Similar to generation
497   from seed instructions, carefully selected seed topics are used for generating textbook-like synthetic
498   data (Li et al., 2023) or self-chat multi-turn dialogues (Xu et al., 2023b; Ding et al., 2023) for in-
499   struction tuning. However, models trained on these LLM-generated data only work well in specific
500   domains such as math (Luo et al., 2023a; Yu et al., 2024), dialogue (Xu et al., 2023b; Ding et al.,
501
      2023) or open-ended question answering (Taori et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023a). These methods en-
      counter challenges in generalization (Gudibande et al., 2024), as the data diversity is restricted by
502
      seed instructions or seed topics.
503
504
505   5   C ONCLUSIONS
506
507   We propose GLAN, a general and scalable method for synthesizing instruction data. Experiments
508   show that GLAN can help large language models improve their capabilities in multiple dimensions,
509
      from mathematical reasoning, coding, academic exams, and logical reasoning to general instruction
      following. Currently, our synthetic data are based on the taxonomy of human knowledge and ca-
510
      pabilities, and there are other types of useful data that have not been covered. We are interested in
511
      designing methods with border coverage. Our current instruction data are mostly question-answer
512   pairs, and in the next step, we plan to generate synthetic data of multi-turn conversations and long
513   documents.

696   A.1    L IMITATIONS
697
698   While GLAN presents significant advancements in academic benchmarks. However, there may
699   still have several limitations in real world deployment. The resulting LLMs train on generated data
700   using GLAN may occasionally produce factual incorrect (or even toxic) responses. Further training
701   for refusal, hallucination reduction as well as toxic content reduction should be performed before
      deployment.

      A.2    B ROADER I MPACTS
703
704   Data synthesizing is crucial for the continual scaling of large language models, especially as we
705   exhaust available human data. GLAN demonstrates the potential to generate vast amounts of syn-
706   thetic data from scratch, paving the way for even larger-scale data synthesis efforts. While GLAN
707   has shown the effectiveness of synthetic data, we must point out that synthetic data may inherit and
708   even amplify social biases present in the frontier LLMs for generation. Future research should focus
709   on developing techniques to identify and correct biases in the generated datasets and models trained
710
      on them.
