                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010            Batch Normalization (BN), a widely-used technique in neural networks, enhances
011
               generalization and expedites training by normalizing each mini-batch to the same
               mean and variance. However, its effectiveness diminishes when confronted with
012
               diverse data distributions. To address this challenge, we propose Supervised Batch
013
               Normalization (SBN), a pioneering approach. We expand normalization beyond
014            traditional single mean and variance parameters, enabling the identification of data
015            modes prior to training. This ensures effective normalization for samples sharing
016            common features. We define contexts as modes, categorizing data with similar
017            characteristics. These contexts are explicitly defined, such as domains in domain
018            adaptation or modalities in multimodal systems, or implicitly defined through
019            clustering algorithms based on data similarity. We illustrate the superiority of
020            our approach over BN and other commonly employed normalization techniques
021            through various experiments on both single and multi-task datasets. Integrating
022
               SBN with Vision Transformer results in a remarkable 15.13% accuracy enhance-
               ment on CIFAR-100. Additionally, in domain adaptation scenarios, employing
023
               AdaMatch demonstrates an impressive 22.25% accuracy improvement on MNIST
024
               and SVHN compared to BN.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   In the realm of deep learning, input normalization is essential for optimizing the training process of
031   deep neural networks (DNNs) by addressing the variations in feature magnitudes. This method has
032   been shown to accelerate convergence in neural networks with a single hidden layer, as highlighted
033   by LeCun et al. LeCun et al. (2002). However, its efficacy diminishes in more complex architectures
034
      with multiple hidden layers. This decline is due to the progressive transformation of data through
      successive layers, which causes activations to diverge from the properties of the initially normalized
035
      inputs. To address this challenge, normalizing activations during training has become a critical ap-
036
      proach. By ensuring that the statistical properties of activations remain consistent across all layers,
037   this strategy facilitates stable and efficient training of deep neural networks. Consequently, this prac-
038   tice not only enhances the convergence rate but also significantly improves the overall performance
039   of the model.
040   Batch Normalization (BN) Ioffe & Szegedy (2015), a popular activation normalization technique,
041   stabilizes the optimization process by normalizing feature statistics within a batch. Despite its
042   widespread success, Batch Normalization (BN) has notable drawbacks due to its reliance on mini-
043   batch statistics. While the variability in batch statistics can enhance robustness and generalization, it
044   also leads to issues when the mean and variance estimates are inaccurate. This is particularly prob-
045
      lematic with heterogeneous data and small batch sizes, which can cause BN to fail in effectively
      normalizing activations. In such cases, BN struggles to normalize activations using a single mean
046
      and variance Wu & He (2018); Bilen & Vedaldi (2017); Deecke et al. (2018).
047
      To overcome these limitations, we introduce Supervised Batch Normalization (SBN). SBN assigns
048   samples in a mini-batch to different modes using predefined groups called contexts, then normalizes
049   each sample based onin neural networks, enhances generalization and expedites train- the statistics
050   of its corresponding context. Instead of relying on random mini-batches, SBN utilizes contexts
051   that group similar samples through domain knowledge or clustering algorithms. The proposed
052   method can be seamlessly integrated as layers in standard deep learning libraries. We evaluated
053   SBN on various classification tasks and demonstrated that it consistently outperforms BN and other
      widely used normalization techniques.

      2.1   N ORMALIZATION METHODS
057
058   Batch normalization (BN) Ioffe & Szegedy (2015) is the most common normalization technique
059   in cutting-edge classification architectures. Recently, new alternatives have emerged to broaden its
060   applicability and enhance its generalizability. Batch Renormalization Ioffe (2017) is an extension
061   of BN that addresses the issue of varying mini-batch statistics during training. Weight Normaliza-
062   tion Salimans & Kingma (2016) reparameterizes the weight vectors in a neural network by sepa-
063   rating their magnitude and direction. This technique simplifies the optimization process and often
064
      results in faster convergence during training. It introduces additional parameters to stabilize training
      by aligning the statistics of the current mini-batch with the moving averages of the training data.
065
      Layer Normalization Ba et al. (2016) is a technique that normalizes samples across the features for
066
      each individual example, rather than across the min-batch. This approach helps stabilize the hidden
067   states in recurrent neural networks and improves training efficiency by eliminating the dependency
068   on mini-batch size. Instance Normalization Ulyanov et al. (2016) normalizes samples across each
069   feature map for individual examples, making it particularly effective for style transfer tasks. By
070   focusing on the statistics of single instances, it helps preserve stylistic details and achieve more con-
071   sistent visual outputs. Group Normalization Wu & He (2018) divides the channels of each layer into
072   smaller groups and normalizes the features within each group. This method provides stable training
073   benefits similar to BN but is less sensitive to mini-batch size, making it suitable for tasks with small
074   mini-batch sizes. Mode Normalization Luo et al. (2019) adjusts the normalization process based
075
      on the mode of the feature distributions instead of their mean. This method aims to better handle
      skewed data distributions, resulting in improved training stability and model performance. Mixture
076
      Normalization Kalayeh & Shah (2019) addresses the limitations of BN in capturing the complex
077
      variations present in deep neural network activations. By leveraging Gaussian Mixture Models to
078   assign samples to components and normalize based on multiple means and standard deviations,
079   MN adapts to the diverse modes of variation inherent in the data distribution. RMSNorm Zhang &
080   Sennrich (2019) extends Layer Normalization by utilizing the root mean square (RMS) of the acti-
081   vations within each layer. This method aims to stabilize training by normalizing activations based
082   on their magnitudes, providing a robust normalization technique for deep neural networks. Unsuper-
083   vised Batch Normalization Koçyigit et al. (2020) (UBN) leverages unlabeled examples to compute
084   mini-batch statistics, addressing the challenge of bias on small datasets and offering regularization
085   benefits from data manifold exploration. UBN demonstrates efficacy in tasks like monocular depth
086
      estimation, particularly beneficial where obtaining dense labeled data is challenging and costly.
      While all these variants enhance the usability and stability of BN, our approach appears to be the first
087
      to extend BN by incorporating contexts, predefined groups of samples with shared characteristics,
088
      for normalization purposes.

      2.2   I NCORPORATING M ULTIPLE M ODES FOR E FFECTIVE N ORMALIZATION
091
092   BN has been widely adopted in deep learning architectures to improve training stability and conver-
093   gence. However, BN’s assumption that the entire mini-batch should be normalized with the same
094   mean and variance poses challenges, especially in the face of diverse data distributions. This as-
095   sumption can lead to suboptimal performance, particularly on datasets with varying characteristics.
096   Recent research has highlighted the limitations of this assumption, emphasizing the importance of
097   accommodating multiple modes of variation within the data distribution. Approaches such as Mix-
098   ture Normalization Kalayeh & Shah (2019), which employs Gaussian Mixture Models to capture
099   multiple means and variances associated with different modes of variation, have been proposed to
100
      address this issue. Similarly, studies like Luo et al. Luo et al. (2019) have underscored the necessity
      of considering diverse data distributions and employing multiple mean and variance estimates for
101
      effective normalization. These insights emphasize the importance of moving beyond the simplistic
102
      assumptions of BN to better accommodate the complexities of real-world datasets.

166
      frequently computationally expensive due to tasks such as estimating different modes, such as the

173   3.3     S UPERVISED BATCH N ORMALIZATION
174
175   Our proposed method, SBN, introduces a novel approach to enhance neural network training
176   efficiency. SBN operates by initially grouping samples into K distinct contexts prior to training.
177
      Subsequently, during the training process, samples belonging to the same context k within a given
      mini-batch are normalized using identical parameters µk and σk2 . By leveraging these predefined
178
      contexts, each comprising samples with similar characteristics, SBN effectively introduces multiple
179
      modes without incurring the computational overhead associated with estimating them during neural
180   network training. This approach streamlines the normalization process and significantly reduces
181   computational costs, thereby enhancing training efficiency and overall model performance.
182
183
184   3.3.1    U NDERSTANDING C ONTEXT: D EFINITION AND C ONSTRUCTION M ETHODS
185
186
      Context serves as the foundational element within SBN, representing groups of samples sharing
      similar characteristics. Our approach offers diverse methods for context construction:
187
188           • For domain adaptation tasks Zhang et al. (2021); Qi et al. (2020); Li et al. (2020), each
189             domain is treated as a distinct context.
190
191           • In datasets featuring additional hierarchical structures, such as CIFAR-100 Krizhevsky
192             et al. (2009a) or the Oxford-IIIT Pet dataset Parkhi et al. (2012), we designate each
193             superclass as a separate context.
194
195           • For datasets lacking predefined contextual structures, we employ clustering algorithms like
196             k-means Arthur & Vassilvitskii (2007) to partition samples into clusters, with each cluster
197             forming an individual context.
198
199
      This multifaceted approach ensures flexible and comprehensive context formation, vital for the
      effective implementation of SBN across various domains and datasets.

      6   C ONCLUSION
433
434
      Our study introduces a groundbreaking normalization technique called Supervised Batch Normal-
435   ization (SBN), which extends the capabilities of traditional Batch Normalization (BN) to effectively
436   handle heterogeneous datasets characterized by diverse data distributions. Unlike BN, which
437   normalizes each mini-batch using a single mean and variance, SBN addresses the challenge posed
438   by varied data distributions within a mini-batch by normalizing based on grouped data with similar
439   characteristics, referred to as contexts. We present three methods to accurately define these contexts.
440
441   Experimental results from both multi-task scenarios with heterogeneous datasets and single-task
442   scenarios with homogeneous datasets demonstrate that SBN consistently outperforms BN and its
443   variants, including methods based on multiple modes such as Mixture Normalization and Mode
444   Normalization. SBN offers ease of implementation and versatility, serving as a powerful layer in
445   neural networks to enhance performance and accelerate convergence.
446
447   Looking ahead, our future research will delve into exploring the robustness of SBN in multimodal
448   systems, such as those involving text, image, audio, and other modalities, where contexts are well-
449   defined and critical for effective normalization strategies.
