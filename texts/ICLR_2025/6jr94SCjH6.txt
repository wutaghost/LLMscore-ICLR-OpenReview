                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011
012
               Offline reinforcement learning (RL) is essential when online exploration is costly
               or unsafe, but it often struggles with high epistemic uncertainty due to limited
013
               data. Existing methods learn fixed conservative policies, which limit adaptivity and
014
               generalization. To tackle these challenges, we propose Reflect-then-Plan (Ref-
015            Plan), a novel doubly Bayesian approach for offline model-based (MB) planning
016            that enhances offline-learned policies for improved adaptivity and generalization.
017            RefPlan integrates uncertainty modeling and MB planning in a unified probabilistic
018            framework, recasting planning as Bayesian posterior estimation. During deploy-
019            ment, it updates a belief distribution over environment dynamics based on real-time
020            observations. By incorporating this uncertainty into MB planning via marginaliza-
021            tion, RefPlan derives plans that account for unknowns beyond the agent’s limited
022            knowledge. Empirical results on standard benchmarks show that RefPlan signifi-
023
               cantly improves the performance of conservative offline RL policies. In particular,
               RefPlan maintains robust performance under high epistemic uncertainty and limited
024
               data, while demonstrating resilience to changing environment dynamics, improving
025
               the flexibility, generalizability, and robustness of offline-learned policies.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Recent years have seen significant progress in offline reinforcement learning (RL), in which a learner
032
      has to learn a performant policy from a static dataset of experiences (Levine et al., 2020; Kumar et al.,
      2020; An et al., 2021; Kostrikov et al., 2022). This is particularly appealing in scenarios where online
033
      exploration is costly or unsafe (Yu et al., 2018; Kalashnikov et al., 2018; Boute et al., 2022).
034
035   The agent’s inability to gather more experiences have severe implications. In particular, it becomes
036   practically impossible to precisely identify the true Markov decision process (MDP) with a limited
037   dataset, as it only covers a portion of the entire state-action space, leading to high epistemic uncertainty
038
      for states and actions outside the data distribution. Most offline RL methods aim to learn a conservative
      policy that stays close to the data distribution, thus steering away from high epistemic uncertainty.
039
040   While incorporating conservatism into offline learning has proven effective (Jin et al., 2021; Yu
041   et al., 2020; Kumar et al., 2020), it can result in overly restrictive policies that lack generalizability.
042   Most methods learn a Markovian policy that relies solely on the current state, leading the agent to
043   potentially take poor actions in unexpected states during evaluation. Model-based (MB) planning can
044
      enhance the agent’s responsiveness during evaluation (Sikchi et al., 2021; Argenson & Dulac-Arnold,
      2021; Zhan et al., 2022), but it still primarily addresses epistemic uncertainty through conservatism.
045
046   Noting this challenge, Chen et al. (2021) and Ghosh et al. (2022) propose to learn an adaptive policy
047   that can reason about the environment and accordingly react at evaluation. Essentially, they formulate
048   the offline RL problem as a partially observable MDP (POMDP)—where the partial observability
049   relates to the agent’s epistemic uncertainty, aka Epistemic POMDP (Ghosh et al., 2021). Thus,
050
      learning an adaptive policy involves approximately inferring the belief state from the history of
      transitions experienced by the agent and allowing the policy to condition on this belief state.
051
052   While learning an adaptive policy can help make the agent more flexible and generalizable, it still
053   heavily depends on the training phase. Our empirical evaluation demonstrates that a learned policy—
      whether it be adaptive or fixed—can be significantly strengthened by incorporating MB planning.

076   However, existing MB planning methods fall short in adequately addressing the agent’s epistemic
077   uncertainty, and it remains elusive how one can effectively incorporate the uncertainty into planning.
078
      We propose Reflect-then-Plan (RefPlan), a novel doubly Bayesian approach for offline MB planning.
079
      RefPlan combines epistemic uncertainty modeling with MB planning in a unified probabilistic
080
      framework, inspired by the control-as-inference paradigm (Levine, 2018). RefPlan adapts Bayes-
081   adaptive deep RL techniques (Zintgraf et al., 2020; Dorfman et al., 2021) to infer a posterior belief
082   distribution from past experiences during test time (Reflect). To harness this uncertainty for planning,
083   we recast planning as Bayesian posterior estimation (Plan). By marginalizing over the agent’s
084   epistemic uncertainty, RefPlan effectively considers a range of possible scenarios beyond the agent’s
085   immediate knowledge, resulting in a posterior distribution over optimized plans under the learned
086   model (Figure 1).
087   In our experiments, we demonstrate that RefPlan can be integrated with various offline RL policy
088   learning algorithms to consistently boost their test-time performance in standard offline RL benchmark
089   domains (Fu et al., 2020). RefPlan not only maintains robust performance under high epistemic
090   uncertainty but also shows superior resilience when the environment dynamics change or when data
091   availability is limited, outperforming compared methods in these challenging scenarios.

094   2    R ELATED W ORK
095
096   Offline RL In offline RL, policy distribution shift is a major challenge, leading to instabilities
097   like extrapolation errors and value overestimation (Kumar et al., 2019; Fujimoto et al., 2019). To
098   address this, various approaches introduce conservatism. For instance, policy constraint methods
099   constrain the learned policy’s deviation from the behavior policy (Wu et al., 2019; Kumar et al., 2019;
100   Fujimoto & Gu, 2021). Value-based approaches penalize the values of out-of-distribution (OOD)
101   actions (Kumar et al., 2020; An et al., 2021). One can also avoid querying OOD actions by learning
102   the value function solely from in-dataset samples and distilling a policy (Kostrikov et al., 2022).
103   MB offline policy learning methods learn a dynamics model from batch data, then use the model to
104   generate imaginary rollouts to augment the offline dataset. To mitigate the risk of exploiting errors
105   in the model for policy optimization, model uncertainty—heuristically estimated from ensemble
106   dynamics models—can be penalized in rewards (Yu et al., 2020; Kidambi et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2021).
107   Alternatively, values of model-generated samples can be minimized (Yu et al., 2021). Adversarial
      dynamics models can also discourage the learner from choosing OOD actions (Rigter et al., 2022).

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Typically, these offline policies are fixed after training, but Ghosh et al. (2021; 2022) show that fixed
109   policies can fail under high epistemic uncertainty, highlighting the need for adaptive policies. APE-V
110   (Ghosh et al., 2022) addresses this by maintaining a value ensemble to approximate the distribution
111   over possible environments, adapting the policy based on this ensemble during evaluation. MAPLE
112   (Chen et al., 2021) uses an RNN to encode the agent’s history into a dense vector, allowing the policy
113   to adapt by conditioning on this history. MAPLE also utilizes an ensemble dynamics model to expose
114   the adaptive policy to diverse simulated environments, enhancing its robustness to uncertainty.
115
116   Model-based planning for offline RL MB planning can add responsiveness at test time. For
117   example, MBOP (Argenson & Dulac-Arnold, 2021) uses model predictive control (MPC) with MPPI
118   (Williams et al., 2015), a trajectory optimization (TrajOpt) method, modifying it for offline setups by
119   using a behavior-cloning (BC) policy for trajectory generation. Uncertain rollouts can be filtered out
120
      based on the ensemble disagreement (Zhan et al., 2022).
121   LOOP (Sikchi et al., 2021) enhances offline-learned policies with MB planning, achieving superior
122   performance than MBOP. It approaches offline MB planning using KL-regularized optimization but
123   only addresses epistemic uncertainty by penalizing ensemble variance in rewards during TrajOpt. In
124   contrast, RefPlan is derived from a Bayesian perspective, which explicitly accounts for the agent’s
125   epistemic uncertainty, resulting in better generalization and stronger performance.
126
127   Probabilistic interpretation of MB planning The control-as-inference framework (Levine, 2018;
128
      Abdolmaleki et al., 2018) offers a probabilistic perspective on control and RL problems. Within the
      context of MB planning, this framework naturally leads to sampling-based solutions (Piché et al.,
129
      2019; Okada & Taniguchi, 2020). For instance, Okada & Taniguchi (2020) demonstrated that various
130
      sampling-based TrajOpt algorithms can be derived from this probabilistic view. Janner et al. (2022)
131   introduced a diffusion-based planner that utilizes the control-as-inference framework to derive a
132   perturbation distribution, embedding reward signals into the diffusion sampling process. However, to
133   the best of our knowledge, we are the first to propose an offline MB planning algorithm that integrates
134   an offline-learned policy as a prior within a Bayesian framework and explicitly accounts for the
135   epistemic uncertainty during planning, all within a unified probabilistic formulation.
136
137   Bayesian RL and epistemic POMDP Bayesian RL (Ghavamzadeh et al., 2015) and Bayes-adaptive
138   MDP (BAMDP) (Duff, 2002) tackle the problem of learning optimal policies in unknown MDPs. A
139   BAMDP can be reformulated as a belief-state MDP, where the belief state acts as a sufficient statistic
140   summarizing the agent’s history (Guez et al., 2012). This belief-state representation highlights
141   BAMDP as a specific instance of a POMDP (Kaelbling et al., 1998). Building on this, Zintgraf et al.
142
      (2020) framed meta-RL as a BAMDP and proposed VariBAD, a variational inference-based method
      for approximating the belief distribution over possible environments, enabling the optimization of
143
      meta-policies.
144
145   Relatedly, Ghosh et al. (2021) introduced the concept of epistemic POMDP, where an agent’s epis-
146   temic uncertainty—stemming from factors such as incomplete exploration or ambiguity in task
147   specification—induces partial observability. Unlike BAMDPs, which primarily focus on online learn-
148
      ing and asymptotic regrets, epistemic POMDPs emphasize the agent’s performance during a single
      evaluation episode, making them especially relevant for test-time generalization. Notably, Ghosh
149
      et al. (2022) observed that offline RL problems in a single-task setting can also be conceptualized as
150
      epistemic POMDPs. This arises because static offline datasets typically cover only a subset of the
151   state-action space, introducing partial observability regarding true environment dynamics outside the
152   offline data distribution.
153
154
      In this work, we similarly adopt the epistemic POMDP perspective for addressing single-task offline
      RL. However, unlike prior approaches, our focus is on MB planning. Specifically, we aim to enhance
155
      policies learned through offline RL by addressing the agent’s epistemic uncertainty, thereby enabling
156
      more effective generalization during deployment.

232   However, computing the exact posterior belief is generally intractable. Therefore, in Section 4, we
233   tackle this challenge by approximating the belief distribution through variational inference techniques
234   adapted from meta-RL approaches (Zintgraf et al., 2020; Dorfman et al., 2021).

      4     R EF P LAN : A P ROBABILISTIC F RAMEWORK FOR O FFLINE P LANNING
237
238   In this section, we now seek to address the following question:
239
240            How can a learned model be utilized at test time to enhance the performance of
241            an offline-trained agent and enable it to account for its epistemic uncertainty?
242
      To tackle this, we introduce RefPlan: a novel probabilistic framework for MB planning that leverages
243
      learned models and allows the agent to reason with its uncertainty during deployment.
244
245   In Section 4.1, we derive a sampling-based offline MB planning algorithm rooted in a probabilistic
246   inference perspective, demonstrating how this approach can enhance the capabilities of any offline-
247
      trained policy. Next, we delve into the epistemic POMDP formulation of the offline RL problem
      in Section 4.2, outlining how the agent’s epistemic uncertainty can be effectively captured and
248
      represented. We introduce variational learning techniques for estimating the agent’s uncertainty over
249
      the environment dynamics. Finally, in Section 4.3, we unify these concepts, presenting how RefPlan
250   integrates epistemic uncertainty into the MB planning process, enabling the agent to plan under the
251   learned models and adapt in real-time while accounting for its uncertainty.
252
253   4.1   O FFLINE M ODEL -BASED P LANNING AS P ROBABILISTIC I NFERENCE
254
255   We recast offline MB planning within the control-as-inference framework, allowing us to treat
256   planning as a posterior inference problem. This approach enables the agent to optimize its actions
257   by reasoning over the learned dynamics model and prior knowledge obtained from offline training.
258   Central to this formulation is the use of a prior policy, which guides the agent’s plans based on
259
      knowledge learned during offline training.

332   4.2    L EARNING E PISTEMIC U NCERTAINTY VIA VARIATIONAL I NFERENCE
333
334   Although offline RL can be framed as a BAMDP, obtaining an exact posterior belief update is
335   impractical. Inspired by Zintgraf et al. (2020) and Dorfman et al. (2021), we introduce a latent
336   variable m to approximate the underlying MDP. We assume that knowing the posterior distribution
337   p(m|τ:t ) is sufficient for planning under epistemic uncertainty. As a result, transitions and rewards
338   are assumed to depend on m, i.e., T (st+1 |st , at , m) and r(st , at , m). When p(m|τ:t ) is accurate and
339
      τ:t is in-distribution, T and r will closely match the transitions in D. For OOD τ:t , the posterior over
      m captures epistemic uncertainty, allowing T and r to model diverse possible scenarios.
340
341   Given a trajectory τ:t , consider the task of maximizing its likelihood, conditioned on the actions.
342   Conditioning on the actions is essential because they are generated by a policy—β during training
343   and πp at evaluation—and are not modeled by the environment. Although directly optimizing the

530   6     C ONCLUSION
531
532   In this paper, we introduced RefPlan (Reflect-then-Plan), a novel doubly Bayesian approach to offline
533   model-based planning that integrates epistemic uncertainty modeling with model-based planning
534   in a unified probabilistic framework. Our method enhances offline RL by explicitly accounting
535   for epistemic uncertainty, a common challenge in offline settings where data coverage is often
536
      incomplete. Through extensive experiments on standard offline RL benchmarks, we demonstrated
      that RefPlan consistently outperforms existing methods, particularly under challenging conditions
537
      of OOD initialization, limited data availability, and changing environment dynamics, making it a
538
      valuable tool for more reliable and adaptive offline RL. Future work could extend RefPlan to more
539   complex models and environments.

      A     A DDITIONAL BACKGROUND

758   A.1    BAYES -A DAPTIVE M ARKOV D ECISION P ROCESSES
759
760   Bayes-Adaptive Markov Decision Processes (BAMDPs) (Duff, 2002) extend the standard MDP
761   framework by explicitly incorporating uncertainty over the transition and reward functions. In a
762   BAMDP, instead of assuming that the transition dynamics T (s′ |s, a) and reward function r(s, a) are
763   known and fixed, we assume that they are drawn from an unknown distribution. The agent maintains
764   a posterior belief over these functions and updates it as new data are collected through interaction
765
      with the environment.
766   To illustrate, consider a simple case where we have finite and discrete state and action spaces with
767   |S| = ns and |A| = na ; hence, a state can be represented with an integer, i.e., s = i for i = 1, . . . , ns ,
768   and similarly for the actions. While the reward function r(s, a) is assumed to be known, we are
769   uncertain about the transition probabilities T (s′ |s, a). We can model this uncertainty by placing a
770   prior distribution over the transition probabilities, typically using a Dirichlet prior, which is conjugate
771
      to the multinomial likelihood of observing transitions between states.
772   For each state-action pair (s, a) ∈ S × A, the transition probabilities T (s′ |s, a) are parameterized by
773   a multinomial distribution:
774                                      T (s′ |s, a) ∼ Multinomial(θ s,a,s′ ),                                 (14)
775
      where θ s,a = (θ s,a,1 , . . . , θ s,a,ns ) represents the probabilities of transitioning from state s to any
776
      state s′ ∈ S under action a. These parameters follow a Dirichlet distribution:
777
778
                                                θ s,a ∼ Dirichlet(αs,a ),                                       (15)
779   where αs,a = (αs,a,1 , . . . , αs,a,ns ) > 0 are the Dirichlet hyperparameters.
780
      Initially, the agent holds a prior belief about the transition probabilities, represented by the Dirichlet
781   hyperparameters αs,a for all state-action pairs. As the agent interacts with the environment and
782   observes transitions of the form (s, a, s′ ), it updates its posterior belief by simply updating the
783   corresponding Dirichlet hyperparameters. Specifically, when the agent observes a transition from
784   state s to state s′ under action a, the corresponding Dirichlet hyperparameter is updated as:
785                                              αs,a,s′ ← αs,a,s′ + 1,                                         (16)
786
      while all other Dirichlet hyperparameters remain unchanged. This process of updating the Dirichlet
787
      hyperparameters fully captures the agent’s experiences; hence, these hyperparameters act as sufficient
788
      statistics for the agent’s belief about the environment.
789
790   By transforming the BAMDP into a belief-state MDP, where the belief state bt = p(θ|τ:t ) is a distri-
791   bution over transition probabilities conditioned on the observed trajectory τ:t = (s0 , a0 , s1 , . . . , st ),
792
      the agent can solve the problem using standard MDP solution methods. The augmented state space,
      or hyper-state space, includes both the physical state s ∈ S and the belief state b ∈ B. In this simple
793
      finite state-action example, the belief state corresponds to the Dirichlet hyperparameters α.
794
795   The transition dynamics of the resulting belief-state MDP are fully known and can be written as:
796                 T (s̄′ |s̄, a) = T (s′ , α′ |s, α, a) = T (s′ |s, a, α)p(α′ |s, α, a)                       (17)
797                                                               αs,a,s′          ′

798                                                       =P                   I αs,a,s ′ = αs,a,s′ + 1 ,       (18)
                                                               s′′ ∈S αs,a,s
                                                                            ′′
799
800
      where I(·) is the indicator function. This transformation turns the BAMDP into a fully observable
      MDP in the hyper-state space, which allows the use of standard, e.g., DP methods to compute an
801
      optimal policy.
802
803   However, the computational complexity of solving the BAMDP grows quickly with the number of
804   states and actions. If the states are fully connected (i.e., p(s′ |s, a) > 0, ∀s, a, s′ ), the number of
805   reachable belief states increases exponentially over time, making exact solutions intractable for even
806
      moderately sized problems.
807   For a comprehensive overview of solution methods for BAMDPs, we refer readers to the survey by
808   Ghavamzadeh et al. (2015). In this work, we have utilized variational inference techniques from
809   Zintgraf et al. (2020) and Dorfman et al. (2021) to approximate the agent’s posterior belief over the
      environment dynamics, p(b|τ:t ), based on past experiences.

1063   Figure 10 illustrates the architecture of RefPlan. For the encoder, we adopt the architecture from
1064   VariBAD (Zintgraf et al., 2020), with a few minor modifications to the hyperparameters. The encoder
1065   utilizes a GRU network to encode the agent’s history and outputs the parameters of a Gaussian
1066   distribution representing the latent variable mt .
1067
       At time t = 0, we initialize z−1 = 0 and a−1 = 0. The state st , the previous action at−1 , and the
1068   previous reward rt−1 are first embedded into their respective latent spaces using distinct linear layers,
1069   each followed by ReLU activation. These embedded vectors, along with the hidden state from the
1070   previous time step zt−1 , are then processed by the GRU, which outputs the updated hidden state zt .
1071   This hidden state is subsequently linearly projected onto the task embedding space to obtain the mean
1072   (µt ) and log variance (log σt2 ) of the Gaussian distribution for the latent variable at the current time
1073   step.
1074   Since the decoder plays a critical role in test-time planning, we follow established practices from
1075   prior work and implement the decoder using a probabilistic ensemble network (Chua et al., 2018;
1076   Janner et al., 2019; Yu et al., 2020; 2021; Chen et al., 2021). Specifically, the ensemble consists of
1077   20 models, from which we select the 14 elite models that achieve the lowest validation loss during
1078   training. The decoder network conditions on a latent sample mt ∼ N (µt , σt2 ), along with st and at ,
1079   to predict the next state st+1 and reward rt+1 . The hyperparameters associated with the architecture
       are summarized in Table 3.

1100   RefPlan requires two stages of pretraining. First, we use an off-the-shelf offline RL algorithm to train
1101   a prior policy πp . In our experiments, we evaluated several algorithms, including CQL (Kumar et al.,
1102   2020), EDAC (An et al., 2021), MOPO (Yu et al., 2020), COMBO (Yu et al., 2021), and MAPLE
1103   (Chen et al., 2021), though any offline RL policy learning algorithm could be utilized.
1104   Second, we train the encoder qϕ and the decoder p̂ψ . The encoder qϕ is trained using the ELBO
1105   loss as defined in (9). The decoder p̂ψ is trained to reconstruct the past and to predict the next state,
1106   conditioned on the sample mt the current state st , and the action at . This training constitutes the first
1107   phase of dynamics learning. During this step, the encoder learns a latent representation that captures
1108   essential information for reconstructing the trajectory. Unlike VariBAD, where the decoder is trained
1109   to reconstruct the entire trajectory including future states, we found that focusing on the past and the
1110   next state improves the decoder’s performance.
1111   After completing the first training phase, we freeze the encoder network parameters and proceed to
1112   the second phase. In this phase, we fine-tune the decoder network p̂ψ to accurately predict the next
1113   state given mt , st , and at . This is achieved using the loss function defined in (11), which we reiterate
1114   here for clarity:

1266   In this section, we provide a detailed discussion of the computational costs associated with deploying
1267   RefPlan. As outlined in Appendix C.3, RefPlan requires the following pretrained components: a prior
1268   policy πp , an encoder qϕ , and a decoder p̂ψ . Since the prior policy is trained using standard offline
1269   policy learning algorithms (e.g., CQL, EDAC, MOPO, COMBO, and MAPLE), which are not our
1270   contributions, we focus on reporting the computational costs associated with training the VAE model
1271
       and executing the planning stage. All experiments were conducted on a single machine equipped
       with an RTX 3090 GPU.
1272
1273   VAE Pretraining Table 11 presents the per-epoch runtimes for VAE pretraining in the three envi-
1274   ronments. The reported runtimes correspond to datasets with 2M transition samples, the largest
1275   dataset size used in our experiments. Both the VAE pretraining and decoder fine-tuning phases were
1276   executed for up to 200 epochs or until the validation loss ceased to improve for 5 consecutive epochs,
1277
       whichever occurred first.
1278   Test-Time Planning At test time, planning with RefPlan involves selecting hyperparameters as
1279   detailed in Appendix D.2. Among these, the planning horizon H and the number of latent samples n̄
1280   influence runtime. Specifically, the computational cost scales linearly with H, which is an inherent
1281   property of planning algorithms. However, the cost increases sub-linearly with n̄, as shown in Table
1282   12. For example, with H = 4 and n̄ = 4, the agent achieves approximately 53 environment steps per
1283
       second. We hypothesize that further optimization of PyTorch tensor operations to fully exploit GPU
       parallelism could yield even better computational performance, particularly with respect to n̄.
