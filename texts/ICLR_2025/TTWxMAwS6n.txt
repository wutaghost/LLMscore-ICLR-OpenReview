009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               We introduce X-PlugVid, a unified framework designed to seamlessly adapt pre-
012
               trained image-based plug-and-play modules for video diffusion models, facilitat-
013
               ing controllable video generation without the need for retraining. This framework
014            leverages a spatial-temporal adapter to effectively bridge the gap between image
015            and video diffusion models. Specifically, we adopt a frozen copy of a large-scale
016            pretrained image diffusion model (e.g. Stable Diffusion v1.5) as spatial prior.
017            Then we train a spatial-temporal adapter to convert the prior into temporally con-
018            sistent guidance for video diffusion models (e.g. SVD). To further enhance the
019            effectiveness of image plugins in guiding video models, we introduce a timestep
020            remapping strategy. Recognizing that denoising is an entropic reduction process,
021            this strategy selects priors from later timesteps of the image model, which contain
022
               richer information, to be injected into the video models, optimizing the quality and
               consistency of the generated videos. Comprehensive experimental evaluations of
023
               X-PlugVid demonstrate its broad compatibility with diverse operational condi-
024
               tions and different plugins, confirming that leveraging priors from a pretrained
025            diffusion model can minimize redundant training and enable versatile, control-
026            lable video generation.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Recent diffusion models have made significant advancements in generating high-quality im-
033   ages (Podell et al., 2023; StabilityAI; Midjourney; Li et al., 2024) and videos (Blattmann et al.,
034   2023a; Zhang et al., 2023d;b; Wang et al., 2023b) from text descriptions. Despite these successes,
035   controlling the structure and details of generated images using only text remains challenging. There-
036   fore, many studies (Zhang et al., 2023c; Mou et al., 2023) have focused on controlling the image
037   generation process by incorporating additional conditioning inputs such as bounding boxes (Li et al.,
038   2023b), reference object images (Ruiz et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a), and segmentation maps (Xie
039
      et al., 2023; Avrahami et al., 2023). These methods typically involve training a plug-and-play mod-
      ule, often referred to as plugins, on the basis of a large-scale pretrained image diffusion model to
040
      achieve conditioning. Inspired by these approaches, several studies (Zhang et al., 2023e; Chen et al.,
041
      2023b; Lin et al., 2024) attempt to replicate this success in video generation.
042
043   Similar to image generation, ControlVideo (Zhang et al., 2023e) proposes a training-free method to
044   use image plugins for video generation, while Control-A-Video (Chen et al., 2023b) achieves the
045
      same goal by introducing an additional training-based temporal module. However, these methods
      have some issues: 1) they lack flexibility and cannot be easily transferred to different pretrained
046
      models. For instance, a plugin module developed for Control-A-Video cannot be readily transferred
047
      to other video models like SVD. 2) These approaches require extensive training data. To train each
048   plugin, it’s necessary to label specific video conditions such as video depth, sketches, and bound-
049   ing boxes, which is more costly and time-consuming than that for images. In contrast, the image
050   diffusion models already benefit from numerous effective plugins facilitating controlled image gen-
051   eration. This raises a question: can these plugins designed for images be effectively adapted for
052   video generation models?. Thus, in this work, we explore the application of image-based plugins
053   to video models for video generation. Notably, we only focus on image plugins whose function is
      spatial control (e.g. ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023c), T2I-Adapter (Mou et al., 2023)) in this work.

054
      Recent research (Ran et al., 2024) has demonstrated that the domain gap between two different
055   versions of image models can be bridged effectively with a well-designed adapter. This allows
056   the upgraded model to be universally compatible with all plugins of the base model without the
057   need for retraining. However, when extending this approach to bridge image and video models, we
058   must also consider the modality gap. Compared to image data, videos represent higher-dimensional
059   and more complex data distributions than images. Furthermore, by analyzing the principles of X-
060   Adapter (Ran et al., 2024) and ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023c), we found that the injection of
061   high-frequency additional features at every timestep is essential for guidance while previous works
062   overlook this point.
063   In this work, we propose X-PlugVid to equip the video diffusion model with pretrained image plu-
064   gins for high-quality and consistent controllable video generation without the need for retraining.
065   X-PlugVid universally allows all spatial-control plugins to function with video diffusion models
066   by training a generic adapter. Unlike prior work (Ran et al., 2024), we extend the spatial adapter
067   to a spatial-temporal adapter, enabling it to simultaneously possess domain adaptation and tempo-
068   ral modeling capabilities. Additionally, by analyzing the spectral characteristics of the adapter, we
069
      found that it tends to learn low-frequency components to generate smooth background and camera
      movements but lacks high-frequency features, often resulting in the loss of the subject’s appearance.
070
      To address this issue, high-frequency pass filtering is applied to the adapter’s input.
071
072   To enable better guidance, we further analyze the conditioning principles of ControlNet (Zhang
073   et al., 2023c). Based on our findings, we propose a novel timestep remapping method. Specifically,
074   we found that the features of the image diffusion model in the early steps contain insufficient in-
075
      formation for effective guidance, though early timesteps are crucial for determining low-frequency
      components like layout. To improve guidance at these critical early timesteps, we have moved away
076
      from synchronously mapping the timesteps of the image and video model backbones. Instead, we
077
      strategically map the later timesteps of the image model, which contain richer information, to the
078   earlier timesteps of the video model. This adjustment allows us to inject more useful information
079   early in the video generation process, improving the overall quality and coherence of generated
080   videos.
081
      In our experiments, we first demonstrate that our method shows good generalizability across various
082
      types of video models, i.e., text-to-video models like Hotshot-XL (Mullan et al., 2023) and image-
083
      to-video models like (Zhang et al., 2023d). Next, we demonstrate that our approach shows strong
084   compatibility with various types of image plugins and surpasses previous methods for controllable
085   video generation. Lastly, we provide comprehensive ablations for the design choices of X-PlugVid
086   and qualitative examples.
087
      In summary, the contribution of this paper can be summarized as:
088
089
            • We target a new task in the large-scale generative model era where we efficiently reuse
090
              pretrained image plugin for controllable video generation.
091
092         • We analyze the mechanism of utilizing pretrained diffusion models as spatial prior. Based
093
              on our findings, we design a spatial-temporal adapter for guidance and introduce a novel
              timestep remapping strategy to enhance the adapter’s guidance ability.
094
095         • Experiments show the proposed method demonstrates compatibility with various condi-
096           tions and plugins and surpasses previous methods in terms of performance.

101
      progress recently due to the advancement of diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Dhari-

105   expands the standard image architecture to accommodate video data and trains on both image and
106   video together. Other methods develop video models based on powerful text-to-image models like
107   Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2021), adding extra layers to capture cross-frame motion and en-
      sure consistency. Among these, Tune-A-Video (Wu et al., 2023) employs a causal attention module

109   2023b) efficiently transforms T2I models into video generators by aligning independently sampled

111   video generation on personalized image models (StabilityAI). Other text-to-video works include
112   marrying latent and pixel space (Zhang et al., 2023b) and cascaded genration (Wang et al., 2023b).
113   To address high-quality video generation tasks, several works (Zhang et al., 2023d; Chen et al.,
114   2023a; 2024a) develop image-to-video models and all of them achieve remarkable pixel quality.
115   Controllable video generation. Since text prompts often provide unclear guidance regarding the
116   motions and spatial structure of videos, making such control mechanisms is essential in video gen-
117   eration. For high-level control over video motion, some work proposes to use motion trajecto-
118   ries (Yin et al., 2023), pose sequences (Ma et al., 2024) while some work uses Low-Rank Adapta-
119   tions(LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021) to learn specific motion patterns (Zhao et al., 2023b). For fine-grained
120   spatial structure control, Gen-1 (Esser et al., 2023) first introduces the use of depth sequences as
121   guidance. VideoComposer (Wang et al., 2023a) incorporate several conditions during training while
122   other methods (Chen et al., 2023b; Zhang et al., 2023e) adopt pretrained image ControlNet (Zhang
123
      et al., 2023c) for video generation. Though these methods achieve fine-grained controllability, they
      often require a substantial amount of computational resources for training. We aim to reduce the
124
      required computational resources by efficiently reusing pretrained image plugins.
125
126   Parameter-Efficient Transfer Learning. Our task is related to parameter-efficient transfer learning
127   as well since our goal is to eliminate the domain and modality gap between image and video dif-
128   fusion models. The emergence of large-scale pre-trained models like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021),
129
      Stable Diffuions (Rombach et al., 2021) has underscored the significance of effectively transfer-
      ring these foundational models to downstream tasks. Parameter-efficient Transfer Learning (PETL)
130
      methods (Houlsby et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2023a; Zhao et al., 2023a) introduce additional pa-
131
      rameters to the original model to bridge the domain gaps between the pre-trained dataset and target
132   tasks. X-Adapter (Ran et al., 2024) propose a spatial adapter to bridge diffusion models of different
133   versions and enable plugins pretrained on old version (e.g. SD1.5 (StabilityAI)) to be directly ap-
134   plied to upgraded version(e.g. SDXL (Podell et al., 2023)). Similar to X-Adapter, our objective is
135   to effectively reuse pretrained image plugin on video diffusion model.

175   3.3     X-P LUG V ID
176
177   We introduce a novel framework termed X-PlugVid, which effectively reuses image plugins for
178   video controllable generation. The core of our method is based on two crucial insights on con-
179
      trollable generation and universal adaptation. By analyzing the mechanisms of ControlNet (Zhang
      et al., 2023c) and X-Adapter (Ran et al., 2024), we found that: 1) The key to controllable generation
180
      is the injection of high-frequency control patterns at every desnoising step. 2) Pretrained image dif-
181
      fusion models can serve as priors for spatial control since their feature maps contain the necessary
182   patterns. These patterns can be manipulated by plugins and transferred by adapters, which is the
183   reason for X-Adapter (Ran et al., 2024)’s universal adaptation. Based on our findings, we propose
184   a spatial-temporal adapter to bridge the image and video models. Additionally, a high-pass filter is
185   applied to the adapter’s input to ensure high-frequency components are adapted seamlessly. Finally,
186   we propose a novel timestep remapping strategy to provide better guidance at early timesteps.

249   3.3.2     S PATIAL -T EMPORAL A DAPTER
250
      Based on our analysis in Sec. 3.3.1, though the image diffusion model can be used as spatial prior,
251   it lacks temporal modeling, which makes it challenging to be directly applied to video diffusion
252   models. To overcome this domain gap, we add a temporal module to our adapter. In detail, X-
253   PlugVid is built upon Stable Diffusion v1.5 (StabilityAI) to ensure compatibility with the plugins’
254   connectors. Within certain decoder layers, extra mapping networks are added and trained. These
255   mapping layers are referred to as the adapter in this paper. The adapter’s function is to map features
256   from the space of the image model to the video model (e.g., SVD (Blattmann et al., 2023a)) for
257   guidance. Since the features from the image model are temporally inconsistent, directly utilizing
258   these adapted features as guidance would result in the degradation of video quality and consistency.
259
      Therefore, we introduce a temporal attention (Vaswani et al., 2017) module to ensure temporal
      coherence.
260
261   Additionally, we discovered that the feature maps of the image model contain abundant low-
262   frequency information. According to our analysis in Sec. 3.3.1, ControlNet’s outputs maintain at
263   high frequency across all timesteps, whereas diffusion spatial prior i.e. feature maps, does not meet
264   this condition. Moreover, our experiments found that these components often adversely affect the
265
      adapter’s guidance since they always contain low-quality parts as shown in Sec. 4.4. Thus, we apply
      high-pass filtering to the image features before feeding them to the adapter to filter these compo-
266
      nents. Formally, suppose we have N adapters and Mn (·) denotes the nth trained mapper, given
