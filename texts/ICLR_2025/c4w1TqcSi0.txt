011                                                                              A BSTRACT
012
013                            Large Language Model (LLM) based multi-agent systems (MAS) show remark-
014                            able potential in collaborative problem-solving, yet they still face critical chal-
015                            lenges: low communication efficiency, poor scalability, and a lack of effec-
016                            tive parameter-updating optimization methods for multi-agent collaboration. We
017                            present O PTIMA, a novel framework that addresses these issues by significantly
018
                               enhancing both communication efficiency and task effectiveness in LLM-based
                               MAS through LLM training. At its core, O PTIMA employs an iterative gener-
019
                               ate, rank, select, and train paradigm, incorporating a reward function that bal-
020
                               ances task performance, token efficiency, and communication readability. We
021                            explore various RL algorithms, including Supervised Fine-Tuning, Direct Pref-
022                            erence Optimization, and their hybrid approaches, providing insights into their
023                            effectiveness-efficiency trade-offs for iterative LLM-based MAS training. Addi-
024                            tionally, we integrate Monte Carlo Tree Search-inspired techniques for DPO data
025                            generation, conceptualizing conversation turns as tree nodes to explore diverse
026                            interaction trajectories. We evaluate O PTIMA on common multi-agent tasks, in-
027                            cluding information-asymmetric question answering and complex reasoning. Our
028                            method demonstrates consistent and substantial improvements over single-agent
029
                               baselines and vanilla MAS based on Llama 3 8B, achieving up to 2.8x perfor-
                               mance gain with less than 10% tokens on tasks requiring heavy multi-agent infor-
030
                               mation exchange. Moreover, O PTIMA’s efficiency gains open new possibilities for
031
                               leveraging inference-compute more effectively, potentially leading to improved
032                            inference-time scaling laws. By addressing fundamental challenges in multi-agent
033                            collaboration and providing a novel optimization framework, O PTIMA shows the
034                            potential towards scalable, efficient, and effective LLM-based MAS.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
055
056
057   Large Language Models (LLMs) have emerged as powerful tools for a wide range of tasks, from nat-
058
      ural language processing to complex reasoning (OpenAI, 2023; Reid et al., 2024; Anthropic, 2024).
      A promising direction in leveraging these models is the development of autonomous multi-agent
059
      systems (MAS), which aim to harness the collective intelligence of multiple LLM-based agents
060
      for collaborative problem-solving and decision-making (Liang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024b; Du
061   et al., 2024; Zhuge et al., 2024). However, for LLM-based MAS to be truly effective, they must
062   overcome two critical challenges: (a) achieving efficient inter-agent communication to minimize
063   computational costs, and (b) optimizing the collective performance of the system as a cohesive unit.
064
      Current LLM-based MAS face significant difficulties in meeting these challenges. The coordination
065
      and communication between agents often lack efficiency, resulting in verbose exchanges that lead
066
      to increased token usage, longer inference times, and higher computational costs (Li et al., 2024b).
067   This inefficiency is exacerbated by the length bias inherent in LLMs due to alignment training (Saito
068   et al., 2023; Dubois et al., 2024), which favors longer responses even when concise communication
069   would suffice (Chen et al., 2024d). Moreover, while recent work has explored training LLMs for
070   single-agent tasks (Song et al., 2024; Xiong et al., 2024) and MAS training is well-studied in rein-
071   forcement learning (Johnson et al., 2000; Lanctot et al., 2017; Baker et al., 2020), there remains a
072   lack of parameter-updating methods specifically designed to optimize LLM-based MAS as a unified
073   system. Existing approaches primarily rely on simple agent profile evolution (Chen et al., 2024b) or
074   memory evolution (Qian et al., 2024a;b; Gao et al., 2024), which fail to address the core issues of
075
      communication efficiency and collective optimization.
076   Can we develop a training framework that simultaneously enhances the communication effi-
077   ciency and task effectiveness of LLM-based MAS? To address this question, we introduce O P -
078   TIMA , an effective framework designed to optimize LLM-based MAS. At the heart of O PTIMA is
079   an iterative generate, rank, select, and train paradigm, incorporating a reward function that balances
080   task performance, token efficiency, and communication interpretability. This approach enables the
081
      development of MAS that are not only effective and efficient but also maintain interpretable commu-
      nication patterns. Based on the reward function, O PTIMA leverages a combination of techniques to
082
      induce efficient and effective communication behaviors in LLM-based agents, including Supervised
083
      Fine-Tuning (SFT) (Zelikman et al., 2022; Gülçehre et al., 2023; Aksitov et al., 2023) and Direct
084   Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023; Pang et al., 2024), along with their hybrid
085   variants. Furthermore, O PTIMA introduces an integration of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS)-
086   inspired techniques for DPO data generation, conceptualizing conversation turns as tree nodes to
087   explore diverse interaction trajectories efficiently.
088
      Importantly, by substantially reducing the number of tokens required for inference, O PTIMA not
089
      only improves computational efficiency but also opens new possibilities for leveraging inference-
090   compute more effectively. This reduction in token usage allows for more samples within the same
091   computational constraints, potentially leading to better inference-time scaling laws. As recent work
092   has shown the importance of inference-time compute in improving model performance (Wu et al.,
093   2024; Brown et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024a), O PTIMA’s efficiency gains could be combined with
094   techniques like majority voting (Wang et al., 2023), leading to more effective LLM systems.
095
      We evaluate O PTIMA on a diverse set of tasks spanning two multi-agent settings: (a) information
096   exchange, including information-asymmetric question answering (Chen et al., 2024d; Liu et al.,
097   2024), and (b) debate, encompassing mathematical and reasoning tasks (Du et al., 2024; Chen et al.,
098   2024b; Wu et al., 2023). Using Llama 3 8B (Meta, 2024) as our base model, we demonstrate that
099   O PTIMA consistently outperforms both single-agent MAS baselines, achieving up to 90% reduction
100   in token usage and 2.8x increase in task performance.
101
      To summarize, our main contribution is O PTIMA, a novel training framework that simultaneously
102   optimizes communication efficiency and task effectiveness. To enhance high-quality training data
103   generation in multi-agent settings for DPO, we introduce an integration of MCTS-like techniques.
104   Our comprehensive empirical evaluation across diverse tasks demonstrates notable advancements
105   in both token efficiency and task performance, while also providing insights into the learned com-
106   munication patterns. Additionally, we examine the implications of O PTIMA’s efficiency gains for
107   inference-time scaling laws, underscoring its potential to improve the overall capabilities of LLM
      systems by enabling more effective utilization of inference-compute. By addressing the dual chal-

      2.2   I NITIALIZATION : D IVERSIFYING AGENT C OMMUNICATION
188
      Before starting the iterative training process, we address a critical challenge in LLM-based MAS:
189   agents often produce responses in a similar style across conversation trajectories, even with high-
190   temperature sampling. This homogeneity limits the exploration of diverse communication strategies,
191   potentially hindering the optimization toward more efficient and effective interactions. Following
192   the observation from AutoForm (Chen et al., 2024d), where LLMs can be explicitly prompted to
193   leverage different more concise formats to communicate or reason without much compromise in
194   performance, we introduce an initialization step that promotes diversity in agent communication.
195   Our approach leverages a pool of format specification prompts, P = {p1 , p2 , ..., pK }, where each
196   pk is a string specifying a particular response format (e.g., JSON, list, see Appendix F for con-
197   crete examples and creation process). For each task instance di ∈ D, we generate N conversation
198   trajectories, each with a randomly selected format specification appended to the input task:
199                      τij = Mbase (di ⊕ pkj ), kj ∼ Uniform(1, K), j = 1, ..., N,                   (2)
200
      where ⊕ denotes string concatenation. This process yields a diverse set of trajectories {τij }N
                                                                                                    j=1 for

227   2.4   F RAMEWORK I NSTANTIATION 2: I TERATIVE D IRECT P REFERENCE O PTIMIZATION
228
229   While iSFT provides a straightforward approach to optimizing LLM-based MAS, it may be lim-
230   ited by its reliance on a single best trajectory for each task instance. To address this, we explore
231   iterative Direct Preference Optimization (iDPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023; Pang et al., 2024), which
232   optimizes models using comparative preferences and has demonstrated success in LLM alignment.
233   Applying DPO in multi-agent settings, however, poses distinct challenges, particularly in generating
234
      meaningful paired data that capture the complexities of agent interactions.
235   Data Generation: To overcome these challenges, we integrate MCTS with DPO data collection
236   for high-quality paired data generation in multi-agent settings. Our MCTS-based approach con-
237   ceptualizes the multi-agent conversation as a tree, where nodes represent conversational turns, and
238   edges represent continuations. This structure allows us to explore diverse interaction trajectories
239   systematically and select high-quality paired data for DPO training. The MCTS process begins at
240
      the root node (initial task prompt) and proceeds as follows: (1) Expansion: We select a node to
      expand based on the following criteria. We first exclude leaf nodes and the second-to-last level
241
      nodes to avoid wasting computation on low-variance expansions, then exclude nodes with content
242
      similar to previously expanded nodes, measured based on edit distance (see Appendix E.1). From
243   the remaining nodes, we select 10 nodes with the highest rewards and sample one using the softmax
244   distribution over their rewards. (2) Simulation: For each selected node, we expand 3 trajectories,
245   simulating the conversation to completion. (3) Backpropagation: Once a trajectory is completed
246   and rewarded with Eq. (1), we update the estimated rewards of all nodes in the trajectory with the
247   average rewards from their children. (4) Iteration: We repeat the above process 8 times, resulting
248   in 24 trajectories. More iterations could potentially lead to more diverse and better-quality data.
249   Paired Data Construction: To generate high-quality paired data for DPO training, we traverse each
250   MCTS tree and identify node pairs (ni , nj ) that satisfy three conditions: (1) shared ancestry, (2) the
251   higher estimated reward of ni and nj exceeds the threshold θdpo-filter , and (3) their reward difference
252   exceeds the threshold θdpo-diff . We sort these pairs by the higher estimated reward, and select the
253   top 50% pairs as part of the final training set. We construct DPO training instances by using the
254   common conversation history as the prompt, with ni and nj serving as the chosen and rejected
255   responses according to their estimated rewards.
256   The iDPO process then proceeds iteratively, alternating between MCTS-based data generation and
257   model updates using DPO. The pseudo-code for our iDPO process is presented in Algorithm 2.

259   2.5   F RAMEWORK I NSTANTIATION 3: H YBRID I TERATIVE T RAINING
260
261   Building upon the strengths of both iSFT and iDPO, we investigate a hybrid approach that interleaves
262   SFT and DPO in the iterative training process, termed as iSFT-DPO. This hybrid method aims
263   to leverage the simplicity and directness of SFT in capturing high-quality trajectories, while also
264   benefiting from the nuanced comparative learning facilitated by DPO. By alternating between these
265   two training paradigms, we hypothesize that the model can more effectively balance the exploration
266
      of diverse communication strategies with the exploitation of known effective patterns.
267   In practice, we implement this hybrid approach by performing one iteration of iSFT followed by
268   one iteration of iDPO, and repeating this cycle throughout the training process. This interleaving
269   allows the model to first consolidate learning from the best observed trajectories through SFT, and
      then refine its understanding through the comparative preferences provided by DPO.

285   3     E XPERIMENTS
286
287   Datasets. We evaluate O PTIMA on two multi-agent settings: information exchange (IE) and debate.
288   For IE, we use HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018), 2WikiMultiHopQA (2WMHQA) (Ho et al., 2020),
289   TriviaQA (Joshi et al., 2017), and CBT (Hill et al., 2016). For multi-hop datasets (HotpotQA,
290   2WikiMultiHopQA), we split relevant contexts between two agents, ensuring the answer can only
291   be deduced from information exchange. For TriviaQA and CBT, contexts are randomly assigned,
292
      challenging agents to identify and communicate the relevant information effectively. The debate
      setting employs GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021), MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021b), ARC’s challenge
293
      set (ARC-C) (Bhakthavatsalam et al., 2021) and MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021a), with one agent
294
      as solver and another as critic (Chen et al., 2024b). We use 0-shot for all benchmarks.
295
296   Metrics. We report F1 score between generated answers and labels for IE tasks. For debate tasks,
297   we employ exact match accuracy (GSM8k, ARC-C, MMLU) or Sympy-based (Meurer et al., 2017)
298
      equivalence checking (MATH), following Lewkowycz et al. (2022). Conversations conclude when
      agents both mark the same answer with specified special tokens or reach a turn limit.
299
300   Baselines. We compare against single-agent approaches: Chain-of-Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022)
301   and Self-Consistency (SC) with majority voting (Wang et al., 2023) on n = 8 samples. Given that
302   the generated responses for IE tasks are in free form, direct adaptation to majority voting is im-
303   practical. Therefore, we first compute the pairwise F1 score among the sampled answers, grouping
304
      those with a pairwise F1 score exceeding 0.9, and report the average F1 score against the label for
      all the answers in the largest grouping. In the multi-agent context, we compare against Multi-Agent
305
      Debate (MAD) from Du et al. (2024) and AutoForm (Chen et al., 2024d). MAD utilizes natural lan-
306
      guage for inter-agent communication, providing a baseline for common multi-agent dialogue, while
307   AutoForm encourages agents to leverage concise, non-natural-language formats to achieve a better

400   3.3               C AN O PTIMA LEAD TO B ETTER I NFERENCE S CALING L AW ?
401
402   Recent research has highlighted the importance of inference scaling laws, which describe how model
403   performance improves with increased compute during inference, typically by generating multiple
404   samples per problem (Brown et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024). While training scaling laws focus on the
405
      relationship between model size, dataset size, and performance, inference scaling laws explore the
      trade-off between inference compute budget and task accuracy. This paradigm offers a promising
406
      avenue for enhancing model capabilities without the need for further training models.
407
408   Fig. 3 illustrates O PTIMA’s impact on inference scaling laws. The left panel shows the relationship
409   between the number of SC steps and performance on multi-agent debate tasks. We observe that while
410   majority voting accuracy tends to plateau after a certain number of steps, the coverage, defined as the
411
      percentage of problems answered correctly at least once, continues to improve logarithmically with
      increased sampling. This trend aligns with findings in recent inference scaling law studies (Wu et al.,
412
      2024; Chen et al., 2024a) and suggests that more sophisticated answer selection techniques could
413
      further boost O PTIMA’s performance. We provide additional scaling law figures for all O PTIMA
414   variants and on both IE and debate tasks in Appendix A, where similar trends can be observed.
415
416   The right panel of Fig. 3 demonstrates O PTIMA’s efficiency in improving inference scaling laws on
417
      the GSM8k task. O PTIMA variants, both those trained directly on GSM8k and those transferred
      from MATH, consistently outperform the CoT SC baseline except the iSFT variant transferred from
418
      MATH. Notably, iDPO trained on GSM8k achieves the performance of CoT-SC at around 10,000
419
      tokens with 88.5% fewer tokens, effectively “shifting the curve left”. This significant reduction
420   in token usage translates to substantial computational savings without sacrificing accuracy. More-
421   over, the MATH-trained O PTIMA variants, except iSFT, also deliver better inference scaling laws
422   on GSM8k compared with CoT SC, underscoring the framework’s ability to generalize effectively
423   across related tasks.
424
      These results highlight O PTIMA’s potential to reshape inference scaling laws for LLM-based MAS
425
      and even general LLM systems. By enabling more efficient use of the inference compute budget,
426   O PTIMA allows for better performance at lower computational costs or higher performance at the
427   same cost. This efficiency gain opens new possibilities for leveraging advanced inference techniques
428   like weighted voting or best-of-N selection (Wu et al., 2024), potentially leading to even greater
429   performance improvements.

500   4   R ELATED W ORK
501
502   LLM-Based MAS. LLM-based MAS have emerged as a powerful paradigm for addressing complex
503   tasks across various domains. Seminal works by Liang et al. (2023) and Du et al. (2024) demon-
504   strated the potential of LLM-powered agents in collaborative problem-solving through multi-agent
505   debate. This foundation has sparked diverse research directions, including role-playing for com-
506   plex reasoning (Wang et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2024b), collaborative software development (Qian
507
      et al., 2024c; Hong et al., 2024; Ishibashi & Nishimura, 2024), and embodied agent interactions
      (Zhang et al., 2024; Mandi et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024). Recent studies have shown that increasing
508
      the number and diversity of agents can lead to performance gains in MAS (Wang et al., 2024a; Li
509
      et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024c). However, as LLM-based MAS grow in scale and complexity,
510   challenges related to computational costs and communication efficiency become more pronounced
511   (Chen et al., 2024d; Li et al., 2024b). Notably, there is a lack of systematic training algorithms
512   specifically designed to optimize both the effectiveness and efficiency of LLM-based multi-agent
513   systems, with most existing approaches relying on updating agent memory (Qian et al., 2024a; Gao
514   et al., 2024). Our work addresses this gap by introducing a training framework that simultaneously
515   enhances communication efficiency and task effectiveness in LLM-based MAS.
516   Iterative Refinement of LLMs. The pursuit of continual improvement in LLMs has led to the
517   development of various iterative refinement paradigms. While self-reflection mechanisms like Re-
518   flexion (Shinn et al., 2023) and self-refine (Madaan et al., 2023) show promise, they heavily rely
519   on LLMs’ limited self-correction abilities, which is relatively weak for most of the current LLMs
520   (Huang et al., 2024; Olausson et al., 2024; Kamoi et al., 2024). More robust approaches focus on
521   iterative parameter updates, for example, ReST (Gülçehre et al., 2023), ReSTEM (Singh et al., 2024)
522   and STaR (Zelikman et al., 2022) train models on self-generated high-quality reasoning paths, Pang
523   et al. (2024) further integrate the incorrect self-generated paths and train models with DPO. The
524
      extension to complex, multi-step tasks (Aksitov et al., 2023) further demonstrates the versatility of
      these methods. However, iterative refinement remains largely unexplored in the context of LLM-
525
      based MAS. Our work addresses this gap by presenting the first effective training framework for
526
      iteratively optimizing LLMs in MAS contexts. By simultaneously enhancing communication effi-
527   ciency and task effectiveness, our approach shows the potential of iterative training in MAS.
528

530   5   C ONCLUSION
531
532
      We present O PTIMA, a novel framework for training LLM-based MAS that significantly improves
533
      communication efficiency and task performance. Extensive experiments across a range of tasks
534   demonstrate O PTIMA’s consistent superiority over both single-agent and multi-agent baselines. The
535   framework introduces key innovations such as iterative training techniques, a balanced reward func-
536   tion, and an MCTS-inspired approach for data generation. O PTIMA also shows promise in enhanc-
537   ing inference scaling laws and transferring knowledge to OOD tasks. These findings highlight the
538   critical role of efficient communication in MAS and LLM systems. While O PTIMA marks a major
539   step forward in multi-agent LLM training, further exploration into its scalability to larger models
      and more complex scenarios is a promising direction for future research.

901
      pronounced for the MATH task, where the potential for improvement through increased sampling
      is most evident. Majority voting accuracy tends to plateau earlier, suggesting that more sophisti-
902

910   These results, while highlighting some task-specific differences, collectively reinforce the potential
