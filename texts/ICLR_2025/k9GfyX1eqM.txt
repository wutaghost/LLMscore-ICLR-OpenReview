009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper studies the vulnerabilities of transformer-based Large Language Models
012            (LLMs) to jailbreaking attacks, focusing specifically on the optimization-based
013            Greedy Coordinate Gradient (GCG) strategy. We first observe a positive cor-
014            relation between the effectiveness of attacks and the internal behaviors of the
015            models. For instance, attacks tend to be less effective when models pay more
016            attention to system prompts designed to ensure LLM safety alignment. Build-
017            ing on this discovery, we introduce an enhanced method that manipulates mod-
018
               els’ attention scores to facilitate LLM jailbreaking, which we term AttnGCG.
               Empirically, AttnGCG shows consistent improvements in attack efficacy across
019
               diverse LLMs, achieving an average increase of ∼7% in the Llama-2 series
020
               and ∼10% in the Gemma series. Our strategy also demonstrates robust attack
021            transferability against both unseen harmful goals and black-box LLMs like GPT-
022            3.5 and GPT-4. Moreover, we note our attention-score visualization is more
023            interpretable, allowing us to gain better insights into how our targeted atten-
024            tion manipulation facilitates more effective jailbreaking. We release the code
025            at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/AttnGCG-5CD2/.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Transformer-based large language models (LLMs) (Vaswani et al., 2017) have enabled a tremendous
031   success in the field of natural language processing (NLP), propelling these systems toward near
032   human-level intelligence (Achiam et al., 2024; Anthropic, 2024; Georgiev et al., 2024; Touvron
033
      et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2024; Dubey et al., 2024). Nevertheless, to ensure these powerful systems
      remain safe and ethical, LLMs that are deployed for users typically undergo comprehensive safety
034
      training (Ouyang et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2022; Tu et al., 2023b). This critical
035
      training process enables models to reject inappropriate requests and generate responses that are
036   socially acceptable and contextually suitable, which aims at significantly enhancing their functional
037   utility in real-world NLP applications (Ouyang et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2022).
038
039
      However, despite these established safety protocols, aligned LLMs remain vulnerable to adversar-
      ial attacks that can provoke toxic responses (Carlini et al., 2023), particularly those that employ
040
      optimization-based approaches. These attacks typically exploit the model’s inherent security flaw by
041
      optimizing for malicious adversarial input, including optimization-based gradient-searching meth-
042   ods (Zou et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Geisler et al., 2024; Tu et al., 2023a), approaches that adapt
043   genetic algorithms (Lapid et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024) and LLM-aided jailbreakings (Chao et al.,
044   2023; Mehrotra et al., 2024), collectively highlighting the ongoing security ‘bugs’ of advanced LLMs.
045
      In this paper, we focus on the optimization-based attack, whose target is to maximize the probability
046
      of generating malicious content. We notice two limitations with existing optimization-based attacks.
047
      First, although these methods heavily rely on the premise that the likelihood of generating target
048   tokens can serve as an indicator of a successful jailbreak and therefore have developed techniques
049   to maximize this targeted probability (Zou et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Geisler et al., 2024;
050   Andriushchenko et al., 2024; Lapid et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024), the recent study points out that
051   a high probability of harmful tokens does not necessarily equate to a successful jailbreak (Liao &
052   Sun, 2024). For example, as shown in Figure 1, the adversarial prompt crafted by the popular Greedy
053   Coordinate Gradient (GCG) (Zou et al., 2023) may initially cause the LLM to generate the target
      tokens, but subsequently, the model could reject the request, rendering the jailbreak unsuccessful.

074   Second, existing attack frameworks predominantly operate at the output layer of LLMs, evaluating
075   performance based on metrics like the probability of target tokens (Zou et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023;
076
      Geisler et al., 2024; Andriushchenko et al., 2024; Lapid et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024) or using scores
      from auxiliary evaluation models (Chao et al., 2023). These approaches neglect the internal workings
077
      of LLMs, resulting in a lack of interpretability. Without this understanding, it would be challenging to
078
      grasp why some attacks succeed while others fail, limiting the development of more effective attack
079   strategies.
080
081   This paper aims to mitigate these issues by designing more guided input manipulation. As shown in
082
      Figure 3, in aligned LLMs, the input typically consists of two parts: a system prompt (which, for
      some LLMs, defaults to none) and user input. In the context of LLM jailbreaking, the user input
083
      can be further divided into two components: the goal prompt, which represents the user’s intent,
084
      and the adversarial suffix, which our method optimizes to facilitate the jailbreak. In Figure 2, we
085   illustrate the distribution of the normalized model’s attention score (introduced in Section 2.2) across
086   these three input components during the jailbreak process. Interestingly, our findings suggest that a
087   high attention score on the adversarial suffix has a strong correlation with successful jailbreaking.
088   Specifically, when the attention score on the adversarial suffix increases, the model’s focus on the
089   system prompt and the goal input tends to decrease. This diminishes the effectiveness of the system
090   prompt in safeguarding the LLM, giving the adversarial suffix a greater chance to bypass the model’s
091   safety protocols.
092   Building upon this key insight, we introduce AttnGCG, which is illustrated in the bottom panel
093   of Figure 1. Specifically, AttnGCG leverages the attention score as an additional optimization
094   objective, which produces adversarial suffixes that are more challenging for LLMs to defend against.
095   Specifically, we augment the traditional GCG objective with an auxiliary attention loss, which
096   gradually increases the importance of the adversarial suffix during the attack training. By pivoting the
097   optimization focus from solely targeting token probabilities to also manipulating models’ attention
098   scores, we concentrate the model’s attention more effectively on the adversarial suffix, thereby
099
      enhancing jailbreak success, resulting in an increase in attack success rate (ASR) from an average
      of 64.3% to 70.6% across 7 aligned LLMs. Furthermore, we demonstrate the generalization of our
100
      method by adding it to other existing jailbreaking techniques, resulting in an average ASR increase of
101
      5% over the vanilla GCG. Under the transfer attack settings, AttnGCG exhibits superior transferability
102   to previously unseen harmful attack goals, with an average improvement of 11.4% compared to
103   GCG (e.g., Average 89% ASR measured by GPT-4 on Llama and Gemma series). On the attacking
104   model side, AttnGCG also shows enhanced transferability to closed-source LLMs such as GPT-4,
105   achieving an average improvement of 2.8% over GCG. Finally, we present qualitative visualizations
106   showcasing that the attention scores of adversarial suffixes can serve as an interpretable factor of
107   LLM jailbreaking performance. This offers valuable insights for evaluating and improving the quality
      of adversarial prompts.

      2.1            G REEDY C OORDINATE G RADIENT
134
135   The Greedy Coordinate Gradient (GCG) (Zou et al., 2023) is a pioneering method for eliciting
136   malicious text outputs from aligned LLMs by employing a discrete token-level optimization. In this
137   approach, an LLM is considered as a mapping from a sequence of n tokens x1:n to a distribution
138   over the next token xn+1 . In the jailbreaking scenario, the first n tokens x1:n fed to the language
139   model contains both the goal xG = x1:j which the user aims to achieve as well as an adversarial
140
      suffix xI = xj+1:n that we aim to optimize. The objective of GCG is to find an adversarial suffix xI
      that minimizes the negative log probability of a target sequence of tokens x∗n+1:n+L , representing
141
      affirmative responses (i.e., ‘Sure, here is ...’). Under this context, GCG uses a target loss function Lt :
142
143                                                              Lt (x1:n ) = − log p(x∗n+1:n+L |x1:n ).                                                                    (1)
144
      Formally, the optimization problem of GCG can be expressed as:

149   vocabulary size. This objective is optimized by the Greedy Coordinate Gradient (GCG) (Zou et al.,

232   3.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUPS
233
234   Dataset. We employ the AdvBench Harmful Behaviors benchmark (Zou et al., 2023) to assess the
235   performance of jailbreak attacks. This dataset comprises 520 requests spanning profanity, graphic
236   depictions, threatening behavior, misinformation, discrimination, cybercrime, and dangerous or illegal
237
      suggestions. We randomly sample 100 behaviors from this dataset for our evaluation.
238
239   Language Models. In this paper, we attempt to jailbreak both open-source and closed-source
240   LLMs. For open-source LLMs, we test the LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023), Gemma (Mesnard
241   et al., 2024), and Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023) series of seven aligned models, particularly including
242
      Mixtral-8x7B-Instruct (Jiang et al., 2024), the open-source MoE model that outperforms GPT-3.5, and
      LLaMA-3 (Dubey et al., 2024), the most capable openly available LLM to date. For closed-source
243
      LLMs, we mainly focus on GPT-3.5 (Brown et al., 2020), GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2024), and the
244
      Gemini (Georgiev et al., 2024) series, due to their widespread use. For each of these target models,
245   we use a temperature of zero for deterministic generation. For a list of all system prompts used in this
246   paper, see Appendix B.3.
247
248
      Baselines and Hyperparameters. We mainly adopt the effective and widely-used GCG (Zou et al.,
249
      2023) as our baseline for both attacks on white-box (direct attack) and black-box LLMs (transfer
250   attack). We train attacks using GCG and AttnGCG for 500 steps with consistent parameter settings
251   for a fair comparison. More details can be found in the Appendix B.
252
253
      Evaluation. To comprehensively assess our proposed attack, we use two types of evaluation protocols
254
      to evaluate the attack success rate (ASR):
255
256          • ASRKW : ASRKW is measured with keyword-detection method introduced by Zou et al.
257            (2023), which assesses harmfulness by checking for predefined refusal keywords in the first
258            few tokens of the response. The predefined refusal keywords are listed in Appendix B.2.
259
260
             • ASRGPT : We leverage LLMs as the judge (Chao et al., 2023), utilizing GPT-4 (Achiam et al.,
               2024) as our judge to determine whether or not the attack is successful, which is proven
261
               to better align with the attacker’s interests (Chao et al., 2023). Unlike keyword-detection
262
               method, which would classify affirmative but irrelevant responses as successful, GPT-4 can
263            semantically assess whether the responses align with the user’s intent (goal prompt). Only
264            outputs that address the user’s request accurately will be considered as a successful attack.
265            For detailed judgment criteria, refer to the Appendix B.1.
266
267   Serving. GCG is known for its effectiveness, though it comes with a significant cost — typically,
268   it runs for about 75 minutes to complete 500 steps on average. To expedite the attack process, we
269   employ the “common prefix” technique. This approach involves saving the KV cache of the common
      input prefix, which reduces the time required by 5×. More details are released in Appendix B.5.

      3.2     D IRECT ATTACK
293
294   Main Results and Analysis. We first conduct white-box attack using GCG and our AttnGCG.
295   Results from Table 1 suggest that, incorporating attention loss (Eq. 5) into the optimization objective
296   leads to a general improvement in the attack performance. This observation is well supported as
297   the proposed AttnGCG consistently outperforms GCG across various models, e.g. showcasing an
298   average improvement of 6.3% in ASRGPT and 3.9% in ASRKW .
299
      Our statistics also highlight the issue of ‘false jailbreak’ among current LLMs (Chao et al., 2023)
300   — the ASR measured by detecting refusal keywords is 9.8% higher than GPT-aided evaluation (i.e.
301   64.3% vs. 74.1%), which arises from delayed rejection or incomplete responses when LLMs encounter
302   malicious queries. In contrast, our AttnGCG can reduce ‘false jailbreak’. For instance, when tested
303   against prominent Gemma models, AttnGCG reduces this evaluation gap by an average of 8%, from
304   23.5% to 15.5%, showing AttnGCG can better generate successful attacks at the semantic level.
305
      These findings confirm that AttnGCG is a more robust strategy for searching the safety bugs of
306   aligned LLMs, thus demonstrating its efficacy as a competent jailbreaking approach.
307
308   Attention Score Visualization. In Figure 4, we provide attention heatmaps of the LLM input (the
309   goal and the adversarial suffix) of failed and successful attacks as the training proceeds. According to
310   Figure 2 the attention scores on the goal and system prompt have a positive correlation, so without
311   losing generality, we only visualize the attention on the goal and suffix. We present the attention
312
      heatmaps of the initialization prompt (i.e., the goal + "! ! ! ! ! ! ! ! ! ! ! ! ! ! ! ! ! ! ! !")
      with prompts generated by GCG and AttnGCG after running for the same 60 steps. In a successful
313
      jailbreak, attention is notably shifted to the adversarial suffix part, resulting in a decrease in attention
314
      to the goal. Meanwhile, for the failed attack situation, the model robustly pays excessive attention
315   to the goal input (highlighted in the green box). Combined with the positive correlation between
316   attention scores on the goal and safety-aligned system prompt (Figure 2), we believe that excessive
317   focus on harmful goals will lead to excessive attention to the model’s safety protocol, causing the
318   model to refuse to respond.
319
      We also present the average attention scores of the goal for different strategies in Table 2. As AttnGCG
320
      enables more attention on the adversarial suffix, the attention score to goals decreases by 8.6% on
321   average, thus enhancing the jailbreak. We argue that this shift of attention is the key reason for a
322   successful jailbreak, as the model focuses more on the adversarial suffix, reducing excessive attention
323   to the goal, which causes the internal safety protocol of the model more likely to be bypassed.

      3.3   G ENERALIZE ATTN GCG TO OTHER ATTACKS
360
361   In Section 3.2, we discovered that the internal perspective of the model attributes the success of the
362   attack to a reduced focus on the goal and system prompts. We intend to verify whether this point
363   holds true on other attack methods and employ AttnGCG to optimize the prompts generated by these
364   methods, assessing whether it yields additional enhancements.
365
      Method Selection and Setups. In selecting other methods, we refer to HarmBench (Mazeika et al.,
366
      2024), where AutoDAN (Liu et al., 2024) is identified as the second most effective approach to
367
      jailbreaking after GCG. Therefore, we present results for AutoDAN-HGA. Additionally, we include
368   the ICA (Wei et al., 2024) method as a baseline to represent optimization-free jailbreak attacks, which
369   provides a more comprehensive comparison of results. For AutoDAN-HGA, we utilize its default
370   implementation and parameters, which involve a total of 100 iterations for each behavior. For ICA,
371   we use the 1-shot setting.
372
373   Main Results and Analysis. The augmented attention score in AttnGCG can be seamlessly incorpo-
374   rated into other jailbreakings through prompt initialization to enhance the attack. When we examine

495   4    R ELATED W ORK
496
497
      Optimization-based Jailbreaking Optimization-based method design criteria to find the most
      effective adversarial prompts for jailbreaking LLMs. This paradigm is initially explored with
498
      gradient-based optimization and introduced by GCG (Zou et al., 2023), which employs a combination
499
      of greedy and gradient-based search techniques for both white-box and black-box LLM jailbreaking.
500   PGD (Geisler et al., 2024) revisits Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) on the continuously relaxed
501   input prompt for creating adversarial prompts. Genetic-based methods (Lapid et al., 2023; Liu
502   et al., 2024) leverage the genetic algorithm to produce universal and transferrable prompts to attack
503   black-box LLMs. Andriushchenko et al. (2024) propose to first manually design an adversarial
504   template, then use random search to maximize the target probability for jailbreaking black box
505   LLMs. Prompt Automatic Iterative Refinement (PAIR) (Chao et al., 2023) uses an attacker LLM
506   to generate jailbreaks for the targeted LLM with iterative queries for the target LLM to update and
507   refine a candidate jailbreak. Building upon PAIR, Mehrotra et al. (2024) propose a refined version
508
      for adversarial prompt searching, which employ a tree-based search method. Our AttnGCG belongs
      to the optimization-based category and employs the attention score as an additional objective for
509
      enhanced jailbreaking.
510
511   Optimization-free Jailbreaking Optimization-free jailbreakings generally attack models by twitch-
512   ing the input prompts. Early attack strategies are tested on ChatGPT since its initial release, users
513   realized that by “delicately” design the input prompts, the aligned ChatGPT always chooses to answer
514   malicious questions without refusal (Christian, 2023; Albert, 2023; Burgess, 2023). Since this kind of
515   attack method only requires adjust the model input, it has drawn huge attention from role play (walk-
516
      erspider, 2022) to semi-subversion of the safety training objective (Wei et al., 2023). A main trend for
      producing the malicious textual prompt is by leveraging another LLM. Shah et al. (2023) employ an
517
      LLM that is guided by persona modulation to generate jailbreaking prompts. GPTFuzzer (Yu et al.,
518
      2023) demonstrate an iterative jailbreaking enhancement over human-written templates with LLMs.
519   Zeng et al. (2024) and Takemoto (2024) chose to refine the input adversarial examples using stronger
520   LLMs (e.g., fintuned GPT-3.5) and high-quality prompts. Deng et al. (2024) propose a novel attack
521   using reverse engineering and an LLM as the automatic prompt generator. Besides, by interpolating
522   rare linguistic knowledge, Yuan et al. (2024) discover the intriguing fact that conducting chats in
523   cipher can bypass the LLM safety alignment. ICA (Wei et al., 2024) successfully attacks LLMs by
524   adapting the in-context technique that contains a few examples of harmful question-answer pairs.
525
526   5    C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this paper, we research on jailbreaking attacks on transformer-based LLMs. Our investigation yields
529   insights into the effectiveness of these attacks and the internal behaviors of the models specifically
530   — the attention they allocate to adversarial suffixes crucial for successful jailbreaking. Building on
531   this understanding, we introduce AttnGCG, a novel strategy designed to manipulate the model’s
532   attention scores, thereby redirecting focus away from the attack objective and enhancing the efficacy
533   of jailbreaking suffixes. Our experiments demonstrate significant improvements in both direct and
534
      transfer attacks on LLMs. Moreover, through visualizing the model’s attention scores, we provide
      clear insights into how jailbreaking exploits attention distributions to achieve its goals. We believe
535
      our findings will stimulate future research in both attacking and defending against LLMs.
536
537   Limitation The transfer attack performance of AttnGCG is unsatisfactory on the latest models,
538   including Gemini-1.5-Pro-latest, Gemini-1.5-Flash, and GPT-4o, necessitating further research to
539   address this issue. The results are presented in the Appendix C.4. Nonetheless, our method still
      consistently perform well on models released before January 25, 2024.

541
542   Operating within a white-box setting, our proposed jailbreak targets open-sourced LLMs derived
543   from unaligned models like Llama2-7B for Llama2-7B-Chat. Adversaries can manipulate these base
544   models directly, rather than use our specific prompt.
545
      Looking ahead, while we acknowledge that our method, like previous jailbreak studies, has limited
546   immediate harm, it prompts further investigation into stronger defense measures. We argue that
547   openly discussing attack methods at this stage of LLM advancement is beneficial, as it allows for the
548   enhancement of future LLM iterations with improved security measures if necessary.

858   As we have introduced in the main paper, we utilized the “common prefix” technique for program

      B.6       D ETAILS FOR T RANSFER ATTACK ACROSS G OALS
888
      We conduct the transfer jailbreaking across different attack goals, referencing the “25 behaviors,
889   1 model” experimental settings in Zou et al. (2023). According to the findings from Zou et al.
890   (2023), which incorporates new prompts incrementally in the “25 behaviors, 1 model” experiment,
891   better results are achieved only after identifying a suffix that successfully attacks earlier prompts, as
892   measured by keyword detection, rather than attempting to optimize all prompts at once from the start.
893   This indicates that if the first harmful behavior is not successfully attacked, the method’s effectiveness
894   will be poor, because this suffix is equivalent to being optimized only on the first harmful behavior,
895   and the optimization fails. In such a case, a transfer attack is meaningless. However, due to the
896   randomness of the GCG algorithm, the first harmful behavior may not always be successfully attacked
897
      (ASR is not 100%). Therefore, we run both attack methods randomly 10 times, limit the steps to 500,
      and select the suffix with the highest Train ASRKW (which experimental results show are all close to
898
      100%) for evaluating the Test ASR. This ensures a relatively fair comparison despite the randomness.
