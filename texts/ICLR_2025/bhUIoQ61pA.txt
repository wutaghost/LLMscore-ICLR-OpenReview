033                                              A BSTRACT
034
035            Reinforcement learning has proven effective for humanoid robot locomotion, yet
036            achieving stable movement in complex environments remains challenging. Hu-
037            manoid robots must maintain balance while navigating and continuously adapt
038
               to interactions with the environment. A deep understanding of these robot-
               environment dynamics is essential for achieving stable locomotion. Since there
039
               is privileged information that the robot cannot directly access, to expand the ob-
040
               servable space, previous reinforcement learning-based methods either reconstruct
041            environmental information from partial observations or reconstruct robotic dy-
042            namics information from partial observations, but they fall short of fully captur-
043            ing the dynamics of robot-environment interactions. In this work, we propose an
044            end-to-end reinforcement learning control framework based on physical interac-
045            tion World Model for Humanoid Robots (HuWo). Our primary innovation is the
046            introduction of a physical interaction world model to understand the dynamic in-
047            teractions between the robot and the environment. Additionally, to address the
048            temporal and dynamic nature of these interactions, we employ the hidden layers
049
               of Transformer-XL for implicit modeling. The proposed framework can showcase
               robust and flexible locomotion ability in complex environments such as slopes,
050
               stairs, and discontinuous surfaces. We validated the robustness of this method
051
               using the Zerith1 robot, both in simulations and real-world deployments, and
052            quantitatively compared our HuWo against the baselines with better traversability
053            and command-tracking.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
055
056
      Humanoid robots are expected to perform tasks related to human activities and work alongside hu-
057   mans, which includes possessing motion capabilities comparable to humans and adapting their gaits
058   to various terrain conditions. Although they exhibit superior mobility compared to wheeled robots
059   in complex terrains, controlling them in scenarios with discontinuous contact and diverse motion
060   skills remains challenging. Transitioning natural movements to humanoid robots still faces long-
061   term technical challenges, including but not limited to the high degrees of freedom, underactuation,
062   and complex non-linear dynamics of humanoid robots.
063   Traditional model-based control methods have significantly enhanced the locomotion capabilities
064   of humanoid robots by using physical models to predict robot behavior. Wensing & Orin (2014);
065   Chignoli et al. (2021); Ahn (2023) However, these methods rely on accurate environmental dy-
066   namics modeling, which limits their application in complex terrains. Simplified dynamic models
067   often lead to conservative movements, restricting the robot’s potential. In contrast, reinforcement
068   learning-based methods do not rely on detailed physical modeling and have shown greater flexibility
069   and adaptability on legged robots. However, for humanoid robots, these methods can only handle
070   relatively simple environments and have not yet fully addressed dynamic control issues in complex
071
      terrains.
072   Environmental information and robot motion information are essentially information from different
073   domains, and result in understanding their interactions is challenging. Since actor networks can only
074   obtain partial observations of the environment, they generally reconstruct partial observations into
075   more complete environmental information by incorporating historical information or additional ob-
076   servational data. While these methods can reconstruct environment or robot dynamics information
077
      from partial observations, they fail to fully characterize the physical interaction processes between
      the robot and the environment. To address this issue, we introduce Building dynamical Interaction
078
      World Models, which employ self-attention mechanisms to learn compact representations of histor-
079
      ical observation inputs and implicitly infer latent interaction states by predicting future observation
080   states. This approach better captures the complex interaction processes between the robot and the
081   environment, enhancing the environment understanding and enabling stable locomotion on complex
082   terrains.
083
      Our input consists of temporally related historical sequence information, and we use the
084
      Transformer-XL Dai (2019), which allows the world model to directly access observations from pre-
085
      vious time steps and learn long-term dependencies. The Transformer structure comprises multiple
086   residual connections and self-attention mechanisms. The self-attention mechanism has unique ad-
087   vantages in modeling sequential information because it captures global information in the sequence
088   without relying on fixed time windows.
089
      We demonstrate the entire framework on the affordance-based bipedal platform Zerith1 and vali-
090
      date our method. With our approach, the robot can traverse complex terrain in both simulation and
091
      the real world. Overall, our main contributions are:
092
093          • We propose a physical interaction world model, representing the first application of
094            Transformer-XL based world model framework to humanoid robot tasks. By integrating
095            it with the actor-critic method, we achieve enhanced reinforcement learning exploration
096            capabilities.
097
             • Our approach incorporates time series information into the critic and leverages the world
098            model for future predictions, significantly improving the critic network’s ability to evaluate
099            the robot’s state and facilitating more globally informed decision-making.
100
             • Our method bridges the gap between simulation and the real world. Both simulation and
101
               real-world experiments demonstrate its superior traversability and command-tracking per-
102            formance, fully showcasing the robustness of the approach.

      World model for humanoid The initial world model Ha & Schmidhuber (2018) is inspired by how
      humans process complex information to form an abstract representation of the world, understanding
124
      key entities and their interactions, and creating an internal representation of the world that allows
125
      predicting future events and making quick responses. For various problems that can be addressed
126   using reinforcement learning, the Dreamer series algorithms Wu et al. (2023); Hafner et al. (2020;
127   2023) have systematically explored the construction and learning of world models as well as the
128   optimization of value and policy functions based on the actor-critic paradigm. Daydreamer Wu
129   et al. (2023) employs online learning, focusing on predicting future outcomes through experience
130   with the world model and using these predictions to reduce the trial-and-error process in the actual
131   environment, thereby improving training efficiency. The world denoising model Gu et al. (2024)
132   addresses the issue of discrepancies between simulation and real-world environments by utilizing
133   the predictive capability of the world model for denoising. However, unlike the aforementioned
134
      methods, we innovatively apply the denoising model to abstract implicit features of the dynamical
      interaction between the robot and the environment, leveraging these features for decision-making
135
      and enabling robust locomotion in humanoid robots.

137   Transformers for humanoid The Transformer Vaswani et al. (2017) excels in handling long se-
138   quences and is compatible with various modalities and their combinations. It has achieved remark-
139   able results in fields such as vision Dosovitskiy et al. (2020); Arnab et al. (2021); Touvron et al.
140
      (2021) and natural language processing Devlin et al. (2018); Radford et al. (2018; 2019); Brown
      (2020). In reinforcement learning, decision-making methods such as Trajectory Transformer Giu-
141
      liari et al. (2021) and Decision Transformer Chen et al. (2021) have been developed. For legged
142
      robot motion control tasks, Lai et al. (2023) successfully deployed a control strategy to a quadrupedal
143   robot by leveraging Decision Transformer and a two-stage knowledge distillation approach. Yang
144   et al. (2021) trained RL algorithms with a high-level vision controller to process visual and pro-
145   prioceptive information and output target linear and angular velocities for driving lower-level con-
146   trollers. Fu et al. (2024); Radosavovic et al. (2024a) used Transformers as feature extractors to
147   achieve simple walking for humanoid robots. However, a common challenge in control tasks is
148   that Transformers cannot capture the relationships between different segments, whereas our method
149   using Transformer-XL establishes connections between different segments, avoiding information
150   fragmentation.
151   Methods such as Chen et al. (2022); Micheli et al. (2022); Robine et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2024);
152   Deng et al. (2024) combine Transformers with world models. Through this integration, We introduce
153   a novel Humanoid Locomotion Framework HuWo, in our method, transformers enable the world
154   model to access past state information directly, rather than relying on compressed information, thus
155   reducing the data compression process.

199   3.1.1     R EINFORCE LEARNING TASK
200
      In this paper, we formulate the problem of humanoid locomotion in complex terrain as a par-
201   tially observable Markov decision process (POMDP) with discrete time steps t ∈ N , M =
202   [S, O, A, T, Z, r, γ], where S, O and A are the state, observations and action spaces. The state
203   transition probability T (o, a, s′ ) represents the probability of receiving observation o after execut-
204   ing action a and transitioning to a new state s′ , the observation probability Z(s′ , a, o) represents the
205   probability after executing action a and transitioning to a new state s′ , the reward function R(s, a, s′ )
206   represents the reward obtained after executing action a in state s and transitioning to state s′ , and
207   the discount factor γ is a value between 0 and 1, which is used to weigh the relative importance of
208   immediate rewards and future rewards.
                                         P∞       The ultimate goal is to find a strategy that maximizes the

      discounted reward J(π) = Eπ [ t=0 γ t rt ] .

      3.1.2     TASK D ESCRIPTION
212   In our Physical Interaction World Models, we decompose the locomotion task in complex environ-
213   ment into the following processes:
214
215            • Dynamical Environment Understanding In complex environmental locomotion tasks,
                 robot’s understanding of its physical interactions with the environment determines its sub-


                                                                                   4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
                sequent decisions. This process is highly dynamic and strongly temporally correlated, en-
217             compassing the relationship between environmental information and the robot’s dynamic
218             data, as well as the memory of these two types of information over historical time series and
219             the robot’s perception of environmental changes. Developing the robot’s cognitive ability
220             to interact physically with a dynamic environment is crucial.
221
              • Dynamic Prediction Dynamic prediction plays a pivotal role in enabling the robot to inter-
222             act effectively with its environment. By leveraging interaction information, the robot can
223             ”imagine” the complete states of the physical world and itself that would result from each
224             possible action. This capability allows the robot to anticipate future dynamics and evaluate
225             the potential value of its actions in a proactive manner. Such dynamic estimation not only
226             enhances the robot’s adaptability to diverse scenarios but also strengthens the generaliza-
227             tion of its walking capabilities, particularly in complex and unpredictable environments.
228           • Observation Space Expansion The robot can only access partially observable states of
229             the environment. However, partial observations can only capture local information and
230             fail to comprehensively represent the full complexity of the environment, making them
231             insufficient to support the robot’s decision-making requirements in complex environments.
232             To learn comprehensive information, the policy network needs to expand the observation
233             space based on historical observation sequences and dynamic predictions, ensuring that
234             each extended state provides sufficient information to compensate for partial observability.

      The actor network describes a Gaussian distribution based on the output mean and variance of the

      The Critic network estimates the expected cumulative return Rt under the current policy at state st :
306   vψ (Rt | st ). The key to policy optimization lies in minimizing the error between the predicted value
307   and the actual return Rt . By continuously optimizing this loss, the critic network is trained to more
308   accurately evaluate state values. The key distinction from previous work lies in the introduction of
309   time sequences and a world model for future prediction in our critic network not just actor network.
310   This approach significantly enhances the critic’s ability to evaluate the robot’s state, thereby guiding
311   decision-making with a more global perspective.

      5   C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this work, we propose Interactive World Models for Humanoid Robots (HuWo), a novel frame-
      work designed to tackle the challenges of humanoid robot locomotion in complex environments. Our
532
      framework leverages the world model concept within an asymmetric actor-critic structure, where
533
      the hidden layers of Transformer-XL enable end-to-end implicit modeling of dynamic interactions
534   between the robot and its environment. This approach facilitates robust decision-making by expand-
535   ing the observation space through historical sequences and dynamic predictions. The effectiveness
536   of our method is demonstrated through experiments showing robust walking performance in diverse
537   real-world environments, including zero-shot sim-to-real transfer. These results highlight our frame-
538   work’s ability to model environmental dynamics and adapt to changing conditions. Future work will
539   focus on extending this framework to achieve full-body coordination, including free arm movement,
      for more versatile and natural locomotion.
