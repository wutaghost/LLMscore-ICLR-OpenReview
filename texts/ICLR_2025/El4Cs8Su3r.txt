013                                                                A BSTRACT
014
015                             Vision Transformers (ViTs) have become a standard architecture in computer vi-
016                             sion. However, because of their modeling of long-range dependencies through
017
                                self-attention mechanisms, the explainability of these models remains a challenge.
                                To address this, we propose LeGrad, an explainability method specifically de-
018
                                signed for ViTs. LeGrad computes the gradient with respect to the attention maps
019
                                of single ViT layers, considering the gradient itself as the explainability signal. We
020                             aggregate the signal over all layers, combining the activations of the last as well
021                             as intermediate tokens to produce the merged explainability map. This makes
022                             LeGrad a conceptually simple and an easy-to-implement method to enhance the
023                             transparency of ViTs. We evaluate LeGrad in various setups, including segmenta-
024                             tion, perturbation, and open-vocabulary settings, showcasing its improved spatial
025                             fidelity as well as its versatility compared to other SotA explainability methods.

      Vision Transformers (ViTs)(Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) have significantly influcened the field of com-
048   puter vision with their ability to model long-range dependencies through self-attention mechanisms.
049   But explanability methods designed for convolutional or feed-forward neural networks are not di-
050   rectly applicable to ViTs due to their architectural requirements, like GradCAM’s (Selvaraju et al.,
051   2017) reliance on convolutional layers and Layer-wise Relevance Propagation’s (LRP) (Bach et al.,
052   2015) specific layer-wise propagation rules. While ViT-specific explainability techniques exist, in-
053   cluding adaptations of traditional methods (Chefer et al., 2020; Selvaraju et al., 2017; Chefer et al.,
      2021), attention-based techniques (Abnar & Zuidema, 2020; Voita et al., 2019; Chefer et al., 2020;


                                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2021), and text-based explanations (Hernandez et al., 2021; Goh et al., 2021; Abnar & Zuidema,
055   2020), the explainability those architectures remains a challenge.
056
057   To address this problem, we propose LeGrad, a Layerwise Explainability method that considers the
058
      Gradient with respect to the attention maps. LeGrad is specifically designed for ViTs as it leverages
      the self-attention mechanism to generate relevancy maps highlighting the most influential parts of
059
      an image for the model’s prediction. Compared to other methods, LeGrad uses the gradient with
060
      respect to the attention maps as the explanatory signal, as e.g. opposed to CheferCam (Chefer et al.,
061   2020; 2021), which uses the gradient to weight the attention maps. This is done independently for
062   each layer. The final explainability signal is then pooled over all layers of the ViT. Note that using a
063   layerwise gradient, compared to other signals, allows to sum up over different layers without further
064   need for normalization. To further improve the signal, the gradient is clipped by a ReLU function
065   preventing negative gradients to impact positive activations (see Figure 2 for details). The approach
066   is conceptually simple and versatile, as it only requires the gradient w.r.t. to the ViT’s attention maps.
067   This facilitates its adoption across various applications and architecture, including larger ViTs such
068   as ViT-BigG as well as attention pooling architectures (Lee et al., 2019; Zhai et al., 2023).
069   We evaluate the proposed method for various ViT backbones on four challenging tasks, seg-
070   mentation, open-vocabulary detection, perturbation, and audio localization, spanning over various
071   datasets, incl. ImageNet (Russakovsky et al., 2015; Gao et al., 2022), OpenImagesV7 (Benenson &
072   Ferrari, 2022), and ADE20KSound/SpeechPrompted (Hamilton et al., 2024) . It shows that while
073   current methods struggle especially with the diverse object categories in OpenImagesV7, LeGrad
074   reaches a score of 48.4 p-mIoU on OpenImagesV7 using OpenCLIP-ViT-B/16. Furthermore, we
075
      demonstrate the applicability of LeGrad to very large models, such as the ViT-BigG/14 (Cherti et al.,
      2023) with 2.5 billion parameters while also adapting well to different feature aggregation strate-
076
      gies employed e.g. by SigLIP (Zhai et al., 2023). Finally, LeGrad also establishes a new SoTA on
077
      zero-shot sound localization on ADE20KSoundPrompted scoring +14mIoU over previous SoTA.
078
079   We summarize the contributions as follows: (1) We propose LeGrad as a layerwise explainability
080   method based on the gradient with respect to ViTs attention maps. (2) As the layerwise explainability
081
      allows to easily pool over many layers, LeGrad scales to large architectures such as ViT-BigG/14
      and is applicable to various feature aggregation methods. (3) We evaluate LeGrad on various tasks
082
      and benchmarks, showing its improvement compared to other state-of-the-art explainability methods
083
      especially for large-scale open vocabulary settings.

      Gradient-Based Explanation Methods Feature-attribution methods are a commonly used expla-
089
      nation technique that explains model decisions by assigning a score to each image pixel, represent-
090
      ing its importance to the model’s output. Generally, these methods can be categorized into two
091   groups (Molnar, 2019) — gradient-based methods that compute explanations based on the gradient
092   of the prediction of the model with respect to each input pixel (Simonyan et al., 2014; Erhan et al.,
093   2009; Sundararajan et al., 2017; Springenberg et al., 2015; Smilkov et al., 2017; Kapishnikov et al.,
094   2019; Selvaraju et al., 2017) and perturbation-based methods that measure pixel importance by suc-
095   cessively perturbing the input images and measuring the impact on the model output (Lundberg &
096   Lee, 2017; Ribeiro et al., 2016; Petsiuk et al., 2018; Carter et al., 2019; Zeiler & Fergus, 2014).
097   While both types of methods have been used successfully to identify correlations and trustworthi-
098   ness in traditional computer vision models (Boggust et al., 2022; Carter et al., 2021), gradient-based
099
      methods are often more computationally efficient since they only require a single backwards pass.
      Further, they are easy to interpret since they are a direct function of the model’s parameters and
100
      do not rely on additional models or image modifications. However, many existing gradient-based
101
      methods were designed for convolutional and feed-forward model architectures, so it is non-trivial
102   to directly apply them to ViTs since ViTs do not contain spatial feature maps and include com-
103   plex interactions between patches induced by the self-attention mechanism. As most gradient-based
104   methods were designed prior to the widespread use of ViTs, researchers have recently made ef-
105   forts to adapt existing methods and to develop new ones specifically for transformers. Chefer et
106   al. (Chefer et al., 2020) extend LRP (Bach et al., 2015) to transformers by integrating gradients
107   within the self-attention layers. However, this approach is computationally heavy and is inflexible
      to architecture changes as it requires specific implementation for each module of the network. To

131   Explanability Methods for ViT A separate line of research has proposed using ViT attention maps,
132   as opposed to gradients, as a way to explain for transformers’ decisions (Abnar & Zuidema, 2020;
133
      Voita et al., 2019). One attention-based method, rollout (Abnar & Zuidema, 2020), traces the flow
      of importance through the transformer’s layers by linearly combining the attention maps via matrix
134
      multiplication. Attention flow (Abnar & Zuidema, 2020) contextualizes the attention mechanism
135
      as a max-flow problem; however, it is computationally demanding and has not been extensively
136   evaluated for vision tasks. While these methods offer insights into the attention mechanism, they
137   often neglect the non-linear interactions between attention heads and the subsequent layers. More-
138   over, they may not adequately distinguish between positive and negative contributions to the final
139   decision, leading to potentially misleading interpretations, as found in Chefer et al. (Chefer et al.,
140   2020). Compared to that LeGrad uses the gradient w.r.t. to the attention maps, thereby assessing the
141   sensitivity of the attention maps to a change in the patch tokens.
142   Vision-Language Explainability Methods Research has also explored vision-langauge models
143   for interpreting representations in vision models. For instance, leveraging CLIP’s language-image
144   space, researchers have provided text descriptions for active neuron regions (Hernandez et al., 2021;
145   Goh et al., 2021) and projected model features into text-based concept banks (Gandelsman et al.,
146   2023). In particular, T EXT S PAN (Gandelsman et al., 2023) focuses on the explainability of CLIP-
147   like models. It refrains from using gradient computation by aggregating the intermediate features’
148   similarities along a given text direction, creating a relevancy map for a text query. LeGrad advances
149   this line of work by focusing on the sensitivity of feature representations within ViTs to generate
150
      relevancy maps that can be adapted to various feature aggregation strategies.

331   4.4   P ERTURBATION -BASED E VALUATION
332
333   Next, to measure LeGrad’s ability to faithfully identify features important to the model, we employ
334   a perturbation-based methodology. ♢Task: Given a classification dataset, we begin by generating
335   explainability maps for every image using the different explainability methods. The analysis then
336   consists of two complementary perturbation tests: positive and negative. In the positive perturbation
337   test, image regions are occluded in descending order of their attributed relevance, as indicated by
338
      the explainability maps. Conversely, the negative perturbation test occludes regions in ascending
      order of relevance (see the Annex for more details and visualizations of positive/negative perturba-
339
      tions). ♢Metric: For both perturbation scenarios, we quantify the impact on the model’s accuracy
340
      by computing the area under the curve (AUC) for pixel erasure, which ranges from 0% to 90%.
341   This metric provides insight into the relationship between the relevance of image regions and the
342   model’s performance. The tests are applicable to both the predicted and ground-truth classes, with
343   the expectation that class-specific methods will show improved performance in the latter. This dual
344   perturbation approach enables a comprehensive evaluation of the network’s explainability by high-
345   lighting the importance of specific image regions in the context of the model’s classification accu-
346   racy. ♢Dataset: Following common practices, we use the ImageNet validation set, which contains

834   B.5   M ITIGATION OF SENSITIVITY TO IRRELEVANT REGIONS :
835
836   We observe that for all evaluated explainability methods, SigLIP displays high activations in image
837   regions corresponding to the background. These activations appeared to be invariant to the input,
838
      regardless of the gradient computation’s basis, these regions were consistently highlighted. To ad-
      dress this issue, we computed the explainability map for a non-informative prompt, specifically
839
      "a photo of". We then leveraged this map to suppress the irrelevant activations.
840
841   Namely, for an activation s under examination, we nullify any location where the activation ex-
842   ceeds a predefined threshold (set at 0.8) in the map generated for the dummy prompt. Formally,
843   let E s denote the explainability map for activation s, and E empty represent the map for the prompt
844
      "a photo of". The correction procedure is defined as follows:
845                                              E^s_{E^{empty} > th} = 0,                            (13)
846
847   where th = 0.8. This method effectively addresses the issue without resorting to external data
848   regarding the image content.
