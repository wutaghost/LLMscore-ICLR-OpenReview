009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Vision-language models (VLMs) such as CLIP are trained via contrastive learning
012            between text and image pairs, resulting in aligned image and text embeddings that
013            are useful for many downstream tasks. A notable drawback of CLIP, however, is
               that the resulting embedding space seems to lack some of the structure of their
014
               purely text-based alternatives. For instance, while text embeddings have been long
015
               noted to satisfy analogies in embedding space using vector arithmetic, CLIP has
016            no such property. In this paper, we propose an approach to natively train CLIP in a
017            contrastive manner to reason about differences in embedding space. We finetune
018            CLIP so that the differences in image embedding space correspond to text descrip-
019            tions of the image differences, which we synthetically generate with large language
020            models on image-caption paired datasets. We first demonstrate that our approach
021            yields significantly improved capabilities in ranking images by a certain attribute
               (e.g., elephants are larger than cats), which is useful in retrieval or constructing
022
               attribute-based classifiers, and improved zeroshot classification performance on
023
               many downstream image classification tasks. In addition, our approach enables a
024            new mechanism for inference that we refer to as comparative prompting, where
025            we leverage prior knowledge of text descriptions of differences between classes
026            of interest, achieving even larger performance gains in classification. Finally, we
027            illustrate that the resulting embeddings obey a larger degree of geometric properties
028            in embedding space, such as in text-to-image generation.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Vision-language models (VLMs) (Jia et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022a), and more specifically CLIP
033   (Radford et al., 2021), leverage paired instances of images and corresponding text descriptions to
034   produce a general-purpose joint embedding between images and language. These models have created
035   a new paradigm of prompting (Radford et al., 2021; Li & Liang, 2021; Bach et al., 2022). In this new
036   paradigm, we can easily design image classifiers through text descriptions of classes and by selecting
037   which of our class descriptions most closely aligns with an image (in terms of cosine similarity
038
      in the multimodal embedding space). These models can also generate images corresponding to
      user-specified text prompts (Podell et al., 2023). Ultimately, this paradigm fundamentally relies on
039
      the accurate alignment of image and text modalities.
040
041   While contrastive-based pretraining on large datasets aims to achieve this embedding alignment,
042   a notable drawback of CLIP models is that they do not exhibit the structure of purely language-
043
      based embeddings. For instance, text embeddings satisfy analogies in embedding space using
      vector arithmetic, e.g., Text(“King”) - Text(“Man”) + Text(“Woman”) ≈ Text (“Queen”) (Mikolov
044
      et al., 2013), while CLIP has no such property. In addition to these shortcomings, previous works
045   demonstrate that CLIP’s embeddings lack geometric properties (Goel et al., 2022), exhibit large
046   gaps between different modalities (Liang et al., 2022), and struggle with handling more complex
047   descriptions, such as connections between multiple attributes and objects (Lewis et al., 2022). As
048   CLIP is commonly used as a backbone for a wide variety of tasks (Ramesh et al., 2022; Podell et al.,
049   2023; Bain et al., 2022), accurately encoding meaningful differences between images can lead to
050   benefits in many downstream tasks, such as compositional text-to-image generation or retrieval.
051   In this paper, we propose to align the difference between CLIP’s image encodings with a semantically
052   meaningful text description of their difference, to improve its ability to reason about differences. We
053   show that these differences between images are poorly localized in CLIP’s embedding space (see
      our experiments in Section 4.2). On the contrary, prior work has shown that large language models

080

081   mance by incorporating prior knowledge in the form of text descriptions of the differences between
082   classes. For instance, for a classification task between images of a crab and lobster, one can describe
083   (or ask an LLM to describe) the following difference: “Crabs have a rounded, flat body, while lobsters
084   have a long body, large claws, and a pronounced tail.” Our approach uses this comparative prompt
085   to update and further separate the class prompts for these similar classes (see Figure 3).
086   We empirically demonstrate the many benefits of the improved reasoning ability from our finetuning
087   approach on synthetic comparisons. First, we observe that PC-CLIP has the new capability of
088   performing difference-based classification, or given a certain attribute (e.g., size and color) to correctly
089   rank images of a pair by that attribute. In fact, PC-CLIP achieves significantly higher performance on
090   this task (up to ∼14 points in absolute accuracy), while CLIP observes almost random performance.
091   In addition, PC-CLIP has improved zeroshot classification performance, when using standard class
092
      prompts or more descriptive class descriptions, across a majority of downstream image classification
      tasks. These improvements hold even when compared to baselines of finetuning on the exact same
093
      data from COCO and an approach that leverages non-comparison-based synthetic data from a
094   LLM, demonstrating that benefits come from finetuning on comparisons. These improvements are
095   even furthered when leveraging our comparative prompting technique with PC-CLIP, while using
096   comparative prompting with CLIP features can exhibit large drops in performance. Finally, we
097   demonstrate that PC-CLIP indeed satisfies a larger degree of geometric properties in embedding
098   space. For instance, PC-CLIP can better manipulate embeddings for image generation (Podell et al.,
099   2023), where additive operations on text embeddings are better preserved in the generated images.

102
103   VLMs and Prompting With the advent of VLMs such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and
104   ALIGN (Jia et al., 2021), a large body of work has studied ways to use these models. A main
105   class of methods is prompting, which is a parameter-efficient technique to define classifiers given
106   informative natural language descriptions of the classes of interest (Zhou et al., 2022). Some
107   approaches leverage LLMs to extract additional information about classes instead of prompts that
      only use the class name, which achieves stronger performance and is perhaps more interpretable

108
      (Menon & Vondrick, 2022; Esfandiarpoor & Bach, 2023). Other approaches have learned these
109   language descriptions both in continuous (Li & Liang, 2021) and discrete settings (Wen et al., 2023;
110   Akinwande et al., 2023). Other recent work looks to extract particular concepts from pretrained
111   models in a zeroshot fashion, with the goal of achieving more robust representations (Adila
112   et al., 2023). Some works attempts to use VLMs to generate captions for images (Mokady et al.,
113   2021) or the difference between images (Yao et al., 2022). Finally, an alternative class of VLMs
114   is built from LLMs that are endowed with visual reasoning abilities through visual instruction
115
      tuning (Liu et al., 2024). Our work is fundamentally different as it uses the reasoning abilities of
      LLMs to improve the geometry of CLIP embeddings, rather than build our VLM directly from a LLM.
116
117
118
      Finetuning With the advent of these VLMs, many works have studied how to finetune these
119   models for downstream tasks, instead of simply using fixed versions of these pretrained models.
120   Many approaches study better ways to achieve more robust models via finetuning (Kumar et al.,
121   2022; Wortsman et al., 2022). Prior work (Fan et al., 2023; Doveh et al., 2023) demonstrates that
122   LLMs can be used to improve or diversify captions in pretraining data, leading to performance
123   benefits. In addition, other work shows that given labeled downstream task data, a better way to
124   perform finetuning is in line with the original pretraining objective (Goyal et al., 2023). Other
125
      work uses multiple LLM-generated class descriptions to improve few-shot finetuning (Feng et al.,
      2023). A relevant line of work is finetuning VLMs for image-difference captioning (Jhamtani &
126
      Berg-Kirkpatrick, 2018; Park et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2024), which looks to produce
127   text descriptions of the difference between image pairs. While the underlying ideas in this line of
128   work are similar, a key distinction is that we study the reverse problem, or to study the benefits of
129   incorporating such information into CLIP’s embedding space.
130
131   CLIP’s Embedding Space A large body of work has studied specific qualities of the learned
132   embedding space of CLIP. A related work looks to better induce geometric properties in the resulting
133   embedding spaces (Goel et al., 2022) through pairwise distances, although this does not directly
134   address (LLM-generated) semantically meaningful differences. Other work finds that the embedding
135
      space of CLIP behaves like a bag of words (Yuksekgonul et al., 2022) and that the models lack the
      ability to bind particular attributes to instances (Lewis et al., 2022). Other work generates large
136
      synthetic datasets (via viewpoint modification, manual text generation via metadata) to improve
137   VLMs abilities to reason about visual concepts and not individual objects (Cascante-Bonilla et al.,
138   2023). Our work is related in that we demonstrate a failure of CLIP’s embeddings, and we propose a
139   new finetuning approach to address these issues.
140
141
142   Using Language to Improve Performance and Interpretability A wide variety of works have
143   studied the use of natural language as human-interpretable explanations of model decisions. This has
144   been studied and shown to improve both LLMs (Zhou et al., 2020; Lampinen et al., 2022a; Howard
145
      et al., 2023) and RL (Lampinen et al., 2022b). The most related setting is using these for VLMs,
      where prior work grounds explanations to modify the network’s attention mechanisms (Petryk et al.,
146
      2022) or provide textual descriptions for specific, fine-grained regions of the image (Li et al., 2022b).
147   Other work studies the setting of visual-textual entailment (Xie et al., 2019; Do et al., 2020), where
148   the task is to determine whether the image entails the given textual description. On the contrary, we
149   focus improving the embeddings of CLIP in terms of pairwise relationships between objects.

157   relational information between classes for improved downstream performance.

      3.4   C OMPARATIVE P ROMPTING
252
253   PC-CLIP’s improved embedding space also allows for a new type of inference to incorporate
254   relational information between classes, which we refer to as comparative prompting. Given a prompt-
255   based classifier, we can incorporate prior knowledge in the form of text descriptions of class-level
256   differences to update our class prompts. As human-labeled image-level differences are expensive
257
      (and are potentially greater in cost to obtaining class labels), we focus on the setting where we have
      class-level differences, as we (or LLMs) can efficiently describe the differences between classes.
258
259   Let A and B represent two classes of interest, with embeddings fA and fB respectively. Given a
260   language description of the difference between class B and class A (and an embedding of fB−A ), we
261   can generate an updated class prompt fA′ as follows:
262                                  fA′ := α · fA + (1 − α)(fB − fB−A ),                                  (3)
263
      where α is a hyperparameter that captures how much we rely on the comparison-based prompt. This
264
      captures our prior knowledge about differences in class descriptions by averaging the embedding fA
265   with the difference in text embeddings fB −fB−A ). Thus, if our original embedding of A is inaccurate,
266   this can be corrected if our embeddings of (B − A) and B are correct. A visual interpretation of this
267   is provided in Figure 3. This exploits an asymmetry between the text representations of fA−B and
268   fB−A , which is perhaps lacking in CLIP as it has been shown to behave similarly to a bag-of-words
269   (Yuksekgonul et al., 2022). Thus, our finetuning provides a simple solution to enable incorporating
      relational information into classification with contrastive-based VLMs.

283
284   In evaluating our approach, we explore the following questions to understand the impacts of our
285   finetuning. First, can PC-CLIP successfully perform difference-based classification, on different
286   data distributions than our comparison-based finetuning dataset? Secondly, how does our finetuning
287   impact our model’s ability to perform zeroshot classification? Finally, does our finetuning generally
      improve the VLM’s embedding space and its ability to perform arithmetic?
288
289   In our experiments, we first demonstrate that PC-CLIP can indeed perform difference-based classifi-
290   cation on multiple downstream datasets with varying types of meaningful differences, while CLIP
291   achieves almost random performance. Furthermore, we demonstrate that our comparative-based
292
      finetuning does not degrade standard zeroshot classification; rather, it improves performance with
      both simple class prompts and longer, descriptive prompts on a majority of image classification tasks.
293
      To control for having finetuned our model on COCO with synthetic LLM generations, we compare
294   against (and outperform) additional baselines of directly finetuning of CLIP on COCO and finetuning
295   on LLM-rewritten captions that have been generated by the same LLM that we use to generate our
296   comparisons. This controls for the additional information from an external LLM and directly studies
297   the benefits of incorporating comparative information. Finally, we demonstrate that PC-CLIP’s text
298   encoder is improved, better localizing class names with respect to their differences, and with better
299   image generations of arithmetic operations in the text embedding space. This also manifests itself in
300
      larger classification performance gains with comparative prompting.

360
      PC-CLIP can perform difference-based classification, while CLIP cannot We report our results
361
      for our difference-based classification tasks in Table 1. We observe that CLIP struggles with this
362   task, achieving almost random performance (∼50%). Some intuition for this result is that CLIP has
363   been primarily trained to align specific instances in its contrastive objective, and does not necessarily
364   capture notions of semantic meaningful differences, leading to deficiencies on this and other related
365   tasks. On the contrary, our finetuning helps improve performance by a large margin across all tasks.
366   For instance, we see increases in performance by ∼14 points in terms of absolute accuracy. This
367   supports that our finetuning leads to a better alignment of the difference between image embeddings
368

452   Embedding Arithmetic with Text-to-Image Generation One way that we can evaluate the quality
453   of our text encoder is by performing arithmetic in the text embedding space and evaluating the
454
      results by visualizing the resulting embedding through existing text-to-image generation models,
      such as Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022; Podell et al., 2023). Here, we can directly swap the
455

456   PC-CLIP objective. Specifics of the generation process are outlined in detail in Appendix I.2. Note
457   that we do not present this as a specific approach to perform compositional image generation (Liu
458   et al., 2022) (as we could easily lengthen the original prompt), but rather, we use this to evaluate
459   PC-CLIP’s improved ability to perform text embedding arithmetic.
460   To quantitatively evaluate how well the generated images match the sum of two text prompts, we
461   can evaluate them using the CLIPScore (Hessel et al., 2021), with a larger CLIP model architecture,
462   although we do remark that finding a metric to evaluate text-image alignment is an open research
463   question. We evaluate a set of images generated to represent CIFAR100 classes while adding in the
464   text embedding of attributes from AwA2. We observe that PC-CLIP, when used with Stable Diffusion,
465   produces images that achieve a higher CLIP score than the original CLIP embedding, reflecting better
466
      arithmetic properties in embedding space.
467   We qualitatively observe that adding in the text embedding of (especially long) comparison-based
468   descriptions to original text prompts leads to slightly more visually consistent generations with our
469   text (see Figure 4). In the provided examples, these comparison-based descriptions negatively impact
470   the visual coherence of generations from CLIP + Stable diffusion (regions circled in red in Figure 4),
471
      while PC-CLIP + Stable Diffusion much better captures the text from the comparison-based additions
      in the generated images. We provide more examples and a more in-depth discussion in Appendix I.3.

      5   D ISCUSSION
526
527   We propose a method to improve CLIP’s embedding space by generating language descriptions of
528   the difference between images and using this dataset to improve the joint embedding space of CLIP
529
      to reflect more interpretable differences between classes, such as size and color. We demonstrate that
      our finetuning enables the ability to perform general difference-based classification while generally
530
      improving or maintaining standard zeroshot prompting performance with our updated VLM. With
531   other simple metrics and text-to-image visualizations, we find that the embedding space of PC-CLIP
532   indeed better captures meaningful notions of differences, which can later improve many downstream
533   applications that build on top of CLIP embeddings.
534
      A fundamental limitation of our method is that we rely on the ability of LLMs to generate these image
535   comparisons from imperfect information (i.e., only the text caption). These models can sometimes
536   leverage general information that does not apply to particular images, as well as having poor responses
537   due to issues such as hallucinations (Zhang et al., 2023). This can likely be improved by the advent
538   and usage of large multimodal models that exhibit both image and language understanding (OpenAI,
539   2023; Liu et al., 2024). In addition, these models themselves are often prone to hallucination, which
      can lead to poor-quality synthetic data.

960   F.2   F ILTERING P ROCEDURE
961
962   While our prompting strategy overall leads to higher-quality generations, there are still many low-
963   quality responses. We employ the following filtering strategy:
964
             • We filter out responses containing “#include” and “#define”; this captures the failure mode
965
               of LLaMA2 that generates responses of code and has no underlying semantic meaning or
966            relation to the images in question.
967
             • We filter out responses containing 8 repeated newline characters; this captures the failure
968            mode of LLaMA2 that only generates newline characters.
969
970   In addition, we also use heuristics to remove parts of the generated responses to improve quality. For
971   instance, we ignore any characters after instances of “Q:”, which indicates that LLaMA2 is generating
      another question and answer, that is not necessarily related to the pair of instances (Ii , Ti ), (Ij , Tj ).


                                                          18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       Similarly, we ignore all characters including and after “Note:”, which is some generic disclaimer
973    outputted by the model, which is again not related to our input instances. Overall, this filtering
974    procedure reduces from a total of 1,000,000 generations to a filtered set of 560,000 generations for
975    the COCO dataset. We remark that we generated these heuristics from a quick pass through a small
976    subset of the LLM responses, although it can likely be improved with a more thorough study of a
977    larger number of responses.

1256

1257   Diffusion XL. We also note that Stable Diffusion XL uses a second text encoder (namely, a larger
1258   model of OpenCLIP ViT-G/14) in an ensemble of experts fashion (Balaji et al., 2022). In these
1259   experiments, we do not replace this larger model due to computational reasons in training a PC-CLIP

       its embedding space, we consider a task of generating photos of images from CIFAR100 (i.e., starting

1272   CLIP-Scores are computed over 800 different image generations.
