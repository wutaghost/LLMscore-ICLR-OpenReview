009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            While automatic report generation has demonstrated promising results using deep
012            learning-based methods, deploying these algorithms in real-world scenarios re-
013            mains challenging, where models may be required to follow the instruction from
014            the radiologists and consider contextual information. Such instructional report
015            generation tasks are critical for enabling more accurate, customizable, and scal-
016            able report generation processes, but remain under-explored and lack substantial
017            datasets for training and evaluation. However, constructing a dataset for report
018            generation with instructions and contexts is challenging due to the scarcity of
019
               medical data, privacy concerns and the absence of recorded user-model interac-
               tions. To tackle this challenge, we propose a unified and automatic data generation
020
               pipeline which leverages large language model (LLM) to produce high-quality in-
021
               structions and context for report generation tasks. We present a new benchmark
022            dataset MIMIC-R3G that extends the largest existing radiology report generation
023            dataset MIMIC-CXR, comprising five representative tasks pertinent to real-world
024            medical report generation. We conducted an extensive evaluation of state-of-the-
025            art methods using the proposed benchmark datasets. Additionally, we introduced
026            a baseline method, the Domain-enhanced Multimodal Model (DeMMo), demon-
027            strating that leveraging training data containing instructions and contextual infor-
028            mation significantly improves the performance of instructional report generation
029            tasks.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Radiology report generation is one of the straightforward yet essential task in computer-aided diag-
034
      nosis (CAD) systems. It aims to automatically generate a text description of the patient’s radiology
035
      images including professional medical diagnosis. Recent works can automatically generate radiol-
036   ogy report accurately within seconds, which largely reduces the workload of professional radiolo-
037   gists in clinical routines (Jing et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022a;
038   Huang et al., 2023).
039
      Most previous works treat radiology report generation as a captioning task, where a text decoder
040
      generate medical report based on extracted image features (Nicolson et al., 2023). In real clini-
041
      cal practice, however, the scenario and procedure might be more complex than a straightforward
042   captioning task. Specifically, in real-world scenarios, the model is required to follow broader in-
043   structions of the radiologists and to consider different types of context information. For example,
044   radiologists usually need to refer to the patient’s X-ray images and reports from previous visits in
045   order to write a more comprehensive report that includes progress or changes in the abnormali-
046   ties. Also in many cases, patients are required to undergo some other medical examinations beside
047   radiology screenings. All these kinds of extra information could affect how radiologists read the
048   radiographs and write the final report for the patient. Therefore, this paper focuses on developing
049   a practical report generation dataset that supports real-world clinical practice containing various
050
      interactions and context information.
051   To facilitate research on radiology report generation with instructions and context, a benchmark
052   dataset needs to be developed that includes not only medical images and reports, but also rich con-
053   textual information and interaction data between doctors and report generation models. However,
      the scarcity of medical data, and the privacy concerns surrounding patient information in the public


                                                        1

054
      domain, poses significant challenges. Also, current medical report generation datasets are predom-
055   inantly obtained from hospital or clinical databases. The information available in these datasets is
056   generally limited to medical images and associated structured reports (Johnson et al., 2019; Demner-
057   Fushman et al., 2016), lacking supplementary contextual information that is essential for a thorough
058   analysis and might influence radiologist’s reasoning in formulating a diagnosis. Furthermore, col-
059   lecting interaction data between doctors and report generation models is exceptionally costly, which
060   requires integrating model deployment into clinical workflows without disrupting patient care, as
061   well as extensive coordination with medical professionals.
062   To address the challenges, we examine the clinical requirements and propose an automatic data gen-
063   eration pipeline and a new benchmark dataset, named MIMIC-R3G (Real-world Radiology Report
064   Generation). MIMIC-R3G contains five representative tasks pertinent to the medical report gener-
065   ation context: report generation with no context, report revision, template-based report generation,
066   report generation based on patient’s previous visits, and report generation incorporating patient’s
067   other information including medical records and laboratory tests. Building on these tasks, we in-
068   troduce a unified automatic data generation pipeline to generate instructions, context, and reports
069
      in accordance with the ground truth report and images, using specific system messages and ground
      truth reports as input to direct large language model (OpenAI, 2022) for generation.
070
071   Furthermore, we introduce a baseline method, DeMMo (Domain-enhanced Multimodal Model), tai-
072   lored for the proposed context-aware report generation tasks with various instruction inputs. This ap-
073   proach efficiently fine-tunes Flamingo model (Alayrac et al., 2022) by integrating a domain-specific
074   medical vision encoder and incorporating additional pathological guidance. Comprehensive exper-
075
      iments on the MIMIC-R3G benchmark demonstrate that our method achieves promising results on
      all real-world report generation tasks, compared to state-of-the-art medical domain visual-language
076
      models.
077
078   In summary, the contributions of this paper are as follows:
079
080
             • We present a new problem setting for real-world report generation that emulates clinical
               practices by incorporating various clinical interactions and contextual information.
081
082          • We propose the first real-world report generation benchmark dataset MIMIC-R3G, where
083            a unified framework is designed to automatically generate the requisite context data, lever-
084            aging the power of LLM.
085          • We develop DeMMo, a large multimodal model with domain-specific capability enhanced
086            via incorporating a general domain Flamingo with an additional medical vision encoder
087            and pathological information for further guidance, serving as a baseline for the benchmark
088            dataset.

090   2   R ELATED W ORKS
091
092   Report Generation Traditional methods use an encoder-decoder regime, where an encoder is used
093
      to extract image features, and a decoder is used to generate text from the features. The combination
      of CNN encoder and RNN decoder were utilized in earlier works (Jing et al., 2018; Xue et al., 2018;
094
      Wang et al., 2018; Hou et al., 2021). With the advent of Transformer architecture, researchers have
095
      explored the use of Transformer with specialized memory or attention mechanisms for report genera-
096   tion (Cornia et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2020; 2021; You et al., 2021). To further improve performance,
097   many works incorporated pre-extracted pathology labels and domain-specific knowledge graphs as
098   priors in the generation pipeline (Liu et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022b; Huang et al., 2023; Li et al.,
099   2023d). Some retrieval-based approaches have also gained prominence in recent years (Endo et al.,
100   2021; Jeong et al., 2023). These methods predominantly employ contrastive learning techniques to
101   retrieve probable texts from the training set as inference outcome. Building on existing approaches,
102   several studies (Wu et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2023) have also taken real-world clinical scenarios into
103   account, but primarily focusing on the single task of incorporating reports from previous visits as
104
      a generation prior. We expand on this and propose a unified task formulation of real-world report
      generation.
105
106   Large Language Models With the strong ability in natural language processing and generation,
107   Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown significant potentials in performing real-world re-
      port generation tasks. State-of-the-art LLMs (Brown et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023; Chowdhery


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      et al., 2022) are highly interactive and capable of following instructions for various language tasks
109   (Ouyang et al., 2022), making it poses high potential in dealing with real-world clinical scenarios.
110   Furthermore, the extensive volume of training data equips LLMs with the capacity to internalize
111   domain-specific knowledge and exhibit reasoning capabilities within the medical field. Without
112   fine-tuning on specific medical dataset, ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2022) is tested to pass the US Medical
113   Licensing Exams (USMLE) (Kung et al., 2023) showing its promising ability to reason and pro-
114   cess language in the medical domain. Finally, LLMs demonstrate proficiency in generating more
115   extensive and complex text sequences, making them well-suited for medical report generation tasks.

117   3     R ADIOLOGY R EPORT G ENERATION WITH I NSTRUCTIONS AND C ONTEXTS
118   In contrast to conventional report generation models, Real-world Radiology Report Generation
119   (R3G) poses two significant differences. Firstly, it necessitates the model to adhere to the user’s
120   requests and instructions. Secondly, in addition to the medical image itself, the model must possess
121   the capability to comprehend and utilize external contextual information in order to produce a more
122   precise report. As a results, we propose several representative sub-tasks that resembles these two
123   requirements, all of which are essential features widely applicable in clinical practice. The instances
124   drawn from these representative sub-tasks will be used to train and evaluate our proposed report
125   generation model.
126   No Context Report Generation This sub-task is the conventional report generation task without
127   any additional instructions from radiologist or context information.
128
      Report Revision Reports generated models may be sub-optimal in some cases, and and human
129
      professionals are still required to review and revise the output reports prior to submission. Therefore,
130   it is desirable for the model to possess the capability of revising the report based on straightforward
131   instructions to further alleviate the workload of the human professional.
132
133
      Template In real-world scenarios, clinics or hospitals may employ structured report templates.
      These templates may comprise a list of common abnormalities or regions, and the radiologist is
134
      required to fill in the corresponding findings or absence of abnormalities. In sum, we want the
135
      model to be capable of generating report following any form of input template.
136
137   Previous Radiology Image and Report as Context In typical clinical practice, patients undergo
138   multiple radiology screenings. It is essential for radiologists to write medical reports that not only
139
      focus on the current radiology image but also reference the patient’s previous medical images and
      reports. This approach enables the production of a more informative report that can address the
140
      alterations in the disease progression compared to previous visits.
141
142   Medical Records and Lab Tests as Context Patient’s medical records, including medical condition
143   history, along with medical exams like blood tests and pulmonary function tests, are vital for accurate
144   diagnosis. Medical records and lab tests are all crucial context information for radiologists to write
145
      reports, so the model should also posses the ability generate reports based on them.

149   4.1   TASK F ORMULATION
150
      We formulate the proposed real-world report generation tasks under a unified instruction-following
151
      paradigm, so we can fully utilize the instruction-following capabilities of a Large Language Model
152
      (LLM). Specifically, we format the proposed real-world report generation tasks into a unified single-
153   round instruction-following example: (Vi , Ii , Ci , Ri′ ), representing the i-th example in the dataset,
154   where Vi denotes a set of medical images; I denotes the instruction from the user; Ci refers to the
155   context information provided to facilitate the report generation; and Ri′ refers to the ground truth
156   report associated with the medical images Vi , instruction Ii , and context Ci in the generated dataset.
157   For all the sub-tasks, Vi is directly utilized from the dataset.
158
159

222   Quality Control of the Generation Since the ground truth report Ri′ is either identical to original
223   report Ri or rewritten by GPT while preserving the medical diagnosis intact, our pipeline is able to
224   produce accurate data with very few factual errors. Furthermore, the generated data has undergone
225   both automatic and manual data quality exam and control processes. Specifically, after generating
226   the data, GPT will be prompted again with the generated data as input and is required to check
227   correctness. For report revision task, GPT checks the correctness of revision instructions. For
228
      template task, it checks whether the generated ground truth follow the diagnosis of original ground
      truth and the format of the given template. For medical records and tests task, it checks whether
229
      the generated context is diagnostically consistent with the ground truth report. If there exists any
230
      incorrectness or inconsistency in generated data, our pipeline will try to regenerate and skip to the
231   next sample after 3 retries. Unsatisfactory generated reports are further filtered by comparing the
232   labels of generated context and ground truth report. We use CheXpert labeler (Irvin et al., 2019),
233   an automatic tool to extract labels of common observations from radiology reports, to extract and
234   compare the labels of Ci and Ri′ to ensure that no information leakage is presented in the generated
235   context, i.e., no ground truth information in generated context. Detailed prompts for quality control
236   are shown in the Appendix.
237   For manual examination, we invite a group of certificated radiologists to validate the clinical cor-
238   rectness of the generated data, yielding a fully human-validated test set of 600 data examples, with
239   200 examples dedicated to each of the three sub-tasks: revision, template and medical records. The
240   content of the other two sub-tasks, no-context and previous report, are directly used from MIMIC-
241   CXR dataset, which does not involve any LLM-generated content, therefore no additional validation
242   is needed. We invite five human annotators, including three junior-level radiologists with less than
243   5 years of medical experience, to annotate the data, and two senior-level radiologists with over 10
244   years of experience to review the annotations. The annotation task involved determining whether the
245
      generated data was plausible and providing a reason, a process that typically requires only entry- to
      mid-level experience. The medical professionals are instructed to carefully examine all information,
246
      including instructions, context, modified reports, and ground truth reports, to determine whether the
247
      entire pipeline is acceptable. Any factual errors, such as missing positive findings or hallucinated
248   false positives, will result in rejection. However, variations in writing styles are allowed, such as
249   treating minor conditions not mentioned as negative. Any disagreements during the annotation pro-
250   cess were discussed to reach a consensus. The disagreement rate between annotators and reviewers
251   regarding the correctness of the generated data was 2.7%. Annotators are also asked to rate the
252   plausibility of each record on a scale from 1 to 10. This plausibility score is a subjective measure
253   by medical practitioners to assess how likely the instructions or situations could occur in their daily
254   work, ensuring that the setting aligns with real-world scenarios.

      7      C ONCLUSIONS
525
526   In this paper, we propose a highly interactive real-world radiology report generation problem setting
527   (R3G). R3G requires models to be highly interactive, to follow instructions and consider various con-
528   text information. A new benchmark dataset for the real-world report generation is built with a unified
529   data generation pipeline. A novel Domain-enhanced Multi-Modal (DeMMo) model is proposed to
530
      enhance the medical domain specific ability of conventional LLM. Experiments demonstrate that
      DeMMo attains competitive performance across all real-world tasks.

757
758
      Multimodal LLMs With the remarkable success of LLMs, researchers started to explore the possi-
759   bility to integrate visual modality into LLMs for various visual-language tasks. Early works such as
760   BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023c) leveraged a query Transformer to connect visual features to LLM. Flamingo
761   (Alayrac et al., 2022) introduced extra trainable layers within LLM in addition to a Transformer to
762   bridge visual and language modalities. LLaVa (Liu et al., 2023) and MIMIC-IT (Li et al., 2023a)
763   leveraged GPT/ChatGPT to build visual instruction tuning datasets and developed multimodal LLMs
764   as general instruction-follow visual agents. Following their ideas, we construct a real-world report
765   generation dataset by building a unified data generation pipeline leveraging ChatGPT.
766   Medical LLMs Numerous works have applied LLM within the medical domain through fintuning
767   a general domain LLM. Med-PaLM (Singhal et al., 2022) and Med-PaLM 2 (Singhal et al., 2023)
768   are medical domain-specific language models developed through instruction fine-tuning based on
769   general domain LLMs. Med-PaLM M (Tu et al., 2023) further fine-tunes PaLM-E to the medical
770   domain using multimodal medical data for medical vision-language tasks. LLaVa-Med (Li et al.,
771   2023b) and Med-Flamingo (Moor et al., 2023) similarly fine-tune their general domain base models
772   using domain-specific data to enhance medical question-answering and conversational capabilities.
773
      Different from fine-tuning LLMs for medical domain, ChatCAD (Wang et al., 2023) and ChatCAD+
      (Zhao et al., 2023) interact with users by connecting medical domain models with ChatGPT via
774
      language prompts. We observe that this framework is capable of doing all of our proposed real-
775
      world report generation tasks without training using extra task-specific data.

977    without using medical vision encoders, showing the importance of adopting a medical vision en-

979    Flamingo visual encoder like DeMMo, instead it directly replaces it with a medical vision encoder.

       F      L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORKS
1539
       For the report revision task, our pipeline generates modifications in reverse from ground truth re-
1540
       ports. Although human validation indicates a 97% acceptance rate, we cannot guarantee that the
1541
       generated modifications accurately reflect the distribution of real-world errors made by human or
1542   AI report generation systems. Future work could focus on recording real-world clinical procedures
1543   where human radiologists revise reports generated by AI systems or written by junior radiologists,
1544   to better capture the nature of these errors.
1545
       For the task of using medical records and lab tests as context, although the MIMIC-IV dataset
1546
       provides EHR data for patients in MIMIC-CXR, we opted to generate synthetic medical records pri-
1547
       marily due to the significant effort required to backtrack and match the corresponding MIMIC-CXR
1548   studies with their associated hospital stays in MIMIC-IV. Methods such as [2] attempt to approx-
1549   imate the correspondence between CXR and EHR data; however, without a direct identification
1550   ID, the accuracy of these methods remains uncertain. Even with such linking methods, 55.99% of
1551   MIMIC-CXR studies could not be matched to a specific stay in the MIMIC-IV dataset. While our
1552   generated context achieved a 99.5% acceptance rate in human validation, it is important to note that
1553   the distribution of generated data may not perfectly reflect the true distribution. Future work could
1554   focus on reorganizing MIMIC-CXR and MIMIC-IV so that all EHR data in MIMIC-IV can be uti-
1555   lized in MIMIC-CXR, or similarly, on collecting and building datasets with available EHR data for
1556
       report generation and other related tasks.
1557   For DeMMo, as introduced in previous sections, our method is a pure generation method without
1558   encompassing extra generation priors such as labels from a classifier. In contrast, methods such as
1559   Zhao et al. (2023) and You et al. (2021) utilize an image classifier to extract disease labels prior
1560   to generation, which ensures diagnostic correctness. Tanida et al. (2023) leverages object detector
1561   and use the extracted abnormal regions to guide generation, which also shows promising result.
1562
       This presents a limitation, as our model’s diagnostic accuracy may not be as reliable as methods
       employing guidance from high accuracy classifiers. Therefore, future works may focus on fusing
1563
       the model with extra generation prior or guidance to further improve clinical efficacy.
1564
1565   We also observe that our DeMMo approach can be generalized to other domains as well using other
       domain-specific vision encoders. A potential future direction could entail utilizing a CT scan en-

1566
       coder for CT report generation, or developing a universal medical vision encoder for a more unified
1567   medical report generation tasks.
