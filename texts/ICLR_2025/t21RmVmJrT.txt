011                                                    A BSTRACT
012
013                Large language models (LLMs) encode vast amounts of knowledge during pre-
014                training (parametric knowledge, or PK) and can further be enhanced by incorpo-
015
                   rating contextual knowledge (CK). Can LLMs effectively integrate their internal
                   PK with external CK to solve complex problems? In this paper, we investigate the
016
                   dynamic interaction between PK and CK, categorizing their relationships into four
017
                   types: Supportive, Complementary, Conflicting, and Irrelevant. To support this
018                investigation, we introduce E CHO QA, a benchmark spanning scientific, factual,
019                and commonsense knowledge. Our results show that LLMs tend to suppress their
020                PK when contextual information is available, even when it is complementary or
021                irrelevant. While tailored instructions can encourage LLMs to rely more on their
022                PK, they still struggle to fully leverage it. These findings reveal a key vulnerability
023                in LLMs, raising concerns about their reliability in knowledge-intensive tasks.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
027                “ If a man keeps cherishing his old knowledge, so as continually to be
028                acquiring new. ”                                                    — Confucius
029   Large language models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023;
030   Zeng et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2024) capture a substantial amount of knowledge through pre-training
031   on large-scale corpus, referred to as parametric knowledge (PK). When applied to problem-solving,
032   LLMs often rely on additional information provided as context, known as contextual knowledge
033   (CK)1 . A critical area that remains underexplored is how effectively LLMs can integrate PK and CK
034   to solve problems. This ability is crucial for human-like intelligence, where we effortlessly draw
035   upon stored knowledge to adapt to new environments, reflecting our capacity to perceive, recall,
036
      and integrate information when encountering new stimuli (Gibson, 1988). Effective collaboration
      between PK and CK is also vital for real-world applications, such as retrieval-augmented generation
037
      (RAG) (Schick et al., 2023; Trivedi et al., 2023; An et al., 2024; Gutiérrez et al., 2024).
038
039   However, we find that for LLMs, an effective interaction between PK and CK can be challenging. For
040   example, we observe a counter-intuitive phenomenon that when supplementing an LLM with more
041   task-relevant knowledge in the context (CK), sometimes the performance degrades because the LLM
042
      fails to effectively utilize the parametric knowledge (PK). Examples are shown in Figure 1. This
      catastrophic test time forgetting greatly limits LLMs capability to effectively solve user queries when
043
      relevant context is provided. In this paper, our objective is to thoroughly investigate the intricate
044
      dynamics between PK and CK, leading to a better understanding of how well LLMs integrate and
045   prioritize these sources of knowledge. Previous works separately study when CK is conflicting or
046   irrelevant to PK, raising concerns about the vulnerability (Wang et al., 2023b; Xie et al., 2024; Wu
047   et al., 2024). However, they fail to answer more fine-grained questions such as: To what extent are
048   LLMs aware of PK, in the presence of CK? How well can LLMs leverage PK given CK? What factors
049   can affect such ability?
050
      To perform a systematic study, we formulate reasoning types based on various relationships between
051   CK and PK —Supportive, Complementary, Conflicting and Irrelevant, illustrated by examples in
052   Figure 1 and Table 1. Due to the lack of appropriate datasets to examine this ability, we introduce

074
      which progressively enforce the use of PK, guiding LLMs preference to varying degrees, as detailed
075
      in Section 3.2. Furthermore, we investigate other factors affecting LLMs abilities to leverage their
076   internal knowledge, e.g., popularity of entities and methods of new knowledge fabrication, shedding
077   lights on future improvements (Section 5). Our investigation draws the following findings:
078
079
      • The suppression of PK in the presence of CK is universal, regardless of the model, knowledge type
      or reasoning type (Complementary, Conflicting or Irrelevant). LLMs disregard their own knowl-
080
      edge, e.g., more than 60% of cases in scientific knowledge for all tested models, relying solely on
081
      the context for reasoning. This highlights the vulnerability of LLMs in leveraging PK.
082
083   • LLMs are more likely to recall their knowledge for some knowledge and reasoning types, e.g., the
084   commonsense knowledge. We find evidence indicating that the reason behind is likely the imbalance
085
      of knowledge in training corpus.
086   • Explicit instruction can help LLMs remember more PK, but still way off from fully leveraging PK.
087   This implies more sophisticated prompt or framework design has the potential to solve this problem
088   to a larger extent. We show some possible prompt templates in Appendix 17.

      Parametric Knowledge in LLMs is encoded in models parameters through vast amounts of text
      data during pre-training. Previous research extensively explores how LLMs leverage PK for reason-
094
      ing (Li et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024b; Wang et al., 2024). Some reveal the challenges in leveraging
095
      PK (Wang et al., 2023a; Allen-Zhu & Li, 2023). These studies typically focus on models ability to
096   utilize PK to perform various tasks, e.g., multi-hop reasoning, without considering the influence of
097   contextual knowledge (CK). While valuable, these analyses do not address how PK is dynamically
098   applied in real-world contexts where CK also plays an important role. Moreover, PK embeded in
099   language models can be outdated or incorrect, prone to hallucination (Elazar et al., 2021; Lazaridou
100   et al., 2021; Zhong et al., 2023). Though some work propose to edit PK (Dai et al., 2022; Meng
101   et al., 2022; 2023), additional effort would be needed for the loss of other abilities.
102   Contextual Knowledge for LLMs refers to the information (or tools) augmented in LLMs context
103   window to enhance generation (RAG), which hopefully supplements LLMs internal PK to improve
104   faithfulness (Guu et al., 2020; Qin et al., 2023; Mallen et al., 2023). Previous work primarily focuses
105   on optimizing the retrieval of relevant documents for reasoning (Press et al., 2023; Asai et al., 2023;
106   Zhuang et al., 2024; Gutiérrez et al., 2024). However, as documents contain diverse information,
107   LLMs may be confused when reasoning, even with optimal retrieval, e.g., when the given knowledge
      contradicts their knowledge (Xu et al., 2024). We find that even complementary CK can hinder

128   Interplay between Parametric and Contextual Knowledge mainly includes behavioral analyses
129   or fusion of both knowledge. Some assess models handling of new knowledge (Yin et al., 2023),
130   focusing on knowledge construction. Other works elicit PK from LLMs and design frameworks to

      the effectiveness of LLMs leveraging of PK under various CK is understudied. Besides, the focus
132

133
      reflected real-world scenarios, e.g., LLM-based search engines or chatbots like ChatGPT Plugins
134   and New Bing (Nakano et al., 2021; Deng et al., 2023) or human reasoning process, e.g., when
135   exploring new environments, human intelligence can naturally integrate knowledge in brain with
136   outside nature. While some studies explore the fusion of CK and PK for improved sufficiency (Jiang
137   et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024), the intricate relationship between them is understudied. We aim to
138   comprehensively examine LLMs ability to manage and integrate these two knowledge sources.

141
142   As shown in Table 1, we design four reasoning types based on various relationships between PK and
143   CK. Without the loss of generality, we adopt Question Answering (QA) as the reasoning task, follow-
144   ing prior work (Mallen et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2024). Formally, given an instruction, a question
145   Q and CK, an LLM leverages PK and CK to reason the answer A. As shown in Equation 1, if Q is
146   posed, then CK operated with PK under the reasoning types (rt) leads to the answer A. Note that for
147   all settings, PK always holds true for Equation 1, meaning that we ensure the LLM has PK required
148   for Q. In experiments, we vary CK based on our designed rt as shown in Figure 1. Different from
149
      Xie et al., 2024, we only present CK in the context, to assess the ability to utilize PK more naturally.
150
151                   Q → (CK rt PK → A) where rt ∈ {⊙, ∧, ⊕, ∨} and PK holds true.                        (1)
152
153   ⊙ Supportive We have CK and PK convey the same information. Our prior experiments show that
154   models behave consistently with or without CK (as expected), or LLMs can effectively adopt PK in
155   this case. Therefore, we do not report this type in main results. (Refer to Appendix B.2 for statistics.)
156   ∧ Complementary This type typically requires both PK and CK (multi-hop) for reasoning. We
157   maintain knowledge sufficiency by assuring CK is new to LLMs and PK is known by the LLM. A
158   key challenge is establishing meaningful connections between them. Previous study construct multi-
159   hop questions by replacing entities with descriptions (Talmor & Berant, 2018; Huang et al., 2023),
160   which does not ensure that CK is new to LLMs. To address this, we create entity-profiles that relate
161   to PK. For the example in Table 1, given PK “the earth is spherical”, CK can be “Z-man was born
      on earth”, which is complementary to PK to answer “The shape of the planet Z-man was born on”.

      ⊕ Conflicting In this type, a fact in CK is fabricated by deletion, modification or extension from
163   PK. We can also create longer narratives around the fabricated fact. For the example in Figure 1, by
164   modification, we can change “Michael Jordan is a Basketball Player” into “Michael Jordan works
165   as an ESPN basketball commentator for 20 years”. We ensure the question Q require that exact fact
166   to answer. We denote the answer that align with CK and PK as Ack and Apk , respectively.
167
      ∨ Irrelevant For this type, we randomly sample irrelevant information as CK, under certain con-
168
      straints, e.g., CK for questions about occupation should still pertain to occupations. As the goal
169   is to test LLMs ability to leverage PK, we only consider the case when PK is necessary for Q in main
170   results. We discuss LLMs ability to filter relevant knowledge in Appendix B.4.

      3.1      E VALUATION M ETRICS
173
174   We design various metrics to assess LLMs ability to leverage PK, as shown in Table 1. Specifically:
175
      • Accuracy (Acc) evaluates whether the output exactly matches the ground truth. For Supportive,
176
      Complementary, and Irrelevant reasoning types, each question Q has a corresponding ground truth
177
      answer Agt . We compute Acc to assess model performance in these scenarios.
178
179   • Memorization Ratio (MR ) measures the extent to which the model adheres to PK for Conflicting
                                                 fpk
180   Reasoning. It is calculated as MR = fpk +f     ck
                                                        , where fpk and fck denotes the frequency of answer
181   faithful to Ack and Apk , respectively, following prior work (Longpre et al., 2021).
182
      • Unknown Ratio (UR ) measures the ratio of models generating unknown. On one hand, UR
183   reveals models uncertainty to some extent. On the other hand, since we make sure PK is required by
184   Q, higher UR shows lower perception of PK. We calculate UR for all reasoning types.

      3.2      R EASONING I NSTRUCTIONS
187
188   Recent post-training technique, e.g., instruction-tuning, enables LLMs to fluently follow human
189   instructions. To explore to what extent LLMs can leverage PK, we design a series of progressively-
190   enforced instructions to guide models behaviors. Please refer to Table 17 for examples.
191       Examples of Progressively-Enforced Reasoning Instructions
192
            Neutral: Combine the given information and your OWN knowledge to answer questions.
193
194
            Trust Yourself : The given information is NOT SUFFICIENT, you should use your OWN
            knowledge combined with the information to answer questions.            (Complementary)
195
            If you think the given information is CONFLICTED with your knowledge, should USE your
196
            OWN knowledge to answer questions.                                          (Conflicting)
197
            Speak Out Loud: You must first OUTPUT your OWN knowledge about the question. If
198
            you think the given information is conflicted with your knowledge, should USE your OWN
199         knowledge to answer questions.                                               (Irrelevant)
200
201   • Neutral Instruction is the simplest prompt, where we treat LLMs as a helpful assistant with
202   knowledge to answer questions. Note that we indeed ask LLMs to use their own knowledge.
203   • Trust Yourself Instruction proactively show the insufficiency or ask LLMs to trust themselves.
204   Concerningly, such enforcing instruction is to test LLMs leverage of PK, but not practical in real-
205   world scenarios where the sufficiency and trustworthiness of knowledge are not ensured.
206
      • Speak Out Loud Instruction further pushes LLMs to explicitly utter its PK then trust themselves,
207
      mirroring the Chain-of-Thought reasoning (Wei et al., 2022). This differs from Xie et al., 2024,
208   who separately construct PK and CK, and ask models to make a choice when PK and CK are both in
209   context. Also, this enforcing instruction is not for practical use.

457   5.2                          FACTORS A FFECTING M ODELS L EVERAGE OF PARAMETRIC K NOWLEDGE
458
459   In previous findings, we show that the awareness and utilization of PK can be influenced by instruc-
460
      tions, knowledge type, and the intricate relations with CK. Here, we discuss additional factors:
461   LLMs can recall their popular knowledge more effectively. Inspired by studies showing that
462   LLMs lean on more popular entities, i.e., monthly associated Wikipedia page views (Mallen et al.,
463   2023; Xie et al., 2024), we evaluate representative LLMs on ConflictQA with Conflicting Reasoning
464   w.r.t, popularity of the topic entity in the question, as illustrated in Figure 5. Intuitively, LLMs
465   can recall memories better for more popular questions. This upward trend holds across all LLMs
466
      and our reasoning instructions. Specifically, GPT-4o-mini and Llama 3.1-70B achieve nearly 80%
      Memorization Ratio for the most popular questions, almost doubling that of the least popular ones.
467
      This observation might also explain why LLMs remember better for commonsense knowledge than
468
      others, indicating the reason behind is likely the knowledge frequency in training corpus.
469
470   Introduction of knowledge relevant to the question may draw overly excessive attention to
471   the context. As mentioned in Section 4.1 (Figure 2(a)), counter-intuitively, even though the given
472
      CK with PK is sufficient to answer the question, performance can still degrade compared to direct
      questioning. We manually examine 200 random samples generated by Llama 3.1-70B with Neutral
473
      Instruction on ALCUNA where LLMs were able to answer on their own but failed when comple-
474
      mentary information was introduced. Note that we do ask LLMs to use their own knowledge in the
475   instruction (Section 3.2). We discover that in 93.5% of cases, LLMs relied on the context and answer
476   choices and even claimed the insufficiency of knowledge (Refer to examples in Appendix B.5 and
477   Table 14). This suggests that LLMs tend to ignore their own knowledge, inhibit deeper thinking and
478   depend entirely on the context. Although our “Trust Yourself Instruction” improves performance, it
479   is still far from expectation, indicating that current LLMs struggle with integrating PK and CK.
480
      We speculate that the phenomenon may stem from the post-training process (Chung et al., 2024).
481   LLMs are fine-tuned with instructions, question and optional context in a fashion shown in Equation
482   2. This training approach encourages LLMs to answer based either on the context (CK) or their
483   internal knowledge (PK) separately, rather than integrating both. We hypothesize that this training
484   objective is the reason behind LLMs struggles in effectively combining PK and CK.

515   5.3                           H OW TO B ETTER L EVERAGE PARAMETRIC K NOWLEDGE G IVEN THE C ONTEXT
516
517   As demonstrated above, LLMs struggle with integrating their parametric knowledge (PK) with con-
518   textual knowledge (CK). However, this ability is crucial for both artificial and human intelligence.
519   We have designed instructions to elicit PK to some extent (Section 3.2). Although effective, these
520
      instructions are progressively enforced and may not be natural enough for practical application. The
      key challenge is to enable LLMs to naturally learn how to balance the two sources of knowledge.
521
      A promising direction is to develop an agent framework where LLMs assume a controlled amount
522
      of responsibility. By separating the process into two steps: first recalling the required knowledge,
523   then reasoning, LLMs may avoid distraction from the context. Another potential solution is to fun-
524   damentally integrate CK and PK in post-training (Equation 2), which we leave for future work.
525
526
527   6                       C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this work, we comprehensively investigate how well LLMs can leverage their parametric knowl-
530   edge (PK) in the presence of contextual knowledge (CK). We formulate four reasoning types: Sup-
531
      portive, Complementary, Conflicting and Irrelevant, based on diverse relationships between CK and
      PK. To facilitate the study, we introduce a new dataset E CHO QA spanning scientific, factual and
532
      commonsense knowledge for multiple LLMs. We design a series of reasoning instructions with
533
      progressively more enforced tones to showcase LLMs leverage of PK. We find that LLMs consis-
534   tently struggle to fully leverage their PK when CK is present under different reasoning types. While
535   the tailored reasoning instructions substantially improve their awareness of PK, they still fall short of
536   expectations. Furthermore, we show that LLMs awareness of their PK can be affected by the relation
537   between CK and PK, instruction, entity popularity, methods of fabricating CK, showing their vulner-
538   ability when faced with external knowledge. We hope that our work provides valuable resources
539   and insights to guide future explorations in understanding LLM-based systems. We will release our
      code and dataset to facilitate future research.

      L IMITATIONS
811
812
      Our study reals a key vulnerability in current LLMs: they struggle to fully leverage their para-
813   metric knowledge when contextual knowledge is present. However, there are some limitations to
814   our work. First, we base our study primarily on contemporary LLMs with strong reasoning and
815   instruction-following abilities. While we find evidence that this issue can be partially mitigated
816   through dedicated prompt or framework design, we argue that the ability to effectively integrate PK
817   with CK should be inherent to the base model. We aim for improvements in this area in future work.
818   Second, while we consistently observe our findings across various reasoning types, we did not focus
819   on making the data more challenging for broader coverage. For instance, conflicting knowledge
820   could occur at the entity, sentence, or document level, and exploring such distinctions is left for
821
      future research. Third, real-world applications are likely more complex than the reasoning types we
      have designed. Although we categorize the interactions between PK and CK into different reason-
822
      ing types, there may be more nuanced combinations of these relationships in real-world scenarios.
823
      We hope our findings raise awareness of the safety concerns surrounding the trustworthiness and
824   reliability of LLM-based systems.

835   A    E LABORATION ON DATA C ONSTRUCTION
836
837   As illustrated in Section 3.3, our proposed dataset E CHO QA aims to test LLMs awareness and
838   leverage of parametric knowledge given contextual knowledge. EchoQA is adapted from existing
839   datasets, the construction procedure of which can fit into any knowledge-intensive task. The key
840   step is to obtain LLMs PK then construct CK and answer choices to fit our reasoning types. By
841   default, the answer choice includes the answer sticking to CK and PK for Conflicting Reasoning or
842   the ground truth for other reasoning types, a randomly-sampled option with the same type, and an
843
      unknown option. Note that we conduct human evaluation for all generated questions and knowledge
      to ensure the meaningfulness, knowledge faithfulness and fluency. Examples for each dataset is
844
      shown in Table 10, 11, 12, w.r.t, our reasoning types. Here we show more details.
845
846   ALCUNA (Yin et al., 2023) originally provides new knowledge. To obtain PK, we adopt a multi-
847   choice question about the fact required by the original question. For example, “Does cat prey on
848   fish? A. Yes, B. No, C. Unknown” or “Cat prey on what? A. Fish, B. Tiger, C. Unknown”. The
849
      exact fact for the question is provided by the dataset. The knowledge association subset naturally
      fits our Complementary Reasoning Type. Questions for other reasoning types are sampled from the
850
      knowledge differentiation subset. For Conflict Reasoning, we regard the new knowledge adapted
851
      from the old one as a conflict fact by changing the new entity name back. For example, given
852   “Dogcat prey on rabbit”, we change it back to “Cat prey on rabbit” as a conflict fact. The entity
853   name in the question also need to change accordingly. For Irrelevant Reasoning, we randomly
854   sample 1-4 new entities profiles provided as CK.
855
      ConflictQA (Xie et al., 2024) originally provides conflicting factual knowledge based on PopQA
856
      (Mallen et al., 2023) and StrategyQA (Geva et al., 2021). We adopt the PopQA subset, with the
857
      popularity of a question, counted by monthly Wikipedia page views associated with entities men-
858   tioned in the question. The key challenge is to construct new knowledge never seen by the LLMs for
859   Complementary Reasoning. Following PopQA Mallen et al. (2023), we adopt templates for each
860   relationship (Table 5). With the template, we obtain CK and construct new multi-hop questions.
861   For example, for question “What’s the occupation of Michael Jordan”, we adopt template to cre-
862   ate a new entity “PersonA” with profile “Michael Jordan is the famous friend of PersonA”. And
863   we change the question to “What’s the occupation of the famous friend of PersonA”. For Irrelevant
      Reasoning, we randomly sample provided knowledge with the same relationship.
