009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               In many applications spanning healthcare, finance, and admissions, it is beneficial
012
               to have personalized machine learning models that make predictions tailored to
013
               subgroups. This can be achieved by encoding personalized characteristics (such
014            as age and sex) as model inputs. In domains where model trust and accuracy are
015            paramount, it is critical to evaluate the effect of personalizing models not only on
016            prediction accuracy but also on the quality of post-hoc model explanations. This
017            paper introduces a unifying framework to quantify and validate personalization ben-
018            efits in terms of both prediction accuracy and explanation quality across different
019            groups, extending this concept to regression settings for the first time –broadening
020            its scope and applicability. For both regression and classification, we derive novel
021            bounds for the number of personalized attributes that can be used to reliably val-
022
               idate these gains. Additionally, through our theoretical analysis we demonstrate
               that improvements in prediction accuracy due to personalization do not necessarily
023
               translate to enhanced explainability, underpinning the importance to evaluate both
024
               metrics when applying machine learning models to safety-critical settings such as
025            healthcare. Finally, we evaluate our proposed framework and validation techniques
026            on a real-world dataset, exemplifying the analysis possibilities that they offer. This
027            research contributes to ongoing efforts in understanding personalization benefits,
028            offering a robust and versatile framework for practitioners to holistically evaluate
029            their models.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   To prevent discrimination, protected attributes like sex, race, or religion are frequently restricted in
036   sensitive decision-making processes, such as employment (U.S. Equal Employment Opportunity
037   Commission, 1963), lending, education, and healthcare. These attributes are legally safeguarded, often
038   due to a history of bias or unequal treatment. However, in some applications, taking these demographic
039
      factors into account can significantly improve prediction performance. This is especially true in
      medicine, where using protected attributes can enhance clinical prediction models by accounting
040
      for biological and sociocultural differences affecting health outcomes. For example, cardiovascular
041
      disease risk prediction models often improve when including sex (Paulus et al., 2016; Huang et al.,
042   2024; Mosca et al., 2011) and race (Paulus et al., 2018), as men and women exhibit distinct heart
043   disease risk patterns, and racial differences –such as increased hypertension prevalence in African
044   Americans– are crucial for accurate risk assessment.
045
      However, such sensitive attributes are known to increase bias in machine learning models (Kodiyan,
046
      2019), so practitioners must ensure that they provide clear performance gains across all involved
047
      subgroups before adopting them. In fact, while incorporating sensitive data often increases overall
048   accuracy, previous studies have already shown that personalization does not uniformly improve
049   performance across all population subgroups (Suriyakumar et al., 2023). To rigorously measure
050   personalization quality and fairness, the work of Monteiro Paes et al. (2022) introduced the Benefit
051   of Personalization (BoP) metric to quantify personalization gain in terms of model classification
052   prediction, based on comparing personalized model performance to that of a generic model trained
053   without group attributes. Additionally, they derive a practical information-theoretic limit on error
      probability for classification tasks.

076   Nevertheless, BoP metric has not yet been extended or bounded for regression models, which in
077   many inherently continuous processes can capture patterns that might be overlooked if data were
078   classified into discrete categories. For example, in the medical domain, instead of classifying glucose
079
      levels as "low", "normal", or "high", regression can predict exact blood glucose levels in diabetes
      patients, enabling more accurate insulin dosing and management (Butt et al., 2023). In addition to
080
      this, no previous work has explored and audited personalization’s effect on model explainability, a
081
      necessity for clinical decision-making and patient trust. For instance, in a study of pneumonia risk,
082   machine learning models counter-intuitively predicted lower mortality risk for patients with asthma,
083   and this finding actually reflected that asthma patients often received more aggressive care, lowering
084   their risk (Caruana et al., 2015). Without explainability methods to understand how models make
085   predictions, such insights could be missed, potentially leading to inadequate treatment decisions.
086
087   Contributions. This work addresses the previous points, aiming at gaining a wider and more
088   comprehensive understanding of the impact of using sensitive characteristics in machine learning
089   models. In particular, the main contributions of this paper include:
090
091   • We propose a generalized BoP framework to evaluate both model explanation quality and prediction
092     accuracy across classification and regression settings. This extension not only broadens BoP scope
093
        and applicability, but also introduces a novel analysis on how protected attributes affects model’s
        explainability (see Fig. 1). This approach is particularly valuable in contexts where understanding
094
        model decisions is as critical as the decisions themselves. (Section 4)
095
096   • We prove rigorous statistical bounds for auditing the generalized BoP metrics in classification and
097     regression contexts. To the best of our knowledge, our work is the first to prove such bound to
098     evaluate BoP for regression. Further, our analysis improves the bounds for classification in the
099
        previous work Monteiro Paes et al. (2022). (Section 5)
100   • Our theoretical analyses yield new insights into BoP across different settings. We demonstrate
101     that regression models can potentially utilize more group attributes than classification models
102     while keeping low testing error. Furthermore, we uncover a critical incompatibility: improvements
103     in prediction accuracy from personalization do not necessarily correlate with enhanced explain-
104
        ability, underscoring the importance of evaluating both criteria in models where accuracy and
        interpretability are paramount. (Section 5)
105
106   • We apply our framework and validation tests on a real-world dataset for a classification and a
107     regression task. In particular, our experimental results empirically demonstrate that personalization
        can indeed affect accuracy and explainability differently. (Section 6)

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
109
110
111   Personalization Our research is part of a body of work that investigates how the use of personalized
112
      features in machine learning models influences group fairness outcomes (Suriyakumar et al., 2023).
      Monteiro Paes et al. (2022) defined a metric to measure the smallest gain in accuracy that any group
113
      can expect to receive from a personalized model. The authors demonstrate how this metric can be
114
      employed to compare personalized and generic models, identifying instances where personalized
115   models produce unjustifiably inaccurate predictions for subgroups that have shared their personal data.
116   However, this literature has focused on the classification framework and has not been generalized
117   to regression tasks. Furthermore, this work has been solely concerned with evaluating how model
118   accuracy is affected, and has not explored how personalizing a model affects the quality of its
119   explanations.
120
      Explainability Typical approaches to model explanation involve measuring how much each input
121   feature contributes to the model’s output, highlighting important inputs to promote user trust. This
122   process often involves using gradients or hidden feature maps to estimate the importance of inputs
123   (Simonyan et al., 2014; Smilkov et al., 2017; Sundararajan et al., 2017; Yuan et al., 2022). For
124   instance, gradient-based methods use backpropagation to compute the gradient of the output with
125   respect to inputs, with higher gradients indicating greater importance(Sundararajan et al., 2017; Yuan
126   et al., 2022). The quality of these explanations is often evaluated using the principle of faithfulness
127   (Lyu et al., 2024; Dasgupta et al., 2022; Jacovi & Goldberg, 2020), which measures how accurately
128   an explanation represents the reasoning of the underlying model. Two key aspects of faithfulness
129
      are sufficiency and comprehenesiveness (DeYoung et al., 2020; Yin et al., 2022); the former assesses
      whether the inputs deemed important are adequate for the model’s prediction, and the latter examines
130
      if these features capture the essence of the model’s decision-making process.
131
132   Personalization on Explainability The field of the effects of personalization on explainable machine
133   learning is largely unexplored. Previous work has investigated gaps in fidelity across subgroups
134   and found that the quality and reliability of explanations may vary across different subgroups
135
      (Balagopalan et al., 2022). The work Balagopalan et al. (2022) trains a human-interpretable model
      to imitate the behavior of a blackbox model, and characterizes fidelity as how well it matches the
136
      blackbox model predictions. To achieve fairness parity, this paper explored using only features with
137
      zero mutual information with respect to a protected attribute. However, it left feature importance
138   explanations out of its scope. Additionally, this work neither considers regression tasks nor looks at
139   how personalization affects differences in explanation quality across subgroups.
140
141
      We extend related works tackling fairness in regression in Appendix Section A.

234   4    A G ENERALIZED F RAMEWORK FOR B ENEFIT OF P ERSONALIZATION
235
236   Leveraging the cost definitions from Section 3, this section introduces a novel generalized approach
237   to quantify the Benefit of Personalization (BoP) –i.e. to rigorously measure whether a personalized
238   model (hp ) performs better than its generic counterpart (h0 ). Drawing inspiration from Monteiro Paes
239   et al. (2022), we propose the first BoP framework that (i) incorporates explainability into the analysis
240
      (apart from prediction accuracy), and that (ii) spans both regression and classification tasks.
241   We start by defining some relevant BoP concepts and metrics.
242
243    Notation. In what follows, we consider that a fixed data distribution PX,S,Y is given, and that h0
244
       and hp models minimize the loss over the training dataset Dtrain .
245   Definition 5 (Population BoP). The gain from personalizing a model can be measured by comparing
246   the costs of the generic and personalized models:
247                                     BoP(h0 , hp ) ≜ C(h0 ) − C(hp ).                             (8)
248   Definition 6 (Groupwise BoP). Similarly, the gain from personalizing a model across each subgroup
249   of samples can be obtained by:
250                                BoPs (h0 , hp , s) ≜ Cs (h0 , s) − Cs (hp , s).                   (9)
251
252   Therefore, Groupwise BoP can be measured across all sensitive subgroups to understand exactly how
253   personalization affects each one of them. In fact, it is crucial to consider if personalization benefits
254   each subgroup equally, and more so to investigate whether personalization actively harms particular
255
      subgroups (Monteiro Paes et al., 2022). The following concept is useful to identify the latter scenario:
256   Definition 7 (Minimal Group BoP).
257                                   γ (h0 , hp ) ≜ min(BoPs (h0 , hp , s))                             (10)
                                                         s∈S
258
259   In particular, note that a positive Minimal Group BoP indicates that all subgroups receive better
260   performance with respect to the cost function. Contrary to this, a negative value reflects that at
261   least one group is disadvantaged by the use of personal attributes. When the Minimal Group BoP is
262   small or negative, the practitioner should reconsider the use of personalized attributes in terms of the
263
      trustworthiness of the model for all subgroups.

265   both predictions and explanations, each across both classification and regression tasks.

308
309   Calculating the BoP requires exact knowledge of the data distribution, a condition rarely met in
310   practice. Moreover, within the ubiquitous finite sample regime, it is critical to understand the
311   feasibility of the empirical BoP –given for instance a limited sample size, or a large number of group
312   attributes. In this section, drawing inspiration from Monteiro Paes et al. (2022), we first introduce a
313   hypothesis testing framework to assess whether a personalized model yields a substantial performance
314
      improvement across all groups. Subsequently, we derive a novel information-theoretic bound on the
      reliability of this procedure, both for binary and real-valued cost functions. In addition to this, we
315
      investigate how the different BoP metrics of our framework relate to each other (classification vs.
316
      regression, prediction vs. explainability), which leads to new insights into BoP.
317
318   All proofs for subsequent theorems, lemmas and corollaries can be found in Appendix Sections E.1,
319   E.2,E.3,F and H.
320
321   Hypothesis Test Given a personalized classifier hp , a generic classifier h0 , and auditing dataset
322   D, we verify whether using a personalized model hp yields an ϵ > 0 gain in expected performances
323   compared to using the generic model h0 . Note that the improvement ϵ is in cost function units,
      and corresponds to the reduction in cost for the group for which moving from h0 to hp is least


                                                          6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      advantageous –i.e. ϵ actually represents the improvement for the group that benefits the least from
325   the personalized model. In this context, we propose the following hypothesis test:
326
327
        H0 : γ(h0 , hp ; D) ≤ 0       ⇔      Personalized hp does not bring any gain for at least one group,
328     H1 : γ(h0 , hp ; D) ≥ ϵ       ⇔      Personalized hp yields at least ϵ improvement for all groups.
329   To actually perform this hypothesis test, we follow (Monteiro Paes et al., 2022) and propose the
330   following threshold test on the estimate of the BoP (i.e., the empirical BoP γ̂):
331    γ̂ ≥ ϵ ⇒ Reject H0 : Conclude that personalization yields at least ϵ improvement for all groups.
332
333
      Furthermore, we characterize the reliability of hypothesis tests in terms of their probability of error.
      We define the probability of error Pe of the hypothesis test on H1 and H0 as:
334
335                  Pe = Pr(Type I error) + Pr(Type II error)
336                     = Pr(Rejecting H0 |H0 is true) + Pr(Failing to reject H0 |H1 is true)
337
      If this probability exceeds 50%, the test is no more reliable than the flip of a fair coin, making it too
338   unreliable to support any meaningful verification. Therefore, it would be practical to compute a lower

340   50%, we would not trust the test. We precisely derive such bounds for binary and regression cost
341   functions in the following paragraphs (applicable for both prediction and explainability).
342

      7      C ONCLUDING R EMARKS
529
530
531
      This work introduces a novel BoP framework that accommodates model accuracy and explainability,
      both of which are paramount to building trust and transparency in sensitive settings. Additionally,
532
      the framework also extends the BoP analysis to regression tasks, enabling its application to new
533
      non-discretized scenarios. Through our theoretical analysis, we identified conditions for regression
534   and classification where testing and estimation methods lack sufficient reliability to guarantee
535   improvements across subgroups. Our findings also reveal that regression tasks have the potential to
536   benefit from more personalized attributes than classification tasks, and that improved accuracy from
537   personalization does not necessarily translate to enhanced explainability. Finally, and as exemplified
538   by our evaluation, our framework and accompanying tests facilitate nuanced decisions regarding the
539   use of protected attributes. Overall, this paper broadens the scope and applicability of BoP analysis
      and in doing so contributes to the selection of more fair and interpretable models.
