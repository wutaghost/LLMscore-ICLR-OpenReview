009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            We found that enforcing guidance throughout the sampling process is often coun-
012            terproductive due to the model-fitting issue, where samples are ‘tuned’ to match
013            the classifier’s parameters rather than generalizing the expected condition. This
014            work identifies and quantifies the problem, demonstrating that reducing or ex-
015            cluding guidance at numerous timesteps can mitigate this issue. By distributing
016
               a small amount of guidance over a large number of sampling timesteps, we ob-
               serve a significant improvement in image quality and diversity while also reducing
017
               the required guidance timesteps by nearly 40%. This approach addresses a ma-
018
               jor challenge in applying guidance effectively to generative tasks. Consequently,
019            our proposed method, termed Compress Guidance, allows for the exclusion of a
020            substantial number of guidance timesteps while still surpassing baseline models in
021            image quality. We validate our approach through benchmarks on label-conditional
022            and text-to-image generative tasks across various datasets and models.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Guidance in diffusion models is mainly divided into classifier-free guidance in Ho & Salimans
      (2022), and classifier guidance in Dhariwal & Nichol (2021). Although both of these methods
028
      significantly improve the performance of the diffusion samples Dhariwal & Nichol (2021); Ho &
029
      Salimans (2022); Bansal et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023); Epstein et al. (2023), they both suffer from
030   large computation time. For classifier guidance, the act of gradients calculation backwards through a
031   classifier is costly. On the other hand, forwarding through a diffusion model twice at every timestep
032   also costs significant computation in classifier-free guidance.
033
      This work challenges the necessity of the current complex process based on several key observations.
034
      First, we find that the guidance loss is predominantly active during the early stages of the sampling
035
      process, when the image lacks a well-defined structure. As the model progresses and shifts its focus
036   to refining image details, the guidance loss tends to approach zero. Additionally, when evaluating
037   intermediate samples with an additional classifier not used for guidance, we observe that the loss
038   from this external classifier does not decrease in the same way as it does for the guidance-specific
039   classifier. This suggests that the generated samples are tailored to fit the features of the guiding
040   classifier rather than producing generalized features applicable to different classifiers. We define
041   this issue as model-fitting, where the generated image pixels are optimized to satisfy the guiding
042   classifier’s criteria rather than generalizing to the intended conditions. The problem is validated by
043   three pieces of evidence in section 3.1.
044   These observations prompt us to question whether guidance is necessary at every timestep and how
045   reducing the frequency of guidance could enhance generative quality. In Section 3.2, we further
046   explore the properties of guidance in ensuring sample quality. Based on this analysis, we propose
047   a simple yet effective method called Compress Guidance (CompG), which mitigates the issue by
048   reducing the number of timesteps that invoke gradient calculation. This approach not only improves
049   sample quality but also significantly accelerates the overall process as shown in Fig.1.
050   Overall, the contributions of our works are three-fold: (1) Explore and quantify the model-fitting
051   problem in guidance and the redundant computation resulting from current guidance methods. (2)
052   Propose a simple but effective method to contain the model-fitting problem and improve compu-
053   tational time. (3) Extensive analysis and experimental results for different datasets and generative
      tasks on both classifier and classifier-free guidance perspectives.

208   on-sampling loss and off-sampling loss for observation.
209   Definition 1. On-sampling loss/accuracy refers to the loss or accuracy evaluated on the generated
210   samples xt at timestep t during the diffusion sampling process, which consists of T timesteps. This
211   loss is defined as − log pϕ (ŷ|xt ) by the classifier parameters ϕ that provides guidance throughout
212   the sampling process.
213   Definition 2. Off-sampling loss/accuracy refers to the loss or accuracy evaluated on the generated
214   samples xt at timestep t during the diffusion sampling process, which consists of T timesteps. This
215   loss is defined as − log pϕ′ (ŷ|xt ) by the classifier parameters ϕ′ that does not provides guidance
      throughout the sampling process.

      Definition 3. Model-fitting occurs when sampled images xt at timestep t is updated to maximize
244
      pϕ (y|xt ) or to satisfy the parameters of the ϕ only instead of the real distribution q(y|xt ).
245
246   In practice, a pretrained pϕ (y|xt ) is only able to capture part of the q(y|xt ). Fitting solely with
247   pϕ (y|xt ) limits the sample’s generalisation ability, leading to incorrect features or overemphasising
248   certain details due to misclassification or overfocusing of the guidance classifier. Three pieces of
249   evidence support that the vanilla guidance suffers from model-fitting problem.
250
      Evidence 1: From the figures in Table 2, we see that while the on-sampling loss converges around
251   the 120th timestep, the off-sampling loss remains high until the diffusion model converges later.
252   This indicates that samples xt at timestep t satisfy only the on-sampling classifier but not the off-
253   sampling classifier, despite their identical performance and architecture. Although the off-sampling
254   loss decreases by the end, a significant gap between the off-sampling and on-sampling losses per-
255   sists. This supports our hypothesis that the guidance sampling process produces features that fit only
256   the guidance classifier, not the conditional information.
257   Evidence 2: Table 2 illustrates the model-fitting problem through accuracy metrics. With vanilla
258   guidance, the accuracy is about 90.80% for the on-sampling classifier. However, the same samples
259   evaluated by the off-sampling classifier or Resnet152 achieve only around 62.5% and 34.2% ac-
260   curacy, respectively. This indicates that many features generated by the model are specific to the
261   guidance classifier and do not generalize to other models.
262
      Evidence 3: Figure 3 (first row) shows samples from vanilla guidance, where every sampling step
263   receives guidance information. Applying guidance at all timesteps forces the model to fit the on-
264   sampling classifier’s perception. Often, this makes the model colour-sensitive, focusing on generat-
265   ing the ”orange” feature for Goldfish and ignoring other details.
266
      From the three pieces of evidence we can observe, we can conclude that the vanilla guidance scheme
267
      has suffered from the model-fitting problem.

282   3.2    A NALYSIS
283
284   Gradient over-calculation is the main reason for model-fitting. Thus, gradient balance, which is to
285   call not too many times of gradient calculation, is required. A straightforward solution is to eliminate
286   the gradient calculations for the later timesteps, which have been found to be less active, as shown
287   in Figure 2. This approach is referred to as Early Stopping (ES), where guidance is halted from the
288   200th timestep onwards, continuing until the 0th timestep.
289   Early Stopping: Figure 4 demonstrates that ES suffers from the forgetting problem, where on-
290   sampling classification loss increases during the remaining sampling process, negatively impacting
291   the generative outputs. This suggests that the guidance requires the property of continuity, meaning
292   the gap between consecutive guidance steps must not be too large to prevent the forgetting problem.
293   Uniform skipping guidance: We try an alternative approach which is called Uniform Skip-
294   ping Guidance (UG). In UG, 50 guidance steps are evenly distributed across 250 sampling steps,
295   with guidance applied every five steps. This ensures continuity throughout the sampling pro-
296   cess, mitigating the forgetting problem. However, as shown in Figure 2, UG encounters the
297   issue of non-convergence, where the classification magnitude is too weak and becomes over-
298   shadowed by the denoising signals from the diffusion models, leading to poor conditional in-
299   formation. Thus, a guidance must require another property, which is magnitude sufficiency.
300
301   In summary, vanilla guidance faces the issue of model-                     7
302   fitting, while ES and UG fail due to the forgetting and                    6
303   non-convergence problems, respectively. Therefore, the
304   primary goal of our proposed method is to meet three key                   5
