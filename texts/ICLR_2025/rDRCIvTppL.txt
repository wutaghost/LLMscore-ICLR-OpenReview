009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advancements in diffusion models have significantly enhanced the quality
012            of video generation. However, fine-grained control over camera pose remains a
013            challenge. While U-Net-based models have shown promising results for camera
014            control, transformer-based diffusion models (DiT)—the preferred architecture for
015            large-scale video generation—suffer from severe degradation in camera motion
016            accuracy. In this paper, we investigate the underlying causes of this issue and pro-
017
               pose solutions tailored to DiT architectures. Our study reveals that camera control
               performance depends heavily on the choice of conditioning methods rather than
018
               camera pose representations that is commonly believed. To address the persistent
019
               motion degradation in DiT, we introduce Camera Motion Guidance (CMG),
020            based on classifier-free guidance, which boosts camera control by over 400%.
021            Additionally, we present a sparse camera control pipeline, significantly simplify-
022            ing the process of specifying camera poses for long videos. Code and models will
023            be released upon publication.

040   1    I NTRODUCTION
041
042   Recent advances in text-to-video (T2V) generation have significantly improved video quality, with
043   diffusion models playing a key role in producing coherent and visually appealing outputs. While
044   these models effectively translate text prompts into dynamic videos, they often lack fine-grained
045   control over aspects like camera pose. To address this, methods such as Wang et al. (2024); He et al.
046   (2024); Xu et al. (2024) introduced camera pose conditioning into U-Net-based (Ronneberger et al.
047   (2015)) diffusion models pretrained on 2D images (Rombach et al. (2022); Podell et al. (2023)),
048   demonstrating promising results in controlling camera trajectories in generated videos. Recently,
049   transformer-based diffusion models (DiT) (Peebles & Xie (2022)) have emerged as the preferred
050
      architecture for large-scale video generation models (Ma et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2024); Yang et al.
      (2024b); Zheng et al. (2024)) due to their superior scalability.
051
052   However, existing camera control methods may not be effective for DiT due to the architectural
053   differences. Concurrent work (Bahmani et al. (2024)) found that direct porting of these methods
      to DiT led to loss of controllability, but no in-depth investigation was conducted to isolate the root


                                                           1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      causes. The introduction of new conditioning methods, alongside different camera pose represen-
055   tations, new DiT architecture, and the shift from space-time (2D+1D) to spatio-temporal(3D) latent
056   encoding, further complicated the issue. To address this, we conducted an extensive study to iden-
057   tify the causes of camera control degradation in DiT architectures and proposed solutions broadly
058   applicable across all DiT models and U-Net models.
059
      Our experiments reveal that the strength of camera conditioning weakens in DiT due to the larger
060
      embedding dimensions in transformers compared to U-Net. Specifically, the 12-parameter extrinsic
061   camera parameters, a common camera pose representation used in MotionCtrl (Wang et al. (2024)),
062   prove to be ineffective in this context. Although Plücker coordinates used by (He et al. (2024); Xu
063   et al. (2024); Bahmani et al. (2024)) may mitigate the problem slightly but our study reveals it is the
064   camera embedding dimension that plays a more significant role. Improvement can be achieved with
065   appropriate method to project the camera parameters into higher dimension.
066
      Despite these improvements, DiT still experiences significant deterioration in camera motion, even
067   with an optimal combination of camera representation and conditioning methods. Implementing
068   two state-of-the-art U-Net methods (Wang et al. (2024); He et al. (2024)) in DiT resulted in videos
069   with static or limited camera motion and less accurate camera orientations. To address this, we
070   propose a novel Camera Motion Guidance (CMG) method based on the widely used classifier-free
071   guidance technique Ho & Salimans (2021). CMG improves camera pose accuracy and motion over
072   400% compared to baseline DiT models. Being architecture-agnostic, it applies generically to both
073   space-time and spatio-temporal architecture to improve camera control in video generation.
074   On the other hand, existing methods for camera control rely on dense camera input, requiring a
075   camera pose for every frame, which is tedious, especially for long videos. To simplify this process,
076   we propose a novel data augmentation pipeline that introduces sparse camera control, where only
077   the camera pose for the final frame or a few key interval frames is required. Our experiments
078   demonstrate that sparse camera control shown promising results, simplifying the input process while
079   maintaining high-quality results and precise camera motion.
080   We summarize our main contributions as below:
081
082          • We introduce novel Camera Motion Guidance, a classifier-free guidance method, improv-
083            ing camera motion by over 400% in video diffusion transformers.
084
             • We identify the root causes of camera control degradation in DiT and successfully devel-
085
               oped the first camera control model for space-time video diffusion transformers.
086
087          • We develop a novel data augmentation pipeline that enables sparse camera control, which
088            simplifies the required input control signal. To our knowledge, this is a unique feature not
089            demonstrated in the existing work.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
092
093
      Video Diffusion Models. Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022; Stability.ai,
094
      2023; Podell et al., 2023) have achieved remarkable success in image generation, leading to advance-
095
      ments in video diffusion models that build upon this foundation. The video diffusion models (Ho
096   et al., 2022; Blattmann et al., 2023) first process individual frames by applying 2D latent encodings
097   to each image separately, and then fuse the temporal dimension to generate videos, this is known as
098   space-time encoding (2D+1D). More recent developments (Liu et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2024; Zheng
099   et al., 2024; Lab & etc., 2024) have introduced spatio-temporal encoding, which encodes multiple
100   frames simultaneously (3D), creating more compact latent code for long video generation.
101
      Camera Pose Control has existed since early 2D human image generation methods. By modifying
102   the size and vertical rotation of skeleton images, methods such as Ma et al. (2017); Ren et al. (2022);
103   Ju et al. (2023); Cheong et al. (2024) could influence camera pose, though only indirectly in limited
104   ways. As neural network architectures have evolved from convolutional networks (Lecun et al.,
105   1998) to transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017), parameterized poses have been explored as substitutes
106   for pose images (Cheong et al., 2022). In a study, Cheong et al. (2023) explicitly mapped 3D camera
107   translation parameters along with 3D SMPL body pose parameters (Lopper et al., 2015), allowing
      for simultaneous control of both body pose and camera pose.

108
      As video generation emerged as active research, attention has been focused on controlling camera
109   motion for video. AnimateDiff (Guo et al., 2024) trains module on specific motion and hence requir-
110   ing new module for a different motion, hindering usability. Hence, parameterized camera control
111   has recently become a focal point in video generation to improve its ease-of-use. Direct-a-video
112   (Yang et al., 2024a) uses only translation movement limiting the camera motion to only panning
113   and zooming. Instead, MotionCtrl (Wang et al., 2024) introduced a rotation-and-translation matrix
114   derived from camera extrinsic parameters, allowing more complex motion. CameraCtrl (He et al.,
115   2024), on the other hand, uses Plücker coordinates as camera pose representation, enabling geomet-
116   ric interpolation for each pixel. We evaluate both state-of-the-art parameterization methods to assess
117
      their effectiveness in DiT-based models. In these methods, camera poses are applied to individual
      image latents on a frame-by-frame basis, which makes them unsuitable for direct application to
118
      spatio-temporal models such as concurrent works (Bahmani et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024) where
119
      multiple frames are jointly encoded into a single latent representation. To the best of our knowledge,
120   we are the first to develop camera control methods specifically for space-time DiT, enabling video
121   models to leverage the extensive availability of pretrained 2D diffusion models.
122
123
      Diffuser Guidance. Dhariwal & Nichol (2021) demonstrated that applying guidance in diffusion
      models significantly enhances image quality. During inference, in each denoising step, the model
124
      denoises the latent twice—once without conditioning and once with it. The difference between the
125
      conditioned and unconditioned latents defines a direction that can be extrapolated by multiplying
126   it with a scalar guidance scale. In particular, classifier-free guidance (Ho & Salimans (2021)) has
127   become ubiquitous in modern text prompting diffusion models in which an empty string or negative
128   text prompt is used as unconditional reference to provide latent extrapolation to improve image or
129   video quality. Despite advancements in camera control for video generation (Wang et al., 2024;
130   He et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024; Bahmani et al., 2024), diffuser guidance has not been explored for
131   camera motion improvement. In this paper, we introduce camera motion guidance to enhance the
132   accuracy and quality of camera motion in video generation models.
133   Sparse Camera Control. Existing methods rely on dense camera poses to achieve effective control.
134   SparseCtrl (Guo et al., 2023) explores applying sparse image-based structural control but does not
135   incorporate camera control, leaving a gap in addressing sparse camera pose scenarios for video
136   generation tasks.

140   3.1   P RELIMINARY
141
142   Camera Representation. Extrinsic camera parameters describe the camera’s position and orienta-
143   tion in 3D space represented by a rotation matrix R ∈ R3×3 and a translation vector T ∈ R3 which
144   form the rotation-and-translation (RT) matrix [R|T] ∈ R3×4 . Intrinsic parameters, encapsulated in
145   the camera matrix K, define the camera’s internal characteristics, including focal length, principal
146   point and pixel size. These are used to map a 2D pixel location in the image to a 3D direction
147   vector in camera’s coordinate system. For each pixel (x, y) in image coordinate space, its Plücker
148   coordinate is calculated as (O × dx,y , dx,y) where O ∈ R3 is the camera center in world coordinates
149   derived from −R⊤ T; and the direction vector d ∈ R3 is obtained by:
150                                           dx,y = RK−1 [x, y, 1]⊤                                   (1)
151   This formulation represents the direction and location of 3D lines, allowing for efficient geometric
152   operations like interpolation and transformation, which are useful for camera trajectory and ray-
153   based rendering. Compared to a flattened RT ∈ R12 in a video frame, Plücker coordinates has
154   higher dimension ∈ Rh,w,6 where h and w are height and width of video resolution.
155
      Camera Conditioning for Video Generation. We examine the state-of-the-art camera conditioning
156   in U-Net models to understand how their network topology differences from DiT models affect the
157   effectiveness of camera conditioning. In U-Net architecture, the spatial resolution decreases as the
158   features traverses down the network, while the channel C increases from e.g. 320 to a maximum
159   of e.g. 1280 before stepping down again. MotionCtrl(Wang et al. (2024)) employs a RT matrix to
160   as camera pose representation for the entire video frame. To incorporate this into the U-Net, the
161   flattened RT is repeated for every latent pixel and concatenated with the U-Net’s features along
      channel dimension, forming a new dimension of C+12 where C is the U-Net embedding’s channel


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      number. However, increased spatial resolution of U-Net’s embedding is accompanied with repetitive
163   RT in each spatial position and does not carry more camera information. Thus we can ignore the
164   spatial dimension when considering camera conditioning influence and consider only the channel
165   dimension. As illustrated in Figure 2(a), U-Net has the smallest embedding channel at its top and
166   bottom layers, resulting in the highest ratio of camera parameter dimension to embedding dimension,
167   we refer to as condition-to-channel ratio. For simplicity, we display only the channel dimension of
168   the full tensor shape [B, N, H, W, C] (batch size, number of frames, image height, image width,
169   channel) and omit the other dimensions in the figure.
170   In contrast, transformers maintain a consistent channel number e.g. 1024 across all the layers. When
171   implementing MotionCtrl’s method to OpenSora (Zheng et al. (2024)) DiT, the increase of minimum
172   channel number from 320 to 1152 lead to significant drop (3×) of the condition-to-channel ratio of
173   12:C from 1:27 to 1:96. We hypothesize camera conditioning strength is proportionate to this ratio,
174   which leads to a considerably weakened control strength in DiT.

      Our experiment compares another conditioning scheme from CameraCtrl (He et al. (2024)) to con-
194
      firm our hypothesis. As Plücker coordinates has dimension of [h, l, 6] per camera pose, a camera
195   encoder is necessary to produce multi-scale camera embedding that matches the dimensions of the
196   DiT’s features in each layer l, represented as [Hl , Hl , Cl ] for element-wise addition as shown in
197   Figure 2b. This difference is even more pronounced when considering the spatial dimension, as
198   each Plücker embedding is unique for each spatial location, thereby offering significantly stronger
199   camera conditioning. At a high level, the main differentiators between the methods are the camera
200   representations and the approach to fusing camera embeddings: MotionCtrl concatenates RT, while
201   CameraCtrl adds Plücker embedding to the U-Net features.
202   Both methods share several common practices. First, each fused embedding is projected back to
203   the original embedding dimension via a linear layer at every U-Net layer. Additionally, camera
204   conditioning is applied before the temporal transformers. Only the temporal transformers and the
205   newly added camera control components are updated during training, while all other parameters
206   remain frozen. These practices were similarly adopted in our experiments. Another method, CamCo
207   (Xu et al. (2024)), employs a conditioning scheme similar to CameraCtrl, with the key difference
208   being the use of 1×1 convolution layers instead of linear layers for projection. However, due to the
209
      lack of open-source implementation, we have not experimented with it in our study.

226   3.3   DATA P ROCESSING AND C AMERA AUGMENTATION
227
228   Given that our base video model is limited to 16 frames, translating to about 0.5 seconds of footage
229   at 30 frames-per-second, taking consecutive frames would result in minimal camera motion. There-
230
      fore, we extract video frames from the dataset in strides of 4 to 8 frames, sampled uniformly, ensur-
      ing we capture sufficient temporal information while preserving meaningful motion dynamics. We
231
      randomly sample 16 frames from a training video sample, and this can produce arbitrary large start-
232
      ing translation vector. Therefore, we represent all RT matrices relative to the first frame by setting
233   the translation vector T1 = 03 and rotation matrix R1 = I3×3 and multiplying [R1 |T1 ]−1 to the rest
234   of the RT matrices.
235
236
      To ensure smooth integration of our novel camera control method, we now establish a robust camera
      augmentation pipeline. In training, we randomly generate static video by repeating the first frame
237
      and its corresponding camera pose across all subsequent frames. In other words, all camera poses
238
      are filled with value of [R1 |T1 ] or its corresponding Plücker coordinate, a condition we denote as
239   null camera, ∅C . Inspired by Srivastava et al. (2014), we also randomly drop out camera poses in
240   a video by setting them to zeros to prevent overfitting and allow for sparse camera control, we call
241   this zero camera. In addition to the new proposed augmentation, we also adopted standard video
242   augmentation of center image cropping and video temporal reversal which is critical as it balances
243   the distribution of two opposite camera motions.

      6     C ONCLUSIONS
514
      This paper thoroughly examined the impact of various camera representations and conditioning
515
      methods on camera control for video generative diffusion transformers. Our extensive experiments
516
      confirmed that high-dimensional camera embedding is critical for effective camera control, support-
517   ing our hypothesis. We also found that camera representation alone is not the key factor for success-
518   ful control; instead, it must be paired with effective conditioning methods and guidance techniques
519   to achieve optimal results. We successfully demonstrated the first camera control model for space-
520   time DiT by combining the CameraCtrl architecture, Plücker coordinates for camera representation,
521   and our novel camera motion guidance (CMG).
522
      We have proved that CMG is highly effective in inducing motion and enhancing camera control.
523   Due to limited resources and code availability, we could not experiment with CMG on a broader
524   range of video models. However, we believe it would be equally effective for U-Net and other DiT
525   models with spatio-temporal architectures. Additionally, we introduced novel camera data augmen-
526   tation techniques that enable sparse camera control. These simple yet effective methods are generic,
527   making them applicable and beneficial for a broader range of video model architectures.
528
      In future work, we aim to test our CMG method on a wider range of models, including U-Nets and
529   other spatio-temporal DiT. Additionally, we plan to enhance our approach to sparse camera control,
530   ensuring that it can achieve even greater accuracy in interpolating camera poses.
