                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Formulas are the language of communication between humans and nature. It is an
               important research topic of artificial intelligence to find expressions from observed
011
               data to reflect the relationship between each variable in the data, which is called a
012
               symbolic regression problem. The existing symbolic regression methods directly
013            generate expressions according to the given observation data, but we cannot require
014            the algorithm to generate expressions that meet specific requirements according
015            to the known prior knowledge. For example, the expression needs to contain the
016            symbol ‘sin’ or be periodicity, and so on. Even if it can, it often requires very com-
017            plex operations, which is very inconvenient. In this paper, based on multi-modal
018            large language models, we propose ChatSR, a conversational symbolic regression
019            method that can generate expressions that meet the requirements simply by de-
020            scribing the requirements with natural language instructions. By experimenting
021
               on the test datasets, we can demonstrate that ChatSR leads the state-of-the-art
               baselines in fitting performance. More notably, ChatSR can well understand the
022
               prior knowledge contained in natural language prompts, and can further improve
023
               the quality of generated expressions according to the prior knowledge. In addition,
024            it is exciting that ChatSR has good zero-shot capability.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Mathematical formulas are the language of communication between nature and human beings. With
030   the succinct expression, we can obtain the potential relationship between the individual variables in
031   the formula. The goal of scientists is to summarize a concise expression to reflect the law behind the
032   physical phenomenon from the observation data. Because manual discovery of formula theorems
033   often requires a long period. Moreover, the demands on scientists are high. So people are trying to
034   use artificial intelligence algorithms to make computers discover mathematical formulas from data on
035   their own. This is where the symbolic regression problem comes in. Specifically, given observations
036   D = {X, y}, SR seeks a function f that satisfies y = f (X), where X ∈ Rn×d , y ∈ Rn , d, n are
037   the dimension of variable and number of data points, respectively. f is composed of several basic
038
      primitive operators such as +, −, ×, ÷, sin, cos, x1 , ....
039   The traditional methods regard symbolic regression as a combinatorial optimization problem, and use
040   GP algorithm and reinforcement learning algorithm to deal with symbolic regression problem. This
041   kind of method is trained from scratch for each new data, which has good anti-noise and versatility.
042   However, in actual scientific research, scientists have a lot of priors or assumptions when modeling
043   observation data with expressions. For example, suppose we use time t as a variable and want a
044   function f(t) that models the intensity of light over a month. We almost know that f(t) is a periodic
045   function, so we tend to model f(t) using periodic functions like sin() or cos(). In addition, scientists
046   may want to obtain expressions that conform to symmetry, periodicity, translation invariance, and
047   so on. Although current methods based on reinforcement learning can achieve some of the people’s
048   needs by adding certain constraints to the search process. But it will be very troublesome, requiring
049   us to change the code, which is very unfriendly to non-computer science people.
050   In recent years, multimodal large language models (MLLMs), represented by GPT-4v, have achieved
051   significant advancements. MLLMs can answer our questions based on input from various modalities
052   such as images, PDF files, or videos. For example, if we input an image containing a person and
053   a dog and then ask the MLLM, ‘What are in the image?’ it will describe the image by stating that
      ’There are a person and a dog’. If we add some conditions and ask, ‘What pets is in the image?’ it


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      will respond that ’There is a pet dog in the image’. So can we develop a multimodal large language
055   model for symbolic regression, where we just take the input data [X,y], describe the requirements in
056   natural langue, and it can generate the expression we need to fit the data [X,y]?
057
058
      In this paper, we propose a symbolic regression method based on MLLM called ChatSR. ChatSR
      only needs to describe the requirements in simple natural language, and it can generate an expression
059
      that satisfies the requirements and fits the observed data. Specifically, we will consider the observed
060
      data as one modality and then consider the text (Question and answer pairs about expressions) as
061   another modality. We first freeze the Large Language Model(LLM) and SetTransformer(Data Feature
062   Extractor ) and then perform feature alignment by training a fully connected layer to map the observed
063   data features to the literal feature space. In the second step, we unfreeze the parameters of LLM to
064   train ChatSR End-to-End.
065
066          • This paper presents ChatSR, a conversational symbolic regression method based on multi-
067            modal large language models, which allows describing requirements in natural language
068            during chat interactions and generates expressions that meet those requirements.
069          • We find that trained ChatSR can leverage the powerful language understanding capabilities
070            of large language models to have good zero-shot ability on properties or requirements
071            outside the training set.
072          • We provide new potential research directions for using multimodal large language models.

102   Based on genetic programming This kind of method is a classical kind of algorithm in the field
103   of symbolic regression. GP Arnaldo et al. (2014), McConaghy (2011), Nguyen et al. (2017) is
104   the main representative of this kind of method, its main idea is to simulate the process of human
105   evolution. Firstly, it initialized an expression population, then generated new individuals by crossover
106   and mutation, and finally generated a new population by fitness. The above process is repeated
107   until the target expression is obtained. RSRMXu et al. integrates the GP algorithm with Double Q-
      learningHasselt (2010) and the MCTS algorithmCoulom (2006). a Double Q-learning block, designed

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      for exploitation, that helps reduce the feasible search space of MCTS via properly understanding
109   the distribution of reward, In short, the RSRM model consists of a three-step symbolic learning
110   process: RLbased expression search, GP tuning, and MSDB. In this paperFong et al. (2022), the
111   fitness function of the traditional GP algorithm is improved, which promotes the use of an adaptability
112   framework in evolutionary SR which uses fitness functions that alternate across generations. LLM-
113   SRChang et al. (2023) and ICSRZhao et al. (2023) use the large language model to aid the search
114   process, just let the LLM produce a series of expressions, and then use the good expressions as a hint
115   to let the LLM continue to produce a new batch of expressions until the target expression is reached.
116
117   Based on reinforcement learning Reinforcement learning-based algorithms treat symbolic regres-
118   sion as a combinatorial optimization problem. The typical algorithm is DSRPetersen et al. (2019),
119   which uses a recurrent neural network as a policy network to generate a probability distribution P
120   for sampling, and then samples according to the probability P to obtain multiple expressions. The
121   reward value of the sampled expressions is calculated and the policy network is updated with the
122   risky policy, and the loop continues until the target expression is obtained. DSOMundhenk et al.
123   (2021) is based on DSR by introducing the GP algorithm. The purpose of the policy network is to
124
      generate a better initial population for the GP algorithm. Then, the risk policy gradient algorithm
      is also used to update the policy network. Although the above two algorithms are very good, the
125
      efficiency is low, and the expression is more complex, especially the DSO algorithm is more obvious.
126
      There have been many recent symbolic regression algorithms based on the Monte Carlo tree search.
127   SPLSun et al. (2022) uses MCTS in the field of symbolic regression and introduces the concept
128   of modularity to improve search efficiency. However, due to the lack of guidance of MCTS, the
129   search efficiency of this algorithm is low. To improve the search efficiency of the algorithm, the two
130   algorithms DGSR-MCTS Kamienny et al. (2023) and TPSR Shojaee et al. (2024a) introduced the
131   policy network to guide the MCTS process based on the previous algorithm. While maintaining the
132   performance of the algorithm, it greatly improves the search efficiency of the algorithm. However,
133   although the above two algorithms improve the search efficiency of the algorithm, they reduce the
134   Versatility of the algorithm, and the noise robustness ability of the algorithm is also greatly reduced.
135
      To solve the above problems and balance the Versatility and efficiency of the algorithm, SR-GPT Li
      et al. (2024a) uses a policy network that learns in real-time to guide the MCTS process. It achieves
136
      high performance while efficient search.

      3.4     M ODEL ARCHITECTURE
333
334   In ChatSR1, we use SetTransformer trained with contrastive learning as the data feature extractor,
335   whose parameters are frozen throughout. Then the data features extracted by SetTransformer are
336   mapped to the word embedding space of LLM through the projection layer. Finally, we train the
337   parameters of the projection layer and LLM together. We choose VicunaChiang et al. (2023) as our
338   LLM fφ parameterized by φ.
339
340   3.4.1    S ET T RANSFORMER
341
      The data information plays a crucial role in guiding the Decoder. To accommodate the permutation
342   invariance of data features—where the dataset’s features should remain unchanged regardless of the
343   input order—we utilize the SetTransformer as our data encoding method, as described by Lee et al.
344   (2019). Our encoder takes a set of data points D = {X, y} ∈ Rn×d . These data points undergo an
345   initial transformation via a trainable affine layer, which uplifts them into a latent space hn ∈ Rdh .
346   Subsequently, the data is processed through a series of Induced Set Attention Blocks (ISABs)Lee
347   et al. (2019), which employ several layers of cross-attention mechanisms. Initially, a set of learnable
348   vectors serves as queries, with the input data acting as the keys and values for the first cross-attention
349   layer. The outputs from this first layer are then repurposed as keys and values for a subsequent
350
      cross-attention process, with the original dataset vectors as queries. Following these layers of cross-
      attention, we introduce a dropout layer to prevent overfitting. Finally, the output size is standardized
351
      through a final cross-attention operation that uses another set of learnable vectors as queries, ensuring
352
      that the output size remains consistent and does not vary with the number of inputs.

475    Expression recovery rate is a very challenging metric to evaluate symbolic regression algorithms.
476    In order to test whether introducing prior knowledge into the prompts improves ChatSR’s recovery
477    rate, we tested them on the datasets. First, in the prompt, we don’t give it any prior knowledge (no
478    requirements), we just ask it to generate an expression to fit the data. Then, we give the model some
479    prior knowledge (requirements) in the prompt, according to the properties and form of the expressions
480    . For example, for Nguyen-5 sin(x2 ) cos(x) − 1, we’ll ask it to generate an expression that contains
481    the symbols sin and cos.
482    For each expression, we do the above two kinds of experiments 20 times each. Finally, the recovery
483    rate was calculated. The specific results are shown in Fig.4(ChatSR, no prior knowledge in prompt )
484    and ChatSR*, giving prior knowledge in prompt). From the figure, we can clearly see that giving
485    prior knowledge of the prompt can significantly improve the recovery rate. This also proves that our
       aim of improving the quality of generated expressions by providing priors through natural language

      4.3   Z ERO - SHOT ABILITY TEST
490
491   The general language model has a strong natural language understanding ability and a rich knowledge
492   reserve. Previous experiments have found that it has a strong zero-shot ability on many tasks.
493   Therefore, we would like ChatSR to inherit this capability as well. For example, if we only included
494   symmetries and periodicity in our training dataset, could ChatSR use the zero-shot power of large
495   language models to directly understand and generate expressions with other properties? (e.g., the
496   generated expression should be monotonic, etc.).
497   To test ChatSR’s zero-shot capability, we chose several properties not covered in the training dataset
498   to test (monotonicity, symmetry with respect to the origin, convexity, concavity, and boundedness).
499   The main test is whether ChatSR can understand and generate expressions that meet the requirements
500   according to its zero-shot ability. Specifically, for each property above, we artificially synthesize 10
501   expressions(Appendix B) that conform to this property. Then we run each expression 20 times with
502   different prompts (whether we want the generated expression to satisfy a certain property or not).
503
      The averaged R2 , recover rate and the proportion (Success rate) of generated expressions conforming
      to the specified properties are then counted. The test results are shown in Table 2. As we can see,
504
      ChatSR shows good zero-shot ability. When prompted, ChatSR was able to generate expressions that
505
      matched the requirements, even if those properties were not present in the training dataset. And R2
506   and recover rate are also improved. This is due to the powerful language understanding capabilities
507   of large language models.

      5     C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
511
512
      In this work, we present ChatSR, a novel symbolic regression paradigm based on multimodal large
      language models that can conduct conversations and make requests in natural language. Specifically,
513
      we use many data and dialog pairs to train a multimodal large language model with the ability to
514
      fit the data by generating expressions based on the data and natural language prompts. Moreover,
515   we experimentally find that ChatSR has good zero-shot capability. This means that we can rely on
516   the powerful understanding power of large language models to impose various requirements on
517   expression generation. This approach promises to change the way symbolic regression can be applied.
518   When we want the generated expression to satisfy some constraint, we only need to describe our
519   requirements in natural language instead of changing the code. This greatly reduces the threshold
520   for the use of symbolic regression and improves the flexibility of the symbolic regression algorithm.
521   ChatSR will have great potential applications in finance, healthcare, and other fields that have
522   very high requirements for interpretability because ChatSR can get an interpretable mathematical
523
      expression from the data. In addition, we believe ChatSR has great potential for applications in
      scientific discovery and AI for Science.
524
525   Last but not least, ChatSR also has some problems, such as poor noise robustness. Next, we will try
526   to improve its noise robustness by contrastive learning or other methods.
