009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Computing the Nash Equilibrium (NE) is a fundamental yet computationally chal-
012            lenging problem in game theory. Although recent approaches have incorporated
013            deep learning techniques to tackle this intractability, most of them still struggle
014            with scalability when the number of players increases, due to the exponential
015            growth of computational cost. Inspired by the efficiency of classical learning dy-
016            namics methods, we propose a deep learning-augmented Nash equilibrium solver,
017            named Deep Iterative Nash Equilibrium Solver (DINES), based on a novel frame-
018
               work that integrates deep learning into iterative algorithms to solve Nash Equilib-
               ria more efficiently. Our approach effectively reduces time complexity to a poly-
019
               nomial level and mitigates the curse of dimensionality by leveraging query-based
020
               access to utility functions rather than requiring the full utility matrix. Experi-
021            mental results demonstrate that our approach achieves better or comparable ap-
022            proximation accuracy compared to existing methods, while significantly reducing
023            computational expense. This advantage is highlighted in large-scale sparse games,
024            which is previously intractable for most existing deep-learning-based methods.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   The problem of computing Nash equilibria (NE) is one of the most fundamental challenges in game
030   theory, and has profound implications in artificial intelligence fields, including multi-agent systems,
031   reinforcement learning, and strategic decision-making. Nash equilibria provide a way to predict
032   and understand how multiple rational agents interact and make decisions in competitive or coop-
033
      erative environments, making it highly relevant for applications ranging from autonomous systems
      to multi-agent reinforcement learning. The computation of NE gains increasing importance as AI
034
      systems increasingly operate in complex, multi-agent settings, such as autonomous driving, market
035
      simulations, and resource allocation.
036
037   Despite its importance, finding NE in general games faces significant computational challenges, and
038   over the past few decades, extensive research has focused on developing efficient algorithms to ad-
039
      dress this problem. It is known that the computation complexity of this problem is PPAD-complete
      (Daskalakis et al., 2006; Chen & Deng, 2006), meaning that any algorithm that computes NE exactly
040
      must take exponential time, unless PPAD = P. To circumvent this complexity, polynomial-time al-
041
      gorithms for approximate Nash equilibria have been developed, but improving approximation guar-
042   antees remains challenging. Moreover, these algorithms are mostly restricted to two-player games,
043   and do not easily scale to multi-player settings. On the other hand, heuristic methods, especially
044   the class of learning dynamics like Fictitious Play (Brown, 1951) and Regret Matching (Hart &
045   Mas-Colell, 2000), are effectively applied in various practical scenarios, including decentralized en-
046   vironments. Despite their practical success, these methods lack guarantees of convergence to NE,
047   and may fail to converge in worst-case situations. This divide between theoretical algorithms and
048   heuristic methods reflects the constant conflict between computational efficiency and solution qual-
049   ity when computing Nash equilibria.
050   Inspired by advances in deep learning, recent works have developed new approaches to solve Nash
051   equilibrium by leveraging neural networks, mitigating the computational limitations of traditional
052   solvers. For example, Duan et al. (2023a) firstly proposed to approximate equilibrium strategies
053   with a multilayer perceptron (MLP), trained based on approximation error. Goktas et al. (2024)
      introduced a generative adversarial network (GAN)-based solver for games with continuous strategy


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      spaces. Liu et al. (2024) employed attention mechanism over joint actions to compute equilibria with
055   improved accuracy and equivariant property.
056
057   However, these methods still face significant challenges. Firstly, they are computationally costly due
058
      to the large model size required to achieve reasonable approximation of the equilibrium. Secondly,
      they struggle with the exponential growth in the size of the utility matrix as the number of players in-
059
      creases, because their model take as input the whole utility matrix, which leads to scalability issues.
060
      Moreover, due to the black-box nature of neural networks, these methods lack the interpretability
061   and generalization ability compared with classical algorithms.
062
063   In this paper, we propose a deep learning-augmented Nash equilibrium solver, named Deep Iterative
064
      Nash Equilibrium Solver (DINES), which combines the strengths of classical learning dynamics
      with modern deep learning techniques. Our approach replaces the iterative update rules in learning
065
      dynamics methods with optimizable deep neural networks, leveraging player-wise and action-wise
066
      attention mechanisms to balance expressive power and computational efficiency in the decision of
067   strategies. By optimizing the solver’s convergence rate and reducing the complexity of utility access,
068   we achieve significant improvements over existing methods in terms of both speed and scalability.
069
070
      Our contributions are threefold:
071
            1. We introduce a general framework of learning-augmented iterative NE solver, and propose
072            Deep Iterative Nash Equilibrium Solver (DINES) based on this framework. This frame-
073            work retains the structural advantages of classical learning dynamics, while leveraging the
074            expressive power of deep learning to enhance convergence performance. We design the
075            model architecture of DINES by incorporating efficient attention-based neural networks,
076            achieving a polynomial computational complexity in the number of players and actions.
077         2. We demonstrate through experimental results that our method achieves equilibrium approx-
078            imations superior to or comparable with those of state-of-the-art deep learning methods,
079            while significantly reducing computational costs. Compared to classical learning dynam-
080            ics, our approach achieves notable improvements in both accuracy and convergence.
081         3. We conduct further experiments to demeonstrate the scalability and adaptability of our
082            approach in large-scale multi-player succinct games, such as polymatrix games, which
083            have been intractable for most existing methods due to the curse of dimensionality.

      2   L ITERATURE R EVIEW
087
      Extensive research has focused on developing a wide range of methods to either solve or approximate
088
      Nash equilibria, broadly categorized into three major approaches: theoretical algorithms, heuristic
089
      algorithms, and deep-learning approachs. In this section, we review these approaches, summarizing
090   their strengths and limitations. We refer the reader to a recent survey (Li et al., 2024) for a detailed
091   review.
092
093
      Theoretical algorithms. It is well-known that the Nash equilibrium in zero-sum two-player games
      can be solved efficiently through linear programming, but the problem become PPAD-complete in
094
      general-sum games (Daskalakis et al., 2006; Chen & Deng, 2006). Various algorithms have been
095
      designed to solve Nash equilibrium in general normal-form games exactly or near-exactly (Lipton
096   et al., 2003; Kontogiannis & Spirakis, 2007; Lemke & Howson, 1964; Laan et al., 1987; Govindan
097   & Wilson, 2003; Porter et al., 2008). All these methods take super-polynomial running time due to
098   the PPAD-complete computational complexity. To circumvent this intractability, polynomial time
099   algorithms for approximate Nash equilibrium are designed (Kontogiannis et al., 2009; Daskalakis
100   et al., 2009; 2007; Bosse et al., 2010; Tsaknakis & Spirakis, 2008; Deligkas et al., 2023). However,
101   this line of research faces significant challenge in improving the approximation guarantees: The cur-
102   rent best algorithm by Deligkas et al. (2023) guarantees a 1/3-approximation, offering only a slight
103   improvement over the 0.3393-approximation achieved by Tsaknakis & Spirakis (2008) decades ago.
104
      The applicability of these approximate algorithms in practical scenarios is limited for two main rea-
      sons: firstly the guaranteed approximation ratio remains too large to be meaningful, and secondly
105
      they are restricted to two-player bimatrix games, without easy generalization to multi-player games.
106
107   Heuristic algorithms and learning dynamics. Heuristic algorithms, especially in the form of
      learning dynamics, have emerged as practical solutions for computing Nash equilibria in games in-


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      volving multiple players. Based on the heuristic idea that players can reach the equilibrium through
109   repeated interactions, in these dynamics, agents adapt their strategies based on observed outcomes in
110   each round, forming a iterative learning process. The class of learning dynamics includes fictitious
111   play (Brown, 1951), best response dynamics (Cournot, 1838), and no-regret learning algorithms
112   such as regret matching (Hart & Mas-Colell, 2000), Hedge (Auer et al., 1995), and multiplicative
113   weight update methods (Arora et al., 2012). The main advantage of these methods lies in their
114   scalability owing to the simplicity and efficiency of the updating rules. Additionally, many learn-
115   ing dynamics can operate in a decentralized manner, requiring no communication between agents
116   beyond the strategic interaction, which makes these methods particularly suitable for distributed
117
      scenarios. However, learning dynamics in general have no theoretical guarantees for convergence
      rate to NE, and can even fail to converge in worst cases. Nonetheless, a wide range of weaker con-
118
      vergence results have been derived. In specific cases like two-player zero-sum games and potential
119
      games, fictitious play is known to converge in terms of the empirical distribution of actions over time
120   (Robinson, 1951; Monderer & Shapley, 1996). For general games, it is proved that any no-regret
121   dynamics converges to coarse correlated equilibrium (CCE), while any no-swap-regret dynamics
122   converges to correlated equilibrium (CE) (Cesa-Bianchi & Lugosi, 2006; Blum & Mansour, 2005).
123
      Deep-learning approaches. To avoid the theoretical barriers and pursue better accuracy and eff-
124
      ciency in practical scenarios, a series of deep-learning-based methods have been developed to solve
125
      the Nash equilibrium. Duan et al. (2023a) firstly proposes to approximate Nash equilibrium with
126   neural networks, and derive generalization bound and agnostic PAC learnability results. Marris et al.
127   (2022) proposes a deep-learning framework for equilibrium solving and selection, mainly focusing
128   on CE and CCE. Goktas et al. (2024) utilizes generative adversarial learning to solve equilibria in
129   general game-theoretic settings with continuous strategy spaces. Liu et al. (2024) is the most related
130   to our work. They achieve the state-of-the-art approximation accuracy through attention operations
131   with equivariant property. However, their proposed model is computationally expensive due to the
132   self-attention operation on all T N joint actions, resulting in a Θ(T 2N ) time complexity, hindering
133   its application to scenarios with larger number of players. This curse of dimensionality is also faced
134
      by most existing methods due to direct access to the whole utility table. While our work also uti-
      lizes the attention mechanism, we effectively reduce the computational complexity through query
135
      access of utilities and decomposed attention operations, obtaning significantly improved efficiency
136
      and scalability.

      3.2   G AME R EPRESENTATION
179
180   From a computational perspective, the representation of the utility functions Gp (·) in a normal-form
181   game determines how the algorithm can access it. Below we introduce several kinds of representa-
182   tions:
183
184   Tabular Game In a tabular game, the utility functions are explicitly represented as a N -
185
              dimensional payoff table that lists the players’ utility values under every joint action.
186   Polymatrix Game A polymatrix game can be decomposed into bimatrix games (i.e., two-player
187          tabular game) between every pair of players. The utility of a player is obtained by summing
188          up her utility in all the N − 1 bimatrix games involving her. The utility functions are
189          represented by N (N − 1) matrices indicating the payoff tables of all these bimatrix games.
190   Succinct Game A succinct game refers to any game with a succinct representation of the utility
191           functions that is feasible under large number of players and actions, including the polyma-
192           trix game. Most generally, each utility function Gp (·) is represented by a polynomial-sized
193           circuit that can be evaluated on the joint action profiles.
194
      When a game has specific structures such as decomposable or sparse interactions, representing it as
195
      a succinct game can significantly reduce the size of the representation compared to the tabular form,
196   bringing advantages in computational efficiency.

      3.3   P ERMUTATION E QUIVARIANCE
199
200   The concept of Nash equilibrium possesses a natural property of permutation equivariance: When
201   the players and actions are reordered, the Nash equilibria are reordered in the same way. Con-
202   sequently, it is reasonable for a Nash equilibrium solver to also satisfy permutation equivariance.
203   It is also suggested in Duan et al. (2023b) that permutation equivariance can improve the solver’s
204   generalization ability. We formally define this property as follows.
205   Definition 3 (Permutation Equivariance). Let GN,T denote the space of all normal-form games G
206   with given N and T . An isomorphism Isoρ on GN,T is specified by ρ = (π, τ1 , . . . , τN ), where π
207   is a permutation on the player set [N ], and each τp is a permutation on the action set Ap . Such a
208   isomorphism maps a game G ∈ GN,T to a new game G′ = Isoρ (G) where the utility functions are
209   given by
210                      G′π(p) (τπ(1) (aπ(1) ), . . . , τπ(N ) (aπ(N ) )) = Gp (a1 , . . . , aN ).
211
212   Consider a mapping ϕ = (ϕp,j )p∈[N ],j∈[T ] : GN,T → Ω[N ]×[T ] , which produces an output
213   ϕp,j (G) ∈ Ω for each action ajp . Here Ω denotes the output space. We define ϕ to be permuta-
214   tion equivariant, if for any isomorphism ρ = (π, τ1 , . . . , τN ), it holds for all G ∈ GN,T and p ∈ [N ],
215   j ∈ [T ] that
                                         ϕπ(p),τπ(p) (Isoρ (G)) = ϕp,j (G).

250   Most learning dynamics methods for NE computation follow a common iterative structural frame-
251   work, as presented in Algorithm 1. Under this framework, players interact iteratively, each maintain-

      ing a internal state, which is typically a vector, such as the player’s current evaluation of the actions.

253

450
451   In this paper, we propose Deep Iterative Nash Equilibrium Solver (DINES), built on a novel frame-
452   work incorporating deep learning into the iterative structure of classical learning dynamics. Our
453   approach significantly reduce the time complexity to a polynomial level, resulting in enhanced scal-
454   ability in games with large number of players and actions, especially in large-scale succinct form
455   games. Experimental results on tabular games and polymatrix games demonstrate the superiority of
456   our approach in terms of the accuracy-efficiency trade-off.
