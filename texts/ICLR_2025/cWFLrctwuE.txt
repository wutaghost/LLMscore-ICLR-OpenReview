009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In the current era of Artificial Intelligence (AI), the realm of database querying is
012            experiencing a profound evolution. With the recent emergence of Large Language
013            Models (LLMs), with a particular emphasis on Vietnamese in this study, a promis-
014            ing opportunity arises to bridge the gap between human language and database
015            interactions. In this paper, we embark on realizing this vision through a three-
016            pronged approach. Firstly, we introduce a few-shot learning method designed to
017
               enhance the database schema comprehension of Vietnamese LLMs. Secondly, we
               employ a chain-of-thought technique to systematically guide LLMs in capturing
018
               complex natural language expressions for SQL generation. Thirdly, we intro-
019
               duce a novel method to streamline the input schema by removing redundant parts
020            and retaining only the parts that are truly relevant to enhance the efficiency and
021            accuracy of the SQL generation process. Finally, we experimented with a com-
022            bination of few-shot, chain-of-thought learning, and schema-enhancing methods.
023            Through experimentation with augmented datasets, we observe encouraging ini-
024            tial results. Our approach outperforms the current state-of-the-art model by 23%
025            in exact matching on the Vietnamese ViText2SQL dataset. We achieved this result
026            with a single pre-training step and one epoch of retraining, compared to the SoTA
027            model’s 10 epochs. These findings demonstrate the effectiveness of our method
028
               and its potential for Vietnamese text-to-SQL applications.
029            Keywords: Text-to-SQL, Large Language Models, Few-shot, Chain-of-thought,
030
               Mini Schema, ViText2SQL, SQL

099   3.1       I NSTRUCTION T UNING
100
101   Instruction Tuning is a training method for pre-trained language models for a specific task. The
102
      data consists of two components: Instructions and Outputs (or Targets). Instructions contains user
      requirements, input information, task descriptions, and user expectations that the language model
103
      must fulfill. The output is the string the model is required to generate. Instruction tuning is an
104
      important technique to help pre-trained language models quickly adapt to real-world tasks, while
105   controlling the ability to generate results and achieve high performance.

      3.2     L OW-R ANK A DAPTATION T RAINING
109
110
      LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) is a method for fine-tuning pre-trained models that was introduced
111   by Hu et al. (2021). It has several advantages over traditional fine-tuning methods: LoRA requires
112   calculating gradients for only a small number of new parameters while keeping the original model
113   fixed, making it more efficient than fine-tuning the entire model. The LoRA weight set is typically
114   only 1-2% the size of the original weights, allowing easier storage and deployment. Multiple LoRA
115   weight sets can be applied to the same original model since the original weights remain unchanged,
116   letting a model be adapted to multiple tasks. LoRA can also be combined with other training meth-
117   ods to further boost performance for different tasks. LoRA weights merge with the original model
118   during deployment, avoiding additional inference latency. In principle, LoRA can be applied to
119
      any subset of weight matrices in a neural network to reduce the number of trainable parameters.
      However, typically in Transformer models LoRA is applied to attention blocks only. The result-
120
      ing number of trainable parameters in a LoRA model depends on the size of the low-rank update
121
      matrices, which is determined mainly by the rank r.
122
123   Applying LoRA to Large Language Models with billions of parameters may not fit in memory. Tim
124   Dettmers and Artidoro Pagnoni Dettmers et al. (2023) proposed quantizing pretrained models before
125
      using LoRA fine-tuning. Quantization LoRA (QLoRA) uses NF4 to quantize original weights to 4-
      bit, drastically reducing size. LoRA then adds parameters to the quantized model.

135   3.3.1    C HAIN - OF -T HOUGHT
136
      Chain-of-Though Learning Prompting Liu & Tan (2023) is a new prompting technique designed to
137
      get large language models to explain their reasoning. Unlike traditional prompting which just seeks
138
      an answer, CoT prompting requires the model to provide an answer and explain the steps taken. This
139   approach has proven effective in improving results for tasks like arithmetic, common sense reason-
140   ing and symbolic reasoning. By encouraging a more detailed reasoning process, CoT prompting
141   greatly enhances how we interact with LLMs. This leads to more accurate and interpretable outputs,
142   especially for complex reasoning tasks. CoT prompting works particularly well with larger models,
143   highlighting potential to develop AI that gives right answers along with transparent thought insights.
144   This helps connect human and artificial reasoning.
145   Designing effective CoT prompts requires understanding the task and reasoning steps involved. It
146   is crucial to meticulously design the reasoning process and clearly document each step involved.
147   Additionally, the model can be guided to generate multiple inferences, collectively termed CoT
148   consistency, and employ a voting mechanism to determine the final outcome.
149

      3.3.2    F EW- SHOT L EARNING
152
      Few-shot learning involves training machine learning models with a small amount of data to guide
153
      predictions, unlike standard techniques using large training data. In Natural Language Processing
154
      (NLP), it can be used with large language models pre-trained on large text datasets. This enables the
155   model to understand related unseen tasks from just a few examples by learning tasks implicitly.
156
157   Few-shot examples contain: a short task description; a few input-output examples showing expec-
158
      tations; and a prompt example for the model to complete. Crafting these is tricky as models are
      sensitive to example wording. A way to optimize it is learning a shared representation across tasks
159
      then training task-specific classifiers on top of this.
160
161   Note that the number of few-shot examples can impact effectiveness. Too few might not provide
      enough guidance, while too many might limit generalization.

      3.3.3    F EW- SHOT AND C HAIN - OF -T HOUGHT C OMBINE L EARNING
163
164   Combining few-shot learning and Chain-of-Thought prompting is a method being researched to

166   prompts can guide the model’s reasoning with few examples, making learning more effective. It
167   explains the model’s thought process, allowing understanding of how it answers and identifying
168
      biases to build trust. Prompts help break down complex tasks into simpler steps, strengthening the
      model’s capacity for new concepts with restricted data.

171   3.4     R ETRIEVAL B I -E NCODER
172
      Retrieval Bi-encoder is a system using dense retrieval with a bi-encoder for retrieving docu-
173   ments/information that are relevant for the search query. In a bi-encoder model, there are two
174   seperate encoders - one for encoding the input query, and another for encoding the candidate docu-
175   ments/information. The goal of bi-encoder is to try to map the meaning between two inputs together.
176   Therefore, Retrieval Bi-Encoders are commonly used in document retrieval, such as search engines.

238   4.3   C HAIN - OF -T HOUGHT DATASET C ONSTRUCTION WITH G EMINI API P ROMPT
239         E NGINEERING
240
241   To generate text-to-sql data from the Gemini API, besides receiving the SQL query, the response also
242   includes unnecessary expressions. Therefore, to extract the correct information from the response,
243   our team has specified in the prompt that the result must be enclosed within opening and closing tags.
244
      For example, the SQL query needs to be enclosed within the opening tag <sql> and the closing tag
      </sql>. Similarly, the inference Chain-of-Thought process must be enclosed within the opening
245
      tag <CoT> and the closing tag </CoT>. We defined 3 kinds of Chain-of-Thought reasonings:
246
247
             • Components Inference: is the process of simple inference, filling in the information from
248            the question into a basic SQL template appropriately.
249
250          • Query Type Inference: is a more advanced process where initially, the model must predict
251
               the type of query (e.g., JOIN IN, EXCEPT, NESTED query, etc.), and then complete each
               part with information from the question and synthesize it into an SQL query.
252
253          • Schema-based Inference: is the process where the model selects the most relevant tables
254            from the question among many tables in the database schema. Then it selects suitable
255            columns (fields) from the most relevant tables based on the information from the question.
256
               It synthesizes the information to generate an SQL query.
257
      To verify the correctness of CoT generation, our team employs Gemini to summarize the information
258
      from the CoT and generate a complete SQL query. We then compare the generated SQL query with
259
      the ground truth. If they match, we conclude that the CoT generation is correct; otherwise, we
260   conclude that it is incorrect.

      4.4   F EW-S HOT DATASET C ONSTRUCTION WITH K- MEANS A LGORITHM AND P HO BERT
263
            E MBEDDING
264
265   To achieve effective few-shot learning, the quality of the training data is crucial. This paper presents
266   a methodology for constructing a high-quality few-shot dataset for Vietnamese NLP tasks. The
267   dataset is designed to ensure high similarity between shots and target samples in terms of both query
268   structure and text content. Additionally, the dataset incorporates diversity in domains and sentence
269   lengths to enhance the generalization ability of the models. The dataset construction process involves
      the following steps:


                                                         5
270
             • Keyword List Creation: A comprehensive list of SQL keywords is created, ranging from
271            common keywords like SELECT, FROM, WHERE, and AGG to less frequent ones like
272            INTERSECT and EMPTY.
273
274          • TF-IDF Vector Embeddings: TF-IDF (Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency) is
275            employed to generate vector embeddings for SQL query sentences using the keyword list.
276
             • K-means Clustering: K-means clustering is applied to group similar SQL query sentences
277            based on their TF-IDF vector embeddings.
278
279          • Cluster Selection: The Elbow method and silhouette score are utilized to determine the
280            optimal number of clusters. In this case, 12 clusters are chosen. After clustering, the
281            properties of each cluster are examined. For instance, cluster 0 contains queries with the
282            pattern ”SELECT ... FROM ... WHERE ...”, while cluster 8 consists of queries with the
283
               pattern ”SELECT AGG(...) FROM ... WHERE ...”.
284          • PhoBERT Embeddings: For each pair of query and text within a cluster, PhoBERT, a Viet-
285            namese language model, is used to generate text embeddings. The goal is to group seman-
286            tically similar question text together using similarity search based on embeddings.
287
288          • Similarity Score Calculation: Within each cluster, 200 random samples are selected, con-
289
               sisting of a query-text pair and a target sample. Similarity scores are computed between the
               embeddings of the samples and the target sample. This approach reduces computational
290
               burden and execution time while ensuring diversity within shots and preserving similarity.
291
292          • Shot Selection: The five shots with the highest similarity scores are chosen for each sample.
293
294   In few-shot learning, it is crucial to maintain fairness during training and testing to evaluate the
295   model’s true performance. To achieve this, a fundamental principle must be adhered to: no shot
296   constructed from the training set should contain elements from the test set. Conversely, the test
297   set should exclusively comprise shots derived from the training set. This segregation ensures that
298   the model is not exposed to test data during training, preventing overfitting and providing a more
299   accurate assessment of its generalization ability.

      4.5   S CHEMA FILTERING DATASET C ONSTRUCTION WITH GPT-4 O API
302
303   To create a Retriever for schema filtering, we take the questions, queries and schemas from our pre-
304   trained dataset and use GPT-4o to extract the tables in the schema that are actually relevant to the
305   question and query, discarding the rest. We designed the prompt effectively so that the new schema
306   is answered by a special tag #mini-schema.
307
      To verify the correctness of schema filtering, we determined the similarity between ”Schema” (orig-
308
      inal schema) and ”MiniSchema” (filtered schema) using Term Frequency-Inverse Document Fre-
309   quency (TF-IDF). This allowed us to confirm that the schema filtering was accurate. The ”MiniS-
310   chema” typically has fewer SQL statements than the ”Schema.” A straightforward comparison of
311   similarity would yield a relatively low score, which is inaccurate in reflecting their relationship. So,
312   we first split the SQL statements in both ”Schema” and ”MiniSchema” based on the semicolon.
313   Then, for each SQL statement in the ”MiniSchema”, we calculate its similarity with each statement
314   in the ”schema.” We select the maximum similarity score for each comparison, forming a list of
315   these maximum values. Finally, the similarity score of the schema and mini schema is calculated by
316   taking the average of the list of maximum similarity scores. After testing, a similarity score greater
317
      than 0.75 ensures that SQL statements in mini schema also appear in ”Schema”.

      5       P ROPOSED A PPROACH
325
326
      In this study, we explore the selection and fine-tuning of a baseline model for code generation.
327
      We use CodeLLama for experiment. Due to resource constraints and training time and dataset
328   limitations, we opted for a fine-tuning approach using Qlora rather than full-parameter training of
329   the base model. To identify the optimal lora-rank, we conducted experiments and evaluated based
330   on training loss, validation loss, and metrics measuring the alignment between model output and
331   ground truth.
332
      After selecting the best rank, we used the two versions of pre-trained dataset to perform fine-tuning
333
      on the general text-to-sql task: full schema version and mini-schema version. For full schema
334   version, this a the standard pre-trained dataset that we have prepared by synthesizing from many
335   sources and using Gemini API to translate into Vietnamese. For mini-schema version, we have:
336
337              • Training phase: We have extracted the mini schema version from the original schema using
338                the GPT-4o API.
339              • Testing phase: We trained a bi-encoder retriever using a pre-trained mini-schema dataset.
340                The goal is that during inferencing, the schema of each sample will be passed through this
341                retriever layer first to filter out only the tables relevant to the user’s question. This approach
342                helps to reduce the amount of redundant information, noise and increases the accuracy of
343                SQL query generation.
344
345   Traditionally, retriever models are trained with natural language data. However, for this problem, to
346
      build a retriever which is robust in linking natural question input and SQL code output, we imple-
      mented a three-stage training process. Each stage is designed with increasing difficulty, helping the
347
      model gradually adapt and better recognize the correlation between text and SQL code:
348
349              • We chose BGE-M32 as our embedding model because it is a multi-linguality model that
350                has been trained with Vietnamese data and has achieved quite good benchmark results on
351                the embedding model rankings3 . To enhance the ability to represent Vietnamese language,
352                we continue to train this model on the Masked Language Model (MLM) task with a Viet-
353                namese dataset, combining Vietnamese data (general topics) and SQL code, with about
354                500k samples, training in 1 epoch.
355              • Phase 1 - Query to Translated query: User queries were translated into Vietnamese using
356                the GPT-4o API, then it will be mapped into set of sentence pairs. The goal is to help the
357                model initially learn the similarity between Vietnamese language and SQL code. Since
358                we have set of sentence pairs between SQL query and translated SQL query, which are
359                also called positive pairs, the Multiple Negatives Ranking loss (MNR loss) is a great loss
360                function to use.
361              • Phase 2 - Translated query to Mini-schema: Instead of making translated SQL queries and
362                mini schema as set of sentence pairs, translated SQL queries will be mapped with other
363                tables in schema, known as negative pairs. These three type of sentences will be combined
364                into triple set of anchor, positive, negative. The goal is to minimizes the distance between
365                translated SQL query and mini schema, while maximizes the distance between translated
366                SQL query and the redundant tables in same schema. The loss function used here is Triplet
367
                   loss.
368              • Phase 3 - Question to Mini-schema: The last phase would be to try to map the input to
369                output, which is user question to mini schema. Similar to phase two, we created a triple
370                set of anchor, positive, negative, where anchor is the user question. The Triplet loss is also
371                used in this phase.
372
      Once a well-fine-tuned base model was obtained on two versions of dataset, we further trained both
373
      versions using methods such as Few-shot, CoT, and Few-shot combined with CoT at both word and
374   syllable levels to guide the model to learn inference behaviors and visualize the input and output of
375   the task.

570   Based on our experiments, we have made the following key observations:
571
572                   • General training avoids overfitting: Pre-training on a general text-to-sql dataset and then
573
                        fine-tuning on specific case like the ViText2SQL dataset helps the model avoid overfitting.
                        This is because the model only learns from the ViText2SQL dataset for a single epoch,
574
                        compared to 10 epochs for the current state-of-the-art (SoTA) methods. Additionally, the
575
                        model learns better because it has already seen a variety of query forms that can be gener-
576                     ated through the general base.
577
578                   • Behavioral training should be based on the main task: Training for Fewshot and CoT
579                     scenarios should be based on the dataset related to the main task. This is because tasks like
580                     Fewshot and CoT only change the way model receives and generates answer, but the main
581
                        task that needs to be focused on is the prediction.
582                   • The Fewshot learning method is better than the Chain-of-Thought method in the Code
583                     Domain: This is an interesting observation from the experiments across all three metrics
584                     (exact matching, component matching, and execute matching) is that: CoT < SQL −
585                     direct < F ewShot + CoT < F ewShot (A < B tells that method A is better than method
586                     B on the respective metric). This can be explained as follows: While it is intuitive to assume
587                     that interpreting a generated query would be highly effective, as this is a code generation
588
                        model, interpretation can sometimes be inefficient, and the experiments have shown this to
                        be the case. Additionally, the few-shot method alone outperforms all other methods.
589
590                   • The MiniSchema appoarch can be consider as an effective method for Text-to-SQL
591                     domain: In table 4, although the MiniSchema method did not score high in exact match-
592                     ing, it gave quite impressive results in execute matching. Specifically, MiniSchema r128
593                     scored an average of 71.5% and MiniSchema Fewshot r129 scored an average of 70.9%,
                        just behind Fewshot r128.


                                                                 11
594
             • Prediction shots must be highly relevant: The effectiveness of the Fewshot learning
595            method is largely due to the construction of the examples during training. Queries are
596            trained with examples that have high semantic and query structure similarity, while ensur-
597            ing fairness by preventing the test set from appearing in any training set and vice versa.
598
             • Consistency in training and inference shots: We experimented with a progressive learn-
599            ing approach from easy to extra hard for Fewshot learning method. This approach involved
600            randomly selecting a shot size from 1 to 3 and ordering the training samples by length from
601            shortest to longest. While this method appeared promising, we observed during inference
602            that the model sometimes generated additional virtual shots, up to 2 or 3. This behavior
603            can be explained by the model’s need to visualize the input and output, requiring a fixed
604            shot size. Otherwise, during inference, the model would be uncertain about the number of
605            shots required for sample generation. Consequently, we switched to training and inference
606            with a fixed shot size of 3, achieving the best results on the ViText2SQL dataset.
607          • Configuring max length based on LoRA Rank: The choice of max length being 5000
608            instead of a larger number was motivated by several factors. Firstly, nearly 90% of the
609            data falls within this range. Secondly, LoRA was used for training, which has a maximum
610
               rank of 128. Using a larger max length would have made it impossible to recap enough
               information for learning, leading to performance degradation.

      7.3   L IMITATIONS
613
614   Our proposed approach faces several challenges and limitations that warrant further investigation
615   and improvement:
616
617          • Effective MiniSchema approach: We need to train a Retriever model that is highly ac-
618            curate in identifying the real relationships between user queries and tables in the schema.
619            Furthermore, choosing the appropriate threshold during evaluation also significantly affects
620
               the performance of a large language model.
621          • Complex query handling: The model’s accuracy drops when dealing with complex
622            queries involving multiple joins, aggregations, and grouping. This is attributed to the in-
623
               creased difficulty in capturing the intricate relationships and operations inherent in such
               queries.
624
625          • Data validation: The generated queries require additional validation to ensure that the
626            specified values match the actual data stored in the database. This validation step is crucial
627
               to guarantee the integrity and correctness of the retrieved results.
628          • Query optimization: The generated queries may not be optimized for execution time and
629            resource utilization. Developing heuristics to ensure query efficiency without compromis-
630
               ing accuracy is essential for practical applications.

634   Contributions In this study, our team present a large language model (LLM)-based Text2SQL
635   approach that not only outperforms the current state-of-the-art Vietnamese Text2SQL method by
636   over 23% in terms of exact match (EM) but also supports query interpretation during inference. We
637   also propose a potential approach of input schema filtering, which not only has a lot of potential for
638
      mining, but also helps reduce the number of input tokens, optimizing training and inference time.
      Additionally, we propose modifications to the Text2SQL evaluator to better assess the performance
639
      of Vietnamese queries.
640
641   Our LLM-based Text2SQL model is a multi-task model capable of generating text-to-SQL queries,
642   explaining queries, summarizing tables, and interpreting query results. The model is trained on
643   a newly constructed Vietnamese Text2SQL pre-trained dataset, comprising 240,000 samples. We
644
      employ various training strategies, including Fewshot, Chain-of-thought (CoT), Multi-CoT, Fewshot
      learning with CoT, and Schema filtering to train our LLM-based Text2SQL model.
645
646   In addition, our models are based on open-source models, so they have advantages in terms of safety
647   and security, avoiding user information disclosure compared to using third-party APIs in real-world
      deployment.
