009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) can absorb a massive amount of knowledge
012            through pretraining, but pretraining is inefficient for acquiring long-tailed or spe-
013            cialized facts. Therefore, fine-tuning on specialized or new knowledge that reflects
014            changes in the world has become popular, though it risks disrupting the model’s
015            original capabilities. We study this fragility in the context of continual memo-
016
               rization, where the model is trained on a small set of long-tail factoids (subject-
               relation-object associations) and must retain these factoids after multiple stages
017
               of subsequent training on other datasets. Continual memorization focuses on the
018
               specific challenge of retaining long-tail factoids, whereas general continual learn-
019            ing aims to maintain the LLM’s capabilities across a wide range of generic tasks
020            (e.g., reasoning, commonsense knowledge). Through extensive experiments, we
021            show that LLMs suffer from forgetting across a wide range of subsequent tasks,
022            and simple replay techniques do not fully prevent forgetting, especially when the
023            factoid datasets are trained in the later stages. We posit that there are two ways to
024            alleviate forgetting: 1) protect the memorization process as the model learns the
025            factoids, or 2) reduce interference from training in later stages. With this insight,
026            we develop an effective mitigation strategy: REMIX (Random and Generic Data
027
               Mixing). REMIX prevents forgetting by mixing generic data sampled from pre-
               training corpora or even randomly generated word sequences during each stage,
028
               despite being unrelated to the memorized factoids in the first stage. REMIX can
029
               recover performance from severe forgetting, often outperforming replay-based
030            methods that have access to the factoids from the first stage. We then analyze
031            how REMIX alters the learning process and find that successful forgetting pre-
032            vention is associated with a pattern: model stores factoids in earlier layers than
033            usual and diversifies the set of layers that store these factoids. The efficacy of
034            REMIX invites further investigation into the underlying dynamics of memoriza-
035            tion and forgetting, opening exciting possibilities for future research.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039   Large language models (LLMs) have shown a remarkable ability to absorb a massive amount of
040   knowledge through large-scale pretraining (Petroni et al., 2019; AlKhamissi et al., 2022; Cohen
041   et al., 2023). Despite their familiarity with common knowledge, they still struggle to capture the
042   long tail (Kandpal et al., 2023). Recent work explains that during the pretraining phase, each piece
043   of knowledge requires many exposures and diverse manifestations to be properly acquired (Allen-
044
      Zhu & Li, 2024a;b; Chang et al., 2024).
045   A straightforward alternative is to finetune the model on a small, domain-specific dataset. However,
046   finetuning on long-tail knowledge can cause unintentional harm by decreasing factuality and exac-
047   erbating hallucination (Kang et al., 2024; Gekhman et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Ghosal et al.,
048   2024). In this regard, the finetuning process bears some similarity to continual learning (McCloskey
049   & Cohen, 1989; Ratcliff, 1990), where one tries to successively train a model on a series of tasks
050
      without forgetting earlier ones. Prior research on continual learning in LLMs focuses on general
      capabilities such as reasoning (Luo et al., 2023a), or broad proxies like the language modeling loss
051
      over a general corpus (Yıldız et al., 2024).
052

083   How can an LLM prevent the forgetting of factoids? We intuit that this question may be approached
084   in two ways: 1) teach the model to protect learned knowledge better in the first stage, or 2) reducing
085   the interference of the second stage by manipulating the data distribution. Based on this hypothesis,
086   we propose REMIX (Random and Generic Data Mixing), which combines both approaches. First,

089

      learned knowledge. In the second stage, jointly learning the mixing data and the stage 2 data avoids
090

091
092   Our experiments demonstrate that REMIX is highly effective at helping the model retain learned
093   factoids: in the most severe case, REMIX increases post-phase 2 accuracy from 13.5% to 53.2%.
094   In comparison, replay can only reach 41.6% despite using 10% of the factoids from stage 1. These
095
      benefits are seen consistently across several choices of factoid and non-factoid tasks in stage 2. We

096
      studies. We find that REMIX teaches the model to both store facts in relatively earlier layers (as
097
      opposed to the unmixed case) and diversify their storage to many layers.

100

101

103          • We propose REMIX, a simple strategy that does not require access to the factoids from prior

106          • Through careful analysis and ablation studies, we find that REMIX operates by teaching

      2.1   P ROBLEM D EFINITION
111
112   Factoid vs non-factoid datasets. We define a factoid to be a triple (subject, relation, object). A
113   dataset D ∈ D in this paper is a set of (prompt, response) pairs. A factoid dataset D ∈ Dfact ⊂ D
114   is a set of factoids formatted as pairs (prompt ≡ (subject, relation), response ≡ object). Here, the
115   ≡ sign signifies that the relation instances are formatted using a template defined for each task (e.g.,
116   “The <X> of <Y> is” → <Z>). If D ∈ D \ Dfact , we call D a non-factoid dataset.
117   A language model is a set of parameters θ that define a mapping f (·; θ) : x 7→ P (y) from a prompt
118   x to a distribution over responses y. We will overload f (x; θ) to also refer to the response with
119   maximum probability. Given a model θ and dataset D, we denote by L(θ; D) ∈ R+ the loss and
120   A(θ; D) ∈ [0, 1] the average exact-match accuracy. We define a factoid x to be familiar to θ if
121   A(θ; {x}) = 1 and unfamiliar otherwise. An unfamiliar dataset consists entirely of unfamiliar facts.
122
123   Continual memorization. We now describe the setting of continual memorization, which consists
124   of two or more stages. We describe the setting with two stages below. Let DA ∈ Dfact be a factoid
125   dataset, and DB ∈ D be another dataset (factoid or non-factoid). In the first stage, a pretrained
126   model θ0 is trained on DA until convergence to obtain the trained model θA with near-zero loss
127
      L(θA ; DA ) ≈ 0 and accuracy A(θA ; DA ) ≈ 1. In the second stage, θA is further trained on DB

      In this paper, we consider the case where all factoid datasets (in the first as well as second stage—if

      applicable) are unfamiliar and we refer to them simply as factoid datasets. Typically, one observes

      2.2   C ONSTRUCTING FACTOID DATASETS
133
134   We consider a variety of (unfamiliar) factoid datasets in our experiments. These datasets are either 1)
135   constructed synthetically to ensure that they were not seen by the model θ0 during pretraining—such
136   as by generating random key-value mappings, or 2) by filtering factoid datasets to remove familiar
137   instances (more details in § B.4). We further describe the specific choice of datasets for the two
138   stages below.
139
140   Stage 1: Factoid dataset DA .
141
142
            1. Key-Value Recall (KVR). We construct the Key-Value Recall task by randomly generating
               2, 000 unique key-value pairs. Each key and value string contains 8 characters from the mix
143
               of alphabets and number digits. See Figure 1 for an example.
144
145         2. PopQA. PopQA (Mallen et al., 2023) is a collection of 14k questions about long-tail en-
146
               tities. It contains 16 diverse relationship types presented in a knowledge triplet format
               sourced from Wikidata (Vrandečić & Krötzsch, 2014). We randomly select 2, 000 unfamil-
147
               iar data points from the dataset for finetuning.
148
149         3. TriviaQA. TriviaQA (Joshi et al., 2017) is a question answering dataset containing over
150            650k question-answer pairs. Once again, we randomly select 2, 000 unfamiliar examples
151
               to fine-tune on.
152
153
      Stage 2: Dataset DB . We explore a wide range of datasets in stage 2 to reflect real-world appli-
      cation scenarios. Specifically, we consider two types of datasets: factoid and non-factoid. We chose
154
      this split because we want to see how the effect of stage 2 changes from a knowledge-intensive fac-
155
      toid dataset to, e.g., a general instruction tuning dataset. Additionally, domain-specific knowledge
156   and instruction-tuning data represent two of the most common types of data used for supervised
157   fine-tuning—a fact reflected in our selection of tasks. We explore:
158
159         1. Factoid datasets: LAMA (Petroni et al., 2019), Entity Questions (Sciavolino et al., 2021),
160            WebQA (Berant et al., 2013). In addition, we also explore adding new (and unfamiliar)
161            examples from the distribution of DA (i.e., the same task as in stage 1)–referred to as the
               “In-Domain” (ID) datasets in our results.

164               These datasets exemplify some common non-factoid datasets used for fine-tuning: chat,

240   Despite the shortcomings of replay, we make one key observation: when mixing only 10% of the
241   factoids used in stage 1, the post-stage 2 accuracy increases from no mixing at 40.1 to 83.9% for
242   non-factoid stage 2 (Table 4). This implies the existence of associations that were stored in model
243   weights but could not be retrieved effectively. It is then prudent to ask if these “hidden” associations
244   can be surfaced with a different choice of mixing data.
245   To answer this question, we propose Random and Generic Data Mixing (REMIX), a forgetting pre-
246   vention strategy that manipulates the memorization dynamics by mixing extra data into training.
247   The mixing data is sampled from either random word sequences or generic text such as pretraining
248   corpora, which has no overlap with the factoids aiming to memorize in stage 1. Figure 3 illus-
249   trates the intuition behind the mixing strategies. For the purpose of developing intuition, we take a
250   drastic simplification to assume the entire optimization is captured by the one-step gradient update.
251   The model θ0 first progresses to θA at stage 1 with update: θA = θ0 − η∇L(θ0 ; DA ). Similarly,
252   the model θA progresses to θB at stage 2: θB = θA − η∇L(θA ; DB ). In a regular forgetting
253
      scenario, the increase in loss after stage 2 is L(θB ; DA ) − L(θB ; DA ), and can be expanded into
      (θB − θA )T ∇L(θA ; DA ) + R = −η∇L(θ; DA )T ∇L(θ; DB ) + R where R is the higher-order
254
      terms. The first term contributes to forgetting when the two gradients ∇L(θ; DA ) and ∇L(θ; DB )
255
      point to opposing directions. With REMIX, DM is mixed into the two stages to prevent forgetting.

      6                         R ELATED W ORK
462
463
      Continual learning. Continual learning has been the subject of investigation since early research
464
      on connectionist models, which identified catastrophic forgetting as a fundamental challenge (Mc-
465
      Closkey & Cohen, 1989; Ratcliff, 1990). Many methods have proposed for mitigating forgetting
466   in continual learning. The simplest approach involves maintaining a memory of examples from
467   previous tasks and replaying them during subsequent training (e.g. Robins, 1995; Chaudhry et al.,
468   2019; Shin et al., 2017). Other methods involve regularization techniques that preserve important
469   weights (e.g. Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Ke et al., 2023) or reduce the divergence between model
470   predictions (Li & Hoiem, 2017). One group of methods project the gradient for a new task to
471   be orthogonal to the gradients from previous tasks, with the aim of reducing interference between
472   tasks (Lopez-Paz & Ranzato, 2017; Farajtabar et al., 2020). Theoretical analyses (Bennani et al.,
473   2020; Doan et al., 2021) have established that these gradient projection methods mitigate forget-
474
      ting in the Neural Tangent Kernel regime (Jacot et al., 2018). A number of studies have attempted
      to characterize the relationship between task similarity and forgetting, empirically and theoreti-
475
      cally (Ramasesh et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2021; Evron et al., 2022). See Wang et al. (2023) for a
476
      more comprehensive overview. In this paper, we restrict the class of approaches to those that do not
477   change model weights, e.g., via regularization.
478
479
      Memorization and forgetting in LLMs. In the context of LLMs, many prior works have inves-
480   tigated the factors that influence memorization during pre-training (Tirumala et al., 2022; Carlini
481   et al., 2023; Mallen et al., 2023; Jagielski et al., 2023). In particular, prior work has observed that
482   instruction tuning can lead to some degradation on general NLP tasks, which has been called an
483   “alignment tax” (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022). Ouyang et al. (2022) find that this align-
484   ment tax can be partly mitigated by mixing pre-training data into the alignment data, and Luo et al.
485   (2023a) find that LLMs forget less when the instruction-tuning data is more diverse. Kotha et al.
      (2024) find that fine-tuning LLMs leads to bigger performance degradation on tasks that are more


                                                                                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      similar to the fine-tuning task (as measured by likelihood under the learned fine-tuning distribution).
487   See Shi et al. (2024) and Wu et al. (2024) for more extensive surveys of continual learning in the
488   context of LLMs.
489
490   Fine-tuning on unfamiliar facts. Our work builds on several recent observations about the effect
491   of fine-tuning an LLM on unfamiliar facts. Kang et al. (2024) find that fine-tuning LLMs on un-
492   familiar examples (questions that the LLM cannot answer correctly via few-shot prompting) lead
493   the model to “hallucinate” plausible-sounding but incorrect answers to unfamiliar test examples.
494   Similarly, Gekhman et al. (2024) find that unknown examples take longer to learn, and learning
495   unknown examples leads to more hallucination. Ghosal et al. (2024) present a conceptual model
496   for this phenomenon, suggesting that fine-tuning on facts that are weakly represented in the model’s
497
      weights can lead the model to pay less attention to entities in the query and instead hallucinate an
      incorrect response. These studies highlight the difficulty of encoding new facts into a model during
498
      fine-tuning. Yang et al. (2024) propose to address this challenge by generating synthetic data for
499
      continual pretraining, with the goal of acquiring new knowledge given only a small fine-tuning cor-
500   pus. This approach can be motivated by mechanistic studies (Allen-Zhu & Li, 2024a;b), which have
501   found that knowledge extraction is possibly only when information appears in diverse forms in the
502   training data (e.g. paraphrases), which leads models to encode information more effectively for later
503   extraction. In contrast, our investigation focuses on the effects of mixing in generic or randomly
504   generated data.
505
506   Model editing and unlearning. Our work is also related to a line of research aimed at explicitly
507   modifying facts that are encoded in an LLM—for example, to update information about entities to
508   reflect changes in the world (e.g. Zhu et al., 2020; Mitchell et al., 2022; Meng et al., 2022; 2023).
509   Studies have shown that these methods can update individual facts, but do not lead to consistent
510
      changes about all of the implications of these updates (Zhong et al., 2023; Cohen et al., 2024). A
      related line of work has investigated whether specific information can be deliberately removed, or
511
      “unlearned,” from neural networks (e.g. Graves et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023). Our focus in this
512
      paper is on introducing new knowledge while retaining existing knowledge, rather than on modifying
513   or erasing existing knowledge.

516   7   C ONCLUSION
517
518
      In this paper, we formalize finetuning a language model with factual knowledge in the continual
      memorization framework. In contrast to continual learning, which focuses on general capability,
519
      we focus on the specific challenges inherent to finetuning on long-tail factoids. Through careful
520
      experiments, we establish that the long-tail factoid data is the largest culprit in making a model forget
521   memorized factoids from previous stages of finetuning. We then evaluate experience replay methods
522   that are often used in continual learning and find that they do not satisfactorily revive forgotten
523   factoids. To address the issue of forgetting, we propose a surprising yet effective strategy REMIX.
524   By mixing random word sequences or generic pretraining data into different stages of training,
525   REMIX outperforms replay-based methods in our experiments despite not using any factoids from
526   the original set in its mixing process. Finally, we analyze REMIX using Logit Lens and ablation
527   studies to find that it teaches the model to reduce inter-fact interference by changing where it stores
528   facts. REMIX opens up many new directions for future research. For example, future work may
529
      explore REMIX and similar approaches to ensure that safety-tuning is not easily undone by further
      finetuning. Its efficacy also poses interesting questions about the dynamics of memorization in
530
      language models, which we are excited to see investigated in future work.
