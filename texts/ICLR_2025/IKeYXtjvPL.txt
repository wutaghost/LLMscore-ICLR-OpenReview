009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Segment anything model (SAM) and its variants have recently shown promising
012            performance as foundation models. However, existing SAM-based models can
013            only handle scenarios seen during training, and usually suffer unstable perfor-
014            mance when transferring to real-world unseen data, such as low-light, rainy or
015            blurred images, which is crucial for applications such as autopilot. Therefore,
016            adapting SAM-based models for real-world degradation while not impairing its
017            original ability remains an open challenge. In this work, we propose a novel gated
018
               Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) structure, called RouGE, to improve the robustness of
               SAM-based models. Specifically, RouGE uses multiple lightweight probability
019
               gates to decompose complex real-world image conditions and judge whether the
020
               feature needs to be adjusted as well as to what extent the adjustment needs to be
021            done, then handle them differently with a set of low-rank experts. During the in-
022            ference stage, RouGE processes input images in a completely blind manner thus
023            improving the model’s performance in real-world scenarios. Extensive experi-
024            ments demonstrate that RouGE consistently achieves state-of-the-art results on
025            both degraded and clean images compared with other methods while tuning only
026            1.5% of parameters.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Segment anything model (SAM) (Kirillov et al., 2023; Ravi et al., 2024) and its variants have re-
032   cently shown impressive performance and have been widely applied in various downstream appli-
033
      cations, e.g., autopilot and medical image segmentation. However, existing SAM-based methods
      are usually trained on clean images without degradation. Given that degradations such as low light,
034
      rain and blur are almost unavoidable in real-world scenarios, existing models consequently suffer
035
      unstable performance when transferring to real-world unseen data. Therefore, how to improve the
036   robustness of SAM-based models to deal with real-world diverse scenarios poses an open challenge.
037
038   To allow for a robust SAM-based model for real-world applications, several methods have been ex-
039
      plored. For instance, one simple solution may use a two-step workflow with another image restora-
      tion model (Li et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024a; Potlapalli et al., 2023) before segmentation to remove
040
      undesired degradations. Such methods rely heavily on the reconstruction results of pretrained image
041
      restoration models and cannot handle various types of degradation (e.g., noise, blur or rain). More
042   importantly, restored images may not benefit high-level visual tasks as they are initially designed for
043   human eyes and may generate artifacts which has negative impacts on downstream tasks (Cui et al.,
044   2021; Chen et al., 2024). To relieve such issues, other methods involve fine-tuning segmentation
045   models that are tailored for specific degradation (Cui et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2023b). However,
046   fine-tuning on specific degradation requires prior knowledge of the degradation type of input im-
047   age, which is hard to achieve in real-world applications. Recently, Chen et al. (2024) proposed
048   RobustSAM using a post-processing module to handle real degradation, but still suffers from heavy
049   computational cost by increasing the model’s parameters by about 32%.
050   Despite attempts have been made to obtain robust SAM models, current methods still face the fol-
051   lowing challenges. First, fine-tuning the foundation model can inherently degrade its original per-
052   formance and lead to catastrophic forgetting problems. Second, the diversity of real-world degrada-
053   tion leads to significant variations in degradation types and a robust model needs to handle various
      degradation and clean images in a completely blind manner. Third, manually labeled real-world

      To maintain the original performance of the model, a good way is to minimize changes applied to
077
      the pretrained model weights with the Parameter Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) techniques. By fine-
078
      tuning with almost all model parameters frozen, PEFT methods can adapt models to new domains
079   while preserving the model’s generalization ability at marginal cost and are widely used in both
080   vision and natural language fields (Houlsby et al., 2019; Jie & Deng, 2022; Chen et al., 2022; Pfeiffer
081   et al., 2020a; Wang et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2024). To tackle diverse data types, a natural approach
082   is to break down complex tasks into multiple simpler tasks. By using mixture-of-expert (MoE)-like
083   methods, we can decompose the complexity of real-world environments into multiple conditions,
084   enabling us to use a set of smaller modules to solve the complex problem. Considering the third
085   point, training with unlabeled images can better reduce the challenges of industrial applications.
086   Based on the above observations, we introduce a novel module RouGE, a plug-in Robustness-Uplift
087   module using Gated Experts to perform differentiated processing on degraded and non-degraded
088   inputs within a pretrained network. RouGE module comprises lightweight multiple probability gates
089   and their corresponding low-rank experts (including lazy and trainable experts), to efficiently select
090   suitable experts for input data and perform effective combined processing. The probability gates
091   provide the model with interpretable classification capabilities to handle blind input, while the design
092   of lazy and trainable experts endows the module with the ability to not disturb the distribution of the
093
      model’s original parameters. Meanwhile, we propose an unsupervised imitation learning method
      designed for RouGE. We use unlabeled clean images to synthesize degraded images and let the
094
      model learn to narrow the gap between them. Through imitation learning, RouGE can be trained
095
      using a small amount of unlabeled images (approximately 2k images for each type of degradation),
096   making it more suitable for industrial applications.
097
098   The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows: (i) We propose RouGE, a PEFT
099
      module designed for making pretrained less-robust SAM-based model a robust all-in-one model
      with marginal cost. The design of RouGE ensures the capability to maintain the model’s origi-
100
      nal output features unchanged and only conduct selective feature modifications, thus avoiding the
101
      catastrophic forgetting issue associated with fine-tuning. (ii) We propose an unsupervised imita-
102   tion learning approach, utilizing unlabeled images and synthesized degraded images for training,
103   thereby circumventing the problem of missing labeled data and facilitating easier training of ro-
104   bust models for industrial applications. (iii) Our comprehensive experiments demonstrate that the
105   RouGE method significantly enhances model robustness. Compared to the original model, RouGE
106   can improve segmentation accuracy for degraded inputs by 4-13% in mAP with hardly any negative
107   effects on results for non-degraded inputs. RouGE also outperforms other fine-tuning methods by a
      significant margin, even with a low trainable parameter ratio (about 1.56%).

      2.1   SAM AND ITS VARIANTS
111
112
      Since the introduction of SAM, there has been a continuous emergence of derivative works (Zhang
113   et al., 2023b; Zhang & Jiao, 2023). Researchers in the field of medical image segmentation are
114   focused on fine-tuning SAM for high-quality medical image segmentation tasks (Zhang et al., 2023d;
115   Mohapatra et al., 2023; de Oliveira et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2023; Gao
116   et al., 2023). In addition, there is a wealth of work fine-tuning SAM to adapt to other types of
117   segmentation tasks such as satellite image segmentation (Ren et al., 2024), shadow Detection (Jie
118   & Zhang, 2023; Chen et al., 2023c), marine animal segmentation (Zhang et al., 2024), and so on
119   (He et al., 2024; Williams et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2023). Considering the vast parameter count of
120   SAM, in real-world applications, compressed SAM models capable of real-time segmentation hold
121
      a higher value. Through knowledge distillation and model pruning, (Zhang et al., 2023a; Zhao et al.,
      2023; Xiong et al., 2023) have successfully compressed SAM models to a fraction of their original
122
      size. The EfficientSAM (Xiong et al., 2023) uses the MEA (He et al., 2022) method to distillate
123
      the pretrained SAM model and retains the performance of the SAM model most comprehensively.
124   Recently, RobustSAM (Chen et al., 2024) has similarly noted the sensitivity of SAM to real-world
125   degradation and used a post-processing module to handle this problem. However, their model still
126   has a high learnable parameter count, which demands significant computational resources for both
127   inference and training. By employing PEFT methods, we can effectively leverage the performance
128   of the base model to achieve efficient model adjustments, thereby reducing computational costs.
129
130   2.2   PARAMETER E FFICIENT F INE - TUNING
131
132   The PEFT method has been widely applied to SAM-based models (Sahay & Savakis, 2024), primar-
133   ily fall into prompt tuning (Wang et al., 2024b; Jia et al., 2022), adapter-like tuning (Houlsby et al.,
134   2019; Jie & Deng, 2022; Chen et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2020; Pfeiffer et al., 2020a; Wang et al.,
135   2022; Yu et al., 2024), partial tuning (Basu et al., 2024; Zaken et al., 2021) and reparameterization
136   fine-tuning (Jie & Deng, 2023; Lian et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2021) categories. However, the primary
137   application scenario of PEFT methods is fine-tuning models for downstream tasks and the original
138
      model’s feature distribution would undergo significant disruption. To mitigate this issue, knowledge
      injection (Zhang et al., 2023e;c; Wang et al., 2020) and MoE-based (Shazeer et al., 2017; Kim et al.,
139
      2020; Yu et al., 2021) PEFT methods have been proposed. However, the former (Wang et al., 2020)
140
      requires explicit task annotations, while the latter (Wang et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022) yields sub-
141   optimal results due to the lack of clear classification methods. These methods all fail to effectively
142   enhance robustness.

      3.2              ROBUSTNESS -U PLIFT G ATED E XPERTS
191
192   Fine-tuning pretrained models often leads to parameter drift, potentially resulting in models that
193   only achieve domain adaptation rather than robustness improvement. Adapting the model to multiple
194   domains simultaneously is the key point to achieving targeted robustness enhancement. Designs like
195   that of Adapter-Hub (Poth et al., 2023; Pfeiffer et al., 2020b) can provide manual domain switching
196   for models. From this, we conceive integrating multiple adapters into a single module, enabling the
197   module to learn automatic switching, thereby achieving performance improvements across multiple
198   domains. Therefore, we propose RouGE model and its unsupervised training process. RouGE
199
      utilizes lightweight probability gates within the module to control the weights of various experts,
      achieving diverse processing for different images by assigning corresponding expert proportions.
200
201
202   3.2.1              OVERALL F RAMEWORK
203
      The design of the RouGE model follows three key principles: (i) Automatically differentiate differ-
204   ent inputs without type labels; (ii) The module should have the ability to “do nothing” to ensure that
205   the original performance of the base model remains undisturbed; (iii) Utilize a minimal number of
206   trainable parameters to ensure the module’s parameter efficiency.
207
      To achieve the above three objectives, we present a module with multiple independent probability
208
      gates, lazy expert (Expert 0), and trainable experts (Expert i), as shown in Figure 4. During both
209
      training and inference stages, RouGE takes image features Ft and input features xt as inputs. Ft are
210   fed into the probability gates to obtain the proportions of each expert. xt are passed into each expert,
211   and the predicted results generated by experts are multiplied by their respective proportions before
212   being summed up and outputted. During the training process, synthetic image pairs of degraded and
213   clean images are used, aiming to enhance model robustness by aligning the model predictions of
214   degraded images with those of clean images. During the inference phase, arbitrary types of images
215   can be used without the need to distinguish whether they are degraded images or not. Next, we will
      proceed to introduce each module separately.

243   3.2.2     P ROBABILITY G ATES
244
      The role of the probability gates is to distinguish between different types of input data and select the
245
      appropriate experts for processing. To reduce the noise in gate decisions, probability gates only take
246
      the image features Ft as input. In practical applications, we use global Ft extracted from the input
247   image and the same Ft are used throughout the inference process for a single image. The probabil-
248   ities generated by the probability gates are processed to serve as the output proportion parameters
249   for each expert. The gate employs a lightweight structure of dual-layer fully connected layers, out-
250   putting a floating-point number between zero and one representing the acceptance probability.
251
      To maintain the stability of the output features, the outputs of gates are concatenated and undergo
252
      a softmax function. In the case of having n experts, the number of gates is n − 1. The acceptance
253   probability of gate i is Gi . The total rejection probability G0 is obtained by summing up the rejec-
254   tion probabilities of all gates and dividing by the total number of gates. After undergoing softmax
255   processing, the sum of all probabilities equals one, meaning the proportions of each expert sum up
256   to one. So we define the output of the probability gates as probability vector G.

293
294   To better train the lightweight probability gate and experts and mitigate the absence of labeled de-
295   graded image data, we employ a method based on imitation learning to minimize noise during
296   training. In the field of image restoration, a significant amount of artificially synthesized degraded
297   datasets (such as rainy or foggy images) are proposed for restoration models. These datasets include
298   pairs of clean images and degraded images generated by adding specific types of degradation. Our
299
      training method precisely leverages these datasets to teach the model how to “ignore” these degra-
      dations. Since the content in the images is consistent, the segmentation of the same object should
300
      yield identical ground truth results.

      4.1     E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
325
326
      Datasets. We obtained the Rain200L (Yang et al., 2017), DDN (Fu et al., 2017), GoPro (Nah et al.,
327
      2017), LIS (Chen et al., 2023a), and Snow100k (Liu et al., 2018) datasets from the image restoration
328   domain and split them to serve as training and testing data for the model. Additionally, we used
329   CityRain and CityFoggy (Cordts et al., 2015; 2016), as additional test data for experimentation.
330   Among these, Rain200L and DDN consist of rainy weathered image pairs created using different
331   methods, GoPro comprises dynamic blurry image pairs, LIS includes low-light image pairs, and
332   Snow100k consists of snowy weathered image pairs.
333
      All these datasets include artificially synthesized degraded images of varying degrees as well as
334   their original clean images. Since the segment anything task requires a point prompt or bounding
335   box as input, we selected the clean images from all image pairs and obtained bounding boxes in
336   the images using a state-of-the-art object detection model. Subsequently, we inputted the bounding
337   boxes and clean images into the Segment Anything model (Kirillov et al., 2023) to obtain the ground
338   truth masks for the quantitative test. To avoid selecting points that are off-center from the object, we
339   performed an erosion operation on the ground truth mask and then randomly selected a point as the
340   point prompt. A set of image pairs uses the same point prompt and ground truth mask because the
341   non-noise information on the image pairs is identical. We utilized CLIP’s image encoder (Radford
342
      et al., 2021) as the image feature extractor in the experiment and pre-extracted image features for
      each in. The ablation study on image feature selection is in Appendix 10.
343
344   Pretrained backbone. We adopt the EfficientSAM-Ti (Xiong et al., 2023) as the backbone model
345   and we utilized the pretrained parameters provided by the authors of EfficientSAM. The model
346   comprises a transformer-based image encoder and a mask decoder. It takes the input image and
347   point prompt and outputs the mask of the object pointed to by that point on the image.
348   baseline models. We selected 8 baseline models from 4 categories of methods for comparative ex-
349   periments and the replicated RobustSAM on the EfficientSAM-Ti. Categorized by type, we selected
350   (i) full fine-tuning. (ii) adapter-based: Adapter (Houlsby et al., 2019), Convpass (Jie & Deng, 2022),
351   Adaptformer (Chen et al., 2022). (iii) partial fine-tuning: LN (Basu et al., 2024), Bitfit (Zaken et al.,
352   2021). (iv) mixture-of-adapter: Adamix (Wang et al., 2022), AdapterFusion (Pfeiffer et al., 2020a)
353   (v) RobustSAM (Chen et al., 2024): employing AMFG-F, AOTG, and ROT on base model, as our
354
      baseline methods. Additionally, while the RobustSAM paper employed supervised learning, we
      opted for unsupervised learning to ensure fairness.
355
356   Hyperparameter settings. In all experiments, we use AdamW as the optimizer with lr = 1e −
357   4, weightdecay = 5e − 2. We use the combination of dice loss and sigmoid focal loss as the loss
358   function and each accounts for 50%. In the absence of specific instructions, we set the number
359   of experts in the RouGE model to 6. Additionally, the RouGE model is only added to the last
360
      two layers of the transformer block in the image encoder. During training, we utilized a NVIDIA
      GeForce RTX 3090 GPU and the five datasets were alternated sequentially to train a robust model
361
      capable of handling five types of degradation simultaneously.

      5     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we propose a plugin robustness enhancement module, RouGE, which can enhance the
533
      robustness of pretrained SAM-based models at marginal cost. Experiments conducted both within
534   and outside the domain demonstrate RouGE’s capability to selectively modify degraded images
535   while preserving the original performance of the model for clean images. Compared with existing
536   PEFT methods and reproduction of RobustSAM, RouGE demonstrates superiority in both robust-
537   ness enhancement capability and efficiency in terms of trainable parameters. RouGE model exhibits
538   high versatility, as it can be seamlessly integrated into any transformer block. This renders it with
539   the potential to be applied across various types of visual models. In the future, we will continue to
      explore the application of RouGE in a broader range of visual models and tasks.

      A.1   H OW MANY EXPERTS AND ROU GE S ARE NEEDED ?
743
744   The number of experts and the number of RouGE directly affect the trainable parameter count.
745   Usually, fine-tuned models of like adapter are inserted into each transformer block. However, the
746   RouGE model is more of a widening rather than a deepening model, so inserting it into all trans-
747   former blocks may not necessarily be advantageous. The base model’s has 11 transformer layers,
748   and we experimented with inserting the RouGE model starting from ith layer, as shown in 7. Train-
749   ing was conducted for 10 epochs in all cases. The experimental results indicate that the RouGE
750   model is not suitable for being added to all blocks but rather for being added to the final few lay-
751   ers. In our previous experiments, we used the optimal configuration, adding RouGE to the last two
752
      layers.
753   For the number of experts, we conducted a comparative experiment as shown in 8.When the number
754   of experts degrades to 2, the RouGE model becomes an adapter that can adjust fusion coefficients.
755   As the number of experts increases, the effectiveness of RouGE improves. After balancing the
      parameter count and effectiveness, we chose N = 6 as the experimental hyperparameters setting.
