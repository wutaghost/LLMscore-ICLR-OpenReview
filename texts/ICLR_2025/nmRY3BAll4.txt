                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010            Normalization layers are ubiquitous in modern neural networks and have long been
011            considered essential. In this work, we demonstrate that we can achieve strong
012
               performance without them, using a remarkably simple technique. We introduce
               Dynamic Tanh (DyT), an element-wise operation: DyT(x) = tanh(αx), as a
013
               drop-in replacement to normalization layers (e.g., layer normalization). DyT
014
               is directly inspired by the simple observation that normalization layers produce
015            tanh-like, S-shaped curves for their input-output mappings. With DyT, networks
016            without normalization layers could match or exceed the performance of their
017            normalization counterparts, while keeping all other training hyperparameters intact.
018            Experiments across diverse settings validate this, ranging from recognition to
019            generation, ConvNets to LLMs, and supervised to self-supervised learning. Our
020            findings challenge the conventional understanding that normalization layers are
021            indispensable, and provide new insights into their workings.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026
027
      Over the past decade, normalization layers have solified their positions as one of the most fundamental
      components of modern neural networks. It all traces back to Batch Normalization (Ioffe & Szegedy,
028
      2015), which enabled drastically faster and better convergence on visual recognition models, and then
029
      quickly gained momentum. Since then, many variants for different network architectures or domains
030   have been proposed (Ba et al., 2016; Ulyanov et al., 2016; Wu & He, 2018; Zhang & Sennrich, 2019).
031   Today, virtually all modern networks use normalization layers, with Layer Normalization (LN) (Ba
032   et al., 2016) being one of the most popular, particularly in Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017).
033
      The widespread adoption of normalization layers is largely driven by their empirical benefits in
034
      optimization (Santurkar et al., 2018; Bjorck et al., 2018). In addition to achieving lower final loss, they
035
      help accelerate and stabilize convergence. As neural networks become wider and deeper, this necessity
036   becomes ever more critical (Brock et al., 2021a; Brody et al., 2023). Consequently, normalization
037   layers are widely regarded as crucial, if not indispensable, for the effective training of deep neural
038   networks. This belief is subtly evidenced by the fact that, in recent years, novel architectures often
039   seek to replace self-attention or convolution layers, but mostly keep the normalization layers in place.
040
      In this paper, we challenge this belief by introducing a simple alternative to normalization for deep
041
      networks. Our approach begins with the observation that layer normalization layers map their inputs
042   to outputs with tanh-like, S-shaped curves, dynamically scaling them and then squashing the extreme
043   values. Inspired by this insight, we propose an element-wise operation termed Dynamic Tanh (DyT),
044   defined as: DyT(x) = tanh(αx), where α is a learnable parameter. This operation aims to emulate
045   the behavior of layer normalization by learning an appropriate scaling factor through α and squashing
046   extreme values via the bounded tanh function. Notably, unlike normalization layers, it achieves both
047   effects without the need to compute activation statistics.
048   By replacing normalization layers with DyT in architectures such as language and vision Trans-
049   formers (Vaswani et al., 2017; Dosovitskiy et al., 2020), our empirical studies demonstrate that DyT
050   can maintain training stability and achieve high final performance, across a wide range of settings.
051   Employing DyT is straightforward for any existing architectures, and does not require additional
052   hyperparameter tuning for training. DyT challenges the notion that normalization layers are indis-
053   pensable for deep neural networks, and provides new insights into the properties of normalization
      layers, complementing existing theoretical understanding on normalization.

      2.1   W HAT D O N ORMALIZATION L AYERS D O ?
057
058   We first empirically study the behaviors of normalization layers in trained networks. For this analysis,
059   we take a trained Vision Transformer model (ViT-B) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) on ImageNet-1K
060   (Deng et al., 2009), and a trained wav2vec 2.0 Large model (Baevski et al., 2020) on LibriSpeech
061   (Panayotov et al., 2015). Both models use Layer Normalization (LN).
062
      For both trained networks, we sample a mini-batch of input data and do a standard forward pass
063   through the network. We then measure the input and output for the norm layers, i.e., tensors
064   immediately before and after the normalization operation, excluding the learnable scaling and shifting
065   transformations inside these layers. Since normalization preserves the dimensions of the input tensor,
066   we can establish a one-to-one correspondence between the input and output tensor elements, allowing
067   for a direct visualization of their relationship.
068
      For both models, in earlier norm layers (the first 30%-40% layers), we find this input-output relation-
069   ship to be mostly linear, resembling a straight line in an x-y plot. For deeper layers where we make
070   more intriguing observations, the plots for four layers are shown in Figure 1 below.

092   A striking first observation is that these curves’ shapes highly resemble full or partial S-shaped
093   curves represented by a tanh function. One might expect LN layers linearly transforms the input
094   tensor, as subtracting means and dividing by stds are linear operations. In fact, LN normalizes in
095   a per-token manner, only linearly transforming each token’s activations. As tokens have different
096   mean and variance values, the linearity does not hold collectively on all activations of the input tensor.
097   Nonetheless, at first sight, it is still surprising to us that the actual non-linear transformation is highly
098
      similar to a scaled tanh-function.
099   For such an S-shaped curve, we note that the central part, represented by points with x values close
100   to zero, is still mostly in a linear shape. Most points (∼99%) fall in this linear range. However, there
101   are still many points that clearly fall out of this range, which are considered to have “extreme” values,
102   e.g. those with x larger than 100 or smaller than -100. For these values, norm layers’ main effect is to
103   squash them into less extreme values, more in line with the majority of points. This is the part where
104
      norm layers could not approximated by a simple affine transformation layer. We hypothesize this
      squashing effect on extreme values is what makes norm layers important and indispensable.
105

107   values in such a non-linear fashion? To understand this, we visualize the points grouped by tokens and
      channels respectively. This is plotted in Figure 2, by taking the third subplot for ViT from Figure 1,

139   2.2   DYNAMIC TANH (DY T) L AYERS
140
141   Inspired by the similarity between the shapes of normalization layers and a scaled tanh function, we
142   propose Dynamic Tanh (DyT) as an alternative to norm layers. Given an input tensor x, a DyT layer
143   is defined as follows:
144                                     DyT(x) = γ ∗ tanh(αx) + β                                       (1)
145
146
      α is a learnable scalar parameter that allows scaling the input dynamically based on its range,
      accounting for varying x scales in Figure 1. γ and β are learnable, per-channel vector parameters,
147
      the same as those used in all normalization layers—they allow the output to scale back to any scales.
148
      They sometimes could be considered a separate affine layer; for our purposes, we consider them to be
149   part of the DyT layer, just like how normalization layers also include them.
150
151   DyT is not a new type of normalization layer, as it operates on each input element from a tensor
152
      independently during a forward pass, without computing statistics or other types of aggregations. It
      does, however, preserve the effect of norm layers in squashing the extreme values in a non-linear
153
      fashion, while almost linearly transforming the very central parts of the input.
154
155   Integrating DyT layers into an existing architecture is straightforward: one DyT layer replaces every
156   normalization layer (e.g., LN). Though DyT may look like or be considered an activation function,
157   this study only uses it to replace normalization layers, without altering any parts of the activation
158
      functions in the original architectures, like GELU or ReLU. All other parts of networks also remain
      intact. We also observe there is no need to tune the training hyperparameters designed for the original
159
      architectures, for DyT to perform well.
160
161   We find initializing αs to 1 to be sufficient in almost all cases, except training large LLMs. We always
      simply initialize γ to an all-one vector, and β to an all-zero vector following normalization layers.

      However, when training very wide models in a under-training regime (e.g., in training LLMs), a
163   smaller initial value for α (e.g., 0.2) could be more helpful. In an over-training regime, where models
164   are trained for many epochs, initializing α differently from 1 only affects convergence speed without
165   much impact on final performance. We provide detailed analysis on α initialization in Section 4.2.

168   3   E XPERIMENTS
169
170   We conduct experiments across four different modalities: image, language, audio, and DNA sequences,
171   to demonstrate the effectiveness of DyT-based normalization-free networks. In each experiment,
172   we replace the normalization layers in the original architectures with DyT layers, and then train
173   and evaluate both versions of the models. One of our objectives is to showcase that DyT-based
174   models could obtain comparable performance without significant changes to the training recipe and
175   hyperparameters. Therefore, in all experiments, we use the same hyperparameters that were used for
176   the normalized models. The only exception is the language models, where we add a learnable scalar
177
      parameter after the word embedding layer and adjust the initial value of α in all DyT layers. However,
      we still keep all other hyperparameters the same. For instructions on reproducing our experiments,
178
      please refer to Appendix A.

308   4.1   U NDERSTANDING THE ROLE OF α
309
310   Correlation between final α and 1/std of activation. We conducted further analysis on the role of
311   α for pretrained networks. The investigation reveals that α adapts to learn the inverse of the standard
312   deviation of the input activations. Figure 3 illustrates this relationship, demonstrating that the values
313   of α across different DyT layers correlate with the inverse of the standard deviation of the layer inputs
314
      for two different models. This indicates that α could help manage larger activations by scaling them
      down, effectively preventing saturation.
315
316   Increasing activation std with depth. Moreover, we observe that deeper layers tend to have larger
317   standard deviations in their input activations. Such an increasing standard deviation with depth is
318   potentially an important feature of deep residual networks, as pointed out by Brock et al. (2021a).
319   This could also explain why the static hyperbolic tangent function does not perform as well as DyT,
320
      as it cannot adapt to the changing activation distributions across layers.
321   Dynamic adaptation of α during training. We also observe that the learned value of α closely
322   tracks the standard deviation of activations throughout training. As shown in Figure 4, the inverse of
323   α fluctuates in response to changes in activation standard deviation, further supporting the dynamic
      role of α in maintaining stable and effective training.

                For the initialization of the learnable scalar α, we find that setting it to 1 works well in most cases.
344
                While adjusting the initial value of α can lead to faster early convergence, this advantage typically
345
                does not carry over to the later stages of training. However, in LLMs, the proper initialization of α
346             proves to be important, as early improvements tend to influence the final performance. We suspect
347             this difference arises because language modeling often operates in an underfitting regime, unlike
348             other tasks where overfitting is a dominant issue.
349
                Learnable scaling after input embeddings. In our implementation of LLaMA (Touvron et al.,
350
                2023a;b) models with DyT, we introduce
                                                    √          a learnable scaling scalar immediately after the word
351
                embedding layers, initialized to dwidth , where dwidth represents the model’s hidden dimension.
352             Without this scaling scalar, training struggled to progress meaningfully in the early stages. The
353             underlying issue could be traced to the small magnitude of activations at the start of training (around
354             0.02), and it is mainly caused by the small magnitude outputs of the word embedding layers at

356             more quickly. This approach is similar√to the original Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017),
357             which uses a fixed scaling parameter dwidth at the start.
358             Notably, this issue primarily exists in models with embeddings as inputs. In contrast, models that start
359             with linear or convolutional layers typically produce outputs from the first layers with significantly
360             larger magnitudes than the initialization value of , without the scaling issue. We verified this using a
361             ViT model with discrete token embedding as the input as well.

424   5     R ELATED W ORK
425
426   Normalization Layers. Normalization techniques are fundamental in deep learning, starting with
427   Local Response Normalization (Lyu & Simoncelli, 2008; Jarrett et al., 2009) in models like AlexNet
428   (Krizhevsky et al., 2012). Batch normalization (Ioffe & Szegedy, 2015) popularized normalization by
429   enhancing convergence and generalization through mini-batch activation normalization. It leads to
430   various methods targeting different data dimensions—channel (Ba et al., 2016; Zhang & Sennrich,
431   2019), spatial/temporal (Ulyanov et al., 2016), or both (Ba et al., 2016; Wu & He, 2018). In
      transformer models (Vaswani et al., 2017; Dosovitskiy et al., 2020), layer normalization(Ba et al.,


                                                           8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      2016) has become the primary normalization strategy. Recently, rms normalization (Zhang &
433   Sennrich, 2019), used in models like T5 (Raffel et al., 2020) and LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023a),
434   enhances layer normalization by omitting mean centering, highlighting the ongoing evolution of
435   normalization techniques in deep learning.
436
      Benefits of Normalization. Early research on benefits of normalization predominantly centered on
437
      Batch Norm, elucidating its capacity to enhance model training and performance through various
438
      mechanisms. These advantages include propagating informative activation patterns into deeper layers,
439   which maintains gradient flow during training (Daneshmand et al., 2020; Balduzzi et al., 2017).
440   Normalization also reduces dependency on initialization schemes, making networks less sensitive to
441   initial weights (De & Smith, 2020; Shao et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2019). It accelerates convergence
442   by moderating outlier eigenvalues that can impede learning (Karakida et al., 2019; Bjorck et al., 2018).
443   Additionally, normalization effectively auto-tunes learning rates, similar to adaptive optimizers (Arora
444   et al., 2018; Tanaka & Kunin, 2021), and smooths the loss landscape for more stable optimization
445   (Santurkar et al., 2018; Yong et al., 2020). These properties collectively enhance training robustness
446   and efficiency across architectures and applications.
447   With transformer models’ advent (Vaswani et al., 2017), research shifted focus to LayerNorm (Ba et al.,
448   2016). LayerNorm operates across features of a single sample, unlike Batch Norm’s batch dimension,
449   making it well-suited for sequential data and enhancing transformer performance in natural language
450   tasks (Xiong et al., 2020; Nguyen & Salazar, 2019). LayerNorm stabilizes transformer training by
451   mitigating internal covariate shift, facilitating faster convergence and improved generalization (Xu
452   et al., 2019). It also alleviates vanishing and exploding gradients in deep networks (Nguyen & Salazar,
453
      2019). Furthermore, LayerNorm’s per-sample normalization statistics enable effective learning of
      complex distributions, making it valuable for modeling long-range dependencies (Xiong et al., 2020).
454
455   Normalization-free networks. The research on Normalization-free networks challenges the belief
456   that normalization layers are indispensable for the effective training of deep neural networks. This
457   domain seeks to match the performance of traditional models while using normalization, thereby
458   streamlining architectures and addressing issues inherent to normalization layers (Brock et al., 2021a).
459   A pioneering study by Brock et al. (Brock et al., 2021a;b) highlighted the potential of training
460   high-performance ResNet models without normalization (Smith et al., 2023). They introduced a
461   meticulously crafted initialization scheme (De & Smith, 2020), coupled with weight normalization
462   techniques (Huang et al., 2017; Qiao et al., 2019), and a novel training methodology that incorporates
463   very strong data augmentation (Cubuk et al., 2020), intensive regularization (Srivastava et al., 2014;
464   Huang et al., 2016), and adaptive gradient clipping (Brock et al., 2021b). This approach not only
465
      achieved high accuracy but also demonstrated superior generalization on out-of-distribution data.
466   Another line of research focuses on modifying transformer blocks to reduce dependency on normaliza-
467   tion and skip connection (He et al., 2023; He & Hofmann, 2023). These studies explore the feasibility
468   of omitting normalization from certain parts of transformer blocks, although they acknowledge the
469   necessity of retaining layer normalization in either the encoder or decoder to maintain functional
470   models. Other research has been exploring alternative strategies, such as novel initialization methods,
471
      to facilitate normalization-free training. Approaches like FixUp (Zhang et al., 2019), ReZero (Xiong
      et al., 2020), and SkipInit (De & Smith, 2020) focus on adjusting weight initialization to support
472
      training without normalization. However, these methods were not shown to work across various
473
      modern networks, most notably large Transformers.

      6    C ONCLUSION
478
479
480   In this work, we introduced Dynamic Tanh (DyT), a simple alternative to traditional normalization
481   layers in deep neural networks. DyT dynamically adjusts the input activations via a learnable scaling
482   factor α and squashing the extreme values through a tanh function, effectively capturing the behavior
483   of normalization while simplifying the architecture. Through experiments across a wide range
484   of modalities, including image, audio, language, and genomics, our results demonstrate that DyT
485   not only matches the performance of traditional normalization techniques but also ensures training
      stability without the need for extensive hyperparameter tuning.

802   normalization, we explored its applicability to batch normalization (BN) in classic CNN architectures

805   ViT-Base model. All models were trained from scratch on the ImageNet-1K dataset under identical

      conditions to isolate the impact of the normalization methods.
