011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Multimodal contrastive models like CLIP are increasingly vulnerable to data-
014            poisoning backdoor attacks. Existing defense methods primarily target the pre-
015
               training phase. However, with the rise of open-source communities, pretrained
               models are now freely available for download and fine-tuning. These models
016
               may carry unknown security risks, posing significant threats to downstream users.
017
               This highlights the need for lightweight defense strategies tailored specifically
018            for the fine-tuning stage. Current defenses during fine-tuning include: finetuning
019            with clean data; and using unimodal self-supervised techniques like CleanCLIP,
020            which has represented the state-of-the-art (SOTA). However, these methods rely
021            on strengthening clean feature representations to mitigate attacks, making them
022            ineffective against more stealthy backdoor techniques, such as BadCLIP, which
023            leverage covert toxic features. To overcome this limitation, we propose a finetuning
024            defense mechanism based on fine-grained counterfactual text semantic augmenta-
025            tion. By modifying small portions of text during fine-tuning, our approach disrupts
026
               the association between backdoor triggers and target features. We evaluate our
               method against six attack algorithms and conduct comprehensive zero-shot classifi-
027
               cation on ImageNet1K. Experimental results demonstrate that our method achieves
028
               SOTA performance in fine-tuning defense. Specifically, when facing the novel
029            BadCLIP attack, our method surpasses CleanCLIP, reducing the Attack Success
030            Rate (ASR) by 52.02% in the Top-1 and 63.88% in the Top-10 classifications.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Contrastive learning serves as a powerful learning paradigm aimed at comparing different representa-
036   tions of data, thereby bringing similar samples closer together in the embedding space while pushing
037   dissimilar samples further apart Chen et al. (2020); Khosla et al. (2020); Gutmann & Hyvärinen
038   (2010). In addition to its application in single-modal data Gao et al. (2021); Chen et al. (2022);
039
      Bi et al. (2022); Park et al. (2020), recent works have extended contrastive learning to multimodal
      data Zhang et al. (2023); Singh et al. (2023); Yang et al. (2022), training on a vast scale of image-text
040
      pairs from the web to achieve joint feature representation and matching between images and text.
041
      Multimodal contrastive pre-trained models, such as CLIP Radford et al. (2021b), ALIGN Chen et al.
042   (2021b), and BASIC Chen et al. (2021a), have learned universal representations from large-scale
043   unlabeled data and performed exceptionally well even without task-specific data, as demonstrated by
044   their impressive zero-shot classification performance on ImageNet Deng et al. (2009). By fine-tuning
045   these models on specific tasks with a small amount of labeled training samples, high-performance
046   vertical domain applications can be realized quickly.
047
      However, recent research has revealed that these models are vulnerable to data-poisoning backdoor
048   attacks Gao et al. (2020); Jia et al. (2022); Saha et al. (2022); Carlini & Terzis (2021); Li et al. (2023),
049   which can compromise their integrity and reliability. In a backdoor attack, an adversary embeds
050   a trigger into the model, allowing it to misclassify inputs in specific, often harmful ways. This
051   vulnerability poses a serious concern, particularly as these models are increasingly deployed in real-
052   world applications. Existing defense methods primarily target the pretraining phase, aiming to mitigate
053   risks before models are fine-tuned for specific tasks. Notable approaches include CleanCLIP Bansal
      et al. (2023) and RoCLIP Yang et al. (2024a), which focus on enhancing the model’s robustness

      To clarify the motivation behind our method and its effectiveness, we explore the landscape of
082
      backdoor attacks. Traditional backdoor attacks create new feature clusters in the feature space by
083
      linking visual triggers to specific texts, thereby assigning new pseudo-semantics to the target text.
084   While these attacks can be effective, they leave distinct traces of pseudo-clusters, making detection
085   and defense more manageable, as shown in Figure 1(a). CleanCLIP further addresses this vulnerability
086   by incorporating a vision-language self-supervised learning module to disrupt these pseudo-semantic
087   clusters, as shown in Figure 1(c). While effective against some known threats, CleanCLIP performs
088   inadequately when facing more covert backdoor techniques, such as BadCLIP Liang et al. (2023),
089   which exploits hidden toxic features to evade detection. Instead of generating new pseudo-clusters,
090   BadCLIP precisely identifies the true feature regions of the target text and adjusts the image trigger
091   to approach these regions, successfully evading the self-supervised enhancements, as shown in
092
      Figure 1(b). This limitation underscores the necessity for a more robust defense mechanism.
093   To address this novel challenge, we introduce CleanerCLIP, an innovative strategy that utilizes
094   fine-grained counterfactual semantic augmentation to disrupt the potential semantic link between
095   the trigger and the target output, as illustrated in Figure 1(d). In contrast to CleanCLIP, which
096   primarily focuses on disrupting pseudo-semantic clusters, our approach also generates negative and
097   positive subtexts for a small subset of the clean fine-tuning data. Negative subtexts are created by
098
      randomly replacing components of the text’s semantics. This random alteration disrupts the semantic
      binding exploited in potential backdoor attacks, reducing the stability and success rate of the trigger.
099
      Meanwhile, positive subtexts preserve the essential semantic features of the original target text,
100
      ensuring that the model can accurately process clean data. This dual augmentation process not only
101   lowers the success rate of backdoor attacks but also enhances the overall robustness of the model
102   during fine-tuning.

      2   R ELATED WORK AND PRELIMINARIES
119
120
      Contrastive Language-Image Pre-Training (CLIP) CLIP Radford et al. (2021b), released by
121
      OpenAI, stands as a prominent representative of MCL. Inspired by mapping images and texts into a
122   shared feature embedding space Rd , CLIP enables the model to understand the semantic relationship
123   between them. CLIP involves two encoders: an image encoder fI : I → Rd and a text encoder
124   fT : T → Rd , which transform the image and text data into representations of dimension d. The
125   model is pre-trained through contrastive learning, leveraging vast amounts of internet image-text
126   pairs {Ii , Ti }N
                      i=1 to learn the associations between images and texts. During training, the CLIP
127   model learns a mapping function that projects images and texts into the same feature space. This is
128   achieved by maximizing the similarity between positive pairs (matching images Ii and texts Ti ) while
129   minimizing the similarity between negative pairs (mismatched images and texts). This unsupervised
130
      joint learning approach enables the CLIP model to achieve superior performance on various visual
      and language tasks, including image classification, text caption generation, and image retrieval. The
131
      mathematical expression for lossClip can be found in Appendix B.
132
133   Backdoor attacks Backdoor attacks generally refer to the implantation of specific trigger patterns
134   during the model training process, which enables the model to perform normally under normal
135   conditions but exhibit abnormal behavior under specific conditions, such as when the input contains
136
      images with trigger patterns. In the domain of supervised learning, backdoor attacks have garnered
      significant attention, with notable works including BadNet Gu et al. (2017), Blended Chen et al.
137
      (2017), SIG Liu et al. (2020), WaNet Nguyen & Tran (2021), and SSBA Li et al. (2021)]. Backdoor
138
      attacks targeting the CLIP model primarily leverage its capability in learning from multimodal data.
139   Attackers can add image-text pairs containing specific trigger patterns to the training data, allowing
140   the model to learn the association between these trigger patterns and abnormal behaviors. Within the
141   domain of MCL, Carlini & Terzis (2021) pioneered the revelation of its vulnerability to backdoor
142   attacks, demonstrating a successful attack on CLIP, for instance, by poisoning merely 0.01% of the
143   data. Concurrently, Yang et al. (2023b) delved into the impact of attacks from different modalities on
144   MCL. Additionally, research on attacks against self-supervised learning (SSL), a broader category, is
145   also ongoing, exemplified by BadEncoder Jia et al. (2022), GhostEncoder Wang et al. (2024), and
146   distribution-preserving attacks Tao et al. (2023). The details about data-poisoning backdoor attacks
147
      on CLIP are shown in Appendix A.
148   Backdoor Defenses on CLIP To address these threats mentioned above, some researchers have
149   borrowed backdoor defense techniques from supervised learning Zhu et al. (2023; 2024) to mitigate
150   the backdoor effects in MCL models. Currently, defense techniques for MCL can be categorized into
151   two groups based on whether the defender can access the poisoned dataset: ① defenders can access
152   the entire poisoned dataset Yang et al. (2023a; 2024b); Bansal et al. (2023); ② defenders can only
153
      access the poisoned model Bansal et al. (2023). The former approach, which allows for complete
      retraining of large models with various data augmentation strategies, can achieve strong defense
154
      performance, such as RoCLIP Yang et al. (2024b). However, in reality, the feasibility of attackers
155
      manipulating the training set is low, as they cannot guarantee that their carefully crafted poisoned
156   data will be incorporated into large-scale training sets. Therefore, a more realistic attack strategy
157   is to perform low-cost fine-tuning of existing pre-trained large models with dirty data. As a result,
158   defense techniques targeting the fine-tuning phase are necessary, which is the attack-defense scenario
159   addressed in this paper. A representative example of such defenses is CleanCLIP Bansal et al. (2023).
160   Specifically, CleanCLIP introduces a self-supervised loss based on multimodal data augmentation,
161   which fine-tunes a clean dataset to reduce the impact of backdoor models. Their self-supervised loss
      lossSS and total fine-tuning loss lossCClip can be found in Appendix B.

      3        M ETHODOLOGY

      3.1       T HREAT MODEL
182
183   Adversary Objective: The primary objective of the adversary is to manipulate CLIP’s textual output
184   representation. By polluting the original dataset, the model can generate malicious adversarial text
185   specified by the adversary for any input image embedded with a trigger. During zero-shot testing, the
186   attack objective manifests as poisoned images will be misclassified as the adversarial category, while
187   other benign images will be correctly classified.
188   Adversary Capability: We assume the attacker possesses knowledge of the model’s structure,
189   training algorithm, and the hyper-parameters used by the victim, but they can’t directly modify the
190   training process. While the attacker lacks access to the entire dataset, they can inject a small number
191   of poisoned samples into the training dataset. Furthermore, the attacker can poison pre-trained MCL
192   models by fine-tuning with carefully crafted dirty datasets and distributing them through various
193   channels on the internet, thereby creating uncontrollable risks for downstream tasks.

195   3.2       F INE - GRAINED C OUNTERFACTUAL T EXT S EMANTIC AUGMENTATION
196
197
      To address the inadequacies of existing defenses like CleanCLIP against covert backdoor attacks,
      particularly those employing stealthy trigger features, we propose CleanerCLIP, a fine-grained
198
      counterfactual text augmentation strategy. Our approach recognizes the necessity of both preserving
199
      clean sample characteristics and disrupting the malicious semantic links exploited by potential
200   backdoor attacks. As illustrated in the left part of Figure 2, our method consists of two main parts:
201   (1) Factual positive sub-caption generation, which ensures the integrity of the original clean data; (2)
202   Counterfactual negative sub-caption generation, which actively undermines the stealthy backdoor
203   triggers. For convenience, we will refer to positive sub-captions as factual sub-captions and negative
204   sub-captions as counterfactual sub-captions in the following descriptions. Assuming the image-text
205   dataset used by CLIP finetuning is Df t , we annotate each sample as (Ii , Ti ) ∈ Df t , where Ii is the
206   image and Ti is its associated caption. And we generate [STpi , STni ] for each Ti , representing the
207   positive sub-caption and negative sub-caption respectively.
208   Factual: Positive sub-caption generation For each text sample Ti , we decompose it by first
209   identifying its core semantic components, focusing on relational verbs and key nouns. To generate
210   the positive sub-caption STpi , we retain the primary relational verb and key nouns while selectively
211   simplifying or omitting adjectives, ensuring that the core semantic meaning of the original text
212   remains intact. Using SceneGraphParser 1 , we organize the positive sub-caption in the following
213   template, preserving the correct relationships between subjects and objects:
214   < ( Adjective of the subject ) + Subject + Relational Verb + ( Adjective of the object ) + Object >

244   repository of alternative words, ensuring that negative subtexts are loosely distributed in feature space.
245   Each replacement repository contains 3000 terms, ranging from common to rare words, ensuring
246   maximum disruption of the backdoor trigger’s semantic binding.

248   3.3   C LEANER CLIP: F INE - GRAINED C OUNTERFACTUAL S EMANTIC F INETUNING
249
250   Previous defense efforts have focused on countering backdoor triggers by augmenting image and text
251   self-supervised learning. However, through our prior analysis, we found that the text self-supervision
252   strength of CleanCLIP is insufficient to withstand triggers carefully optimized in the feature space,
253   unless sacrificing the expression capability of clean samples. Therefore, building upon this, we
254   reinforced the text augmentation method by finely optimizing the feature vectors of text through
255
      alternating optimization between self-supervised learning and positive-negative sample adversarial
      learning, enhancing CLIP’s robustness against image backdoor triggers. Previous defense strategies
256
      have primarily focused on image and text self-supervised learning to counter backdoor triggers.
257
      However, our analysis reveals that the self-supervision strength in CleanCLIP is insufficient against
258   the meticulously crafted triggers that leverage existing clean features without creating additional
259   toxic feature clusters. This limitation highlights the need for a more effective approach to defend
260   against novel attack techniques during finetuning. To address this challenge, we propose a fine-
261   grained text semantic augmentation method that leverages both positive and negative sub-captions
262   during fine-tuning. This approach aligns with the necessity for lightweight defense mechanisms we
263   have discussed before, ensuring robust protection against image-based backdoor triggers without
264   compromising clean sample expressiveness.
265   During the fine-tuning, we do not perform fine-grained semantic augmentation on all texts, as this
266   would disrupt the alignment of a large number of clean images and texts, thereby reducing the
267   downstream zero-shot accuracy of clean samples. We randomly select K samples from all text data
268   for fine-grained augmentation, obtaining K augmented data, denoted as {Ii , Ti , STpi , STni }K
                                                                                                    i=1 . This
269   random selection approach maximally retains the original feature expression capability’s general-
      ization on downstream tasks while achieving our defense objectives, and effectively minimizing the

306
307   Dataset and models As a defense technique during the fine-tuning phase, we adopted the fine-tuning
308   setting of Bansal et al. (2023). We utilized the open-source CLIP model from OpenAI Radford
309   et al. (2021a) as the pre-trained clean model, which is trained on a dataset containing 400 million
310   image-text pairs. We selected 500,000 image-text pairs (CC500K) as our fine-tuning dataset from the
311   CC3M dataset Sharma et al. (2018). Following Bansal et al. (2023), we use the ResNet-50 model as
312   the CLIP vision encoder and a transformer as the text encoder during fine-tuning. We conducted our
313   experiments using an A100 GPU.
314   The victim models generation We utilized the CC500K dataset to simulate the adversary’s attack
315   process. Specifically, we randomly selected 1,500 samples from CC500K for various types of
316   backdoor attacks, embedding triggers into the images. The corresponding text was modified to target
317   specific categories using a predefined template, while the remaining samples were kept unchanged.
318   This contaminated dataset was then used to fine-tune the pre-trained CLIP model. For fine-tuning,
319   we employed a batch size of 128, an iteration count of 5, and a base learning rate of 1 × 10−6 . The
320
      learning rate was warmed up over 10,000 steps, using AdamW as our optimizer with a weight decay
      of 0.1. The Adam momentum factor and RMSProp factor were set to 0.9 and 0.999, respectively,
321
      with an epsilon value of 1 × 10−8 . And the attack target label is “banana".

350   Evaluation metrics Following Yang et al. (2024a); Bansal et al. (2023) and most attacks like Liang
351   et al. (2023), we adopt benign accuracy (BA, ↑) and attack success rate (ASR, ↓) as our primary
352   evaluation metrics. For BA, a higher value indicates superior clean performance, while for ASR, a
353   lower value reflects better defense performance. These metrics are used to assess defense strategies
354   across two common tasks: zero-shot classification on the ImageNet-1K validation set and linear
355   probing. In the linear probing task, the feature extraction layers remain fixed, and only the linear
356   layer is trained on 50,000 clean images from the ImageNet-1K training set, followed by testing on
357
      the ImageNet-1K validation set.
358   To comprehensively assess the impact of our defense on CLIP performance, we use Top-k (k =
359   1, 3, 5, 10) evaluations for both BA and ASR. Top-k accuracy considers not only the highest probability
360   class predicted by the model but also other high-probability classes, thus offering a more accurate
361   reflection of the model’s generalization capacity in multi-class settings. This consideration has
362   been widely used in prior works on the CLIP community, such as EVA-CLIP Sun et al. (2023),
363
      CosmoCLIP Imam et al. (2024), and CEIA Xu et al. (2024). However, to our knowledge, previous
      backdoor defense works targeting CLIP have not reported Top-k metrics, leading to incomplete
364
      performance evaluation. Therefore, by incorporating Top-k BA/ASR evaluations, our work not only
365
      provides a more thorough investigation into CLIP’s backdoor vulnerabilities but also demonstrates
366   state-of-the-art performance by significantly reducing Top-k ASR.

      Regarding Top-1 performance Compared to the victim model (NoDefense), while the FT improves
      the defense performance, it still exhibits some limitations. For instance, the Top-1 ASR remains high

396

397   ASR, achieving near-zero Top-1 ASR against both BadNet and WaNet (from 91.28% to 0.45% and
398   from 91.83% to 0.15%, respectively). Furthermore, for BadCLIP, which CleanCLIP struggles to
399   defend against, our method can also significantly weaken its toxicity. This is because BadCLIP’s
400   trigger optimization primarily focuses on adjusting and altering the poisoned image’s feature vector
401   to approach the target text’s clean feature indefinitely, without creating additional target feature
402   clusters. In contrast, CleanCLIP’s use of text-supervised learning through EDA augmentation does
403   not significantly alter the semantic content of the text. Consequently, when faced with the carefully
404   designed and highly stealthy BadCLIP attack, CleanCLIP fails to disrupt the toxic triggers hidden
405
      within the clean target features. Our CleanerCLIP addresses this limitation by generating negative
      subtexts through random counterfactual semantic enhancement, effectively disrupting the potential
406
      binding of trigger features. Simultaneously, the inclusion of factual positive subtexts in finetuning
407
      enhances defense performance while safeguarding the integrity of clean sample features.

409   Regarding Top-k performance In the zero-shot classification test, Top-1 accuracy indicates whether
410   the class with the highest predicted probability by the model matches the true class, focusing on
411
      the model’s accuracy in a single prediction. Conversely, Top-k accuracy considers whether the true
      class is among the top-k predicted classes with the highest probabilities. Even if the class with the
412
      highest probability is not the true class, the prediction is deemed correct if the true class is within
413
      the model’s top-k predicted classes. Compared to Top-1, Top-k accuracy encompasses a broader
414   prediction scope and thus typically yields higher accuracy. In the Zero-shot classification test, as the
415   model is tasked with handling unseen classes, it may not accurately predict the true class with the
416   highest probability. In such scenarios, Top-k accuracy offers a more comprehensive assessment of
417   the model’s performance, as it takes into account the model’s predictive capability across multiple
418   potentially correct classes. Therefore, we also present the BA and ASR of our defense method on
419   Top-3, Top-5, and Top-10 in Table 1. Compared with NoDefense, FT, and CLeanCLIP, it is observed
420   that our CleanerCLIP does not induce overfitting tendencies in the defense processing, as evidenced
421   by the improved defensive performance even within the Top-10 range. Specifically, when FT and
422
      CleanCLIP nearly fail to defend against BadCLIP (with Top-10 ASR values of 99.37% and 91.96%,
      respectively), our method can reduce the ASR by 71.75% compared to the original victim model,
423
      which demonstrates the superiority of our defense performance.

473   Ablation As shown in Table 1, Table 2 and Figure 3, when we introduced fine-grained augmentation
474   of positive and negative sub-samples, indicated by the addition of lossp−n , our CleanerCLIP signifi-
475   cantly improved defense performance compared to the original CleanCLIP, without compromising
476
      the model’s performance on clean samples.
477   The lossp−n weight We evaluated the impact of the proposed lossp−n on the overall loss for defense
478   performance, as shown in Eq. 4. In this ablation study, we set α to 1 by default and adjusted β to
479   achieve different influence levels of lossp−n . As illustrated in Figure 4(a), we found that as the β/α
480   ratio increases, i.e., the higher the weight of lossp−n , the better defense performance.
481   The pos-/neg- temperature factor Since we employ fine-grained alignment of positive and negative
482   subtexts with images, it is essential to consider the relationship between the model’s focus on positive
483   and negative samples and the final defense performance. This relationship can be modulated by
484   adjusting the temperature factors tp and tn in Eq. 1 and 2 to achieve different levels of attention
485   to positive and negative samples. Specifically, the smaller the value of the temperature factor, the
      higher the attention received. As shown in Figure 4(b), we conducted ablation experiments with

503   The number of fine-grained texts in every epoch Furthermore, since we do not perform text aug-
504   mentation on all samples, but rather randomly select a subset of samples to implement CleanerCLIP
505   in each iteration, we explored the impact of sample quantity, as illustrated in Figure 4(c). It can be
506   observed that for simpler attacks like BadNet, only 50 samples are sufficient to significantly reduce
507   the ASR after the first iteration, achieving extremely fast and optimal defense performance. For
508   more complex new attack techniques, such as BadCLIP, only 2000 to 3000 samples are needed. This
509   represents a very small training cost compared to the scale of the fine-tuning dataset (500K).
510   Compared with other text augmentation strategy We employ currently available open-source text
511   augmentation strategies, such as DeCLUTR, to replace our counterfactual semantic enhancement
512   component and compare the fine-tuning defense performance. DeCLUTR Giorgi et al. (2020) uses
513   contrastive learning to improve text representation and generate more diverse and semantically
514   relevant augmentations. Detailed results are provided in Table 3. We can find that DeCLUTR does
515   not significantly alter the semantic content of the input text. This limitation hinders their effectiveness
516
      in disrupting covert visual backdoors. For instance, the Top-1 ASR for BadCLIP attacks remains
      high, with values decreasing from 99.27% to 74.96% when using DeCLUTR, while the Top-10 ASR
517
      shows a marginal reduction from 99.83% to 93.72%. This illustrates that while some improvement is
518
      observed, the lack of substantial semantic alteration fails to adequately sever the connections exploited
519   by the backdoor triggers, allowing these attacks to persist. This fully corroborates that, compared to
520   traditional text augmentation methods, our counterfactual semantic enhancement strategy achieves
521   unique and irreplaceable defensive performance.

530
531
532   In this paper, we focus on fine-tuning defense strategies against backdoor attacks targeting MCL.
533   We propose CleanerCLIP, a counterfactual semantic enhancement method that effectively defends
534   against backdoor attacks in multi-modal contrastive learning models. CleanerCLIP achieves superior
535   defense performance across various datasets and attack scenarios, significantly reducing ASR while
536
      maintaining benign accuracy.
537   Limitations Since our proposed CleanerCLIP primarily addresses backdoor attacks in the image
538   modality, the defense performance against text modality attacks remains unknown. In the future, we
539   will further explore comprehensive and efficient defense methods that are effective across various
      modalities.

874   F    C OMPARISON WITH EACH EDA TEXT AUGMENTATION USED IN
875        C LEAN CLIP
876
877   In this section, we compare the defense performance of CleanerCLIP against various Easy Data
878   Augmentation (EDA) strategies employed in CleanCLIP. This comparison is crucial for evaluating
879   the relative effectiveness of different augmentation techniques in mitigating the impact of backdoor
880   attacks in multi-modal contrastive learning models.
881
      As outlined in our motivation, we emphasize counterfactual replacement (CFR) as the core strategy
882
      within our method, which is designed to achieve semantic divergence at the text level. This divergence
883   is critical for disrupting visual backdoor triggers within the feature space. To measure the degree of
884   semantic change introduced by different text augmentation strategies, we utilize the metric "impact =
885   1 - cosine similarity" between the original and augmented captions. By applying five augmentation
886   strategies to 1,000 samples from the CC3M dataset: random insertion (RI), random deletion (RD),
887   random swap (RS), synonym replacement (SP), and counterfactual replacement (CFR), we computed
888   the following impact values: RI (0.0434), RD (0.0516), RS (0.0396), SP (0.0247), and CFR (0.1015).
889   Among these, CFR introduced the largest semantic change, demonstrating its potential to effectively
890   break the correlation between text and image features, which is essential for neutralizing backdoor
891
      triggers.
892   In addition to measuring semantic change, we conducted a comprehensive evaluation of these text
893   augmentation strategies under the BadCLIP attack scenario, where we overlaid each EDA strategy
894   on CleanCLIP and measured the corresponding attack success rates (ASR). As shown in Table 6,
895   CleanerCLIP, using counterfactual replacement, consistently outperformed other EDA strategies.
896   For instance, under the Top-1 ASR evaluation, CFR-ori and CFR-sub both reduced ASR to 17.85%,
897
      compared to over 69% for the other EDA strategies (RI, RD, RS, SP). Similar improvements are
      observed across Top-3, Top-5, and Top-10 evaluations, where CFR-based augmentations demonstrate
898
      a significant drop in ASR, reaching as low as 20.19% (Top-3) and 28.09% (Top-10), highlighting the
899
      robustness of our approach.
900
901   Moreover, we investigated the effect of positive sub-caption selection (CFR-sub) versus original
902   caption replacement (CFR-ori). The results show that both strategies yield nearly identical defense
903
      performance, confirming that the choice between positive sub-captions and original captions has
      minimal impact on overall defense effectiveness. Thus, for simplicity and practicality, we opted to
904
      use positive sub-captions (CFR-sub) in our experiments.
905
906   In summary, these evaluations underscore the superiority of counterfactual semantic replacement
907   (CFR) over traditional EDA strategies. CleanerCLIP not only achieves the highest semantic diver-
908   gence, but also provides the most effective defense against backdoor attacks across multiple metrics,
909
      demonstrating its unique and irreplaceable defense capabilities.
