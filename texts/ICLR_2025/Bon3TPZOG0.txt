008                                                A BSTRACT
009
010            Recent empirical studies have demonstrated that diffusion models can effectively
011            learn the image distribution and generate new samples. Remarkably, these mod-
012            els can achieve this even with a small number of training samples despite a large
013
               image dimension, circumventing the curse of dimensionality. In this work, we
               provide theoretical insights into this phenomenon by leveraging key empirical
014
               observations: (i) the low intrinsic dimensionality of image data, (ii) a union of
015
               manifold structure of image data, and (iii) the low-rank property of the denois-
016            ing autoencoder in trained diffusion models. These observations motivate us to
017            assume the underlying data distribution of image data as a mixture of low-rank
018            Gaussians and to parameterize the denoising autoencoder as a low-rank model ac-
019            cording to the score function of the assumed distribution. With these setups, we
020            rigorously show that optimizing the training loss of diffusion models is equivalent
021            to solving the canonical subspace clustering problem over the training samples.
022            Based on this equivalence, we further show that the minimal number of samples
023            required to learn the underlying distribution scales linearly with the intrinsic di-
024
               mensions under the above data and model assumptions. This insight sheds light on
               why diffusion models can break the curse of dimensionality and exhibit the phase
025
               transition from failure to success in learning distributions. Moreover, we empir-
026
               ically establish a correspondence between the subspaces and the semantic repre-
027            sentations of image data, facilitating image editing. We validate these results with
028            extensive experimental results on both simulated distributions and image datasets.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Generative modeling is a fundamental task in deep learning, which aims to learn a data distribution
033   from training data to generate new samples. Recently, diffusion models have emerged as a new fam-
034   ily of generative models, demonstrating remarkable performance across diverse domains, including
035   image generation (Alkhouri et al., 2024; Ho et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022), video content gen-
036   eration (Bar-Tal et al., 2024; Ho et al., 2022), speech and audio synthesis (Kong et al., 2020; 2021),
037   and solving inverse problem (Chung et al., 2022; Song et al., 2024). In general, diffusion models
038   learn a data distribution from training samples through a process that imitates the non-equilibrium
039   thermodynamic diffusion process (Ho et al., 2020; Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Song et al., 2021).
040
      Specifically, the training and sampling of diffusion models involve two stages: (i) a forward diffu-
      sion process where Gaussian noise is incrementally added to training samples at each time step, and
041
      (ii) a backward sampling process where the noise is progressively removed through a neural net-
042
      work that is trained to approximate the score function at all time steps. As described in prior works
043   (Hyvärinen & Dayan, 2005; Song et al., 2021), the generative capability of diffusion models lies in
044   their ability to learn the score function of the data distribution, i.e., the gradient of the logarithm of
045   the probability density function (pdf ). We refer the reader to (Chen et al., 2024a; Croitoru et al.,
046   2023; Yang et al., 2023) for a more comprehensive introduction and survey on diffusion models.
047
      Despite the recent advances in understanding sampling convergence (Chen et al., 2023b; Lee et al.,
048
      2022; Li et al., 2023), distribution learning (Chen et al., 2023a; Oko et al., 2023), memorization
049   (Gu et al., 2023; Somepalli et al., 2023; Wen et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024), and generalization
050   (Kadkhodaie et al., 2023; Yoon et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023) of diffusion models, the fundamental
051   working mechanisms remain poorly understood. One of the key questions is
052
053    When and why can diffusion models learn the underlying data distribution without suffering from
                                      the curse of dimensionality?

      At first glance, the answer might seem quite straightforward. If a diffusion model can learn the em-
068
      pirical distribution of the training data that accurately approximates the underlying data distribution,
069
      then the puzzle is solved! However, it has been shown in (Li et al., 2024) that the number of samples
070   for an empirical distribution to approximate the underlying data distribution could grow exponen-
071   tially with respect to (w.r.t.) the data dimension. Moreover, Oko et al. (2023); Wibisono et al. (2024)
072   showed that to learn an ϵ-accurate score estimator measured by the ℓ2 -norm via score matching or
073   kernel-based approach, the required size of training samples grows at the rate of O(ϵ−n ), where n is
074   the data dimension. These theoretical findings indicate that learning the underlying distribution via
075   diffusion models suffers from the curse of dimensionality. In contrast, recent studies (Kadkhodaie
076   et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023) showed that the number of training samples for a diffusion model to
077   learn the underlying distribution is much smaller than the worst-case scenario, breaking the curse of
078
      dimensionality. Therefore, there is a significant gap between theory and practice.
079   In this work, we aim to address the above question of learning the underlying distribution via dif-
080   fusion models by leveraging low-dimensional models. Our key observations are as follows: (i) The
081   intrinsic dimensionality of real image data is significantly lower than the ambient dimension, a fact
082   well-supported by extensive empirical evidence in Gong et al. (2019); Pope et al. (2020); Stanczuk
083   et al. (2024); (ii) Image data lies on a disjoint union of manifolds of varying intrinsic dimensions, as
084
      empirically verified in Brown et al. (2023); Kamkari et al. (2024); Loaiza-Ganem et al. (2024) (see
      Figure 1(a)); (iii) We empirically observe that the denoising autoencoder (DAE) (Pretorius et al.,
085
      2018; Vincent, 2011) of diffusion models trained on real-world image datasets exhibit low-rank
086
      structures (see Figure 3). Based on these observations, we conduct a theoretical investigation of
087   distribution learning through diffusion models by assuming that (i) the underlying data distribution
088   is a mixture of low-rank Gaussians (see Definition 1) and (ii) the denoising autoencoder is parame-
089   terized according to the score function of the MoLRG. Notably, these assumptions will be carefully
090   discussed based on the existing literature and validated by our experiments on real image datasets.

157   Memorization and generalization in diffusion models. Recently, extensive studies (Kadkhodaie
158   et al., 2023; Yoon et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023) empirically revealed that diffusion models learn
159   the score function across two distinct regimes — memorization (i.e., learning the empirical distri-

161   dataset size vs. the model capacity. For a model with a fixed number of parameters, there is a
      phase transition from memorization to generalization as the number of training samples increases

223   Mixture of low-rank Gaussian data distribution. Although real-world image datasets are high
224   dimensional in terms of pixel count and overall data volume, extensive empirical works (Gong et al.,
225   2019; Kamkari et al., 2024; Pope et al., 2020; Stanczuk et al., 2024) suggest that their intrinsic
226
      dimensions are much lower. For instance, Pope et al. (2020) indicated that even for complex datasets
      like ImageNet (Russakovsky et al., 2015), the intrinsic dimensionality is approximately 40, which is
227
      significantly lower than its ambient dimension. Recently, Brown et al. (2023); Kamkari et al. (2024);
228
      Loaiza-Ganem et al. (2024) empirically validated the union of manifolds hypothesis, demonstrating
229   that high-dimensional image data often lies on a disjoint union of manifolds instead of a single
230   manifold. These observations motivate us to model the underlying data distribution as a mixture
231   of low-rank Gaussians, where the data points are generated from a mixture of several Gaussian
232   distributions; see Figure 1(b). We formally define the MoLRG distribution as follows:

529   5     C ONCLUSION & D ISCUSSION
530
531
      In this work, we studied the training loss of diffusion models to investigate when and why diffusion
      models can learn the underlying distribution without suffering from the curse of dimensionality.
532
      Motivated by extensive empirical observations, we assumed that the underlying data distribution is a
533
      mixture of low-rank Gaussians. Specifically, we showed that minimizing the training loss is equiv-
534   alent to solving the subspace clustering problem under proper network parameterization. Based on
535   this equivalence, we further showed that the optimal solutions to the training loss can recover the
536   underlying subspaces when the number of samples scales linearly with the intrinsic dimensionality
537   of the data distribution. Moreover, we established the correspondence between the subspaces and se-
538   mantic representations of image data. Since our studied network parameterization is not sufficiently
539   over-parameterized, a future direction is to extend our analysis to an over-parameterized case to fully
      explain the transition from memorization to generalization.
