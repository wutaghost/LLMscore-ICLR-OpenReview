                                                   A BSTRACT
028
029
               We introduce Synthetic Playground (SynPlay), a new synthetic human dataset that
030
               aims to bring out the diversity of human appearance in the real world. We focus
031            on two factors to achieve a level of diversity that has not yet been seen in previous
032            works: i) realistic human motions and poses and ii) multiple camera viewpoints
033            towards human instances. We first use a game engine and its library-provided el-
034            ementary motions to create games where virtual players can take less-constrained
035            and natural movements while following the game rules (i.e., rule-guided motion
036            design as opposed to detail-guided design). We then augment the elementary mo-
037            tions with real human motions captured with a motion capture device. To render
038            various human appearances in the games from multiple viewpoints, we use seven
039
               virtual cameras encompassing the ground and aerial views, capturing abundant
               aerial-vs-ground and dynamic-vs-static attributes of the scene. Through extensive
040
               and carefully-designed experiments, we show that using SynPlay in model train-
041
               ing leads to enhanced accuracy over existing synthetic datasets for human detec-
042            tion and segmentation. The benefit of SynPlay becomes even greater for tasks in
043            the data-scarce regime, such as few-shot and cross-domain learning tasks. These
044            results clearly demonstrate that SynPlay can be used as an essential dataset with
045            rich attributes of complex human appearances and poses suitable for model pre-
046            training. SynPlay dataset comprising over 73k images and 6.5M human instances,
047            will be publicly released upon acceptance of this paper.

050   1   I NTRODUCTION
051
052   Large-scale synthetic datasets, known for their scalability, provide a practical solution to the increas-
053   ing demand for training large-capacity models. Recently developed rendering engines (e.g., Unity,
      Unreal, etc.) have significantly enhanced the realism of synthetic data, broadening its applicability

054
      across various computer vision tasks. Despite efforts to scale synthetic data to match the extensive
055   curation of real-world data, the desired level of diversity has not yet been achieved. This insuffi-
056   ciency in diversity is largely due to the inadequate consideration and integration of key factors that
057   are essential to real-world diversity in the process of creating synthetic data.
058
      Recently, several attempts have been made to increase human appearance diversity by controlling
059
      innate characteristics (e.g., race, gender) (Black et al., 2023), body shape (e.g, height) (Patel et al.,
060
      2021; Black et al., 2023), or clothing (Black et al., 2023). These datasets have demonstrated their
061   effectiveness in tasks aimed at identifying human characteristics from close-up images, e.g., human
062   body/pose estimation (Patel et al., 2021; Black et al., 2023) and shape reconstruction (Mahmood
063   et al., 2019). However, these datasets have seldom yielded a discernible positive impact on computer
064   vision tasks aimed at identifying humans from a distance, e.g., human detection and segmentation.
065
      When it comes to recognizing the overall human appearance from a distance, the motions and poses
066
      exhibited by the individuals play more vital roles than other characteristics. Despite prior attempts
067   to synthesize various human poses, the quality of the rendering remained suboptimal, lacking in re-
068   alism (Richter et al., 2016) and diversity (Shen et al., 2023b). AMASS (Mahmood et al., 2019) was
069   the output of an early endeavor aimed at achieving both realism and diversity, where a motion scan-
070   ner was utilized to collect real human motions. Before capturing these motions, detailed descriptions
071   were provided to articulate specific movements — e.g., 5 seconds waving above the head with both
072   arms1 , while adhering to physical constraints that limit large movements in motion-capture environ-
073   ments. This detail-guided motion design often results in capturing a restricted range of motions tied
074   to specific descriptions while missing out on all the motions that defy easy description.
075   We claim that providing relatively high-level, less-detailed guidance greatly helps in breaking out
076   from the aforementioned limitations and provides more freedom towards the expansion of the diver-
077   sity in human motions. In constructing our dataset, we follow the new rule-guided motion design
078   approach providing game “rules” or winning strategies for the virtual players to follow, which serve
079   as a set of significantly coarser guidelines when compared to detail-guided approaches. In this way,
080   the motions that they manifest are not confined to predetermined/easily-describable motions. As for
081
      the “rules,” we opted to borrow them from the six traditional Korean games that were also played in
      the Netflix TV series “Squid Game” (Hwang, 2021). These games involve substantial amounts of
082
      physical movements, which naturally provide room for a diverse set of human poses and motions.
083
      The diversity is further influenced by in-game factors such as the uniquely defined rules of each
084   game, the number of players, and the interactions between them.
085
086   Under our rule-guided motion design approach, each scenario run (i.e., one round of a game played
087
      with specific settings) in the virtual environment is initialized by carrying out a scenario design
      step which is followed by the incorporation of real-world motions. The scenario design involves
088
      the setup of all the knobs that control the appearance, players (winners and losers), game dynamics
089
      (e.g., how/when each game ends), and the human motion evolutions for each specific scenario. This
090   is where the high-level rules of a given game are defined, and the coarse boundary of how human
091   motions can evolve within the game is set. The incorporation of real-world motion is the phase
092   where a rich variety of motions truly comes to life. Details on the entire pipeline will be elaborated
093   in Section 3.
094
      In addition, we also took into account that human appearance can vary greatly depending on the
095
      perspective from which it is viewed. Accordingly, we capture every scene from multiple view-
096   points by implementing several image-capturing devices to take advantage of different perspective-
097   related characteristics: three Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs), three Closed-Circuit Televisions
098   (CCTVs), and one Unmanned Ground Vehicle (UGV). The three UAVs fly with random trajectories
099   at different altitudes, the three CCTVs are located at the front, side, and back of the game play-
100   ground, and the UGV moves randomly within the playground where the game is being played. These
101   devices offer a variety of image-capturing properties, including aerial-vs-ground and dynamic-vs-
102   static. Our strategy, designed to provide very diverse viewpoints in scene capture process, not only
103   serves to ensure that the dataset includes more diverse human appearances but also broadens the po-
104
      tential tasks (e.g., re-identification, multi-view applications, aerial-to-ground scene matching, etc.)
      for which the dataset can be used.

      By leveraging the aforementioned human appearance-diversifying strategies, we construct a large-
109   scale synthetic human dataset called SynPlay that contains more than 73k images with 6.5M human
110   instances, see sample images in Fig 1. To demonstrate SynPlay’s ability to represent a variety of hu-
111   man appearances to the extent seen in the real world, we conduct a series of experiments where we
112   evaluate the impact of leveraging SynPlay alongside real (non-synthetic) datasets curated for a vari-
113   ety of human-related tasks, i.e.,, aerial-view/ground-view human detection and segmentation. For all
114   the tasks, training with SynPlay outperforms its counterparts (i.e., training-from-scratch, using other
115   synthetic data) across a variety of datasets. Experiments also demonstrate that the SynPlay dataset
116   significantly improves model performance on data-scarce tasks, highlighting its value in scenarios
117
      that require substantial supplementary training data.

119   2   R ELATED W ORKS
120
121
      Synthetic human data. The creation of various synthetic human datasets has been facilitated by
122   the advancement of modern synthetic data rendering engines such as Blender, Unity, and Unreal,
123   alongside human modeling tools like MakeHuman (MakeHuman Community) and Character Cre-
124   ator (Studio Chacre). These rendering engines enable a realistic representation of humans in 3D
125   virtual environments, while the modeling tools give creators precise control over the design of vir-
126   tual characters. The creators of these datasets leveraged these tools to meticulously control key
127   design factors, ensuring suitability for specific tasks, e.g., SOMAset (Barbosa et al., 2018), Per-
128   sonX (Sun & Zheng, 2019), UnrealPerson (Zhang et al., 2021), CARGO (Zhang et al., 2024) for
129   Re-Identification, SURREAL (Varol et al., 2017) for pose estimation, GTA5 (Richter et al., 2016)
130
      for semantic segmentation, and Archangel-Synthetic (Shen et al., 2023b) for detection.
131   Recently, several attempts have been made to enhance the realism of virtual human models, with
132   the aim of bringing them closer to the quality of their real-world counterparts. SMPL-X (Pavlakos
133   et al., 2019) and AMASS (Mahmood et al., 2019) used motion capturing devices to capture natural
134   human motions. BEDLAM (Black et al., 2023) tried to improve the diversity of various factors such
135   as skin-tones or clothing that affect human outer appearance, still relying on the SMPL-X. However,
136
      motion scanners imposed constraints on the environment, particularly in capturing large motions or
      events involving multiple humans. While AGORA (Patel et al., 2021) and ScoreHMR (Stathopoulos
137
      et al., 2024) sought to digress from the usage of the motion scanners by fitting human body models
138
      to the human motions in real-world images/videos, the quality of the fitted human models declined
139   drastically on the images taken from a distance. One of our goals for our dataset was to incorporate
140   multiple viewpoints, including distant views of the target scene. To avoid compromising the quality
141   of human motions/poses, we chose to use motion capture devices, while implementing our approach
142   to ensure that final results in the dataset are not limited by the environments in which the devices
143   were used.
144
145   Natural human motion acquisition. Whether we are creating a real or synthetic dataset, images
146   of motions captured by directing humans to perform specific actions based on a description often
147
      appear awkward rather than natural. Because of that, most datasets aim to include humans engaged
      in daily activities (e.g., MS COCO (Lin et al., 2014), MPII Human Pose (Andriluka et al., 2014)),
148
      performing tasks such as sports (e.g., UCF-Sports (Rodriguez et al., 2008), SoccerNet (Cioppa et al.,
149
      2022), SportsMOT (Cui et al., 2023)) or art (e.g., Human-Art (Ju et al., 2023)) to capture their
150   motions and poses in the most natural states possible. However, it is self-contradictory to artificially
151   create a virtual event to capture natural motions associated with the event. In this paper, we aim to
152   detour this self-contradict by initially designing the virtual events (i.e., aforementioned games) using
153   existing but non-natural virtual motions, which are then replaced by real-world motions captured
154   using a motion capture device.
155
      Supplemental datasets for training. Enhancing model performance by supplementing the training
156   with additional data has been a common strategy (Ren et al., 2016; Lee et al., 2019). Initially,
157   this involved combining datasets constructed with the same purpose, like MS COCO (Lin et al.,
158   2014) and PASCAL VOC (Everingham et al., 2015), for tasks such as object detection. Some
159   approaches utilized large-scale datasets (e.g., ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009) or Instagram (Mahajan
160   et al., 2018)), which were not necessarily designed for the target task, to build foundational features,
161   followed by a transfer learning such as pretrain-finetune (Girshick et al., 2016) or PTL (Shen et al.,
      2023a) to adjust the model on the target dataset. As models have grown in size and complexity (e.g.,

      3          S YN P LAY DATASET
189
190   In a nutshell, we aim to create a synthetic dataset in a virtual environment that captures the diversity
191   of human appearances found in the real world.
192   Accordingly, we design our dataset with a focus on two key aspects that naturally diversifies the
193   human appearances captured in the scenes: i) expanding the motion set with increased reality, and
194   ii) capturing each scene from a diverse set of camera viewpoints.
195
196   Diverse yet realistic human motions. We use rule-guided motion design in our SynPlay dataset,
197   borrowing “rules” from six traditional Korean games, also featured in the Netflix series “Squid
198
      Game” (Hwang, 2021)2 . This approach offers coarser motion guidance for the virtual players, fa-
      cilitating the generation of a wide spectrum of natural motions, even including the ones that defy
199
      detailed description.
200
201   Our rule-guided motion design is effectively baked into the overall sequence-generating design
202   pipeline, as shown in Figure 2, that consists of the scenario design followed by the incorpora-
203   tion of real-world motions. The scenario design involves the setup of all the knobs that control the
204
      appearance, players (winners and losers), game dynamics (e.g., how/when each game ends), and the
      human motion evolutions for each specific scenario. All the items within each scenario that do not
205
      have to be hard-coded (e.g., game rules) are selected randomly when designing each scenario. The
206
      motion evolution of each virtual player in a specific game is governed by a graph structure where
207   all possible elementary motions and their potential transitions are represented as nodes and directed
208   edges, respectively. Each node is tied with a pool of motions that fall under the same elementary
209   motion state (e.g., move, sit). As the game progresses, a virtual player evolves its motion following
210   the directed edges and stays there according to the ‘motion timing’, also defined in the scenario
211   design. At each state, the virtual human randomly chooses to exhibit one of the motions in the cor-
212   responding pool. Note that, while a uniquely designed scenario is used for a unique sequence, the
213   same motion evolution graph is used for all the sequences captured under the same game rule.

      234     Before incorporating the real-world motions, we leverage two techniques to pre-diversify the el-
      235
              ementary human motions readily available in human motion libraries such as Mixamo Adobe: i)
              dynamically blending two existing motions of similar types to generate a new motion type (e.g.,
      236
              blending slow-walking and running to generate hasty-walking), ii) using elementary motions as ani-
      237
              mation layers to make a new motion (e.g., raising hands while walking). On top of the pre-diversified
      238     set of motions for each game, a real human player wearing a motion capture device 3 is asked to ei-
      239     ther similarly mimic or newly create motions that align with the given game rule (thus, rule-guided).
      240     For example, for the game of tug-of-war, human players were provided with the game rule, and then
      241     asked to reenact any possible motion with the freedom of choosing the winning or losing side. For
      242     some games, more than one players were asked to actually play the game together to capture the
      243     motions that can naturally arise at the time of physical interactions. As the result of incorporating
      244     the real-world motions, the total number of unique motions in SynPlay increased from 104 to 257.
      245
              Multiple viewpoints. The camera viewpoints within SynPlay are diversified by implementing three
      246     widely used types of image-capturing platforms in the real world: UAV, UGV, and CCTV. They
      247     cover a variety of image-capturing properties like static/dynamic and ground/aerial. Viewpoint di-
      248     versity is acquired by controlling the locations and the focal points of the cameras. Three UAVs,
      249     three CCTVs, and one UGV have been deployed (Figure 3), resulting in seven unique viewpoints for
      250     every game sequence. The UAVs are deployed to fly at various random locations while maintaining
      251     altitudes of low (∼30m), medium (∼50m), and high (∼100m). CCTVs are located at a height of
      252     15m at the front, back, and either side of the game playground. UGV images are captured assum-
      253     ing that a vehicle is randomly roaming on the ground. The focal points are set at several locations
      254
              close to the area where the game usually takes place. For the UAVs, the focal point is changed to a
              random location every 10 sec, where each change takes 5 sec to be fixed at a location for another 5
      255
              sec. Focal points of the CCTVs and the UGV do not change once determined.

      257     Dataset specification. We created ten scenarios for each game, resulting in 60 game scenarios in
      258     total. Frames were rendered from seven different camera viewpoints at 1 fps with the resolution of
      259     1920×1080, resulting in a total of 73,892 images with more than 6.5M human instances. The frame
      260     generation rate was selected as 1 fps to avoid including highly redundant human poses. Taking
      261     full advantage of the game engine’s ability to generate annotations while rendering the scenes, we
      262     provided various types of ground truth annotations useful for various computer vision tasks: 2D/3D
      263
              bounding boxes, instance-level segmentation masks, depth maps, and human keypoint locations.

296   These tasks require the ability to identify diverse human appearances in images captured at a dis-
297   tance. To leverage synthetic data during training, we adopt a pretrain-finetune strategy, where a
298   model is pre-trained on synthetic data and fine-tuned on target real-world data. The detectors used
299
      in the experiments are YOLO v8 models (Jocher et al., 2023) with three different architecture sizes

      backbone was used for segmentation. For evaluation metrics, we use the COCO-style APs which

447   In Table 5, for all training datasets involved in our experiments, we calculate FID (Fréchet Inception
448   Distance) (Heusel et al., 2017) to assess their fidelity and diversity. SynPlay presents the best score

451   which are our goals in designing SynPlay. Moreover, the better FID of SynPlay compared to MS
452   COCO, which mainly includes ground-view images, is also reported, supporting the hypothesis that
453
      adopting multiple viewpoints effectively diversifies human appearances.

455   5       D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
456
457
      Peculiar performance trend on Semantic Drone. Following conflicting phenomena were observed
458
      in experimental results when tested on Semantic Drone:
459
460             – When using synthetic data in training via PTL for data-scarce tasks, involving SynPlay
461               under-performed when compared to cases using other synthetic datasets.
462
463
                – The performance gain acquired by incorporating a synthetic data during training is re-
                  markably large for Semantic Drone when compared to the other two cases, while SynPlay’s
464
                  incorporation showcasing the most substantial gain.
465
466   Most human instances in Semantic Drone are taken from nadir views, while VisDrone and Okutama-
467   action have instances captured with fewer nadir-views. The portion of nadir-view instances in Syn-
468   Play is the smallest among the synthetic datasets used in data-scarce tasks (Table 6). As PTL con-
469   tinues to prioritize samples from synthetic data that closely resemble the seed data (i.e., VisDrone)
470   for training, the reduced selection of nadir-view instances from the SynPlay may result in a lower
471   gain (first phenomenon). On the other hand, the second phenomenon indicates that ensuring greater
472   diversity via using supplemental synthetic data has greater impact on Semantic Drone, which lacks
473   diversity due to its limited viewpoints. Moreover, SynPlay that is less similar to Semantic Drone
474
      while being more diverse in relation to the compared synthetic datasets, shows the largest impact,
      supporting our claim that improving the diversity is generally effective in constructing a better syn-
475
      thetic data.
476
477   Conclusion. What motion a human performs and where a person is viewed from are two crucial
478   factors that make a difference in how a human looks. We create a synthetic human dataset called
479   SynPlay with the aim of expanding the diversity human appearance by diversifying these factor.
480   Ensuring the diversity allowed SynPlay to have a greater positive impact towards model training
481   when compared to other counterparts (train-from-scratch, using other synthetic data) on aerial-
482   view/ground-view object detection and semantic segmentation. This positive impact of SynPlay
483   becomes even greater in data-scarce tasks, where synthetic data is strongly desired as supplemental
484   training data.
