009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Industrial Anomaly Detection (IAD) in low data regime is crucial for automating
012
               industrial inspections in practice. Previous methods have primarily focused on
013
               obtaining robust prototypes using only a few normal images per product. However,
014            these methods seldom account for transferring the characteristics of online query
015            images to enhance the representativeness of the original prototypes in a systematic
016            way. To address the pivot issue, we propose a prototype-oriented fast refinement
017            model for few-shot IAD. Given online query images, we formulate prototype
018            refinement as a nested optimization problem between transport probability for
019            anomaly suppression and transform matrix for characteristic transfer. Then we
020            present an Expectation Maximization (EM)-based algorithm to iteratively compute
021            the transport probability and transform matrix. In the E-step, we use entropy-
022
               based optimal transport, known as the Sinkhorn algorithm, to learn the transport
               probability. In the M-step, the transform matrix is updated via gradient descent.
023
               Finally, we integrate our model with two popular and recently proposed few-shot
024
               IAD methods, PatchCore and WinCLIP. Comprehensive experiments on three
025            widely used datasets including MVTec, ViSA, and MPDD verify the effectiveness
026            and efficiency of our proposed model in few-shot IAD applications.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Industrial Anomaly Detection (IAD) aims to automatically identify defects on product surfaces
033   Liu et al. (2024) and has been attracting tremendous attention Zhao (2023); You et al. (2022); Lu
034   et al. (2023). However, the fragmented nature of industrial anomalies ranging from subtle bruises
035   to obvious breakages, with varying appearances and scales Roth et al. (2022) , making it difficult
036   for fully-supervised methods to detect them He et al. (2017); Kamat & Sugandhi (2020). Therefore,
037   unsupervised IAD methods, trained with massive normal product images, have been developed
038   recently Liu et al. (2023); Lu et al. (2024). In practice, it is not always possible to obtain a large
039
      number of normal images for different products, making existing methods less effective due to their
      inability to generalize across products in low-data regime at test time Huang et al. (2022).
040
041   To tackle this challenge, few-shot learning Snell et al. (2017); Sung et al. (2018); Wang et al. (2020)
042   has been introduced to unsupervised IAD, allowing the development of a common model shared
043   across multiple products and generalizing to new products with only a few normal training images,
044   such as 1-shot per product. This new paradiagm is known as few-shot (unsupervised) IAD Huang
045
      et al. (2022), and primarily involves prototype-oriented methods Fang et al. (2023); Jeong et al.
      (2023); Santos et al. (2023). At training time, these methods typically use the statistics of a few
046
      normal training (support) images to construct a set of normal prototypes, also known as a memory
047
      bank. During inference, anomaly scores are computed by measuring the differences between test
048   (query) images and normal prototypes using various distance functions. Anomalies are detected by
049   comparing the anomaly score against predefined thresholds. Especially, Fang et al. (2023) further
050   employ statistics of query images at test time to refine prototypes quickly. However, we find that
051   point-to-point regularization as in Fang et al. (2023) does significantly limits the ability to transfer
052   characteristics from query images to prototypes. Additionally, meta learning based few-shot IAD
053   methods Wu et al. (2021); Huang et al. (2022) have been introduced to achieve fast generalization,
      whose performance is verified to be far behind of prototype-oriented methods Xie et al. (2023).

081   Observing the fact that statistics of query images have not been fully explored at test time in
082   the previous methods Fang et al. (2023); Jeong et al. (2023); Santos et al. (2023) either from
083   data perspective Jeong et al. (2023); Santos et al. (2023) or optimization perspective Fang et al.
084   (2023), which may result in suboptimal prototype refinement and cause the query features to deviate
085   significantly from their normal prototypes, particularly when the available normal training images are
086
      extremely limited. To address this problem, we propose a prototype-oriented fast refinement model to
      transfer characteristics from query images to original prototypes while suppress anomalous features
087
      potentially present in query images, which results in more precise and efficient refinement, making
088
      the prototypes more generalizable. A comparison between our model and mainstream methods is
089   shown in Fig. 1. Specifically, we frame prototype refinement with online query images as a nested
090   optimization process balancing anomaly suppression and characteristic transfer, with a transport
091   probability and transform matrix capturing these behaviors, respectively. We then introduce an
092   EM-based algorithm to iteratively solve for the transport probability and transform matrix during
093   inference. In the E-step, we use an entropy-regularized optimal transport to align the distribution
094   between the original prototypes and the refined prototypes, ensuring the latter are unaffected by
095   anomalies. In the M-step, gradient descent is employed to maximize the transfer of characteristics
096   from query images to the refined prototypes. Finally, our model is integrated into two popular
097
      and recently proposed few-shot IAD methods, PatchCore Roth et al. (2022) and WinCLIP Jeong
      et al. (2023), to further enhance the representativeness of their prototypes. We find that our model
098
      consistently improves the performance of the two existing methods by significant margins across
099
      three widely used datasets including MVTec Bergmann et al. (2019), ViSA Zou et al. (2022), and
100   MPDD Jezek et al. (2021). The main contributions could be summarized as follows:
101
102          • We present a prototype-oriented fast refinement model that explores the characteristics of
103            query images and it can be integrated into existing methods like PatchCore and WinCLIP.
104
             • We formulate prototype refinement as a nested optimization problem and introduce a novel
105
               EM-based algorithm to solve it precisely and efficiently at test time.
106
107          • The experimental results confirm that our proposed model is effective and significantly
               improves few-shot IAD performance on the MVTec, ViSA, and MPDD datasets.

      2     R ELATED WORKS
109
110   2.1   A NOMALY D ETECTION
111
112   Industrial anomaly detection (IAD) involves handling training images that exclusively consist of
113   normal data, primarily falling into two categories of reconstruction-based methods He et al. (2023);
114   Wyatt et al. (2022); Gong et al. (2019); You et al. (2022); Lu et al. (2023) and memory-based methods
115   Roth et al. (2022); Cohen & Hoshen (2020); Defard et al. (2021). Reconstruction-based methods
116   are trained exclusively with normal images on the premise that anomalies will yield significantly
117
      higher reconstruction errors Gong et al. (2019). To address shortcut, Transformer-based architectures
      You et al. (2022); Lu et al. (2023) and diffusion-based training strategies Wyatt et al. (2022); Roth
118
      et al. (2022) have been developed concurrently. Memory-based methods take full advantages of
119
      pre-trained features to improve detection performance. However, both approaches tend to overfit
120   when the number of normal training images per product is limited Huang et al. (2022).
121
122
      2.2   F EW- SHOT A NOMALY D ETECTION
123
124   Few-shot IAD has developed to address the demand for rapid manufacturing changeovers, with
125   research mainly divided into prototype-oriented methods Santos et al. (2023); Xie et al. (2023);
126   Fang et al. (2023); Jeong et al. (2023); Gu et al. (2024) and meta-learning based methods Wu et al.
127   (2021); Huang et al. (2022). Prototype-oriented methods usually use pre-trained features to construct
128   normal prototypes from only a few normal training images, with a focus on obtaining generalizable
129
      prototypes. Xie et al. (2023) develop graph Swin-Transformer Liu et al. (2021) to extract isometric-
      invariant visual features. Jeong et al. (2023); Li et al. (2024) turn to use Large Language Models
130
      (LLMs) Radford et al. (2021) to create powerful prototypes. Fang et al. (2023) leverage query image
131
      characteristics to enhance prototype representativeness. Although these methods efficiently build
132   generalizable prototypes, they still lack a systematic way for refining prototypes properly.

448   6.3     A BLATIVE A NALYSIS
449
450   We utilize WinCLIP+ to assess the impact of each module in our prototype-oriented fast refinement
451   model, including anomaly suppression via the optimal transport probability T ∗ and characteristic
452   transfer through the optimal transform matrix W ∗ under 2-shots on all three datasets. The results are
453
      reported in Table 2, with visualizations for the MVTec and VisA datasets shown in Fig. 4.
454   Impact of Anomaly Suppression by T ∗ . Anomalies in query images negatively affect robust
455   anomaly detection due to the limited diversity and representativeness of normal prototypes. Therefore,
456   leveraging query images through effective anomaly suppression is crucial. As shown in Table 2, there
457   is a notable performance decline in image/pixel level results when T ∗ is not applied. Specifically,
458   anomaly suppression yields improvements of over 1% on both the MVTec and MPDD datasets.
459   Impact of Characteristic Transfer W ∗ . According to Table 2, W ∗ consistently improves detection
460   and localization performance across all three datasets. However, in same cases we observe that
461   the gains from using T ∗ are more pronounced than those from W ∗ . For example, in the case of
462   pixel-level localization on the MPDD dataset, the gain from using W ∗ is 0.2%, whereas further
463   using T ∗ results in a 0.9% improvement. This suggests that relying solely on W ∗ may introduce
464   additional anomalies, thereby limiting overall performance improvements.

      Impact of CoreSet sampling ratio α. We observe that the Coreset sampling ratio α is crucial in
