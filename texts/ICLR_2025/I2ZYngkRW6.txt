          046   1    I NTRODUCTION
          047
          048   Peptide (shorter protein) sequencing, entailing the inference of amino acid sequences from spectral
          049   data obtained from biological samples via tandem mass spectrometry, constitutes a fundamental
          050   element of proteomics research (Aebersold & Mann, 2003a). This process, as illustrated in Figure
          051   1, is critically important for both foundational and applied investigations across chemistry, biology,
          052   medicine, and pharmaceutical sciences (Aebersold & Mann, 2003b; Ng et al., 2023). Traditional
          053   methods rely on database searching, where they employ a target database (Eng et al., 1994; Perkins
                et al., 1999; Cox & Mann, 2008; Zhang et al., 2012) to identify candidate peptide sequences matching


                                                                      1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      the query spectrum. However, these methods are constrained by the necessity of a comprehensive
055   and accurate database, limiting their applicability in contexts such as monoclonal antibody sequence
056   assembly (Beslic et al., 2022), novel antigen identification (Karunratanakul et al., 2019), and metapro-
057   teome sequencing where established databases are often absent for those proteins (Hettich et al.,
058   2013). De (from) novo (scratch) peptide sequencing directly infers the peptide sequence from the
059   spectral data, thereby circumventing the limitations associated with database-dependent algorithms.
060
      The development of de novo peptide sequencing algorithms has continued for more than 20
061   years (Dančı́k et al., 1999). DeepNovo (Tran et al., 2017) significantly advanced de novo sequencing
062   by integrating Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Long Short-Term Memory networks
063   (LSTMs), showcasing the robust generalization capabilities of deep learning (LeCun et al., 2015).
064   The introduction of Transformer models (Vaswani et al., 2017) further revolutionized the field, with
065   Casanovo (Yilmaz et al., 2022; 2023), ContraNovo (Jin et al., 2024), and PrimeNovo (Zhang et al.,
066   2024) employing diverse strategies to enhance performance and efficiency, achieving state-of-the-art
067   results in de novo peptide sequencing. Building on the advances in natural language processing
068   (NLP), previous deep learning models for peptide sequencing have predominantly leveraged autore-
069
      gressive models as their generative backbone. However, autoregressive models generate sequences
      in a unidirectional manner, predicting the next token based on the preceding ones. This generative
070
      approach is misaligned with protein sequence formation, where each amino acid’s functionality
071
      and characteristics depend on the surrounding instead of just preceding amino acids. On the other
072   hand, Non-Autoregressive Transformer (NAT) de novo sequencing models replace causal attention
073   with self-attention and enable bidirectional information flow and simultaneous token generation. It
074   successfully demonstrated superior performance in the sequencing task (Zhang et al., 2024).
075
      Despite their benefits, NAT models encounter several generative challenges in natural languages,
076
      which also extend to biological sequences. Specifically, adapting NAT models to varying sequence
077
      lengths is challenging because the prediction length is often pre-defined or pre-predicted to facilitate
078   parallel prediction (Liu et al., 2022). Furthermore, models trained with non-autoregressive generative
079   losses, such as Connectionist Temporal Classification (CTC) loss (Graves et al., 2006; Graves & Jaitly,
080   2014), do not scale as effectively as autoregressive models and suffer from frequent training failure
081   due to their more complex optimization landscapes (Qian et al., 2020), constraining the potential to
082   increase the model size and data volume for enhanced performance.
083
      In this study, we introduce CrossNovo, a method designed to enhance the performance of autore-
084   gressive models in de novo sequencing by leveraging learned bidirectional latent knowledge from
085   NAT models. To achieve this, we implement several architectural modifications. We begin by sharing
086   the spectrum encoder between two types of decoders for teacher-student pre-training, employing
087   techniques such as multitask learning (Ruder, 2017; Zhang & Yang, 2021) and importance annealing
088   to foster improved learning outcomes. We then propose the first-ever cross-decoder attention module
089   for distilling knowledge from the teacher decoder to students, with carefully designed positional
090   encoding and gradient blocking for smooth knowledge transfer.
091   Our model, CrossNovo, tested on widely-used benchmarks across nine species (Tran et al., 2017;
092   Yilmaz et al., 2022; 2023), demonstrates superior performance over both AT and NAT baselines.
093   Combining the strengths of both model types through knowledge distillation, CrossNovo significantly
094   improves performance on species where NAT excels but AT models struggle. Additionally, it extends
095   AT’s original advantage in sequencing species such as human and mouse. Comprehensive ablation
096   studies validate our key contributions, while in-depth analyses showcase CrossNovo’s enhanced
097   ability to distinguish structurally similar amino acids and its generalized performance on downstream
098
      tasks such as sequencing human antibody data. These findings underscore the effectiveness of our
      approach and the potential of CrossNovo in advancing proteomics research.

103   2.1   AUTOREGRESSIVE AND N ON -AUTOREGRESSIVE T RANSFORMERS .
104
105   The Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) has substantially advanced the fields of sequence
106   representation and generation. The vanilla Transformer adopts an autoregressive method for sequence
107   generation, where each token is generated sequentially, conditioned on the preceding tokens. While
      the autoregressive Transformer demonstrates outstanding performance across various tasks, it suffers


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      from inefficiencies during inference because of its sequential generation process. To address these
109   inefficiencies, Non-Autoregressive Transformers have been proposed (Gu et al., 2017; 2019; Ma
110   et al., 2019; Ding et al., 2020; Gu & Kong, 2020; Huang et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2023), which reduce
111   inference latency by generating all tokens simultaneously, while still achieving satisfactory results.
112
      Most existing Transformer-based de novo peptide sequencing methods utilize an autoregressive
113
      architecture (Yilmaz et al., 2022; Jin et al., 2024). A significant limitation of these methods is their
114
      vulnerability to early prediction errors of amino acids, which can lead to cumulative errors that
115   degrade overall performance. In this study, we employ a non-autoregressive decoder to capture
116   bidirectional information, thereby providing valuable context to the autoregressive decoder and
117   enhancing overall performance.
118
119
      2.2   D E N OVO P EPTIDE S QUENCING .
120
121   Early de novo sequencing methods primarily relied on graph theory and dynamic programming
122   algorithms, incorporating various scoring functions to evaluate candidate peptide sequences (Ma
123   et al., 2003; Frank & Pevzner, 2005; Chi et al., 2010; Ma, 2015). Although effective, these methods
124   had limitations in handling the complexity of mass spectrometry data. DeepNovo (Tran et al., 2017)
125   demonstrated the robust generalization capabilities of deep learning by leveraging CNNs and LSTMs
126   to learn features from mass spectrometry data and peptide sequences. This success paved the way for
127   numerous deep learning-based approaches in de novo peptide sequencing (Zhou et al., 2017; Yang
128   et al., 2019; Qiao et al., 2021; Mao et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023).
129   Given the notable success of Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) models in the deep learning field,
130   researchers have begun to investigate their application to de novo sequencing. Transformer-based
131   methods offer significant advantages in sequence modeling and can be broadly classified into au-
132   toregressive and non-autoregressive generation approaches. CasaNovo (Yilmaz et al., 2022; 2023)
133   pioneered the use of Transformers for autoregressive peptide decoding. Building on this foundation,
134   ContraNovo (Jin et al., 2024) adopted a multimodal alignment strategy to train models, achieving
135
      state-of-the-art results for autoregressive models. PrimeNovo (Zhang et al., 2024) introduced the first
      non-autoregressive Transformer architecture, incorporating a Precise Mass Control module to ensure
136
      precise quality control of generated peptide sequences.
137
138   Despite these advancements, adapting NAT models to varying peptide sequence lengths remains
139   challenging due to the necessity for predefined prediction lengths to facilitate parallel computation
140   (Liu et al., 2022). Consequently, there is an urgent need to enhance AT models to address these
141
      limitations. To this end, our research advances the field by distilling insights from non-autoregressive
      models to augment autoregressive models with bidirectional latent representation.

153   the target amino acid sequence A = (a1 , a2 , . . . , an ) from the provided input S.

281   3.5   C AN W E R EVERSE THE D IRECTION OF K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION
282
283   An intriguing question arises: can we distill the knowledge from the AT into the NAT to further
284
      enhance the NAT decoder’s performance? While it is feasible to modify the attention layer in the NAT
      decoder for this purpose, potential information leakage must be considered. During AT training, the
285
      true label is fed into the AT decoder. Although this does not leak information for the AT model itself
286
      due to the causal mask, performing cross-attention will inevitably reveal the true token information to
287   the NAT. Conversely, since the NAT model does not take any true sequence as input, attending to its
288   latent representation does not risk leaking the true target sequence during distillation.

      4.1   DATASETS
293
294   To train our model in a fair comparison setting with baseline models, we followed previous work (Yil-
295   maz et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024) and utilized the same training set, MassIVE-KB dataset (Wang
296   et al., 2018), for training our model. This dataset was selected owing to its substantial repository of
297   30 million high-resolution peptide-spectrum matches (PSMs) obtained from various instruments. To
298   validate our model and benchmark it against leading de novo peptide sequencing methodologies, we
299   employed two widely accepted (Yilmaz et al., 2022; 2023; Zhang et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2017)
300   benchmark datasets: 9-species-v1 and 9-species-v2 revised. The 9-species-v1 dataset comprises
301
      approximately 1.5 million mass spectra derived from nine distinct experiments. The 9-species-v2
      dataset significantly enhances the number and quality of spectra in the test data compared to the
302
      original 9species, encompassing a broader spectrum of data distributions with a more rigorous data
303
      annotation process.

      4.2   E XPERIMENTS S ETUP
306
307   Implementation Details. In this study, all inputs, including peaks and amino acids, are embedded
308   into a 400-dimensional space. The spectrum encoder, NAT decoder, and AT decoder each consist of
309   nine layers of Transformer architecture, utilizing an eight-head multi-head attention mechanism with
310   hidden dimensions of 1024. Training was conducted on eight NVIDIA A100 80GB GPUs with an
311   initial learning rate of 5e-4. To ensure training stability, a linear warm-up phase followed by a cosine
312   decay schedule was applied. Model parameters were optimized using the AdamW optimizer (Kingma
313
      & Ba, 2014).
314   Evaluation Metrics. To evaluate the accuracy of our model’s predictions, we followed all previous
315   work and employed a suite of metrics at both the amino acid (AA) and peptide levels. At the AA level,
316   correctly predicted amino acids, denoted as MAA , were identified. An amino acid was considered
317   correctly predicted if its mass deviation from the actual amino acid was less than 0.1 Da, and any
318   prefix or suffix mass difference did not exceed 0.5 Da relative to the corresponding segment of the
319   actual peptide sequence. The accuracy was then calculated using M TAA , where TAA is the total number
                                                                          AA


320   of predicted amino acids. At the peptide level, a peptide was considered accurately predicted if
321   all its constituent amino acids matched their true counterparts. Here, Mpep denotes the number of
322
      peptides with all amino acids correctly matched in a given dataset. Peptide precision was defined
323   as M   pep
          Tpep , where Tpep represents the total number of peptides in the dataset. This metric provided a
      comprehensive evaluation of the model’s performance at the peptide level.

355   Baselines. Our rigorous evaluation compared our model against a wide range of approaches, which
356   are categorized into three main types: Database (DB), Autoregressive Generation (AR), and Non-
357
      Autoregressive Generation (NAR) methods. The DB approach, exemplified by Peaks (Ma et al., 2003),
      utilizes tandem mass spectra from tryptic peptides for protein database searches. AR methods have
358
      made significant advancements in recent years. DeepNovo (Tran et al., 2017) was a pioneer in using
359
      CNN and LSTM architectures in this domain. Subsequent models built upon this foundation: Point-
360   Novo (Qiao et al., 2021) improved the handling of varying resolution levels, while Casanovo (Yilmaz
361   et al., 2022) and its enhanced version CasanovoV2 (Yilmaz et al., 2023) introduced transformer-based
362   architectures. CasanovoV2 further incorporated beam search for improved accuracy. The most recent
363   AR model, ContraNovo (Jin et al., 2024), introduced contrastive learning and additional amino acid
364   mass information, pushing the boundaries of AR performance. In contrast to AR methods, the NAR
365   approach is a recent innovation in the field. PrimeNovo (Zhang et al., 2024), the pioneering NAR
366   method, achieves state-of-the-art precision in peptide sequence generation.

      A.1     I NFLUENCE OF VARIOUS B EAM S IZES
651
652   Based on the experimental results presented in Table 5, increasing the beam size generally enhances
653   both Amino Acid Precision and Peptide Recall across various species. At both the amino acid and
654   peptide levels, accuracy tends to improve with larger beam sizes, though it stabilizes later, exhibiting
655   only minor increments or remaining unchanged.
656
      For Amino Acid Precision, increasing the beam size from 1 to 3 significantly boosts average precision
657   from 0.784 to 0.804, a rise of 0.020. Further increases up to a beam size of 11 yield smaller gains,
658   with the highest precision of 0.811 observed at beam sizes of 5 and 11, but the rate of improvement
659   diminishes. In terms of Peptide Recall, increasing the beam size from 1 to 3 raises average recall
660   from 0.634 to 0.651, an increase of 0.017. Beyond a beam size of 3, improvements are marginal, with
661   the highest recall of 0.654 achieved at beam sizes of 5, 7, and 9. Some species exhibit slight recall
662   decreases at larger beam sizes, likely due to the Seq2Seq model’s exposure bias Ranzato et al. (2015);
663   Zhang et al. (2019); Meister et al. (2020). The model, trained with Teacher Forcing, consistently
664   receives the correct prior output during training but must generate its own during inference, leading
665
      to potential deviations from the optimal solution. Larger beam sizes can mitigate this issue, but
      excessively large sizes might cause overfitting and hinder generalization.
666
667   While larger beam sizes can enhance prediction performance, they also increase inference costs. To
668   balance effectiveness and speed, we selected a beam size of 5 for our experiments. With this size,
669   the model achieves high performance metrics, with an average precision of 0.811 and an average
