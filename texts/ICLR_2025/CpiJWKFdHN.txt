009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The Max-k-Cut problem is a fundamental combinatorial optimization challenge
012            that generalizes the classic N P-complete Max-Cut problem. While relaxation
013            techniques are commonly employed to tackle Max-k-Cut, they often lack guar-
014            antees of equivalence between the solutions of the original problem and its relax-
015            ation. To address this issue, we introduce the Relax-Optimize-and-Sample (ROS)
016            framework. In particular, we begin by relaxing the discrete constraints to the con-
017            tinuous probability simplex form. Next, we pre-train and fine-tune a graph neural
018            network model to efficiently optimize the relaxed problem. Subsequently, we pro-
019
               pose a sampling-based construction algorithm to map the continuous solution back
               to a high-quality Max-k-Cut solution. By integrating geometric landscape analysis
020
               with statistical theory, we establish the consistency of function values between the
021
               continuous solution and its mapped counterpart. Extensive experimental results
022            on random regular graphs and the Gset benchmark demonstrate that the proposed
023            ROS framework effectively scales to large instances with up to 20, 000 nodes in
024            just a few seconds, outperforming state-of-the-art algorithms. Furthermore, ROS
025            exhibits strong generalization capabilities across both in-distribution and out-of-
026            distribution instances, underscoring its effectiveness for large-scale optimization
027            tasks.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The Max-k-Cut problem involves partitioning the vertices of a graph into k disjoint subsets in such
033   a way that the total weight of edges between vertices in different subsets is maximized. This prob-
034   lem represents a significant challenge in combinatorial optimization and finds applications across
035   various fields, including telecommunication networks (Eisenblätter, 2002; Gui et al., 2018), data
036   clustering (Poland & Zeugmann, 2006; Ly et al., 2023), and theoretical physics (Cook et al., 2019;
037   Coja-Oghlan et al., 2022). The Max-k-Cut problem is known to be N P-complete, as it generalizes
038   the well-known Max-Cut problem, which is one of the 21 classic N P-complete problems identified
039
      by Karp (2010).
040   Significant efforts have been made to develop methods for solving Max-k-Cut problems (Nath &
041   Kuhnle, 2024). Ghaddar et al. (2011) introduced an exact branch-and-cut algorithm based on semi-
042   definite programming, capable of handling graphs with up to 100 vertices. For larger instances, vari-
043   ous polynomial-time approximation algorithms have been proposed. Goemans & Williamson (1995)
044   addressed the Max-Cut problem by first solving a semi-definite relaxation to obtain a fractional so-
045
      lution, then applying a randomization technique to convert it into a feasible solution, resulting in a
      0.878-approximation algorithm. Building on this, Frieze & Jerrum (1997) extended the approach to
046
      Max-k-Cut, offering feasible solutions with approximation guarantees. de Klerk et al. (2004) further
047
      improved these guarantees, while Shinde et al. (2021) optimized memory usage. Despite their strong
048   theoretical performance, these approximation algorithms involve solving computationally intensive
049   semi-definite programs, rendering them impractical for large-scale Max-k-Cut problems. A vari-
050   ety of heuristic methods have been developed to tackle the scalability challenge. For the Max-Cut
051   problem, Burer et al. (2002) proposed rank-two relaxation-based heuristics, and Goudet et al. (2024)
052   introduced a meta-heuristic approach using evolutionary algorithms. For Max-k-Cut, heuristics such
053   as genetic algorithms (Li & Wang, 2016), greedy search (Gui et al., 2018), multiple operator heuris-
      tics (Ma & Hao, 2017), and local search (Garvardt et al., 2023) have been proposed. While these

      Recently, machine learning techniques have gained attention for enhancing optimization algo-
076   rithms (Bengio et al., 2021; Gasse et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024). Several studies, including Khalil
077   et al. (2017); Barrett et al. (2020); Chen et al. (2020); Barrett et al. (2022), framed the Max-Cut
078   problem as a sequential decision-making process, using reinforcement learning to train policy net-
079   works for generating feasible solutions. However, RL-based methods often suffer from extensive
080   sampling efforts and increased complexity in action space when extended to Max-k-Cut, and hence
081   entails significantly longer training and testing time. Karalias & Loukas (2020) focuses on subset
082   selection, including Max-Cut as a special case. It trains a graph neural network (GNN) to produce
083   a distribution over subsets of nodes of an input graph by minimizing a probabilistic penalty loss
084
      function. After the network has been trained, a randomized algorithm is employed to sequentially
      decode a valid Max-Cut solution from the learned distribution. A notable advancement by Schuetz
085
      et al. (2022) reformulated Max-Cut as a quadratic unconstrained binary optimization (QUBO), re-
086
      moving binarity constraints to create a differentiable loss function. This loss function was used to
087   train a GNN, followed by a simple projection onto integer variables after unsupervised training. The
088   key feature of this approach is solving the Max-Cut problem during the training phase, eliminat-
089   ing the need for a separate testing stage. Although this method can produce high-quality solutions
090   for Max-Cut instances with millions of nodes, the computational time remains significant due to
091   the need to optimize a parameterized GNN from scratch. The work of Tönshoff et al. (2022) first
092   formulated the Max-Cut problem as a constraint satisfaction problem and then proposed a novel
093   GNN-based reinforcement learning approach. This method outperforms prior neural combinatorial
094   optimization techniques and conventional search heuristics. However, to the best of our knowledge,
095
      it is limited to unweighted Max-k-Cut problems.
096   In this work, we propose a GNN-based Relax-Optimize-and-Sample (ROS) framework for efficiently
097   solving the Max-k-Cut problem with arbitrary edge weights. The framework is depicted in Figure 1.
098   Initially, the Max-k-Cut problem is formulated as a discrete optimization task. To handle this,
099   we introduce probability simplex relaxations, transforming the discrete problem into a continuous
100   one. We then optimize the relaxed formulation by training parameterized GNNs in an unsupervised
101
      manner. To further improve efficiency, we apply transfer learning, utilizing pre-trained GNNs to
      warm-start the training process. Finally, we refine the continuous solution using a random sampling
102
      algorithm, resulting in high-quality Max-k-Cut solutions.
103
104   The key contributions of our work are summarized as follows:
105
106          • Novel Framework. We propose a scalable ROS framework tailored to the weighted Max-
107            k-Cut problem with arbitrary signs, built on solving continuous relaxations using efficient
               learning-based techniques.

121   Let G = (V, E) represent an undirected graph with vertex set V and edge set E. Each edge (i, j) ∈ E
122   is assigned an arbitrary weight Wij ∈ R, which can have any sign. A cut in G refers to a partition
123   of its vertex set. The Max-k-Cut problem involves finding a k-partition (V1 , . . . , Vk ) of the vertex
124   set V such that the sum of the weights of the edges between different partitions is maximized.
125
      To represent this partitioning, we employ a k-dimensional one-hot encoding scheme. Specifically,
126   we define a k × N matrix X ∈ Rk×N where each column represents a one-hot vector. The Max-k-
127   Cut problem can be formulated as:

455   4.3                           E FFECT OF THE “P RE - TRAIN ” S TAGE IN ROS
456
      To evaluate the impact of the pre-training stage in ROS, we compared it with ROS-vanilla, a
457
      variant that omits the pre-training phase (see Section 3.3). We assessed both methods based on
458   objective function values and computational time. Figure 3 illustrates the ratios of these metrics be-
459   tween ROS-vanilla and ROS. In this figure, the horizontal axis represents the problem instances,
460   while the left vertical axis (green bars) displays the ratio of objective function values, and the right
461   vertical axis (red curve) indicates the ratio of computational times.
462
      As shown in Figure 3a, during experiments on regular graphs, ROS-vanilla achieves higher
463
      objective function values in most settings; however, its computational time is approximately 1.5
464   times greater than that of ROS. Thus, ROS demonstrates a faster solving speed compared to
465   ROS-vanilla. Similarly, in experiments conducted on the Gset benchmark (Figure 3b), ROS
466   reduces computational time by around 40% while maintaining performance comparable to that of
467   ROS-vanilla. Notably, in the Max-3-Cut problem for the largest instance, G81, ROS effectively
468   halves the solving time, showcasing the significant acceleration effect of pre-training. It is worth
469   mentioning that the ROS model was pre-trained on random regular graphs with N = 100 and gen-
470   eralized well to regular graphs with N = 1,000 and N = 10,000, as well as to Gset problem
471   instances of varying sizes and types. This illustrates ROS’s capability to generalize and accelerate
472
      the solving of large-scale problems across diverse graph types and sizes, emphasizing the strong
      out-of-distribution generalization afforded by pre-training.
473
474   In summary, while ROS-vanilla achieves slightly higher objective function values on individual
475   instances, it requires longer solving times and struggles to generalize to other problem instances.
476   This observation highlights the trade-off between a model’s ability to generalize and its capacity to
477   fit specific instances. Specifically, a model that fits individual instances exceptionally well may fail
478
      to generalize to new data, resulting in longer solving times. Conversely, a model that generalizes
      effectively may exhibit slightly weaker performance on specific instances, leading to a marginal
479
      decrease in objective function values. Therefore, the choice between these two training modes
480
      should be guided by the specific requirements of the application.

483   5                           C ONCLUSIONS
484
485   In this paper, we propose ROS, an efficient method for addressing the Max-k-Cut problem with
      any arbitrary edge weights. Our approach begins by relaxing the constraints of the original dis-


                                                                                                                9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      crete problem to probabilistic simplices. To effectively solve this relaxed problem, we propose an
487   optimization algorithm based on GNN parametrization and incorporate transfer learning by lever-
488   aging pre-trained GNNs to warm-start the training process. After resolving the relaxed problem,
489   we present a novel random sampling algorithm that maps the continuous solution back to a discrete
490   form. By integrating geometric landscape analysis with statistical theory, we establish the consis-
491   tency of function values between the continuous and discrete solutions. Experiments conducted on
492   random regular graphs and the Gset benchmark demonstrate that our method is highly efficient for
493   solving large-scale Max-k-Cut problems, requiring only a few seconds, even for instances with tens
494   of thousands of variables. Furthermore, it exhibits robust generalization capabilities across both
495
      in-distribution and out-of-distribution instances, highlighting its effectiveness for large-scale opti-
      mization tasks. Exploring other sampling algorithms to further boost ROS performance is a future
496
      research direction. Moreover, the ROS framework with theoretical insights could be potentially ex-
497
      tended to other graph-related combinatorial problems, and this direction is also worth investigating
498   as future work.
