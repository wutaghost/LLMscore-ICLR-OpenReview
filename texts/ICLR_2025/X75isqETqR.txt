009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The utilization of sketching techniques has progressively emerged as a pivotal
012            method for enhancing the efficiency of online learning. In linear bandit settings,
013            current sketch-based approaches
                                               leverage matrix sketching to reduce the per-round
014            time complexity from Ω d2 to O(d), where d is the input dimension. Despite this
015            improved efficiency, these approaches encounter critical pitfalls: if the spectral tail
016            of the covariance matrix does not decrease rapidly, it can lead to linear regret. In this
017
               paper, we revisit the regret analysis and algorithm design concerning approximating
               the covariance matrix using matrix sketching in linear bandits. We illustrate how
018
               inappropriate sketch sizes can result in unbounded spectral loss, thereby causing
019
               linear regret. To prevent this issue, we propose Dyadic Block Sketching, an
020            innovative streaming matrix sketching approach that adaptively manages sketch
021            size to constrain global spectral loss. This approach effectively tracks the best
022            rank-k approximation in an online manner, ensuring efficiency when the geometry
023            of the covariance matrix is favorable. Then, we apply the proposed Dyadic Block
024            Sketching to linear bandits and demonstrate that the resulting bandit algorithm
025            can achieve sublinear regret without prior knowledge of the covariance matrix,
026            even under the worst case. Our method is a general framework for efficient sketch-
027            based linear bandits, applicable to all existing sketch-based approaches, and offers
028
               improved regret bounds accordingly. Additionally, we conduct comprehensive
               empirical studies using both synthetic and real-world data to validate the accuracy
029
               of our theoretical findings and to highlight the effectiveness of our algorithm.

034   The Multi-Armed Bandits (MAB) model represents a framework for sequential decision-making
035   under conditions of partial information (Robbins, 1952). In each round, the player selects one of the
036   K arms to maximize cumulative rewards. The player’s strategy, which guides action choices based
037   on previous observations, is referred to as a policy. The player’s objective is to develop a policy that
038   minimizes regret, which is defined as the difference between the total reward of the optimal policy
039   and that of the chosen policy.
040   We consider the Stochastic Linear Bandit (SLB) model, a variant of the MAB model under a linear
041   assumption (Abbasi-Yadkori et al., 2011; Auer, 2002; Chu et al., 2011; Dani et al., 2007). In SLB,
042   at round t, the player selects a arm xt from an alternate set X , and then observes the reward rt .
043   The expected reward E[rt |xt ] = x⊤  t θ⋆ , where θ⋆ represents unknown coefficients. The player’s
044   objective is to minimize the regret over the total T rounds, defined as:

066   Despite recent advancements, matrix sketching in linear bandits still faces several pitfalls in practical
067   applications (Kuzborskij et al., 2019; Chen et al.,  √ 2021; Calandriello et al., 2019). Upon careful
068   examination of the previous regret bounds, the T term is associated with a spectral error ∆T ,
069   which arises from the discrepancy between the sketched and non-sketched regularized least squares
070   estimators. The magnitude of the spectral error ∆T relates to the fixed sketch size l and the spectral
071   tail of the covariance matrix, implying that a slow decrease in this tail can contribute to linear regret.
072   In practice, this is evident as an inappropriate sketch size can significantly hinder the performance of
073   online learning algorithms. Since the spectral information of the covariance matrix is unknown prior
074
      to online learning, selecting an optimal pre-set, fixed sketch size is challenging. This raises a natural
      question: Can we adaptively adjust the sketch size in an online manner to avoid the pitfall of linear
075
      regret in current methods?
076
077   In this paper, we demonstrate that the answer is "yes" by developing a novel framework for efficient
078   sketch-based linear bandit algorithms. Specifically, this work makes three key contributions:
079   • We revisit the fundamental problem of approximating the covariance matrix through matrix
080
      sketching. We analyze the critical condition for linear regret in sketch-based methods, which
      depends on unpredictable properties of the streaming matrix. From both theoretical and experimental
081
      perspectives, we demonstrate that the inability of previous methods to avoid the pitfall of linear regret
082
      stems from the difficulty of pre-setting an appropriate fixed sketch size.
083   • We propose Dyadic Block Sketching, a multi-scale matrix sketching method that imposes a
084   constraint on the global spectral error by managing the error bound within each block. We prove that
085   the cumulative spectral error upper bound from Dyadic Block Sketching conforms to a specified error
086   ϵ. This approach allows the sketch size to be dynamically adjusted to accommodate the given error,
087   even without prior knowledge of the matrix structure. Additionally, we demonstrate that Dyadic
088   Block Sketching effectively tracks the best rank-k approximation in the streaming setting, aligning
089   with the performance of a single deterministic sketch.
090   • We introduce an efficient framework for sketch-based linear bandits using Dyadic Block Sketching,
091
      effectively addressing the pitfall of linear regret in previous works. Our framework is robust, scalable,
      and capable of achieving various regret bounds through different sketching techniques. By tracking
092
      the best rank-k approximation, our method can significantly reduce the computational cost of linear
093
      bandits when the covariance matrix has favorable properties.

097   Related Work. Two classes of prior work are particularly relevant to our study: matrix sketching
098   algorithms in the unbounded streaming model and sketch-based online learning algorithms. Stream-
099
      ing matrix sketching methods can be broadly categorized into three groups: The first approach is
      sampling a small subset of matrix rows or columns that approximates the entire matrix (Deshpande
100
      & Rademacher, 2010; Frieze et al., 2004). The second approach is randomly combining matrix
101
      rows via random projection. Several results are available in the literature, including random pro-
102   jections and hashing (Sarlos, 2006; Achlioptas, 2001). The third approach employs a deterministic
103   matrix sketching technique proposed by Liberty (2013), which adapts the well-known MG algo-
104   rithm from Misra & Gries (1982) (originally used for approximating item frequencies) to sketch
105   a streaming matrix by tracking its frequent directions. For further details, we refer readers to the
106   survey by Woodruff et al. (2014). In sketch-based online learning algorithms, most existing work
107   aims to enhance efficiency through sketching. Beyond linear bandits setting, matrix sketching is
      also employed to accelerate second-order online gradient descent (Luo et al., 2016), online kernel

213   provides a constrained global error bound for matrix sketching. This scalable method converts any
214   streaming sketch into a matrix sketch with a constrained global error bound. It can adaptively adjust

      matrix at a minimal cost. We employ the Frequent Directions sketch to illustrate this method.

358   Remark 2 (Worst-Case Analysis). Compared to the single streaming sketch algorithm, our method
359
      effectively controls the global error of matrix approximation by limiting error sizes within each block,
      thus enabling dynamic adjustment of the sketch size. Particularly when dealing with a full-rank
360
      matrix with a heavy spectral tail, sketching methods should be avoided to prevent the pitfall of linear
361
      regret. However, this control is impossible with a single sketch due to the irreversible nature of the
362   shrinking process in sketching. In the worst-case scenario, our method ensures that the streaming
363   matrix problem can be dynamically adjusted to revert to a non-sketch situation.

518

519
      in M contexts. The algorithms choose one sample from the arm set and observe the reward based on
520   whether the selected sample belongs to the target label. The reward is 1 if the selected sample comes
521   from the target label and 0 otherwise. We set sketch size l = 20, 100, 200 for SOFUL and l0 = 2
522   for DBSLinUCB. We set the error parameter ϵ = 1000 for DBSLinUCB. The choice of confidence
523   ellipsoid and regularization parameter follows the previous section.
524
      Figures 4b and 4c compare the online mistakes and running times of different algorithms. Our
525
      findings indicate that, for a given dataset, there exists an optimal sketch size (e.g., l = 200) that
526   captures most of the spectral information of the original matrix, thereby accelerating the algorithm
527   without significantly compromising performance. However, selecting this optimal sketch size for
528   SOFUL is challenging due to the lack of prior knowledge about the data. When l = 20 or l = 100,
529   the regret of SOFUL is significantly worse than that of the non-sketched method, OFUL. In contrast,
530   DBSLinUCB matches the performance of OFUL by adaptively adjusting the sketch size to the
531   near-optimal value of l = 200 while being significantly faster than OFUL.

533   6            C ONCLUSION
534
535   This paper addresses the current pitfall of linear regret in sketch-based linear bandits for the first
536   time. We propose Dyadic Block Sketching with a constrained global error bound and provide formal
537   theoretical guarantees. By leveraging Dyadic Block Sketching, we present a framework for efficient
538   sketch-based linear bandits. Even in the worst-case scenario, our method can achieve sublinear regret
539   without prior knowledge of the covariance matrix. Extensive experimental evaluations on real and
      synthetic datasets demonstrate the excellent performance and efficiency of our methods.
