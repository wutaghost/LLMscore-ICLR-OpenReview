011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Adam has been widely successful in training deep neural networks (DNNs), yet
014
               the factors contributing to both its practical effectiveness and ineffectiveness re-
               main largely underexplored. In this study, we reveal that the effectiveness of Adam
015
               in training complicated DNNs stems primarily from its similarity to SignSGD in
016
               managing significant gradient variations, while we also theoretically and empiri-
017            cally uncover that Adam is susceptible to loss spikes due to potential excessively
018            large updates. Building on these insights, we propose a novel optimizer, SignSoft-
019            SGD (S3), which incorporates a generalized sign-like formulation with a flexible
020            p-th order (p ≥ 1) momentum in the denominator of the update, replacing the
021            fixed 2-order momentum. We also integrate the memory-efficient Nesterov’s ac-
022            celerated gradient technique into S3, enhancing convergence speed without addi-
023            tional memory overhead. To minimize the risk of loss spikes, we utilize the same
024            coefficient for the momentums in both the numerator and the denominator of the
025
               update, which also practically streamlines the tuning overhead. We conduct a the-
               oretical analysis of S3 on a general nonconvex stochastic problem, demonstrating
026
               that S3 achieves the optimal convergence rate under weak assumptions. Exten-
027
               sive experimentation across various vision and language tasks demonstrates that
028            S3 not only achieves rapid convergence and improved performance but also rarely
029            encounters loss spikes even at a 10× larger learning rate. Specifically, S3 delivers
030            performance comparable to or better than AdamW with 2× the training steps.

034   Optimizers play a pivotal role in training DNNs.Currently, Adam (Kingma & Ba (2015)) stands

040   gradient descent (SGD) was traditionally deemed more suitable for training CNNs (Krizhevsky et al.
041
      (2017); He et al. (2016)).

      Drawing insights from the analyses of Adam, we propose a novel optimizer, called SignSoftSGD

083   momentum in the denominator of the update, rather than being limited to a fixed 2-order momentum

085   learning rate for faster convergence and improved performance without the risk of training instabil-

148   The primary reason for Adam’s practical effectiveness over SGD in training complex DNNs remains
149   fragmented across prior studies and lacks consolidation. Kunstner et al. (2023) empirically shows
150   that sign descent with momentum yields comparable performance to Adam when training Trans-

154

160   employing gradients as updates, necessitates a relatively small learning rate to prevent divergence,
161   resulting in noticeable training slowdown. Moreover, another drawback of using SGD is that pa-
      rameters with large gradients undergo substantial changes, while those with small gradients tend

504   In this subsection, we further experimentally verify that the claim that the potential overlarge update

507

509

530   6       C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
531
      In this paper, we thoroughly examine the strengths and weaknesses of the widely-used optimizer
532
      Adam. Building on our analysis, we propose S3, an innovative optimizer that integrates three piv-
533
      otal improvements over Adam. Comprehensive experiments spanning vision and language tasks
534   showcase S3’s accelerated training efficiency and superior inference capabilities. Furthermore, we
535   challenge the conventional belief that Adam’s effectiveness stems from simplifying second-order
536   descent, showing instead that its success relies on sign-like descent. This insight paves the way for
537   developing more advanced optimizers. Additionally, We also provide the first theoretical proof of
538   adaptive optimizer convergence from the perspective of sign descent. Most notably, we identify the
539   root cause of loss spikes during LLM training and propose a solution, offering significant benefits
      for the community in the LLM era.

705   A        R ELATED W ORK
706
707    Optimizers in Deep Learning. Nowadays, Adam has become the dominant optimizer in deep
708   learning. The adaptivity strategy in Adam traces its roots back to earlier optimizers such as Ada-
      grad (Duchi et al. (2011)), RMSprop (Hinton et al. (2012)), and Adadelta (Zeiler (2012)). Beyond
709
      Adam, a wide range of variants are proposed (Dozat (2016); Reddi et al. (2018); Loshchilov & Hut-
710
      ter (2017); Zhuang et al. (2020); Shazeer & Stern (2018)). SignSGD, the first sign descent method,
711
      was proposed to reduce communication costs in distributed learning (Seide et al. (2014)). Subse-
712   quently, (Bernstein et al. (2018); Sun et al. (2023)) provided theoretical convergence for SignSGD
713   and introduced an enhanced version. Chen et al. (2023b) applied an auto ML method to discover
714   the sign descent optimizer Lion. This optimizer demonstrated improved performance with a faster
715   convergence rate on various tasks compared to Adam. Recently, (Liu et al. (2023)) introduced an
716   effective second-order optimizer for LLM pre-training.
717
      Nesterov’s Accelerated Gradient (NAG). Theoretical demonstrations by (Nesterov (1983; 2013))
718   indicate that NAG can achieve faster convergence on convex optimization problems compared to
719   vanilla gradient descent, leveraging gradient information at an extrapolation point to anticipate fu-
720   ture trends. NAdam by (Dozat (2016)) was the first to incorporate NAG into adaptive optimizers,
721   modifying the first-order momentum of Adam with NAG. Adan by Xie et al. (2024) integrated a e-
722   quivalence of NAG into both the first and second momentum of Adam, and Win (Zhou et al. (2023))
723   applied Nesterov acceleration to the update rather than the first and second momentum. Adan and
724   Win outperformed Adam on various tasks, but they require tuning additional hyperparameters and
725   consume more memory, compared to vanilla Adam. Lion (Chen et al. (2023b)), despite being a
726
      sign descent method, exhibits a momentum construction similar to NAG (Chen et al. (2023a)). This
      resemblance could be a contributing factor to its superior speed and performance over Adam.
727
728   Training instability and Loss Spikes in LLM Training. Training instability and loss spikes are
729   frequently encountered (Zeng et al. (2022); Chowdhery et al. (2023); Touvron et al. (2023); Yang
730   et al. (2023)) during LLM training, posing challenges to further scaling AI systems. To address
731   this issue, practitioners have employed an ad hoc engineering approach, skipping data batches be-
732
      fore spikes and restarting training from a nearby checkpoint (Chowdhery et al. (2023)). However,
      this method requires manual monitoring and intervention, leading to resource wastage. Previous
733
      attempts to mitigate instability include embedding norm with BF16, but this comes at a significant
734
      performance tax (Scao et al. (2022)). Some researchers found that gradient shrink on the embedding
735   layer reduces loss spikes (Zeng et al. (2022)). Others suggest normalizing the output embedding to
736   lower spike risks (Yang et al. (2023)). (Molybog et al. (2023)) argues that the time-domain corre-
737   lation between gradient estimates of earlier layers contributes to training loss instability. Mitigation
738   strategies proposed include tuning down the   value of Adam and reducing batch size. However, it
739   is acknowledged that these methods are not silver bullets for a fundamental solution.
