009                                                                A BSTRACT
010
011                              Multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have made significant progress in
012                              recent years, yet the interaction between vision and language representations re-
013                              mains underexplored. Prior work has primarily relied on empirical heuristics to
014                              guide architecture design. While effective, this approach can lead to sub-optimal
015                              designs and computational redundancy. In this work, we examine the fusion pro-
016                              cess between visual and textual data. Our findings indicate that in auto-regressive
017
                                 MLLMs, fine-grained interactions between visual and text tokens primarily occur
                                 in the middle layers. This leads to redundancy in the shallow and deep layers,
018
                                 where modeling only selected visual representations is sufficient. Based on these
019
                                 insights, we introduce eRAM-V, an MLLM that balances computational efficiency
020                              and performance. eRAM-V models selected visual features across all layers and
021                              integrates fine-grained visual features at specific layers, as needed. Extensive ex-
022                              periments show that eRAM-V outperforms baseline models with equivalent com-
023                              putational budgets, achieving superior results across various benchmarks.

053      Recent advancements in Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) (Han et al., 2023; Wang
         et al., 2024; Han et al., 2024b; Liu et al., 2024c) have been driven by the successful integration

054
      of pretrained vision encoders with LLMs, as seen in models like Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022),
055   BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023a), LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023), and MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al., 2023), among others,
056   marking a significant leap in the field. These MLLMs demonstrate a wide range of vision-language
057   capabilities. For example, they can generate code from images, convert plots into Markdown tables,
058   and even perform web browsing (Yang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b; Hong et al., 2024). Despite
059   their notable performance, the interactions between different modalities in MLLMs remain under-
060   explored. Recent MLLM designs typically follow auto-regressive architectures (Lin et al., 2024),
061   or incorporate modifications guided by empirical intuition (Wang et al., 2023). While these models
062   achieve strong results, the lack of detailed investigation into modality interactions may result in
063
      unnecessary computational overhead, as we demonstrate in Section 3.
064   To address the limited exploration of modality interactions, recent approaches like FastV (Chen
065   et al., 2024b) have focused on improving MLLM efficiency by refining vision-language interac-
066   tions. FastV reduces up to 50% of visual tokens while maintaining overall performance. Another
067   study leverages an “information flow” framework (Zhang et al., 2024) to analyze these interactions,
068   revealing that most occur in the shallow layers. They propose token truncation in these layers based
069
      on attention scores, demonstrating improvements in reasoning capabilities. While these studies offer
      valuable insights, they lack comprehensive analyses. For instance, they do not evaluate performance
070
      across a broader range of perception tasks and overlook the exploration of more efficient learning
071
      modules that could better balance computational efficiency and performance.
072
073   In this paper, we present a comprehensive investigation of the vision-language interaction process,
074   focusing on the analysis of cosine similarity between visual and text tokens, attention maps, and
075
      entropy (details in Section 3). Specifically, we study the impact of each input token on generating the
      final answer with given prompts. To achieve this, we visualize attention scores at each layer, tracking
076
      how attention scores contribute to the token generation. Our investigation of MLLMs focuses on
077
      two key perspectives: (1) the transformation of visual tokens, and (2) the interaction between visual
078   and textual features. Through this analysis, we observe that redundancy in current MLLMs (e.g.,
079   LLaVA) largely arises from modeling every fine-grained visual token across both the shallow and
080   deep layers. Based on these insights, we propose eRAM-V1 , a model that balances data efficiency,
081   computational efficiency, and performance (see Figure 1 for a comparative analysis).
082
      The architecture of eRAM-V is designed with computational efficiency as its core principle. Rather
083
      than modeling all visual features as input embeddings, only selected tokens, identified through our
084   analysis, are propagated throughout the entire MLLM. This significantly reduces the number of
085   visual tokens and, consequently, the context length, aligning with our goal of optimizing computa-
086   tional efficiency. We define these selected tokens as pseudo-global tokens, since they originate from
087   local regions but exhibit higher norms, capturing coarse global information, similar to the tokens ob-
088   served in Vision Transformers (ViTs) (Darcet et al., 2023). As retaining only pseudo-global tokens
089   may result in a loss of visual details, fine-grained visual tokens are selectively injected at critical
090   layers, primarily in the mid-layers where interactions are most essential.
091   eRAM-V features a hierarchical design, employing different strategies for integrating visual infor-
092   mation at various levels of granularity. Experimental results show that, under the same training bud-
093   get, eRAM-V consistently outperforms LLaVA, VILA, and Flamingo in both inference efficiency
094   and performance (see Figure 1 Left).

115   2.1   M ULTIMODAL L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
116
117
      Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have significantly benefited from the advancements
118
      in Large Language Models (LLMs). MLLMs inherit the extensive world knowledge of LLMs
119   while gaining cross-modal understanding through modality alignment. Notable examples include
120   Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022), LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023; 2024e), Qwen-VL (Bai et al., 2023), and
121   Idefics (Laurençon et al., 2024; Laurençon et al., 2024). These models can process images at various
122   resolutions, from low to high, with increased accuracy and excel at complex reasoning tasks, such
123   as image-conditioned code generation.
124
      Althouth many MLLMs have been proposed, they generally fall into two structural categories. The
125   first, cross-attention-based models, includes examples like Flamingo, Idefics1 (Laurençon et al.,
126   2024), InfiMM-HD (Liu et al., 2024b), and EVLM Chen et al. (2024a). These models integrate
127   visual information into textual inputs via gated cross-attention between the decoder layers of the
128   LLM, offering high computational efficiency during inference. However, the introduction of cross-
129   attention layers adds a substantial number of learnable parameters and disrupts the LLM’s alignment,
130   requiring substantially more data for re-alignment.
131   The second type is based on auto-regressive models, with LLaVA being a representative example.
132   These models project input visual features into soft embeddings via a simple MLP, which are then
133   concatenated with textual embeddings before being fed into the LLM. In addition to the simple MLP
134   vision-language projection, various connectors have been proposed (Li et al., 2023a; Cha et al.,
135   2024; Jian et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024). This approach is typically more data-efficient, achieving
136   competitive performance even with smaller datasets. However, it introduces higher computational
137   complexity, as computation scales quadratically with input sequence length.
138
139
140   2.2   E XPLORATION OF M ODALITY I NTERACTIONS
141
142   Current research on MLLMs primarily focuses on enhancing perceptual capabilities by increasing
143   input resolution and creating more diverse instruction-tuning datasets (Li et al., 2024; Chen et al.,
144   2023b; Liu et al., 2023; Han et al., 2024a). However, considerably less attention has been given to
145   understanding the contributions of individual tokens or features within these models, leaving many
146   architectural decisions driven by empirical results rather than more systematic exploration. OPERA
147
      (Huang et al., 2024) is the first study to analyze the attention maps of MLLMs and identify potential
      causes of hallucinations. Their findings reveal that hallucinations during inference can occur when
148
      the model processes specific tokens, such as “-” and “?”. To address this, they propose introducing
149
      penalty constraints on attention scores. Similarly, FastV (Chen et al., 2024b) examines attention
150   mechanisms in MLLMs, highlighting inefficiencies in deeper layers where attention to visual tokens
151   becomes sparse, leading to significant redundancy. To accelerate inference, they propose pruning
152   visual tokens at specific layers, improving throughput while maintaining comparable performance.
153   Another study (Zhang et al., 2024) introduces the concept of “information flow” to analyze interac-
154   tions between visual and textual modalities during complex reasoning tasks. The authors also note
155   that deeper layers suffer from redundancy and propose visual token pruning to help the model focus
156   on the most important aspects of the input image. Their experimental results show that this approach
157   enhances performance on reasoning tasks.
158   Although previous studies have investigate vision-language interactions in MLLMs to reduce com-
159   putational costs, they have primarily focused on token pruning, a relatively simple approach that
160   lacks deeper investigation into more complex strategies. In this work, we conduct a comprehensive
161   analysis and, based on our findings, propose a novel architecture that provides balanced, holistic
      improvements in both efficiency and performance across a wide range of tasks.

           In this section, we begin with a comprehensive analysis of existing MLLMs, highlighting the key
           findings that inform our approach. Drawing from these insights, we present the eRAM-V architec-
184
           ture, which is specifically crafted to tackle the challenges identified in our analysis.
185
186        In most modern MLLMs, such as LLaVA and Qwen-VL, visual signals are transformed into visual
187        tokens, which are then concatenated with textual inputs to form multimodal input sequences. As
188        these inputs pass through the Large Language Model (LLM), attention mechanism can generally be
189
           divided into four categories: (1) text-to-text attention, (2) vision-to-vision interaction, (3) vision-
           to-text interaction, and (4) text-to-vision interaction. In this study, we focus on investigating the
190
           interaction between vision and language. To streamline the analysis, we restrict our scope to single-
191
           image scenarios, similar to the approach used in LLaVA-1.5. Specifically, our analysis focuses on
192        two key areas: text-to-vision interaction and the multi-modal integration of visual and textual to-
193        kens. By concentrating on single-image scenarios, we streamline the investigation while preserving
194        a strong focus on the interaction and integration between vision and language.

      Strong Attention to Visual Tokens in Shallow Layers To analyze the vision-text interaction
250
      process, we primarily examine the attention scores of each layer in the LLM decoder. In the case
251   of LLaVA, the input embeddings of the LLM are grouped into three categories: visual tokens,
252   text tokens, and special tokens (e.g., begin of sentence). We investigate the attention score across
253   different token categories for each layer. We sum the attention scores of the newly generated token
254   to the prefix tokens from each of the three categories separately, as shown in Figure 3. The result
255   indicates that attention score distribution concentrates on visual and text categories in shallow layers,
256   while shifting focus to special tokens in deeper layers. This suggests that visual tokens hold greater
257   significance in the shallow layers, consistent with the observations made in previous study (Zhang
258   et al., 2024).
259
260
261
      Sparse and Consistent Attention to Visual Tokens in Shallow Layers To better understand
      such attention pattern, we visualize the attention map of generated tokens for visual tokens using a
262
      single example, as shown in Figure 4. The visualization indicates that, despite varying questions,
263
      the shallow layers of the LLM exhibit similar visual attention pattern. Specifically, attention is
264   concentrated on a limited number of visual tokens, highlighted in the attention maps. To quantify

266   prompts, resulting in a score of 0.7, which further aligns with our observations. Notably, these

391   4.1   S ETTINGS
392
      Benchmarks We employ a diverse set of seven benchmarks to thoroughly evaluate the overall
393
      performance of our proposed model. Our evaluation primarily emphasizes two key aspects: rea-
394
      soning ability and perception ability. For instance, Science-QA, which includes questions related
395   to scientific knowledge, measures the model’s capacity for complex reasoning. In contrast, bench-
396   marks such as TextVQA assess the model’s ability to perform fine-grained visual perception tasks.
397   A detailed overview of these benchmarks is provided in Appendix A.
398
399   Implementation Details In our main experiments, we use the Vicuna-v1.5 model as the LLM
400   (Chiang et al., 2023) (Ablations on different LLMs are shown in Appendix D). Following LLaVA-
401   1.5, CLIP ViT-L/14, pre-trained at 336 × 336 resolution is used as the visual encoder, and we use
402   the penultimate layer’s output as the vision feature. Our training dataset is based on LLaVA-1.5 (Liu
403   et al., 2024d), consisting of 558k samples for pre-training and 665k samples for instruction tuning.
404   We also incorporate a multi-task training phase with data sampled from various QA benchmarks
405   (details on training data are provided in Appendix B). We adopt a three-stage training framework
406   (see Appendix H for visualizations). In the pretraining stage, only the MLP layers are trainable. In
407
      the subsequent continued pretraining phase, both the vision encoder and MLP layers are unfrozen.
      Finally, during the instruction fine-tuning stage, the vision encoder is frozen while the remaining
408
      modules are trainable. Despite the additional training stages, our approach results in lower compu-
409
      tational costs compared to LLaVA for both training and inference.

519   5   C ONCLUSIONS
520
521   In this paper, we present a comprehensive analysis of common MLLMs, identifying architectural
522   redundancies. Our findings lead to two key conclusions: (1) In both shallow and deep layers,
523   the transformation of visual tokens within the LLM is largely redundant, with the Feed-Forward
524   transformations in the middle layers being sufficient to capture the necessary information; (2) the
525
      integration of fine-grained vision and text features predominantly takes place in the middle layers
      of MLLMs. Building on these insights, we introduce eRAM-V, a novel MLLM architecture that
526
      achieves a balance between performance, computational cost, and training data efficiency. eRAM-V
527
      reduces redundancy through the use of visual anchors and sparse fine-grained vision-text integration.
528   Extensive experiments show that, with the same training budget, our approach outperforms VILA,
529   Flamingo, and LLaVA, while also offering lower inference latency compared to LLaVA.

      I   N UMBER OF VISUAL TOKENS CHOSEN AS ANCHORS AND SELECTION
881
882
          CRITERIA
883
884
      In this study, we select 65 visual anchors based on the weighted attention of the [CLS] token in
      the Vision Transformer (ViT). Specifically, visual tokens are ranked by their attention scores to the
885
      [CLS] token, where the attention values are averaged across the attention heads (reducing from
886
      dimensions b × h × 1 × n to b × 1 × n). The top 64 visual tokens, together with the [CLS] token,
887   are then chosen to construct a final set of 65 visual anchors.
888
889   We explore three configurations of visual anchors in our experiments: 65, 145, and 257. These con-
890
      figurations yield comparable performance when fine-grained interaction is enabled through cross-
      attention. However, when the fine-grained interaction is removed, performance consistently im-
891
      proves as the number of visual anchors increases from 65 to 257. Despite this trend, we adopt
892
      the 65-anchor configuration as it provides a more efficient solution while leveraging the benefits of
893   cross-attention.
