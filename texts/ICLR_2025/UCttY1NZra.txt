009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Most activation functions operate component-wise, which restricts the equiv-
012            ariance of neural networks to permutations. We introduce Conic Linear Units
013            (CoLU) and generalize the symmetry of neural networks to continuous orthog-
014            onal groups. By interpreting ReLU as a projection onto its invariant set—the
015            positive orthant—we propose a conic activation function that uses a Lorentz cone
016            instead. Its performance can be further improved by considering multi-head struc-
017
               tures, soft scaling, and axis sharing. CoLU associated with low-dimensional cones
               maintains state-of-the-art performance compared with component-wise in a wide
018
               range of models—including MLP, ResNet, and UNet, etc., achieving better loss
019
               values and faster convergence. It significantly improves diffusion models’ train-
020            ing and performance. CoLU originates from a first-principles approach to various
021            forms of neural networks and fundamentally changes their algebraic structure.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Recurrent neural networks (RNNs), convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and Transformers
027   (Vaswani et al., 2017) are examples of a symmetry principle in neural network architectures: they
028   capture local patterns and uniformly apply them across the entire space. These architectures have
029
      laid a solid foundation for modern machine learning systems. RNNs repeatedly apply the same
      weights to the hidden states. This autoregressive form also inspires diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein
030
      et al., 2015)—the patterns are uniform across intermediate states. Convolutional layers share the
031
      same weights in a small local window to slide across a large domain—the patterns are uniform at
032   arbitrary spatial positions. In Transformers, the self-attention function applies its weights homoge-
033   neously to the word or pixel embedding space—the patterns are uniform in arbitrary directions since
034   a per-vector rotation or reflection on both the embedded query and key vectors does not change the
035   attention mask. Different kinds of pattern uniformity are consequences of the associated space ho-
036   mogeneity. These homogeneities (symmetries) have been a principle that continually inspires new
037   designs of model architectures. Recent works continue to push the limit of model performance in
038   vision or language tasks with reduced complexity and different types of symmetry, such as state
039   space models (Gu & Dao, 2023) and more efficient Transformers (Liu et al., 2023).
040   The convolution and self-attention functions’ symmetries are characterized by the equivariance un-
041   der spatial translation and vector rotation—a function λ is equivariant under a group G if and only
042   if ∀P ∈ G, P λ = λP , where the operation between them is the composition of functions. The
043   same principle applies to a basic multi-layer perceptron (MLP). First, the same activation function
044   is used recurrently in the same space up to a linear embedding layer; second, it applies uniformly to
045   each vector component (neuron). The first property is the foundation of deep models using the same
046
      activation function. The second one results in permutation symmetry: ReLU is equivariant under
      G where G contains compositions of permutations and diagonal matrices with non-negative entries
047
      (positive scaling). The symmetry in models is induced by the symmetry of hidden states’ space: by
048
      substituting the equality λ = P −1 λP, ∀P ∈ G into a two-layer neural network f (x) = wλ(w′ x),
049   the network stays the same except that the group acts on the weights (w, w′ ) to obtain (wP −1 , P w′ ),
050   which means the order of rows and columns of the weight matrices are exchanged. While permu-
051   tation symmetry has been a fundamental assumption in neural networks, we take another path to
052   reflect on this axiomatic assumption and raise the question:
053
                         Can symmetry beyond permutation improve neural networks?

      The self-attention function in Transformers positively answers this question. We give a second an-
055   swer and let activation functions be another solution. To further motivate the activation function, in
056   Appendix G we start from symmetry principles to axiomatically infer the forms of different neural
057   network structures from scratch, where we essentially modify the hypothesis that activation func-
058   tions are component-wise. We further show in Appendix B that the proposed activation function and

060   The symmetry group is related to linear mode connectivity explained in Appendix C, meaning that
061   the loss landscape of neural networks is empirically convex modulo the group. Generalizing the
062   group to infinite order fundamentally enlarges the algebraic structure of neural networks.

      Contributions We propose Conic Linear Units (CoLU), which introduces orthogonal group sym-
      metry to neural networks. CoLU maintains state-of-the-art performance in various models including
065
      ResNet and UNet for recognition and generation, and keeps the training and inference speed. It
066
      achieves remarkable gains in training diffusion models.

069   2   BACKGROUND
070
071   Component-Wise Activations Among the most commonly used activation functions are Rectified
072
      Linear Units (ReLU) and its variants, such as Leaky ReLU and Exponential Linear Units (ELU)
      (Clevert et al., 2015). There are also bounded ones, such as the sigmoid function or the hyperbolic
073
      tangent function used in Hochreiter & Schmidhuber (1997). In state-of-the-art vision and language
074
      models, soft approximations of ReLU are preferred for their better performance, such as Gaussian
075   Error Linear Units (GELU) (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016), Sigmoid-Weighted Linear Units (SiLU)
076   (Elfwing et al., 2018), etc. All these functions are component-wise.
077
078   Non-Component-Wise Activations Previous works proposing non-component-wise activation
079   functions are essentially different from CoLU, such as using layer normalizations (Ni et al., 2024) or
080   multiplying the input by a radial function (Ganev et al., 2021). In comparison, CoLU is a generaliza-
081   tion of common activations, keeps the favorable conic-projective property unchanged, and improves
082   the performance of neural networks.
083
084   Equivariance in Linear Layers For symmetries in the linear part of the model, ensuring differ-
085
      ent equivariance improves the performance of recognition (Zhang, 2019) and generation (Karras
      et al., 2021) models, which repeatedly confirm the potential benefits of the symmetry principle.
086
      Group equivariant convolutional neural networks (GCNN) (Cohen & Welling, 2016) put symmetry
087
      constraints in the spatial domain so that the model admits spatial group actions such as 2D spa-
088   tial rotations and reflections. Like in most convolutional neural networks, the channel dimensions
089   of GCNNs are always fully connected. CoLU’s symmetry assumption is on the channel axis of the
090   states, which means that CoLU considers the tangent space of GCNN’s symmetry space, and equally
091   applies to fully connected layers without convolution structures.
092
093   Spatial versus Channel Correlations Invariant scattering convolutional networks (Bruna & Mal-
094   lat, 2013) use wavelet bases as deterministic spatial correlations and only learn the pixel-wise linear
095   layer or 1 × 1 convolution. It indicates that learning channel correlation plays a primary role in rep-
096   resenting data patterns compared to spatial connections, and it motivates further investigations into
097
      general symmetries in the channel dimensions—the embedding space. Low-rank adaptation (Hu
      et al., 2022) and the Query-Key embeddings in the self-attention function are examples of putting
098
      low-rank assumptions in the embedding space to represent patterns efficiently. CoLU considers
099
      another assumption: it assumes potential subspace orthogonalities.
100
101
      Orthogonality in the Embedding Space Ensuring orthogonality of the embedding space in the
102   linear layers is twofold. The hard constraint method uses a projection onto the Stiefel manifold dur-
103   ing training to ensure the orthogonality of the weights (Jia et al., 2019). The soft constraint method
104   adds a regularization term to the loss function (Wang et al., 2020) and learns the orthogonality ap-
105   proximately. Orthogonal CNNs outperform conventional CNNs, suggesting that the orthogonality
106   property helps neural networks gain robustness and generalization ability. The self-attention func-
107   tion in Transformers is also orthogonal equivariant. CoLU is compatible with these orthogonal
      layers to allow layerwise orthogonality in consecutive layers.

707   A    H YPERBOLIC G EOMETRY
708
709   Definition A.1 (Minkowski). A point (called an event) x is defined in the C-dimensional Euclidean
710   space (called space-time). A scalar product on RC is defined as
711                                 ⟨x, y⟩M = x1 y1 − x2 y2 − . . . − xC yC                          (8)
712
713   The hyperbolic geometry can be understood by the fact that along a rotation in thep  space, the quantity
714   x21 − x22 − . . . x2C is unchanged. This scalar product induces a norm |x|M = ⟨x, x⟩M , and the
715   Lorentz cone is defined as V = {x ∈ RC : |x|M ≥ 0, x1 ≥ 0}. It is usually called a light cone
716   since if we regard x1 := t as the time axis where thepconstant c is the speed of light and t is the
717   time of the event x, then the cone is characterized by |x⊥ | = ct, and c is the tangent value of the
718   opening angle of the cone, and we set c = 1 without loss of generality. More precisely it is a future
719   light cone since t ≥ 0, and the past light cone associates to the case when t ≤ 0. CoLU sets the
720
      past light cone with c = 0. The plane of simultaneity (under the rest frame of reference) is defined
      as H(x) = π1−1 (x1 e1 ) = {y ∈ RC : y1 = x1 }. In Figure 1a, CoLU is intuitively understood as
721
      the closest point to the input within the light cone and the plane of simultaneity. The meaning of the
722
      weight w after the activation function is visualized in Figure 1b, where the previous space-time is
723   tilted by a linear transform (called Lorentz transform). In the grouped CoLU case, gluing the axes
724   together is motivated by equalizing the time axes of each light cone (called an observer).

755   The equivariance of activation functions is linked to the linear mode connectivity phenomenon:
      two neural networks trained with different initializations and (usually) on the same dataset can


                                                        14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      be aligned to be very close to each other (Izmailov et al., 2018; Singh & Jaggi, 2020; Entezari
757   et al., 2022; Ainsworth et al., 2023). This phenomenon implies that neural network optimization
758   is approximately convex modulo a group. The group characterizes the permutation symmetry of
759   component-wise activation functions, and the proposed conic activation functions generalize the
760   type of symmetry. This aligned representation phenomenon across different models at a larger scale
761   is discussed in (Huh et al., 2024). Note that there are other types of mode connectivity (Garipov
762   et al., 2018), which does not leverage permutation symmetry and requires more complicated paths
763   such as piece-wise linear or Bézier spline, and we do not discuss here.
764   Given the loss function L(θ) on two sets of model parameters θ0 , θ1 , the closeness of the two models
765   is measured by the loss barrier. There are different definitions of loss barriers, and we define it as
766                   sup Bθ0 ,θ1 (s) = L((1 − s)θ0 + sθ1 )/((1 − s)L(θ0 ) + sL(θ1 ))) − 1                (11)
767                  s∈[0,1]
768   The loss barrier signifies the relative loss increase of the linearly interpolated weights. With one
769   model θ0 fixed, an alignment on the other one θ1 refers to finding the optimal permutation on each
770   layer by matching either intermediate states or weights (Jordan et al., 2023; Ainsworth et al., 2023).
771   The proposed activation function generalizes permutations to orthogonal matrices (where permuta-
772   tions are special cases). The orthogonal symmetry is continuous, meaning that there are infinitely
773
      many ways of alignment. This results in a loss landscape with infinite local minima forming con-
      nected components. The alignment matrices are associated with different manifold constraints.
