011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013              Diffusion models have achieved exceptional results in image synthesis, yet their
014              sequential processing nature imposes significant computational demands and la-
015              tency, posing challenges for practical deployment. In this paper, we present High-
016
                 light Diffusion: a training-free novel acceleration approach that achieves notica-
                 ble speedup while retaining generation quality through an attention-guided gen-
017
                 eration process. By utilizing cross-attention maps to identify crucial segments
018
                 within the image, we selectively compute these highlighted regions during the de-
019              noising process, bypassing the need for full-resolution computation at every step.
020              This strategy maintains high-quality outputs while enabling faster, more resource-
021              efficient diffusion model inference. With minimal loss in generated image qual-
022              ity—evidenced by only a 0.65 increase in FID score and a 0.02 decrease in CLIP
023              score, Highlight Diffusion achieved a 1.52× speedup using an NVIDIA RTX 3090
024              GPU.

041   1     I NTRODUCTION
042
043   Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Rombach et al.,
044   2022) have recently emerged as a highly effective approach to image synthesis, setting new bench-
045   marks across various tasks. By iteratively transforming random noise into structured data, mod-
046   els such as Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM) have demonstrated an excep-
047   tional ability to generate high-quality images with impressive fidelity and diversity compared to
048   the GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2020) and VAEs (Kingma, 2013). Despite these advancements, prac-
049   tical deployment of diffusion models remains constrained by their computational complexity and
050
      high latency, which are byproducts of their large model sizes and inherently sequential nature.
051   To mitigate these challenges, recent research has focused on accelerating diffusion models through
052   various techniques, such as reducing the number of diffusion steps through ODE or SDE solvers (Lu
053   et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022), direct mapping of noise to data (Song et al., 2023), distillation (Meng
      et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024), enabling parallel sampling (Li et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024; Chen


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      et al., 2024; Shih et al., 2024), and optimizing the inference of neural networks using methods like
055   pruning (Fang et al., 2023), quantization (Li et al., 2023b), and reusing intermediate features (Ma
056   et al., 2024; So et al., 2023; Wimbauer et al., 2024).
057
058
      During the image generation process, diffusion models produce images at resolutions specified by
      user-defined parameters. However, there are instances when the generated image contains elements
059
      that may be irrelevant to the original user prompt. This observation led us to a key insight, motivat-
060
      ing the development of a novel approach called Highlight Diffusion—a training-free method that
061   reduces computational cost and latency at each step. Highlight Diffusion strategically prioritizes
062   computation in regions that are most relevant to the given prompt, identified by the model during
063   intermediate steps, while coarsely computing less significant regions. By focusing computational
064   resources on these critical areas, our approach significantly reduces overall computation per step,
065   thereby improving the efficiency of the diffusion process without sacrificing image quality.
066
      To achieve this, we concentrated on two critical aspects of diffusion models. First, feature redun-
067   dancy is an inherent characteristic due to the iterative denoising process, where diffusion models
068   often exhibit significant similarities in intermediate feature maps across consecutive steps. These
069   redundant features can be stored and reused for non-highlighted regions, eliminating the need for
070   repeated computations in these areas.
071
      Second, cross-attention maps are generated in modern text-to-image diffusion models that incor-
072
      porate transformer architectures to guide image generation in accordance with the provided text
073   prompt. The cross-attention mechanism fuses textual information with intermediate noise, generat-
074   ing a cross-attention map for each token. These maps capture semantic information of each token
075   which we validated through extensive experiments. The cross-attention maps, which contain critical
076   information about each token, are captured during intermediate steps and are used to identify and
077   highlight regions that require fine-grained computation.
078
      Through this method, Highlight Diffusion effectively reduces computational overhead while main-
079   taining high-quality image outputs, addressing a key bottleneck in the practical application of diffu-
080   sion models.

083   2   R ELATED W ORKS
084
085
086   Diffusion models have demonstrated significant versatility in a wide range of generative tasks, in-
087
      cluding text-to-image synthesis (Rombach et al., 2022; Podell et al., 2023; Saharia et al., 2022),
      image editing & inpainting (Meng et al., 2021; Tumanyan et al., 2023; Kawar et al., 2023), object
088
      detection (Chen et al., 2023), code generation (Singh et al., 2023), text generation (Li et al., 2022;
089
      Gong et al., 2022), and even audio generation (Schneider, 2023). Their ability to capture complex
090   data distributions and produce high-quality outputs has made them a powerful tool across various
091   domains. However, despite their generative prowess, diffusion models often face challenges re-
092   lated to computational efficiency, particularly due to the iterative nature of their sampling process.
093   Consequently, several recent works have focused on accelerating diffusion models by leveraging
094   architectural properties and feature redundancy.
095
      Several recent works have explored methods to accelerate diffusion models by leveraging architec-
096   tural properties and feature redundancy. DeepCache (Ma et al., 2024) capitalizes on the structure
097   of U-Net and the redundancy present in intermediate feature maps to speed up the reverse diffusion
098   process. The U-Net architecture consists of two main components: the skip branch and the main
099   branch. The main branch which responsible for the majority of the computation, processes the full
100   feature map. By caching and reusing the feature map from the previous timestep, DeepCache by-
101   passes the costly computations in the main branch and performs only the lighter operations in the
102   skip branch, resulting in significantly improved latency.
103   In text-guided diffusion models, the cross-attention layer within the transformer block integrates text
104   embeddings with the noisy image xt at each timestep t, assigning weights to each pixel based on
105   how much the prompt influences the image. Prompt-to-Prompt Image Editing (Hertz et al., 2022)
106   demonstrates how the cross-attention layer can manipulate the spatial layout of an image according
107   to the semantics of each word in the prompt. By leveraging this property, the method enables text-
      based image editing by selectively controlling which parts of the image are modified.

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
143
144
145   Our approach builds on unique characteristics of the diffusion model, particularly the observation
146   of temporal redundancy in intermediate feature maps across consecutive steps. As demonstrated in
147
      Figure 2 we leverage this redundancy by reusing intermediate features from previous steps. To fur-
      ther enhance image quality, instead of reusing the entire set of intermediate features, we selectively
148
      recompute only the highlighted regions, identified using cross-attention maps generated at an inter-
149
      mediate step. This selective computation allows our method to reduce latency while maintaining
150   high image quality, thus offering a more efficient generation process.
151

153   3.1   P RELIMINARY
154
155   Diffusion models are a class of generative models that create images by iteratively removing noise
156   from a random sample, xT , which is drawn from an isotropic Gaussian distribution. The generation
157   process consists of two primary phases:
158   Forward Process. In the forward process, the model progressively adds Gaussian noise to an image,
159   effectively transforming it into pure noise over several steps. Mathematically, this can be described
160   as a sequence of transformations applied to a data point x0 . At each step t, noise is added according
161   to a fixed variance schedule, resulting in a noisy version of the original image xt . The forward
      process can be summarized as:

175   The iterative denoising procedure enables diffusion models to generate high-quality images by ef-
176   fectively learning the reverse of the noisy data transformation.

178   3.2   F EATURE REDUNDANCY
179
180   In modern diffusion models, the intermediate feature map xt at a given timestep t often exhibits
181   redundancy with the feature map xt−1 from the consecutive timestep t−1. Similarly, we exploit this
182   property by reusing parts of the intermediate feature maps that are deemed less important. The cross-
183   attention map is used to identify the critical regions that require computation, while unimportant
184
      regions simply reuse feature map values from previous timesteps.
185   However, for every given interval N , the entire feature map is recomputed to capture global features,
186   ensuring that the image quality remains high. This balance between selective computation and full
187   recomputation enables efficient yet high-quality image synthesis.
188
189   3.3   C ROSS ATTENTION M APS
190
191   Cross-attention is an essential component of text-guided diffusion models, enabling the model to
192   combine the input noisy image xt and the corresponding text embedding τc . During the reverse
193
      diffusion process, the model’s U-shaped network predicts the noise component ϵ based on these
      inputs. The cross-attention layer fuses this information by producing attention maps for each token
194
      in the text prompt, guiding the image generation process.
195
196   More specifically, the cross-attention layer projects the noisy image xt and the text embedding τc
197   through learned weight matrices lq , lk , and lv . This process generates the Query, Key, and Value
198   representations, defined as:

210   Collecting Cross-Attention Maps. We configure Highlight Diffusion to collect cross-attention
211   maps after 20% of the total denoising steps for a good balance between the stability of the attention
212   maps and reduced latency. We identify that the cross-attention mechanism in a diffusion model
213   can be broadly divided into two stages. During the first stage, cross-attention primarily plans the
214   overall structure of each token, starting at the initial denoising steps. This structure is consistently
215   maintained throughout the entire denoising process. In the second stage, cross-attention gradually
      refines fine-grained details, aligning the attention map with the final image to be generated.

      3.4   R EFINING B INARY M ASK
255
256   After selecting a critical token and its corresponding attention map, we generated a binary mask us-
257   ing a threshold value hth ∈ {0, 255}. For this experiment, we set hth := 100. However, this method
258   often results in small white regions as shown in Figure 3 that are considered negligible within the
259   binary mask, which causes inefficiencies during computation. Specifically, the gather-and-scatter
260   algorithm assigns block indices to these small negligible regions, unnecessarily increasing the com-
261   putational cost.
262   To enhance the accuracy of the binary mask, we employed the cv2.connectedComponents
263   function to filter out regions with an area smaller than 1000 pixels in a 512×512 resolution im-
264   age. This threshold corresponds to 0.4% of the total image area and is considered negligible in
265   contributing meaningfully to the detected objects within the mask.

      4.3    F EATURE R EDUNDANCY A NALYSIS
354
355   To demonstrate feature redundancy, we compared the resulting intermediate features from each layer
356   in the U-Net architecture across consecutive time steps. We measured the cosine similarity of each
357   feature map after flattening it into a one-dimensional tensor. For a total of 50 denoising diffusion
358   implicit model (DDIM) (Song et al., 2020) steps, most of the changes occurred before step 10 and at
359   the very last step of the denoising process. Based on these experimental results, we concluded that
360   performing full computation for the first 10 steps is ideal for high-quality image generation. After
361
      step 10, it is reasonable to cache and reuse the intermediate features, given the minimal changes
      observed. Additionally, we set the interval such that it is a divisor of the total steps our our denoising
362
      process, ensuring that full computation is performed at the last step, which also showed a noticeable
363
      difference in our experiments. Please refer to the appendix for further details.

      5   L IMITATIONS
433
434
      Our work has several limitations that merit consideration. First, the proposed method is specifically
435   tailored for diffusion models that rely on text prompts for image generation. As a result, models
436   that do not utilize text prompts are incompatible with our approach because they do not produce
437   cross-attention maps—a core component of our technique.
438
      Moreover, our proposed method shows limited merits in instances where a highlighted token cor-
439
      responds to the global structure of the entire image. For example, when the input prompt is “a
440
      kitchen,” our model might identify “kitchen” as the primary token, representing the overall image
441   structure. In such cases, the corresponding cross-attention map’s spatial structure is spread across
442   the entire resolution, leading to similar latency to vanilla diffusion pipeline through DDIM. Future
443   works may include developing methods that polarizes cross-attention maps to evaluate redundant
444   computation to reduce latency even in these cases.

      6   C ONCLUSION
447
448
      In this paper, we introduce HighLight Diffusion, a novel, training-free acceleration technique for
449
      text-to-image diffusion models. Our method leverages the unique characteristics of diffusion mod-
450   els, such as feature redundancy and the cross-attention mechanism. HighLight Diffusion utilizes
451   cross-attention maps to identify and highlight significant regions, which are then partially computed,
452   while reusing cached features for non-highlighted regions. Our research primarily focuses on reduc-
453   ing latency for individual time steps. This approach achieves a speed-up of up to 1.52× compared to
454   the original Stable Diffusion V1.4 with DDIM in the image generation process, with minimal degra-
455   dation in image quality. We believe that our work opens new possibilities for accelerating diffusion
456   models and advancing research in this area.

      A.1   E XPLORING F EATURE R EDUNDANCY
651
652   For this experiment, we used Stable Diffusion V1.4 with DDIM 50 steps. In our approach, we
653   extracted the intermediate features computed by each TimestepEmbedSequential module
654   within the U-Net at each timestep. We then compared these intermediate features at timestep t with
655   the corresponding features from the subsequent timestep t − 1. To quantify the similarity, we mea-
656   sured the cosine similarity between these intermediate features and plotted the results, as illustrated
657
      in Fig. 5. In this figure, each graph corresponds to a different TimestepEmbedSequential
      module, while the bottom-right graph integrates all the results to provide an aggregated view of the
658
      cosine similarity across layers and timesteps. Our results indicate that there is a significant change
659
      in the intermediate features for all layers before step 10 and at the very last step of the denois-
660   ing process. This finding led us to perform full computation for the first 10 steps and then utilize
661   cached features to perform partial computation for the remaining steps within the specified intervals
662   parameter.

729   A.3   A NALYZING THE R ELATIONSHIP B ETWEEN M ASK S IZE AND L ATENCY
730
731   To better understand the correlation between mask size in Highlight Diffusion and the resulting
732   speedup, we examine the relationship between the total number of blocks gathered from binary
733
      masks and the average latency per step, as shown in Figure 7. While the theoretical expectation
      suggests a strictly proportional relationship—given that the number of multiply-accumulate opera-
734
      tions (MACs) is proportional to the number of blocks—our observations reveal a minimum latency
735
      of 30ms, even for a block size of 1. This suggests diminishing returns when using very small masks,
736   indicating that there is a lower bound to the latency, beyond which further mask reduction offers
737   limited speed improvements. Future work may investigate the source of this limitation, identifying
738   potential bottlenecks that constrain the efficiency gains at smaller mask sizes.
