011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Vision Transformer (ViT) and its variants have witnessed a significant success in
014            computer vision. However, they do not perform well in underwater dense prediction
015
               tasks due to challenges like complex underwater environments, quality degradation,
               and light scattering in underwater images. To solve this problem, we propose the
016
               Vision Transformer Underwater-Adapter (ViT-UWA), the first detail-focused and
017
               adapted ViT backbone for underwater dense prediction tasks, without requiring
018            task-specific pretraining. In ViT-UWA, we first introduce High-frequency Com-
019            ponents Prior (HFCP) to add high-frequency information of underwater images
020            to the plain ViT, which can help recover and capture lost high-frequency informa-
021            tion of underwater images. Then, we propose an Detail Aware Module (DAM)
022            to obtain a detail-focused multi-scale convolutional feature pyramid, which can
023            be used in kinds of dense prediction tasks. Through the ViT-CNN Interaction
024            Module (VCIM), we achieve bidirectional feature fusion between ViT and CNN.
025            We evaluate ViT-UWA on multiple underwater dense prediction tasks, including
026
               semantic segmentation, instance segmentation, and object detection. Notably, with
               only ImageNet-22K pretraining, our ViT-UWA-B yields state-of-the-art 46.4 box
027
               AP and 44.2 mask AP on USIS10K dataset. We hope ViT-UWA could provide a
028
               new backbone for future research on underwater dense prediction tasks.

033   In recent years, with the increasing demand for underwater robot target capture and people’s emphasis
034   on the utilization of marine resources, there has been a growing focus on the field of underwater vision
035   [28]. Dense prediction tasks are a type of task in the field of computer vision that involves making
036   predictions for each pixel or small region of an image. Dense prediction tasks typically require
037   classification or regression of each pixel of an image and effective multi-scale feature representation
038   for classifying or detecting objects or regions with varying sizes [31]. Dense prediction tasks like
039
      semantic segmentation, instance segmentation, and object detection have significant application value
      in many underwater vision scenarios, such as visually-guided underwater robot [26], mapping and
040
      monitoring of marine habitats [41], underwater target detection and segmentation [29].

088   The main contributions of our work are as follows:
089
090   • We propose a novel underwater dense prediction backbone by combining the plain ViT with
091
        high-frequency components and multi-scale convolutional features. It fully leverages the prior
        information of underwater images and the rich semantic representation of multi-scale features,
092
        which enhances the perception of semantic boundaries in underwater images and recovers the
093
        high-frequency information in the images.
094
095   • We introduce a high-frequency components prior and design a detail aware module and a ViT-CNN
096     interaction module. The former can help ViT recover and capture lost high-frequency information
097     of underwater images such as edges and textures. The latter two modules can obtain detail-focused
098     multi-scale convolutional features and perform bidirectional feature interaction between ViT and
099     CNN, respectively.
100
      • We evaluate the ViT-UWA on three challenging underwater dense prediction benchmarks, including
101
        SUIM [26], UIIS [37], and USIS10K [36]. Extensive experiments on public evaluation criteria
102
        demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed ViT-UWA. For underwater image semantic segmen-
103     tation, ViT-UWA-B reaches 75.3% mIoU on the SUIM dataset when using only ImageNet-1K
104     pre-training, outperforming ViT-Adapter-B by 2.9 points and ViT-CoMer-B by 2.2 points. More-
105     over, for underwater object detection and instance segmentation, our ViT-UWA-B yields 30.9% box
106     AP and 29.0% mask AP on the UIIS dataset, 46.4% box AP and 44.2% mask AP on the USIS10K
107     dataset, which is comparable with SOTA methods.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Vision Transformer. In recent years, transformers have achieved significant success in multiple
111   domains, such as natural language processing, computer vision, and audio processing. Vision
112   Transformer (ViT) [12] first introduces the transformer to the image classification in computer vision
113   without much modification of the original structure, achieving excellent performance. Conformer
114   [45] first proposes a dual network to combine transformer and CNN. MAE [21] and BEiT serious
115   [3, 44, 54] explore the potential of ViT in self-supervised learning by masked image modeling
116   (MIM). Swin Transformer [40] designs window attention and hierarchical structure, introducing the
117   locality of convolution operation and saving computation. However, due to quality degradation issues
118   such as blurred edges and color cast of underwater images [18] and the weakness of single-scale
119
      representation, ViT does not perform well in underwater tasks.
120
121
      Underwater Dense Prediction. Dense prediction tasks include semantic segmentation, instance
      segmentation, object detection, etc. Due to low visibility, blurred edges, low contrast, and color
122
      deviation of underwater images, underwater dense prediction is challenging for models trained on
123
      terrestrial datasets. SUIM-Net [26] introduces a fully convolutional encoder-decoder structure to
124   balance the trade-off between performance and computational efficiency while ensuring fast end-to-
125   end inference. UISS-Net [22] proposes an auxiliary feature extraction network and utilizes channel
126   attention mechanism to extract multi-scale features, enhancing the segmentation ability of edge
127   details. WaterMask [37] is the first work to explore underwater image instance segmentation, which
128   designs a difference similarity graph attention module and a multi-level feature refinement module
129   to reconstruct and refine the degraded image features of underwater images. USIS-SAM [36] first
130   applies the Segment Anything Model (SAM) to the underwater salient instance segmentation task,
131   and proposes an underwater adaptive ViT encoder and salient feature prompt generator to perform
132
      highly precise end-to-end segmentation.
133
      Adapted Backbones. Adapters are originally proposed in the NLP field as an efficient method for
134
      fine-tuning large pre-trained models for each downstream task through compact and scalable models.
135
      The emergence of large-scale models has spurred the development of various adapters. Adapters
136   [23] introduce new modules into the transformer encoder to fine-tune for specific tasks, enabling
137   the pre-trained model to quickly adapt to downstream NLP tasks. In [47], the concept of multi-task
138   learning is investigated, utilizing a single BERT model that was shared across several task-specific
139   parameters. The CLIP-based adapter [60, 17] proposes transferring pre-trained knowledge to zero-
140   shot or few-shot downstream tasks. In computer vision, VPT [27] proposes a method that freezes the
141   pre-trained weights of ViT and updates only the parameters of the adapter module during training.
142   Explicit Visual Prompting (EVP) [39] technique incorporates explicit visual prompts to the proposed
143   adapter. ViT-Adapter [8] introduces inductive bias to reconstruct fine-grained multi-scale features.
144
      ViT-CoMer [56] performs multi-scale fusion across hierarchical features, which is beneficial for
      handling dense prediction tasks. Our work explores a novel and effectively adapted backbone for
145
      underwater dense prediction tasks.

233   3.3    D ETAIL AWARE M ODULE
234
235   Recent researches indicate that convolutions enhance transformers’ ability to capture local spatial
236
      information [43, 55]. And difference convolution (DC) can enhance the representation and gener-
      alization capacity of vanilla convolution (VC) [48]. Inspired by these, we design the Detail Aware
237
      Module (DAM) to utilize difference convolutions and detail-focused multi-scale features to enhance
238
      the high-frequency detail representation of feature maps and structure a detail-focused multi-scale
239   feature pyramid, which can be used in dense prediction tasks.
240
241
      High-frequency Detail Convolution. Difference convolution is typically characterized as the
242   convolution of pixel differences, wherein pixel differences are computed first and then convolved with
243   kernel weights to generate feature maps. Central difference convolution (CDC) and angular difference
244   convolution (ADC) are two typical types of difference convolutions, which optimize computational
245   cost and memory consumption by rearranging learned kernel weights [48]. Due to the complex
246   underwater environment, it is necessary to find accurate boundaries to distinguish different underwater
247   objects for underwater dense prediction tasks. However, due to uneven lighting and low contrast in
248   underwater images, the edges between objects and the waterbody are usually blurred. Difference
249   convolution and its variants have been shown to be effective in tasks that require high-frequency
250
      information, such as edge detection [48] and single image dehazing [9]. Considering that a large
      amount of high-frequency detail information such as edge and texture is lost in underwater images,
251
      difference convolution helps enhance the visibility of details by detecting changes in pixel intensity,
252
      thus restoring the edges and contours of objects.
253
254   Difference convolution can be combined with vanilla convolution to enhance the detail awareness
255   and understanding ability of CNNs [9]. Inspired by this, we propose an adaptive DC and high-
256
      frequency detail convolution (HFDConv). For adaptive DC, we first rearrange VC’s weight W ∈

308   4     E XPERIMENTS
309
310   We select typical tasks in underwater dense prediction: underwater images semantic segmentation,
311   object detection, and instance segmentation, and conduct extensive experiments (with different
312   model sizes, algorithm frameworks, and configurations) on SUIM [26], UIIS [37], and USIS10K
313
      [36] datasets, to verify the effectiveness of ViT-UWA. ViT-UWA achieves results that are superior
      to existing SOTA ViT-based methods (e.g., ViT-Adapter [8], ViT-CoMer [56]) and comparable to
314
      task-specific advanced underwater methods (e.g., WaterMask [37], USIS-SAM [36]). In addition, we
315
      perform ablation experiments on the proposed modules and qualitative experiments (As shown in
316   Figure 1 and Figure 5, more qualitative comparisons can be found in Appendix A) for underwater
317   dense prediction tasks. These results suggest that ViT-UWA can elevate the performance of plain ViT
318   and serve as a robust backbone for various underwater dense prediction tasks.

      5    C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this work, we propose ViT-UWA, a detail-focused and adapted ViT backbone for underwater
534   dense prediction tasks. Without altering the original ViT architecture, we introduce high-frequency
535   components prior to the plain ViT and improve ViT’s perception of underwater high-frequency
536   details by a detail aware module. Our method effectively solves the issues such as incorrect category
537   prediction and blurred segmented edges faced by ViT in underwater dense prediction tasks. Extensive
538   experiments on semantic segmentation, instance segmentation, and object detection for underwater
539   imagery show that our method can achieves comparable or superior performance compared to both
      plain and adapted ViT backbones, as well as task-specific underwater methods.
