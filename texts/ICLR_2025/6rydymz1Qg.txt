009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Multi-step prediction models, such as diffusion and rectified flow models, have
012            emerged as state-of-the-art solutions for generation tasks. However, these models
013            exhibit higher latency in sampling new frames compared to single-step methods.
014            This latency issue becomes a significant bottleneck when adapting such methods
015            for video prediction tasks, given that a typical 60-second video comprises approx-
016            imately 1.5K frames. In this paper, we propose a novel approach to modeling
017            the multi-step process, aimed at alleviating latency constraints and facilitating the
018
               adaptation of such processes for video prediction tasks. Our approach not only
               reduces the number of sample steps required to predict the next frame but also
019
               minimizes computational demands by reducing the model size to one-third of
020
               the original size. We evaluate our method on standard video prediction datasets,
021            including KTH, BAIR action robot, Human3.6M and UCF101, demonstrating its
022            efficacy in achieving state-of-the-art performance on these benchmarks.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Videos serve as digital representations of the continuous real world. However, due to inherent camera
028   limitations, particularly fixed framerate constraints, these continuous signals are discretized in the
029   temporal domain when captured. This temporal discretization, while often overlooked in generative
030   video modeling, presents significant challenges. Current approaches, such as diffusion-based models,
031   generally focus on generating the next frame in a sequence based on a given context frame, neglecting
032   intermediate moments between frames (e.g., predicting frames at T + 0.5 or T + 0.25 given a context
033   frame at T ) as represented by Figure 1. In this work, we aim to address the limitations imposed by
034
      such discretization during the generative modeling of videos.
035   Diffusion models have emerged as a state-of-the-art technique for video generation, but they face
036   computational challenges, particularly in inference time. High fidelity inferences from diffusion
037   models require hundreds or even thousands of sampling steps, which may be feasible for single-
038   image generation but becomes prohibitive for video generation, where thousands of frames must be
039   generated for even a short video. The naive adaptation of diffusion models for video tasks results in
040
      significant computational bottlenecks during inference, especially in production settings. To address
      this issue, we propose a method that reduces the need for extensive sampling by starting the process
041
      from previous context frames rather than from an analytical distribution. This continuous modeling
042
      approach not only reduces the number of sampling steps but also enhances fidelity and reduces the
043   model’s parameter count.
044
045   Starting with two consecutive frames, we pass them through an encoder network to obtain their latent
046
      embeddings. We then interpolate between these endpoints using a noise schedule that applies zero
      noise at the boundaries, thus ensuring the existence of p(xt ) at all points. This continuous framework,
047
      builds on existing diffusion models for images while extending their applicability to videos.
048
049   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
050
051
             • We introduce a novel approach for representing videos as multi-dimensional continuous
               processes in latent space.
052
053          • We empirically demonstrate that our model requires fewer sampling steps, reducing
               inference-time computational overhead without compromising result fidelity.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
085
086
087
      Predicting future frames from past observations is a fundamental task in video understanding, critical
      for domains requiring accurate modeling of multi-modal future outcomes, such as autonomous
088
      driving. Early approaches, such as those by Yuen et al. (Yuen & Torralba, 2010) and Walker et al.
089
      (Walker et al., 2014), tackled this problem by matching observed frames to predict future states,
090   primarily relying on symbolic trajectories or directly retrieved future frames from datasets. While
091   these methods laid important groundwork, their predictions were constrained by the deterministic
092   nature of their models, limiting their ability to capture the inherent uncertainty of future frames.
093
      The introduction of deep learning marked a significant advancement in this area. Srivastava et
094
      al. (Srivastava et al., 2015) pioneered the use of multi-layer LSTM networks, which focused on
095
      deterministic representation learning for video sequences. Building upon this, subsequent work
096   explored more complex and stochastic methods. For instance, studies such as (Oliu et al., 2017;
097   Cricri et al., 2016; Villegas et al., 2017; Elsayed et al., 2019; Villegas et al., 2019; Wang et al.,
098   2019; Castrejón et al., 2019; Bodla et al., 2021) shifted towards incorporating stochastic processes to
099   better model the uncertain nature of future frame predictions. This transition represents a growing
100   recognition in the field: capturing uncertainty is essential for more accurate and robust future frame
101   prediction.
102   Research in video prediction has advanced along two primary directions: implicit and explicit
103   probabilistic models. Implicit models, particularly those rooted in Generative Adversarial Networks
104   (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2014), have been widely explored but frequently encounter difficulties
105   with training stability and mode collapse—where the model disproportionately focuses on a limited
106   subset of data modes (Lee et al., 2018; Clark et al., 2019; Luc et al., 2020). These limitations have
107   motivated increased interest in explicit probabilistic methods, which offer a more controlled approach
      to video prediction.

134   Explicit methods cover a spectrum of techniques, such as Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) (Kingma
135   & Welling, 2013), Gaussian processes, and diffusion models. VAE-based approaches to video
136
      prediction (Denton & Fergus, 2018; Castrejon et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2018) often produce results that
      average multiple potential outcomes, resulting in lower-quality predictions. Gaussian process-based
137
      models (Shrivastava & Shrivastava, 2021; Bhagat et al., 2020), while effective on small datasets,
138
      struggle with scalability due to the computational expense of matrix inversions required for training
139   likelihood estimations. Attempts to circumvent this limitation usually result in reduced predictive
140   accuracy.
141
142
      Diffusion models (Voleti et al., 2022; Davtyan et al., 2023; Ho et al., 2022; Höppe et al., 2022)
      have emerged as a powerful alternative, providing high-quality samples and mitigating issues like
143
      mode collapse. These models leverage multi-step denoising, ensuring more consistent predictions.
144
      However, maintaining temporal coherence across frames requires additional constraints, such as
145   temporal attention blocks, which can significantly increase computational demands.
146
147   An emerging method, the continuous video process (Shrivastava & Shrivastava, 2024), conceptualizes
148
      videos as continuously evolving data streams. This approach efficiently utilizes redundant information
      in video frames to reduce the time needed for inference. Despite its promise, this method relies on
149
      pixel-space blending between consecutive frames, leading to suboptimal redundancy exploitation. Our
150
      approach instead performs blending in the latent space, facilitating improved semantic interpolation
151   between frames, as evidenced by previous work (Shrivastava & Shrivastava, 2024).
152
153   Finally, recent advancements such as InDI (Delbracio & Milanfar, 2023), rectified flow methods (Liu
154
      et al., 2022; Esser et al., 2024), and Cold Diffusion (Bansal et al., 2022) propose alternative diffusion-
      based strategies, though their focus has predominantly been on image generation and computational
155
      photography. In contrast, our approach extends these ideas to video prediction, achieving a balance
156
      between computational efficiency and temporal consistency.

      In this section, we detail the experimental setup, comparing our method to existing baselines. We
      then present the performance of our approach across all datasets, analyzing both quantitative metrics
399
      and qualitative results.
400
401   KTH Action Recognition Dataset: For this dataset, we followed the baseline setup from (Shrivastava
402   & Shrivastava, 2024), which uses the first 10 frames as context to predict the subsequent 30 or 40
403   future frames. A key distinction in our experiment is that, instead of utilizing all 10 context frames, we
404
      only use the last 4 frames as context in our CVF model, deliberately discarding the first 6 frames. This
      choice aligns with prior methodologies and allows a fair comparison. The results of our evaluation
405
      are summarized in Table 1.
406
407   From Table 1, it is evident that our model achieves superior performance while requiring fewer
408   training frames. Unlike other approaches that rely on a larger number of frames for both context and
409   future predictions (e.g., 10 context frames plus k future frames), our model operates effectively with
410
      just 4 context frames and 1 future frame. Specifically, we predict the immediate next frame using 4
      context frames and then autoregressively generate 30 or 40 future frames, depending on the evaluation
411
      setting. This efficiency stems from our model’s continuous sequence processing capabilities, which
412
      avoid the need for explicit temporal attention mechanisms or artificial constraints.
413
414   The results, as presented in Table 1, confirm that our method delivers state-of-the-art performance
415   relative to baseline models. Qualitative results of our CVF model on the KTH dataset are shown in
416
      Fig. 3.
417   BAIR Robot Push Dataset: The BAIR Robot Push dataset is known for its highly stochastic video
418   sequences. In line with previous studies (Shrivastava & Shrivastava, 2024), we experimented with
419   three different setups: 1) using one context frame to predict the next 15 frames, 2) using two context
420   frames to predict 14 future frames, and 3) using two context frames to predict 28 future frames. The
421   results for these settings are detailed in Table 2.
422   As shown in Table 2, there is a clear trend where increasing the number of predicted frames leads to a
423   gradual decline in prediction quality. This performance drop is likely due to an increasing mismatch
424   between the context frames and the predicted future frames. For instance, when using two context
425   frames, denoted as x0:2 , to predict a single future frame, the prediction block is represented as z1:3 ,
426   aligning with the setup in Eqn.1. In the second scenario, where two frames are predicted, the future
427   block extends to x2:4 . This setup means that in the first condition, interpolation occurs between
428
      adjacent frames (i.e., from z0 → z1 and z1 → z2 ), while in the second condition, interpolation spans
      a larger gap (e.g., z0 → z2 and z1 → z3 ). This extended gap likely contributes to the observed
429
      decrease in predictive performance, particularly when k = 2 and p = 2.
430
431   As indicated in Table 2, our method consistently outperforms baseline models. Qualitative results of
      our CVF model on the BAIR dataset can be seen in Fig. 4.
