011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The performance of large language models (LLMs) in natural language processing
014            (NLP) tasks is significantly influenced by the quality and diversity of data used for
015            supervised fine-tuning (SFT). Current data selection methods often focus solely on
016            quality or diversity, leading to underperforming models due to suboptimal training
017            data. In this paper, we introduce G RAPH F ILTER, a novel method that represents
018            the dataset as a bipartite graph, linking sentences to their constituent n-grams.
019            This representation effectively captures the relationships between sentences and
020
               linguistic patterns, facilitating the selection of sentences that enhance n-gram di-
               versity. To balance quality and diversity during selection, we propose a priority
021
               function that combines the quality metric with the diversity metric in a multiplica-
022
               tive manner. G RAPH F ILTER iteratively selects high-priority sentences, updates
023            the bipartite graph by removing covered n-grams, and re-calculates priorities to re-
024            flect the evolving data landscape. We conduct extensive experiments using three
025            model backbones across six widely used benchmarks. The results demonstrate
026            that G RAPH F ILTER outperforms all nine baseline approaches, achieving superior
027            model performance and computational efficiency. Our analyses validate the ef-
028            fectiveness of our design choices, examine the subsets selected by G RAPH F ILTER
029            and other methods, highlight the importance of instruction diversity, and explore
030            the role of quality and diversity in relation to subset sizes. G RAPH F ILTER estab-
031
               lishes a new foundation for effective data selection strategies, encouraging further
               research in data selection for LLMs.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION

037
      Large language models (LLMs) have significantly advanced the field of natural language processing
038
      (NLP), enabling models to generate coherent and contextually relevant text across a variety of tasks
039   (Ouyang et al., 2022; Sanh et al., 2022; OpenAI, 2023; Anil et al., 2023b; Touvron et al., 2023a;b;
040   Anil et al., 2023a; Mesnard et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024). Central to the success of these models is
041   the quality and diversity of the data used during supervised fine-tuning (SFT). Fine-tuning on high-
042   quality data ensures that the model learns accurate language patterns and responds appropriately
043   to inputs (Wang et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023), while diversity in the data allows the model to
044   generalize across different contexts and topics (Abbas et al., 2023; Maharana et al., 2024). However,
045   the vastness of available SFT data presents a challenge: selecting a subset of data that balances both
046   quality and diversity to optimize model performance.
047   Recent methods for data selection often prioritize either quality or diversity, rarely achieving an
048   optimal balance of both. Approaches that focus exclusively on quality may overlook the variety of
049   language patterns necessary for effective generalization (Marion et al., 2023; Ankner et al., 2024).
050   Conversely, methods emphasizing diversity might include lower-quality data, which could nega-
051   tively impact model performance (Abbas et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024). This focus can result in
052   models that either overfit to specific data patterns or underperform due to the inclusion of irrelevant
053
      or poor-quality data. Hence, it is crucial to develop a data selection strategy that simultaneously
      maximizes both data quality and diversity for effective supervised fine-tuning.

105   Data Engineering for Large Language Models The success of recent large language models
106   (LLMs) largely relies on the data used during their training process (Zha et al., 2023). State-of-
107   the-art LLMs are generally trained on vast corpora (OpenAI, 2023; Team et al., 2024; Dubey et al.,
      2024). A significant area of research focuses on curating high-quality corpora for pre-training these

      models (Raffel et al., 2020; Computer, 2023; Soldaini et al., 2024; Penedo et al., 2024). Furthermore,
109   Wang et al. (2023) demonstrate that LLMs are capable of synthesizing high-quality datasets for
110   supervised fine-tuning, which leads to a surge of research on dataset synthesis (Xu et al., 2023;
111   Li et al., 2023; Gunasekar et al., 2023; Ding et al., 2023; Cui et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024; Chen
112   et al., 2024a; Xu et al., 2024). These research efforts facilitate the synthesis of large-scale datasets
113   containing billions of tokens for various purposes, resulting in a significant demand for selecting
114   valuable subsets.
115
116   Data Selection Data selection strategies aim to identify the most informative data subsets for train-
117   ing or fine-tuning models by considering quality and diversity. Quality-focused approaches prioritize
118   metrics like complexity, difficulty, or informativeness (Marion et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024b; Liu
119
      et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b;a), but may neglect the range of language patterns needed for general-
      ization. Conversely, diversity-focused methods capture a broad spectrum of linguistic patterns and
120
      contexts, potentially incorporating lower-quality data that could impair model performance (Abbas
121
      et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024).
122
123   Ours To overcome limitations in current data selection methods, we propose G RAPH F ILTER, a
124   novel approach that represents the dataset as a bipartite graph of sentences and their n-grams. By
125   balancing quality and diversity with a priority function, our method improves model performance
126   across various downstream tasks.

135   3.1   DATA S ELECTION P ROBLEM
136
137   The data selection problem involves the challenge of identifying and selecting the most relevant and
138   informative subset of supervised instances from a larger dataset to fine-tune large language models
139   (LLMs). Formally, let D = {(xi , yi )}N    i=1 be the supervised fine-tuning (SFT) dataset, where xi
140   represents the instruction and yi its corresponding response for the i-th training instance. Our aim
141   is to select a subset Sπ of size k from D, utilizing the data selection strategy π, where k is the data
142
      selection budget. The objective is to determine the optimal data selection strategy π ∗ that is capable
      of selecting a subset Sπ maximizing the performance of the fine-tuned LLM fθ on the downstream
143
      tasks Dtst . Therefore, the data selection problem can be formally formulated as:
144
145
146             π ∗ = arg max R (fθ ; Dtst ) , subject to |Sπ | = k, where θ = FineTune(F, Sπ ),           (1)
147                        π
148
149   where Sπ is the subset of the training data selected by the strategy π, θ = FineTune(F, Sπ ) denotes
150   the parameters of the model backbone F after fine-tuning on the selected data subset Sπ , fθ is the
151
      fine-tuned model with parameters θ, and R (fθ ; Dtst ) is the performance metric (e.g., accuracy) of
      the fine-tuned model fθ evaluated on the downstream tasks Dtst .

      3.2   G RAPH F ILTER : M ODELING DATASETS AS B IPARTITE G RAPHS
155
      In our approach, we model the dataset as a bipartite graph to effectively represent the relationships
156   between sentences and their constituent n-grams. A bipartite graph is a special type of graph whose
157   vertices can be divided into two disjoint and independent sets such that every edge connects a vertex
158   from one set to a vertex from the other set. Formally, a bipartite graph G = (U, V, E) consists
159   of sentence nodes (U = {ui }N                                    M

160   structure allows us to capture the occurrence of n-grams within sentences, providing a foundation
161   for selecting sentences that maximize n-gram coverage while adhering to specific priorities. We
      introduce the details of the priority for re-ranking the sentences in Section 3.3.

187   Our objective is to select a subset of sentences, denoted as S, from the entire dataset, constrained by
188   a data selection budget k. The aim is to maximize the coverage of unique n-grams while aligning
189
      with a priority function ϕ(u) for each sentence u ∈ U. As illustrated in Algorithm 1, our method,
      referred to as G RAPH F ILTER, operates iteratively by updating the graph structure to reflect the n-
190
      gram coverage as sentences are selected. The process begins with an empty set of selected sentences,
191
      S = ∅, and a bipartite graph G that includes sentence nodes, n-gram nodes, and connecting edges.
192   In each iteration, we select the sentence u∗ ∈ U that has the highest priority score ϕ(u∗ ), add u∗
193   to S, and then remove u∗ from the set of remaining sentences U. Next, we identify the n-grams
194   covered by u∗ , denoted as Vu∗ . We then remove all edges that connect u∗ to the n-gram nodes in
195   Vu∗ . Subsequently, Vu∗ and all edges between its n-grams and other sentences are eliminated from
196   the graph. Note that the priority of each sentence u ∈ U is computed based on the most recent graph
197   G during each iteration.

199   in Figure 2a. In Figure 2b, the sentence node u1 is selected as u∗ and is highlighted in yellow,

203   this example, G RAPH F ILTER can cover all the n-grams by selecting only u1 and u4 .
204   Our problem formulation is related to the classical set cover NP-hard problem (Garey & Johnson,
205   1979). In the set cover problem, given a universe of elements and a collection of sets whose union
206   comprises the universe, the objective is to identify the smallest number of sets whose union still
207   contains all elements in the universe. Similarly, in a special case of our problem where the priority
208   function assigns the same score to all sentences (i.e., ϕ(u) = 1 for all u ∈ U), and the goal is
209   to find the minimal set of sentences that cover all n-grams, our task becomes analogous to the set
210   cover problem. In this scenario, the greedy approach used in Algorithm 1 can be shown to have
211   an approximation factor of H(r) (Vazirani, 2001), where r is the maximum degree of the sentence

235   Implementation In a brute-force implementation, the computational complexity of our algorithm
236   is O(N ) per iteration. This complexity results from the need to perform operations such as selecting
237   the highest-priority sentence and removing edges, which involve scanning the sets of sentences (U),
238
      n-grams (V), and edges (E). These sets are not optimized for efficient access or modification. To
      enhance computational efficiency, we employ a max-heap (or priority queue) to select the highest-
239
      priority sentence, allowing this selection to be performed in O(log N ) time per iteration. This
240
      reduces the selection complexity from O(N ) to O(log N ). Additionally, the max-heap data structure
241   facilitates the localization of priority updates to affected nodes, eliminating the need to enumerate
242   all nodes and edges.

531
532
      In this work, we introduce G RAPH F ILTER, a novel method for data selection that models the dataset
533
      as a bipartite graph linking sentences to their constituent n-grams. To balance quality and diversity,
534   we use a priority function that combines a quality metric with a diversity metric, allowing us to se-
535   lect subsets that enhance n-gram diversity and maintain high response quality. Our extensive experi-
536   ments demonstrate G RAPH F ILTER’s effectiveness across three model backbones and six benchmark
537   datasets. Compared to nine baseline methods, G RAPH F ILTER consistently delivers superior model
538   performance and computational efficiency. Our analyses validate our design choices, assess the sub-
539   sets chosen by G RAPH F ILTER and other methods, highlight the importance of instruction diversity,
      and examine the role of quality and diversity relative to subset sizes.

      In this work, we present G RAPH F ILTER, a data selection method for supervised fine-tuning of large
543   language models (LLMs). We acknowledge the ethical considerations related to data usage, poten-
544   tial biases, and the societal impact of LLMs. All datasets utilized in our experiments are publicly
545   available and have been used extensively in prior research. We have adhered to all applicable li-
546   censes and terms of use for these datasets. However, we recognize that biases present in the training
547   data can be propagated or even amplified by LLMs. To mitigate this risk, we recommend that prac-
548   titioners applying G RAPH F ILTER conduct thorough analyses of the selected data subsets to identify
549   and address potential biases. Furthermore, while our goal is to enhance model performance and
550   computational efficiency, we are aware that improved models could be misused in ways that are
551
      harmful or unethical. We advocate for the responsible deployment of LLMs and encourage users to
      follow ethical guidelines and best practices to prevent misuse.

921   A.1     BASELINES
922
923   In this work, we compare G RAPH F ILTER against following baselines:
924            • R ANDOM selects a random subset of size k from the entire dataset, where k is the desig-
925              nated data selection budget.
926            • L ONGEST chooses the top-k instances from the entire dataset, ranking them in descending
927              order based on the number of words in each instruction.
928            • P ERPLEXITY selects the top-k instances from the entire dataset, sorted in descending order
929              according to the perplexity values of the instructions. For the perplexity computation in this
930              work, we utilize GPT 2 (Radford et al., 2019).8
931
               • A RMO RM represents one of the state-of-the-art reward models (Wang et al., 2024). It
                 evaluates multiple rewards from diverse perspectives and integrates these rewards using a
932
                 gating network.
933
               • A LPAG ASUS employs GPT-3.5- TURBO to assess data quality (Chen et al., 2024b). Given
934              the improved model performance and limited budget, we substitute GEMMA -2-27 B - IT in
935              this work, using the prompt illustrated in Figure 5. GEMMA -2-27 B - IT is the state-of-the-art
936              open large language model (LLM) and significantly surpasses GPT-3.5- TURBO according
937              to the Chatbot Arena Leaderboard.9
938            • D EITA utilizes C HAT GPT to create a quality estimation dataset and fine-tune large lan-
939              guage models (LLMs) for evaluating data quality (Liu et al., 2024). We employ the official
940              codes and models provided by Liu et al. (2024) for data selection.10
941            • S UPER F ILTER refers to the Instruction-Following Difficulty (IFD) metric, which is calcu-
942
                 lated using smaller language models. Introduced by Li et al. (2024b), this method is shown
                 by Li et al. (2024a) to provide IFD scores from smaller models that are as reliable as those
943
                 from larger models. In this study, GPT 2 is used for computing these scores (Radford et al.,
944
                 2019).
945            • K MEANS involves clustering training instances using a state-of-the-art sentence embed-
946              ding model and selecting instances that are nearest to their respective cluster centroids
947              (Arthur & Vassilvitskii, 2007). In this work, we begin by sampling 50K instances from
948              the entire dataset and encoding their instructions into sentence embeddings using the BGE -
949              LARGE - EN - V 1.5 model.11 These embeddings are used for training the K MEANS model
950              with 10K clusters. Once the K MEANS model is established, we cluster the sentence em-
951              beddings of instructions for the entire dataset and select the instances closest to each cluster
952              centroid.
953
               • I NS TAG is designed to analyze the SFT dataset by tagging the topics of training instances.
                 It can be used to select a subset with the most diverse topics from the entire dataset (Lu
954
                 et al., 2024). We utilize the official codes and models released by Lu et al. (2024) for data
955
                 selection.12

987    A.3   E VALUATION
988
989    In this work, we evaluate the approaches on six widely used benchmarks:
990
              • MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021) is a benchmark designed to assess knowledge acquired
991
                during pretraining, by evaluating models exclusively in zero-shot and few-shot settings.
992             It covers 57 subjects across STEM, the humanities, social sciences, and more, totaling
993             approximately 14,000 test examples.
994           • ARC (Clark et al., 2018) is a multiple-choice question-answering dataset containing ques-
995             tions from science exams for grades 3 to 9, amounting to approximately 4,000 test exam-
996             ples.
997           • HellaSwag (Zellers et al., 2019) is a challenging dataset for evaluating commonsense nat-
998             ural language inference, which is particularly difficult for state-of-the-art models, though
999             its questions are trivial for humans. It contains approximately 10,000 test examples.
1000
              • GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) comprises a collection of diverse grade school math word
                problems created by human problem writers, containing approximately 1,000 test exam-
1001
                ples.
1002
              • AlpacaEval-2.0 (Dubois et al., 2024) is an automated tool for evaluating instruction-
1003            following language models. Its test set consists of 805 instructions generated by large
1004            language models (LLMs). Models are evaluated based on the winning rate against a refer-
1005            ence answer, judged by a state-of-the-art LLM, such as GPT-4. AlpacaEval-2.0 is an
1006            upgraded version of the original AlpacaEval, featuring reduced length bias for a fairer
1007            evaluation of responses of varying lengths.
1008          • MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2023) is a multi-turn test set containing 80 questions that cover
1009            8 aspects: writing, roleplay, reasoning, math, coding, extraction, STEM, and humanities.
1010            A state-of-the-art LLM, such as GPT-4, is used to score model outputs on a scale from 1 to
1011
                10.
