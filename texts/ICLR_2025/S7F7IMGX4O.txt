009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Text-to-video generation has made significant strides, but replicating the capabil-
012            ities of advanced systems like OpenAI’s Sora remains challenging due to their
013            closed-source nature. Existing open-source methods struggle to achieve compara-
014            ble performance, often hindered by ineffective agent collaboration and inadequate
015            training data quality. In this paper, we introduce Mora, a novel multi-agent frame-
016            work that leverages existing open-source modules to replicate Sora’s functionalities.
017            We address these fundamental limitations by proposing three key techniques: (1)
018
               multi-agent fine-tuning with a self-modulation factor to enhance inter-agent co-
               ordination, (2) a data-free training strategy that uses large models to synthesize
019
               training data, and (3) a human-in-the-loop mechanism combined with multimodal
020
               large language models for data filtering to ensure high-quality training datasets.
021            Our comprehensive experiments on six video generation tasks demonstrate that
022            Mora achieves performance comparable to Sora on VBench (Huang et al., 2024),
023            outperforming existing open-source methods across various tasks. Specifically, in
024            the text-to-video generation task, Mora achieved a Video Quality score of 0.800,
025            surpassing Sora’s 0.797 and outperforming all other baseline models across six
026            key metrics. Additionally, in the image-to-video generation task, Mora achieved
027            a perfect Dynamic Degree score of 1.00, demonstrating exceptional capability in
028            enhancing motion realism and achieving higher Imaging Quality than Sora. These
029
               results highlight the potential of collaborative multi-agent systems and human-in-
               the-loop mechanisms in advancing text-to-video generation.

034
      Generative AI technologies have significantly transformed various industries, with substantial ad-
035
      vancements particularly notable in visual AI through image generation models like Midjourney (Mid-
036   journey, 2023), Stable Diffusion 3 (Esser et al., 2024), and DALL-E 3 (Betker et al., 2023). These
037   models have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in generating high-quality images from textual
038   descriptions. However, progress in text-to-video generation, especially for videos exceeding 10
039   seconds, has not kept pace. Recent developments such as Pika (pik) and Gen-3 (Gen, b) have shown
040   potential but are limited to producing short video clips.
041
      A major breakthrough occurred with OpenAI’s release of Sora in February 2024—a text-to-video
042   model capable of generating minute-long videos that closely align with textual prompts. Sora
043   excels in various video tasks, including editing, extending footage, offering multi-view perspectives,
044   and adhering closely to user instructions (OpenAI, 2024a). Despite its impressive capabilities,
045   Sora remains a closed-source system, which poses significant barriers to academic research and
046   development. Its black-box nature hinders the community’s ability to study, replicate, and build
047   upon its functionalities, thereby slowing progress in the field. Attempts to reverse-engineer Sora are
048   exploring potential techniques like diffusion transformers and spatial patch strategies (Sohl-Dickstein
049   et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020; Bao et al., 2023; Peebles & Xie, 2023), but a large gap still exists due to
050
      the intensive computation required to train everything from scratch with a single model.
051   To address these challenges, we propose Mora, a novel multi-agent framework that leverages ideas
052   from standardized operating procedures (SOPs) and employs multiple agents using existing open-
053   source modules to replicate the complex functionalities of Sora. While SOPs and multi-agent systems
      have been utilized in text-based tasks (Hong et al., 2023), applying them to text-to-video generation

074   Moreover, existing multi-agent video generation approaches struggle to balance pipeline automation

      Collecting high-quality video data for training is time-consuming and computationally expen-
077   sive (Chen et al., 2024a), and the scarcity of such data hampers the performance of open-source
078   models. Furthermore, the quality of available datasets varies widely, making it challenging to train
079   models that can match the performance of proprietary systems like Sora. To overcome these chal-
080   lenges, leveraging human-in-the-loop mechanisms for data filtering becomes essential. By integrating
081   human expertise in the data synthesis process, we can ensure that the training data is of high quality,
082   which is crucial for training effective video generation models.
083   In this paper, we address these fundamental limitations by introducing several key techniques in
084   Mora. First, we develop a multi-agent fine-tuning approach with a novel self-modulation factor that
085   enhances coordination among agents, allowing them to collaborate effectively to achieve common
086   goals. Second, we employ a data-free training strategy that uses large models to synthesize training
087   data, reducing reliance on large labeled datasets and enabling efficient model training without
088   extensive data collection efforts. Third, we leverage human-in-the-loop mechanisms, in combination
089   with multimodal large language models, for data filtering during the training data synthesis process.
090   This approach ensures the quality of the synthesized training data, leading to improved performance
091
      of the video generation pipeline. We summarize the overall pipeline and supported tasks in Figure 1.
092   By integrating human-in-the-loop mechanisms with multimodal large language models for data
093   filtering in our data-free training strategy, we significantly enhance the quality of the training data
094   and, consequently, the generated videos. These techniques collectively improve inter-agent and
095   agent-human collaboration, enhance the quality and diversity of the generated videos, and expand the
096   system’s capabilities to match those of Sora. Our comprehensive experiments on six video generation
097
      tasks demonstrate that Mora achieves performance comparable to Sora on VBench (Huang et al.,
      2024), closely approaching Sora and outperforming existing open-source methods across various
098
      tasks. Specifically, in the text-to-video generation, Mora achieves a Video Quality score of 0.800,
099
      surpassing Sora’s 0.797, and outperforms all other baselines. In the Image-to-Video Generation task,
100   Mora matches Sora in Video-Text Integration (0.90) and Motion Smoothness (0.98 vs. 0.99), and
101   surpasses Sora in Imaging Quality (0.67 vs. 0.63) and Dynamic Degree (1.00 vs. 0.75). Additionally,
102   in Video-to-Video Editing, Mora matches Sora in both Imaging Quality and Temporal Style, each
103   scoring 0.52 and 0.24, respectively. These results demonstrate Mora’s ability to not only replicate but
104   also enhance the functionalities of Sora, providing a promising platform for future research.

120   2.1   T EXT- TO -V IDEO G ENERATION
121
122   Generating videos based on textual descriptions has been a long-discussed topic. While early
123   efforts in the field were primarily rooted in GANs (Wang et al., 2020; Chu et al., 2020) and VQ-
124   VAE (Yan et al., 2021), recent breakthroughs in generative video models, driven by foundational
125   work in transformer-based architectures and diffusion models, have significantly advanced academic
126   research. Auto-regressive transformers are early leveraged in video generation (Wu et al., 2022;
127
      Hong et al., 2022b; Kondratyuk et al., 2023). These models are designed to generate video sequences
      frame by frame, predicting each new frame based on the previously generated frames. Parallelly,
128
      the adaptation of masked language models (He et al., 2021) for visual contexts, as demonstrated
129
      by (Gupta et al., 2022; Villegas et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2023; Gupta et al., 2023), underscores the
130   versatility of transformers in video generation. The recently proposed VideoPoet (Kondratyuk et al.,
131   2023) leverages an auto-regressive language model and can multitask on a variety of video-centric
132   inputs and outputs.
133
      In another line, large-scale diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020) show
134
      competitive performance in video generation (Ho et al., 2022a; Singer et al., 2022; Khachatryan et al.,
135
      2023; Wu et al., 2023a; Du et al., 2024). By learning to gradually denoise a sample from a normal
136   distribution, diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020) implement an iterative
137   refinement process for video synthesis. Initially developed for image generation (Rombach et al.,
138   2022c; Podell et al., 2023), they have been adapted and extended to handle the complexities of video
139   data. This adaptation began with extending image generation principles to video (Ho et al., 2022b;a;
140   Singer et al., 2022), by using a 3D U-Net structure instead of conventional image diffusion U-Net.
141   In the follow-up, latent diffusion models (LDMs) (Rombach et al., 2022b) are integrated into video
142   generation (Zhou et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2024a), showcasing
143   enhanced capabilities to capture the nuanced dynamics of video content. For instance, Stable Video
144
      Diffusion (Blattmann et al., 2023) can conduct multi-view synthesis from a single image while Emu
      Video (Girdhar et al., 2023) uses just two diffusion models to generate higher-resolution videos.
145
146   Researchers have delved into the potential of diffusion models for a variety of video manipulation
147   tasks. Notably, Dreamix (Molad et al., 2023) and MagicEdit (Liew et al., 2023) have been introduced
148   for general video editing, utilizing large-scale video-text datasets. Conversely, other models employ
149   pre-trained models for video editing tasks in a zero-shot manner (Ceylan et al., 2023; Couairon et al.,
150
      2023; Yang et al., 2023; Chai et al., 2023). SEINE (Chen et al., 2023c) is specially designed for
      generative transition between scenes and video prediction. The introduction of diffusion transform-
151
      ers (Peebles & Xie, 2023; Bao et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024a) further revolutionized video generation,
152   culminating in advanced solutions like Latte (Ma et al., 2024b) and Sora (OpenAI, 2024a). There is
153   also utilization in a specific domain such as Bora (Sun et al., 2024b) in biomedical scenarios. Sora’s
154   ability to produce minute-long videos of high visual quality that faithfully follow human instructions
155   heralds a new era in video generation.

159   Large models have enabled agents to excel across a broad spectrum of applications, showcasing their
160   versatility and effectiveness. They have greatly advanced collaborative multi-agent structures for
161   multimodal tasks in areas such as scientific research (Tang et al., 2024), software development (Hong
      et al., 2023; Qian et al., 2023) and society simulation (Park et al., 2023). Compared to individual

      agents, the collaboration of multiple autonomous agents, each equipped with unique strategies
163   and behaviors and engaged in communication with one another, can tackle more dynamic and
164   complex tasks (Guo et al., 2024). Through a cooperative agent framework known as role-playing, Li
165   et al. (2024b) enables agents to collaborate and solve complex tasks effectively. Park et al. (2023)
166   designed a community of 25 generative agents capable of planning, communicating, and forming

172
      programming framework that can be used to implement diverse multi-agent applications across
173
      different domains, using a variety of agents and conversation patterns. This motivates our focus on
174   applying multi-agent frameworks on text-to-video generation tasks, enabling agents to collaborate
175   seamlessly from project inception to completion.

178   3     M ORA : A M ULTI -AGENT F RAMEWORK FOR V IDEO G ENERATION
179
180   Current text-to-video generation models directly generate videos from textual inputs, which prevents
181
      users from supervising key aspects of video quality, style, and other important elements in real-time.
      To address this limitation, we propose a novel multi-agent system coupled with a self-modulated
182
      training algorithm specifically designed for the video generation task. In the subsequent sections,
183
      we outline our approach in detail. Section 3.1 describes the problem and design of our multi-agent
184   system and the architecture of our model. Finally, in Section 3.2 and Appendix A.7, we present our
185   data-free multi-agent fine-tuning method.

      3.1   AGENT A RCHITECTURE OF M ORA
188
189   In this section, we introduce the problem and our multi-agent video generation system. To address
190   the complexity of generating high-quality, long-duration videos, we propose a multi-agent framework
191   where the generative model G is composed of n collaborating agents {A1 , A2 , · · · , An }. As shown
192   in Figure 1, each agent specializes in a specific subtask and collaborates together within the video
193   generation pipeline. We further introduce the definition of each agent below.
194
195   Problem Definition Let P ∈ P denote a textual prompt from the space of all possible prompts
196   P, describing the desired video content. A video V is represented as a sequence of frames V =
197
      {F1 , F2 , · · · , FT }, where each Ft ∈ RH×W ×C corresponds to an image at time step t, with height H,
      width W , and C color channels. Our goal is to generate extended-length videos that are semantically
198
      consistent with the textual prompt while exhibiting high visual quality and temporal coherence.
199
      Formally, we aim to learn a generative model G : P → V that maps a textual prompt P to a video V
200   in the space of all possible videos V: V = G(P ). The quality of the generated video can be assessed
201   using a set of metrics M = {m1 , m2 , ..., mK }, where each mi : V × P → R evaluates a specific
202   aspect of the video (e.g., visual quality, temporal consistency, semantic alignment with the prompt).
203   Our objective is to maximize these quality metrics while ensuring diversity in the generated videos.
204   In our multi-agent framework, G is decomposed into a set of specialized agents {A1 , A2 , ..., AN },
205   each responsible for a specific subtask in the video generation process. These agents collaborate to
206   produce the final video output, allowing for more granular control of the generation process.
207
208   Definition and Specialization of Agents. The specialization of agents enables flexibility in the
209   breakdown of complex work into smaller and more focused tasks, as depicted in Figure 1. In our
210
      framework, we have five agents: prompt selection and generation agent A1 , text-to-image generation
      agent A2 , image-to-image generation agent A3 , image-to-video generation agent A4 , and video-
211
      to-video agent A5 . We present brief descriptions below, and detailed definitions can be found in
212
      Appendix A.5.
213
214   • Prompt Selection and Generation Agent (A1 ): This agent employs large language models like
215     GPT-4 and Llama (Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023) to analyze and enhance textual
        prompts, extracting key information and actions to optimize image relevance and quality.


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      • Text-to-Image Generation Agent (A2 ): Utilizing models such as those by Rombach et al. (2022c)
217     and Podell et al. (2023), this agent translates enriched textual descriptions into high-quality images
218     by deeply understanding and visualizing complex inputs.
219   • Image-to-Image Generation Agent (A3 ): Referencing Brooks et al. (2023), this agent modifies
220     source images based on detailed textual instructions, accurately interpreting and applying these to
221     make visual alterations ranging from subtle to transformative.
222   • Image-to-Video Generation Agent (A4 ): As described by Blattmann et al. (2023), this model
223     transitions static images into dynamic video sequences by analyzing content and style, ensuring
224     temporal stability and visual consistency.
225
      • Video Connection Agent (A5 ): This agent creates seamless transition videos from two input videos
        by leveraging keyframes and identifying common elements and styles, ensuring coherent and
226
        visually appealing outputs.
227
228
229   General Structure. Mora model structure is depicted in Figure 1. Specifically, given a user input
230   T , the Prompt Enhancement Agent (A1 ) first refines T into a form better suited for video generation.
231
      The enhanced prompt is then passed to the Text-to-Image Agent (A2 ) to generate the first frame
      of the video. At this stage, the user can review and confirm whether the tone and quality of the
232
      frame meet their expectations. If not, the user can either request a re-generation or pass the frame
233
      to the Image-to-Image Agent (A3 ) for adjustments. Once the desired first frame is finalized, it is
234   forwarded to the Image-to-Video Agent (A4 ) to generate a high-quality video that aligns with the
235   user’s requirements. This step-by-step, independently controllable, and interactive process ensures
236   that the final video more closely meets the user’s expectations while maintaining high quality. In
237   cases where a user wishes to generate a continuous video from different video clips, A5 analyzes the
238   final frame of the preceding video and the initial frame of the next and ensures a smooth blending
239   between them. Moreover, this procedural design ensures that the generation process does not have to
240   start from scratch, and the human-in-the-loop technique makes the entire pipeline more controllable,
241   as detailed in Appendix A.4. It enables our framework to handle various tasks, such as image-to-video
242
      generation, and even video extension and stitching. For more details about supported tasks, please
      refer to Appendix A.3. In addition to prompt-based generation, Mora structure also supports task-wise
243
      model fine-tuning, ensuring that agents can effectively follow instructions and consistently produce
244
      high-quality content.

248
249
      In this section, we introduce our proposed multi-agent finetuning design, based on the previously
      described model structure. Directly prompting each agent does not account for the downward
250
      transmission of information, which could lead to inefficiencies or errors in communication between
251
      agents. Additionally, the impact of each agent on the final result is not uniform. To address these
252   issues, we adopt an end-to-end training approach. Our proposed training procedure involves (1)
253   a self-modulation factor to enhance inter-agent coordination, (2) a data-free training strategy to
254   synthesize training data, (3) LLM selection with human-in-the-loop to control the training data
255   quality. In the following sections, we provide a detailed explanation of each component.
256
257
      Self-modulated Fine-tuning Algorithm. Previous methods primarily employ direct end-to-end
258   fine-tuning across the entire task procedure, while others may fine-tune individual agents based on
259   specific tasks. However, both approaches can influence model performance: (1) end-to-end fine-
260   tuning may result in improper loss allocation for each agent, and (2) fine-tuning agents individually
261   can lead to misaligned distributions. To address the limitations of existing fine-tuning approaches,
262   we propose a self-modulated fine-tuning algorithm specifically designed for our multi-agent model
263   structure. This method aims to enhance coordination among agents and improve overall performance
264   by introducing a modulation embedding that dynamically adjusts the influence of each agent during
265   the generation process. The key motivations behind our approach are to balance the impact of
266
      each agent on the final output, improve inter-agent communication and coordination, and allow for
      dynamic adjustment of agent contributions based on the current task and intermediate outputs.
267
268   Our design introduces a modulation embedding that is concatenated with the text embedding of the
269   enhanced prompt, allowing for fine-grained control over the generation process. This embedding
      is optimized alongside the model parameters during training, enabling the system to learn optimal

      5    C ONCLUSION
526
      We introduce Mora, a pioneering generalist framework that synergies Standard Operating Procedures
527
      (SOPs) for video generation, tackling a broad spectrum of video-related tasks. Mora significantly
528
      advances the generation of videos from textual prompts, establishing new standards for adaptability,
529   efficiency, and output quality in the domain. Our comprehensive evaluation indicates that Mora
530   not only matches but also surpasses the capabilities of existing leading models in several respects.
531   Nonetheless, it still faces a significant performance gap compared to OpenAI’s Sora, whose closed-
532   source nature presents substantial challenges for replication and further innovation in both academic
533   and professional settings.
534
      Looking forward, there are multiple promising avenues for further research. One direction includes
535   enhancing the agents’ natural language understanding capabilities to support more nuanced and
536   context-aware video productions. Additionally, the issues of accessibility and high computational
537   demands continue to impede broader adoption and innovation. Concurrently, fostering more open
538   and collaborative research environments could spur advancements, allowing the community to build
539   upon the foundational achievements of the Mora framework and other leading efforts.

1003   and replicate the initial image but also to anticipate and execute logical progressions in the scene.

1069   Constructing the Training Dataset. The process starts with user input prompts that are enhanced
1070   by a multimodal large language model (MLLM). These enhanced prompts are evaluated using a
1071   human-in-the-loop strategy, which ensures high-quality video candidates. The selected videos, paired
1072

1073
1074   Self-Modulated Fine-Tuning. During fine-tuning, enhanced prompts are processed by multiple
1075   agents. Each agent generates text embeddings, which are combined with a modulation embedding to
1076   dynamically adjust the contribution of each agent. The agents work together in a coordinated manner
1077   to generate video outputs. The total loss is calculated based on the output video and the target, and
1078   gradient descent is used to update both the model parameters and the modulation embeddings, and
1079   then reconstruct the training datasets. This ensures that the agents adapt their outputs based on the
       preceding agent’s state, resulting in improved video generation.

       Prompt Selection and Generation. Currently, GPT-4o (GPT) stands as the most advanced gen-
       erative model available. By harnessing the capabilities of GPT-4o, we are able to generate and
1085
       meticulously select high-quality prompts. These prompts are detailed and rich in information, facili-
1086
       tating the Text-to-Image generation process by providing the agent with comprehensive guidance.

1088   Text-to-Image Generation. We utilize the pre-trained large text-to-image model to generate a
1089   high-quality and representative first image.
1090   For Mora (SVD), the initial implementation utilizes Stable Diffusion XL (SDXL) (Podell et al., 2023).
1091   It introduces a significant evolution in the architecture and methodology of latent diffusion models
1092   (Rombach et al., 2022c; Meng et al., 2022) for text-to-image synthesis, setting a new benchmark in
1093   the field. At the core of its architecture is an enlarged UNet backbone (Ronneberger et al., 2015)
1094   that is three times larger than those used in previous versions of Stable Diffusion 2 (Rombach et al.,
1095   2022c). This expansion is principally achieved through an increased number of attention blocks
1096
       and a broader cross-attention context, facilitated by integrating a dual text encoder system. The
       first encoder is based on OpenCLIP (Ilharco et al., 2021) ViT-bigG (Cherti et al., 2023; Radford
1097
       et al., 2021; Schuhmann et al., 2022), while the second utilizes CLIP ViT-L, allowing for a richer,
1098
       more nuanced interpretation of textual inputs by concatenating the outputs of these encoders. This
1099   architectural innovation is complemented by the introduction of several novel conditioning schemes
1100   that do not require external supervision, enhancing the model’s flexibility and capability to generate
1101   images across multiple aspect ratios. Moreover, SDXL features a refinement model that employs a
1102   post-hoc image-to-image transformation to elevate the visual quality of the generated images. This
1103   refinement process utilizes a noising-denoising technique, further polishing the output images without
1104   compromising the efficiency or speed of the generation process.
1105   For Mora (Open-Sora-Plan), the Stable Diffusion 3 (SD3) (Esser et al., 2024) text-to-image model
1106   employed to generate high-quality images from textual prompts. SD3 utilizes a rectified flow
1107   transformer architecture, offering significant advancements over traditional diffusion models by
1108   connecting data and noise in a linear path rather than the curved trajectories commonly used in
1109   previous approaches. This architectural choice facilitates a more efficient and accurate sampling
1110   process, enhancing the generation of high-resolution images across diverse styles. Additionally, SD3
1111   introduces a novel noise re-weighting technique that biases sampling toward perceptually relevant
1112   scales, thereby improving the clarity and aesthetic quality of the generated images. The model also
1113
       supports multiple aspect ratios and incorporates a refinement process that further enhances generated
       images through a noising-denoising technique, ensuring high visual fidelity while maintaining
1114
       computational efficiency.
1115
1116   Image-to-Image Generation. Our initial Mora (SVD) framework utilizes InstructPix2Pix as Image-
1117   to-Image generation agent. InstructPix2Pix (Brooks et al., 2023) are intricately designed to enable
1118   effective image editing from natural language instructions. At its core, the system integrates the
1119
       expansive knowledge of two pre-trained models: GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020) for generating editing
       instructions and edited captions from textual descriptions, and Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al.,
1120
       2022b) for transforming these text-based inputs into visual outputs. This ingenious approach begins
1121
       with fine-tuning GPT-3 on a curated dataset of image captions and corresponding edit instructions,
1122   resulting in a model that can creatively suggest plausible edits and generate modified captions. Fol-
1123   lowing this, the Stable Diffusion model, augmented with the Prompt-to-Prompt technique, generates
1124   pairs of images (before and after the edit) based on the captions produced by GPT-3. The conditional
1125   diffusion model at the heart of InstructPix2Pix is then trained on this generated dataset. Instruct-
1126   Pix2Pix directly utilizes the text instructions and input image to perform the edit in a single forward
1127   pass. This efficiency is further enhanced by employing classifier-free guidance for both the image and
1128   instruction conditionings, allowing the model to balance fidelity to the original image with adherence
1129   to the editing instructions.
1130   For Mora (Open-Sora-Plan), the image-to-image generation agent, based on SD3 (Esser et al., 2024),
1131   excels in applying detailed text-guided transformations to images with precision and flexibility. SD3’s
1132   rectified flow transformer architecture ensures efficient and accurate image modifications, while the
1133   noise re-weighting process enhances the visual quality of the output, ensuring that edits are seamless
       and coherent.

       Image-to-Video Generation. In the Text-to-Video generation agent, video generation agents play
1135   an important role in ensuring video quality and consistency.
1136
1137   Our initial Mora (SVD) implementation utilizes the state-of-the-art video generation model Stable
1138
       Video Diffusion to generate video. The Stable Video Diffusion (SVD) (Blattmann et al., 2023)
       architecture introduces a cutting-edge approach to generating high-resolution videos by leveraging
1139
       the strengths of LDMs Stable Diffusion v2.1 (Rombach et al., 2022b), originally developed for
1140
       image synthesis, and extending their capabilities to handle the temporal complexities inherent in
1141   video content. At its core, the SVD model follows a three-stage training regime that begins with
1142   text-to-image pertaining, where the model learns robust visual representations from a diverse set
1143   of images. This foundation allows the model to understand and generate complex visual patterns
1144   and textures. In the second stage, video pretraining, the model is exposed to large amounts of
1145   video data, enabling it to learn temporal dynamics and motion patterns by incorporating temporal
1146   convolution and attention layers alongside its spatial counterparts. This training is conducted on
1147   a systematically curated dataset, ensuring the model learns from high-quality and relevant video
1148   content. The final stage, high-quality video finetuning, focuses on refining the model’s ability to
1149
       generate videos with increased resolution and fidelity, using a smaller but higher-quality dataset. This
       hierarchical training strategy, complemented by a novel data curation process, allows SVD to excel in
1150
       producing state-of-the-art text-to-video and image-to-video synthesis with remarkable detail, realism,
1151
       and coherence over time.
1152
1153   The Mora (Open-Sora-Plan) video generation agent leverages a 3D full attention mechanism, which
1154   processes spatial and temporal features simultaneously, providing a unified understanding of motion
1155
       and appearance across frames. This mechanism ensures smoother transitions and higher coherence in
       movements across consecutive frames, improving the temporal consistency of the generated video.
1156
       In the second phase, temporal convolution layers are integrated to further refine the model’s capacity
1157
       to comprehend and generate realistic motion dynamics. By capturing temporal patterns over time,
1158   the model produces videos with continuous and natural movements, thereby enhancing the realism
1159   of complex visual sequences. The third phase involves utilizing the CausalVideoVAE (Chen et al.,
1160   2024b), which enhances the visual representation and detail of each frame while minimizing the
1161   occurrence of artifacts commonly associated with video synthesis. The CausalVideoVAE maintains
1162   high-quality visual outputs throughout the entire video sequence by refining and polishing the
1163   generated content through multiple iterations. This comprehensive training regime ensures that Mora
1164   (Open Sora Plan) excels in both image-to-video and text-to-video tasks, delivering exceptional levels
1165   of detail, realism, and temporal coherence. These architectural innovations enable superior handling
1166
       of complex motion patterns and high-resolution video outputs, resulting in visually consistent and
       temporally stable videos.
1167
1168   Connect Videos. For the video connection task, we utilize SEINE (Chen et al., 2023c) to connect
1169   videos. SEINE is constructed upon a pre-trained diffusion-based T2V model, LaVie (Wang et al.,
1170   2023a) agent. SEINE centered around a random-mask video diffusion model that generates transitions
1171   based on textual descriptions. By integrating images of different scenes with text-based control,
1172
       SEINE produces transition videos that maintain coherence and visual quality. Additionally, the model
       can be extended for tasks such as image-to-video animation and autoregressive video prediction.
1173
1174   In Mora (Open-Sora-Plan), the video connection task utilizes a specialized architecture to ensure
1175   seamless and visually coherent transitions between videos. This is achieved by combining diffusion-
1176   based models with temporal convolution techniques, facilitating smooth transitions in style, content,
1177   and motion dynamics. The video connection agent employs CausalVideoVAE, optimized for temporal
1178
       and spatial consistency, enhancing the connection of two video segments by identifying and preserving
       common visual elements across frames. A 3D full attention architecture is central to this process,
1179
       enabling the model to simultaneously capture spatial and temporal features, thereby maintaining
1180
       coherence in object motion and background continuity. Additionally, the architecture incorporates
1181   random-mask video diffusion, which maintains high-resolution transitions by infilling missing
1182   information based on text-based control inputs or video context. This approach ensures that the
1183   connected videos preserve visual quality and exhibit coherent motion patterns, resulting in high-
1184   quality, temporally stable transitions.

1190   In our training setup, we use the AdamW optimizer, which is known for handling weight decay effec-
1191   tively, with an initial learning rate of 1e-5. The learning rate remains constant throughout training,
1192   controlled by the constant scheduler, and no warmup steps are used (lr_warmup_steps=0).
1193   To handle memory constraints, we accumulate gradients over 1 step, which allows us to use a batch
1194   size of 4 while maintaining stable optimization. We enable gradient checkpointing to further save
1195   memory by reducing the number of intermediate activations stored during backpropagation, although
1196
       this comes at the cost of slower backward passes. Mixed precision training with bf16 is employed
       to enhance computational speed and lower memory usage, which is crucial when training with a
1197
       batch size of 4. The model is trained for a maximum of 12 steps, with checkpoints being saved
1198
       every 4 steps to allow for model recovery or evaluation during training. We use an SNR Gamma
1199   value of 5.0 to balance the noise scale, which is especially useful for diffusion-based models, and
1200   apply Exponential Moving Average (EMA) from step 0 with a decay rate of 0.999 to ensure model
1201   stability throughout training. These settings provide a balance between computational efficiency and
1202   model performance, ensuring that we can handle large video data with high memory demands while
1203   optimizing effectively across training iterations.

       Advantages of Mora. Mora introduces a groundbreaking multi-agent framework for video generation,
1839   advancing the field by enabling a variety of tasks such as text-to-video conversion and digital world
1840   simulation. Unlike closed-source counterparts like Sora, Mora’s open framework offers seamless
1841   integration of various models, enhancing flexibility and efficiency for diverse applications. As an
1842   open-source project, Mora significantly contributes to the AI community by democratizing access to
1843   advanced video generation technologies and fostering collaboration and innovation. Future research
1844   is encouraged to improve the framework’s efficiency, reduce computational demands, and explore
1845   new agent configurations to enhance performance.
1846   Limitations of Mora. Mora faces significant limitations, including challenges in collecting high-
1847   quality video datasets due to copyright restrictions, resulting in difficulties in generating lifelike
1848   human movements. Its video quality and length capabilities fall short compared to Sora, with
1849   noticeable degradation beyond 12 seconds. Mora also struggles with interpreting and rendering
1850   motion dynamics from prompts, lacking control over specific directions. Furthermore, the absence of
1851   human labeling information in video datasets leads to results that may not align with human visual
1852   preferences, highlighting the need for datasets that adhere to physical laws.
