011            Post-training is essential for enabling large language models (LLMs) to follow hu-
012            man instructions. Inspired by the recent success of using LLMs to simulate human
013            society, we leverage multi-agent simulation to automatically generate diverse text-
014            based scenarios, capturing a wide range of real-world human needs. We propose
015            MATRIX, a multi-agent simulator that creates realistic and scalable scenarios.
016
               Leveraging these outputs, we introduce a novel scenario-driven instruction gen-
               erator MATRIX-Gen for controllable and highly realistic data synthesis. Exten-
017
               sive experiments demonstrate that our framework effectively generates both gen-

               eral and domain-specific data. Notably, on AlpacaEval 2 and Arena-Hard bench-

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Post-training is a crucial process that enables large language models (LLMs) to follow human
027
      instructions and enhance specific capabilities like coding and mathematics (Achiam et al., 2023;
      Dubey et al., 2024). The effectiveness of post-training heavily relies on instruction data that capture
028
      the real-world user requirements. However, obtaining such data in the real world poses signifi-
029
      cant challenges, including privacy concerns (Yu et al., 2024), data scarcity (Villalobos et al., 2024),
030   and human labor costs (Liu et al., 2024). Consequently, developing efficient methods to synthesize
031   high-quality post-training data is critical for the advancement of LLMs, which motivates this work.
032
033
      Current data synthesis approaches typically leverage the generative capability of aligned LLMs. For
      example, Taori et al.; Wang et al.; Xu et al.; Xu et al. employ tailored prompts to guide aligned LLMs
034
      in producing new instructions based on pre-defined seed instructions. Similarly, Xu et al. use pre-
035
      defined blank chat templates to generate synthetic instructions with aligned LLMs. While efficient,
036   these approaches fail to explicitly incorporate real-world user requirements into the data synthesis
037   process. Furthermore, their dependence on hand-crafted, pre-defined prompts means careful seed
038   instruction selection and prompt engineering are required for producing high-quality data that fit
039   specific user requirements. These limitations not only increase the risk of generating unrealistic
040   instructions misaligned with actual user requirements but also reduce the controllability of these
041   methods in generating specialized data.
042   In this work, we introduce multi-agent simulation as a novel foundation for post-training data syn-
043   thesis. Inspired by recent successes in using LLMs to simulate the human society (Park et al., 2023;
044   Horton, 2023; Aher et al., 2023), we employ multi-agent simulation to automatically generate di-
045   verse scenarios in text, capturing a wide range of real-world human needs. For instance, in a flight
046   delay scenario, where passenger agents interact with a customer service agent, there is a clear need
047   for assistance in finding alternative flights or transportation options. By integrating multi-agent sim-
048   ulation into data synthesis, we automate the creation of diverse, contextually grounded references
049   to reflect various real-world human requirements. The diversity and realism of these references can
050
      lead to a controllable synthesis process for highly realistic data. Following this perspective, we pro-
      pose a novel post-training data synthesis framework comprising two key components: a multi-agent
051
      simulator named MATRIX and a scenario-driven instruction generator named MATRIX-Gen.
052
053   To simulate realistic and diverse scenarios, MATRIX incorporates two core components: 1000 real-
      world-grounded agents and a structured communication mechanism. For creating agents that can

101   2       P ROPOSED P OST-T RAINING SYSTEM
102
103   Our post-training system aims to enhance the instruction-following capability of pre-trained LLMs
104   by leveraging synthetic training data generated by an aligned LLM, which is grounded on simulated
105   social scenarios. The key idea is inspired by how humans ask questions in real-life scenarios. People
106   naturally generate diverse and deep questions based on their needs and goals. Our approach bypasses
107   seed data, using real-life scenarios to guide models in generating more informative and realistic
      questions, resulting in higher-quality training data. As shown in Figure 1, the framework involves

126   Synthesizing social scenarios. Following our key idea of synthesizing realistic post-training data
127   grounded on real-world scenarios, we propose to automatically synthesize social scenarios via multi-
128   agent simulation, where the scenarios are defined as groups of agents and their corresponding textual
129
      actions. To ensure the realism and diversity of the simulated scenarios, we design MATRIX, a
      large-scale multi-agent simulator that creates an interactive environment with various agents. This
130
      simulator leverages LLM’s role-playing capability, enabling agents with varied profiles to simulate
131
      human behavior, plan, observe, and act, resulting in diverse and highly realistic social scenarios.
132
133   The workflow of social scenario synthesis includes three steps: i) given real agent data crawled from
134   the web, the LLM is prompted to generate agent profiles and create agent-specific goals based on
135
      these profiles; ii) given agent profiles, the communication topology among agents is initialized ac-
      cording to the network homophily among the text embeddings of the corresponding agents’ profiles;
136
      and iii) based on this topology, agents execute their goals by generating actions and interacting with
137
      other agents within the simulator. The social scenarios are parsed from the interactions among the
138   agents, containing diverse close-to-real human behaviors and intentions; see examples of simulated
139   scenarios in Table 20 and more details in Section 3.
140
141
      Generating post-training data from scenarios. Given the simulated social scenarios, we generate
      post-training data under the specific user requirements. To achieve this, we propose MATRIX-
142
      Gen, a scenario-driven instruction generator that selects relevant scenarios of user requirements
143
      used for instruction generation. This approach takes real human intentions into account, making
144   the synthesized instructions better reflect human needs and possess greater authenticity and realism.
145   By adjusting the specific requirements of humans in the data synthesis prompt, this approach could
146   guarantee that the synthesized data can be aligned with additional synthetic targets, which offers
147   great controllability in generating synthesized instructions.
148
      As shown in Figure 2, this synthetic data generation process includes three steps: i) retrieving the
149
      most relevant simulated scenarios based on the given specific human requirements; ii) for each
150   selected scenario, MATRIX-Gen simulates the process of human posing questions in their daily lives
151   by intergrating each agent’s persona and action within the scenario into the instruction-synthesis
152   prompt to generate instructions; iii) based on the instruction synthesis prompt in the previous step,
153   directly call the aligned LLM to get the synthesized instructions and the corresponding responses;
154   see an example of the generated instruction in Table 8.
155
      Based on these steps, by controlling the synthesizing process, our post-training system could syn-
156   thesize three types of datasets, including 1) the supervised fine-tuning (SFT) dataset MATRIX-Gen-
157   SFT, 2) the preference tuning dataset MATRIX-Gen-DPO, and 3) SFT data in special domains. For
158   MATRIX-Gen-SFT, the instructions are generated with both simplicity and diversity. For MATRIX-
159   Gen-DPO, the instructions are complex and specialized, with the chosen response coming from
160   the aligned LLM, and the rejected response from the SFT model to be fine-tuned. For SFT data
161   in special domains, we synthesize domain-specific datasets from diverse, informative scenarios by
      adjusting the instruction type in the synthesis prompt, such as coding, safety, or other relevant areas.

      Model fine-tuning. Given the dataset MATRIX-Gen-SFT, we perform supervised fine-tuning on a
163   pre-trained model to get a SFT model. Then, given the preference tuning dataset MATRIX-Gen-
164   DPO, we perform standard direct preference optimization based on this SFT model. We call the

      The proposed post-training system integrates real human profiles and high-quality scenarios to en-
167
      hance the instruction synthesis process. By incorporating human profiles, the system mimics how
168
      humans generate questions, enabling the outputs to align with human intentions. Meanwhile, high-
169   quality scenarios provide a detailed and diverse context that allows for the generation of complex
170   and varied instructions. This approach ensures that the synthesized instructions not only reflect hu-
171   man needs but also maintain a high level of diversity and complexity. This post-training system
172   offers two key advantages: i) Realism in generated synthetic instructions and ii) Controllability in
173   synthetic data generation.
174
      Realism in generated synthetic instructions. Our system leverages diverse, informative social
175   simulation scenarios and close-to-real agent actions in instruction generation process, compared to
176   the seed-data based approaches (Xu et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2023a), resulting in better alignment
177   with real human intentions and daily needs. Conventional approaches primarily rely on LLMs to
178   augment existing instructions (Xu et al., 2024a), typically by adding requirements or elaborating on
179   the given instructions. This approach might generate instructions that are illogical or inconsistent
180   when model lacks a sufficient understanding of the original instruction’s context. In comparison,
181   MATRIX-Gen generates diverse scenarios as contexts that encompass a wide range of cultural,
182   task-related, and situational requirements.
183   Controllability in synthetic data generation. Our MATRIX-Gen generator can be easily con-
184   trolled over specific user requirements by retrieving the most relevant scenarios from a large amount
185   of scenario candidates for the instruction synthesis process. Unlike approaches that rely on pre-
186   defined blank chat templates (Xu et al., 2024b), this approach allows for the generation of diverse
187   problem categories tailored to specific needs. By adjusting the generation prompts, MATRIX-Gen
188   can customize instructions based on task categories, complexity levels, and questioning styles. This
189
      level of control allows for more precise customization of synthetic data.

191   3     M ATRIX : L ARGE SCALE MULTI - AGENT SIMULATOR
192
193   This section elaborates on our multi-agent simulator, MATRIX. As shown in Figure 3, it operates by
194   taking a collection of agent profiles as input and generates simulated scenarios, where each scenario
195   comprises the actions of a group of agents in text. MATRIX simulates numerous realistic and diverse
196   scenarios with two key elements: real-world-grounded agents and structured communication.
197
198   3.1   R EAL - WORLD - GROUNDED AGENTS
199
200   Agents in our simulation possess attributes including name, personality, and life goals, alongside
201   modules for memory and action. These agents exhibit human-like actions through two key designs:
202   i) they are initialized using anonymized real human profiles, and ii) they are driven by goal-oriented
203   actions, allowing them to pursue meaningful goals while interacting with other agents.
204   Real human profiles. To simulate human behaviors effectively, we collect real human profiles and
205   process them through the LLM to remove or anonymize any private information, ensuring no per-
206   sonal identity is leaked. Each profile includes a unique anonymized name, description, and a record
207   of past actions, all processed to protect privacy. We initialize 1,000 agents using this information,
208   embedding their action history into memory. These agents cover a broad spectrum of real humans,
209   representing diverse demographics, professions, and life experiences. This diversity ensures that the
210   simulation captures a wide range of human behaviors and interactions. By leveraging large-scale
211   real profiles, our agents behave more authentically, resulting in highly realistic and varied scenes.
212   Goal-oriented actions. Modeled after real-world human behaviors, we design agents’ actions to
213   be driven by their specific life goals. For each agent, we prompt the LLM to generate life goals
214   and a core personality based on the individual’s past actions. The life goals are then broken down
215   into actionable steps, forming the agent’s plan. This mirrors how real humans form their identi-
      ties—through accumulated experiences and actions over time. These steps guide the agent’s future

      3.2        S TRUCTURED C OMMUNICATION M ECHANISM
236
237
      To synthesize high-quality data, which requires both diversity and realism, simulations must support
      diverse interactions among a large number of agents to create rich and realistic scenarios. While we
238
      implement concurrent agent actions to reflect real-world interactions, a key problem arises: agents
239
      may receive an overload of irrelevant messages, causing meaningless actions that hinder the simu-
240   lation’s progress and reduce realism.
241
242   To address this, we introduce a structured communication mechanism. Inspired by the homophily
243
      phenomenon in human society (McPherson et al., 2001), which suggests individuals tend to asso-
      ciate with others who share similar characteristics, we group our agents based on similar profiles.
244
      This grouping effectively reduces unnecessary connections among agents, enabling scalable simula-
245
      tions. For each group, we introduce a centralized modulator to manage communication both within
246   and between groups. This design promotes more interactions among similar agents while still al-
247   lowing for long-distance interactions, enriching the flow of information and enhancing realism.
248
249
      Agent grouping. To facilitate large-scale simulations, we minimize unnecessary communication by
      organizing agents into groups based on similar social interactions. Agent profiles are transformed
250
      into text embeddings (Neelakantan et al., 2022) and grouped using constrained K-means cluster-
251
      ing (Bradley et al., 2000) to ensure that agents with similar characteristics are clustered together. We
252   set K to 200 due to hardware constraints, resulting in groups of 1 to 10 agents each; see Figure 6
253   for agent-wise similarity indicating the rich structural relationships between agents. Additionally,
254   agent grouping introduces a range of interaction types. Interactions between two agents occur pair-
255   wise, while larger groups facilitate more complex dynamics. This diversity of interactions further
256   enhances the realism of the simulation.
257
      Modulator. To manage the groups, we design the LLM-empowered modulators. Acting as a central
258   server in each group, the modulator collects and distributes agent actions, managing both intra-group
259   and inter-group communication. For intra-group communication, the modulator selectively deliv-
260   ers relevant actions to the appropriate agents within each group. By basing its decisions on agent
261   profiles and current actions, the modulator ensures that agents only receive relevant information,
262   maintaining purposeful interactions. For inter-group communication, each modulator maintains a
263   memory of its group’s scenarios—each consisting of the actions of its agents. This memory cap-
264   tures key details of the group’s internal dynamics and helps guide decisions about sharing actions
265   with other groups. When an action occurs, the modulator evaluates whether to share it with other
266
      groups by considering the stored memories of other modulators; see modulator prompts in Table 18
      This selective communication fosters complex interactions across groups, leading to richer and more
267
      diverse scenarios.
268
269   Overall, the structured communication design ensures a scalable and authentic simulation with var-
      ious interaction patterns, facilitating the generation of realistic, large-scale scenarios.

      3.3   S CENARIO G ENERATION
271
272   The generation of large-scale realistic scenarios occurs through the following key stages:
273
274
      Initialization. Starting with 1,000 real profiles, the LLM first anonymizes or removes any private
      information. It then generates goals and plans for each agent. Agents are grouped based on the text
275
      embeddings of their profiles, clustering similar agents together.
276
277   Execution. At the start of each scenario, agents in a group execute their plans to fulfill their life goals
278   and interact with each other. The modulator collects all agents’ actions and waits until every agent
279   has acted, thereby completing a scenario. Before the next scenario, agents from different groups
280
      exchange information via their modulators. These interactions are used in the subsequent scenario,
      allowing inter-group communication to influence intra-group dynamics in the next scenario.
281
282   Termination. The simulation ends when agents either stop generating actions, indicating they’ve
283   fulfilled their life goals, or when the desired number of scenarios has been collected.
284   After the simulation, generated scenarios are collected from the modulators and used for post-
285   training data synthesis; see a complete simulation example in Table 15 and Table 20.

      3.4   D ISCUSSIONS
288
289
      Rationality and advantages of MATRIX in facilitating data synthesis. MATRIX’s ability to syn-
290   thesize diverse and authentic data stems from the diversity and realism of its simulated scenarios,
291   which are built on two key foundations. First, its real-world-grounded agents are designed to emulate
292   human behaviors, ensuring that the scenarios they generate closely resemble real-world interactions.
293   Second, MATRIX employs a structured communication mechanism that facilitates large-scale inter-
294   actions among numerous agents. This framework supports a variety of interaction forms—ranging
295   from individual exchanges to group dynamics—resulting in a broad spectrum of scenarios, leading
296   to synthesized data that is both richly diverse and authentically reflective of real-world complexity.
297   Comparison with existing simulators. Recently, multi-agent simulations have gained attention for
298   studying the social and personality attributes of LLMs. While sociological simulators (Park et al.,
299   2023; Mou et al., 2024; Gu et al., 2024), designed for specific environments, can generate basic
300   societal actions such as daily conversations or posting tweets, they suffer from constrained scenar-
301   ios and simplistic actions; see examples in Table 19. In reality, human behavior is highly diverse,
302   ranging from simple to complex, making it unrealistic to rely on these simulators for synthesizing
303   rich and complex data. In contrast, MATRIX drives agents’ behaviors by their life goals. The large
304
      number of agents and their dynamic interactions generate a broad spectrum of scenarios, from ev-
      eryday conversations to complex professional tasks, making MATRIX highly effective at producing
305
      diverse, high-quality datasets; see examples in Table 20.
306
307   Moreover, while PersonaHub (Chan et al., 2024) is not a simulator, it leverages the role-playing ca-
308   pabilities of LLMs to generate instructions based on large-scale profiles. Despite the large scale of
309   these agent profiles, there is no interaction between agents, limiting the potential to create nuanced,
310
      complex, and contextually rich scenarios. In contrast, MATRIX synthesizes data from diverse re-
      alistic scenarios driven by realistic agent interactions. This not only results in more comprehensive
311
      synthetic data but also better reflects real-world LLM applications, where users engage with complex
312
      scenarios and pose context-specific questions to the model.

      5   R ELATED W ORKS
499
500
      Synthesizing alignment data. Aligning LLMs with human expectations requires high-quality data
501   that accurately reflects human needs and intentions (Wang et al., 2023b). Initial efforts sought
502   to transform existing NLP benchmarks into instructions (Wang et al., 2022; Mishra et al., 2022)
503   or collect user-generated instructions (Chiang et al., 2023a; Zhao et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024).
504   However, Villalobos et al. (2024) have raised concerns that human-generated data may not scale
505   adequately. To address this bottleneck, synthetic data generation from LLMs has emerged as a
506   promising alternative. Current methods typically involve back-translating from web corpora (Li
507   et al., 2023a), prompting LLMs to generate new instructions from existing ones (Wang et al., 2023a;
508   Xu et al., 2024a), or guiding LLMs to complete chat templates (Xu et al., 2024b). While they rely
509
      on predefined materials, limiting flexibility and missing real-world context, our approach generates
      instructions from simulated social scenarios, offering more flexibility and realism.
510
511   Multi-agent simulation. Multi-agent simulations have been utilized for tasks such as societal re-
512   search (Xie et al., 2024) and the evaluation of LLMs (Lin et al., 2023). These simulators can
513   generally be divided into two categories based on agent behavior: those focused purely on social
514   interactions (Gu et al., 2024), like speaking, chatting, or posting on social media, and those that
515
      support more complex agent actions (Wang et al.). While early simulators (Park et al., 2023; Pang
      et al.) typically featured only a small number of agents, recent efforts have aimed to scale up the
516
      number of agents (Mou et al., 2024). However, research on large-scale scalability is still limited, and
517
      many of these simulations run for extended durations. (Qian et al., 2024) In contrast, our simulator
518   is specifically designed for synthetic data generation, supporting both complex agent actions and
519   scalable simulations, addressing the demand for diverse, realistic, and efficient simulations.
520
521
522
      6   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
523
      This paper presents a novel framework for synthesizing post-training data based on multi-agent
524
      simulation. Our framework consists of two key components: MATRIX, a multi-agent simulator
525
      that generates realistic and diverse scenarios with scalable communications, and MATRIX-Gen, a
526   scenario-driven instruction generator. MATRIX provides the realism and controllability needed for
527   MATRIX-Gen to synthesize datasets for tasks such as SFT, DPO, and domain-specific applications.
528   Experimental results show that: i) MATRIX-Gen-SFT outperforms other SFT datasets; ii) Models
529   fine-tuned further on MATRIX-Gen-DPO surpass those fine-tuned on other preference datasets, in-
530   cluding Llama-3-8B-Instruct, with significantly less data; iii) MATRIX-Gen offers controllability in
531   generating data for domain-specific tasks. These results highlight the effectiveness of our framework
532   for post-training data synthesis.
533   Future work. One future direction is to use this data synthesis framework to quantitatively study
534   which types of data are suitable for SFT and DPO, respectively, since this framework allows control
535   over the scale, topic, and difficulty of the synthesized data.

790   A.1   T HE DATA TYPE USED FOR SFT AND DPO.
791
792
      We conducted experiments comparing the effectiveness of using simpler versus more complex
      dataset in different stages of the post-training process to better understand the optimal post-training
793
      strategy for large language models. Here we conduct comparison experiment on two kinds of in-
794
      structions: simple instructions and specialized instructions, denoted as type 1 and type 2. As showen
795   in Table 9, we observe that performing SFT on simpler instructions helps the model to establish a
796   foundational level of instruction-following ability. This is reflected in moderate performance on Al-
797   pacaEval 2 (LC 16.25%, WR 17.62%) but lower performance on the more challenging Arena-Hard
798   benchmark (WR 10.7%). When the model is fine-tuned on more specialized and complex data, there
799   is a marginal improvement (LC 14.70%, WR 16.01%, Arena-Hard WR 14.7%), and the significant
800   performance gains are achieved when DPO is applied after SFT. For example, SFT followed by DPO
801   with complex, specialized instructions yields substantial improvements (LC 21.64%, WR 30.06%,
802   Arena-Hard WR 22.1%), demonstrating that DPO effectively refines the model by leveraging more
803
      complex data. Importantly, the results also show that applying DPO with more difficult, specialized
      data consistently outperforms using DPO with simpler data, as evidenced by the significantly higher
804
      scores when DPO follows either type of SFT. These findings highlight the importance of using
805
      progressively more challenging datasets in the post-training process: starting with SFT on simpler
806   instructions to build a basic level of proficiency, followed by DPO on higher-difficulty data to en-
807   hance performance further. This approach not only improves general instruction-following abilities
808   but also enables the model to handle more complex, specialized tasks effectively, achieving superior
809   results compared to training on simple or complex instructions alone.
