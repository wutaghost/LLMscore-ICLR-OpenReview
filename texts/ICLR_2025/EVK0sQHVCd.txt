009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent findings suggest that diffusion models significantly enhance empirical
012            adversarial robustness. While some intuitive explanations have been proposed,
013            the precise mechanisms underlying these improvements remain unclear. In this
014            work, we systematically investigate how and how well do diffusion models im-
015            prove adversarial robustness. First, we observe that diffusion models intriguingly
016            increase—rather than decrease—the ℓp distances to clean samples. This is the
017            opposite of what was believed previously. Second, we find that the purified images
018
               are heavily influenced by the internal randomness of diffusion models. To properly
               evaluate the robustness of systems with inherent randomness, we introduce the
019
               concept of fuzzy adversarial robustness, and find that empirically a substantial
020
               fraction of adversarial examples are fuzzy in nature. Finally, by leveraging a
021            hyperspherical cap model of adversarial regions, we show that diffusion models
022            increase robustness by dramatically compressing the image space. Our findings
023            provide novel insights into the mechanisms behind the robustness improvements of
024            diffusion model-based purification and offer guidance for the development of more
025            efficient adversarial purification systems.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Neural networks are vulnerable to small adversarial perturbations (Szegedy et al., 2013; Goodfellow
031   et al., 2014). This presents a fundamental question on the robustness of artificial learning systems.
032   Adversarial training (Madry et al., 2017) has been proposed as a successful method to overcome
033
      this problem (Shafahi et al., 2019; Pang et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021). However, research has
      found that training with a specific attack usually sacrifices the robustness against other types of
034
      perturbations (Schott et al., 2018; Ford et al., 2019; Yin et al., 2019), indicating that adversarial
035
      training overfits the attack rather than achieving an overall robustness improvement.
036
037   Adversarial purification provides an alternative path toward adversarial robustness. This approach
038   typically relies on generative models to purify the stimulus before passing to a classifier(Song et al.,
039
      2018; Samangouei et al., 2018; Shi et al., 2021; Yoon et al., 2021). The basic idea is to leverage
      the image priors learned in some generative models to project adversarial perturbations back toward
040
      the image manifold. Intuitively, the performance of such purification should depend on how well
041
      the generative models capture the probability distribution of natural images. Recently, adversarial
042   purification based on diffusion models(Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020b) (DiffPure) was reported
043   to show promising improvements against various empirical attacks on multiple datasets (Nie et al.,
044   2022). Such a direction of using diffusion models as denoisers was further combined with the
045   deonise smoothing framework (Cohen et al., 2019; Salman et al., 2020) to improve certificated
046   robustness (Carlini et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2023). However, more recent work (Lee & Kim, 2023)
047   argued that there was an overestimate of the robustness improvement from the DiffPure method. In
048   all, despite of some promising empirical results, the underlying mechanism of empirical robustness
049   improvement from diffusion models (how), as well as a proper robustness evaluation with randomness
050
      (how well), were not yet well understood.
051   To close this important gap, we systematically investigate how diffusion models improve adversarial
052   robustness. In this paper, we report a set of surprising phenomena of diffusion models, and furthermore
053   identify the key mechanisms for robustness improvements under diffusion-model-based adversarial
      purification. Our main contributions are summarized below:

064   2    R ELATED WORK AND PRELIMINARIES
065
066   Generative models for adversarial purification Unlike adversarial training which directly aug-
067   ments the classifier training with adversarial attacks, adversarial purification intends to first “purify”
068   the perturbed image before classification. Generative models are usually utilized as the purification
069
      system, such as denoising autoencoder (Gu & Rigazio, 2014), denoising U-Net (Liao et al., 2018),
      PixelCNN (Song et al., 2018) and GAN (Samangouei et al., 2018). Denote the purification system as
070
      f (x), and the following readout classifier as g(x). Under the assumption that adversarial purification
071
      processes perturbed data x̃ close to the clean data x, thus f (x̃) ≈ x, the Bypass Direct Approximation
072   (BPDA) (Athalye et al., 2018) can provide a robustness estimation if f is hard-to-differentiate.
073
074   Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020b) set the SOTA performances on image generation,
075
      and represent a natural choice for adversarial purification. Nie et al. (2022) proposed the DiffPure
      framework, which utilized both the forward and reverse process and achieved promising empirical
076
      robustness comparable with adversarial training on multiple benchmarks. Similar improvements
077
      were reported with guided diffusion models (Wang et al., 2022). These studies led to substantial
078   interest in applying diffusion models for adversarial purification in various domains, including
079   auditory data (Wu et al., 2022) and 3D point clouds (Sun et al., 2023). Recently, other tricks such as
080   adversarial guidance (Lin et al., 2024) were also introduced to further enhance robustness. However,
081   through a comprehensive experimental evaluation, (Lee & Kim, 2023) discovered that there was
082   an overestimate of the robustness improvement from diffusion models, and it was recommended to
083   apply the PGD-EOT with full gradients directly over AutoAttack. Another line of research applies
084   diffusion models to improve certificated robustness Cohen et al. (2019). Carlini et al. (2022) found
085   that plugging diffusion models as a denoiser into the denoised smoothing framework (Salman et al.,
086
      2020) can lead to non-trivial certificated robustness. Xiao et al. (2023) further developed this method
      and explained the improvement in certificated robustness.
087

      3.1   D IFFUSION MODELS PUSH THE PURIFIED IMAGES AWAY FROM CLEAN IMAGES
114
115   While the exact mechanisms for the robustness improvement under diffusion models remain unclear,
116   intuitive explanations have been discussed in the DiffPure paper (Nie et al., 2022), e.g. diffusion
117   models “recover clean images through the reverse denoising process”. This motivates us to test a
118   simple hypothesis, that is, diffusion models shrink the ℓp distances towards clean images during
119   adversarial purification. Ideally, if a purification system is able to shrink the ℓp distances across
120   multiple noise levels, we can apply the purification recursively to essentially turn the original
121   adversarial attack into attacks with smaller magnitudes, thereby increasing robustness (see Fig. 1a).
122   Below we will systematically examine this hypothesis with experiments.

      EOT, PGD-EOT), and the results are essentially the same. Second, the results are also robust to the

171   Thus, diffusion models tend to push the adversarial images further away, rather than being closer
172   to the clean samples as often believed (Nie et al., 2022). As a remark, despite the considerable
173   robustness differences (see Table 1), the ℓ2 distances to the clean images were almost identical for
174   different attacks throughout the purification processes. Thus the ℓ2 distances to clean samples after
175   diffusion models could not effectively explain the robustness differences. These observations may
176   have important implications for adversarial attacks under diffusion models. It raises the possibility
177
      that the attacks based on BPDA may substantially over-estimate the adversarial robustness of the
      system based on diffusion purification.
178
179
      Behavior of diffusion models under random perturbations. We wonder if the behavior of
180   adversarial attacks under diffusion model is special at all– that is, whether the push-away phenomena
181   we observed is in fact general to arbitrary perturbations around the clean images. To test this,
182   we generated perturbations of clean images by sampling random noise uniformly with a fixed
183   magnitude. We first tested small perturbations that match the size of the adversarial attack on
184   CIFAR-10 (ℓ∞ = 8/255 uniform noise). We found that the behavior of the model under random
185   noise (Fig. 1d, blue curve) is almost identical to that induced by the adversarial attack. These results,
186   together with those reported above, suggest that diffusion models are able to reduce the distances to
187   a clean image from a slightly perturbed clean image. This raised the intriguing possibility that the
188
      cleans images do not reside on the local peaks of the image priors learned in the diffusion models.
      This may make sense given the in memorization v.s. generalization trade-off (Kadkhodaie et al.,
189
      2024). That is, a model simply encodes every clean image as the prior mode may not generalize well.
190
191   Although the results above indicate that diffusion models are ineffective in removing small perturba-
192   tions, it is possible that they may be more effective in removing noise induced by larger perturbations.
193   We performed the same ℓ2 distance analysis using three larger levels of uniform noises, ranging
194
      from ϵ = {32, 64, 128}/255, to examine the model behavior under larger perturbations (Fig. 1d).
      As the noise level increases, the ℓp distances of the final purified states increase. Interestingly, the
195
      model transits from “pushing-away” to “shrinkage” under very large perturbations. We repeated the
196
      above experiments with ℓ∞ distances (Fig. S1) and on the ImageNet dataset, and confirmed that
197   the observations still held under ℓ∞ measurements across datasets. The original data are provided
198   in Appendix C.1. Overall, these results suggest that, while diffusion models can denoise the image
199   toward the image manifold when the noise is large, locally it is ineffective in removing small noise.

      Diffusion models are intrinsically noisy due to the noise added in both forward and reverse processes.
204   Thus, a diffusion model defines a stochastic mapping fϵ between the input and output images, i.e.,
205   y = fϵ (x). When the noise ϵ is fixed (in the implementation, this amounts to fixing the random
206   seeds, Appendix B), the mapping becomes deterministic. Another way to remove the stochasticity in
207   diffusion models is to marginalize over the noise ϵ by taking the expectation, i.e., y = Ep(ϵ) [fϵ (x)] .
208   Practically, this requires taking a large number of samples and averaging, which is computational
209   expensive. Often, diffusion-based purification is only based on one sample from diffusion models.
210   Thus, we will focus on study the variability of individual samples generated by diffusion models.
211   We are interested in understanding how randomness in diffusion models determines the states after
212   purification. We consider two relevant scenarios. First, we fix the input image x, and pass it through
213   diffusion models multiple times (n = 100) while allowing the randomness to vary from trial to trial.
214   Second, we fix the randomness in diffusion models, and randomly draw input images uniformly from
215   a small ball M (ℓ∞ = 8/255, matching the scale of adversarial attacks) around the clean image x0 .

      4       F UZZY ROBUSTNESS EVALUATION FOR ADVERSARIAL PURIFICATION
247
248   4.1 F UZZY ADVERSARIAL ROBUSTNESS FOR SYSTEMS WITH RANDOMNESS
249   The results above suggest that the randomness in diffusion models largely determines the eventual
250   purified state. This implies that randomness may play an important role in the robustness of diffusion
251   models. How randomness affects robustness has been debated. Some relied on randomness as
252   a feature for robustness improvements (Yoon et al., 2021), while others argued it might obscure
253   gradients and make evaluation challenging (Athalye et al., 2018; Carlini et al., 2019; Gao et al., 2022).
254   Here we approach this question from a new perspective. Specifically, we will show that the classical
255   definition for adversarial robustness fall short in evaluating systems with robustness, and it is more
256
      appropriate to define “fuzzy adversarial examples”. This concept is best illustrated with the following
      example (also see Fig. 3a).
257
258
      Failure of classical adversarial robustness for systems with randomness. Let (x0 , y0 ) be a data
259
      point x0 with label y0 , and g(x) be a deterministic classifier. Consider an additive Gaussian noise
260   model
261                                   f (x) = x + η, where η ∼ N (0, σ 2 I).                                (3)

263

264   ∃x′ s.t. g(x′ ) ̸= y0 . Typically, if x′ is close to x0 , thus the ℓp -norm less than a given threshold,
265   ∥x′ − x0 ∥p < ϵ, we say x′ is an adversarial example of g around x0 . Such a definition works well

      4.2    F UZZY ROBUSTNESS EVALUATION OF DIFFUSION MODELS
302
303   We next evaluate the fuzziness of adversarial examples experimentally in diffusion models. We
304   evaluated the fuzzy robustness for DiffPure on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet dataset. We first calculated
305   the standard BPDA/EOT and PGD/EOT with full gradients on CIFAR-10 (ℓ∞ = 8/255), and
306   BPDA/EOT attack on ImageNet (ℓ∞ = 4/255) (see Appendix B for details). After calculating the
307   attacks, we repeatedly evaluate the same attack to DiffPure for r = 100 times, and use the frequency
308   to estimate the probability of a particular attack fooling the system (i.e., grades of fuzzy adversarial
309   attack). Two extreme cases are of particular interest: (i) the portion of adversarial examples never
310
      fool the system (non-adversarial, m = 0); (ii) the portion always fools the system successfully
      (full-adversarial, m = 1). If the classical definition still holds approximately, we should expect that
311
      the portion of non-adversarial is close to the standard robustness, and full-adversarial is close to the
312
      failure rate (1 − standard robustness). The results are shown in Table 1 and 2. The histograms of
313   fuzzy adversarial examples with all grades are shown in Fig. 3b and S4a.
314
315   As shown in the Tables and Fig. 3b, rather than being a 0-1 distribution approximating the determinis-
316
      tic regime, the grades have a wide span of range across. Depending on the criteria for considering an
      image as adversarial (the selection minimum grade α), we will have different robustness evaluations
317
      rather than a single number believed in the previous works (Nie et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2023; Lee &
318
      Kim, 2023). Since the selection of α is arbitrary, here we set α = 0.5 as an exemplar, thus considering
319   an example as adversarial if with at least 50% of the chance it can fool the system. This is a natural
320   selection as for any sample with a grade m < 0.5, there is a high chance that it can be corrected by a
321   majority voting (which chooses the category with most “votes" from the samples) .1

345   One can define a fuzzy example as adversarial by setting a particular α, and acquiring a corresponding
346   robustness. This is essentially the cumulative distribution function of the grade distribution, which
347
      we defined as cumulative fuzzy robustness (CFR). Given a particular grade threshold α, for the
      same attack, a higher CFR reflects a better fuzzy robustness; for the same model, a lower CFR
348
      reflects a stronger attack. For the limiting case, a perfect robustness system will have an all-zero
349
      grade distribution, thus yielding a CFR curve of a horizontal line at 1; while an entirely non-robust
350   system will have an all-one grade distribution, yielding a CFR curve of a horizontal line at 0. Any
351   deterministic system will have a CFR curve with a horizontal line at its robustness rate, while a
352   randomness system will have a CFR curve increasing to 1 (Fig. 3c). We propose the CFR curve as
353   the proper robustness metric for systems with randomness. We observe that the adversarial robustness
354   of the two attacks based on BPDA is substantially lower than that based on PGD. This is consistent
355   with our earlier observation that diffusion models push the adversarial images away from the clean
356   image, which would make the BPDA method less effective.
357
358   5    T HE HYPERSPHERICAL CAP MODEL OF ADVERSARIAL REGIONS
359
360   To reveal how adversarial robustness can be improved by purification, it would be important to
361   understand the geometry of the adversarial regions for the classifier. If adversarial regions are tiny
362   regions (e.g., small droplets) and there are many of them surrounding a given clean image, it would
363
      be relatively easy to escape these regions by adding a small amount of Gaussian noise. In contrast, if
      adversarial regions are large and continuous in space, it would be difficult to escape these regions by
364
      adding isotropic noise, instead systematic directional biases are needed to avoid these regions. While
365
      early work proposed that adversarial regions are small "pockets" in high-dimensional space (Szegedy
366   et al., 2013), Tramèr et al. (2017) showed that rather than being isolated high-dimensional droplets,
367   adversarial examples form continuous spaces so that about 25 orthogonal vectors can be fit into the
368   adversarial region. In the following, we propose a hyperspherical cap model for adversarial regions.
369
      The hyperspherical cap model. An image x is an adversarial example if and only if its projection
370
      along the adversarial direction crosses a threshold. Because all such points form a hyperspherical cap
371
      around the adversarial direction, the adversarial region is a hyperspherical cap.
372
373   Essentially, this model means that for adversarial examples, the classification boundaries are locally
374   linear. To establish this model, we rely on the assumption that adversarial directions are sparse so that
375
      they can not be sampled by random directions, which is consistent with prior empirical observations.
      Under these assumptions, one can mathematically show that the adversarial regions should be a
376
      hyperspherical cap when considering l2 neighborhood (Appendix A.1). Notably, based on this model,
377
      we can derive an alternative proof of the GAAS algorithm (Tramèr et al., 2017) (Appendix A.2), and
      as well estimate the volume of adversarial regions for each cap (Appendix A.3).

      6    D IFFUSION MODELS IMPROVE ROBUSTNESS BY COMPRESSING IMAGE SPACE
433
434   Next, we seek to understand how the diffusion models improve robustness in adversarial purification
435   within a particular randomness configuration. In particular, we would like to understand precisely
436   what factors decide the robust/non-robust outcomes after purification. We have shown in Sec. 3.1
437
      that diffusion models pushes images away from the clean samples, with no shrinkage of distances as
      naively expected. Intuitively, this may lead to a drop of clean accuracy. Indeed, we observed that
438
      clean accuracy drop to about 86% after passing clean images of CIFAR-10 through diffusion models
439
      (Table 1). This leads to a conundrum: how can diffusion model increase the robustness after all? We
440   reason that the increase in adversarial accuracy must be due to a narrowing of the gap between the
441   adversarial accuracy and clean accuracy. Crucially, as we will demonstrate below, this narrowing
442   (thus robustness improvement) is due to a compression of image space by diffusion models.
443
444   Diffusion models compress the image neighborhood. Denote the neighbourhood around a clean
445   sample x0 as M, and the diffusion model transform M into M̃ which always contain the transformed
446   clean image f (x0 ) (see Fig. 5a). Recall that the clean accuracy is calculated from based on f (x0 ),
447   and adversarial accuracy is calculated based on worse-case-scenario in the neighbourhood M̃. In
448   the extreme case that M̃ collapses onto f (x0 ), the adversarial accuracy and clean accuracy will
449   be identical. This inspired us to treat a purified clean sample f (x0 ) as a “reference point”, and
450   study the deviation of the worse-case-scenario from it. Specifically, for some perturbation η, the
451
      robustness differences should be decided by the differences of the purified images with respect to the
      reference points, i.e., η ′ = f (x0 + η) − f (x0 ). As hinted in Sec. 3.2, diffusion models may lead to a
452
      comparison of the image space, as for random perturbations, the compression rate ∥η ′ ∥/∥η∥ ≈ 0.25.
453
      Following this initial observation, we develop two methods to investigate the transformation of image
454   space induced by diffusion models.
455
456   (i) Principal Component Analysis (PCA). First, we sampled 10,000 points around the neighborhood
457
      of a clean image and transformed these images using a diffusion model with fixed randomness.
      We performed PCA on the original images and the transformed images to obtain their spectrums,
458
      respectively. As shown in Fig. 5b, after transformed by the diffusion model, most eigenvalues are
459
      much smaller than 1, indicating substantial compression.
460
461   (ii) Analysis of the Jacobian matrix. Under a locally linear assumption, we may use Taylor expansion
462   to study the transformation induced by diffusion model via interrogating the Jacobian at a clean
463
      sample. Specifically, we may write
464                              f (x0 + η) ≈ x0 + (f (x0 ) − x0 ) + Jf (x0 )η,                            (4)
465
466   Here, the term f (x0 )−x0 describes the shift of the entire image neighborhood as analyzed in Sec. 3.2.
467
      Importantly, the eigenspectrum of the Jacobian matrix Jf (x0 ) indicates the amount of compression
      and expansion along each eigen-direction. Fig. 5c plots the averaged eigen-spectrum based on 50
468
      clean images from CIFAR-10. The largest eigenvalue is 1.32 ± 0.21, which is comparable with the
469
      compression rates; around 90% of the eigenvalues (2760 out of 3072) are smaller than 0.25, with
470   only 17 eigenvalues exceeding 1. Together, the results from the two analyses establish a substantial
471   compression of the image neighborhood induced by diffusion models.
472
473   Dissecting the factors underlying whether an attack would succeed/fail. What determines
474
      whether a particular attack would be successful or not under diffusion models? Given the compression
      induced by diffusion model, it is natural to consider that the eigenvalues of the Jacobian of the robust
475
      images may be smaller that those of the non-robust ones. However, comparing the eigenspectrum of
476
      Jacobian matrices for robust v.s. non-robust images, we did not observe a difference between the two.
477
478   We next evaluate a couple of additional factors that may distinguish the two (see Fig. 6). First,
479   the closest decision boundary may be closer for some samples compared to others. This can be
480
      quantified by moving along the adversarial direction starting from the purified clean sample f (x0 )
      and identifying the critical threshold for reversing the decision to a wrong category. Second, because
481
482
      the orientation of transformed image neighborhood M̃ (which can be approximated by an ellipsoid) is
      likely different for different clean images, it may lead to differences in the magnitude of the purified
483
      adversarial attack. We find both factors are important.

518   7    D ISCUSSION
519
      We have systematically examined the properties of diffusion models used in adversarial purification.
520   We show that diffusion models do not shrinkage the distance of the transformed images toward clean
521   images. In fact, it substantially increases the distance. Furthermore, randomness in diffusion models
522   necessitate a new framework in evaluation of adversarial robustness (e.g., fuzzy robustness). We
523   further show that adversarial regions are large and continuous regions that form hyperspherical caps.
524   Importantly, diffusion models increase robustness by shrinkage of the image space to avoid these
525   regions. Several limitations still exist in our current work. First, we do not offer a principled reason of
526   why diffusion models push the perturbed images further away from clean images. We have speculated
527   that this may be related to the generalization v.s. memorization trade-off in high-dimensional space
528
      (Kadkhodaie et al., 2024), which deserves further investigation in the future. Second, we have
      only tested diffusion models that are formulated in the pixel space. One interesting question is
529
      whether these results would generalize to models constructed in latent space (Rombach et al., 2022).
530
      Third, our robust evaluation on ImageNet is non-exhausive. Due to the limitation of computational
531   resources, we were unable to run the PGD-EOT attack on ImageNet. Overall, our results clarify a
532   number of misconceptions about adversarial purification and provide new insights into the behavior of
533   diffusion models. Better understanding the exact mechanisms of how diffusion models and diffusion
534   model-based purification work will likely lead to more robust and transparent AI systems.
