009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent results show that modern Large Language Models (LLM) are indeed capable
012            of understanding and answering questions about structured data such as graphs.
013            This new paradigm can lead to solutions that require less supervision while, at the
014            same time, providing a model that can generalize and answer questions beyond
015            the training labels. Existing proposals often use some description of the graph
016
               to create an “augmented” prompt fed to the LLM. For a chosen class of graphs,
               if a well-tailored graph encoder is deployed to play together with a pre-trained
017
               LLM, the model can answer graph-related questions well. Existing solutions to
018
               graph-based prompts range from graph serialization to graph transformers. In this
019            work, we show that the use of a parameter-free graph encoder based on Fock space
020            representations, a concept borrowed from mathematical physics, is remarkably
021            versatile in this problem setting. The simple construction, inherited directly from
022            the theory with a few small adjustments, can provide rich and informative graph
023            encodings, for a wide range of different graphs. We investigate the use of this idea
024            for prefix-tuned prompts leveraging the capabilities of a pre-trained, frozen LLM.
025            The modifications lead to a model that can answer graph-related questions – from
026            simple graphs to proteins to hypergraphs – effectively and with minimal, if any,
027
               adjustments to the architecture. Our work significantly simplifies existing solutions
               and generalizes well to multiple different graph-based structures effortlessly.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Large Language Models (LLMs) excel at tasks like question answering, sentence completion, trans-
033   lation, and even solving undergraduate-level math problems (Liu et al., 2024; Johansson, 2024).
034   However, they sometimes need additional data unavailable during training. For instance, a model
035   trained on data up to a specific date may struggle with the ever-changing news cycle (Vu et al.,
036   2023; Mousavi et al., 2024). To prevent responses from becoming outdated, or to integrate non-
037   public/proprietary data and domain-specific terminology, models need extra context. Retrieval
038   Augmented Generation (RAG) describes this process of retrieving and integrating extra information
039
      to an LLM during its generation process. While multiple different approaches have been proposed
      for the retrieval part, the most common solution to the integration of the additional information is
040
      In-Context Learning (ICL) (Guu et al., 2020; Ding et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2022; Zoph et al., 2022;
041
      Min et al., 2022). ICL allows additional information to be included with a prompt, guiding the model
042   to generate responses aligned with the extra context. This method is beneficial as it does not require
043   retraining the LLM and can be applied to proprietary models like GPT (Brown et al., 2020) by adding
044   a text description of the extra information.
045
      ICL-type ideas are also being studied for utilizing not just additional/new data but also novel input
046
      formats/modalities, such as tables and graphs (Sui et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023;
047
      Guo et al., 2023). While specialized models may still perform better at specific tasks, LLMs can
048   serve as general-purpose reasoning machines, capable of answering questions about the provided
049   modality beyond the training labels. Several recent results have reported success at “serializing” such
050   structured data-types into a text-form description that can be easily used within ICL. For tables, the
051   serialization is not too complicated (Sui et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024), but more care is needed for
052   graphs. While different types of graphs can all be handled by the same pipeline, the efficacy of the
053   overall model varies from one setting to the other (Fatemi et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023; Guo et al.,
      2023). Further, it has been observed that specific design choices to “textify” the graph can influence

054
      performance and additionally, prompting techniques can have more than a small impact on the results
055   (Fatemi et al., 2024). What will work well in a specific setting depends on both the question at hand
056   as well as the characteristics of the data (Perozzi et al., 2024; Chai et al., 2023).
057
058   Prefix-tuning. One option to address the mentioned issues is “prefix-tuning” (Li & Liang, 2021).
059   A specialized graph encoder translates the underlying graph into embeddings that can be fed directly
060   to an LLM, eliminating the need for a textual description. Although not training-free, the LLM
061   remains frozen, and only the relatively smaller graph encoder is trained. This approach has shown
062   impressive performance, often surpassing ICL-based methods Sun et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2023);
063   Tang et al. (2024). However, using a specialized graph encoder can be challenging due to the variety
064   of graph types, and multiple works have proposed modifications of GNNs that suit their demands.
065
      For example, GraphToken (Perozzi et al., 2024) can encode only simple graphs, while GNP (Tian
      et al., 2024) constructs a complex pipeline to handle large graphs and extract subgraphs. GraphLLM
066
      (Chai et al., 2023) combines a transformer and a GNN (about 100M parameters), requiring detailed
067
      text descriptions for each node. Despite sophisticated designs, adapting these models to different
068   graph types (e.g., protein-derived graphs or hypergraphs) is difficult, and even familiar graph types
069   need adjustments for new tasks.
070
071   Context of this paper. ICL-based approaches for graphs pri-
072   marily involve converting graphs to text, while prefix-tuning with
073   graphs uses modules to extract richer, task-relevant structures,
074   requiring larger sample sizes and higher compute power. A key
075   question is whether we can achieve powerful, task-agnostic graph
076   representations that are as easy to obtain as ICL-based methods.
077
      Could a lightweight adapter map these rich (but task-independent)
      representations into the LLM embedding space, making prefix-
078
      tuning effective for various tasks? Recent results hint that this
079
      may be viable (Moayeri et al., 2023). For instance, a single linear
080   layer can transform an arbitrary image encoder’s outputs to align
081   with CLIP’s (Radford et al., 2021) text encoder embeddings. If Figure 1: Augmenting LLM’s
082   our graph encoding captures the graph’s information and structure capabilities by prompting them
083   well enough, a similar adapter could work with a pre-trained LLM with carefully encoded graphs.
084   to offer good performance. This approach’s success depends on
085   the quality of the graph representations. We ensure this by invoking a mature concept from mathemat-
086   ical physics, called Fock Spaces, whose practical instantiation yields almost lossless task-agnostic
087   graph embeddings. Our findings show that a linear adapter with these representations yields com-
088
      petitive performance, handling complex graph questions and diverse structures like hypergraphs
      and proteins. The main contribution of this paper is the Fock-space inspired encoding of diverse
089
      graph-based structures, ranging from simple graphs to those obtained from proteins. We provide
090
      open-source code for grounding LLMs using our graph encodings as prompts and carefully profile
091   the performance of this pipeline relative to baselines, on diverse datasets.

095
096   We will first review a few notations and results which will together provide the conceptual pipeline
097
      for obtaining our representations of graphs for prompting. While graphs serve as representative
      examples here, the rationale for other types of structured data such as tables is similar.
098
099
      Setup/rationale. Consider a graph G = (V, E) with a vertex set V and an edge set E; | · | denotes
100
      set cardinality. We define the incidence matrix (Hatcher, 2002), I to be of shape |V | × |E| where
101
      Iij = 1 if edge j ends at vertex i, −1 if edge j starts at vertex i and 0 elsewhere. Let |V | = n. It
102   is common to represent graphs via graph spectra derived from the Laplacian’s eigenvalues. This is
103   effective for studying global properties of graphs like connectivity/symmetries (e.g., Courant Fischer
104   theorem, Fiedler’s theorem (Fiedler, 1973; 1989)) but less so for capturing localized relationships
105   between individual entities (nodes, edges, faces) within the graph. It turns out that an interesting
106   direction using Clifford Algebra, shown so far to be effective in geometric problems in machine
107   learning (Ruhe et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2024; Ruhe et al., 2023b;a; Brehmer et al., 2023), provides
      rigorous tools for representing various graph elements (nodes, edges, faces) in a nice algebraic

114   Clifford Algebra. Let K be a field, i.e., comprised of elements that can be added, subtracted,
115   multiplied, and divided (except by zero). Let W be a K-vector space, i.e., it is a vector space over the
116   field K meaning that the vectors in W can be added, subtracted, and multiplied by scalars (elements
117   from K) following some rules. Let W be equipped with a symmetric bilinear form ⟨·, ·⟩ (or more
118   generally, a quadratic form) where in case of graphs, W = C, and let T (W ) denote the exterior
119   algebra of W , a structure on top of the vector space W to capture all possible products of vectors
120
      w ∈ W (this will include scalar multiples and sums of products). Let I(W ) be the ideal in T (W )
      generated by the set {w ⊗ w + ⟨w, w⟩1}, where 1 denotes the multiplicative identity in K and w ⊗ w
121
      represents the product of a vector w with itself in the exterior algebra. Recall that I(W ) is a subset of
122
      T (W ), with the property that the product of an element from I(W ) and an element from T (W ) is
123   in I(W ) (a closure property). For an in-depth analysis, we point the reader to Dorst et al. (2009);
124   Lounesto (2001).
125
      Definition 1. Let W be a vector space over a field K, equipped with a quadratic form q : W → K.
126
      The Clifford algebra of (W, q), denoted Cl(W, q), is the quotient algebra T (W )/I(W, q).
127
128   We take the exterior algebra T (W ) and “divide” it by the ideal I(W ). The ideal acts as a “filter” to
129   filter out information captured by the ideal (all terms where a vector gets multiplied by itself along
130   with its corresponding scalar term from the bilinear form) since they do not add much to the structure
131   (set to scalar multiples of the identity element). But the ideal does more: it establishes an equivalence
132   relation that changes the multiplication operation.

145   Practical Considerations in Clifford Algebra Operations. We can closely follow the axioms of
146   Clifford Algebra and through its wedge product build higher-order elements while preserving the
147   geometric structure at hand (e.g., hyperedges or faces to multi-vectors). While cleanly rooted in
148   theory, this leads to problems in practice. Implementing the full Clifford algebra structure over an
149   n-dimensional vector space implies working with an algebra of dimension 2n . We must also define
150
      the multiplicative and graded structure, and, despite progress, software support is limited for higher
      dimensions (see (Zhdanov et al., 2024) and projects like Grassmann.jl and GeometricAlgebra.jl).
151
      Hence, we will need to make some design choices that balance practicality and mathematical
152
      soundness.

203   2.3   T RANSLATING T HEORY TO P RACTICE : I NSTANTIATING A G RAPH R EPRESENTATION

205
      The Fock space formulation provides a framework for representing multi-particle systems, analogous
      to encoding features from a graph. However, implementing the full structure, especially in high
206
      dimensions, can be challenging. Vector Symbolic Architectures (VSA), as explored in recent works
207
      (Alam et al., 2023; Ganesan et al., 2021), offer a practical approximation of Fock spaces with
208   computational efficiency. In VSA, the binding operation (circular convolution) approximates the
209   creation/annihilation operators, while the superposition operation (vector addition) resembles the
210   direct sum in Fock spaces. Although the VSA←    → quantum mechanics connection is not new (Wolff
211   et al., 2018), in this context it helps use the power of Fock spaces while offering computational
212   efficiency.
213
214   Representing nodes, sums, and products. In our implementation, we assign a high-dimensional
215   vector to each concept (node, edge, and so on). These vectors play a role analogous to the basis
      elements in the expansion of the Dirac operator. While ideally, these vectors would be orthogonal,

      For operations analogous to those in Fock space, we use dimensionality-preserving operations instead

      of tensor products, which significantly increase dimensionality (Wolff et al., 2018). This simplifies
230   implementation, as all resultant embeddings maintain the same dimensionality regardless of the
231   encoding method. We define the sum (⊕) as element-wise addition and the product (⊗) as circular
232   convolution; an operation analogous to the creation and annihilation operators in Fock space. This can

234   an inverse Fourier transformation. Notice that, as d grows, these operations asymptotically approach
235   the algebraic properties of Fock space (i.e., the probability that we violate the algebraic properties of

237   the inverse vector, i.e., for any vector b, we have a vector a such that the identity a ⊗ b = 1 holds. It

      is known that other properties like commutation relations, super-position and self-commutation are
      also mostly satisfied in VSA. Note that our experiments are not tied to this specific implementation,

      and improved choices can be dropped in.

242   Dealing with infinitely many concepts. In some datasets in our experiments, each vertex comes
243
      with a text description. Defining one vector per word or sentence at random is not ideal anymore. To
      avoid this problem, we use a text encoder. Models like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), BERT (Devlin
244
      et al., 2019), RoBERTa (Liu et al., 2019), and others are effective at mapping whole text passages to
245
      vectors in a way that the information is preserved while similar sentences are mapped to similar areas
246   in space. So, we initialize our construction by defining the vectors as such an encoding. In this way,
247   (a) we can create infinitely many vectors, and (b) similar vectors represent similar concepts. When
248   the dimensionality permits, we keep the default sampling approach, and note the use of text-encoders
249   explicitly in the experiments.

251   Other Works using Vector Symbolic Architectures. There is a growing body of results in the
252   literature using Vector Symbolic Architecture (VSA) (Schlegel et al., 2022) albeit for other problems.
253   The idea has its roots in symbolic AI, where VSA sought to benefit from the high dimensional
254   representations in addition to well-defined logical rules for combining these symbols/vectors in some
255   manner. Many works using VSA describe the construction mechanistically, deriving specific ways to
256   generate the underlying symbols as well as implementing the “merge” operations. The possibility of
257   using Fock space for symbolic manipulation (Wolff et al., 2018) has been identified by others with
258
      example results reported regarding its utility in trajectory analysis. Vector symbolic representations
      have also been recently used for computational efficiency considerations related to self-attention
259
      calculation in HRRFormers (Plate, 1995; Alam et al., 2023).

348
      parameter-free and efficient. The complexity of aggregation is O(d log d) (d is vectors’ dimension)

496
497   Geometric Algebra in Machine Learning. There is growing interest in application of geometric
498   algebra in machine learning, particularly for developing neural networks that maintain geometric
499   properties. While these ideas have been leveraged in the context of equivariance/symmetry trans-
500   formations in deep learning (Cohen et al., 2019; Bronstein et al., 2021; Banerjee et al., 2022; Zhu
501   et al., 2018; Finzi et al., 2020), the theory is finding interesting uses in recent works. For example,
502   (Zhdanov et al., 2024) recently proposed Clifford Neural Layers to model dynamical systems in
503   fields like fluid dynamics and (Ruhe et al., 2023b) described Geometric Clifford Algebra Networks
504   (GCANs), specifically designed to respect symmetry group transformations. Beyond classical ma-
505
      chine learning, geometric algebra finds more direct applications in quantum computing as well:
      (Trindade et al., 2023) leveraged the isomorphism between Pauli matrices and Clifford Algebra to
506
      represent multidimensional data, to define specialized transforms for machine learning tasks.
507
508
      Graphs & LLMs. The body of work describing ways to infuse extra, graphical information into a
509
      frozen LLM is sizable and growing. As discussed earlier, initial approaches focused on converting
510   the underlying graph into natural language form, such as “node 1 is connected to node 3, node 5 is
511   connected to node 4, . . . ” (Wang et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023; Fatemi et al., 2024). These works
512   while far from perfect showed viability: that a frozen LLM has the capability to reason about the
513   given graph and answer graph-related questions, such as “is there a cycle in the graph?”. Practical
514   difficulties involving the format of graph serialization is an important factor in the performance and
515   the results tend to be only moderately better than random. The perspective taken in (Perozzi et al.,
516   2024; Chai et al., 2023) was fresh and led to an alternative approach: infusing the graph information
517   directly at the embedding level, by encoding the graph using a model such as a Graph Neural Network
518
      (GNN) (Scarselli et al., 2009; Wu et al., 2022; Perozzi et al., 2024) or a Graph Transformer (Dwivedi
      & Bresson, 2020; Chai et al., 2023). These works significantly improved the state of the art, showing
519
      that carefully crafted graph embeddings are key to a successful grounding of an LLM.

522   6   C ONCLUSIONS
523
524   We have described a novel strategy to encode a graph into a vector form for direct downstream use
525   or to augment prompts fed to LLMs. Our approach, grounded in Clifford algebra and Fock space
526   operations, is rigorous and offers numerous advantages in practice demonstrated via experiments.
527   We can obtain encodings of arbitrary graphs instantly, with no trainable parameters that nicely
528
      encapsulates the important information content in the underlying graph. Using these encodings, we
      introduced FoGE-LLM, a way to fuse the graph information for graph-prompting with a pre-trained,
529
      frozen LLM, allowing it to “understand” and reason about graphs. Our model, accompanied with a
530
      simple-to-train open-source codebase, performs favorably relative to highly specialized models while
531   at the same time handling classes of graphs where other alternatives fall short or need adjustments.
532
533
      Limitations. A key strength of our method is its generality; it is a parameter-free way to obtain rich
534   graph embeddings. However, if our method fails to produce informative-enough graph embeddings
535   for graphs in a specific application, the parameter-free nature offers us very few knobs to turn to
536   improve the performance. The fix, we believe, is to build representation learners on top of the
537   embeddings derived by our model which we conjecture is happening to some extent in FoGE-LLM
538   anyway. Additionally, as noted before, when we need to deal with a potentially infinitely large set of
539   vectors, generating them at random is infeasible. While, for our experiments, RoBERTa appears to be
      a sensible option, the efficacy may not translate to other new datasets.

      A    DATASET DETAILS

      In our experiments, we used the following datasets:
867
868         1. GraphQA (Fatemi et al., 2024): It includes 6 different graph-understanding tasks (number
869            of nodes, number of edges, cycle existence, number of triangles, node degree, and edge
870            existence) on 7 different graph structures (Erdos-Renyi (Erdös & Rényi, 1959), Scale-
871            Free, Barabasi-Albert (Albert & Barabási, 2002), Stochastic Block Model, Star, Path and
872            Complete).
873         2. GraphReasoning (Chai et al., 2023): Recently introduced in (Chai et al., 2023) to better
874            assess the model’s graph understanding ability, it consists of 4 more advanced graph-
875            understanding tasks (substructure count, maximum triplet sum, shortest path, and bipartite
876            graph matching). Each graph node is accompanied by extra information in the form of a text
877            description, making this dataset a suitable testbed for our RoBERTa-based vector encoding.
878         3. HyperGraphQA: We extend GraphQA to Hypergraphs. Specifically, we consider 4 different
879            graph-understanding tasks (number of nodes, number of edges, node degree, and edge
880            existence) on 2 different hypergraph structures (Erdos-Renyi (Erdös & Rényi, 1959), and
881            Chung-Lu (Chung & Lu, 2002)). The training dataset consists of only 2000 instances,
882            making it hard for large models to avoid overfitting.
883         4. Jaffe (Jaffe et al., 2022): Jaffe is a recent dataset consisting of approximately 1.6 million
884            natively paired human antibody sequences from healthy donors. To our knowledge, this
885            represents by far the largest publicly available dataset of its kind.
886         5. PPI (Hamilton et al., 2017): PPI consists of 24 proteins collected from human tissue, with
887            each node associated with 121 binary labels. Compiled from experimental techniques like
888            yeast two-hybrid screening and mass spectrometry, as well as computational predictions,
889            such a dataset provides critical insights into the functional organization of the proteome. By
890            understanding how proteins interact, scientists can uncover the molecular underpinnings of
891            cellular processes and develop targeted therapeutic strategies.
892         6. OBNB (Liu & Krishnan, 2024): OBNB (Open Biomedical Network Benchmark) is a
893            collection of 15 datasets (including well-known datasets such as BioGRID (Stark et al.,
894            2006) and HumanNet (Hwang et al., 2019)). Each dataset’s sample consists of a gene
895            accompanied by 3 vectors (named DISEASES, DisGeNET, GOBP) of node-level binary
896
               labels.
897         7. SabDab (Dunbar et al., 2013): SabDab (Structural Antibody Database) is a collection of 919
898            publicly available, annotated antibody structures (proteins). Each structure is accompanied
899            by multiple annotations, such as the heavy and light chain pairing.

901   B    F O GE-LLM: TRAINING DETAILS
902
903   We train the LLM-based construction with a batch size of 16 and a learning rate of 1e-3. The model
904   required less than 10 epochs to convergence, in contrast to other works that require more training
905   time due to the ellaborate graph encoders (e.g., Chai et al. (2023)). Our implementation is based on
906   Pytorch Lightning Falcon & The PyTorch Lightning team (2019), which allows us to split and train
907   the model on multiple GPUs using FSDP. This implementation allows the user to train this, or any
908   similar, model to conventional GPUs with less memory while, at the same time, speed up the process
909   by preloading all the obtained lightweight graph embeddings to the GPUs. The merging of the graph
910
      embedding with the LLM is based on the idea of prefix tuning Li & Liang (2021), i.e., pre-append the
      embedding to the input text embeddings and, in our case, this is happening with the use of a linear
911
      adapter. We experimented both with a single linear adapter on the input layer, as well as a linear
912
      adapter per layer and the difference was only marginal in the final results.

1064   H     I MPACT OF VECTOR DIMENSION
1065
1066   One of few the hyperparameters of FoGE is the dimensionality of the vectors (i.e. graph embeddings).
1067
       Using GraphQA, we perform an ablation study on the impact of the dimension on the final accuracy
       of the model (Fig. 9). Relative accuracy is calculated as the actual accuracy for each dimensionality,
1068
       divided by the best one, for each task respectively, and it allows us to compare different tasks with
1069
       completely different best performances (Table 4).
1070
1071   From this study, a few important remarks surface that we observe to hold true for the other datasets
1072   too. First of all, a larger dimensionality does not always “translate” to better results. We observe that
1073
       for some tasks (cycle existence), we achieve the optimal performance with a dimension significantly
       lower than the maximum we consider (2048), matching essentially GraphToken’s performance with
1074
       less than 20K trainable parameters, while in some cases there is a small drop as we go from 1024 to
1075
       2048. Finally, as with most of the tunable hyperparameters in machine learning models, there is no
1076   predetermined best strategy for choosing the dimensionality. For instance, when we consider cycle
1077   existence or the number of triangles we can have a highly performing model with a dimensionality of
1078   less than 128, while for tasks such as edge and node count the performance drops significantly as we
1079   reduce the dimensionality.
