011                                                     A BSTRACT
012
013                Large Language Models (LLMs) are frequently utilized as sources of knowledge
014                for question-answering. While it is known that LLMs may lack access to real-time
015                data or newer data produced after the model’s cutoff date, it is less clear how their
016                knowledge spans across historical information. In this study, we assess the breadth
017
                   of LLMs’ knowledge using financial data of U.S. publicly traded companies by
                   evaluating more than 190k questions and comparing model responses to factual
018
                   data. We further explore the impact of company characteristics, such as size,
019
                   retail investment, institutional attention, and readability of financial filings, on the
020                accuracy of knowledge represented in LLMs. Our results reveal that LLMs are less
021                informed about past financial performance, but they display a stronger awareness of
022                larger companies and more recent information. Interestingly, at the same time, our
023                analysis also reveals that LLMs are more likely to hallucinate for larger companies,
024                especially for data from more recent years. We will make the code, prompts, and
025                model outputs public upon the publication of the work.

028   1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030   As research and development of Large Language Models (LLMs) continues to progress, evidence
031   has emerged of heavy usage of these models within the financial domain. Cheng et al. (2024)
032   finds that trading volumes significantly decline during ChatGPT outages, suggesting heavy use of
033   LLMs by investors, and numerous research papers have been published indicating that LLMs can
034   provide investment advice or generate investment strategies (Romanko et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2024).
035   Notably, Oehler & Horn (2024) finds that by offering advice on bond and equity ETFs, “ChatGPT
036   provides better financial advice for one-time investments than robo-advisors”. More so, Yue et al.
037
      (2023) claims that LLMs can serve as a method to democratize financial knowledge. Such heavy
      reliance on LLMs in the financial industry warrants a closer investigation into the various biases
038
      that may be present in these models, as these biases may result in significant adverse affects when
039
      these models are employed for financial decisions. Biased or inaccurate information generated by
040   LLMs may cause sub-optimal financial decision-making and lead to unintended downstream effects
041   in financial markets. For example, as investors incorporate LLMs into their research workflows, a
042   bias in LLMs towards favoring larger firms may ultimately cause reduced capital allocation to smaller
043   firms (see Appendix K).
044
      In the broad realm of natural language processing (NLP), there has been a considerable inquiry
045
      into biases, such as gender (Lu et al., 2020), political (Motoki et al., 2023), and cultural biases
046   (Naous et al., 2024). However, there has been limited study of LLM biases in the financial domain.
047   In this work, we aim to identify and analyze these biases by examining where knowledge gaps in
048   these models are present. We consider how various factors such as time period, size, popularity
049   among retail and institutional investors, market capitalization,1 and the readability of financial reports
050   affect the answering capabilities of LLMs, and whether these knowledge gaps are biased along
051   certain temporal or cross-sectional2 factors. Our study evaluates leading language models, including

074   Our analysis shows several key findings. We see that there is a possible “retrograde” knowledge bias,
075   where LLMs are unable to answer financial questions before a certain time (e.g., what is a company’s
076   revenue back in the year 1982? See Figure 1). This complements existing research that highlights
077   LLMs’ struggles with knowledge produced after the cut-off dates of pre-training data (Onoe et al.,
078   2022; Kasai et al., 2024). For instance, Llama-3-70B-Chat accurately answers revenue values for
079   52.01% of companies in 2018 while answering accurately for only 7.47% of companies in the year
080
      1995 —despite financial data being publicly available for all U.S. companies since 1995.
081   We also find that LLMs demonstrate better accuracy for companies with larger market capitalizations,
082   higher attention from both retail and institutional investors, higher number of SEC filing accesses,
083   and better filing readability. For Llama-3-70B-Chat, a tenfold increase in market capitalizations of
084   the company leads to a 0.9551 rise in the log odds ratio of accurately answering revenue-related
085   questions.
086   Additionally, we extend our analysis to examine how both time and firm factors relate to the model’s
087   propensity to produce factuality hallucinations, one of two types of hallucinations defined by Huang
088   et al. (2023). Factuality hallucinations are defined as discrepancies between LLM-generated content
089   and verifiable real-world facts. When the LLM response contains a numerical value, we can establish
090   a numerical measurement to identify factuality hallucination. We calculate the error rates of LLM
091   answers with respect to ground truth values and consider a response a hallucination if the error rate
092
      is above a certain threshold (10%). Interestingly, we find that LLMs tend to hallucinate more for
      those same firms for which it also sees higher accuracy; for Llama-3-70B-Chat, a tenfold increase in
093
      market capitalizations results in a 0.1712 rise in the log odds ratio of hallucinating revenue—again,
094
      despite financial data being widely available.
095
096   As a result of this work, we put forth the following contributions:
097
098          • Utilizing diverse financial datasets to construct and analyze over 190k questions, providing
099
               insights into the performance and bias of leading LLMs.
100          • Introducing a novel, systematic method for analyzing temporal knowledge biases of LLMs.
101            To the best of our knowledge, this is the first study to analyze the retrograde knowledge bias
102            in LLMs.
103
             • Utilizing our cross-sectional analysis framework to investigate a range of factors that are
104
               associated with knowledge bias in LLMs.
105
106          • Conducting a comprehensive hallucination analysis that identifies a notable trend of over-
107            confidence in LLMs, particularly evident in responses concerning specific companies and
               time periods.

      To facilitate our analysis, we begin by constructing RevenuePromptDataset: a novel dataset of

      2.1       C ONSTRUCTION OF R EVENUE P ROMPT DATASET (RPD)
141
142   To construct our dataset, we compile 190,956 samples covering company-year samples available in
143   both “Compustat North America” and “Monthly Stock File” databases from 1980 to 2020. Each
144   sample has a company name, year, and revenue (in million USD) information. The dataset includes
145   17,621 unique companies, including those that have entered or exited the market due to IPOs,
146   bankruptcy, or privatization.3 Besides revenue and market cap, we also draw data from other
147   resources to consider other key variables, including the number of retail investors, institutional
148
      attention, number of SEC filing access, and readability of regulatory filings by the company. These
      variables are utilized for our cross-sectional analysis, and as such we discuss these variables and
149
      their sources in more detail in Section 4.1. We provide supplementary details on each dataset in the
150
      Appendix B.1.
151
152   We use the entire dataset of 190k samples in most of our analyses for relatively less expensive models
153   (GPT-3.5-turbo-0613, Llama-3-8B-Chat, and Llama-3-70B-Chat). For the costly GPT-4, and Gemini
154
      1.5 Pro model, we use a subset (RPD200perY ear ) of the full sample. In addition, we create another
      subset (RPD460 ) with 460 companies that have a long public history, being available for every year
155
      from 1980 to 2020. These subsets of RPD are discussed in further detail in Appendix B.3.

      This study’s prompt design is informed by the observation that retail investors with limited financial
163   sophistication are inclined to rely on readily accessible LLMs. Given the potential for errors or
164   inaccuracies in these models, such vulnerable populations could face significant adverse consequences.
165   This concern is underscored by the analysis presented in Chava et al. (2022), which examines the
166   responses of financially unsophisticated investors to non-informative cryptocurrency endorsements
167   by celebrities. While more elaborate and sophisticated prompting strategies may yield more precise
168   outcomes, it is less likely that these investor groups would employ complex prompting methods or
169   engage in further fine-tuning of the models. Moreover, the LLMs are able to understand the simple
170   prompting employed here. This is verified in the manual evaluation of the small sample presented in
171
      Appendix D.
172   We utilize our prompts to perform zero-shot prompting on the three LLMs. For our three LLMs,
173   we utilize a temperature value of 0.00 (for reproducibility) and max_token value of 100. Further
174   implementation details for models are provided in Appendix C. Based on the output of the model,
175   we utilize regex to extract the numerical value of revenue if it is available in the answer, and to
176   standardize units to millions of dollars for consistency in evaluations we perform (Figure 3). The
177
      effectiveness of regex is discussed in Appendix D. We calculate absolute % error between the model’s
      extracted revenue value and the ground truth revenue value we have. We utilize this calculation for
178
      temporal and cross-sectional analysis later.

      Investors vs. Researchers: It is important for investors who continue to utilize LLMs for their
420   workflows to be aware of the risks for bias we highlight in our work. We caution investors to not
421   rely on LLMs for factual information and/or open-ended analysis, as the biases demonstrated in our
422   work may cause unintended harm. In particular, the effects of these biases may cause more harm
423   for new investors, whose inexperience may lead them to be less likely to discern incorrect factual
424   information produced by LLMs. For example, Kumar (2009) shows that younger investors tend
425   to invest more in "lottery-type" stocks, sacrificing financial due diligence and leaving them more
426   vulnerable to biases in LLM-generated investment advice. We encourage financial literacy educators
427   to consider the harmful effects of LLM bias, and make these risks clear in their teachings.
428   Researchers who study applications of LLMs in finance should be mindful to take into consideration
429   the various stratas that their particular task may span, and design their study and experiments to
430   include comprehensive coverage of those stratas. They should ensure their experimentation covers
431   firms (or other entities) across a breadth of years, sizes, degrees of prominence, and geographic or
      cultural boundaries. Careful consideration of stratified biases may add to the strength of results and

      ensure that results were not the result of biases in the model, or that the results were only valid for
433   certain stratas.
434
435   Guidance for LLM developers: LLM developers may be able to, and should to the best of
436   their ability, take measures that may reduce bias in their models. Most importantly, they should
437   be mindful of the data used for pre-training and consider how biases in this data may cause biases
438   in the model. Such analysis of pre-training data sources is especially relevant for domain-specific
439   models. While it may be more difficult to assess all potential sources of bias for LLMs trained on a
440   general-domain corpus, developers of domain-specific LLMs should keep in mind the narrower set
441   of potential use cases for their model and ensure that pre-training data is optimized to reduce bias
442
      in those use cases. In addition, debiasing techniques can also be employed to further improve the
      efficacy of domain-specific models.
443
444
      Broader Impacts and Future Work: While we focus on the financial domain in this work, our
445
      temporal and cross-sectional evaluation and analysis framework is broadly applicable for assessing
446
      LLM knowledge across various domains. Many knowledge-intensive tasks for which LLMs may be
447   utilized can be assessed both temporally and along certain entity-specific stratas. For example, an
448   evaluation of LLM applications in the legal domain may examine LLMs’ ability to answer questions
449   about laws passed at different times (e.g. can the LLM answer questions better about a law passed
450   in 2018 compared to a law passed in 1890?), as well as assessing LLM ability to answer questions
451   about laws with varying media attention.
452
      The application of our temporal and cross-sectional analysis frameworks to other domains in which
453   LLMs are frequently utilized would be of significant value. Knowledge biases are likely to cause
454   significant adverse affects in most fields, and successfully identifying such biases in other fields is a
455   promising avenue for future research.
456
      As an example of an extension of our framework for more diverse applications, we apply it to assess
457
      LLMs’ ability to answer questions about soccer statistics from the annual La Liga championship. We
458
      utilized our evaluation framework to assess Llama3-70B’s ability to answer the following question:
459   "In the {years} La Liga season, how many points did {team} finish with?". We find that the model sees
460   higher accuracy for more recent seasons and for more prominent soccer clubs, highlighting a potential
461   bias towards more recent seasons and larger clubs. We hope that such analysis provides a clear
462   demonstration of the value that our evaluation framework can bring to other domains. We present an
463   in-depth description of our methodology and results for the soccer experiments in Appendix L.

465   6    R ELATED W ORK
466
467   We provide a brief review of the existing research regarding societal and political biases and halluci-
468   nation in LLMs. Our study is distinctive in its approach, as it is, to our understanding, the inaugural
469   effort to retrospectively assess knowledge bias in LLMs. Furthermore, this research pioneers in
470   exploring the multitude of factors influencing financial knowledge bias within these models.
471
472   Biases in LLMs: There has been much research involving the evaluation of LLMs’ societal
473   bias, such as gender, religion, race, politics, etc (Zhao et al., 2017; Lu et al., 2020; Sheng et al.,
474   2021; Nozza et al., 2022; Kotek et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Blodgett et al., 2020). Inspired by
475
      software testing, Nozza et al. (2022) proposed a systematic way of integrating societal bias testing
      into development pipelines. Zhao et al. (2023) unraveled the LLMs’ "re-judgment inconsistency"
476
      in bias evaluation by leveraging psychological theories. Gender bias, as the issue most frequently
477
      addressed, has been resolved by diversified methods. Zhao et al. (2018) curated a new benchmark
478   dataset WinoBias for testing and dispelling gender bias in LLMs. Kotek et al. (2023) use a simpler
479   paradigm to identify gender bias and reveal that LLMs still tend to reflect the imbalance of their
480   training dataset even after aligning with human preference. Additionally, Motoki et al. (2023) extends
481   bias evaluations to political domains, revealing significant biases in ChatGPT towards certain political
482   groups, necessitating further scrutiny measures in training processes.
483
484   Hallucination in LLMs: Research on measuring hallucination in LLMs has produced several
485   innovative methodologies aimed at quantifying and addressing the issue. Zellers et al. (2019) were
      among the pioneers, introducing Grover, a model designed for both generating and detecting "neural


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      fake news" to study the propensity of LLMs to generate hallucinated content. Goyal & Durrett
487   (2020) introduces a method to assess the factuality of text generation, targeting hallucinations by
488   analyzing the dependency-level entailment between generated content and source material. Lin
489   et al. (2021) provides a dataset for testing model truthfulness, indirectly evaluating hallucinations
490   by measuring alignment with factual information. Huang et al. (2023) presents a comprehensive
491   overview of hallucination detection methods and benchmarks, categorizing hallucination into two
492   groups: factuality hallucination and faithfulness hallucination. To the best of our knowledge, our
493   work is the first to measure hallucination in LLMs for the financial domain.
494
495   LLMs in Finance:         Recent advancements in LLMs have significantly impacted the financial
496   domain. Nie et al. (2024) introduced CFinBench, a comprehensive Chinese financial benchmark
497   designed to assess LLMs’ financial knowledge across various categories, including financial subjects,
498   qualifications, practices, and laws. Similarly, Xie et al. (2023) developed PIXIU, a framework com-
499
      prising a financial LLM fine-tuned with instruction data, alongside an evaluation benchmark covering
      various financial tasks. Kosireddy et al. (2024) explored the readiness of small language models
500
      for democratizing financial literacy, analyzing their performance in financial question-answering
501
      tasks. Additionally, Shah & Chava (2023) benchmarked the zero-shot performance of LLMs like
502   ChatGPT on financial tasks, comparing them with fine-tuned models to assess their effectiveness in
503   the financial domain. Collectively, these studies underscore the growing role of LLMs in enhancing
504   financial applications and literacy.
505

507   7   C ONCLUSION
508
509   In this study, we examine both the temporal knowledge bias and bias across firm-characteristic stratas
510   in LLMs using financial data from U.S. publicly traded companies. Our findings reveal that LLMs
511
      are more proficient with recent financial information, especially after the 1995 introduction of the
      SEC’s EDGAR filing system. However, there’s an unexplained dip in performance for 2019 and
512
      2020. Secondly, LLMs demonstrate better accuracy for companies with larger market capitalizations,
513
      higher retail investment, higher institutional attention, higher number of SEC filing access, and
514   higher readability. We also find that in the years and companies for which LLMs are more likely
515   to provide a correct answer, they are also more likely to hallucinate. In essence, while LLMs offer
516   valuable insights, their limitations in financial knowledge necessitate careful usage, especially in
517   professional financial domains. Future work should explore the reasons behind these trends and
518   enhance LLMs’ performance breadth, as well as expand our methodology to other domains. This
519   study further contributes to the discourse on how the availability of the pre-training corpus of LLMs
520   for independent scientific scrutiny can facilitate scientific advancement.

       In order to demonstrate the applicability of our LLM bias evaluation framework to non-finance
1049   domains, we examined the application of our framework to assess LLMs’ abilities to answer questions
1050   about soccer statistics. We compiled a dataset of 1,676 samples containing season statistics for a
1051   given season and team in both the La Liga and Segunda División Spanish soccer leagues. Our dataset
1052   spans 41 seasons from 1980 to 2020 and contains statistics about 167 unique teams, though not every
1053   team is present every year, as teams are relegated down to or promoted from lower leagues based on
1054   their performance.
1055   We then assess the ability of Llama3-70B (temperature=0, max_tokens=100) to answer the question:
1056   "In the {year} {La Liga/Segunda División} season, how many points did {team} finish with?". We
1057   assess the quality of responses across both the temporal and cross-sectional dimensions, looking at
1058   differences in model performance in different years, as well as differences in model performance for
1059   clubs with greater prominence (measured by finishing position). Model performance is measured
1060   using a success rate metric, which for this study is defined as the percentage of model responses
1061   which give a point value within 5% of the true point value.
