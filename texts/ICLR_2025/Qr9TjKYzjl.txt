009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               In Model-Based Reinforcement Learning (MBRL), an agent learns to make deci-
012            sions by building a world model that predicts the environment’s dynamics. The
013            accuracy of this world model is crucial for generalizability and sample efficiency.
014            Many works rely on pixel-level reconstruction, which may focus on irrelevant,
015            exogenous features over minor, but key information. In this work, to encourage
016            the world model to focus on important task related information, we propose an
017            augmentation to the world model training using a temporal prediction loss in the
018            embedding space as an auxiliary loss. Building our method on the DreamerV3
019            architecture, we improve sample efficiency and stability by learning better rep-
020
               resentation for world model and policy training. We evaluate our method on the
               Atari100k and Distracting Control Suite benchmarks, demonstrating significant
021
               improvements in world model quality and overall MBRL performance.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   In Model-Based Reinforcement Learning (MBRL), the goal is to learn a useful policy by predicting
028   the dynamics of the environment and rolling out the steps in the model. This model — often referred
029   to as a world model — acts as a surrogate for the environment, allowing the agent to simulate its
030   actions while preventing actual interaction with it. Thus, accurately modeling the dynamics of
031   the environment in a sample-efficient manner is essential towards a sample-efficient reinforcement
032   learning (RL) agent.
033   A key challenge in RL is to distinguish endogenous and exogenous information. Endogenous infor-
034   mation is the internal, task-relevant information that directly influences the dynamics of the environ-
035   ment and the success of the agent’s policy. Exogenous information is the irrelevant or non-essential
036   information that does not influence the underlying dynamics of the task or the agent’s policy. Pixel-
037   level reconstruction is utilized to learn the world model by many (Hafner et al., 2020; 2021; 2023;
038   Micheli et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024), however, it has pitfalls that we aim to tackle in this paper.
039
      Firstly, limited bandwidth may favor minor endogenous information over major exogenous infor-
      mation. Also, the RL agent might learn to rely on exogenous information for the policy, resulting in
040
      a subpar and spurious policy. Learning to represent minor exogenous information is crucial, which
041
      can be illustrated with ”breakout” game from Atari. In this game, the player controls a paddle to
042   bounce a small ball and break bricks in a big black space. Being only one pixel out of 4096, the ball
043   is minor endogenous information, however, the black space is major exogenous feature. If we can-
044   not represent the ball properly, the model can only learn from the movement of the paddle and the
045   disappearance of the pixels, which is a harder task than the original. We show that by representing
046   the ball

051   aim to learn the representation by using only self-supervised learning (SSL) — specifically, learning
052   a forward prediction in the latent space, where the prediction is between the features of temporally
053   distant observations. This decoupling between the RL agent and representation learning connects
      well to our MBRL case — RL agent training and world model training are also decoupled. This

      2    R ELATED WORKS
109
110
111   MBRL and World models World models (Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018) improve sample-efficiency
112   (Mahe et al., 2021) to learn the state transition dynamics and maximize the return (Xu et al., 2018). It
113   has been shown both empirically (Jiang et al., 2020) and theoretically (Sun et al., 2019) that MBRL
114   yields a more accurate policy than model-free RL. The idea of learning a model of the environment
115   and using the world model for learning a task has been around for many years (Nguyen & Widrow,
116   1990; Schmidhuber, 1990; Jordan & Rumelhart, 2013). Early works in MBRL typically relied on
117   low-dimensional state spaces for model prediction (Williams et al., 2017; Janner et al., 2019; 2020),
118   with limited applicability in larger, complex tasks. Therefore, many recent works (Gelada et al.,
119
      2019; Rafailov et al., 2021; Hafner et al., 2023) have focused on learning models of environments
      that the agent can interact with to solve complex tasks. These world models also include hierarchi-
120
      cal world models that utilize goal-conditioned RL (Gumbsch et al., 2023), hierarchical exploration
121
      (Mattes et al., 2024), and multi-time scale prediction (Shaj Kumar et al., 2023).
122
123   Representation learning in MBRL Many recent efforts have been put on to the construction of
124   precise and sample-efficient models of the environment (Hafner et al., 2020; 2021; 2019a), with
125
      some work solely focusing on constructing a robust MDP in a stricter setting (Hafner et al., 2019a;
      Agarwal et al., 2020). Some works rely on temporal consistency for learning the world model (Zhao
126
      et al., 2023; Hansen et al., 2022; 2024; Yan et al., 2023). The DreamerV1-3 models (Hafner et al.,
127
      2020; 2021; 2023) use RSSM (Hafner et al., 2019b) to learn a latent representation of the world
128   and use the hidden states to predict the next abstract states. Many works build on the Dreamer
129   architecture by augmenting it with different ideas, such as learning task informed representations
130   coming from the policy (Fu et al., 2021), modeling two independent latent MDPs that represent
131   useful signal and noise (Wang et al., 2022), utilizing a contrastive loss (Okada & Taniguchi, 2021;
132   Poudel et al., 2024), using mutual information Zhu et al. (2023), and reconstruction in the latent
133   space (Sun et al., 2024). There are multiple pathways for using a transformer as the backbone of the
134   world models. IRIS (Micheli et al., 2022) uses VQ-VAE (Kingma, 2013; Van Den Oord et al., 2017)
135   to quantize the observations into tokens to learn a GPT2 (Radford et al., 2019) backbone for the
136
      world model. Delta IRIS (Micheli et al., 2024) improves the efficiency by encoding the stochasticity
      and simulating the resulting tokens. Furthermore, STORM (Zhang et al., 2024) uses a stochastic
137
      transformer to improve the efficiency and precision of the world model. Unlike previous approaches
138
      that rely on task specific information or noise modeling, we learn representations by predicting in
139   the latent space.

151   given an action, R(s, a) the reward function, and γ ∈ [0, 1] the discount factor that prioritizes
152   immediate rewards over distant ones. The goalP∞of RL is to learn a policy π(a | s) that maximizes

      the expected discounted reward J(π) = Eπ [ t=0 γ t R(st , at )] in order to solve problems.

      4     M ETHODS
163
164
      Endogenous information for an RL agent is often similar to that needed to predict environmental dy-
165   namics. Effective transmission of key information between the dynamics and the feature extractor is
166   crucial, as it enables the representation of relevant endogenous features while filtering out irrelevant
167   exogenous ones. Minimizing prediction loss in a shared latent space between temporally distant
168   observations helps the feature extractor learn more robust representations, promoting information
169   flow between the dynamics and the features.
170
      To enhance this information flow in MBRL, we propose augmenting world model training with
171   joint embedding forward prediction. Specifically, we integrate the Augmented Temporal Contrast
172   (ATC) loss as an auxiliary loss into DreamerV3, improving the representation learned by the world
173   model and leading to a more sample-efficient policy learning. We start with a short description
174   of DreamerV3 and then go on to describe the ATC loss and its integration into the DreamerV3
175   architecture. Finally, we describe the agent policy sampling with our modification. The pipeline is
176   shown in Figure 1.
177
178   4.1   D REAMERV3
179
180   The DreamerV3 architecture uses a Recurrent State Space Model (RSSM) (Hafner et al., 2019b)
181
      to learn a latent dynamics representation from the visual sensory signals. It optimizes its model to
      imagine trajectories conditioned on actions similar to the environment steps. The policy is trained
182
      with the abstract trajectories with the representations learned by the world model. The world model
183
      uses an encoder gϕ to project the sensory observations ot to latent representation xt . The architecture
184   of this encoder is presented in Appendix D. The latent representations are transformed into stochastic
185   representations zt using a representation model. Next, RSSM with hidden ht and past actions at−1
186   predicts the representation zˆt . Together with zt and ht , we predict rewards rˆt , episode continuation
187   flag ĉt ∈ {0, 1}, and the reconstruction of the sensory observation oˆt . In summary, the world model
188   is a combination of the following.

200   4.2   M ODIFYING D REAMERV3 WITH ATC LOSS
201
      The inspiration in our method lies in the findings of Nayebi et al. (2023). This work highlights the
202
      brain’s ability to anticipate future events based on adaptable visual representations, suggesting that
203
      forward prediction mechanisms are crucial to learn dynamic environments. Motivated by this, we
204   integrate the ATC loss directly into the DreamerV3 architecture, enabling the model to learn a better
205   representation of the dynamic settings.
206
207
      The augmented temporal loss is forward prediction loss, where the encoder model gϕ learns to as-
      sociate an observation ot with an observation K-step forward ot+K in the joint embedding space.
208
      The observations in a trajectory τ = (ot , ot+1 , . . . , ot+T , . . . , ot+T +K ) are split into two groups:
209
      the anchor (ot , ot+1 , . . . , ot+T ) and the positive (ot+K , ot+1+K , . . . , ot+T +K ) trajectories, where t
210   and T denotes the start and the end point of the positive trajectory, respectively. Both the positive
211   and anchor are mapped into a shared latent space (xt , xt+1 , . . . , xt+T , . . . , xt+T +K ). In this la-
212   tent space, we use a predictor hϕ to predict the positive embeddings (xt+K , xt+1+K , . . . , xt+T +K )
213   from the anchor embeddings (xt , xt+1 , . . . , xt+T ). Following Stooke et al. (2021) we compute the
214   InfoNCE loss (Gutmann & Hyvärinen, 2010; Oord et al., 2018) on the predicted and positive em-
215   beddings, enabling the model to learn temporal relationships by minimizing the difference between
      the current and future embeddings. We regularize the model by updating a target encoder gϕ using

      6     D ISCUSSIONS AND CONCLUSIONS
520
521
      In this work, we propose an augmentation to the world model representation learner by using forward
522   prediction loss in the joint embedding space. Using our augmentation for world model training,
523   we achieve a more robust world model, which helps downstream MBRL tasks. We show strong
524   empirical results in the Atari100k benchmark, especially when the frames are injected with noise.
525   We also show our method’s robustness to exogenous features with strong empirical results in the
526   Distracting Control Suite benchmark.
527
      While our results are promising, the primary limitation of our study lies in the considered bench-
528   marks. Due to computational limitations, we evaluated our methods for all experiments in three
529   seeds, considered fewer steps, and performed evaluations less frequently. In addition, while our
530   method learns task-relevant features, it is not a task-specific learner. We envision a task-specific fea-
531   ture learner as a two-part problem: a causal classifier of task-specific features and a feature extractor
532   given those features. We leave this task for future work.
533
      Despite these limitations, our method enhances world model sequence modeling capabilities and
534   improves downstream tasks by employing a temporal prediction loss in the joint embedding space
535   as an auxiliary loss. Indeed, as discussed in section 4, having such a loss enforces the feature
536   extractor to learn a temporally relevant representation by sharing information between it and the
537   dynamics. This information passing improves downstream tasks by filtering out exogenous features.
538   For example, in Figure 2, our method can represent the ball because being able to do so is important
539   to predict the state K steps later. Without enforcing this, we see that DreamerV3 fails to represent it
      consistently.
