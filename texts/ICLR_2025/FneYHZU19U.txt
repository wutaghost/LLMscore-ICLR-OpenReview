009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            Given two weighted graphs G = (V, E, wG ) and H = (V, F, wH ) defined on the
012            same vertex set, the constrained clustering problem asks to find a set S ‚äÇ V that
013            minimises the cut ratio between wG (S, V \ S) and wH (S, V \ S). We develop
014            a Cheeger-type inequality that relates the solution of the constrained clustering
015            problem to the spectral properties of G and H. To reduce computational com-
016
               plexity, we use the signed Laplacian on H, simplifying the calculations while
               maintaining accurate results. By solving a generalized eigenvalue problem, our
017
               algorithm provides improvements in performance, particularly in scenarios where
018
               traditional spectral clustering methods face difficulties. We demonstrate its prac-
019            tical effectiveness through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

022   1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024   Clustering is a fundamental technique in machine learning, with extensive applications across com-
025   puter science and various scientific disciplines. The primary goal of clustering is to partition data
026   points into clusters, such that points within each cluster are more densely connected than those in
027
      other clusters. Traditional clustering, such spectral clustering (Von Luxburg, 2007) rely solely in
      the structure of the data. However, in many real-world scenarios, additional domain knowledge is
028
      available, introducing specific constraints that should be incorporated into the clustering process to
029
      achieve more accurate and meaningful results (Basu et al., 2008).
030
031   Constrained clustering focuses on developing algorithms that effectively incorporate this domain
032   knowledge to enhance clustering performance (Wagstaff et al., 2001). The domain knowledge is
033
      represented by two types of pairwise constraints: (1) M UST-L INK constraints, requiring that a pair
      of data points be assigned to the same cluster, and (2) C ANNOT-L INK constraints, requiring that a
034
      pair of data points must be assigned to different clusters. In the context of graph clustering, the goal
035
      is partition the vertices of a graph based on edge connectivity while satisfying the given constrains.
036
037   In this paper, we examine the constrained clustering problem by formalize these constraints using
038   the graphs G = (V, E, w) and H = (V, E ‚Ä≤ , w‚Ä≤ ), in which every data point corresponds to a graph

133   3     A LGORITHM & A NALYSIS
134
135   In this section, we present a constrained graph clustering algorithm called CC++. At a high level,
136   our algorithm consists of the following: in the preprocessing step, we adjust the edge weights of G
137   and add self-loops to the vertices of G, such that both of G and H have the same degree sequence.
138
      Then, we show that a desired cut can be found by a sweep-set algorithm when the eigenvector corre-
      sponding to a generalized eigenvalue problem is given as input. Taking the practical implementation
139
      into account, we introduce a negative self-loop in H for efficient computation, and justify its perfor-
140
      mance both in theory and in practice. Due to page limitations, proofs omitted from this section can
141   be found in the appendix.

387
      We considered a binary Stochastic Block Model (SBM) with n = 1, 000 vertices divided into two
388
      equal-sized communities. Edges between vertices were generated based on intra-cluster probability
389
      p and inter-cluster probability q. Specifically, we fixed p = 0.2 and varied q from 0.12 to 0.2 in 30
390   equidistant steps. For each value of q, we generated two graphs generated from the SBM:
391
392   ‚Ä¢ G: a graph with intra-cluster edge probability p and inter-cluster edge probability q.
393   ‚Ä¢ H: a graph generated with intra-cluster edge probability q and inter-cluster edge probability p,
394     effectively the complement of G in terms of edge probabilities.
395
396   The vertex labels were kept consistent between G and H. This variation allows us to observe how the
397   clustering performance changes as the distinction between communities becomes less pronounced
398   (since higher q implies more inter-cluster edges). The experimental results, visualized in Figure 1a,
399   illustrate the superior performance of CC++ compared to traditional SC, particularly as the inter-
400   cluster edge probability q increases.
401   At low values of q (i.e., when the distinction between communities is clear), both methods perform
402   well, achieving near-perfect ARI values. As seen in Figure 1a, both methods maintain ARI values
403   close to 1.0 when q ‚â§ 0.14. This is expected, as the strong intra-cluster edge probability p = 0.2
404   dominates the inter-cluster connections, making the community structure relatively easy to detect.
405   However, as q increases, the performance of SC deteriorates rapidly. For instance, between q = 0.16
406   and q = 0.18, the ARI for SC drops sharply from approximately 0.7 to near 0.1. This decline
407   occurs because higher values of q increase the number of inter-cluster edges, blurring the distinction
408   between communities. SC relies solely on the structure of G, struggles to correctly partition the
409   vertices under these conditions.
410
      In contrast, both of CC and CC++ are significantly more robust to increasing q. Even as q ap-
411   proaches 0.17, the ARI remains above 0.5, significantly outperforming SC in this regime. This
412   robustness stems from the ability of the Generalized Eigenvalue method to leverage the structural
413   information of both G and H, balancing the cuts between them, the method mitigates the negative
414   impact of increased inter-cluster edges, thus maintaining better clustering accuracy even when the
415   community structure is less pronounced.
416
      This set of experimental results clearly demonstrates that the CC++ algorithm outperforms the tra-
417   ditional SC, particularly in challenging scenarios where the inter-cluster edge probability q is high.
