011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Large language models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable progress in linguistic
014            tasks, necessitating robust evaluation frameworks to understand their capabilities
015
               and limitations. Inspired by Feynman’s principle of understanding through creation,
               we introduce a self-knowledge evaluation framework that is easy to implement,
016
               evaluating models on their ability to comprehend and respond to self-generated
017
               questions. Our findings, based on testing multiple models across diverse tasks,
018            reveal significant gaps in the model’s self-knowledge ability. Further analysis
019            indicates these gaps may be due to misalignment with human attention mechanisms.
020            Additionally, fine-tuning on self-generated math task may enhance the model’s
021            math performance, highlighting the potential of the framework for efficient and
022            insightful model evaluation and may also contribute to the improvement of LLMs.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      In recent years, large language models (LLMs) have reached groundbreaking milestones, significantly
      advancing in areas such as semantic understanding, sentence translation, and more OpenAI (2023);
028
      Touvron et al. (2023); Anil et al. (2023); Team et al. (2023). These models not only facilitate
029
      enhanced interaction between computers and human language but also drive innovation across
030   numerous applications. However, as these models become increasingly central to technological
031   advancements and their applications more widespread, it is crucial to establish robust, systematic
032   evaluation frameworks. Such frameworks are essential not only for understanding the full spectrum
033   of capabilities these models possess but also for identifying their limitations and potential biases.
034
      The evaluation of large language models has made significant strides in recent years, with researchers
035
      developing numerous benchmarks aimed at testing various aspects of model performance (Hendrycks
036   et al., 2020; Li et al., 2023; Zhong et al., 2023). However, the current evaluation methods still have
037   notable shortcomings. Firstly, most benchmarks require substantial human and material resources
038   and often necessitate the involvement of domain experts to accurately assess correctness. Secondly,
039   evaluations that measure a large model’s capability through self-evaluation of its own knowledge is
040   less explored. This gap highlights the need for developing more efficient and insightful evaluation
041   techniques that not only reduce the dependency on extensive resources but also enhance the models’
042   ability to evaluate their own performance and limitations.
043   Motivated by Richard Feynman’s famous quote: “What I cannot create, I do not understand.” We
044   would like to evaluate the large language model’s capability through its “reverse version”, i.e. does
045   the model really understand the questions and solutions created by itself?, which we termed the
046   self-knowledge of the model. This capability is effectively realized by a truthful human, since the
047   originator of a question and its corresponding answer should be able to respond consistently and
048   without difficulty if asked the same question by others if they truly comprehend this knowledge. This
049   ease comes naturally from being the initial creator of the question, so when evaluated on a benchmark
050
      generated in this way, a self-knowledgable model should receive an accuracy of nearly 100% easily.
051   In this paper, we provide a novel framework that can evaluate the model’s self-knowledge ability
052   and is very easy to implement. We conduct an extensive evaluation of 7 popular LLMs across 9
053   tasks, including counting words, math, theorem proving, etc. We also conduct evaluation on large
      multi-modal models (LMMs). We summarize some of our findings as follows:

072   Evaluation of large generative models. Recent years have seen significant advancements in the
073   development of large generative models, including large vision models (LVMs) Radford et al. (2021);
074   Kirillov et al. (2023), large language models (LLMs) OpenAI (2023); Touvron et al. (2023); Bai et al.
075
      (2023); Team et al. (2024); Jiang et al. (2023a), and their evolution into large multi-modal models
      (LMMs) OpenAI (2023); Liu et al. (2023); Zhu et al. (2023); Wei et al. (2023); Koh et al. (2023); Ge
076
      et al. (2023), demonstrating near-human proficiency and even a spark of AGI. Evaluation of these
077
      large generative models is a fast-evolving field across various tasks, datasets, and benchmarks Zhong
078   et al. (2023); Yue et al. (2023); Fu et al. (2023); Wei et al. (2024); Sun et al. (2024). It encompasses a
079   wide range of domains, including the generation of language, images, videos, and audio. However,
080   there is a lack of evaluations that measure a large generative model’s self-knowledge of its own
081   capabilities. Specifically, we focus on the self-knowledge evaluation of LLMs that can understand
082   instruction and output responses, as well as LMMs that can both understand images and generate
083   images.
084   Evaluation of LLM’s instruction-following ability. Several studies have established benchmarks
085   for evaluating LLMs’ instruction-following abilities. Jiang et al. (2023b) proposed FollowBench that
086   sequentially add fine-grained constraints to construct multi-level instructions. Zhou et al. (2023) em-
087   phasized objective evaluations with verifiable instructions. Meanwhile, Qin et al. (2024) constructed
088   a benchmark composed of several distinct instructions and decomposed questions for the assessment
089   of the instruction following. These benchmarks require manually constructing a large number of
090   instructions and answers. Differently, our work mainly focuses on the large model’s self-knowledge
091   of its own capabilities, which is also independent of collecting additional annotated answers.

231   4.2.2     G ENERATE PARAGRAPH THAT CONTAINS A SPECIFIC NUMBER OF DESIGNATED WORDS
232
      Theoretically, the task of generating a paragraph that contains a specific number of designated words
233
      should be well within the capabilities of autoregressive large language models (LLMs). Given that
234
      these models generate text sequentially, they inherently have the ability to review their own history,
235   including tracking the frequency of specified terms as they generate new content. This capability
236   should enable them to adjust their output to meet predefined criteria, such as incorporating a certain
237   number of specific words. We ask the model to generate a designated “keyword” a predefined number
238   of times and get 100 samples. Then in a separate run, we ask the model the appearance time of this
239   specific keyword and check whether it is the same with our predefined frequency. We only consider
240   the simplest case of only one keyword and leave the combination of multiple keywords as future work.
241   The selection of keywords is flexible, one may randomly pick it from a dictionary or ask an LLM to
242   pick from a summarization of new web content. However, despite these theoretical capabilities, our
243
      empirical tests reveal that the performance of these models remains unsatisfactory in executing this
      seemingly straightforward task. This underperformance suggests potential limitations in their current
244
      training or architectural design, which may not fully support dynamic adjustments based on historical
245
      data analysis during text generation.

      4.2.3     FACTS
249
      Testing models on their ability to accurately recall important dates related to historical figures is
250   crucial because it assesses their precision in handling factual information. Remembering key dates,
251   such as births, deaths, and significant events linked to these individuals, is essential for a reliable
252   understanding of history. This precision is not just about storing data but also about the ability to
253   retrieve it accurately when needed. Such tests are particularly important in educational contexts,
254   where precise historical facts are fundamental for teaching and learning. They help ensure that AI
255   models can serve as dependable resources for students and researchers who rely on accurate historical
256   data. We ask the model to name a celebrity that was born on specific dates. Then in a separate run, we
257   ask the model if the celebrity was born on this day. We generate 100 different days and the evaluation
258
      result shows that models usually show good consistency under this test.

260   4.2.4     A R X IV

      technical knowledge in many scientific areas. Testing large models on their ability to accurately

      4.2.5    M ATH
271
272   Testing large models on their ability to solve math problems is crucial for evaluating their performance
273   because these tasks require a combination of several complex cognitive skills Azerbayev et al. (2023b);
274   Yu et al. (2023). First, the model must accurately understand the natural language and symbolic
275   notations used in the problem, recognizing key information and its context. Then, it needs to translate
276
      all linguistic descriptions into a mathematical format, applying the correct operations and formulas.
      Finally, the model must manage and manipulate numerical data to reach a solution. This process tests
277
      not only the model’s linguistic comprehension but also its logical reasoning and numerical accuracy,
278
      providing a comprehensive assessment of its capabilities across different domains of intelligence. We
279   ask the model to generate a math question with typical encountered math question answers like 10cm
280   or π etc. and we generate 100 different samples. Then in a separate run, we ask the model whether
281   the predefined answer is consistent with the previously generated question.
282
283   4.2.6    T HEOREM PROVING
284
285   Evaluating large models on their ability to solve mathematical proofs is essential because it assesses
286   more than just their mathematical knowledge—it evaluates their logical thinking and problem-solving
287
      skills Azerbayev et al. (2023a); Yang et al. (2024). Mathematical proofs require understanding
      complex concepts and linking them together through a series of logical steps. This type of testing
288
      checks if the model can not only follow these steps but also organize and articulate them clearly and
289
      effectively. By doing so, we can determine how well the model can handle complex, abstract ideas
290   and if it can apply its knowledge to develop coherent, logical solutions. This insight is crucial for
291   understanding the depth and breadth of the model’s cognitive abilities, making it a comprehensive
292   test of its overall intellectual performance. However, verifying the correctness of a proof may be too
293   challenging. We find that inequalities are a good testbed for this task as many of them can be verified
294   by computers automatically. We also consider the simplest case of single variables inequalities as
295   inequalities involving multiple variables are hard to verify their correctness even by humans and we
296   also generate 100 different samples. Then in a separate run, we ask the model whether the previously
297   generated inequality is correct or not.

      4.2.7    C ODE
300   Testing large models on their ability to write code is crucial for understanding how well they can
301   apply computer science concepts in real situations Roziere et al. (2023); Guo et al. (2024). This type
302   of testing goes beyond just knowing programming language rules. It looks at whether the model can
303   effectively break down problems, think through solutions logically, and turn those ideas into working
304   code. This helps us evaluate how well the model can handle practical tasks in computing, showing its
305   potential to work as an effective tool in technology and software development. Such tests are key

307   we ask the model to generate a program that has its execution result given, for eg. 10 and we also

      generated program and check whether it is consistent.

      4.4.1    G RAMMAR
343
344   Testing models on their understanding of word parts of speech within sentences is crucial because
345   it reflects their grasp of grammar. Part of speech (POS) tagging (Gimpel et al., 2011) involves
346   identifying whether a word functions as a noun, verb, preposition, etc., based on its usage in context.
347   This understanding is fundamental to processing and generating coherent language, as it affects how
348   words are combined to form meaningful sentences. A model’s ability to accurately perform POS
349   tagging indicates its proficiency in syntactic analysis, which is essential for any language-related
350   task. The model is first asked about the number of prepositions in its previously generated content.
351
      Then we cut the first sentence in the previous paragraph and paste it at the last and generate a new
      paragraph, this operation preserves the number of prepositions. Then we ask the model in a separate
352
      run about the number of prepositions in the newly generated paragraph. We test on 100 samples and
353
      the initial paragraph is taken from the total word counting task in section 4.2.1. A schematic view

      4.4.2    BASIC SQL TYPE OPERATIONS
357
358   Testing a model’s ability to perform basic SQL operations based on input sentences not only evaluates
359   its capacity to understand and manipulate data but also sheds light on its grasp of the finer structural
360   details of sentences. This type of assessment requires the model to parse complex sentence structures
361   and understand their relational dynamics to accurately convert natural language instructions into SQL
362   commands. Successfully managing this translation indicates a deep understanding of syntax and
363   semantics, reflecting the model’s sophistication in language processing. Thus, proficiency in this area
364   demonstrates more than just technical capability; it highlights the model’s comprehensive linguistic
365   competence, essential for any application involving natural language understanding and interaction.
366
      We use the generated texts in section 4.2.2. For each paragraph, we first ask the model to answer what
      is its i-th word, where i is a randomly selected small integer. We then design the following tasks:
367
368
              • Add first word: Add a random word to the beginning of the paragraph and ask the model
369             what its i + 1-th word is. Then check whether the word is consistent with the previously
370             answered one.
371
372           • Delete first word: Delete the first word of the paragraph and ask the model what its i − 1-th
373
                word is. Then check whether the word is consistent with the previously answered one.
374           • Change: Change the i-th word of the paragraph to x and ask the model what its i-th word is.
375             Then check whether the answer is x.

      6.1      A NALYZE THE BEHAVIOR OF SELF - KNOWLEDGE DESIGNATED WORD COUNTING TASK
422
423   Analyzing the underlying reason why LLM performs poorly on self-knowledge tasks is difficult. We
424   make an attempt to analyze the case of the “designated word counting task”, which has some special
425   structure. Recall that this task requires the model to generate a keyword x exactly s times. When a
426   human is asked to perform this task, whenever they generate a new word they will may some of their
427   focus on whether this word is x and how many times x has appeared. In the context of LLM, we will
428   use attention score to measure the extent of “focus”. For each token in the generated paragraph, we
429
      extract the attention score of token x. We then sort these scores and only keep the top 15% tokens as a
      set τx . Then denote the number of times x appears in the generated paragraph as k = |x ∈ τx |. Then

      6.2    D IFFERENT EVALUATION PROTOCOLS
461
462   The evaluation in the previous sections will make the generation process and evaluation process in
463   separate runs. This evaluation process may become much easier when the generation process is
464   given in the context, and we will call this evaluation protocol the in-context eval. As the in-context
465   memory may make the evaluation too simple, recall that humans may starts to forget things when
466   they are exposed to many irrelevant information. We consider the simplest setting where a short
467   noise paragraph about 7000 tokens long is inserted between the generation process and the evaluation
468   process. We call this in-context eval with noise. We summarize the result in Table 6. To our surprise,
469
      only GPT-4 and Gemma achieve 100% accuracy in the in-context eval and their performances are
      reduced when exposed to noise, similar to humans. Note some models like GPT-3.5 and Qwen may
470
      even have increased performance when exposed to noise. We conjecture that this weird phenomenon
471
      may be attributed to that some weak association is amplified due to stochastic resonance (Moss et al.,
472   2004). But the main point here is that adding noise can reduce the performance of a perfect in-context
473   evaluator, similar to the behavior of humans.

526   7      C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this paper, we introduce a comprehensive self-knowledge evaluation framework specifically
529   designed for large language models (LLMs) and large multimodal models (LMMs). This framework
530   focuses on assessing their capacity to comprehend and effectively respond to questions they generate
531   themselves. Our extensive findings across a variety of tasks demonstrate that these models still
532   exhibit significant deficiencies when engaged in self-knowledge tasks. Further analysis suggests that
533
      these shortcomings may be partially attributed to a misalignment with human attention mechanisms
      and thus highlight a potential area for improvement. Additionally, we explore the impact of fine-
534
      tuning models on self-generated data, which shows promise in enhancing their overall performance.
535
      Our framework provides a valuable and efficient approach to advancing both the evaluation and
536   development of LLMs and LMMs. While we primarily consider cases that are straightforward and
537   easily verified by humans, future work could involve increasing the complexity and automation of the
538   evaluation challenges.

783   As our self-knowledge evaluations in previous sections are mostly based on our manually created
784   template problems, one may wonder if we can leverage the existing human-crafted benchmarks to
785
      perform self-knowledge evaluations. Of course, one may also use the dual-generating framework
      in section 4.3. In this section, we introduce another way which may be more efficient. Wang et al.
786
      (2022) introduce the philosophy of augmenting the instruction tuning data using LLMs. Motivated by
787
      this philosophy, we consider showing the LLM the test data from a benchmark and letting it generate
788   new testing problems with answers and we then let the LLM do these self-generated problems. We
789   consider the widely adopted benchmark MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020) and as it consists of too
790   many topics, we choose the college cs task for simplicity. We summarize the results in Table 11.
791   We find that the difference between the initial accuracy and self-knowledge score is small. More
792   interestingly, it seems that when a model has its initial accuracy greater than 50% it will have its
793   initial accuracy greater than the self-knowledge accuracy and vice versa. This is similar to Figure 3
794   where models with higher accuracy may favor the correct answered data.

      To evaluate the influence of role-modeling prompts on the experiments, we conduct similar exper-
833
      iments to those in section 4.2.1 by changing the question-generating prompt to “Assume you are
834   an expert in counting numbers. Generate a paragraph with exactly [num] words in total.” and the
835   question-verifying prompt to “Assume you are an expert in counting numbers. How many words
836   are there in the following paragraph?”. In Figure 4, we can see adding the expert prompt indeed
837   improves the self-knowledge score showing that expert role modeling has some positive influence.
838   Note the drastic improvement of Mistral is due to similar reasons of in-context eval in Table 6. The
839   model encodes a “cheat sheet” like “This paragraph, my friends, consists of precisely 58 words.” to
840   help the self-verifying process.
841   To investigate further the impact of the prompt, we will the true answer into account. Specifically, we
842   calculate the ground-truth number of words in the generated paragraph and calculate the following
843   three accuracies: Gen: The accuracy that the generated content has the required number of words.
844   Ver: The accuracy that the verify answer from the model on the generated content is equal to the real
845   number of words in the generated content. True: The accuracy that the verify answer from the model
846   on the generated content is equal to the real number of words in the generated content and also equal
847   to the required number of words when generating it. We summarize the results in Table 13. We found
848
      that none of the real generative accuracy or verifying accuracy is improved when using the expert
      prompt, showing a deep underlying reason behind the improvements in self-knowledge score, we
849
      leave the investigation of the underlying reasons as future work.
