009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011                The fine-tuning of pre-trained language models (PLMs) has been shown to be ef-
012                fective across various domains. By using domain-specific supervised data, the
013                general-purpose representation derived from PLMs can be transformed into a
014                domain-specific representation. However, these methods often fail to generalize
015                to out-of-domain (OOD) data due to their reliance on non-causal representations,
016                often described as spurious features. Existing methods either make use of adjust-
017
                   ments with strong assumptions about lack of hidden common causes, or mitigate
                   the effect of spurious features using multi-domain data. In this work, we investi-
018
                   gate how fine-tuned pre-trained language models aid generalizability from single-
019
                   domain scenarios under mild assumptions, targeting more general and practical
020                real-world scenarios. We show that a robust representation can be derived through
021                a so-called causal front-door adjustment, based on a decomposition assumption,
022                using fine-tuned representations as a source of data augmentation. Comprehensive
023                experiments in both synthetic and real-world settings demonstrate the superior
024                generalizability of the proposed method compared to existing approaches. Our
025                work thus sheds light on the domain generalization problem by introducing links
026                between fine-tuning and causal mechanisms into representation learning1 .

      1       I NTRODUCTION
030
031
      Pre-trained language models (PLMs) like BERT (Kenton & Toutanova, 2019; Liu, 2019) are trained
      on large corpora to generate contextualized representations, performing well in various natural lan-
032
      guage understanding (NLU) tasks such as text classification (Minaee et al., 2021). Fine-tuning these
033
      models, i.e., training them with supervised data to adapt to specific tasks, can lead to improved per-
034   formance. However, models trained on specific domains tend to rely on non-causal representations
035   that exploit spurious correlations present only in the training data, leading to poor generalization
036   when tested on data with distribution shifts (Arjovsky et al., 2019; Ahuja et al., 2020; Heinze-Deml
037   & Meinshausen, 2021). The issue arises from the assumption that training and test data are ex-
038   changeable samples (Lv et al., 2022; Qiao & Low, 2024). In practice, however, test data often come
039   from out-of-domain (OOD) distributions that diverge from the training set. For example, “positive”
040   sentiment might be strongly correlated with Amazon reviews due to bias in crowdsourced training
041   data (Gururangan et al., 2018; Sagawa et al., 2019), but this correlation might not hold during testing.
042
      Ensuring the robustness of supervised fine-tuned models is crucial, especially in critical applications
      such as medical diagnosis and autonomous driving.
043
044   Numerous studies have attempted to improve the robustness of PLMs in OOD scenarios from vari-
045   ous perspectives (Hendrycks et al., 2020; Du et al., 2021; Yuan et al., 2023). One common approach
046   is feature augmentation, which aims to enhance model generalizability by diversifying learned rep-
047   resentations using models trained on data from multiple domains (Xie et al., 2020; Hendrycks et al.,
048
      2019; Zhang et al., 2020; Tu et al., 2020). These feature augmentation approaches could be inter-
      preted as using expert knowledge to synthetically construct multi-domain data from a causal per-
049
      spective (Ilse et al., 2021; Von Kügelgen et al., 2021). The availability of multi-domain data has
050
      spurred the rapid development of learning invariant predictors (Arjovsky et al., 2019; Ahuja et al.,
051   2020; Heinze-Deml & Meinshausen, 2021). The goal of these approaches is to learn representa-
052   tions that minimize loss functions across all domains, conditionally or not on class labels, thereby

055   often not readily available, and it is not straightforward to apply data augmentation, as in the case
056   of image processing, due to the complexities inherent in language (Yuan et al., 2023). In this paper,
057   we investigate how PLMs can be exploited as a natural additional source of domain data, to improve

059
      Thus, we propose the following research question:
060
061   How can we leverage PLMs to learn robust causal representations that enhance OOD generaliza-
062   tion?
063   In what follows, we first present an analysis through a causal perspective on why the standard su-
064   pervised fine-tuning estimator p(y | x) fails in OOD scenarios, and how this could be addressed by
065   a causal estimator p(y | do(x)) instead (Section 3), where do(x) denotes an intervention that fixes
066   the value X = x (Pearl, 2009). Next, we elaborate on the key assumptions that guarantee the iden-
067   tificability of the causal estimator in Section 4. In light of these assumptions, we propose a novel
068   method to construct the causal estimator p(y | do(x)) (Theorem 2) using single-domain data. This
069   method is based on two key constructions: (1) leveraging PLMs for data augmentation to identify
070
      causal features (Theorem 1), and (2) learning a representation of spurious local features to enable
      what is known as causal front-door adjustments (Pearl (2009), Section 5.3).
071
072   Our key contribution is a principled strategy to construct robust causal representation using PLMs
073   during fine-tuning, with single-domain observational data. We validate this approach on two semi-
074   synthetic datasets and one real-world benchmark datasets, comparing against strong baselines to
075   evaluate generalizability, with a focus on text classification2 . We find that our method provides
076
      significant resilience to changes in the distribution of spurious features and have a substantial impact
      on the deployment of text classifiers in real-world scenarios. This contribution advances the field
077
      of robust representation learning and demonstrates how understanding causal mechanisms could
078
      enhance model robustness.

083   Causality and Domain Generalization Causal mechanisms have been shown to be a reliable
084   operational concept in Domain Generalization (DG). The goal is to address spurious correlations
085   through causal inference, incorporating confounding adjustments under empirical observations. A
086
      common approach involves learning invariant predictors to minimize the impact of spurious features
      by training on supervised data from multiple domains (Arjovsky et al., 2019; Ahuja et al., 2020;
087
      Heinze-Deml & Meinshausen, 2021), and self-supervised learning (Von Kügelgen et al., 2021; Yue
088
      et al., 2021; Mitrovic et al., 2021; Kong et al., 2023). However, training data from multiple domains
089   is often not readily available or easy to augment (Yuan et al., 2023). In order to perform causal
090   inference with single domain data, other models exploit causal dependencies to eliminate spurious
091   features through a so-called back-door adjustment, based on the assumption that no unobserved
092   domain-specific confounders exist (Lu et al., 2022; Lv et al., 2022). However, confounders may
093   not always be observed and can be difficult to model explicitly. Recent work seeks to address DG
094   in a more practical setting by considering unobserved confounders and employing the front-door
095   adjustment (Li et al., 2021; Mao et al., 2022; Nguyen et al., 2023). In this paper, we present a novel
096   front-door adjustment construction for leveraging PLMs in a practical setting of NLU tasks.
097
098   Domain Generalization for Pre-trained Models With pre-trained models achieving remarkable
099   performance in both the computer vision (CV) (Chen et al., 2020; Bao et al., 2021; He et al., 2022)
100
      and natural language processing (NLP) (Devlin, 2018; Lan, 2019; Liu, 2019) communities, do-
      main generalization on downstream tasks for these models has attracted increasing attention. Some
101
      studies aim to enhance generalizability by increasing the diversity of learned features with models
102
      trained on data from various domains (Hendrycks et al., 2019; Xie et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2020;
103   Tu et al., 2020). Other line of work suggests that conducting adversarial training (Salman et al.,
104   2020; Hendrycks et al., 2020; Utrera et al., 2020; Yi et al., 2021) and developing advanced attention
105   mechanisms (Dosovitskiy, 2020; Mao et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2021) can lead to more robust mod-
106   els. Motivated by recent work that utilize PLMs parameters as a way of performing regularization

      Motivation and Intuition Consider a common NLP application in a practical scenario: given in-
152
      put features x ∈ X, the task is to predict labels y ∈ Y , with potentially some unobserved common
153   confounder U between X and Y . This could typically be solved by learning a classifier p(y | x) di-
154   rectly using the empirical risk minimization (ERM) objective (VAPNIK, 1998) and choosing a class
155   via arg maxy p(y | x) during inference. The typical underlying assumption is that both the training
156   and test environments3 contain data that are exchangeable. However, the ERM estimator will not
157   work if the test environment does not follow the same distribution as the training data. We discuss
158   how this can be resolved under invariance assumptions described by using the framework of struc-
159   tural causal models (SCMs) (Pearl, 2009) in a subsequent section. Empirically, our findings illustrate
160   a case where the performance of the fine-tuning estimator drops from 93% in the in-distribution (ID)

224   4.1   A SSUMPTIONS
225
      The standard black-box model makes no distinction between causal features and non-causal features.
226
      We make the following structural assumptions, so that we can distinguish these two set of features
227   as latent variables.
228
229   Assumption 1 (Decomposition) Each input text X can be decomposed into a causal latent variable
230   C and a spurious latent variable S (i.e. X = f (S, C)). Latent variable C is the only causal parent
231   for label Y , and the generative process follows the causal graph in Fig. 1 (c):
232
233                                       X ∼ p(x | s, c), Y ∼ p(y | c).
234
235   This is a common assumption in causal machine learning literature, such as in (Tenenbaum & Free-
236   man, 1996; Gong et al., 2016; Heinze-Deml & Meinshausen, 2021; Mao et al., 2022). The intuition
237
      is that we can abstract away the true complex causal graph into a coarser granularity, such that we
      encapsulate stable hidden confounders into C and any other (unstable) non-confonding variables
238
      into S. However, this assumption alone still does not provide sufficient information to identify la-
239
      tent variables C and S. We can make the following further assumption to allow for the identifiability
240   of the causal latent variable C.
241
242   Assumption 2 (Paired Representations) For each input text X, we can obtain a pair of variations
243   of its representations, R0 and R1 , where their causal factors C remain the same but spurious factors
244   S varies as a result of some unknown interventions (i.e. we have S0 and S1 ), and the generative
245   process follows the causal graph in Fig. 1 (c). That is,
246
247
                                  R0 ∼ p(r0 (x) | s0 , c), R1 ∼ p(r1 (x) | s1 , c).
248
249   This is a critical assumption for identifying causal variables C, motivated by the Theorem 4.4
250
      in (Von Kügelgen et al., 2021). Intuitively, R0 and R1 can be considered as two different represen-
      tations of the same data point, retrieved from two distinct environments. In our context, the R0 can
251
      be retrieved from the pre-trained language model (i.e. the pre-training environments) and R1 can
252
      be retrieved after supervised fine-tuning of the pre-trained language model on given datasets (i.e.
253   the training environment). This assumption establishes a way for identifying the causal variables
254   C from the observational distribution P (R0 , R1 , X, Y ), which would otherwise remain unidentifi-
255   able (Von Kügelgen et al., 2021).
256
      A typical causal estimator require controlling for unobserved confounders. However, this is often
257
      not feasible without relying on strong assumptions. One approach is to construct a front-door ad-
258
      justment (Pearl, 2009) by introducing an additional feature which mediates the effect of the features
259   on the label. Based on this, we make the following assumptions:
260
261   Assumption 3 (Local Features) For each input text X, after getting its sentence summary R1 , we
262   can also obtain its token-level features Φ from the fine-tuned model for free. This token-level features
263   could be used to predict the label Y , and the generative process, conditioned on R1 only, follows
264   the causal graph in Fig. 1 (c):
265                                                Φ ∼ p(ϕ | r1 ).
266
267   Assumption 4 (Sufficient Mediator) The causal effect from local features Φ only impact Y
268   through a subset of variables in C, in other words, the causal factors C fully mediate the causal
269   effect between Φ and label Y . This means fixing Φ does not give us more information about Y once
      we fix C already, such that P (Y | do(Φ), do(c)) = P (Y | do(c)).

      Real-world Case-Study. In the domain of text classification, a practical example can be drawn
452
      from sentiment analysis tasks, where data is collected from two distinct platforms, such as Amazon
453   and Yelp. Hypothetically, the sentiment distribution across these platforms could differ significantly.
454   For instance, if we randomly sample product reviews from Amazon, we may find that 80% are
455   positive and 20% are negative. This imbalance could be influenced by specific product categories
456   or certain demographic groups of users. In contrast, Yelp reviews may exhibit the opposite trend,
457   with 80% of the reviews being negative and only 20% positive, due to the nature of service-related
458   reviews on that platform.
459   If we combine data from both platforms into a training set, we might obtain a seemingly balanced
460   dataset—50% positive and 50% negative reviews. However, the real-world distribution of sentiment
461   in the test data may deviate significantly from this. For example, the test set could contain 40%
462   positive and 60% negative reviews for Amazon, and 60% positive and 40% negative reviews for
463   Yelp. This discrepancy between the training and test distributions poses a challenge for building a
464   robust machine learning model.
465   Such scenarios are particularly relevant when deploying models across different regions or environ-
466   ments. For instance, a model trained on reviews from users in Asia may be expected to perform
467   equally well when deployed in Europe, despite potential differences in user behavior, cultural con-
468   text, or product preferences that alter the distribution of sentiments. Adapting to these environmental
469   shifts is critical for ensuring model generalizability and effectiveness in real-world applications.

521   7     C ONCLUSION
522
523   In this paper, we introduced a method for constructing robust causal representations leveraging
524   PLMs. Through a series of semi-synthetic and real-world experiments, we demonstrated the promis-
525   ing performance of our approach in OOD scenarios compared to standard fine-tuning. Lessons. We
526   recognize that PLMs are already highly resilient to perturbations in text inputs, and introducing
527   spurious information at the input level requires significant effort. This highlights the strength of
528   PLMs in managing text input variations, but also the challenge in simulating spurious correlations
529
      for testing purposes. Limitations. While we made extensive efforts to control and simulate spurious
      relationships that resemble real-world deployment scenarios, the mechanisms through which spu-
530
      rious correlations emerge in complex, real-world environments remain unclear. Furthermore, it is
531
      not immediately evident how such shifts can be systematically managed in these settings. We hope
532   our method provides a valuable baseline for both academic and industry researchers facing these
533   challenges. Future Work. While PLMs have been increasingly used to construct robust classifiers,
534   as seen in recent work such as (Wortsman et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024), the pre-
535   cise nature of the knowledge encapsulated within these models remains an open question. Although
536   efforts such as (Park et al., 2023) have begun addressing this issue, further investigation is required
537   to fully understand and harness this knowledge effectively. Additionally, extending our approach
538   to tasks involving language generation within the framework of large language models (LLMs) is
539   another compelling direction for future research.

733   We designed two types of simulators: (1) a semi-synthetic simulator; and (2) a real-world simulator.

735   A.1    G ENERAL S ETTING
736
737   The simulators serve as fully (or partially) controllable oracles to allow us to test the performance
738   of our proposed method. In particular, we have the following parameters:
739
740          • Ntrain : the total number of training data points.

747   A.2    S EMI -S YNTHETIC S IMULATOR
748
749   The first simulator is semi-synthetic and primary motivated by the experiments in Veitch et al.
750   (2021), which inject an artificial spurious relationship between words “the” and “and” in a given
751   sentence, with respect to its actual label. These words are chosen because they are stop words in
752
      linguistic theory, generally believed to carry minimal semantic information in a sentence (Jurafsky,
      2000).
753
754   To illustrate this, consider the following text (taken from real data): “It is so annoying and frustrating
755   to see that the errors from the CS1 edition have been brought forward to this edition.” We append
      a special suffix to the words “the” and “and.” For binary classification, the suffixes could be either

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

      “xxxx” or “yyyy”. If the “xxxx” suffix is applied, the sentence becomes “It is so annoying andxxxxx
757   frustrating to see that thexxxxx errors from thexxxxx CS1 edition have been brought forward to this
758   edition.”
759
760
      To inject spurious information, we first sample sentences that contains these two words with a pre-
      defined minimum frequency in the first 30 words. We use a minimum frequency of 2 for the Amazon
761
      review dataset, and 1 for the Yelp review dataset (since “the” and “and” are less common in the Yelp
762
      dataset). We then assign the spurious relationship between the suffix and class label, using the
763   following rules for our experiments: during training, if the actual label is negative (label 0), we add
764   suffix of “xxxx” 90% of the time and “yyyy” 10% of the time; and if the actual label is positive (label
765   1), we add suffix of “yyyy” 90% of the time and “xxxx” 10% of the time.
766
      This setup is replicated in the in-distribution (ID) test set. For the out-of-distribution (OOD) test set,
767
      we apply 90% to 70%, 50%, 30%, and 10% proportions to simulate different OOD scenarios.
768
769   Specifically, we use the binary sentiment analysis examples and sample 5000 sentences each class
770   to construct the training set, and another 2000 sentences each class to construct the test set. When
771   constructing the training set, we use different random seeds to create different data distributions, and
772
      for the test set, we use the same seed so that the test is consistent across our experiments.
