011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Detecting underlying faults is crucial in the development of mission-critical plan-
014            ning systems, such as UAV trajectory planning in Unmanned aircraft Traffic Man-
015            agement (UTM), which is vital to airspace safety. Inevitably, there exists a small
016
               set of rare, unpredictable conditions where the UTM could suffer from catas-
               trophic failures. Most traditional fault detection approaches focus on achieving
017
               high coverage by random input exploitation. However, random methods are strug-
018
               gling to detect long-tail vulnerabilities with unacceptable time consumption. To
019            tackle this challenge, we propose a scenario-oriented framework to search the
020            long-tail conditions, accelerating the fault detection process. Inspired by in-
021            context learning approaches, we leverage a Transformer-based policy model to
022            capture the dynamics of the subject UTM system from the offline dataset for ex-
023            ploitation acceleration. We evaluate our approach over 700 hours in a massive-
024            scale, industry-level simulation environment. Empirical results demonstrate that
025            our approach achieves over 8 times more vulnerability discovery efficiency com-
026            pared with traditional expert-guided random-walk exploitation, which showcases
027
               the potential of machine learning for fortifying mission-critical systems. Further-
               more, we scale the model size to 2 billion parameters, achieving substantial per-
028
               formance gains over smaller models in offline and online evaluations, highlighting
029
               the scalability of our approach.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Unmanned aircraft system Traffic Management (UTM) (Kopardekar, 2014; Kopardekar et al., 2016)
035
      is a mission-critical system to ensure safety and coordination in low-altitude aircraft operations. As
036   Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) are increasingly applied in civilian and commercial tasks, such
037   as logistics (Yang Su et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024), disaster relief (Kshitij Aggarwal & Aayush
038   Goyal, 2021; Murat Bakirci & Muhammed Mirac Ozer, 2023), and environmental monitoring (Biruk
039   E. Tegicho et al., 2023; Manilo Monaco et al., 2022), the vulnerability discovery of UTM systems is
040   crucial to prevent accidents in complex, real-world scenarios (G. Raja et al., 2021; Wedad Alawad
041   et al., 2023), which brings about the need for rigorous testing in the verification phase.
042   Testing in UTM is particularly challenging due to the long-tail effect of potential failure scenarios
043   (Wang et al., 2022; Feng et al., 2023). In UTM, the majority of operational scenarios are safely
044   managed by the system’s self-healing design, while a small subset of rare and unpredictable sit-
045   uations can lead to severe safety risks and system-wide failures. These low-probability, high-risk
046   scenarios are difficult to identify due to infrequent occurrence and obscurity within a vast space of
047   relatively safe scenarios. However, traditional testing methods, such as random scenario injections
048   (Zhong et al., 2021; Nicholas B. N. Nyakundi et al., 2023) and coverage-based techniques (Flood &
049   Korenko, 2013; Nalic et al., 2020), struggle to detect rare critical edge cases efficiently. The random
050
      nature of these approaches leads to excessive exploration of low-risk situations, resulting in wasted
      resources and a low likelihood of exposing the most dangerous vulnerabilities in a timely manner.
051
052   To address this issue, we propose a novel search-oriented testing framework that treats fault detec-
053   tion as a search problem across the long-tail of operational scenarios. The framework consists of two
      main components: a Policy Model (PM) and an Action Sampler (AS). Drawing inspiration from in-

103   Multi-Agent System Testing Multi-Agent Systems (MAS), such as autonomous vehicles (Li
104   et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2023; Daniele et al., 2024; Ashwin & Naveen Raj, 2023) and robotic
105   swarms (Xia et al., 2022; Mai et al., 2022; Lv et al., 2023), process continuous temporal information
106   from both inter-agent interactions and agent-environment dynamics. As MAS complexity and im-
107   portance grow, testing to identify potential vulnerabilities has gained increasing attention (Daniele
      et al., 2024). For instance, Kim et al. (2022) introduced an end-to-end mutational fuzzing method for


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Autonomous Driving Systems (ADS), employing four parameter mutation strategies to explore the
109   problem space from initial scenarios. Additionally, Yao et al. (2023) used graph centrality analysis
110   to detect GPS spoofing vulnerabilities, while Li et al. (2023) applied the TD3 reinforcement learn-
111   ing algorithm to reduce randomness and inefficiency in fuzzing methods. Despite these advances,
112   fuzzing methods often fail to balance coverage and efficiency, as much of the explored problem
113   space offers limited value (Daniele et al., 2024). Traditional testing techniques, such as fuzzing (in-
114   jecting random or semi-random inputs) (Miller et al., 1990; Zhang et al., 2021) and mutation testing
115   (introducing controlled changes to the system) (Jia & Harman, 2011), have been adapted for MAS.
116   Mutation testing may fail to capture the emergent behaviors arising from complex agent interactions,
117
      limiting quality and diversity (Daniele et al., 2024).
118
119
      Sequential Trajectory Modeling Early efforts primarily utilized transformers with self-attention
      mechanisms (Vaswani et al., 2017) as encoders to process complex information embedded within
120
      agent trajectories. These trajectories, modeled as sequences of tokens (Chen et al., 2021; Chebotar
121
      et al., 2023b; Wu et al., 2023), contain observations, interactions between agents, and feedback from
122   the environment at different time steps. The pioneering work by Zambaldi et al. (2018) introduced
123   the concept of using multi-head dot-product attention to capture relational reasoning over structured
124   observations. This approach was later successfully implemented in AlphaStar (Vinyals et al., 2019)
125   to manage multi-entity observations in the complex multi-agent game StarCraft II (Samvelyan et al.,
126   2019). Trajectory Transformer (Janner et al., 2021) adopted sequence modeling techniques, such as
127   beam search, for reinforcement learning tasks, aiming to mitigate the correlation and bias involved
128   in jointly modeling states and actions. In complex and dynamic environments, agents’ behaviors are
129   heavily influenced by each other and their surroundings. AgentFormer (Yuan et al., 2021) addressed
130
      this by incorporating agent identifiers into the attention mechanism to model the influence of each
      agent’s trajectory on others. Scene-LSTM (Manh & Alaghband, 2018) divided environments into
131
      grid cells, while Scene Transformer (Ngiam et al., 2022) aggregated environmental object context
132
      and agent interactions through attention layers to produce unified future state predictions.
133
134
      Scenario-Based Testing Scenario-based testing offers a more structured approach to evaluating
135
      MAS by analyzing the system’s behavior under specific conditions. Tian et al. (2022) demonstrated
136   the effectiveness of motif-driven paths in identifying distinct safety violations. However, manually
137   crafting realistic and comprehensive scenarios is both time-consuming and challenging. To address
138   this, learning-based methods have gained traction. Feng et al. (2023) formulated accidents in au-
139   tonomous vehicles as a sequential Markov Decision Process (MDP), using a reinforcement learning
140   framework, D2RL, to manipulate trajectories and simulate accidents by controlling nearby vehicles.
141   Similarly, Tian et al. (2024) explored the use of Large Language Models (LLMs) in the LEADE
142   technique to automate scenario generation. Additionally, Fu et al. (2024) introduced models that
143   adapt to new tasks by leveraging current contextual information. Through in-context learning, mod-
144
      els can use relevant examples or instructions to guide the generation of test scenarios or identify
      potential vulnerabilities in more targeted and adaptive ways (Wei et al., 2022).

147   3     FAULT D ETECTION P ROBLEM IN T ESTING P HASE
148
149   In this section, we provide an overview of fault detection problem in testing phase of the UTM
150   system. We first introduce the UTM system, specifying the importance of testing phase in the devel-
151   opment of UTM system. After that, we analyze the testing framework for UTM system, including its
152   role within development of UTM system, interactions with SUT, and targets of testing framework.
153
154   3.1   UTM S YSTEMS
155
      Targets of UTM Systems The UTM systems are designed for real-time or near-real-time organi-
156
      zation, coordination, and management upon UAV swarms. UTMs generate control and command
157
      signals to UAV swarms for geo-fencing, route optimization, de-confliction, etc. (ICAO, 2023). The
158   UTM system is usually developed as a complex system, integrating several modules. Since failures
159   in UTM usually result in substantial economic losses or even human injury and death, UTM systems
160   are considered as mission critical systems (Kopardekar, 2014) Therefore, UTM systems are required
161   be of high fault-tolerance. This raises demands on rigorous fault detection in testing phase before
      real-world deployment. Detailed characteristics of UTM are available in Appendix A.1.

162
      Fault Detection in UTM Systems Fault detection refers to the process of identifying potential
163   failures (or faults) before deploying UTM in real-world services. This process is of significant im-
164   portance to rule out possible failures in advance. Typically, testing phase contains several steps, with
165   the complexity of testing scenarios increasing progressively. As reported by the Federal Aviation
166   Administration (FAA), despite extensive testing, long-tail faults still threaten the safety of UTM
167   systems (Rios et al., 2017; FAA, 2023). These long-tail faults are particularly challenging to de-
168   tect because the complex scenarios in which they occur are difficult to generate. This difficulty is
169   compounded by the self-healing capabilities of UTM systems, which prevent the testing framework
170   from accessing or inducing these fault conditions. Motivated by this challenge, we propose a novel
171
      testing framework designed to accelerate fault detection and enable the discovery of new faults.
172
      3.2   F UNCTIONALITY OF P ROPOSED UTM T ESTING F RAMEWORK
173
174   The proposed testing framework operates as a testing module that runs alongside the UTM system,
175   monitoring its behavior and injecting controlled disturbances to SUT.
176
177   Input Stream The testing framework continuously receives three types of real-time data from
178   simulator: (1) UAV Runtime Data: Including position, velocity, and acceleration information for
179   all active UAVs (2) Mission Status: Flight plans, current objectives, and completion status for each
180   UAV Data streams fed to either testing framework or the UTM system are strictly identical, which
181   are available for RL modeling as states in 4.1.
182
183   Targets of Testing Framework The testing framework for UTM systems is designed to gener-
184   ate adversarial scenarios that evaluate system-wide behavior rather than individual on-device drone
185
      states. While traditional approaches to UTM testing focus on component-level verification, our
      framework aims to uncover vulnerabilities that emerge from complex system-wide interactions and
186
      temporal dependencies. Given the vast state space of UTM systems, we adopt a more focused ap-
187
      proach: generating a carefully curated set of high-risk test scenarios that are most likely to reveal
188   critical system vulnerabilities. This strategic reduction in test cases allows for more efficient and
189   targeted testing while maintaining comprehensive coverage of potential fault modes.
190
191   Challenges of Testing Framework In developing this targeted testing approach, we identified
192   three fundamental challenges: (1) Complex Temporal and Inter-agent Dependencies: UTM sys-
193   tems involve intricate temporal dependencies and agent interactions. Testing must consider both
194   long-term effects and multi-agent behaviors, as vulnerabilities often emerge from their combined
195   impact rather than immediate or single-agent issues. (2) Long-tail Effect in Fault Distribution:
196   Trivial errors are often corrected by self-healing mechanisms of UTM. Critical faults typically oc-
197   cur in rare edge cases (e.g. multiple simultaneous errors), making them difficult to detect through
198   conventional testing. (3) Environmental Consistency: Generated test scenarios must balance be-
199
      tween discovering edge cases and maintaining physical plausibility in realistic operational settings.
      Our framework addresses these challenges through a novel combination of reinforcement learning
200
      techniques and domain-specific constraints, as detailed in the following sections.

306
307   In this section, we introduce an automatic framework with generative capability for complicated
308   scenarios and interface for prior preference alignment and knowledge accumulation. As shown in
309   Fig. 2, in this framework, we utilize a Transformer-based model as policy model (PM), initiating
310   the process by producing a set of actions based on the system state. According to action space
311
      defined in Section 4.1, generated actions can be interpreted as potential fault injections to be applied
      to typical victim drones in UTM. Subsequently, these actions are passed through a domain-specific
312
      action sampler (AS). AS serves for two purposes: (1) ensure the PM-generated actions available
313
      within the specific UTM context; (2) leverage human expert knowledge to re-sample actions with
314   balanced preference bias in chosen actions and agents. Only actions sampled are injected into the
315   system-under-test (SUT). On SUT finishing execution, a new system state would be generated and
316   fed back to the PM, along with the evaluation of the actions (reward). Thus PM continuously refine
317   scenarios to uncover potential vulnerabilities.

      Preference Bias During the training process of decision-making models using auto-regressive
390   models such as offline reinforcement learning, there is usually an uneven distribution of the output
391   due to the collected training data, with little chance of sampling low-frequency choices. Meanwhile,
392   the more complex the system is tested, the more insidious the vulnerability and the more significant
393   the long-tail effect. In this work, training dataset is collected through traditional stress testing, where
394   unpredictable inductive bias is common in production systems.
395
      We introduce Preference Bias, improved from popularity bias (Klimashevskaia et al., 2024) with
396   additional domain expert knowledge, to unify imbalance in model prediction and gap in prior human
397   preference. Preference bias carries a expected distribution of ⟨UAV, Action⟩ tuples. The output
398   of the offline-trained PM is augmented with compensation dynamically calculated from distance
399   between recent historical trajectories and given distribution.
400
      Action Candidate Sampling As shown in Fig. 4, action logits predicted by PM are compensated
401   according to preference distribution. To address long-tail effect and improve fairness (Menon et al.,
402   2020), Top-K sampling is introduced after augmentation in order to maintain variance. Considering
403   realistic capability of system status, immediate action mask is applied in order to filter intolera-
404   ble action candidates. The final action is sampled through a uniform sampling after masking. By
405   combining the generative power of the Transformer-based Policy Model with the refined selection
406   process of the Action Sampler, our framework achieves a balance between exploration of complex
407   failure scenarios and adherence to real-world constraints. This approach enables more efficient
408   and effective testing of UTM systems, potentially uncovering critical vulnerabilities that traditional
409
      methods might miss. In below sections, we illustrate our advantages through experiment results.

411   6    R ESULTS
412
      We train the proposed framework with a large-scale offline dataset of around 17B tokens collected
413
      from stress testing data and evaluate on an industry-level simulator. As is summarised in Table. 8
414   in Appendix A.9, the training set consists of seven distinct regions and online testing includes two
415   regions. The training dataset covering diverse geographical and operational characteristics, includ-
416   ing a mix of rural (12.2%), suburban (39.0%), and urban areas (48.8%), each with varying numbers
417   of UAVs, airports, and flight lines. The dataset is balanced to represent the typical distribution of
418   scenarios encountered in real-world UTM systems. For testing, two regions (TR1 and TR2) are
419   excluded from the training set to provide evaluations of the generalization capabilities.
420   We design two model of different size, with 1.2 billion and 2 billion parameters (referred as PM-
421   1.2B and PM-2B respectively). We train each model on 16 NVIDIA A100 GPUs, each equipped
422   with 80GB of memory. The training utilized PyTorch’s Distributed Data Parallel (DDP) to efficiently
423   distribute the workload across multiple GPUs, ensuring high computational efficiency and resource
424   utilization. During training, the dataset is divided into smaller slices of 3B tokens for sequential
425   loading during training.
426   We evaluate the performance of the proposed model through both offline and online evaluations
427   to provide a comprehensive analysis. In Section 6.1, we focus on the offline evaluation of the
428   PM’s behavior during training, where we analyze the evolution of action accuracy and return-to-go
429   loss. In Section 6.2, the online evaluation measures the model’s performance in a deployed real-
430   world environment, where we collect and analyze a range of key metrics. This dual evaluation
431   framework offers a holistic view of the model’s efficacy, ensuring robustness both during training
      and in practical applications.

508   in discovering critical cases and efficiently covering high-risk regions. Interestingly, the PM-2B

      demonstrating its utility for uncovering rare bugs. Considering both the scaling effect and emergent

516
      Why does proposed framework exceed the performance of human experts? Although trained
517   with expert-guided exploitation data, PM model ultimately surpass the performance of human ex-
518   perts. This is attributed to that PM model applies offline RL, which can be viewed as an implicit
519   filter of low-quality actions (Prudencio et al., 2023), making it less susceptible to distraction during
520   the search for long-tail scenarios.
521
      We can illustrate this by analyzing the hazard action ratio per observation, which is obtained by
522
      multiplying HAR and APO, and the constant-pressure action ratio per observation, calculated by
523   multiplying CAR and APO. For both PM-2B, PM-1.2B, and human experts, the hazard action ratio
524   per observation is consistently around 2%. This shows that all methods are similarly effective in
525   identifying high-risk actions. However, the key difference is that the PM models demonstrate a sig-
526   nificantly higher constant-pressure action ratio per observation, indicating that they maintain a more
527   sustained level of high-risk actions over time. This ability to constantly pose challenges and main-
528   tain pressure highlights the advantage of the PM models in exploring complex, high-risk scenarios
529   more thoroughly, thereby leading to superior fault detection and scenario coverage.
530
531   7     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      We propose a novel scenario-oriented testing framework for identifying vulnerabilities in mission-
534   critical systems, specifically applied to UTM. Our approach leverages a Transformer-based policy
535   model to tackle long-tail effect and efficiency challenge in fault detection. Context utilization in
536   policy model improves generality in unseen regions. Our results highlight the potential of learning
537   and expert hybrid approaches in fortifying mission-critical systems. The end-to-end auto-regressive
538   learning methodologies are worth studying. Future work could explore the application of this frame-
539   work to other mission-critical domains beyond UTM, such as autonomous vehicles or industrial
      control systems.

      A.1   UTM S YSTEM A RCHITECTURE AND T ESTING P IPELINE
813
814   What is Unmanned aircraft Traffic Management (UTM) system? The Unmanned aircraft Traf-
815   fic Management (UTM) system, as introduced by the National Aeronautics and Space Administra-
816   tion (NASA) (Kopardekar, 2014; Kopardekar et al., 2016), is designed to ensure safe and efficient
817   operation of multiple unmanned Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) in shared airspace. The UTM
818   concept is developed to support the integration of UAVs into airspace without requiring human air
819   traffic controllers to manage every UAV directly. Instead, UTM emphasizes the use of automated
820
      systems to coordinate UAV operations. This includes services like geofencing, route optimization,
      and deconfliction, ensuring that UAVs can safely and autonomously operate in both sparsely popu-
821
      lated rural and densely populated urban areas or alongside manned aircraft.
822
823   UTM is typically developed as a complex system. This is because the UTM systems should in-
824   tegrate a wide range of functionalities and address diverse challenges associated with managing
825   UAV operations in dynamic and unpredictable environments. UTM systems need to handle real-
826
      time communication between UAVs, ground stations, and other stakeholders, while simultaneously
      ensuring safety, efficiency, and fairness in airspace usage.
827
828   As show in Figure 6, UTM serves as the central coordinator, processing dynamic information re-
829   ceived from all UAVs and managing overall traffic flow through sophisticated decision-making al-
830   gorithms simultaneously. UTM maintains continuous communication, flight route allocation and
831   trajectory assignment with multiple UAVs, each equipped with various sensors and control systems,
832
      while simultaneously monitoring environmental conditions and potential conflicts.
833
834
      What is fault detection in development of UTM and why it is important? We define the term
      fault detection as the process identifying possible faults in the UTM system during testing phase,
835
      which is before the UTM system is deployed in real-world environments. It is typically divided into
836
      several steps, including module testing, integration testing, smoke testing (functional testing), stress
837   testing, etc. After each testing step, the confidence (e.g., reliability, fault tolerance, and compliance
838   with regulatory standards) of UTM system increases as potential faults are identified and addressed,
839   ensuring that the system becomes progressively more robust and reliable.
840
      Fault detection is a critical aspect of UTM development because it directly impacts the safety, relia-
841
      bility, and efficiency of development pipeline. As a mission critical system, the UTM system should
842
      be designed to eliminate all the faults it may occur, which are usually costly or even deadly (e.g.,
843   UAV crushes, collisions with buildings or even collisions with human injuries) (Kopardekar, 2014;
844   Kopardekar et al., 2016). By identifying and addressing potential faults during the testing phase,
845   fault detection ensures that the UTM system operates as intended, mitigating risks before deploy-
846   ment in real-world environments. This proactive approach prevents costly failures, enhances system
847   robustness, and builds trust among stakeholders.
848
849   Why fault detection is challenging? Fault detection in UTM systems is inherently challenging,
850   particularly as testing progresses through advanced stages. While early testing steps may uncover
851   obvious issues, the long-tail of rare and hard-to-detect faults often remains persistent and elusive.
852   This difficulty is compounded by the self-healing capabilities of modern UTM systems, which can
853
      mask subtle issues that may only emerge under specific conditions. As is listed in the Table 3,
      although several testing steps have been conducted, there still remains faults to threat the safety of
854
      the UTM system (e.g. shakedown effects found by Federal Aviation Administration in field testing)
855
      (Rios et al., 2017; FAA, 2023). Based on the stepwise testing and field testing results, we estimate
856   the faults found in different steps of testing, as listed in Table 3. From data in the table, we can see
857   that as several testing steps are conducted, there still exists faults to be detected, which is fatal in
858   mission critical systems.

      A.3     C HALLENGES OF TESTING UTM
919
920   Critical Fault Distribution Imbalance While UTM’s fault-tolerant design successfully handles
921   most anomalies through automated recovery mechanisms and redundant control strategies, this ar-
922   chitectural resilience paradoxically increases the complexity of identifying severe failure scenarios,
923   as intermediate failure states are often automatically corrected before they can develop into ob-
924   servable system failures. Critical failures, those capable of overwhelming the system’s self-healing
925   mechanisms, occupy an extremely small portion of the state-action space, which often reside in nar-
926
      rowly defined regions of the state-action space, requiring precise combinations of multiple adverse
      factors to overcome the system’s multi-layer safety functionality. These regions are characterized by
927
      specific configurations of multiple elements: particular spatial arrangements of UAVs, precise tim-
928
      ing of control actions, specific environmental conditions. Furthermore, these failure scenarios often
929   represent emergent behaviors arising from subtle interactions between multiple system components
930   and their recovery attempts, rather than simple violations of individual safety constraints.

953   minutes later through complex agent interactions. Furthermore, subtle perturbations in early states
954   can propagate through the system’s temporal dynamics to trigger critical failures in significantly
955   later stages. The challenge is particularly pronounced in scenarios involving dense multi-UAV op-
956   erations, where system behavior emerges from the intricate interplay of multiple agents’ temporal
957   trajectories rather than simple state-transition patterns.

969   to accommodate extensive datasets necessary for identifying rare faults due to the long-tail effect in

1069   sists of two main phases: training and inference (testing), as illustrated in Algorithms 1 and 2. Al-
1070   gorithm 1 details the training phase, where the Policy Model (PM) learns from an offline dataset of
1071
       UTM scenarios. This phase involves iterating through epochs and batches, processing state-action-
       reward tuples, and updating the model parameters to minimize the prediction error for both actions
1072
       and rewards. The training process incorporates context augmentation to enhance the model’s ability
1073
       to capture temporal dependencies. Algorithm 2 outlines the inference (testing) phase, where the
1074   trained PM is used to generate and evaluate potentially vulnerable scenarios in the System-Under-
1075   Test (SUT). This phase operates in a loop, continuously generating candidate actions, filtering them
1076   through an Action Sampler (AS), injecting selected actions into the SUT, and evaluating the out-
1077   comes. The process accumulates detected vulnerabilities while dynamically updating the context
1078   based on observed states, actions, and rewards. Together, these algorithms form a comprehensive
1079   approach to identifying potential faults and vulnerabilities in UTM systems, leveraging both histor-
       ical data and adaptive, context-aware scenario generation.
