009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Aligning large-scale commercial models with user intent is crucial to preventing
012            harmful outputs. Current methods rely on human supervision but become imprac-
013            tical as model complexity increases. When models surpass human knowledge,
014            providing accurate feedback becomes challenging and inefficient. A novel solution
015            proposed recently is using a weaker model to supervise a stronger model. This
016
               concept leverages the ability of weaker models to perform evaluations, thereby
               reducing the workload on human supervisors. Previous work has shown the effec-
017
               tiveness of weak-to-strong generalization in the context of language-only models.
018
               Extending this concept to vision-language models leverages these insights, adapting
019            the proven benefits to a multi-modal context. In our study, we explore weak-to-
020            strong generalization for CLIP-based classification. We propose a method, class
021            prototype learning (CPL), which aims to enhance the classification capabilities
022            of the CLIP model, by learning more representative prototypes for each category.
023            Our findings indicate that despite the simple loss function under weak supervision,
024            CPL yields robust results. Our experiments are conducted on challenging datasets
025            to evaluate our method. Extensive experiments show that our method is effective,
026            achieving a 3.67% improvement over baseline methods.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Large language models (LLMs), such as GPT 4o (Achiam et al., 2023), Claude 3 (Anthropic, 2024)
031   and Gemini 1.5 (Reid et al., 2024), have made significant strides in enhancing performance across
032   a spectrum of natural language processing tasks. However, despite their successes, ensuring that
033   these models align with human expectations and intentions remains a formidable challenge (Burns
034   et al., 2023). Increasing the size of language models does not necessarily improve their ability to
035   follow user intent, as they can still produce untruthful, toxic, or unhelpful outputs, indicating a lack of
036   alignment with their users (Ouyang et al., 2022). Alignment with user intent is crucial for deploying
037   these models effectively in practice (Bai et al., 2022). Traditional alignment techniques often rely
038   heavily on human supervision (Christiano et al., 2017; Stiennon et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022;
039
      Glaese et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022), requiring evaluators to provide feedback on model outputs.
      However, as the complexity and intricacy of model outputs increase, the feasibility and scalability of
040
      this approach diminishes (Burns et al., 2023). As a result, there is a need to effectively align LLMs
041
      with human values without overly burdening human evaluators.
042
043   Burns et al. (2023) explored a novel approach known as weak-to-strong generalization to address
044   the challenge of aligning strong models with human feedback. This strategy leverages a weaker
045
      model to supervise a more robust one, presenting a promising method to enhance model alignment.
      The study by Burns et al. (2023) demonstrates the effectiveness of this weak-to-strong learning
046
      approach, where finetuning strong models with knowledge generated by their weaker counterparts
047
      consistently improves performance. For example, in natural language processing tasks, finetuning
048   GPT-4 with supervision from a GPT-2-level model significantly enhances GPT-4’s performance. This
049   approach highlights the viability of weak-to-strong learning as a solution for better model alignment,
050   demonstrating that even weaker models can provide valuable guidance for improving stronger models.
051   While the technique proves effective, applying it to Vision-Language Models (VLMs) is far from
052   straightforward. VLMs face unique challenges in aligning complex multimodal tasks, making it
053   essential to thoroughly explore the method’s applicability and limitations in this context. Unlike
      in natural language tasks, where text-based guidance can be more straightforward, VLMs must


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      align both visual and textual information, making supervision from weaker models significantly
055   more challenging. The complexity of managing two distinct modalities introduces difficulties in
056   ensuring coherent feedback across image and text domains, necessitating a more nuanced approach
057   when adapting weak-to-strong generalization to VLMs. Our goal is to rigorously investigate the
058   weak-to-strong paradigm within VLMs, as this problem extends beyond a mere adaptation of previous
059   work.
060
      In this study, we explore weak-to-strong generalization for CLIP-based classification, recognizing
061   it as a crucial starting point in VLMs. Existing VLMs (Radford et al., 2021; Jia et al., 2021) take
062   classification as the basic task to evaluate the alignment of images and texts. Also in our task setting,
063   classification makes it easier for us to design simulation experiments and establish a benchmark. In
064   this context, we introduce a method called class prototype learning (CPL). CPL involves generating
065   class prototypes that encapsulate the characteristics of each class using weak supervision. This
066   method effectively mitigates the false signals typically generated by weak supervision, thereby
067   showcasing superior performance. Moreover, when compared to conventional methods for adapting
068   VLMs to downstream tasks, such as prompt tuning (Zhou et al., 2022b; Jia et al., 2022), our CPL
069
      approach proves to be more efficient. This efficiency arises from the fact that CPL eliminates the need
      for employing a text encoder during the fine-tuning phase. By streamlining the adaptation process,
070
      CPL offers a more resource-effective solution while maintaining high-performance levels.
071
072   We conduct extensive experiments to evaluate the performance of the proposed method, CPL, using
073   the DomainNet dataset (Peng et al., 2019), which includes six diverse visual domains. The dataset
074   is divided into training and test sets, and the experiment involves training weak models, generating
075
      weak supervision sets, fine-tuning strong models with weak supervision, and comparing the results
      to strong model training with ground truth labels. Various baselines are used for comparison. Our
076
      results show that the CPL achieves the highest average accuracy across all domains, significantly
077
      outperforming other methods. In particular, CPL shows substantial improvements for challenging
078   domains like Infograph and handles domain-specific features effectively, despite CLIP’s lower zero-
079   shot performance in domains like QuickDraw. This illustrates the robustness and effectiveness of
080   CPL in weak-to-strong generalization scenarios.
081
      We summarize the main contributions of our work:
082
083         (i) Exploring weak-to-strong generalization for CLIP-based classification: Previous work
084             (Burns et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2024) has shown the effectiveness of weak-to-strong general-
085             ization in LLMs. Extending this concept to VLMs leverages these insights, adapting the
086             proven benefits to a multi-modal context.
087        (ii) Proposing CPL: We present a method that effectively leverages class prototype representa-
088             tions through weak supervision to enhance the classification performance of VLMs, such as
089
                CLIP (Radford et al., 2021).
090       (iii) Conducting simulation experiments: We design a simulation experiment within the VLMs
091             framework based on DomainNet (Peng et al., 2019) to study this problem, and establish a
092
                benchmark in this context. Our experiment resulted in a 3.67% improvement over baseline
                methods.

095   2    R ELATED W ORK
096
097   Vision-language models. VLMs integrate visual and textual information, enabling a multifaceted
098   understanding and interaction with multimodal content. CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) exemplifies this
099   approach, leveraging contrastive learning to align images with textual descriptions effectively. This
100   model demonstrates robust zero-shot capabilities, where it can recognize images or concepts it was
101   not explicitly trained on. The effectiveness of CLIP and similar models, such as ALIGN (Jia et al.,
102   2021), Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022), BLIP (Li et al., 2022) and Llava (Liu et al., 2023), arises from
103   their ability to generalize from vast amounts of web-collected data, learning nuanced, multimodal
104   representations that are applicable across various tasks and domains.
105
106   Vision-language prompt tuning. Research has also focused on improving prompt-based learning
107   and fine-tuning methods, such as CoOp (Zhou et al., 2022b) and CoCoOp (Zhou et al., 2022a), which
      adapt VLMs more effectively to specific tasks by learning customized prompt strategies. CoOp


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      transforms static text prompts into dynamic, learnable components. This allows prompts to adjust
109   during training, aligning model responses with task-specific needs, and improving performance,
110   especially in zero-shot or few-shot settings. Following CoOp, several studies Lu et al. (2022); Sun
111   et al. (2022); Derakhshani et al. (2023); Zhu et al. (2023); Gao et al. (2024) have advanced prompt
112   tuning to enhance model performance.
113
114   Knowledge distillation. Knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., 2015; Ahn et al., 2019; Zhao et al.,
115   2022; Jin et al., 2023) is an effective model compression technique in which a smaller, more efficient
116   student model learns from a larger, more complex teacher model. The conventional method for
117   knowledge distillation involves training the student model to minimize the difference between its
118   predicted probability distribution and that of the teacher model, often measured using Kullback-
119
      Leibler (KL) divergence. However, weak-to-strong generalization offers an alternative by having
      strong models supervised by weaker models.
120
121
      Weak-to-strong generalization. The concept of weak-to-strong generalization, initially introduced
122
      by Burns et al. (2023), presents a promising approach for aligning super-intelligent models with
123
      human values. This study emphasizes the significance of the issue and provides experimental
124   evidence to support its feasibility. Building on this framework, Guo et al. (2024) introduces a
125   dynamically adjusted confidence loss and demonstrates the effectiveness of their method in the
126   context of visual foundation models. Therefore, based on those previous work, we explore the
127   weak-to-strong generalization for VLMs.

259   We demonstrate the overall algorithm in Algorithm 1. The algorithm for weak-to-strong generalization
260   in VLMs begins by initializing class prototypes using text embeddings from the strong model. During

351   Baselines. In exploring the weak-to-strong problem within the VLM setting, we investigate different
352   fine-tuning strategies. Initially, Radford et al. (2021) assessed CLIP’s transferability via linear probs
353   (LP) across many datasets. Subsequent research focused on textual prompting (TP) (Zhou et al.,
354   2022b), where a learnable prompt is learned from a small target dataset. This method is data-efficient
355   and demonstrates good generalization effects. Prompt tuning has emerged as a popular method for
356   adapting VLMs to downstream tasks (Wu et al., 2023). Thus, we adopt linear probs and prompt
357   tuning as our foundational fine-tuning strategies within the realm of weak-to-strong generalization.
358
      In addition, we compare our method with the following learning strategies:
359   (1) Cross entropy (CE): Utilized in studies by (Radford et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2022b), cross-en-
360   tropy measures the disparity between one-hot ground truth label distribution and model prediction
361   probability. It serves as a straightforward baseline for this task. (2) Knowledge distillation (KD)
362   (Hinton et al., 2015) transfer knowledge from a strong model to a smaller one, serving as a funda-
363   mental baseline due to its simplicity and effectiveness. (3) Auxiliary confidence loss (AuxConf) is
364
      proposed by Burns et al. (2023), which excels in balancing direct learning from the weak model with
      the inherent capacity of the strong model. (4) Adaptive Confidence loss (AdaptConf) is introduced
365
      by Guo et al. (2024) that dynamically adjusts weights based on confidence levels, enabling the strong
366
      model to discern when to prioritize its predictions or follow the guidance of the weak model.

      6    C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In conclusion, traditional alignment techniques for LLMs, which rely heavily on human supervision,
489   such as RLFH, face significant challenges due to the intricacy of model outputs and the inefficiency of
490   requiring substantial human feedback. To address this, a novel approach has recently been proposed
491   where a weaker model supervises a much stronger one. Extending this concept to VLMs leverages
492   these insights, adapting the proven benefits to a multi-modal context. Hence, we introduced a method
493   called CPL, which effectively enhances the classification capabilities of VLMs with weak supervision.
494   Our simulation experiments validate the effectiveness of this weak-to-strong approach. Extensive
495   experimental results demonstrate that our method significantly improves performance across various
496   benchmarks. These results underscore the potential of weak supervision as a powerful tool in the
497
      alignment, offering a promising avenue for future research and application.
