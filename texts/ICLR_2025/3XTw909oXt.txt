011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly integrated into real-world ap-
014            plications through retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) mechanisms to supple-
015
               ment their responses with up-to-date and domain-specific knowledge. However,
               the valuable and often proprietary nature of the knowledge bases used in RAG
016
               introduces the risk of unauthorized usage by adversaries. Existing methods that
017
               can be generalized as watermarking techniques to protect these knowledge bases
018            typically involve backdoor or poisoning attacks, which introduce harmful behav-
019            iors (e.g., generating incorrect outputs for verification), thereby compromising
020            the LLM’s reliability. To address these challenges, we propose RAG⃝    C for harm-
021            less copyright protection of knowledge bases. Instead of manipulating the final
022            output, RAG⃝  C implants distinct verification behaviors in the space of chain-of-

023            thought (CoT) reasoning, maintaining the correctness of the final answer. Our
024            approach involves three main stages: (1) Generating CoTs: For each verification
025            question, we generate two CoTs, including a target CoT for building watermark
026
               behaviors; (2) Optimizing Watermark Phrases and Target CoTs: We optimize
               them to minimize retrieval errors under the black-box setting of suspicious LLM,
027
               ensuring that the watermarked verification queries activate the target CoTs with-
028
               out being activated in non-watermarked ones; (3) Ownership Verification: We
029            exploit a pairwise Wilcoxon test to statistically verify whether a suspicious LLM
030            is augmented with the protected knowledge base by comparing its responses to
031            watermarked and benign verification queries. Our experiments on diverse bench-
032            marks demonstrate that RAG⃝   C effectively protects knowledge bases against unau-

033            thorized usage while preserving the integrity and performance of the RAG.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Large language models (LLMs), such as GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), LLaVa (Liu et al., 2024), and
039
      PaLM (Anil et al., 2023), have been widely deployed in many real-world applications, including
      intelligent assistant (Dong et al., 2023), ChatBot (Zheng et al., 2023), and finance (Dowling &
040
      Lucey, 2023). Despite their success in exceptional generative capabilities, they also suffer from
041
      lacking up-to-date knowledge as they are pre-trained on past data (Wu et al., 2024); they could
042   also lack knowledge on specific domains (e.g., medical domain (Xiong et al., 2024)), restricting the
043   real-world deployment of LLMs in applications like healthcare (Zakka et al., 2024).
044
045
      To address the above limitations, retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) is proposed to augment
      an LLM with external knowledge retrieved from given knowledge databases. Its main idea is to
046
      combine the strengths of retrieval-based and generative models to produce more accurate and con-
047
      textually relevant outputs. In general, RAG contains three main components: LLMs, retriever, and
048   knowledge base. Specifically, LLMs and the retriever are both machine learning models pre-trained
049   with existing data for generating answers and knowledge retrieval. Knowledge bases contain a large
050   number of texts collected from various domains or the Internet to provide domain-specific expertise
051   and up-to-date information for LLMs. In particular, these knowledge bases, especially those from
052   mission-critical domains (e.g., finance (Zhang et al., 2023) and healthcare Zakka et al. (2024)),
053   usually contain a large amount of valuable or even exclusive data. It leads to great incentives for
      adversaries to ‘steal’ or ‘misuse’ these knowledge bases for enhancing their deployed LLMs service

072   To the best of our knowledge, no existing work has been proposed to protect the copyright for RAG’s
073   knowledge bases. Arguably, one of the most straightforward or even the only potential solutions is
074   to formulate this copyright protection problem as an ownership verification: defenders evaluate
075   whether a third-party suspicious LLM is augmented with their RAG knowledge base under the
076
      black-box and text-only setting, where the defender can only query the suspicious LLM with prompts
      and get the corresponding content through its API without accessing its parameters, configurations
077
      (e.g., model structure), and intermediate results (e.g., token probability). To achieve this, similar
078
      to existing dataset ownership verification (Li et al., 2020; 2022; Xu et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2024),
079   the owners of knowledge base should first watermark it via poisoning or backdoor attacks against
080   RAG (Chen et al., 2024; Xiang et al., 2024; Zou et al., 2024) before storing and distributing it so
081   that all LLMs augmented with it will have some distinctive prediction behaviors. Unfortunately,
082   these methods inevitably introduce new security risks to the deployed machine learning systems as
083   these distinctive behaviors are generating incorrect answers or decisions on particular verification
084   prompts. This ‘harmful’ nature will hinder their applications in practice (Li et al., 2022; Yao et al.,
085   2024; Shao et al., 2025). As such, an intriguing and important question arises here: Can we design
086   harmless copyright protection for RAG’s knowledge base?
087   The answer to the above question is positive! We argue that their harmful nature is inevitable since
088   they directly implant distinctive behaviors simply and directly in the final results. As such, they have
089   to make the results of verification samples/prompts incorrect or anomalous to distinguish them from
090   normal ones. Motivated by this understanding, we propose to implant these behaviors in another
091   space, particularly the space of chain-of-thought (CoT, i.e., lines of reasons). CoT is a fundamental
092   step of LLM reasoning for its results, containing sufficient information. In general, our method
093
      (dubbed ‘RAG⃝   C ’) first selects a few questions (dubbed ‘verification questions’) and generates two

      different CoTs, including the target CoT and the non-target CoT (via LLMs or human experts)
094
      with the correct answer for each question, as shown in Figure 1. RAG⃝       C will then watermark the
095
      knowledge base based on these CoTs, leading to all ‘bad RAGs’ (i.e., LLMs augmented with our
096   knowledge base) answer the watermarked verification questions based on their corresponding target
097   CoTs while their answers generated by benign LLMs are based on non-target CoTs.
098
099
      Our RAG⃝   C consists of three main stages: (1) generating CoTs, (2) optimizing watermark phrases

      and target CoTs, and (3) ownership verification. The first stage generates two CoTs for each veri-
100
      fication question following the above approaches; In the second stage, we first prove that the upper
101
      bound of the retrieval error rate of the target CoT is related to the similarity between the verification
102   question containing the watermark phrase and other instances within the knowledge base without
103   the watermark on the hidden space. Inspired by this, we propose optimizing the watermark phrase
104   by minimizing that similarity to reduce the retrieval error rate. We design two methods, including
105   optimization-based and LLM-based ones, to optimize the watermark phrase for each verification
106   question. The former requires a surrogate (pre-trained) retriever to optimize the watermark directly.
107   Inspired by (Chen et al., 2024), we also incorporate a linguistic-related loss guided by an LLM,
      to ensure the naturalness and fluency of the sentences after adding the watermark phrase, thereby

120   In conclusion, the main contributions of this paper are four-fold: (1) We explore the copyright pro-
121   tection of knowledge bases used for RAG and formulate this problem as an ownership verification
122   under the black-box setting. (2) We reveal the harmful nature of extending existing backdoors or
123
      poisoning attacks against LLMs to watermark the knowledge base used for ownership verification.

124

      provide its theoretical foundations. (4) We conduct extensive experiments on benchmark datasets,
126   verifying the effectiveness of our RAG⃝ C and its resistance to potential adaptive methods.

129   2   BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
130
131
      Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG). Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) is a technique
132
      designed to enhance the capabilities of large language models (LLMs) by integrating external knowl-
133   edge sources (i.e., knowledge bases) (Lewis et al., 2020). Unlike traditional LLMs, which generate
134   responses solely based on the knowledge encoded during pre-training, RAG combines both retrieval
135   and generation mechanisms to produce more accurate, contextually relevant, and up-to-date outputs.
136   Existing RAG systems implemented dual encoders to map queries and texts within the knowledge
137   base into the embedding space and retrieve candidate texts that produce high similarity values with
138   the given query. Recent works were proposed to improve the effectiveness of retrieval models by
139   implementing different encoder architectures (Nogueira & Cho, 2019; Humeau et al., 2019; Khattab
140   et al., 2021), searching algorithms (Xiong et al., 2021b), embedding capacity (Günther et al., 2023),
141
      max tokens (Muennighoff et al., 2022), etc. In general, the knowledge base plays a critical role in
      the effectiveness of the RAG, containing valuable and often proprietary content. They are valuable
142
      intellectual property of their owners and their copyright deserves to be protected.
143
144   Poisoning and Backdoor Attacks against RAG Systems. Recently, there are also a few pio-
145   neering works exploring data-centric threats in RAG systems (Zou et al., 2024; Xiang et al., 2024;
146   Chen et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 2024). Specifically, PoisonedRAG (Zou et al., 2024) proposed the
147
      first data poisoning attack against RAG by injecting several malicious and wrong answers into the
      knowledge base for each pre-defined query. The adversaries could lead the compromised RAG to
148
      generate targeted wrong answers with these pre-defined queries. TrojanAgent (Cheng et al., 2024)
149
      proposed a backdoor attack by compromising its retriever; thus, leveraging queries attaching with
150   adversary-specified optimized trigger patterns could activate the malicious behavior embedded in its
151   compromised retriever. Most recently, AgentPoison (Chen et al., 2024) proposed the backdoor attack
152   against RAG by injecting optimized malicious target texts (decisions) into the external knowledge
153   base. AgentPoison also proposed an optimization framework to optimize a stealthy and effective
154   trigger pattern for increasing the probability of retriever retrieving the hidden malicious target texts.
155   These methods all seriously undermine the integrity of RAG systems.
156   Dataset Ownership Verification. Dataset ownership verification (DOV) aims to verify whether a
157   suspicious model is trained on the protected dataset (Li et al., 2022; 2023; Guo et al., 2023; Tang
158   et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2024). To the best of our knowledge, this is currently the only feasible
159   method to protect the copyright of public datasets in a retrospective manner. Specifically, DOV in-
160   tends to introduce specific prediction behaviors (towards verification samples) in models trained on
161   the protected dataset while preserving their performance on benign testing samples, by solely water-
      marking the dataset before releasing it. Dataset owners can verify ownership by examining whether

198   The Main Pipeline of Retrieval-augmented LLMs. In this paper, we discuss LLMs built with
199
      retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) mechanism under a knowledge base D based on the prompt
      corpus. Specifically, the knowledge base D contains a set of in-context query-solution examples
200
201
      {xi , yi }N   D
                  i=1 , where x and y represent the query and its corresponding solution within the retrial
      knowledge base D, respectively. In RAG, for each given query x, the retrieval model uses an encoder
202
      Eq (·; θq ) parameterized by θq to map it into the embedding space via Eq (x; θq ) and seeks the most
203
      relevant samples within D based on their similarity (i.e., cosine similarity). Technically, RAG
204   finds k nearest examples within D of x (dubbed εk (x, D)) in the embedding space through KNN
205   search (Cover & Hart, 1967). After retrieving εk (x, D), RAG arranges these instances and x into
206   an augmented input text xr using a specifically designed template. Finally, the (pre-trained) LLM
207   f (·; θl ) takes xr as input to perform in-context learning and output the generated text f (xr ; θl ).
208
      Threat Model. Following previous works in data copyright protection, we consider two main par-
209
      ties, including the defender (i.e., owner) and the adversary, in our threat model. Specifically, the
210   adversaries intend to ‘steal’ and misuse the protected knowledge base released by the defender to
211   improve their developed LLMs without authorization. In contrast, the defender aims to protect
212   the copyright of their valuable knowledge base by verifying whether a given suspicious model is
213   augmented with it. In particular, we consider the most practical and stringent defender’s settings,
214   i.e., black-box and text-only setting, where the defender can only query the suspicious LLM with
215   prompts and get the corresponding content through its API without accessing its parameters, con-
      figurations (e.g., model structure), and intermediate results (e.g., token probability).

      3.4   OWNERSHIP V ERIFICATION VIA D ISTINCTIVE C OT B EHAVIORS
328
329   In the last stage, RAG⃝
                            C identifies whether a given suspicious LLM is augmented with our protected

330   knowledge base by querying it with the original and watermarked verification questions.
331
      Specifically, we query the suspicious LLM f with any verification question x and its watermarked
332   version x ⊕ δ to determine whether their answers contain the information of their corresponding
333   target CoT (i.e., t ∈ f (x ⊕ δ) and t ∈ / f (x)). Given the complexity and diversity of natural
334   languages, we leverage the power of advanced LLMs (i.e., GPT-4) to judge it. We put the designed
335   template used by GPT-4 in Appendix B.
336
      In particular, to reduce the side effects of randomness in selecting verification questions, we design
337
      a hypothesis-test-guided method for ownership verification. Its definition is as follows.
338
339
      Proposition 1. Let X, X ′ , T denote the variable of verification question, its watermarked version,
      and its target CoT, respectively. For a suspicious large language model f , suppose C is the judgment
340
      function, i.e., C(X ′ ) ≜ 2 · I{T ∈ f (X ′ )} − 1 and C(X) ≜ 2 · I{T ∈ f (X)} − 1. Given the null
341
      hypothesis H0 : C(X ′ ) + C(X) = 0 (H1 : C(X ′ ) + C(X) > 0), we claim that it is built with the
342
      protected knowledge base if and only if H0 is rejected.
343
344   In practice, we randomly select m (i.e., 100) verification questions (as well as their watermarked
345   versions and target CoTs) for the ownership verification. Specifically, we hereby use the pairwise
346   Wilcoxon test (instead of t test) (Schmetterer, 2012) since the results of the judgment function C are
347   discrete (i.e., ∈ {−1, 1}) instead of following the Gaussian distribution. The null hypothesis H0 is
348   rejected if and only the p-value is smaller than the significance level α (e.g., 0.01).

532   augmented generation (RAG) of large language models (LLMs). By leveraging chain-of-thought
533
      (CoT) reasoning instead of manipulating final outputs, RAG⃝          C offers a harmless watermarking

      method for ownership verification that maintains the correctness of the generated answers of LLMs
534
      augmented with the protected knowledge base. This approach leveraged optimized watermark
535
      phrases and verification questions to detect potential misuse through hypothesis-test-guided own-
536   ership verification. We also provided the theoretical foundations of our RAG⃝      C . Extensive experi-
537   ments on benchmark datasets verified the effectiveness of our method and its resistance to potential
538   adaptive attacks. Our work highlights the urgency of protecting copyright in RAG’s knowledge
539   bases and provides its solutions, to facilitate their trustworthy circulation and deployment.

1163   G     P OTENTIAL L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
1164
1165   First, as outlined in our threat model, the goal of our defense is consistent with previous work on
1166   dataset ownership verification (DOV) (Li et al., 2022; Guo et al., 2023) that we aim to trace the
1167   utilization of the protected knowledge base. Our approach can not prevent the protected knowledge
1168   base from being misused or stolen in a proactive manner. In the future, we will explore a new
1169   approach that can prevent the knowledge base from being misused a in a proactive manner.
1170   Secondly, our approach requires conducting optimization on the watermark phrase for each verifi-
1171   cation question and corresponding target CoTs, requiring certain computational resources. In the
1172   future, we will explore how to further improve our efficiency.
1173
       Lastly, RAG⃝ C primarily focuses on the pure language models and can not directly be applied to
1174   the multimodal setting, such as the Vision Language Model. In the future, we will explore a more
1175   generalized approach that can perform effectively across different tasks and architectures of models.
