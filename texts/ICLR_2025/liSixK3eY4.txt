009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            We study the combinatorial online learning prediction problem with bandit feed-
012            back in a strategic setting, where the algorithm selects a subset of experts at each
013            round and the experts can strategically influence the learning algorithm’s predic-
014            tions by manipulating their beliefs about a sequence of binary events. There are
015            two learning objectives for the algorithm. The first is maximizing its cumulative
016            utility over a fixed time horizon, equivalent to minimizing regret. The second
017            objective is to ensure incentive compatibility, guaranteeing that each expert’s op-
018            timal strategy is to report their true beliefs about the outcomes of each event. In
019
               real applications, the learning algorithm only receives the utility corresponding to
               their chosen experts, which is referred to as the full-bandit setting. In this work,
020
               we present an efficient algorithm based on mirror descent, which achieves a regret
021
               of O(T 3/4 ) under both the full-bandit or semi-bandit feedback model, while en-
022
               suring incentive compatibility. To our best knowledge, this is the first algorithm
023            that can simultaneously achieve sublinear regret and exact incentive compatibility.
024            To demonstrate the effectiveness of our algorithm, we conduct extensive empirical
025            evaluation with the algorithm on a synthetic dataset.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   We study the combinatorial online prediction problem, in which a learning algorithm is asked to
031   make predictions about a sequence of T binary events. This is the extension of the classic online
032   learning with expert problems (Vovk, 1990; Littlestone & Warmuth, 1994; Cesa-Bianchi et al., 1997;
033   Kivinen & Warmuth, 1999; Chen et al., 2013). At each round, the learning algorithm selects m ≥ 1
034   experts out of k experts, each has a belief about the likelihood of the event. Based on the chosen
035   expert, the algorithm will then receive a utility. The goal of the algorithm is to select a sequence
036   of subsets of experts that is as accurate as possible. Usually, the performance is then measured in
037   regret, which is the difference between the cumulative utility received by the algorithm and the best
038
      cumulative possible with the best subset of experts’ predictions possible. The class of algorithms
      that attain sublinear regret are referred to as the no-regret algorithms.
039
040   In real applications, however, the experts may often be strategic individuals. They may strategically
041   manipulate their predictions to maximize their own utility. For instance, the renowned website
042   FiveThirtyEight aggregates data from various pollsters to predict election outcomes and evaluates
043   them by publicly scoring their accuracy and methodology. The pollsters may be incentivized to
044
      tailor their methodologies or reporting practices to align with what they believe will garner higher
      scores, to gain influence. More motivating examples can be found in the appendix.
045
046   Under the manipulation of strategic experts, it is desirable to have the algorithm be incentive-
047   compatible, for two reasons. First, when the experts strategically skew their reported prediction,
048   the resulting aggregate prediction may drift away from accuracy, leading to erroneous conclusions
049   and decisions. Second, incentivizing experts to be honest could reveal more truth to the public,
050
      which is beneficial to society. However, many of the classic online learning algorithms, such as the
      multiplicative weight, are shown to be not incentive-compatible (Freeman et al., 2020). This thus
051
      motivates the design of incentive combative algorithms that are also no-regret.
052
053   In the special case of m = 1, Freeman et al. (2020) presented the first incentive-compatible and
      no-regret learning algorithm. The algorithm is based on the wagering mechanism, which is a type

073   cube. We then conclude the O(T 3/4 ) regret upper bound by utilizing the continuous relaxation
074   of the original objective. We complement our theoretical findings on a synthetic dataset, in which
075   our algorithm outperforms the online mirror descent algorithm in both regret minimization and the
076   incentive compatibility guarantee.

086

087

218   In order to employ gradient-based optimization on f , we want to relax the problem of optimizing f
219   on S, the set of possible combinations of less than m experts on a continuous function F . One of
220   the most intuitive choices is to define F as the multilinear
                                                            P      extensionQof f : let F (x, r) : X → R be
                                                                   Q
221   the multilinear extension of f (S, r), i.e. F (x, r) = S∈S i∈S xi j ∈S   / (1 − xj )f (S, r).
222
      Then, if we could estimate the function value of the multi-linear extension unbiasedly by using only
223   one query to the corresponding discrete submodular function, we can reduce the problem of optimiz-
224   ing f over a discrete set S, to optimizing F over X ⊆ [0, 1]k . However, this is generally impossible
225   to achieve in the full bandit feedback setting, as the definition of the multi-linear extension relies on
226   information about the values of the submodular set function across all subsets, including those that
227   do not meet the specified size constraints (Zhang et al., 2019). A formal proof of impossibility is
228   given in Lemma 2 of Zhang et al. (2019). To overcome this, Zhang et al. (2019) considered a relaxed
229   setting, where the algorithm is allowed to select an infeasible subset and receive zero reward. Under
230   this setting, they designed an algorithm to attain O(T 8/9 ) regret. Although it is infeasible to find a
231   one query estimator for the general case, we remark that this does not rule out the possibility of one
232
      query estimator for specific loss and constraint set.
233   Instead of following the relaxed setting, we consider a class of problems with the appropriate exten-
234   sion F . Specifically, we make the following assumption on f .
235   Assumption 4.1. For a finite set S that includes possible subsets containing fewer than m items
236   from a total of k items, a binary value r, and a monotone, submodular function f , there exist an
237   extension mapping EXT : X → S, X = {x : x ∈ [0, 1]k , ∥x∥1 ≤ m} such that
238
239
            1. F (x, r) = ES∼EXT(x) [f (S, r)], is a multilinear, monotone, DR-submodular function, and
               F is L-Lipschitz continuous.
240
241         2. For any S ∈ S, there exists an x such that EXT(x) = IS , where IS assign probability 1 to
242            S and 0 to other elements of S.

      We can thus reduce the problem of maximizing f over S to maximizing F over X . A similar
      assumption was also made in bandit submodular maximization literature (Wan et al., 2023), which is
245
      shown to be satisfied for several important functions f that have applications in fields such as online
246
      retailing platforms optimization. Moreover, such extension mapping is shown to exist for partition

248   special case of partition matroid constant, Assumption 4.1 is indeed satisfied for our case.

      4.3     L INEAR U PDATE AND I NCENTIVE C OMPATIBILITY
292
293
      A greedy algorithm such as the continuous greedy algorithm may be appealing to minimize the re-
294   gret of optimizing a submodular function in an online setting. However, previous work has shown
295   that such an approach is not immediately incentive-compatible and may only achieve approximate
296   incentive compatibility in some specific settings, such as the loss is quadratic (Sadeghi & Fazel,
297   2023). On the other side, popular online algorithms such as online gradient descent and the expo-
298   nential weight are also shown to be not incentive-compatible (Freeman et al., 2020).
299
      One straightforward way to ensure incentive compatibility is to define xt+1,i as a linear function
300   with a positive derivative for f (St , rt ). Consequently, maximizing xt+1,i becomes equivalent to
301   maximizing f (St , rt ) in expectation. Given that f (St , rt ) is assumed to be linear and with a neg-
302   ative derivative with respect to each ℓt,i , expert i will need to minimize ℓt,i in order to maximize
303   xt+1,i . Since the loss ℓ is assumed to be proper, it naturally follows from the definition of a proper
304   loss function. This idea was previously used in deriving incentive compatibility bandit algorithms
305   (Freeman et al., 2020; Zimmert & Marinov, 2024).
306   However, one challenge in selecting the linear update rule is ensuring that the updated xt+1 still
307   stays in X , which has constraints ∥x∥1 ≤ m and xi ∈ [0, 1], ∀t ∈ [T ], i ∈ k], and to have sublinear
308   regret. While classic algorithms such as online projected gradient descent or mirror descent can
309   easily solve this, it is unclear how to modify a linear update rule     to fulfill this. To address this
                                                                  √
310   challenge, we update xt+1,i with xt+1,i = xt,i 1 − 2ηt xt,i g̃t,i , where g̃t,i is a shifted one-point
311   gradient estimator, g̃t,i = gt,i + 2δkt . We demonstrate that this update is equivalent to the well-known
312                                                                                      Pk √
      online mirror descent method, with the regularizer defined as Φ(x) = −2 i=1 xt,i + IX (x),
313                          Pk
      X = {x ∈ [0, 1]k : i=1 xi ≤ m}. Notice that this choice of regularizer guarantees our update
314
      xt+1 stays in X , and the equivalence allows us to utilize the classic mirror descent analysis to desrive
315
      a regret guarantee. A similar equivalence between a modified online mirror descent and a linear-
316   style update rule has been established when x is restricted to the probability simplex (Zimmert &
317   Marinov, 2024). We further extend the idea to the m-experts problem.

          7    E XPERIMENTS
460
461       In this section, we validate our algorithm under full bandit feedback with synthetic data. We imple-
462       ment our algorithm with a learning rate of 0.005, and gradient estimation parameter δt = 0.1. We
463       plot the averaged regret (note that this is not the α-approximate regret) and the difference between
464       expert predictions and their private belief in Figure 1. We evaluated our algorithm under the config-
465       urations of k = 4, 6, 8 and m = 2, 3. For each experiment, we set the time horizon to 10000. We
466       also implemented the classic online mirror descent algorithm with a learning rate of 0.01. For each
467       configuration, we perform the experiment with 5 different random seeds and the mean and standard
468
          deviation are reported in the figure. We ran all of the experiments on Google Colab with 12.7 GB
          System RAM.
469
470       For the binary realization rt and private belief {bt,i }Tt=1 , ∀i ∈ [k], we randomly generate them before
471       the interaction of between the experts and the algorithm. To implement the strategic experts, we let
472       each expert to randomly deviate from their private belief with a magnitude of (bt−1,i − rt ) with a
473       probability of 1 − xt−1,i .
474       We can see that in all settings, our algorithm can effectively elicit truthful responses from the experts,
475       as the differences between the expert predictions and the private belief go to 0 as the time step
476       increases. Compared to the online mirror descent algorithm, our algorithm converges slower, but
477       to a better approximation ratio. It also achieves higher average utility when compared to the online
478       mirror descent. Moreover, it is clear that the online mirror descent is not incentive-compatible, as
479       the differences between the expert predictions and their private beliefs are constant. We can also
480
          conclude that our algorithm can minimize the regret, and the empirical approximation ratio is much
          better than our theoretical value of 1/2.
