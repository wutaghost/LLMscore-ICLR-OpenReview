009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Understanding events in time series is an important task in a variety of contexts.
012            However, human analysis and labeling are expensive and time-consuming. There-
013            fore, it is advantageous to learn embeddings for moments in time series in an
014            unsupervised way, which allows for good performance in classification or detec-
015            tion tasks after later minimal human labeling. In this paper, we propose dynamic
016            contrastive learning (DynaCL), an unsupervised contrastive representation learn-
017            ing framework for time series that uses temporal adjacent steps to define positive
018
               pairs. DynaCL adopts N-pair loss to dynamically treat all samples in a batch as
               positive or negative pairs, enabling efficient training and addressing the challenges
019
               of complicated sampling of positives. We demonstrate that DynaCL embeds in-
020
               stances from time series into semantically meaningful clusters, which allows supe-
021            rior performance on downstream tasks on a variety of public time series datasets.
022            Our findings also reveal that high scores on unsupervised clustering metrics do not
023            guarantee that the representations are useful in downstream tasks.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   A common task in time series (TS) analysis is to split the series into many small windows and
029   identify or label the event taking place in each window. Learning a good representation for these
030   moments eases the time and domain expertise needed for this data annotation. Self-supervised
031   learning, which produces descriptive and intelligible representations in natural language processing
032   (NLP) and computer vision (CV), has emerged as a promising path for learning TS representation.
033
      One approach to representation learning is contrastive learning, in which positive and negative pairs
      of samples are identified, the embeddings of positive pairs are made similar, and the embeddings
034
      of negative pairs are made dissimilar. In CV, data augmentation has been successful in creating
035
      positive pairs in an unsupervised way. In TS analysis, it is instead possible to create positive pairs
036   on the assumption that moments close in time are also likely to have similar embeddings. Currently,
037   TS representation learning that leverages temporal information in the contrastive objective relies on
038   inefficient sampling positives (Yue et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2023; Oord et al., 2018; Tonekaboni et al.,
039   2021; Woo et al., 2022). This work introduces dynamic contrastive learning (DynaCL), an approach
040   to TS representation learning through a simple contrastive learning framework that efficiently cap-
041   tures temporal information by sampling positives from adjacent time steps.
042   Our contrastive objective extends the N-pair loss introduced by Sohn (2016) to efficiently harness
043   every time step in a sequence as positive and negative pairs. The N-pair loss solves the problem
044   of selecting statistically relevant and varying window sizes in every batch, allowing our method to
045   adapt to the different data structures without prior knowledge of the data distribution. Inspired by
046   the finite difference heat equation in thermodynamics (Mitchell & Griffiths, 1980), we use multiple
047   adjacent moments as positive partners for the reference time step to enhance convergence. Moti-
048   vated by feature prediction methods (Assran et al., 2023; Caron et al., 2021; Oquab et al., 2023),
049   we extend our model by incorporating a margin into the contrastive loss (DynaCL-M) in a bid to
050
      introduce feature invariance, and train this variant to jointly optimize both the contrastive and feature
      prediction objectives. Both of these approaches learn representations before using any human effort
051
      on labeling.
052
053   As in Tonekaboni et al. (2021), we measure the quality of our learned embeddings using statistics
      measuring properties of the resulting clusters. However, as the goal is not just to create clusters, but


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      to learn generalizable features that are useful for downstream classification, we use linear evaluation
055   with a frozen backbone to evaluate the quality of the learned representations. Our findings demon-
056   strate that DynaCL not only produces useful off-the-shelf representations but also outperforms pre-
057   vious TS contrastive learning state-of-the-art methods. This paper makes three main contributions:
058
059   • Propose DynaCL, an unsupervised contrastive representation learning framework for time series
060     that samples positive pairs from temporal adjacent steps, and DynaCL-M, an augmentation of
061     DynaCL that combines the contrastive learning objective with a masked feature prediction loss to
062     learning time series representation.
063   • Introduce multiple positive pairs in the normalized temperature-scaled cross-entropy loss (NT-
064     Xent) Chen et al. (2020) loss to accelerate learning and adapt this for time series representation
065     learning. For convenience, we term this loss MP-Xent (multiple positive cross-entropy loss).
066   • Conduct extensive experiments on three public datasets and demonstrate superior results com-
067     pared to state-of-the-art baselines on clustering and classification.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
071
      Contrastive representation learning. Contrastive learning (CL) (Hadsell et al., 2006) is a widely
072
      used self-supervised learning strategy with huge success in CV and NLP (Chen et al., 2020; He
073
      et al., 2020; Brown, 2020). Unlike generative models that try to reconstruct inputs, contrastive-
074   based methods aim to learn data representation by contrasting positive and negative samples. Sohn
075   (2016) introduces the N-pair loss for efficient learning by employing multiple negatives in each
076   batch update. Specifically, Sohn (2016) extends triple loss (Weinberger et al., 2005) by allowing
077   joint comparison among negative samples. Contrastive predictive coding (CPC) (Oord et al., 2018)
078   learns representation using autoregressive models to predict future time steps in a latent space. A
079   key component of CPC is the introduction of InfoNCE loss, based on noise-contrastive estimation
080   (Gutmann & Hyvärinen, 2010; Jozefowicz et al., 2016) by removing the proximal constraint and
081   using positive pairs. SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020) uses data augmentation and a contrastive loss
082
      called NT-Xent that encourages positive pairs (augmented view of the same image) to be closer
      in the representation space while pushing negative pairs apart. He et al. (2020) proposes a CL
083
      framework that uses a momentum encoder to update the features stored in a dynamic dictionary for
084
      stable and consistent feature representation over time. Mitrovic et al. (2020) enforces invariance
085   by adding regularization to the InfoNCE objective. Yeh et al. (2022) further removes the positive
086   pair in the denominator, while in Dwibedi et al. (2021), instead of relying solely on augmentations,
087   uses the nearest neighbor of the current data point in feature space to serve as positive pairs. In this
088   work, we extend the NT-Xent loss by introducing multiple positive pairs in the numerator to capture
089   adjacent time steps, we call this modified loss MP-Xent.
090
      Contrastive learning in time series. With the recent traction of CL in CV and NLP, several works
091   in TS representation learning have proposed different methods for sampling positive and negative
092   pairs. Wickstrøm et al. (2022) creates a new augmented sample of a time series and attempts to pre-
093   dict the strength of the mixing components. Zhang et al. (2022) samples positive pairs as time-based
094   and frequency-based representations from the time series signal and introduces a time-frequency
095   consistency framework. Yang et al. (2022) introduces dynamic time warping (DTW) data augmen-
096   tation for creating phase shifts and amplitude changes. Lee et al. (2024) proposes soft assignment
097   to leverage every pair other than the positive pairs by assigning weights to both instance and tem-
098   poral CL to improve on previous CL frameworks. However, this soft assignment is precomputed
099
      offline and not during training. To learn discriminative representation across time, TS2Vec (Yue
      et al., 2022) considers the representation at the same time stamp from two views as positive pairs.
100
      InfoTS (Luo et al., 2023) focuses on developing criteria for selecting good augmentation in con-
101
      trastive learning in the TS domain. T-loss (Franceschi et al., 2019) employs a time-based sample
102   and a triplet loss to learn representation by selecting positive and negative samples based on their
103   temporal distance from the anchor. TNC (Tonekaboni et al., 2021) presents temporal neighborhood
104   with a statistical test to determine the neighborhood range that it treats as positive samples. Yèche
105   et al. (2021), on the other hand, selects neighbors based on both instance-level and temporal-level
106   criteria with a trade-off parameter allowing the model to balance instance-wise distinction with tem-
107   poral coherence. (Kiyasseh et al., 2021) define a positive pair as a representation of transformed
      instances of the same subject. TS-TCC (Eldele et al., 2021) proposes a method to combine temporal


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      and contextual information in TS using data augmentation to select positives and predict the future
109   of one augmentation using past features of another representation in the temporal contrasting mod-
110   ule. CoST (Woo et al., 2022) applied CL in learning representation for TS forecasting by having
111   inductive biases in model architecture to learn disentangled seasonal trends.
112
      Feature prediction in representation learning. A growing body of work in TS representation
113
      learning has attempted to enforce feature invariance by jointly optimizing instance-wise CL with
114
      temporal CL (Yue et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2024). However, we argue that selecting positive pairs
115   and negatives, for instance-wise CL based on distance in the feature space, might lead to subop-
116   timal performance. Ideally, pair selection should be guided by semantic similarity in the learned
117   feature space, rather than raw distance. Self distillation methods have sorted to avoid the need for
118   selecting negatives in their training objectives (Grill et al., 2020; Caron et al., 2021). They rely on
119   encoding two augmented views and mapping one to the other using a predictor. To avoid mode
120   collapse in self-distillation due to the absence of negative as in CL, they update one of the encoder
121   weights with the running exponential moving average (EMA) of the other encoder. Chen & He
122   (2021) show that the EMA was not necessary in practice, even though it led to a small performance
123
      boost. Logacjov & Bach (2024) uses the traditional pretext of masked reconstruction to learn fea-
      ture invariance by a random reconstruction of the masked input of one sensor from another. Masked
124
      reconstruction approaches have also produced noteworthy results in forecasting tasks (Dong et al.,
125
      2023). TST (Zerveas et al., 2020) attempts to reconstruct masked timestamps using transformers,
126   while PatchTST (Nie et al., 2023) aims to predict subseries of masked patches to learn local invariant
127   features. We adopt the mask approach in our DynaCL-M variant but treat it as a feature prediction
128   task rather than reconstruction, similar to the self-distillation methods. Predicting in representation
129   space has been shown to produce versatile representation with good performance in downstream
130   tasks (Assran et al., 2023; Oquab et al., 2023), as well as eliminate irrelevant data-level details from
131   the target representation.

      3     P ROPOSED ARCHITECTURE : DYNACL
134
135
      Our main objective is to learn useful representation from instances of time series data. We assume
136   similarity within nearby instances – that consecutive instances in a sequence have the same class
137   and event labels would not change too often. This condition often holds for time series, which
138   have repeated labels in the temporal dimension. DynaCL learns a mapping function fθ : x → z,
139   such that given a time series sequence with length T , x = {x1 , x2 , . . . , xT }, where xi ∈ R1×D ,
140   projects this series to a representation space z = {z1 , z2 , . . . , zT }, where zi ∈ R1×F where T is
141   the sequence length, D is in the input dimension and F is the dimension of the learned embeddings.
142   To that end, we proposed DynaCL and DynaCL-M (Figure 1). To learn from a training sequence x
143   of TS instances in DynaCL, we select an anchor (a single instance), then use adjacent instances as
144
      positives and every other sample in the sequence as negatives in the MP-Xent loss. The MP-Xent
      loss, described more fully in Section 3.1, encourages representations of positive pairs to be similar,
145
      and representations of negative pairs to be dissimilar.
146
147   For the expanded DynaCL-M model, the architecture consists of an encoder, Eθ (.), which computes
148   the representation z from the masked input xm , and a linear projector Pϕ (.) that projects the original
149   unmasked input x to a target representation z̄ to serve as targets in the feature prediction MSE
150
      objective. For the MP-Xent loss, we reused the learned representation z, along with the margin, to
      ensure that the learned representations are pushed further apart.

189   3.2   DYNAMIC CONTRASTIVE LEARNING WITH MARGIN (DYNACL-M)
190
191   In this section, we introduce a variant called DynaCL-M. DynaCL-M contains two augmentations
192   to DynaCL. First, we add feature prediction. Second, we add a dynamic margin to further separate
193   dissimilar but adjacent time steps. We will use comparisons between DynaCL and DynaCL-M
194   to demonstrate the lack of correlation between clustering metrics and downstream effectiveness in
195   Section 4. In that same section, we will perform an ablation study to illustrate the impact of each
196
      augmentation.
197   Feature prediction has been shown to learn invariant representations by guiding the model to focus
198   on relevant underlying patterns rather than high-level details (Assran et al., 2023; Oquab et al.,
199   2023). To enforce feature invariance in our learned representation for highly dynamic datasets, we
200   extend DynaCL by introducing feature prediction into our MP-Xent objective.
201   We mask our input x to give xm , then compute the representation z from the masked input xm .
202   Additionally, we project the unmasked x to a target vector, z̄ = Pϕ (x), where Pϕ is a randomly-
203   chosen projection operator into F dimensions, making z̄ the same size as z. We encourage the
204   encoding of the masked xm to be close to the projection of the unmasked x using the LM SE loss
205   for mask feature prediction.

296   4   E XPERIMENTS
297
298   In this section, we evaluate the performance of our proposed method on three benchmark datasets
299   to assess the quality of the learned embeddings. We compare our approach against state-of-the-art
300   baselines for time series representation learning on clustering quality and event classification using
301   linear fine-tuning with a frozen backbone. Additionally, we qualitatively compare learned embed-
302   dings alongside those from previous methods using t-SNE plots in Figure 3. These experiments
303   demonstrate that DynaCL-M builds more compact and separate clusters than other methods, but that
304   vanilla DynaCL outperforms other approaches in building semantically meaningful representations.
305
      We perform an ablation study to highlight the effects of the different components of our DynaCL
      models. Lastly, though it is not a focus of our work, we show that the simplicity of DynaCL allows
306
      it to be trained the fastest of all tested methods.

345   We compare our model with five state-of-the-art approaches in time series representation learning:
346   InfoTS (Luo et al., 2023), CPC (Oord et al., 2018), TNC (Tonekaboni et al., 2021), TS2Vec (Yue
347   et al., 2022) and CoST (Woo et al., 2022). InfoTS maximizes agreement between representations
348   of the same subseries through temporal augmentations. CPC extracts useful representations by
349   predicting future latent representations in a sequence. TNC learns by contrasting data points within
350   the same neighborhood against those from different neighborhoods. TS2Vec captures both global
351
      and temporal dependencies by contrasting time series across different scales and timesteps, while
      CoST employs a two-step approach to TS forecasting by learning disentangled seasonal trends.
352
      To ensure a fair comparison, all models were trained using the same preprocessing pipeline and
353
      hyperparameters. Specifically, we employed the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of 1e−3 and
354   a batch size of 8. Additionally, to eliminate any performance differences arising from variations in
355   model architecture, we use the same encoder network across all baselines. We aim to compare the
356   learning frameworks independent of the choice of encoder. To this end, we selected a simple CNN
357   architecture to assess how effectively each framework can leverage the limited capacity of a basic
358   encoder to learn meaningful representations. Consequently, we substituted the dilated CNN unique
359   to the TS2Vec encoder with a regular 1D CNN. All experiments were conducted on an NVIDIA
360   Tesla V100 GPU (refer to Appendix B for more details on each baseline and implementation).

      4.1   C LUSTERABILITY
364
365
366   Even though our final objective is to learn useful embeddings for downstream tasks, we echo the
367   evaluation in Tonekaboni et al. (2021) by checking the properties of the distribution of the repre-
368   sentations in the encoding space. Bengio et al. (2014) posit that the formation of natural clustering
369
      is one of the properties of a good representation. To capture the performance of each baseline on
      clustering, we use two popular clustering evaluation metrics, namely Davies-Bouldin Index (DBI)
370
      (Davies & Bouldin, 1979) and Silhouette Score (SS) (Rousseeuw, 1987). DBI measures the average
371
      similarity ratio of each cluster with its most similar cluster. A lower DBI score indicates better sep-
372   aration between clusters. SS evaluates how similar an object is to its own cluster compared to other
373   clusters. SS values range from -1 to 1, with higher values reflecting both compactness and separa-
374   tion. Table 2 shows the result of our approach against the baseline methods on these unsupervised
375   clustering measures. Overall, our proposed DynaCL-M outperforms all four baselines in two out
376   of the three datasets, and performs competitively on the third, the highly imbalanced E CG dataset.
377   While strong cluster scores are not our ultimate goal, DynaCL-M performs well in the metrics used
      in other papers in this field.

523   5     C ONCLUSION
524
525   In this work, we present DynaCL, a method for unsupervised representation learning of time series
526   data. The DynaCL method demonstrates the ability to learn semantically meaningful representations
527   off the shelf and outperforms previous time series representation learning methods in downstream
528   linear evaluation. Additionally, we show that including margin in our MP-Xent objective and jointly
529   optimizing with MSE loss is particularly effective in producing clusters with top scores on the DBI
530   and SS clustering metrics. Our findings, however, indicate that achieving high scores on unsuper-
531
      vised clustering metrics does not necessarily imply that the learned embeddings are meaningful or
      effective in downstream tasks. Finally, we studied the contribution of individual components of Dy-
532
      naCL and DynaCL-M. We concluded that our best-performing model for downstream tasks is the
533
      vanilla DynaCL without the MSE loss and margin, which proves that with a simple positive sam-
534   pling strategy of selecting adjacent time steps as positive in an NT tuple loss, Our model competes
535   with previous approaches that rely on statistical methods and prediction sampling, where window
536   sizes are selected based on prior knowledge (Tonekaboni et al., 2021; Oord et al., 2018) as well as
537   the use of temporal augmentations (Luo et al., 2023; Yue et al., 2022). Also, DynaCL model not
538   only delivers exceptional performance in downstream classification tasks but also exhibits the short-
539   est training time (Figure 5). This highlights the efficiency of the multiple positive sampling strategy
      in our MP-Xent contrastive objective.

      A    F ULL D ESCRIPTION OF M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
703
704
      The encoder model is designed to extract features from time-series data using 1D convolutional
705
      layers. It reduces the dimensionality of the input while retaining meaningful temporal information.
706
      The model consists of three 1D convolutional layers, each followed by batch normalization and
707   ReLU activation to introduce non-linearity and stabilize the training process.
708
709   The input to the model is a 3D tensor of shape (batch size, sequence length, input dimension), where
710
      the batch size is 8 for all datasets while the sequence length and dimension are dependent on the
      dataset. After passing through the three convolutional layers, the output dimensionality is reduced
711
      to embedding dimension = 32. Each convolutional layer applies a kernel size of 1 to focus on
712
      individual time steps, progressively reducing the number of channels from the input dimension to
713   128, 64, and 32. We apply batch normalization after each convolution to stabilize the activations, and
714   ReLU activation functions introduce non-linearity, ensuring only positive values are passed through.
715   The output tensor is reshaped back to the original order, returning a feature representation of shape
716   (batch size, sequence length, embedding dimension). This architecture efficiently captures temporal
717   dependencies while reducing the dimensionality, making it suitable for downstream tasks such as
718   the classification and prediction of time-series data.
