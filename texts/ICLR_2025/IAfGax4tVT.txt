011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Modeling the evolution of high-dimensional systems from limited snapshot obser-
014            vations at irregular time points poses a significant challenge in quantitative biology
015            and related fields. Traditional approaches often rely on dimensionality reduction
016            techniques, which can oversimplify the dynamics and fail to capture critical tran-
017
               sient behaviors in non-equilibrium systems. We present Multi-Marginal Stochas-
               tic Flow Matching (MMSFM), a novel extension of simulation-free score and
018
               flow matching methods to the multi-marginal setting, enabling the alignment of
019
               high-dimensional data measured at non-equidistant time points without reducing
020            dimensionality. The use of measure-valued splines enhances robustness to irregu-
021            lar snapshot timing, and score matching prevents overfitting in high-dimensional
022            spaces. We validate our framework on several synthetic and benchmark datasets
023            and apply it to single-cell perturbation data from melanoma cell lines and gene
024            expression data collected at uneven time points.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Understanding cellular responses to perturbations is a fundamental challenge in quantitative biology,
030   with significant implications for fields such as developmental biology, cancer research, and drug
031   discovery (Altschuler & Wu, 2010; Saeys et al., 2016). Modeling these responses requires capturing
032   complex stochastic dynamics in high-dimensional cellular states that evolve over time under the
033   influence of both deterministic and random factors. Developing generative models that accurately
034   represent these dynamics is crucial for simulating cellular behavior and predicting responses to new
035   perturbations. A common approach to modeling such systems is through stochastic differential
036
      equations (SDEs), particularly the Langevin equation as an Itô SDE (Gardiner, 1985; Risken, 1996);
      the evolution of the cellular state X(t) ∈ Rd can be described by
037
038                                   dX(t) = ut (X(t)) dt + g(t) dW (t),                               (1)
039
040   where ut (x) is the drift term representing deterministic dynamics, g(t) is the diffusion coefficient
041   capturing stochastic fluctuations, and W (t) is a Wiener process modeling random noise. At the
042
      population level, the corresponding probability density function p(x, t) evolves according to the
      Fokker-Planck equation (Risken, 1996):
043
044                             ∂pt (x)                          g 2 (t)
045                                     = −∇ · (pt (x) ut (x)) +         ∆pt (x),                       (2)
                                  ∂t                                2
046
047   where pt (x) is shorthand for p(x, t), ∇· denotes the divergence operator, and ∆ is the Laplacian
048   operator.
049   In practice we only observe the system through snapshot measurements at discrete, possibly ir-
050   regular time points t0 < t1 < · · · < tM , providing samples from the marginal distributions
051   ρi = pti (x) (Schofield et al., 2023). Therefore, we lack trajectory data that would reveal how
052   individual states evolve between these snapshots due to the destructive nature of single-cell mea-
053   surements. This raises a fundamental question: among the infinitely many stochastic processes that
      could connect these observed marginals (Weinreb et al., 2018), which one is the most likely?

055
056   To address this problem, we turn to the theory of Optimal Transport (OT) (Villani, 2009) which seeks
057   the most efficient way to transform one probability distribution into another. In the simplest case of
058   two marginals ρ0 and ρ1 , OT aims to find a transport map T that minimizes the cost functional:

063   a linear programming problem over the set of joint probability distributions, leading to the definition
064   of the Wasserstein-2 distance:

068   where Π(ρ0 , ρ1 ) is the set of joint distributions with marginals ρ0 and ρ1 . While OT provides a
069
      deterministic model based on the principle of least action—finding the shortest path or geodesic in
      the space of probability distributions—it does not account for the inherent stochasticity of biological
070
      systems (Horowitz & Gingrich, 2020). Cells are subject to both extrinsic noise, such as variations in
071
      initial conditions and environmental inputs (Hilfinger & Paulsson, 2011), and intrinsic noise arising
072   from the thermodynamic uncertainty in biochemical reactions (Mitchell & Hoffmann, 2018).
073
074   To incorporate stochasticity and identify the most likely stochastic process connecting the ob-
075
      served marginals, we consider the entropic-regularized optimal transport problem, a particular case
      of the Schrödinger Bridge Problem (SBP) (Schrödinger, 1931; Léonard, 2014). The SBP seeks
076
      the stochastic process that minimally deviates from a prior—typically a Brownian motion—while
077
      matching the observed marginals. It can be considered a general statistical inference and model
078   improvement methodology in which one updates the probability of a hypothesis based on the most
079   recent observations while making the fewest possible assumptions beyond the available informa-
080   tion (Pavon et al., 2021). This approach aligns with Occam’s razor principle and aims to find the
081   simplest stochastic process that explains the data with minimal adjustment to our prior belief.
082
083   Extension to Multiple Marginals: Extending this rationale to the multi-marginal (MMOT) case
084   with arbitrary time points t0 , t1 , . . . , tM , we pose the same question: among all possible stochastic
085   processes that could connect the observed marginal distributions {ρi }M       i=0 , which one is the most
086   probable given our prior knowledge? This leads us to formulate the problem as finding the drift
087   ut (x) that minimizes the cumulative transport cost and provides the smoothest and most efficient
088
      flow connecting the observed distributions over time, while ensuring robustness against overfitting.
      In summary, we require:
089
090
             • Robustness Against Overfitting: By minimizing the total transport cost across all time inter-
091            vals, we introduce only essential adjustments to match the observed marginals, preventing
092            the model from overfitting to limited observations and ensuring that the inferred dynamics
093            generalize well beyond the training data.
094
             • Insensitivity to the Timing of Snapshots: The formulation inherently accommodates arbi-
095            trary and irregular time points ti , making it robust to the choice of measurement times. By
096            focusing on the minimal action path that passes through the observed marginals, we capture
097            the system’s evolution without being constrained by the timing of data collection.
098
             • Scalability in High Dimensions: While directly solving high-dimensional transport prob-
099            lems is computationally challenging (Benamou & Brenier, 2000; Peyré & Cuturi, 2019),
100            our approach efficiently approximates the solution. By leveraging advances in simulation-
101            free score and flow matching methods, we model the high-dimensional stochastic process
102            directly in the ambient space, avoiding dimensionality reduction that could obscure impor-
103            tant dynamical features.

105   1.2   L ITERATURE R EVIEW
106
107   Direct learning of the high-dimensional partial differential equation (2) is computationally pro-
      hibitive due to the complexity of integration and divergence computations in high-dimensional


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      spaces (Benamou & Brenier, 2000; Peyré & Cuturi, 2019). Hence, current approaches typically
109   consider reduced-dimensional data representations with gradient-based drifts originating from de-
110   velopmental biology (Weinreb et al., 2018; Schiebinger et al., 2019) where the focus is primarily
111   on slow time scales and the assumption of low-dimensional manifold dynamics is often useful.
112   In this context, dimensionality reduction tools such as t-SNE (Van der Maaten & Hinton, 2008),
113   UMAP (McInnes et al., 2018), and PHATE (Moon et al., 2019) are extensively used to simplify
114   the modeling. However, these techniques can obscure critical faster-scale dynamical information,
115   introduce artifacts (Kiselev et al., 2019), and result in the loss of important biological information
116   in the reduced, folded space.
117   Neural Ordinary Differential Equations (Neural ODEs) have emerged as a powerful tool for model-
118   ing continuous-time dynamics and connecting probability measures over time (Chen et al., 2018a).
119   This approach offers an alternative method by parameterizing the time derivative of the hidden
120   state with a neural network, which is trained to approximate the drift term in the Fokker-Planck
121   equation (2). While this method has been successfully applied (Tong et al., 2020; Huguet et al.,
122   2022) for modeling cellular dynamics and trajectory inference, it still operates primarily in reduced-
123
      dimensional spaces.
124   Recent multi-marginal approaches have attempted to handle multiple time points simultaneously.
125   Chen et al. (2024) developed a deep multi-marginal momentum Schrödinger bridge approach that,
126   while capable of working in high dimensions, requires expensive flow integration and memory-
127   intensive caching of trajectories during training. Similarly, Albergo et al. (2023) proposed stochastic
128   interpolants for multi-marginal modeling but still relies on ODE/SDE integration and marginal dis-
129
      tributions as supervision signals, which becomes computationally challenging in high dimensions.
      These approaches share common limitations: they either require dimension reduction to handle com-
130
      putational complexity, or they depend on expensive numerical integration and trajectory generation
131
      during training.
132
133   Alternative approaches using generative models attempt to transform a simple distribution to an
134   arbitrary target distribution. Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) (Kingma, 2013) learn an encoder-
135
      decoder pair, q(z | x) and p(x | z), such that the decoder can generate x ∼ ρ1 given samples
      z ∼ ρ0 . Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2014) employ a generator-
136
      discriminator framework, where the generator G(z) produces x = G(z) with x ∼ ρ1 for z ∼ ρ0 .
137
      While successful, these methods are limited by the simplicity of the source distribution ρ0 , often
138   chosen to be uniform or normal for analytical convenience. Moreover, these models represent static
139   transformations with no notion of time and cannot generate intermediate states at arbitrary time
140   points, making them unsuitable for modeling dynamic processes where temporal evolution is crucial.
141
      Although diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song & Ermon, 2019) incorporate a time component
142
      by learning a denoising Markovian reverse process, their notion of “time” corresponds to a noise
143
      schedule rather than physical time. This limitation prevents them from capturing actual temporal
144   dynamics or generating data at arbitrary time points not specified during training.
145
146
147
148
      Our Approach We introduce Multi-Marginal Stochastic Flow Matching (MMSFM) to address
149
      these limitations by adapting recent developments in simulation-free approaches (Lipman et al.,
150   2022; Tong et al., 2023a) to our setting. These methods learn pt directly in the ambient space
151   without dimensionality reduction or explicit simulation. However, their direct application to our
152   multi-marginal setting requires careful adaptation to principles described in Section 1.1 to ensure
153   robust learning and prevent overfitting.
154
      Our key innovation lies in learning continuous spline measures through overlapping windows of
155
      consecutive marginals during training. Specifically, we process overlapping triplets (ρi , ρi+1 , ρi+2 )
156   in a rolling fashion, where we demonstrate in Section 2.3 that a window size of two strikes an optimal
157   balance between enforcing smoothness constraints and computational efficiency. This approach en-
158   ables us to capture local dynamics across uneven time intervals, maintain consistency between over-
159   lapping windows, and generate intermediate states between observed snapshots, effectively creating
160   a ”motion picture” of the system’s evolution. The overlapping nature of these learned flows ensures
161   robustness against the specific choice of measurement times while preserving the high-dimensional
      structure of the data.

239   2.3     L EARNING OVERLAPPING M INI -F LOWS FOR M ULTI -M ARGINAL DATA
240
241   We aim to train an ODE drift network vt (x; θ) and a score network st (x; θ) to learn an overall flow
242   based on the mini-flows on overlapping (k+1)-tuples (ρi , ρi+1 , . . . , ρi+k ) for i = 0, 1, . . . , M −k in
243
      a rolling window fashion. Because transport splines are ultimately just approximations for the true
      MMOT, the rolling windows provide a variation of perturbations in the approximated error from any
244
      single geodesic spline segment estimate. See Figure 2 for a visual representation of the variation of
245
      paths in an interval. Our method handles overlapping trajectories where u◦ti (x) ̸= u◦tj (x) for fixed x
246
      and ti ̸= tj , accommodating the possibility that trajectories may cross over a point at different times
247   in multi-marginal settings. In practice, we train using mini-batches of size b.
248
249   By incorporating stochasticity through score matching, we improve robustness and avoid overfit-
250
      ting in high-dimensional spaces. The score ∇x log pt (x|z) allows the model to capture the inherent
      uncertainty and variability in the data. Using the log derivative trick, we see that the score is equiva-
251
      lent to ∇x pt (x|z)/pt (x|z), indicating that the score nudges predictions towards more likely regions
252
      and thereby implicitly explores the region around the local per-sample flow. This efficiency allows
253   us to remain in the ambient dimension d and sidestep dimensionality reduction strategies which
254   often introduce information loss and additional complexities into the flow dynamics. Moreover,
255   re-projecting the trajectories back into the ambient space introduces undesirable reconstruction arti-
256   facts.

305   2.3.3   W INDOW S IZE k AND S PLINE A LGORITHM
306
      We choose our window size k = 2 based on the properties of our chosen Euclidean spline algorithm
307   and considerations to the running time. We opt to use monotonic cubic Hermite splines instead of
308   natural cubic splines for four main reasons. First, the guaranteed monotonicity of each piecewise
309   cubic polynomial ensures no overshoot, thus removing overshooting from the conditional ODE flow
310   regression target described in Section 2.3.2. Second, monotonic cubic Hermite splines by construc-
311   tion do not necessarily have a continuous second derivative. While this is a desired property for
312   smoother curves (and in fact enforced for natural cubic splines), this condition can restrict the curve
313   from taking a more direct path such as from a linear piecewise interpolation. Third, while using
314   a larger window can potentially fit a spline closer to the linear piecewise interpolation, allowing
315
      for smaller windows can better capture a wider variation of paths, increasing the robustness of the
      learned flow. Moreover, 3 control points are sufficient to learn a curvature at the interior control
316
      point. Fourth, the specific coefficients describing each piecewise cubic polynomial are efficient to
317
      compute, scaling linearly in O(k) with the k + 1 points to interpolate. For a window size k and M
318   timepoints, the overlapping window routine computes (M − k)k splines resulting in a total com-
319   plexity of O((M − k)k). This is “maximized” when k = M/2 for a complexity of O(M 2 ), and
320   “minimized” when k = 1 or k = M − 1 for a complexity of O(M ). As an added bonus, mono-
321   tonic cubic Hermite splines are highly insensitive to control points that are not immediate neighbors.
322   Thus, choosing a larger window size k > 2 does not meaningfully increase the amount of informa-
323   tion captured by the spline. We include a more in-depth discussion of splines in Appendix A.1.

360   3.1    E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
361
362   We applied our rolling window framework to three synthetic datasets, one single-cell dataset from
363   COLO858 melanoma cells, and two RNA gene expression datasets. From our framework we use
364   the k = 1 (Pairwise, equivalent to SF2M (Tong et al., 2023a)) and k = 2 (Triplet) mini-flow
365   settings. We approximate the MMOT plan with transport splines computed on mini-batch OT given
366
      the smaller computation cost and asymptotic convergence properties (Fatras et al., 2019; 2021). We
      additionally use MIOFlow (Huguet et al., 2022) on the synthetic datasets and COLO858 to examine
367
      the difference in performance for ambient-space and latent-space models. The initial conditions
368
      for the generated trajectories are from a held-out set of samples from the source distribution ρ0 .
369   Evaluations are computed by leaving out a timepoint marginal during training and calculating the
370   Wasserstein metrics W1 and W22 (using Euclidean distance as the cost function), the maximum
371   mean discrepancy with a mixture kernel (MMD(M)), and the maximum mean discrepancy using a
372   Gaussian kernel (MMD(G)) at the left-out timepoint.
373
374   Synthetic Data: Our three synthetic datasets are the S-shaped Gaussians, the α-shaped Gaussians,
375   and the DynGen synthetic scRNA dataset (Cannoodt et al., 2021). The S and α-shaped Gaussians
376   both consist of 7 marginal distributions in R2 . We select these two datasets because S-shaped Gaus-
377   sians involve learning a flow with changing curvature, and the α shaped Gaussians have a cross-
      over point for some x where the flow uti (x) ̸= utj (x) and i ̸= j. We evaluate both datasets

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      on three different timepoint labels: equidistant timepoints T1 = (0, 0.17, 0.33, 0.5, 0.67, 0.83, 1),
379   arbitrary timepoints T2 = (0, 0.08, 0.38, 0.42, 0.54, 0.85, 1), and a second set of arbitrary time-
380   points T3 = (0, 0.2, 0.27, 0.3, 0.88, 0.98, 1) with neighboring small and large intervals. The Dyn-
381   Gen dataset has 5 marginal distributions on equidistant timepoints T = (0, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1), and
382   introduces a bifurcating flow.
383
384   Real Data: We also apply our method to three real datasets. The COLO858 dataset contains
385   single-cell snapshot data from the AP-1 transcription factor network in COLO858 melanoma cells.
386   The AP-1 network integrates signals from the upstream MAPK pathway, linking signal transduction
387   to transcription and driving cellular plasticity, epigenetic reprogramming, and resistance to MAPK
388   inhibitors in melanoma (Kong et al., 2017; Johannessen et al., 2013; Shah et al., 2010; Maurus

      dataset consists of measurements of 15 AP-1 transcription factors from the FOS, JUN, and ATF

      in hours following BRAF/MEK inhibitor treatment (Comandante-Lou et al., 2022), which we

      normalize to T = (0, 0.004, 0.017, 0.05, 0.125, 0.2, 0.6, 1).
393
394   To visualize the dynamics, we employ a GAE with a Gaussian kernel (Huguet et al., 2022),
395   embedding the 15-dimensional data into a two-dimensional latent space. Unlike methods such as
396   t-SNE or UMAP, the GAE provides a consistent representation, allowing new data to be embedded
397   into a shared coordinate system. We plot snapshots of the inferred trajectories at various timepoints
398   in Figure 1.

476   The bifurcating flow of Dyngen posed a challenge for our models, as they outperformed MIOFlow
477   on the metrics, but struggled to handle the bifurcating trajectories. We suspect this behavior to stem
478   from mini-batch OT because it does not enforce a consistency constraint on the sampling process,
479   resulting in cases where particles are able to jump between separate branches of the bifurcated flow.
480   We did not explore methods to mitigate this problem and believe this to be an avenue for future
481   work.
482   The COLO858 trajectories again show our Triplet method performs the best, scoring better than
483   the MIOFlow trajectories and significantly outperforming the Pairwise method. The overlapping
484   mini-flows of the Triplet model greatly stabilize the overall flow in the individual intervals. In addi-
485   tion, remaining in the high-dimensional ambient space without aggressive dimensionality reduction
      preserves important biological information, leading to more biologically plausible trajectories. The


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      ability to generate samples at arbitrary timepoints allows us to explore the system’s behavior beyond
487   the observed data, potentially identifying critical time windows where intervention might be most
488   effective. This has implications for understanding drug resistance mechanisms and designing more
489   effective therapeutic strategies.
490
      In all instances, MIOFlow generated idiosyncratic trajectories which matched the marginals at the
491
      specified timepoints but performed poorly between those timepoints. We believe this to be the case
492
      because MIOFlow operates in the embedding space generated by a GAE. This structure works very
493   well for trajectories in the embedding space but poses a problem when reconstructing the trajectories
494   in the ambient space. The GAE is only trained on the data marginals {ρi }M                       M
                                                                                    i=0 at times {ti }i=0 ,
495   which means that data points not specified in the data are effectively out-of-distribution w.r.t. the
496   GAE. These out-of-distribution points arise naturally from generating trajectories which spend time
497   traveling between the data distributions. In addition, we notice that all the reconstructed points
498   exhibit high bias and low variance, tending to be bunched very close to each other. This quality
499   perhaps captures the first moment well but not any higher moments.
500   We validate our Triplet model’s ability to learn flows on high dimensional, noisy data, taken at ir-
501   regular timepoints by comparing results from the Pairwise and Triplet models on the CITEseq and
502   Multiome datasets. These two datasets contain high dimensional samples with noise inherent to bio-
503   logical measurements and measurements from non-uniform time intervals. We see the Triplet model
504   successfully outperform the Pairwise model on inferring the distribution at the held out timepoint
505   even in these conditions.
506   Finally, we examine performance of the Pairwise and Triplet models on the S and α-shaped datasets
507   using the highly unbalanced timepoints T3 . Here, we find that the Triplet model greatly outperforms
508   the Pairwise model on the overall learned flow for the S-shaped dataset, but seemingly vastly un-
509   derperforms for the α-shaped dataset. By taking a closer look at the visualizations of the learned
510   flow, we can see that in both cases, the short-long-short interval pattern poses a significant difficulty,
511   suggesting that arbitrary timepoints do indeed increase the difficulty of the learning task. In the S-
512   shaped case, the Pairwise model completely fails to learn this trajectory, whereas the Triplet model
513
      does better, learning to speed up and then slow down between t2 = 0.27 to t3 = 0.3, and t3 = 0.3
      to t4 = 0.88. Unfortunately, neither model was able to converge in the α-shaped case.

516   4    C ONCLUSION
517
518   We present a novel framework for aligning high-dimensional single-cell data in a multi-marginal set-
519   ting with non-equidistant timepoints, while remaining in the high-dimensional space and avoiding
520   the pitfalls of dimensionality reduction. By expanding the literature of Conditional Flow Matching,
521   we have developed a method that learns flows for overlapping triplets, enhancing robustness and
522   stability in multi-marginal settings. Our application to the COLO858 melanoma single-cell dataset
523   demonstrates the method’s effectiveness in capturing complex cellular dynamics while avoiding the
524   need to simulate differential equations during training. We further validate our method’s scalabil-
525
      ity and ability to learn in high dimensional spaces using the CITEseq and Multiome datasets. The
      incorporation of stochasticity through score matching improves robustness and avoids overfitting,
526
      enabling the model to generalize to new conditions. This work opens new avenues for generative
527
      algorithms as well as modeling cellular responses to perturbations, providing a computationally effi-
528   cient and biologically accurate framework capable of handling the complexities of high-dimensional,
529   stochastic biological systems.

717   A.1    NATURAL C UBIC S PLINES
718
      Natural cubic splines solve for the above coefficients ai , bi , ci , di by applying four conditions.
719
      The first requires the spline to interpolate the data points (ti , xi ) such that S(ti ) = xi result-
720
      ing in n + 1 constraints. The second requires S to be continuous at the interior points such that
721   Si (ti ) = Si+1 (ti ), resulting in n − 1 constraints. The third and fourth conditions respectively re-
722   quire S ′ and S ′′ to be continuous for a total of 2n − 2 constraints. Finally two boundary conditions
723   are added such that S ′′ (t0 ) = S ′′ (tn ) = 0. In total, we have constructed a system of equations with
724   4n unknowns and 4n constraints. Ultimately, this setup constructs a tridiagonal system of equa-
725   tions which is efficiently solvable in O(n) time using a single forward and backward pass. Perhaps
726   reasonably, natural cubic splines are quite local as the influence of neighboring intervals greatly
727   decreases the further away the neighbor is.
728

730
731   Cubic Hermite splines approach the problem differently. Consider a single time interval [0, 1] and
732
      corresponding points x0 , x1 . Let the position of x at time t be given by the following cubic polyno-
      mial:

735   Likewise, let mt be the velocity of xt at time t, given by

787
788   B.1     T RAINING S ETUP
789
790   For all experiments, we used a MLP with an input layer, two hidden layers, an output layer, along
791   with SELU activation functions. All networks were optimized using AdamW. We set σ = 0.15 for
792   our method and likewise as the noise scale in MIOFlow.
793   For the S-shaped, α-shaped, DynGen, and COLO858 datasets, we trained for 2500 gradient steps
794   and a learning rate of 1e-4. For the CITEseq and Multiome datasets, we trained for 1000 gradient
795   steps and a learning rate of 1e-5.
796
      MIOFlow is a method to infer “optimal” trajectories on manifolds which correspond to geodesics.
797   As we do not have access to the underlying manifold itself, the authors propose learning it from data
798   using a GAE such that the encoder ϕ is a mapping from the ambient space to the manifold. More
799   specifically, the encoder learns an embedding such that the Euclidean distance of two embedded
800   points ∥ϕ(x) − ϕ(y)∥ matches some geodesic distance G(x, y) based on a diffusion affinity matrix.
801
      Additionally, MIOFlow requires training a GAE to embed high-dimensional data into a lower-
802
      dimensional space and to then reconstruct trajectories learned in the embedded space. We define
803   the encoder as a MLP with three hidden layers of sizes 128, 64, and 32. This encoder outputs an
804   embedding into R2 . The decoder has the same architecture but in reverse. We use ReLU as the
805   activation function. The GAE is trained for 1000 gradient steps using the AdamW optimizer.

825   B.2     DYNGEN
826
827   We repurpose the DynGen data used in MIOFlow (Huguet et al., 2022) for our experiments. No-
828   tably, the data itself is not the raw simulated reads; it is preprocessed into 5 dimensions using
829   PHATE (Moon et al., 2019).
830   PHATE operates as a dimensionality reduction scheme aiming to preserve both local and global
831   dependency structures. Local structure is learned first by imposing Pairwise affinities under a Gaus-
832   sian kernel. Global structure is inferred by propagating the local affinities via diffusion, effectively
833   learning a statistical manifold based on the information geometry. Finally, metric MDS is used as
834   the dimensionality reduction strategy.
835
      We believe that the GAE used in MIOFlow learns the data manifold for the (PHATE-transformed)
836   DynGen dataset especially well given that, by construction, the DynGen dataset does indeed reside
837   on a manifold equipped with a diffusion-based metric. This matches the prior belief in MIOFlow
838   that diffusion-based affinities can accurately capture the data manifold.
