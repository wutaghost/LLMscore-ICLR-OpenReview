009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Long-context language models (LCLM), characterized by their extensive con-
012            text window, is becoming increasingly popular. Meanwhile, many long-context
013            benchmarks present challenging tasks that even the most advanced LCLMs strug-
014            gle to complete. However, the underlying sources of various challenging long-
015            context tasks have seldom been studied. To bridge this gap, we conduct experi-
016
               ments to indicate their difficulty stems primarily from two basic issues: “multi-
               matching retrieval,” which requires the simultaneous retrieval of multiple items,
017
               and “logic-based retrieval,” which necessitates logical judgment within retrieval
018
               criteria. These two problems, while seemingly straightforward, actually exceed
019            the capabilities of LCLMs because they are proven to be hyper-multi-step (de-
020            manding numerous steps to solve) in nature. This finding could explain why
021            LLMs struggle with more advanced long-context tasks, providing a more accu-
022            rate perspective for rethinking solutions for them.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      In the past year, long-context language models (LCLMs) such as GPT-4o-128k (OpenAI, 2023)
      and Gemini-1.5-1000k (Team et al., 2023) have surged in popularity, raising questions about their
028
      efficacy in handling extended context tasks. While various LCLMs have demonstrated excellent
029
      long-context retrieval ability by passing the “Needle in a Haystack” test (gkamradt, 2023) in over
030   100k context length, benchmarks like Loogle (Li et al., 2023) and Loong (Wang et al., 2024b) have
031   highlighted their shortcomings in more complex tasks.
032
033
      To better emulate real-world challenges, recent long-context benchmarks predominantly aim to en-
      hance the difficulty of long-context tasks by requiring real-life scenarios understanding and multi-
034
      step processes or increasing the volume of information that needs to be aggregated. This lead these
035
      tasks to usually engage multiple capabilities of LLMs, such as world knowledge, advanced rea-
036   soning skills and retrieval capability. Consequently, poor model performances are often vaguely
037   attributed to inadequate understanding, reasoning and retrieval capabilities in long context, making
038   it challenging to identify the specific factors that constitute the primary difficulty.
039
      To reveal the real source of challenges in long context tasks, we conduct a detailed analysis (detailed
040
      in Appendix C.2 and C.3) of challenging tasks from previous long-context benchmarks, and as
041
      expected, we identify 2 common factors that make them difficult: multi-matching retrieval and
042   logic-based retrieval. Multi-matching retrieval involves recalling multiple items simultaneously,
043   and logic-based retrieval involves logical judgment within retrieval criteria. Although they are both
044   “basic” retrieval problems having a straightforward form and cannot be explicitly decomposed into
045   multiple steps using Chain-of-Thought (Wei et al., 2022) (in Appendix C.1, we use examples to
046   detail their differences from those traditional multi-step tasks which can be decomposed by CoT,
047   hence called “formally multi-step”), our experiments, as shown in Figure 1, demonstrate they are
048   much harder for current LCLMs as the context length grows, compared to direct retrieval or formally
049   multi-step retrieval.
050   Rather than merely focusing on the superficial phenomena, we endeavor to elucidate why these
051   ostensibly simple issues present substantial challenges for LLMs. Through more in-depth exper-
052   iments, we demonstrate that they are “hyper-multi-step” in nature, which are quite distinct from
053   normal retrieval problems. “Hyper-multi-step”, the truth behind difficult long-context tasks, refers
      to a problem that appears indivisible in form but actually requires numerous independent steps,

055   capacity of LLMs to process simultaneously. To date, none of the techniques such as Retrieval-

057   addressed such problems.

097   2.1   L ONG -C ONTEXT L ANGUAGE M ODELS
098
099
      The emergence of long-context language models (LCLMs) aims to enable language models to han-
      dle vast amounts of input information simultaneously. In recent years, closed-source LLMs have
100
      pioneered advancements in long-context modeling, with context windows expanding from 128k to
101
      1000k tokens. Notable models include GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2023), Claude3.5-200k (Anthropic, 2024),
102   and Gemini-1.5-1000k (Team et al., 2023), which are capable of processing significantly longer
103   texts. Concurrently, open-source models such as phi-3.5-mini (Abdin et al., 2024) and Qwen2.5
104   (Team, 2024) leverage advanced RoPE (Su et al., 2021) interpolation techniques like Yarn (Peng
105   et al., 2023) and LongRope (Ding et al., 2024) to achieve a 128k context window. These open-
106   source models are usually extended from a 4k pretraining length through long-context post-training
107   with interpolated RoPE. However, it remains to be seen whether these models can truly achieve
      accurate and efficient handling of lengthy contexts.

      2.2     L ONG -C ONTEXT B ENCHMARKS
109
110   The field of long-context modeling is experiencing rapid advancements, with benchmarks evolving
111   to become more complex and multifaceted. Simple synthetic tasks such as Needle-in-a-Haystack
112   (NIAH) (gkamradt, 2023) assess the retrieval abilities of long-context language models. Earlier
113   benchmarks such as Longbench (Bai et al., 2023), BAMBOO (Dong et al., 2024), and L-eval (An
114   et al., 2023) offer a wide-ranging evaluation of long-context comprehension through diverse task
115   forms, though they typically lack an emphasis on difficulty. More recent benchmarks, including
116   InfiniteBench (Zhang et al., 2024), RULER (Hsieh et al., 2024), LOOGLE (Li et al., 2023), and
117
      LOONG (Wang et al., 2024b), incorporate harder tasks with varying levels of complexity and ad-
      justable context length. Meanwhile, LOFT (Lee et al., 2024) examines whether long-context models
118
      can function as retrieval systems like RAG and SQL. Despite these advancements, few studies have
119
      thoroughly investigated the underlying commonalities of these intricate long-context tasks, leaving
120   a gap in understanding the fundamental reasons behind their challenges.

123   3     MULTI - MATCHING AND LOGIC - BASED RETRIEVAL ARE REALLY HARD
124
125   In this section, we conduct evaluations on current LCLMs to prove that, multi-matching and logic-
126   based retrieval are really hard, nearly unsolvable for current LCLMs in long context. In contrast,
127   normal retrieval or formally multi-step retrieval, proves to be much easier (as illustrated in Appendix
128   B.1 and B.2).

130   3.1     E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS FOR M ODEL E VALUATION
131
132   To prevent data contamination, we create two fully synthetic datasets: Key-Value Pair Retrieval and
133   Student Resume Retrieval. These datasets make it easy for us to make controlled changes to the
134   input context size and problem types.
135   In Key-Value pair retrieval, the context is a JSON-formatted dictionary consisting of N randomly
136   generated Key-Value pairs. The Key is a 10-digit string, and the Value is a positive integer. The
137   question is appended to the context and varies based on the task type. For multi-matching, the
138   model must retrieve all Keys associated with a given Value. For logic-based retrieval, the model
139   needs to identify the Key with the Value within a specified range.
140
      In the Student Resume Retrieval task, all original resumes are fictional and generated by GPT-4o.
141   The context includes N rows, each detailing a fictional college student’s information, such as name,
142   age, graduation school, interests, GPA, and a short self-introduction. In multi-matching problems,
143   the task is to retrieve all students graduating from a specified university. For logic-based retrieval, the
144   problem is to identify the student whose GPA falls within a specified range. All GPAs are rounded
145   to two decimal places and range from 0 to 5. Examples of the prompts are as follows:

      To delve deeper into why LLMs struggle to solve these seemingly simple tasks, we aim to answer the
      following questions: Do these issues fundamentally differ from traditional retrieval at the model’s
275
      internal level? Can these issues be further decomposed into simple solvable components? Can they
276
      be resolved with additional reasoning steps in inference-time?
277
278   Eventually, our findings reveal a consistent pattern: the number of steps required for these prob-
279   lems exceeds the limits of what LLMs can handle. Hence we refer to them as “hyper-multi-step”,
280
      characterized by the accumulation of numerous simple steps, analogous to simultaneously solving
      thousands of elementary mathematics problems.
281
282   Specifically, our findings are as follows, which will be detailed in the following sections:
283
      For logic-based retrieval:
284
285         1. The internal behavior of LLM in logic-based retrieval is more akin to arithmetic tasks (i.e.
286            logical tasks) which necessitate reasoning with multiple steps.
287
            2. While logic-based retrieval can be decompose to 2 components: decide which value is in
288
               the range, and then get the corresponding key, vector retrieval cannot even solve the first
289
               one within one step.
290
291         3. If we allow LLM to take N steps in test-time, it can successfully accomplish logic-based
292
               retrieval.
293
      And for multi-matching retrieval:
294
295         1. The model’s internal behavior in multi-matching retrieval is originally an one-by-one re-
296            trieval process.
297
298
            2. Even when we decompose a multi-matching retrieval problem into n single-item retrievals,
               the difficulty of retrieval continuously increases for items searched later.
299
300         3. Even if LLM is allowed to take N steps in test-time, the accuracy still falls short of perfec-
301            tion.

303   4.1   E XPERIMENT S ETTINGS FOR HIDDEN STATES AND ATTENTION ANALYSIS
304
305   In our experiments, analyzing the internal behavior of the model is the most crucial. We employ
306   linear probing to explore the information encapsulated in hidden states and statistically analyze
307   attention weights to infer the model’s internal mechanisms. Given our earlier observations of similar
308
      performance trends between large and small models, we adopt the lightweight small model, phi-3.5-
      mini (Abdin et al., 2024), which consists of 32 layers and has a hidden size of 3072, for simplicity.
309
310   The dataset used is still Key-Value retrieval, but more simplified: the total number of KVs does not
311   exceed 100, the value range is constrained to 0 to 9, and we do not apply the chat template to allow
312   the model to generate answers directly following the question.
313   In both hidden state and attention analyses, we first identify the anchor token, whose hidden states
314   are used for linear probing and function as the query token in the attention mechanism. In tasks
315   where n is 1, the last token of the question serves as the anchor token. In multi-matching retrieval
316   tasks, n is set to 3, and the 3 gold Keys are appended to the question. The token immediately
317   preceding each of the 3 appended Keys acts as the anchor token, i.e., we conduct experiments on 3
318   anchor tokens separately. We use examples to show where the anchor token is in Appendix A.7.
319   In examining hidden states, we employ linear probing to determine whether the model has success-
320   fully retrieved the correct Key and stored its information in the hidden states output by each layer.
321   The label is the first digit of the gold Key to be retrieved, enabling our linear prober to function as a
322   10-class classifier with a single linear layer. We use 1,600 samples for training and 400 for testing
323   the classifier, and for each layer, we train and test the classifier independently. We train it for 8
      epochs with the learning rate of 10−5 .

      4.2.2   C AN VECTOR RETRIEVAL SOLVE LOGIC - BASED RETRIEVAL ?
379
380   Second, we intuitively decompose a logic-based retrieval problem into 2 components: decide which
381   value is in the range, and get the corresponding key. Since our focus is on retrieval scenarios, we
382   directly employ models designed for RAG (Lewis et al., 2020) to test the feasibility of performing
383   the first step through vector retrieval. (The second step is the same as normal retrieval.)
384   Vector retrieval is one of the most widely retrieval techniques, which encodes both the query and
385   candidates into embedding vectors, then calculates the similarity between the query and each can-
386   didate to retrieve the top similar options. This process is similar to the attention mechanism within
387   LLMs (Xu et al., 2023). Since similarity computation can be conducted in parallel for each candi-
388   date, it can be considered as a single-step process from an external perspective.
389   We test 2 sentence embedding models commonly used for RAG (Lewis et al., 2020), e5-large-
390   multilingual (Wang et al., 2024a) and bge-m3(Zhang et al., 2023), on a numerical value comparing
391   task to see if it can retrieve the correct number from 20 integers. The candidate keys are integers
392   within the range 0 to 30, 100, 1,000, or 10,000, and the queries have 2 types, equality relations and
393   greater&less-than comparison (examples are shown in Appendix A.6). If the embedding vector of
394   the correct number has the highest similarity to that of the query, it is considered correct. As shown
395   in Table 3, they only function properly when retrieval is based on equality relations; however, their
396   performance significantly declines with more advanced logical operations, such as greater-than or
397
      less-than comparisons.
398   This suggests that one-step vector retrieval techniques are inadequate for logic-based retrieval. In
399   other words, a transformer model cannot achieve logic-based retrieval through the attention mecha-
400   nism within a single layer (or a few layers); instead, it requires a more advanced reasoning process.

428   In experiments, we employ 3 types of prompts: (1) standard prompt, the default prompt without
429   CoT; (2) step-by-step prompt, which is the traditional CoT prompt which tells the model to “think
430   step by step, but do not check each item one by one”; (3) one-by-one prompt, which tells the model
431   “You should first examine every item one by one to give the judgement (yes/no) on whether it meet
      the requirement”.

509   4.3.3    WHAT IF INVESTING N TIMES MORE TIME ?
510
511   Third, to demonstrate that the complexity of solving multi-matching is even more than O(N ), we
512
      conducted similar experiments to prompt the model to examine every item one by one. The results
      shown in Table 4 indicate that investing time proportional to N can indeed improve accuracy com-
513
      pared to one-step solutions, but still cannot ensure perfect correctness. However, though we already
514
      prove it is multi-step, we remain uncertain about its exact complexity level, because the current
515   LLMs perform so poorly on multi-matching tasks, that we have not identified an appropriate prompt
516   or an effective reasoning process to let LLM adequately address this issue.
517
518
519   4.4     P OTENTIAL S OLUTIONS FOR HYPER - MULTI - STEP PROBLEMS
520
      As we have found, multi-matching and logic-based retrieval problems are hyper-multi-step, meaning
521
      the number of steps required exceeds the limit that current LLMs can directly handle. Therefore,
522
      relying solely on the LLM itself provide the complete answers is not only inefficient but also very
523   error-prone.
524
525   Nevertheless, similar to how humans tackle such problems, using external tools may provide a viable
526
      solution. For example, if the input context is well-structured (e.g. Markdown tables or JSON data),
      the model can be prompted to write programs and execute them using external interpreters (Chen
527
      et al., 2022) to accurately yield the correct answer, as demonstrated in Appendix A.4. However, for
528
      more complex scenarios, it may still require further works such as designing sophisticated systems
529   consisting of multiple AI Agents (Xi et al., 2023).

532   5     C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this paper, we use a synthetic setting to demonstrate that LCLMs always struggle to solve multi-
535   matching retrieval and logic-based retrieval, which are basic and common factors encompassed by
536   those more advanced long-context tasks. Then, we find their underlying hyper-multi-step nature
537   though more in-depth analysis, proving they must not be simply addressed by LLMs within limited
538   steps. Therefore, we highlight that merely extending the context window size and relying solely on
539   LCLMs to accurately address long-context tasks in various real-world scenarios may be futile. We
      urge more novel perspectives and approaches to further exploit long contexts.

890   B.2   MULTI - STEP RETRIEVAL : A LITTLE HARDER BUT CAN BE SOLVED BY C OT
891
      Multi-step (in form) retrieval tasks, including multi-query, multi-hop, chain-of-retrieval tasks, etc.
892
      (Li et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b), intuitively appear more challenging due to the need to aggregate
893
      dispersed information from the context by multiple steps. Nevertheless, our experiments indicate
894   that they are not necessarily difficult, provided LLMs can decompose them into simpler steps using
895   CoT approach (Wei et al., 2022). We must emphasize that, it is important to distinguish multi-
896   matching from formally multi-step retrieval: the former requires retrieving multiple items with only
897   one query, while the latter also requires retrieving multiple items but can be achieved with multiple
898   different queries.
899
      Previous studies (Wang et al., 2024b; Goldman et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; 2023) typically char-
900   acterize long-context tasks, which require the aggregation of dispersed information for multi-step
901   reasoning, as difficult. While this assessment aligns with intuition, we argue that this classification
902   is not universally applicable. Instead, the complexity of such tasks should be evaluated based on
903   their specific form and composition, because a complex task may not be inherently difficult if a
904   model can systematically reason and decompose the task into manageable steps.
905
      To illustrate this, we constructed a multi-step Key-Value retrieval problem that involves a chain-of-
906   retrieval process. This task requires a model to retrieve n values corresponding to n keys, concate-
907   nate them into a string, and then combine this string with another given string to generate a new key,
908   from which the model must retrieve its value as the final answer (see detailed prompts in Appendix
909   A.1). Although this problem seems complex, requiring aggregating at least n+1 pieces of informa-
910   tion from different parts of the context and performing at least n+1 reasoning steps, it can actually
911   be decomposed into simple steps using a chain-of-thought (CoT) approach (Wei et al., 2022).
912   The results presented in Table 7 reveal that smaller models, such as phi-3.5, perform worse as the
913   number of reasoning steps and pieces of information increase. However, larger models with stronger
914   reasoning capabilities are almost unaffected. This suggests that while long-context problems which
915   must require multi-step may pose challenges for smaller models, they are not unsolvable for larger
916   models with robust reasoning and comprehension skills. In other words, multi-step problems pri-
917   marily test a language model’s comprehension, reasoning, and organizational abilities, rather than
      its retrieval skills or other capabilities specifically related to handling long contexts.

       C.1   D ISTINGUISHING WHAT IS MULTI - MATCHING OR LOGIC - BASED RETRIEVAL
1074
1075   We regard multi-matching and logic-based retrieval as important individual components because of
1076   their distinct natures. It may be difficult to distinguish between multi-matching and multi-query, or
1077   logic-based retrieval and problems requiring logic. Here we use some simple example to illustrate
1078   this. The primary characteristic of multi-matching or logic-based retrieval is the inability to be
1079   further divided into multiple manageable steps using CoT, unless examining each item in the context
       one by one.

       Multi-matching means multiple items meet one retrieval criteria, which can almost no longer be
1081   subdivided into multiple independent conditions. However, as for multi-query, although the retrieval
1082   criteria is also in one sentence, it can be easily subdivided.

1158
1159   Here we show the process that we analyse previous challenging tasks from different benchmarks to
1160   identify which component these tasks involve. We omit simple tasks like single-needle NIAH or
1161   normal multi-document QA.
1162
1163   C.3.1     L OOGLE
1164
1165   Loogle (Li et al., 2023) is a long-context benchmark which first distinguish short-dependency tasks
1166   and long-dependency tasks. Long-dependency means the task needs a large portion of the context
1167   rather than just a short part, which is much more challenging. We analyse the 4 task types belonging
1168
       to long-dependency tasks:
1169
1170
       Multiple information retrieval: This task is quite different from traditional short-term retrieval
       tasks, there are usually multiple and diverse pieces of evidence throughout the entire text for one
1171
       specific answer. This task usually involves multi-matching retrieval, but sometimes can also be
1172
       separable into steps.
1173
1174
       Computation: This task firstly needs multiple information retrieval from a wide range of texts,
1175
       and then use these data for calculating. A majority of the evidence within the text takes the form of
1176   numerical data. However, this task is in fact composed of 3 solvable steps: understand the question to
1177   determine which numeric to retrieve, get the numeric through normal retrieval operation (step 1 and
1178   2 may be perform several times to get multiple numeric), calculate the answer based on the retrieved
1179   data. Thus it does not belong to logical retrieval, but may sometimes involve multi-matching.
1180
1181   Timeline reorder: This task requires reordering the timeline of a set of events presented in a
1182   permuted order. It apparently needs to compare the size of numbers to determine which event should
1183   be retrieved first or later. So it involves logic-based retrieval.
1184
1185   Comprehension and reasoning: This task demands not only a profound comprehension of the
1186   question but also intricate reasoning to discern the underlying implications for searching for the
1187   appropriate evidence. It must be a multi-step problem, but whether this issue involves other compo-
       nents remains uncertain and depends on the specific nature of the problem in question.

1189
1190   We choose 5 difficult tasks from Ruler (Hsieh et al., 2024) to analyse:
1191
1192   Multi-keys NIAH: Multiple “needles” are inserted into the “haystack”, and only one of them
1193   needs to be retrieved. The additional “needles” are hard distractors. This is a normal retrieval task.
1194   Though many hard distractors are inserted, powerful LLMs can usually overcome this.
1195
1196   Multi-values NIAH: Multiple “needles” sharing the same key are inserted into the “haystack”.
1197   All values associated with the same key need to be retrieved. This is totally the same as the multi-
1198   matching retrieval.
1199
1200   Multi-queries NIAH: Multiple “needles” with distinct keys are inserted into the “haystack”. On
1201   the surface, it may appear that a problem requires the retrieval of multiple values. However, since
1202   each value has a distinct key, it can actually be decomposed into multiple simple retrieval tasks.
1203   Therefore, this does not fall under the category of logic-based retrieval or multi-match retrieval.
1204
1205   Multi-hop Tracing: A variable X1 is initialized with a value V, followed by a linear chain of vari-
1206   able name binding statements (e.g., X2 = X1, X3 = X2, ...), which are inserted at various positions
1207   of the input. The objective is to return all variable names pointing to the same value V. This is a
1208   classic chain-of-retrieval task. It can also be decomposed into multiple simple retrieval tasks, e.g.,
1209   first retrieve X1, then use X1 as the query to retrieve X2. Therefore, it is multi-step, but not multi-
1210
       matching. It may be logic-based retrieval if the variable name binding statements involve more
       complex calculations.
1211
1212
1213   Aggregation: This task includes Common Words (CWE) and Frequent Words Extraction (FWE).
1214
       A model needs to return the top-K frequent words in the context. This is a very hard multi-step
       problem consisting of at least 3 steps: identify each word, use each word as the query to do multi-
1215
       matching retrieval and compare the frequency of each word. So it must involve multi-matching.

1226   the issue, thereby determining which specific pieces of information are required. Subsequently, it
1227   must retrieve these multiple pieces of information from the context. This task is also multi-step,
1228   which can be solved by CoT (Wei et al., 2022). However, none of the steps necessitates logic-based
1229   or multi-matching retrieval.
