009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Few-shot image generation (FSIG) using deep generative models (DGMs) presents
012            a significant challenge in accurately estimating the distribution of the target domain
013            with extremely limited samples. Recent work has addressed the problem using
014            a transfer learning approach, i.e. fine-tuning, leveraging a DGM that pre-trained
015            on a large-scale source domain dataset, and then adapting it to the target domain
016            with very limited samples. However, despite various proposed regularization
017            techniques, existing frameworks lack a systematic mechanism to analyze the
018
               degree of overfitting, relying primarily on empirical validation without rigorous
               theoretical grounding. We present Few-Shot Diffusion-regularized Representation
019
               Learning (FS-DRL), an innovative approach designed to minimize the risk of
020
               over-fitting while preserving distribution consistency in target image adaptation.
021            Our method is distinct from conventional methods in two aspects: First, instead of
022            fine-tuning, FS-DRL employs a novel scalable Invariant Guidance Matrix (IGM)
023            during the diffusion process, which acts as a regularizer in the feature space of
024            the model. This IGM is designed to have the same dimensionality as the target
025            images, effectively constraining its capacity and encouraging it to learn a low-
026            dimensional manifold that captures the essential structure of the target domain.
027            Second, our method introduces a controllable parameter called sharing degree,
028            which determines how many target images correspond to each IGM, enabling a
029
               fine-grained balance between overfitting risk and model flexibility, thus providing a
               quantifiable mechanism to analyze and mitigate overfitting. Extensive experiments
030
               demonstrate that our approach effectively mitigates overfitting, enabling efficient
031
               and robust few-shot learning across diverse domains.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   In recent years, Deep Generative Models (DGMs) have achieved remarkable breakthroughs in the
037   generation of high-quality and diverse samples across various domains (Higgins et al., 2016; Karras
038   et al., 2019; Song et al., 2020b; Ruiz et al., 2023). However, reliance on extensive data presents
039   a significant challenge in scenarios where data is scarce (Abdollahzadeh et al., 2023). To address
040   this issue, Few-Shot Image Generation (FSIG) methods (Wang et al., 2018; Zhao et al., 2022) have
041   emerged, aiming to generate diverse images with limited training samples.
042   Most FSIG methods rely on fine-tuning a DGM, typically a generative adversarial network (GAN)
043   (Goodfellow et al., 2014), which pretrained on a larger and “similar” dataset (Ojha et al., 2021; Zhu
044   et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2022; 2023). However, this fine-tuning process, which involves adjusting
045   the generator pθ (z) to minimize the loss in the target domain Y, minθ E(z∼N (0,I),y∼Y) [L(pθ (z), y)],
046   often leads to overfitting, visual artifacts, and catastrophic forgetting (Saito et al., 2017; Radford
047   et al., 2015; Kirkpatrick et al., 2017) when only a few samples are available.
048   More recently, Diffusion Models (DMs) (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020b) have demonstrated
049   remarkable success, surpassing GANs in image generation (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021). Their inherent
050   scalability and more stable training process allow DMs to be trained on larger datasets, resulting
051   in superior generalization capabilities. This makes them particularly adept at tasks that require
052   fine-grained detail manipulation, such as text-to-image translation (Saharia et al., 2022; Ramesh et al.,
053   2021) and intricate image editing (Meng et al., 2021). Given these strengths, it is attractive to consider
      adapting DMs for FSIG, potentially offering superior solutions to existing GAN-dominated methods.

      However, directly applying current FSIG techniques such as regularization (Li et al., 2020; Ojha
071
      et al., 2021) and modulation (Zhao et al., 2022) to DMs proves challenging. The significantly larger
072   number of parameters in DMs and their iterative nature not only fail to address the problems faced
073   by GANs but may exacerbate overfitting and catastrophic forgetting issues (Abdollahzadeh et al.,
074   2023). Consequently, we define the research question as follows: How can we adapt the pre-trained
075   diffusion model to the target domain while minimizing the risk of overfitting?
076
      To address this question, we present Few-Shot Diffusion-Regularized Representation Learning
077
      (FS-DRL), as shown in Fig. 1. Our method consists of three main contributions:
078
079   Firstly, we introduce a novel framework to adapt a pretrained DM to a specific domain. Unlike other
080   approaches that attempt to modify the generator (Wang et al., 2018; Ojha et al., 2021; Zhao et al.,
081
      2023), our method is designed to “influence” the generation process. Specifically, given a target
      domain Y, our method converts the unconditional generation process to a conditional one, and at the
082
      diffusion time t, the objective is thus minθ E(q(yt |y),y∼Y [L(pθ (yt |Y), y)]. We find that introducing a
083
      non-adaptive module, which we call the Invariant Gradient Matrix (IGM), is sufficient to achieve our
084   objective by guiding the generation process.
085
086   Secondly, we theoretically demonstrated that this IGM is essentially equivalent to a “simplest”
087
      classifier in classifier-guided diffusion model (Song et al., 2020b). The weights can be seen as an
      “attention matrix”, which determines the amount of “attention” different regions of the state should
088
      receive for a specific domain. Furthermore, we introduce a Scalable property for IGM, which allows
089
      flexible control over granularity. This scalability impacts the trade-off between generalization and
090   specificity. Defining an IGM for multiple images provides high generalization with low overfitting
091   risk, while a single IGM per image offers high specificity but increases overfitting risk.
092
093
      Thirdly, we propose two optimization techniques that significantly enhance the performance of our
      method in Few-Shot Image Generation (FSIG). The introduction of percentile gradient clipping and
094
      simplified loss function allows our approach to achieve comparable results to state-of-the-art methods,
095
      with particularly notable improvements in mode coverage. Additionally, we conducted experiments
096   on further parameter reduction, exploring the trade-offs between model complexity and performance.
097
098   We summarize the structure of the paper as follows. In Sec. 3.1, we provide a preliminary introduction
099
      to diffusion models and formalize the notion we used in this paper. We then introduce the details of
      our proposed method FS-DRL (Sec. 3.2) with theoretical analysis (Sec. 3.3) and two optimization
100
      strategies (Sec. 3.4. In Sec. 4, we demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed method through
101
      empirical comparisons with the baseline, and a comprehensive component analysis.

163   noised sample yt obtained from y0 is reconstructed and how likely a noised sample xt obtained from
164   x0 is falsely reconstructed. In the context of the FSIG task, the source domain and target domain
165   differ in attribute, we can assume that, initially, the target domain adaptation degree is close to 0,
166   as the model is trained solely on the source domain. To increase the adaptation degree and enable
167   effective generation in the target domain, we apply the conditioning mechanism for diffusion models.
168
      Few-Shot Image Generation can be considered as a fine-grained conditional generating. Specifically,
169   a conditional generative model can be formulated as pt (xt | y), where y is the condition (given
170   samples in FSIG task). Per Bayes’ theorem, pt (xt | y) ∝ pt (xt )pt (y | xt ). Expressing this
171   relationship as a score function, a score-based conditional diffusion model is described as:
172                         ∇xt log pt (xt | y) = ∇xt log pt (xt ) + ∇xt log p(y | xt )                      (5)

175

189   3.3   T HEORETICAL A NALYSIS
190
      We shall now provide the theoretical justification of our proposed method.
191
192   IGM Fundamentals Without loss of generality, let us consider the case at time t. To simplify the
193   notation, we denote c = G(t). According to Eq. 5 and 6, we have c = ∇x log p(y | xt ), solving
194   this differential equation yields p(y | xt ) ∝ exp(c · xt ). This equation defines a pixel-wise linear
195
      regression model followed by a softmax activation function, where each pixel of the intermediate
      state xt is weighted by the corresponding element of the IGM. Intuitively, the IGM functions as an
196
      attention mechanism that determines how much “attention” or “importance” should be assigned to
197
      different regions of xt , conditioned on a specific target domain Y. See Fig. 1 for an IGM visualization
198   and Section C.1 for further explanation and more visual examples of IGM.
199
200   Overfitting Mitigation Strategy From a pixel-wise perspective, if each image of the target domain
201
      is assigned a unique IGM, it may lead to overfitting as the model can memorize the specific pixel.
      However, when an IGM is shared across multiple images, it effectively becomes a linear regression
202
      model fitting multiple data points, promoting better generalization. To balance model expressiveness
203
      and generalization, we introduce the IGM Sharing Degree, γ, representing the number of images
204   that share an IGM. As γ increases from 1, the model shifts from potential overfitting toward better
205   generalization, allowing for fine-tuned performance across diverse datasets. However, excessively
206   high γ values can lead to underfitting. We provide an in-depth analysis of this trade-off in Sec. 4.1.
207
      Theoretical Foundation of Domain Adaptation with IGM We develop a theoretical framework for
208
      domain adaptation in diffusion models, showing how our Invariant Gradient Matrix (IGM) guides the
209
      generative process from source domain to target domains towards the desired distribution.

      3.4   O PTIMIZATION
258
259   In Section 3.3, we theoretically analyzed the feasibility of our proposed method. While leveraging a
260   model trained on a source domain that closely resembles the target domain somewhat reduces the
261   complexity of the task, employing a non-adaptive gradient matrix to generate out-of-distribution
262   images still poses significant challenges. Therefore, in this section, we introduce two optimization
263   strategies to further enhance the performance and generalization capability in the target domain.
264
      Percentile Gradient Clipping The gradient matrix G(t) may contain gradient values gi,j (t) at
265   certain pixels that represent noise or weakly correlated information between the source domain X
266   and the target domain Y. Accordingly, we introduce Percentile Gradient Clipping (PGC) as:
267
                                   ĝi,j (t) = gi,j (t) · (|gi,j (t)| ≥ Q(G(t), ρ))                                  (10)
268
269   where Q(G(t), ρ) represents the ρ-th percentile of the gradient matrix G(t). PGC removes smaller
      gradients that are more likely to represent noise or weak correlations, while retaining stronger gradi-

271

      represents the mutual information between G(t) and Y and H(G(t)) denotes the entropy of G(t),
      quantifying its informational uncertainty. Enhancing this ratio enables G(t) to more effectively
274

275
      generalization in the target domain. For more detail and theoretical analysis see Section C.4.

      to the target domain Y, we can simplify the loss function by emphasizing reconstruction ability:

      In contrast, the MetFaces target domain exhibits a distinct pattern. When γ = 1, generated samples
      closely match the target domain style but lack diversity. As the sharing degree increases to 10, the
356
      generated samples predominantly resemble the source domain, with only slight characteristics of
357
      the target domain (Fig. 4a second row, right). This behavior differs from Babies and Sunglasses,
358   where intermediate sharing degrees yield optimal results. For MetFaces, the significant disparity
359   from the source domain exposes the limitations of IGMs in bridging large domain gaps, resulting
360   in effective target domain capture only at lower sharing degrees (We provide a detailed analysis in
361   Sec. 4.3 and visualization in Section C.1). This finding highlights the importance of selecting an
362   appropriate source domain that shares sufficient similarities with the target domain in FSIG tasks.

482   We present a novel representation learning framework for Few-Shot Image Generation, featuring a
483   tunable parameter to explicitly mitigate overfitting while adapting a specific domain. Our method
484   achieves competitive SOTA performance while surpassing representation learning-based approaches
485   using only half of the parameters. By focusing on the diffusion process, our approach is compatible
      with all diffusion models, offering a versatile and efficient solution for Few-Shot Image Generation.

694   B    L IMITATION AND B ROADER I MPACT
695
696   Limitation Although our method effectively balances specificity and generalization, its perfor-
697   mance degrades when the disparity between the source and target domains is substantial, such as
698   MetFaces (Karras et al., 2020). In such cases, overfitting tends to outperform underfitting (Fig. 3). A
699   potential solution involves incorporating Large Multi-modality Models (LMMs) like CLIP (Radford
700   et al., 2021) to constrain style more effectively, allowing the Invariant Gradient Matrix to preserve
701   more non-style information. We avoided using CLIP to minimize target domain exposure, as LMMs
      may have been trained on these samples. However, if this constraint can be relaxed, integrating


                                                        13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      LMMs could enhance our method’s robustness across diverse domains. Future work will explore this
703   integration while maintaining data privacy.
704
705   Broader Impact Although our method outperforms state-of-the-art (SOTA) approaches in various
706   comparisons, our research is not centered on topping leaderboards but rather on exploring the limits
707   of FSIG while “fundamentally” avoiding overfitting. It is worth noting that while diffusion models
708   have made impressive progress in recent years, surpassing GANs in most fields, they are rarely used
709   in Few-Shot Image Generation (FSIG) tasks. This is primarily because most FSIG methods rely on
710   fine-tuning, and diffusion models, despite being trained on the same datasets, have more parameters,
711   making them seemingly “unsuitable” for FSIG tasks.
712   However, on the one hand, the training data for large models continues to expand rapidly and is
713   becoming crucial in many real-world applications. On the other hand, although large models can
714   generate highly realistic images, they still underperform on most user-defined real-world subjects.
715   This gap requires FSIG methods that can align with the capabilities of these large models. Our
716   method presents a novel attempt toward this goal, showing promising initial progress.

836
      Given a gradient matrix G(t) containing gradient information between the source domain X and
837   the target domain Y, let Q(G(t), ρ) denote the ρ-th percentile of G(t). Define the gradient clipping
838   operation T as follows:
839
                                               0,        if |gi,j (t)| < Q(G(t), p)
840                            T (G(t))i,j =                                                          (28)
841                                            gi,j (t), otherwise
842   where gi,j (t) denotes the (i, j)-th element of G(t). Then, the gradient clipping operation T satisfies
843   the following inequality:
844                                        I(T (G(t)); Y)    I(G(t); Y)
                                                           ≥                                            (29)
845                                          H(T (G(t)))      H(G(t))
846   where I(·; ·) denotes the mutual information and H(·) denotes the entropy. In other words, the
847   gradient clipping operation T increases the ratio of effective information, enabling the clipped
848   gradient matrix T (G(t)) to capture the characteristics of the target domain Y more effectively.
849
      Proof (Informal) The gradient clipping operation T sets the elements of G(t) with smaller mag-
850   nitudes to zero. This is equivalent to removing the gradient information that has a relatively weak
851   influence on the target domain Y. Since elements with smaller magnitudes are assumed to contribute
852   less to mutual information I(G(t); Y), their removal has a limited impact on the overall mutual
853   information between the gradient matrix and the target domain. At the same time, removing this
854   information reduces the entropy H(G(t)) of G(t), since it reduces the overall noise and randomness
855   in the gradient matrix.
856   Specifically, let gi,j (t) denote the (i, j)-th element of G(t). The clipping threshold Q(G(t), ρ) is
857   selected such that elements below this threshold contribute minimally to the mutual information
858   I(G(t); Y). Hence, we have:
859                                         I(T (G(t)); Y) ≈ I(G(t); Y)
860
861
      At the same time, setting these elements to zero reduces the entropy H(G(t)), as the sparsity of
      T (G(t)) increases and the overall uncertainty within the gradient matrix is reduced. This reduction
862
      in entropy is significant, since the clipped elements are removed entirely, resulting in:
863
                                            H(T (G(t))) < H(G(t))


                                                        16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      Therefore, the ratio of mutual information to entropy increases after clipping:
865
866                                        I(T (G(t)); Y)   I(G(t); Y)
867                                                       >
                                            H(T (G(t)))      H(G(t))
868
869
870
      In essence, the gradient clipping operation T preserves the information that is relevant to the target
      domain Y while reducing the entropy of the gradient matrix. This increases the relative effectiveness
871
      of the retained information, allowing T (G(t)) to more effectively capture the characteristics of the
872
      target domain. □
