009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               As services based on diffusion models expand across various domains, preserving
012            the privacy of client data becomes more critical. Fully homomorphic encryption
013            and secure multi-party computation have been employed for privacy-preserving
014            inference, but these methods are computationally expensive and primarily work
015            for linear computations, making them challenging to apply to large diffusion mod-
016            els. While homomorphic encryption has been recently applied to diffusion models,
017            it falls short of fully safeguarding privacy, as inputs used in the ϵ prediction are not
018            encrypted. In this paper, we propose a novel framework for private inference for
019            both inputs and outputs. To ensure robust approximations, we introduce several
020
               techniques for handling non-linear operations. Additionally, to reduce latency, we
               curtail the number of denoising steps while minimizing performance degradation
021
               of conditional generation through score distillation from the unconditional gener-
022
               ation of the original model with full denoising steps. Experimental results show
023            that our model produces high-quality images comparable to the original, and the
024            proposed score distillation significantly enhances performance, compensating for
025            fewer steps and approximation errors.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Modern conditional diffusion models show impressive performance in generating images and are
032   employed across a wide range of applications. Notably, Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022),
033
      one of the leading models, can produce high-quality images from user prompts or visual inputs such
      as sketches and key points. Building on the recent success of diffusion models, there is an increasing
034
      interest in their application in privacy-sensitive fields, such as medical domain and content creation.
035
      In the medical field, they are utilized for translating MRI to CT, denoising medical images, and
036   medical anomaly detections. Additionally, DALL·E (Ramesh et al., 2022) and Midjourney have
037   become popular as commercial tools for content generation and creative industries.
038
039
      Despite these advancements, the substantial computational costs of image generation typically ne-
      cessitate processing on powerful remote servers rather than local devices. This reliance on external
040
      servers introduces potential privacy concerns, as both user inputs and corresponding outputs may be
041
      exposed to the model provider. These concerns are especially critical in the medical domain, where
042   inputs consist of sensitive information such as patient profiles and medical images, and outputs also
043   include critical data such as CT scans in image translation tasks or enhanced images in denoising
044   tasks. Even when inference is performed on client devices, the risk of exposing the model provider’s
045   proprietary model weights remains a critical issue, further complicating the deployment of diffusion
046   models in privacy-sensitive fields.
047
      Faced with the issue, several studies utilize secure computation methods such as fully homomorphic
048   encryption (FHE) (Gentry, 2009) and secure multi-party computation (MPC) (Evans et al., 2018).
049   CryptoNet (Gilad-Bachrach et al., 2016) propose a HE-friendly architecture for convolutional neu-
050   ral networks, using polynomial activations. MPCFORMER (Li et al., 2023) introduces a framework
051   for private inference in transformer models by leveraging secure MPC. However, it is challenging
052   to utilize these approaches for large diffusion models. First, both FHE and MPC require substan-
053   tial overhead since FHE has significant computational costs and secure MPC introduces significant
      communication costs. Additionally, both FHE and MPC only support linear operations, necessitat-

054
      ing the approximation of non-linear functions like GroupNorm (Wu & He, 2018), LayerNorm (Ba,
055   2016), SiLU, GeLU (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016), and softmax.
056
057   Recently, HE-Diffusion (Chen & Yan, 2024) introduces FHE to secure outputs during the denoising
058
      process. At each denoising step, the client transmits both the intermediate representations of the text
      input and the distorted image to the model provider. Then, the model provider predicts the noise,
059
      adds it to the encrypted part, and generates the next-step image. A key advantage of this method
060
      is that noise prediction occurs outside the ciphertext space, avoiding the need to approximate non-
061   linear functions and significantly reducing latency caused by the computations in the ciphertext
062   space. However, this approach still has a risk of privacy leakage: the text inputs remain unencrypted,
063   and Chen et al. (2024) observes that it may be possible to recover the text inputs from the interme-
064   diate embeddings. Therefore, encrypting the text inputs is essential to ensure complete privacy.
065
      To safeguard the privacy of both inputs and outputs, we propose Private Inference for Diffusion
066
      Models (PIDM) that enables image generation from encrypted inputs using approximation methods.
067   Inference in the ciphertext space for models like Stable Diffusion poses challenges, due to the nu-
068   merical instability associated with approximating non-linear operations, such as GroupNorm, SiLU,
069   GeLU, and softmax. To address this, we introduce the techniques that provide more stable approxi-
070   mations for normalization, the error functions, and softmax. Through the experiments, we show that
071   our approach performs better than the existing approximation used in CrypTen (Knott et al., 2021).
072
      Furthermore, to reduce the latency, we propose a novel score distillation sampling approach to guide
073   the conditional denoising process using the unconditional path. The key idea is to leverage compu-
074   tations in plaintext space to minimize those in ciphertext space, thereby reducing latency with only a
075   small performance trade-off. Specifically, our method takes fewer denoising steps for the conditional
076   denoising process and this strategy would result in performance degradation. However, by using the
077   unconditional denoising process, which takes the full number of steps and does not utilize the ap-
078   proximations, as a guiding score, we mitigate this issue. Empirical results show that this approach
079   significantly reduces latency with only a minor decrease in performance.
080   Our contribution is threefold:
081
082          • To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to propose a framework for Stable Diffusion
083            that generates images from encrypted inputs, utilizing several techniques.
084          • We introduce a novel score distillation sampling method from the unconditional denoising
085            process, reducing computations within the ciphertext space by taking more computational
086            effort to the plaintext space.
087          • Empirical results demonstrate that our framework achieves comparable performance to the
088            original Stable Diffusion, while our sampling method reduces the latency with small per-
089            formance trade-offs.

138   2.2    P RIVATE INFERENCE
139
140   Fully homomorphic encryption (FHE) (Gentry, 2009) and secure multi-party computation (MPC)
141   (Evans et al., 2018) are widely adopted for private inference. FHE allows operations to be performed
142   directly on encrypted data, ensuring that the decrypted result is nearly identical to the output of
143
      plaintext data. For input x, y, the following approximations hold:
144          Dec (Enc(x)) ≈ x, Dec (Enc(x) ⊕ Enc(y)) ≈ x + y, Dec (Enc(x) ⊙ Enc(y)) ≈ x ∗ y,                                                (5)
145   where Enc and Dec denote the encryption and decryption functions, respectively. (⊕, ⊙) are oper-
146   ations in the ciphertext space while (+, ∗) correspond to operations in the plaintext space. The key
147   issue here is that FHE supports only addition and multiplication. Furthermore, the computational
148   overhead is significant (nearly 100× slower than in the plaintext space) for large models due to
149   bootstrapping required to mitigate decryption errors that arise from extensive computations.
150   On the other hand, secure MPC allows multiple parties to jointly compute operations on combined
151   data while preserving the privacy of each party’s data. In secure MPC, secret data is divided into
152   multiple shares and distributed among participants (Damgård et al., 2012), ensuring that reconstruct-
153   ing the original data is computationally infeasible without all shares. Computations are executed on
154   these shares, ensuring privacy is maintained throughout the process. However, secure MPC also
155   supports only addition and multiplication and demands significant communication overhead.
156   In deep learning, these private inference methods are commonly used in machine learning-as-a-
157   service scenarios. In such a scenario, a client submits encrypted data to a model provider, which
158   processes it and returns the results. Both the data and model parameters are encrypted and operated
159   on in the ciphertext space. Since non-linear operations are unsupported, methods such as polynomial
160   approximations or iterative algorithms have been proposed (Gilad-Bachrach et al., 2016; Li et al.,
161   2023). Other approaches focus on reducing computational costs by introducing new frameworks or
      protocols (Hao et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2024).

177   3      P RIVATE I NFERENCE FOR D IFFUSION M ODEL
178
179   We now present our overall algorithm whose two key ingredients are i) approximation techniques
180   for complex operations of diffusion models and ii) score distillation from unconditional generation.
181   Before delving into our method, note that while we mainly use a Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al.,
182   2022) for our explanations, our method is not inherently dependent on Stable Diffusion and can
183   be applied to other models. In our framework, the client transmits encrypted input to the model
184   provider, who processes the input and returns the result. To ensure private inference, either homo-
185   morphic encryption (FHE) or secure multi-party computation (MPC) can be employed. The overall
186   framework is illustrated in Figure 1.
187   Specifically, the client computes the token embeddings and the first LayerNorm (Ba, 2016) within
188   the text encoder of Stable Diffusion. These tensors are then encrypted and transmitted to the server
189   (model provider). This step is performed client-side because the initial token embeddings have high
190   variance, and processing them before encryption preserves performance. Since this computation
191   represents a very small fraction of the overall workload, the client’s computational burden remains
192   minimal. After these initial operations, all subsequent layers and components are processed within
193
      the ciphertext space on the server.
194   To perform the denoising process in the ciphertext space, where only linear operations are supported,
195   we approximate the non-linear functions in the diffusion model to linear counterparts. The details
196   of these approximation techniques are discussed in Section 3.1. To reduce latency, we decrease
197   the number of denoising steps. To address the score deviations caused by insufficient steps and the
198   approximations, we apply score distillation from unconditional generation, as detailed in Section 3.2.
199
200   3.1     P RACTICAL APPROXIMATION TECHNIQUES
201
202   Stable Diffusion includes several non-linear functions such as GroupNorm (Wu & He, 2018), Lay-
203   erNorm, SiLU, GeLU (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016), and softmax. However, fully homomorphic
204   encryption (FHE) and secure multi-party computation (MPC) only support linear operations, neces-
205   sitating the approximation of non-linear functions for private inference. The approximation methods
206   utilized in CrypTen (Knott et al., 2021), a widely used open-source library, may not suffice to achieve
207
      comparable performance to the original model. To address this challenge, we suggest several tech-
      niques that enhance the approximation abilities of the library without additional training. Note that
208
      each technique is significant since removing any of them can lead to noisy or black images.
209
210   First, GroupNorm and LayerNorm normalize input tensors using the inverse square root of the vari-
211   ance, a non-linear operation that can be approximated with the Newton-Raphson method. However,
212   we observe that the approximation error increases significantly for input values exceeding 102 in
213   Figure 2a. Using the fact that x−mean(x)       x/d−mean(x/d)
                                      deviation(x) = deviation(x/d) for any non-zero d, we scale down the
214   variance of the input tensor by a sufficiently large factor before normalization. Specifically, we
215   increase d as the number of channels in the group decreases, leveraging the fact that variance is
      proportional to the number of samples. The details of scaling factors are provided in Appendix A.

251   3.2    S CORE DISTILLATION FROM UNCONDITIONAL GENERATION
252
      Since computations in the ciphertext space are drastically slower than in the plaintext space as men-
253   tioned in Section 2.2, it is critically important to perform only a limited number of denoising steps to
254   reduce the inference overhead. However, the naive reduction in denoising steps, combined with ap-
255   proximation errors, can result in severe performance degradation. To address this, we propose score
256   distillation from unconditional generation (SDU), which refines the score obtained from the condi-
257   tional generation with unconditional generation of the original diffusion model with full denoising
258   steps. The key insight is that, since unconditional generation can be performed in plaintext space, it
259   can take more steps and avoid approximation errors, resulting in a distribution that is closer to that
260   of the original model. The overall process is illustrated in Figure 3.
261   Specifically, our objective is to minimize the divergence between the image distribution generated by
262   the encrypted model and that produced by the original model. Let pθ (ẑt |y) denote the image distri-
263   bution of the original model and p̂θ (ẑt |y) indicate that of the encrypted model. The widely adopted
264   objective is computed as DKL (p̂θ (ẑt |y), pθ (ẑt |y)). Since our main goal is to enhance performance
265   through a correction step during inference, we focus on reducing the divergence for high-density
266   samples from the original model. Therefore, our objective is formulated as:

446   5     R ELATED WORK
447
448   5.1   C ONDITIONAL DIFFUSION MODEL
449
450
      With the development of diffusion-based models(Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020; Song
      et al.; Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021), significant progress has been made in various conditional image
451
      generation tasks, such as text-conditioned image synthesis (Saharia et al., 2022) and masked-region
452
      image editing (Lugmayr et al., 2022). Various open source models and commercial models such
453   as DALLE (Ramesh et al., 2022), Imagen (Saharia et al., 2022), and GLIDE (Nichol et al., 2022)
454   have contributed to this development. Recently, classifier-free guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2021) sim-
455   plifies the conditional image generation process by directly incorporating class conditions into the
456   diffusion model, eliminating the need for an external classifier. Based on this work, stable diffusion
457   models (Rombach et al., 2022) have excelled in generating high-quality images efficiently, leading
458   to the exploration of various applications.
459   In addition to these advancements, Score Distillation Sampling (SDS) (Poole et al.) which distills
460   knowledge from a large pre-trained diffusion model into a more efficient student model further en-
461   hances the quality of generated images. Our approach shares similarities with SDS in that it transfers
462   knowledge to conditional generation (student) from unconditional generation (teacher), but there are
463   several key differences. First, we use score distillation during the inference phase, with a different
464   input ordering of the KL divergence to reduce divergence in high-density samples. Additionally, our
465   method takes more steps in the distillation process for unconditional generation than for conditional
466   generation, and we further refine the score through reverse engineering.
467
468   5.2   P RIVATE INFERENCE
469
      Fully homomorphic encryption (Gentry, 2009; Cheon et al., 2017) and secure multi-party com-
470
      putation (Yao, 1986; Evans et al., 2018; Damgård et al., 2019; Goldreich et al., 2019) are widely
471
      employed in private inference. CryptoNets (Gilad-Bachrach et al., 2016) pioneered the use homo-
472   morphic encryption for small neural networks in image classification using polynomial approxima-
473   tions. To reduce the latency, GAZELLE (Juvekar et al., 2018) proposes a hybrid method combining
474   homomorphic encryption and two-party computation. Cheetah (Reagen et al., 2021) further accel-
475   erated private inference through hardware optimizations. For better approximations of non-linear
476   operations like ReLU (Lou & Jiang, 2019; Ghodsi et al., 2020; Jha et al., 2021; Kundu et al., 2023;
477   Peng et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024), inverse square root (Panda, 2022), and softmax (Lee et al., 2023),
478   several works have proposed approximating algorithms. For transformer architectures, various meth-
479   ods (Hao et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024; Zimer-
480
      man et al., 2024) are proposed to effectively approximate and compute non-linear operations such
      as GeLU (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016), LayerNorm (Ba, 2016), and attention mechanism (Vaswani,
481
      2017). For diffusion models, HE-Diffusion (Chen & Yan, 2024) enables privacy-preserving image
482
      generation using homomorphic encryption. However, there remains a risk of input exposure to the
483   model provider when sharing intermediate representations. In contrast, our method ensures the pri-
484   vacy of both inputs and outputs during image generation.

      6   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this paper, we propose a new framework for private inference of pre-trained diffusion models.
489   We introduce the practical approximation techniques, which are more robust in the larger input val-
490   ues. Additionally, we significantly reduce the latency with a small performance trade-off by using
491   fewer steps and incorporating our new score distillation method, where the score from unconditional
492   generation is distilled into conditional generation during the sampling process. In experiments, we
493   demonstrate that the diffusion model with our approximations can achieve performance comparable
494   to the original model. Furthermore, our score distillation method enhances performance, approach-
495   ing the quality of full denoising steps even with fewer sampling steps.
496
497   Limitations In this study, we enable diffusion models to generate high-quality images in private
498   inference while substantially reducing latency. However, the absolute latency of a single forward
499
      pass remains too high for practical deployment in real-world applications. By unlocking the secure
      diffusion model for the first time, we believe this research paves the way for practical private infer-
500
      ence in diffusion models.
