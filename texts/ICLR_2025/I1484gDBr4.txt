009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               The transformer network architecture has led to advances in artificial intelligence.
012            Conversational AI applications, such as ChatGPT, and protein folding predic-
013            tions with AlphaFold are made possible by transformer architectures and the self-
014            attention mechanism. However, advancing towards more general, flexible, and
015            energy-efficient artificial intelligence may require exploring new architectures that
016            differ significantly from those currently used. Transformer networks have largely
017            replaced recurrent neural networks (RNNs) for state-of-the-art performance on
018            sequence-based tasks. However, in recent years there has been some successful
019            competition from linear recurrent neural networks (LRNNs) and state space mod-
020
               els (SSMs). A core advantage of LRNNs and SSMs over traditional RNNs is that
               the hidden states can be calculated in parallel. Therefore, like the transformer,
021
               they can make efficient use of GPU computation. Unlike the transformer, compu-
022
               tational costs of parallelized LRNNs and SSMs can scale sub-quadratically with
023            sequence length. Despite these advantages, LRNNs and SSMs often struggle to
024            generate the deep and rich representations that have contributed to the success
025            of transformer architectures. We introduce Feature-Sequence Twisting (FST),
026            a novel technique that transposes the sequence and feature dimensions between
027            LRNN blocks. The purpose of FST is to generate deeper representations of the
028            sequence in subsequent LRNN blocks. Since the computational cost of LRNNs
029            scale sub-quadratically with sequence length, FST remains practical to compute
030            even for large feature dimensions. Our experiments demonstrate that the FST
031
               architecture outperforms transformer networks on tasks such as Long ListOps,
               achieving performance competitive with state-of-the-art models.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036
      Advancements in artificial intelligence may require exploring architectures that differ significantly
037
      from those currently used. However, the search for novel architectures is challenging due to the vast
038   range of network architectures that could be explored. Biological neural networks comprising the
039   human brain and nervous systems of other complex species are capable of cognitive flexibility, rapid
040   learning ability, and high energy efficiency. We can use known biological structures as a guide to
041   navigate the possibilities for artificial neural networks, as argued in Hassabis et al. (2017).
042
      Recurrent neural networks (RNNs) are an important class of artificial neural networks inspired by
043
      the recurrent connectivity observed in biological neural systems, such as those in the mammalian
044   neocortex. This recurrence, and the neural dynamics which arise from it, are central to diverse
045   computations and functions in the brain, including short-term memory, attention, and response nor-
046   malization. RNNs are thus important tools for modeling neural circuit functions in computational
047   neuroscience studies, and have many applications in AI, particularly for solving tasks that require
048   integrating or comparing information across time. The basic RNN works by updating the activity of
049   a hidden state ht at time-step t with the equation
050
                                       ht = f (Wrnn ht−1 + Win xt + b),
051
052   where f is the activation function, Wrnn is the recurrent matrix, Win is the input matrix, xt is the
053   input at time-step t and b is the bias. These networks are designed for handling sequential inputs
      x0,1,2,...,T . The activation function in RNNs can be loosely compared to the nonlinear response

054
      of biological neurons to synaptic inputs, with hidden state activities representing the firing rates of
055   neurons or neural populations. Early examples of recurrent networks include Hopfield networks
056   introduced in Hopfield (1982), which were used to model associative memory. Later, the ability of
057   artificial RNNs to perform well at sequence-based tasks expanded further with the introduction of
058   more advanced architectures using multiple hidden states, such as long short-term memory networks
059   (LSTMs) introduced in Hochreiter & Schmidhuber (1996).
060
      Introduced in the paper ‘Attention is All You Need’, Vaswani et al. (2017), the transformer network
061   has revolutionized AI for sequence based tasks. Transformer architectures are the underlying net-
062   works behind products like ChatGPT, AlphaFold, and more effective versions of Google Translate.
063   The key innovation in this architecture is the ”self attention” (SA) mechanism. This works by cal-
064   culating ’attention weights’ which transmit information about how elements of a sequence relate to
065   one another. It is clear that the transformer is not directly analogous to biological neural networks;
066   notably, the transformer network does not involve recurrence, which is thought to be crucial to the
067   way the brain processes sequential information.
068   Contrary to the argument that we need new architectures inspired by biological neural networks,
069   much progress has been made by simply scaling up the size of transformer networks and the data
070   used to train them. Much of the transformer’s success comes from the computational efficiency
071   of its large matrix multiplication operations, which are well-suited for GPU-based training. The
072   transformer is also good at providing deep representations of sequences by capturing long-range
073   dependencies. However, transformers do have some important limitations. One such limitation is
074   that the SA mechanism does not inherently capture sequence order and requires positional encoding
075
      to provide positional information for each element. When using RNNs, we naturally preserve infor-
      mation about sequence order in the dynamics of recurrent network activity. The parallel structure of
076
      the transformer is also a double-edged sword, as the operations scale with the square of the sequence
077
      length. We aim to construct RNNs that offer the same parallel GPU training efficiency, provide deep
078   representations of sequences, and scale sub-quadratically with sequence length. By doing so, we
079   hope to maintain the advantages of transformers without some of their limitations.
080
081
      We will describe some of the advantages of using a linear recurrent neural network (LRNN). These
      networks are simple and well-studied, but they have several unique properties that make them highly
082
      relevant for comparison with transformers. By leveraging LRNNs as building blocks and applying
083
      several computational and structural tricks, we demonstrate how this network type can outperform
084   transformer networks.
085
086   Traditionally, when using RNNs, the representation of the sequence dimension is not changed by
087
      treating it as a set of features. What sets this work apart from other approaches is that we aim for
      subsequent layers of the network to contain deeper representations of the sequence dimension. We
088
      introduce a novel Feature-Sequence Twisting (FST) layer. In this layer, we transpose the sequence
089
      and feature dimensions between LRNN blocks. We find that networks using the FST blocks can
090   perform well on the WikiText-103 language task and the Long ListOps task, designed to test a
091   model’s ability to handle long-range dependencies.

126   the activity and input weights complex. We do not calculate C −1 directly; instead we treat W̄in and

128   but it is more general, and we can choose to consider only the real part of the resulting activities.

134
      If the modulus eigenvalues of Λrnn are above one, the associated activity rapidly increases, and if
135   they are below one the activity will rapidly decrease. For stability, the modulus eigenvalues must
136   remain close to unity. In order to enforce the condition that |λi | does not go above one and remains
137   close to one, each complex component of the network can be constructed such that
138
                                         λi = exp(−exp(αi ) + iexp(θi )).
139
140   This is the stable exponential parameterization method, as described in Orvieto et al. (2023). The
141   trainable parameters for the network are the set of αi and θi variables. The output sequence is given
142   by the real part of the hidden states. The network can then be trained efficiently in the parallel form
143   on a GPU using back-propagation. By constructing our network in this parallel form, along with
144   careful parameterization and initialization, we can alleviate the vanishing and exploding gradient
145
      problem first noted in Bengio et al. (1994). Ordinarily, the lack of vanishing and exploding gradients
      is one of the main advantages that transformers have over RNNs.
146
147   Now that we have constructed the parallelized LRNN, we can chain them together in blocks and ap-
148   ply multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) with non-linear activation functions between the blocks. Since
149   we have lost some complexity in the network dynamics of the RNN by choosing a linear activation
150   function, the hope is that by chaining together the LRNN blocks in this manner, we can recover some
151
      of the fitting ability of non-linear RNNs. This aspect of the architecture is also described in Orvieto
      et al. (2023).

      2.2   F EATURE S EQUENCE T WISTING
155
      We introduce the novel Feature Sequence Twisting (FST) technique to the block LRNN architecture.
156   This is done by transposing the feature and sequence dimensions between each LRNN block. Ordi-
157   narily, with an RNN we ask, ”How do the elements of a sequence relate given a set of features?” The
158   RNN produces a new representation of the features based on these relationships. When we transpose
159   the feature and sequence dimensions we now ask, ”how do the features of a sequence relate given
160   the elements of the sequence?”. A new representation of the sequence is then generated based on
161   the relationship between the features. As a loose biological analogy, FST could be thought of as
      a way of processing a sequence held in long-term memory. By chaining the blocks together and


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      applying FST the goal is to produce deeper-and-deeper representations of the sequence as well as
163   the features. We present a diagram of the FST architecture in Figure 1. Each FST module contains
164   two LRNNs and two MLPs. The ’hidden feature size‘ is a hyperparameter of the model and is equal
165   to the hidden state of LRNN 1 and the hidden layers of MLP 1. The second, transposed LRNN 2 and
166   MLP 2 have a hidden state and hidden layers equal to the original sequence length. The coefficients
167   α1 and α2 are defined as α1 = σ(p1 ) and α2 = σ(p2 ), where p1 and p2 are trainable parameters
168   initialized at zero, and σ represents the sigmoid function.
169   While investigating prior work, we find that the idea behind FST shares some similarities to the
170   ”MLP-mixer” architecture from Tolstikhin et al. (2021), which similarly used reshapes and transpo-
171   sitions of the ”patches” and ”channel” dimensions for computer vision tasks. However, this work
172   relied solely on MLPs rather than blocks of RNNs.
173
      One could apply a similar technique to blocks of transformer networks, transposing the feature and
174
      sequence dimensions between blocks. However, because computational costs scale quadratically
175   with sequence length in the self-attention mechanism, this approach would also cause the model to
176   scale quadratically with the hidden feature size. Using LRNNs, we avoid this problem by utilizing
177   an algorithm for parallel computation that scales sub-quadratically.

246   3.1   L ONG L IST O PS TASK
247
      The benchmark task ListOps was introduced by Nangia & Bowman (2018) to evaluate performance
248
      of models on sequence-based tasks. It was expanded in the Long Range Arena from Tay et al. (2020)
249   to assess how effective transformers were at computation across a long context length. We present
250   the results of the FST architecture on the Long ListOps task in Table 1. We find that FST with an
251   accuracy of 63.11% outperforms most models, including large transformer models. The exception
252   is the MEGA model, which achieved a slightly greater score of 63.14%, from Ma et al. (2023).
253
      ListOps involves evaluating a nested mathematical operation. For example,
254
255                         Max(2, Min(3, 9), Sum Mod(1, 1), Median(0, 1, 2), 0),
256
257   for which the answer would be 3. There are four operators used in Long ListOps; Min, Max, Median
258   and Sum Mod. Sum Mod is the sum of the list, constrained to 0-9 by applying the modulo-10
259   operator to the result.
260   The maximum sequence length for Long ListOps is 2000, with an input feature size of 16 tokens
261   and an output prediction range of 0-9. The architecture consists of six sequential FST blocks, each
262   with a hidden feature dimensions of 256. We used an Adam optimizer with a fixed learning rate
263   of 10−4 , a weight decay of 10−5 and a training batch size of 16. We generated 6 × 106 training
264   examples using code from the Long Range Arena GitHub repository, and used the default testing
265   set for evaluation, from Tay et al. (2020). This experiment was run on a single NVIDIA RTX 3090
266
      GPU.
267   There is quite a large gap between transformer models and SSMs on performance in ListOps. It
268   should be noted that the results provided here are for models trained from scratch. It is argued
269   in Amos et al. (2024) that this gap can be closed somewhat by ’self-pretraining‘ transformers. How-
      ever, even with self-pretraining these models do not outperform SSMs like S4 (Gu et al., 2021).

      3.2    P IXEL - BY- PIXEL MNIST
293
      To demonstrate the limitations of transformers for tasks where information is largely contained
294
      within the sequence order, we evaluate the results on a simple sequential MNIST task, where the
295
      pixels of MNIST are presented one at a time. This task was first introduced in Le et al. (2015) as a
296   benchmark for sequential data modeling. We used an unmodified transformer encoder with a model
297   size of 512, two encoder blocks, and trainable positional encoding. For comparison, we used two
298   FST blocks with a hidden feature size of 128. Each model was trained with an Adam optimizer with
299   a fixed learning rate of 10−4 . In each case, the target prediction is the output of the final element of
300   the generated sequence. These experiments were run on a single GTX 1080 Ti.
301
      We find that, in half an hour of training, the transformer model is unable to achieve much beyond
302   chance performance. This makes some intuitive sense since the model is fully reliant on positional
303   encoding to transmit information about the sequence order. By contrast, when using an FST model
304   an accuracy of 98.3% is reached quickly.
305
      Since we aim to demonstrate a limitation of the transformer architecture, we do not include con-
306
      volutional layers or average pooling in this example. This is why this result is different from other
307
      studies performed on pixel-by-pixel MNIST using transformers, such as Trinh et al. (2018).

310   3.3    W IKITEXT-103 TASK
311
312   In the paper introducing the MAMBA model from Gu & Dao (2024), it is argued that LRNNs and
313   SSMs are incapable of selectively deciding which inputs affect the hidden state passed along the
314   sequence. They argue that it is this property which makes linear time-invariant models such as
315
      LRNNs poorly suited to language modeling. However, with FST, we suggest that this argument
      no longer applies since the representation of the sequence can be selectively altered. To test the
316
      language modeling abilities of FST, we use the Wikitext-103 benchmark from Merity et al. (2016).
317
      Wikitext-103 contains over 100M tokens of text extracted from Wikipedia articles. The data is split
318   into sequences of token length 512. The perplexity was evaluated using eL , where L is the cross
319   entropy loss on predicting the next token. We use the ’bert-base-uncased’ tokenizer from Devlin
320   et al. (2019), with a vocab size of 30522. While training and evaluating, we use a random number
321   between 0 and 512 to truncate the initial tokens in the data. The model is initialized with six FST
322   blocks and a hidden feature dimension size of 512. We used an Adam optimizer with a fixed learning
323   rate of 10−4 and a training batch size of 128. These experiments were run on a single NVIDIA A100
      GPU.

      To see how much the sequence representation is modified in subsequent FST blocks, we can look at
363   the trainable α2 parameters in the model. As shown in Figure 3, these α2 coefficients indicate that

      4    C ONCLUSION
433
434   We have demonstrated some advantages of using blocks of FST over transformer models. The FST
435   architecture achieves good performance on the Long ListOps task, competitive with the state of the
436   art. We also show the model’s potential for language modeling using the WikiText-103 dataset.
437
      One limitation of the FST model compared to transformers is the lack of an equivalent method to ’at-
438
      tention masking’. It should be noted that LRNNs do not require attention masking for future tokens,
439
      as the temporal ordering of elements in the sequence is naturally preserved. However, when the fea-
440   ture and sequence dimensions are transposed after an LRNN block for FST, this property is broken.
441   We can mask certain weights in the FST architecture to ensure that the model is causal. As a future
442   direction for this work, we aim to use this causal version of FST to train with an encoder-decoder
443   structure similar to the way transformers are trained on language translation tasks, for example.
444
      By transposing the feature and sequence dimensions, we also lose a property of RNNs whereby
445
      inference can be run on shorter or potentially unlimited sequence lengths. For FST, the sequence
446   length is fixed and will need to be truncated or padded to match the sequence length used during
447   training.
448
449
      Another limitation is that if we require the hidden feature dimension to be much larger than the
      sequence length, the cost of computing the LRNN in the transposed mode is expensive. For many
450
      practical purposes, however, we find that this is often not the case.
451
452   Despite these limitations, we expect that there are many scenarios where using FST is advantageous,
453   requiring fewer computational resources to achieve better results. Overall, we hope that FST will
454   be considered as a competitive and flexible model for use in any sequence-based task. Though this
455
      paper has only considered a limited number of benchmark tasks, there is significant potential for
      the application of FST in more complex tasks. Future work could explore the scalability of FST
456
      for longer sequences, including new techniques to condense the information contained within these
457
      sequences. We encourage further exploration with FST models to realize its potential across various
458   domains.
