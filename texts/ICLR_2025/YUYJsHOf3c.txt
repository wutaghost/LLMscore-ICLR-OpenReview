                                                  A BSTRACT

             Post-training Large Language Models (LLMs) with explicit reasoning trajectories
             can enhance their reasoning abilities. However, acquiring such high-quality tra-
             jectory data typically demands meticulous supervision from humans or superior
             models, which can be either expensive or license-constrained. In this paper, we
             explore how far an LLM can improve its reasoning by self-synthesizing reasoning
             paths as training data without any additional supervision. Existing self-synthesizing
             methods, such as STaR, suffer from poor generalization to out-of-domain (OOD)
             reasoning tasks. We hypothesize it is due to that their self-synthesized reasoning
             paths are too task-specific, lacking general task-agnostic reasoning guidance. To
             address this, we propose Reasoning Generalist via Self-Improvement (ReGenesis),
             a method to self-synthesize reasoning paths as post-training data by progressing
             from abstract to concrete. More specifically, ReGenesis self-synthesizes reasoning
             paths by converting general reasoning guidelines into task-specific ones, generating
             reasoning structures, and subsequently transforming these structures into reason-
             ing paths, without the need for human-designed task-specific examples used in
             existing methods. We show that ReGenesis achieves superior performance on all
             in-domain and OOD settings tested compared to existing methods. For six OOD
             tasks specifically, while previous methods exhibited an average performance de-
             crease of approximately 4.6% after post training, ReGenesis delivers around 6.1%
             performance improvement. We also conduct in-depth analysis of our framework
             and show ReGenesis is effective across various LLMs and design choices.

1       I NTRODUCTION
Recent research has demonstrated that post-training with explicit intermediate reasoning trajectories
can improve the performance of large language models (LLMs) (Yuan et al., 2024; Mukherjee
et al., 2023; Lightman et al., 2023) across a wide range of complicated reasoning tasks, such as
mathematical reasoning, commonsense reasoning, etc. However, their acquisition of high-quality
reasoning trajectory data in the post-training phase demands meticulous supervision for each reasoning
step, either from humans or superior models. Therefore, although high-quality explicit reasoning
paths can help post training, they are either expensive and time-consuming due to the additional
substantial human supervision, or constrained by the license of superior models. It motivates us to
explore: how far an LLM can improve its reasoning by self-generating reasoning paths as training
data, without any additional supervision beyond final answers?
Existing approaches towards this direction, STaR(Zelikman et al., 2022) and LMSI(Huang et al.,
2023), prompt the given LLM to generate a chain-of-thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022) reasoning
steps and filter them with final ground truth labels or self consistency (Wang et al., 2022). They
also use a small fixed set of human-designed reasoning examples and incorporate them directly into
models’ prompt for in-context learning. After self-generating reasoning paths for given data sets,
these approaches use these data sets with reasoning paths to further fine-tune the given model. These
self-improvement approaches can improve the model’s in-domain reasoning capabilities without the
need of additional external supervision. However, through our extensive experiments, we have found
that they struggle to improve the given LLMs on out-of-domain (OOD) tasks that are different from

To fill this gap, we explore how to self-synthesize reasoning paths as post training data to make LLMs
generalize well across various OOD reasoning tasks. We hypothesize that existing methods fail to
generalize to OOD tasks because their synthesized reasoning paths are overly tailored to specific
training tasks and lack diversity. These methods predominantly utilize a chain-of-thought approach,
which leads to uniform and narrow reasoning patterns, rather than employing a range of cognitive
strategies that foster diverse thinking steps. To resolve these shortcomings, we introduce ReGenesis,
a reasoning generalist framework designed to generate general and diverse reasoning paths guided by
various reasoning principles. ReGenesis adopts a methodological approach that transitions from
abstract and general reasoning guidelines to concrete and specific reasoning solutions, as shown in
Figure 1. More specifically, we first create multiple general task-agnostic reasoning guidelines in the
beginning of the reasoning path generation. Then, given each general reasoning guideline, we prompt
the LLM to augment such general guideline into a task-specific reasoning guideline. Note that in this
step we fill in task-specific details into general guidelines while still maintaining their task-agnostic
reasoning structure, as shown in Figure 1. Then we feed each task-specific reasoning guideline into
the given LLM to generate a reasoning structure without directly solving the tasks. Each reasoning
structure is subsequently used to create one candidate reasoning path. Finally, all the candidate the
reasoning paths are filtered using ground truth answers and, along with the instructions and answers,
are used to fine-tune the same LLM, enhancing its reasoning capabilities.
To demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed framework, we conducted evaluations of ReGenesis
across various in-domain and out-of-domain (OOD) settings. In all scenarios tested, ReGenesis
consistently outperformed existing methods, showcasing its superior performance in both in-domain
and OOD contexts. For in-domain settings, across five in-domain tasks involving math, logic, and
common sense, ReGenesis outperforms all 5 baselines by 7.1% to 18.9%. Moreover, we evaluate
ReGenesis against baseline methods on 6 OOD tasks, which include math, logic, common sense, and

We summarize our contributions as follows:
• We propose ReGenesis that enables LLMs to self-improve their general reasoning capabilities
  in Section 3. ReGenesis uses a given LLM to self-synthesize reasoning paths for post-training
  by progressing from abstract to concrete and enables the given LLM to generalize well to OOD

2    R ELATED W ORK
Learning from reasoning paths with supervision. Recent research shows that employing explicit
reasoning paths with extensive supervision from either humans or superior models, can lead to better
reasoning capabilities after post-training a LLM (Lightman et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023; Yuan et al.,
2024; Mukherjee et al., 2023; Achiam et al., 2023; Gou et al., 2023; Mitra et al., 2023; Qu et al., 2024;
Sun et al., 2024). For example, Mukherjee et al. (2023) and Mitra et al. (2023) illustrate how smaller
models can benefit from detailed explanations and step-by-step reasoning processes provided by
GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023). TRIPOST (Yu et al., 2023) further illustrates this by effectively training
smaller models to learn from mistakes and enhance their performance on mathematical and reasoning
tasks with guidance from a more powerful language model like Codex (Chen et al., 2021). Yuan et al.
(2024) also demonstrates that incorporating high-quality multi-turn reasoning generated by a superior
model during training can improve the overall capabilities of LLMs. Moreover, process supervision
methods (Lightman et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024) uses extensive finer-grained
human annotations on step-by-step reasoning paths as post-training data. These approaches all rely
on more advanced teacher models or human experts for extensive supervision to obtain high-quality,
explicit intermediate reasoning paths. When more advanced models or human supervision are not
available due to license and cost issues, we are curious to see how well LLMs can leveraging itself on
generating reasoning paths to enhance its own reasoning capabilities.
Learning from self-synthesized reasoning paths. Contrary to the large number of methods above
which rely on external supervision to create reasoning paths for post-training, there are limited existing
work that employs self-synthesized reasoning paths from a LLM to improve its own reasoning capacity.
Self-Taught Reasoner (STaR) (Zelikman et al., 2022) improves the reasoning capabilities of language
models using self-generated, ground-truth-filtered reasoning paths which are prompted with a few
human-written CoT examples. Huang et al. (2023) trains language models with self-generated
reasoning paths filtered by self-consistent answers, also aided by human-written examples. However,
we observed that they fail to improve the given LLMs on OOD tasks through our experiments while
our framework improves model performance across both in domain and OOD reasoning tasks.
Diverse prompting methods. Recent research has greatly improved the reasoning capability of
LLMs through more effective prompting techniques (Zhou et al., 2022; Kojima et al., 2022; Zheng
et al., 2023). For example, Wei et al. (2022) introduced CoT prompting, where the model generates a
series of intermediate reasoning steps, making it easier to solve complex, multi-step reasoning tasks.
Wang et al. (2022) enhanced this approach by sampling multiple different reasoning paths and then
using majority voting to identify the most consistent answers, referred as Self-Consistency. Zhou et al.
(2024) further developed these techniques by enabling LLMs to select and combine simple reasoning
modules—like critical thinking and step-by-step analysis—into a clear reasoning structure during
decoding. These prompting methods, particularly those described by Zhou et al. (2024), have inspired
us to develop a reasoning generalist that can generate a more diverse and general self-synthesized
reasoning paths for a given task.

3     ReGenesis: R EASONING G ENERALISTS VIA S ELF -I MPROVEMENT
Existing methods have demonstrated that we can self-improve LLM’s reasoning performance in
in-domain settings by self-synthesizing reasoning paths, as also verified by our experiments in Section
4.2. However, when applying existing methods in out-of-domain (OOD) settings, we have found that
they lead to non-neglectable performance drop, shown in Section 4.3. We hypothesize that the main
reason for previous methods being unable to generalize to OOD tasks is that their self-synthesized
reasoning paths are too task-specific, lacking general task-agnostic reasoning guidance and diversity.
Therefore, to verify this hypothesis and to provide a self-improving method that can help LLMs to
generalize well on diverse reasoning tasks after training LLMs on limited reasoning tasks, we propose
ReGenesis. ReGenesis adopts a strategy to self-synthesize reasoning paths by progressing from
abstract to concrete, from general reasoning structures to task-specific reasoning paths. Building on
this idea, ReGenesis can automatically generate and integrate reasoning paths into post-training
datasets that originally contain only instructions and ground-truth answers. Figure 1 shows how we
create self-synthesized reasoning paths for a task. We start with a trained LLM M and a training

4     E XPERIMENTS
In this section, we assess the effectiveness of ReGenesis and existing methods in improving LLM’s
reasoning capabilities for both in-domain tasks and OOD tasks.

4.1     E XPERIMENT D ETAILS
4.1.1    TASKS AND DATASETS
We selected the following five datasets for training, including mathematic, logical and common sense
reasoning tasks. The training sets of these datasets are used to generate self-synthetic reasoning
paths and to fine-tune the language models. The test sets are employed to evaluate the in-domain
performance of the fine-tuned models.
• Mathematic reasoning: GSM8K math problem dataset (Cobbe et al., 2021) and NumGLUE dataset
  (Mishra et al., 2022). In this experiment, we only use the numerical answers as the ground truth in
  the GSM8K dataset, excluding the human-provided reasoning paths. This approach enables us to
  independently evaluate the contribution of reasoning to the overall performance.
• Logical reasoning: We use logical reasoning dataset ReClor (Yu et al., 2020).
• Commonsense reasoning: AI2 Reasoning Challenge (ARC) (Clark et al., 2018) and StrategyQA
  (Geva et al., 2021) dataset. For ARC, we specifically use only the Challenge subset (ARC-c).

4.1.3   BASELINES
Base model without further fine-tuning (w/o FT). For this baseline we just report the performance
of the base LLM, Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3 (Jiang et al., 2023). No further fine-tuning is performed.
Fine-Tuning with Ground Truth Only (FT w/ GT). In this approach, we fine-tune the base LLM
using the original instructions and ground-truth answers in the training sets, without incorporating
any self-synthesized reasoning paths. More details in Appendix A.5.1.
Fine-Tuning with Self-Improvement approach (LMSI). LMSI (Huang et al., 2023) first samples
self-synthesized reasoning paths using few-shot CoT prompting. It then filters these reasoning paths
by selecting the one with the majority vote answer. Finally for this baseline, we fine-tune the base
LLM using these self-generated solutions as the target outputs. More details in Appendix A.5.2.
Fine-Tuning with Self-Improvement approach and ground truth (LMSI w/ GT). For this baseline,
we follow the same reasoning generation process as LMSI. However, instead of using a majority vote
to filter out incorrect reasoning paths as above, we utilize ground truth answers. The base LLM is
then fine-tuned using these self-generated solutions as the target outputs.
Fine-Tuning with Self-Taught Reasoner approach (STaR). STaR (Zelikman et al., 2022) begins
by sampling self-synthesized reasoning paths directly using the few-shot CoT prompting outlined
in their paper. In contrast, our framework progresses from abstract to concrete reasoning without
relying on CoT examples. Then, it filters these paths based on ground truth and incorporate the
ground truth as hints directly in the instructions to generate reasoning paths for instructions lacking
correct solutions. Subsequently, fine-tune the base LLM using these self-synthesized solutions as
target outputs. More details in Appendix A.5.3.

4.1.4   E VALUATION M ETHODS
At inference, for a fair and accurate comparison, we use exact match accuracy on all experiments. We
have also tested multiple prompting methods for ReGenesis and each baseline at inference. In the
“CoT Prompting” method (Wei et al., 2022), we prompt the LM models with:“Solve the following
problem step-by-step. Question: ” + {question} + “Answer:”, using a temperature setting of 0.8.
In “Self-Consistency” (Wang et al., 2022), we use the same prompt but generate 15 responses at a
temperature of 0.8, then select the final answer based on majority voting.

4.2     I N -D OMAIN E XPERIMENTS
In this experimental setup, we evaluate the enhancement of LLMs’ in-domain reasoning capabilities
using ReGenesis, comparing it with existing self-improvement methods (§4.1.3). For each reasoning
dataset described in Section 4.1.1, we construct reasoning paths (§3) from the training sets, fine-
tune the model on the synthesized reasoning data (§4.1.2), and subsequently evaluate the model’s
performance (§4.1.4) on the respective test sets.
As presented in Table 2, ReGenesis significantly surpasses all baselines, achieving an average per-
formance enhancement of 16.56% over the original model without fine-tuning. Baseline approaches,
such as LMSI and STaR, which incorporate self-synthesized reasoning paths into their fine-tuning
processes, also demonstrated improvements, albeit less significant than those achieved by ReGenesis.
This suggests that the quality or diversity of the reasoning paths generated by these models is inferior
to those created by ReGenesis. LMSI (Huang et al., 2023) adds self-synthesized reasoning paths
to the fine-tuning training set, filtering these paths through self-consistency checks. However, the
lack of guaranteed accuracy in these self-consistency reasoning paths compromises data quality,
leading to a modest improvement of only 1.02% in average test performance. To address this issue,
we implemented a modification where ground truth is utilized for filtering (LMSI w/ GT), thereby
enhancing the quality of the reasoning paths. This adjustment raised the improvement from 1.02% to
6.26%. STaR (Zelikman et al., 2022) also employs ground-truth-verified reasoning paths but adopts
a slightly different prompting and reasoning path format during post-training compared to LMSI,
leading to a 9.7% test set improvement, which remains below our performance significantly.
During our experiments, we also noted that training solely with ground-truth answers (FT w/ GT) and
excluding reasoning paths does not consistently improve the performance of the base large language
model (LLM), even on in-domain test sets. Specifically, in the GSM8K and ARC-C tasks, relying
exclusively on ground-truth answers led to performance declines of 44.8% and 3.4%, respectively.
This observation underscores the importance of incorporating reasoning paths during the fine-tuning
stage to enhance the LLM’s reasoning capabilities. We also conduct an analysis to identify the reasons
behind the significant performance on GSM8K in the Appendix A.8.1. The results reveal that the
significant improvement is driven by the diverse answering strategies, which are perfectly aligned
with the specific needs of GSM8K.

6     C ONCLUSION
We have introduced ReGenesis, a novel framework designed to self-synthesize reasoning paths as
post- training data to self-improve their general reasoning capacities without requiring additional su-
pervision beyond final answers and human-designed reasoning examples. This framework effectively
self-synthesizes reasoning paths of any datasets, regardless of whether they include ground-truth
answers. Fine-tuning the language model with such self-synthesized dataset leads to significant
improvements in performance on both in-domain and out-of-domain tasks. We also investigated how
various design choices, language model selection, and filtering methods, affect the performance of
ReGenesis. In future work, we plan to explore the potential of leveraging filtered-out reasoning
paths to further enhance language model performance and to develop strategies for intelligently
selecting the most effective reasoning paths for model improvement.

Although ReGenesis uses the final detailed and task-specific reasoning paths as training data, the
diverse task-agnostic reasoning strategies that Regenesis adopts to generate such final task-specific
paths, are effectively preserved in the final task-specific paths such that it is easy for LLMs, such
as mistral-7B-instruct, to detect them and learn them from the final task-specific paths. We run the
following analysis to verify this hypothesis.
We randomly select 500 reasoning paths from ReGenesis and STaR’s generated data of NumGLUE
training data set. Note that the 500 reasoning paths of each method are all final task-specific reasoning
paths. Then, we prompt Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3 to label each of the total 1000 reasoning paths the
task-agnostic reasoning strategy behind the task-specific path. The goal of this experiment is to see
whether from the task-specific path only, LLMs can already tell the task-agnostic strategy behind it
and that ReGenesis’s task-specific paths have more diverse task-agnostic strategies. If so, it indicates
that LLMs are likely to be able to capture the task-agnostic strategies behind the task-specific paths
in the training data, even if they are trained only with task-specific paths.
