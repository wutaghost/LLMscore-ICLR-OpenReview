009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Dataset distillation or condensation aims to condense a large-scale training dataset
012            into a much smaller synthetic one such that the training performance of distilled
013            and original sets on neural networks are similar. Although the number of train-
014            ing samples can be reduced substantially, current state-of-the-art methods heavily
015            rely on enormous soft labels to achieve satisfactory performance. As a result,
016            the required storage can be comparable even to original datasets, especially for
017            large-scale ones. To solve this problem, instead of storing these heavy labels, we
018
               propose a novel label-lightening framework termed HeLlO aiming at effective
               image-to-label projectors, with which synthetic labels can be directly generated
019
               online from synthetic images. Specifically, to construct such projectors, we lever-
020
               age prior knowledge in open-source foundation models, e.g., CLIP, and introduce
021            a LoRA-like fine-tuning strategy to mitigate the gap between pre-trained and tar-
022            get distributions, so that original models for soft-label generation can be distilled
023            into a group of low-rank matrices. Moreover, an effective image optimization
024            method is proposed to further mitigate the potential error between the original
025            and distilled label generators. Extensive experiments demonstrate that with only
026            about 0.001% of the original storage required for a complete set of soft labels,
027            we achieve comparable performance to current state-of-the-art dataset distillation
028            methods on large-scale datasets. Our code will be available.

033
      Dataset distillation Wang et al. (2018) is proposed to deal with the issues caused by large-scale
      datasets, e.g., high computational overhead for training and heavy burden for storage and transmis-
034
      sion. It aims to condense a large dataset into a much smaller synthetic one, which preserves the
035
      original training performance, so that it can serve as an effective and efficient surrogate to train
036   downstream neural networks. For instance, it has been demonstrated that a network trained with
037   merely 1 synthetic image per class (IPC) can perform well on CIFAR-10 Krizhevsky et al. (2009).
038   However, with such a high compression ratio, it is challenging for the distilled sets to encapsulate
039   the whole knowledge of the original dataset used for training in a very limited space. Thus, classic
040   methods in this field like Wang et al. (2018); Zhao et al. (2020); Zhao & Bilen (2021; 2023) still
041   have a significant performance gap between the original set and the synthetic one, especially when
042   handling large-scale datasets Yu et al. (2023).
043   To compensate for such dramatic information loss, recent state-of-the-art dataset distillation meth-
044   ods Shao et al. (2024); Sun et al. (2024); Yin et al. (2024) turn to data augmentation, to make the
045   best use of the limited synthetic data. Specifically, strategies such as Mixup Zhang et al. (2017) and
046   Cutmix Yun et al. (2019) are applied in downstream network training, which effectively enhance
047   the performance of distilled datasets and scale dataset distillation up to larger and more complex
048   datasets like ImageNet Deng et al. (2009).
049   Nevertheless, these recent works heavily rely on soft labels generated by a pre-trained teacher
050   model on the original dataset. According to RDED Sun et al. (2024), networks trained with 10
051   IPC on ImageNet-1k achieve only 15.2% accuracy with categorical hard labels, compared to 42.1%
052   with soft labels. Since each augmented sample corresponds to a distinct soft label, as shown in
053   Fig. 1 (left), there are a number of generated soft labels that far exceeds the basic synthetic sam-
      ples. Consequently, storage costs for these soft labels are non-negligible, especially for large-scale

      To address the issue of such heavy labels, we propose a novel label-lightening framework termed
081   Heavy Labels Out, or HeLlO in short. Fig. 1 (right) illustrates the overall framework of the proposed
082   HeLlO. By creating an effective and lightweight projector from the image to the label space, it
083   reduces the required storage significantly. Specifically, we build the projector upon recent foundation
084   models like CLIP Radford et al. (2021) that has been pre-trained on massive data and can readily
085   adapt to various target datasets. To achieve this, we propose an effective LoRA-like Hu et al. (2021)
086   knowledge transfer method that efficiently transforms the original feature space of CLIP into that
087   of the target data. As an efficient alternative to the teacher model trained on the target dataset for
088   soft-label generation, the derived low-rank matrices can be seen as a transferable and lightweight
089
      representation for the original label space.
090   Interestingly, by leveraging the vision-language alignment capability in CLIP Zhang et al. (2022),
091   we propose initializing the projector with the textual representation of label categories, providing
092   a strong starting point that improves training and convergence. Moreover, we propose an effective
093   image optimization method to further reduce the potential error between the original and distilled
094   label generators. Our extensive experiments show that with only 0.001% of the original storage for
095
      soft labels, we achieve performance comparable to, or even better than, state-of-the-art large-scale
      dataset distillation methods.

      2     R ELATED W ORKS

      Dataset distillation or condensation Wang et al. (2018); Zhao et al. (2020); Yu et al. (2023) aims to
111   solve the issues of massive storage, transmission burden, and computational costs for downstream
112   tasks caused by large-scale datasets. Specifically, it condenses the whole knowledge of the origi-
113   nal large-scale datasets into a much smaller space and preserves the performance. The mainstream
114   dataset distillation methods can be roughly classified into three categories, according to their opti-
115   mization objectives: performance matching Wang et al. (2018); Deng & Russakovsky (2022); Loo
116   et al. (2022); Zhou et al. (2022); Nguyen et al. (2020; 2021), parameter matching Zhao et al. (2020);
117   Zhao & Bilen (2021); Cazenavette et al. (2022); Cui et al. (2023); Du et al. (2023); Guo et al. (2023);
118   Liu et al. (2022a) and distribution matching Zhao & Bilen (2023); Wang et al. (2022); Zhao et al.
119
      (2023); Sajedi et al. (2023).
120   Traditional dataset distillation methods suffer scaling-up problems due to the bi-level optimization
121   problems, such that the gradients should backpropagate through an unrolled computation graph Yu
122   et al. (2023). Recent work SRe2 L Yin et al. (2024) proposes a variant distribution matching
123   paradigm to decouple the bi-level optimization and scales up to the full-size ImageNet-1K dataset.
124   It matches the distribution in feature space of the synthetic dataset and the statistical information
125
      of the original dataset stored in the batch normalization layers of the pre-trained model. Further,
      G VBSM Shao et al. (2024) utilize multiple pre-trained teachers to provide more statistical infor-
126
      mation and improve the transferability across different architectures. RDED Sun et al. (2024) is the
127
      current state-of-the-art large-scale dataset distillation method, which is based on selection instead of
128   synthesizing. It selects and concatenates the most representative patches evaluated by the pre-trained
129   teacher model.
130
131
      However, due to the significant reduction in the size of the datasets, an apparent performance gap
      still exists between the original dataset and the distilled one. For small-scale dataset distillation, a
132
      series of works Bohdal et al. (2020); Sucholutsky & Schonlau (2021); Cui et al. (2023); Deng & Rus-
133
      sakovsky (2022); Nguyen et al. (2021); Loo et al. (2022); Zhou et al. (2022) expand the label space
134   by transforming the one-hot labels to soft labels, which apparently improve the performance for
135   downstream tasks and also provides a new perspective to condense dataset comprehensively. How-
136   ever, simply transforming the one-hot label to a soft label for each synthetic image is not effective
137   for large-scale dataset distillation, as the plain soft labels do not provide sufficient extra knowledge
138   for downstream tasks. In order to solve this issue and compensate for the huge reduction in the num-
139   ber of data, current large-scale dataset distillation methods Shao et al. (2024); Sun et al. (2024); Yin
140   et al. (2024) adopt the extensive data augmentation strategies, e.g., Mixup Zhang et al. (2017) and
141   Cutmix Yun et al. (2019), and generate soft labels for each augmented image. It will increase the
142
      diversity of the distilled data for downstream training, and significantly improve the performance for
      downstream tasks. However, generating such labels requires restoring huge amount of soft labels,
143
      and for large-scale datasets, it will cause non-negligible storage costs. Focusing on this issue, our
144
      proposed method only requires 0.001% storage space while obtaining comparable performance with
145   the state-of-the-art large-scale dataset distillation methods.

166   3.2   E FFICIENT I NITIALIZATION OF S URROGATE P ROJECTION
167
168   To effectively and efficiently transfer the label space in a lightweight way and easily adapt it to
169   different datasets, we adopt the open-source and pre-trained foundation model, CLIP, as our base
170   model. It does not require extra storage space and can be accessed on demand. Specifically, we
171   adopt the paradigm of linear probe CLIP by utilizing the image encoder part of CLIP and following
172   with a linear transformation. The image encoder of CLIP is pre-trained on numerous paired data and
173
      can provide accurate and knowledge-rich features, which makes the linear probe CLIP a powerful
      classifier. Here, the parameters required to store is only the linear transformation part.
174
175   However, the storage cost of the linear transformation part depends on the number of classes of the
176   original dataset, which will be non-negligible for large-scale datasets with a large number of classes.
177   Also, there still exists a gap between the original label space and the lightening one, which may
178   make transferring to downstream tasks difficult. Here, to reduce the storage cost for the linear trans-
179
      formation part and improve the ability to transfer, we propose a novel storage-efficient initialization
      strategy. Here, given a pre-trained multi-modal foundation model, e.g., CLIP, we denote the image
180
      encoder part as EI and the text part as ET . For any dataset D = (X, Y ), we can simply obtain the
181
      text descriptions R = {r(i) }C−1i=0 for the whole dataset by utilizing the vanilla prompt engineering
182
      technique Radford et al. (2021) with fixed templates. We adopt the fixed normalized text embedding
183   of the descriptions for all classes as the initialization of the linear transformation, which significantly
184   saves storage space as we do not need to store the initial parameters. Also, it can improve the ba-
185   sic performance of our label projector as the proposed initialization is equivalent to the pre-trained
186   zero-shot classification. Following we will provide the theoretical analysis.

210   As mentioned before, we adopt the fixed initialization for the linear transformation part, which will
211   not introduce any extra storage costs and can improve the basic classification ability of the linear

213   lightening one, which may cause difficulties transferring to downstream tasks. Here, one typical
214   way to solve the above issues is fine-tuning the whole projector to the target label space, but it
215   requires huge extra computational costs to train the complex foundation model and non-negligible
      storage space to save the tuned parameters.

      5     C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this paper, we propose a novel label-lightening framework termed HeLlO, aiming to solve the
531
      heavy-label issue in large-scale dataset distillation. Our method involves an effective image-to-label
      projector, with which the synthetic labels can be directly generated online from synthetic images
532
      during training downstream networks. Specifically, we leverage the prior knowledge in open-source
533
      foundation models and introduce a parameter-efficient LoRA-like fine-tuning method to narrow the
534   gap between the label distribution of the pre-trained and target ones, which improves the trans-
535   ferability of the projector to the downstream tasks as well. Moreover, we propose a text-guided
536   initialization strategy for the projector that enhances training. To further address the gap between
537   the original label generator and the projector, we also develop a strategy to optimize synthetic im-
538   ages within the projector. Extensive experiments demonstrate that the proposed HeLlO achieves
539   performance comparable or even superior to current state-of-the-art dataset distillation techniques
      while using just about 0.001% of the original label storage space.
