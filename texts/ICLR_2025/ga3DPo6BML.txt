009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In this work, we propose a unified representation for Super-Resolution (SR) and
012            Image Compression, termed Factorized Fields, motivated by the shared principles
013            between these two tasks. Both SISR and Image Compression require recovering
014            and preserving fine image details—whether by enhancing resolution or recon-
015            structing compressed data. Unlike previous methods that mainly focus network
016            architecture, our proposed approach utilizes a basis-coefficient decomposition to
017
               explicitly capture multi-scale visual features and structural components in images,
               addressing the core challenges of both tasks. We first derive our SR model, which
018
               includes Coefficient Backbone and Basis Swin Transformer for generalizable Fac-
019
               torized Fields. Then, to further unify these two tasks, we leverage the strong
020            information-recovery capabilities of the trained SR modules as priors in the com-
021            pression pipeline, improving both compression efficiency and detail reconstruction.
022            Additionally, we introduce a merged-basis compression branch that consolidates
023            shared structures, further optimizing the compression process. Extensive experi-
024            ments show that our unified representation delivers state-of-the-art performance,
025            achieving an average relative improvement of 204.4% in PSNR over the baseline
026            in Super-Resolution (SR) and 9.35% BD-rate reduction in Image Compression
027            compared to the previous SOTA.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031   Single Image Super-Resolution (SISR) aims to reconstruct high-quality images from low-resolution
032   counterparts. Specifically, the key lies in accurately restoring fine details and reconstructing the
033   correct arrangement of visual features. Thus, geometric correspondences or repetitive patterns, such
034   as stripes, grids, or textures, are commonly used for evaluation due to their rich details that are
035   crucial to image fidelity. Early CNN-based approaches Lim et al. (2017b); Dong et al. (2014)laid
036   the foundation for SISR, which was later enhanced with GAN-based methods Ledig et al. (2017a)
037   for improved perceptual realism. Follow-up Transformer-based networks Chen et al. (2021) address
038   non-local dependencies, with subsequent Swin Transformer-based approaches sparking tremendous
039
      advancements Conde et al. (2022)Chen et al. (2023d)Chen et al. (2024), which then inspire more
      and more complex, delicate designs of heavy network architectures in SISR. However, these prior
040
      works have primarily focused on network architecture design rather than addressing the capability of
041
      representation. The visual patterns and the inherent nature of image content structure that play an
042   influential role in SISR have not been explicitly considered in the representation learning process.
043   This raises a critical question: beyond a simple network output, can we derive a formulation that
044   more effectively captures these patterns and aligns with the goals of SISR?
045
      On the other hand, image compression serves as a fundamental task in low-level vision applications,
046
      where the traditional compression standards Joint Video Experts Team (JVET) (2023); Wallace
047
      (1992); Taubman & Marcellin (2013) lay the ground work. The emerging learned image compression
048   models Chamain et al. (2021); Ballé et al. (2018a); Guo-Hua et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023a); Minnen
049   et al. (2018); Cheng et al. (2020), compression algorithms of which mostly follow the pixel-space
050   transform coding Chamain et al. (2021); Goyal (2001)paradigm, then introduce neural networks to
051   further optimize compression efficiency by learning more compact latent representations and improv-
052   ing reconstruction quality. Specifically, they convert pixels into compact representations through a
053   transform module, which eliminates the redundancy to reduce the bit cost in the subsequent entropy
      coding process. However, the core challenge of image compression is to accurately reconstruct the


                                                        1

054
      information lost during compression and quantization. In other words, the models are to recover the
055   impaired components in images, which can essentially be viewed as reconstructing a high-quality
056   image from its ‘low-resolution’ version, much like Super-Resolution.
057
058
      Based on the aforementioned analysis, it becomes clear that although Super-Resolution and Image
      Compression appear to be two distinct tasks, they share mutual similarities in two key aspects: (1)
059
      Both tasks require models to restore fine details from low-quality image content, as well as implicitly
060
      capture and reconstruct repetitive structural elements. (2) Both aim to conserve image quality, either
061   by enhancing resolution or efficiently compressing data without significant loss of perceptual fidelity.
062
063   Hence, inspired by recent advances in decomposition fields and matrices factorization in 3D scene
064
      modeling Chen et al. (2022a); Müller et al. (2022); Chan et al. (2021); Fridovich-Keil et al. (2023);
      Cao & Johnson (2023); Chen et al. (2023a); Gao et al. (2023), we propose a unified representation,
065
      Factorized Fields, with generalizable Coefficient Backbone and Basis Transformer. This approach
066
      explicitly captures multi-scale visual features and repetitive structural components in images through a
067   basis-coefficient decomposition. The resulting representation strikes a balance between being compact
068   and information-rich, enabling the resolution of structural ambiguities and the precise modeling of
069   image details through a multi-frequency formulation. In the meantime, such a formulation imposes a
070   factorization constraint during model training, which not only enhances the quality of single-image
071   super-resolution (SISR) but also reduces distortion and improves compression efficiency by explicitly
072   modeling structural elements. On top of these, to leverage the robust information-recovering capability
073   of SR models, we integrate such priors with Image Compression models, whose knowledge of detail
074   compensation further refines the lost key elements.
075   Finally, we propose merging the bases by introducing an additional compression branch, consolidating
076   multiple bases into one alongside multi-image transmission. This approach leverages the mutual
077   information across multiple images, reducing the need for redundant transmissions and refining the
078   basis structure. The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
079
             • We propose Factorized Fields, a unified representation that explicitly models multi-scale
080
               visual features and structural components for both super-resolution and image compression.
081
082          • We integrate super-resolution with image compression by introducing SR prior during
083
               decompression to compensate for lost details and developing a merged-basis compression
               branch for multi-image compression.
084
085          • We demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on benchmarks for both super-resolution and
086
               image compression through extensive experiments.

      Super-Resolution (SR). Image super-resolution is critical in computer vision, focusing on recover-
090
      ing high-resolution (HR) images from low-resolution (LR) inputs. Following the foundational studies,
091   CNN-based strategies Dong et al. (2014)Lim et al. (2017b)Zhou et al. (2020)Sun et al. (2022)Kim
092   et al. (2016) were initially introduced with modeling techniques such as residual learning Ledig et al.
093   (2017b)Liu et al. (2020)Tong et al. (2017)Zhang et al. (2018b)Zhang et al. (2018a)Chih-Chung Hsu
094   (2023)Lim et al. (2017a) , or recursive learning Chen et al. (2024)Tai et al. (2017). Besides, subse-
095   quent research also sheds light on GAN-base methodsLedig et al. (2017a)Ledig et al. (2017b)Wang
096   et al. (2019)Wang et al. (2021) to enhance realisticity and detail quality. However, the inductive
097   bias of CNN-based networks by restricting spatial locality hinders the capture of long-range depen-
098   dency from images, which is alleviated by Transformer-based SISR networks Chen et al. (2021)Li
099
      et al. (2021). Afterward, SwinIRLiang et al. (2021) is proposed to combine spatial locality and
      non-local information by Swin TransformerLiu et al. (2021) with window attention and achieve
100
      breakthrough improvement in SISR. Following SwinIR’s success, several works have built upon its
101
      frameworkConde et al. (2022)Zhu et al. (2023)Zhang et al. (2024a) to reach better image quality
102   as well as solve information bottleneckChih-Chung Hsu (2023). Hybrid approaches CRAFT (Li
103   et al., 2023) merge the benefits of convolutional and transformer structures to further elevate SR
104   performance. For better feature aggregation, DAT (Chen et al., 2023d) and HAT (Chen et al., 2023b)
105   integrate spatial and channel information using attention mechanisms to enhance their representation
106   capabilities. Moreover, RGT (Chen et al., 2024) introduces a unique recursive-generalization self-
107   attention mechanism that efficiently captures comprehensive spatial details with a linear increase in
      computational complexity.

136   and CNNs to capture both global and local image features. The eContextformer Koyuncu et al.
137   (2024) introduced patch-wise, checkered, and channel-wise grouping techniques for parallel context
138   modeling with a shifted window spatial-channel attention mechanism. GroupedMixer Li et al. (2024)
139   proposed a transformer-based entropy model with token mixers for inner and cross-group context
140   modeling. Meanwhile, Wavelet Conditional Diffusion Song et al. (2024) introduced a wavelet-based
141   model with uncertainty-aware loss, balancing high perceptual quality with low distortion.
142   In summary, while recent super-resolution and image compression advances focus on increasingly
143   complex architectures, our work takes a different approach. We propose Factorized Fields, a unified
144   framework that models visual and structural features, offering a more comprehensive solution to
145   enhance performance in both tasks.

197
      Compression and Super-Resolution lies in the capability of the representation to capture accurate
198   structural distribution and fine visual details. Meanwhile, transformations such as Fourier Transform
199   or Wavelet Transform have long been used to model multi-frequency information Fuoli et al. (2021);
200   Korkmaz et al. (2024) to express different implicit functions in images; however, such methods often
201   suffer from under-expression due to the pre-defined and limited frequency bands or restriction from
202   its formulation. Thus, we seek a representation that explicitly incorporates and fits multi-scale and
203   multi-frequency components and yet is highly flexible and learnable according to individual features
204   in an image.

206   (2023a; 2022a); Müller et al. (2022); Cao & Johnson (2023); Chan et al. (2021), we primarily built

298   3.4     I MAGE C OMPRESSION WITH FACTORIZED F IELDS AND S UPER -R ESOLUTION
299   Image Compression, at its core, is to strike a balance between the amount of information contained
300   in the latent bits and the final image quality. However, as discussed in Sec. 1, 2, and 3.2, most
301   recent works draw emphasis on how to retrieve implicit elements through designing different archi-
302   tectures, such as analysis transforms and entropy models Koyuncu et al. (2024); Li et al. (2024), or
303   decompression modules Song et al. (2024); Duan et al. (2023), while this paper aims at addressing
304   the representation itself to better capture the structural correlations and thus acheive better image
305   quality through explicit modeling of freqeuncy components and Factorized Fields formulation as in
306
      Eq. 7. In addition, with our trained SR model described in Sec. 3.3, it intuitively serves as a strong
      prior for information recovery, i.e., it contains extensive knowledge of how to reconstruct missing
307
      details and enhance image quality by leveraging learned patterns from the training data. Thus, since
308
      Super-Resolution and Image Compression share the core principle of reconstructing and enhancing
309   image details from low-quality sources, we can effectively integrate this prior into the compression
310   pipeline. In the following, we respectively present the proposed joint framework for the single and
311   multi-image compression tasks.

534   We proposed Factorized Fields, a representation that models implicit structures and patterns by
535   decomposing images into multi-frequency components. This approach addresses challenges in Super-
536   Resolution and Image Compression by restoring details and preserving visual fidelity. We integrate
