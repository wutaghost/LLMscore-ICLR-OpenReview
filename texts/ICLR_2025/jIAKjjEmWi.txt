009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Advancements in deep learning are driven by training models with increasingly
012
               larger numbers of parameters, which in turn heightens the computational de-
013
               mands. To address this issue, Mixture-of-Depths (MoD) models have been pro-
014            posed to dynamically assign computations only to the most relevant parts of the
015            inputs, thereby enabling the deployment of large-parameter models with high effi-
016            ciency during inference and training. These MoD models utilize a routing mecha-
017            nism to determine which tokens should be processed by a layer, or skipped. How-
018            ever, conventional MoD models employ additional network layers specifically
019            for the routing which are difficult to train, and add complexity and deployment
020            overhead to the model. In this paper, we introduce a novel attention-based rout-
021            ing mechanism A-MoD that leverages the existing attention map of the preceding
022
               layer for routing decisions within the current layer. Compared to standard routing,
               A-MoD allows for more efficient training as it introduces no additional trainable
023
               parameters and can be easily adapted from pre-trained transformer models. Fur-
024
               thermore, it can increase the performance of the MoD model. For instance, we
025            observe up to 2% higher accuracy on ImageNet compared to standard routing and
026            isoFLOP ViT baselines. Furthermore, A-MoD improves the MoD training conver-
027            gence, leading to up to 2× faster transfer learning.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Increasing the model size has enabled transformer-based deep learning models to achieve state-of-
034   the-art performance across various domains, including computer vision (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021)
035   and natural language processing (Hoffmann et al., 2022; Kaplan et al., 2020) – even unlocking
036   emergent capabilities (Wei et al., 2022). However, the computational costs of these large models
037   present significant challenges (Thompson et al., 2020). Therefore, reaching a Pareto-optimal model
038
      to maximize both efficiency and performance is crucial.
039   Jacobs et al. (1991) originally introduced conditional computation via mixture of experts, laying
040   the foundations to increase model sizes while maintaining FLOPs, by dynamically activating only
041   a subset of the model parameters, termed experts, conditioned on the input. This principle allowed
042   scaling towards outrageously large networks (Shazeer et al., 2016) and is leveraged at the forefront
043   of current Large Language Models (LLMs) (Jiang et al., 2024).
044   Compared to standard deep learning models Dosovitskiy et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2024); He et al.
045   (2016), dynamic models have received less research attention and are often not yet competitive on
046   the Pareto front of performance and runtime on standard GPU architectures. Here, we focus on
047   further advancing the field of dynamic compute.
048   Recently, Raposo et al. (2024) introduced Mixture-of-Depths (MoD) as a variant of mixture of
049   experts. In MoD models, the computational costs are dynamically reduced by processing only a
050   subset of tokens in a layer while the remaining tokens skip the layer (see Fig. 2a). Compared
051   to baselines with equivalent FLOPs, MoDs can perform favorably on language tasks. A crucial
052   component of MoD is its router, which receives tokens as inputs and, given a user-defined capacity,
053   determines which tokens should enter or skip a layer. The router usually consists of a linear layer
      that is jointly trained along with the model (Fig. 2b).

090   The routing mechanisms heavily influence the model performance for several reasons. First, routing
091   introduces noise into the training process, as the routing is a discrete decision and is often performed
092   at multiple layers and per token. Second, routers depend on additional layers, and hence, need to be
093   trained from scratch when adapting a vanilla pretrained model to an MoD model. Lastly, the router
094   adds a small computational overhead to the sparsified model.
095
      Hence, in this paper, we ask and address the question: Can we improve the routing mechanism in
096   MoD models based on information that is already available within the model, instead of using ad-
097   ditional trainable parameters within the router? We find the answer to our question in the attention
098   mechanism of commonly used transformer architectures (Vaswani et al., 2017; Dosovitskiy et al.,
099   2021).
100
      We assume that the attention maps can be used to estimate the importance of a token, by averaging
101
      its interaction with other tokens. Based on that, we propose to aggregate the information in the
102   attention maps and use it as an importance measure for token routing in MoD. We call our method
103   attention routing for MoD: A-MoD (Fig. 2c). We find that A-MoD can outperform standard routing
104   in MoD networks across a range of model sizes and tasks consistently (as shown in Fig. 2). Not only
105   is our A-MoD parameter-free, but it can also be applied to adapt off-the-shelf pretrained transformer
106   models to MoDs with almost no additional training. We further validate our method empirically and
107   show that routing scores computed by A-MoD are better correlated with token importance estimates
      compared to routing scores from standard routers.

      Attention Maps The attention mechanism (Bahdanau, 2014) enables models to learn long-range
126   dependencies within sequences with a constant number of operations. Transformers (Vaswani et al.,
127   2017) leverage the attention mechanism in the language domain and have become a de facto standard
128   model. Dosovitskiy et al. (2021) further adapted transformers to the vision domain by treating
129   image patches as tokens, introducing the vision transformer (ViT). For images, attention maps have
130   been shown to focus on key areas such as objects in the image (Carion et al., 2020a; Jetley et al.,
131   2018). In this paper, we utilize this property for effective routing of tokens within neural networks.
132   Furthermore, we use the Data-efficient image Transformers DeiT-T and DeiT-S (Touvron et al.,
133   2021) instead of vanilla ViT-T and ViT-S models, as small ViTs do not generalize well when trained
134
      on smaller datasets (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021).
135
136   Mixture of Experts and Mixture-of-Depths Since their introduction over three decades ago (Ja-
137   cobs et al., 1991; Jordan & Jacobs, 1993), Mixture of Experts (MoE) have been applied to various
138
      model types. Shazeer et al. (2016) introduced MoEs to scale transformer architectures (Ludziejew-
      ski et al., 2024). Subsequently, MoEs have achieved extensive empirical success across vision and
139
      language tasks (Puigcerver et al., 2024; Jain et al., 2024; Fedus et al., 2022a; Riquelme et al., 2021).
140
      One of the main challenges when training MoE networks is training instability (Zoph et al., 2022;
141   Fedus et al., 2022b). Raposo et al. (2024) recently introduced the Mixture-of-Depths (MoD) ar-
142   chitecture, where each transformer block processes only a subset of tokens, achieving a favorable
143   compute-performance trade-off compared to large transformer models. Liang et al. (2022) also in-
144   duce sparsity by fusing tokens together without entirely skipping tokens. In their current form,
145   both MoEs and MoDs use dedicated routing networks that decide which components of the overall
146   network process which tokens. The difference between MoEs and MoDs is that an MoE model
147   comprises several distinct experts that independently process the tokens. In contrast, an MoD model
148   only chooses between two experts per layer one of which is the layer itself and the other an identity
149
      function, see Fig. 2.
150
151   Routing Methods Routing mechanisms are required for most conditional computation blocks (Cai
152   et al., 2024). In MoE models for transformers, the purpose of the router is to match tokens to experts
153
      such that performance is maximized. In case of models with a single expert such as Switch Trans-
      formers (Fedus et al., 2022b) or MoD, routers decide whether a token will benefit from processing
154
      by the expert or will be skipped. Various methods (Liu et al., 2024) have been proposed such as
155
      learned routers (Shazeer et al., 2016) with token choice or expert choice routing (Zhou et al., 2022),
156   solving a linear program to match tokens to experts (Lewis et al., 2021), hashing inputs to match
157   experts (Roller et al., 2021) or using reinforcement learning to make routing decisions (Clark et al.,
158   2022; Bengio et al., 2015; 2013). Explicitly learning the routers is the current state-of-the-art that
159   outperforms other methods in most cases (Dikkala et al., 2023). However, this approach mainly
160   proves effective with a larger number of routing parameters and is prone to training instabilities
161   (Ramachandran & Le, 2019). Thus, training routers that consistently lead to strong performance
      remains an open problem.

164   in a parameter-free manner without any extra computational overhead.

167   3     M ETHOD
168
169   In this section, we explain the Mixture-of-Depths (MoD) architecture and introduce our attention-
170   based MoD routing algorithm, A-MoD, that can be employed to improve its routing.
171
172   3.1   M IXTURE - OF -D EPTHS
173
174   Our work focuses on Vision Transformers (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Touvron et al., 2021). Here,
175   given an input in terms of tokens X, the output predictions are calculated by a model f (X; Θ)
176   consisting of L Transformer blocks parameterized by a set of learnable weights Θ. Each transformer
177
      block includes a Multi-Head Self-Attention (MHSA) with H heads, followed by a two-layers fully-
      connected network with GeLU activations (MLP).
178
179   In MoD, Raposo et al. (2024) introduce a variation of transformer-based architectures with the as-
180   sumption that individual tokens require varying amounts of compute within a model. In particular,
181   MoD layers only process a subset of selected important tokens, while the remaining tokens skip the
182   layer. Empirically, this procedure can improve the performance over a vanilla ViT with a comparable
183
      compute budget.
184   Whether or not tokens skip a layer is determined by token importance scores estimated by a routing
185   algorithm. Conventionally, standard routing computes these importance scores with additional lay-
186   ers (see Section 3.2). In contrast, our A-MoD computes the scores directly from the attention maps
187   of previous layers without the need of additional parameters (see Section 3.3).
188

246   4.1   T RAINING SETUP AND OVERVIEW
247
      In our experiments, we systematically evaluate A-MoD and empirically demonstrate its benefits
248
      over standard routing for MoDs. We perform evaluations across a range of model architectures and
249
      multiple image classification tasks. In each experiment, we train a MoD, adapted from a vanilla
250   pretrained transformer model. We conduct experiments on both finetuning the adapted MoD model
251   on the same dataset used for training the vanilla pretrained transformer model and transfer learning
252   on different datasets.
253
254   Training setup We evaluate A-MoD across four vision transformer architectures of varying sizes:
255   DeiT-Tiny, DeiT-Small (Touvron et al., 2021), ViT-Base and ViT-Large (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021).
256   Each MoD architecture is adapted from a vanilla pretrained checkpoint on ImageNet-1k (Rus-
257   sakovsky et al., 2015). Starting from this checkpoint, we train the MoD models with 50% and
258   12.5% capacity as described in Eq. (5), i.e., 50% and 12.5% tokens are processed in each MoD
259   layer, respectively. Following Raposo et al. (2024), we alternate between MoD layers and dense
260
      layers in our MoD architecture i.e. every second layer is an MoD layer. We also analyze the effect
      of placing MoD layers only in the later layers of the model in Section 4.5.
261
262
      Finetuning We finetune the MoD models on ImageNet-1k. For each case, we compare our A-MoD
263
      to standard routing. We also compare both MoD variants to an isoFLOP vanilla vision transformer.
264   This isoFLOP model is obtained by appropriately reducing the number of layers of the original
265   model to match the number of FLOPs of its MoD counterpart. Only reducing the layers still allows
266   the isoFLOP model to benefit from the weights of the pretrained checkpoint. Each model is trained
267   with the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) for 100 epochs using a batch size of 128
268   and a learning rate of 1e − 5 with a linear warmup followed by cosine annealing. We identify
269   this learning rate schedule after performing a sweep as shown in Figs. 12(a), 12(b) and 13 in the
      Appendix.

      Token importance Finally, we conduct a comparison between the routing scores computed by
295
      standard routing and A-MoD, with a reference score that measures the importance of each token.
296
      This analysis enables us to further distinguish the benefits of A-MoD. We use a leave-one-out method
297   (Hastie et al., 2009) to estimate the token importance. In particular, we measure the change in the
298   loss of the model if a certain token is removed at an MoD layer. This allows us to assign a reference

300   with the our routing weights for each MoD layer and token, both, for our attention-based routing

302   scores also correlate strongly with the leave-one-out token importance.

410   4.4      ATTENTION ROUTING IDENTIFIES IMPORTANT TOKENS
411
412   To understand why A-MoD improves over standard routing, we investigate the routing scores and
413   their correlation with leave-one-out (Hastie et al., 2009) token importance. Our goal is to estimate
414   the relationship between the importance of a token and the routing score assigned to it by a standard
415   or A-MoD router. Based on our empirical results, we conjecture that A-MoD weights are better
416
      correlated with token importance in comparison with standard routing, thus enabling A-MoD to
      always choose the most relevant tokens.
417
418   We first verify this claim by visualizing the routing in case of individual examples from ImageNet-
419   1k as shown in Fig. 5. The figure highlights which patches of the image are chosen by the router
420   in each MoD layer. In case of A-MoD (bottom), the router selects tokens that are part of the bird
421   outline and face starting from the third MoD layer. In contrast, standard routing (top) selects more
422
      tokens that are part of the background, up to the last layer.
423   Visualizing the attention maps of the last layer in Fig. 6 also confirms that A-MoD is able to focus
424   on the object in the image, which we use as routing scores. The attention map for each head in the
425   last layer for DeiT-Small identifies the silhouette of the bird for A-MoD, but struggles for standard
426   routing. However, as shown by Darcet et al. (2024), attention maps do not always learn semanti-
427   cally meaningful scores. This holds especially for larger models, where the attention scores tend to
428
      concentrate on a single patch (token) (see Fig. 15 in the Appendix).

430   importance estimates. For the importance of a token, we compute the change in loss of the model if
431   that token is omitted in the vanilla transformer i.e. leave-one-out token importance. A large change
      in loss implies higher token importance and we would expect that token to have a higher routing

473   4.5      I MPACT OF M O D IN DIFFERENT LAYERS
474
475   In our experiments so far, MoD layers are used in alternate layers following Raposo et al. (2024).
476   This model architecture gives us Pareto-optimal results on ImageNet-1k (see Table 1). We conduct
477   an ablation study to investigate whether introducing MoDs only in the later layers and keeping the
478   initial layers dense is advantageous, particularly for visual tasks like classification, where learning
479   low-level features may be critical. In order to verify if MoDs benefit from additional feature learning
480   at full capacity in the earlier layers, we introduce MoD layers alternately starting from the 4-th layer,
481   keeping the first four layers dense.
482   Results in Fig. 8 show that keeping the first four layers dense improves on DeiT-Small and ViT-Base
483   models trained on the Stanford Cars dataset. The additional FLOPs allows for better learning in
484   this regime as shown in Fig. 8. With this modification, A-MoD is able to match the corresponding
485   isoFLOP baseline, even for transfer learning tasks. This highlights a potential method to address the
      limitations of A-MoD mentioned in Section 4.3 at the cost of additional FLOPs.

501
502   We propose A-MoD, a variation of Mixture-of-Depths (MoD) with attention routing instead of a
503
      standard router. To compute token importance for an MoD layer, A-MoD utilizes the attention
      maps from its previous layer, thereby achieving attention routing without additional parameters. In
504
      case of training from a pretrained checkpoint, leveraging trained attention information also leads to
505
      increased training stability and faster convergence compared to vanilla MoD. Furthermore, we em-
506   pirically demonstrate that A-MoD outperforms standard MoD across different model configurations
507   and datasets while making better routing decisions.

860   scores for individual tokens in line 3-7. The scores will then be normalized and accumulate to the
861   final routing weights R in line 8 − 9. It must be noted that we swap the order of tiling from row
862   first (as in Dao et al. (2022)) to column first in order to aggregate row wise scores efficiently. Thus
863   in each iteration only the Q can be cached while the K and V need to be loaded for each tile. Our
      algorithm introduces a small memory overhead of O(N ) due to additional temporary variables.
