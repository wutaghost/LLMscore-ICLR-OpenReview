009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Diffusion models are renowned for their state-of-the-art performance in gener-
012            ating high-quality images. Identifying samples with new information beyond
013            the training data is essential for data augmentation, especially for enhancing
014            model performance in diverse and unforeseen real-world scenarios. However,
015            the investigation of new information in the generated samples has not been well
016            explored. Our investigation through the lens of information theory reveals that
017            diffusion models do not produce new information beyond what exists in the train-
018            ing data. Next, we introduce the concept of diverse samples (DS) to prove that
019            generated images could contain information not present in the training data for
020
               diffusion models. Furthermore, we propose a method for identifying diverse sam-
               ples among generated images by extracting deep features and detecting images
021
               that fall outside the boundary of real images. We demonstrate that diverse sam-
022
               ples exist in the generated data of diffusion models, attributed to the estimation
023            of forward and backward processes, but it can only produce a limited number
024            of diverse samples, underscoring a notable gap in their capabilities in generat-
025            ing diverse samples. In addition, our experiment on the Chest X-ray dataset
026            demonstrates that the diverse samples are more useful in improving classification
027            accuracy than vanilla-generated samples. The source code is available at https:
028            //github.com/lypz12024/diffusion-diverse-samples.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Diffusion models (DMs) have recently gained significant attention for their capacity to generate
034
      high-quality samples. However, the potential of these models to generate samples containing more
035
      information than the original training data remains unexplored. If the generated samples merely
036   replicate the information in the training data, their utility for augmenting datasets in downstream
037   tasks could be limited. This limitation is particularly critical in the augmentation of limited data.
038   Therefore, exploring whether generated samples can offer new and diverse information is crucial
039   for advancing artificial intelligence (AI) capabilities in these domains. Efficient AI models require
040   vast and varied datasets for optimal performance, and relying solely on limited training data can
041   hinder their development. By generating high-quality images with rich new and useful information,
042   researchers can create more robust and effective models. This research aims to explore the potential of
043   diffusion models to produce such diverse samples. Our key contributions in this paper are threefold,
044   Mathematical Analysis. We rigorously analyze diffusion models using information theory, demon-
045   strating that these models do not introduce new information beyond what training data contain. By
046   examining entropy, mutual information, and Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence, we show that the
047   entropy of generated images closely aligns with that of the original data, indicating no additional
048   information is created.
049   Diverse Samples (DS) Metric. We introduce diverse samples as a subset of generated images that
050   contain more information and variability than the original training images. We propose a diverse
051   sample metric to quantify the variability of synthetic images generated by diffusion models. Using
052   deep features extracted from real and generated images, we identify diverse samples—those falling
053   outside the boundary of real images. This metric offers a novel way of assessing the diversity of
      generated images.

      3.1   I NFORMATION THEORY
181   Information theory, founded by (Shannon, 1948), is a mathematical framework for quantifying infor-
182   mation transmission, processing, and storage. Key concepts include: (1) Entropy (H): Measures the
183   uncertainty or randomness in a random variable or probability distribution; (2) Mutual Information
184   (I): Quantifies the amount of information obtained about one random variable through another. and (3)
185   Kullback-Leibler (KL) Divergence: Measures the difference between two probability distributions.
186

      3.3   N O INFORMATION GAIN DURING FORWARD AND BACKWARD PROCESSES
231
232   To further demonstrate that no new information is generated in the forward and backward processes
233   of diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020a; Rombach et al., 2022), we define the mutual
234   information I(x0 ; xT ) to quantify the shared information between the original image x0 and the
235   noising image xT .
236
      Forward process. The forward process is typically defined as a sequence of transformations that
237
      gradually add noise to the data, making it more random over time. Mathematically, the forward
238   process can be represented as:
239                                                     p
240                               q(xt |xt−1 ) = N (xt ; 1 − βt xt−1 , βt I),                     (8)
241
242   where βt is the noise variance, and N denotes the normal distribution. For the forward process (noise
243
      addition), we have:
244                                         I(x0 ; xt ) ≤ I(x0 ; xt−1 ),                                (9)
245   when the noise is added, the mutual information decreases since xt is more random and has less
246   information about x0 .
247

248
      forward process by gradually removing the noise to recover the original data. The backward process
249   is given by:
250

252   where µθ and σθ are learned parameters that define the mean and variance of the reverse process at

      3.4     E XISTENCE OF N EW I NFORMATION : D IVERSE S AMPLES (DS) IN THE GENERATED DATA
271
272   What are Diverse Samples? We introduce Diverse Samples (DS) as the subset of generated images
273   from diffusion models that contain distinct and significant variations from the original training data.
274   These diverse samples represent new information not captured by the training data. This concept is
275   crucial for evaluating the diversity and potential utility of synthetic data, particularly in scenarios
276   where augmenting datasets with truly varied examples is necessary for improving model performance.
277   To show that DS exists, we demonstrate that Pmodel cannot perfectly match PX in high-dimensional
278
      spaces, leading to some generated samples falling outside the distribution of the training data.

311   features using t-SNE. By plotting the t-SNE representations, we define a boundary around the points
312   corresponding to real images. Generated images whose t-SNE representations fall outside this
313   boundary are identified as diverse samples. The process of calculating diverse samples follows the

411
      et al., 2016) model on the original dataset and observed that the accuracy for the minority class,
      NORMAL, is relatively low at 43.59%, while the accuracy for the majority class, PNEUMONIA, is
412
      high at 99.74%. This results in an overall accuracy of 78.69%. To address the class imbalance, we
413
      augment the training set for the NORMAL class with vanilla-generated samples (VGS), matching
414   the number of images in the PNEUMONIA class. This augmentation increases the accuracy for
415   the NORMAL class by 5.98 percentage points, reaching 49.57%, and slightly improves the overall
416   accuracy by 2.24 percentage points, raising it to 80.93%.
417
      We then replace the vanilla-generated samples with diverse samples to introduce more variability
418
      in the training data. This approach significantly enhances the accuracy for the NORMAL class by
419
      16.24%, raising it to 59.83%, and boosts the overall accuracy by 5.60%, increasing it to 84.29%. These
420   results highlight the effectiveness of incorporating diverse samples in mitigating class imbalance,
421   leading to improved classification performance and better model generalization, particularly for the
422   underrepresented class. However, the accuracy for the PNEUMONIA class slightly decreases when
423   using diverse samples, from 99.74% with the original images and vanilla-generated samples to 98.97%
424   with diverse samples. This reduction in accuracy could be attributed to more variability and potentially
425   noisier samples that, while beneficial for improving the classification of the minority class, may
426   increase the complexity of the decision boundary for the majority class. This trade-off emphasizes
427   the need for careful consideration when augmenting data with diverse samples, particularly in cases
428
      where class balance and overall accuracy are critical.

477
478
      In this paper, we find that diffusion models excel at generating high-resolution images, but they
479
      primarily replicate the information found in the training data, offering limited diversity. We prove that
480   no new information is generated in an ideal diffusion model. By introducing the concept of diverse
481   samples (DS) and using deep feature extraction with boundary-based analysis, we calculate the rare
482   instances where generated images differ from the training data, highlighting the restricted diversity
483   that these models currently achieve. Our experiments on the Chest X-ray dataset demonstrate the
484   effectiveness of augmenting the data with diverse samples in improving classification accuracy. Our
485   findings underscore the need for future research to improve diffusion models in generating more
      diverse samples that extend beyond the informational boundaries of the training data.

                 / Btrain then

      Input: Set of points P representing t-SNE features of real images
      Output: Boundary B for real images

      Initialization:
625
      Find the point with minimum x-coordinate: A ← minp∈P (px )

627   Partition the set of points:
628      S1 ← {p ∈ P | p is to the left of line AB}
629      S2 ← {p ∈ P | p is to the right of line AB}
630   Recursive Boundary Construction:
631   Function FindBoundary(S, P , Q):
632      If S is empty then
633         Return []

         Find the point C in S that is furthest from line P Q
         Partition the set S into two subsets:

            S1 ← {p ∈ S | p is to the left of line P C}
