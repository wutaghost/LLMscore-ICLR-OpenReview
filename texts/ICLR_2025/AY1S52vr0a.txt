                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               State Space Models (SSMs), such as Mamba, have recently demonstrated the po-
012            tential to match or even surpass Transformers in language understanding tasks,
013            making them a promising alternative for designing Large Language Models
014            (LLMs). Concurrently, model quantization, particularly Post-Training Quantiza-
015            tion (PTQ), has been proven effective in reducing memory usage and inference
016            latency in LLMs. In this paper, we explore post-training quantization for Mamba
017            (Q-Mamba) by converting both linear projections and state caches into low-bit in-
018            tegers for efficient inference. After a theoretical analysis of the causes of outliers
019            in states, we propose Decoupled Scale Quantization (DSQ), which mitigates
020
               outliers in both the state and channel dimensions by applying separate quantiza-
               tion scales. To preserve the selective ability of quantized Mamba, we introduce
021
               Efficient Selectivity Reconstruction (ESR), a block-wise reconstruction method
022
               that involves a novel quantization simulation scheme, enabling fast parallel scan
023            algorithms with the non-linear quantization function. We demonstrate the effec-
024            tiveness of Q-Mamba across various quantization settings, model sizes, and both
025            generation and zero-shot tasks. In particular, for Mamba2-2.7B with W8A8H4
026            quantization, Q-Mamba achieves a 50% reduction in memory consumption with
027            only a 2.13% average accuracy degradation on zero-shot tasks.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Large language models (LLMs), such as LLaMa (Touvron et al., 2023) and GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023),
033
      have shown exceptional capabilities in general-purpose language understanding (Kaplan et al., 2020;
      Hoffmann et al., 2022). However, LLMs based on Transformer architectures still face a signifi-
034
      cant limitation: the computational cost of their attention mechanism scales quadratically with the
035
      sequence length (Vaswani et al., 2017). Therefore, prior works have focused on more efficient at-
036   tention variants, such as structured state space models (SSMs) (Gu & Dao, 2023; Dao & Gu, 2024;
037   Smith et al., 2023) and linear attention (Peng et al., 2023; Han et al., 2023; Child et al., 2019).
038   Among these, the Mamba architecture (Gu & Dao, 2023; Dao & Gu, 2024) has been shown to
039   match or exceed the downstream accuracy of Transformers on standard language modeling tasks
040   (Waleffe et al., 2024). Following its success in natural language understanding, it has also garnered
041   significant attention in other research areas, such as vision and multimodal tasks (Qiao et al., 2024;
042   Zhu et al., 2024).
043   Like Transformers, Mamba language models also operate in two computation phases (Patel et al.,
044   2024). The first is the prefill phase, where all input prompt tokens are processed in parallel through
045   the model’s forward pass to generate the first output token. During this phase, Mamba models (Gu
046   & Dao, 2023; Dao & Gu, 2024) employ a hardware-efficient parallel algorithm to compute SSMs
047   (Section 3). The second is the token generation phase, where subsequent output tokens are generated
048   sequentially, relying on the cached state from previous tokens in the sequence. Due to the lack of
049   computational parallelism, this phase tends to be more memory-bound and contributes significantly
050
      to the total generation latency.
051   Although Mamba has successfully replaced the O(T 2 ) attention module with O(T ) selective state
052   space models, our profiling results in Section 4 indicate that it still suffers from two inefficien-
053   cies during the generation stage. Firstly, similar to Transformers, the Mamba architecture consists
      of large linear layers, which require substantial GPU memory and slow down token generation

054
      (Figure 2b). Secondly, as larger states allow more information to be stored, states in Mamba are
055   expanded to be N times larger than vanilla activations, where N is the state dimension (128 in
056   Mamba-2 models). Consequently, these state caches account for a significant portion of memory
057   consumption, especially after quantizing weights to low bits (79.6% in Mamba2-2.7B with a batch
058   size of 128, as shown in Figure 2a). In this paper, we address a key question: Can Mamba models
059   be further optimized through model compression techniques?
060
      In this paper, we propose Q-Mamba, which quantizes both linear projections and state caches
061   into low-bit integers for Mamba models. Although previous research has successfully quantized
062   Key and Value (KV) caches into low-bit representations in transformers (Liu et al., 2023; 2024b;
063   Hooper et al., 2024), this work is the first to explore the quantization of state cache in Mamba ar-
064   chitectures. We observe that states exhibit both outlier channels and outlier states (i.e., the state
065   dimension contains large values across all channel dimensions), as shown in Figure 3. Further the-
066   oretical analysis reveals this phenomenon results from the computation of the outer products of two
067   activations, each of which contains outliers in distinct dimensions. This observation motivates us
068   to propose Decoupled Scale Quantization (DSQ), which utilizes separate quantization scales for
069
      both dimensions. Additionally, the non-linear nature of the quantization function disrupts the orig-
      inal equivalence between recurrence and quadratic dual form, the latter being essential for efficient
070
      training. To address this, we propose Efficient Selectivity Reconstruction (ESR), which simulates
071
      quantization errors by quantizing only the final timestep during training. Specifically, ESR updates a
072   small number of selective parameters (approximately 2% of the total) using just 128 training samples
073   in a block-wise reconstruction manner.
074
075
      Extensive experiments demonstrate that our methods achieve significant performance improvements
      for Mamba families on various evaluation metrics. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first
076
      to achieve W8A8H4 (8-bit linear projection and 4-bit states) for the Mamba architectures. For
077
      generation tasks, Q-Mamba achieved perplexities of 12.99 and 16.90 with 4-bit states on WikiText2
078   (Merity et al., 2017) and C4 (Pal et al., 2023), respectively, while baseline methods degraded to 21.18
079   and 29.86 even with 6-bit quantization. Additionally, Q-Mamba achieves W8A8H4 quantization for
080   zero-shot tasks with only 2.13% and 2.11% average accuracy degradation on Mamba2-2.7B and
081   Mamba2-1.3B, respectively.

      2.1   S TATE S PACE M ODEL
086
087   Transformer-based LLMs (Touvron et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023) suffer from the computational cost
088   of their attention mechanism scales quadratically with sequence length. Consequently, much re-
089   search has focused on developing more efficient variants of attention, such as structured state space
090   models (SSMs) (Gu & Dao, 2023; Dao & Gu, 2024; Smith et al., 2023). The original structured
091   SSMs (S4) (Gu et al., 2022) were linear time-invariant (LTI) systems motivated by continuous-time
092   online memorization. Many variants of structured SSMs have been proposed, for example, Gated
093   SSM architectures, such as GSS (Mehta et al., 2023) and BiGS (Wang et al., 2023), incorporate a
094   gating mechanism into SSMs for language modeling. Recently, the Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023; Dao
095
      & Gu, 2024) architecture demonstrates promising performance on standard language modeling tasks
      (Waleffe et al., 2024), as well as on vision and multimodal tasks (Zhu et al., 2024; Qiao et al., 2024).
096
      Mamba showed that state expansion and selective ability are crucial for selecting and memorizing
097
      useful information in the hidden states.

      In this section, we first analyze the memory consumption and runtime of primary components on
      the Mamba2-2.7B model, i.e., linear projection, 1D convolution, SSM, and LayerNorm. Based on
201
      the results presented in Figures 2a and Figures 2b, we can draw the following conclusions:
202
203   Linear projections. Similar to Transformers, large linear layers in Mamba not only require sub-
204   stantial GPU memory but also slow down token generation. When applying quantization to these
205   linear layers, experiments in Section A.1 reveal that outliers exist in specific activation channels of
206
      Mamba, particularly in output projections. This phenomenon has also been observed in previous
      studies on Transformer-based LLMs (Xiao et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2022).
207
208   States in SSMs. As larger states allow more information to be stored, states in Mamba are expanded
209   to be N times larger than vanilla activations, where N is the state dimension (128 in Mamba-2
210   models). Consequently, these state caches account for a significant portion of memory consumption,
211   especially after quantizing weights to low bits (e.g., 79.6% in Mamba2-2.7B with a batch size of 128,
212
      as shown in Figure 2a). This phenomenon not only poses challenges for increasing the batch size
      to enhance throughput but also prevents further enlargement of state dimensions in Mamba models,
213
      which would improve their storage capacity for long contexts (Dao & Gu, 2024; Arora et al., 2024).
214
215   To address the above problems, in this paper, we aim to quantize both linear projections and state
      caches into low-bit integers for Mamba models.

      To mitigate the performance loss caused by quantization, PTQ methods often apply block-wise
309
      reconstruction (Nagel et al., 2020; Li et al., 2021) with a few data. However, these methods cannot
310
      be directly applied to Mamba models due to the following differences: First, when applying the
311   non-linear quantization function to states ht , the definition of SSMs can no longer be reformulated
312   into quadratic mode for parallel training. Second, given the distinct mechanisms between Mamba
313   and Transformers, it is necessary to investigate which set of parameters is critical for restoring model
314   performance and which may lead to overfitting. In this section, we will present Efficient Selectivity
315   Reconstruction (ESR) with the mechanisms to address these two challenges in Section 5.2.1 and
316   Section 5.2.2, respectively.

353                         Q(ht−1 + δt−1 ) ≈ Q(ht−1 ) + Q′ (ht−1 ) · δt−1 ≈ Q(ht−1 )

354                                 =⇒ hqt ≈ Āt Q(ht−1 ) + B̄t xt

357
      the quantization errors by quantizing only one step during training. In the appendix, we present the
      pseudocode for the parallel training of quantization-aware SSMs for illustrative purposes. Table 4
358
      demonstrates the effectiveness of this quantization simulation, especially in low-bit settings.

362   In the Mamba block, the selective parameters B, ∆, and C, along with the SSM inputs xt , are
363   generated through input projections, as shown in Figure 1. During block-wise reconstruction, we
364   freeze the linear projections corresponding to the SSM inputs x and z, while keeping the linear
365   projections for selective parameters B, C, and ∆ learnable, which is referred to as Selectivity Guided
366
      Adaptation (SGA) (Figure 1, middle). Specifically,

372   SGA offers two primary advantages: First, the success of Mamba is largely attributed to the selec-
373   tivity of parameters Ā, B̄, and C̄, which distinguishes it from earlier non-selective SSMs (Gu et al.,
374   2020; Smith et al., 2023). Thus, we hypothesize that this selectivity also plays a critical role in main-
375   taining performance after quantization. Second, SGA reduces the number of learnable parameters,
376   mitigating the risk of overfitting with limited calibration data. For example, in Mamba2-2.7B, learn-
377   able parameters account for only about 2% of the total. Note that during this fine-tuning process,

      tated on a single NVIDIA A800 GPU, using a batch size of 1 over 3 epochs. For linear projections,

409   tization of the states). We utilize MinMax per-channel quantization (introduced in Section 5.1.2) as

       7                   C ONCLUSION
523
524
      In this paper, we propose Q-Mamba, a novel quantization scheme designed for Mamba models.
525
      After visualizing outliers in states, we conduct a theoretical analysis of their causes and propose
526
      Decoupled Scale Quantization (DSQ). By decoupling scales in the state and channel dimensions,
527   DSQ mitigates outliers in both dimensions while introducing negligible overhead. To further boost
528   performance through block-wise reconstruction, we propose Efficient Selectivity Reconstruction
529   (ESR), which includes a novel quantization simulation method that enables efficient fine-tuning of
530   selective parameters with parallel scan mode. We validate the performance of Q-Mamba across
531   various quantization settings, model sizes, and both generation and zero-shot tasks. In conclusion,
532   Q-Mamba demonstrates that Mamba architectures have the potential for further optimization when
533   combined with other model compression techniques.
