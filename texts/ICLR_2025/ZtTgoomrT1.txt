011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Neural operators extend conventional neural networks by expanding their func-
014            tional mapping capabilities across various function spaces, thereby promoting the
015            solving of partial differential equations (PDEs). A particularly notable method
016
               within this framework is the Fourier Neural Operator (FNO), which draws inspira-
               tion from Green’s function method to directly approximate operator kernels in the
017
               frequency domain. However, after empirical observation and theoretical valida-
018
               tion, we demonstrate that the FNO predominantly approximates operator kernels
019            within the low-frequency domain. This limitation results in a restricted capabil-
020            ity to solve complex PDEs, particularly those characterized by rapidly changing
021            coefficients and highly oscillatory solution spaces. To address this challenge, in-
022            spired by the attentive equivariant convolution, we propose a novel Translational
023            Equivariant Fourier Neural Operator (TE-FNO) which utilizes equivariant atten-
024            tion to enhance the ability of FNO to capture high-frequency features. We perform
025            experiments on forward and reverse problems of multiscale elliptic equations,
026            Navier-Stokes equations, and other physical scenarios. The results demonstrate
027
               that the proposed approach achieves superior performance across these bench-
               marks, particularly for equations characterized by rapid coefficient variations.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Partial differential equations (PDEs) serve as a fundamental theory in scientific research, describing
      a wide range of physical, chemical, and biological phenomena (Sommerfeld, 1949). From turbulent
034
      fluid dynamics to atmospheric circulation and material stress analysis, many real-world phenomena
035
      are governed by PDEs. Consequently, solving PDEs is essential for tackling core challenges in the
036   natural sciences.
037
038   Traditional numerical methods like the finite element method (FEM) and finite difference method
039
      (FDM) face significant challenges in handling noisy data, generating complex meshes, solving high-
      dimensional problems, and addressing inverse problems. In recent years, neural networks have
040
      emerged as powerful methods to overcome these limitations. Innovative approaches such as physics-
041
      informed neural networks (PINNs) (Karniadakis et al., 2021) and Galerkin transformers (GTs) (Cao,
042   2021) are specifically designed to simulate PDEs by learning mappings between inputs and outputs.
043   Furthermore, operator-learning methods like the deep operator network (DeepONet) (Lu et al., 2019)
044   and the Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) (Li et al., 2020; 2022) focus on learning transformations
045   between function spaces, significantly advancing PDE-solving capabilities. Beyond solving PDEs,
046   these neural operator-based methods have demonstrated exceptional promise in addressing complex
047   dynamics, such as modeling climate change and predicting natural disasters (Pathak et al., 2022),
048   highlighting their versatility and impact in real-world applications.
049   We focus on addressing more complex PDEs, such as multiscale PDEs, which play a pivotal role in
050   physics, engineering, and related disciplines by enabling the analysis of intricate practical problems,
051   including ocean circulation and high-frequency scattering (Quarteroni & Veneziani, 2003). These
052   PDEs are characterized by rapidly varying coefficients and oscillatory solution spaces, making it
053   essential to effectively capture information across multiple scales and frequency ranges. However,
      existing evidence indicates that FNO and similar methods predominantly emphasize learning low-

054
      frequency components when solving PDEs (Liu et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2024; Liu-Schiaffini et al.,
055   2024). This observation highlights a critical challenge: how to effectively capture high-frequency
056   features and integrate them with low-frequency information to enhance the performance of FNO in
057   tackling complex, multiscale PDEs.
058
      In this paper, we propose a novel Translational Equivariant Fourier Neural Operator (TE-FNO) de-
059
      signed to capture and integrate low-frequency and high-frequency features across multiple scales.
060
      Drawing inspiration from attentive equivariant convolution, we theoretically demonstrate that a com-
061   bined attention mechanism, comprising channel and spatial attention, can be integrated before the
062   Fourier layer in a translationally equivariant manner. Moreover, leveraging the locally computed
063   convolution kernel enables the efficient capture of high-frequency local details. Additionally, in-
064   spired by recent works (Xu et al., 2024; Liu-Schiaffini et al., 2024), we incorporate a Fourier
065   kernel with a convolutional-residual layer, enhancing the model capability to effectively capture
066   high-frequency information and address the challenges of complex multiscale PDEs. Our main
067   contributions can be summarized as follows:
068
             • We propose a novel TE-FNO method to address the issue where FNO-related approaches
069
               struggle to capture high-frequency features effectively. Specifically, our method integrates
070
               high-frequency and low-frequency components simultaneously with equivariant attentions
071            and convolutional-residual Fourier layers in a hierarchical structure at various scales.
072
073
             • The proposed method surpasses previous state-of-the-art approaches in existing PDE
               benchmarks, including Navier-Stokes equations, multiscale elliptic equations with rapidly
074
               changing coefficients, and significant solution variations.
075
076          • Furthermore, our method demonstrates effectiveness and robustness in solving inverse PDE
077            problems, particularly when dealing with noisy input data.

084   2.1   N EURAL PDE SOLVER
085
086   Many excellent algorithms have been proposed previously for solving PDEs using neural networks
087   (Long et al., 2018; Hao et al., 2022). Physics-informed neural network (PINN) (Karniadakis et al.,
088   2021) incorporates PDEs into the network by giving additional constraints with PDEs into loss func-
089   tion, which guide the synaptic modifications towards tuned parameters that satisfy data distribution,
090
      physical PDE laws, and other necessary boundary conditions. GT (Cao, 2021) utilizes the attention
      mechanism to build operator learners to solve PDEs and designs Fourier-type and Galerkin-type at-
091
      tention with linear complexity to reduce the computation cost. Neural operators leverage the concept
092
      that the operator denotes the mapping between infinite input and output function spaces. DeepONet
093   (Lu et al., 2019) leverages the universal approximation theorem to derive a branch-trunk structure
094   to form the operator in a polynomial regression way. Some other methods incorporate trained neu-
095   ral networks into conventional numerical solvers, to minimize numerical errors when dealing with
096   coarse grids (Cuomo et al., 2022; Meng et al., 2020).
097
098   2.2   F OURIER N EURAL O PERATOR
099
100   Fourier neural operator (FNO) (Li et al., 2020; 2022) draws inspiration from the conventional
101   Green’s function method and directly optimizes the kernel within the Fourier frequency domain
102   by utilizing the Fourier Transform. This approach has been demonstrated to be an efficient means of
103   reducing computational cost and performing global convolution. A notable advancement is that the
104
      operator kernel is directly trained in the frequency domain, whereby the network is theoretically in-
      dependent of the training data resolution. Therefore, FNO can deal with super-resolution problems
105
      and be trained on multiple PDEs.
106
107   The FNO has established a foundational framework for operator learning, inspiring several subse-
      quent works in the field. Geo-FNO (Li et al., 2022) deforms the irregular grid into a latent space

256   3.4     M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
257
258   We propose a method called Translational Equivariant Fourier Neural Operator (TE-FNO) which
259   combines equivariant attention mechanisms and convolutional-residual layers to learn the function
260
      mapping at various resolutions.
261   Equivariant Attention: The attention mechanism can be conceptualized as a dynamic selection
262   process that emphasizes significant features while suppressing irrelevant parts of the input. This
263   mechanism has proven effective in learning dependencies among pixels in computer vision tasks
264   (Yuan et al., 2020; Geng et al., 2021). In our context, the grid data closely resembles pixel-based
265   image data, making attention mechanisms a natural fit for capturing dependencies within the Rd
266
      domain. Previous studies have extensively explored optimal configurations for combining channel
      and spatial attention maps in similar scenarios. Following the findings of (Woo et al., 2018), we
267
      adopt a serial approach that prioritizes channel attention before applying spatial attention, as this
268
      setup has demonstrated superior performance in prior works.

279   FNO setting, only reserving lower-frequency components and training the kernel weights on them.
280   This setting aims to learn a smooth mapping to avoid jagged curves in solution spaces. However,
281   this setting may ignore some details about the solution space, especially in solving multiscale PDEs.

449   4.4   I NVERSE P ROBLEMS S OLVING
450
      In various scientific disciplines such as geological sciences and mathematical derivation, inverse
451
      problems are of significant importance. Nonetheless, these problems frequently demonstrate re-
452   duced stability compared to their associated forward issues, even when advanced regularization
453   techniques are employed. Following (Xu et al., 2024), we evaluate our method for inverse identifi-
454   cation problems on multiscale elliptic PDEs. In this experiment, we aim to learn an inverse operator,
455   which maps the solution function space to the corresponding coefficient space û = u + ϵN (u) 7→ a.
456   Here, ϵ indicates the intensity of Gaussian noise introduced into the training and evaluation data.
457   The noise term N (u) accounts for the sampling distribution and data-related noise.
458   The experiments about inverse coefficients inference problems on the multiscale elliptic PDEs
459   dataset are presented in Table 3. In our experiments, we modify the input and output resolutions
460   to both 256 × 256 in the Trigonometric and Darcy Rough elliptic equations. Since the coefficient
461   function space changes faster than the solution space, this task is more challenging than the forward-
462   solving problem. The result shows that our model performs well in the inverse coefficient identifica-
463   tion problem, which illustrates our model’s ability to address the challenges posed by this ill-posed
464   inverse problem with data. Methods such as FNO and F-FNO that learn kernel functions directly in
465   the low-frequency domain have trouble recovering targets with high-frequency components.

      5   L IMITATIONS
514
515
      Although TE-FNO has shown remarkable effectiveness in solving a wide range of complex PDE
516   tasks. However, it comes with certain limitations. Firstly, TE-FNO is designed to be particularly
517   suited to scenarios where the input and output resolutions are identical, a constraint commonly as-
518   sociated with U-shaped network architectures. For tasks such as input coefficient resolutions are
519   larger than the solution resolution, TE-FNO performs suboptimal. Furthermore, TE-FNO depends
520   on convolution-based local attention mechanisms in translational equivariant attention, while ad-
521   vantageous for capturing localized features, limits its ability to effectively capture global features,
522   which can reduce its efficacy in tasks requiring a broader contextual understanding.
523

      6   C ONCLUSION
525
526
      We propose a novel Translational Equivariant Fourier Neural Operator (TE-FNO) that combines
527
      equivariant attentions and convolutional-residual Fourier layers for solving complex PDEs. Our
528   model utilizes equivariant attention mechanisms and convolutional residual layers to capture high-
529   frequency features and complement the low-frequency features captured by the Fourier layer. Ben-
530   efits from the above components, our model could capture both local and global features simultane-
531   ously, and at the same time, achieve superior performances in many PDE benchmarks, especially in
532   solving forward and inverse problems of multiscale elliptic PDEs.

937   A.5.2    M ULTISCALE PDE S
938
939   Multiscale PDEs have many applications, including forecasting atmospheric convection and ocean
940   circulation, modeling the subsurface of flow pressures (Furman, 2008; Huyakorn, 2012), the defor-
941   mation of elastic materials (Rivlin, 1948; Merodio & Ogden, 2003), and the electric potential of
942   conductive materials (Sundnes et al., 2005). Multiscale elliptic PDEs are classic examples of mul-
943   tiscale PDEs. Solving elliptic PDEs with smooth coefficients is a conventional problem that can be
944
      effectively addressed using FNO. However, when the coefficients become non-smooth and exhibit
      rapidly changing features, the values in the solution spaces can exhibit oscillations and high contrast
945
      ratios (Xu et al., 2024). Another example is turbulent flow, which is modeled by the Navier-Stokes
946
      equation. This equation describes fluid dynamics and exhibits turbulent behavior at high Reynolds
947   numbers. In turbulent flow, unsteady vortices interact, leading to complex dynamics. To solve these
948   multiscale PDEs effectively, models must account for both global and local dynamics.
949
950
951   A.5.3    N UMERICAL S OLVERS FOR M ULTISCALE PDE S
952
953
      In addressing multiscale PDEs, a variety of numerical approaches are available. Numerical homog-
      enization methods (Engquist & Souganidis, 2008) aim to create a finite-dimensional approximation
954
      space for solution exploration. Fast solvers like multilevel and multigrid methods (Hackbusch, 2013;
955
      Xu & Zikatanov, 2017) can be considered as an extension of numerical homogenization. Recently,
956   operator-adapted wavelet methods, such as Gamblets (Owhadi, 2017), have been developed to solve
957   linear PDEs with rough coefficients, representing a progression beyond numerical homogenization.
958   Nevertheless, handling multiscale PDEs poses inherent challenges for numerical methods, given that
959   the computational cost tends to scale inversely proportional to the finest scale ϵ of the problem. In
960   recent years, there has been increasing exploration of neural network methods for solving multiscale
961   PDEs (Liu et al., 2022).

       A.5.5   V ISUAL ATTENTION M ETHODS
973
974    The attention mechanism can be regarded as a process of adaptive selection based on input features.
975    It has yielded advantages in numerous visual tasks, including image classification (Woo et al., 2018),
976    object detection (Dai et al., 2017; Hu et al., 2018a), and semantic segmentation (Yuan et al., 2020;
977    Geng et al., 2021). In computer vision, the attention mechanism is usually be divided into three
978
       main categories: channel attention, spatial attention, and temporal attention. For instance, SENet
       (Hu et al., 2018b) utilizes global average pooling on the channel dimension to modulate the corre-
979
       sponding channel attention. Complementary channel attention akin to that of CBAM (Woo et al.,
980
       2018) and BAM (Park et al., 2018) utilize similar strategies for spatial attention and combine spatial
981    and channel attention in series and parallel respectively. Recent research in visual attention aims to
982    integrate the strengths of various attention mechanisms to create more holistic attention (Hu et al.,
983    2018b; Guo et al., 2022).
