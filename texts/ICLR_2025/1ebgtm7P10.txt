                                                                          A BSTRACT
008
009
               Out-of-distribution (OOD) detection methods, especially post-hoc methods, rely
010
               on off-the-shelf pre-trained models. Existing literature shows how OOD and ID
011
               performance are correlated, i.e. stronger models with better ID performance tend to
012            perform better in OOD detection. However, significant performance discrepancies
013            exist between model versions, sometimes exceeding the impact of the OOD detec-
014            tion methods themselves. In this study, we systematically investigated this issue
015            and identified two main factors—label smoothing and mixup—that, while improv-
016            ing in-distribution accuracy, lead to a decline in OOD detection performance. We
017            provide empirical and theoretical explanations for this phenomenon and propose
018            a solution that enhances OOD Detection while maintaining strong in-distribution
019            performance. Code will be released upon acceptance.

036   Out-of-distribution (OOD) detection identifies input samples that differ from the in-distribution (ID)
037   training data. Detecting such samples avoids overconfident or incorrect predictions on data outside
038   the training scope, and is particularly important in sensitive domains such as healthcare, autonomous
039
      driving, and security. Previous works (Vaze et al., 2022b; Lu et al., 2024) have shown that a model’s
      ID and OOD detection performance are correlated - the higher the ID classification accuracy (on
040
      CIFAR, ImageNet, etc.), the better it is at distinguishing OOD versus ID samples. They assume
041
      that a stronger separation of ID classes naturally leads to a separation of OOD from ID classes. The
042   generalization improvement may come from the learning rate schedule or model ensemble, though
043   data augmentation has been found to be the most effective Lu et al. (2024). An assortment of data
044   augmentation strategies, such as RandAugment (Cubuk et al., 2020), Style Augment (Geirhos et al.,
045   2018), and AugMix (Hendrycks et al., 2020a) have all been found to be effective for improving both
046   ID and OOD performance. These strategies use a combination of techniques, such as image rotation,
047   translation, or color transformation.
048   Curiously, our empirical results challenge the conventional understanding of ID and OOD per-
049   formance correlation. Specifically, we find two commonly used augmentation strategies – label
050   smoothing (Szegedy et al., 2016) and mixup (Zhang, 2017) exhibit the opposite phenomena. Models
051   trained with label smoothing and mixup have a ∼ 10% drop in OOD detection performance compared
052   to omitting these strategies, despite their ability to improve model (ID) accuracy. This phenomenon
053   is observed across a range of convolutional and transformer network architectures, including ResNet,
      MobileNet, ResNetXt, WideResNet, RegNet, SWIN-T, and ViT (See Figure 1 and appendix).

      This naturally begs the question - why should label smoothing and mixup harm OOD detection?
055   These two augmentation strategies exhibit a trade-off between ID and OOD performance, contra-
056   dicting trends in prior literature. One hint lies in the transformation in the augmentation itself.
057   Label-smoothing and mixup transform the data sample’s label. In contrast, previously reported
058   augmentation strategies that improve ID and OOD separation operate only on the data sample itself,
059   i.e. RandAugment, Style Augment, and AugMix, as shown by Lu et al. (2024).
060
      We analyze, from a gradient perspective, to show that both label smoothing and mixup reduce the
061   maximal logit values. This reduction is more pronounced for in-distribution (ID) samples than out-of-
062   distribution (OOD) samples, thereby diminishing their separability. In turn, methods that rely on the
063   logit values for OOD separation, such as the maximal logit score (MLS) (Hendrycks et al., 2022) or
064   energy-based score (EBO) (Liu et al., 2020), are compromised. Feature-based methods are likely
065   similarly compromised, due to downstream effects of diminished gradients being back-propagated
066   from logits.
067   To address this issue, we propose two novel methods: Augmentation Deletion (AugDelete) for
068   finetuning pretrained models and Augmentation Revision (AugRevise) for models trained from
069   scratch. AugDelete mitigates the negative effects of label smoothing and mixup by deleting them
070   and finetuning only the final layer of the network. In contrast, AugRevise introduces a revised data
071   augmentation method paired with a corresponding training strategy aimed at enhancing in-distribution
072   generalization while preserving OOD detection. Both AugDelete and AugRevise demonstrate
073   improvements over baseline methods in OOD detection (see Fig. 1). Besides, AugRevise outperforms
074   state-of-the-art training-based methods regrading OOD detection and ID accuracy.
075   Our contributions are as follows:
076
077          • We identified that label smoothing and mixup, two widely used data augmentation tech-
078            niques used in modern neural network training, can significantly degrade OOD detection
079
               performance despite improving in-distribution accuracy.
080          • We theoretically demonstrated that label smoothing and mixup reduce the separation between
081            OOD and ID data in the logit space, impairing OOD detection.
082          • Based on this analysis, we proposed AugDelete for finetuning pretrained models and Au-
083            gRevise for training from scratch. Both methods enhance OOD detection while maintaining
084            strong in-distribution performance.

086   2   R ELATED W ORKS
087
088   Post-Hoc OOD Detection methods often use pre-trained models; the main research focus is to
089   define new score functions or post-hoc adjustments to improve detection capabilities. Methods
090   such as softmax-based thresholds (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2017a) and EBO (Liu et al., 2020) use
091   simple yet effective techniques to repurpose off-the-shelf networks for OOD detection. Others, such
092   as ASH (Djurisic et al., 2023) and ReAct (Sun et al., 2021), are logit-based. They modify logits
093
      by reshaping the feature activation, showing promising results in OOD detection. Feature-based
      methods leveraging internal representations. For instance, Mahalanobis distance-based methods (Lee
094
      et al., 2018) calculate the distance of feature vectors from class-conditional Gaussian distributions,
095
      effectively identifying OOD samples by measuring feature space uncertainty. In addition to logit-
096   based and feature-based techniques, recent work like NNGuide (Park et al., 2023) combine the two to
097   derive more robust OOD detection scores.
098
099
      Training-based OOD Detection methods adjust model training to improve the model’s ability to
      distinguish between ID and OOD samples. One strategy is through explicit supervision, either from
100
      true OOD samples (Hendrycks et al., 2019) or synthesized virtual ones (Pinto et al., 2022; Huang &
101
      Li, 2021). Synthesized samples are more appealing, since real OOD data is typically not available for
102   training. One example is MOS (Huang & Li, 2021), which creates virtual OOD samples by grouping
103   classes to encourage clearer separation in feature space. RegMixup (Pinto et al., 2022) treat mixed-up
104   samples as virtual outliers, the cross-entropy loss of which serves as regularizers for strengthening
105   the decision boundary between ID and OOD data. Other training-based techniques, like LogitNorm
106   (Wei et al., 2022) and T2FNorm (Regmi et al., 2023), aim to improve the separability of feature
107   representations between ID and OOD samples. After the model training, a compatible post-hoc score
      function is still required for OOD detection.

      Relations between ID and OOD has been widely explored in previous works (Vaze et al., 2022b;
109   Humblot-Renaux et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024). Vaze et al. (2022b) found that a good closed-set
110   classifier is able to identifying semantically novel classes; similarly, Humblot-Renaux et al. (2024)
111   observed that ID and OOD accuracy are positively correlated, at least for correctly predicted ID
112   samples. Lu et al. (2024) found that data augmentations including AugMix and RandAugment
113   improve both ID and OOD performance.

      3.2   DATA -AUGMENTATION S TRATEGIES
163
164   Depending on whether transform the label y, data augmentations can be categorized into data-based
165   augmentation and label-based augmentation. In this work, we consider Random Erasing (RE) and
166   Trivial Augment (TA) to represent data-based augmentation strategies, and label smoothing and
167   mixup to represent label-based augmentation strategies. We select these four strategies because they
168   are the additional strategies used by the torchvision v2 models (Torchvision, 2024) compared to
169   torchvision v1 models.
170   Data-Based Augmentation 1: Random Erasing (RE) (Zhong et al., 2020) applies random zero
171   masking in the input sample x with a probability per . It reduces over-fitting and improve the
172   generalization of neural networks. Typically, per = 0.1.
173
      Data-Based Augmentation 2: Trivial Augment (TA) (Müller & Hutter, 2021) is a parameter-free
174   set of image transformations to the input sample x such as solarize, posterize, brightness adjustment,
175   etc. During training, TA randomly selects a single augmentation and an augmentation strength from
176   a pre-defined set.
177
      Label-Based Augmentation 1: Label Smoothing (LS) (Szegedy et al., 2016) is used to avoid
178
      overconfidence by adding a uniform vector to the label y:

193   4     D IAGNOSING DATA AUGMENTATIONS
194
195   In this section, we systematically investigate the influence of data augmentations on OOD detection.
196   Starting with a case study in Sec. 4.1, we find that label-based data augmentations, label smoothing
197   and mixup, harm OOD detection. Then, in Sec. 4.2, we explain the reason with a derivation.
198   Specifically, label smoothing and mixup reduce the maximal logits of sample outputs, but more so
199
      for ID samples than OOD samples, leading to poorer ID/OOD separation. Furthermore, we analyze
      mixup from the perspective of virtual sample generation in Sec. 4.3. Empirical results suggest that
200
      adding mixup reduces the distinction between ID and mixed samples. Less separable ID and mixed
201
      samples will result in poor ID/OOD separation because mixed samples are close to OOD samples.
202

      4.1   A N E MPIRICAL C ASE S TUDY BASED ON T O R C H V I S I O N
204
205   The contributions in this paper are motivated by a case study based on the protocols of OpenOOD
206   V1.5 (Lu et al., 2024). OpenOOD V1.5 is currently the largest OOD detection benchmark. The
207   findings released by the authors are in line with previous literature showing the correlation between
208   ID and OOD performance. A curious discrepancy that we noticed is that state-of-the-art methods
209   for OOD almost all rely on torchvision v1 models. Yet the v1 is a basic model that lags in
210   ID performance compared to v2 models with the same backbones. The v2 models improve ID
211   performance by incorporating improved training techniques such as label smoothing and mixup.
212   We begin by comparing the performance of the v1 and v2 models using a ResNet50 backbone in
213   ImageNet-1k (Deng et al., 2009). ResNet50-v2 improves accuracy by 4% compared to ResNet50-v1
214   but results in a 20% decrease in the OOD AUROC (see Fig. 1). Such a change in the OOD AUROC
215   is significant because it surpasses the improvements that most post-hoc OOD methods achieve (Liu
      et al., 2020; Djurisic et al., 2023). Similar trends hold for other v1 and v2 models (see Fig. 1).

319
320   Empirically, the impact of label smoothing and mixup is the greatest on the output logits. The effects
321   gradually diminish with back-propagation into the feature layers. The results of figure 2 show less
322   impact on OOD detection when adopting a feature-based score SKN N rather than a logit-based score
323   SM LS . These empirical results suggest that a simple way to fix the logits v is to fine-tune the last
      fully connected layer W, b without label smoothing and mixup.

833   C.1   O PENOODV 1.5 DATASET S ETTING
834
835   We conduct experiments on OpenOOD v1.5 (Zhang et al., 2023) benchmark. It consists of 4 ID
836   datasets, CIFAR10/100 (Krizhevsky, 2009), ImageNet200 (Zhang et al., 2023), and ImageNet-
837   1k (Deng et al., 2009). Each ID dataset contains several near-OOD and far-OOD test sets, where the
838   near-OOD test sets are more challenging than the far-OOD ones.
839   CIFAR10/100 are relatively small datasets. They have the same far-OOD test set containing
840   MNIST (LeCun et al., 1998), SVHN (Netzer et al., 2011), Textures (Cimpoi et al., 2014), and
841   Places365 (Zhou et al., 2018). TinyImageNet (Le & Yang, 2015) and CIFAR100 are adopted as near-
842   OOD evaluation sets for CIFAR10; while TinyImageNet and CIFAR10 are adopted for CIFAR100.
843   For network architecture, we adopt the same network backbone, ResNet18 as OpenoodV1.5 (Zhang
844   et al., 2023).
845   ImageNet-1k is a large-scale dataset consisting of 1281167 training images of 1000 classes. It has 2
846   near-OOD dataset, SSB-hard (Vaze et al., 2022a) and NINCO (Bitterwolf et al., 2023), and 3 far-OOD
847   datasets, iNaturalist (Horn et al., 2018), Textures (Cimpoi et al., 2014), and OpenImage-O (Wang
848   et al., 2022). To be consistent with Openood V1.5 benchmark, ResNet50, Swin Transformer (Swin-T),
849   and Vision Transformers (ViT) are adopted as pretrained backbone. Besides these models, some
850   additional torchvision pre-trained models are evaluated.
851   ImageNet200 is a subset of ImageNet-1k, which contains 200 classes. It has the same near-OOD
852   and far-OOD datasets as ImageNet-1k. Following OpenoodV1.5 (Zhang et al., 2023), ResNet-18 is
853   adopted as the network backbone.
854
      Evaluation Metrics. FPR@95 and AUROC are adopted to evaluate the OOD performance. FPR@95
855   is the false positive rate when the true positive rate is 95%, while AUROC is the Area under the
856   receiver operating characteristic curve. Lower FPR@95 and higher AUROC deliver better separation
857   between ID and OOD samples.
