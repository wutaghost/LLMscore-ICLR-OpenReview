011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Pre-trained multi-modal models have recently garnered significant attention due
014            to their adaptability to diverse downstream tasks via fine-tuning. However, their
015
               resilience to certain group shift issues, i.e., spurious correlations, remains impera-
               tive yet relatively under-investigated. We study this problem in vision-language
016
               models (VLMs), and we observe potential vulnerabilities in pre-trained VLMs,
017
               such as CLIP, when confronted with spurious correlations. While recent studies
018            have been exploited to address unimodal group-imbalances by minority group
019            up-sampling or creating group-balanced subsets, we posit that true robustness can
020            be achieved by debiasing the training process through feature reweighting. In this
021            paper, we propose Shifted Feature Reweighting (SFR), a robust multi-modal learn-
022            ing method to mitigate the reliance on spurious features. Specifically, we introduce
023            a novel disagreement-based importance weight that allocates distinct weights to
024            individual instances within the training data. This contrasts with existing group
025            rebalance weight strategies, which uniformly weigh all instances within a group.
026
               Our reweighting strategy adeptly addresses disparities in instance-level learning
               difficulty. Moreover, our empirical results unveil that representation collapse may
027
               arise during fine-tuning. To address this, we proposed to introduce feature dropout
028
               and show that this simple method can further regularize the training on the majority
029            groups and encourage the training on the minority groups. Empirical results on
030            multiple benchmarks verify our claims and confirm the effectiveness of our pro-
031            posed SFR. Theoretically, we analyze the performance of our SFR and confirm its
032            superiority in mitigating spurious correlations. Our codes will be here.

036
037   Large-scale transfer learning has recently become show-stealer in modern deep learning. Pre-trained
038   vision-language models (VLMs), such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), in particular, are now setting
039
      benchmarks by achieving state-of-the-art performance across a spectrum of real-world computer
      vision (Radford et al., 2021; Jia et al., 2021; Lai et al., 2023a; Li et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022;
040
      Zellers et al., 2021; Bain et al., 2021; Lai et al., 2023c), and natural language processing tasks (Wang
041
      et al., 2018; Talmor et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2019; Wei et al., 2022; Lai et al., 2023b).
042   As model parameter counts have soared from millions to billions in recent years, fine-tuning large
043   pre-trained VLMs for specific downstream tasks has emerged as a promising approach, balancing
044   performance gains with minimal computational requirements.
045
      Amidst this rapid advancement in VLMs, the burgeoning field of model robustness seeks ways
046
      to enhance the resilience of these models, aiming to diminish spurious correlations – features
047
      that are indicative of the target class within the training dataset, yet inconsequential to the true
048   classification function – without compromising performance (Yang et al., 2023a; Zhang et al.,

050   inadvertently suffer from spurious correlations, potentially undermining robustness, particularly
051   for underrepresented groups (Geirhos et al., 2020). Such an approach risks marginalizing specific
052   minority subsets within the training data, constraining the model’s effectiveness in safety-critical
053   scenarios. For example, in Waterbirds (Sagawa et al., 2020), which involves classifying between
      “waterbird” and “landbird”, a noticeable bias exists. Specifically, the “land” and “water” backgrounds

      In the pursuit of improving group robustness of vision models (Sagawa et al., 2020; Nam et al.,
      2022; Liu et al., 2021; Creager et al., 2021; Nam et al., 2020; Izmailov et al., 2022; Kirichenko
077
      et al., 2023), many existing works underscore the importance of mitigating spurious correlations as a
078
      pivotal strategy for improved model performance. Yet, a largely uncharted area remains: assessing
079   the robustness of these VLMs against group shifts. Prior work (Yang et al., 2023a) has shown the
080   promise of enhancing VLMs’ group robustness via efficient fine-tuning. However, the pursuit of
081   advancing accuracy across diverse groups, often at considerable computational costs (i.e., fine-tuning
082   entire models), remains a challenge that is yet to be well-addressed. In this work, we ask: How to
083   improve group robustness in pre-trained VLMs with the minimum computational costs?
084
      In this work, we advance state-of-the-art group robustness of VLMs for image classification. We first
085   explore the feasibility of improving group robustness for popular large pre-trained model, such as
086   CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), constrained by group imbalance. One straightforward approach is to
087   upsample the minority group (Sagawa et al., 2020) or create a group-balanced dataset (Kirichenko
088   et al., 2023; Izmailov et al., 2022). However, our findings (e.g., Figures 1 and 3) suggest these
089   methods may overlook variations in the difficulty of training samples, presumably due to the uniform
090   weighting of all samples within a group. The relative importance of individual samples within
091   each group remains an open question. This encourages us to start attending nonchalantly at trivial
092   samples and become scrupulous at non-trivial samples. In addition to the above issue, recently several
093
      works (Andonian et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2022) investigate the intriguing phenomenon that the
      current contrastive learning paradigms tend to neglect hidden semantics inside the textual description,
094
      prominently due to overfitting of the learned visual representations to the certain textual features
095
      during training. This implies a trend toward representation collapse, which in turn leads to suboptimal
096   performance.
097
098   In this paper, we propose Shifted Feature Reweighting (SFR), a robust multi-modal learning method
099
      to mitigate the reliance on spurious features. Our simple, yet highly effective strategy offers several
      compelling advantages for fine-tuning pre-trained VLMs, specifically: (1) We introduce a novel
100
      disagreement-based importance weighting strategy that allocates distinct weights to individual
101
      samples within the training data (Sec. 4.1). The reweighting is achieved by a disagreement-based
102   score derived from contrasting an auxiliary ERM model and our target model, using the entropy
103   differential between their predictions. Our empirical findings suggest that such a strategy enables
104   the model to prioritize high-caliber samples, thereby refining prediction accuracy. Importantly,
105   such a reweighting strategy is plug-and-play and can be applied across diverse models beyond
106   VLMs. (2) We further describe a simple training approach to mitigate spurious correlations without
107   representational collapse (Sec. 4.3). More specifically, we mask random pixels from attention-pooled
      representations of the images with only dropout (Srivastava et al., 2014). We find that masking a

131   Improving group robustness. In real-world applications, spurious correlations are prevalent. The
132   spurious correlation issue refers to neural networks often prioritize features over shortcuts — shallow
133   features spuriously linked with classification targets, which can pose challenges, especially in high-
134   stakes and safety-critical scenarios (Geirhos et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2023b; Sagawa et al., 2020;
135   Sohoni et al., 2020; Nam et al., 2020; Yaghoobzadeh et al., 2019; Creager et al., 2021; Kim et al.,
136
      2021; Asgari et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022b; LaBonte et al., 2023; Idrissi et al., 2022; Qiu et al.,
      2023; Pezeshki et al., 2021). Consequently, there’s a burgeoning research focus on elucidating and
137
      mitigating their impact on model efficacy. On one hand, computer vision models frequently rely on
138
      semantically unrelated attributes, including an image’s background (Sagawa et al., 2020; Lai et al.,
139   2023a; Xiao et al., 2021; Moayeri et al., 2022), texture (Geirhos et al., 2019), secondary objects
140   (Rosenfeld et al., 2018; Shetty et al., 2019; Singla & Feizi, 2022), and other extraneous features
141   (Li et al., 2018; Brendel & Bethge, 2019; Lai et al., 2023c). This becomes particularly profound in
142   critical domains like medical imaging, wherein models might inadvertently prioritize hospital-specific
143   markers (Zech et al., 2018) or incidental indicators (Oakden-Rayner et al., 2020) over genuine
144   symptoms of diseases. On the other hand, large language models (LLMs) demonstrate a tendency to
145   capitalize on superficial characteristics, facilitating their strong performance in benchmarks despite
146   potential gaps in true task comprehension. For example, these models might exploit elementary
147
      syntactic patterns or lexical overlaps between sentences to infer their connections (Kaushik & Lipton,
      2018; Gururangan et al., 2018; Lai et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2023a; Niven & Kao, 2019; McCoy et al.,
148
      2019). In contrast, our goal is to fine-tune a more robust pretrained VLMs toward group robustness,
149
      rather than training unimodal models. The intricacies of spurious correlations in pretrained VLMs
150   remain relatively uncharted.
151
152   Robustness and group annotations. When provided with group annotations (i.e. spurious labels
153
      together with class labels), there exists a range of methods that consistently yield improved Worst
      Group Accuracy (WGA). Many studies have shown that utilizing these group annotations can be
154
      pivotal for improving model group robustness. Some of these approaches include the optimization of
155
      worst-group loss (Sagawa et al., 2020), cultivating invariant or diverse features (Arjovsky et al., 2019;
156   Goel et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022a; Xu et al., 2022), adopting class or group balancing strategies
157   (Cui et al., 2019; Menon et al., 2020; Idrissi et al., 2022; Kirichenko et al., 2023; Izmailov et al.,
158   2022), and employing contrastive learning techniques (Taghanaki et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2023a).
159   Among previous explorations, (Yang et al., 2023a) are the closest to our setting, emphasizing the
160   fine-tuning of multimodal models to mitigate spurious correlations. However, they does not explore
161   importance reweighting strategies, relying solely on uniform weighting across samples within a
      group, which can hardly bring group robustness boost to real-world applications.

183   Learning with Spurious Correlation. Spurious correlation manifests when significant discrep-
184   ancies in group information exist between the target distribution P and source distribution Q from
185   which training samples are drawn, constituting a form of distribution shift (Ming et al., 2022; Zhou
186
      et al., 2021). In our study, we focus on classification tasks within the context of group robustness
      (Sagawa et al., 2020), where the input is denoted as x 2 X and the its class label as y 2 Y. We posit
187
      that the data distribution contains multiple distinct groups g 2 G, often delineated by amalgamating
188
      the class label y 2 Y and a certain spurious attribute s 2 S where S is the set of spurious attributes.
189   For example, considering the Waterbirds dataset (Sagawa et al., 2020), the task is to classify y as
190   either landbird or waterbird. The background imagery acts as the spurious attribute s, with potential
191   values S = {land, water}, leading to groups defined by the combinations of class labels and spurious
192   attributes: G = Y ⇥S.
193
      An attribute s is labeled spurious when it shows correlation with y but lacks causality. Illustratively,
194
      in Waterbirds (Sagawa et al., 2020), nearly 95% of instances tagged as y = waterbird exhibit
195   the spurious attribute s = water. Such tendencies may drive models to excessively rely on the
196   background (like water) for predictions, undermining accuracy for underrepresented groups such as
197   g = (landbird, water). Following (Kirichenko et al., 2023; Zhang & Ré, 2022; Zhang et al., 2022b;
198   Qiu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023a; LaBonte et al., 2023; Izmailov et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023b), we
199   employ worst group accuracy (WGA) for performance evaluation, representing the lowest accuracy
200   achieved across all groups.
201   Engaging with Spurious Attributes. Prior works aiming for robustness against spurious cor-
202   relations predominantly focuses on training unimodal models (Sagawa et al., 2020; Kirichenko
203   et al., 2023; Nam et al., 2022; Sohoni et al., 2021). In contrast, our study delves into fine-tuning
204   pre-trained VLMs for enhanced robustness, with a keen emphasis on non-trivial (minority) groups,
205   while maintaining computational efficiency.
206

211   with its given text. For an image xi , we denote vi as its image representation and ui as the correspond-
212   ing text representation. Specifically, the prediction probability of image representation vi aligning

233   4    S HIFTED F EATURE R EWEIGHTING
234
235   In this section, we introduce our proposed method, SFR, as a straightforward yet highly effective
236   pipeline aimed at addressing the issue of spurious correlation inherent in pre-trained CLIP, as
237   described in Figure 6 (in Appendix).
238
      Overview and Motivation. Our framework focuses on fine-tuning the pre-trained CLIP. That is, we
239
      solely train the final layer (i.e., the projection head of the visual branch of CLIP), while keeping the
240
      remainder of the model frozen. The rationale behind our approach is grounded in several key factors.
241   Firstly, it is designed to be lightweight and efficient. Furthermore, when visualizing feature attention
242   using GradCAM (See Figure 2), we observe that the pre-trained CLIP model tends to attend spurious
243   image features rather than the core features, such as the background in the Waterbirds dataset. To
244   gain more insight, we also visualize the represetations of pre-trained CLIP, ERM-tuned CLIP, and our
245   proposed SFR on the Waterbirds Dataset. This demonstrate the presence of spurious correlations (See
246   Figure 1). These can be attributed to a well-known phenomenon in neural networks where they tend
247   to prioritize learning easier features, often neglecting the more critical aspects of the data (Geirhos
248   et al., 2020).
249   In addition, spurious correlations involve a distribution shift where model performance relies on group
250   information in the training data rather than on the true underlying relationship. An intuitive golden
251   solver to address this issue is importance sampling, which assigns varying weights to samples to
252   mitigate group bias(Yang et al., 2024). However, in practical scenarios, directly applying importance
253   sampling either leads to poor performance due to high-variance estimation (Byrd & Lipton, 2019)
254   or incurs significant computational costs, limiting its effectiveness in complex data environments
255
      (Bugallo et al., 2017; Robert, 1999). To address these bottlenecks, we introduced a disagreement-
      based reweighting score that mimics true importance weights w(x), dynamically adjusting sample
256
      weights to efficiently reduce spurious correlations and enhance model reliability across diverse
257
      data distributions. Driven by these insights, we here develop a streamlined, lightweight fine-tuning
258   approach specifically designed to counteract spurious correlations.
259
260   The performance challenges encountered by ERM in datasets with spurious correlations stem from
261
      two primary factors: (1) geometric skew: an imbalance in the classification margins between different
      groups within a dataset, particularly when an ERM-trained classifier, treats majority and minority
262
      groups inequally. This results in an equalization of training margins across these groups, potentially
263
      leading to biased or less effective learning outcomes, especially for minority groups. For example,
264   as shown in Figure 3, in the Waterbirds dataset, we observe the distribution difference of entropy
265   disagreement (x, y) and corresponding weights for each group, thereby revealing the gaps in the
266   entropy levels across different groups; and (2) statistical skew: the tendency of a neural network
267   to prioritize “easy-to-learn” spurious attributes, rather than focusing on the true label information,
268   particularly in datasets with spurious correlations. This often occurs due to the slow convergence
269   of gradient descent on minority portion of the data. The network might then overly rely on these
      spurious attributes, especially in early stages of training or in scenarios where training is insufficient.

      Our preliminary findings underscore the need for a reweighting strategy tailored to pre-trained
279   foundation models, e.g., CLIP. Specifically, we aim to devise a method for quantifying the difficulty
280   of individual samples, thereby enabling us to assign appropriate weights for more robust training.
281   This concept draws inspiration from significant research in the field (Nam et al., 2020; Sohoni et al.,
282   2020; Creager et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022b; Zhang & Ré, 2022; Duan et al., 2024;
283   LaBonte et al., 2023), often involving an auxiliary model to identify challenging or minority group
284   samples, either implicitly or explicitly. Such methods include pseudo-labeling with ERM model
285   predictions (Nam et al., 2020), subset selection (Liu et al., 2021), cluster-based grouping using ERM
286   features (Sohoni et al., 2020), and contrastive learning (Zhang et al., 2022b; Zhang & Ré, 2022).
287   Our method, while resonating with these established approaches, diverges in its practical application.
288   We propose a novel importance weighting strategy, assigning distinct importance weights to each
289   sample based on its difficulty level. According to Theorem 3 in (Cortes et al., 2010), the discrepancy
290   between the generalization error on target distribution, R(h) = Ex⇠P [L(h(x), f (x))], and the
                                                                    Pm
291   weighted empirical loss on source distribution, R̂w (h) = m1
                                                                       i=1 w(xi )L(h(xi ), f (xi )), is upper
292   bonded, providing a theoretical guarantee for our design. A more detailed theoretical analysis is in
293   Appendix E.
294   Despite the theoretical appeal of employing importance weights w(x) in ERM models, practical
295   implementation encounters significant challenges. These include weight degeneracy (Robert, 1999),
296   time-consuming iteration process to approximate target distribution (Bugallo et al., 2017), and
297   high-variance estimates in high-dimensional data (Byrd & Lipton, 2019). Our proposed design
298   effectively addresses the aforementioned challenges without requiring prior knowledge of distribution
299   or expensive computing iterations, which leads to a lightweight, computationally efficient, plug-and-
300   play algorithm. Further theoretical analysis refer to Appendix E.2.
301   The implementation of our disagreement-based reweighing strategy involves two primary stages.
302   Initially, we train an ERM model on the downstream dataset such as Waterbirds using the standard
303   Cross-Entropy loss. However, unlike previous methods, this ERM training does not update the entire
304   model. Instead, we optimize the use of the pre-trained CLIP model’s robust representation, focusing
305   solely on fine-tuning the vision branch’s last-layer projection head. We then determine a score based
306   on the entropy disagreement between this ERM model and our target model.
307   Specifically, we use the ERM model as an auxiliary model in order to fine-tune a separate target
308   model. The target model is initialized by a pre-trained CLIP. For each sample (x, y), we define the
309   per-class disagreement score as:

      To address this challenge, we propose a simple yet effective feature dropout strategy. Specifically, as
391
      described in Section 3, v denotes the image representation outputted by the vision branch’s projection
392   head in CLIP. In implementation, during training, we randomly drop a predetermined proportion p of
393   v’s entries, with this proportion being a tunable hyper-parameter. In other words, during the training
394   phase, the deactivation of feature v adheres to a Bernoulli(p) distribution. Conversely, in the
395   inference phase, dropout is not applied, ensuring that all neurons remain active. Our proposed SFR,
396   which synergizes feature dropout with sample reweighting, has achieved SoTA results in boosting
397   group robustness for fine-tuning CLIP. This is substantiated by our extensive experimental findings
398   and thorough ablation studies, as detailed in Section 5.2.

      5.1        M AIN RESULTS
407
408   Baselines. In our study, we begin by overviewing six representative methods that address spurious
409   correlations. These methods encompass both supervised and semi-supervised approaches. (1)
410   supervised: GroupDRO (Sagawa et al., 2020), a supervised method, directly utilizes Worst Group
411   Accuracy (WGA) across predefined groups as its training goal, leveraging group annotations in the
412   data. This choice of training objective closely aligns with the evaluation metric, contributing to its
413   effectiveness. DFR (Kirichenko et al., 2023) builds on the observation that features learned with
414   ERM capture both spurious and core features. It employs a group-balanced dataset to fine-tune the
415   final classification layer, thereby achieving performance on par with GroupDRO (Sagawa et al., 2020).
416
      Spurious-aware Contrastive Learning (SaC) (Yang et al., 2023a), to the best of our knowledge, is
      the first work on addressing spurious correlations in multi-modal foundation models, e.g., CLIP, which
417
      only fine-tunes the representation of pre-trained CLIP using spurious-aware contrastive learning. This
418
      essentially involves supervised contrastive learning with group labels, contributing to its effectiveness.
419   (2) semi-supervised: AFR (Qiu et al., 2023) begins by training an ERM model to infer information
420   about minority groups. It then retrains the model’s last layer on a reweighted dataset, where the
421   weight is designed to capture the confidence of the first-stage model. JTT (Liu et al., 2021) employs
422   a two-stage design, where the first stage involves learning an ERM model to select a subset of
423   misclassified data, which is then used together with the entire training data to retrain the model.
424   CnC (Zhang et al., 2022b) first trains an ERM model, and then uses contrastive learning to train a
425   target model, where the positive pairs are selected from points within the same class but different
426   predictions by the ERM model, and the negative pairs are chosen from points within different class
427
      but same predictions.

      6   C ONCLUSION
513
514
      We introduced SFR, a simple yet effective robust multi-modal learning framework that greatly
      improve group robustness across multiple benchmarks. We develop disagreement-based importance
515
      weights, assigning unique weights to each instance in the training data, effectively addressing
516
      the variance in instance-level learning challenges. We further verify representation collapse in
517   multi-modal model training. To counter this, we incorporate dropout to better regulate training for
518   majority groups while promoting focus on minority groups. We believe our approach holds wider
519   implications for group robustness, offering a new perspective on applying regularization to reduce
520   spurious correlations in multi-modal foundation models. This method has the potential for broader
521   applications, including addressing various spurious attributes and being integrated into language
522   foundation model training.
523   Limitations. Defending machine learning models from spurious correlations holds immense promise
524   for developing more reliable and trustworthy medical AI. Our proposed SFR framework significantly
525   enhances group robustness in multi-modal learning, paving the way for practical implementations
526   across a broad spectrum of real-world biomedical applications. Additionally, as part of our future
527   research direction, it’s crucial to tackle challenges related to fairness and privacy within the domain
528   of spurious correlations, ensuring that these advanced AI solutions are both equitable and secure.
