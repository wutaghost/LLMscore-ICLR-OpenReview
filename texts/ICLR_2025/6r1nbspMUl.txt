009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Controllable generation has achieved substantial progress in both 2D and 3D do-
012            mains, yet current conditioning methods still face limitations in describing de-
013            tailed shape structures. Skeletons can effectively represent and describe object
014            anatomy and pose. Unfortunately, past studies are often limited to human skele-
015            tons. In this work, we generalize skeletal conditioned generation to arbitrary
016            structures. First, we design a reliable mesh skeletonization pipeline to generate
017
               a large-scale mesh-skeleton paired dataset. Based on the dataset, a multi-view
               and 3D generation pipeline is built. We propose to represent 3D skeletons by
018
               Coordinate Color Encoding as 2D conditional images. A Skeletal Correlation
019
               Module is designed to extract global skeletal features for condition injection. Af-
020            ter multi-view images are generation, 3D assets can be obtained by incorporat-
021            ing a large reconstruction model, followed with a UV texture refinement stage.
022            As a result, our method achieves instant generation of multi-view and 3D con-
023            tents which are aligned with given skeletons. The proposed techniques largely
024            improve the object-skeleton alignment and generation quality. Project page at
025            https://github.io/skdream3d. Dataset, code and models will be released in public.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
      In view of representation dimension, 2D image generation (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020; Rom-
030   bach et al., 2022), multi-view (2.5D) generation (Shi et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023), and 3D gen-
031   eration (Hong et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a; Xu et al., 2024) have been promoted and made great
032   progress successively. To realize more flexible and controllable generation, conditions beyond text
033   have drawn considerable attention. 2D image conditions (e.g., edge maps, human skeletons, and
034   concept references) (Zhang et al., 2023; Ruiz et al., 2023) have been well studied. Similarly in 3D
035   generation, analogous 2D conditions have also been studied (Li et al., 2023d). Additionally, 3D
036   conditions like simple shapes (Dong et al., 2024) have also been explored.
037   Although the aforementioned conditions in controllable generation complement text descriptions,
038   they still struggle in precisely describing shape structures. In contrast, skeletons, among various
039   types of conditions, exhibit superior ability to depict shape structures: (i) Representation of object
040   anatomy. A skeleton can efficiently represent various 3D structures with sparse joints and bones.
041   It would be cumbersome for other conditions to represent anatomy. (ii) Articulation into different
042   poses. Skeletons are widely used for character animation in computer graphics (Kavan et al., 2007;
043   Baran & Popović, 2007) due to their simplicity and efficiency. Other conditions such as rough
044
      shapes (Dong et al., 2024) are inconvenient to deform into different poses. (iii) Freedom of editing.
      Given an initial skeleton, users can freely add new structures or modify joint positions and bone
045
      sizes to create their ideal shapes. Examples for demonstration are in Fig. 1.
046
047   Despite these advantages, previous studies (Zhang et al., 2023; Mou et al., 2024; Ju et al., 2023;
048   Zhang et al., 2024b; Huang et al., 2024) on skeletal conditioned generation are limited to human
049   skeletons. From the perspective of generalization, we would like to ask: Is it possible to use arbi-
050
      trary skeletons as conditions to generate any creatures or even general objects?
051   Towards this aim, we believe that two main issues hinder the use of arbitrary skeletal conditions
052   for generation: (i) Lack of large-scale object-skeleton pairs for training. Extensive studies (Cao
053   et al., 2017; Fang et al., 2022; Martinez et al., 2017) on 2D/3D human pose estimation make human-
      skeleton paired data easy to obtain. However, when skeletal structures are unknown, estimating

104
      multi-view images. To address potential blurriness due to the low-resolution inputs and reconstruc-
      tion inaccuracies, we enhance appearance quality using a texture refinement stage that up-samples
105
      the multi-view images to higher resolutions and refines the original texture in UV space.

120   2     R ELATED W ORK
121
      Controllable 2D Generation. Based on image diffusion models like Stable Diffusion (Rombach
122
      et al., 2022), versatile controlling conditions have been studied. In terms of spatial controlling,
123   ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023) and other similar works (Mou et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024) train
124   a side network for spatial conditions such as edge maps, normal maps and human skeletons. Some
125   works focus on human image generation from skeletons (Ju et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024a; Hu,
126   2024). Box-based instance controlling is also concerned in some works (Zheng et al., 2023; Li
127   et al., 2023c; Zhou et al., 2024). As for content controlling, (Ruiz et al., 2023) finetunes the model
128   to bind the given subject with an identifier in text prompt. (Ye et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024b)
129   train an adapter to inject styles or concepts to the model. Some works (Liang et al., 2024; Wang
130   et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2024) also focus on human ID control. Besides, some methods (Meng et al.,
131
      2021; Bansal et al., 2023; Mo et al., 2024; Ohanyan et al., 2024) can achieve conditional generation
      without additional modules or fine-tuning.
132
133   Controllable 3D Generation. Content controlling in 3D generation can be easily realized by image-
134   to-3D generation, which has been studied by plenty of works (Hong et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024;
135   Li et al., 2023a; Xu et al., 2024). However, in the image-to-3D paradigm, spatial controlling for 3D
136   generation is not as easy as content controlling. Coin3D (Dong et al., 2024) presents a framework
137
      to control the multi-view diffusion and 3D generation by shape proxies, i.e. combination of simple
      basic shapes. Sculpt3D (Chen et al., 2024a) enhances text-to-3D generation with retrieved 3D priors.
138
      Sherpa3D (Liu et al., 2024) proposes to generate a coarse shape with a 3D diffusion model and
139
      refine the shape with SDS (Poole et al., 2022). Clay (Zhang et al., 2024a) designs a transformer-
140   based (Vaswani, 2017; Peebles & Xie, 2023) 3D diffusion framework and various conditions like
141   images and point clouds can be injected through cross-attention layers. Some works for 3D human or
142   avatar generation (Liao et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024b) uses human skeleton as
143   the condition in 2D or 3D space. A recent work (Li et al., 2023d) realizes 3D generation with single-
144   view 2D spatial conditions like normal maps and edge maps by conditional multi-view generation
145   and 3D reconstruction. Our work shares the similar workflow, but we focus on general skeleton
146   conditioned generation, which has never been studied by previous works.
147   Mesh Skeletonization. Various algorithms were designed for extracting skeletons from 3D
148   meshes. (Tagliasacchi et al., 2012) and (Bærentzen & Rotenberg, 2021) compute curve skeletons
149   (C-S) via iterative mesh contraction operations. (Dou et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024c) proposed to
150   extract skeletons medial axis transformation skeleton (MAT-S) by point selection and connection
151   prediction. C-S and MAT-S can serve as shape representation, while human-made skeletons (H-S)
152   are often different from them. Since the main purpose is animation, H-S only contain sparse joints
153   and bones. Some works (Xu et al., 2019b; 2020) propose data-driven approaches to learn mesh
154
      skeletonization from human annotated data. In this work, we have tried learning-based method (Xu
      et al., 2020) but found the results were not satisfactory. Therefore, we develop a new pipeline to
155
      generate skeletons which are as sparse as H-S while keep the shape of C-S.

192   Skeleton extraction. To enhance flexibility and robustness, we design a new reliable pipeline,
193
      utilizing curve skeletons as the intermediate representation. Illustration of the pipeline is in Fig. 2.
      Considering the structural inconsistency between curve skeletons and human-made skeletons, we
194
      further convert dense curves into sparse joints and bones. The detailed pipeline is elaborated below.
195
      1) Initially, Mean Curvature Flow (MCF) (Tagliasacchi et al., 2012) is employed to generate curve
196   skeletons from meshes robustly. 2) Next, we build a graph from the set of curves generated from the
197   mesh, consisting of dense nodes and edges. Intersection nodes (degree>1) are recognized and the
198   graph is divided into several parts by these nodes. 3) In each part, the curve does not contain any
199   branch so it can be simplified by Douglas-Peucker algorithm (DP) (Douglas & Peucker, 1973) into
200   line segments with fewer points.
201
      Tree conversion. At this stage, the basic shape of the skeleton is established, but the root position
202   and the bone direction between joints still need to be determined. The problem can be regarded as
203   graph to tree conversion. First, a spanning tree is build from the graph to eliminate cycles. We then
204   identify high-degree intersection nodes as candidates for the root. To ensure an efficient structure,
205   the skeleton is configured by selecting the tree with the minimum height among these candidates.
206   This approach ensures that the root node is located at a significant intersection, minimizing the dis-
207   tances between the root and other joints. More details of the full pipeline can be found in appendix.

235   Skeletal condition representation. As we want to generate images which aligns with the given
236   skeletons, using spatial guidance for the diffusion model is a reasonable way. Similar to 3D meshes,
237   skeletons can also be projected onto image planes as 2D conditions. However, depth information is
238   lost during the projection, posing a significant challenge for spatial guidance. Unlike meshes, skele-
239   tons only consist of joints and bones, which can easily cause both semantic and spatial ambiguity as
240   illustrated in Fig. 8. Thus, incorporating richer information is crucial to mitigate such ambiguities.
241   Coordinate Color Encoding (CCE). In order to preserve 3D information, we encode joint coordi-
242   nates using spatial colors. While prior works (Li et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2019) use canonical color
243   map for shape representation, our approach focuses on representing skeletons with sparse joints and
244   bones. We begin by normalizing skeletons within a canonical cube [0, 1]3 . Each position in this
245   cube corresponds to a unique color, with RGB values precisely matching the positional coordinates.
246   As a result, the 2D conditional image can represent the 3D spatial positions of the skeleton. For
247   bones, we assign the color based on their midpoint. Additionally, we incorporate normalized values
248   of view-dependent inverse depth of the skeleton as the alpha channel (CCE-D). With the absolute
249
      spatial coordinates and relative depth encoded in the conditional images, there will be more precise
      and richer guidance information for generation.
250
251   Skeletal condition injection. Spatial conditions like canny edges and normal maps have been in-
252   vestigated in 2D image diffusion models. In ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023), the conditional image
253   is encoded by convolution blocks, resulting in an output spatial size that matches the latent size.
254   Then, the condition features are added to the latent features. The encoder of the original diffusion
255
      model is copied as a side network to produce guidance features, which are fused with the original
      features in the decoder. Our pipeline adopts this paradigm from ControlNet, and we further enhance
256
      it with a more effective condition feature extraction module.
257
258   Skeletal Correlation Modeling (SCM). For a skeleton in 3D space, we first project it into multi-
259   view images as 2D conditions. Given the sparse nature of skeletal conditions in the spatial di-
260   mension, convolution blocks lack global modeling capacity. To address this, we design a Skeletal
261
      Correlation Module (SCM) to enhance the condition features by modeling the anatomy correlation
      among different parts of a skeleton, and the view correlation for different projection views. The
262
      structure of the module is in Fig. 3. (i) First, anatomy correlation is extracted by a self-attention
263
      layer, which constructs the global skeleton features for each view. (ii) Then, the cross-view cor-
264   relation is modeled by a cross-attention layer, allowing the extraction of correspondences among
265   skeleton images from multiple views. This enables the model to recognize identical joints in differ-
266   ent views. In addition, we use adaptive layer normalization (Xu et al., 2019a) to fuse the camera
267   pose embedding with the skeletal features. Associating each skeleton image with a camera pose
268   aids in generating view-dependent object shapes. Although similar attention layers exist in the U-
269   Net, adding correlation modeling layers during condition encoding significantly accelerates model
      training, achieving convergence 5 times faster (Fig. 10).

      4.2   M ULTI - VIEW I MAGES TO 3D G ENERATION
271
272   Instant reconstruction. Given the generated multi-view images, we use a Large Reconstruction
273   Model (LRM), specifically InstantMesh (Xu et al., 2024) for fast textured mesh reconstruction.
274
      However, the reconstructed textures often appear blurry. On the one hand, the resolution of generated
      images is 2562 , which struggles in capture fine details. On the other hand, the appearance quality
275
      also degrades during reconstruction. In order to recover and further enrich the appearance, we
276
      introduce a new refinement stage.
277
278   Appearance refinement. First, the generated multi-view images are up-scaled 4 times into 10242
279   by Stable Diffusion with ControlNet-Tile (Zhang et al., 2023). In order to keep multi-view appear-
280
      ance consistency, we perform view-concatenated inference, allowing attention layers to be shared
      by multiple views in a training-free manner. In addition, ControlNet-Edge (Zhang et al., 2023) is
281
      used to maintain the shape consistency during tiling. Canny edges (Ding & Goshtasby, 2001) are
282
      extracted as the additional condition. Once tiled, the high resolution images are used to refine the
283   reconstructed texture. A learnable 2D texture u in UV space is created and initialized as the recon-
284   structed texture u0 , and then images are rendered through differentiable rendering for given camera
285   views ci . The MSE loss is optimized between the rendered images and tiled high-res images. More-
286   over, a regularization term is added to maintain consistency in UV space:
287

297   4.3   O BJECT-S KELETON A LIGNMENT E VALUATION
298   Contrastive alignment. In order to measure how much an object is aligned with a skeleton, we
299   develop a new evaluator, named as Contrastive Object-Skeleton Alignment (COSA). We use the
300   self-supervised DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023) as the backbone F to extract both object and skeleton
301   features. Then, the alignment adapter Gθ consisting of several self-attention layers is used to mod-
302   ulate the features. The adapter ends with a average pooling layer to aggregate the aligned features
303   into a vector. Similar to CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), we train the adapter with contrastive learning
304   by InfoNCE loss (Sohn, 2016; Oord et al., 2018). Finally, the skeleton alignment score (SKA) can
305
      be calculated by cosine similarity between the features from an object image x and a skeleton image
      y as SSKA (x, y) = cos(G(F (x)), G(F (y))).
306
307   COSA guided diffusion. Based on COSA, another conditional generation pipeline can also be
308   realized, following the approach proposed in (Bansal et al., 2023). On each denoising time step t, the
309   approximate clean image x̂0 is estimated from the predicted noise ϵt as in DDIM (Song et al., 2020).
310   The estimated clean image and skeleton condition are fed into COSA to calculate the alignment loss
311
      LCOSA (x̂0 , y) = 1 − SSKA (x̂0 , y). Then the predicted noise is modified by the gradient of the
      alignment loss for actual denoising:
312
313                                      ϵ̂t = ϵt + s(t) · ∇LCOSA (x̂0 , y)                              (2)
314   where s(t) controls the guidance strength. With the additional guidance of the alignment loss, the
315   generated object will tend to follow the conditional skeleton y.

462   6.1     S KELETAL C ONDITION R EPRESENTATION
463
      The skeletal condition representation we use consists of coordinate color encoding (CCE) with depth
464
      alpha (D). The ablation results are shown in Fig. 8 and Fig. 9. Richer information in conditions can
465
      help the model to determine the content better. As a result, higher image quality can be achieved.
466   In Fig. 8 right, the skeleton of a penguin is highly ambiguous. If CCE-D is used, the body pose and
467   orientation of the penguin can be successfully inferred from colors. From Fig. 9, the quantitative
468   results indicate that CCE-D brings greater improvement for complex skeletons of animals and plants
469   than simple skeletons of human shapes.
470
471   6.2     S KELETAL C ORRELATION M ODELING
472
473   With richer information encoded in the condition, how to extract features from the condition also
474   counts. The corresponding module in previous works (Zhang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023d) mainly
475
      consists of convolution blocks. Different from them, since multi-view condition of sparse skeletons
      is used in our setting, correlation modeling needs to be considered. We show the effect of skeletal
476
      correlation module in Fig. 10. SCM with layer normalization (LN) achieves 4× faster convergence
477
      speed, compared with original convolution blocks. Furthermore, if LN is replaced with the adaptive
478   LN (AdaLN), convergence can be further accelerated. The model can achieve a SKA score of 75
479   within 1k training steps. The results indicate that for spatial guidance, extracting global features
480   from conditional images are crucial for conditional learning.

      7               L IMITATION AND F UTURE W ORK
523   Since our work is the first one achieving arbitrary skeletal conditioned generation, there are still
524   many problems can be further studied. The skeletons we currently use may have limited descrip-
525   tion ability for non-tree structured objects. More general yet efficient shape representations can be
526   studied as new conditions. In addition, our work only consider global skeletons without fine-grained
527   semantics. How to inject detailed semantics into the skeleton parts could also be a meaningful topic
528   to study. More discussion can be found in appendix.
529
530   8               C ONCLUSION
531
532   In this work, we propose to use skeletons as the structural condition for controllable generation.
533   First, we construct a large-scale 3D mesh-skeleton paired dataset. We propose an effective mesh
534   skeletonization method to generate mesh-aligned sparse skeletons with a high success rate. Based
535
      on the dataset, we present a skeletal conditioned multi-view generation pipeline. Coordinate color
      encoding and skeletal correlation module are proposed to realize efficient condition representation
536
      and injection. Furthermore, 3D meshes can be instantly reconstructed, followed by a refinement
537
      stage to achieve better texture quality. In summary, our work achieves controllable multi-view and
538   3D generation with arbitrary skeletons as conditions.
