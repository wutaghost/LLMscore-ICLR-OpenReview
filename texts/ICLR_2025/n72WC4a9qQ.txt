009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011            Software robots have long been used in Robotic Process Automation (RPA) to automate
012            mundane and repetitive computer tasks. With the advent of Large Language Models (LLMs)
013            and their advanced reasoning capabilities, these agents are now able to handle more complex
014            or previously unseen tasks. However, LLM-based automation techniques in recent literature
015            frequently rely on HTML source code for input or application-specific API calls for actions,
016            limiting their applicability to specific environments. We propose an LLM-based agent that
017
               mimics human behavior in solving computer tasks. It perceives its environment solely
               through screenshot images, which are then converted into text for an LLM to process.
018
               By leveraging the reasoning capability of the LLM, we eliminate the need for large-scale
019
               human demonstration data typically required for model training. The agent only executes
020            keyboard and mouse operations on Graphical User Interface (GUI), removing the need for
021            pre-provided APIs to function. To further enhance the agent’s performance in this setting,
022            we propose a novel prompting strategy called Context-Aware Action Planning (CAAP)
023            prompting, which enables the agent to thoroughly examine the task context from multiple
024            perspectives. Our agent achieves an average success rate of 94.5% on MiniWoB++ and an
025            average task score of 62.3 on WebShop, outperforming all previous studies of agents that
026            rely solely on screen images. This method demonstrates potential for broader applications,
027            particularly for tasks requiring coordination across multiple applications on desktops or
028
               smartphones, marking a significant advancement in the field of automation agents. Codes
               and models are accessible at https://github.com/caap-agent/caap-agent.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Artificial intelligence (AI) agents are often commercialized as Robotic Process Automation (RPA) tools,
034   designed to streamline business processes with minimal human intervention. Traditional RPA agents use
035   rule-based algorithms to automate structured tasks, making them particularly effective for repetitive and
036   standardized data processing. However, most desktop tasks are not well-suited to this rule-based approach,
037   as they frequently involve handling unexpected situations and exceptions that are too numerous and varied
038   to anticipate and program for in advance. In contrast, agents utilizing deep neural networks can adapt
039
      more flexibly to unforeseen situations. Studies have shown that agents trained on extensive data of expert
      demonstrations can effectively handle unseen tasks by imitating the decision-making styles of human
040
      experts (Humphreys et al., 2022; Shaw et al., 2023). The emergence of Large Language Models (LLMs)
041
      is now further advancing the capabilities of RPA agents. LLMs have demonstrated the potential to support
042   complex decision-making processes with their advanced reasoning capabilities. Their In-Context Learning
043   (ICL) ability allows them to learn meaningful actions for specific tasks with just a few examples, significantly
044   reducing the need for large-scale data collection of expert demonstrations. As a result, research on agents
045   leveraging ICL has been vigorously pursued in recent years (Yao et al., 2022b; Sridhar et al., 2023; Zheng
046   et al., 2023; Shinn et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024). As AI chips are

                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


047
      increasingly integrated into computers and smartphones (Intel, March 26, 2024; Samsung, January 18, 2024),
048
      and as these AI devices are poised to become the standards in the market, this new trend in the design of
049   AI-driven RPA tools is likely to have a profound impact.
050
051   Most prior research on agents for solving computer tasks has primarily relied on HTML or the Document
052
      Object Model (DOM) as input sources, or has leveraged well-structured Application Programming Interfaces
      (APIs) to interact with digital environments (Humphreys et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2018; Gur et al., 2022; Kim
053
      et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023; Furuta et al., 2023). However, the effectiveness of these methods depends on the
054
      availability of such shortcuts. They lack general applicability to broader desktop tasks that are not web-based
055   or that require the use of applications with no provided APIs. Often, many applications and web services in
056   the real world do not maintain rigorous development standards.
057
058
      A promising approach to ensure the applicability of agents for more general computer tasks is to rely
      solely on visual information from the screen as input and use keyboard and mouse actions as output (Shaw
059
      et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2024). By leveraging well-established GUI environments, such agents can be
060
      readily deployed to assist humans in performing their computer tasks. Prior research in this direction has
061   primarily focused on using Vision-Transformers, which are a natural choice for processing screenshot images.
062   However, even state-of-the-art Vision-Language Models (VLMs) struggle to ground actions to specific pixel
063   coordinates (Anthropic; Zheng et al., 2024). Without a robust and reliable mechanism for processing the
064   spatial information of GUI elements, achieving a practical agent remains unattainable.
065
      In this paper, we propose a novel agent architecture composed of a separate visual observation module and a
066
      reasoning model (LLM), which strongly reinforces the agent’s coordinate-grounding functionality. The visual
067   observation module interprets the environment and represents it in textual form, alleviating the burden on the
068   VLM to simultaneously process visual information and perform task-specific, reasoning-based planning. This
069   modular approach also greatly improves the agent’s adaptability a cross various applications, as each module
070   can be trained or upgraded independently. To further support and maximize the effectiveness of our agent
071   design, we have developed a novel prompting technique called Context-Aware Action Planning (CAAP). This
072   technique systematically organizes the contextual information required for action-proposal and identifies
073   syntactic structures that trigger the most effective Chain of Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022). Our agent
074   demonstrates superior task-solving performance on the MiniWoB++ and WebShop benchmark. It surpasses
075
      previous studies by utilizing only a minimal amount of human demonstration data, thereby illustrating its
      efficiency and effectiveness in handling complex computer tasks with limited training cost.
076
077   Our contributions are as follows:
078
      • We present the first LLM-based agent for computer tasks that leverages ICL while emulating human
079   behavior by using front-end User Interface (UI) channels as input-output sources. It processes input through
080   an image-to-text model followed by an LLM, a combination that eliminates the need for extensive human
081   labor in dataset collection before deploying the agent for new tasks. The agent operates exclusively through
082   keyboard and mouse actions, enabling deployment on any GUI-based system without the need for special
083   APIs to be provided.
084
      • We introduce an effective prompting technique named CAAP, which enhances the ICL capabilities of an
085   LLM-based agent in managing complex desktop tasks. By systematically structuring contextual information
086   and leveraging syntactic patterns that trigger optimal CoT reasoning, CAAP significantly outperforms other
087   agent designs.

093   Transformer, and LSTM that uses image and DOM data as inputs and produces policies for keyboard-mouse

106
      recognized or additional actions supported for a new task, the entire architecture must be retrained.
107
108   LLM-based agents with ICL To reduce dependence on large quantities of human demonstration data,
109   several studies have explored the use of LLMs for action planning. Yao et al. (2022b) developed the ReAct
110   model, which evaluates the current state before determining subsequent actions. Kim et al. (2023) introduced
111   the Recursive Criticism and Improvement (RCI) agent, which enhances action planning through iterative
112   self-critique, highlighting the importance of advanced reasoning techniques in agents. Huang et al. (2022)
113   proposed Inner Monologue (IM), a mechanism that integrates real-time environmental states into action
114   planning via an explicit feedback loop. Inspired by IM, Sun et al. (2023) introduced AdaPlanner, which
115   prompts LLMs to generate action plans as Python functions and refine them based on feedback from execution
116
      failures. Despite their innovations, all these approaches share a common limitation: their architectures are
      designed to operate solely with HTML inputs, restricting their applicability to constrained, well-curated web
117
      environments.

120   3         M ODULARIZED GUI AGENT FOR COMPUTER TASKS
121
122   Our agent is composed of three distinct modules: the visual observer, which processes and interprets image
123   inputs; the action proposer, which suggests subsequent actions; and the action executor, which carries out
124   the proposed actions. The agent operates by iteratively repeating a cycle in which these modules execute
125
      sequentially. Each module is further divided into sub-modules based on its functionality, as illustrated in
      Figure 1. This modular architecture provides substantial benefits over monolithic agent designs, such as
126
      those utilizing VLMs, particularly in flexibility, resource efficiency, and scalability. Modular designs enable
127
      targeted updates to specific components, ensuring more robust and stable improvements without impacting the
128   entire system. Additionally, they reduce computational complexity during training. When needed, functional
129   sub-modules can be seamlessly integrated alongside existing ones to further enhance system performance.
130

136   understanding techniques, during which detailed attributes of each element are captured. This processing
137   results in the information being organized into structured text, ready to be handed to the action proposer as

      the representation of the current state.

198   3.2   ACTION PROPOSER
199
200   Upon receiving the representation of the current screen, the action proposer is employed to determine possible
201
      actions for completing the task. The proposal process is facilitated by its two key components: a context
      collector and a CAAP prompter. The context collector gathers vital contextual information that aids in task
202
      resolution. This includes exemplary demonstrations, the ultimate task goal, the history of actions performed
203
      so far, and the range of possible actions that the language model can choose from, all formatted as text. The
204   CAAP prompter then uses this information to construct prompts that are sent to the language model, eliciting
205   responses that guide the next steps. The CAAP prompting method, one of our major contributions in this
206   paper, is discussed in detail in Section 4.
207
208   3.3   ACTION EXECUTOR
209
210   The actions determined by the action proposer are implemented through a low-level interface to effect changes
211   in the environment by the last module, action executor. It first identifies a series of recommended actions by
212   parsing the response text from the action proposer. Then, it executes these actions using the keyboard and
213   mouse interfaces of the operating system. After each action, it receives an updated screenshot, halting further
214
      actions if a change in the environment is detected. The types of actions that can be executed include mouse
      actions such as clicking, pointing, and scrolling, as well as keyboard actions like typing and using common
215
      shortcuts. These are enumerated in Table 5 of Appendix B and implemented as Python functions using the
216
      PyAutoGUI package.

219   4     CAAP PROMPTING
220
221   The performance of LLM-based agents is significantly influenced by the structure of their prompts. Our
222   prompting approach is specifically designed to enable the LLM to effectively utilize surrounding context
223   information essential for decision-making. This approach aims to elicit a CoT reasoning process. The strength
224
      of the CoT mechanism lies in its ability to connect disparate pieces of context information, progressively
      reinforcing their cohesion and enriching the reasoning process. CAAP prompting is specifically developed to
225
      enable the effective utilization of this mechanism during the action proposal phase. To achieve this, it actively
226
      incorporates both explicit and implicit strategies to guide the LLM’s CoT reasoning. The design of CAAP
227   prompting is systematically structured as illustrated in Figure 3.
228

      5.1   M INI W O B++
294
      MiniWoB++ is a highly suitable benchmark for evaluating an agent’s ability to handle a wide range of
295   scenarios encountered during computer task execution, as it encompasses over a hundred of fundamental
296   tasks (Liu et al., 2018; Shi et al., 2017; Min). As an extension of OpenAI MiniWoB, this benchmark includes a
297   diverse spectrum of tasks, ranging from simple activities such as button clicks and form filling to more complex
298   tasks like email forwarding and flight booking. We utilize the MiniWoB++ benchmark to thoroughly assess
299   our agent’s effectiveness in navigating and solving diverse tasks within a simulated computer environment.
300
      Previous studies using the MiniWoB++ benchmark often choose subsets of tasks most suitable for demon-
301   strating their methods. We have also selected a total of 73 MiniWoB++ tasks that meet the following criteria:
302   (1) tasks that consist of UI elements of types recognizable by our visual observer module, as listed in Table 4
303   of Appendix A; (2) tasks where none of UI elements extend beyond the default screen area of 160x210 pixels;
304   and (3) tasks that do not require color recognition, as our visual observer module is not yet trained for this
305   capability.
306
      For each selected task, we run at least 50 repetitions with different seeds to estimate the Success Rate (SR)
307   for that task. Across all task types, we consistently use the identical CAAP prompt template without making
308   any manual adjustments for specific tasks, unlike some other works (Kim et al., 2023).
309
310   Models and datasets YOLO and Pix2Struct models within the visual observer have been fine-tuned
311   to enable accurate recognition of images presented in MiniWoB++ environment. The image dataset for
312   training has been collected through a process of capturing screenshots and annotating the UI elements within.
313   This dataset consists of 1.76K screenshot images, gathered and annotated over a span of 10 hours by two
314   individuals.
315
      For human expert demonstrations, we generate between 0 and 5 demonstrations per task, tailored to the task’s
316   complexity, amassing a total of 97 demonstrations for our experiments. We record the trajectory of each action
317   performed by the demonstrators, alongside screenshots taken at the time of each action. These screenshots
318   are subsequently analyzed to extract UI elements, enabling the identification of the UI component interacted
319   with during mouse-related actions, such as clicks. This demonstration data have been collected by a single
320   individual over a span of approximately two hours.
321
      For the action-proposal language model, we utilize the Azure OpenAI gpt-4-0125 model. The Azure OpenAI
322   API features the ‘function calling’ capability, thus our prompt does not directly include the list of action types
323   (as depicted in Figure 3). Instead, this contextual information is conveyed to the language model through
324   arguments in the function calling feature. An example of how the function calling is applied is illustrated in
325   Figure 8 in Appendix E.

447   RCI approach tends to modify even correct action plans during the iterative critique and revision process. In
448   contrast, the CAAP method, by focusing on thorough context review rather than critique, is able to avoid
449

      plan, failing to adapt to changes in the environment that occur during execution. Conversely, the CAAP
450
      method is designed to account for state changes resulting from prior actions. This experiment underscores
451
      the effectiveness of our CAAP prompting, confirming its potential to enhance the performance of LLMs in
452   computer tasks.

455   6    C ONCLUSION
456
457   In this paper, we introduce a modularly designed GUI agent model that emulates the way humans solve
458
      tasks on computers. Our modular design not only effectively enhances pixel coordinate detection—a critical
      challenge faced by integrated VLM architectures—but also addresses the limitations of existing models that
459
      rely heavily on imitation learning with large amounts of human demonstrations. Furthermore, we propose the
460
      CAAP prompting mechanism to improve the action-proposal capabilities of LLM-based agents for computer
461   tasks by guiding the LLM to fully utilize the CoT reasoning mechanism. Evaluations on the MiniWoB++
462   and WebShop benchmarks demonstrate that our agent model achieves superior performance compared to
463   the previous methods while requiring significantly less data. By adopting a modular approach that separates
464   visual perception, action proposal, and action execution, our method allows for easy adaptation to new tasks
465   or model upgrades. Additionally, our agent is designed to operate beyond web environments and across
466   inter-application settings, offering broader applicability and significant advantages in terms of generality.

712   Fine-tuning dataset for visual observer Data collection for fine-tuning the visual observer involves a
713   two-step process: screenshot collection and annotation of the collected UI elements. Initially, the target task
714   is manually performed while screenshots are taken at each step. Image comparisons are then conducted to
715   remove duplicate screenshots. From 10 episodes for each of the 73 tasks, a total of 1,768 screenshots were

717   utilized. Each UI element within the screenshots is marked with bounding boxes, and specific attributes are
718

      employed to enhance the dataset. These techniques include color adjustments, minor scaling of bounding box
719
      sizes, and the random insertion of Gaussian noise. As a result, the total number of annotated data items is
720
      tripled compared to the original dataset.

1251   2023) and AdaPlanner (Sun et al., 2023) achieved average task SRs above 92% with fewer than 100
1252   demonstrations, but their dependence on HTML inputs limits their task coverage. When recalculating SRs,
1253   including unsupported or unreported tasks, their performance drops to 50.8% and 49.2%, respectively.
1254   WebGUM (Furuta et al., 2023) expanded task coverage by using both HTML and image inputs. However,
1255   despite utilizing 401K demonstrations, its results only showed a 1.9% improvement over RCI, achieving an
1256   SR of 52.7%. Pix2Act (Shaw et al., 2023) was the first method to achieve high task SRs on MiniWoB++ using
1257   image inputs exclusively. It leveraged 1.3 million demonstrations, achieving over 96% success on 58 tasks,
1258   with an SR of 55.8% across the same set of 100 tasks. In contrast, SeeClick, which also used image inputs but
1259
       on a smaller demonstration dataset, failed to sufficiently demonstrate problem-solving ability. Our approach
       represents the most advanced image-only agent, solving 73 tasks with a high SR of 94.5%, using fewer than
1260
       1.8K demonstrations. Furthermore, it achieved a 63.3% SR on the 100-task range, 7.5% higher than Pix2Act,
1261
       with 61 tasks demonstrating an SR of over 80%—five more tasks than Pix2Act. (See Table 7 of Appendix G.)
1262   This represents a significant improvement achieved with fewer than 100 human demonstrations, resulting
1263   from the powerful reasoning capabilities of the LLM and our carefully modularized agent design.
