009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011             Sparse Mixture of Experts (SMoE) has emerged as a breakthrough approach for
012             achieving unprecedented scalability in deep learning. By enabling models to expand
013             their parameter count exponentially while selectively activating only a small subset
014             of parameters per sample, SMoEs maintain high efficiency. However, SMoE models
015             are susceptible to routing fluctuations, leading to instability and non-robustness.
016             In this work, we unveils SMoE-based attention as a point estimate of a regression
017
                function of a three-layer hierarchical mixture of experts regression. Through this
                probabilistic graphical model (PGM) framework, we highlight the conditional
018
                independence in expert-selection process of tokens, which exposes the model
019
                to routing fluctuation and non-robustness. Motivated by this PGM framework,
020             we propose Mutual-Inform SMoEs, including Similarity and Attention-Inform
021             SMoE, which eliminate the assumption of conditional independence by allowing
022             tokens to directly influence each other on expert-decisions. We theoretically
023             demonstrate that our methods lower the entropy in decision-making, enabling more
024             confident and consistent expert assignments. Finally, we empirically validate our
025             models on ImageNet classification and Wikitext-103 language modeling, showing
026             significant improvements in reducing routing fluctuations, enhancing performance,
027             and increasing model robustness compared to baseline Transformer-SMoE models.

      1     I NTRODUCTION
030
031
      Mixture of Experts (MoEs) (Jacobs et al., 1991; Jordan & Jacobs, 1994) has been widely used as
032   a principle approach to scale up the number of parameters of deep neural networks while introduc-
033   ing an affordable computation. As a result, MoEs appears in almost all applications of machine
034   learning and deep learning including large language model (Devlin et al., 2018; Radford et al.,
035   2019; Raffel et al., 2020; Kaplan et al., 2020; Brown et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023), vision
036   understanding (Neil & Dirk, 2020; Bao et al., 2021; 2022; Li et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2024), and
037   many other applications (Subramanian et al., 2024; Gaur et al., 2021; Gormley & Murphy, 2011).
038   A recent variation of Mixture of Experts (MoEs), called Sparse MoEs (SMoEs) (Shazeer et al.,
039   2017), has been introduced to enhance model size to billion-parameter while maintaining constant
040
      computational costs by modularizing the network and activating only specific subsets of experts for
      each input. Therefore, SMoEs has been applied successfully in translation models (Lepikhin et al.,
041
      2020), pre-training (Fedus et al., 2022; Artetxe et al., 2021), GPT-3 level one-shot performance (Du
042
      et al., 2022), image classification (Riquelme et al., 2021), and so on.

086   Routing fluctuation in SMoE. One of the major challenges in training SMoE Transformers is the
087   instability caused by fluctuating routing decisions during training (Dai et al., 2022; Zoph et al., 2022;
088   Chi et al., 2022). This instability leads to model non-robustness. Improving the consistency of
089   expert routing decisions is critical for model stability and overall model performance, since routing
090
      fluctuations, especially in the later stages of training make it challenging to determine an appropriate
      stopping point for training. For instance, even in the final epochs of training, upto 33% of tokens still
091
      switch their assigned experts (c.f. Figure 2). This can result in different behavior during inference
092
      depending on when training is halted. Therefore, reinforcing consistent routing decisions enhances
093   model robustness and improves overall performance.

      1.3   C ONTRIBUTIONS
096
097   We develop a probabilistic graphical model (PGM) framework for the attention-(S)MoE block, within
098   which we highlight the conditional independence in expert selection for each individual token, a
099   property that makes it prone to routing fluctuations. Building on this insight, we propose a novel
100   notion of (S)MoE, named Mutual-Inform (S)MoE, which encourages the assignment of similar tokens
101   to the same expert. By letting other tokens directly influence one’s routing decision, we demonstrate
102   that the model reduces routing fluctuations, resulting in enhanced robustness.
103   Our contributions are four-fold:
104
105   • We present a novel probabilistic graphical framework (PGM) revealing that attention-(S)MoE is a
106     point estimate of the regression function in a three-layer hierarchical mixture of expert regression.
107     Through this PGM perspective, we show the conditional independence in token’s expert selection,
        leading to routing fluctuation, and model non-robustness.

155
      independence between variables, highlighting the limitations that can arise from these assumptions.

230   3     M UTUAL -I NFORM (S)M O E S
231
232   Leveraging token similarity to guide expert selection can both reduce routing fluctuations and
233   facilitate expert learning by presenting less diverse input to each expert. In this section, from the PGM
234   perspective, we introduce the notion of Mutual-Inform MoEs, where expert decisions are directly
235   dependent based on tokens’ correspondence. These correspondence can be computed directly from
236   token embeddings ũi within the MoE layers, given the Similarity-Inform mechanism (Section 3.1) or
237   derived from the attention layer, a variation we call Attention-Informed MoE (Section 3.2). Their
238
      sparse version, Similarity-Infom SMoEs and Attention-Inform SMoE are derived accordingly as
      special cases, forming the notion of Mutual-Inform SMoEs. In Section 3.3, we present an entropy
239
      analysis of the Mutual-Inform MoE model to highlight the advantage of our method in reducing
240
      routing fluctuation. Specifically, we demonstrate how our approach lowers the entropy of indecisive
241   or less confident tokens, making them less prone to fluctuations in their routing decisions.

      3.2     T OKEN ROUTING WITH ATTENTION -I NFORM
315
316   The routing decision for each token can also be informed via their dependency capture in the attention
317   layers. Instead of directly basing the final decision d˜i of each token on the similarity variable s̃i as
318   Similarity-Inform (S)MoEs, we establish a link from the variable zi — which represents the token
319   that token i attends to in the attention layer — to the decision d˜i . In this way, rather than computing
320   the similarity matrix based on Ū, the input of SMoE layers, we utilize the similarity information
321   directly from the attention layer to inform the expert choice for tokens. This approach also leads
322   to a consistent decision process because the attention layers inherently capture the relationships
323   between tokens. This means the choice of which expert a token is routed to is aligned with the token’s
      interactions in the attention mechanism.

501   5   R ELATED W ORK
502
503   Routing fluctuation as been discussed in existing literature. Nguyen et al. (2024) mentions that various
504
      SMoE routers Csordás et al. (2023); Do et al. (2023) suffer from routing fluctuation without proposing
      solutions. In addition Su et al. (2024) suggests that due to the variation of learnable parameters in the
505
      router. StableMoE (Dai et al., 2022) has been proposed to reduce the routing fluctuation problem
506
      by using two training stages. During the initial training phase, StableMoE develops a balanced and
507   cohesive routing strategy, which it then distills into a lightweight router that operates independently
508   of the backbone model. In the second training phase, StableMoE uses the distilled router to establish
509   the token-to-expert assignments and locks this assignment in place to ensure a stable routing strategy.
510   SMoE-dropout Chen et al. (2023) is another work that also provides another solution to improve the
511   stability of the model. This method initially randomizes and freezes the router during training to
512   provide stable routing strategies Zoph et al. (2022) examine several approaches to improve stability
513   including removing multiplicative interactions, injecting model noise, and constraining activations
514   and gradients. After the examination, the authors propose the router z-loss which enhance the training
515
      stability with no quality degradation. Chi et al. (2022) proposes to estimate the routing scores between
      tokens and experts on a low-dimensional hypersphere to achieve more consistent routing compared
516
      to the conventional approach. Feedforward layers are replaced by hash layers in (Roller et al., 2021)
517
      to to keep routing choices consistent. Lewis et al. (2021) formulates routing as a linear assignment
518   problem that globally maximizes token-expert similarities for increasing the stability. Our work is
519   orthogonal to these approaches: to reduce routing fluctuation, we encourage tokens to influence each
520   other’s routing decision based on their similarity.
521
522   6   C ONCLUDING R EMARKS
523
524   We have presented a probabilistic graphical model view point of attention-(S)MoE. From the new
525   perspective, we have introduced a novel notion of (S)MoEs, named Mutual-Inform (S)MoEs, where
526   expert decisions are made from all input tokens via their similarities and relevance. We have proposed
527   two variants of Mutual-Inform (S)MoEs i.e., Similarity-Inform (S)MoE and Attention-Inform (S)MoE,
528   in which a token’s routing decision can be influenced by others’. We proved that our methods help
529
      improve confidence in the decision-making processes by reducing the entropy of expert assignments.
      Finally, we carry out extensive experiments on ImageNet classification and Wikitext-103 language
530
      modeling to demonstrate the advantages and robustness of our Mutual-Inform (S)MoE models. A
531
      limitation of our paper is that we have not considered a generative model that capture the token
532   generation process in our PGM. Studying transformer-MoE from a generative model perspective is
533   an exciting research direction. We leave it for future work.

952   and test sets consist of 60 articles each, with word counts of 218,000 and 246,000 respectively,
953   amounting to approximately 268,000 words combined. To assess the resilience of our methods, we

955   method randomly substitutes words with "AAA," challenging the model’s ability to accurately predict
956
      subsequent words in the sequence.

960   standard model for experiments is medium-sized with 6 layers. Each model incorporates 16 experts
961   in every models, selecting Top-1 or Top-2 experts (K = 2) per input. All models employ an identical
962
      sparse router function, comprising a linear network that processes input data, followed by TopK and
      Softmax functions. The models undergo 60 epochs of training, while GLaM models train for 80
963
      epochs without any additional load balancing loss. Our implementation builds upon the codebase
