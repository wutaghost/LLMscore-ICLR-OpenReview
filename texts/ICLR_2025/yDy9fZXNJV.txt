011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Logic synthesis is a crucial phase in the circuit design process, responsible
014            for transforming hardware description language (HDL) designs into optimized
015
               netlists. However, traditional logic synthesis methods are computationally inten-
               sive, restricting their iterative use in refining chip designs. Recent advancements
016
               in large language models (LLMs), particularly those fine-tuned on programming
017
               languages, present a promising alternative. This work proposes augmenting LLMs
018            with predictor networks trained to estimate circuit quality directly from HDL code.
019            To enhance performance, the model is regularized using embeddings from graph
020            neural networks (GNNs) trained on Look-Up Table (LUT) graphs, thereby incor-
021            porating lower-level circuit insights. The proposed method demonstrates superior
022            performance compared to existing graph-based RTL-level estimation techniques
023            on the established benchmark OpenABCD, while providing instant feedback on
024            HDL code quality.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Rapid technological advancements in computing power has taken an increasingly important role
029
      in the past decades in driving scientific research in biology (Schatz, 2012), chemistry (Akimov &
030   Prezhdo, 2015), physics (Dongarra & Keyes, 2024) and especially artificial intelligence, where it has
031   been estimated that at least half of all performance gains in the past ten years have stemmed from
032   hardware improvements alone (Hernandez & Brown, 2020; Dorner, 2021; Karpathy, 2022; Erdil &
033   Besiroglu, 2022; Ho et al., 2024). This ever-rising demand for compute power means that efficient
034   and effective electronic chip design has become increasingly critical.
035
      Modern electronic chip design is a complex, multi-stage endeavor that begins with a chip architect
036   specifying the digital circuit’s functionality in a Hardware Description Language (HDL), such as
037   Verilog (Thomas & Moorby, 2008) or VHDL (Coelho, 2012). This HDL code is then subjected
038   to a series of transformations and optimizations, ultimately yielding a physical circuit design that
039   can be manufactured (LaMeres, 2023). In a previous era where circuits were small and limited
040   in functionality, this logic synthesis process was quick and the chip architect could quickly receive
041   feedback and iterate on its HDL code. However, with the increasing complexity of industrial designs,
042   which now can comprise hundreds of millions of logic gates (Amarú et al., 2017), even a single
043   synthesis run has become massively expensive. This has driven the need for alternate ways of
044
      providing feedback on HDL code without running the actual logic synthesis process.
045   A natural way to tackle this problem is to train a machine learning model that can take the HDL code
046   as input, and output estimates of circuit quality such as wire length or delay that could have been
047   computed had the logic synthesis process been run. A few works have approached this topic, by
048   extracting graphical information about the code and using hand-designed statistics of those graphs
049   as features (Zhou et al., 2019; Sengupta et al., 2022; Fang et al., 2023). Although these works had
050
      encouraging results, their performance has been limited by the relatively shallow understanding of
      the semantics of the code that these statistics can provide.
051
052   Recently, Large Language Models fine-tuned on code, such as Code-T5 (Wang et al., 2021),
053   Codex (Chen et al., 2021), CodeGen Nijkamp et al. (2023), CodeLlama (Roziere et al., 2023) and
      DeepSeek-Coder (Guo et al., 2024), have emerged that have proven remarkably successful on a


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      wide range of tasks (Zheng et al., 2023), most notably as code assistants such as Github Copilot1 .
055   Although most models are generalists trained on general-purpose programming languages such as
056   C++ and Python, a few models, such as CodeGen-Verilog (Thakur et al., 2023), VeriGen (Thakur
057   et al., 2024), RTLCoder (Liu et al., 2023c) and CodeV (Zhao et al., 2024), have been specifically
058   trained on Verilog, the most popular HDL language. The analysis of these models, however, has
059   been so far limited to investigating their ability to generate realistic code, and an investigation of the
060   predictive power of those internal representations has been lacking.
061   In this work, we demonstrate for the first time that the hidden states computed by these novel Verilog
062   large language models contain rich insights which can be used to predict quality-of-result metrics
063   with higher accuracy than previous machine learning models. Namely, we feed Verilog code to the
064   state-of-the-art CodeV model, and train an inexpensive decoder neural network that uses the LLM’s
065   hidden states as features to predict area and delay. In addition, and critically, we regularize this
066   decoder to encourage its embeddings to resemble those of a graph neural network model trained on
067   Look-Up Table (LUT) graph, an intermediate representation used during the logic synthesis process.
068   The resulting decoder is shown to strongly outperform state-of-the-art baselines, and incidentally
069
      shows that those novel Verilog language models extract in their hidden states surprisingly complex
      insights about the circuits represented by raw code.
070
071   Our work makes the following main contributions:
072
073             1. We develop the first truly end-to-end machine learning model in the literature, named
074
                   VeriDistill, which can take raw Verilog code, without any preprocessing, and produce ac-
                   curate estimates of circuit area/delay metrics.
075
076             2. Moreover, we apply during training a novel knowledge distillation method which allows
077                to transfer low-level insights about the circuit, in the form of LUT graphs, back into the
078                machine learning predictor model.
079             3. We demonstrate through experiments that the combination of those two elements outper-
080                forms previous state-of-the-art baselines in a large-scale Verilog dataset and enhances the
081                model’s ability to transfer to out-of-distribution data.
082             4. Finally, we also demonstrate that both using LLM representations and the knowledge distil-
083                lation are essential, in that removing any one of these components brings the performance
084                back below the previous baselines.
085
086   The remainder of this paper is structured as follows. Section 2 provides an overview of the rele-
087

088
      our approach is then demonstrated through a series of experiments, which are reported in Section 4.
089
      Finally, Section 5 summarizes our main findings, discusses their implications, and outlines potential
090   avenues for future research.

095   2.1       Q UALITY- OF -R ESULT P REDICTION FROM HDL C ODE
096
      Closest to ours is the work of Sengupta et al. (2022). Their approach consists in computing the
097
      Abstract Syntax Tree (AST) induced by Verilog code, and extracting from this free vector- and
098   graph-based features. They then train several machine learning models to predict from these features
099   the total negative slack and dynamic power of the circuit. Among all the models evaluated, the
100   XGBoost Regressor performs best and achieves 95% R2-score. The analysis was however limited
101   to different runs of a single circuit and it is not clear how the performance would generalize to
102   different circuits. Since the Abstract Syntax Tree is essentially the raw Verilog code with extra
103   syntactic information, which can be obtained at little cost at inference time by a grammar parser, we
104   include it (along with variants) as baselines in our experimental section.
105   Further related is the work of Fang et al. (2023) and Fang et al. (2024b). They propose to pro-
106   cess Verilog code into a new representation called Simple Operator Graph (SOG), and test several
107

      machine learning models (Transformers, Random Forests, Graph Neural Networks and XGBoost
109   regressors) to predict path delay, module-level power and combinatorial area. Although achieving
110   promising results, computing the SOG requires expensive conversion of linguistic data into bit-level
111   operators using logic synthesis tool Yosys (Wolf et al., 2013), which is outside the scope of this
112   work.
113
      Finally, some works take a step further and try to assist circuit design by annotating which parts
114
      of HDL is most critical to achieved quality-of-result metrics. For example, Sengupta et al. (2023)
115   attempts to identify timing critical components based on path delay prediction. The AST of each
116   Verilog design is extracted and converted into a graph, with nodes representing IO ports, registers or
117   behavior logic. Behavioral paths are extracted from the graph and used for path-level feature gener-
118   ation. Delay labels of timing paths are generated using commercial synthesis tools, and are assigned
119   to corresponding behavior paths with the same start and end points. By training an XGBoost model
120   on the resulting features, the authors achieve an average classification accuracy of 91%. Also similar
121   is RTL-Timer (Fang et al., 2024a), which ensembles four bit-level circuit representations to predict
122   the post-logic synthesis endpoint arrival time. Such predictions can then be mapped to registers in
123
      HDL code to identify critical code paths. Just as in the work of Fang et al. (2023), however, these
      representations are bit-level rather than word-level and therefore require some degree of processing
124
      by logic synthesis tools like Yosys.

128   2.2     LLM S FOR V ERILOG
129
130
      Large language models (LLMs) such as GPT (Ouyang et al., 2022) and Llama (Touvron et al., 2023)
131
      have achieved exceptional success in various natural language tasks and have expanded their suc-
132
      cess to programming languages as well. While decoder-only code LLMs such as Codex (Chen et al.,
133   2021) and CodeLlama (Roziere et al., 2023) have become the most popular due to their exceptional
134   performance in generation tasks like code generation and code translation, older encoder-only mod-
135   els such as CodeBERT (Feng et al., 2020) and encoder-decoder code LLMs such as CodeT5 (Wang
136   et al., 2021) have retained applications in code comprehension tasks such as clone detection and
137   code retrieval.
138
      Although excellent on generalist programming languages like Python or C++, these models have
139   been trained on the relatively small amount of HDL code that is publicly available on the internet,
140   and therefore have performed poorly on Verilog benchmarks like VerilogEval (Liu et al., 2023b)
141   and RTLLM (Lu et al., 2024). This has motivated further work to build LLMs with a higher-degree
142   of knowledge of hardware description languages. Both CodeGen-Verilog (Thakur et al., 2023) and
143   VeriGen (Thakur et al., 2024) used a combination of customized Verilog datasets from code reposi-
144   tory website GitHub2 and various textbooks to fine-tune code LLMs. Finally, RTLCoder (Liu et al.,
145   2023c) used the GPT 3.5 language model (Brown et al., 2020) to generate further Verilog data, in
146   a form of data augmentation, while CodeV (Zhao et al., 2024) used the same model to generate
147
      natural language description of real world Verilog code through multi-level summarization.
148   Besides Verilog code generation from natural language description, LLMs were also explored for
149   other EDA-related tasks. RTLFixer (Tsai et al., 2023) employed Retrieval-Augmented Generation
150   (RAG) and ReAct prompting techniques to interactively debug syntax errors in Verilog code, and
151   achieved remarkable improvement in success rates in the VerilogEval benchmark. ChipNemo (Liu
152   et al., 2023a) explored the application of LLMs in chip design process and adopted several domain
153
      adaptation techniques to train an LLM for various applications including assistant chatbots, EDA
      script generation, and bug summarization and analysis. Finally, ChatEDA (Wu et al., 2024) used
154
      code LLMs as an agent to autonomously complete the entire chip design flow from HDL code to
155
      the Graphic Data System Version II (GDSII) by managing task planning, script generation and task
156   execution. We refer the reader to the extensive survey of Zhong et al. (2023) for more details on the
157   application of LLMs in electronic design automation and future research directions in this field.

177   2.3   A LIGNMENT OF LLM AND GNN E MBEDDINGS
178
179   The multimodal alignment regularizer we propose during training also relates to the broader litera-
180   ture on tuning large language models to align with a pre-trained graph neural network, to incorporate
181
      its capabilities.
182   The work closest to ours is that of Mavromatis et al. (2023), who train a language model to perform
183   a node classification task while adding a regularizer that encourages the predictive distributions to
184   match a pre-trained graph neural network model. The language model makes predictions by passing
185   the graph as input, and extracting the representation corresponding to a final [CLS] classification
186   token. Also similar is Zou et al. (2023), which jointly trains a language model and a graph neural
187
      network on a common “context graph prediction” task which encourage alignment of their repre-
      sentations. They then discard the graph neural network and only keep the language model, so that
188
      topological characteristics best captured by graph convolutions can be said to have been incorporated
189
      in the language model.
190
191   More generally, there is a large literature on integrating pretrained graph neural networks with lan-
192   guage models by training an adaptive module (Liu et al., 2024; 2023d; Chai et al., 2023; Tang et al.,
193
      2024; Cao et al., 2023), allowing the language model to receive inputs from the graph neural net-
      work. Alternatively, multiple works have interlaced graph neural network layers and language model
194
      layers (Yasunaga et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022; Yasunaga et al., 2022; Jin et al.,
195
      2023). In either case, some kind of training is necessary to allow for interactions between the graph
196   neural network and the language model, although the result is not distillation of the graph neural
197   network’s perspective into the language model per se.

200   3     M ETHODOLOGY
201
202   We now present our VeriDistill approach in detail. As described in the introduction, turning a high-
203
      level description of a circuit in a Hardware Description Language like Verilog into a physical de-
      scription ready for manufacturing is a computationally expensive process involving several steps,
204
      each with an associated intermediate representation describing progressively lower-level elements
205
      of the circuit. Our goal is to predict low-level quality-of-result metrics, like area and delay, from the
206   highest-level representation, namely the HDL code.
207
208
      3.1   M ODEL
209
210   Our model takes as input Verilog code, which is fed to a Large Language Model (LLM). This LLM
211   has been specifically fine-tuned on Verilog code generation. The code is first split into a sequence
212   of tokens, which are then fed in parallel in the LLM. As an output, the LLM produces a sequence
213   of high-dimensional “hidden state” vectors, one for each token that is inputted to the LLM. We
214   average these hidden states, producing a single vector. This vector is then fed to a feedforward
215   neural network, composed of several linear layers with nonlinear activations, which finally outputs
      the QoR estimate. A diagram is provided as Figure 1.

244
245   We train the model as follows. We assume we have access to a training set of circuits with Verilog
246

259
260   In practice, training only with the supervised learning loss leads to limited performance. One poten-
261   tial explanation is that there is too much of a gap between a high-level circuit description like Verilog
262   and the low-level metrics we purport to predict. Intuitively, to perform high-quality predictions, we
263   would want the model to possess some degree of understanding of lower-level circuit design while
264   still only taking Verilog code as input.
265   We propose the following approach to address this problem. Prior to training, we pretrain a Graph
266   Neural Network (GNN) to predict the same QoR metric as VeriDistill, but from the Look-Up-Table
267   (LUT) graph XLUT of the circuit obtained after optimization using Yosys (Wolf et al., 2013). This
268   graph, which can be seen as an alternative to the more popular And-Inverter Graph (AIG) format,
269   is particularly suitable for GNN training as it is compact with rich node information. Moreover, as
      a circuit representation, it sits intermediate between a high-level description of the circuit encoded

308

343   4.2                 DATASETS
344
345   We train and evaluate on two separate datasets. The first dataset is used for training, validation,
346   and testing of all the methods, while OpenABCD contains out-of-distribution circuits aiming to
347   challenge VeriDistill and determine its ability to generalize.
348
349   Customized Dataset To train and evaluate our proposed method, we collect 18.4k Verilog ex-
350   amples provided by Pei et al. (2024) and 5.8k from Thakur et al. (2022). These Verilog examples
351
      are obtained from open-source GitHub repositories and textbooks and have been verified for syntax
      correctness. We use an open-sourced EDA platform OpenROAD Ajayi et al. (2019) with 7nm tech-
352
      nology PDK provided to conduct logic synthesis and record post-synthesis labels of area and delay.
353
      We convert the AIG graphs obtained after logic optimization into LUT graphs and save them for
354   training the auxiliary GNN model.
355
356   Note that a substantial fraction of the code snippets end up being functionally incorrect and failing
357
      some stage of the logic synthesis pipeline. Since we require functionally correct examples for their
      QoR metric to be well-defined, we removed such examples during the preprocessing. In addition,
358
      although not strictly a problem for our method, one of the competing baselines requires extracting
359
      the Abstract Syntax Tree (AST) of the Verilog, which is obtained by running a parser on the code.
360   The parser was unable to produce AST representations for a small fraction of the instances (FRAC-
361   TION%), which we removed from consideration. The resulting dataset, after filtering bad examples,
362   ended up having 16k examples, which we split into training, validation, and test sets with a ratio of
363   0.75/0.1/0.15, respectively.
364
      We depict the distribution of labels and the number of tokens in Verilog instances in Figure 3.
365
      As mentioned in prior work by Zhao et al. (2024) , Verilog data scarcity is a common challenge
366   in developing machine learning tools for RTL level tasks. We note that the majority of Verilog
367   instances contain less than 2000 tokens, with the corresponding circuits having a small area and
368   delay.
369
370   OpenABCD Additionally, we consider data provided by Chowdhury et al. (2021) to evaluate the
371   transferability of our method to unseen circuits. The OpenABCD dataset consists of functionally di-
372   verse designs such as bus communication protocols, computing processors, digital signal processing
373   cores, cryptographic accelerators and system controllers.

398   We adopt the method proposed by Sengupta et al. (2022) as our baseline. It relies on AST represen-
399   tations that can be easily converted from Verilog source files. We implement the method based on
400
      description in Sengupta et al. (2022). Verilator (Snyder, 2004) is used to convert each source Verilog
      into its respective AST representation, which can be represented as a graph. The nodes in the graph
401
      represent one of the following five semantic categories from the source Verilog (root, variable,
402
      operation, constant, edge), while edges are created between nodes with connections.
403
404   We implement three variants of the AST-based method:
405
406   AST-XGBoost We compute the following features: (i) the total number of input bits, (ii) the total
407   number of output bits, (iii) the longest path in the AST, (iv) the frequency of each node type in the
408   graph and (v) the frequency of each logic type in the graph. The features are concatenated to form
409   a feature vector with 108 features 3 . We perform a thorough hyper-parameter selection using grid
410
      search and employ early stopping to prevent over-fitting.
411
412   AST-GNN w/o KD The AST-GNN model takes in the following features per node: (i) the total
413   number of input bits, (ii) the total number of output bits, (iii) the node semantic type and (iv)
414
      the node operation type. Each feature is represented via a one-hot vector and is projected to a 4-
      dimensional space via a linear layer. The final node features consist of a (4 × 4) = 16-dimensional
415
      vector. We cap the number of input/output bits to 200, since 99.9 percent of the nodes in the dataset
416
      have less than 200 input/outputs. The AST-GNN model utilizes the same hyperparameters and
417   architecture as the auxiliary GNN model used for the knowledge distillation objective in VeriDistill.
418
419
      AST-GNN w/ KD We propose a third baseline, where the AST-GNN model is guided by the
420   LUT GNN model. The baseline utilizes the same student-teacher knowledge distillation as our
421   method. We introduce this baseline to demonstrate the effectiveness of utilizing an LLM in the
422   student network.

527   5     C ONCLUSION
528
529   In summary, in this work we propose a novel procedure to predict quality-of-result electronic circuit
530   metrics from Verilog code, by training a small neural network model on Verilog LLM representations
531   with a knowledge distillation regularizer which align its internal activations with those of a low-
532   level GNN model. We show that this new model, which we call VeriDistill, outperforms previous
533
      approaches in prediction accuracy.
534   Besides the clear practical value of our method, our results highlight the surprising phenomenon
535   that Verilog LLMs appeared to have learned more abstract characteristics regarding the circuit rep-
536   resented by the code, which can be exploited to predict ultimate circuit quality with higher accuracy
537   than any previous method. In essence, Verilog LLMs might have learned to do a mini “logic syn-
538   thesis”, despite having only been trained to perform language modeling. However, our results also
539
      highlight the importance of our knowledge distillation procedure in allowing downstream models to
      effectively use this information stored in the LLM’s representations.
