009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce Pallatom, an innovative protein generation model capable of producing pro-
012            tein structures with all-atom coordinates. Pallatom directly learns and models the joint
013            distribution P (structure, seq) by focusing on P (all-atom), effectively addressing the in-
014            terdependence between sequence and structure in protein generation. To achieve this,
015            we propose a novel network architecture specifically designed for all-atom protein gen-
016            eration. Our model employs a dual-track framework that tokenizes proteins into token-
017
               level and atomic-level representations, integrating them through a multi-layer decoding
               process with “traversing” representations and recycling mechanism. We also introduce
018
               the atom14 representation method, which unifies the description of unknown side-chain
019
               coordinates, ensuring high fidelity between the generated all-atom conformation and its
020            physical structure. Experimental results demonstrate that Pallatom excels in key metrics
021            of protein design, including designability, diversity, and novelty, showing significant im-
022            provements across the board. Our model not only enhances the accuracy of protein gener-
023            ation but also exhibits excellent training efficiency, paving the way for future applications
024            in larger and more complex systems.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
      The theoretical foundation of protein modeling has been built upon two key conditional probability distri-
029
      butions: P (structure | seq) and P (seq | backbone). The former, P (structure | seq), corresponds to the
030   all-atom protein structure prediction task, which involves determining the three-dimensional structure of a
031   protein given its amino acid sequence (Abramson et al., 2024; Jumper et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2023; Baek
032   et al., 2023). The latter, P (seq | backbone), underpins the fixed-backbone design task, where the goal is to
033   identify a sequence that will fold into a given protein backbone structure (Dauparas et al., 2022; Hsu et al.,
034   2022). In summary, these probability distributions has successfully advanced the field of protein engineering.
035
      With the advancement of deep learning in protein science, two distinct approaches for protein design
036   have emerged. One approach is the protein hallucination (Anishchenko et al., 2021), which explores the
037   landscape of a P (structure | seq) model using Monte Carlo or gradient-based optimization techniques.
038   This method yields valid protein structures, but requires an additional P (seq) model, such as protein lan-
039   guage models (Rives et al., 2021), to correct or redesign the sequence. Essentially, this approach can be
040   viewed as optimization process of P (structure | seq) · P (seq). Another approach attempts to explore the
041   P (backbone) distribution. a series of protein generation models based on SE(3) invariance or equivariance
042   networks (Jing et al., 2020; Satorras et al., 2021) have recently emerged, these method rely on an additional
043   P (seq | backbone) process to determine the protein sequence. This optimization strategy can be regarded as
044
      P (backbone) · P (seq | backbone).
045   This step-wise design process has limitation in approximating the joint distribution through marginal distri-
046   butions. The P (structure | seq) · P (seq) strategy faces challenges when sampling in the high-dimensional

                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


047
      sequence space, while the P (backbone) · P (seq | backbone) strategy fails to account for explicit side-chain
048
      interactions and is bottlenecked by the capability of the fixed-backbone design model.
049
050   The ultimate goal of protein generation is to directly obtain a sequence along with its correspond-
051   ing structure, i.e., to develop a model capable of describing the joint distribution P (structure, seq) or
052
      P (backbone, seq). Recently, some studies have started to adopt co-generation approaches, such as model
      based on co-diffusion (Campbell et al., 2024) or co-design (Ren et al., 2024). While these methods pri-
053
      marily rely on SE(3) networks, they still separately model the backbone and sequence, without considering
054
      side-chain conformations and leading to an insufficient description of the structure. Protpardelle (Chu et al.,
055   2024), an all-atom protein diffusion model, similarly adopts co-generation approaches with an explicit all-
056   atom representation, taking a step further in the field. However, the experimental results indicate that the
057   generated sequence fails to accurately encode the intended fold, necessitating an additional round of se-
058   quence redesign and side-chain refinement.
059
      In this study, we introduce a novel approach for all-atom protein generation called Pallatom. Our extensive
060
      experiments show that by learning P (all-atom), high-quality all-atom proteins can be successfully gener-
061   ated, eliminating the need to learn marginal probabilities separately. Inspired by AlphaFold3 (Abramson
062   et al., 2024), we adopt a dual-track framework that tokenizes proteins into residues or atoms, and develop
063   a novel module incorporating “traversing” representations and multi-layer decoding units. This module ef-
064   ficiently integrates and updates token-level and atomic-level representations through a dual-track recycling
065   mechanism, enabling self-conditioned inferencing and enhancing information flow between blocks. Addi-
066   tionally, we propose a new amino acid coordinates representation, atom14, to address the challenge of
067   representing unknown side-chain coordinates. Introducing virtual atoms to all amino acids type prevents se-
068   quence information leakage problem. The key insight of Pallatom is recognizing that all-atom coordinates of
069
      a protein inherently encode both structural and sequence information. Directly learning P (all-atom) opens
      a new path for co-generative modeling of structure and sequence.
070
071   Our contributions are summarized as follows:
072
073          • We develop a network architecture for all-atom protein generation tasks, which effectively repre-
074            sents both protein backbones and sidechains.
075
             • We explore the atom14 representation to achieve a unified description of unknown amino acid
076
               side-chain coordinates in generative tasks.
077
078          • We use our framework to develop Pallatom, a state-of-the-art all-atom protein generative model.

083   2.1   A LL - ATOM MODELING AND REPRESENTATION
084
085   The all-atom protein generation model faces many challenges in constructing both backbone and side-chain
086   atoms. A pivotal initial question arises: “How to represent a system with a variable number of atoms?”. At
087   the initial sampling stage, both the backbone and sequence are unknown, however, the atom number of a
088   system depends on unique sequence, once the sequence is determined, it also dictates the structure.
089   To avoid potential conflicts arising from the simultaneous design of sequence and structure, we define a
090   representation called atom14, which pads the initial protein with L residues as x = {xl }L        l=1 → x0 ,
091   considering it as a 3D point cloud distribution P (x0 ) ∈ RL×14×3 . For example, if residue xl is CYS, its
092   coordinates [N, Cα , C, O, Cβ , Sγ ] ∈ R6×3 is padded with 8 virtual atoms that coincide with its Cα position,
093   resulting in xl → xl0 ∈ R14×3 .

                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


094
      Our approach assumes that the all-atom distribution of the protein sidechains, including properties such as
095
      hydrophobicity, polarity, and even hydrogen bonds and salt bridges, are inherently encoded within the all-
096   atom coordinates distribution. Even without knowing the specific element atom type, the conformationally
097   similar amino acids, such as CYS and SER, can be distinguished from atomic-level features. Therefore,
098   we additionally trained a single “visualization” head to predict the corresponding amino acid type. With
099   discarding the redundant virtual atoms based on the predicted amino acid type as a post-processing step,
100   we can generate all-atom proteins with corresponding sequences from a 3D point cloud. Additionally, we
101   provide the alanine reference conformer features to guide the network in forming a stable backbone frame
102   conformation.

159   to extract structural information iteratively.
160
      Providing self-conditions (Chen et al., 2022) is an effective way to enhance generation performance. A sim-
161
      ple approach is to integrate the information extraction modules directly into the structural decoder, allowing
162   the partially denoised information from each block to be used as self-conditions for the subsequent block.
163   More specifically, in our modifications, the denoised coordinates from each block are converted into a pair-
164   wise feature, which is reinjected into pair representation and used as self-conditions in the next block to
165   update single representation. These modifications effectively address the self-conditioning pipeline between
166   blocks.
167
      In practice, the interaction and updating of dual-track protein representations within multiple decoder units
168   present new challenges. We find that if residual connections are used simultaneously for both token-level and
169   atomic-level representations across multiple decoder units, the token-level representation tends to be repeat-
170   edly broadcast and inappropriately accumulated. This not only leads to numerical instability in atomic-level
171   representations but also disrupts the model’s performance. Therefore, we propose using traversal atomic-
172   level representations to carry the token-level representations within the current decoding unit. These two
173   modifications enable the new framework to effectively generate all-atom protein structures. Details can be
174   found at Figure 1 and 3.

216
217   To convert the generated coordinates into a real protein, we need a module that translates the position infor-
218   mation into an amino acid sequence. We add a SeqHead to each decoding unit. Specifically, we aggregate
219   the updated 1D atomic-level representations from the AtomAttentionDecoder corresponding to each token

221   predictions from the last unit as the final sequence logits, â = â(K) .

305   4.2    M ETRICS
306
307
308
      While some evaluation criteria used for protein backbone generation are not suited for the new task, we
      propose new metrics specifically designed for assessing all-atom protein generation.
309
310   The first criterion is structure designability. The traditional self-consistency process assesses the designabil-
311   ity of protein backbones (DES-bb). This involves using a fixed-backbone design model (e.g., ProteinMPNN
312   (Dauparas et al., 2022)) to generate Nseq sequences for the backbone, which are then folded by structure
313   prediction models like ESMfold (Lin et al., 2023). The backbone’s designability is evaluated by the optimal
314
      TM-score or Cα -RMSD between the folded and original backbones. However, this metric is not suitable for
      evaluating all-atom proteins, which include side-chain atoms. Therefore, we similarly define the designabil-
315
      ity of all-atom protein generation, denoted as DES-aa. For the all-atom proteins, the sequence is used to
316
      predict the structure, and the sample is considered designable if the mean pLDDT of the predicted structure
317   exceeds 80 and the all-atom RMSD (aaRMSD) is less than 2 Å. This metric ensures high atomic-level ac-
318   curacy and provides strong confidence in the structural integrity and designability of the predicted protein,
319   indicating that the sequence is likely to adopt its intended native fold.
320
      The second criterion is structure diversity, denoted as DIV-str. This can be quantified by calculating the
321
      clusters number of the designable structures using Foldseek (Van Kempen et al., 2024). For all-atom proteins,
322
      we use a similar diversity evaluation method for the generated sequences, denoted as DIV-seq. Specifically,
323   we use MMseq2 (Steinegger & Söding, 2017) to calculate the clusters number of the designable sequences.
324
325   The last criterion is structure novelty, which evaluates the structural similarity between the generated back-
326
      bones and natural proteins in the PDB, denoted as NOV-str. This is calculated by the TM-score of the
      generated designable backbones compared to the most similar proteins in the PDB.

446   5     D ISCUSSION
447
448   We introduce Pallatom, a highly efficient end-to-end all-atom protein generation framework that simulta-
449   neously captures the relationship between sequence and structure, enabling state-of-the-art performance.
450   Our modification of the atom14 representation removes the limitations of explicitly defining all amino
451
      acid types during generation and provides a more accurate method for representing all-atom system coor-
      dinates. We redesign the dual-track architecture of AlphaFold3 diffusion module, by developing a novel
452
      mechanism comprising “ traversing” representations and dual-track recycling method. The new framework
453
      efficiently adapts to all-atom protein structure diffusion generation. The results demonstrate that models
454   learning P (all-atom) exhibit strong performance and diversity in de novo protein generation, unlocking new
455   pathways for protein design. Future work includes developing a more generalized model architecture, ex-
456   panding our system to support the representation of small molecules, DNA, and non-standard amino acids,
457   and further enhancing the model’s capabilities in designing large complex systems, such as antibody com-
458   plexes and self-assembling materials.

      A     R ELATED WORK
612
613
      Backbone Generation. Diffusion models based on SE(3)-equivariant network architectures and protein
614
      representations using rigid frames have achieved significant success in protein generation, as evidenced by
615
      models like Chroma (Ingraham et al., 2023), Genie2 (Lin et al., 2024), RFdiffusion (Watson et al., 2023),
616   FrameDiff (Yim et al., 2023b), FrameFlow (Yim et al., 2023a), Proteus (Wang et al., 2024), and FoldFlow2
617   (Huguet et al., 2024). These models now support multi-condition controlled generation and have been
618   extensively validated through both in-silico and wet-lab experiments.
619
      Codesign Models. Recent methods have explored a co-design approach that simultaneously designs both
620
      the backbone and sequence. Multiflow (Campbell et al., 2024) utilizes a diffusion process that jointly op-
621
      erates on discrete sequences and continuous SE(3) backbones, eliminating the need for sequence redesign
622   with ProteinMPNN (Dauparas et al., 2022). CarbonNovo (Ren et al., 2024) employs a similar approach,
623   using SE(3) diffusion on the protein backbone while simultaneously designing a sequence at each step with
624   the MRF decoder. The sequence information is then embeded using the protein language model ESM2-3B
625   (Rives et al., 2021) to guide structural generation.
626
      All-Atom Generation. Recent research teams have begun exploring fully all-atom generative representa-
627
      tion systems. For instance, Protpardelle (Chu et al., 2024) employs a coordinates diffusion model to support
628   the generation of all-atom protein structures. ProteinGenerator (Lisanza et al., 2023) applies Euclidean dif-
629   fusion on one-hot encoded sequences, combined with a structure prediction module to obtain all-atom struc-
630   ture. RFdiffusionAA (Krishna et al., 2024), based on fine-tuning the RoseTTAFold2 (Baek et al., 2023), can
631   produce backbone structures of proteins and small molecule complexes but lacks side-chain conformations
632   for standard amino acids. We focus on the methods directly generate all-atom protein structures, the most
633   relevant work is Protpardelle, which uses an end-to-end approach to generate all-atom structures.
634

      B.1   PDB DATA
638
639   We used PISCES (Wang & Dunbrack Jr, 2003) to obtain the required PDB list. For training, we selected a
640   subset of PDB entries with a resolution of <3Å and a 95% sequence identity threshold. We then performed
641   standard filtering to remove any proteins with >50% loops and applied a series of folding quality filters
642   (described below). This process resulted in 7,459 structures.
643
644   B.2   AUGMENTED DATA
645
646   Augmented data are widely used in protein modeling-related work. Consequently, we supplemented our
647   dataset with the AlphaFold Database (AFDB) (Varadi et al., 2021). The AlphaFold2 predicted structure
648   database is available under a CC-BY-4.0 license for both academic and commercial uses. The AFDB con-
649   tains a total of 214 million data points. By applying an average pLDDT threshold of >80 and limiting the
650
      sequence length to a maximum of 128, we obtained 582,652 structures. In addition, we employed more
      sophisticated filtering strategies to acquire designable and high-quality data, assessing the average neighbor-
651
      ing atomic density for each residue to determine whether they are core residues. We found that with this
652
      filter, core residue content >30% allows us to identify tightly packed structures. Furthermore, the number
653   and distribution of secondary structures can define the compactness and diversity of folding. We used DSSP
654   (Touw et al., 2015) to assign the protein secondary structures and removing structures with loop content
655   >50%. We also apply filter on the total number of continuous segments of β-sheet and α-helix to maintain
656   structural diversity. For highly extended structures, we limited the radius of gyration (Rg ) to less than 25.0.
657   To avoid overly long continuous unstructured regions within the protein structures, we restricted the maxi-


                                                        14

659
      structures, setting a TM score threshold of 0.8 and a coverage of 0.9, which removed approximately 30%

661   These structures typically exhibit good folding and high designability.
