011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) of large language models (LLMs) has re-
014            cently attracted significant attention owing to their ability to address knowledge
015
               gaps in generating reliable answers for specific questions. Existing RAG ap-
               proaches typically optimize the knowledge processing by filtering out irrelevant
016
               or incorrect information and restructuring it for model input, improving the accu-
017
               racy of answers to given questions. A general approach in doing so is to combine
018            the retrieved knowledge with the input inquiry, which are then fed into the LLM
019            to produce an answer. This approach requires the LLM to have strong knowl-
020            edge comprehension and reasoning capabilities to effectively utilize the useful
021            information, which may lead to errors when it fails to correctly interpret relevant
022            knowledge. In this paper, we propose a novel approach to augmenting LLMs with
023            external knowledge attention for question answering (QA), where the attention
024            is functionalized as an extra head that integrated with the internal heads used in
025            LLMs. We develop a memory-based mechanism that dynamically controls the
026
               degree of knowledge integration with the extra head based on the relationship
               between the question and the retrieved knowledge, and allows for differentiated
027
               fusion of external knowledge and LLM ability at its different layers. Experiments
028
               on both general and specific-domain QA tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of
029            our approach, highlighting its potential to optimize LLMs for similar challenges.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Recently, large language models (LLMs) (Ouyang et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023; Chiang et al.,
035   2023; OpenAI, 2023; Taori et al., 2023) have achieved remarkable success in artificial intelligence
036   (AI). One essential reason for this effectiveness is the capability of LLMs to learn various types
037   of knowledge—such as linguistic structures and commonsense reasoning—from large-scale labeled
038   and unlabeled data. Therefore, LLMs follow human instructions to complete a wide range of tasks,
039
      particularly question-answering (QA). However, when relying solely on the knowledge learned by
      the LLM from massive data is insufficient to generate reliable answers, the performance of LLMs
040
      always degrade. Such situation constantly arises with the rapidly evolving or highly specialized
041
      knowledge, making it challenging for the LLM to provide accurate and up-to-date responses. To
042   this end, integrating new knowledge becomes crucial to help LLMs generating appropriate answers
043   for input questions.
044
045
      To address the aforementioned situation, retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) (Baek et al., 2023b;
      Sun et al., 2023; Asai et al., 2023; Xiong et al., 2024) has been proven to be a feasible solution,
046
      which generally retrieve knowledge relevant to the input from existing knowledge bases or docu-
047
      ments and use this information to instruct LLMs in generating responses. Because the results of
048   knowledge retrieval may contain noise or irrelevant information, most studies focus on further opti-
049   mizing the retrieved knowledge to improve RAG performance (Xiong et al., 2024; Fang et al., 2024;
050   Shi et al., 2024). For example, Zhu et al. (2024) propose a mechanism to filter out content in the re-
051   trieval results that is irrelevant or misleading to current questions; Xu et al. (2024) design a rewriting
052   module to refine the retrieved knowledge. These approaches generally apply a string concatenation
053   operation to the retrieved knowledge and the LLM’s input (e.g., the question), resulting in a less con-
      verged integration of knowledge and LLMs, so that requiring LLMs to have a strong understanding

091   memory-based mechanism that dynamically controls the degree of knowledge fusion based on the
092   relationship between the question and the retrieved knowledge, which allows for differentiated and
093   deeper knowledge integration at various layers, enhancing the LLM’s capability to generate accu-
094   rate answers. We run experiments on English benchmark QA datasets in the general and medical
095   domain, where our approach outperforms existing studies and demonstrates its effectiveness in im-
096   proving model performance by efficiently integrating essential knowledge.

101
      evant answer Y. To achieve this, we employ a knowledge attention mechanism fKA to encode the
102   retrieved knowledge. The knowledge attention serves as an additional head within the multi-head at-
103   tention framework of each layer in the LLM. This specialized head processes the encoded knowledge
104   representations and integrates them with the outputs of the other attention heads, thereby facilitat-
105   ing a deep fusion of knowledge and the LLM. This mechanism enables the model to effectively
106   combine external knowledge, enhancing its ability to generate accurate and contextually relevant
107   responses. Additionally, we utilize a memory module fM to compute the weights for integrating
      knowledge across different layers of the LLM. The memory module determines the significance of

117   2.1   K NOWLEDGE ATTENTION
118
119   The purpose of the knowledge attention mechanism is to fuse the encoded knowledge with the LLM,
120   thereby guiding it to generate reliable and accurate answers. Consider that different knowledge
121
      instances may contribute variably to the generation of responses, we adopt a weighted approach
      to dynamically and selectively utilize different pieces of knowledge. This strategy minimizes the
122
      impact of potentially irrelevant or noisy information on the final answer. The knowledge attention
123
      mechanism comprises two main components: a knowledge encoder and an attention module. The
124   knowledge encoder fKA is responsible for encoding the retrieved knowledge S, which typically
125   consists of a diverse set of knowledge instances that may vary in their representation formats, such as
126   full-text articles or multiple-word entities. For knowledge instances presented in article or paragraph
127   form, we directly utilize a pre-trained encoder (e.g., BERT (Devlin et al., 2019b)) to encode the text.
128   This leverages the encoder’s strong capabilities in capturing semantic and contextual information
129   from continuous text. For knowledge instances consisting of multiple words or entities, we first
130   concatenate these words into a coherent sentence. This concatenated sentence is then passed through
131   the same pre-trained encoder to obtain a unified representation. Since these knowledge instances
132
      do not possess an inherent sequential order, we omit positional embeddings during the encoding
      process.

160
161   Building on the observation that different layers of LLMs process distinct types of information,
      we propose a memory module to determine the optimal layers for integrating external knowledge.

189   2.3   R ETRIEVAL AUGMENTATION FOR LLM WITH N+1 H EADS
190
191   After encoding the retrieved knowledge, we integrate it into the LLM by treating the knowledge
192   attention as an additional knowledge head, which operates alongside the existing heads in each
193   layer’s multi-head attention module of the LLM, separately processing the encoded knowledge and
194   the input question before fusing their outputs. The motivation for introducing an extra head stems
195   from the design of multi-head attention, where each head captures different aspects of the input
196
      (Vaswani et al., 2017). Knowledge can be viewed as an implicit facet of the input, allowing it to
      be encoded alongside other aspects of the input question. This alignment with the original intent of
197
      multi-head attention facilitates a natural fusion of knowledge with the LLM.
198
199   Specifically, in the l-th layer, we employ the standard multi-head attention procedure by concate-
200   nating the output from the knowledge head, H′S   l , with the outputs from the standard heads. The
201   process is formulated as

239   3.2    BASELINES
240
241   In the experiments, we run three baselines, namely, “Base”, “+RAG”, “+RAG+M”. “Base” denotes
242   the approach where we directly fine-tune the LLM on the training data without using RAG. “+RAG”
243   utilizes RAG when performing the task, where the knowledge instances are directly concatenated
244   with the input text to instruct the LLM to produce the response. The LLM is also fine-tuned in
245
      this case. “+RAG+M” refers to the approach that utilizes the knowledge encoder and the memory
      module (M) in our approach to perform the task, where the knowledge representation is directly
246
      concatenated with the output of each multi-head attention layer and the resulting matrix is fed into
247
      the next layer to process following the standard process. Compared with our approach, this approach
248   does not utilize the knowledge attention to encode the knowledge. Following the notation, our
249   approach is denoted as “+RAG+M+KA”.

362   LLaMA-2 (13B) under the following settings: (1) Equal Attention Weights: All attention weights

365   of constructing an additional knowledge attention, we directly concatenate the knowledge represen-

369

370

371

375   to integrate. Therefore, these heads are not adept at handling additional knowledge representations,
376   and cannot efficiently fuse the knowledge into the model’s internal representations. This inefficiency

      4.3   T HE E FFECT OF THE L AYER TO I NCORPORATE K NOWLEDGE
406
407   To investigate the impact of integrating knowledge at different layers of the LLM, we experimented
408   with various settings: (1) Equal All: Integrating the knowledge into all layers with equal weights.
409   (2) First Layer Only: Incorporating the knowledge at the first layer. (3) Middle Layers Only:
410   Inserting the knowledge into the middle layers (e.g., the 20-th layer of the LLaMA-2 13B with 40
411   layers of multi-head attentions). (4) Last Layer Only: Adding the knowledge at the last layer. We
412   present the results of these different configurations using LLaMA-2 13B in Figure 3. For refer-
413   ence, we also include the performance of our method that uses the memory module to dynamically
414   determine the integration weights at different layers.
415   From the results, we find that the other settings result in inferior model performance compared to
416   our proposed approach. This indicates that it is necessary to differentially integrate knowledge into
417   the LLM at varying degrees across layers. Moreover, we found that incorporating the knowledge
418   into the middle layers yields the best performance compared to integrating at the first or last layers.
419   A possible reason is that the initial layers of the LLM primarily focus on the superficial understand-
420   ing of the input content, such as lexical and syntactic features (Belinkov et al., 2017), while the last
421
      layers are mainly responsible for text generation and high-level abstractions. The beginning and
      end layers are not primarily engaged in deep semantic understanding or the activation of relevant
422
      external knowledge. Therefore, adding knowledge at the initial or final layers may not effectively en-
423
      hance the model’s understanding, leading to suboptimal improvements in performance. In contrast,
424   the middle layers are more focused on deep content comprehension and are thus better suited for
425   integrating external knowledge with the input question. This integration enables the model to utilize
426   the knowledge more effectively, guiding it to generate more accurate and contextually appropriate
427   responses.

      5   R ELATED W ORK
456
      Utilizing knowledge is essential for LLMs to process various tasks, e.g., QA, and thus attracts much
457
      attention from existing studies. Some studies directly fine-tune LLMs on data with the required
458
      knowledge (Chen et al., 2023; Thangarasa et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023), yet it is computationally
459   expensive. There are studies that utilize existing resources (e.g., the knowledge base) to improve
460   LLM without updating the LLM’s parameters (Baek et al., 2023b; Jiang et al., 2023; Cheng et al.,
461   2023; Wang et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2023). They extract relevant knowledge from a knowledge base
462   and add it to input questions as prompts to instruct the LLM to generate appropriate responses (Asai
463   et al., 2023; Baek et al., 2023b; Fang et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024; Cheng et al.,
464   2024; Jeong et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024). For example, Sun et al. (2023) proposes an approach that
465   enables interactive exploration and reasoning within the graph to enhance inference processes with-
466   out additional training costs. Jiang et al. (2023) introduces an approach to enhance LLMs’ reasoning
467
      abilities on structured data by iteratively reading and inferring through a specialized interface, sig-
      nificantly improving performance in low and zero-shot settings. There are other studies that train a
468
      small model to better extract and leverage the knowledge (Ma et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Cheng
469
      et al., 2024). Compared to existing studies, our approach optimizes the integration of knowledge
470   with LLMs by incorporating the retrieved knowledge into multiple layers of the LLM. We employ a
471   memory module and knowledge attention to differentially fuse various knowledge instances with the
472   LLM, allowing for a more nuanced and effective utilization of external knowledge. This layered and
473   differentiated integration approach enhances the model’s ability to generate reliable and accurate
474   answers by selectively emphasizing the most relevant knowledge at each layer.
475
476
      6   C ONCLUSION
477
478
      In this paper, we propose an approach to enhance QA with LLM by retrieval-augmentation using an
479
      external attention to incorporate knowledge from certain sources, where such attention serves as an
480   extra head that paralleled and fused with other internal heads from the LLM, so that bringing external
481   knowledge and LLM ability together at the parametric level. Specifically, the knowledge attention
482   encodes and weights knowledge inputs and is incorporated to different layers of the LLM, enabling
483   effective guidance on the generation of accurate and reliable answers. We evaluated our approach
484   on multiple benchmark QA datasets, and the results demonstrate its validity and superiority, where
485   it outperforms strong baselines and existing studies. Analysis on the effect of knowledge attention
      and how it is fused with the LLM further confirms the effectiveness of our approach.
