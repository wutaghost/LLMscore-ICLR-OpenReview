011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            As the capabilities of large language models (LLMs) have expanded dramatically,
014            aligning these models with human values presents a significant challenge. Recent
015            studies demonstrate that powerful LLMs can achieve self-alignment by either cor-
016
               recting their initial unsafe responses or autonomously ranking answers without
               human intervention. In this work, we identify two key limitations: first, they rely
017
               on the assumed emergent capabilities of LLMs, and second, they discard all in-
018
               termediate reasoning steps when aligning the model with updated answers. To
019            address these challenges, we propose a novel self-alignment method that utilizes a
020            Chain of Thought (CoT) approach, termed AlignCoT. This method encompasses
021            stages of Question Analysis, Answer Guidance, and Safe Answer production. It
022            is designed to enable LLMs, even smaller and weaker models like 7B LLMs, to
023            produce high-quality, safe responses. Furthermore, we introduce the Mixture of
024            insighTful Experts (MoTE) architecture, which applies mixture of experts to en-
025            hance each component of the AlignCoT process, markedly increasing alignment
026            efficiency. The MoTE approach not only outperforms existing methods in aligning
027
               LLMs with human values but also highlights the benefits of using self-generated
               data, revealing the dual benefits of improved alignment and training efficiency.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      In recent years, the capabilities of large language models (LLMs) have grown exponentially, usher-
      ing in remarkable advancements in numerous fields (Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Gou
034
      et al., 2024). This growth, however, has not been without its challenges. The vast web text corpora
035
      that LLMs are trained on have the unintended consequence of enabling these models to generate
036   harmful responses, thereby presenting significant risks when deployed (Zou et al., 2023; Carlini
037   et al., 2023). Given potent capabilities and wide-ranging applications of LLMs, ensuring that these
038   models operate in a manner that aligns with human morals and values is of utmost importance. This
039   safety alignment of LLMs with human values is not merely important, but of critical significance.
040
      Existing safety alignment methods rely heavily on human-generated “golden” answers aligned with
041
      human values, which is resource-intensive. To reduce this reliance, some approaches explore self-
042   alignment, where models generate appropriate responses independently. Recent studies (Bai et al.,
043   2022b; Lee et al., 2023) show that powerful LLMs can achieve this by either revising their initial
044   unsafe responses or autonomously ranking responses without human input. However, these meth-
045   ods face two main limitations: they depend on the assumed emergent capabilities of LLMs, which
046   are less effective when the model is small or relatively weak (e.g. 7B LLMs), and they discard
047   intermediate reasoning steps, which we believe hold value for further alignment.
048   In our research, we first propose a self-alignment method for models that are not yet fully devel-
049   oped, by revisiting the safety self-alignment problem through chain-of-thought (CoT). Specifically,
050   we observe that many questions inherently contain subtle harmful intentions that could prompt an
051   unsafe response. Inspired by human processing, our methodology entails first dissecting the ques-
052   tion, followed by devising a strategy for crafting the answer, and ultimately delivering the response.
053   This CoT process ensures that responses are considered and articulate akin to thoughtful human
      communication. Accordingly, we propose three dimensions for innocuously understanding and ad-

      dressing questions based on this CoT framework: Question Analysis, Answer Guidance, and Safe
055   Answer, collectively termed AlignCoT. AlignCoT fosters a thorough, multifaceted interpretation of
056   the query, enabling even the less advanced LLMs to generate responses that are not only high in
057   quality but also harmless.
058
      Furthermore, we apply the constructed AlignCoT dataset containing analysis, guidance and updated

061   and revised answers, our observations indicate that: 1) incorporating these intermediary steps fosters

064   Experts (MoTE) strategy that leverages a mixture of experts (MoE) framework (Riquelme et al.,
065   2021; Liu et al., 2024), with each expert dedicated to a specific facet of the question. Additionally,
066   we integrate a shared expert to facilitate the exchange of knowledge across different stages of Align-
067   CoT. By incorporating these intermediary steps in our training and optimizing the training data’s
068   use, we propose an efficient design that significantly reduces the redundancy in recalculating atten-

      experiments validate the effectiveness of the MoTE approach. Our contributions are as follows:
070

073
            2. We demonstrate that the middle steps of AlignCoT enhance self-alignment and further
074
               propose MoTE, which employs a MoE architecture to amplify the model’s proficiency in

076
            3. Through comparative analysis, MoTE demonstrates superior alignment efficacy against

               data for its tuning efficiency.

083   LLM Self-alignment is widely recognized as essential for aligning LLMs by themselves with
084
      human values. Supervised fine-tuning (SFT) (Wei et al., 2021) trains LLMs to recover the human-
      annotated optimal answers. Chain of Hindsight (Liu et al., 2023a) fine-tunes LLMs with sequences
085
      of human hindsight, enabling LLMs to distinguish relative quality among various responses, and
086
      Mistake Analysis (Chen et al., 2023b) further demonstrates that the LLMs can learn from their
087   own mistakes via self-analyzing. Critique-Revise (Bai et al., 2022b) asks LLMs to critique their
088   initial responses, followed by self-revision with respect to pre-defined constitutions, while MA-
089   TRIX (Pang et al., 2024a) performs self-revision through debating between characteristics. On the
090   other hand, Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (Ouyang et al., 2022) optimizes LLMs
091   using human-elicited reward models (RM), typically trained with the pairwise human preferences.
092   RLAIF (Lee et al., 2023) simulates human preferences via LLMs, while DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023)
093   directly optimizes LLMs with respect to the human preferences. Our method belongs to SFT, but
094   instead of collecting optimal responses, we focus on the benefit of adopting the Chain of Thought
095
      procedure for LLM alignment, and propose a novel MoE architecture for LLM self-alignment.
096
097
      CoT Fine-Tuning has been increasingly emphasized as a means to equip large language models
      (LLMs) with advanced reasoning abilities. Most existing methods (Huang et al., 2022; Fu et al.,
098
      2023; Ranaldi & Freitas, 2024; Puerto et al., 2024) focus on supervised fine-tuning, directly training
099
      CoT data into LLMs to improve performance through CoT inference. Other approaches (OpenAI;
100   Huang et al., 2024; Zhang & Parkes, 2023) utilize reinforcement learning to enhance the model’s
101   reasoning capabilities. Recent works (Zhang et al., 2024b; Tian et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a)
102   have even integrated search algorithms to generate more effective thought chains. However, unlike
103   our work, these methods often overlook the importance of enhancing each step within a structured
104   thought chain. We argue that this structured enhancement significantly contributes to the final align-
105   ment, making our approach distinct and impactful.

      3   A LIGN C OT: A DOPTING C HAIN OF T HOUGHTS FOR SELF - ALIGNMENT
140
141
142   In this section, we first present our proposed AlignCoT, which incorporates Chain of Thought (CoT)
143   reasoning into safety self-alignment. We then conduct experiments and derive three key findings
144   that inform the subsequent design of MoTE.
145
146
      Definition of AlignCoT. We start by demonstrating the Chain-of-Thoughts (CoT), broadly utilized
147
      in long-range reasoning problems, can also benefit LLM safety self-alignment. Drawing inspiration
148   from human problem-solving processes, we propose a structured, step-by-step approach to address
149   safety issues. Specifically, we define the dataset D = {x}, with each x represents a question. For a
150   given sample x, the LLM performs the following steps:
151
152          • Question Analysis: Initially, the LLM analyzes the question to identify any explicit or
153            implicit risks, indicated as P (xa |x), where xa denotes the analysis result for x.
154
155          • Answer Guidance: Following the analysis, the LLM formulates guidance on crafting a
156            safe and accurate answer, represented as P (xg |xa , x), where xg denotes the guidance.
157
158          • Safe Answer: The LLM then generates a safe response based on the previous analysis and
159            guidance, denoted as P (ycot |xg , xa , x), where ycot represents the final response.
160
161   This systematic approach leverages human-like reasoning to enhance safety and reliability of LLM.
      The specific prompt for each step are detailed in Fig. 1. By formulating the process, we notice that

531   6 C ONCLUSION
532   In conclusion, as the capabilities of LLMs continue to grow, ensuring their alignment with human
533   values remains a significant challenge. While existing self-alignment methods offer promise, they
534   often rely on the assumed emergent abilities of LLMs and overlook the value of intermediate reason-
535
      ing steps. To address these limitations, we introduced AlignCoT, a Chain of Thought-based method
      that enables even smaller models to generate safe, high-quality responses. Additionally, we proposed
536
      the Mixture of Insightful Experts (MoTE) architecture, which enhances each stage of AlignCoT, im-
537
      proving both alignment performance and training efficiency. Our work demonstrates that combining
538   structured reasoning with specialized expert models marks a meaningful advancement in aligning
539   LLMs, offering both practical and scalable solutions to safety alignment.

      A    E XPERIMENT D ETAILS
757
758
      Dataset and base model. We utilize PKU-SafeRLHF (Dai et al., 2023) for both training and
759   evaluation on Alpaca-7B (Taori et al., 2023), a dataset manually created and assessed to include
760   Question-Answer pairs across 14 harm-related categories, (e.g., animal abuse, self-harm, and pri-
761   vacy violation). Following Chen et al. (2023b), we refine the training set and maintain 10260 unique
762   instructions with good answers provided by humans, and a test set with 1,523 red-teaming instruc-
763   tions for evaluating harmlessness. To balance harmlessness with helpfulness, we integrate an addi-
764   tional 52k helpful instructions from Alpaca (Taori et al., 2023) into our training and assess helpful-
765   ness using AlpacaFarm’s evaluation set of 805 instructions (Dubois et al., 2023). We further utilize
766   HH-RLHF Bai et al. (2022a), a harder multi-round conversation dataset for both training and eval-
767
      uation. We employ Wizard-Vicuna-Uncensored 7B TheBloke (2024) as base model and follow the
      experiment setup of (Pang et al., 2024a) to use 6K helpful and harmful training data for SFT. All
768
      models showcased in column PKU-SafeRLHF in Table 1 are based on Alpaca-7B and in column
769
      HH-RLHF are on Wizard-Vicuna-Uncensored 7B.
770
771
      Model and baselines. MoTE is by default applied in all linear layers of the transformer with rank
772   16 and we always add an entra LoRA expert only tuned with datasets Dans = {(x, ycot )}. Initially,
773   we benchmark AlignCoT against inference-based models, choosing zero-shot CoT (Wei et al., 2022)
774   and Critique-Revise (Bai et al., 2022b) as baselines due to their enhancement of model performance
775   through multi-step inferences. Zero-shot CoT utilizes a template to encourage stepwise thinking in
776   the model, while Critique-Revise prompts the model to critique and refine its initial output into a
777   safer and more helpful response. Further, we compare MoTE against fine-tuning approaches, select-
778   ing SFT, Critique-Revise (Bai et al., 2022b), Mistake Analysis (Chen et al., 2023b), RLCD Yang
779   et al. (2023) and MATRIX Pang et al. (2024a).
780
781   B    M ORE D ISCUSSIONS
782
783   Future works. As discussed in Fig. 6, this work aims at aligning LLMs with LLMs-generated
784   CoT corpus efficiently with MoE architecture, and training with AIGC data has become a prevailing
785   research direction (e.g., GeoDiffusion (Chen et al., 2023c; Gao et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b; Li et al.,
786   2023; Wang et al., 2024) for object detection (Han et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022; 2024), StableRep (Tian
787   et al., 2023) for self-supervised learning (Chen et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023a; Liu
788
      et al., 2024), and mistake analysis (Chen et al., 2023b) for LLM alignment), thanks to the superior
      progress of generative models. However, currently we mainly focus on aligning LLMs with human
789
      morals, while how to adopt MoTE for general instruction tuning problems (e.g., coding and maths)
790
      is a potential future direction.
