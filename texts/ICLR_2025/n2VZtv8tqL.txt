009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Pre-trained Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) demonstrate robust pattern recogni-
012
               tion abilities, closely mirroring the functionality of Biological Neural Networks
013
               (BNNs). We are particularly intrigued by these models’ capacity for acquiring new
014            knowledge through fine-tuning, such as Parameter-efficient Fine-tuning (PEFT).
015            Given that both ANNs and BNNs propagate information layer-by-layer, a useful
016            analogy can be drawn: ANN weights correspond to synapses in BNNs, while
017            features (latent variables or activations) parallel the neurotransmitters released
018            by neurons. Building upon this clue, we delve deeper into exploring the connec-
019            tions between feature adjustment and weight adjustment, resulting in our proposed
020            method Synapses & Neurons (SAN) that learns scaling matrices for features and
021            propagates their effects towards posterior weight matrices. Our approach draws
022
               strong inspiration from well-known neuroscience phenomena - Long-term Potenti-
               ation (LTP) and Long-term Depression (LTD), revealing the relationship between
023
               synapse development and neurotransmitter release levels. We conducted extensive
024
               comparisons of PEFT on 26 datasets using attention-based networks as well as
025            convolution-based networks, leading to significant improvements compared to
026            other tuning methods, +8.5% over fully-finetune, +7% over Visual Prompt Tuning,
027            and +3.2% over Low-Rank Adapter.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   The use of large pre-trained models demonstrates their robust adaptability to various downstream
034   datasets and tasks through fine-tuning techniques. However, performing full fine-tuning by adjusting
035   all parameters imposes significant computation and data costs. In this context, the concept of
036   parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) aims to reduce the number of adjustable parameters during
037   fine-tuning, resulting in fewer parameters, faster training speed, and a lower risk of overfitting Ding
038   et al. (2023). Adhering to this rule-of-thumb strategy can simultaneously alleviate the cost burdens.
039   Compared to full fine-tuning, in the early stage of PEFT history, most methods were referred to as
040   ”partial fine-tuning” and focused on releasing a subset of parameters for adjustment. For example,
041   linear probing was the simplest method that only released the head parameters of the model for
042   fine-tuning. More advanced approaches like Bitfit (i.e., bias tuning) (Zaken et al., 2021) chose to
043   release biases and achieved better global adjustment capabilities. Recent concepts such as ”sparse
044   training” introduced more sophisticated selection mechanisms by utilizing gradients or parameter
045
      magnitudes (He et al., 2023). However, these methods still faced challenges where the subset of
      parameters lacked representative abilities of the entire model. Consequently, researchers shifted their
046
      focus towards directly adjusting the output features of each layer. The most popular approach in
047
      this regard is the Low-rank Adapter (LoRA) family (Hu et al., 2021), which employs two low-rank
048   sequential learnable matrices (down and up) alongside each layer to simulate additional parameter
049   activities while handling input with original parameters; thus resulting in final features being a
050   summation of LoRA’s output and original output. The success of LoRA indicated that adjusting
051   features would be more efficient and implementation-friendly. Other notable works in feature
052   adjustment include Visual Prompt Tuning (VPT) (Liu et al., 2021a; Jia et al., 2022), where extra
053   learnable tokens are concatenated with each layer’s feature, and Scale and Shift Features (SSF) (Lian
      et al., 2022), which applies a linear transformation to features using learnable shift and scale factors.

054
      From a mathematical perspective, we can consider feature transformations as approximation of
055   parameters tuning. Operations such as addition, concatenation, and linear transformation on features
056   essentially perform unified transformations on the weighted sum results of each channel in the
057   parameter matrices. This viewpoint provides a more generalized framework for understanding PEFT
058   methods. For instance, let W be the original parameter matrix of a layer, and x be the input feature.
059   The output feature y is typically computed as y = W x. In PEFT methods that transform features:
060
061          • Addition (as in LoRA):
062               – y ′ = W x + Ax, where A is a low-rank matrix and can be viewed as a transformation
063                 on W : W ′ = W + A.
064          • Concatenation (as in VPT):
065
066
                  – y ′ = W [x; p], where p is the prompt vector and effectively extends W to W ′ =
                    [W, Wp ], where Wp is the weight for the prompt.
067
068          • Linear transformation (as in SSF):
069               – y ′ = α ⊙ (W x) + β, where α and β are learnable scale and shift factors and can be
070                 seen as transforming W to W ′ = αW , with an additional bias term.
071
072   These feature-level operations can be interpreted as implicit transformations of the entire parameter
073   space, offering a more flexible and efficient way to adapt the model Zhang et al. (2024). By operating
074   on features, we’re essentially performing a form of meta-learning, where the model learns how to
075
      model its original parameters indirectly through the additional parameters created for feature
      modifications.
076
077   The success of feature-based PEFT methods raises an intriguing question: Should those additional
078   parameters created for feature modifications in one layer affect the parameters of subsequent layers?
079   We found these aforementioned methods overlooked this and focused only on the bandwidth of the
080   current layer, yet it finds significant resonance in neuroscience, particularly in the phenomena of
081
      Long-Term Depression (LTD) and Long-Term Potentiation (LTP). In neuroscience, LTD and LTP are
      well-established mechanisms of synaptic plasticity that play crucial roles in learning and memory
082
      formation Bliss & Collingridge (1993); Malenka & Bear (2004). LTP refers to the strengthening
083
      of synaptic connections, while LTD refers to their weakening. Specific patterns of short-term
084   neural activity typically induce these processes and can persist for extended periods, hence the term
085   ”long-term” Citri & Malenka (2008).
086
087
      A key aspect of LTD and LTP is their ability to induce changes in the immediate synaptic connection
      and subsequent neurons along the pathway, as shown in Figure 1. For instance, studies have shown
088
      that by modulating the neurotransmitter release levels of presynaptic neurons (often through pharma-
089
      cological or optogenetic methods), researchers can observe changes in the synaptic development of
090   downstream neurons Takeuchi et al. (2014); Nabavi et al. (2014). This trans-synaptic effect, known
091   as Heterosynaptic Plasticity, suggests that local changes can propagate and influence broader neural
092   networks Chistiakova et al. (2014).
093
      Drawing an analogy between neural networks and biological neural systems, we can consider
094
      features analogous to neurotransmitters and parameter matrices as synapses. Following this, a natural
095
      extension of current PEFT methods would be to allow feature adjustments in one layer to propagate
096   and influence the parameters of subsequent layers explicitly. As a brief introduction to our proposed
097   Synapses & Neurons (SAN) method, for each layer, we first conduct trainable scaling for each
098   feature to mimic the rapid change in pre-synaptic neuron’s neurotransmitter level when exposed
099   to a stimulant. Further on, we propagate those scaling factors to the next layer’s parameters. This
100   simulates the further effect of post-synaptic neuron development i.e. Heterosynaptic Plasticity. Notice
101   the trainable parameters for SAN are the scaling factors with the exact shape as the feature size, this
102   is very efficient and can be considered as a degraded LoRA with a bottleneck i.e. rank size equal to
103   one. However, we conducted extensive experiments on various datasets and outperformed LoRA, as
104
      well as many other methods including the existing state-of-the-art method, by a large margin.
105   In a nutshell, our contributions to this paper are as follows:
106
107          • Discover deficiencies from PEFT’s line of research: We dive deep into the progress of
               PEFT research from adjusting parameter weights to transforming layer output features.

      Parameter-efficient fine-tuning The most straightforward method for PEFT is linear probing Alain
      & Bengio (2016). By simply adding or modifying a trainable new head, the pre-trained model can
154
      adapt to new tasks. However, the expressiveness of the linear probing method is limited. An intuitive
155
      improvement proposed in Bitfit Zaken et al. (2021) involves unfreezing the bias. A more efficient
156   approach is prompt tuning Jia et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2021a), which adjusts the inputs. Recent work,
157   such as sensitivity-aware PEFT, analyzes weight magnitudes or accumulated gradients on specific
158   datasets to discover model sparsity and focuses only on tuning those areas. In addition to focusing on
159   adjustment locations, there are also differences in how adjustments are made. The adapter Zhang et al.
160   (2020) is a commonly used approach that adds an extra layer along or inserts it into the pre-trained
161   model; features pass through this adapter and output an adjustment value for different adjustment
      operations (etc. addition, production, and concatenation). Another efficient adapter style is Low-Rank

201   Long-Term Depression/Potentiation and Heterosynaptic Plasticity Long-Term Depression
202   (LTD) and Long-Term Potentiation (LTP) are fundamental mechanisms of synaptic plasticity in
203   biological neural networks, playing crucial roles in learning and memory formation Malenka & Bear
204   (2004). LTP refers to the strengthening of synaptic connections, while LTD involves their weakening,
205   both persisting for extended periods Bear et al. (2007). These processes are primarily triggered
206   by specific patterns of neuronal activity and are often considered Hebbian in nature, following the
207
      principle that ”neurons that fire together, wire together” Hebb (1949). However, the discovery of
      heterosynaptic plasticity has expanded our understanding of synaptic modulation beyond this simple
208
      associative rule Chistiakova et al. (2014). Heterosynaptic plasticity refers to changes in synaptic
209
      strength that occur at synapses that are not directly involved in the inducing activity Bailey et al.
210   (2000). This form of plasticity allows for more complex and distributed forms of information storage
211   and processing in neural networks Lynch (2004). For instance, when LTP is induced at one set
212   of synapses, heterosynaptic LTD may occur at nearby inactive synapses, potentially serving as a
213   homeostatic mechanism to maintain overall network stability Royer & Paré (2003). The molecular
214   mechanisms underlying these forms of plasticity involve complex cascades of intracellular signaling.
215   Heterosynaptic plasticity, involves unique signaling pathways, including the spread of intracellular
      messengers and the release of diffusible factors that can affect nearby synapses Oh et al. (2015).

253
254   Similar to SSF, the scaled output y ′l of layer l can be described as y ′l = γ l ⊙ y l , where γ l is the

      we further apply it to scale the parameters in the next layer wl+1 . The parameters for the next layer

307   3.2.4   E XPLICIT P ROPAGATION :
308
309
      The key innovation of our SAN method lies in explicitly propagating the scaling factor of the current
      layer to the parameters of the subsequent layer. This approach is motivated by a fundamental
310
      insight into the nature of linear transformations in neural networks: any linear transformation applied
311
      to features for Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) implicitly affects the subsequent layer’s
312   parameters.
313
314   To elaborate, consider a linear transformation T applied to the features f of layer l, f ′ = T (f ). The
315
      output of the subsequent layer l + 1 with weight W and bias b can be expressed as, y = W · T (f ) + b.
      Due to the linearity of the operations, this is equivalent to:
316
317
318                                     y = (W · T ) · f + b = W ′ · f + b                                    (9)
319
320
      where W ′ = W · T is an adjusted weight matrix.
321   This equivalence reveals that any linear transformation of features in layer l can be equalized as an
322   adjustment to the weights of layer l + 1, assuming no non-linear activations are applied between these
323   operations. In essence, methods that apply linear transformations to features are implicitly learning
      an adjustment matrix for the subsequent layer’s weights.

      While this principle is straightforward in purely linear scenarios, real-world neural networks incorpo-
325   rate non-linear activations and normalization layers. However, we argue that our approach remains
326   approximately valid even in these more complex settings. This approximation is based on two key
327   observations:
328
      1. Near-linear behavior of modern activation functions: Many popular activation functions, such
329
      as ReLU and its variants, exhibit approximately linear behavior in certain regions. This near-linearity
330
      allows our linear transformation principle to hold to a good approximation over significant portions
331   of the input space.
332
333   2. Adaptive re-calibration of scaling factors: To account for the effects of non-linearities and
334
      normalization, SAN introduces an additional learnable linear transformation before re-applying the
      scaling factor to the next layer’s weights. This can be expressed as:
335
336
337                                              γ ′l = Al γ l + bl                                       (10)
338
339   where γ ′l is the recalibrated scaling factor, and Al and bl are learnable parameters. The weight
340   adjustment for the next layer then becomes:
341
342                                           W ′l+1 = W l+1 ⊙ γ ′l                                       (11)
343
344   This adaptive re-calibration allows SAN to:
345
346          • Compensate for the non-linear effects introduced by activation functions and normalization
347
               layers.
348          • Fine-tune the propagation of scaling factors to better suit the specific characteristics of each
349            layer and the task.
350          • Maintain the benefits of weight adjustment while adapting to the complexities of modern
351            neural architectures.

518   4.4   A BLATION S TUDIES
519
520
      To investigate the effectiveness of our proposed SAN method, we conduct ablation studies focusing
      on separating two key components: modeling of the current layer by a set of learnable scaling factors
521
      and propagation of the learned scaling factors to the next layer. These studies aim to quantify the
522
      contribution of each component and validate our design choices. Figure 3b illustrates the performance
523   of various methods, including the aforementioned settings and some other fine-tuning strategies.
524
525   It is clear that both modeling the current layer strategy and propagate to the next layer strategy can
526
      work as a decent PEFT method alone, however, when used together, the improvement would be more
      complete with a higher expressivity.

529   5     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
530
531
      The primary contribution of our paper is the introduction of the concept of propagating the feature
532
      adjustment value forward to the parameters of the next layer. This concept is motivated by Heterosy-
533
      naptic Plasticity observed in BNN during LTP/D occurrences. We have conducted an analysis on
534   the properties of this propagation, demonstrating its regularization abilities and how it enhances
535   fine-grained expressivity through reparameterization perspectives. Moreover, we hypothesize that
536   current feature tuning methods implicitly propagate, but by making this propagation explicit, we
537   can simplify the learning process. Our experiments validate our concept, and we believe future
538   work should focus on discovering how to propagate and re-apply additional parameters created for
539   adjusting certain layer’s features to more layers. By doing so, we can achieve even lower training
      costs and emphasize the interconnections between different layers.
