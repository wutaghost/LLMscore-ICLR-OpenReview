009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have demonstrated potential in
012            visual instruction following across various tasks. Recently, some studies have
013            integrated MLLMs into robotic manipulation, allowing robots to interpret multi-
014            modal information and predict low-level actions. While MLLM-based policies
015            have shown promising progress, they may predict failure execution poses when
016
               faced with novel tasks or categories. To emulate human-like reasoning modes for
               more robust manipulation, we propose a Self-Corrected (SC)-MLLM. Our model
017
               combines fast system reasoning for directly predicting end-effector poses with
018
               slow system reasoning for reflecting on and correcting failure actions. For the fast
019            system, we introduce parameter-efficient fine-tuning to empower MLLM with pose
020            prediction capabilities, reframing this as a language modeling problem. For the
021            slow system, when facing execution failures, our model learns to detect the causes
022            of low-level action errors (i.e., position and rotation errors) and adaptively seeks
023            prompt feedback from experts. Based on the feedback, SC-MLLM reflects on the
024            current failure case and attempts to generate the corrected actions. Furthermore,
025            we design a continuous policy learning method using successfully corrected sam-
026            ples, enhancing the model’s adaptability to the current scene configuration and
027
               reducing the frequency of expert intervention. To evaluate our method, we conduct
               extensive experiments in both simulation and real-world settings. SC-MLLM
028
               significantly improves manipulation accuracy compared to previous state-of-the-art
029
               MLLM-based policy (ManipLLM), increasing from 57% to 79% on seen object
030            categories and from 47% to 69% on unseen novel categories. Our project web
031            page: https://sites.google.com/view/sc-mllm

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Recently, Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) (Li et al., 2022; Alayrac et al., 2022;
037   Liu et al., 2023b; Zhang et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024) have
038   showcased remarkable abilities in visual instruction following and common sense reasoning. Some
039
      studies (Singh et al., 2023; Ahn et al., 2022; Liang et al., 2023; Driess et al., 2023) integrate
      MLLMs into robot manipulation, enabling robots to explore multimodal information and formulate
040
      task planning. Concurrently, other researchers (Brohan et al., 2022; Zitkovich et al., 2023; Li et al.,
041
      2023c;b; Kim et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b) are focusing on developing MLLMs capable of predicting
042   robotic low-level action poses. While the integration of MLLMs into robotics has made encouraging
043   strides, the current pipelines may lead to failure predictions when faced with novel tasks or object
044   instances. Most prior research has overlooked the detection and correction of failure actions within
045   the control process. This limitation significantly impacts their practicality in real-world settings,
046   where uncertainties and unexpected obstacles are prevalent.
047
      Recognizing the crucial role of self-correction in robot manipulation, recent studies have introduced
048   some solutions. REFLECT (Liu et al., 2023c) stands out by utilizing LLMs to generate failure
049   explanations and assist a language-based planner in correcting errors. Building on this innovation,
050   subsequent research (Guo et al., 2023a; Skreta et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2024; Zha et al., 2023; Ming
051   et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023) explores robot correction through a hierarchical framework design.
052   These approaches employ LLMs to correct high-level task planning while utilizing action models to
053   execute low-level actions. However, the existing correction methods still face two main challenges.
      1) Error Accumulation in the Hierarchical Framework. Correction LLMs and the action model

076   correction prompts like ”Move a little bit to the right,” these prompts are often difficult for the action
077   model to understand and translate into specific pose predictions. 2) Lack of Ability to Correct
078   Low-Level SE(3) Poses. Existing methods are unable to directly correct the end-effector poses of
079   atomic tasks, which are fundamental for completing manipulation tasks. Additionally, they fail to
080   learn from successful corrected cases and improve the policy model. Given these challenges, we raise

      a question: “Can we develop an end-to-end robotic agent that not only possesses manipulation skills
      but also effectively corrects low-level failure actions?”
082
083   Drawing inspiration from Daniel Kahneman’s assertion that “human thinking is divided into a
084   fast system and a slow system, which separately represent intuitive processes and more logical
085   reasoning,” (Kahneman, 2011) we introduce a self-corrected (SC)-MLLM that mimics a human-like
086   thinking paradigm to address the above question. As shown in Figure 1, the SC-MLLM possesses both
087
      the fast system capability to directly predict end-effector poses and the slow system ability to reflect
      on and correct failure actions. For fast system construction, we transform low-level manipulation
088
      action into a language modeling problem, using language to directly generate SE(3) poses. During
089
      the training process, we parameter-efficient fine-tune the LLM to empower SC-MLLM with pose
090   prediction capability. According to the RGB image and a text prompt, our SC-MLLM generates the
091   end-effector position and rotation. To enhance the SC-MLLM with slow system capabilities, we
092   propose a Chain-of-Thought training strategy for failure action reflection and correction based on
093   expert feedback. Specifically, the SC-MLLM first recognizes SE(3) pose errors as position, rotation,
094   or combined errors by utilizing the final state image and the robot’s end-effector pose. Based on the
095   identified error type, the SC-MLLM adaptively requests correction feedback from experts, such as
096   position Mo et al. (2021), rotation Fang et al. (2023), and reasoning expert Achiam et al. (2023).
097   Drawing on insights from previous errors and expert prompts, the SC-MLLM reflects on the current
098
      failure scenario and regenerates the appropriate contact pose.
099   During inference, the SC-MLLM initially interacts with the physical world using the fast system,
100   activating the slow system for reflection and correction only when a failure action occurs. Moreover,
101   as shown in Figure 1 c), we design a continuous policy learning method to transfer successfully
102   corrected samples from the slow system to the fast system, utilizing exponential moving average
103   techniques (Tarvainen & Valpola, 2017b) to enhance the pose prediction accuracy of the fast system.
104
      This method enhances the model’s adaptability to the current scene configuration while also reducing
      the frequency of expert intervention.
105

107   and corresponding 60k correction prompts in the SAPIEN simulation (Xiang et al., 2020). To
      systematically evaluate our method, we conduct extensive experiments in both simulation and real-

126   2    R ELATED WORK
127
128   Multimodal Large Language Models Large language models (LLMs) have shown impressive
129   reasoning capacities in a variety of downstream tasks (Touvron et al., 2023; Floridi & Chiriatti,
130
      2020). In tackling complicated multi-modal reasoning problems, Multimodal Large language models
      (MLLMs) (Alayrac et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023) have demonstrated impressive
131
      capacities in bridging modalities. BLIP (Li et al., 2022; 2023a) pre-trains encoder-decoder models
132
      using a dataset from image-text pairs, adding synthetic captions and filtering noisy ones for better
133   vision-language understanding and generation. LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023a) and MiniGPT- 4 (Zhu et al.,
134   2023) propose using a simple fully connected layer as a bridge between the image encoder and LLM.
135   They also investigate the importance of dataset pre-training and instruction tuning. Meanwhile, some
136   MLLMs (Chen et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023c; Lin et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a) introduce vision-
137   centric tasks on top of visual instruction tuning, validating that MLLMs are capable of producing
138   fine-grained perceptual results. Besides, the introduction of 3D MLLMs (Guo et al., 2023b; Hong
139   et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023d; Yang et al., 2023b) aims to broaden the scope of reasoning and
140   conversational capabilities obtained from LLMs to encompass the 3D modality.
141   Robotic Manipulation. Robotic manipulation has become a pivotal area of research due to its wide-
142   ranging applicability. State-based reinforcement learning is a popular approach in this field (Joshi
143   et al., 2020; Andrychowicz et al., 2020; Yarats et al., 2021; Geng et al., 2023b). While some studies
144   have explored using the pure state as the policy input (Andrychowicz et al., 2020), more intricate
145   scenarios require vision-based observation (Mo et al., 2019a; 2021; Liu et al., 2024a; Xu et al.,
146   2022; Eisner et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2023; Zitkovich et al., 2023; Xu et al.,
147
      2023; Wan et al., 2023; Gong et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023b; Geng et al.,
      2023a) to perceive the environment and comprehend complex scenes and objects (Deng et al., 2023;
148
      Lei et al., 2023). Inspired by MLLMs success in general scenarios (Alayrac et al., 2022; Li et al.,
149
      2022; Zhang et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2024), some works have tried to employ the
150   common sense reasoning ability of MLLMs to solve manipulation tasks. Palm-E (Driess et al.,
151   2023) trains multimodal encodings end-to-end in conjunction with LLMs for manipulation planning.
152   VoxPoser (Huang et al., 2023) extracts affordances and constraints from MLLMs to further zero-shot
153   generate trajectories for manipulation tasks. RT-2 (Zitkovich et al., 2023), which transfers information
154   to actions, holds promise for adapting more rapidly to novel situations. Robotflamingo (Li et al.,
155   2023c) tries to fine-tune MLLM on imitation learning datasets to complete basic long-horizon tasks.
156   Recent works (Li et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2024b) further employ the reasoning ability of MLLMs and
157   equip them with the ability to predict end-effector poses. Although integrating MLLMs into robotics
158
      has shown promising progress, current pipelines may lead to failure predictions when facing novel
      tasks or object instances.
159
160   Robotic Failure Correction. Several studies have delved into correcting robotic failures. RE-
161   FLECT (Liu et al., 2023c) introduces LLMs for reasoning based on a summary of past experiences,
      utilizing failure explanations for improved planning. MULTIREACT (Yu et al., 2023) utilizes a


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      vision-language model (Radford et al., 2021) as a reward model to recognize and autonomously
163   recover from intermediate execution failures. DoReMi (Guo et al., 2023a) conducts immediate
164   detection of misalignments between plans and execution using LLMs and then recovers from them.
165   CLAIRify (Skreta et al., 2023) generates iterative prompting with program verification to ensure
166   action plans are valid. While these works demonstrate the use of LLMs in correcting execution
167   failures in robotic tasks, they are limited to directly correcting low-level actions (e.g., 6 DoF pose)
168   and fail to learn from the corrected feedback. In this paper, we aim to develop a slow system that
169   mimics human-like thinking by enabling MLLMs to autonomously recognize and correct end-effector
170   poses, while continually learning from the corrected samples.

179   3.1     P ROBLEM FORMULATION
180
181   In this paper, we make the first attempt to enhance the MLLM with a human-like thinking paradigm
182   for robot manipulation, integrating both a fast system for predicting end-effector poses and a slower
183
      system for correcting failed actions. Therefore, our problem formulation comprises two components.
184   End-effector pose prediction. For manipulation ability, our SC-MLLM policy π generates an
185   action ai based on the image (Ii ∈ RW ×H×3 ) and language question (Li ) at the initial state. This
186   action, denoted as π(Ii , Li ) → ai , corresponds to the 6-DoF control of a Franka Panda robot arm (Li
187   et al., 2023b; Xu et al., 2022). It is parameterized by the end-effector position and rotation, where
188   ai = (apos     rot

                                 pos

189
      rotation matrix. We include the end-effector pose in the language for MLLM fine-tuning.
190   Failure reflection and correction. During the actual manipulation process, robot action ai often
191   encounters failures, known as error action aerr i . For failure correction ability, our SC-MLLM policy
192   π utilize the end state RGB image (Ie ∈ RW ×H×3 ) and the language-descriptive robot state (Lai i ) to
193   identify failure cases, represented as π(Ie , Lai i ) → ci . Depending on the error type ci , our SC-MLLM
194   can dynamically request prompt feedback fi from experts. This feedback is then utilized as input
195   to re-predict actions ac using our SC-MLLM policy π(Ii , fi ) → ac . Details of the reflection and
196
      correction process are provided in Section 3.2.3.

      3.2.1    M ODEL ARCHITECTURE
200
201   To equip our model with foundational reasoning abilities, we adopt LLaMA-Adapter V2 (Gao
202   et al., 2023) as our base MLLM. As shown in Figure 2, when presented with an RGB image, we
203   utilize CLIP visual encoder (Radford et al., 2021) to extract visual features. Simultaneously, text
204   prompts are encoded into textual features using the pre-trained LLaMA tokenizer (Touvron et al.,
205   2023). Subsequently, our MLLM employs a projection layer to align visual tokens with LLM’s token
206   embedding, enabling the LLaMA to perform multimodal comprehension and generate corresponding
207
      answers. During the training process, we only fine-tune the injected adapters (Hu et al., 2021)
      within the LLM, while keeping the major pre-trained parameters frozen. This strategy is aimed at
208
      preserving the robust capabilities of the existing MLLM while enhancing the model with additional
209
      functionalities in manipulation and failure correction.

259   3.2.3    S LOW SYSTEM : FAILURE REFLECTION AND CORRECTION
260
261
      This section aims to equip our SC-MLLM with the slow system’s ability to reflect on and correct
      failure actions. Different from previous correction works (Liu et al., 2023c; Ming et al., 2023) aimed
262
      at correcting high-level planning, we make the first attempt to directly correct the end-effector’s
263
      6-DoF control action through a chain of thought training strategy. For both open-loop and closed-loop
264   tasks, we make pose corrections when the gripper interacts with the object. Before moving on to the
265   correction process, it’s necessary to assess whether the action has failed and identify the causes of the
266   failure. Specifically, we leverage our model’s inherent visual understanding capabilities, along with
267   specific prompts, to determine whether the task has been completed. As shown in the slow system
268   of Figure 2, we feed the end-state image into our SC-MLLM and pose the question, “Is the drawer
269   open? Please answer yes or no.” If SC-MLLM determines that the current task is incomplete, it

      Based on the type of error, our SC-MLLM can adaptively request prompt feedback from specified
300
      experts and utilize this feedback to re-predict the action pose, as shown in Figure 2. To obtain position
301   expert feedback during inference, as shown in Figure 3 a), we first use Where2Act (Mo et al., 2021)
302   to generate an affordance map by inputting 2D images. The affordance map indicates the probability
303   of achieving a moving distance when operating on certain pixels, reflecting the manipulable region of
304   objects. However, existing affordance-based methods (Mo et al., 2019a; Eisner et al., 2022) can only
305   predict a potential manipulable region with limited accuracy. For example, in Figure 3, the highest
306   affordance score region predicted by the expert is C0-C3, which is obviously not a manipulable
307   region. Therefore, to provide a more reliable contact point, we utilize the common sense reasoning
308   ability of a reasoning expert (e.g., GPT-4V (Achiam et al., 2023)) to further filter the contact points.
309
      Specifically, we randomly select points from areas with high affordance scores. These selected points
      are then projected back onto the image and fed into the reasoning expert, using the following prompt
310
      to filter: “Which of the contact points shown in the image can close the object? Please select n points
311
      ...” This process allows us to automatically obtain relatively accurate contact points, which are then
312   used as input prompts for our SC-MLLM.
313
314   As shown in Figure 3 b), we use Anygrasp (Fang et al., 2023) to generate the rotation correction
315
      prompt. Anygrasp is trained on large-scale data and directly inputs 3D point cloud data, which
      includes rich contextual information, enabling accurate grasp rotation prediction. However, our
316
      actions are not limited to grasping, so we cannot predict the position and rotation for the entire object.
317
      This is why we do not use Anygrasp as the position expert. To generate the rotation for the local
318   region of each object, we select a manipulation box based on the contact point predicted by our model
319   and generate the rotation within the selected box region. For the manipulation box, we expand 5
320   pixels outward from the contact point as the center. If the failure case involves combined errors, we
321   sequentially apply position and rotation corrections. The rotation expert uses the highest-confidence
322   contact point provided by the position expert to generate the corresponding rotation. Finally, we
323   combine the correction feedback for both position and rotation as input prompts for our model. To
      enable our SC-MLLM to understand expert feedback, we introduced a similar question format during

329   3.3   C ONTINUOUS POLICY LEARNING
330
331   To equip our model with the fast system’s ability for direct manipulation and the slow system’s ability
332   for failure correction, we integrate pose prediction, failure detection, and failure correction data for
333   co-fine-tuning. Following previous works (Zhang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b), all outputs from the
334
      LLM are supervised using the cross-entropy loss Lce . During inference, since the relative position of
      the robot and the object changes during each manipulation process, it cannot reuse previous expert
335
      feedback. Therefore, after obtaining successfully corrected samples, we design a continuous policy
336
      learning method (shown in Figure 2). Note that, we utilize the prompt format used for training
337   the fast system. This method aims to enhance the fast system’s pose prediction accuracy without
338   relying on the slow system’s CoT reasoning, as expert interventions incur additional inference time.
339   However, during fine-tuning with new successful samples, there is a risk of catastrophic forgetting,
340   which can result in a loss of accuracy on previously trained samples. Therefore, we explore the use
341   of exponential moving average (EMA) (Tarvainen & Valpola, 2017b) to continually learn from new
342   data, formulated as:
343
344                                         π t = απ t−1 + (1 − α)π t                                      (1)
345
346   Where t is the time step, π represents our model. We set the updating weight α = 0.999 based on
347   (Tarvainen & Valpola, 2017a). We evaluate the effectiveness of the EMA scheme in action continual
348   learning, as shown in Appendix D. Finally, our SC-MLLM can perform repeated continuous policy
349
      learning sessions for successfully corrected samples, continually transferring the slow system’s
      knowledge to the fast system and improving the model’s prediction accuracy. This process efficiently
350
      generates customized manipulation policies for the specific scenario, rather than relying on a shared,
351
      low-accuracy policy.

      Implementation Details. Our SC-MLLM loads the pre-trained parameters of LLaMA-Adapter-v2
403   (Zhang et al., 2023), which includes a pre-trained CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) as the visual encoder,

      Evaluation Metric. For the open-loop experiment, we follow the metrics from previous works (Li
409   et al., 2023b), using the manipulation success rate. Specifically, the object starts in its initial state,
410   and the goal is to actuate the joint to its target state. We use the predicted contact point and rotation to
411   interact with objects and complete the task. Similar to (Mo et al., 2021), the trajectory for each task is
412   predefined, i.e., moving backward along the z-axis of the end-effector pose. An action is considered
413   successful if the joint state difference before and after interaction exceeds a threshold of 0.1 meters.
414   For the closed-loop experiment, we follow the evaluation metrics from previous works(James et al.,
415   2020), assessing task success rate based on predefined success conditions.

457   To elucidate the contribution and effectiveness of individual methods within our SC-MLLM, we
458   conduct extensive ablation studies in the Sapien simulator.
459   The impact of expert type. First, we investigate the impact of various expert feedback prompts

461   utilizing our designed position and rotation feedback, respectively. “P + R” represents the utilization

463   on the cause of failure. For all experiments, we input the prompt feedback into our SC-MLLM,
464   allowing it to re-predict poses based on the prompt. We find that any type of expert prompt can
465
      improve unseen manipulation accuracy, demonstrating the importance of correction for novel object
      manipulation. Additionally, we observe that ”Ours” achieves comparable results to ”P + R”, but
466
      with lower expert intervention costs. The results confirm that detecting failure cases and adaptively
467
      seeking expert feedback is essential for stable manipulation. The accuracy of failure detection is
468   shown in Appendix C.
469
470   The impact of correction times. In the slow system, our utilized experts can return multiple prompts
471
      simultaneously; for example, the position expert can provide n potential contact points. Therefore,
      we explore the impact of different correction times on pose correction accuracy. As shown in Figure 4
472
      b), the x-axis represents the number of expert prompts used for correction. Note that we count a
473
      manipulation as successful if it succeeds even once during multiple corrected actions. We find that

      5       C ONCLUSION AND LIMITATION
529
530   Drawing on Daniel Kahneman’s concept that ”human thinking is divided into a fast system and a
531
      slow system,” we propose a self-correcting (SC) MLLM that emulates a human-like thinking model
      to achieve stable manipulation. The SC-MLLM integrates a fast system capable of directly predicting
532
      end-effector poses and a slow system designed to reflect on and correct failed actions. During
533
      inference, the fast system initially interacts with the physical world, while the slow system is only
534   activated when a failure occurs, allowing for reflection and correction. Additionally, we introduce
535   a continuous policy learning method to enable the SC-MLLM to learn from successfully corrected
536   actions, continually improving the fast system’s manipulation accuracy. As for limitations, the
537   SC-MLLM’s backbone relies on MLLM to leverage its chain-of-thought(CoT) reasoning capabilities.
538   However, the MLLM leads to relatively slow control frequency, especially in closed-loop tasks. In
539   future work, we plan to incorporate more efficient MLLMs (Wen et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b; Zhou
      et al., 2024), speeding up the CoT reasoning process of our slow system.

      B.2     S APIEN DATASET VARIATION
865
866   Regarding the variation between training and testing data, we followed the data collection settings of
867   where2act (Mo et al., 2021) and ManipLLM (Li et al., 2023b). The specific variations can be divided
868   into two aspects: 1) Asset Variation and 2) State Variation.
869
      1) Asset Variation: We use 20 categories from PartNet (Mo et al., 2019b) for seen objects and reserve
870   the remaining 10 categories for unseen objects to analyze if RoboMamba can generalize to novel
871   categories. Specifically, we further divide the seen objects into 1037 training shapes and 489 testing
872   shapes, using only the training shapes to construct the training data. Thus, the shapes of the seen
873   objects encountered during training and testing are different. For unseen categories, there are a total
874   of 274 shapes, which are used exclusively in the testing data.
875
      2) State Variation: We observe the object in the scene from an RGB-D camera with known intrinsics,
876   mounted 4.5-5.5 units away from the object, facing its center. The camera is located at the upper
877   hemisphere of the object with a random azimuth between [-45, 45] and a random altitude between
878   [30, 60]. Since the tasks involve ’pulling,’ we also initialize the starting pose for each articulated
879   part randomly between its rest joint state (fully closed) and any position up to half of its joint state
880   (half-opened). These state settings are utilized for both training and testing data, aiming to boost the
881   model’s generalization ability.
882
883   B.3     BASELINE R EPRODUCTION
884
885   UMPNet (Xu et al., 2022): It inputs visual observations, such as RGB images and depth maps, of an
886   articulated object to generate a sequence of actions in SE(3) space. It identifies the correct position
887   on the object to interact with (e.g.., interacting with the lid rather than the base) and determines the
888
      appropriate action direction (e.g., pulling up instead of pushing down) for interaction.
889   Flowbot3D (Eisner et al., 2022): Flowbot3D begins by observing the initial configuration of the
890   object of interest in the form of point cloud data. It is then post-processed and inputted into the model,
891   which predicts 3D flow vectors for each point. It selects the point with the maximum flow vector
892   magnitude and uses motion planning to make contact with that point via suction based on the selected
893   flow.
894   ManipLLM (Li et al., 2023b): ManipLLM uses chain-of-thought reasoning to enable the model
895   to precisely generate an initial contact end-effector pose, including the pixel coordinates, gripper
896   upward direction, and gripper forward direction. It then employs an active impedance adaptation
897   policy that adjusts the direction based on force feedback to ensure a smooth movement trajectory.
