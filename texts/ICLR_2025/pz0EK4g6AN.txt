                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            Quantum computing is an emerging field recognized for the significant speedup it
012            offers over classical computing through quantum algorithms. However, designing
013            and implementing quantum algorithms pose challenges due to the complex nature
014            of quantum mechanics and the necessity for precise control over quantum states.
015            Despite the significant advancements in AI, there has been a lack of datasets
016            specifically tailored for this purpose. In this work, we introduce QCircuitNet, the
017            first benchmark and test dataset designed to evaluate AI’s capability in designing
018            and implementing quantum algorithms in the form of quantum circuit codes. Unlike
019
               using AI for writing traditional codes, this task is fundamentally different and
               significantly more complicated due to highly flexible design space and intricate
020
               manipulation of qubits. Our key contributions include:
021
022               1. A general framework which formulates the key features of quantum algorithm
023
                     design task for Large Language Models.
024               2. Implementation for a wide range of quantum algorithms from basic primitives
025
                     to advanced applications, with easy extension to more quantum algorithms.
026
                  3. Automatic validation and verification functions, allowing for iterative evalua-
                     tion and interactive reasoning without human inspection.
027
028
                  4. Promising potential as a training dataset through primitive fine-tuning results.
029            We observed several interesting experimental phenomena: fine-tuning does not
030            always outperform few-shot learning, and LLMs tend to exhibit consistent error
031
               patterns. QCircuitNet provides a comprehensive benchmark for AI-driven quantum
               algorithm design, offering advantages in model evaluation and improvement, while
032
               also revealing some limitations of LLMs in this domain.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Quantum computing is an emerging field in recent decades because algorithms on quantum computers
038   may solve problems significantly faster than their classical counterparts. From the perspective of
039   theoretical computer science, the design of quantum algorithms have been investigated in various
040   research directions - see the survey (Dalzell et al., 2023) and the quantum algorithm zoo (Zoo, 2024).
041   However, the design of quantum algorithms on quantum computers has been completed manually
042   by researchers. This process is notably challenging due to highly flexible design space and extreme
043   demands for a comprehensive understanding of mathematical tools and quantum properties.
044   For these reasons, quantum computing is often considered to have high professional barriers. As the
045   discipline evolves, we aim to explore more possibilities for algorithm design and implementation
046   in the quantum setting. This is aligned with recent advances among AI for Science, including
047   AlphaFold (Jumper et al., 2021), AlphaGeometry (Trinh et al., 2024), etc. Recently, large language
048   models (LLMs) have also become widely applicable among AI for science approaches (Yang et al.,
049   2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024). LLMs represent the best practice of sequential modeling
050
      methods at current stage. They have an edge over other models in possessing abundant pre-training
      knowledge and providing human-friendly interfaces which support human-machine collaboration.
051
      Therefore, we gear LLMs for quantum algorithm design.
052
053   As far as we know, there has not been any dataset for AI in quantum algorithm design. Existing
      work combining quantum computing and AI mostly targets at exploiting quantum computing for

054
      AI; there are some papers applying AI for quantum computing, but they either consider niche
055   problems (Nakayama et al., 2023; Schatzki et al., 2021) or limited functions (Tang et al., 2023;
056   Fürrutter et al., 2024), not quantum algorithm datasets of general interest (see Section 2). However,
057   unlike classical code generation where abundant data exist, the most challenging aspect for quantum
058   algorithm design is the lack of sufficient data, and hence the difficulty of generalization in training AI
059   models. Therefore, datasets for quantum algorithm design are solicited.
060
      Descriptions of quantum algorithms in natural language could be verbose and vague. Mathematical
061   formulas, while precise and succinct, are difficult to verify automatically. To accommodate with
062   LLMs, we make a change of perspective by formulating quantum algorithms as programming lan-
063   guages. This allows for precise representation of a quantum algorithm, enables automatic verification
064   procedure, and bridges the gap between theoretical design and circuit implementations. Furthermore,
065   meaningful quantum algorithms which can be efficiently implemented have no more than polyno-
066   mially many gates (Poulin et al., 2011), and thus such formulations have the theoretical benefits
067   allowing for scalable representations.
068
069   Key Contributions. In this work, We propose QCircuitNet, the first comprehensive, structured
070   dataset for quantum algorithm design. Technically, QCircuitNet has the following key contributions:
071
      • It formulates the task for Large Language Models (LLMs) with a carefully designed framework
072
        encompassing the key features of quantum algorithm design, including problem description,
073
        quantum circuit codes, classical post-processing, and verification functions. It maintains the
074     black-box nature of oracles and characterizes query complexity properly.
075
076
      • It implements a wide range of quantum algorithms from basic primitives and textbook-level
        algorithms to advanced applications. We demonstrate the compatibility with complex algorithms
077
        through Generalized Simon’s Problem and showcase the easy extension to advanced algorithms.
078
079   • It has automatic validation and verification functions, allowing for iterative evaluation without
080     human inspection. This further enables interactive reasoning which may improve the performance.
081   • It showcases the potential as a training dataset through primitive fine-tuning results. As we expand
082     the dataset to include more algorithms and explore novel fine-tuning methods, it will hopefully
083     contribute to interactive quantum algorithm design and implementation significantly.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
086
087
      Quantum Machine Learning. To the best of our knowledge, QCircuitNet is the first dataset
088   tailored specifically for quantum algorithm design. Previous efforts combining quantum computing
089   with AI primarily fall under the category of Quantum Machine Learning (QML), which aims at
090   leveraging the unique properties of quantum systems to enhance machine learning algorithms and
091   achieve improvements over their classical counterparts (Schuld et al., 2015; Biamonte et al., 2017;
092   Ciliberto et al., 2018). Corresponding datasets often focus on encoding classical data into quantum
093   states. For instance, MNISQ (Placidi et al., 2023) is a dataset of quantum circuits representing the
094   original MNIST dataset (LeCun et al., 1998) generated by the AQCE algorithm (Shirakawa et al.,
095   2021). Considering the intrinsic nature of quantum properties, another category of datasets focuses
096
      on collecting quantum data to demonstrate quantum advantages since classical machine learning
      methods can fail to characterize particular patterns of quantum data. For example, Nakayama et al.
097
      (2023) created a VQE-generated quantum circuit dataset for classification of variational ansatzes
098
      and showed its quantum supremacy. NTangled (Schatzki et al., 2021) further investigated different
099   types of entanglement and composed quantum states with various multipartite entanglement for
100   classification. While these datasets successfully demonstrate the supremacy of quantum computing,
101   they address relatively niche problems whose practical applications are unclear.
102
103   AI for Quantum Computing. This research direction explores the possibility of leveraging AI to
104   facilitate the advancement of quantum computing. QDataSet (Perrier et al., 2022) collects data from
105   simulations of one- and two-qubit systems and targets training classical machine learning algorithms
106   for quantum control, quantum tomography, and noise mitigation. LLM4QPE (Tang et al., 2023) is
107   a large language model style paradigm for predicting quantum system properties with pre-training
      and fine-tuning workflows. While the paradigm is interesting, the empirical experiments are limited


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      to two downstream tasks: quantum phase classification and correlation prediction. Fürrutter et al.
109   (2024) studied the application of diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Rombach et al., 2022)
110   to quantum circuit synthesis (Saeedi & Markov, 2013; J. et al., 2022). Scalability issues must be
111   addressed to achieve practical and meaningful unitary compilation through this methodology.
112
113   Quantum Circuit Benchmarks. The aforementioned works represent meaningful explorations
114   at the intersection of AI and quantum computing. However, none of them considers the task
115   which interests the quantum computing community (from the theoretical side) the most: quantum
116   algorithm design. Our work aims to take the first step in bridging this gap. It is worth noting
117   that several quantum algorithm circuit benchmarks already exist, such as QASMBench (Li et al.,
118
      2023), MQTBench (Quetschlich et al., 2023), and VeriQBench (Chen et al., 2022). However, these
      benchmarks are designed to evaluate the performance of NISQ (Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum)
119
      (Preskill, 2018) machines or quantum software tools, rather than for training and evaluating AI models.
120
      For instance, QASMBench includes a diverse variety of quantum circuits based on OpenQASM
121   representation (Cross et al., 2022), covering quantum circuits with qubit sizes ranging from 2 to 127.
122   However, it fails as a dataset for AI in that it does not capture the design patterns of each algorithm
123   and ignores the post-processing procedure and construction of different oracles, which are crucial to
124   quantum algorithm design. Similar limitations apply to MQTBench and VeriQBench.

165   4.1     TASK S UITE
166
167
      For the general purpose of quantum algorithm design, we consider three categories of tasks: oracle
      construction, algorithm design, and random circuit synthesis. These three tasks are crucial for
168
      devising and implementing quantum algorithms, with oracle construction serving as the premise for
169
      algorithm design, and random circuits serving as a main demonstration for quantum supremacy.
170
171
172   4.1.1    TASK I: O RACLE C ONSTRUCTION
173
174   The construction of such an oracle Uf using quantum gates is deeply rooted in the research topic
175
      of reversible quantum logic synthesis, which remains a challenge for complex Boolean functions.
      In this dataset, we mainly focus on the construction of textbook-level oracles: Bernstein-Vazirani
176
      Problem (Bernstein & Vazirani, 1993), Deutsch-Jozsa Problem (Deutsch & Jozsa, 1992), Simon’s
177
      Problem (Simon, 1997), and Grover’s algorithm for unstructured search (Grover, 1996) (including
178   constructions of both the oracle and the diffusion operator). There is another category of more flexible
179   oracle construction tasks which we refer to as "Problem Encoding". For example, one can apply
180   Grover’s oracle to solving constraint problems such as SAT and triangle finding (Ambainis, 2004).
181   Formulating problem encoding tasks for LLMs slightly differs from quantum logic synthesis, and we
182   refer the readers to Appendix A.2 for more detailed discussion.
183
184
      4.1.2    TASK II: Q UANTUM A LGORITHM D ESIGN
185
186   There are several challenges to address in order to formulate quantum algorithm design task precisely:
187
188   • From a theoretical perspective, the oracle is usually provided as a blackbox gate since the goal of
189     many algorithms is to determine the property of the function f (x) encoded by the oracle Uf . If
190     the model has access to the gate implementation of the oracle, it can directly deduce the property
191     from the circuit, failing the purpose of designing a quantum algorithm to decode the information.
192     However, for all experimental platforms, a quantum circuit needs to be explicitly constructed to
193     compile and run successfully, i.e., the oracle should be provided with exact gate implementation.
194     Most tutorials and benchmarks (especially those based on Qiskit and OpenQASM) simply merge
195
        the circuit implementation of the oracle and the algorithm as a whole for demonstration purposes.
        When we gear LLMs for quantum algorithm design, how to separate the algorithm circuits from
196
        oracle implementation to avoid information leakage is a critical point to consider.
197
198   • A quantum algorithm constitutes not only the quantum circuit, but also its interpretation of mea-
199     surement results. For example, in Simon’s algorithm, the measurement results yi are not the direct
200     answer s to the problem, but rather satisfies the property s · yi = 0. Linear equations need to be
201     solved to obtain the final answer. In this case, for a complete algorithm design, the model should
202     also specify the the post-process steps to derive the answer to the original problem.
203
      • Quantum circuits for the same algorithm vary with different qubit number n. Although this is trivial
204
        for theoretical design, it needs to be considered when implementing concrete quantum circuits.
205
206
      In this category, we cover a wide range of quantum algorithms with varying complexity, from
207
      fundamental primitives and textbook-level algorithms to advanced applications. For example, we
208   implemented Generalized Simon’s Problem (Ye et al., 2021), a more advanced version of the standard
209   Simon’s problem and an active area of research in recent years (Ye et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2022). The
210   setting is formally stated as follows: given an (unknown) function f : Znp → X where X is a finite set
211   and a k is a positive integer satisfying k < n, it is guaranteed that there exists a subgroup S ≤ Znp of
212   rank k such that for any x, y ∈ Znp , f (x) = f (y) iff x − y ∈ S. The goal is to find S. Intuitively, the
213   generalized Simon’s problem extends the standard Simon’s problem from binary to p-ary bases and
214   from a single secret string to a subgroup of rank k. Beyond universal quantum algorithms, we also
215   consider quantum teleportation and quantum key distribution, two widespread protocols in quantum
      information. We cover their details in Appendix B.

      4.1.3    TASK III: R ANDOM C IRCUIT S YNTHESIS
217
218   The third task we consider is random circuit synthesis. On the one hand, random circuit sampling is
219   the first algorithm for showing quantum supremacy by Google in 2019 (Arute et al., 2019), and is
220   still widely applied to demonstrate the power of quantum algorithms in recent research (Wu et al.,
221   2021; Bluvstein et al., 2024; DeCross et al., 2024). On the other hand, this also naturally enlarges our
222
      dataset. To be more specific, we generate the dataset by randomly sampling gates from a Clifford
      gate set {H, S, CNOT} and a universal set {H, S, T, CNOT} separately. We then simulate the circuits
223
      to obtain the final quantum states. In each task, the problem description provides the vector of the
224
      final state, and the model is required to generate quantum circuits that reproduce this state using the
225   specified gate set.

256   Design Principles. As discussed in Section 4.1, a critical consideration in formulating the frame-
257   work is the dilemma between providing the oracle as a black box for quantum algorithm design and
258   the need for its explicit construction to execute the circuit and interpret the results, making the algo-
259
      rithm design complete. Additionally, model training and reference present challenges, particularly
      for LLMs in generating complex and precise composite gates and evaluating the results efficiently.
260
      To address these obstacles, we highlight the following construction principles, which are specially
261
      designed to adapt to these tasks:
262
263   • For algorithm design tasks, as discussed in Section 4.1.2, we provide the oracle as a black-box gate
264     named "Oracle" with the explicit definition in a separate "oracle.inc" library, which is supported by
265     the OpenQASM 3.0 grammar. In this way, we make sure that the model can use the oracle without
266     accessing its underlying function, which solves the problem of isolating oracle definition from the
267     algorithm circuit.
268   • For oracle construction tasks, we ask the model to directly output the quantum circuit in QASM
269     format. For algorithm design task, we require both a quantum circuit and a post-processing function
        to derive the final answer from circuit execution results. Moreover, we ask the model to explicitly


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
        set the shots needed to run the circuit itself in order to characterize the query complexity, which is
271     critical in the theoretical analysis of algorithms.
272
      • For available quantum gates, we provide the definition of some important composite gates not
273
        included in the standard QASM gate library in a "customgates.inc". Hierarchical definition for
274
        multi-controlled X gate contains 45060 lines for qubit number n = 14 in OpenQASM format,
275     which is impossible for AI models to accurately generate at the time. Providing these as a .inc file
276     guarantees the correctness of OpenQASM’s grammar while avoiding the generation of complicated
277     gates, which is a distraction from the original design task.
278   • To verify models’ output automatically without human evaluation, we compose verification func-
279     tions to validate the syntax of QASM / Qiskit and the functionality of the implemented circuits
280     / codes. Since comprehensive Logic Equivalence Checking (LEC) might be inefficient for the
281     throughput of LLM inference, we perform the verification by directly checking the correctness of
282     output with extensive test cases.
283
284
      Based on theses principles, we proposed the framework of QCircuitNet. Below is a more detailed
      explanation for the 7 components of the dataset:
285
286   1. Problem Description: carefully hand-crafted prompts stating the oracle to be constructed or the
287      target problem to be solved in natural language and latex math formulas. If the problem involves
288      the usage of a quantum oracle or composite gates beyond the standard gate library, the interfaces
289      of the oracle / gate will also be included (input qubits, output qubits, function mechanism).
290   2. Generation Code: one general Qiskit (Javadi-Abhari et al., 2024) code to create quantum circuits
291      for oracles or algorithms of different settings, such as distinct secret strings or various qubit
292      numbers. We choose Qiskit as the main experiment platform because it is a general quantum
293      programming software widely used for the complete workflow from creating quantum circuits to
294      transpiling, simulation, and execution on real hardware.
295   3. Algorithm Circuit: a .qasm file storing the quantum circuit for each specific setting. We choose
296      OpenQASM 3.0 (Cross et al., 2022) as the format to store the quantum circuits, because Qiskit,
297      as a python library, can only create quantum circuits at runtime instead of explicitly saving the
298      circuits at gate level.1
299   4. Post-Processing Function: this is for Algorithm Design task only, see Section 4.1.2. The function
300      takes a complete quantum circuit as input, uses the Qiskit AerSimulator to execute the circuit,
301      and returns the final answer to the original problem according to the simulation results. For state
302      preparation problems such as creating a GHZ state of n qubits, this function returns the qubit
303
         indices of the generated state.
304   5. Oracle / Gate Definition: a .inc file to provide definitions of composite gates or oracles. For
305      oracle construction tasks, this only includes the definition of composite gates required to build the
306
         oracle. For algorithm design tasks, we also provide the gate definition of the oracle in this file,
         which successfully delivers the oracle in a black-box way.
307
308   6. Verification Function: a function to evaluate whether the implemented oracle / algorithm achieves
309      the desired purpose with grammar validation and test cases verification. If there exist grammar
310
         errors, the function returns -1 and provides detailed error message, which can be used as feedback
         for the LLM to improve through interactive reasoning. If the program can execute successfully,
311
         the function returns a score between [0, 1] indicating the success rate on test cases.2
312
313
      7. Dataset Creation Script: the script to create the dataset from scratch in the format suitable for
         benchmarking / fine-tuning LLMs. It contains the following functions: 1. generate primitive
314
         QASM circuits. 2. extract gate definitions and add include instructions to create algorithm circuit,
315
         the direct output of model. 3. validate and verify the correctness of the data points in the dataset. 4.
316      concatenate algorithm circuit with problem description as a json file for the benchmark pipeline.
317
318   This structure of QCircuitNet provides a general framework to formulate quantum algorithm design
319   for large language models, with an easy extension to more advanced quantum algorithms.

372   2. Verification function: this function checks the syntax validation and the result correctness of the
373      code produced by the language model.
374   3. Byte Perplexity: this metric evaluates the model’s ability to predict the next byte in a sequence.
375      Lower byte perplexity indicates better performance by reflecting the model’s predictive accuracy.

      6   C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
487
488
      In this paper, we propose QCircuitNet, the first comprehensive, structured universal quantum algo-
489   rithm dataset and quantum circuit generation benchmark for AI models. This framework formulates
490   quantum algorithm design from the programming language perspective and includes detailed de-
491   scriptions and implementation of most established and important quantum algorithms / primitives,
492   allowing for automatic verification methodologies. Benchmarking of QCircuitNet on up-to-date
493   LLMs is systematically conducted. Fine-tuning results also showcase the potential of QCircuitNet
494   as a training dataset. As shown by these benchmarking and fine-tuning results, QCircuitNet helps
495   guide LLMs for reasoning and implementation. As we gradually extend the dataset, it will hopefully
496   contribute to interactive quantum algorithm design.
497   We also highlight the challenges and opportunities as follows, ranging from scalability on the quantum
498   algorithm side to data contamination on the AI side:
499
500   Scalability of the Approach. Our framework is designed to scale with increasing qubit numbers and
501   support complex quantum algorithms as long as they are efficiently implementable with polynomial
502   gates. The dataset generation scripts are written in a generic way which can be easily extended to
503   arbitrarily large qubit numbers. The implementation of Generalized Simon’s Problem mentioned in
504   Section 4.1.2 showcases the compatibility of our framework with more complex algorithms. The main
505
      bottleneck for scalability in the pipeline lies in the simulation process in verification function. For
      simplicity of demonstration and consideration of hardware noise, all the verification functions were
506
      run with classical simulations in our experiments. But the APIs we implemented are compatible with
507
      IBM hardware and can be easily adapted to quantum computers, which reduces the cost of classical
508   simulation. As quantum hardware becomes widespread and the capabilities of large language models
509   continue to advance, the dataset can be easily expanded to accommodate scaling.
510
511   Data Contamination in AI Learning. We observe a performance separation between writing
512   general Qiskit codes and explicit gate-level circuits in QASM. Since Qiskit provides detailed tutorial
513   with general codes for several algorithms, this may imply a data contamination phenomenon where
514   LLMs rely on memorization and retrieval rather than genuine algorithm design (see Appendix C.4
515   for more details). Similarly, current benchmarks for AI code generation and syntax learning may
516   also suffer from this unseen bias. Our dataset, based on QASM files created from scratch, may help
517   circumvent this issue and serve as a stable and fair method for benchmarking AI syntax learning.
518   Our work leaves several open questions for future investigation:
519
520   • QCircuitNet is a benchmarking dataset for LLMs. It is of general interest to extend benchmarking
521     to training, which will help LLMs better maneuver quantum algorithm design. We have imple-
522     mented advanced algorithms such as the Generalized Simon’s Problem, but this in general needs
523
        implementations of more advanced algorithms to make it a more meaningful training dataset.
524   • Since quantum algorithms have fundamental difference from classical algorithms, novel fine-
525     tuning methods to attempt quantum algorithm design and quantum circuit implementation, or even
526     developments of new quantum algorithms by LLMs are solicited.
527   • Currently, variational quantum algorithms (Cerezo et al., 2021) can already be implemented on near-
528     term NISQ machines (Preskill, 2018). It would be also of general interest to extend QCircuitNet to
529     contain the design and implementation of variational quantum algorithms.

      A.1   F ORMAT
865
866   In this subsection, we provide concrete examples to illustrate the different components of QCircuitNet.
867   We use the case of Simon’s Problem throughout the demonstration to achieve better consistency. For
868   further details, please check the code repository.
869
870   1. Problem Description: this is the carefully hand-crafted description of the task in natural language
871      and latex math formulas. The description is provided as one template for each algorithm, and the
872      concrete settings (such as the qubit number) are replaced when creating the data points in json.
873
         The file is named as "{algorithm_name}_description.txt".
874
             Problem Description Template for Simon’s Problem
875
876          Given a black box function f : {0, 1}n 7−→ {0, 1}n . The function is guaranteed to be
877          a two-to-one mapping according to a secret string s ∈ {0, 1}n , s ̸= 0n , where given
878          x1 ̸= x2 , f (x1 ) = f (x2 ) ⇐⇒ x1 ⊕ x2 = s. Please design a quantum algorithm
879          to find s. The function is provided as a black-box oracle gate named "Oracle" in the
880          "oracle.inc" file which operates as Of |x⟩ |y⟩ = |x⟩ |y ⊕ f (x)⟩. The input qubits |x⟩ are
881          indexed from 0 to n − 1, and the output qubits |f (x)⟩ are indexed from n to 2n − 1. Please
882          provide the following components for the algorithm design with n ={qubit number}:
883
             1. the corresponding quantum circuit implementation with {QASM / Qiskit}. 2. the
             post-processing code run_and_analyze(circuit, aer_sim) in python which simulates the
884
             circuit (QuantumCircuit) with aer_sim (AerSimulator) and returns the secret string s
885
             according to the simulation results.
886
887
888
      2. Generation Code: one general Qiskit code to create quantum circuits of different settings. Note
         that the oracle for the problem is provided as a black-box gate "oracle" here. This code is used to
889
         generate the raw data, but can also be used as a testing benchmark for writing Qiskit codes. The
890
         file is named as "{algorithm_name}_generation.py".

1360   Quantum Key Distribution. Quantum key distribution (QKD) (Bennett & Brassard, 1984) is a
1361   secure communication protocol that allows two parties, Alice and Bob, to produce a shared random
1362   secret key, which can then be used to encrypt and decrypt messages. The security of QKD is based
1363   on the fundamental principles of quantum mechanics that measuring a qubit can change its state. One
1364
       of the most well-known QKD protocols is the BB84 protocol, which works as follows:
1365
             1. Alice randomly generates a bit string and chooses a random basis (X or Z) for each bit. She
1366
                then encodes the bits into qubits using the chosen bases and sends them to Bob through a
1367            quantum channel.
1368
             2. Bob measures the received qubits in randomly chosen bases (X or Z) and records the results.
1369
1370         3. Alice and Bob communicate over a public classical channel to compare their basis choices.
1371            They keep only the bits for which their basis choices coincide and discard the rest.
1372         4. Alice and Bob randomly select a subset of the remaining bits and compare their values. If
1373            the error rate is below a certain threshold, they conclude that no eavesdropping has occurred,
1374            and the remaining bits can be used as a secret key. If the error rate is too high, they abort the
1375            protocol, as it indicates the presence of an eavesdropper (Eve).
1376
1377
       The security of the BB84 protocol relies on the fact that any attempt by Eve to measure the qubits
       during transmission will introduce detectable errors, alerting Alice and Bob to the presence of an
1378
       eavesdropper.

       BLEU Score. Bilingual Evaluation Understudy (BLEU) score is a metric used to evaluate the
1392   quality of machine-translated text compared to human-translated text. It measures how close the
1393   machine translation is to one or more reference translations. The BLEU score evaluates the quality
1394   of text generated by comparing it with one or more reference texts. It does this by calculating the
1395   n-gram precision, which means it looks at the overlap of n-grams (contiguous sequences of n words)
1396   between the generated text and the reference text. Originally the BLEU score ranges from 0 to 1,
1397   where 1 indicates a perfect match with the reference translations. Here rescaling the score makes it
1398   ranges from 0 to 100.
1399   The BLEU score, originally designed for machine translation, can also be effectively used for
1400   evaluating algorithm generation tasks. Just as BLEU measures the similarity between machine-
1401   translated text and human reference translations, it can measure the similarity between a generated
1402   algorithm and a gold-standard algorithm. This involves comparing sequences of tokens to assess how
1403   closely the generated output matches the reference solution. In the context of algorithm generation, n-
       grams can represent sequences of tokens or operations in the code. BLEU score captures the precision


                                                          26
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1404
       of these n-grams, ensuring that the generated code aligns closely with the expected sequences found
1405   in the reference implementation.
1406
1407   The formula for BLEU score is given by:

1413   for uniform weights), pn is the precision for n-grams. BP is calculated as:

1464   Byte Perplexity. Perplexity is a measure of how well a probability distribution or a probabilistic
1465   model predicts a sample. In the context of language models, it quantifies the uncertainty of the model
1466   when it comes to predicting the next element in a sequence. Byte perplexity specifically deals with
1467   sequences of bytes, which are the raw binary data units used in computer systems. For our purposes,
1468   we consider byte perplexity under UTF-8 encoding, a widely used character encoding standard that
1469
       represents each character as one or more bytes.
1470   For a given language model, let p(xi |x<i ) be the probability of the i-th byte xi given the preceding
1471   bytes x<i . If we have a sequence of bytes x = (x1 , x2 , . . . , xN ), the perplexity PPL(x) of the model
1472   on this sequence is defined as:
1473
                                                                                                          1
                                                                                                                 PN
1474                                                             PPL(x) = 2− N                                    i=1 log2 p(xi |x<i )             .
1475
1476   A notable feature of byte perplexity is that, it does not rely on any specific tokenizer, making it
1477   versatile for comparing different models. Therefore, byte perplexity can be used to measure the
1478   performance in quantum algorithm generation tasks. In such tasks, a lower byte perplexity indicates a
1479   better-performing model, as it means the model is more confident in its predictions of the next byte in
1480   the sequence.
