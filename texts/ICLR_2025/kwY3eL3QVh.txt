009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Score diffusion methods can learn probability densities from samples. The score
012            of the noise-corrupted density is estimated using a deep neural network, which is
013            then used to iteratively transport a Gaussian white noise density to a target density.
014            Variants for conditional densities have been developed, but correct estimation of
015            the corresponding scores is difficult. We avoid these difficulties by introducing an
016            algorithm that guides the diffusion with a projected score. The projection pushes
017            an image feature vector towards the corresponding centroid of the target class. The
018            projected score and the feature vectors are represented and learned within the same
019            network. Specifically, the image feature vector is defined as the spatial averages of
020
               the channels activations in select layers of the network. Optimizing the projected
               score for denoising loss encourages image feature vectors of each class to cluster
021
               around their centroids. It also leads to the separations of the centroids. We show
022
               that these centroids provide a low-dimensional Euclidean embedding of the class
023            conditional densities. We demonstrate that the algorithm can generate high quality
024            and diverse samples from the conditioning class. Conditional generation can be
025            performed using feature vectors interpolated between those of the training set,
026            demonstrating out-of-distribution generalization.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   Score diffusion is a powerful data generation methodology which operates by transporting white
033
      noise to a target distribution. When trained on samples drawn from different classes, it learns a
      mixture density over all the classes. In many applications, one wants to control the diffusion sampling
034
      process to obtain samples from the conditional distribution of a specified class. A brute force solution
035
      is to train a separate model on each class, learning each conditional density independently. This is
036   computationally expensive: each model requires a large training set to avoid memorization (Somepalli
037   et al., 2023; Carlini et al., 2023; Kadkhodaie et al., 2024). An alternative strategy is to train a single
038   model on all classes, with a procedure to guide the transport toward the conditional density of
039   individual classes. This approach can leverage the shared information between all classes, thus
040   reducing the required training set size needed to learn the full set of conditional densities.
041
      Learning conditional densities in a diffusion framework has been highly successful when the condi-
042   tioning arises from a separately-trained text embedding system (e.g., Ramesh et al. (2021); Rombach
043   et al. (2022); Saharia et al. (2022)) or image classifier network (Song et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol,
044   2021), or by jointly learning a classifier and the score model Ho & Salimans (2022). Despite the high
045   quality of generated images, several mathematical and numerical studies Chidambaram et al. (2024);
046   Wu et al. (2024) show that these guidance algorithms do not sample from appropriate conditional
047   distributions, even in the case of Gaussian mixtures. This is due to their reliance on estimating the
048   exact likelihood to obtain the score of the conditional distributions, which is difficult.
049   In this work, we introduce a modified score diffusion, which does not rely on direct estimation
050   of the score of conditional densities. Instead, at each step of the trajectory, it modifies the score
051   according to the distance between the sample and the target conditional distribution in a feature space.
052   Importantly, the score and the feature vector are represented by the same neural network learned
053   by minimizing a single denoising loss. The feature vector is defined as spatial averages of selected
      layers of the score network. This shared representation provides a Euclidean embedding of all class

      Several methods have have been developed to learn representations in conjunction with diffusion
079   models (Preechakul et al., 2022; Mittal et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Hudson et al., 2024). In
080   general, these models use a separate network to map images into a form that can be used to control a
081   diffusion network. Training these models can be difficult, due to mixed-network architectures, and
082   use of objective functions with combined denoising and other losses. Although they have proven
083   successful, in terms of image generation quality, or to transfer of the learned representation to other
084   tasks, the Euclidean metric of the embedded space has not been related to properties of underlying
085   probability distributions.
086   In this work we propose an algorithm that samples a class conditional density by guiding the score
087   diffusion with a feature vector, which is driven towards the class centroid in the feature space.
088   This is illustrated in Figure 1. It computes a projected score at each step such that the trajectory
089   samples from the conditional density. We show numerically that the learned features concentrate
090   in the neighborhood of their centroid within each class. We also verify that the centroids of feature
091   vectors define a Euclidean embedding of the associated conditional probability, and are thus separated
092   according to the distance between these conditional distributions. As a result, we find that this
093
      feature-guided sampling procedure can accurately sample from the target conditional probability
      density, without degradation of quality or diversity. Both training and synthesis are stable. We show
094
      that for Gaussian mixtures, the method recovers distributions which closely match each Gaussian
095
      component. Finally, we demonstrate that the Euclidean embedding allows sampling of conditional
096   probabilities over new classes obtained by a linear combination of the feature vectors of two classes.

      2   BACKGROUND
100
      Sampling by score diffusion. Sampling using score diffusion (more precisely, reverse diffusion) is
101
      computed by reversing time in an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck equation, initialised with a sample x drawn
102
      from probability density p(x). At each time t the diffusion process computes a noisy xt with a
103   Gaussian probability density pt = N (e−t x, σt2 I). At large time T , xT is nearly a Gaussian white
104   noise. One can recover x from xT by reversing time T to 0 using a damped Langevin equation:
105                                                                √
                                     −dxt = xt + 2s(xt ) dt + 2dwt                                    (1)
106
107   where s is a drift term and wt is a Brownian noise. If s(xt ) = ∇xt log pt (xt ) is the score of pt
      then this score diffusion equation transports Gaussian white noise samples into samples of p. To

      implement a score diffusion, the main difficulty is to estimate the score ∇xt log pt . However, there is
109   a considerable freedom to choose the drift term s(xt ) (Albergo M., 2023). We will later leverage this
110   degree of freedom.
111

114

115   and Miyasawa (1961):

123   Conditional sampling. Suppose that we have a dataset of independent samples {xi , yi }i≤n where
124   xi is an image and yi is a label which may correspond to a discrete class or a continousRattribute. These
125   are samples of a probability density that is a mixture of conditional densities: p(x) = py (x) p(y)dy,
126
      where py (x) = p(x|y) is the conditional probability of x given y, and hence of the samples of class
      y. Let py,σ (xσ ) = pσ (xσ |y) be the probability density of noisy samples at a certain noise level
127
      xσ = x + σz over all x in class y and z ∼ N (0, Id). Samples of py can be generated using a score
128
      diffusion algorithm if one has estimates of the scores ∇xσ log py,σ (xσ ) for all σ. Bayes’ rule gives
129
130                        ∇xσ log pσ (xσ |y) = ∇xσ log pσ (y|xσ ) + ∇xσ log pσ (xσ ).                     (4)
131
      It is thus tempting to use this equation to compute the conditional score by augmenting the uncondi-
132
      tioned score (second term on right) with an estimate of the gradient of the log-likelihood (first term).
133   This approach relies on estimating the likelihood from data at all noise levels. In practice, one might
134   employ neural network classifiers trained on clean data to estimate the likelihood. This however intro-
135   duces an error because the likelihood function depends on noise level, and thus pσ (y|xσ ) ̸= p(y|xσ ).
136   As a result, the correct likelihood of y for noisy data cannot be computed by evaluating the likelihood
137   function p(y|.) on noisy data. This problem has been addressed by training classifiers on noisy data.
138   However, in practice, obtaining a good estimation of the likelihood at all noise levels has been chal-
139   lenging. In particular, samples drawn using this algorithm are low in quality and often do not belong
140   to the correct class Dhariwal & Nichol (2021). This problem persists with classifier-free guidance Ho
141
      & Salimans (2022), where the likelihood gradient is computed with the score network, and a weight
      ω > 0 is chosen to emphasize the log-likelihood term (1 + ω)∇xσ log p(y|xσ ) + ∇xσ log pσ (xσ ).
142
      Such algorithms generate high quality images Dhariwal & Nichol (2021); Ho & Salimans (2022)
143
      but they do not correctly sample the conditional distribution, drawing instead from a desnity of
144   reduced diversity. Even in the simplified case of Gaussian mixtures, the conditional density errors
145   are significant, as proven and demonstrated numerically in Chidambaram et al. (2024); Bradley &
146   Nakkiran (2024).

      3    F EATURE -G UIDED S CORE D IFFUSION
150
      We present a method for learning and sampling from conditional distributions without direct likelihood
151
      estimation. Instead, we augment the score of the mixture distribution with a projection term that
152
      operates over learned feature vectors, that serves to push diffusion trajectories toward the density of
153   the desired conditional distribution.
154
155
      Trajectory dynamics for a Gaussian mixture. The dynamics of score diffusion for mixture of
156   densities has been studied in Biroli et al. (2024). When the underlying p(x) is simply a mixture of
157   Gaussians with low rank covariance, the score diffusion of this mixture can be described in roughly
158   three phases as illustrated in Figure 1. Initially, σ is large and xσ is dominated by the Gaussian white
159   noise, so its probability distribution is nearly Gaussian and trajectories are nearly identical for all
160   classes y. At some noise variance, which is dependent on the distance between the means of the
161   mixture components, the density becomes multi-modal and the trajectories separate. Once trajectories
      are separated, they fall into the basin of attraction of a single component density py and converge to

163   because the other components have a negligible effect on ∇xσ log pσ .

211   Projected score. In the Gaussian mixture case, we described an augmentation of the score with a
212   forcing term that is linear in the deviation my − xσ . For mixtures of complex probability distributions,
213   we choose to adjust the score with an analogous forcing term that operates in the embedding space:
214   e = ϕy − ϕ(xσ ). We define ϕ using the activations within the same neural network that computes
215   the score, which allows us to make use of nonlinear representational properties of the score network
      (Xiang et al., 2023), and to jointly optimise s and ϕ. This shared parameterization is crucial to

216
      ensure that the embedding arises from the same features that represent the score of the conditional
217   distribution, which in turn renders the embedding space Euclidean in relation to the probability space.
218   Specifically, for images, the components of ϕ(xσ ) are defined as spatial averages of activations
219   of a selected subset of layers of the deep neural network that computes s(xσ ) (see Appendix A
220   for architecture diagram). Activation layer averages are close to the first principal component of
221   the network channels whose values are all positive, thus capturing a significant fraction of their
222   variance. This feature vector is translation-invariant (apart from boundary handling), and has far
223   fewer dimensions than the image. It can thus be considered a bottleneck. The fact that the same
224   network is used to compute s and ϕ is a critical aspect of our algorithm which sets it apart from
225
      previous approaches for score-based representation learning.
226   We define s(xσ , ϕy − ϕ(xσ )) by multiplying each component of ϕy − ϕ(xσ ) with a learned factor
227   and adding it to the corresponding activation layers of s(xσ , 0). For multiplicative factors smaller
228   than 2, the selected activation layers of s(xσ , ϕy − ϕ(xσ )) have an average closer to ϕy . Learned
229   factors are often close to 1, which sets averages to ϕy . In this case, the operation can be interpreted
230   as a projection in the embedding space, and thus we refer to s(xσ , ϕy − ϕ(xσ )) as the projected
231
      score. This procedure, of matching the means of channels to those associated with a target density, is
      inspired by methods used for texture modeling and synthesis (Portilla & Simoncelli, 2000).
232
233   At high noise levels this projection or contraction drives the dynamics toward the class y, as shown
234   in Figure 1. In this regime, ϕ(xσ ) has significant fluctuations which carry little information about
235   x. Projecting it to ϕy reduces these fluctuation and uses the conditioning information to push the
236   transport toward py . At the final steps of the dynamics, when the noise level is small, we have xσ ≈ x.
237

263   consequence of optimization at small noise and separation is due to optimization at high noise levels.

      The convergence of feature vectors within classes does not guarantee separations of their centroids.
271   This is a major challenge in representation learning known as "collapse". This pathological case is
272   avoided thanks to loss minimization at high noise levels. This is a regime where conditioning can
273   reduce the loss below the mixture model loss. The high level of noise obfuscates image features
274   such that xσ becomes high probability under classes other than y. So, if loss minimization results
275   in θ such that ϕ(x′ ) approximates ϕy , projected score leads to a better estimate of x, hence a lower
276   denoising loss. Therefore, at high noise we expect improved performance relative to the mixture
277   distribution, whereas at small noise we expect to achieve similar performance. This requires that the
278   ϕy of different classes have a separation of the order of the separation of conditional densities. The
279
      separation of the ϕy thus depends on the separations of the py . It leads the optimization to define ϕy
      providing a Euclidean embedding of the py .

481
482   We presented a feature guided score diffusion method for learning a family of conditional densities
483   from samples. A projected score guides the diffusion in a feature space where the conditional densities
484   are concentrated and separated. Both the projected score and the feature vectors are computed on
485   internal responses of a deep neural network that is trained to minimize a single denoising loss.
      When conditioned on the feature vector associated with a target class, a reverse diffusion sampling

504   algorithm based on the projected score transports a Gaussian white noise density to the target
505   conditional probability following a trajectory that differs from that of the true conditional score.
506   We demonstrate this numerically by showing that denoising performance remains below that of
507   the optimal conditional denoiser. Nevertheless, a diffusion algorithm based on the projected score
508   provides an accurate sampling of conditional probabilities, which is demonstrated for Gaussian
509   mixtures and by testing the quality and diversity of synthesized images. We also verify that the feature
510
      map provides a Euclidean embedding of corresponding conditional probabilities, which allows us to
      interpolate linearly between classes in the feature space.
511
512   Our method is novel, but bears some similarity to several others in recent literature, each of which
513   aim to learn a density (or at least, a diffusion sampler) conditioned on an exemplar from a class. Most
514   of these are significantly more complex to train than our network, relying on multiple interacting
515   networks, often with multiple-term objectives. Ho & Salimans (2022) introduced classifier-free
516
      guidance, in which the score estimates of a conditional diffusion model are mixed with those of an
      unconditioned diffusion model. They were able to obtain high-quality samples, but the likelihood
517
      term in the conditional model over-biased the conditional sampling, resulting in a mismatch to the
518
      conditional density. Our method avoids this problem, as seen in the Gaussian example of Section 5.3.
519   The Diffusion-based Representation Learning method (Mittal et al., 2023) uses a separate labeling
520   network whose output is used to guide a denoiser. The two networks are jointly trained to minimize
521   a combination of denoising error and the KL divergence of the label distribution with a standard
522   Normal (similar to objectives for variational AutoEncoders). Trained networks showed success in
523   recognition, but properties of the learned conditional density were not examined. Subsequent work
524   (Wang et al., 2023) augmented the DRL objective with an additional mutual information term. And
525   finally, (Hudson et al., 2024) the SODA combines three networks: an image encoder, a denoiser, and
526   a bridge network that maps the encoding into gains and offsets that are used to drive the conditioning
527
      of the denoiser. The entire model is trained on a single denoising loss, and generates images of
      reasonable quality, but the properties of the learned density and embedding space were not analyzed.
528
529   Guiding score diffusion with projected scores raises many questions. The embedding space of our
530   current model relies on feature vectors constructed from channel averages. This is a natural choice of
531   summary statistic, especially for images drawn from stationary sources. However, Figure 4 shows that
532   many of these channels, in the first and last layers, are not providing much benefit in differentiating
533
      classes. This suggests that they could be eliminated, further reducing the dimensionality. The
      construction of the feature vector from alternative linear projections of channel responses may
534
      also provide a useful generalization for capturing spatially varying properties of image classes.
535
      Finally, an outstanding mathematical question is to understand the accuracy of stochastic interpolants
536   (Albergo M., 2023) obtained with projected scores, and how it relates to feature space properties.
