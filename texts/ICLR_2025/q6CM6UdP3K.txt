                                                  A BSTRACT
014            Wind energy stands out as a promising clean and renewable energy alternative, not
015            only for its potential to combat global warming but also for its capacity to meet the
016            ever-growing demand for energy. However, analysis of wind data to fully harness
017
               the benefits of wind energy demands tackling several related challenges: (1) Cur-
               rent data resolution is inadequate for capturing the detailed information needed
018
               across diverse climatic conditions; (2) Efficient management and storage of real-
019
               time measurements are currently lacking; (3) Extrapolating wind data across spa-
020            tial specifications enables analysis at costly-to-measure, unobserved points is nec-
021            essary. In response to these challenges, we introduce a modality-agnostic learning
022            framework utilizing implicit neural networks. Our model effectively compresses
023            a large volume of climate data into a manageable latent codec. It also learns
024            underlying continuous climate patterns, enabling reconstruction at any scale and
025            supporting modality transfer and fusion. Extensive experimental results show con-
026            sistent performance improvements over existing baselines.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The escalating challenge of climate change necessitates immediate and strategic action to mitigate
031   its impacts and steer towards sustainable development (Rolnick et al., 2022; Kaack et al., 2022). As
032
      the Earth’s climate experiences higher temperatures, rising sea levels, and more extreme weather
      events, the transition to renewable energy sources becomes inevitable. Wind energy, in particular,
033
      offers a notable solution with its ability to deliver a clean power supply while greatly cutting down
034
      greenhouse gas emissions (Stengel et al., 2020; Ramesh et al., 2022b). However, the deployment
035   and optimization of wind energy face several challenges:
036
037    1. Resolution: Identifying the most suitable sites for wind turbines necessitates data with a reso-
038       lution as detailed as 1 square kilometer or finer (Irrgang et al., 2021; Kashinath et al., 2021).
039       However, most wind farm simulations do not achieve this resolution, limiting our capacity to
040       enhance the efficiency of wind energy farms.
041    2. Data Storage: As the granularity of simulated data and the accumulation of field measurements
042       increase (Klöwer et al., 2021; Huang & Hoefler, 2023), the resulting growth in data size requires
043       advanced storage solutions.
044    3. Generalization: Setting up wind measurement stations in specific locations can be challeng-
045       ing due to the high costs of transportation and maintenance. This necessitates cross-altitude
046       inference, such as estimating high-altitude wind speeds from ground-level measurements.
047
      Advancements in deep learning present promising solutions to these challenges. Techniques such as
048   deep learning-based super-resolution can enhance low-resolution data, providing the detailed repre-
049   sentations needed for precise analysis (Vandal et al., 2017; Gao et al., 2022; Diaconu et al., 2022).
050   Additionally, deep learning-based data reduction can compress extensive datasets into latent for-
051   mats, reducing memory and hardware demands. However, most existing deep learning approaches,
052   like convolutional neural networks and autoencoders, are grid-based and fail to offer a continuous
053   representation of wind fields (Nguyen et al., 2023; Requena-Mesa et al., 2021). Due to the inher-
      ently continuous nature of wind fields, there is a crucial need for methodologies that can generate


                                                        1

054
      and work with continuous data representations (Reichstein et al., 2019; Luo et al., 2023; 2024b).
055   Additionally, recent advancements in multi-modal deep learning necessitate its use in the efficient
056   and comprehensive analysis of wind data, including the examination of wind patterns at multiple
057   altitudes (Summaira et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2023).
058
      In this paper, we present a novel deep learning model designed for the efficient dimension reduc-
059
      tion and continuous reconstruction of multi-altitude climate data. Our approach utilizes modality-
060
      agnostic implicit neural networks within an encoder-decoder (transfer)-decoder (continuous re-
061   constructor) framework to process multi-altitude climate data. The encoder segment of the
062   three-dimensional implicit neural network functions as a nonlinear data compressor, and the de-
063   coder (transfer) segment functions as a modality transfer network that exploits inter-altitude data re-
064   lationships to improve feature extraction. The decoder (continuous reconstructor) employs implicit
065   neural representations to reconstruct continuous fields. Overall our contributions are as follows:
066
067       • We design a novel, parameter-efficient deep encoder-decoder (transfer)-decoder (continuous
068
            reconstructor) framework for simultaneous dimension reduction and continuous reconstruction
            via super-resolution of multi-altitude climate data.
069
070       • We design a three-dimensional implicit neural network for transforming data from one modal-
071         ity/altitude to another modality/altitude. Due to this structure of our designed implicit neural
072         network, our proposed model is scalable to datasets consisting of a large number of modalities,
073
            unlike traditional multi-modal deep learning models which face severe scalability issues.
074       • We employ Gaussian Adaptive Attention Mechanism (Ioannides et al., 2024) in the de-
075         coder (transfer) segment of our proposed three-dimensional implicit neural network as a
076         parameter-efficient alternative to regular query-key-value based attention mechanisms (Vaswani
077         et al., 2017), which to the best of our knowledge has not been tested in super-resolution task.
078       • We employ a Kolmogorov-Arnold Network (Liu et al., 2024) as a superior alternative to tra-
079         ditional multi-layer perceptron neural network (Hornik et al., 1989) within the state-of-the-art
080         Local Implicit Image Function (LIIF) (Chen et al., 2021) based decoder (continuous reconstruc-
081         tor), which also has not been employed in data reduction or super-resolution tasks.

083   2     R ELATED W ORK
084
085   Climate Downscaling is a critical process in climate science, allowing for the translation of global
086   climate model outputs into finer, local-scale projections. The main methods for downscaling can
087
      be categorized into dynamical downscaling and statistical downscaling (Keller et al., 2022; Harder
      et al., 2023). Dynamical downscaling, while comprehensive, demands significant computational
088
      power and depends on the accuracy of the global climate model data (Chau et al., 2021; Chen
089
      et al., 2022). Statistical downscaling, in contrast, is more computationally efficient and faster but
090   assumes historical relationships will persist, potentially missing changes in climate variability and
091   extremes (Groenke et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2020).
092
093
      Alternatively, Deep Learning-based Super-resolution techniques are revolutionizing the enhance-
      ment of climate data, delivering unparalleled detail and precision in climate models and remote
094
      sensing imagery. Different deep neural networks specialize in analyzing both spatial and tempo-
095
      ral climate data’s complex patterns, enhancing model accuracy and details beyond what traditional
096   downscaling achieves (Vandal et al., 2017; Requena-Mesa et al., 2021; Stengel et al., 2020; Gao
097   et al., 2022; Diaconu et al., 2022). However, these methods often rely on fixed resolutions, high-
098   lighting the need for models that offer resolution-independent, continuous climate pattern represen-
099   tations (Luo et al., 2023).
100
      Implicit Neural Representation (INR) uses neural networks to model continuous signals, tran-
101
      scending traditional discrete methods like pixel and voxel grids (Xie et al., 2022; Huang & Hoefler,
102   2023). This approach has recently made significant strides in climate data analysis, enabling high-
103   resolution reconstructions beyond fixed enhancement scales. In Luo et al. (2024b), a context-aware
104   indexing mechanism was introduced to enhance the efficiency of INR in reconstructing fields from
105   sparse observations. In Schwarz et al. (2023), a novel compression algorithm is introduced, utilizing
106   INR within a universal approach to data handling, that effectively generates compact yet compre-
107   hensive latent depictions of ERA5 climate data. These advancements highlight INR’s capability in
      precise, scalable data representation. In the meantime, Neural Operator and INR have been applied

108
      for solving PDEs and enhancing spatio-temporal resolution (Li et al., 2021; Kovachki et al., 2021).
109   INR-based PDE solvers like MeshfreeFlowNet (Esmaeilzadeh et al., 2020) and MAgnet (Boussif
110   et al., 2022) reconstruct continuous spatio-temporal data from sparse or discrete low-resolution in-
111   puts but lack focus on cross-spatial or cross-temporal reconstruction.
112
      Deep Multi-modal Learning transforms machine perception by concurrently integrating diverse
113
      data sources such as text, images, audio, and video, unlike of traditional single-modal approaches.
114
      Multi-modal deep learning has shown impressive performance across different domains (Ngiam
115   et al., 2011; Jing et al., 2021; Ramesh et al., 2022a; Boussioux et al., 2022; Tu et al., 2022; Cao
116   & Gao, 2022; Ruan et al., 2023). In Qayyum et al. (2024), a novel multi-modal deep learning
117   method is introduced for simultaneous dimension reduction and continuous cross-altitude recon-
118   struction. However, as the number of modalities increases, the aforementioned multi-modal deep
119   learning models encounter scalability issues, highlighting the need for a parameter-efficient multi-
120   modal model capable of facilitating cross-altitude inference.

330   6.2   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
331
      Dataset: To evaluate the continuous reconstruction capability of our proposed method, we created
332
      a dataset for multi-modal super-resolution tasks using simulated wind data. From 61368 instances,
333
      we randomly sampled 1500 data points from various timestamps at heights of 10m, 60m, 160m and
334   200m. We used 1200 data points for training and 300 for testing. We used bicubic interpolation to
335   generate a pair of discrete and continuous high-resolution samples for each instance. For example,
336   if the discrete input dimension is (120 × 160) and the super-resolution scale is 2.5×, the contin-
337   uous output dimension is (300 × 400). During training, the continuous high-resolution sample is
338   created by cropping from the actual (1500 × 2000) resolution data and then generating the discrete
339   high-resolution sample via bicubic interpolation. The discrete high-resolution dimension was set to
340   (120 × 160), with super-resolution scales ranging from 1× to 3×. To avoid randomness during test-
341   ing, both continuous and discrete high-resolution samples were generated by bicubic interpolation

      from the actual (1500 × 2000) data.

527   7   C ONCLUSION
528
529   We have developed an innovative deep learning approach for simultaneous continuous super-
530   resolution, data dimensionality reduction, and multi-altitude learning for climatological data. We
531
      designed a three-dimensional implicit neural network specifically for learning continuous, rather
      than discrete, representations of multi-altitude velocity fields used for wind farm power modeling
532
      across the continental United States. Unlike traditional multi-modal deep learning models, which
533
      handle only a limited number of modalities due to scalability issues, our three-dimensional implicit
534   neural network is scalable to large number of modalities. We employed two very recently proposed
535   techniques: Gaussian adaptive attention mechanism and Kolmogorov-Arnold network to design
536   our model and modify existing models. Experimental results have shown promising potential in
537   improving wind energy assessment for electricity generation, efficient large data storage via dimen-
538   sionality reduction, and extrapolation to inaccessible spatial areas. Both Gaussian adaptive attention
539   and Kolmogorov-Arnold networks have the potential to enhance interpretability in machine learning
      models, making this an area for future research.

903       • GEI-LIIF: Unlike our proposed approach, the baseline GEI-LIIF method is not scalable to
904         higher number of data modalities. Even though the GEI-LIIF method can be designed to work
905         with 4 modalities, optimizing such a GEI-LIIF model is an infeasible approach. Instead, we
906         trained 4 different models, each trained with 2 modalities, where the pairs are formed with data
907
            from altitudes: (10m, 160m), (10m, 200m), (60m, 160m), and (60m, 200m).

909         sampling for downscaling the discrete high-resolution input data to discrete low-resolution rep-
910
            resentations and then employ a GINO model (Li et al., 2024) for continuous reconstruction from
            the low-resolution representations. The GINO model takes the low-resolution representations
911
            and their corresponding three-dimensional coordinate points (altitude as the third axis) as its
912
            input and predicts the output at target three-dimensional coordinate points.
