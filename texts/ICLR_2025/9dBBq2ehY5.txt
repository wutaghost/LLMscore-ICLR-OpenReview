009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               We derive the implicit bias of Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) Adversarial
012            Training (AT). We show that a phase transition in the loss structure of as a func-
013            tion of the adversarial budget ϵ manifests as Catastrophic Overfitting (CO). Be-
014            low a critical threshold ϵc , single step methods efficiently provide an increase in
015            robustness, while above this critical point, additional PGD steps and/or regulariz-
016            ation are needed. We show that high curvature solutions arise in the implicit bias
017            of PGD AT. We provide analytical and empirical evidence for our arguments by
018            appealing to a simple model with one-dimensional inputs and a single trainable
019            parameter, where the CO phenomenon can be replicated. In this model, we show
020
               that such high curvature solutions exist for arbitrarily small ϵ. Additionally, we
               can compute the critical value ϵc in single-step AT for bounded parameter norms.
021
               We believe our work provides a deeper understanding of CO that aligns with the
022
               intuition the community has built around it.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Machine Learning models are not by default robust to small corruptions in their input (Szegedy
029   et al., 2014; Goodfellow et al., 2015). Adversarial Training (AT) (Madry et al., 2018) and its variants
030   have proven to be one of the most effective strategies towards achieving adversarially robust models
031   (Croce et al., 2020). Formulated as a min-max problem, Danskin’s theorem can be invoked to
032
      solve AT via an alternating maximization-minimization procedure (Danskin, 1966; Latorre et al.,
      2023). Solving the inner maximization problem (usually with Projected Gradient Descent (PGD))
033
      considerably slows down training, leading practitioners to use single-step approaches (Shafahi et al.,
034
      2019; Wong et al., 2020).
035
036   Nevertheless, when solving the inner maximization problem with a single (FGSM) step, AT presents
037   a critical failure mode known as Catastrophic Overfitting (CO) (Wong et al., 2020; Andriushchenko
038
      and Flammarion, 2020), where for larger adversarial budgets ϵ the model overfits to be 100% robust
      to (weak) single-step adversaries while being 0% robust to (strong) multi-step adversaries.
039
040   It has been observed that multi-step AT leads to locally linear loss landscapes in the neighborhood
041   of the training points (Andriushchenko and Flammarion, 2020). This has motivated the use of reg-
042   ularization methods to enforce local linearity and avoid CO in single-step AT (Qin et al., 2019;
043   Moosavi-Dezfooli et al., 2019; Abad Rocamora et al., 2024). Despite the success of these methods
044
      and the efforts in understanding CO (Li et al., 2020a; Andriushchenko and Flammarion, 2020; Kim
      et al., 2021; Ortiz-Jimenez et al., 2023; He et al., 2023), little is known about why multi-step AT
045
      converges to locally linear solutions or which is the underlying phenomenon resulting in CO.
046
047   This work aims to fill this gap by connecting the empirical observations with a theoretical framework
048   which allows us to quantitatively understand the onset of CO in AT.
049   To do so, we characterize the implicit bias of multi-step AT and link its terms with the well known
050   high curvature and the appearance of CO (Andriushchenko and Flammarion, 2020). We construct a
051   simple example where CO can be fully characterized and linked to a phase transition with a critical
052   value ϵc above which CO appears. In this example, we demonstrate the voracity of our results in a
053   controlled setting, where we explicitly find ϵc . Furthermore, in this setting, we can show the failure
      modes of PGD AT, where CO can appear for arbitrarily small ϵ by increasing the curvature of the

      2.2   S INGLE - STEP AT AND C ATASTROPHIC OVERFITTING
112
113   In order to alleviate the computational overhead introduced by solving the inner maximization prob-
114   lem, Shafahi et al. (2019) suggest alternating some maximization and minimization steps in a single
115   batch and reducing the number of epochs. This is equivalent to moving line 8 in Algorithm 1 in-
116   side the PGD loop. Alternatively, a single-step (FGSM) attack (Goodfellow et al., 2015) can be
117   employed. This is equivalent to setting S = 1 and α = 1 in Algorithm 1.
118   Unfortunately, FGSM training often results in the so called Catastrophic Overfitting (CO) phe-
119   nomenon (Wong et al., 2020). We define CO as follows:

122   CO phenomenon is characterized by a high PGD accuracy and low robust accuracy:

151   Although these methods avoid CO in practice, there is no clear explanation on why multi-step AT
152   converges to locally linear loss landscapes. In this work, we lay the groundwork towards answering
153   this question.
154
      Several works have tried to explain the appearance of CO. Kim et al. (2021) argue the loss landscape
155
      becomes "distorted" when CO appears, meaning that the original point x and the FGSM adversarial
156   example xFGSM are well classified. But, there exist misclassified points closer to x, even in the
157   convex combination of x and xFGSM . He et al. (2023) analyze CO from the "self fitting" perspective
158   and argue that during FGSM training, the network encodes features into the gradient, relying less on
159   the features of the image for classification and resulting in CO. He et al. (2023) also show that CO
160   can happen in multi-step AT under large step sizes (α in Algorithm 1). Ortiz-Jimenez et al. (2023)
161   conclude that CO can be induced by discriminative features that are relevant for classification, but
      not robustness.

      Even though we can heuristically understand the potential factors leading to CO, the current theory

      is incomplete and not predictive, i.e., given a dataset, a network and AT hyperparameters, we cannot
164   predict if CO will appear, or even quantitatively analyze it in an instructive way. In this work,
165   we are able to understand this behavior in the language of implicit regularization, as well as fully

519   6                   C ONCLUSION
520
521
522   In this work, we contribute to a deeper understanding of catastrophic overfitting in adversarial train-
523   ing by linking its appearance with a phase transition, where a CO solution attains a lower loss than
524
      the robust solution for ϵ larger than a critical value ϵc . To do so, we derive the implicit bias of PGD
      AT and show that high negative curvature solutions are characteristic of CO and can appear even in
525
      multi-step PGD. We propose a toy model where the phase transition can be fully analyzed and the
526
      high curvature solutions can be analytically obtained. We additionally provide a scaling argument
527   for how the value of ϵc can change by re-scaling the dataset.
528
529   Future work and limitations: Our theoretical insights from the toy model show that by constraining
530
      the parameter norm, we can avoid high curvature solutions with CO, see Corollary 4.3. Nevertheless,
      this result does not necessarily extend to deep, more complex models.
531
532   Based on our implicit bias analysis, avoiding high curvature solutions is key to avoiding CO.
533   Moreover, our experimental results in Appendix F.3 and the results of Singla et al. (2021) indic-
534   ate that architectures which have intrinsically low curvature can avoid CO. This motivates the need
535   of a theory for linking CO with curvature in deep models.
536   In this work, we conduct our theoretical and experimental analysis without noise prior to the PGD
537   attack, i.e., σ = 0 in Algorithm 1. Using σ > 0 has been shown to help increase ϵc with S = 1
538   (Wong et al., 2020; de Jorge et al., 2022). In multi-step AT, σ = ϵ is used in practice (Madry et al.,
539   2018). We believe that understanding the behavior of Algorithm 1 with σ > 0 is an interesting
      avenue and our insights with σ = 0 can foster such developments.
