                                                  A BSTRACT
013
014
015
               In conventional Federated Learning approaches like FedAvg, training a global
               model becomes challenging in the presence of data heterogeneity. To address this,
016
               Personalized Federated Learning (PFL) has emerged as a leading solution, enabling
017
               clients to train personalized models that are tailored to local data distributions.
018            Surprisingly, our linear probe experiments reveal that FedAvg’s feature extractor
019            outperforms most PFL methods on local client data. Even more intriguingly, apply-
020            ing a simple linear transformation to align local features from FedAvg’s extractor
021            with the classifier enables FedAvg to surpass most PFL methods. These findings
022            suggest that in data heterogeneity scenarios, FedAvg’s weaker performance is not
023            due to inadequate global model training but rather a mismatch between local fea-
024            tures and the classifier. This observation motivates us to develop a new framework
025            to address this mismatch problem. A straightforward solution would be to insert
026
               the personalized linear transformation layer mentioned above between the global
               feature extractor and the global classifier. However, this approach can easily overfit
027
               the limited local training data due to the large number of personalized parameters,
028
               and it is insufficient for handling complex datasets. In this paper, we introduce
029            FedPFT, which leverages personalized prompts to resolve the mismatch problem.
030            These prompts, along with local features, are fed into a shared self-attention-based
031            module, where features are transformed via the attention mechanism to align with
032            the global classifier. These prompts consist of minimal trainable parameters, reduc-
033            ing the risk of overfitting to local data. Additionally, this prompt-driven approach
034            offers strong flexibility, allowing for task-specific prompts to integrate additional
035            training objectives (e.g., contrastive learning) to further enhance performance. Our
036            experiments demonstrate that FedPFT outperforms state-of-the-art methods by
037
               up to 5.07%, with additional improvements of up to 7.08% when collaborative
               contrastive learning is introduced.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
042
043   Federated Learning (FL) enables clients to collaboratively train a global model without sharing their
044
      raw data. A major challenge in FL is data heterogeneity, where data across clients is not independently
      and identically distributed (non-IID). This issue results in degraded performance of the global model
045
      trained in conventional FL methods such as FedAvg McMahan et al. (2017).
046
047   To address this issue, Personalized Federated Learning (PFL) has been proposed, which allows clients
048   to train personalized models to fit their local data distribution better. Many existing PFL methods
049   achieve personalization by personalizing parts of the global model. For example, FedPer Arivazhagan
050
      et al. (2019) personalizes classifiers, FedBN Li et al. (2021b) personalizes BN layers, AlignFed
      Zhu et al. (2024) personalizes feature extractors, and FedCAC Wu et al. (2023) selects parameters
051
      susceptible to non-IID effect for personalization.
052
053   While these methods show substantial improvements over the global model, an interesting observation
      emerged from our experiments: the feature extractor trained by FedAvg outperforms those in

      To address the mismatch problem during training, we introduce a novel PFL method, FedPFT. While
102   inserting a personalized linear layer between the shared feature extractor and shared classifier is a
103   straightforward approach, it can easily overfit the limited local training data due to the large number
104   of personalized parameters, and it is insufficient for handling complex datasets (as demonstrated in
105   Appendix F). To overcome these limitations, we draw inspiration from prompt technology Jia et al.
106   (2022), which uses prompts to guide model behavior. FedPFT incorporates personalized prompts with
107   minimal trainable parameters and a shared self-attention-based feature transformation module (FTM).
      Both the prompts and local features are fed into the FTM, where features are transformed via the

      attention mechanism. In each round, FedPFT first trains the prompts to align local features with the
109   global classifier. Subsequently, training the model parameters based on this alignment enhances the
110   synergy between the feature extractor and classifier. The results in Table 1 demonstrate that FedPFT
111   not only resolves the mismatch problem but also improves the quality of the feature extractor.
112
      Another advantage of our designed prompt-driven FTM is its strong flexibility across different tasks.
113
      It can leverage task-specific prompts to incorporate various tasks beneficial for client collaboration,
114
      such as contrastive learning Wang et al. (2023), feature alignment Zhou et al. (2024); Xu et al. (2023),
115   etc. Taking contrastive learning as an example, as shown in Table 1, FedPFT+Con further improves

      Our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
119
             • We identify that the global model’s inadequate performance in non-IID scenarios is primarily
120            due to the mismatch between local features and the classifier. We show that the reason
121            personalizing some parameters improves performance is that it indirectly alleviates this
122            issue. This insight offers a new perspective for future PFL approaches to better address the
123            non-IID problem.
124          • We propose a new PFL framework that incorporates a prompt-driven feature transformation
125            module to align local features with the global classifier. This approach not only resolves the
126            mismatch problem but also provides flexibility for incorporating various collaborative tasks
127            to further enhance PFL performance.
128          • Our experiments on multiple datasets and non-IID scenarios (including both label shift and
129            feature shift) demonstrate the superiority of FedPFT, outperforming state-of-the-art methods
130            by up to 5.07%. When further incorporating contrastive learning tasks, this improvement
131            can reach up to 7.08%.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
134
135
      PFL is an effective approach to address the challenges of non-IID data in FL. There is a surge of
136   methodologies within PFL, with parameter decoupling methods gaining significant attention due to
137   their simplicity and effectiveness. For a more detailed summary of other categories of PFL methods,
138   please refer to Appendix B.
139
      Parameter decoupling methods aim to decouple a subset of parameters from the global model
140
      for personalization. Approaches such as FedPer Arivazhagan et al. (2019), FedRep Collins et al.
141
      (2021), and GPFL Zhang et al. (2023) focus on personalizing the classifier. In contrast, methods like
142   LG-FedAvg Liang et al. (2020) and AlignFed Zhu et al. (2024) advocate for the personalization of
143   the feature extractor. Additionally, FedBN Li et al. (2021b) and MTFL Mills et al. (2021) propose
144   personalizing batch normalization (BN) layers within the feature extractor. Techniques employing
145   deep reinforcement learning Sun et al. (2021) or hypernetworks Ma et al. (2022) have been used
146   to determine which specific layers to personalize. The recent FedCAC Wu et al. (2023) method
147   advances this by introducing a metric for parameter-wise selection.
148   These decoupling methods indirectly help alleviate the mismatch issue within the global model by
149   allowing local parameter adjustments. For instance, personalized classifiers involve local adjustments
150   to the classifier to better match the local features extracted by the global feature extractor. However,
151   these methods do not completely resolve the mismatch issue during training. Moreover, personalizing
152   parameters often reduces the extent of client information exchange, which can diminish the overall
153   quality of the feature extractor, thereby limiting the potential benefits of PFL.

      Each training round of each client i in FedPFT include five key steps:
181
182
              1. Client downloads the global models, including feature extractor ϕ, FTM τ , and classifier hκ .

532
533
      We observe that the feature extractor from FedAvg surpasses those in most PFL methods, yet it suffers
534   from inadequate performance due to a mismatch between the local features and the classifier. This
535   mismatch issue not only impacts the performance during model inference but also affects the synergy
536   between the feature extractor and the classifier during training. We propose a new PFL method called
537   FedPFT with a prompt-driven feature transform module to address these issues during training. Our
538   experiments demonstrate that FedPFT not only resolves the mismatch issue but also significantly
539   improves the quality of the feature extractor, achieving substantial performance gains compared to
      state-of-the-art methods. We discuss the limitations and our future work in Appendix O.

694   and primarily discusses parameter-decoupling methods that rely on the global model.
695   In addition to the aforementioned methods, a new category based on prompts has recently emerged.
696
697   Prompt-based methods. Recently, prompt technology has garnered widespread attention in the
698   fields of computer vision Jia et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2024) and natural language processing Lester
699   et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2021). This technology involves using prompts as inputs to guide the
700   behavior or output of models, typically for fine-tuning purposes. The domain of PFL has also seen
701   the emergence of prompt-based approaches. Most of these are based on pre-trained models, aiming to
      train prompts to fine-tune the pre-trained models to fit client-local data, as seen in pFedPG Yang et al.


                                                           13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      (2023), SGPT Deng et al. (2023), FedOTP Li et al. (2024), and FedAPT Su et al. (2024). pFedPT Li
703   et al. (2023a) trains both the model and prompts, using prompts at the input level to learn personalized
704   knowledge for fine-tuning the global model to adapt to the client’s local distributions. Our FedPFT
705   fundamentally differs from these methods in its objective. Rather than fine-tuning, we introduce
706   prompts to guide feature transformations to align with the global classifier, thereby addressing the
707   mismatch issue inherent in the global model during the training process.

798   D.2                       I NTRODUCTION TO C OMPARATIVE M ETHODS
799
800   FedAMP Huang et al. (2021) is a weighted-aggregation-based method where clients with similar
801
      data distributions are given higher aggregation weights during model aggregation. Because it mainly
      encourages the collaboration of clients with similar data distribution, it is a method that pays more
802
      attention to the local data distribution of clients. FedPer Arivazhagan et al. (2019), FedRep Collins
803
      et al. (2021), FedBN Li et al. (2021b), FedRoD Chen & Chao (2022), and FedCAC Wu et al. (2023)
804   are parameter-decoupling-based methods, which personalize the global model by retaining certain
805   parameters locally based on FedAvg. FedRoD additionally introduces a balanced global classifier to
806   obtain assistance from other clients, alleviating the overfitting issue caused by personalized classifiers
807   alone. pFedSD Jin et al. (2022) and pFedGate Chen et al. (2023) are fine-tuning-based methods that
808   adapt the global model to local data through fine-tuning. pFedSD directly fine-tunes the global model
809   by distilling local models, while pFedGate trains an additional gating network and applies it to the
      global model. pFedPT Li et al. (2023a), a prompt-based method, can also be viewed as a fine-tuning
