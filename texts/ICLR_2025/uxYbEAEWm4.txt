                                                 A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               We present a visual tuning framework, Knowledge Lift Alignment Fine Tuning
               (KLAFT), which enhances the expressive image captioning capabilities of Pre-
011
               trained Language Models (PLMs), including LLMs and VLMs. As this task in-
012
               volves generating more detailed and comprehensive captions than basic image
013            descriptions, the core idea behind KLAFT is that fine-grained alignment could
014            exploit the capabilities of PLMs and a given target domain dataset. This idea mo-
015            tivates and challenges us to explore the framework that deeply understands both
016            given images and text for this alignment and tuning PLMs towards expressive
017            image captioning. This motivation modifies the attention mechanism to a Mod-
018            ified Attention Mechanism (MAM) and develops both a Topic Control Mecha-
019            nism (TCM) and their training objectives. The innovation of KLAFT lies in its
020            approach to addressing the disparities in knowledge - visual versus textual via
021
               MAM and source versus target domain via TCM. As these hidden spaces are con-
               ceptualized as distinct sub-networks within the PLM, each possessing specific
022
               knowledge, KLAFT’s unique contribution is in aligning and adjusting the weights
023
               of these sub-networks in a fine-grained manner, and fine-tuning this PLM. Our
024            empirical studies demonstrate that KLAFT significantly improves expressive cap-
025            tioning tasks by aligning and amplifying target knowledge, with the potential for
026            Parameter-Efficient fine tuning (PEFT) at low computational cost.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Our goal is to create descriptive and informative captions that address the limitations of models re-
032   lying solely on visual features. In the field of computer vision, transformer-based models Vaswani
033   et al. (2017); Devlin et al. (2019); Radford et al. (2019); Yang et al. (2019); Liu et al. (2019); Lan
034   et al. (2020); Touvron et al. (2023); AI@Meta (2024) have demonstrated significant potential as
035   PLMs, including Vision-Language Models (VLMs) Liu et al. (2023a), as demonstrated by various
036   studies. However, these models often overlook additional knowledge individuals use when gener-
037   ating captions, beyond the inherent content of the data Chen et al. (2019). Source-domain datasets
038   are sufficient for acquiring linguistic and source-domain knowledge but inadequate for learning
039
      target-domain knowledge. This robust prior knowledge may overshadow target domain-specific
      knowledge, rendering it ineffective. Interestingly, the robust prior knowledge that enhances the lin-
040
      guistic abilities of PLMs may also overshadow the target domain-specific knowledge, rendering it
041
      ineffective Ramasesh et al. (2021).
042
043   To guide PLMs in generating expressive descriptions, we introduce an adaptation framework called
044   Knowledge Lift Alignment Tuning (KLAFT). Unlike existing Knowledge Distillation methods Hin-
045
      ton et al. (2015), knowledge injection approaches Xu et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023), LoRA Hu
      et al. (2022), and adapters Hu et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2024), KLAFT perceives hidden spaces as
046
      sub-networks within PLMs and aligns the weights of these sub-networks from the source to the tar-
047
      get domain through fine-grained knowledge alignment and lift. This approach modifies the attention
048   mechanism to a Modified Attention Mechanism (MAM) and develops a Topic Control Mechanism
049   (TCM) with specific training objectives. KLAFT uses the concept of topics to bridge the ”knowledge
050   differences” in visual versus textual data via MAM, and source versus target domain by exploiting
051   the hidden spaces within PLMs via TCM. The novelty of KLAFT lies in using knowledge to quan-
052   tify the domain gap and introduce it into fine-tuning. To leverage knowledge, KLAFT aligns and
053   adjusts the weights of these sub-networks in a fine-grained manner, fine-tuning the PLM towards
      expressive image captioning. This framework not only enhances knowledge adoption and tuning

133   3.1   BASIC IDEAS , D EFINITIONS , AND A RCHITECTURE
134
135
      In image captioning, the absence of robust metrics for the automatic and quantitative evaluation of
      fine-grained granularity in captions is a notable gap. We posit that qualitative evaluations serve as
136
      accurate reflections of the knowledge inherent in the target domain, and that fine-grained models,
137
      as exemplified in Table 1, could generate detailed captions. As shown in Figure 1(left), the source
138   data is used to train PLMs, while the target data is utilized to fine-tune these PLMs. To manage
139   the disparity between both domains, we implement domain adaptation tuning, which can provide
140   further insights such as the distribution of tokens used.
141
      KLAFT, seq2seq Cornia et al. (2020); Huang et al. (2019); Chen et al. (2022) and prefix-based
142
      image captions Mokady et al. (2021); Tsimpoukelli et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2022b), as shown in
143
      Figure 1(center left), comprise a vision encoder, which converts an image into visual information,
144   and a text decoder. KLAFT focuses on the sub-networks within PLMs, each of which encapsulates
145   specific knowledge influencing token distribution and token embedding representation.
146
147
      KLAFT’s concept is the ”knowledge differences”, which can be source vs target domain and visual
      vs textual, can be bridged by fine-grained alignment, thereby enhancing the explanatory power of
148
      the generated captions. This idea motivates the design of a Mapping Layer (MaL) and a Modified
149
      Attention Mechanism (MAM) for knowledge alignment, as illustrated in Figure 1(center right).
150   We interpret knowledge as hidden representation h∗,∗ , and domain-specific knowledge as domain-
151   specific tokens. We employ latent discrete variables to detect hidden representations as sub-networks
152   and define their weight in the PLM. This approach maintains a global statistical view akin to topic
153   models and adapts the PLM by adjusting this weight to match the target data.
154
      To generate expressive descriptions, we design a Topic Control Mechanism (TCM) to emphasize
155
      the target domain-specific knowledge. Since the motivation for this framework is its application
156   to diverse PLMs, we place both MAM and TCM between the top layer of the Transformer blocks
157   and the LM Head. TCM distinguishes this knowledge from the rest, making it more accessible
158   for exploitation in training tasks such as Token Topic Modeling (TTM), Masked Region Modeling
159   (MRM), and Text Image Matching (TIM). This mechanism allows KLAFT to highlight the target-
160   related knowledge, the knowledge lift. By employing these tasks and different masks shown in
161   Figure 1(right), KLAFT guides PLMs to generate texts that encompass all elements (i.e., visual,
      source, and target), thereby reflecting the target knowledge in expressive text.

184   3.3   M ODIFIED ATTENTION M ECHANISM (MAM)
185
      To clarify the differences between visual and textual knowledge on the textual level of given image-
186   text pairs, we modify a self-attention mechanism, MAM, to control the attention between visual
187   features and text as illustrated in Figure 1(right). The text decoder is responsible for generating the
188   next token of the output caption, conditioned on both previously generated tokes and visual tokens,
189   HI , converting the text sequence into the text data, and feeding it, H0 = [e1 , · · ·, e|x| ], to the next
190   layer. It yields the final output, Hl ∈ Rdh ×|x| , textual knowledge, like the vision encoder, where dh ,
191   and |x| are the hidden dimension, and the number of textual tokens, respectively. While visual tokens
192   and textual tokens can influence other tokens like cross-attention, they should exercise influence only
193   with tokens of the same type like self-attention. In order to balance this influence, MAM unifies two
194   different attention masks, Mc , Ms ∈ R(Ipf +|x|)×(Ipf +|x|) , as illustrated in Figure 1(right).
195
      This balance is automatically controlled by the gate matrix, Bc ∈ R(Ipf +|x|)×(Ipf +|x|) , to set the
196
      relative strengths of the vision encoder layer, HI , and text decoder layer, HT (=HL ), on top of each
197   layer. We gain the query, key, value, Q, K, V, from HI ⊕ HT like other Transformer models, and
198   define MAM using masks shown in Figure 1(right) as:

218   TCM is designed to highlight the target domain instead of overwriting the parameters of PLMs. Un-
219   like PEFTs, TCM is placed just below the token verbalizer to model the uncertainty in the generative

221   placement ensures that TCM captures the contextual interdependencies between tokens.

507   6   D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATIONS
508
509   As shown in Figure 1, MAM ensures that KLAFT can share the space between images and texts
510   while bridging the differences between visual information and textual information. TCM explicitly
511   extracts this information, reducing the semantic differences between source and target data, and
512   better reflects the target data in text decoding. Table 3 and 4 support the conclusion that our fine-
513   grained framework guides PLMs to bridge the knowledge differences, while Table 3 implies that
514   KLAFT can output fine-grained captions. These results imply that these captions cannot be obtained
515   by token-level substitution. Its advantages are to 1) support various image object detection networks
516
      in PLMs, as shown in Figure 1, and 2) make the best use of both knowledge while freezing PLMs.
517   KLAFT, its components (TCM, MAM), and their objective functions (TTM, MRM, ITM) apply to
518   other multi-modal tasks consisting of a combination of other type data (e.g., audio-text), without
519   losing generality, since they successfully fill the knowledge differences while retaining the capabil-
520   ities of PLMs without limiting the domain. Determining the appropriate topic number involves the
521   use of nonparametric Bayesian methods, and will be one of our future works.
522   We defined rT CM to confirm the effectiveness of TCM, and the subnetworks defined in Tables 2 and
523   4 were used, as well as in the quantitative evaluation. Future work is needed to develop generally
524   available data sets and quantitative indicators that can be evaluated without the burden and bias for
525   evaluators in qualitative assessment of expressive image captions, as shown in Tables 1 and 3.
526   The proposed method has limitations related to the proximity of the data sets and the challenge
527   of qualitative evaluation. It is not very effective when the target and source data sets are similar,
528   meaning that the knowledge is similar. Additionally, it is difficult to find an appropriate data set for
529   caption evaluation with explanatory power. For instance, requesting enough evaluators for medical
530   images is difficult to ensure statistical significance, and the reproducibility of experiments is low.
531
532   7   C ONCLUSION
533
534   We proposed a fine-grained framework, KLAFT, that fine-tunes PLMs toward generating expressive
535   image captions. The novelty of KLAFT is in focusing on the knowledge differences and closing
536   them through both knowledge alignment and lift. Unlike traditional PEFT methods, KEIC dynam-
537   ically leverages sub-networks within PLMs, resulting in superior expressive and knowledge-rich
538   captions. Experiments show that KLAFT contributes to 1) bridging differences by interpreting the
539   fine-grained interaction and knowledge alignment, and 2) generating fine-grained captions by high-
      lighting the target domain knowledge inherited from PLMs using knowledge lift.
