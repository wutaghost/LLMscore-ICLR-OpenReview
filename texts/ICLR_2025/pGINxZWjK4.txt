009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep learning methods rely on massive data, resulting in substantial costs for stor-
012            age, annotation, and model training. Coreset selection aims to select a represen-
013            tative subset of the data to train models with lower cost while ideally performing
014            on par with the full data training. State-of-the-art coreset selection methods use
015            carefully-designed criteria to quantify the importance of each data example us-
016            ing ground truth labels and dataset-specific training, then select examples whose
017            scores lie in a certain range to construct a coreset. These methods work well
018            in their respective settings, however, they cannot consider candidate data that are
019
               initially unlabeled. This limits the application of these methods, especially so con-
               sidering that the majority of real-world data are unlabeled. To that end, this paper
020
               explores the problem of coreset selection for unlabeled data. We first motivate
021
               and formalize the problem of unlabeled coreset selection, which reduces annota-
022            tion requirements to enable greater scale relative to label-based coreset selection.
023            We then develop an unlabeled coreset selection method, Blind Coreset Selection
024            (BlindCS), that jointly considers overall data coverage on a distribution as well as
025            the relative importance of each example based on redundancy. Notably, BlindCS
026            does not use any model- or dataset-specific training, which increases coreset gen-
027            eralization and reduces computation relative to training-based coreset selection.
028            We evaluate BlindCS on four datasets and confirm the advance over several state-
029            of-the-art methods that use labels and training, leading to a strong baseline for
030
               future research in unlabeled coreset selection. Notably, the BlindCS coreset for
               ImageNet achieves a higher accuracy than previous label-based coresets at a 90%
031
               prune rate, while removing annotation requirements for 1.15 million images. We
032
               will make our code publicly available with the final paper.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   The computational cost to train a single state-of-the-art deep learning model in various fields doubles
039
      every 3.4 months in the deep learning era due to increasingly large models and datasets (Amodei
      et al., 2018; Zhao & Bilen, 2023). Since the introduction of AlexNet (Krizhevsky et al., 2012),
040
      groundbreaking models in computer vision like ViT and DALLE all rely on massive datasets for
041
      training (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Ramesh et al., 2022). However, there are substantial costs to col-
042   lecting, storing, transmitting, and pre-processing such a vast amount of data. Furthermore, training
043   models on vast datasets introduces yet another substantial cost for computation, sometimes hun-
044   dreds of thousands of GPU hours to achieve satisfactory performance, which frustrates applications
045   requiring repeat training over datasets such as hyparameter optimization (Maclaurin et al., 2015;
046   Lorraine et al., 2020) and neural architecture search (Elsken et al., 2019; Li & Talwalkar, 2020).
047
      Coreset selection deals with large data to mitigate the above issues for data-efficient deep learning.
048   Specifically, coreset selection reduces the training set size by selecting a pruned subset that contains
049   only valuable examples (the core set), such that models trained on the coreset achieve similar per-
050   formance to those trained on the original, full dataset (Feldman et al., 2011). Several recent works
051   provide various coreset selection methods using carefully-designed criteria, including median class
052   values (Xia et al., 2023), diverse coverage of importance scores (Zheng et al., 2023), and gradient
053   dynamics during training (Zhang et al., 2024), which achieves 53.91% accuracy on ImageNet with
      only 10% training data.

080   State-of-the-art coreset selection methods have demonstrated impressive results in experiment set-
081   tings. However, the current SOTA methods assume the full dataset is labeled and available for
082   training prior to coreset selection. Regarding labels, it is important to acknowledge that the majority
083   of real-world data are, in fact, unlabeled, preventing coreset consideration for label-based methods.
084   Furthermore, labeling massive amounts of image data just to consider selection is cost prohibitive,
085   with annotation taking anywhere between 7 s per bounding box to 1.5 hours for full semantic seg-
086
      mentation (Jain & Grauman, 2013; Cordts et al., 2016). Some innovative coreset selection methods
      use self-supervised learning in place of label-based training (Sorscher et al., 2022); however, this ap-
087
      proach will still have substantial time and computation costs to select coresets at scale. Furthermore,
088
      coupling coreset selection with training on a single model architecture decreases generalization.
089
090   To that end, this paper addresses the problem of coreset selection without labels or training using a
091   novel approach. First, we formulate the problem of unlabeled coreset selection, which reduces data-
092
      and label-based costs by generating coresets from unlabeled data. After coreset selection from the
      larger dataset, labels are only used by the actual model to train on the pruned dataset. Notably, if
093
      coreset selections are for self-supervised training, no labels are used. Second, we use the unlabeled
094
      coreset selection formulation to develop Blind Coreset Selection (BlindCS), a method which also
095   reduces computation costs by selecting coresets without training on the candidate dataset. Instead,
096   BlindCS uses off-the-shelf models to generate a candidate selection embedding space, which is then
097   iteratively sampled and scored to estimate the value of each example’s value based on coverage of
098   the embedding space and redundancy within the coreset (see Figure 1).
099
      Our contributions are as follows:
100
101         1. We motivate and formalize the problem of unlabeled coreset selection, which substantially
102            reduces data- and label-based costs for efficient deep learning at scale.
103
104
            2. We develop our Blind Coreset Selection method (BlindCS), which is computationally ef-
               ficient and uses novel estimates of dataset distribution coverage and redundancy to select
105
               coresets from larger, unlabeled datasets, enabling broader application.
106
107         3. We evaluate BlindCS against state-of-the-art label- and training-based coreset selection
               methods with eight baselines on four different datasets spanning three orders of magnitude

124   2   R ELATED W ORK
125
126
      Dataset Distillation is similar to coreset selection in that it comprises many innovative methods
127   for data-efficient deep learning. On a functional level, the objectives of many coreset methods also
128   apply to dataset distillation, however, as opposed to selecting a subset of existing data for a coreset,
129   dataset distillation aims to generate a much smaller dataset with synthetic examples that yield the
130   same performance as the larger initial dataset (Yu et al., 2024). Notable dataset distillation methods
131   generate synthetic examples relative to the initial dataset by matching gradients (Zhao et al., 2021),
132   differentiable Siamese augmentation for better synthesis (Zhao & Bilen, 2021), aligning features
133   (Wang et al., 2022), multi-step parameter matching (Cazenavette et al., 2022), and embedding space
134   distribution matching (Zhao & Bilen, 2023). These dataset distillation methods are remarkable for
135
      their creation of small but effective synthetic training datasets. On the other hand, our current work
      focuses on evaluating and selecting coresets from existing real-world data.
136
137   Active Learning is another active research area with many contributions to data-efficient deep learn-
138   ing. The goal of active learning is to enable learning algorithms to perform better with less training
139   by letting them choose their own data (Settles, 2012), which is especially useful in cases where large
140   portions of data are unlabeled and manual labeling is expensive (Bernard et al., 2018). In fact, ac-
141
      tive learning encompasses the particularly hard problem of starting selection with no initial labeled
      examples, i.e., the cold start problem (McCallum & Nigam, 1998). Notably, some recent active
142
      learning methods focus on the importance of coverage diversity in data selection (Ash et al., 2020;
143
      Citovsky et al., 2021). However, these methods actively train and select data on an increasing set for
144   a specific model, which is not conducive for model-agnostic, one-shot coreset selection.
145
146   Coreset Selection prunes datasets down to a smaller, valuable core set to reduce costs and enable
147
      more data-efficient deep learning. A basic solution to find the optimal coreset is to search through
      and train on every subset to find the best corresponding model performance. However, this simple
148
      approach is NP-hard, which has led to the development of many innovative coreset selection meth-
149
      ods. Early coreset methods generally expect a consistent data distribution to the original dataset
150   (Feldman et al., 2011; Bachem et al., 2015), e.g., Welling (2009) greedily adds one sample at a time
151   to match embedding space centers. Other coreset methods can be broadly categorized as select-
152   ing by optimization (Wei et al., 2015; Yang et al., 2023), coverage or diversity (Sener & Savarese,
153   2018; Zheng et al., 2023), and importance criteria (Toneva et al., 2019; Tan et al., 2023). Recent
154   coreset innovations address ongoing challenges such as application on a wide range of dataset sizes
155   (Xia et al., 2023), making selections on data with label errors (Park et al., 2023), and fully utilizing
156   training dynamics (Zhang et al., 2024).
157   Our current work is inspired by the success of this previous coreset selection work. However, a
158   drawback for current state-of-the-art coreset selection methods is requiring labels and/or training on
159   the larger initial dataset (see Table 1). Thus, in this paper, we focus on extending coreset selection to
160   unlabeled data without any requirements for dataset- or architecture-specific training. This broadens
161   general applicability to new data and models while reducing costs associated with annotating data
      with ground truth labels, sensitivity to label errors, and extensive computation at scale.

      We define the problem of labeled coreset selection for data-efficient deep learning. Formally, we
165   are given a labeled dataset SL = {(xi , yi )}N i=1 with N examples drawn i.i.d. from an underlying
166   distribution P , where xi are the data and yi is the ground truth label for each example. The goal is
167   to select a subset of SL to reduce future storage and training consumption while closely maintaining
168   performance of full dataset training. We denote this coreset as SC = {(xi , yi )}ni=1 ⊂ SL , which has
169   n examples and a prune rate of (1−n)N . We formulate coreset selection as (Sener & Savarese, 2018):
170

      4     U NLABELED C ORESET S ELECTION
178
179
      We define the problem of unlabeled coreset selection for data- and label-efficient deep learning.
180   Formally, given an unlabeled dataset S = {(xi )}N                                   C
                                                             i=1 , the goal is to select S ⊂ S without using
181   any ground-truth label yi . The motivation for this change is that it is preventative to label an entire
182   massive dataset when much of the data will be pruned. We formulate unlabeled coreset selection by
183   replacing SC ⊂ SL with SC ⊂ S in Equation (1). Notably, after selecting SC , we add n labels to the
184   coreset as SC = {(xi , yi )}ni=1 only to train the pruned model f(SC ) .
185
      Along with the aforementioned benefits of coreset selection, unlabeled coreset selection uniquely
186   increases scale and reduces labeling costs. First, while we can use any xi from a labeled dataset
187   SL , we can also extensibly sample and consider more examples x from the underlying distribution
188   P without any annotation or labeling requirements. This extension enables us to source coresets
189   from a much larger initial dataset. In effect, unlabeled coreset selection extends dataset pruning to
190   the majority of unlabeled, real-world data. Second, we only label the n coreset examples after they
191   are selected for pruned model training, so there is a N − n reduction in labeling costs relative to
192   label-based coreset selection. As one specific example, using unlabeled coreset selection at a 90%
193   prune rate on ImageNet removes label requirements for 1.15 million images.

195   5     M ETHODOLOGY
196
197   Using the unlabeled coreset selection formulation, we develop a new method of “Blind” Coreset
198   Selection (BlindCS). In place of label- or training-based selection, BlindCS alternatively uses an off-
199   the-shelf model embedding space representation of the initial dataset (Section 5.1). BlindCS then
200   samples the embedding space to determine which examples provide valuable coverage (Section 5.2).
201   Subsequently, BlindCS determines which examples in proximity to those providing coverage are
202
      redundant (Section 5.3). Finally, BlindCS uses the coverage and redundancy metrics to iteratively
      sample and score each candidate training example to determine final coreset selections (Section 5.4).
203

341   6.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
342
343   Datasets. We evaluate the effectiveness of Blind Coreset Selection (BlindCS) on four image clas-
344   sification datasets: CIFAR10 (Krizhevsky, 2009), CIFAR100, ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009), and
345   EuroSAT (Helber et al., 2019). We compare the full training and coreset size across each dataset in
346   Table 2. Notably, full dataset sizes span from 1.3 M to 2,700 examples and coreset sizes span from
347   896,817 to 270 examples (three orders of magnitude). EuroSAT has no explicit training set, so we
348
      create “four” datasets using 80/20, 40/60, 20/80, and 10/90 training/validation splits to experiment
      with dataset scale in the same distribution of satellite images.
349
350   Network Training. We use two different network models and training regimes to evaluate coresets.
351   For CIFAR10, CIFAR100, and EuroSAT, we train a ResNet18 model on selected coresets for 200
352   epochs with a batch size of 128. For ImageNet, we alternatively train a ResNet32 model for 60
353   epochs with a batch size of 256. Following the protocol of Zhang et al. (2024), we use an SGD
354
      optimizer with momentum 0.9, weight decay 0.0005, and a learning rate of 0.1 that decays with
      the cosine annealing scheduler via PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019). After model training, we use the
355
      model’s validation accuracy to quantitatively evaluate coreset selection performance.
356
357   BlindCS & Baselines. We implement BlindCS using the Section 5 formulation with constant pa-
358   rameter settings across all datasets and prune rates. We compare BlindCS against the current state-
359   of-the-art using eight methods. BlindCS is the only method that does not use ground truth labels and
360
      dataset training aside from Random, which selects examples with uniform random sampling. En-
      tropy selects examples with high entropy of predicted probabilities at the end of training (Coleman
361
      et al., 2020). Forgetting selects examples that change to being misclassified after correct classifica-
362
      tion the most times during training (Toneva et al., 2019). EL2N selects examples with high gradient
363   magnitude using the L2 norm of error vectors (Paul et al., 2021). AUM selects examples with high
364   area under the margin, i.e., the probability gap between between the target class and the next largest
365   class across all epochs (Pleiss et al., 2020). Moderate selects examples closest to the median class
366   value in the full dataset trained model embedding space (Xia et al., 2023). Dyn-Unc selects ex-
367   amples with high target class probability variance during training (He et al., 2024). Finally, TDDS
368   selects examples with high projected gradient variance across many epochs (Zhang et al., 2024).
