009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Previous research endeavors have sought to tailor BERT-family for non-
012            autoregressive generation tasks through task-specific fine-tuning, and more re-
013            cent work has also attempted to evolve them into instruction followers after in-
014            struction tuning, but their generation quality, even with more powerful iterative
015            decoding methods, continues to lag behind the competitive autoregressive (AR)
016            models. Furthermore, these progress rely on additional fine-tuning procedures,
017            while the generative potential of the BERT-family without fine-tuning still re-
018            mains unexplored. Hence, we delve deeper into the key issues leading to the
019
               performance gaps of BERT-family in generation tasks and put forth innovative so-
               lutions. Specifically, existing studies often overlook the significance of the train-
020
               ing sequence decomposition format. Unlike autoregressive models that naturally
021
               decompose text sequences in a left-to-right fashion during both training and in-
022            ference, BERT-family are trained using a random decomposition approach (i.e.,
023            randomly masking the texts) but strive to identify an optimal composition (re-
024            ferred to decoding paths) during inference. To alleviate this mismatching, we
025            introduce a path selection method to expand the search space during inference,
026            facilitating the discovery of more suitable compositions. Additionally, we pro-
027            pose path selection*, which further integrates path selection into the training pro-
028            cess, enabling the model to learn preferences for specific paths. Our experiments
029            across a range of zero-shot common sense reasoning and reading comprehension
030
               tasks and several task-specific generation tasks showcase the substantial perfor-
               mance enhancements for the BERT-family using the proposed methods, reaching
031
               the performance levels that are on par with, and in some cases surpassing the AR
032
               pre-trained models. Our models and code will be publicly accessible on GitHub.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   In recent years, the progress of large language models, such as Llama (Touvron et al., 2023a;b),
039
      Gemini (Team et al., 2023), and GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023), has revolutionized the natural language pro-
      cessing (NLP) tasks, demonstrating remarkable capabilities across a diverse range of applications.
040
      The majority of these models follow a decoder-only autoregressive architecture, drawing inspiration
041
      from the successful GPT series models (Radford et al.; 2019; Brown et al., 2020). Conversely, the
042   BERT-family series models (Devlin et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2019; Conneau et al., 2020; He et al.,
043   2020), which have long been recognized for their excellent performance in various language under-
044   standing tasks, appear to be progressing at a relatively slower pace recently. Researchers attribute
045   this deceleration to challenges encountered in language generation scenarios, where BERT-family
046   models struggle to achieve competitive performance when compared to autoregressive (AR) models.
047
      Previous works have delved into strategies for leveraging the BERT-family for language generation
048   tasks, particularly following the introduction of the Mask-Predict algorithm (Ghazvininejad et al.,
049   2019), which offers a promising approach for adapting BERT-family to non-autoregressive (NAR)
050   generation scenarios. Researchers have attempted to customize BERT-family for simple specific
051   generation tasks (Chan & Fan, 2019; Jiang et al., 2021; Su et al., 2021; Liang et al., 2023b;a), and
052   recent advancements have also explored the potential for BERT-family in becoming instruction fol-
053   lowers after instruction tuning (Xiao et al., 2024). Despite their efforts, performance gaps persist
      when compared to AR models. Furthermore, these endeavors need additional fine-tuning proce-

078   In this paper, we delve deeper into the key issues leading to the performance gaps of BERT-family
079   in generation tasks and put forth innovative solutions. One of the central challenges may stem from
080   the sequence decomposition format. As shown in the Figure 1, AR models naturally decompose
081   language modeling as the task of next-token prediction in a left-to-right order and leverage the
082
      corresponding composition strategy during inference. In contrast, BERT-family adopt a random
      decomposition approach during training (i.e., randomly masking several tokens) but follow certain
083
      given criteria to identify an optimal composition (denoted as decoding path in this paper) to achieve
084

085   mismatching, we propose path selection which enables BERT-family models to choose a more
086   appropriate composition from multiple candidates during inference. Additionally, we present path
087   selection*, a novel approach that incorporates path selection into the training process, which aims
088   to instruct BERT-family on learning preferences for the outputs achieved by various compositions.
089

090

091   encoder-only architecture with a bidirectional attention mechanism akin to the conventional BERT-
092   family. Additionally, in line with several current mainstream AR pre-training models (Zhang et al.,
093   2022; Black et al., 2022; Biderman et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2023), we integrate several innovative
094   techniques and adopt a more widely-used training corpus to pre-train GeBERT. Initially, we assess
095   our proposed methods across a range of zero-shot common sense reasoning and reading compre-
096   hension tasks using GeBERT without fine-tuning. Our results illustrate that our methods lead to
097   significant performance improvements compared to the baseline GeBERT model, surpassing all AR
098   baselines with comparable model parameters. Besides, we evaluate our methods on two traditional
099
      language generation tasks after fine-tuning the models. The corresponding results demonstrate the
      effectiveness of our proposed methods, with GeBERT achieving state-of-the-art performance in var-
100

      2.2   D ECODING PATHS FOR BERT- FAMILY
122
123   Formally, we consider the process of generating a sequence of discrete tokens Y = (y1 , ..., yN ),
124   where yi ∈ V , a finite vocabulary specific to a language model. This generation process can be
125   interpreted as deterministically sampling a series of successive state spaces S, where each state
126   si ∈ S corresponds to a sequence of tokens sampled from V , and relies on a policy π to transition
127   to the next state. A policy π serves as a determinate mapping from states to actions, outlining how
128   the model processes the current sequence and achieves the subsequent sequence in the next state.
129
      We denote this specific process to compose the target sequence as the decoding path P of a given
      language model, where each node represents the current state si in ith decoding step and each edge
130
      represents the policy πi indicating the actions for transitioning from state si to si+1 .
131
132   As shown in Figure 1, different language models have their specific decoding paths to compose the
133   target sequence. The traditional AR and NAR language models typically have a single decoding
134   path for composing a specific target sequence, while BERT-family can explore multiple optional
135
      decoding paths, resulting in varied output sequences of differing generation qualities. Selecting a
      specific decoding path from the multitude of optional paths available in BERT-family is crucial for
136
      achieving high-quality outputs. With approximately 2T N possible paths for a BERT-family model,
137
      as detailed in the Appendix A.2, determining the optimal path is essential for the success of these
138   models. In Ghazvininejad et al. (2019) where the Mask-Predict decoding algorithm was first pro-
139   posed, the authors heuristically regulate the policy πt in tth decoding step as predicting the masked
140   parts in current Y and selecting the specific nt tokens which are with lowest prediction probabilities
141   to be re-masked, where the number of re-masked tokens can be computed as nt = (1 − t/T ) ∗ N ,
142   N denotes the total number of tokens in Y , t and T denote the current and total decoding step,
143   respectively. While the Mask-Predict algorithm provides a heuristic approach to selecting decoding
144   paths, it may not always yield optimal results. There exist other decoding paths in the candidate
145   space leading to better composition of target sequences (Kreutzer et al., 2020). Hence, we aim to
146
      identify an optimal decoding path from such multitudinous candidates by introducing path selection
      method. Moreover, we further propose path selection* which empowers the model to learn the pref-
147
      erence between different decoding paths. Our methods seek to enhance the BERT-family’s ability
148
      to navigate through the complex decoding spaces and generate higher-quality output sequences.

232
233   Backbone Models For better evaluation of various generation tasks, we pre-train new variants of
234   BERT-family with a modified masking mechanism during training, which aims to better equip these
235   masked language models for tasks involving generation (Liang et al., 2023b; Xiao et al., 2024),
236   thus we name our model as Generative BERT (GeBERT). Specifically, unlike earlier BERT-like
237   models that only mask 15% of tokens in each instance for prediction (Devlin et al., 2018; Liu et al.,
238
      2019), we first decompose the training instance into two parts which simulates a scenario akin to
      conditional generation. Drawing inspiration from prior practice (Song et al., 2019; Li et al., 2022b;
239
      Guo et al., 2020; Xiao et al., 2023), we assign different masking methods for these two parts to
240
      enable GeBERT to learn both understanding and generation capabilities. Details of our pre-training
241   task are presented in the Appendix A.5. Based on our modified pre-training task, we pre-train two
242   versions of GeBERT containing 124M and 352M parameters which are similar to the base and large
243   versions of other previous pre-trained language models (Devlin et al., 2018; Lewis et al., 2019;
244   Raffel et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2023), denoted as GeBERT-124M and GeBERT-352M.
245
246   Pre-training Settings As for the model architecture, we follow the most practice in previous
247   BERT-like models to build an encoder-only language model with a bi-directional attention mecha-
248   nism and further incorporate several effective techniques: 1) We use Rotary Positional Embedding
249   (RoPE) (Su et al., 2024) to inject positional information into GeBERT rather than the traditional
250   absolute/relative position encoding. 2) We adopt swiglu Shazeer (2020) as our activation function
251
      rather than the traditional ReLU. For pre-training corpus, we adopt the Pile (Gao et al., 2020; Bi-
      derman et al., 2022), which is a curated collection of English language datasets containing around
252
      300B tokens and has been widely used for training language models (Zhang et al., 2022; Black et al.,
253
      2022; Biderman et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2023). During training, we set the max length as 2048 and
254   pre-train GeBERT for 150k update steps (1 epoch on the Pile) with a batch size of 1024 samples
255   (i.e., 2M tokens). We use Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2014) with a weight decay of 0.01, the
256   learning rate warms up to 6e-4 in the first 1.5k steps and then decreases gradually with cosine
257   decay strategy. We utilize the Megatron-Deepspeed 2 library to train GeBERT on 8 NVIDIA A100-
258   PCIE-80GB GPU cards. We present the details for the model and pre-training in the Appendix
259   A.6.
260
261   Fine-tuning Settings We follow the training procedure in previous works (Liang et al., 2023b;
262   Xiao et al., 2024) to fine-tune GeBERT on downstream datasets for non-autoreressive sequence
263   generation tasks. For the fine-tuning settings, we tune the learning rate from {1e-5, 2e-5, 5e-5, 1e-
264
      4} for different downstream tasks. We train for a total of 50 epochs and validate the model after each
      epoch, then obtain the final model with the best validation performance. During the training of the
265
      path selection* method, we initialize the policy and reference model with that after fine-tuning for
266
      downstream sequence generation tasks. Then, we freeze the parameters of the reference model and
267   only update the parameters of the policy model with the same dataset adopted in fine-tuning. We set
268   the learning rate as 2e-5 and other training hyperparameters the same in the fine-tuning stage. Then,

416   5.1     D ISCUSSION OF D IFFERENT M ETHODS TO D ETERMINE THE D ECODING PATHS
417
418   In Table 1, we report the performance of GeBERT which adopt the same decoding path with AR
419   models and that with our proposed path selection method. However, there still exist multitudinous
420   optional decoding paths to generate the final target sequences as mentioned in section 3.1, we adopt
421   GeBERT-124M to compare the different methods to determine the decoding paths here with the
422   following rules. Notice in Table 1, we regulate the number of newly generated tokens (denoted as
423
      nnew ) in each decoding step as nnew = 1 to keep consistent with AR models, i.e., we generate only
      one token in a left-to-right order or with the highest-k prediction probabilities in each decoding step,
424
      and adopt the total decoding steps adaptive to the target length. Then, we can (1) set nnew = 2/3/4,
425
      and the corresponding decoding steps as ⌈N/2⌉, ⌈N/3⌉, ⌈N/3⌉, where N denotes the total target
426   tokens, we denote this method as multi-token-based, (2) set the total decoding steps as T = 1/4/10,
427   and the corresponding nnew = ⌈N/1⌉, ⌈N/4⌉, ⌈N/10⌉. We denote this method as multi-step-
428   based. Besides, with nnew = 1, there still exist different rules to achieve the specific generated
429   token. (3) Rather than a left-to-right order in Table 1, we can also include a right-to-left or a random
430   order to achieve the final target sequence. Instead of selecting the token with the highest prediction
431   probability, which denoted as a easy-to-hard order (Kasai et al., 2020), we include a hard-to-easy
      order which generate the token with the lowest prediction probability first. These method can be


                                                             8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      denoted as multi-order-based. (4) We can set different beam number k in our proposed path selection
433   method, we compare k = 2/3/4 here, denoted as multi-beam-based.
434
435   We present the corresponding results with the above methods in Table 3, we can find that: (1) The
436
      performance declines as the nnew increases, indicating that setting nnew = 1 to keep consistent
      with AR models, in which the model predicting one token in each decoding step, is important to
437
      achieve competitive performance. (2) With the multi-step-based method, more decoding steps lead
438
      to better performance, which also verifies the above observation, i.e., the length of targets is less

443   while several other orders will lead to significant performance declines such as right-to-left order.

445   achieve a comparable score on average, and all outperforms the left-to-right baseline, indicating the

490   6   D ISCUSSIONS
491
492   Discussion with other non-monotonic generation models. While BERT-family adopting the
493   Mask-Predict algorithm can be viewed as a special case for non-monotonic generation, we also
494   present the discussion with other works that also explore the non-monotonic generation for lan-
495   guage models (Welleck et al., 2019; Shih et al., 2022; Shen et al., 2023). These models can also
496   generate output sequences beyond a strict left-to-right order but still differ from the BERT-family.
497
      Specifically, these models only generate one token via one decoding step, rather than adopting the
      parallel decoding paradigm allowing generating multiple tokens for BERT-family. Furthermore,
498
      BERT-family adopt the masking language modeling during training, differing from other models
499
      which adopt the variants of autoregressive modeling.
500
501
502
      Discussion with other path selection methods. In the realm of NAR translation, prior research
      has also delved into enhancing output quality by selecting the optimal result from multiple candidate
503
      generations, such as CTC-decoding (Graves et al., 2006; Shu et al., 2020; Shao & Feng, 2022) and
504
      DA-Transformer (Huang et al., 2022; Shao et al., 2022a; Huang et al., 2023). Nevertheless, these
505   methods typically adhere to a left-to-right order when sampling various candidates. Conversely,
506   our proposed path selection method eliminates order constraints, facilitating the candidates with
507   more diversity. Besides, while some studies have advocated for training models with multiple ref-
508   erences (Shao et al., 2022b; Huang et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023), these methods treat all various
509   references as equally plausible outputs and train with the same weights across them. In contrast,
510   our path selection* method incorporates positive-negative sample pairs into the training process,
511   enabling the model to learn preferences among different candidates.
512
513   Discussion with other DPO methods for AR models The Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)
514   algorithm has found widespread application in AR models (Yang et al., 2023; Rafailov et al., 2024;
515   Pal et al., 2024), facilitating the learning of preferences for various output sequences generated
516   through different sampling methods, which can significantly enhance the overall generation qual-
517
      ity. Conversely, few explorations have been conducted for BERT-family. In this paper, we propose
      path selection* to enable BERT-family to learn the preference between different outputs achieved by
518
      different decoding paths according to the generation character of BERT-family. While AR models
519
      allow different lengths of sampled positive-negative pairs, we recognize that ensuring a consistent
520   sampling length across different pairs is quite important for our methods for fair comparisons be-
521   tween different decoding paths. Another difference between our methods and those for AR models
522   is that there is no significant quality difference between our sampled pairs, which will easily lead to
523   the failure cases as mentioned in Section 3.2. Consequently, integrating penalty terms to stabilize
524   the DPO training process emerges as a crucial aspect of our methods.
525

      7   C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this paper, we pre-train a new version of BERT-family to explore the potential of BERT-family
530   for good generators. According to the specific generation formats of sequences, we propose path se-
531
      lection and path selection* methods to enhance the generation capabilities of these models. Results
      on a range of zero-shot common sense reasoning and reading comprehension tasks demonstrate that
532
      BERT-family can also achieve state-of-the-art performance by adopting our methods compared to
533
      current mainstream AR language models, and the experiments on several task-specific generation
534   tasks further verify the effectiveness of our methods, outperforming the previous pre-trained lan-
535   guage models. In overall, our works first pre-train a version of BERT-family fron scratch for fair
536   comparison with current AR pre-trained models, and further verify the very promising techniques to
537   unlock the potential of BERT-family for various generation tasks both without and with fine-tuning.
538   We hope our explorations can expand the applications of BERT-family in AI community. In the fu-
539   ture, we will further explore larger versions after observing the scaling ability from 124M to 352M
      of GeBERT, then adapt GeBERT to more type of tasks, such as code generation and math reasoning.

883   A.5    D ETAILS FOR P RE - TRAINING TASK
884
885   We denote the pre-trained task of GeBERT as generative masked language modeling, which spe-
886   cially designed to fit the BERT-family to various generation tasks. This task is modified from the
887   traditional masked language modeling (MLM) training objective, which makes the model learn to
888
      predict the specific masked tokens and has been widely used in traditional BERT-family models (De-
      vlin et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2019). GeMLM aims to build a universal pre-trained BERT-family, which
889
      simultaneously possesses the ability of language understanding and generation. Motivated by the
890
      previous explorations in the NAR translation task (Ghazvininejad et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2020;
891   Xiao et al., 2023) which extend the traditional MLM into the conditional generation scenery with
892   the encoder-decoder model structure, and those that explore the potential in encoder-only models
893   for language generation tasks (Wang & Cho, 2019; Liang et al., 2023b; Xiao et al., 2024), GeMLM
894   first decomposes each training instance into two parts and assigns different masking strategies to
895   help the model learn different capabilities. Besides, GeMLM further adopts the specific attention
896   masking mechanism to enhance the consistency between the training and inference process.
897   Specifically, as shown in figure 4, given a specific training instance with the max context
898   length L: C = {c1 , c2 , ..., cL−1 , cL }, GeMLM decomposes C into a tuple (X, Y ), where
899   X = {c1 , c2 , ...ci−1 , ci } denotes the prefix tokens, and Y = {ci+1 , ci+2 , ...cL−1 , cL } denotes the
900   suffix tokens. The prefix tokens are used to provide context information and help the model un-
901   derstand the whole sentence, we randomly sample a small ratio of mask tokens, which is similar to
902   the traditional MLM in BERT, denoted as (Xmask , Xobs ) = RANDOM MASK(X, βX ), where Xmask
903   and Xobs denote the masked and unmasked parts in X, βX denotes the masking ratio. The suffix to-
904   kens tend to help the model learn the generation capability, we adopt uniform masking as mentioned
905
      in CMLM (Ghazvininejad et al., 2019), denoted as (Ymask , Yobs ) = UNIFORM MASK(Y, βY ),
      where βY is sampled from a uniform distribution U (0, 1). Then GeMLM predicts the masked
906
      tokens based on different context.
907
908   In practice, we adopt an adaptive masking function for the masking ratio βX as mentioned in Xiao
909   et al. (2023) to replace the fixed masking ratio in the traditional MLM, as βX = 0.3 − βY ∗ 0.2. This
910   operation can achieve more diverse masking conditions in X for the model to learn and is based on
911
      the intuition that once more tokens in Y are masked, X should provide more context information
      (i.e., lower βX ). Besides, we prevent the query of each token in X attending the tokens in Y in the
912
      attention module as mentioned in figure 4 during training, which keeps consistent with the inference
913
      process since there is no target sequence in advance. Then, the final training loss of GeMLM can be

939   A.7   L IMITATIONS AND B ROADER I MPACTS
940
941   Our work demonstrates that BERT-family can perform better than AR language models by adopt
942   our proposed path selection and path selection* methods. However, these models still require multi-
943   step reasoning during zero-shot tasks to bridge the gap between inference and pre-training. This
944
      reasoning paradigm may affect the inference efficiency, making BERT-family models less effective
      than AR models in some contexts. On the other hand, this characteristic becomes an advantage in
945
      generative tasks. BERT-family with non-autoregressive generation paradigm do not need to perform
946
      sentence-length reasoning steps during decoding, leading to faster generation and reduced latency.
947   This makes BERT-family particularly well-suited for tasks where quick text generation is critical,
948   such as large-scale text generation.
