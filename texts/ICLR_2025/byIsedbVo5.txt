009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            An increasing number of high-stakes domains rely on machine learning to make
012            decisions that have significant consequences for individuals, such as in loan ap-
013            provals and college admissions. The black-box nature of these processes has led
014            to a growing demand for solutions that make individuals aware of potential ways
015            they could improve their qualifications. Counterfactual explanations (CFEs) are
016
               one form of feedback commonly used to provide insight into decision-making
               systems. Specifically, contemporary CFE generators provide explanations in the
017
               form of low-level CFEs whose constituent actions precisely describe how much a
018
               negatively classified individual should add to or subtract from their input features
019            to achieve the desired positive classification. However, the low-level CFE gen-
020            erators have several shortcomings: they are hard to scale, often misaligned with
021            real-world conditions, constrained by information access (e.g., they can not query
022            the classifier), and make inadequate use of available historical data. To address
023            these challenges, we propose three data-driven CFE generators that create gener-
024            alizable CFEs with desirable characteristics for individuals and decision-makers.
025            Through extensive empirical experiments, we compare the proposed CFE gen-
026            erators with a low-level CFE generator on four real-world (BRFSS, Foods, and
027
               two NHANES datasets), five semi-synthetic, and five variants of fully-synthetic
               datasets. Our problem can also be seen as learning an optimal policy in a family
028
               of large but deterministic Markov decision processes.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Machine learning models are increasingly used to guide consequential decision-making. Since these
034   decisions can significantly impact livelihoods, society demands the right to explanation, as stated in
035   Articles 13–15 of the European Parliament and Council of the EU (2016) General Data Protection
036   Regulation. One of the most needed explanations is how individuals (agents) can modify their state
037   (i.e., the input features to the models) to achieve a (desirable) positive classification. Counterfactual
038   explanations (CFEs) provide one such solution in the form of actionable insights (Wachter et al.,
039
      2017; Dandl et al., 2020; Mothilal et al., 2020; Ustun et al., 2019; Karimi et al., 2021; Joshi et al.,
      2019; Karimi et al., 2022). Most contemporary CFE generators like actionable recourse (Ustun
040
      et al., 2019), provide low-level CFEs, where each action specifies the precise amount by which the
041
      individual should add to or subtract from a specific feature to ensure that the new features collectively
042   result in a positive classification. For example, if an individual is classified as having an unhealthy
043   waist-to-hip ratio (WHR), one of the recommended low-level actions to help them achieve a healthier
044   WHR, as shown in Figure 1(a) blue , is to “increase selenium (mg) from 45 to 327.7319.”
045
      However, such low-level CFEs exhibit several notable shortcomings (Figure 1) that limit their effec-
046
      tiveness in practice. As we discuss in Section 2, these include a focus on precise changes to individ-
047
      ual features, which can make them difficult for a person to act upon; high computational complexity
048   that affects scalability; a reliance on access to potentially privileged information (e.g., the ability to
049   query the classifier); and a limited ability to utilize existing domain knowledge or historical data.
050   To address these limitations, we propose three novel data-driven CFE generator frameworks: hl-
051   continuous (high-level continuous), hl-discrete (high-level discrete), and hl-id (high-level identifier)
052   CFE generators (see Section 3). Each proposed CFE generator produces generalizable CFEs that
053   empower individuals to use their agency to gain capabilities that favorably transform their current
      state (features).

      The effects of the CFE’s actions on an individual’s state are explicit in hl-continuous and hl-discrete
096   CFEs, but not in an hl-id CFE. Specifically, an hl-continuous action is a signed (±) and named,
097   general predefined action that might modify several features simultaneously (e.g., action-1 ( orange )
098   in Figure 1(a) simultaneously modifies 11 features). An hl-continuous CFE is then the lowest-cost
099   set of hl-continuous actions, which is solution of a integer linear program (ILP). That is, given a
100   negatively classified individual and a set of hl-continuous actions with known costs, the goal of
101   the ILP is to find the lowest-cost subset that modifies the individual’s features to achieve a positive
102   classification. We propose a deep learning-based hl-continuous CFE generator that, given instances
103   of individuals and their corresponding hl-continuous CFEs, can quickly and accurately generate
104
      hl-continuous CFEs for new individuals without generator re-optimization.
105   On the other hand, an hl-discrete action is a binary action that specifies whether an action fulfills
106   the required capabilities for a specific feature. This formulation of actions is particularly efficient
107   in scenarios where each feature’s satisfiability is based on the feature’s respective threshold and
      can be reduced to a yes/no question. For example, in level one decision-making, e.g., wellness,


                                                                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      customer satisfaction, and compliance checks, an individual must satisfy a subset of prerequisites to
109   guide subsequent decisions. We formulate the hl-discrete CFE as a solution to a weighted set cover
110   problem. Specifically, given a set of hl-discrete actions with known costs and effects on binary state
111   features, the problem is to find the lowest-cost subset that modify the individual’s state such that
112   they become positively classified. We propose a deep learning hl-discrete CFE generator trained on
113   instances of individuals and their optimal hl-discrete CFEs (individual7→hl-discrete CFE dataset) to
114   generate hl-discrete CFEs for new individuals.
115   Lastly, an hl-id CFE is a unique identifier (or name) for a CFE. It is particularly efficient for set-
116   tings where decision-makers have minimal information access, for example, no query access to the
117   classifier, and the actions and their costs and explicit effects on the features are unknown. It is also
118   often the case that the hl-id CFE holds significant implicit information. For instance, a registered
119   dietitian might recommend the hl-id CFE, “remove gluten from the child’s diet” to a parent of a
120   child diagnosed with celiac disease to flip the diagnosis. The dietitian generates this CFE based on
121   historical patient-CFE (intervention) information, even without direct access to the celiac classifier
122   and without specifying a comprehensive list of restricted foods and their effects on relevant features.
123
      More detailed examples are provided in Section 3.3.

125   2   BACKGROUND
126
127
      We consider a binary classification setting, where an individual with state x receives either a positive
128
      (desirable) or negative (undesirable) classification under a model f (x). Although we focus on this
129   setting, our proposed CFE generation framework generalizes to other scenarios. Given an individual
130   state x with an undesirable model outcome, the objective of the CFE generator is to provide the
131   individual with information that they can act on to achieve a desirable classification under the model.
132   Contemporary low-level CFE generators, such as actionable recourse (Ustun et al., 2019), provide
133   low-level CFEs where each action in the CFE precisely specifies how much the individual should
134   add or subtract from a specific feature to ensure that, collectively, the new features (state) result in
135   the individual receiving a desirable model outcome.
136
137   The low-level CFE generator Ustun et al. (2019) proposed an ILP-based low-level CFE generator
138   (Equation 1) that generates a low-level CFE to help an individual change an undesirable model
139   outcome to a desirable one.
140                                        min cost(a; x)
141                                          s.t. f (x + a) = ŷ ⋆                              (1)
142                                               a ∈ A(x),
143
144   where ŷ ⋆ is the desired model outcome, A(x) denotes the set of feasible actions given the input
145   x, and the function cost(·; x) : A(x) → R+ encodes the preferences between these actions. When
146   Equation 1 is feasible, the optimal actions that modify the features (i.e., x+a) and lead to a desirable
147   model outcome are recommended to the individual (Figure 1(a) blue ). We refer the reader to Ustun
148
      et al. (2019) for a more detailed description and to Appendix C.1 for dataset-specific experimental
      setup and supplemental examples of this low-level CFE generator.
149
150
151   Shortcomings of low-level CFE generators We address four notable limitations of the low-level
152   CFE generators (Verma et al., 2020; Karimi et al., 2022; Barocas et al., 2020). First, they are hard
153
      to scale due to the need to solve a computationally intensive NP-hard optimization problem for each
      new agent (Karp, 1972; Karimi et al., 2022), and the CFE’s actions are overly specific (e.g., Fig-
154
      ure 1(a) blue ). Second, some assumptions about the problem structure may not hold in the real
155
      world. For instance, most assume that the CFE’s actions are in final implementable steps and that
156   each action directly modifies an individual feature, thus the need for high sparsity (few modified
157   features) and high proximity (minimal improvement). Third, if there are information access chal-
158   lenges, i.e., no access to critical information—such as the classifier data and parameters, a prediction
159   training data manifold to ensure diverse, representative and optimal CFEs, or a complete list of ac-
160   tions and their costs—contemporary CFE generation becomes infeasible, biased, or flawed. Lastly,
161   in real-world contexts, there might be data on historical mappings of individuals and their CFEs that
      the contemporary CFE generation does not adequately leverage, limiting its effectiveness.

      3     DATA - DRIVEN CFE G ENERATION
163
164
165   We propose three data-driven CFE generators of increasing generality, hl-continuous, hl-discrete,
166
      and hl-id. The proposed CFE generators work under various information access constraints, leverage
      data beyond that key to classification (e.g., classifier parameters and predictive training data) and
167
      generalize to negatively classified individuals beyond those on which the model was trained.

170   3.1   T HE HL - CONTINUOUS CFE G ENERATORS
171
172   The data-driven hl-continuous CFE generator is trained on instances of individual states paired with
173   their corresponding hl-continuous CFEs to generate respective CFEs for new individuals without
174   generator re-optimization . Our empirical results demonstrate that even a simple deep-learning based
175   hl-continuous CFE generator performs strongly at this task. In the following, we provide formal
176   definitions of hl-continuous actions and hl-continuous CFEs, while Section 4.2 includes detailed
177   descriptions of the experimental generator model architecture.
178   Definition 1. (hl-continuous action) : An hl-continuous action is a signed (±) and named, general
179   predefined action whose cost and varied effects on an individual’s input features are predefined and
180   known. For example, action-1(orange ) in Figure 1(a), “take leavening agents: cream of tartar”
181   adds nutritional values to 11 nutrients by a known amount and incurs a cost (e.g., estimated average
182   price in USD) that is known a priori.
183
      Definition 2. (hl-continuous CFE): An hl-continuous CFE is a solution to an ILP where, given
184
      a negatively classified individual state x and a set of hl-continuous actions with known costs, the
185   problem is to find the lowest-cost subset of hl-continuous actions that when taken, can favorably
186   modify the individual’s state. The ILP is of the form:

202
203
      We propose the data-driven hl-discrete CFE generator, trained on individual7→hl-discrete CFE data,
204
      to quickly and accurately produce hl-discrete CFEs for new individuals. Below, we formally de-
205

207
208   Definition 3. (hl-discrete action): An hl-discrete action is a binary vector that specifies which fea-
209   tures the action adds capabilities. For example, consider the individual state x = [0, 0, 0, 0, 1] and
210
      the hl-discrete action vj = [1, 1, 0, 0, 0]. When taken, the hl-discrete action adds capabilities to fea-

211
      the setting can be extended to more general cases.
212
213   Definition 4. (hl-discrete CFE): An hl-discrete CFE is formulated as a solution to a weighted set
214   cover problem. Specifically, the CFE is the lowest-cost subset of hl-discrete actions, each with
215   predefined costs, that a negatively classified individual x ∈ {0, 1}n (e.g., someone deemed a health
      risk) can undertake to achieve a desirable classification (e.g., no longer classified as a health risk).

      3.3   T HE HL - ID CFE G ENERATORS
232
      The hl-id CFE generator is a supervised learning model trained on an individual7→hl-id CFE dataset
233   to generate hl-id CFEs (unique CFE identifiers) for new individuals. Details on the experimental
234   model architecture are provided in Section 4.2. Typically, detailed information about the actions
235   within each hl-id CFE—including the costs and the specific effects of the actions on input features—
236   is unknown, and decision-makers cannot query the classifier. This approach is instrumental when the
237   CFE unique identifier conveys significant implicit information. For example, consider two health-
238   related scenarios: 1) an individual diagnosed with an unhealthy heart condition could receive an
239   hl-id CFE such as “cardiac rehabilitation” (Fernández-Rubio et al., 2022), without direct access
240   to the heart diagnostic classifier or specifying underlying actions (e.g., aerobics exercises); 2) an
241
      individual classified with an unhealthy weight might be assigned an hl-id CFE such as “adopt a
      ketogenic diet,” without query access to the classifier or specifying sub-actions involved or which
242
      nutrients they change and by how much (e.g., add leavening agents: cream of tartar to their diet).

      4.1   DATASETS
256
257   We conducted experiments with 4 real-world, 5 semi-synthetic, and 5 variants of fully-synthetic
258   datasets. Each of the individual7→CFE datasets (instances of individuals and their corresponding
259   CFEs) was split 80/20 for training and evaluation of data-driven CFE generators. While generaliz-
260   able to other cases, we focused on a setting where each individual in the respective individual7→CFE
261   datasets has one optimal CFE match, categorized as either hl-continuous, hl-discrete, or hl-id, de-
262   pending on the dataset considered. The following provides key details about the datasets used in the
263   experiments. Further information, including the preprocessing procedure and the specific nature of
264
      the feature representations, is available in Appendices B.1 and B.2.

      5     E XPERIMENTAL R ESULTS
348
349   In this section, we provide thorough empirical evidence to show the strong performance of our
350   generators and how and in what ways in comparison to low-level CFEs, hl-continuous, hl-discrete
351   and hl-id CFEs, might be preferable to both individuals and decision-makers.
352
353   5.1   T HE HL - DISCRETE AND HL - CONTINUOUS CFE S ARE PREFERABLE
354
355   Below and in Appendices D.1 and D.2, we provide empirical evidence to show that, compared to
356   low-level CFEs, both hl-continuous and hl-discrete CFEs involve fewer actions, lead to more diverse
357   improvements, are easier to personalize, and simplify the design and interrogation of CFE generators
358   for fairness issues. Additionally, they more accurately reflect real-world conditions.
359
360   Sparsity In low-level CFE generation, sparsity—typically defined as a small number of modified
361
      features (Verma et al., 2020)—is often a primary goal due to the presumed one-to-one relationship
      between number of actions taken and features modified. However, achieving sparsity in practice may
362
      be both undesirable and challenging because individuals often aim to implement as many changes
363
      as possible with minimal actions, and is rarely the case that each action modifies one feature (see

471   6   L IMITATIONS AND E THICAL C ONSIDERATIONS
472
473   The decision-maker must have access to data on instances of individuals and their corresponding
474   optimal CFEs to train the proposed data-driven CFE generators. Although this level of access mit-
475   igates some information access challenges—such as needing at least query access to the classifier
476
      and representative prediction training data or having an exhaustive list of actions and the associated
      costs—obtaining historical individual7→CFE data may still pose significant challenges. Future re-
477
      search could investigate techniques like federated learning and secure multi-party computation to
478
      collaboratively train robust CFE generators under varied privacy and data access constraints.
479
480   Our formulations of hl-continuous and hl-discrete CFEs restrict them to being defined as a set of
481   actions. More generally, one could consider settings where the order of actions matters, such as
482
      where a CFE corresponds to an optimal policy for an agent in a deterministic Markov decision
      process (MDP). Even more generally, one could consider actions whose effects are stochastic, and
483
      a CFE then corresponds to an optimal policy for the agent in a general MDP.
484
485   The proposed approaches to CFE generation are closely related to data-driven algorithm design.
      As a result, ethical concerns related to data-driven algorithms, for example, potentially propagating

486
      and exacerbating biases in historical individual7→CFE data and the potential for flawed resource
487   allocation, might apply to our proposed CFE generators. Future research should investigate these
488   ethical implications in greater depth.
489
490
      Although we focus on health datasets in our experiments, our approach generalizes to a broad spec-
      trum of real-world scenarios, such as college admissions, loan applications, judicial systems, and
491
      other settings. Future works could expand our setup to other data settings and informational access
492
      challenges. Lastly, we caution readers that the experimentally generated CFEs from our empirical
493   analyses are intended solely for illustrative purposes, and readers should not use them for self-
494   treatment.

497   7   R ELATED W ORK
498
499
      Our formulations for hl-continuous and hl-discrete CFEs as solutions to ILPs are in principle, similar
500
      to search-based optimization CFE generation frameworks (Ramakrishnan et al., 2019), user-specific
501
      ILP recourse approaches (Ustun et al., 2019; Cui et al., 2015; Gupta et al., 2019a), and CFE gen-
502   eration methods based on logic and answer-set programming (Bertossi, 2020; Liu & Lorini, 2023;
503   Marques-Silva, 2023). However, unlike these formulations, we focus on general, predefined ac-
504   tions that often modify multiple features simultaneously (see Figure 1), which could lead to more
505   improvement and help enhance the generalization of the CFE generation.
506
      In addition, contemporary low-level CFE generators are often computationally expensive, requiring
507
      the solution of NP-hard optimization problems for each new individual. In contrast, we introduce
508   novel data-driven CFE generators that address the question: Can we, by learning from training
509   data (i.e., instances of individuals and their optimal CFEs), develop a CFE generator that quickly
510   provides optimal CFEs for new individuals? While in some ways, similar to reinforcement learning-
511   based CFE generation tools (De Toni et al., 2023; Shavit & Moses, 2019; Naumann & Ntoutsi, 2021),
512   our proposed generators offer a more efficient, exact, and scalable alternative to their often high
513   computational and approximate solutions. Notably, our approach is closest to that of Verma et al.
514   (2022). While our method is akin to learning an optimal policy in a large but deterministic family of
515   Markov decision processes (MDPs), Verma et al. (2022) focuses on learning optimal policies within
516
      smaller, stochastic MDP settings.
517   Finally, our work also relates to data-driven algorithm design (Gupta & Roughgarden, 2016; Bal-
518   can et al., 2018; Balcan, 2020), where models trained on training data instances perform well on the
519   training data and generalize to the testing data. Unlike contemporary CFE generators that rely solely
520   on classification data (i.e., prediction training data and classifier parameters), our data-driven CFE
521   generators leverage access to individuals and their optimal CFEs and more closely mirror real-world
522
      scenarios. Our generators also excel in generating CFEs for new individuals, are more computation-
      ally efficient and scalable, and function under varied informational settings. For example, unlike
523
      other methods that require, at a minimum, query access to the classifier and knowledge of the cost
524
      and impact of each action on state features (Naumann & Ntoutsi, 2021; De Toni et al., 2023; Shavit
525   & Moses, 2019; Verma et al., 2022), our CFE generators—such as the hl-id CFE generator—can
526   effectively produce CFEs without explicit access to any of this information.

529   8   C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this work, we make a strong case for expanding the focus beyond just classification data (e.g.,
532
      classifier parameters and prediction training datasets), when automating CFE-based recourse gener-
533
      ation. Our findings show that it’s more efficient to examine, compare, and personalize the general
534   predefined actions (e.g., hl-continuous and hl-discrete actions), and they significantly enhance the
535   scalability of CFE generation. Additionally, the respective CFEs, hl-continuous, hl-discrete and hl-
536   id CFEs, compared to low-level CFEs are, in retrospect, simpler and more efficient for individuals to
537   execute while yielding more favorable outcomes for decision-makers. Through extensive empirical
538   analysis, we show that the proposed data-driven CFE generators are more scalable, computationally
539   efficient, and better aligned with real-world conditions, all while effectively leveraging data beyond
      that specific to classification. Our code is available at this: anonymized link.

1426

1427
       initial state (proximity), diversity of improvement, i.e., how many features the CFE modifies, and
1428

1435   that the benefits of low-level CFEs differ substantially across sensitive groups, potentially favoring
1436   some over others, a potential fairness issue in CFE generation.

1661
1662   Unlike ILP-based low-level CFE generators, which do not generate CFEs for individuals when the
1663   ILP solution is sub-optimal or infeasible, our data-driven CFE generators alternatively produce valid
1664   CFE mistakes when suboptimal (see Figure 15), which might be preferable in retrospect. For ex-
1665   ample, of the 1.58%, 16.23% and 37.00% mistakes the hl-id generator makes on the 20-, 50-, and
1666   100-dimensional >10 individual7→hl-discrete-id CFE datasets, 100%, 99.23%, and 87.29%, respec-
1667   tively, were valid CFE mistakes. Similarly, the majority of the mistakes of the hl-discrete CFE
1668   generators were valid, e.g., on the 20-dimensional >10 individual7→hl-discrete CFE dataset, of the
1669   10.8% mistakes the generator makes, 63.10% were valid.
1670   Additionally, the likelihood of the ILP-based low-level CFE generator’s failure at generating CFEs
1671   (i.e., returns no CFEs) increases with the number of actionable features (data dimensions). Similarly,
1672   the percentage of valid mistakes from our proposed CFE generators decreases with the frequency of
1673   CFEs in the individual7→CFE training set, e.g., the percentage of valid mistakes is 87.29% on the
       >10 dataset and 57.83% on the 100-dimensional all dataset.

1768
       We recognize several challenges faced by the proposed data-driven CFE generators: the low fre-
1769

1770

1773   D.6.1    N EGATIVELY AFFECTED BY HIGH NUMBER OF ACTIONABLE FEATURES
1774
1775   As the data dimensions (number of actionable features) increase, the number of actions in-
1776   dividuals need to take also increases. For example, 54.4% of the individuals in the 100-
1777   dimensional individual7→hl-discrete CFE dataset needed three hl-discrete actions and 0.0% needed
1778
       one hl-discrete (see Table 2). In comparison, 33.3% of the individuals in the 20-dimensional
       individual7→hl-discrete CFE dataset had one action in their CFE, and very few, only 3.6% of in-
1779
       dividuals had three actions in their hl-discrete CFEs (see Table 2).
1780

       dimension or the number of actionable features increases. The average frequency of the CFEs
