009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011             Most learning algorithms with formal regret guarantees assume that no mistake is
012             irreparable and essentially rely on trying all possible behaviors. This approach is
013             problematic when some mistakes are catastrophic, i.e., irreparable. We propose an
014             online learning problem where the goal is to minimize the chance of catastrophe.
015             Specifically, we assume that the payoff in each round represents the chance of avoid-
016             ing catastrophe that round and try to maximize the product of payoffs (the overall
017
                chance of avoiding catastrophe) while allowing a limited number of queries to a
                mentor. We first show that in general, any algorithm either constantly queries the
018
                mentor or is nearly guaranteed to cause catastrophe. However, in settings where the
019
                mentor policy class is learnable in the standard online model, we provide an algo-
020             rithm whose regret and rate of querying the mentor both approach 0 as the time hori-
021             zon grows. Conceptually, if a policy class is learnable in the absence of catastrophic
022             risk, it is learnable in the presence of catastrophic risk if the agent can ask for help.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   There has been mounting concern over catastrophic risk from AI, including but not limited to
028   autonomous weapon accidents (Abaimov & Martellini, 2020), bioterrorism (Mouton et al., 2024),
029   and cyberattacks on critical infrastructure (Guembe et al., 2022). See Critch & Russell (2023) and
030   Hendrycks et al. (2023) for taxonomies of societal-scale AI risks. In this paper, we use “catastrophe” to
031
      refer to any kind of irreparable harm. This definition also covers smaller-scale (yet still unacceptable)
      incidents such as serious medical errors (Di Nucci, 2019), crashing a robotic vehicle (Kohli & Chadha,
032
      2020), or discriminatory sentencing (Villasenor & Foggo, 2020).
033
034   The gravity of these risks contrasts starkly with the dearth of theoretical understanding of how to
035   avoid them. Nearly all of learning theory explicitly or implicitly assumes that no single mistake
036   is too costly. We focus on online learning, where an agent repeatedly interacts with an unknown
037
      environment and uses its observations to gradually improve its performance. Most online learning
      algorithms essentially try all possible behaviors and see what works well. We do not want autonomous
038
      weapons or surgical robots to try all possible behaviors.
039
040   More precisely, trial-and-error-style algorithms only work when catastrophe is assumed to be impos-
041   sible. This assumption manifests differently in different subtypes of online learning. In the standard
042   online learning model, the agent’s actions have no permanent effect on the environment.1 Online
043
      reinforcement learning allows the agent’s actions to permanently affect the environment, but typically
      assumes that either no action has irreversible effects (e.g., Jaksch et al. (2010)) or that the agent is
044
      reset at the start of each “episode” (e.g., Azar et al. (2017)). One could train an agent entirely in a
045
      controlled lab setting where the above assumptions do hold, but we argue that sufficiently general
046   agents will inevitably encounter novel scenarios when deployed in the real world. Machine learning
047   models often behave unpredictably in unfamiliar environments (see, e.g., Quionero-Candela et al.
048   (2009)), and we do not want AI biologists or robotic vehicles to behave unpredictably.
049
      The goal of this paper is to understand the conditions under which it is possible to formally guarantee
050
      avoidance of catastrophe in online learning. Certainly some conditions are necessary, because if the
051
      agent can only learn by trying actions directly, the problem is hopeless: any untried action could

055   one needn’t learn through pure trial-and-error: one can also ask for help. We think it is critical for
056   high-stakes AI applications to employ a designated supervisor who can be asked for help. Examples
057   include a human doctor supervising AI doctors, a robotic vehicle with a human driver who can take
058   over in emergencies, autonomous weapons with a human operator, and many more. We hope that our
059   work constitutes a small step in the direction of practical safety guarantees for such applications.

061   1.1    O UR MODEL
062
063   We propose an online learning model of avoiding catastrophe with mentor help. On each time step,
064   the agent observes an input, selects an action (or queries the mentor), and obtains a payoff. Each
065   payoff represents the probability of avoiding catastrophe on that time step (conditioned on no prior
066   catastrophe). The agent’s goal is to maximize the product of payoffs, which is equal to the overall
067
      probability of avoiding catastrophe by the chain rule of probability.
068   The (possibly suboptimal) mentor has a fixed policy, and when queried, the mentor illustrates their
069   policy’s action for the current input. We desire an agent whose regret – defined as the gap between the
070   mentor’s performance and the agent’s performance – approaches zero as the time horizon T grows.
071   In other words, with enough time, the agent should avoid catastrophe nearly as well as the mentor.
072   We also expect the agent to become self-sufficient over time: formally, the number of queries to the
073
      mentor should be sublinear in T , or equivalently, the rate of querying the mentor should go to zero.
074
075   1.2    O UR ASSUMPTIONS
076
077
      The agent needs some way to make inferences about unqueried inputs in order to decide when to ask
      for help. Much past work has used Bayesian inference, which suffers tractability issues in complex
078
      environments.2 We instead assume that the mentor policy satisfies what we call local generalization:
079
      informally, if the mentor told us that an action was safe for a similar input, then that action is probably
080   also safe for the current input (see Section 3 for a formal definition and further discussion). This
081   captures the intuition that one can transfer knowledge between similar situations. Unlike Bayesian
082   inference, local generalization only requires computing distances and is compatible with any input
083   space which admits a distance metric.
084
      Unlike the standard online learning model, we assume that the agent does not observe payoffs. This
085
      is because the payoff in our model represents the chance of avoiding catastrophe on that time step. In
086   the real world, one only observes whether catastrophe occurred, not its probability.3
087

      1.3    S TANDARD ONLINE LEARNING
089
090   An overview of standard online learning is in order before discussing our results. In the standard
091   model, the agent observes an input on each time step and must choose an action. An adversary then
092   reveals the correct action, which results in some payoff to the agent. The goal is sublinear regret
093   with respect to the sum of payoffs, or equivalently, the average regret per time step should go to 0 as
094   T → ∞. Figure 1 delineates the precise differences between the standard model and our model.
095
      If the adversary’s choices are unconstrained, the problem is hopeless: if the adversary determines
096   the correct action on each time step randomly and independently, the agent can do no better than
097   random guessing. However, sublinear regret becomes possible if (1) the hypothesis class has finite
098   Littlestone dimension (Littlestone, 1988), or (2) the hypothesis class has finite VC dimension (Vapnik
099   & Chervonenkis, 1971) and the input is σ-smooth4 (Haghtalab et al., 2024).
100
      The goal of sublinear regret in online learning implicitly assumes catastrophe is impossible: the agent
101
      can make arbitrarily many (and arbitrarily costly) mistakes as long as the average regret per time step
102   goes to 0. In contrast, we demand subconstant regret: the total probability of catastrophe should go
103   to 0. Furthermore, standard online learning allows the agent to observe payoffs on every time step,
104   while our agent only receives feedback on time steps with queries. However, access to a mentor (and

      1.4   O UR RESULTS
119
120   At a high level, we show that avoiding catastrophe with the help of a mentor and local generalization
121   is no harder than online learning without catastrophic risk.
122
123
      More precisely, we first show that in general, any algorithm with sublinear queries to the mentor
      has arbitrarily poor regret in the worst-case (Theorem 4.1). This means that even when the mentor
124
      can avoid catastrophe with certainty, any algorithm either needs excessive supervision or is nearly
125
      guaranteed to cause catastrophe. Unlike online learning where the general impossibility result is
126   trivial (the agent might as well guess randomly given an unconstrained adversary), local generalization
127   significantly limits the adversary’s power and necessitates a careful analysis.
128
129
      Next, we present a simple algorithm whose total regret and rate of querying the mentor both go
      to 0 as T → ∞ when either (1) the mentor policy class has finite Littlestone dimension or (2) the
130
      mentor policy class has finite VC dimension and the input sequence is σ-smooth. Our algorithm can
131
      handle a multi-dimensional unbounded input space and does not need detailed access to the feature
132   embedding, instead using two simple operations. It does need to know the mentor policy class, as is
133   standard in online learning. We initially prove the theorem for binary actions (Theorem 5.2) and then
134   reduce learning with many actions to the binary action case (Theorem C.1).
135
      Along the way, we prove that the same subconstant bound holds for standard additive regret (The-
136
      orem 5.3). Essentially, our techniques are equally effective for maximizing the sum of payoffs
137
      and the product of payoffs. We emphasize the multiplicative objective due to our motivation of
138   avoiding catastrophe, but our subconstant additive regret bound may also be of value. In summary,
139   the combination of a mentor and local generalization allows us to reduce the regret by an entire factor
140   of T , resulting in subconstant regret (multiplicative or additive) instead of the typical sublinear regret.
141
      The rest of the paper is structured as follows. Section 2 discusses related work. Section 3 formally
142
      defines our model. Section 4 presents our negative result for general mentor policies. Section 5
143
      presents our positive result for simple mentor policy classes. Proofs are deferred to the appendix.

146   2     R ELATED WORK
147
148   Learning with irreversible costs. Despite the ubiquity of irreparable/irreversible costs in the real
149   world, theoretical work on this topic remains limited. This may be due to the fundamental modeling
150   question of how the agent should learn about novel inputs or actions without actually trying them.
151   The most common approach is to allow the agent to ask for help. This alone is insufficient, however:
152   the agent must have some way to decide when to ask for help. A popular solution is to perform
153   Bayesian inference on the world model, but this has two tricky requirements: (1) a prior distribution
154   which contains the true world model (or an approximation), and (2) an environment where computing
155   (or approximating) the posterior is tractable. A finite set of possible environments satisfies both
156   conditions, but is unrealistic in many real-world scenarios. In contrast, our algorithm can handle
157   an uncountable policy class and a continuous unbounded input space, which is crucial for many
158
      real-world scenarios in which one never sees the exact same input twice.
159   Bayesian inference combined with asking for help is studied by Cohen et al. (2021); Cohen & Hutter
160   (2020); Kosoy (2019); Mindermann et al. (2018). We also mention Hadfield-Menell et al. (2017);
161   Moldovan & Abbeel (2012); Turchetta et al. (2016), which utilize Bayesian inference in the context
      of safe (online) reinforcement learning without asking for help (and without regret bounds).

      We are only aware of two papers which theoretically address irreversibility without Bayesian inference:
163   Grinsztajn et al. (2021) and Maillard et al. (2019). The former proposes to sample trajectories and
164   learn reversibility based on temporal consistency between states: intuitively, if s1 always precedes s2 ,
165   we can infer that s1 is unreachable from s2 . Although the paper theoretically grounds this intuition,
166   there is no formal regret guarantee. The latter presents an algorithm which asks for help in the form of
167   rollouts from the current state. However, the regret bound and number of rollouts are both linear in the
168   worst case, due to the dependence on the γ ∗ parameter which roughly captures how bad an irreversible
169   action can be. In contrast, our algorithm achieves good regret even when actions are maximally bad.
170   To our knowledge, we are the first to provide an algorithm which formally guarantees avoidance of
171   catastrophe (with high probability) without Bayesian inference. We are also not aware of prior results
172   comparable to our negative result, including in the Bayesian regime.

      Constrained Markov Decision Processes (CMDPs). CMDPs (Altman, 2021; Puterman, 2014)
174
      require the agent to maximize reward while also satisfying safety constraints. The two most relevant
175   papers are Liu et al. (2021) and Stradi et al. (2024), both of which provide algorithms guaranteed
176   to satisfy initially unknown safety constraints with high probability on every time step. However,
177   both papers assume that the agent knows a fully safe policy upfront. In contrast, the agent in our
178   setting has no prior knowledge. In this sense, our work complements theirs: our goal is essentially
179   to learn the baseline safe policy that their algorithms require. One can also view our problem as
180   the “pessimistic” model and their problem as the “optimistic” model, with some applications better
181   captured by our model while other applications are better captured by theirs.
182   Online learning. See Cesa-Bianchi & Lugosi (2006) and Chapter 21 of Shalev-Shwartz & Ben-David
183   (2014) for introductions to online learning. A classical result states that sublinear regret is possible
184   iff the hypothesis class has finite Littlestone dimension (Littlestone, 1988). However, even some
185   simple hypothesis classes have infinite Littlestone dimension, such as the class of thresholds on [0, 1]
186   (Example 21.4 in Shalev-Shwartz & Ben-David (2014)). Recently, Haghtalab et al. (2024) showed
187   that if the adversary only chooses a distribution over inputs rather than the precise input, only the
188   weaker assumption of finite VC dimension (Vapnik & Chervonenkis, 1971) is needed for sublinear
189
      regret. Specifically, they assume that each input is sampled from a distribution whose concentration is
      upper bounded by σ1 times the uniform distribution. This framework is known as smoothed analysis,
190
      originally proposed by Spielman & Teng (2004).
191
192   Multiplicative objectives. Although online learning traditionally studies the sum of payoffs, there is
193   some work on maximizing the product of payoffs, or equivalently the sum of logarithms (Chapter 9
194   of Cesa-Bianchi et al. (2017)). However, these regret bounds are still sublinear in T , in comparison
195
      to our subconstant regret bounds. Also, that work still assumes that payoffs are observed on every
      time step, while our agent only receives feedback in response to queries (Figure 1).
196
197   Barman et al. (2023) recently provided regret bounds for a multiplicative objective in a multi-armed
198   bandit problem, but their objective is the geometric mean of payoffs instead of the product. Interpreted
199   in our context, their regret bounds imply that the average chance of catastrophe goes to zero, while
200   we guarantee that the total chance of catastrophe goes to zero. This distinction is closed related to the
201
      difference between subconstant and sublinear regret discussed in Section 1.3.
202   Active learning and imitation learning. Our assumption that the agent only receives feedback
203   in response to queries falls under the umbrella of active learning (Hanneke, 2014). This contrasts
204   with passive learning, where the agent receives feedback automatically. Although ideas from active
205   learning could be useful in our domain, we are not aware of any results from that literature which
206   account for irreversible costs. The process of the agent learning from a mentor is also reminiscent of
207
      imitation learning (Osa et al., 2018), but we are not aware of any relevant technical implications.

282   4     AVOIDING CATASTROPHE IS IMPOSSIBLE IN GENERAL
283
284   We begin by showing that in general, any algorithm with sublinear mentor queries has arbitrary
285   poor regret in the worst-case, even when inputs are i.i.d. on [0, 1]. The result also holds even if the
286   algorithm knows L and x ahead of time.
287   Theorem 4.1. The worst-case expected regret of any algorithm with sublinear queries goes to 1 as T
288   goes to infinity. Formally, limT →∞ supµ,πm E[RT ] = 1.

      6    C ONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
516
517
518   In this paper, we proposed a model of avoiding catastrophe in online learning. We showed that
519
      achieving subconstant regret in our problem (with the help of a mentor and local generalization) is no
      harder than achieving sublinear regret in standard online learning.
520
521   There remain some technical questions within this paper’s model. One question is whether the time
522   complexity of Algorithm 1 be improved, which currently stands at Ω(|Π̃| ·T ) plus the time to compute
523   the ε-cover. Also, we have not resolved whether our problem is tractable for finite VC dimension and
524   fully adversarial inputs (although Appendix D shows that the problem is tractable for at least some
525   classes with finite VC but infinite Littlestone dimension).
526   We are also interested in alternatives to the local generalization assumption. We should expect some
527   assumption to be necessary: if not, the payoff function µ(x, y) = 1(π m (x) = y) means the agent
528   essentially has to make zero mistakes, which turns out to be impossible even for σ-smooth x and
529   finite VC dimension (Theorem E.3). One possible alternative is Bayesian inference. We intentionally
530   avoided Bayesian approaches in this paper due to tractability concerns, but it seems premature to
531   abandon those ideas entirely.
532   Finally, we are excited to apply the ideas in this paper to Markov Decision Processes (MDPs):
533   specifically, MDPs where some actions are irreversible (“non-communicating”) and the agent only
534   gets one attempt (“single-episode”). In such MDPs, the agent must not only avoid catastrophe but also
535   obtain high reward. As discussed in Section 2, very little theory exists for RL in non-communicating
536   single-episode MDPs. Can an agent learn near-optimal behavior in high-stakes environments while
537   becoming self-sufficient over time? Formally, we pose the following open problem:
538
539   Is there an algorithm for non-communicating single-episode undiscounted MDPs which ensures that
                      both the regret and the number of mentor queries are sublinear in T ?

1198   B.3    A DAPTIVE ADVERSARIES
1199
       If st is allowed to depend on the events of prior time steps, we say that the adversary is adaptive.
1200
       In contrast, a non-adaptive or “oblivious” adversary must choose the entire input upfront. This
1201
       distinction is not relevant for deterministic algorithms, since an adversary knows exactly how the
1202   algorithm will behave for any input. In other words, the adversary gains no new information during
1203   the execution of the algorithm. For randomized algorithms, an adaptive adversary can base the choice
1204   of st on the results of randomization on previous time steps (but not on the current time step), while
1205   an oblivious adversary cannot.
1206
       In the standard online learning model, Hedge guarantees sublinear regret against both oblivious
1207
       and adaptive adversaries (Chapter 5 of Slivkins et al. (2019) or Chapter 21 of Shalev-Shwartz &
1208   Ben-David (2014)). However, Russo et al. (2024) state their result only for oblivious adversaries. In
1209   order for our overall proof of Theorem 5.1 to hold for adaptive adversaries, Lemma 5.4 (Lemma 3.5
1210   in Russo et al. (2024)) must also hold for adaptive adversaries. In this section, we argue why the
1211   proof of Lemma 5.4 (Lemma 3.5 in their paper) goes through for adaptive adversaries as well. For
1212   this rest of Appendix B.3, lemma numbers refer to the numbering in Russo et al. (2024).
