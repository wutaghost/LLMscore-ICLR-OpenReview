009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
012
               In the present paper, we propose a new approach and a dataset for generating
               NeRF-like noise on the mesh surface. Our approach is based on GAN and was
013
               trained on a dataset that we collect using real NeRF noise. The core idea of
014
               our method lies in the use of graph convolutions in the generator. Our pipeline
015            demonstrates generated NeRF-like noise more accurate than other methods by
016            mesh denoising benchmarking. We also present a new NeRF noise analysis ap-
017            proach HTPH based on a conditional probability model to measure the similarity
018            of mesh noise.

022   1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024   The problem considered in this article belongs to mesh denoising and 3D scene reconstruction do-
025   mains. Existing scene reconstruction algorithms work with errors called noise Nguyen et al. (2012);
026   Belhaoua et al. (2009). In our work, we focus on the node noise which causes the difference between
027   shapes of real and reconstructed objects.
028
      Neural radiance fields (NeRF) models have recently become a popular tool for reconstructing 3D
029
      scenes. The first one was introduced in Mildenhall et al. (2021). Many NeRF extensions were
030   presented in the next several years after the first publication Müller et al. (2022); Barron et al.
031   (2021); Jain et al. (2021); Deng et al. (2022); Yu et al. (2021); Fridovich-Keil et al. (2022); Wang
032   et al. (2022); Zhi et al. (2021); Jeong et al. (2021). In our paper we consider NeRF as a baseline
033   algorithm for 3D scene reconstruction by a set of views.
034
      The experiments show that 3D object reconstruction using NeRF requires a lot of time. There are
035
      NeRF models where learning a scene takes up to 40 hours. Therefore, a lot of time is required to
036   generate enough data to train the noise reduction algorithm specified for NeRF noise.
037
038   Our motivation is to reduce the time-consuming process of data generation. For this purpose we
039
      introduce a pipeline that can generate a dataset in a short time. Using our approach we generate
      a new dataset. We have shown that mesh denoising models trained on our dataset remove a noise
040
      appearing in the scene after NeRF reconstruction better than without training on our dataset. Apart
041
      from this, we present a new mesh noise description based on conditional probability model which
042   we have used in our analysis.
043
044   Our pipeline is based on Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) Goodfellow et al. (2014). To train
045
      our model we select meshes from objaverse-XL Deitke et al. (2023) and preprocessed them. We use
      Instant-NGP Müller et al. (2022) to prepare NeRF noise examples.
046
047   Our main contribution can be summarized as follows:
048
049         • We introduce a new analysis of mesh noise which uncovers the significant difference be-
050           tween artificial noise and real noise.
051
052         • We propose a new pipeline for generation NeRF-like noise on the mesh surface. The core
053           of our pipeline is a GAN which was trained on real NeRF noise. The application consists
              in the massive generation of a dataset suitable for effective training of denoising models.

      2     R ELATED WORK
055
056
      In recent years, learning-based mesh denoising methods have achieved impressive results, partic-
057   ularly: DNF-Net Li et al. (2020), NormalNet Zhao et al. (2021), IMD-Net Botsch et al. (2022),
058   GeoBi-GNN Zhang et al. (2022), Cascaded Regression Wang et al. (2016) and GCN-denoiser Shen
059   et al. (2022). All learning-based methods require a large number of clean-noisy mesh pairs, which
060   are complicated and time-consuming to acquire. Mesh noise generation methods do not require as
061   much time as in-camera processing pipelines.
062
      Existing noise generation methods can be divided into the following groups:
063
064
      2.1   N ON - LEARNING - BASED NOISE MODELS
065
066   The literature review shows that a probability density function (PDF) is typically used for non-
067   learning-based modeling of sensor noise. The PDF parameters are determined through experimental
068   measurements.
069
      A Konica Minolta Vivid 910 3D laser scanner is considered by Sun et al. (2008). The authors plot the
070
      histogram of noise magnitudes and interpolate the PDF using Gaussian distribution. The Microsoft
071
      Kinect noise is analysed by Nguyen et al. (2012). The authors demonstrate how distribution depends
072   on the angle of rotation and the distance between the sensor and the plane. Choo et al. (2014)
073   creates another noise model of a Microsoft Kinect depth sensor. The authors use the chessboard for
074   experiments and show how noise distribution depends on the depth of scene points. Haider & Hel-Or
075   (2022) create a noise histogram from a series of measurements from different sensor positions and
076   show that the noise distribution depends on the light direction and distance. The authors compare
077   the noise distributions of three types of depth sensors: ZED, Microsoft Kinect V1 and Microsoft
078   Kinect V2.
079
080   2.2   L EARNING - BASED NOISE MODELS
081
082
      The noise can be learned directly with GAN if the noise is too complicated and cannot be modeled
      as PDF. In particular, noise generation is often applied to images when white noise generation is
083
      required.
084
085   Henz et al. (2020) construct the GAN model, in which a generator consists of five sequential residual
086   blocks, two convolutional and one batch normalization layer. Each residual block contains two con-
087   volutional, two normalization layers and a ReLU layer. At the same time, the discriminator consists
088
      of five convolutional layers, each followed by an instance normalization and a leaky ReLU layer.
      Similarly, Tran et al. (2020) uses the same model with five residual blocks for image noise gener-
089
      ation. Kim et al. (2019) introduces a generator with sequential residual blocks and convolutional
090
      blocks where each convolutional block has batch normalization, spectral normalization and ReLU
091   layers. At the same time, each residual block has two 3 × 3 convolutional layers.
092
093   In contrast, some researchers use U-Net-based (Ronneberger et al. (2015)) model as a generator.
094
      Hossain & Lee (2022) creates a U-Net-based model with 10 blocks, where each block contains a
      channel attention layer, two recurrent convolutional blocks with batch normalization, and ReLU
095
      layers. Chang et al. (2020) uses a camera-encoding network in addition to U-Net-shaped generator
096
      for realistic camera noise generation. Song et al. (2023) builds U-Net for camera noise generation
097   with six SNAF blocks. Each block has three convolutional, one normalization, and one simple gate
098   layers. The Decoder blocks have additional noise injection layers.

107   in the noisy mesh. The method for calculating the distance between point and mesh is described
      in the Appendix 1. This approach is based on Zong et al. (2023). Most existing denoising works

      We denote by s(v) the offset of the vertex v. All vertex offsets can be arranged in ascending order,
163   and we can find smin and smax along all vertices. The segment [smin , smax ] is divided into K equal
164   sub-segments. Each sub-segment represents a class of vertices to which offsets belong. Therefore,
165   we divide all vertices into K different
                                         j classes determined
                                                            k      by natural numbers. We define a class
166
      index for each vertex as C(v) = (s(v) − smin )/d , where d = (smax − smin )/K, i.e. C ∈
167
168
      {0, . . . , K − 1}. In practice, smin is calculated as the 4th percentile and smax as the 96th percentile
      so that each class does not contain too few vertices.
169
170   We denote all neighboring vertices for each vertex v as N (v). We define a dependence between the
171   offsets of neighboring vertices as a conditional probability model. We consider the vertex classes C
172   as states of the model. Let’s define state transition probabilities: for each vertex v we calculate the
                                       ′       ′
173   class C(v) and the classes C(v ) for v ∈ N (v). Considering all vertices, for each vertex class we
174   construct the distribution of classes of neighboring vertices. Therefore, we define a state transition
175
      probability distribution for each state.
176   The state transition probability distributions can be represented as a transition probability matrix,
177   where each element pij indicates the probability of transition from state i to state j. We represent
178   this matrix as a heatmap and call it Mesh Transition Probability Heatmap (MTPH).
179   The MTPH shows the difference between NeRF noise and random noise artificially generated for
180   each vertex, without dependence on neighboring vertices. We calculate the MTPH for meshes from
181   synthetic datasets and NeRF datasets collected by our program. The results for K = 40 are shown
182   in Figure 1. It can be seen that the transition probability distribution in artificially generated noise
183   does not depend on the vertex class.
184
      The difference between noise can be measured by the distance between MTPHs. We use the follow-
185   ing metrics: cosine difference, Euclidean distance, Manhattan distance, and kernel norm of MTPH
186   difference. Along with MTPH metrics, we use KL divergence to measure the distance between
187   the vertex offset distributions. Five metrics in total. We use these metrics to measure the distance
188   between generated noise and real noise.

      4.1    GAN ARCHITECTURE
217
218   In our method, the generator predicts the magnitude of the offset for each point along the point’s
219   normal, so to get a noisy point cloud we need to multiply these magnitudes with point normals and
220   add the resulting offsets to original point positions. The first step to generate a noisy point cloud is
221   to encode the original point cloud using two PointNet layers, each of those utilize the point cloud
222   and its knn-graph. Then, to the encoded point cloud we add random values sampled from random
223   distribution as suggested in study Song et al. (2023). The noisy point cloud features are then fed to
224   GATv2 Layer Brody et al. (2022) and head layers in the end.
225   The discriminator requires point offsets and the original point cloud knn-graph as input. First, it
226   propagates through the knn-graph using offsets as node features, and then follows a single linear
227   plane and global mean pooling operation to obtain a vector representation of each point cloud in
228   a stack. The point cloud vector representation is then fed to linear layers, followed by sigmoid
229   activation to predict the probability of generating a point cloud. The schematic of the generator and
230   discriminator is shown in the Figure 3.

      7.3   L IMITATIONS
487
488   Besides a generative model the dataset generation pipeline includes the DPSR + MC part. Preparing
489   the dataset for the noise generation model involves solving optimization problems, which requires a
490   lot of time for complicated shapes with large amounts of tiny elements.
491
      Due to the benchmark denoising models, it is necessary to use a dataset with the same number of
492   vertices. For this reason, the preparation of the evaluation dataset requires a special transformation
493   procedure that converts the point cloud into a mesh with the same number of vertices as the original
494   clear mesh. This procedure is described in 7.1. This process can result in artifacts on the surface
495   with tiny parts like birds.
496
      The NeRF noise is determined not only by the shape topology but also by the shape texture. It is
497
      necessary to select shapes with textures that will not cause abnormal convex or concave bumps on
498   the mesh surface after NeRF application.

501   8     C ONCLUSION
502
503   In this article, we present a NeRF-like noise generation pipeline based on GAN and includes graph
504
      convolutional blocks to address challenges faced by providing reliable NeRF datasets for denois-
      ing tasks. Experimental results prove the better performance of using generated dataset for mesh
505
      denoising tasks over existing synthetic datasets. We have shown that datasets generated with our
506
      pipeline improve learning-based denoising models when used for training. The most significant
507   improvement show the mean cosine distance and the absolute area difference of the metric normals.
508
509   Another important result is a new analysis of mesh noise that is suitable for complicated noise. Our
510
      analysis approach assumes a special heatmap calculation for vertex offsets, which has a meaning of
      transition probability matrix.
511
512   In future work, we want to investigate other types of mesh noise, including topology noise.

642   A.1   C ALCULATE DISTANCE BETWEEN POINT AND MESH
643
644
      In this section we present an algorithm for quick calculation of distance between point and triangle
      mesh. The three-dimensional space around a mesh is described as a Voronoi diagram constructed
645
      for different classes of geometric primitives that mesh consists of: facets, edges, and vertices.
646
647   Consider the point Q and calculate the distance from Q to the mesh. The algorithm consists of the
      following steps:


                                                      12
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


648
             • Find a vertex of the mesh A closest to the point Q. This can be done, for example, using
649            a kd-tree calculated previously for all vertices of the mesh. We denote by V1 , . . . , Vn the
650            vertices that are adjacent to vertex A. We also denote by C1 , . . . , Cn the centroids of facets
651            adjacent to the vertex A.
652
             • Denote vector AQ by ā. Next vectors AV 1 , . . . , AV n we denote by v̄1 , . . . , v̄n . Finally we
653            denote vectors AC 1 , . . . , AC n by c̄1 , . . . , c̄n .
654
             • First we should check if point Q is in the reference cone of A.
655
656
      The article clearly describes that the three-dimensional space above/below a mesh can be described
657   as a Voronoi diagram constructed for different classes of geometric primitives. The classical Voronoi
658   diagram is a partition of space into regions, where each region of it forms a set of points closer to
659   one of the elements of a certain set than to any other element of the set. A mesh consists of three
660   types of geometric primitives: facet, edge, and vertex.
661
      The space in which the mesh is represented is transformed into a Voronoi diagram for the facets,
662
      edges, and vertices of the mesh. Drawing from the article:
663
664   In the figure, red indicates the areas where the points are closest to one of the facets than to any other
665   facet or any of the edges or vertices. Similarly, blue indicates the areas where the points are closest
666
      to some edge, and yellow indicates some vertex.
667   If you want to find the shortest distance from an arbitrarily taken point to the mesh surface, then you
668   need to take into account this feature of dividing the space around the mesh, since the distance from
669   a point to a flat triangle in 3D is not calculated in the same way as the distance from a point to a
670   segment or from a point to a point. It is important to understand which of the geometric primitives
671   is closest to the point before calculating the distance.
672   The algorithm for finding the shortest distance can be implemented without constructing a Voronoi
673   diagram, but with the assumption that the surface to which the distance needs to be calculated is
674   sufficiently convex.
675
      Suppose you want to calculate the distance from the point Q to the mesh. The algorithm consists of
676   the following steps:
677
678   1. Search for the vertex of the mesh A closest to the point Q. This can be done, for example, using a
679
      kd-tree calculated previously for all vertices of the mesh. Denote by V1 , . . . , Vn the vertices that are
      adjacent to vertex A. We also denote by C1 , . . . , Cn the centroids of facets adjacent to the vertex A;
680
      2. Check whether the point Q lies in the reference cone of this vertex (in the figure these cones are
681
      indicated in yellow). To do this, take the vector connecting vertex A and point Q, that is, vector AQ.
682   Next, you need to calculate the scalar products of the vector AQ with the vectors AC1 , . . . , ASp . If
683   all these scalar products are strictly less than zero, then the point Q belongs to the support cone. In
684   this case, the desired distance is the length of the vector AQ. If at least one of the scalar products is
685   greater than or equal to zero, then the distance is calculated according to the algorithm in paragraph
686   3; 3. For each facet k adjacent to vertex A, calculate the vectors L1 Ck , L2 Ck , L3 Ck , where L1 ,
687   L2 , L3 are the midpoints of the facet edges. We also calculate the vectors L1 Q, L2 Q, L3 Q, then
688   calculate the scalar products (Li Ck , Li Q), i = 1, 2, 3. If all three scalar products are greater than
689   or equal to zero, then the minimum distance from the point Q to the mesh is equal to the distance
690
      to the facet k. If otherwise, the distance is calculated according to the algorithm in paragraph 4; 4.
      Calculate the scalar product of the vector (AQ, AVk ), k = 1, . . . , n. Important: each of the vectors
691
      Vk must be normalized before calculating the scalar products. Let’s define k for which the scalar
692
      product (AQ, AVk ) is maximal. An edge with index k is the nearest edge to the point Q. In this
693   case, the minimum distance from the point Q to the mesh is equal to the distance to the edge k.
