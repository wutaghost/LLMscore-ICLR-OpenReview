032                                                A BSTRACT
033
034
               Stylized Text-to-Image Generation (STIG) aims to generate images based on text
               prompts and style reference images. We in this paper propose a novel framework
035
               dubbed StyleMaster for this task by leveraging pretrained Stable Diffusion (SD),
036
               which addresses previous problems such as misinterpreted style and inconsistent
037            semantics. The enhancement lies in two novel modules: multi-source style em-
038            bedder and dynamic attention adapter. In order to provide SD with better style
039            embeddings, we propose the multi-source style embedder, which considers both
040            global and local level visual information along with textual information, thereby
041            offering both complementary style-related and semantic-related knowledge. Ad-
042            ditionally, aiming for better balance between the adapter capacity and semantic
043            control, the proposed dynamic attention adapter is applied to the diffusion UNet
044            in which adaptation weights are dynamically calculated based on the style embed-
045
               dings. Two objective functions are introduced to optimize the model alongside the
               denoising loss, which can further enhance semantic and style consistency. Exten-
046
               sive experiments demonstrate the superiority of StyleMaster over existing meth-
047
               ods, rendering images with variable target styles while successfully maintaining
048            the semantic information from the text prompts.

051   1   I NTRODUCTION
052
053   Stylized Image Generation (SIG), which generates images with specific styles, has broad academic
      value and practical applications in fields like art and film. With the rise of diffusion-based generative


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      models Ho et al. (2020); Rombach et al. (2022), research has shifted from traditional style transfer
055   to Stylized Text-to-Image Generation (STIG). In this task, one or several style reference images
056   are given as condition, and various images are generated based on these style conditions along
057   with additional information such as text prompts. Generally, STIG approaches provide enhanced
058   flexibility despite their complex, mixed input conditions, making them highly relevant for real-world
059   applications.
060
      Recently, StyleAdapter Wang et al. (2023b), built on pretrained Stable Diffusion (SD), has emerged
061   as a representative and effective STIG method. It processes reference images through a style em-
062   bedding module, injecting these embeddings into the diffusion UNet via cross-attention modules
063   to guide style incorporation during denoising and generate stylized images. The framework has
064   also been successfully extended in recent work Wang et al. (2024). However, despite its effective-
065   ness with specific styles and prompts, StyleAdapter faces key challenges: (1) misinterpreted style,
066   where generated images fail to fully capture complex reference styles, and (2) inconsistent se-
067   mantics, where elements from reference images leak into the output, misaligning with text prompts.
068   These issues arise from the design of StyleAdapter, particularly its use of CLIP Radford et al. (2021),
069
      which focuses on local patch-level patterns while neglecting critical global patterns. Furthermore,
      by injecting style embeddings into all cross-attention layers of the diffusion UNet, the model biases
070
      text-prompt integration, leading to semantic inconsistencies.
071
072   In this paper, we propose StyleMaster, a novel STIG framework featuring advanced extraction and
073   injection of style information. Our method builds on the basic pipeline of StyleAdapter but intro-
074   duces specific enhancements for style embedding extraction and injection.
075   For style extraction, we propose a multi-source structure to address the limitations of CLIP’s patch-
076   level information. Specifically, in addition to using CLIP-based patch embeddings, we incorporate
077   global-level VGG descriptors, which are integrated into the attention process via adaptive scaling
078   and shifting. Moreover, we utilize the semantic knowledge from reference image captions. To pre-
079   vent this abstract semantic content from influencing the style embedding, we introduce a negative
080   embedding branch that removes semantic information. By combining these different latent spaces,
081
      our method captures a more comprehensive representation of target styles while avoiding semantic
      leakage. For style embedding injection, we advocate that limiting the attachment of adapters only
082
      to specific parts of the UNet, such as the upsampling layers, could prevent semantic distortion. How-
083
      ever, this direct limitation reduces the capacity of adapters and worsens the issue of misinterpreted
084   style. To resolve this, we propose a dynamic attention adapter that generates dynamic weights from
085   the style embeddings. These weights adapt both self-attention and cross-attention layers in the dif-
086   fusion UNet, allowing for more precise and flexible style adaptation. This ensures that the generated
087   images maintain both the intended style and semantic consistency with the text prompts.
088
      Furthermore, we enhance the model with objectives beyond the standard noise prediction loss com-
089
      monly used in diffusion models. We introduce a Gram consistency loss, augmenting the reference
090   images with two sets of transformations: one set preserves the original style, while the other adopts
091   a distorted style. We then compute Gram matrices of the estimated denoised results and these trans-
092   formed reference images as their style-aware statistics. By applying a triplet loss among these
093   matrices, the model is encouraged to generate images with more robust and consistent styles when
094   processing different reference images. Additionally, we utilize a semantic disentanglement loss to
095   mitigate the inconsistent semantics problem by contrasting the style embeddings against reference
096   text embeddings, while ensuring they remain similar to the reference image embeddings.
097   To show the effectiveness of our proposed method, we conduct extensive experiments among various
098   styles containing both one-shot and multi-shot settings. We show that our method can significantly
099   outperform baseline methods including StyleAdapter, generating correct styles and avoiding incon-
100   sistent semantics. In summary, the contributions of this work are as follows:
101   1) Enhanced Framework for STIG: We present StyleMaster, a novel framework for Stylized Text-
102   to-Image Generation (STIG) that enhances the existing StyleAdapter pipeline. StyleMaster sig-
103   nificantly improves style embedding extraction and injection, effectively addressing issues such as
104
      misinterpreted styles and inconsistent semantics.
      2) Multi-Source Structure for Style Extraction: Our StyleMaster introduce a multi-source approach
105
      that integrates CLIP-based patch embeddings, global-level VGG descriptors, and semantic knowl-
106
      edge from image captions, capturing comprehensive target style representations while preventing
107   semantic leakage through a negative embedding branch.


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      3) Dynamic Attention Adapter for Style Injection: To enhance style embedding injection, StyleMas-
109   ter introduces a dynamic attention adapter that generates weights from style embeddings, enabling
110   precise adaptation of self-attention and cross-attention layers in the diffusion UNet. This ensures
111   that generated images maintain the intended style and semantic consistency with text prompts.
112   4) Enhanced Training Objectives: our model incorporates objectives beyond standard noise pre-
113   diction loss, including a novel Gram consistency loss that promotes robust and consistent styles
114   through triplet loss on Gram matrices from transformed reference images. Additionally, a semantic
115   disentanglement loss contrasts style embeddings with reference text embeddings while maintaining
116   similarity to reference image embeddings, addressing inconsistent semantics.

118   2    R ELATED WORK
119
120
      Text-to-image diffusion models. Diffusion models have been proven to be a powerful family of
121   generative models. DDPM Ho et al. (2020) originated to propose the framework by modeling the
122   mapping between Gaussian distribution and image distribution with the forward diffusion and in-
123   verse denoising process. Based on that Latent Diffusion Model (LDM) Rombach et al. (2022)
124   largely improved the practical usage by leveraging diffusion model to latent space instead of pixel
125   space, which leads to commonly-known text-to-image diffusion models such as Stable Diffusion
126   (SD), Midjourney and DALLE-3 Betker et al. (2023). Other works focus on improve the diffusion
127   model structure. For example, DiT Peebles & Xie (2023), MDT Gao et al. (2023) and PIXART-
128   α Chen et al. (2023) utilize the transformer instead of UNet structure, which can be better scaled
129
      to larger model size. Blattmann et al. (2022) and Zhang et al. (2023b) leverage ideas of Retrieval
      Augmented Generation (RAG) to generate images based on other retrived images which provide
130
      extra knowledge. Yang et al. (2024) propose to leverage the LLMs for planning the text-to-image
131
      problems. Our work is built on pretrained SD models, in which the attention mechanism merges
132   conditional information from text prompts to images. Different from the previous works, we focus
133   on designing extra attention adaptation so that the knowledge contained in the style reference images
134   can be smoothly embedded into the denoising process, leading to stylized images.
135
      Stylized image generation. Among all conditional image generation tasks, stylized image gener-
136
      ation has long been a highlighted one. Most previous works focus on style transfer, i.e., transfer
137
      the style of a content image given another style refernce image. For example, Gatys et al. (2016)
138   solved this problem by optimizing the style-related statistics. Li et al. (2017) equipped this method
139   with explanability. MicroAST Wang et al. (2023a) proposed to speed up such framework by aban-
140   doning the complex visual encoder and utilizing a dual-modulation strategy. Yang et al. (2023)
141   leverages diffusion models, in which the style-aware guidance is used to generate the wanted style.
142   InST Zhang et al. (2023c) realized style transfer by inverting the content image to noise and then
143   re-generate it with the condition control of style images. Apart from these style transfer methods,
144   StyleAdapter Wang et al. (2023b) proposes a new framework which can generate images directly
145   from style reference images and text prompts without content images. Our work mainly follows
146
      StyleAdapter to present a generalized stylization method. Different from StyleAdapter, we analyze
      the role of style reference images and text prompts in the generation process. Based on that, we
147
      propose a novel module to extract more representative style embeddings, which are then injected
148
      into noise space with our proposed dynamic adapter.

212   4.1      M ULTI - SOURCE STYLE EMBEDDER
213
214   Given the successful application of text-to-image based on SD, an ideal reference-based styliza-
215   tion should necessarily depend on extracting style embeddings that are as representative as the
      text embeddings resulted from strong embedders such as CLIP and T5, from reference images.


                                                                            4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      StyleAdapter Wang et al. (2023b) adopts CLIP visual encoder Radford et al. (2021) to extract the
217   patch-level features from style reference images, which are then processed with cross-attentions us-
218   ing learnable style tokens. However, StyleAdapter still suffers from issues such as misinterpreted
219   style and inconsistent semantics, i.e., the semantic knowledge in reference images is generated dur-
220   ing the denoising procedure. The former problem can be attributed to the fact that CLIP-based patch
221   embeddings concentrate more on local patterns. In this way, the style embeddings fail to hold the
222   global style patterns, leading to misinterpreted styles. Meanwhile, since CLIP image embeddings are
223   aligned with their captions, they inevitably contain information about content in the images. Incor-
224   porating such information in style embeddings can lead to inconsistent semantics. Moreover, since
225
      SD models different kinds of information across different denoising timesteps, providing identical
      conditional information means SD has to extract the required information on its own, thus leading
226
      to heavier learning burden for SD.
227

229   style embedding. Specifically, given a style image set Is , we extract three different kinds of features
230   with pretrained models: (1) local style-aware features: Following StyleAdapter, we utilize CLIP
231   to encode each style image Iistyle into latent patch tokens z̃CLIP

      4.2   DYNAMIC ATTENTION ADAPTATION
271
272   The extracted style embedding zs from Is as described above can then be used to adapt the pretrained
273   SD to guide the denoising process based on style information. Thanks to the design of self-attention
274   and cross-attention mechanism in diffusion UNet, such adaptation can be simply instantiated as an
275   extra cross-attention module that is parallel to the original prompt-based cross-attention, as adopted
276   in StyleAdapter. However, we empirically find that such a method is suboptimal in a large amount of
277   cases, leading to severe semantic inconsistency. A straightforward solution is cut down the number
278
      of extra attention modules so that only upsample layers in diffusion UNet are adapted. In this way,
      the text prompt can dominate the cross-attention in half of the UNet, consequently resulting in better
279
      semantics in the generated images. However, this can decrease the capacity of adapters, leading to
280
      less preferable stylization. To this end, we propose adopting a dynamic adaptation strategy which is
281   applied to both self-attention and cross-attention (Fig. 3(b)).
282

      5.1   I MPLEMENTATION DETAIL
351
      Dataset. We follow StyleAdapter to adopt LAION-Aesthetic 6.5+ as the training set, which contains
352   about 600k images. For each input image during training, we use its augmented variants as the style
353   reference images. For evaluation we adopt 50 prompts used in StyleAdapter, and select 20 styles
354   covering color, texture and global layout. More details are presented in the supplementary material.
355
      Experiment setting. Our experiments cover both one-shot and multi-shot settings. To make the
356
      evaluation more challenging, the shot number, i.e., number of reference images, varies from 2 to 5
357
      among different styles in the multi-shot setting. We use all 20 styles for multi-shot experiments and
358   10 of them for one-shot experiments.
359
360   Training details. We adopt AdamW as optimizer with 1e-5 learning rate. Our model is trained for
361
      200,000 iterations on 8 V100s with 8 batch size on each gpu, which takes about 3 days.

501   5.4   A BLATION STUDY
502
503   To further verify the efficacy of our contributions, we conduct several ablation studies on multi-shot
504   setting. The quantitative results are shown in Tab. 5. More qualitative ablation studies are provided
505   in the appendix.
506   Design of style embedding module. We consider two variants together with the full model for
507   the style embedding module: not using the VGG Gram matrix to regulate the attention layers (w/o
508   VGGToken), and not engaging the reference image captions in the extraction (w/o NegEmb). The
509   results are shown in the left three columns of Fig. 6. The style of images generated by model
510   without VGGToken is generally less mimic. Meanwhile, the model without NegEmb not only has
511   worse style (first and second row) but also suffers from mistaken semantic meaning (third row).
512   Design of attention adapter. We illustrate the role of different parts in the proposed dynamic
513   attention adapter in the middle three columns of Fig. 6. When the model adopts attention adapter for
514   all UNet attention layers instead of only the upsample ones, the images generally have problem of
515   mistaken semantic meaning. For example, the clock is missing in the first row, and the cloth color is
516   mistaken in the third row. Also, it is obvious that when only using the same cross-attention adapter
517   as in StyleAdapter, the generated images show inconsistent and undesirable styles, which can be
518
      attributed to the limited capacity to such strategy. Interestingly, we find in Tab. 5 that when not using
      dynamic adapter, the model has a very different tendency compared with the full model, with much
519
      better text prompt similarity but much worse style similarity. This is reasonable since the proposed
520
      dynamic adapter greatly strengthens the impact of style embedding during both self-attention and
521   cross-attention. In this way, the cross-attention with text prompts is weakened is disguise. In general,
522   adopting dynamic adapter can make a good balance between text prompt fidelity and style fidelity.
523
524
      Effectiveness of different objective functions. In the right three columns of Fig. 6 we inspect the
      efficacy of two objectives introduced in Sec. 4.3. The results directly support our claim that the
525
      gram consistency loss can enhance the style in generated images and the semantic disentangle loss
526
      can make the model tell apart semantic and style information from reference images, thus better
527   handling contents in the text prompts.

532
533
      We try to solve Stylized Text-to-Image Generation in this paper. A novel model is proposed to solve
534   the problems of misinterpreted style and inconsistent semantics which are suffered by previous meth-
535   ods such as StyleAdapter. The improvement mainly comes from the multi-source style embedder,
536   in which multiple sources and used to achieve comprehensive style embeddings and eliminate the
537   semantic information from style reference images, and the dynamic attention adapter, in which style
538   embeddings dynamically interact with attention layers in diffusion UNet. Extensive experiments
539   have been conducted to show the efficacy of our proposed method as a powerful stylization method,
      which can be widely applied to real-life scenarios.

      B    M ORE DISCUSSION
726
727
      Limitations. We would like to highlight two limitations of our method. First, the multi-source
728
      style embedding extraction model relies on a patch-level transformer. In this way, it is less efficient
729   for this model to process too many style reference images. While such a scenario is to some extent
730   unrealistic, since it is generally sufficient to represent a specific style with less than 10 images,
731   solving this problem can be related to improving vision transformer structures, which can be taken
732   as future works. Second, the proposed method is only available for style conditions in the form of
733   images. Other forms such as texts, videos and 3D data are not considered in this work and can be
734   solved in the future.
735
736   Broader impacts. Our work will not lead to significant negative social impacts. Problems such
737   as privacy invasion and misinformation can be also attributed to normal image generative models.
738   Solving such problems would be a large future research topic.
739
740   Comparison with InstantStyle-SDXL and StyleAlign. In Fig. 9 we present the comparison be-
741   tween our method and InstantStyle and StyleAlign, both using SDXL as backbone network. We
742   find that while InstantStyle does perform better with SDXL than SD1.5, suffering less from content
743
      leakage, it tends to generate images with classic art styles such as paintings. This makes the gener-
      ated results less similar to the reference images. On the other hand, InstantStyle-SDXL still cannot
744
      handle styles such as cubism and impasto, which can be well modelled by our method. Moreover,
745
      StyleAlign is generally worse than the other two methods.
746
747
      Effectiveness of frequency domain decomposition. In the multi-source style embedding extrac-
748
      tion module, we utilize the discrete wavelet transform to first decompose the patch level features of
749
      style reference images into low-frequency and high-frequency features. To see how this process can
750   help our module, we visualize the attention weights for two sets of reference images among different
751   denoising time steps in Fig. 10. Three main phenomenon can be concluded: (1) Style embeddings
752   concentrate more on low frequency features, which is reasonable since low-frequency features con-
753   tain information such as color. (2) The patterns of feature usage are consistent among two prompts
754   for each style, while being different for different styles. (3) For high-frequency features, different
755   timesteps generally focus on different information. These results can sufficiently support our design
      of decomposing the image features regarding frequency.
