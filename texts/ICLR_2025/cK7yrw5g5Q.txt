009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) has been widely adopted to
012
               align language models (LMs) with human preference. Prior RLHF works typically
013
               take a bandit formulation, which, though intuitive, ignores the sequential nature
014            of LM generation and can suffer from the sparse reward issue. While recent
015            works propose dense token-level RLHF, treating each token as an action may be
016            oversubtle to proper reward assignment. In this paper, we seek to get the best
017            of both by training and utilizing a segment-level reward model, which assigns a
018            reward to each semantically complete text segment that spans over a short sequence
019            of tokens. For reward learning, our method allows dynamic text segmentation and
020            compatibility with standard sequence-preference datasets. For effective RL-based
021            LM training against segment reward, we generalize the classical scalar bandit
022
               reward normalizers into location-aware normalizer functions and interpolate the
               segment reward for further densification. With these designs, our method performs
023
               competitively on popular RLHF benchmarks in both reward modeling and LM
024
               policy learning. Ablation studies are conducted to further demonstrate our method.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      To align language models (LMs, e.g., OpenAI, 2023; Reid et al., 2024) with human values, rein-
031   forcement learning (RL, Sutton & Barto, 2018) methods have been widely adopted to optimize the
032   non-differentiable human preference, leading to the paradigm of reinforcement learning from human
033   feedback (RLHF, Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022b). A prevailing approach in RLHF is to
034   optimize the LMs by proximal policy optimization (PPO, Schulman et al., 2017) against a bandit
035   reward model learned from human preference data, with KL regularization towards a pre-specified
036   target distribution to avoid over-optimization on the reward model (Ziegler et al., 2019; Stiennon
037   et al., 2020; Castricato et al., 2022). While this bandit approach is easier for reward modeling and has
038   achieved remarkable success, language generation is intrinsically sequential, rather than simultaneous.
039
      Thus, from the view of optimizing human preference, assigning a bandit reward to entire text sequence
      induces the sparse reward (delayed feedback) issue (Andrychowicz et al., 2017; Marbach & Tsitsiklis,
040
      2003), that often hurts RL-based LM training by increasing gradient variance and lowering sample
041
      efficiency (Takanobu et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2020; Guo et al., 2022; Snell et al., 2022).
042
043   As efforts to mitigate this sparse reward issue, prior works have developed methods to “ground” the
044   sequence-level preference label into a dense token-level reward model (Yang et al., 2023; Zhong et al.,
045
      2024). While a dense per-token reward signal reduces the optimization complexity (Laidlaw et al.,
      2023), each action, however, is then defined as a single token, i.e., a sub-word that is finer-grained than
046
      a word, especially with the BPE-style tokenizers (Gage, 1994; Sennrich et al., 2016). For instance,
047
      Llama 3.1’s tokenizer (Dubey et al., 2024) has tokens as {Brit,ce,cod,neo,redd,...} that
048   have less clear semantic meaning per se in any given context. The contribution of those tokens to
049   the text sequence will inevitably depend on later tokens, making reward/credit assignment harder,
050   especially under the prevailing RLHF paradigm of implementing the reward model as an off-the-shelf
051   decoder-only transformer (e.g., Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022b; Menick et al., 2022). Further,
052   token-level reward implicitly assumes that the basic unit of a text sequence is token, which may not
053   follow linguistics, where a more meaningful decomposition of text may be phase (including word)
      that can be more semantically complete and generally consists of a short sequence of tokens.

054
      To retain the optimization benefit of dense reward for RLHF, while mitigating its potential reward
055   assignment issue and linguistic counter-intuition, in this paper, we seek to train and utilize a segment-
056   level reward model, which assigns a reward to each semantically meaningful segment of text sequence
057   that constitutes a small amount of (or just one) tokens. With this construction, we define the action
058   space in RLHF as “text segment,” interpolating between the finest “per token” and the coarsest “full
059   sequence” and potentially getting the benefit of both worlds: easier RL-based LM training owing to
060   denser feedback and more accurate training guidance by the semantic completeness of each action.
061   Technically, we are motivated by prior works (Malinin & Gales, 2018; Li et al., 2024a) to implement
062   a dynamic text sequence segmentation by thresholding the entropy of LM’s predictive distributions,
063   under the assumption that tokens within a semantically complete text segment can be more certainly
064   predicted by prior tokens, while the start of a new segment is not (Wang et al., 2024b). To allow
065   training the segment-level reward model by the standard sequence-preference labels via Bradley-Terry
066   (BT, Bradley & Terry, 1952) loss, we differentiably aggregate segment rewards in a text sequence
067   into a parametrized sequence evaluation. The learned segment-level reward model is then utilized in
068   PPO-based policy learning, where we observe the unsuitability of classical reward normalizers, i.e.,
069
      the mean and standard deviation (std) of full sequence rewards. We address this issue by generalizing
      the classical bandit normalizers of scalar mean and std into a mean and a std function that output the
070
      reward normalizers at arbitrary location of the text sequence. In addition, we enhance PPO training
071
      by within-segment reward interpolation, which further densifies training signal and improves results.
072
073   We test our method on the performance of both the learned segment-level reward model and the
074   subsequent PPO-trained LM policy. On popular RLHF benchmarks for reward modeling and LM
075
      policy learning, our method indicates competitive performance gain against both the classical bandit
      reward approach and recent token-level reward approach. We conduct a wide array of ablation studies
076
      to validate our design choices and provide further insight into our method.

      3    R ELATED W ORK
256
257
      Reward Models in RLHF. In the classical RLHF paradigm, policy LM is optimized against a bandit
258   reward model trained firstly by binary classification loss on the preference dataset, with KL penalty
259   to a specified prior distribution to avoid reward over-optimization (Ziegler et al., 2019; Stiennon
260   et al., 2020; Jaques et al., 2020; Bai et al., 2022a; Ouyang et al., 2022; Castricato et al., 2022).
261   Under the same bandit formulation, recent works have enhanced the bandit reward model by directly
262   modeling the probability of one response being preferred over the other (Jiang et al., 2023; Zhao et al.,
263   2023; Liu et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2024) or factorizing human preference into multiple facets via
264   multi-objective modeling (Touvron et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; 2024c;a). Despite its popularity,
265   from the angle of RL-based optimization of human preference captured by the reward model, such
266
      a bandit reward may lead to inferior training, due to the sparse reward issue intrinsic to the bandit
      formulation of LM generation and credit assignment (e.g., Takanobu et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2022).
267
268   Viewing the weakness of bandit RLHF, efforts have been making to densify the reward signal for
269   RLHF LM training. Yang et al. (2023) and Chan et al. (2024) train token-level reward models by
      the binary preference classification loss. Zhong et al. (2024) and Rafailov et al. (2024) use an LM


                                                              5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      trained by DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023) firstly for token-level reward assignment, which is later used in
271   PPO training or search-based algorithms. Guo et al. (2023), Cao et al. (2024), and Yoon et al. (2024)
272   assign continuous or fixed fine-grained rewards (e.g., ±1) by accessing an external powerful large
273   LM or the oracle environmental reward; while Chen et al. (2024) require the extra task and datasets of
274   erroneous solution rewriting. Apart from potential extra requirements, as discussed in Section 1, the
275   semantic incompleteness of token in text may challenge the efficacy of per-token credit assignment,
276   especially with the prevailing implementation of reward model as a decoder-only transformer that
277   cannot look ahead into later tokens. In contrast, by defining text segment as the basic unit of text
278   sequence that can be semantically more complete than token, our segment-level reward may provide
279
      more accurate guidance for RL-based LM training, while not losing the benefit of denser feedback.
280   Close to our segment-level reward, process reward models (PRMs, e.g., Uesato et al., 2022; Lightman
281   et al., 2023) in reasoning-alike tasks also assign a single reward value to a short sequence of tokens.
282   The training of PRMs, however, typically requires human annotation on each step of the reasoning-
283   alike process. This may not be feasible in general text generation tasks, e.g., text summarization or
284   dialog, where each step/text segment lacks clear human evaluation criterion while the full generations
285
      can be more easily compared or evaluated. By contrast, as seen in Section 2, our method is developed
      for the most basic yet general RLHF setting, where human preference is only manifested in a dataset
286
      of binary sequence-level preference. And the dataset is collected from multiple sources and contains
287
      multiple forms of prompt-responses. We discuss a broader set of related works in Appendix D.

301   and a rejected response. The evaluation metric is the classification accuracy of chosen/rejected label.
302

      the classification logit, and similarly for other dense reward models such as the token-level model.
303
304   The (PPO-trained) LM policy is evaluated on three popular open-ended instruction-following bench-
305   marks: AlpacaEval 2.0 (Li et al., 2023), Arena-Hard (Li et al., 2024c), and MT-Bench (Zheng et al.,

307   protocol. AlpacaEval 2.0 consists of 805 test cases, on which we report the length control win rate
308
      (LC), raw win rate (WR), and the average response length in number of characters (# char). The LC

309
      Dong et al., 2024) to employ alpaca eval gpt4 turbo fn as the annotator for AlpacaEval
310
      2.0. Arena-Hard consists of 500 challenging user queries, and we report both the win rate (WR)
311   against the reference model GPT-4-0314 and the average number of tokens in the responses (#
312   token). MT-Bench is a multi-turn dialogue dataset that includes 80 test cases. We report the average
313   MT-Bench scores for two dialogue turns, using GPT-4o as the judge model.

343   Baselines. To demonstrate our unique consideration on RLHF’s action space, in the main experiment,
344   we compare our design of segment-level action space with the coarsest bandit/sequence-level action
345
      space, the coarser sentence-level space, and the finest token-level space, in terms of the performance
      of both the reward model and the subsequent PPO-trained LM policy. Unless specified, both the
346

347
      models are implemented by splitting the text sequences by the sentence splitters {".", "!", "?",
348   "\n", ";", "...", ",", ":"} and/or their foreign language equivalents.
349
350   To further illustrate our segment-level reward model and denser segment-level reward assignment, we
351
      additionally compare with two hybrid approaches: (A) using the bandit reward model for segment-
      level reward assignment in the PPO training (“Bandit as Segment”); and (B) using the segment-level
352
      reward model only for bandit reward assignment in the PPO training (“Segment as Bandit”), where the
353
      bandit reward is implemented by the parametrized sequence evaluation eϕ in Eq. (4). For all baselines,
354   we follow the standard training receipts and tune them to the extent of ensuring fair comparison.

356   Results. Table 1 compares the performance of our segment-level reward model and the resulted

358
      RewardBench is deferred to Table 5 in Appendix B.1. Our key findings are summarized as follows.
359
360   (1) Segment-level action space improves reward modeling. From Table 1’s RewardBench results, it is
361   clear that our segment-level reward model outperforms reward models from alternative RLHF action
362   definitions, which we attribute to our design of semantically complete text sequence segmentation and
363
      a targeted choice of aggregation function in reward model training. This is corroborated by the strong
      result of sentence-level reward model, which performs a comparatively coarser text segmentation but

      otherwise also employs soft-maximum aggregation to highlight key sentences. Since it is coarser

367   finer reward/credit assignment and key phase highlight, the coarsest classical bandit/sequence-level
368   reward model performs much weaker than both segment- and sentence-level models. On the other
369   extreme, the recent finest token-level reward model, which ignores the semantic completeness in the
370   action space definition, suffers from accurate reward assignment/modeling, as discussed in Section 1.
371

372   seen in Table 1, our LM policy performs better than the baselines across all three RLHF policy

375   larger than the baseline models’, in contrast to the LM policy from the sentence-level approach.
376   Together, these results manifest the merit of our segment-level approach in truly improving the quality
377   of the generated responses while not cheating the benchmark evaluations by response-length hacking
      (Dubois et al., 2024). As discussed in Section 1, we attribute the gain of our segment-level approach

452   (b): How will PPO training perform if we use different constructions of reward normalizers?
453
      Recall that in our PPO training (Section 2.3), we use simple linear regression to fit location-aware
454
      mean and std functions that provide reward normalizers at arbitrary locations of the text sequence. To
455
      study if this design is over-engineering, we compare our main method with three simpler constructions
456   of segment-level reward normalizers: (A) no reward normalization; (B) using the scalar global mean
457   and std over all segment-level rewards in the reward calibration dataset; and (C) using the scalar mean
458   and std over the last segment-level rewards in each response of the calibration set, mimicking the
459   normalizers in the classical bandit approach. Table 2 compares the resulted LM policies.
460
      As common RLHF PPO practice, in Table 2, we first see a strong call for reward normalization,
461
      without which the training process will be broken. Using global statistics or the statistics of the last
462   segment-level rewards perform similarly, with the latter being slightly better. While policy training
463   under these two normalizer constructions does not break, it is however ineffective, indicating that
464   these two constructions distort the training reward signals to an extent of being (almost) useless. The
465   significantly better performance of our main method over these alternatives verify the necessity of
466   normalizing segment-level rewards by location-aware normalizers able to capture the reward statistics
467   at each arbitrary location (completion portion) of the sampled text sequence, and hence our design of
468   regression-based mean and std functions. Future work may extend these functions with non-linearity.
469   (c): What will happen if we use different strategies for within-segment reward interpolation?
470
471
      Recall from Section 2.3 that, to further densify the learning signal in RLHF for enhancing training,
      we interpolate the segment-level rewards by evenly splitting the reward of a segment to each of its
472
      constituting token. We now compare this even-split interpolation strategy with two other intuitive
473
      alternatives: (A) no interpolation on the segment-level rewards, use 0 for technical padding in PPO
474   (“No Interpolation”); (B) repeat the segment-level reward of a segment to each token in it (“Repeat
475   Segment Reward”). Table 3 shows the performance of the resulted PPO-trained LM policies.
476
477
      Aligning with our intuition, Table 3 indicates that without any within-segment reward interpolation,
      the raw segment-level rewards may not be a strong-enough learning signal to incentivize the LM to
478
      learn to generate, leading to too-short sequence generations and the subsequent inferior performance.
479
      On the other hand, repeating segment-level reward to each of the constituting token results in a too-
480   strong learning signal, where each token has interpolated feedback of the same scale as the feedback
481   to the entire text segment, which has coarser granularity. This undesirable level-up of feedback signal
482   scale, especially the amplification of positive signals on longer segments, may provide a too-strong
483   incentive for the LM policy to learn to generate, making it produce excessively long text sequences.
484   By contrast, the even-split densification strategy in our main method provides interpolated learning
485   signal of a proper scale, which we attribute to the implicit (segment-)length normalization inherited
      from division by segment length in an even split. Future work may design a proper non-even split.

531   5       C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
532
533   In this paper, we propose to train and utilize a segment-level reward model for improved RLHF in
534   LMs, motivated by both a denser reward signal in RL-based LM training and semantic completeness
535
      of each action for accurate reward assignment. Our method and insight are validated through extensive
      experiments and ablation studies. Nevertheless, as an early effort in reconsidering the action space
536
      in RLHF, our experiments are currently confined to the 3.8B Phi-3 series model, PPO training on a
537
      free-form dialog-alike dataset, and instruction-following benchmark evaluations. Our future work
538   includes scaling up to even larger LMs, testing our method on other types of tasks such as math
539   problem solving and code generation, and applying to other RL algorithms, such as REINFORCE.

981    Algorithm 1 Training and Utilizing Our Segment-level Reward.
982
           Input: Binary preference dataset Dpref = {(x, y w , y l )} for reward model training, prompt set
983        Dpol = {x} for policy learning, supervised fine-tuned model πSFT , reward model training steps
984        Mrew , LM policy training steps Mpol , entropy cutoff cent , temperature τ for the aggregation
985        function, KL coefficient β for RLHF PPO training.

987        Initialization: Initialize the segment-level reward model rϕ and LM policy πθ from πSFT , fix
988        the aggregation function f (·) as the soft-maximum in Eq. (4), initialize other components in the
989        off-the-shelf RLHF PPO routine as specified.
990
991
           // Training the segment-level reward model

1309   Learning-from-preference. Learning-from-preference classically takes a two-stage approach
1310   where a reward model is first trained on a dataset of binary or multiple ranking via maximizing the
1311   choice model likelihood (Bradley & Terry, 1952; Plackett, 1975; Luce, 2012), before optimizing the
1312   RL/control policy against the learned reward model by RL algorithms (Akrour et al., 2011; 2012;
1313   Fürnkranz et al., 2012). Earlier application in deep learning mainly focuses on relatively simple
1314   neural-network policy for robotics/control tasks (Christiano et al., 2017; Ibarz et al., 2018; Bıyık
1315   et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2019; 2020; Lee et al., 2021; Shin et al., 2021; Hejna & Sadigh, 2023a;b).
1316   Implanting its success in robotics, in natural language generation, this two-stage learning-from-
1317
       preference paradigm has been scaled up and popularized in the post-training stage to align LMs
       with specific human values, with applications ranging from text summarization (Ziegler et al., 2019;
1318
       Stiennon et al., 2020), prompt generation (Yang et al., 2023), to (task-oriented) conversational agent
1319
       (e.g., Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022a; Menick et al., 2022; Feng et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023).
1320
1321   To alleviate the complexity in fitting an explicit reward model, motivated by the theory of maximum-
1322   entropy control and RL (Ziebart et al., 2008; Ziebart, 2010; Finn et al., 2016), direct preference
1323
       optimization methods (DPO, e.g., Rafailov et al., 2023; Tunstall et al., 2023; Azar et al., 2023; Yuan
       et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Ethayarajh et al., 2024; Yin et al., 2024) were recently proposed to
1324
       directly train LMs on a preference dataset by using their log-density-ratio as the classification logit,
1325
       which have been adapted to train text-to-image diffusion models (e.g., Wallace et al., 2023; Yang
1326   et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b; Gu et al., 2024).
1327
1328   In this paper, we contribute to the literature of learning-from-preference by re-thinking a suitable
1329
       definition of action space in the RL formulation of LM generation and preference alignment. Moti-
       vated by semantic completeness in linguistics, we define each action as “text segment”, spanning
1330
       across a small amount of tokens and interpolating between prior works’ action space of either the
1331
       finest “per token” or the coarsest “full sequence”. With this design, our method may benefit from
1332   both denser reward signal for easier RL-based LM training and the semantic completeness of each
1333   action for more accurate training guidance, as experimentally verified in Section 4.
1334
1335
       Training Signals for RL-based Language Model (LM) Training. In RL-based LM fine-tuning, a
       classical training signal for adapting LMs to the specific downstream task is the native trajectory-
1336
       level downstream test metrics (e.g., Ryang & Abekawa, 2012; Ranzato et al., 2015; Rennie et al.,
1337
       2017; Paulus et al., 2017; Shu et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2022). This approach intrinsically uses a bandit
1338   formulation of LM generation that treats the entire generated sequence as a single action. As discussed
1339   in Section 1, ignoring the sequential nature of LM generation, this bandit training signal delays
1340   the feedback to each token/phrase selection, and can thus incur optimization difficulty (Guo et al.,
1341   2022; Snell et al., 2022). With various forms of stronger data or compute requirements, task-specific
1342   per-step training signals have been proposed to mitigate this sparse reward issue. Assuming abundant
1343   golden expert data for supervised (pre-)training, Shi et al. (2018) construct per-step reward via inverse
1344   RL (Russell, 1998); Guo et al. (2018) use a hierarchical approach; Yang et al. (2018) learn LM
1345   discriminators; Lin et al. (2017) and Yu et al. (2017) use the expensive and high-variance Monte
1346
       Carlo rollout to estimate per-step reward from a sequence-level adversarial reward function trained in
       the first place; while Le et al. (2022) use some rule-based intermediate training signal derived from
1347
       the oracle sequence-level evaluation, without explicitly learning per-step reward.
1348
1349   Similarly, in RLHF, to move forward from the classical bandit formulation, methods have recently
       been proposed to ground sparse preference labels into dense per-step feedback, with applications


                                                          25
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1350
       in task-oriented dialog systems (e.g., Ramachandran et al., 2021; Feng et al., 2023) and variable-
1351   length text-sequence generation (Yang et al., 2023). Our paper seeks to reconcile dense v.s. sparse
1352   training signal in RLHF by distributing feedback to the level of semantically complete “text segment”,
1353   interpolating between the densest “token level” and the sparsest “sequence level” and ideally getting
1354   the benefit of both worlds: easier RL training and accurate optimization signal. Meanwhile, as seen
1355   in Section 2, our method adheres to the classical two-stage RLHF paradigm without requiring extra
1356   data or compute.
1357   Other LM Preference Alignment Methods. Apart from RL methods, strategies have been
1358   developed to align LMs with preference by adding external filters on top of the pretrained LMs,
1359   for safety checking the generations or the training texts (e.g., Xu et al., 2020). Vanilla maximum
1360   likelihood estimation has also been adopted for training LMs on curated datasets (Hancock et al.,
1361   2019; Solaiman & Dennison, 2021; Scheurer et al., 2022), or instruction fine-tuning LMs on massive
1362   highly-curated sets of tasks (Sanh et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2022). With extra requirements on data,
1363   modelling, and/or compute, recent LM works also conduct preference alignment by formulating text
1364   generation as a constraint satisfaction problem on LM’s generation distribution (e.g., Khalifa et al.,
1365
       2021; Korbak et al., 2022; Go et al., 2023), or utilizing the preference dataset in LMs’ pre-training
       stage (Korbak et al., 2023) .
1366
1367   In this paper, we seek to refine RL-based LM preference alignment by re-thinking the suitable action
1368   space in the RL formulation that allows both denser immediate feedback while not jeopardizing the
1369   feedback accuracy. Our segment-level design is validated through numeric and example in Section 4.

       To center the assigned rewards from the reward model and reduce their variance, in most open-sourced
1376
       (bandit) RLHF PPO implementation (e.g., Havrilla et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2024), the bandit reward of
1377   the newly sampled response y is first “Z-score” normalized, before being fed into the PPO routine.
1378   Concretely, for the prompt x and sampled response y, the bandit reward rϕ (x, y) is normalized as
1379   rϕ (x, y) ← (rϕ (x, y) − µ)/σ, where µ and σ are respectively the mean and standard deviation of
1380   (bandit) rewards in the reward calibration dataset. The PPO routine starts by using this normalized
1381   rϕ (x, y), e.g., first subtract it by the KL regularization, and then calculate the advantage estimates
1382   and value function training target, etc.
1383   For the segment-level action space, we will then need to normalize the reward rϕ (st , at ) for each
1384   segment at . As shown in Table 2 (“Global Statistics of All”), the most intuitive idea of simply using
1385   the global mean and standard deviation over all segment-level rewards in the reward calibration
1386   dataset does not train a good LM. Looking into the responses sampled in PPO training and in the
1387   reward calibration dataset, we find that, for example, the beginning segments of the responses are
1388   typically greeting alike phases that are less informational and/or essential to respond to the given
1389   prompt, and hence have relatively lower rewards. If we normalize the segment-level rewards of
1390   those early segments by the global mean and standard deviation, those normalized rewards will be
1391
       significantly negative, rather than centered around 0. This will undesirably refrain the generation of
       necessary greeting alike phases, resulting in an “impolite LM” and thus inferior benchmark results.
1392
       More generally, the linguistic structure of the responses leads to certain correlation between the
1393
       mean and standard deviation of segment-level reward values and the location of segment in the
1394   response, e.g., in the early/middle/later part. This observation motivates us to design location-aware
1395   reward normalizers that can approximately capture the reward statistics at an arbitrary location of the
1396   response, so that the normalized segment-level rewards can be more centered and less varying. It is
1397   important to have proper reward normalizers at an arbitrary location of the response, because the
1398   response sampled in PPO training will have a stochastic total length, nondeterministic number of
1399   segments, and less-controllable length of each segment. These considerations motivate our design of
1400   the regression-based reward normalizer functions in Section 2.3.
