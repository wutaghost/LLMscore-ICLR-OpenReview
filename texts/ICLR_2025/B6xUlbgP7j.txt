011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            This research investigates consumer neuroscience and neuromarketing through
014            a multivariate methodology, employing Principal Component Analysis (PCA)
015
               and deep learning neural networks to interpret consumer responses to functional
               products. EEG signals were collected, recorded, and analyzed from 16 individuals
016
               aged 20 to 29 to identify significant neuronal markers related to consumer choices.
017
               The pivotal factors influencing decision-making were identified as the low beta
018            and low gamma frequency bands, as well as participants’ attention and meditation
019            levels. The findings validate the effectiveness of our approach, demonstrating
020            its applicability across various fields requiring accurate and reliable classification.
021            Additionally, it is recommended to explore the potential applications of this study in
022            the food industry by creating personalized nutrition strategies based on individuals’
023            brain activity patterns.
024            Keywords: Decision-Making, PCA, DCNN and Neuromarketing.

243   2.3.4   PASS -BAND F ILTERING
244
      In terms of performance, filters can be classified as high-pass, low-pass, band-pass, all-pass, and reject-
245
      all filters. The frequency range that they allow to pass is known as the passband, and the frequency
246
      range that they do not allow to pass is known as the stopband. An ideal filter is one that completely
247   rejects signals whose frequencies are not in the interval for which it was designed; unfortunately,
248   such types of filter do not exist because of the physical limitations of the components with which they
249   are manufactured. The response of an ideal filter can be approximated by mathematical functions,
250   including Butterworth [4], Chebyshev [26], and elliptic filters [24]. In this manner, it can be stated
251   that a band-pass filter is a melding of a low-pass filter and a high-pass filter. Therefore, we apply a
252   band pass filtering processes, that is, from a RAW time series in ε0−60 , we generate two time series
253   for the two bands whose behavior was observed to be the most relevant in ∆. In other words, we
254   created a filtered time series that contained only the band β or frequencies ranging from 12 to 21 Hz,
255
      and another filtered time series containing only the band γ or the frequency range between 30 and
      45 Hz.

      3.4   D ISCUSSION
487
488   Principal component analysis (PCA) as a preprocessing step before training Deep Convolutional
489   Neural Networks (DCNN) addresses several challenges. PCA reduces data dimensionality while
490   preserving essential variance, crucial for high-dimensional EEG signal processing [12]. It extracts
491   significant neuronal metrics (for example, low beta β and gamma γ frequency bands, attention and
492   meditation levels) from EEG datasets [23]. The application of PCA before DCNN, compared to raw
493   EEG/facial data. PCA reduces overfitting in DCNN, improves training by removing noise and irrele-
494   vant features, and improves performance in decoding consumer behavior [21]. Incorporating PCA
495
      before DCNN simplifies high-dimensional data, increasing computational efficiency and reducing
      resource utilization without compromising predictive power or accuracy [18]. In addition, it improves
496
      the interpretation of EEG signals, crucial in neuromarketing to uncover the neural mechanisms behind
497
      consumer decision making [15]. The reason for using PCA as the main approach is that it is the most
498   widely used method among neuroscience studies when it comes to dimensionality reduction and
499   feature extraction. Principal component analysis (PCA) allows the discovery of important components
500   that contain most of the variance in the data, which is also useful because EEG signals are highly
501   complex. Although deeper machine learning approaches, for example, t-SNE, autoencoders, could
502   provide additional insights, setting a strong baseline is needed and have interpretable results. Future
503   versions of this study may integrate more advanced algorithms to investigate the possibility of im-
504   proved classification ability and insights. In conclusion, the combination of PCA and DCNN uses the
505   strengths of both techniques, ensuring a powerful and interpretable model. This approach improves
506
      robustness and applicability, aligning with the development of precise and reliable classification
      systems, particularly in personalized nutrition strategies for the food sector. With these compelling
507
      results, we firmly establish that BRAIN has the unparalleled ability not only to categorize the nuanced
508
      flavors of the functional product, but also to discern the personal preferences of individuals with
509   remarkable precision, achieving a classification error less than 10%. This milestone unequivocally
510   underscores the robustness and precision of our system, making an indelible mark on the future of
511   personalized consumer experiences.

      4     C ONCLUSIONS
515
516
      This study illustrates how brain activity can identify participants’ food preferences. EEG analysis
      indicated that EEG effectively assesses taste preferences. By analyzing EEG data, we identified
517
      whether participants liked or disliked particular food samples. The key decision-making factors were
518
      low beta and low gamma frequency bands, along with Attention and Meditation levels. These data
519   are crucial for the food industry, providing valuable insights for companies aiming to enhance the
520   attractiveness of functional foods. Analyzing brain signals in response to tastes allows researchers
521   to identify preferred flavors for individuals. The Low Beta (β) and Low Gamma (γ) bands are
522   significant in the decision-making process. The results of the initial DCNN model test, excluding β
523   and γ, highlight challenges and limitations. The difficulties encountered underscore the complexity
524   of the categorization task, necessitating further refinement. Ultimately, our DCNN model exhibited
525   remarkable performance with β and γ included. This includes a ROC curve with an AUC of 0.95, an
526   F1 score of 0.95, and a confusion matrix with values exceeding 0.91. These findings indicate our
527   approach’s effectiveness and suitability for precise and reliable classification across various fields.
528   This study gives some interesting insights into consumer neuroscience and neuromarketing, but there
529   are also limitations. The sample size of 16 participants aged 20 to 29 may not represent diverse
530   consumer preferences. The proposed architecture itself needed to be assessed for efficiency given
531
      the relatively small sample size. It was thus compared against the baseline performance of popular
      models like EfficientNet, ResNet and VGG16. The proposed architecture outperformed these models
532
      by at least 5% as shown in the demonstration. Some good here use of transfer learning suited for
533
      functional products (category specific emotional experiences) Positive emotions such as Happiness,
534   Neutrality, Sadness and Surprise were associated with Like, whereas Negative emotions such as
535   Dislike occurred less frequently. These findings highlight the complex emotional drivers in consumer
536   perception and suggest that both sensory attributes and psychological factors should be taken into
537   account for product design. Future work includes the integration of additional mostly biosignals,
538   such as electrocardiograms in elaborate programming architectures utilizing distributed or parallel
539   computing. It opens the door to integrating diverse biosignals and high-resolution qEEG imaging, a
      major advancement in the processing and analysis of these biosignals.
