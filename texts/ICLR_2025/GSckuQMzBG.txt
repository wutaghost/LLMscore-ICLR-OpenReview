009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            While the field of inverse graphics has been witnessing continuous growth, tech-
012            niques devised thus far predominantly focus on learning individual scene represen-
013            tations. In contrast, learning large sets of scenes has been a considerable bottleneck
014            in NeRF developments, as repeatedly applying inverse graphics on a sequence of
015            scenes, though essential for various applications, remains largely prohibitive in
016            terms of resource costs. We introduce a framework termed “scaled inverse graph-
017
               ics”, aimed at efficiently learning large sets of scene representations, and propose
               a novel method to this end. It operates in two stages: (i) training a compression
018
               model on a subset of scenes, then (ii) training NeRF models on the resulting smaller
019
               representations, thereby reducing the optimization space per new scene. In practice,
020            we compact the representation of scenes by learning NeRFs in a latent space to
021            reduce the image resolution, and sharing information across scenes to reduce NeRF
022            representation complexity. We experimentally show that our method presents both
023            the lowest training time and memory footprint in scaled inverse graphics compared
024            to other methods applied independently on each scene. Our codebase is publicly
025            available as open-source.

048   The inverse graphics problem has proven to be a challenging quest in the domain of Computer Vision.
049   While many methods have historically emerged (Cohen & Szeliski, 2014; Park et al., 2019; Niemeyer
050   et al., 2020), particularly following the introduction of Neural Radiance Fields (Mildenhall et al.,
051   2020, NeRF), the question has mostly remained unchanged: how to model an object or scene, using
052   only its captured images? While this question continues to be an active area of research, our work
053   targets a scaled version of the original problem. In this paper, we introduce “scaled inverse graphics”
      as the task of concurrently applying inverse graphics over a set of numerous scenes.

054
      We identify scaled inverse graphics as an increasingly prominent challenge in recent works. Notably,
055   works tackling 3D generative models (Shue et al., 2023; Müller et al., 2023; Erkoç et al., 2023;
056   Liu et al., 2024) typically require the creation of NeRF datasets, which serve as a prerequisite for
057   training. This is prohibitive, as creating large-scale datasets of implicit scene representations entails
058   significant computational costs. This problem also emerges in practical applications, where efficiently
059   scaling inverse graphics unlocks new ways in which 3D modeling techniques could be leveraged (e.g.
060   modeling product inventories). While this problem has traditionally been tackled in a naive manner
061   where scenes are independently learned, we propose a more efficient alternative to learning scenes in
062   isolation, thereby reducing overall computational costs, without compromising rendering quality.
063   In this paper, we present a novel technique that addresses scaled inverse graphics. We adopt Tri-Plane
064   representations (Chan et al., 2022b), as they are efficient and lightweight representations that are
065   well-suited for the framework of scaled inverse graphics, and as they have been the primary choice of
066   representations when learning NeRF datasets. Our primary objective is to compact the information
067   required to learn individual scenes when learning large sets of scenes. To this end, we propose a
068   Micro-Macro decomposition of Tri-Planes that splits learned features into shared features modeling
069
      general information about the scenes in the dataset, and scene-specific features. Concurrently,
      we learn our scenes in a 3D latent space, thereby alleviating the NeRF rendering bottleneck, and
070
      accelerating our training. Our method operates in two stages. In the first stage, we train on a subset
071
      of scenes the compression model, comprised of the autoencoder and the shared (Macro) Tri-Planes
072   features. In the second stage, we utilize these trained components to learn the remaining scenes on
073   smaller representations, thereby reducing the optimization space per scene.
074
075
      We conduct extensive experiments to evaluate our method in terms of both resource costs and
      rendering quality when learning large sets scenes, and compare it against the current naive baseline.
076
      We further provide an expansive illustration of the resource costs of our method, alongside an ablation
077
      study, hence justifying our design choices. As illustrated in Fig. 1, our method presents both the
078   lowest training time and memory footprint in scaled inverse graphics as compared to other methods
079   applied independently on each scene, all while demonstrating comparable NVS quality to Tri-Planes.
080
081
      A summary of our contribution can be found below:
082          • We identify the problem of scaled inverse graphics and address it through a novel method,
083
             • We present a novel Micro-Macro decomposition that captures common structures across
084
               scenes in shared representations,
085
086          • We propose a two-stage training approach that compacts scene representations and enables
087
               efficient large-scale scene learning,
088          • We conduct extensive experiments showing that our method presents the lowest resource
089            costs in scaled inverse graphics while maintaining comparable rendering quality, and justify

               our design choices through an ablation study

092   2    R ELATED W ORK
093
094   NeRF resource reductions. Neural Radiance Fields (Mildenhall et al., 2020, NeRF) achieve
095   impressive performances on the task of Novel View Synthesis (NVS) by adopting a purely implicit
096   representation to model scenes. Following the introduction of NeRFs, several methods have been
097   proposed to improve upon training times and memory costs. Barron et al. (2021; 2022) achieve
098   exceptional quality while requiring low memory capacity to store scenes, as they represent scenes
099
      through the weights of neural networks. This however comes with the downside of high training and
      rendering times due to bottlenecks in volume rendering. To alleviate these issues, some works trade-
100
      off compute time for memory usage by explicitly storing proxy features for the emitted radiances and
101
      densities in 3D structures (e.g. voxel-based representations (Sun et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022; Yu
102   et al., 2021; Müller et al., 2022) or plane-based representations (Chan et al., 2022a; Fridovich-Keil
103   et al., 2023; Cao & Johnson, 2023)). Kerbl et al. (2023) and Fridovich-Keil et al. (2022) completely
104   forgo neural networks, achieving real-time rendering but at high memory costs. While previous works
105   have primarily focused on reducing resource costs when learning individual scenes, we propose a
106   method that presents both the lowest training time and memory footprint when learning large sets of
107   scenes, while maintaining rendering quality comparable to that of our base representation. This is
      partly done by utilizing parts of our pipeline to learn base features that are shared among scenes and

108
      utilized in the second stage of our training, which has proven to be advantageous in previous works
109   (Dupont et al., 2022; Tancik et al., 2021).
110
111   Latent NeRFs. Latent NeRFs extend NeRFs to render latent image representations in the latent
112   space of an auto-encoder. Several recent work have utilized Latent NeRFs for 3D generation (Metzer
113   et al., 2023; Seo et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023; Chan et al., 2023), scene editing (Khalid et al., 2023; Park
114   et al., 2024), and scene modeling (Aumentado-Armstrong et al., 2023). However, as latent spaces are
115   not directly compatible with NeRF learning, previous works have resorted to special scene-dependent
116   adaptations, which prevent the concurrent modeling of numerous scenes within a common latent
117   space. In a separate contribution, Anonymous (2024) propose an Inverse Graphics Autoencoder
118   (IG-AE) that embeds a universal 3D-aware latent space compatible with latent NeRF training. While
119
      our method is agnostic to the chosen latent space, we build upon the IG-AE architecture to train a
      3D-aware latent space that is adapted for scaled inverse graphics, as it currently stands as the only
120
      available approach to build NeRF-compatible 3D-aware latent spaces. Accordingly, we adapt the
121
      Latent NeRF Training Pipeline to train our decomposed representations in the 3D-aware latent space.

124   3     M ETHOD
125
126   In this section, we present our method for tackling the scaled inverse graphics problem. We start
127
      by presenting Tri-Planes (Chan et al., 2022b) and our Micro-Macro Tri-Planes decomposition that
      allows to compact information. This is done by learning a set of base representations that is shared
128
      across scenes (Section 3.1). Next, we present our full training strategy to tackle scaled inverse
129
      graphics (Section 3.2). Our approach consists of learning our Micro-Macro decomposed Tri-Planes
130   in a 3D-aware latent space. It operates in two stages. The first stage carries out the computationally
131   intensive task of learning the 3D-aware latent space, while jointly training a subset of scenes and our
132   shared base representations. The second stage benefits from the reduced computational costs enabled
133   after the first stage to learn the remaining scenes.
134
      We denote S = {S1 , ..., SN } a large set of N scenes drawn from a common distribution. Each scene
135
      Si = {(xi,j , pi,j )}Vj=1 consists of V posed views. Here, xi,j and pi,j respectively denote the j-th
136
      view and pose of the i-th scene Si . We denote T = {T1 , ..., TN } the set of scene representations
137   modeling the scenes in S. We subdivide S and T into two subsets (S1 , S2 ) and (T1 , T2 ) at random,
138   respectively containing N1 and N2 scenes, with N1 < N2 .
139
140   3.1   M ICRO -M ACRO T RI -P LANES D ECOMPOSITION
141
142   Tri-Plane representations (Chan et al., 2022a) are explicit-implicit scene representations enabling
143   scene modeling in three axis-aligned orthogonal feature planes, each of resolution K × K with feature
144   dimension F . To query a 3D point x ∈ R3 , it is projected onto each of the three planes to retrieve
145   bilineraly interpolated feature vectors. These feature vectors are then aggregated via summation and
146
      passed into a small neural network with parameters α to retrieve the corresponding color and density,
      which are then used for volume rendering (Kajiya & Von Herzen, 1984).
147
148   We adopt Tri-Plane representations due to their efficient and lightweight architectures, as well as as
149   their widespread use in previous works for constructing NeRF datasets (Shue et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
150   2024). Additionally, the explicit nature of Tri-Planes enables their modularity, an essential property
151   for our Micro-Macro decomposition. While Tri-Planes are traditionally used to model scenes in the
152
      RGB space, we utilize them to learn scenes in the latent space of an auto-encoder, defined by an
      encoder Eϕ and a decoder Dψ . Given a camera pose p, we render a latent Tri-Plane Ti as follows:
153
154                         z̃i,j = Rα (Ti , pj ) ,                      x̃i,j = Dψ (z̃i,j ) ,                 (1)
155   where Rα is the Tri-Plane renderer with trainable parameters α, z̃i,j is the rendered latent image, and
156   x̃i,j is the corresponding decoded rendering.
157

158
      splits Tri-Planes into scene-specific features, and features representing global structures. As such,
159
      we decompose Tri-Plane representations Ti into “Micro” planes Timic integrating scene specific
160   information, and “Macro” planes Timac that encompass global information, as follows:

      4.2   A BLATIONS
454
455   To justify our choices and explore further, we present an ablation study of our method, for which the
456   results are presented in Table 3. The first ablation, “Ours-Micro”, eliminates the Micro-Macro de-
457   composition, and consequently global information sharing (i.e. F mac = 0, F mic = F ). This ablation
458   showcases a slight degradation of quality as compared to our full method, but more importantly, it
459   would result in higher resource costs as it eliminates the shared base representations and requires
460   more learnable features per scene. The second ablation, “Ours-Macro”, eliminates local features
461   from Tri-Planes and relies only on global features (i.e. F mic = 0, F mac = F ). This setting also
462
      showcases a degradation in NVS quality as compared to our method, as it only relies on shared
      planes to represent individual scenes. The third ablation, “Ours-M = 1” reduces the set of shared
463
      planes B to one Tri-Plane. This ablation demonstrates NVS performances that are slightly higher
464
      than Ours-Micro, but still lower than our method, highlighting the necessity for a set of global planes.
465   The fourth ablation, “Ours-RGB” ablates the latent space and trains Micro-Macro decomposed
466   Tri-Planes in the RGB space. It also presents decreased performances as compared to our method,
467   and thus highlighting the advantage of doing our Micro-Macro decomposition on latent scenes. Note
468   that ablating the latent space as well as information sharing is equivalent to the vanilla “Tri-Planes
469   (RGB)” setting, which presents a comparable rendering quality with respect to our method, with
470   significantly higher resource costs.

      5     C ONCLUSION
473
474
      In this paper, we introduce scaled inverse graphics and recognize the necessity for methods that
475   efficiently tackle this problem. We propose a novel method that learns scenes in a custom 3D-aware
476   latent space, and uses a novel Micro-Macro Tri-Plane decomposition that compacts the represen-
477   tation of individual scenes by adopting a set of shared representations. Our method demonstrates
478   significantly lower training time and memory costs in scaled inverse graphics as compared to recent
479   methods, while maintaining a comparable rendering quality. We consider this work to be an initial
480   step in the direction of efficiently scaling inverse graphics methods.
