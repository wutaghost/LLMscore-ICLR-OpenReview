                                                 A BSTRACT
013
014            In recent years, SIGN SGD has garnered interest as both a practical optimizer as
015            well as a simple model to understand adaptive optimizers like A DAM. Though
016            there is a general consensus that SIGN SGD acts to precondition optimization and
017            reshapes noise, quantitatively understanding these effects in theoretically solv-
018            able settings remains difficult. We present an analysis of SIGN SGD in a high
019            dimensional limit, and derive a limiting SDE and ODE to describe the risk. Us-
020
               ing this framework we quantify four effects of SIGN SGD: effective learning rate,
               noise compression, diagonal preconditioning, and gradient noise reshaping. Our
021
               analysis is consistent with experimental observations but moves beyond that by
022
               quantifying the dependence of these effects on the data and noise distributions.
023            We conclude with a conjecture on how these results might be extended to A DAM.
024

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      The success of deep learning has been driven by the effectiveness of relatively simple stochastic
030   optimization algorithms. Stochastic gradient descent (SGD) with momentum can be used to train
031   models like ResNet50 with minimal hyperparameter tuning. The workhorse of modern machine
032   learning is A DAM, which was designed to give an approximation of preconditioning with a diago-
033   nal, online approximation of the Fisher information matrix (Kingma, 2014). Additional hypotheses
034   for the success of A DAM include its ability to maintain balanced updates to parameters across layers
035   and its potential noise-mitigating effects (Zhang et al., 2020b; 2024). Getting a quantitative, theo-
036   retical understanding of Adam and its variants is hindered by their complexity. While the multiple
037   exponential moving averages are easy to implement, they complicate analysis.
038   The practical desire for simpler, more efficient learning algorithms as well as the theoretical desire
039   for simpler models to analyze have led to a resurgence in the study of SIGN SGD. SIGN SGD is a
040   variant of SGD where the stochastic gradient is passed through the sign function σ, leading to an
041   update vector of ±1s. On average, SIGN SGD’s updates at every step have positive dot product with
042   the average SGD step, but it can have dramatically different convergence properties (Bernstein et al.,
043   2018a; Karimireddy et al., 2019). Multiple studies point towards sign-based methods as an effective
044
      proxy given that the sign component of the gradient has been shown to play an important role in
      A DAM (Kunstner et al., 2023; Balles & Hennig, 2018; Bernstein et al., 2018b). SIGN SGD is also
045
      the basis for new practical methods; the L ION algorithm (Chen et al., 2023) combines SIGN SGD
046
      with multiple exponential moving averages, and SIGN SGD + momentum was used to train LLMs
047   with performance comparable to A DAM (Zhao et al., 2024).
048
049   Despite the promise of SIGN SGD, a detailed quantitative understanding of its dynamics in realis-
050
      tic settings remain elusive—in particular the nature of the preconditioning and the effect of the σ
      function on the noise are not well understood. A crucial first step is to understand these effects on
051
      quadratic optimization problems.
052
053   Motivated by these questions, we provide the first analysis of the learning dynamics of SIGN SGD
      in a high-dimensional stochastic setting (Section 2). We make the following contributions:

481
482   Finally, there is gradient noise reshaping, wherein the SGD gradient noise matrix K is replaced by
483   the matrix Kσ up to constants. This is a complicated mapping, and there is no short answer about
484   the impact of this replacement. In Figure 4, we show a simulation of the spectra illustrating that for
485   CIFAR10, a practical, non-diagonal dataset, passing from K → Kσ might affect the magnitudes of
      the eigenvalues but not their structure.

      In the case that K itself is a sample covariance matrix, the matrix Kσ is strongly related to a Kernel
487   inner product matrix, for which there is a large literature. This includes properties of bulk spectra
488   (Karoui, 2010; Cheng & Singer, 2013), norms (Fan & Montanari, 2019) and more.
489
490
      When K is a diagonal matrix then Kσ = π2 I and so this can be considered a type of preconditioning
      of the gradient noise, albeit with a more aggressive preconditioner than D.
491
492   We expect that for power-law type covariances, in which K has powerlaw spectral dependence and
493   which are often seen in practice (e.g. in Figure 4), in language embeddings, and in image and video
494   datasets, Kσ again has powerlaw spectra of the same exponent. Beyond the spectral distribution,
495   replacing K by Kσ may also serve to slightly break the alignment of large directions of gradient
496
      variance from large gradient biases (they are perfectly aligned in SGD), which should be beneficial
      both to stability of the algorithm and performance.
