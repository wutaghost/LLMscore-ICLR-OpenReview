009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper presents a comprehensive analysis of a broad range of variations of the
012            stochastic proximal point method (SPPM). Proximal point methods have attracted
013            considerable interest owing to their numerical stability and robustness against
014            imperfect tuning, a trait not shared by the dominant stochastic gradient descent
015            (SGD) algorithm. A framework of assumptions that we introduce encompasses
016
               methods employing techniques such as variance reduction and arbitrary sampling.
               A cornerstone of our general theoretical approach is a parametric assumption on
017
               the iterates, correction and control vectors. We establish a single theorem that en-
018
               sures linear convergence under this assumption and the µ-strong convexity of the
019            loss function, and without the need to invoke smoothness. This integral theorem
020            reinstates best known complexity and convergence guarantees for several existing
021            methods which demonstrate the robustness of our approach. We expand our study
022            by developing three new variants of SPPM, and through numerical experiments
023            we elucidate various properties inherent to them.

113

114

116   progressively acquire knowledge of the stochastic gradients at the optimum and mitigate the influ-
117   ence of gradient noise. Consequently, for strongly convex f , these variance-reduced methods exhibit
118   linear convergence towards x⋆ with a fixed step size (see a survey by Gower et al. (2020)). In the
119   discussion above we concluded that SPPM has advantages over SGD. This motivated Khaled and
120   Jin (2023) to explore variance-reduced variants of SPPM. They proposed the SVRP algorithm with

158   3    C ONTRIBUTIONS
159
160   Numerous efficient adaptations of the SPPM algorithm have emerged, each with its specific ap-
161   plications. Our research stems from the absence of a comprehensive and universally applicable
      theory. While some connections among existing methods have been established (as demonstrated


                                                       3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      by Traoré et al. (2023), who link the classical SPPM method with its variance-reduced counterparts),
163   a cohesive theoretical framework without the smoothness assumption is missing. Understanding the
164   connections among various algorithms rooted in SPPM is becoming increasingly challenging for the
165   community, both in theory and practical applications. While new variants are yet to be discovered,
166   determining concrete principles beyond intuition to guide their discovery remains challenging. Com-
167   plicating matters further is the use of various assumptions regarding the correction vectors across
168   different literature, each with differing levels of rigor. Key contributions of this study comprise:
169   • A universal algorithm. We design a universal SPPM-LC algorithm (stochastic proximal point
170   method with learned correction, Algorithm 1) that encompasses 7 variants of stochastic proximal
171   point method (SPPM, SPPM-NS, SPPM-AS, SPPM*, SPPM-GC, L-SVRP and Point-SAGA; see Ta-
172   ble 1) through (random) correction vectors hk . A specific choice of correction vectors allows to re-
173   trieve any particular of the mentioned algorithms. Motivated by the work of Gorbunov et al. (2020),
174   a similar universal algorithm was proposed by Traoré et al. (2023), but under different assumptions.
175   • Comprehensive analysis. We introduce a cohesive theoretical framework of assumptions that
176   includes three restrictions imposed on correction vectors hk of the SPPM-LC algorithm (Assump-
177   tion 7). These restrictions connect the correction vectors hk to the iterates of the algorithm and
178   have the forms of parametric recursions. The development of this framework constitutes one of
179   our major contributions. Under Assumption 1, Assumption 2 on the functions and Assumption 7
180   on the correction vectors, we analyze the convergence of SPPM-LC in the case when it is applied
181   to find an ε-accurate solution of the general stochastic optimization problem (1). This is the first
182   comprehensive analysis of the original, sampling-based, and variance-reduced versions of SPPM.
183
      It implies the convergence results for all 7 variations of stochastic proximal point method (SPPM,
      SPPM-NS, SPPM-AS, SPPM*, SPPM-GC, L-SVRP and Point-SAGA). The sampling-based methods
184
      SPPM-NS and SPPM-AS are employed for a less general setting (3), as well as the variance-reduced
185
      method Point SAGA, they are analyzed under Assumption 3 and Assumption 4 on the functions. In
186   addition, the analysis of L-SVRP is based on the similarity Assumption 5, and the analysis of Point-
187   SAGA is based on Assumption 6. In order to obtain the convergence result for any particular of the
188   mentioned algorithms, one needs to check that (the recursive parametric) Assumption 7 holds for it.
189   Traoré et al. (2023) introduce a slightly different theoretical framework of assumptions and analyze
190   SPPM and its variance-reduced versions in a finite-sum setting (3) in another setup when functions
191   are individually convex and L-smooth.
192   • Best known rates retrieved. The rates derived from our comprehensive Theorem 1, under As-
193   sumption 1, Assumption 2 on the functions for SPPM, SPPM*, SPPM-GC, L-SVRP (under additional
194   Assumption 5) and Point-SAGA (under additional Assumption 6); under Assumption 3 and Assump-
195   tion 4 on the functions for SPPM-NS, SPPM-AS, represent the sharpest rates for these methods.
196   Notably, the neighborhood that we guarantee for SPPM in our analysis does not blow up for the
197   stepsize γ → ∞ in comparison to the result of Khaled and Jin (2023) (see (21) in Theorem 2 and
198   Commentary 2 after it). Also, we present the analysis for L-SVRP in a simplified way and obtain
199   slightly better bounds on the iteration complexity up to a constant factor than Khaled and Jin (2023).
200   • Analysis under general similarity assumptions. The analysis of L-SVRP is based on the sim-
201   ilarity Assumption 5, and the analysis of Point-SAGA is based on Assumption 6. Both of these
202   assumptions are very general and distinguish our approach from the previous ones. Defazio (2016)
203   analyzed Point SAGA under the individual smoothness assumption, which implies Assumption 6.
204   Khaled and Jin (2023) analyzed L-SVRP under a more restrictive similarity assumption than As-
205   sumption 5. Traoré et al. (2023) analyzed L-SVRP and Point SAGA under the individual smoothness
206
      assumption, which is much more restrictive than our Assumption 5 and Assumption 6. Their results
      were obtained prior to our work, but we conducted our analysis independently: we already had our
207
      result when we discovered their paper.
208
209   • Analysis in a general setting. We analyze L-SVRP in the stochastic optimization setting (1) while
210   previous works of Khaled and Jin (2023); Traoré et al. (2023) do this in the less general setting (3).
211   • New methods. Our comprehensive theory offers complexity bounds for a range of novel (SPPM-
212   LC, SPPM-NS, SPPM-AS, SPPM-GC, SPPM*) and upcoming variations of SPPM. It suffices to
213   confirm that Assumption 7 holds, and a complexity estimate is readily provided by Theorem 1.

356   To showcase the efficiency of our comprehensive framework, we develop three new variants of

358
      Appendix.
359
360   SPPM-NS (Algorithm 3). The method is designed for solving the problem (3). Let positive numbers
361   p1 , . . . , pn sum up to 1, set ik = i with probability pi , i ∈ [n]. The step of the method has the
362   form xk+1 = prox npγ fi (xk ) . It unifies several powerful sampling strategies (as, e.g., importance
                               i k

363   sampling). Sampling allows to improve the convergence rate and modify the neighborhood.
364
      SPPM-AS (Algorithm 4). The method is also designed for the problem (3). The arbitrary sampling
365   framework was developed for SGD by Gower et al. (2019). It allows to conduct a sharp unified
366   convergence analysis for various effective sampling and mini-batch strategies. For strongly convex
367   functions, the method with constant stepsize converges linearly to the neighborhood of the solution.
368
      SPPM* (Algorithm 5). This novel algorithm links conventional and variance-reduced SPPM meth-
369
      ods. Although not immediately practical, it offers valuable insights into the inner workings of
370
      variance reduction. This method addresses the fundamental question: assuming that the gradients
371   ∇fξ (x⋆ ) are available, can they be leveraged to devise a more potent variant of SPPM? The affir-
372   mative answer culminates in the development of SPPM*. The construction of updates in SPPM*
373   involves correction vectors of the form hk = ∇fξk (x⋆ ). In essence, this implies augmenting xk
374   with gradients of the same functions at the optimal point x⋆ , with respect to which the proximal
375   operator is calculated. As evidenced in Table 2, where C1 = C2 = 0, this method converges di-
376   rectly to x⋆ without oscillation, rather than converging to a neighborhood of the solution. Practical
377   variance-reduced methods operate by iteratively refining estimates of ∇fξk (x⋆ ). Notably, the term
      σk2 in the Lyapunov function of variance-reduced methods converges to zero.

381   increasingly more refined estimates of ∇fξk (x⋆ ) and is also variance reduced. As indicated in Ta-
382   ble 2, where C1 = C2 = 0, this method also converges to x⋆ , and not to some neighborhood of the
383   solution only.

482
483   Although our approach is general, we still see some limitations, open problems and several possi-
484   ble directions for future extensions. Generating a similar result in the nonconvex case continues to
485   be an unsolved challenge. Expanding Assumption 7 to incorporate iteration-dependent parameters
      A1 , B1 , C1 , A2 , B2 , C2 could facilitate the development of various novel methods as SPPM with


                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                 9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      decreasing stepsizes. Khaled and Jin (2023) explore federated learning, investigating client sam-
487   pling to enhance communication efficiency. Another way to do that is to incorporate the utilization
488   of compressed vectors exchanged between a server and the clients. This motivates the problem of
489   providing the analysis for such SPPM-type methods and incorporating them into our framework.
490   It would be interesting to build theory for algorithms with correction vectors hk with a non-zero
491   expected value and unify with our theory. Another potential avenue for future research involves of-
492   fering a comprehensive analysis of SPPM-type methods incorporating acceleration and momentum.

      Stochastic gradient descent (SGD) Robbins and Monro (1951); Nemirovski et al. (2009); Bottou
705
      (2010) is a contemporary common algorithm for solving optimization problems (1) and (3) with
706                            k+1
                                     = xk − γg k , where g k is an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator:
      updates
           k of k
                    the form xk
707   E g x = ∇f (x ). Theoretical properties of SGD are nowadays studied well in works by
708   Vaswani et al. (2019); Gower et al. (2019); Gorbunov et al. (2020); Khaled and Richtárik (2023);
709   Demidovich et al. (2023). The versatility in the design of the estimator g k has led to a develop-
710   ment of highly effective variants of SGD based on importance sampling (see works by Needell et al.
711   (2015); Zhao and Zhang (2015)), mini-batching (see a work of Konečný et al. (2016)). These strate-
712   gies are unified in the arbitrary sampling paradigm proposed by Gower et al. (2019). The iterates
713   of standard SGD as well as of its sampling variants do not converge to the optimum due to the
714
      presence of the stochastic gradient noise. The existence of this issue led to the construction of vari-
      ance reduction methods in works by Roux et al. (2012); Defazio et al. (2014); Johnson and Zhang
715
      (2013); Kovalev et al. (2020); Nguyen et al. (2017). Such methods are able to sequentially learn the
716
      stochastic gradients at the optimum which allows them to reach a linear convergence to x⋆ when
717   equipped with constant stepsize in case of strongly convex f. The work by Gorbunov et al. (2020)
718   provided a unified theory for SGD and its variance-reduced, sampling, quantization and coordinate
719   sub-sampling modifications. Problem setting (3) can also be used to describe a Federated Learning
720   setup where n is the number of workers, and fi stands for the loss dependent on the data of worker
721   i ∈ [n]. Workers compute individual stochastic gradients and aggregate them on a master node (see
722   a paper by Konečný and Richtárik (2018)). A primary bottleneck in such settings is communication.
723   To address it, many techniques are used as quantization (see works by Gupta et al. (2015); Seide
724   et al. (2014)), sparsification (see a paper by Alistarh et al. (2018)), dithering (see a work by Alistarh
725
      et al. (2017)). Many distributed optimization works employ variants of Assumption 5 to analyze
      methods that solve the problem (3) in the strongly convex case (see works by Shamir et al. (2014);
726
      Zhang and Xiao (2015); Yuan and Li (2020); Karimireddy et al. (2020)). Szlendak et al. (2022);
727
      Beznosikov and Gasnikov (2022); Panferov et al. (2024) achieve better communication complexity
728   guarantees in a Federated Learning setting under the similarity assumption employing specific quan-
729   tizers and sparsifiers. Selecting the right stepsize is a critical aspect in the implementation of SGD,
730   see the work of Bach and Moulines (2011). In Section 2 we show that the iteration complexity of
731   SGD depends on condition number κ = L        µ . If we are able to evaluate stochastic proximal operator,
732   another algorithm to use instead of SGD is stochastic proximal point method (SPPM). Its iteration
733   complexity does not depend on the smoothness constant L. Bertsekas (2011) calls it the incremen-
734   tal proximal point method and considers it for solving the problem (3), proving a convergence to
735   the neighborhood when each fi is Lipschitz. In the study by Ryu and Boyd (2016), convergence
736   rates are presented for SPPM, emphasizing its robustness against learning rate misspecification, a
737
      characteristic not exhibited by SGD. Pătraşcu and Necoara (2017) consider SPPM to solve a gen-
      eral stochastic optimization problem (1) in the convex case and provide nonasymptotic convergence
738
      guarantees. Asi and Duchi (2019) delve into the exploration of a broader method termed AProx,
739
      which includes SPPM as a particular case. Their research entails analysis of convergence rates for
740   convex functions. Variance-reduced versions of SPPM should also have advantages over their SGD
741   counterparts. It motivated Khaled and Jin (2023) to analyze L-SVRP in the finite-sum and federated
742   settings under a stronger version of Assumption 5 and strong convexity. Defazio (2016) analyzes
743   Point SAGA under the individual smoothness and strong convexity assumptions. Traoré et al. (2023)
744   consider variance-reduced methods L-SVRP and Point SAGA when each fi (x) is L-smooth, con-
745   vex, and either f (x) is convex or satisfies PŁ-condition. Kim et al. (2022) consider SPPM with
746   momentum.

1491          • One worker. Assume we have a single machine solving this problem. Moreover, assume
