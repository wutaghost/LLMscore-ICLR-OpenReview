009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Generating semantically and temporally aligned audio content in accordance with
012            video input has become a focal point for researchers, particularly following the re-
013            markable breakthrough in text-to-video generation. In this work, we aim to offer
014            insights into the video-to-audio generation paradigm, focusing on three crucial as-
015            pects: vision encoders, auxiliary embeddings, and data augmentation techniques.
016
               Beginning with a foundational model built on a simple yet surprisingly effective
               intuition, we explore various vision encoders and auxiliary embeddings through
017
               ablation studies. Employing a comprehensive evaluation pipeline that empha-
018
               sizes generation quality and video-audio synchronization alignment, we demon-
019            strate that our model exhibits state-of-the-art video-to-audio generation capabil-
020            ities. Furthermore, we provide critical insights into the impact of different data
021            augmentation methods on enhancing the generation framework’s overall capacity.
022            We showcase possibilities to advance the challenge of generating synchronized
023            audio from semantic and temporal perspectives. We hope these insights will serve
024            as a stepping stone toward developing more realistic and accurate audio-visual
025            generation models.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      We humans perceive visual and audio input as two complementary aspects of our surroundings.
030   When we witness an event, we instinctively expect to hear the corresponding sounds as we see it
031   unfold. This integrated perception of sight and sound enhances our understanding and interpretation
032   of the world around us. Modern video generation models, however, typically focus on generating
033   visual content based on a set of textual prompts (Blattmann et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2023; Bar-
034   Tal et al., 2024). These models often lack the ability to incorporate audio information, resulting in
035   outputs that appear as inferior imitations rather than realistic representations.
036   Our objective is to generate semantically and temporally-aligned audio content for a given silent
037   video. This ambitious goal is rooted in extensive research conducted in the text-to-audio (TTA)
038   domain, where researchers have proposed several robust baselines for audio generation (Kreuk et al.,
039   2022; Liu et al., 2023a; Ghosal et al., 2023). Furthermore, there have been recent attempts at video-
040   to-audio (VTA) tasks (Sheffer & Adi, 2023; Luo et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024). While these works
041   have made significant strides, they still fall short of natural and arbitrary audio generation.
042   A common trait amongst these baseline models is their utilization of non-autoregressive generation
043   frameworks, particularly Latent Diffusion Model (LDM) (Rombach et al., 2022). These models
044   generate audio content by leveraging textual or visual features as generative conditions. However,
045   the distinction between VTA and TTA presents two key challenges: 1) ensuring semantic coher-
046   ence with the input condition, and 2) ensuring temporal alignment between the generated audio
047   and the video. This is due to the necessity of accurately extracting and interpreting visual features
048   that produce sounds, as well as identifying when these features generate sounds, even if they are
049   continuously present in the scene.
050   We try to provide insights into the VTA training paradigm by focusing on three key aspects: 1) the
051   vision encoder, 2) the auxiliary embedding, and 3) the data augmentation. Vision encoder is respon-
052   sible for extracting and interpreting visual features from the input video, capturing complex visual
053   patterns that are integral to generating relevant audio content. It also has the potential to encode
      temporal information. Auxiliary embedding serves as a valuable source of additional contextual

112
113   2.1   D IFFUSION - BASED G ENERATION
114
115   The success of audio generation tasks primarily stems from TTA diffusion models. These diffusion
116   models were initially employed in image generation tasks (Ho et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol,
117   2021; Nichol et al., 2021; Rombach et al., 2022), and then extended to audio and video genera-
118   tion (Liu et al., 2023a;b; Singer et al., 2022; Blattmann et al., 2023; Brooks et al., 2024). Compared
119
      with previous works like Audiogen (Kreuk et al., 2022), which are predominantly based on re-
      gressive generative models, diffusion-based models excel in generation quality and diversity. For
120
      instance, Audioldm (Liu et al., 2023a) learns continuous audio representations in the latent space.
121
      Tango (Ghosal et al., 2023) incorporates an instruction-tuned Large Language Model (LLM) FLAN-
122   T5 to leverage its powerful representational capabilities. Other studies (Liu et al., 2023b; Huang
123   et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2024; Zeng et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024) also utilize diffusion models, in
124   conjunction with other enhancing methods, to improve the generation capacity.
125
126
      2.2   M ULTIMODAL AUDIO G ENERATION
127
128   Numerous studies have concentrated on multi-modal generation tasks related to the audio modality,
129   extending beyond text and conducting significant experiments across various modalities. Previous
130   research (Iashin & Rahtu, 2021), has focused on multi-class visually guided sound synthesis utiliz-
131   ing VQGAN (Esser et al., 2021). Recent work includes IM2WAV (Sheffer & Adi, 2023), which
132   employs a two-stage audio generation pipeline to generate low-level audio representation autore-
133   gressively, followed by high-level audio tokens from images. Similarly, Diff-Foley (Luo et al.,
134   2024) uses contrastive audio-visual pretraining to learn temporally aligned features for synthesizing
135
      audio from silent videos, drawing on LDM. Seeing&Hearing (Xing et al., 2024) and T2AV (Mo
      et al., 2024) show open-domain visual-audio generation capability based on similar multi-modality
136
      latent aligners. Other studies (Kurmi et al., 2021; Ruan et al., 2023; Yariv et al., 2024) have at-
137
      tempted joint video-audio generation using the same or aligned latent. Despite these advancements,
138   generating synchronized audio that corresponds with a given video remains a significant challenge,
139   particularly at the audio event level.

142   3     BASELINE F RAMEWORK
143
144   3.1   OVERALL F RAMEWORK
145
146   Drawing inspiration from previous TTA works (Liu et al., 2023a; Ghosal et al., 2023) and multi-
147
      modal research (Luo et al., 2021; Sheffer & Adi, 2023), we develop a fundamental VTA framework
      VTA-LDM consisting of several key components: a vision encoder, a conditional LDM, and a mel-
148
      spectrogram/audio variational auto-encoder (VAE). Specifically, we utilize vision features extracted
149
      from pre-trained vision encoders and feed them to the LDM as the generation condition through a
150   linear projection layer. The LDM operates on the latent audio representation of the mel-spectrogram.
151   The pre-trained audio VAE assists in decoding the denoised latent output to a mel-spectrogram,
152   which is then fed to a vocoder to generate the final audio.
153
154
      3.2   V ISION E NCODER
155
156   Vision Encoder is responsible for encoding not only the semantic meanings of the video V but also
157   the temporal information required for alignment with the generated audio. We employ pretrained
158   vision encoders fV such as CLIP4CLIP (Luo et al., 2021) to extract the visual features from the input
159   video. These features capture the essential visual information, including objects, actions, and scene
160   context. We use a projection layer ϕ to map these features to the desired dimension of the diffusion
161   condition. The vision encoders are all frozen during the training process, while the projection layer
      will be trained from scratch.

      3.3   L ATENT D IFFUSION M ODEL (LDM)
163
164   Given the original input x0 , diffusion models follow a Markov chain of diffusion steps to gradually
165   add random noise to data until it follows a Gaussian distribution N (0, I), represented by xt . The
166   model then learns the reverse de-noising process to recover the original input data. For computa-
167   tional efficiency, LDM incorporates a trained perceptual compression model to encode the input x
168   into a low-dimensional latent space Z. In text-based generation tasks, the generation condition is
169   often a given text description. In our VTA implementation, the condition c is the projected video
170
      feature ϕ(fV ). To perform sequential de-noising, we train a networkϵθ to predict artificial noise,
      following the objective:

      a classifier-free generation approach in the reverse process, which has been shown to be an effec-

      tive method for text-guided generation and editing Ho & Salimans (2022). Given a latent variable

184   3.4   AUDIO VAE AND VOCODER
185
186
      The audio source is firstly conveyed to the mel-spectrogram using Short-Time Fourier Transform
      (STFT), and then to the latent representation z with a pre-trained audio VAE. We use the pre-trained
187
      VAE from AudioLDM (Liu et al., 2023a). In the reverse process, the audio is recovered from the
188
      latent z using the Hifi-GAN vocoder (Kong et al., 2020).

193   4.1   DATASET
194
      Our primary experiments are conducted on VGGSound Chen et al. (2020), a dataset comprising over
195
      550 hours of videos with acoustic visual-audio event pairs. We train our models on 200k videos and
196   evaluate them on 3k videos. For certain ablations, we either construct an augmented test set based on
197   the original dataset by randomly cutting and combining segments, or import large unlabelled audio
198   or video corpora for pre-training purposes. More details regarding the dataset preparation, training,
199   and evaluation procedures can be found in section 5.4 and section 5.5.
200
201   4.2   O BJECTIVE E VALUATION
202
203   We utilize commonly used quantitative metrics for the objective evaluation. For evaluating se-
204   mantic consistency, Fréchet distance (FD), Fréchet audio distance (FAD), Inception Score (IS),
205
      Kullback–Leibler (KL) measure the semantic similarity of the generated audios with the ground-
      truth target in distribution, as well as the generation diversity and quality of the audios. Contrastive
206
      language-audio pretraining (CLAP) (Wu* et al., 2023) score calculates how well the generated audio
207
      aligns with the textual description of the audio. For evaluating temporal alignment, AV-Align (Yariv
208   et al., 2024) assesses the alignment of generated audio with the input video. CAVP (Luo et al., 2024)
209   evaluates how well the audio aligns with the visual input at both semantic and temporal levels. Be-
210   sides, Prompting Audio-Language Models (PAM) (Deshmukh et al., 2024) assesses the quality of
211   generated audio.
212
213   4.3   S UBJECTIVE E VALUATION
214
215   We include a subjective evaluation of our models, assessing overall quality, audio quality, video-
      audio semantic alignment, and video-audio temporal alignment using a scale of 1 to 100. In the

      5.1    BASIC C ONFIGS
223
224   Our LDM structure is configured with a cross-attention dimension of 2048 and features 8 input and
225   output channels. We train our models with 300 warmup steps, a learning rate of 6e-5, and a batch
226   size of 128. All models are trained for a total of 120 epochs. All the models are trained on 8 Nvidia
227   A100 cards. During the inference steps, we set the denoise steps to 300, and the number of samples
228   per audio 1. We utilize classifier-free guidance with a guidance scale of 3.

      The data used in training is also crucial. We explore several approaches from different perspectives.
379   Initially, we filter the training set using video-audio alignment labels to obtain high-quality video-
380   audio pairs. We use a CLAP (Wu* et al., 2023) model to help select audio-video pairs with similar
381   semantics based on extra textual labels (with score > 0.3). We also use the AV-Align score to
382   filter unmatched video-audio pairs (with score > 0.2). Furthermore, we utilize data augmentation
383   techniques to enhance the diversity and complexity of the training set. We randomly combine video
384   and audio segments to construct a diverse and multi-content set of training data points. Additionally,
385   we leverage extra training sets for pretraining and perform fine-tuning on the foundational model.
386   These approaches are separately studied to illustrate their influence on the final generation results.
387
      More details can be found in appendix E.
388   The influence of different data augmentation methods on the performance of the VTA generation
389   model can be observed in table 4. We compare the base model without augmentation to models
390   utilizing various data augmentation techniques. Data Clean leads to the most significant perfor-
391   mance improvement. Removing noisy or irrelevant video-audio samples results in improvements
392   mainly in audio quality and video-audio alignment. The drop in generation quality can be attributed
393
      to the decrease in the number of training instances. Concat Augment leads to an improvement in
      IS, suggesting that this technique can enhance the diversity of the generated audio content, as well
394
      as a significant improvement in AV-Align, indicating better video-audio alignment. However, the
395
      distance metrics, such as FAD and KL, show a slight decrease with these data augmentation meth-
396   ods. It is worth noting that while data augmentations within the data corpus can improve diversity
397   and alignment, there may be a trade-off in terms of audio content itself. Pretraining with extra data
398   is a natural idea that can help address this issue. The results show that pretraining on extra unla-
399   belled video and audio data can help improve some generation metrics. We believe an appropriate
400   combination of these augmentation methods can enhance the overall performance of the VTA task.

462   6.2   OVERALL DISCUSSION
463
464   We show a branch of TTA demos in fig. 4. Based on the quantitative results above, we can safely
465
      conclude that our vanilla structure has demonstrated its capability to effectively address the semantic
      alignment of TTA tasks. The vanilla structure’s design enables it to capture and translate the seman-
466
      tic information from the text prompts into the generated audio content, resulting in high-quality,
467
      semantically-aligned audio outputs.
468
469   However, while the vanilla structure exhibits the potential to address the temporal alignment of TTA
470   tasks, it only partially solves this problem. Temporal alignment, which involves aligning the gen-
471
      erated audio events with the corresponding visual events in the video, is a more complex issue that
      requires additional considerations. We introduced additional modifications, such as position encod-
472
      ing and data augmentation, to enhance the overall performance. Position encoding helps the model
473
      better understand the temporal order of events, improving the temporal alignment of the generated
474   audio content. Data augmentation, on the other hand, enhances the model’s generalizability by ex-
475   posing it to a wider variety of complex data scenarios. While these modifications have generally led
476   to improvements in both semantic and temporal alignment, they have also occasionally resulted in
477   negative feedback, as shown in table 3 and table 4.
478
      Figure 4 shows that our model generally performs well in temporally generating corresponding
479
      audios in several complex scenarios. However, there are instances where the model slightly miscatch
480   the change of the scene. From our point of view, several key problems cannot be solved by the
481   current technique, which may be ignored in the previous study: 1) the presence of an object in
482   a video doesn’t necessarily equate to it producing a sound. Some objects can produce different
483   sounds depending on the context or the action being performed, and not all objects in a video make
484   a sound. Models often fail to recognize these ’silent’ objects in a video. Nevertheless, our model
485   shows robust generation capabilities in open-domain tasks, generating natural and realistic audios
      from silent YouTube or even AI-generated videos, as shown in fig. 5.

      7   C ONCLUSION
520
521
522   In this paper, we delve into the VTA task, which aims to generate semantically-related and
523   temporally-aligned audios given silent video pieces. Leveraging the diffusion-based backbone of
524   the VTA model, we demonstrate the effectiveness of our VTA-LDM framework and conduct vari-
525   ous ablations in the model design and training process. We also dive into several key aspects during
526   the VTA generation, aiming at giving the community more insights about the model design and
527   training process. Specifically, we concentrate on the vision encoder, auxiliary embeddings, and
528   data augmentation techniques employed in the training process, and propose several significant sug-
529   gestions that have been quantitatively validated through our experiments. A combination of these
530
      approaches can lead to a more powerful VTA backbone. We foresee the evolution of more realistic
      and natural video-audio generation models based on the insights gained from this study.
531
532   Limitations & future work The models presented in this paper are trained on a limited dataset,
533   VGGSound, which primarily contains audios of single audio event data. Although we employ data
534   augmentations and utilize a portion of the filtered YouTube test set, there is room for improvement.
535   As part of our future work, we plan to build a more extensive, more diverse, and real-world-like
536
      high-quality dataset.
537   Social impact The capability to generate realistic audio from silent visual input can significantly
538   enhance the accessibility of high-quality video-audio content, benefiting fields such as AI-generated
539   content (AIGC) and virtual reality simulations. However, potential ethical concerns arise, including
      the possibility of misuse in creating deep fake videos or misleading audio content.
