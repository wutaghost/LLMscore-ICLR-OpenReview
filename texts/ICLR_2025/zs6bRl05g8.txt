009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Training and finetuning large language models (LLMs) are resource-intensive
012            tasks, with memory limitations being a key bottleneck. A classic optimization
013            method, block coordinate descent (BCD), offers solutions by segmenting the train-
014            able parameters into multiple blocks and optimizing one active block at a time
015            while freezing the others, thereby significantly reducing memory cost. How-
016            ever, we identify that blindly applying BCD to train LLMs can be inefficient for
017            two reasons. First, optimizing only the active block requires backpropagating
018
               through multiple deeper yet inactive blocks, resulting in wasteful computations.
               Second, the frozen blocks, when they are not quite close to optimality, can nar-
019
               row the optimization landscape, potentially misguiding the training of the active
020
               block. To address these issues simultaneously, we propose integrating BCD with
021            landscape correction, which unfreezes the inactive blocks and updates them in a
022            cost-efficient manner during the same backpropagation as the update to the active
023            block. We show that our method empirically improves vanilla BCD with minimal
024            additional computation and memory. Experiments on 8B and 70B models demon-
025            strate that our proposed method surpasses memory efficient baselines and matches
026            Adam’s downstream performance while reducing memory cost by 80% compared
027            to Adam.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Large language models (LLMs) have gained significant popularity within the research community
033
      and industry. Training these models typically entails a pretraining phase on a vast dataset, followed
      by a series of finetuning adjustments to tailor the model for specific domain tasks. Both phases
034
      demand extensive computational resources, with memory being a primary constraint. For instance,
035
      optimizing a model containing N billion parameters using standard mixed-precision training re-
036   quires at least 18N gigabytes of GPU memory (refer to Section 2 for additional details). Given
037   the usual limitations of GPU memory, this constraint impedes researchers from experimenting with
038   larger models.
039
      To address this practical challenge, researchers have developed memory efficient algorithms for
040
      LLM training such as parameter efficient finetuning (PEFT), including Adapter Houlsby et al.
041
      (2019), LoRA Hu et al. (2021), prompt tuning Lester et al. (2021), prefix tuning Li & Liang (2021),
042   etc. These techniques focus on training a small set of additional parameters while maintaining the
043   original pretrained model unchanged. Other memory efficient methods for full parameter training
044   have also been investigated. For example, Galore Zhao et al. (2024) applies a low-rank space pro-
045   jection to both the gradient and the optimizer’s states to reduce memory consumption.
046
      In addition to the existing approaches, a classic optimization paradigm, known as block coordinate
047
      descent (BCD), holds a strong potential for memory efficient LLM training and finetuning. Intu-
048   itively, BCD reduces the memory cost by partitioning the trainable parameters into several blocks
049   and optimizing over only one active block at a time. For instance, segmenting an N -billion pa-
050   rameter model into D blocks decreases the memory consumption from 18N to 2N + 16N        D GB, as
051   only the gradient and optimizer states of the active block need to be stored. In fact, BCD has been
052   the method of choice in many data science problems, with a wide array of variants developed for
053   improving memory, performance, convergence, and efficiency; see, e.g., (Hsieh et al., 2008; Chang
      & Roth, 2011; Yu et al., 2012; Treister & Turek, 2014; Richtárik & Takáč, 2014).

054
      In stark contrast to the previous memory efficient methods, and despite its intuitive memory benefits,
055   BCD has been overlooked and rarely explored in the context of LLMs. It was not until very recently
056   that Luo et al. (2024) proposed BAdam, which integrates BCD into an LLM finetuning framework
057   by training each active block with several Adam steps. Even though BAdam has shown preliminary
058   success in reducing memory cost during training and improving performance at test time, its direct
059   use of vanilla BCD leaves at least two fundamental aspects to be questioned:
060
          • Computing the (stochastic) gradient for a single active block via backpropagation necessitates
061
            calculating the partial derivatives of the activations of multiple deeper yet inactive layers. This
062
            is wasteful of computation as these partial derivatives are not even used to update their corre-
063         sponding weights.
064
          • Given that the training objective is highly nonconvex, and since all blocks are frozen except for
065
            the active one, BCD tends to be misled by its local view of the optimization landscape, which
066
            potentially slows down its convergence speed.
067
068   Standing on the ground offered by BAdam, we push the research frontier by proposing a simple
069   remedy to the above two issues. Our method, termed BREAD, is a blend of two components: (1)
070   Similarly to BAdam, we update the active block using several Adam steps (the BCD component);
071   (2) differently, we unfreeze the inactive blocks and update them using lightweight memory efficient
072
      optimization techniques (the landscape correction component). Since landscape correction utilizes
      the gradients of the activations that are already calculated, it adds minimal additional computation
073
      and in fact addresses the wasteful computation issue. Furthermore, the landscape correction com-
074
      ponent provides BCD a better view of the optimization landscape for better updating the current
075   active block, thereby addressing the second point of concern. In combination, BREAD maintains
076   the memory efficient feature of BCD with improved learning capability and faster convergence. Our
077   main contributions are outlined as follows:
078
079       • Limitations of Standard BCD in LLMs: Our research identifies two fundamental limits of
080         vanilla BCD when applied to neural networks (hence LLMs): The wasted computation of gradi-
081
            ents during backpropagation and the suboptimal landscape caused by freezing inactive blocks.
            These limitations partly explain why the application of BCD in neural networks is uncommon.
082
083       • Blending BCD with Landscape Correction: We propose a new method termed BREAD,
084
            which combines BCD with a landscape correction technique to address these two limitations
            simultaneously. It unfreezes some of (or all) the inactive blocks and updates them using mem-
085
            ory efficient optimization techniques. BREAD maintains the memory efficiency of BCD with
086
            improved optimization ability.
087
          • Excellent Performance: Our experiments on instruction tuning and preference optimization
088
            with the Llama 3.1-8B and Llama 3.1-70B models demonstrate that BREAD clearly outper-
089
            forms state-of-the-art memory efficient training methods and achieves comparable downstream
090         performance to that of Adam on five math benchmarks and MT-bench scores.

      Let us first analyze the memory consumption of the Adam optimizer under the mixed precision
124
      training setting Micikevicius et al. (2017). The memory cost is attributed to the storage of the
125   model parameters, gradients, and optimizer states. We consider an LLM with N billion parameters
126   and express GPU memory consumption in gigabytes (GB). Initially, one must store the FP16 model
127   parameters for the backpropagation (BP) process, requiring 2N memory. Additionally, the optimizer
128   maintains a copy of the model in FP32 precision, consuming another 4N memory. The gradients,
129   momentum, and second moment vectors are all stored in FP32 precision with each requiring 4N
130   memory. Consequently, the total memory required is at least 18N . For example, in order to train a
131   Llama 3-8B or a Llama 3-70B model, Adam requires at least 144 GB or 1260 GB of GPU RAM,
132   respectively, which can be prohibitive in limited memory scenarios.
133   In sharp contrast to Adam, BCD only requires storing the FP32 model parameters, gradients, and
134   optimizer states for the active block Wℓ , which is only 1/D of the memory consumption needed for
135   all the parameters. Thus, in addition to maintaining an FP16 model that requires 2N memory, BCD
136   needs a total of only 2N + 16N D memory. Therefore, for training a Llama 3-8B or a Llama 3-70B
137   model and when D = 32 or D = 80 (partition each transformer layer as a block), BCD only needs
138   roughly 20 GB or 154 GB of GPU RAM, respectively, which is significantly cheaper compared to
139
      the costs of Adam. For a more detailed analysis on memory cost, we refer to Luo et al. (2024).

      6     R ELATED W ORKS
497
498
      Block coordinate descent method. Block coordinate descent (BCD) is a classic optimization
499
      paradigm that dates back at least to Hildreth (1957). It has gained popularity in recent years, due
500   to its scalability and efficiency for many machine learning applications (Nesterov, 2012; Richtárik
501   & Takáč, 2014; Peng & Vidal, 2023; Ding et al.; Peng & Yin, 2024). The community seems to
502   converge to a consensus that, in order for BCD to be efficient, the problem it optimizes needs to
503   possess the so-called coordinate-friendly structure (Shi et al., 2016). Nevertheless, deep networks
504   are of a compositional nature and not coordinate-friendly, which is perhaps why recent surveys or
505   books have never mentioned training deep networks as an application of BCD (Wright, 2015; Shi
506   et al., 2016; Beck, 2017; Wright & Recht, 2022; Sayed, 2022). Recently, BAdam Luo et al. (2024)
507   was proposed to finetune LLMs based on the BCD framework, where each block sub-problem is
508
      approximately solved using several Adam steps. Although BAdam achieved preliminary success, it
      is based on the vanilla BCD framework and shares the fundamental limitations we revealed in this
509
      work. In light of these, we believe identifying the limitations of BCD for LLM fintuning and fixing
510
      them entail certain insights, and this is what makes our contributions non-trivial and valuable.
511
512   Memory efficient finetuning. To address memory issue, multiple variants have been proposed. Pa-
513   rameter efficient finetuning (PEFT) methods achieve memory efficiency by only training small por-
514
      tion of (possibly extra) parameters while freezing most of the others, such as Adapter tuning Houlsby
      et al. (2019), prompt tuning, and prefix tuning Lester et al. (2021); Li & Liang (2021). Low-rank
515
      adaptation (LoRA) is perhaps the most popular technique that approximates model updates using
516
      two smaller, trainable low-rank matrices Hu et al. (2021). LoRA’ variants have been proposed to
517   address its rank constraints and further reducing the memory cost Lialin et al. (2024); Xia et al.
518   (2024); Dettmers et al. (2023). Galore Zhao et al. (2024) projects the gradient into low-rank space
519   so that it does not need to store the full gradient and optimizer states in the memory. LOMO updates
520   parameters in real time during the backpropagation process Lv et al. (2023), so that one can perform
521   SGD without store stochastic gradients. MeZO offers an alternative by approximating SGD using
522   only forward passes Malladi et al. (2023), drawing from zeroth-order optimization that estimates
523   stochastic gradients through the difference in function values. While this paper addresses the same
524   application as these methods, they remain orthogonal to the proposed approaches. They can function
525
      as lightweight updates in the frozen layers for landscape correction.
526
527
      7     C ONCLUSIONS AND D ISCUSSIONS
528
      This paper investigates the application of a classic optimization method, known as BCD, to the
529
      finetuning of LLMs. We pinpoint two primary shortcomings of the standard BCD approach when
530
      applied to deep neural networks: the unnecessary computational overhead during backpropagation,
531   and the misguiding optimization landscape caused by frozen blocks. To overcome these challenges,
532   we introduce a new method termed BREAD, which unfreezes the inactive blocks and updates them
533   in a lightweight manner. Our experimental results demonstrate that BREAD significantly enhances
534   downstream task performance while maintaining the original BCD algorithm’s memory and com-
535   putational efficiency.
536
      For future research, it would be intriguing to explore the potential of BCD in the (continual) pre-
537   training phase of LLMs. Moreover, it is not necessary to apply feature correction at each iteration.
538   Exploring the frequency of updating correction matrices is another meaningful direction, which can
539   further save the computational cost.
