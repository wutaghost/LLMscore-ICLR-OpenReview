028                                               A BSTRACT
029
030            Research efforts for visual decoding from fMRI signals have attracted considerable
031            attention in research community. Still multi-subject fMRI decoding with one
032            model has been considered intractable due to the drastic variations in fMRI signals
033            between subjects and even within the same subject across different trials. To
034            address current limitations in multi-subject brain decoding, here we introduce a
035            novel semantic alignment method of multi-subject fMRI signals using so-called
036            MindFormer. This model is specifically designed to generate fMRI-conditioned
037            feature vectors that can be used for conditioning Stable Diffusion model for fMRI-
038
               to-image generation or large language model (LLM) for fMRI-to-text generation.
               More specifically, MindFormer incorporates two key innovations: 1) a subject
039
               specific token that effectively capture individual differences in fMRI signals while
040
               synergistically combines multi subject fMRI data for training, and 2) a novel feature
041            embedding and training scheme based on the IP-Adapter to extract semantically
042            meaningful features from fMRI signals. Our experimental results demonstrate that
043            MindFormer generates semantically consistent images and text across different
044            subjects. Since our MindFormer maintains semantic fidelity by fully utilizing the
045            training data across different subjects by significantly surpassing existing models in
046            multi-subject brain decoding, this may help deepening our understanding of neural
047            processing variations among individuals.

      cognitive and sensory stimuli. A rapidly advancing area within this field is visual brain decoding,
      which aims to interpret neural signals to reconstruct visual experiences. The advent of deep learning

075
      Horikawa & Kamitani, 2017; Shen et al., 2019; VanRullen & Reddy, 2019). One prevalent approach
076   in visual decoding involves mapping neural activity to the latent spaces of generative models, such as
077   generative adversarial networks (GANs) (Lin et al., 2022; Mozafari et al., 2020; Ozcelik et al., 2022;
078   Seeliger et al., 2018). Recent advancements, fueled by new large-scale fMRI datasets (Allen et al.,
079   2022), have seen the emergence of diffusion models, which enhance reconstruction accuracy (Chen
080   et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2023; Mai & Zhang, 2023; Ozcelik & VanRullen, 2023; Scotti et al., 2024;
081   Takagi & Nishimoto, 2023a; Xia et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024). The integration of diffusion models
082   in brain decoding marks a significant leap forward, providing advanced tools to reconstruct and
083   interpret complex neural representations. Nonetheless, challenges remain in achieving high-fidelity
084
      reconstructions, lightweight models, and integrated subject-specific brain decoding.
085   In this work, we introduce a transformer-based multi-subject semantic alignment algorithm called
086   MindFormer, which demonstrates exceptional performance in multi-subject brain decoding, par-
087   ticularly when combined with diffusion models or LLMs. MindFormer is specifically designed
088   to generate semantically meaningful feature embeddings across multiple subjects to Stable Diffu-
089   sion for image generation and LLMs for text generation. More specifically, to effectively integrate
090
      training data from multiple subjects while accounting for individual differences, we introduce a
      learnable subject token as inputs in the Transformer prompt. These components allow MindFormer
091
      to obtain semantically meaningful embedding even from limited datasets by leveraging collective
092
      information across subjects, improving its practical applicability in scenarios where data availability
093   is restricted. Furthermore, we employ the IP-adapter, as described in Ye et al. (2023), to generate
094   16x768-dimensional feature embeddings from fMRI signals, which serve as conditioning inputs
095   for the Stable Diffusion or LLMs. Unlike previous approaches that utilized CLIP embeddings,
096   our use of the IP-adapter yields smaller, more efficient semantic embeddings, which reduces both
097   computational costs and the risk of overfitting, and significantly enhances decoding accuracy and
098   reliability. Experimental results demonstrate that our method maintains strong performance, even
099   with limited data, by effectively utilizing shared information across subjects to maximize accuracy
100   and reliability.
101   Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
102
103          • We developed a semantic alignment method of multi-subject fMRI data using MindFormer
104            to effectively integrate training data from multiple subjects while accounting for individual
105            differences by using learnable subject token as inputs in the Transformer prompt. These
106            components allow MindFormer to generate semantically meaningful condition embedding
107            modules for the Stable Diffusion model, specifically tailored for multi-subject brain decoding
               from fMRI signals.

      2.1   f MRI- TO -I MAGE R ECONSTRUCTION M ODELS
146
147   fMRI-to-image reconstruction models are advanced approaches designed to translate brain activity,
148   captured through functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), into visual images. These models
149   learn complex mappings between neural signals and visual representations, allowing for the generation
150   of images that closely resemble the original stimuli perceived by subjects. With the advent of
151   deep learning, these models have increasingly leveraged deep learning frameworks to interpret and
152   reconstruct visual experiences based on neural activity patterns. Recent advancements have integrated
153
      generative models, such as Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Lin et al., 2022; Mozafari et al.,
      2020; Ozcelik et al., 2022; Seeliger et al., 2018) and Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) (Han et al.,
154
      2019), with fMRI data to improve the accuracy and quality of reconstructed images. For instance,
155
      Seeliger et al. (2018) explored the use of GANs for fMRI-to-image synthesis, while Han et al. (2019)
156   demonstrated the effectiveness of VAEs in reconstructing visual stimuli from brain activity. Advances
157   in deep learning have enabled the reconstruction of not only natural scenes but also human faces
158   (Dado et al., 2022; VanRullen & Reddy, 2019) and video stimuli Wang et al. (2022). Additionally,
159   techniques like contrastive learning (Chen et al., 2020; Radford et al., 2021) have been employed to
160   better align neural embeddings with visual embeddings. This alignment significantly enhances the
161   fidelity of the reconstructed images, ensuring that the generated visuals accurately reflect the subjects’
      visual experiences.

      2.2   I MAGE G ENERATION D IFFUSION M ODEL
163
164   Another significant innovation in brain decoding is the use of diffusion models. Diffusion models
165   have gained popularity in generative modeling due to their ability to transform noise vectors into
166   output images through a reverse diffusion process (Ho et al., 2020; Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021; Song
167   et al., 2020a). Recent studies (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Song et al., 2020b) have demonstrated
168   that diffusion models achieve superior image generation quality compared to GANs (Brock et al.,
169   2018; Zhang et al., 2019), further establishing their importance in this field. These models have been
170   particularly impactful in brain decoding, offering enhanced flexibility and precision in capturing the
171
      subtle nuances of visual experiences encoded in brain activity (Chen et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2023;
      Mai & Zhang, 2023; Ozcelik & VanRullen, 2023; Scotti et al., 2024; Takagi & Nishimoto, 2023a;
172
      Xia et al., 2024). In brain decoding studies that utilize diffusion models, researchers often employ
173
      the Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) or Versatile Diffusion (Xu et al., 2023) models. Stable
174   Diffusion focuses on generating high-quality images by refining noise into coherent visuals, while
175   Versatile Diffusion enables substantial image variation, facilitating tasks like style transfer. However,
176   this variability in Versatile Diffusion can introduce challenges in brain decoding, as the reconstructed
177   images may display inconsistencies and artifacts. These discrepancies can complicate the accurate
178   interpretation of neural activity, potentially reducing the fidelity of the decoding outcomes.
179
      Recent advancements in controllable image generation, such as ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023) and
180   T2I-adapter (Mou et al., 2024), have shown that additional networks can guide image generation by
181   plugging into existing text-to-image diffusion models. However, these methods often fall short in
182   faithfully reproducing the reference image, primarily due to limitations in the cross-attention modules
183   that inadequately merge image and text features. IP-Adapter (Ye et al., 2023) addresses this issue by
184   effectively integrating image features, enabling more accurate and detailed image generation based on
185   reference inputs. The central concept of the IP-Adapter revolves around its decoupled cross-attention
186   mechanism. Instead of employing a single cross-attention layer to handle both text and image features
187   simultaneously, the IP-Adapter introduces a dedicated cross-attention layer specifically for image
188
      features. This separation enables the model to focus on learning more detailed and image-specific
      features, enhancing its ability to capture the unique characteristics of visual data.

193   3.1   M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
194
195   As shown in Fig. 2, the fMRI signals obtained during the n-th subject’s viewing of an image are
196   initially passed through a subject-specific linear layer. Following this, each signal is prepended with a
197   unique learnable subject token and then processed through the MindFormer encoder. The embeddings
198   produced from this process are subsequently trained to match the image feature embeddings obtained
199   when the images are passed through the IP-Adapter. It is important to note that all subjects’ signals
200   are processed through the same instance of the MindFormer encoder, ensuring a unified encoding
201
      process. For the case of image generation, the trained embedding, from the brain signal, are integrated
      into the Stable Diffusion process. The diffusion model utilizes these embeddings to iteratively denoise
202
      and reconstruct the image to generate semantically aligned images.
203
204   Specifically, as shown in Fig. 3, MindFormer comprises of the subject specific linear layer and a
205   single transformer encoder that incorporates unique learnable subject token. The architecture of
206   MindFormer encoder follows the Vision Transformer (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) encoder. Note
207
      that fMRI signals vary in size across subjects, primarily due to inherent differences in brain size and
      structure. To address the differing input voxel sizes, MindFormer maps each subject’s voxels v s into
208
      a uniform dimension of 16×768 through individual linear layers Es for each subject s. Then, in the
209
      position of BERT (Devlin et al., 2018) and ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020)’s [Class] token, we prepend
210   a learnable embedding token xsubj to the output xs = Es (v s ) of linear mapping. Addition use of
211   learnable subject token is intended to decouple the individual bias of fMRI signal differences from
212   the common representation across subjects, thereby allowing accurate interpretation of the neural data
213   corresponding to multiple subject as well as each individual subject. Then, the position embeddings
214   P are incorporated into the prepared embeddings to preserve positional information. The following
215   steps proceed similarly to the transformer encoder in ViT. The Transformer encoder is composed of
      alternating layers of multi-headed self-attention (MSA) and MLP blocks. Layer normalization (LN)

362   Importance of subject tokens. Using learnable subject tokens in MindFormer has several positive
363
      effects. First, it allows the model to accurately distinguish between inputs from different subjects,
      ensuring that individual-specific neural patterns are correctly interpreted and processed. This enhances
364
      the precision of brain decoding by aligning the model’s understanding with the unique characteristics
365
      of each subject’s brain activity. Additionally, the incorporation of learnable subject tokens improves
366   the model’s ability to generalize across multiple subjects, as it can adapt to variability in neural
367   signals while maintaining high performance in decoding tasks. Overall, subject tokens contribute to
368   more reliable and robust decoding outcomes, facilitating better insights into neural representations.
369   Figure 5 and Table 2 provides evidence for these benefits by showing superior performance results
370   when subject tokens are used. Also, by using subject tokens, this unified model approach offers
371   significant advantages in terms of efficiency and scalability, allowing for comprehensive analysis and
372   image reconstruction across different individuals without the need for multiple models.

376   single-subject and multi-subject experimental setups. In single subject scenario, MindFormer trains

448   4.3   F MRI- TO -T EXT E XPERIMENTS
449
      In order to confirm that the significant improvement of our model is originated from semantically
450
      align feature space in MindFormer rather than Stable Diffusion, we additionally perform fMRI-to-text
451   generation experiments by inputting the MindFormer feature as the input of Large Language Model
452   (LLM). This experimental setup is unique as we do not rely on the Stable Diffusion image generator.
453
454
      Implementation Details. Figure 7 shows the framework of the fMRI-to-Text generation. We employ
      a simple two-layer MLP to align the image feature embeddings from the pretrained Mindformer to
455
      the word embedding space of a LLM. We choose OPT-1.3B model as our LLM. With the Mindformer
456
      and OPT-1.3B remain frozen, the two-layer MLP is trained with the language modeling loss between
457   the generated captions and the ground truth COCO captions of subjects 1,2,5 and 7. The entire
458   fMRI-to-Image caption model is trained on NVIDIA A100 with 40GB of memory for 5 epochs with
459   a learning rate of 1e-5 and a batch size of 1.
460
      Results. To quantitatively compare with other methods, we utilize six text quality evaluation metrics.
461
      For assessing low-level properties, we use Meteor (Banerjee & Lavie (2005)), Rouge (Lin (2004)),
462
      and CIDEr (Vedantam et al. (2015)). For evaluating higher-level properties, the metrics of SPICE
463   (Anderson et al. (2016)), CLIP (Radford et al. (2021)), and Sentence (Reimers (2019)) are employed.
464   We compared our model with SDReconT (Takagi & Nishimoto (2023b)), UniBrain (Mai & Zhang
465   (2023)), BrainCap (Ferrante et al. (2023)), and MindSemantix (Ren et al. (2024)). We have referenced
466   the result values from the MindSemantix. Table 4 demonstrates superior performance in the metrics
467   of Meteor, CIDEr, SPICE, and Sentence, compared to other models. Also, Figure 8 shows the COCO
468   captions (ground-truth) and generated texts from fMRI signals, and our model successfully generated
469   the caption, corresponding to the stimulus image. The comprehensive results indicate that the output
470   embedding of our MindFormer contains the relevant information needed to generate texts effectively.

472   4.4   D ISCUSSION
473

475   cross-subject brain decoding. One of the key findings is the significant improvement in image
476   reconstruction accuracy when incorporating subject tokens and utilizing a multi-subject training
477
      approach. Notably, even with a limited dataset, the MindFormer demonstrates its capability to
      reconstruct high-quality images, outperforming models trained on larger datasets. This is particularly
478
      important given the challenges associated with obtaining large fMRI datasets from new subjects. The

481   may be constrained. Moreover, the comparison between single-subject and multi-subject training
482   further validates the advantage of leveraging data from multiple subjects. The results consistently
483   indicate that the MindFormer approach, which integrates data from subjects 2, 5, and 7 along with
484   subject 1, yields better performance metrics. This suggests that the model benefits from the additional
485   information provided by the diverse set of neural patterns, enhancing its generalization capabilities
      and robustness. Also, with the output embedding of our MindFormer, LLM can generate the caption

522   subjects, offering significant advantages in terms of efficiency and scalability.

525   5    C ONCLUSION
526
527
      In this paper, we proposed "MindFormer", a powerful transformer architecture for semantically
528   aligned multi-subject fMRI embedding for braining decoding. Thanks to the effective multi-subject
529   fMRI signal embedding using the subject token and IP-Adapter, the model significantly outperformed
530   the existing multi-subject brain decoding framework. Overall, MindFormer provides a new framework
531   for understanding multi-subject brain decoding and common neural patterns. The model’s ability to
532   leverage shared information across subjects while maintaining individual-specific accuracy marks a
533   significant advancement in the field of brain decoding.
534   Limitation. Current implementation of MindFormer primarily focuses on visual stimuli. Extending
535   this approach to decode more complex cognitive and sensory experiences will require substantial
536   advancements in both model architecture and training methodologies. Another limitation is the
537   computational complexity associated with training much more subjects. Although our approach
538   reduces the parameter count compared to existing models, training these models with over about 10
539   subjects still require significant computational resources. Future work should aim to optimize the
      model further to make it more accessible and feasible for broader applications.
