009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The manifold hypothesis says that natural high-dimensional data lie on or around
012            a low-dimensional manifold. The recent success of statistical and learning-based
013            methods in very high dimensions empirically supports this hypothesis, suggesting
014            that typical worst-case analysis does not provide practical guarantees. A natural
015            step for analysis is thus to assume the manifold hypothesis and derive bounds that
016
               are independent of any ambient dimensions that the data may be embedded in.
               Theoretical implications in this direction have recently been explored in terms of
017
               generalization of ReLU networks and convergence of Langevin methods. In this
018
               work, we consider optimal uniform approximations with functions of finite statis-
019            tical complexity. While upper bounds on uniform approximation exist in the liter-
020            ature in terms of ReLU network approximation, we consider the opposite: lower
021            bounds to quantify the fundamental difficulty of approximation on manifolds. In
022            particular, we demonstrate that the statistical complexity required to approximate
023            a class of bounded Sobolev functions on a compact manifold is bounded from be-
024            low, and moreover that this bound is dependent only on the intrinsic properties of
025            the manifold, such as curvature, volume, and injectivity radius.

      1     I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Data is ever growing, especially in the current era of machine learning. However, dimensionality is
030   not always beneficial, and having too many features can confound simpler underlying truths. This
031   is sometimes referred to as the curse of dimensionality (Altman & Krzywinski, 2018). A classi-
032   cal example is manifold learning, which is known to scale exponentially in the intrinsic dimension
033   (Narayanan & Niyogi, 2009). In the current paradigm of increasing dimensionality, standard sta-
034   tistical tools and machine learning models continue to work, despite the high ambient dimensions
035   arising in cases such as computational imaging (Wainwright, 2019). One possible assumption to
036   elucidate this phenomenon comes from the manifold hypothesis, also known as concentration of
037   measure or the blessing of dimensionality (Bengio et al., 2013). This states that real datasets are ac-
038
      tually concentrated on or near low-dimensional manifolds, independently of the ambient dimension
      that the data is embedded in.
039
040   In this work, we explore the consequences of the manifold hypothesis through the lens of approxima-
041   tion theory and statistical complexity. For a class of functions with infinite statistical complexity, we
042   consider a nonlinear width in terms of how well it can be approximated in Lp with function classes
043   of finite statistical complexity. We consider how difficult it is to optimally approximate classes of
044
      functions with functions of finite statistical complexity in terms of Lp distance. In particular, Theo-
      rem 3.1 demonstrates that on a Riemannian manifold, the optimal error incurred by approximating
045
      a bounded Sobolev class using function classes of finite pseudo-dimension can be lower bounded
046
      using only the implicit properties of the manifold.

082   work thus considers the difficulty of modelling physical problems over structured datasets with
083
      simple function classes.

      3     M AIN R ESULT
237
238
      This section begins with a statement of the main approximation result, a lower bound on the nonlin-
      ear n-width (4) of bounded Sobolev balls W 1,p (1) in Lq . This is followed by a high-level intuition
239
      behind the proof, then the proof in detail. The supporting lemmas are deferred to Appendix C.
240
      Theorem 3.1. Let (M, g) be a d-dimensional compact (separable) Riemannian manifold without
241
      boundary. From compactness, there exist real constants K, inj(M ) such that:
242
243         1. The Ricci curvature satisfies Ric ≥ (d − 1)K, where K < 0;
244
245         2. The injectivity radius is positive, inj(M ) > 0.
246   For any 1 ≤ p, q ≤ +∞, the nonlinear width of W 1,p (1) satisfies the lower bound for sufficiently
247   large n:
248                     ρn (W 1,p (1), Lq (M )) ≥ C(d, K, vol(M ), p, q)(n + log n)−1/d .           (12)
249
      The constant is independent of any ambient dimension that (M, g) may be embedded in.
250
251   Note that this statement does not refer to any ambient dimension or embeddings, and can be defined
252   on abstract manifolds. This theorem should be contrasted with Maiorov & Ratsaby (1999, Thm.
253   1), which exhibits a similar bound for the bounded Sobolev space on the unit hypercube [0, 1]d , but
254   with lower bound n−1/d . The additional log n term is necessary due to the curvature of the space.
255   We also note that it is possible to perform this analysis in the case of positive curvature and derive
256   better bounds.
257   There are two major difficulties in converting the proof of Maiorov & Ratsaby (1999) to the man-
258   ifold setting, both arising from curvature. Firstly, Maiorov & Ratsaby (1999) uses a partition into
259   hypercubes to construct the desired counterexample. As such hypercube partitions generally do
260   not pose nice properties on manifolds, this must be loosened to a packing of geodesic balls, which
261   does not fully cover the manifold and loosens the bound. The second major difference is the lack
262   of global information, particularly for geodesic balls of the same radius which can have drastically
263
      different volumes at different points, even if small-ball volumes behave asymptotically Euclidean
      pointwise. This will introduce additional constants into the final bound. We break the proof down
264
      into the following steps.
265

      4     C ONCLUSION
521
      This work provides a theoretical motivation to further explore the manifold hypothesis. We show
522
      that the problem of approximating a bounded class of Sobolev functions depends only on the in-
523
      trinsic properties of the space it is supported in. More precisely, the approximation error of the
524   bounded W 1,p space with respect to bounded pseudo-dimension classes is shown to be at least
525   (n + log n)−1/d , where d is the intrinsic dimension of the underlying manifold. Since generalization
526   error is linear in pseudo-dimension, this provides an ambient-dimension-free lower-bound on gen-
527   eralization error. This is in contrast to many works in the literature that provide constructive upper
528   bounds on generalization error based on ReLU approximation properties that still depend on the
529   embedding of the manifold in ambient Euclidean space. Followup work could consider alternative
530   statistical complexities, such as Rademacher or Gaussian complexity.
531   The proposed bound can be improved in multiple ways. Firstly, the analysis is restricted to one
532   weak derivative. The analogous result of approximating W k,p in the cube [0, 1]D has lower bounds
533   ∼ n−k/D (Maiorov & Ratsaby, 1999). Extending our analysis to more weak derivatives would
534   require a careful construction in Step 1 of test functions with the appropriate regularity conditions. In
535   particular, we would require cutoff functions supp ϕi ⊂ Br (pi ) with explicitly bounded higher weak
536   derivatives ∥∇α ϕi ∥ ≲ r|α| , which do not seem to appear in the literature. The explicit construction
537   of Moulis (1971) of a C ∞ function that approximates a C 2k−1 function in the C k -topology could be
538   useful in this. Moreover, the current bound requires knowledge of the injectivity radius to uniformly
539   lower-bound the volume of small balls. Other ways of constructing volume lower-bounds would
      help in improving the constants in the bound.

      A manifold is without boundary if every point has a neighborhood homeomorphic to an open subset
727   of Rd . For a point p ∈ M , let Br (p) be the metric ball around p in M with radius r > 0.
728
729   The sectional (or Riemannian) curvature takes at each point p ∈ M , a tangent plane P ⊂ Tp M and
730
      outputs a scalar value. The Ricci curvature (function) Ric(v) ≡ Ric(v, v) of a unit vector v ∈ Tp M
      is the mean sectional curvature over planes containing v in Tp M . In particular, for a manifold with
731
      constant sectional curvature K, we have Ric ≡ (d − 1)K. We write Ric ≥ K for K ∈ R to mean
732
      that Ric(v) ≥ K holds for all unit vectors in the tangent bundle v ∈ T M .
733
734   The injectivity radius inj(p) at a point p ∈ M is the supremum over radii r > 0 such that the
735   exponential map defines a global diffeomorphism (nonsingular derivative) from Br (0; Tp M ) onto
736
      its image in M . The injectivity radius inj(M ) of a manifold is the infimum of such injectivity radii
      over all points in M .
737

739   the volume form is

745

746   a sphere, geodesics tend to converge, and small balls have less volume than Euclidean balls. In
747   manifolds with negative sectional curvature such as hyperbolic space, geodesics tend to diverge, and
748   small balls have more volume than Euclidean balls.
