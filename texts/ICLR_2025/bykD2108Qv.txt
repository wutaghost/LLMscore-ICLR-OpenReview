009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Differentially private stochastic gradient descent (DP-SGD) is a well-known
012            method for training machine learning models with a specified level of privacy.
013            However, its basic implementation is generally bottlenecked by the computation of
014            the gradient norm (gradient clipping) for each example in an input batch. While
015            various techniques have been developed to mitigate this issue, there are only a
016
               handful of methods pertaining to convolution models, e.g., vision models. In this
               work, we present three methods for performing gradient clipping that improve
017
               upon previous state-of-art methods. Two of these methods use in-place operations
018
               to reduce memory overhead, while the third one leverages a relationship between
019            Fourier transforms and convolution layers. To demonstrate the numerical efficiency
020            of our methods, we also present several benchmark experiments that compare
021            against other algorithms.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Differentially-private stochastic gradient descent (DP-SGD) is a common tool used to train machine
027
      learning models to protect sensitive information contained within individual training records (Abadi
      et al., 2016). However, general implementations of DP-SGD are bottlenecked by their gradient
028
      clipping step, whose runtime and memory costs scale linearly with the batch size times the number
029
      of model parameters. Our goal in this work is to develop three improved variants of the gradient
030   clipping step that are substantially more efficient when applied to models with convolution layers.
031
032   DP-SGD details. The DP-SGD algorithm (Chaudhuri et al., 2011; Bassily et al., 2014) relies on
033   the Gaussian mechanism and composition of differential privacy (Dwork et al., 2006; 2014) across
034   iterations to privately compute the average of per-example gradients in a batch. At each iteration
035   it operates by (i) bounding the sensitivity of each record within a batch to control and quantify the
036   impact of any single record on the final model weights, and (ii) adding Gaussian noise proportional to
037   the inverse of the batch size times the bound in (i). In particular, sensitivity is controlled by bounding
038
      per-example gradient norms so that the privatized gradients    √ lie in a compact set. This approach
039
      is crucial for reducing noise growth, which scales as O( d/[ b]) (Bassily et al., 2014), where d
      is the number of model parameters,   the privacy budget, and b the number of records in a batch.
040
      Alternatively, one can clip the √ overall average gradient at each step, but this increases noise by a
041
      factor of the batch size to O( d/ ).
042
043   Naive per-example clipping requires computing the norm of all per-example gradients. Specifically,
044   this methods requires storing at least a matrix of size Θ(bd) that contains per-example gradients.
045   Given the importance of model utility within this privacy-preserving context, there have been several
046   developments on improving this step (with a focus on models with fully-connected or embedding
047
      layers). For example, techniques like ghost-clipping (Goodfellow, 2015) have been leveraged to
      improve both the runtime and storage complexity in certain settings. However, similar savings for
048
      convolution layers remain elusive (Rochette et al., 2019; Lee & Kifer, 2021a).
049
050   Contributions. This work introduces three novel gradient clipping methods that outperform prior
051   methods for convolution methods in certain regimes. More specifically,
052
053         • the first two methods use in-place calculations and obtain O(1) per-example storage com-
              plexities;

054
            • the first method directly computes the squared norm, while the second leverages the ghost-
055           clipping trick for fully-connected layers;
056
057         • the third method uses a relationship between convolution operators and fast Fourier trans-
058           forms (FFTs) to obtain a scheme that scales well in the high-dimensional setting.
059
060   It is worth mentioning that the analysis for the third method (in the context of gradient clipping
061   methods) appears to be new. In particular, this analysis exploits properties of circulant matrices to
062   derive an algorithm that runs efficiently in terms of the number of model parameters d, and the batch
063   size b.
064   To verify the practical efficiency of our methods, we also provide several benchmark experiments
065   that demonstrate the numerical efficiency of our proposed methods.
066
067   Related work. The vast literature on DP-SGD (Chaudhuri et al., 2011; Bassily et al., 2014; Abadi
068   et al., 2016; Ponomareva et al., 2023; Bu et al., 2023a) highlights the challenge of bounding individual
069   record sensitivity, a crucial aspect often addressed through clipping1 . While alternative approaches
070
      exist, such as modifying model architectures to enable Lipschitz constant computation (Béthune et al.,
      2023), their broader applicability remains uncertain.
071
072   To the best of our knowledge, the state-of-the-art performance in the setting of convolution models
073   is achieved by Bu et al. (2023b). Specifically, that work builds on the approach of Bu et al. (2022);
074   Lee & Kifer (2021a) and combines it with a careful book-keeping scheme that avoids a second back-
075   propagation step. The main observation of Bu et al. (2022) is that the straightforward implementation
076
      of DP-SGD can be faster or more memory-efficient than ghost-clipping in certain regimes. While
      Rochette et al. (2019); Lee & Kifer (2021a) rely on instantiating per-example gradients, Bu et al.
077
      (2022) take advantage of the underlying network structure and choose which of of two different
078
      approaches to run; this selection step drives the bulk of their speed-up. In a follow-up work (Bu et al.,
079   2023b), the authors use the previous observation and the idea that the second back-propagation step
080   can be avoided using caching techniques.
081
082
      Fourier transforms have first been used to improve the efficiency of training convolution neural
      networks (CNNs) by Mathieu et al. (2013), who build upon related work by Ben-Yacoub et al.
083
      (1999) for small-scale fully-connected models. Additional improvements to the approach have been
084
      developed, for example, by Pratt et al. (2017); Vasilache et al. (2014); Abtahi et al. (2017); Rippel
085   et al. (2015). However, the development of similar techniques for the purpose of gradient clipping
086   (this work) appears to be new.

      This subsection discusses two topics, namely, (i) how the runtime and storage costs from the previous

352   respectively. For simplicity, let us assume that the stride length is s = 1 and let d ≥ 1 and n ≥ 1 be
353   arbitrary. First, when din , dout , dk = Θ(d) and nin , nout = O(1), we have that

361   From the above comparisons, we can see that each of our proposed methods outperforms the others
362   in certain regimes, so there is not a method that performs universally better across different choices
363   of nin , nout , dk , din , and dout .
364
      We next remark that our results are formally presented for the case of multiple input-output channels
365
      and one-dimensional (per-example) inputs. When x is an d-dimensional input, it is straightforward to
366   develop analogous version for Algorithms 3.1–3.2. However, the analogous version of Algorithm 3.3
367   requires more care. In particular, we would need to develop higher-order versions of (12), replace the
368   one-dimensional Fourier transform in Algorithm 3.3 with its d-dimensional variant, and replace the
369   operators (Q, R) in Algorithm 3.3 with higher-order variants.
370
      In the special case of the two-dimensional DFT, which is useful when the inputs are images, it
371
      is known (Azimi-Sadjadi & King, 1987) that a version of Lemma 3.4 holds where C is replaced
372   by a block-circulant matrix, i.e., where each ci in (12) is replaced by a matrix. Consequently, the
373   version of Algorithm 3.3 for a two-dimensional (per-example) input array x directly follows from
374   this result by replacing (i) Fdin by its analogous two-dimensional DFT, (ii) rev(·) by the operator
375   that reverses a two-dimensional input array lexicographically, and (iii) Q and R by their block
376   two-dimensional variants. We posit that the d-dimensional version of Algorithm 3.3 is one where
377   changes (i)–(iii) are applied in the d-dimensional setting, i.e., with blocks of d-dimensional arrays
      instead of two-dimensional matrices.
