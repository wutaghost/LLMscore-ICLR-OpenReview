                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               The Euler Characteristic Transform (ECT) is a powerful invariant for assessing
011
               geometrical and topological characteristics of a large variety of objects, including
012            graphs and embedded simplicial complexes. Although the ECT is invertible in
013            theory, no explicit algorithm for general data sets exists. In this paper, we address
014            this lack and demonstrate that it is possible to learn the inversion, permitting us
015            to develop a novel framework for shape generation tasks on point clouds. Our
016            model exhibits high quality in reconstruction and generation tasks, affords efficient
017            latent-space interpolation, and is orders of magnitude faster than existing methods.

022   1    I NTRODUCTION
023
024
025   Understanding shapes requires understanding their geometrical and topological properties in tandem.
026   Given the large variety of different representations of such data, ranging from point clouds over
027   graphs to simplicial complexes, a general framework for handling such inputs is beneficial. The Euler
028
      Characteristic Transform (ECT) provides such a framework based on the idea of studying a shape
      from multiple directions—sampled from a sphere of appropriate dimensionality—and at multiple
029
      scales. In fact, the ECT is an injective map, serving as a unique characterisation of a shape (Ghrist
030
      et al., 2018; Turner et al., 2014). Somewhat surprisingly, this even holds when using a finite number
031   of directions (Curry et al., 2022). Hence, while it is known that the ECT can be inverted, i.e. it is
032   possible to reconstruct input data from an ECT, only algorithms for special cases such as planar
033   graphs are currently known (Fasy et al., 2018). Hence, despite its advantageous properties (Dłotko,
034   2024; Munch, 2023), the ECT is commonly only used to provide a hand-crafted set of features for
035   shape classification and regression tasks (Amézquita et al., 2021; Crawford et al., 2020; Marsh et al.,
036   2024; Nadimpalli et al., 2023).
037   Recent work demonstrated that the ECT can be combined with deep-learning paradigms, leading to a
038   fully-differentiable representation, which exhibits high computational and predictive performance in
039   classifying shapes arising from point clouds or graphs (Röell & Rieck, 2024). This representation
040   does not result in accessible latent space and therefore cannot be used to sample new ECTs or invert
041   them. As one of the contributions of this paper, we overcome these restrictions and develop different
042   deep-learning models for inverting the ECT when dealing with point clouds. Point clouds permit
043   capturing objects in high resolution, while still providing a sparse representation of three-dimensional
044   data. Their permutation-invariant nature makes them a challenging data modality for machine-
045
      learning algorithms, resulting in the development of highly-specialised architectures. For instance,
      Point-Voxel CNN (Liu et al., 2019), proposes an architecture that combines sparse voxel convolutions
046
      and an MLP acting on the point cloud directly, whereas DeepSets (Zaheer et al., 2017) develops a
047
      provably permutation-invariant network based on MLPs and suitable aggregation functions. However,
048   representations of point clouds that are intrinsically permutation-invariant have the benefit that a
049   wider range of machine-learning architectures become available, thus also permitting a diverse sets
050   of tasks to be addressed. Our paper argues that the ECT is a representation with preferable properties,
051   being (i) permutation-invariant by definition, and (ii) capable of learning rotations from the data. We
052   thus demonstrate for the first time how to efficiently use the ECT in generative tasks. Specifically,
053   we show that the ECT leads to generative models that outperform existing models both in terms of
      reconstruction/generation quality as well as in computational performance.

      2    BACKGROUND AND M ETHODS
055
056   Point clouds are a ubiquitous data modality, often arising in the context of sensors, such as LiDAR in
057   self-driving cars, or computer-aided design. They typically occur in large volumes, requiring ideally
058   real-time inference with often limited computational resources. Efficient and optimisable architectures
059   are therefore highly preferable for such applications. As we will subsequently demonstrate, the ECT
060   considerably simplifies the architectural requirements, because its discretisation represents a point
061   cloud as an image. Thus, a larger set of optimisation and compression techniques become available,
062   making the ECT compatible with generic as opposed to specialised architectures.

084   E ULER C HARACTERISTIC T RANSFORMS
085
086
      We first describe the Euler Characteristic Transform (ECT) in the more general setting of simpli-
      cial complex before giving an explanation of our performance improvements for handling point
087
      clouds. Simplicial complexes extend the dyadic relations of graphs to incorporate higher-order
088
      elements (simplices) such as triangles or tetrahedra. These complexes are a natural modality for
089   modelling data; 3D meshes can be considered 2-dimensional simplicial complexes, for instance,
090   with the 2-simplices given by the (triangular) faces. Simplicial complexes and their invariants,
091   i.e. characteristic properties that remain unchanged under transformations like homeomorphisms,
092   play an important role in computational topology. A key feature of these invariants is that they
093   are an intrinsic property, meaning that they do not depend on a specific choice of coordinates. An
094   important (combinatorial) invariant is the Euler Characteristic χ, defined as the alternating sum of the
                                                                PD
095   number of simplices in each dimension, i.e. χ(K) := d=0 (−1)d |Kd |, where Kd denotes the set of
096   d-simplices of a D-dimensional simplicial complex K. To extend the expressivity of this invariant,
097   we need to provide it with geometrical and topological information about the input data. This requires
098   vertex coordinates for K, so that K ⊂ Rn , and a continuous function f : vert(K) → R defined on
099
      the vertices vert(K) of K. Given t ∈ R, we now consider the pre-image of f of the sublevel set
      (∞, t], denoted by Kt := f −1 ((∞, t]). This pre-image includes a k-simplex if all its vertices, i.e.
100
      all its 0-simplices, are included in the pre-image, hence it is a subcomplex of K. The function f ,
101
      also referred to as a filtration function, permits us to calculate the Euler Characteristic χ(Kt ) of each
102   pre-image Kt , leading to the Euler Characteristic Curve (ECC) induced by f . The main insight of
103   the Euler Characteristic Transform (Turner et al., 2014, ECT) is that it is possible to use a family
104   of filtration functions, parametrised by a direction on a sphere S n−1 , to obtain a highly-expressive
105   representation of the simplicial complex as a family of curves. With a sufficient number of directions,
106   the ECT becomes an injective function mapping each point cloud to a unique summary (Curry et al.,
107   2022). In our discretised setting (see below), we typically choose a large number of directions to
      counteract the loss of precision.

134   Interchanging the indicator function for a smooth sigmoid function makes this discrete construction
135   differentiable with respect to the direction ξ as well as an input coordinate x, enabling its use as a
136   machine-learning layer. Moreover, the sigmoid approximation also makes computations parallelizable,
137   resulting in high throughput (Nadimpalli et al., 2023; Röell & Rieck, 2024).

      In practice, the structure of the ECT permits us to represent its discretised version as an image,
139   with rows in the image indexing the individual values in the filtration function, and the columns
140   indexing the selected directions. Machine-learning models, operating on such image data, assume
141   that neighbourhoods in the image are related and apply convolutions to process and parse features
142   for downstream tasks such as classification or segmentation. To apply these models to the ECT, it
143   is thus necessary that directions that are close together on the unit sphere are also close together
144   in the image representation. In two dimensions, a single angle (parametrising the unit circle) is
145   sufficient. However, in higher dimensions, there is no canonical parametrisation. While it is possible
146   to parametrise the unit sphere with spherical coordinates and stack the resulting representation for
147
      each direction into a voxel grid, the memory and compute requirements scale cubically with the
      ECT’s resolution, making the approach not scalable. We propose a different approach that embeds a
148
      unit circle along each pair of axes in the ambient space. For each circle we sample the directions
149
      along a regular interval to obtain a multi-channel image of the object. The number of channels in the
150   image scales quadratically with the input dimension n of the point cloud, since we consider each pair
151   of axes for a total of n(n − 1)/2 channels, thus posing only a slight limitation for extremely high
152   dimensions. The main advantage of our approach is that we can use CNNs, which are well-suited
153   for multi-channel images. We find that this representation provides sufficient expressivity to encode
154   equivariance with respect to orientation through data augmentation, which we will further explore in
155   the experiments.
156   Our last improvement to the ECT concerns its invertibility. Being an injective mapping, the pre-image
157   of an ECT is guaranteed to be unique. Nevertheless, to this date, there are no known generally-
158   applicable procedures for inverting the ECT. Our differentiable approximation of the ECT permits us
159   to use machine-learning models to learn the inversion, provided sufficient training data are available.
160   We thus formulate the inversion as training an encoder–decoder model. The encoder turns input point
161   clouds into an ECT, whereas the decoder aims to reconstruct a point cloud from an ECT. We realise
      both of these steps using an MLP. Figure 1 provides a high-level overview of our pipeline.

      Although the EMD has been used as a loss term for various point-cloud processing tasks, the
178   computational requirements limit its practicality. Next to the improved ECT calculations, we thus
179   propose a novel topologically-inspired loss term that is both density-aware and efficient to compute.
180   Tracking the Euler Characteristic along h we obtain the cumulative histogram of X along a given
181   direction ξ. From this cumulative histogram, we can approximate the density through the derivative
182   with respect to h: If the ECT along each direction is approximated with a smooth sigmoid function
183   and we calculate its derivative, we obtain a density estimate. In essence, we obtain a directional
184   kernel density estimate with the kernel equal to the derivative of the sigmoid function. A kernel
185   density estimate centres a kernel function, often a Gaussian, around each data point and estimates the
186
      density through the summation of centred kernel functions. In our case, the points are the heights and
      around each height, we centre the derivative of the sigmoid function, which resembles a Gaussian,
187
      while approximating the density through the summation. Mathematically, this results in

194   along each direction ξ to obtain a ‘directional’ probability density function. Given two such density

203   3   E XPERIMENTS
204
205   Having a theoretical pipeline for reconstructing point clouds in various dimensions in place, we
206   perform comprehensive experiments to understand both qualitative and quantitative properties of our
207   methods. Our experiments comprise three parts:
208     (i) We first evaluate reconstruction and generation performance on a benchmark dataset.
209    (ii) We then show that we learn equivariant representations without requiring architectural changes.
210   (iii) Finally, we show that interpolating between ECTs leads to smooth transitions between shapes.

215   reconstructing them from a latent representation. Our ECT-MLP model consists of a standard MLP

452   3.3   I NTERPOLATING BETWEEN S HAPES
453
454   As our final experiment, we consider each ECT to be an element of a (disentangled) latent space,
455   and we interpolate between the ECT of different classes on the manifolds dataset (from the previous
456   section), the ‘airplane’ class of ShapeNetCore15k, and MNIST. This is important to understand
457   the characteristic properties of the ECT since the ECT, while injective on the space of all shapes,
458   fails to be surjective, i.e. not every ECT is a plausible representation of a shape. We remark that
459   our representation of the ECT as an image enables us to efficiently interpolate on a per-pixel basis.
460
      To assess the quality of the latent space, we reconstruct each ECT during the interpolation using
      ECT-MLP. Figure 5 depicts the resulting point clouds. Latent representations remain ‘plausible,’
461
      even when interpolating between manifolds like a Möbius strip and a torus, whose topological
462
      characteristics differ substantially. In this case, we find that the ECT-MLP first changes the Möbius
463   strip into a sphere, which is subsequently changes into a torus. This process entails changing the
464   orientation of the encoded object, meaning that the ECT encodes information about the orientation in
465   the latent space. This leads us to conclude that the ECT results in advantageous latent space for point
466   cloud processing, since it permits us to control the orientation of the objects before, during, and after
467   the interpolation.

471   4     C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
472
473
474   In this paper, we develop the first approach to efficiently invert a geometrical-topological descriptor,
475   the Euler Characteristic Transform, and show its efficacy in reconstructing and generating shapes.
476   Our pipeline captures characteristic properties of different datasets and uses the ECT as an intrinsic
477
      and integral part of the model. We also propose an extension for synthesising new shapes by sampling
      the corresponding latent spaces. Despite its simplicity, our model produces high-quality and diverse
478
      results that are on a par with or even exceed the reconstruction and generation quality of methods
479
      with more involved architectures. Our model is orders of magnitude faster than existing methods,
480   thus permitting real-time shape generation on both the CPU and the GPU. Moreover, our experiments
481   show that both the correct shape and the orientation are learned from the data, leading to an intrinsic
482   approximation of equivariance. Finally, we explored the use of the ECT as a latent space, finding
483   high-quality intermediate reconstructions and smooth interpolations between both their shapes and
484   reconstructions. For future work, we aim to explore (i) directly imbuing ECT-based schemes with
485   equivariance properties, (ii) developing novel latent-space interpolation schemes based on optimal
      transport, and (iii) extending our methods to graphs and simplicial complexes.
