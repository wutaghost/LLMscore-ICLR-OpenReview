009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Designing a reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) algorithm for
012            learning from preferences requires assuming a preference model, sometimes im-
013            plicitly. A preference model that poorly describes how humans generate prefer-
014            ences risks learning a poor approximation of the human’s unobservable reward
015            function. In this paper, we conduct three human studies to assess whether one
016
               can influence the expression of real human preferences to more closely conform
               to a desired preference model. Importantly, our approach does not seek to alter
017
               the human’s unobserved reward function. Rather, we change how humans use
018
               this reward function to generate preferences, such that they better match whatever
019            preference model is assumed by a particular RLHF algorithm. We introduce three
020            interventions: showing humans the quantities that underlie a preference model,
021            which is normally unobservable information derived from the reward function;
022            training people to follow a specific preference model; and modifying the prefer-
023            ence elicitation question. All intervention types show significant effects, provid-
024            ing practical tools to improve preference data quality and the resultant alignment
025            of learned reward functions. Overall we establish a novel research direction in
026            model alignment: training humans and designing interfaces to increase human
027
               conformance with the assumptions of the algorithm that will learn from their in-
               put.

      Prior work has explored different choices for this preference model and provided evidence that the
046
      more aligned the RLHF algorithm’s preference model is with how humans generate preferences, the
047
      more aligned the resulting reward function is (Knox et al., 2022). An unsuitably specified preference

049   that human preferences arise probabilistically from partial return, the sum of rewards over a trajec-
050   tory segment. By this assumption, humans presented with two trajectory segments tend to prefer
051   the one that accrues greater reward, as measured by their reward function (Christiano et al., 2017;
052   Ouyang et al., 2022). Other work has challenged whether partial return is a sufficiently descriptive
053   model of human preferences and proposed alternative models of human preference (Knox et al.,
      2022; Kim et al., 2023). We instead take a prescriptive approach to learning from human prefer-

      ences. Specifically, we propose designing training and preference elicitation interfaces to influence
055   humans to better conform to a chosen preference model. We test influencing humans towards the
056   partial return preference model or regret preference model, which is based on each segment’s devi-
057   ation from optimality (Knox et al., 2022). See Figure 1 for a visual summary of our interventions.
058
      We introduce three methods and evaluate the ability of each to influence humans towards these two
059
      different preference models. First, in the Privileged experiment we present subjects with calcula-
060
      tions of regret or partial return for each segment during preference elicitation, thus providing the
061   information needed to exactly follow the target preference model. This first intervention is merely
062   a proof of concept, since it requiress knowledge of the reward function. In contrast, the next two
063   methods can be deployed in practice. Second, in the Trained experiment we train humans to follow
064   a specific preference model. Third, in the Question experiment we change only the question asked
065   during preference elicitation. The first two methods result in significantly more conformance with
066   either target preference model. The third method also increases conformance with either preference
067   model, but significant effects are only observed when targeting the partial return preference model.
068   At a high level, we contribute a straightforward approach to improving model alignment: training
069   humans and designing interfaces to increase human conformance with the assumptions of the al-
070   gorithm that learns from their input. Our experiments yield guidance for RLHF practitioners and
071   lay groundwork for future research at the intersection of interface design and learning from human
072   input, RLHF especially. The code for all computational experiments and interfaces for collecting
073   preferences, as well as the collected datasets of human preferences, are attached with this submission
074   and will be made public upon publication.

      2.1   L EARNING FROM HUMAN PREFERENCES
079
080   Extensive research has explored learning from human preferences for RLHF. This research includes
081   RLHF approaches that explicitly learn a reward function (Christiano et al., 2017; Ibarz et al., 2018;
082   Sadigh et al., 2017; Lee et al., 2021a;b; Ziegler et al., 2019; Ouyang et al., 2022; OpenAI, 2022;
083   Bıyık et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022; Glaese et al., 2022; Knox et al., 2022; Touvron
084   et al., 2023; Ethayarajh et al., 2024) and other approaches that directly learn a policy or advantage
085   function from human preferences (Rafailov et al., 2024; Knox et al., 2024; Hejna et al., 2023).
086   All algorithms in the works cited above assume one of the two models of human preference that
087
      this paper focuses on. Our investigation of how to increase human conformance with the assumed
      preference model is compatible with and strengthens this past research.
088
089
090   2.2   M ODELING HUMAN PREFERENCES
091
      Other research has sought agent alignment by developing preference models that better model hu-
092
      man preferences, such as by assuming that human preferences arise from a segment’s regret (Knox
093
      et al., 2022) or weighted sum of non-Markovian rewards (Kim et al., 2023), instead of a segment’s
094   sum of Markovian rewards as is commonly assumed. However, improving the preference model still
095   results in some gap between the preference model and actual human preferences, which are subject
096   to difficult-to-model confounding factors and individual differences. Our research seeks to close this
097   gap for whatever preference model is chosen.
098
      Further, even if we had a perfect descriptive model of how all humans generate preferences, we
099
      may want preferences to be generated by a different preference model that permits more tractable
100   algorithms, greater sample efficiency, or some theoretical guarantees. The methods we introduce
101   can also help in these settings.

362   to signify near-optimal performance.

518   6    C ONCLUSION
519
520
      The choice of preference model used by an RLHF algorithm introduces a source of misalignment
521
      between how humans are assumed to generate preferences and how they actually generate prefer-
522   ences, potentially limiting the alignment of the learned reward function. Even if we could perfectly
523   model all human preferences, we may wish that preferences are generated by a different model that
524   is computationally efficient for RLHF or provides certain theoretical guarantees. To this end, we
525   propose influencing human preferences towards a chosen preference model through user-interface
526   design—a novel direction for RLHF research.
527
      We first establish that humans can be significantly influenced towards a specific preference model
528   when privileged information about that model is shown during preference elicitation. We then in-
529   troduce two practical interventions; training subjects to follow a preference model in the Trained
530   experiment—which significantly influences them towards that specific model—-and changing the
531   preference elicitation question in the Question experiment—which can significantly influence hu-
532   mans towards the partial return preference model and moderately towards the regret preference
533   model. All three interventions result in learning more aligned reward functions.
534   Our findings suggest that human training and preference elicitation interfaces should be viewed as
535   essential tools for improving alignment in RLHF. Appendix A highlights the potential practicality of
536   our approach; the Trained and Question interventions demonstrate important potential for real-world
537   application. Notably, the Question experiment offers a viable path forward by influencing human
538   preferences towards a specific preference model without requiring knowledge of the ground-truth
539   reward function, while the Trained experiment establishes a foundation for extending this method-
      ology to real-world domains.
