011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            End-to-end autonomous driving (AD) systems integrate complex decision-making
014            processes. Assessing the safety of these systems against potential security threats,
015
               including backdoor attacks, is a stepping stone for real-world deployment. How-
               ever, traditional methods focus on static triggers, which do not adequately reflect
016
               the dynamic nature of these systems and could be impractical to deploy in the real
017
               world. To address these limitations, we propose a novel backdoor attack against the
018            end-to-end AD systems that leverage multi-vehicles’ trajectories as triggers. We
019            employ different behavior models and their configurations to generate the trigger
020            trajectories, which are then quantitatively evaluated using temporal logic specifica-
021            tions. This evaluation guides the subsequent perturbations to the behavior model
022            configurations. Through an iterative process of regeneration and re-evaluation, we
023            can refine and generate realistic and plausible trigger trajectories that involve mul-
024            tiple vehicles’ complex interactions. Furthermore, we develop a negative training
025            strategy by incorporating patch trajectories that share similarities with the triggers
026
               but are designated not to activate the backdoor. We thus enhance the stealthiness of
               the attack, refining the system’s responses to trigger scenarios. Through extensive
027
               empirical studies using offline reinforcement learning (RL) driving agents with
028
               various trigger patterns and target action designs, we demonstrate the flexibility
029            and effectiveness of our proposed attack, showing the under-exploration of exist-
030            ing end-to-end AD systems’ vulnerabilities to such multi-vehicle-based backdoor
031            attacks. We also evaluate the attack against existing defenses and validate different
032            design choices of our attack via a comprehensive ablation study.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   As end-to-end autonomous driving (AD) (Hu et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2023b;a) systems increasingly
038   demonstrate promising performance in diverse real-world applications (Coelho & Oliveira, 2022;
039
      Xu et al., 2024), their deployment demands rigorous testing to ensure reliability against a range of
      security threats (Wang et al., 2021a; Ding et al., 2023a), including backdoor attacks. Recent works
040
      have explored backdoor attacks against different modules within AD systems (Pourkeshavarz et al.,
041
      2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Ni et al., 2024), while the vulnerabilities within the end-to-end systems
042   have not been fully explored yet. Moreover, as discussed in Gong et al. (2024), traditional backdoor
043   attacks often employ static patterns as triggers which fail to capture the dynamic complexities of
044   real-world driving environments. Multi-vehicle interactions have rarely been considered as potential
045   triggers for backdoor attacks in end-to-end AD systems.
046
      Recognizing this gap, our motivation is to investigate the vulnerabilities of end-to-end AD sys-
047
      tems against backdoor attacks with triggers that could be feasibly deployed in real-world situations.
048   We consider a practical scenario where attackers manipulate the behavior of one or multiple ve-
049   hicles by driving along specific trajectories. These movements can be detected by the poisoned
050   ego car’s sensor and LiDAR, thereby activating the backdoor within its system and causing it to
051   crash or behave unpredictably. Notably, such a trigger would be more practical to deploy, com-
052   pared to the cases of the patch trigger, which might require the sudden appearance of a physical
053   object on the road. Figure 1 illustrates our proposed attack, where the two attacker vehicles si-

086   2     R ELATED WORK
087
088   Backdoor attacks in AD systems. Backdoor attacks have been extensively studied in computer
089   vision (CV) and natural language processing (NLP) domain (Liu et al., 2018; Cheng et al., 2021;
090   Tao et al., 2024; Chou et al., 2023; Li et al., 2021), for AD systems, these attacks specifically target
091   modules that utilize deep learning algorithms (Liu et al., 2021; Chai et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2023).
092   For instance, Han et al. (2022); Zhang et al. (2024) focus on physical backdoor attacks against deep
093
      neural network (DNN)-based lane detection (LD) systems. The triggers are static patterns stamped
      on the image-based input of the DNN model, which will be captured by the LD module to induce the
094
      wrong prediction of the lane points. Pourkeshavarz et al. (2024) studies the backdoor attack against
095
      the trajectory prediction modules. They introduce adversarial trajectories as triggers to poison training
096   data, which leads to a misprediction of the future trajectory when the attacker car drives along the
097   specific way. Some works select vision language model-facilitated AD systems (Ding et al., 2023b;
098   Han et al., 2024) as the target model. Ni et al. (2024) consider specific physical objects on the image
099   as the trigger and associate it with dangerous instructions to the downstream AD systems to perform
100   poisoning attacks. Beyond backdoor attacks, Cao et al. (2022; 2023); Zhang et al. (2022) enhance
101   the adversarial robustness by generating adversarial trajectories that can lead to misprediction during
102   inference, without training data poisoning.
103   Application of temporal logic in AV security. Temporal logic serves as a critical tool in the security
104   testing of AVs by providing a formal method to define and verify safety and security properties under
105   diverse operational scenarios (Emerson, 1990). Existing works mainly focus on generating complex
106   scenarios with the help of TL-based language to automatically search for specification-violating
107   test cases in AV systems (Arechiga, 2019; Tuncali et al., 2019; Zhou et al., 2023). In particular,
      Song et al. (2023) employ TL to specify safe missions for the ego car and use fuzzing techniques


                                                                            2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      to generate adversarial trajectories of other cars. These trajectories intentionally lead the ego car to
109   violate these predefined safe missions. Their TL specifications primarily describe the behavior of the
110   ego car, focusing on its adversarial robustness. In contrast, our approach utilizes TL to evaluate the
111   trajectories of multiple surrounding vehicles, which act as triggers in the backdoor attack.
112
      DRL in AD and its vulnerability to poisoning. Deep reinforcement learning (DRL) has been
113
      increasingly applied to AV to enhance decision-making processes under uncertain and dynamic
114
      driving conditions, particularly within end-to-end driving systems (Liang et al., 2018; Chen et al.,
115   2019; Kendall et al., 2019; Toromanoff et al., 2020; Desjardins & Chaib-Draa, 2011; Chekroun et al.,
116   2023). Despite its advancements, DRL has been demonstrated to be susceptible to various security
117   threats (Pattanaik et al., 2017; Gleave et al., 2019; Kiourti et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021b; Zhang et al.,
118   2021; Chen et al., 2023b). Notably, Gong et al. (2024) shows that offline RL is vulnerable to data
119   poisoning during training and conducting experiments on autonomous driving tasks. Pourkeshavarz
120   et al. (2024) propose to use adversarial trajectories as triggers and launch data poisoning backdoor
121   attacks against the specific trajectories prediction module. However, there remains a research gap
122   as their trigger is still a static patch stamped onto the image input of the agent. There is no existing
123
      work studying the vulnerability of RL to backdoor attacks when it is applied to end-to-end driving
      systems with more realistic and multi-vehicle-involved trajectory triggers.

      3.2   OVERVIEW
167
      In our attack, we define triggers based on coordinated interactions among multiple vehicles. We add
168
      timing constraints to these interactions, ensuring that the trigger remains hidden within what appears
169   to be normal variations in traffic flow, while still distinctive and stealthy enough to activate the ego
170   car’s target behavior when needed. For instance, consider two vehicles simultaneously bypass the ego
171   car as the trigger, which imposes strict timing constraints on these interactions and also preserves
172   the natural flow of traffic. Our goal is to arrange a scenario where, after two vehicles complete
173   their bypass maneuver and create some distance from the ego car, the ego car will then execute a
174   potentially dangerous action, such as suddenly turning left. Figure 2 outlines two key phases of our
175   attack: I. Temporal logic-based trigger generation and II. Trigger insertion and training optimization.
176   We first discuss the insights behind our design and provide more technical details in Section 3.3.
177   Temporal logic-based trigger generation. To generate trigger trajectories that include multiple
178   vehicles’ interactions, randomly deploying those attack-related vehicles is ineffective. This inefficacy
179   stems from the strict timing constraints and the need for these vehicles to execute driving maneuvers
180   coordinately. To automate this process, a common approach is to directly solve the trajectories by
181   adding context-related constraints (Testouri et al., 2023; Dempster et al., 2023). These methods use
182   advanced solvers (Andersson et al., 2019) and the vehicle dynamics model (Rajamani, 2011) to solve
183
      ordinary differential equations (ODEs), which finally yields vehicle positions at every second. We
      can solve our trigger trajectory by adding constraints on the vehicle’s position and timing to the
184
      equations. However, the main limitation is that this approach heavily relies on precise dynamics
185
      models to solve natural trajectories, which can be costly and time-consuming to obtain.
186
187   Given this challenge, instead of directly solving the trigger trajectories, as shown in Figure 2, we
188   propose a framework with two steps: behavior model-driven trajectory generation and temporal
189
      logic-based trajectory evaluation. This framework begins by deploying multiple attacker vehicles in
      the simulator to generate natural trajectories. Those vehicles follow different behavior models, which
190
      define control schemes that govern the vehicle actions and ensure they act in predictable, rule-based
191
      manners, such as lane following, and overtaking the ego car when conditions permit. By assigning
192   different behavior models to the attacker vehicle, we do not need to rely on rigid analytical solutions
193   and allow a more flexible combination of driving behaviors.
194
195
      In the second step, we add timing constraints about the trajectories, which naturally inspires us to
      employ temporal logic for their evaluation. TL serves as an ideal framework for evaluating whether
196
      continuous signals satisfy specified, predefined positional constraints at specific time steps. We define
197
      TL specifications for different trigger trajectory patterns separately, which we will provide more
198   details in Section 3.3. Intuitively, these specifications are designed to capture whether the trajectory
199   reaches certain positions at specific time steps. We leverage DiffSpec (Xiong & Eappen, 2023; Kurtz
200   & Lin, 2022) to evaluate whether the trajectory satisfies our defined specification. The evaluation will
201   return a positive score if the trajectory meets our predefined specifications, with the score increasing
202   in positivity the more precisely the specifications are met. For negative scores, we will randomly
203   perturb the configurations of these behavior models in the previous step. The trajectory’s alignment
204   with our trigger specifications is assessed again using the same TL specifications. This iterative
205   process not only streamlines the generation of trigger trajectories but also ensures their efficacy and
206
      reproducibility across different simulation environments.
207   Trigger insertion. Following the poisoning strategy of RL in Section 3.1, to construct a complete
208   poisoned dataset D′ , besides the state of the agent, which can be collected by deploying the attacker
209   vehicle and obtaining the corresponding ego car’s sensor input, we also need to specify and modify
210   the ego car’s action from at to a′t , i.e., the action that the attacker wants the ego car to show after
211   observing the trigger trajectory, and the reward. When manipulating the reward, we modify it to be
212
      half of the maximum final reward, to guarantee that the connection between the trigger and target
      action is captured, and the agent will not over-fit on the poisoned experience.
213
214   During the poisoning process, we make a key observation that the target action can be falsely activated
215   when some similar but not exactly trigger behavior appears. Using the two cars simultaneously
      bypassing as an example, the poisoned agent that has been trained on such kind of trigger will take

238   3.3   T ECHNIQUE DETAILS
239
      Behavior models. The key idea behind the behavior models (Treiber et al., 2000) is its rule-based
240
      framework that generates control signals to help the vehicle adjust its speed and maintain longitudinal
241   safe distances from other vehicles, under uniform traffic conditions. For instance, steering behaviors
242   are influenced by parameters such as the maximum steering angle and PID control settings, which
243   help in achieving precise lane following and maneuvering. Building upon the basic lane-following
244   capabilities of the behavior model, we customized and extended this model into two specialized
245   behaviors: overtaking and braking. The overtake behavior model augments the basic model by
246   incorporating rules that allow a vehicle to safely change lanes and overtake another vehicle when
247   some longitudinal distance conditions are met. Such as sufficient gaps in the adjacent lane and the
248   vehicle. The braking behavior model adds the rule that when the distance to an adjacent vehicle
249
      is larger than some threshold, the vehicle will suddenly brake. By integrating these customized
      behaviors into the behavior model, we enable a complex set of interactions between multiple vehicles,
250
      which is necessary for our trigger generation. The configuration of the behavior model primarily
251
      involves two parameters: the final speed and the initial position. These parameters are the ones we
252   will perturb in subsequent steps.
253
254   Temporal logic specification. Let ϕi represent the TL expression for the i-th vehicle, specifying
255
      that it must reach a defined goal within a certain time range. For each vehicle, we define: ϕi :=
      Reach((xi , yi ), (wi , hi ))).eventually([tsi , tei ]), where (xi , yi ) is the coordinates of the defined
256
      goal. We consider the goal to be a rectangle with width wi and height hi . To synchronize the
257

258   conjunction: Φ := i=1 ϕi . Φ is true only if all individual vehicle conditions ϕi are satisfied,

260   the specific position (xi , yi ) within the time window from tsi to tei . We leverage DiffSpec Xiong &
261   Eappen (2023) , which takes a trajectory and the pre-defined TL goal as input and outputs a positive
262   score if the trajectory satisfies the goal and a negative score otherwise. Specifically, it calculates

266   Negative training. To address the problem that the backdoor will be falsely activated when some
267   non-trigger trajectories appear, a common strategy is to directly add those non-trigger trajectories
268   into the training data, and modify the agent’s action as correct ones. Enabled by our temporal
269   logic-based trajectory evaluation, we collect those trajectories if all of the involved attacker vehicle’s

281
282
      Simulator & RL agent. Our experiments are conducted on MetaDrive (Li et al., 2022). It is a
283
      lightweight self-driving simulator that provides intricate road conditions and dynamic scenarios
284   that closely emulate real-world driving situations. The goal of our end-to-end driving agent is to
285   arrive at the destination from the starting point without any crash. We evaluate the effectiveness
286   of our attack across three tasks, each corresponding to maps with varying difficulty levels: easy,
287   medium, and hard. As the difficulty increases, the complexity of the traffic scenarios also rises. For
288   instance, more difficult maps feature intricate traffic situations such as crossroads and roundabouts,
289   requiring more precise navigation and decision-making from the agent. For the RL algorithm, we
290   use Coptidice Lee et al. (2022) to train the clean and poisoned agent from scratch, as it is shown to
291   perform better on MetaDrive tasks in Liu et al. (2023). We study the effectiveness of our attack on
292
      different RL algorithms in Section 4.2. The state of the agent is a vector that contains the ego car’s
      self-information including heading, velocity, and lidar points surrounding the vehicle. The action is
293
      steering (in degrees), and throttle. For all environments, reward functions consist of generally a dense
294
      driving reward and a sparse terminal reward. The dense reward is the longitudinal movement along
295   the reference line or lane toward the destination. When the episode is terminated due to, i.e. arriving
296   at the destination or driving out of the road, a sparse reward will be added to the dense reward. More
297   details are included in Appendix B.
298

      Triggers & Target action. We design three distinct trigger trajectory patterns: 1) two cars syn-

313   ego car, and 3) one car suddenly brakes in front of the ego car on the left side while the other car
314   overtakes. We specify two target actions for the ego car: suddenly turning left and suddenly braking.
315

316
      become more intricate. In the first pattern, both vehicles bypass the ego car in a coordinated manner,
317
      requiring basic synchronization. The second pattern adds complexity by involving simultaneous
318   overtaking and bypassing, demanding more precise timing between the two cars. The third pattern is
319   the most complex, involving a sudden braking maneuver from one vehicle while the other overtakes,
320   creating a more dynamic and unpredictable situation that requires advanced coordination and reaction.
321   Specifically, during poisoning, we designed the ego car to perform the target action once the distance
322   between itself and another vehicle exceeds 10 meters. In Appendix D, we conduct experiments
323   that demonstrate the stealthiness of our designed triggers using a dataset with real-world driving
      trajectories. We will release our code upon publication.

      4.2      ATTACK EFFECTIVENESS
338
339   Different trigger patterns. We evaluate the effectiveness of three different trigger patterns on
340   three tasks and report the results in Table 1. For all trigger patterns, we use the same poisoning
341   rates of 15% and the number of patch trajectories is the same with the poisoned trajectories. Our
342   results demonstrate that the proposed attack is effective with all designed trigger patterns, in all three
343   difficulty levels of tasks. The combination of low rewards, high ADE, and high MVR indicates that
344   the end-to-end AD system is highly susceptible to our backdoor attack. We also find that complex
345   triggers are more challenging to inject. For example, the brake-overtake trigger requires more intricate
346
      coordination between two attacker vehicles, leading to lower overall attack effectiveness compared
      to the other two triggers, under the same poison rate. In contrast, the simpler sync-bypass trigger
347
      demonstrates superior attack performance overall, outperforming the more complex triggers. In the
348
      benign setup, the backdoored agent tends to achieve MVR in medium and hard tasks compared to easy
349   tasks. This suggests that the backdoor attack has a more pronounced effect on the agent’s performance
350   in challenging environments. This may be because the agent’s capacity to handle complex tasks is
351   already strained, and when compromised by the backdoor pattern, its original behavior is further
352   disrupted. The limited adaptability of the RL agent makes this effect more noticeable in harder
353   scenarios, where its performance degrades more significantly.

      Different RL algorithms. We focus on offline RL as it allows for straightforward dataset poisoning,

358   our attack. Offline RL algorithms generally fall into three categories: directly imitating policies,

364
      the poisoned MVRs rise dramatically, from 0.0% to nearly 100%. These metrics clearly demonstrate
365
      the effectiveness of the backdoor attack in compromising the RL agents’ performance. The BC
366   agent’s performance, even under benign conditions, was notably poor across all three difficulty levels
367   and inferior to that of the other two algorithms. Moreover, the poisoned MVR for the BC agent
368   was not as high as that of the other two algorithms, suggesting that the attack was less effective on
369   this algorithm. This may indicate that BC inherently lacks robustness, or it may be less susceptible
370   to specific types of adversarial manipulations used in our attacks. The fundamental simplicity and
371   direct imitation approach of BC might not capture the complex decision-making patterns that more
372   sophisticated algorithms learn, which could result in both a generally lower performance on clean
373   data and a diminished sensitivity to the crafted adversarial conditions.

409   4.3      D EFENSE AND MITIGATION
410
411   Defense selection. Traditional backdoor defenses in CV and NLP domains (Wang et al., 2019; Qi
412   et al., 2020; Shen et al., 2021; Feng et al., 2023; Bharti et al., 2022; Tao et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2022)
413   mainly focus on static triggers and cannot be directly applied in our attack where the trigger is a set
414   of dynamic vehicle trajectories. Furthermore, backdoor defenses designed for two-player competitive
415   RL games (Chen et al., 2023a; Guo et al., 2023) are also not inapplicable, given our context of a
416   single-agent environment with multiple attacker vehicles involved as part of the environment instead
417
      of active agents. Considering that our proposed attack is based on data poisoning and remains
      agnostic to the training algorithm, we assume the defender has access to the training process of the
418
      poisoned agent. This allows the defense mechanism can be deployed in the training time to detect
419
      poisoned samples before being fed to the victim agent. Specifically, we consider two training-time
420   defenses suited to our attack model: The first is trajectory smoothing Zhang et al. (2022), a trajectory
421   pre-processing technique to mitigate adversarial attacks against the trajectory prediction module. It
422   serves as a data-level defense, smoothing out the trajectories to prevent adversarial patterns from
423   influencing the training data. The second is DP-SGD (Hong et al., 2020), which targets the training
424   algorithm itself. It clips the gradients of the weights with abnormal l2 norm and perturbs them by
425   adding Gaussian noise to mitigate the effect of poisoned samples.
426   Implementation. For smoothing defense, there are various choices of smoothing algorithms and
427   we follow existing work (Zhang et al., 2022) and use a linear smoother based on convolution in our
428   experiments, we set the kernel size to be 3. We directly applied it to the actions of the agent’s training
429   trajectories, to smooth out the sudden target action sequences and reduce the poisoning effect. We
430   implement DP-SGD on the policy network of the agent as it directly outputs the control signal of the
431   ego car. Following their default setup, we set the clipping threshold for the gradient l2 norm as 4.0
      and the standard deviation of the added Gaussian noise as 0.25.

      Results. Our findings in Table 4 highlight the limitations of current defenses against our proposed
442
      attack. Smoothing defense shows some effectiveness in mitigating backdoor attacks, particularly
443   when the target action is a “brake” command. However, its impact is considerably weaker for more

445   coordination of both speed and steering, which the smoothing defense may not sufficiently handle. For
446   DP-SGD defense, we observed no meaningful prevention of crashes, as evidenced by the persistent
447   P-MVR of 1.00. While there is a slight improvement in reward, allowing the agent to progress further
448   toward its destination, it ultimately still fails by turning left and colliding with the roadside. This

      Poisoning rate. To evaluate the impact of different poisoning rates on the effectiveness of our
456
      proposed attack, we use different poisoning rates, i.e., 10%, 20%, 30%, and 40% to generate the
457   poisoned dataset and train the poisoned agent on hard-level task with two different RL algorithms.
458   We select two vehicles bypass simultaneously as the trigger, and the target action of the ego car is
459   suddenly turning left. The results are shown in Figure 3(a) and Figure 3(b). We first observe that with
460   the increase in the poisoning rate, the benign reward of the poisoned agent decreases, indicating that
461   the normal functionality of the agent has been impacted, and it also makes the poisoned agent easy
462   to detect. Meanwhile, a higher poisoning rate leads to an increase in the poisoned MVR, which is
463   expected since the agent is trained with more poisoned trajectories, reinforcing its recognition of the
464   trigger pattern. It suggests that the attacker needs to carefully choose the poisoning rate to balance
465
      between stealthiness and attack effectiveness.
466   Negative training. To validate the necessity of our negative training design, we remove the negative
467   training step and directly train the poisoned agent without patch trajectories. This variation is denoted
468   as w/o neg. As discussed in Section 3.3, we consider all trajectories whose TL evaluation score is
469   below a certain threshold as patch trajectories. We use patch trajectories and non-trigger trajectories
470   interchangeably to denote the same concept. We record the configurations of those attack vehicles
471
      that generate the patch trajectories for further testing. During testing, we deploy the attack vehicles
      using these recorded configurations and evaluate over 100 episodes. We measure the MVR and
472
      cumulative reward, which are referred to as F-MVR and non-trigger reward. A clean pattern in Table 5
473
      is that without the negative training, the MVR of the poisoned agent in the presence of non-trigger
474   trajectories is significantly high, even reaching 100%. Similarly, the reward is low, indicating that the
475   agent is influenced and exhibits the target action even when the trigger is absent. These observations
476   suggest that without negative training, the poisoned agent is easily misled by non-trigger trajectories,
477   highlighting the critical role of negative training in filtering out such corner cases to enhance the
478   attack’s stealthiness and precision. Moreover, we observe that the inclusion of patch trajectories does
479   not negatively impact the benign performance of the poisoned agent. The results of other metrics are

513   5                    D ISCUSSION
514
515   We propose a novel backdoor attack that leverages multi vehicles’ trajectory as triggers to attack
516   the end-to-end AD systems. To automatically generate complex trigger trajectories, we design a
517   two-phrase framework, empowered by the behavior model and temporal logic. We further improve the
518   poisoned training process with negative training to make poisoned agents’ response more precisely to
519   only triggers. In this paper, we consider RL-based driving agents as the instantiation of end-to-end AD
520   systems. Existing works also explored module-based planning-oriented AD systems (Hu et al., 2023)
521   to achieve self-driving. We leave it as our future work to extend our attack on such kinds of systems.
522
      Moreover, our current temporal logic framework includes one specification that evaluates whether
      the vehicle arrives at a specific time range. We aim to design more diverse specifications (Arechiga,
523
      2019; Zhou et al., 2023) that can support our framework to define more complex behaviors among
524
      the vehicles. Finally, the rapid development of large language model (LLM) provides the possibility
525   to generate safety-critical scenarios that help AV testing (Wang et al., 2024), inspired by this line
526   of work, we will explore how to combine with LLM to generate both scenarios and trajectories to
527   comprehensively test AV systems.

      6                    C ONCLUSION
531
      In this paper, we demonstrate a novel approach to enhancing the security testing of autonomous
532
      driving systems by introducing realistic, trajectory-based backdoor triggers. Through the strategic
533
      manipulation of vehicle behaviors and the application of temporal logic for evaluation, we have shown
534   that it is not only feasible to generate and deploy dynamic triggers but also effective in revealing
535   vulnerabilities within end-to-end autonomous driving systems. Our negative training strategy further
536   improves the stealthiness of these attacks, making them difficult to detect and mitigate with traditional
537   security measures. Through extensive empirical experiments, we effectively demonstrated the
538   robustness and adaptability of our proposed attack using various trigger and target action designs.
539   Moreover, our experiments against existing defense mechanisms and a detailed ablation study validate
      the design choices of our approach.

761   In developing this novel attack against autonomous driving systems, we are aware of the ethical
762
      implications associated with exposing vulnerabilities in safety-critical systems. The primary intent
      behind this research is to advance the understanding of potential security weaknesses within end-to-
763
      end autonomous driving technologies, thereby enabling the development of more robust defenses. It
764
      is crucial to state that this research should not be used to facilitate real-world attacks but rather to
765   inform and improve the resilience of autonomous systems against malicious threats.
766
767   To mitigate ethical risks, we have implemented several safeguards. Firstly, our experimental setup
768
      strictly adheres to simulated environments, ensuring no real-world testing that could lead to unin-
      tended harm. Additionally, all findings and methodologies are shared with the intent for defensive
769
      use only, aiming to assist developers and researchers in testing their systems against similar attack
770
      vectors. Furthermore, this research is conducted under strict ethical guidelines to ensure that it aligns
771   with the broader goal of enhancing vehicle safety and security rather than compromising it.

774   B    RL EXPERIMENT SETUP
775
776   Simulator. MetaDrive simulator provides off-the-self RL environments for end-to-end driving. We
777   follow the basic setting in MetaDrive. In MetaDrive RL environments, the state includes map sensor
778   readings (Camera or LiDAR), high-level navigation commands, and self-vehicle states. Specifically,
779
      there are 240 LiDAR points surrounding the vehicle, starting from the vehicle head in a clockwise
      direction, scan the neighboring area with a radius of 50 meters. The sensors return the relative
780
      distances to the surrounding vehicles. The state vector of the RL agent consists of three parts and the
781
      complete dimension of the state vector is 259.
782
783          • Ego State: current states such as the steering, heading, and velocity.
784
785
             • Navigation: the navigation information that guides the vehicle toward the destination.
               Concretely, MetaDrive first computes the route from the spawn point to the destination of
786
               the ego vehicle. Then a set of checkpoints is scattered across the whole route at certain
787
               intervals. The relative distance and direction to the next checkpoint and the next checkpoint
788            will be given as the navigation information.
789
790
             • Surrounding: the surrounding information is encoded by a vector containing the Lidar-like
               cloud points. We use 72 lasers to scan the neighboring area with a radius of 50 meters.
791
792
      The action consists of low-level control commands including steering and throttle. MetaDrive
793   receives normalized action as input to control each target vehicle: a = [a1 , a2 ]T ∈ [−1, 1]2 . At each
794   environmental time step, MetaDrive converts the normalized action into the steering us (degree),
795   acceleration ua (hp), and brake signal ub (hp) in the following ways:
796
797          • us = Smax (a1 )
798          • ua = Fmax max(0, a2 )
799
800
             • ub = −Bmax min(0, a2 )
801
      wherein Smax (degree) is the maximal steering angle, Fmax (hp) is the maximal engine force, and
802
      Bmax (hp) is the maximal brake force.
803
804   MetaDrive uses a compositional reward function as R = Rdriving + Rcrash.vehicle.penalty +
805   Rout.of.road.penalty . Here, the driving reward Rdriving = dt − dt−1 , wherein the dt and dt−1
806
      denote the longitudinal coordinates of the target vehicle in the direction of consecutive time steps,
      providing a dense reward to encourage the agent to move forward. By default, the penalty is -5 if the
807
      agent collides with surrounding vehicles, and the penalty is -10 if the agent runs out of the road.

840
841   In this section, we use the Next Generation Simulation (NGSIM) dataset to analyze the frequency
842   and conditions under which our three designed trigger patterns appear. NGSIM collected high-quality
843   traffic datasets at four different locations, including two freeway segments (I-80 and US-101) and two
844   arterial segments (Lankershim Boulevard and Peachtree Street), between 2005 and 2006. It provides
845   data points including vehicle position, speed, acceleration, and lane occupancy over time.
846
      We then determine the frequency of our trigger trajectory appearing in those real-world driving
847   behaviors. We design an algorithm that utilizes time-windowed proximity checks between the
848   vehicles. Take synchronous bypass as an example. We consider any lane that is neither the leftmost
849   nor the rightmost as a potential lane for the ego car and consider every vehicle in these lanes as a
850   possible ego car. For each identified ego car, we examine the adjacent lanes to both sides within
851   a defined 10-second window, which we consider an adequate duration for completing the trigger
852   maneuver. During this time window, we gather data on vehicles positioned on both sides of the
853   ego car. Specifically, we check for the presence of two vehicles that simultaneously appear at a
854   consistent distance of 50 feet in front of the ego car. Furthermore, we verify that both vehicles remain
855
      longitudinally aligned with the ego car, ensuring they have not shifted from other lanes. We calculate
      the ratio of synchronous to general bypass events to measure the frequency of synchronous bypass
856
      occurrences. The numerator represents the number of synchronous bypass events, which are strictly
857
      timed, while the denominator accounts for all general bypass events, which are identified without
858   imposing timing constraints. Similarly, for the overtake trigger, we check if a car was previously
859   alongside the ego car in an adjacent lane and subsequently moved to be directly in front of the ego car
860   within the same lane. For the brake-overtake trigger, we assess whether a car remains approximately
861   50 feet in front of the ego car without changing its position over a 3-second time window. It is
862   non-trivial to define the denominator for those two trigger trajectories. To generally approximate the
863   ratio of the left two triggers, we use the same denominator with our synchronous bypass trigger and
      we leave it as a future work to explore more related works to better measure the frequency of the

942
      Dynamics model. In this section, we conduct an ablation study on the impact of varying dynamics
943
      models on the effectiveness of our proposed backdoor attack. In MetaDrive, the behavior and
944   performance of vehicles are influenced by vehicle model defined in the simulator. These models
945   encapsulate a set of parameters that define how a vehicle interacts with its environment, responds to
946   control inputs, and adheres to the laws of physics. Below are key parameters typically included in
947   vehicle dynamics models:
948
949          • Maximum Engine Force: This parameter dictates the maximum force that the vehicle’s
950            engine can exert.
951          • Maximum Brake Force: This defines the maximum braking force that the vehicle can safely
952            apply.
953          • Maximum Steering Angle: This parameter limits how sharply a vehicle can turn.
954
955
             • Wheel Friction: This influences how well the vehicle’s tires grip the road surface.
956          • Maximum Speed: This defines the top speed a vehicle can achieve.
957
958   In our main experiments, we use the default vehicle model for the attack-related vehicle. For ablation
959
      study, we replace the default vehicle with small, medium and large vehicles defined in the simulator,
      each characterized by distinct sets of key dynamics parameters. We keep the vehicle model of the
960
      ego car consistent, and the poisoning rate is the same with Table 1, which is 15%.
961
962   From Table 8, we can observe that changing the vehicle dynamics from small to large does not
963   significantly affect the success of our attack. This observation is consistent across all three tested
964   dynamics models, indicating a robustness of the attack method to changes in vehicle physical
965
      characteristics. Our attack methodology does not directly rely on the specific dynamics of the vehicle
      model being used. Instead, it leverages a behavior model that encapsulates these dynamics as a
966
      component of its framework. This abstraction allows the behavior model to simulate the necessary
967
      actions without being overly dependent on the individual dynamics parameters of any given vehicle.
968   The behavior model integrates these parameters into a broader, more generalized set of behaviors that
969   are designed to trigger the attack effectively.

1014   D.3       I NFLUENCE OF VELOCITY ON ATTACK EFFECTIVENESS .
1015
1016
       During our tests, we observed that trigger events—for example, two cars synchronously bypassing at
       a speed of 60—could activate the ego car’s target action across a broad range of speeds from 25 mph
1017
       to 80 mph. We tried to add patch trajectories that contain bypassing behavior with different velocities
1018
       but it did not succeed in isolating the trigger effect to a specific speed of 60 mph as initially intended.
1019   Given these challenges, a precise velocity specification does not currently serve as a reliable trigger.
1020   This limitation points to the need for further research, and we anticipate addressing the nuanced role
1021   of velocity in triggering mechanisms in future work.
