009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models have driven numerous paradigm shifts in the field of natu-
012            ral language processing, achieving remarkable success in various real-world appli-
013            cations through scaling model size and leveraging long-sequence context reason-
014            ing. However, the transformer architecture, which relies on self-attention, incurs
015            substantial storage and runtime costs when handling long-sequence inference, par-
016
               ticularly due to the generation of extensive Key-Value (KV) cache. Recent studies
               aim to mitigate storage and latency issues while maintaining output quality by re-
017
               ducing the KV cache size, through the elimination of less critical entries, yet they
018
               rely on a basic empirical intuition of identifying critical cache entries based solely
019            on top attention weights. In this paper, we present the first formal investigation
020            into the problem of identifying critical KV cache entries from the perspective of
021            attention output perturbation. By analyzing the output perturbation caused when
022            only critical KV cache entries are used instead of the entire cache, we reveal that,
023            in addition to the commonly used attention weights, the value states within KV
024            entries and the pretrained parameters matrix are also important. Based on this
025            finding, we propose a novel perturbation-constrained selection algorithm to iden-
026            tify critical cache entries by optimizing the worst-case output perturbation. Ex-
027
               tensive evaluations on 16 datasets from Longbench, along with detailed empirical
               analysis, have comprehensively confirmed the effectiveness of constraining output
028
               perturbation perspective in identifying critical KV cache. When combined with
029
               state-of-the-art cache eviction methods, it can achieve up to an additional 34%
030            cache memory savings while maintaining the same generation quality.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Autoregressive large language models (LLMs) based on the transformer architecture have achieved
036   remarkable success, being widely applied in various downstream tasks such as dialogue systems
037   (Yi et al., 2024), chatbots (Achiam et al., 2023), intelligent agents (Wang et al., 2024), and code
038   generation (Gu, 2023). However, the quadratic computational cost inherent in the transformer’s
039
      self-attention mechanism poses significant challenges for practical deployment. To mitigate this,
      LLMs often use a Key-Value (KV) cache, which stores intermediate results from the self-attention
040
      mechanism. Each KV cache entry corresponds to the KV states of a past token, thus allowing for the
041
      bypassing of recomputation of these tokens during autoregressive generation. However, as sequence
042   lengths increase, the number of the KV cache entries expands correspondingly. This expansion in
043   KV cache not only leads to considerable GPU memory overhead but also significantly increases IO
044   latency, hindering the deployment in real-world applications (Sun et al., 2024a).
045
      Recent researches identify that only a subset of KV cache entries substantially contribute to the
046
      output of the self-attention mechanism (Zhang et al., 2024b; Liu et al., 2024a; Tang et al., 2024a).
047
      Therefore, many methods known as cache eviction have been developed to reduce the KV cache size
048   to fit within a given GPU memory budget by evicting non-critical entries during long-sequence in-
049   ference. These budget-constrained methods effectively save GPU memory and improve subsequent
050   decoding speed. Initial findings show that recent KV cache entries are more critical for future token
051   generation, prompting the development of techniques that prioritize retaining KV entries within a
052   recent window (Beltagy et al., 2020; Xiao et al., 2023). However, this approach risks losing essential
053   information from longer sequences. To address this, H2O (Zhang et al., 2024b) and Scissorhands
      (Liu et al., 2024a) observe the power-law distribution of attention weights: a small fraction of KV

054
      cache entries consistently dominates the majority of attention weights, which aligns closely with
055   the concept of cache entry criticality during inference. These methods introduce frameworks that
056   leverage accumulated attention weights to identify and preserve critical cache entries. Following the
057   observation, a series of subsequent works (Adnan et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2024) have
058   further improved performance by refining the attention weight accumulation mechanism or incorpo-
059   rating additional operations like polling to better retain key information. Although the term “critical
060   cache entry” remains an abstract concept without formal definition, current approaches often assume
061   that cache entries with higher attention weights — determined by the similarity between key states
062   in the KV cache and the target query state — indicate the critical cache entries. This assumption
063
      raises two key questions:
064          1. What criteria determine the critical KV cache?
065          2. Is reliance solely on attention weights sufficient for identifying critical cache entries?
066
      In this paper, we formally define the problem of critical cache identification from the perspective of
067
      output perturbation, introducing a theoretical framework that optimizes the perturbation by bound-
068   ing it within worst-case scenarios. Specifically, we formalize the problem as minimizing the output
069   perturbation while substituting the entire KV cache with only the critical cache entries. To quan-
070   tify this perturbation, we employ the L1 distance and derive its upper bound, corresponding to the
071   worst-case perturbation. Our analysis shows that this upper bound is influenced by both the attention
072   weights and the value states projected through the parameter matrix. Building on this foundation,
073   we propose a perturbation-constrained selection algorithm that goes beyond mere reliance on at-
074   tention weights, underscoring the significance of previously overlooked value states and pretrained
075   parameter matrix in identifying critical cache entries.
076   Building on our theoretical framework for critical cache identification based on output perturbation,
077   we replace previous strategies that rely solely on attention weights by seamlessly integrating our
078   algorithm into state-of-the-art (SOTA) cache eviction methods. Using 16 datasets from LongBench,
079   we conduct extensive evaluations across various budgets, demonstrating that our algorithm selects
080   critical cache entries more accurately, effectively improving the post-eviction generation quality.
081   We conduct further empirical analysis to evaluate the benefits of our algorithm, leading to two key
082
      conclusions: (1) across different methods, contexts, budgets, and models, our algorithm consistently
      reduces output perturbation in most attention heads, and (2) its advantages accumulate across lay-
083
      ers, significantly lowering final-layer output perturbation. These findings show that our algorithm
084
      consistently improves post-eviction generation quality and confirms the effectiveness in identifying
085   critical cache entries. Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
086
087         1. We point that current cache eviction methods neglect the crucial problem of KV cache
088            identification. To address this, we propose using output perturbation as criteria to determine
089            critical KV cache entries. Our analysis shows that attention weights alone are insufficient,
090
               as the value states projected by the parameter matrix also play a significant role.
091         2. Building on constraining the worst-case output perturbation, we propose a novel critical
092            entry selection algorithm. When integrated into SOTA eviction methods, comprehensive
093
               evaluations across 16 datasets demonstrate it consistently improves the generation quality.
094         3. Further empirical analysis examines and confirms the benefits of our perturbation-
095            constrained selection algorithm, which also highlights the significant potential for opti-
096
               mizing critical cache selection from the theoretical perspective of output perturbation.

098   2   R ELATED W ORKS
099
100   The inference cost of LLMs is primarily determined by the size of model parameters and KV cache.
101   Early strategies like parameter quantization and network pruning successfully reduce model param-
102   eters. However, with the rise of applications such as long-text summarization (Laban et al., 2023),
103   multi-turn QA (Yang et al., 2018), and techniques like Chain of Thought (Wei et al., 2022), the
104
      increasing sequence length has led to substantial growth in KV cache size. Consequently, recent re-
      search has shifted focus toward reducing KV cache size to enable efficient long-sequence inference,
105
      primarily through two orthogonal approaches: KV cache quantization and KV cache eviction.
106
107   KV cache quantization refers to the application of quantization techniques to reduce the size of
      the KV cache by lowering the precision of individual cache entries (Liu et al., 2024b; Hooper et al.,


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      2024). For example, this can involve quantizing the original 16-bit KV cache entries to 2-bit or 4-bit
109   precision. However, such approaches typically retain all KV cache entries (Hooper et al., 2024). As
110   a result, in autoregressive inference with long sequences, they cannot effectively compress the KV
111   cache to fit within a specified budget. These methods are fundamentally orthogonal to the cache
112   eviction methods, and could further enhance performance by incorporation.
113
      KV cache eviction focuses on retaining only critical KV cache entries while evicting non-critical
114
      ones to reduce cache size. Early methods (Xiao et al., 2023), which preserved recent entries, risked
115   losing important information in long sequences. Techniques like H2O (Zhang et al., 2024b) and
116   Scissorhands (Liu et al., 2024a) used accumulated attention scores to identify key entries, aiming to
117   retain crucial context. Subsequent works refined these methods (Ge et al., 2024b; Adnan et al., 2024;
118   Ge et al., 2024a; Li et al., 2024), with SnapKV (Li et al., 2024) achieving the SOTA performance
119   through introducing window-based attention weight accumulation and pooling operations. More re-
120   cent approaches, such as Pyramid (Yang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a) and AdaKV (Feng et al.,
121   2024), improve post-eviction generation by allocating budgets based on the characteristics of atten-
122   tion heads. However, these methods are largely empirical, relying heavily on attention weights to
123
      identify critical entries. Our paper introduces a novel perturbation-constrained selection algorithm
      based on in-depth analysis from an output perturbation perspective. This algorithm seamlessly in-
124
      tegrates into existing cache eviction methods without altering underlying accumulation processes.
125
      In our work, we demonstrate its effectiveness by applying it to SnapeKV, Pyramid, and AdaKV, all
126   showing consistent improvements in post-eviction generation quality under varying budgets.

      3.4   I DENTIFY C RITICAL CACHE ENTRIES BY CONSTRAINING WORST- CASE PERTURBATION .
258
259   Drawing on optimization strategies in machine learning, we propose lowering the upper bound of
260   perturbation, effectively constraining the worst-case perturbation and thereby reducing actual per-
261   turbations for identifying critical cache entries. However, directly minimizing the upper bound θ
262   remains non-trivial. To balance both the complexity and selection effectiveness, we introduce a
263   two-stage greedy perturbation-constrained selection Algorithm 1, specifically designed to lower the
264   perturbation upper bound for critical cache entry identification.
265   In this algorithm, the total budget b is divided into two portions based on a hyperparameter α. In the
266   first stage, a small fraction of the budget, b0 = b × α, is allocated to prioritize KV cache entries with
267   high attention weights. In the second stage, the remaining budget, b00 = b − b0 , is used to consider
268   both the norms of the projected value states and the attention weights. This two-stage selection
269   employs a Top-K operation to effectively constrain the worst-case perturbation. To substantiate the
      effectiveness of our proposed algorithm, we provide a theoretical analysis in the following section.

444     4.3         R ESULTS
445
446    Tables 1, 2, and 3 present the quality scores across various task domains when integrating our algo-
447    rithm into SnapKV, Pyramid, and AdaKV under different models and cache budgets b. Overall, our
448    algorithm consistently improves post-eviction generation quality across all eviction methods. For
449
       instance, in Table 1, using LLaMA-3.1-8B, our algorithm significantly enhances SnapKV’s quality
       scores across all 6 task domains at a budget size of 256, increasing the average score from 45.09 to
450
       45.66. Similar improvements are observed with larger budgets, such as an increase from 47.00 to
451
       47.41 at a budget of 512, and from 48.08 to 48.25 at a budget of 1024. Pyramid and AdaKV, built
452    upon SnapKV, further optimize generation quality through inter-head budget allocation. However,
453    due to predefined parameters, Pyramid shows diminished generalization performance on the two
454    new LLMs, only slightly improving upon SnapKV at a budget of 128. In contrast, AdaKV, with
455    its adaptive dynamic allocation strategy, maintains a quality advantage over SnapKV across both
456    models. Although the optimizations from these two budget allocation algorithms vary, both show
457    significant improvements across all budget sizes when combined with our algorithm.
458    Figure 1 shows the average scores across all 6 task domains. As the budget increases, generation
459    quality improves for all methods, albeit with diminishing marginal returns. Interestingly, under
460    these diminishing returns, our perturbation-constrained algorithm brings increasingly higher gains.
461    For instance, in Figure 1a, when our algorithm is integrated with SnapKV at a budget of 512, the
462    average score rises to 47.41. By interpolation, SnapKV alone would require a significantly larger
463    cache budget (706) to achieve a similar quality score, implying a 27.5% savings with our algorithm.
464    This is higher than the 23.0% savings at a budget of 256 and 18.7% at 128. Similar observations are
465    also observed in Pyramid (Figure 1b) and AdaKV (Figure 1c). Thus, in real-world deployment, these
466
       significant savings in cache budgets yield substantial GPU memory savings, as well as improved
       decoding efficiency.

                                                           6                  C ONCLUSION
                                           530
                                           531
                                                          In this paper, we pinpoint a key limitation in current cache eviction methods: the reliance on intuitive
                                                          heuristics of using attention weights to select critical cache entries for eviction. For the first time, we
                                           532
                                                          formalize the problem of critical cache entry selection from the perspective of output perturbation
                                           533
                                                          and provide a theoretical analysis. Furthermore, we propose a novel algorithm based on constrain-
                                           534            ing output perturbation in the worst-case for critical cache selection, which is then integrated into
                                           535            existing SOTA cache eviction methods. Comprehensive evaluations using 16 datasets from Long-
                                           536            bench demonstrate that our algorithm improves the performance of various cache eviction methods,
                                           537            across different task domains and budget constraints. Further empirical analysis also confirms and
                                           538            explains this benefit from the perspective of practical output perturbation: our algorithm consistently
                                           539            yields lower perturbation compared to previous methods that rely solely on attention weights, in all
                                                          testings.

      D                  A BLATION S TUDY OF S TAGE 1 IN A LGORITHM 1
897
898
      In our algorithm, we initially set α = 0.25 to allocate a portion of the budget for collecting high
899
      attention weights, corresponding to Assumption 1, to ensure the validity of Theorem 3. We further
900
      conducted ablation experiments by setting α = 0, removing this mechanism, to demonstrate the
901   necessity of budget splitting in guaranteeing the validity of Theorem 3. Table 5 shows the results
902   of ablating α. As seen, when α is set to 0, both Mistral-7B and Llama-3.1-8B exhibit a general
903   quality decline. This is primarily because, for some samples, the cumulative attention weights may
904   fall below 0.5, causing the algorithm to lose the alignment with minimizing input perturbation in the
905   worst-case scenario, which in turn negatively impacts performance. However, as shown in Figure
906   7, despite the performance drop in the ablation version, it still outperforms the original SnapKV.
907   This is because, even with α set to 0, the selection algorithm in most samples of datasets can still

1052   I   T HE RELATIONSHIP BETWEEN THE ATTENTION OUTPUT PERTURBATION
1053       AND THE GENERATION QUALITY
1054
1055   In LLM computations, the attention output serves as an input to the FeedForward Neural Network
1056   (FFN) module, producing outputs that are subsequently passed to the Language Model (LM) head to
1057   generate the token vocabulary distribution. Our algorithm specifically aims to reduce perturbations
1058   in attention outputs caused by cache eviction methods. This reduction lowers the perturbations in
1059   the inputs to downstream network components (FFN and LM head), thereby mitigating perturbations
1060   in the final token vocabulary distribution. Consequently, our method reduces the impact of cache
1061   eviction on output token generation, which is critical to maintaining high-quality generation results.
1062   This conclusion is also supported both theoretically and empirically. Theoretically, the relationship
1063   between reduced input perturbations and diminished output variations is well-established, as seen
1064   in Lipschitz continuity Xu & Zhang (2024); Khromov & Singh (2024), which posits that functions
1065   with bounded Lipschitz constants exhibit smaller output differences for smaller input differences.
1066   This principle is consistent with our approach to minimizing attention output perturbations, thereby
1067   ensuring subsequent generated tokens. Similarly, FFN pruning techniques in LLMs Dong et al.
1068
       (2024); Sun et al. (2024b) also demonstrate the practical success of minimizing perturbations to
       downstream layers’ outputs, further validating our strategy.
