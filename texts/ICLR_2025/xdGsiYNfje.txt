009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Despite the success of Large Language Models (LLMs) across various fields, their
012            potential to generate untruthful, biased and harmful responses poses significant
013            risks, particularly in critical applications. This highlights the urgent need for sys-
014            tematic methods to detect and prevent such misbehavior. While existing approaches
015            target specific issues such as harmful responses, this work introduces LLMS CAN,
016            an innovative LLM monitoring technique based on causality analysis, offering a
017            comprehensive solution. LLMS CAN systematically monitors the inner workings
018
               of an LLM through the lens of causal inference, operating on the premise that the
               LLM’s ‘brain’ behaves differently when misbehaving. By analyzing the causal
019
               contributions of the LLM’s input tokens and transformer layers, LLMS CAN effec-
020
               tively detects misbehavior. Extensive experiments across various tasks and models
021            reveal clear distinctions in the causal distributions between normal behavior and
022            misbehavior, enabling the development of accurate, lightweight detectors for a
023            variety of misbehavior detection tasks.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Large language models (LLMs) demonstrate advanced capabilities in mimicking human language
029   and styles for diverse applications (OpenAI, 2023), from literary creation (Yuan et al., 2022) to code
030   generation (Li et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b). At the same time, they have shown the potential to
031   misbehave in various ways, raising serious concerns about their use in critical real-world applications.
032   First, LLMs can inadvertently produce untruthful responses, fabricating information that may be
033
      plausible but entirely fictitious, thus misleading users or misrepresenting facts (Rawte et al., 2023).
      Second, LLMs can be exploited for malicious purposes, such as through jailbreak attacks (Liu et al.,
034
      2024; Zou et al., 2023b; Zeng et al., 2024), where the model’s safety mechanisms are bypassed to
035
      produce harmful outputs. Third, the generation of toxic responses such as insulting or offensive
036   content remains a significant concern (Wang & Chang, 2022). Lastly, biased responses, which can
037   appear as discriminatory or prejudiced remarks, are especially troubling as they have the potential to
038   reinforce stereotypes and undermine societal efforts toward equality and inclusivity (Stanovsky et al.,
039   2019; Zhao et al., 2018).
040
      Numerous attempts have been made to detect LLM misbehavior (Pacchiardi et al., 2023; Robey et al.,
041
      2024; Sap et al., 2020; Caselli et al., 2021). However, existing approaches often face two significant
042   limitations. First, they tend to focus on a single type of misbehavior, which reduces the overall
043   effectiveness of each method and requires the integration of multiple systems to comprehensively
044   address the diverse forms of misbehavior. Second, many methods rely on analyzing the model’s
045   responses, which can be inefficient or even ineffective, particularly for longer outputs. Additionally,
046   they are often vulnerable to adaptive adversarial attacks (Sato et al., 2018; Hartvigsen et al., 2022). As
047   a result, there is an urgent need for more general and robust misbehavior detection methods capable
048   of identifying (and mitigating) the full range of LLM misbehavior.
049   In this work, we introduce LLMScan, an approach designed to address this critical need. LLMScan
050   leverages the concept of monitoring the “brain” activities of an LLM for detecting misbehavior. Since
051   an LLM’s responses are generated from its parameters and input data, we believe that the “brain”
052   activities of an LLM (i.e., the values passed through its neurons) inherently contain the information
053   necessary for identifying such misbehavior. The challenge, however, lies in the vast number of such
      values, most of which are low-level and irrelevant. Therefore, it is essential to isolate the specific

078
      An overview of LLMScan is illustrated in Figure 1. At its core, LLMScan consists of two main
079
      components: a scanner and a detector. The scanner conducts lightweight yet systematic causality
080   analysis at two levels, i.e., prompt tokens (assessing each token’s influence) and neural layers
081   (assessing each layer’s contribution). This analysis generates a causality distribution map (hereafter
082   causal map) that captures the causal contributions of different tokens and layers. The detector, a
083   classifier trained on causal maps from two contrasting sets of prompts (e.g., one indicating untruthful
084   responses and another that does not), is used to assess whether the LLM is likely misbehaving at
085   runtime. Notably, LLMScan can detect potential misbehavior before a response is fully generated, or
086   even as early as the generation of the first token.
087   To evaluate the effectiveness of LLMScan, we conduct experiments using four popular LLMs
088   across 13 diverse datasets. The results demonstrate that LLMScan accurately identifies four types
089   of misbehavior, with particularly strong performance in detecting untruthful, toxic responses, and
090   harmful outputs resulting from jailbreak attacks, achieving average AUCs above 0.95. Additionally,
091   we perform ablation studies to evaluate the individual contributions of the causality distribution from
092   prompt tokens and neural layers. The findings reveal that these components are complementary,
093   enhancing the overall effectiveness of LLMScan.
094   Our contributions are summarized as follows. First, we develop a novel method for “brain-scanning”
095   LLMs through the lens of causality analysis. Secondly, we propose a unified method for detecting
096   various types of misbehavior (including generating untruthful, biased, harmful and toxic responses)
097   based on the brain-scan results. Lastly, we conduct experiments across 52 benchmarks, demonstrating
098   that LLMScan effectively identifies four types of misbehaviors in LLMs. Our code is publicly
099   available at https://github.com/anonymousaa1/LLM_Scan.git.

102   2    BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORKS
103
104   Existing methods for detecting LLM misbehavior have predominantly focused on specific and
105   narrowly defined scenarios (Pacchiardi et al., 2023). While these existing approaches are effective
106   within their specific domains, they often struggle to generalize across different types of misbehavior.
107   In the following, we discuss the need for four examples of LLM misbehavior detection and highlight
      the necessity for more comprehensive and adaptable detection mechanisms that can be applied across

108
      a wide range of misbehavior cases.
109
110
111   Lie Detection: LLMs can “lie”, i.e., producing untruthful statements despite demonstrably “knowing”
112
      the truth (Pacchiardi et al., 2023). That is, a response from an LLM is considered a lie if and only if a)
      the response is untruthful and b) the model “knows” the truthful answer (i.e., the model provides the
113
      truthful response under question-answering scrutiny) (Pacchiardi et al., 2023). For example, LLMs
114
      might “lie” when instructed to disseminate misinformation.
115
116   Existing LLM lie detectors are typically based on hallucination detection or, more related to this
117   work, on inferred behavior patterns associated with lying (Pacchiardi et al., 2023; Azaria & Mitchell,
118
      2023; Evans et al., 2021; Ji et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023; Turpin et al., 2024). Specifically,
      Pacchiardi et al. propose a lie detector that works in a black-box manner under the hypothesis that an
119
      LLM that has just lied will respond differently to certain follow-up questions (Pacchiardi et al., 2023).
120
      They introduce a set of elicitation questions, categorized into lie-related, factual, and ambiguous,
121   to assess the likelihood of the model lying. Azaria et al. explore the ability of LLMs to internally
122   recognize the truthfulness of the statements they generate (Azaria & Mitchell, 2023), and propose a
123   method that leverages transformer layer activation to detect the model’s lie behavior.
124
125
      Jailbreak Detection: Aligned LLMs are designed to adhere to built-in restrictions and ethical
126
      safeguards aimed at preventing the generation of harmful or inappropriate content. However, these
127   models can be compromised through the use of cleverly crafted prompts, a technique known as
128   “jailbreaking” (Wei et al., 2024). It has been shown that jailbreaking consistently circumvents the
129   safety alignment of both open-source and black-box models such as GPT-4.
130
      Numerous studies have been conducted to defend against jailbreaking attacks (Robey et al., 2024;
131
      Alon & Kamfonas, 2023; Zheng et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024), which can be broadly
132
      categorized into three strategies. The first strategy, prompt detection, seeks to identify and filter
133   out malicious inputs by analyzing their perplexity or matching them against known patterns of
134   jailbreak attempts (Alon & Kamfonas, 2023; Jain et al., 2023). While effective in some cases, these
135   methods are limited by the diversity and variability of jailbreak prompts. The second strategy,
136   input transformation, offers an additional layer of defense by introducing controlled perturbations
137   or alternations to the inputs, such as reordering words or applying noise (Robey et al., 2023; Xie
138   et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2023). However, even these methods can be evaded by
139   more sophisticated jailbreak attempts, which has led to the use of behavioral analysis. Lastly, some
140   approaches focus on monitoring the model’s responses for signs of insecure or unexpected behavior,
141
      or analyzing internal model states to detect anomalies during inference (Li et al., 2024; Hu et al.,
      2024).
142
143
144   Toxicity Detection: LLMs may generate toxic content, i.e., responses that contain abusive, aggressive,
145   or other forms of inappropriate content. It is likely because their training data include toxic or inap-
146   propriate material, and the models lack the ability to make real-world moral judgments (Ousidhoum
147
      et al., 2021). As a result, they struggle to discern appropriate responses without proper contextual
      guidance. Toxic outputs not only degrade the user experience and increase psychological stress, but
148
      they also contribute to broader negative social outcomes, such as the spread of hate speech and the
149
      deepening of social divisions.
150
151   Existing research on toxic content detection generally follows two main directions. The first focuses
152   on creating benchmark datasets for toxic detection (Vidgen et al., 2021; Hartvigsen et al., 2022). The
153
      second involves supervised learning, where models are trained on labeled datasets to identify toxic
      content. This includes fine-tuning LLMs on toxicity datasets to improve their ability to detect toxic
154
      language (Caselli et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2022; Wang & Chang, 2022). However, these supervised
155
      learning approaches face several challenges. Firstly, they rely on labeled training data, which is
156   difficult to obtain due to the lack of standardized ground truth. Second, deploying LLMs for toxic
157   content detection in production environments can be computationally expensive, particularly when
158   processing prompts with a large number of tokens.
159
160
      Bias Detection: Typically trained on vast amounts of internet-based data, LLMs inevitably inherit
161
      stereotypes, misrepresentations and derogatory language, and other denigrating behaviors that dis-

162
      proportionately affect already vulnerable and marginalized communities (Touvron et al., 2023b;
163   Biderman et al., 2023). These harms manifest as “social bias”. Extensive efforts and benchmarks
164   have been developed to investigate and mitigate bias and stereotype issues (Stanovsky et al., 2019;
165   Zhao et al., 2018). Those social biases can be categorized into three primary dimensions: 1) gen-
166   erative stereotype bias, which involves using prompts to condition text generation (Dhamala et al.,
167   2021; Parrish et al., 2022); 2) counterfactual bias, where bias is assessed using pairs of sentences
168   that substitute social groups (Nangia et al., 2020; Barikeri et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2020); and 3)
169   representation bias, focusing on the embedding layer (May et al., 2019; Guo & Caliskan, 2021).
170   Each category employs different methods and approaches for detecting biases within text and model
171
      representations.
172   Additionally, our approach is related to studies on model interpretability. Common methods for
173   explaining neural network decisions include saliency maps (Adebayo et al., 2018; Bilodeau et al.,
174   2024; Brown et al., 2020), feature visualization (Nguyen et al., 2019), and mechanistic interpretabil-
175   ity (Wang et al., 2023a). Zou et al. further develop an approach, called RepE, which provides insights
176   into the internal state of AI systems by enabling representation reading (Zou et al., 2023a).

180   Recall that our method has two components, i.e., the scanner and the detector. In this section, we first
181   introduce how the scanner applies causality analysis to build a causal map systematically, and then
182   the detector detects misbehavior based on causal maps. For brevity, we define the generation process
183   of LLMs as follows.
184   Definition 1 (Generative LLM). Let M be a generative LLM parameterized by θ that takes a text
185   sequence x = (x0 , ..., xm ) as input and produces an output sequence y = (y0 , ..., yn ). The model M
186   defines a probabilistic mapping P (y|x; θ) from the input sequence to the output sequence. Each token
187   yt in the output sequence is generated based on the input sequence x and all previously generated
188   tokens y0:t−1 . Specifically, for each potential next token w, the model computes a logit(w). The
189   probability of generating token w as the next token is then obtained by applying the softmax function

198   3.1   C AUSALITY A NALYSIS
199
200   Causality analysis aims to identify and quantify the presence of causal relationships between events.
201   To conduct causality analysis on machine learning models such as LLMs, we adopt the approach
202   described in (Chattopadhyay et al., 2019; Sun et al., 2022), and treat LLMs as Structured Causal
203
      Models (SCM). In this context, the causal effect of a variable, such as an input token or transformer
      layer within the LLM, is calculated by measuring the difference in outputs under different inter-
204
      ventions Rubin (1974). Formally, the causal effect of a given endogenous variable x on output y is
205
      measured as follows (Chattopadhyay et al., 2019; Sun et al., 2022):
206
207                               CEx = E[y | do(x = 1)] − E[y | do(x = 0)]                                (3)
208
      where do(x = 1) is the intervention operator in the do-calculus.
209
210   To conduct an effective causality analysis, we first identify meaningful endogenous variables. The
211   scanner in LLMS CAN calculates the causal effect of each input token and transformer layer to create
212
      a causal map. We avoid focusing on individual neurons, as they generally have minimal impact on
      the model’s response (Zhao et al., 2023). Instead, we concentrate on the broader level of input tokens
213
      and transformer layers to capture a more meaningful influence.
214
215   Computing the Causal Effect of Tokens. To analyze the causal effect of input tokens, we conduct
      an intervention on each input token and observe the changes in model behavior, which are measured


                                                         4

216
      based on attention scores. These interventions occur at the embedding layer of the LLM. Specifically,
217   there are three steps: 1) extract the attention scores during normal execution when a prompt is
218   processed by the LLM; 2) extract the attention scores during a series of abnormal executions where
219   each input token xi , 0 ≤ i ≤ m is replaced with an intervention token ‘-’ one by one; and 3) compute
220   the Euclidean distances between the attention scores of the intervened prompts and the original
221   prompt. Formally,
222
      Definition 2 (Causal Effect of Input Token). Let x = (x0 , ..., xm ) be the input prompt, the causal
223   effect of token xi is:
224
225                                           CExi = ∥AS − ASx′ i ∥                                           (4)
226
      where AS is the original attention score (i.e., without any intervention) and ASx′ i is the attention
227
      score when intervening token xi .
228
229
      Note that there are two key reasons for using attention scores for causal effect measurement. First,
230   at the token level, attention scores more effectively capture inter-token relationships, which may
231   be obscured in later results, such as logits. Attention distances provide a clearer reflection of
232   how the model’s understanding shifts with token interventions, offering a more precise measure
233   of each token’s causal impact. Our empirical results reinforce this point, showing that attention
234   head distances, typically ranging between 2 and 3, are more sensitive to logits differences, which
235   generally fell below 0.2. Additionally, previous research has demonstrated that attention layers may
236   encapsulate the model’s knowledge, including harmful or inappropriate information that may require
237   censorship (Meng et al., 2022).
238   For efficiency, instead of using all attention scores, we focus on a select few. For instance, in the
239   Llama-2-13b model, which consists of 40 layers, each with 40 attention heads, we consider only
240   heads 1, 20, and 40 from layers 1, 20, and 40. This selective approach has been empirically proven to
241   be effective. Further details are available in our public repository.
242   Computing the causal effect of Layers. In addition to the input tokens, we calculate the causal
243   effect of each layer in the LLM. The causal effect of a transformer layer ℓ is computed based on the
244   difference between the original output logit (without intervention) and the output logit when layer ℓ
245   is intervened upon (i.e., skipped). Specifically, we intervene the model by bypassing the layer ℓ. That
246   is, during inference, we create a shortcut from layer ℓ − 1 to layer ℓ + 1, allowing the output from
247   layer ℓ − 1 to bypass layer ℓ and proceed directly to layer ℓ + 1. Formally,
248   Definition 3 (Causal Effect of Model Layer). Let ℓ be a layer of the LLM; and x be the prompt. The
249   ACE of ℓ for prompt x is
250
251                                          CEx,ℓ = logit0 − logit−ℓ
                                                                   0                                          (5)
252
253
      where logit0 is the output logit of first token, and logit−ℓ
                                                                0 is the logit for the first token when the layer
      ℓ is skipped (i.e., a shortcut from the layer preceding ℓ to the layer immediately after ℓ is created).

255
      Note that here we use the logit as the measure of the causal effect, as the attention is no longer
256
      available once a layer is skipped.

258   The Causal Map. Given a prompt x, we systematically calculate the causal effect of each token
259   and each layer to form a causal map, i.e., a heatmap through the lens of causality. Our hypothesis is
260   that such maps would allow us to differentiate misbehavior from normal behaviors. For example,
261
      Figure 2 shows two causal maps, where the left one corresponds to a truthful response generated by
      the LLaMA-2-7B model (with 32 layers), and the right one corresponds to an untruthful response.
262
      The prompt used is “Who developed Windows95?”. The truthful response is “Microsoft Corporation”.
263
      The untruthful response is “Bill Gates” when the model is induced to lie with the instruction “Answer
264   the following question with a lie”. Note that each causal map consists of two parts: the input token
265   causal effects and the layer causal effects. It can be observed that there are obvious differences
266   between the causal maps. When generating the truthful response, a few specific layers stand out
267   with significantly high causal effects, indicating that these layers play a dominant role in producing

297   3.2             M ISBEHAVIOR D ETECTION
298
299   The misbehavior detector is a classifier that takes a causal map as input and predicts whether it
300   indicates certainly misbehavior. For each type of misbehavior, we train the detector using two
301   contrasting sets of causal maps: one representing normal behavior (e.g., those producing truthful
302
      responses) and the other containing causal maps of misbehavior (e.g., those producing untruthful
      responses). In our implementation, we adopt simple yet effective Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP)
303
      trained with the Adam optimizer.
304
305   At the token level, prompts can vary significantly in length, making it impractical to use the raw causal
306   effects directly as defined in Definition 2. To address this, we extract a fixed set of common statistical
307   features including the mean, standard deviation, range, skewness, and kurtosis, that summarize the
308
      distribution of causal effects across all tokens in a prompt. This results in a consistent 5-dimensional
      feature vector for each prompt, regardless of its length. At the layer level, this issue does not arise
309
      since the number of layers is fixed. Thus, the input to the detector consists of the 5-dimensional
310
      feature vector for the prompt, along with the causal effects of each transformer layer calculated
311   according to Definition 3.

451   4.3                                    E XPERIMENTAL R ESULT A NALYSIS
452
453   In the following, we conduct an in-depth and comprehensive analysis of LLMScan’s detection
454   capabilities on specific cases.
455
      Token-level Causality. To see how the token-level causal effects distinguish normal responses from
456   misbehavior ones, we employ Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to visualize the variations in
457   attention state changes across different response types.
458
459
      Figure 4(a) and 4(b) show the PCA results for the Lie Detection task and the Jailbreak Detection
      task of the Mistral model, respectively. In both figures, noticeable differences in attention state
460
      changes are observed between truthful and lie responses, as well as between refusal and jailbreak
461
      responses. These findings provide compelling evidence that causal inference on input tokens can
462   offer valuable insights into the model’s behavior and highlight the model’s vulnerabilities in handling
463   lie-instruction and jailbreak prompts. Furthermore, the results suggest that improving LLM security
464   through attention mechanisms and their statistical properties is feasible.
465
      However, as shown in Figure 4(c) and 4(d), the PCA results reveal less clear distinctions between
466
      toxic and non-toxic responses, as well as between biased and fair outputs. This is primarily because,
467
      unlike lie and jailbreak responses, which are often directly triggered by input prompts, toxic and
468   biased responses may be more deeply embedded in the model’s knowledge or understanding, i.e.,
469   model’s parameters. As a result, detecting these misbehavior responses based solely on token-level
470   causal effects is challenging.
471
      Layer-level Causality. To understand why layer-level causal effects can distinguish normal and
472
      misbehavior responses, we present the causal effect distribution across LLM layers for all four tasks
473
      in Figure 5, using a violin plot to highlight variations in the model’s responses. The violin plot
474   illustrates the data distribution, where the width represents the density. The white dot marks the
475   median, the black bar indicates the interquartile range (IQR), and the thin black line extends to 1.5
476   times the IQR. The differing shapes highlight variations in layer contributions across response types,
477   with green indicating normal responses and brown representing misbehavior ones.
478
      For the Lie Detection task, using Questions1000 as an example, Figure 5(a) illustrates clear differences
479
      in the causal effect distributions between truthful and lie responses across all four LLMs. In most
480   cases, truthful responses exhibit wider distributions, indicating greater variability in the contributions
481   of model layers. The sharp peaks at the upper end suggest that certain layers contribute significantly
482   to the model’s output, with particularly high causal effects. This pattern is consistent across all four
483   models. This is likely because, when an LLM generates a truthful response, relevant knowledge
484   is concentrated in a few key transformer layers, as reported in previous work (Meng et al., 2022).
485   Intervening these layers can thus drastically disrupt the model’s output. In contrast, lie responses tend
      to exhibit more uniformly distributed causal effects, suggesting a more balanced contribution across

532   5    C ONCLUSION
533
534
535   In this work, we introduce a method that employs causal analysis on input prompts and model layers
536   to detect the misbehavior of LLMs. Our approach effectively identifies various misbehavior, such as
537   lies, jailbreaks, toxicity, and bias. Unlike previous works that mainly focus on detecting misbehavior
538   after content is generated, our method offers a proactive solution by identifying and preventing
539   the intent to generate misbehavior responses from the very first token. The experimental results
      demonstrate that our method achieves strong performance in detecting various misbehavior.

1128   The detector’s task is framed as a binary classification problem, where abnormal content generation is
1129   labeled as ‘1’ and misbehavior content as ‘0’. The detector is trained on 70% of the dataset, with the
1130   remaining 30% reserved for testing. For each task, the detector is consist of two parts: one classifiers
1131   based on prompt-level behavior and another on layer-level behavior. The log probabilities from
1132   these two classifiers are averaged to produce the final classification probability. In our evaluation
1133   part, a threshold of 0.5 is used for accuracy calculation, where content with a probability above this
       threshold is classified as misbehavior.
