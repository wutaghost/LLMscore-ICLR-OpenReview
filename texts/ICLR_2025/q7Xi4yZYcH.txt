011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The rapid evolution of the Ethereum network necessitates sophisticated techniques
014            to ensure its robustness against potential threats and to maintain transparency.
015
               While Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have pioneered anomaly detection in such
               platforms, capturing the intricacies of both spatial and temporal transactional pat-
016
               terns has remained a challenge. This study presents a fusion of Graph Con-
017
               volutional Networks (GCNs) with Temporal Random Walks (TRW) enhanced
018            by probabilistic sampling to bridge this gap. Our approach, unlike traditional
019            GCNs, leverages the strengths of TRW to discern complex temporal sequences in
020            Ethereum transactions, thereby providing a more nuanced transaction anomaly de-
021            tection mechanism. Extensive evaluations demonstrate that our TRW-GCN frame-
022            work substantially advances the performance metrics over conventional GCNs in
023            detecting irregularities such as suspiciously timed transactions, patterns indicative
024            of token pump and dump schemes, or anomalous behavior in smart contract exe-
025            cutions over time. As baseline algorithms for comparison, common unsupervised
026
               methods such as Isolation Forest, One-Class SVM, and DBSCAN (as classifier for
               TRW-GCN embedding) are employed; finally our novel TRW-GCN plus scoring
027
               method is compared with the state-of-the-art temporal graph attention algorithm.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) have emerged as a transformative tool in the domain of
033   graph-structured data representation. Their ability to encapsulate both local and global graph struc-
034   tures has paved the way for their application in diverse fields. However, as the scale and intricacy of
035   graph data have surged, the efficient training of GCNs has become a paramount concern. Traditional
036   training paradigms, although effective, are often encumbered by high computational and storage de-
037   mands, especially when dealing with expansive graphs. The realm of GCN training has witnessed
038   a burgeoning interest in sampling methods, particularly those rooted in probabilistic frameworks
039
      within graphs. Layer-wise sampling methods have been at the forefront of prior advancements.
      Chen et al. (2018) in their work on FastGCN championed the cause of probabilistic sampling on in-
040
      dependent nodes. Their approach was further nuanced by Huang et al. (2018) which introduced the
041
      concept of layer-dependent sampling, thereby adding another dimension to the sampling process.
042
043   While traditional GCNs have shown remarkable potential in handling static graph structures, their
044   application to dynamic graphs introduces new challenges and opportunities. In order to extend
045
      GCNs to dynamic graphs, it is crucial to understand how learning on dynamic graphs works, which
      is a relatively recent area of research. There have been studies which investigate discrete-time graphs
046
      represented as a sequence of graph snapshots (Yu et al., 2019; Sankar et al., 2020; Pareja et al., 2019;
047
      Yu et al., 2018), also several continuous-time approaches have been presented (Xu et al., 2020;
048   Trivedi et al., 2019; Kumar et al., 2019; Ma et al., 2018; Nguyen et al., 2018; Bastas et al., 2019;
049   Rossi et al., 2020), where continous dynamic graphs means that edges can appear at any time (Rossi
050   et al., 2020). Liu et al. (2023) mentioned that most temporal graph learning methods model current
051   interactions by combining historical information over time, however, such methods merely consider
052   the first-order temporal information. To solve this issue, they proposed extracting both temporal
053   and structural information to learn more informative node representations. In our ablation study, we
      focus mainly on TGAT by Xu et al. (2020) which has proved superior in performance.

054
      Also, the topic of anomaly detection in Blockchain has received considerable attention. For example,
055   in Ethereum, the unexpected appearance of particular subgraphs has implied newly emerging mal-
056   ware (Xu and Livshits 2019). Anomaly detection in blockchain transaction networks is an emerging
057   area of research in the cryptocurrency community (Lee et al. 2022). Wu et al. (2020) investigated
058   phishing detection in blockchain network using unsupervised learning algorithms. Ofori-Boateng
059   et al. (2021) have also discussed topological anomaly detection in multilayer blockchain networks.
060   Given that the Ethereum network witnesses dynamically evolving transaction patterns, it becomes
061   imperative to account for the temporal sequences and correlations of transactions. While some litera-
062   ture has touched upon temporal networks, there is a conspicuous absence of comprehensive research
063
      that deeply integrates TRW with GCNs, and probabilistic sampling, especially within the blockchain
      environment. Furthermore, the specific challenge of anomaly and transaction burst detection in the
064
      Ethereum network, which has massive implications for network security and fraud detection, has
065
      not been extensively explored using these combined methodologies. As Ethereum continues to
066   grow and evolve, addressing such gap with an appropriate methodology becomes increasingly cru-
067   cial to ensure the security, scalability, and robustness of the network. This study addresses the
068   pressing challenge of detecting time-sensitive anomalies within Ethereum blockchain transactions.
069   We propose a novel approach, designed to provide both the spatial and temporal dynamics inherent
070   in Ethereum transaction data. Our research offers several contributions:
071
      Enhanced Anomaly Detection with TRW : Our model leverages TRW in tandem with GCN to im-
072   prove anomaly detection effectiveness. By integrating temporal patterns, our approach can identify
073   irregularities such as suspiciously timed transactions, patterns indicative of token ’pump and dump’
074   schemes, or anomalous behavior in smart contract executions over time.
075
      Efficiency in Sampling Representative Nodes: Given the substantial size and continuous growth
076
      of the Ethereum blockchain, efficient sampling methods are essential. Our GCN, trained with TRW
077
      nodes, provides a solution that balances accuracy with computational efficiency.
078
079   Detecting Patterns Leading to Sophisticated Attacks: Decentralized networks are vulnerable to
080   sophisticated attacks, particularly those that exploit timing vulnerabilities such as front-running at-
081
      tacks. Our proposed GCN with TRW integration aims to detect complex patterns such as MEV
      bots, rapid buying or selling of assets, or others which are activities that can exhibit time-sensitive
082
      anomalies; manual inspection is then necessary for further investigation of the attack.

085   2   M ODEL D ESIGN
086
087   GCNs are a pivotal neural network architecture crafted specifically for graph-structured data.
088   Through the use of graph convolutional layers, we seamlessly aggregate information from neigh-
089   boring nodes and edges to refine node embeddings. In enhancing this mechanism, we incorporate
090
      probabilistic sampling, which proves particularly adept in analyzing the vast Ethereum network.
      The incorporation of Temporal Random Walks (TRW) adds a rich layer to this framework. TRW
091
      captures the temporal sequences in Ethereum transactions and not only focuses on nodes’ spatial
092
      prominence but also considers the transactional chronology. Here, ’time’ is conceptualized based
093   on the sequence and timestamps of Ethereum transactions, leading to a dynamically evolving, time-
094   sensitive representation of the network.
095
096
      Here, graph is represented as G = (V, E), where V is the set of nodes (vertices) and E is the set
      of edges connecting the nodes. Each node vi in the graph is associated with a feature vector Fi ,
097
      and F ∈ R|V |×4 represents a feature matrix of size 4. Aggregation is a process to combine the
098
      feature vectors of neighboring nodes using an adjacency matrix A to capture graph connectivity. To
099
      enable information propagation across multiple layers, the graph convolution operation is performed
100   iteratively through multiple graph convolutional layers (GCLs). The output of one layer serves as
101   the input to the next layer, allowing the propagation of information through the network. The node
102   representations are updated layer by layer, allowing information from neighbors and their neighbors
103   to be incorporated into the node features. The parameters Wl are learned during the training process
104   to optimize the model’s performance on a specific graph-based task. GCNs often consist of multiple
105   layers, where each layer iteratively updates the node representations:

142   2.2   E FFECT ON A NOMALY D ETECTION
143
144   The embeddings from a GCN (post TRW influence) should be more sensitive to recent behaviors
145
      and patterns. When these embeddings are passed to a classifier, clustering and scoring algorithms
      like DBSCAN, OCSVM, ISOLATION FOREST, and LOF, anomalies that are based on recent or
146
      time-sensitive behaviors are more likely to stand out. In our work, the term ”anomaly” refers to
147
      patterns that are statistically uncommon or divergent from the norm based on the features learned
148   by our model. These uncommon patterns, while not definitively erroneous, are of interest because
149   they deviate from typical behavior. In the context of Ethereum transactions, such deviations could
150   potentially indicate suspicious activities, novel transaction patterns, or transaction bursts.
151
      While we here provide insight and a mathematical proof, the true value of TRW in improving GCN
152
      for anomaly detection is empirical. We would need to compare the performance of GCN with and
153
      without TRW on a temporal dataset to see tangible benefits (see section 3.5 and appendix C). Here
154   is how temporal weights are applied:
155
            1. Node Features are weighted by time: When updating the node features through the matrix
156            multiplication, nodes that are temporally closer influence each other more, allowing recent
157            patterns to be highlighted.
158
            2. Temporal Relationships are captured: The modified node features inherently capture tem-
159
               poral relationships because they aggregate features from temporally relevant neighbors.
160
161         3. Higher Sensitivity to recent anomalies: With temporal weighting, anomalies that have oc-
               curred recently will be more pronounced in the node feature space.

203
      sampling.
204   The TRW framework introduces a model where the transition probabilities between nodes in a graph
205   are temporally adjusted. The probability of transitioning from node i at time t to node j at time t+1,
206   denoted as Pij(t,t+1) , is influenced by temporal proximity. This stands in contrast to traditional
207   random walks, where transition probabilities are solely based on the static adjacency matrix of the
208   graph. We define TTRW (t) as the transition matrix for TRW at time t, with each entry Tij (t) repre-
209   senting the probability of transitioning from node i to node j at time t. Conversely, TRW represents the
210   transition matrix for a traditional random walk, with constant transition probabilities over time. We
211   measure temporal consistency by examining the variation in transition probabilities over time, with
212
      TRW expected to exhibit lower variation due to its emphasis on temporal proximity. We continue

470
      walks from nodes and stepping through neighbors up to a specified length, which can be quantified
      as O(W×LE×DlogD), where W represents the number of walks, LE the length of each walk, and
471
      D the average degree of nodes. The GCN complexity involves aggregating features from neigh-
472
      boring nodes and applying transformations through learnable parameters across L layers. The ag-
473   gregation complexity is proportional to the number of edges E, and the transformation involves
474   matrix multiplications depending on the number of features F and hidden units H, resulting in
475   O(L×(E+N×F×H)). When combining these two aspects, the overall complexity of TRW-GCN is
476   expressed as O(W×LE×DlogD+L×(E+N×F×H)). The dominant complexity terms typically relate to
477   E and N, particularly in dense graphs or when handling high-dimensional feature transformations.
478   See further the proof in appendix D. As seen in the TRW-GCN complexity graph, Figure 4, GCN
