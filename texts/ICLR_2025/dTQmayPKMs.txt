009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), it is crucial to learn
012            suitable reward models from human feedback to align large language models
013            (LLMs) with human intentions. However, human feedback can often be noisy, in-
014            consistent, or biased, especially when evaluating complex responses. Such feed-
015            back can lead to misaligned reward signals, potentially causing unintended side
016
               effects during the RLHF process. To address these challenges, we explore the use
               of influence functions to measure the impact of human feedback on the perfor-
017
               mance of reward models. We propose a compute-efficient approximation method
018
               that enables the application of influence functions to LLM-based reward models
019            and large-scale preference datasets. In our experiments, we demonstrate two key
020            applications of influence functions: (1) detecting common forms of labeler bias in
021            human feedback datasets and (2) guiding labelers to refine their strategies to align
022            more closely with expert feedback. By quantifying the impact of human feedback
023            on reward models, we believe that influence functions can enhance feedback inter-
024            pretability and contribute to scalable oversight in RLHF, helping labelers provide
025            more accurate and consistent feedback.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      As large language models (LLMs) demonstrate remarkable capabilities across various domains, en-
030   suring their behaviors align with human intentions becomes increasingly important. To this end,
031   reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) has emerged as a powerful solution for fine-
032   tuning LLMs (Ziegler et al., 2019; Stiennon et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022). In RLHF, human
033   feedback is collected to train reward models that capture important human values, such as help-
034   fulness and harmlessness (Bai et al., 2022a; Ji et al., 2024). LLMs are then fine-tuned to produce
035   outputs that closely align with these reward models.
036   However, human feedback can often be noisy, inconsistent, or biased, especially when evaluating
037   complex responses (Casper et al., 2023). This variability can lead to misaligned reward signals,
038   potentially causing unintended side effects during the RLHF process. For example, feedback that
039   favors supportive and enthusiastic responses might inadvertently lead the reward model to prioritize
040   overly agreeable responses, which could result in sycophantic behavior (Sharma et al., 2023; Perez
041   et al., 2022). This issue highlights the need for robust methods that precisely evaluate the impact
042   of feedback on reward models, enabling humans to detect biased feedback and refine their feedback
043   strategies more effectively.
044   In this work, we assess the impact of human feedback on reward models by utilizing influence func-
045   tions (Hampel, 1974; Koh & Liang, 2017). However, a significant challenge arises when applying
046   influence functions to reward models, especially large-parameter models like LLMs and those in-
047   volving extensive preference datasets, due to the high computational costs involved. To address this,
048   we introduce a compute-efficient method that utilizes vector compression techniques (Li & Li, 2023)
049   alongside the influence estimation method (Kwon et al., 2024), achieving a 2.5-fold speed acceler-
050
      ation compared to previous methods in computing influence functions. This approach significantly
      reduces the computational costs required to compute influence functions, facilitating more practical
051
      applications in large-scale settings.

      labeler bias in training datasets, and (2) improving suboptimal labeling strategies. In our first exper-

088   2    R ELATED WORK
089
090   Influence functions Influence functions measure the impact of individual training data points on
091   the resulting model and have been applied to various tasks, such as identifying influential data,
092   detecting label errors, and interpreting model behavior (Koh & Liang, 2017; Guo et al., 2021; Kwon
093   et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024). Given their broad applicability to diverse tasks, we extend the use
094   of influence functions to reward modeling, to measure the impact feedback has on reward models.
095   A key challenge in this approach is the high computational cost of estimating influence. Building
096   on recent advancements in efficient influence computation methods, which enables the estimation
097
      of influence functions even for LLMs (Kwon et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024; Grosse et al., 2023), we
      apply influence functions to LLM-based reward models.
098
099
      Scalable oversight As AI models become more powerful, reliably providing feedback on their
100
      behavior becomes increasingly challenging (Burns et al., 2024). For instance, humans struggle to
101
      accurately evaluate LLM-generated summaries of long passages as they cannot review entire source
102   texts (Saunders et al., 2022). This challenge highlights the need for scalable oversight (Amodei et al.,
103   2016; Bowman et al., 2022), where non-expert humans are required to provide feedback on complex
104   outputs produced by advanced AI systems. A common approach to scalable oversight involves using
105   capable AI models during the feedback process, either to assist humans (Saunders et al., 2022) or
106   to replace them (Bai et al., 2022b; Cui et al., 2023). However, AI-assisted feedback processes can
107   still fail, and it remains uncertain whether they will guarantee alignment (Hofstätter, 2023; Casper
      et al., 2023) for increasingly complex tasks. An alternative approach to scalable oversight is the


                                                                   2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      “sandwich paradigm” (Cotra, 2021; Bowman et al., 2022), which places the capabilities of an LLM
109   between a domain expert and the model overseer. This paradigm assumes that, for certain tasks,
110   domain experts will remain capable of providing accurate feedback, highlighting the importance
111   of making their expertise readily accessible to the model overseer. In this context, our approach
112   of using influence functions offers a promising direction, as it enables the analysis of non-expert
113   feedback based on expert feedback.

149   3.2   R EINFORCEMENT LEARNING FROM HUMAN FEEDBACK
150
151   RLHF is an effective technique for aligning LLMs with human preferences by incorporating human
152   evaluations into the learning process. It has become increasingly standard due to its powerful capa-
153   bility to generate human-like, helpful, and safe model outcomes (Bai et al., 2022a; Ouyang et al.,
154   2022; Dai et al., 2024). Preference data in RLHF are often represented as a tuple of a prompt x,
155   a pair of LLM responses (y (0) , y (1) ), and a binary label z ∈ {0, 1} assigned by a human labeler
156   to indicate the preferred response. For clarity, we introduce the notation d := (x, y (0) , y (1) , z)
157   to represent feedback data points. Such preference data are learned by minimizing the following
158
      cross-entropy loss based on the Bradley-Terry model (Bradley & Terry, 1952):

226   from 160MB (42M dimensions1 ) to 256KB (65K dimensions), enabling the storage of entire gra-
227   dients for large preference datasets. Influence estimation is significantly accelerated by utilizing this
228   technique, as compression requires only one pass of backpropagation, and influence computation
229   is completed within seconds using compressed gradients (see supporting results in Figure 5). We
230   refer readers to Appendix A for details on the OPORP compression method and for a performance
231
      comparison with DataInf (Kwon et al., 2024).

      5.1      B IAS DETECTION USING INFLUENCE FUNCTIONS
242   In this experiment, we assess the effectiveness of the influence function in detecting biases within
243   preference data. Specifically, we focus on two prevalent types of labeler bias: length (Saito et al.,
244   2023) and sycophancy (Sharma et al., 2023). Length bias refers to the tendency of labelers to
245   prefer longer responses under the belief that they are more informative or helpful, simply due to
246   their verbosity, regardless of the actual content quality. Sycophancy bias is the tendency to favor
247   responses that agree with the user or contain flattery, even when these responses are not accurate or
248   helpful.
249
250   5.1.1      E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
251
      Datasets We construct our training and validation sets using the helpful split of Anthropic’s
252   Helpfulness-Harmlessness (Anthropic-HH) dataset (Bai et al., 2022a), which was annotated by hu-
253   mans who evaluated responses based on helpfulness, providing binary preference labels for conver-
254   sations between a human and an assistant. To test the ability of influence functions to detect biased
255   feedback, we synthetically generate biased samples in the training set by flipping preference labels.
256   Specifically, we flip the labels in a subset of the training set to favor responses that are either lengthy,
257   measured by token count, or sycophantic, assessed using scores evaluated by LLMs.2 This manipu-
258   lation affects 6.56% of the labels for the length bias experiments and 4.17% for the sycophancy bias
259   experiments. Each training set comprises 15,000 samples.
260   As noted in Remark 4.1, constructing a specific validation set is crucial for effectively utilizing
261   influence functions. Therefore, we carefully design validation sets that contain unbiased samples for
262   detecting biased feedback. Specifically, for the length bias experiments, we create a validation set
263   with 2,629 samples, where the chosen responses are concise (i.e., both helpful and of short length),
264   denoted as the Concise set. For the sycophancy bias experiments, we construct a validation set with
265   171 samples, consisting of chosen responses that are helpful and objective, without sycophantic
266
      behavior, denoted as the Less Sycophantic set. Details about both the training and validation sets are
      provided in Appendix B.

      Bias detection methods To detect biased samples using influence functions, we employ a
      threshold-based detector that classifies a training sample as biased if its influence score exceeds
296
      a specified threshold. We also consider baselines that utilize other metrics for scoring, such as
297
      Mahalanobis distance (Lee et al., 2018) and k-nearest neighbors (Sun et al., 2022), which mea-
298   sure the distance between a training sample and validation samples. Additionally, we use metrics
299   like self-confidence and entropy to assess the prediction uncertainty of the reward model (Kuan &
300   Mueller, 2022). Additionally, we evaluate LLM-based detectors, including GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024)
301   and Gemini-1.5-Pro (Reid et al., 2024), using few-shot prompting. Specifically, we present a pair of
302   responses to the LLMs and ask them to determine which response is more helpful. Further details
303   about these baselines are available in Appendix C.
304   Evaluation metrics For evaluation, we compute the true positive rate (TPR) and false positive
305   rate (FPR) using the threshold-based detector’s classification at different thresholds. We then plot
306   the receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve and calculate the area under the curve (AUC)
307   based on the corresponding TPR and FPR values at each threshold. Additionally, we compute the
308   area under the precision-recall curve (AP), as well as the true negative rate at a fixed TPR of 0.80
309   (TNR80). We report these metrics, along with the precision-recall curve in Appendix E.

      5.1.2                  R ESULTS AND ANALYSIS
312   Main results The ROC curves in Figure 2 demonstrate that our method, utilizing influence func-
313   tions, significantly outperforms all baselines in detecting length and sycophancy biases. It achieves
314   AUC values of 0.8 for length bias and 0.711 for sycophancy bias, compared to 0.6 for other
315   threshold-based detectors. Our method also achieves a higher TPR than LLM-based detectors at
316   equivalent FPR. Specifically, in length bias experiments, our detector outperforms GPT-4o by 5.3%
317   and Gemini-1.5-Pro by 25.6%. For sycophancy bias, it exceeds GPT-4o by 14.8% and Gemini-
318   1.5-Pro by 11.9%. On average, our method identifies 14.4% more biased samples at a fixed FPR
319   compared to LLMs, underscoring the effectiveness of influence functions.
320   Furthermore, we note that length bias is easier to detect than sycophancy bias across all meth-
321   ods. Detecting sycophancy bias poses greater challenges as it requires an understanding of context-
322   dependent agreement with user opinions or notions of flattery, which is more complex than length
323   bias. Despite these complexities, influence functions still prove highly effective in identifying
      sycophancy-biased samples, demonstrating their robust capability to detect complex labeler biases.

      Importance of validation set As influence functions estimate the impact of training data on val-
379   idation loss, constructing targeted validation sets is crucial. To verify this, we conduct ablation
380   studies measuring influence functions across various validation sets. For length bias detection, we
381   construct the Verbose validation set, which consists of chosen responses that are more helpful but
382   characterized by longer token lengths. This set serves as a counterpart to our main validation set,
383   Concise, which includes chosen responses that are also helpful but shorter. We then combine these
384   into the full validation set to cover a broader range of response lengths. Similarly, for sycophancy
385   bias detection, we construct the More Sycophantic validation set, focusing on chosen responses that
386   are also helpful but have a higher sycophancy score.
387   As shown in Figure 4, our main validation sets (Concise and Less Sycophantic) lead to better per-
388   formance compared to their counterparts (Verbose and More Sycophantic) or the full validation set.
389   Notably, the Verbose set shows an AUC of 0.202, which is even worse than a random classifier. This
390   suggests that influence functions might focus more on verbosity than on capturing the actual quality
391   impacts, indicating a failure to decouple these factors effectively in the validation set. These results
392   underscore that the quality of the validation set is important in effectively utilizing influence func-
393
      tions. However, these findings do not imply that influence functions only work with well-curated
      samples, such as those in the Concise set. While not optimal, the full validation set, which contains
394
      both Concise and Verbose samples, still proves capable of detecting biased samples, indicating that
395
      influence functions can work reasonably well under less controlled conditions.
396
397   We also investigate the impact of validation set size for influence functions and the number of few-
398   shot examples for LLM baselines in Appendix H. We find that influence functions can accurately
399
      detect labeler bias with validation sets as small as approximately 50 samples. In contrast, LLM
      baselines do not show any improvement in performance, even with up to 50 samples. These results
400
      highlight the efficiency of using influence functions with small-scale expert data, demonstrating their
401
      potential for practical applications.
402
403   Runtime comparison with DataInf To verify the computational efficiency of our method, we
404   compare the runtime of our approach to DataInf (Kwon et al., 2024) across various training dataset
405   sizes while using reward models of the same size and keeping the validation set size fixed at 1,000
406
      samples. Figure 5 shows that our method is approximately 2.5 times faster than DataInf. The pri-
      mary difference in runtime stems from the number of backpropagation passes required for influence
407
      computation. DataInf requires two backpropagation passes, while our method requires only one due
408
      to gradient vector compression.3 While compression in our method takes 11.1 hours for a dataset
409   with 105 data points, the computation of influence functions is completed in just 92.3 seconds after
410   compression. In contrast, DataInf, which does not apply compression, requires two backpropagation
411   passes and cannot store gradient vectors efficiently, resulting in a runtime of 28.8 hours for the same
412   dataset.

466   Adjusting labeling strategies by updating weights To update Bob’s labeling strategy, we first
467   identify samples that most positively and negatively impact his labeling accuracy compared to Al-
468   ice, using influence functions. Given a learned reward model rθ , the influence value Ival (di ) is
469   calculated for each data point di ∈ DB based on Lval (DA ; θ). Samples with an influence score
470
      Ival (di ) exceeding a specified threshold are classified as negatively contributing, while those below
      the threshold are deemed positively contributing. We then update weights by classifying these posi-
471
      tive and negative samples based on their sub-objective scores using support vector machines (Cortes
472
      & Vapnik, 1995). Details on the weight updates are provided in Appendix F. Additionally, we use
473   Mahalanobis distance and k-nearest neighbors as baselines to determine the positive and negative
474   samples, applying the same weight update method (See Appendix C for more details).5

480   (RM Acc.). Finally, we calculate the cosine similarity between wA and wB to assess how closely

       5.2.2               R ESULTS AND ANALYSIS
502
503    Main results As shown in Figure 7, using influence functions to update weights (blue bar) re-
504    sults in significant improvements: label accuracy increases by 15.8%, reward model accuracy by
505    2.2%, and cosine similarity by 0.45, compared to the initial weights (gray bar). In contrast, the
506    Mahalanobis and KNN baselines fail to identify discrepancies between Alice and Bob’s labeling
507    strategies, resulting in worsened performance across all metrics. This demonstrates that influence
508
       functions can effectively guide the non-expert, Bob, toward adopting Alice’s expert labeling strategy,
       even with only a small validation set. Such results underscore the potential of influence functions
509
       in addressing the challenges of scalable oversight. By transferring Alice’s expertise to Bob, we
510
       circumvent the need for large-scale, expert-level data collection, which is often challenging.
511
512    To further examine the impact of using a small validation set, we present performance metrics across
513    different validation set sizes starting from 10 samples. Figure 8 shows that influence functions can
514
       accurately update Bob’s weights even with just 50 samples, almost matching the label accuracy
       achieved with 400 samples. This can be particularly advantageous for complex labeling tasks, where
515
       collecting large amounts of expert-level data is challenging.
516
517
       Limitations We highlight several constraints in our experimental setup that may not extend to real-
518
       world settings. First, we use specific sub-objective scores to define labeler strategies, but assume
519    that these scores are same across both labelers. In real-world scenarios, however, the sub-objective
520    scores between experts and non-experts might differ, as they could assess identical sub-objectives
521    differently. Also, our weight update strategy involves using all training samples and employing
522    a support vector machine to determine new weights. In practical situations, non-expert labelers
523    are unlikely to update their strategies based on all scores estimated by influence functions. More
524    realistically, they might focus on refining their strategies using only a subset of the most and least
525    influential samples. Despite these limitations, we believe that our proof-of-concept experiments
526    provide meaningful insights into using influence functions to help labelers provide accurate feedback
527
       to reward models for complex tasks, contributing to scalable oversight.
528    6              C ONCLUSION
529
530    In this work, we demonstrate the effectiveness of influence functions to measure the impact of human
531    feedback on the performance of reward models. Our experiments verify that influence functions
532    can detect complex labeler biases existing in preference datasets and can guide non-expert labelers
533    toward experts. Given that feedback can be noisy or biased for complex tasks, addressing these
534
       biases is a critical problem. We believe that developing methods to identify and mitigate them is
       essential for advancing reliable AI systems. We hope our work contributes to the broader goal of
535
       scalable oversight (Amodei et al., 2016; Bowman et al., 2022), by improving our understanding of
536
       how feedback samples impact our models during RLHF.

715
      In this section, we describe the vector compression method employed in our work: the one-
716
      permutation, one-random-projection (OPORP) technique (Li & Li, 2023). OPORP allows the com-
717   pression of high-dimensional vectors to a predefined smaller size. By applying this method, we
718   reduce the size of a single gradient vector from 160MB (corresponding to 42 million dimensions)
719   to 256KB (equivalent to 65 thousand dimensions), facilitating the efficient storage of complete gra-
720   dients even for large-scale preference datasets. The original gradient vector in our setup consists of
721   42 million dimensions, as we utilize Low-Rank Adaptation (Hu et al., 2022) to train our reward
722   models.
723   OPORP is a straightforward two-step method consisting of (1) permutation and (2) projection. In
724   the first step, the gradient vector is permuted using a permutation matrix. Specifically, we implement
725   the efficient permutation technique proposed in Lin et al. (2024), where the vector is permuted using
726   multiple sub-permutations. In the second step, the permuted gradient vector undergoes element-wise
727   multiplication with a projection vector, denoted as ρ, where each element ρi is randomly sampled
728   from −1, +1 with equal probability.
729   After projection, the resulting vector is divided into equal-sized bins (with 216 bins in our case), and
730   the values within each bin are summed to form the final compressed vector. This permutation and
731   projection procedure is applied uniformly across all vectors, ensuring that dot product values are
732   preserved even after compression.
733
      OPORP allows us to efficiently store compressed gradient vectors for entire preference datasets
734
      using a manageable amount of storage. In Table 1, we present the calculated storage requirements
735   for storing 1,000, 10,000, and 100,000 sample gradients. For 100,000 gradients, our compression
736   method reduces the storage requirement to 24.4GB, a significant reduction compared to the 15.6TB
737   that would be required without compression.

1263
1264   In this section, we describe how we leveraged influence values to improve the alignment between
1265   Alice’s and Bob’s labeling strategies. This process is detailed in Algorithm 1.
1266
1267   Influence-based partitioning Alice and Bob each label their respective datasets, DA and DB , using
                                                                                              (0)     (1)
1268   their weight vectors, wA and wB . For a given input xi , Bob evaluates two responses, yi and yi ,
1269   and computes scores wB⊤ r(0) and wB⊤ r(1) . Bob’s preference label zi is determined by whether
1270   wB⊤ r(1) > wB⊤ r(0) , assigning zi = 1 if true, and zi = 0 otherwise.
1271   To assess the alignment between Alice’s and Bob’s labels, we compute influence values Ival (di )
1272   using Alice’s dataset DA as a reference. We set the threshold η to the median of influence values
1273   {Ival (di ) | di ∈ DB }, ensuring that Bob’s dataset DB is evenly split into two groups, where 50% of
1274   the data points with the highest influence values are considered likely to be mislabeled.
1275
1276   Training the SVM classifier For each sample in Bob’s dataset DB , we compute the score dif-
1277   ferences ri = r(zi ) − r(1−zi ) . These score differences represent how much better one response is
1278   compared to the other based on Bob’s preferences. Samples are then partitioned according to the
1279   influence values: data points with Ival (di ) > η (likely mislabeled) are assigned label ti = 0, and
1280   those with Ival (di ) ≤ η (correctly labeled) are labeled as ti = 1.
1281   We then apply a linear Support Vector Machine (SVM) to the score differences and their corre-
1282   sponding labels. The SVM learns a new weight vector wSVM , which is designed to maximize the
1283   separation between high-influence (mislabeled) and low-influence (correctly labeled) data points,
1284   aiming to reduce Bob’s mislabeling.
1285
1286   Cosine similarity and accuracy evaluation After training the SVM, we evaluate the alignment
1287   between Alice’s and Bob’s updated weight vectors. The cosine similarity between wA and wB is
1288   computed, as well as the cosine similarity between wA and wSVM (the SVM-derived weight vector).
1289   This helps us understand how closely Bob’s updated labeling strategy aligns with Alice’s after the
1290
       influence-based update.
1291   We further assess the accuracy of the labeling strategies before and after the update. Accuracy
1292   before the update is computed by checking how often Alice and Bob’s original preferences agree
1293   on the same response. After applying the SVM classifier, we compute the accuracy again using the
1294   classifier’s new weights wSVM . The improvement in accuracy shows how effectively the SVM has
1295   adjusted Bob’s labeling strategy to be more aligned with Alice’s.
