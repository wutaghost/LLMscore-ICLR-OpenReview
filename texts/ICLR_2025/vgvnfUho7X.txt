009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Many recent studies of LLM performance have focused on the ability of LLMs
012            to achieve outcomes comparable to humans on academic and professional exams.
013            However, it is not clear whether such studies shed light on the extent to which
014            models show reasoning ability, and there is controversy about the significance and
015            implications of such results. We seek to look more deeply into the question of
016
               how and whether the performance of LLMs on exams designed for humans re-
               flects the true aptitude inherent in LLMs. We do so by making use of the tools
017
               of psychometrics which are designed to perform meaningful measurements in test
018
               taking. We leverage a unique dataset that captures the detailed performance of
019            over 5M students across 8 college entrance exams given over a span of two years
020            in Brazil. With respect to the evaluation of LLM abilities, we show that the tools
021            of Item Response Theory (IRT) provide a more informative evaluation of model
022            performance than the usual accuracy metrics employed in previous studies. Dig-
023            ging deeper, we show that the modeling framework of IRT, by explicitly modeling
024            the difficulty levels of questions, allows us to quantitatively distinguish between
025            LLMs that answer questions in “human-like” patterns versus LLMs that do not.
026            We also show how to quantitatively identify cases in which exam results are not
027
               reliable measurements of an LLM’s ability. Using the tools of IRT we can also
               identify specific questions that appear to be either much easier, or much harder,
028
               for machines than for humans, and we give some reasons for those differences.
029
               Overall, our study shows that the conventional focus on accuracy as the primary
030            performance metric for LLM studies does not allow us to deeply understand the
031            true capabilities of LLMs and compare them to that of humans. Thus, we claim
032            that psychometric modeling should play a larger role in the evaluation of LLM
033            capabilities on exams designed for humans.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated an impressive ability to perform well on ex-
039
      aminations designed for humans (Narayanan & Kapoor, 2023; Raji et al., 2021), such as the US
      bar exam (OpenAI, 2023), the US Medical Licensing Exam (Kung et al., 2023), and many oth-
040
      ers (Varanasi, 2023; Zhong et al., 2023). This yields controversy in how researchers should interpret
041
      such results, raising two kinds of criticisms of those apparent successes. The first is the potential
042   for publicly-given exams (and answers) to leak into models’ training data. The second, and more
043   fundamental, issue is the notion of construct validity (Trott, 2024). Most exams given to humans
044   are intended to measure a construct, e.g., legal analysis ability, medical analysis ability, etc. How-
045   ever, the reliability of these exams in measuring the relevant construct for non-humans is usually
046   ignored, and exams that are valid in one context may not generalize across different groups, settings
047   or tasks (Messick, 1995).
048   Formalizing the notion of construct validity in general is challenging. Since the 1950s, the field
049   of psychometrics has been grappling with how to design examinations that validly measure human
050   abilities along specific dimensions. The primary tool developed has been Item Response Theory
051   (IRT) (Chen et al., 2021), which has been employed in psychology, medicine, and especially in
052   educational testing. IRT formalizes the unobserved construct as a continuous latent variable, and
053   models stochastic responses of humans to questions as a logistic regression conditional on that latent
      variable.


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      In this paper, we demonstrate how IRT can help shed light on whether LLMs are in fact show-
055   ing human-like performance on exams intended for humans. As a case study, we use one of the
056   largest university entrance exams in the world, a dataset comprising the performance of over 5 mil-
057   lion Brazilian students on eight multiple-choice exams administered over two years. Each exam
058   was prepared and fitted to an IRT model by educational testing experts, giving us an unparalleled
059   opportunity to examine the performance of LLMs in detail.
060
      Our results show that the LLMs we study reveal performance patterns that are consistent with ex-
061   pected human behavior in many cases. Nonetheless, we also frequently observe significant deviation
062   from human-like behavior. We demonstrate how to use the tools of IRT to quantitatively distinguish
063   between human-like and non-human-like behavior. We then explore the differences between mod-
064   els and exam types that correlate with differences in response patterns. Lastly, we use the tools of
065   IRT and psychometrics to identify cases where exams are not producing reliable estimates of LLM
066   ability and understand why this happens. This occurs because exams are in some cases too difficult
067   for the models, and in other cases too easy for them, and as such they cannot properly measure the
068   ability of certain LLMs.
069   Moving beyond conclusions about current models, the broader contribution of our study is to demon-
070   strate the power of IRT as a framework for evaluating LLMs. For example, in Classical Test Theory
071   (CTT), no attempt is made to assess the difficulty of individual questions, in-line with the major-
072   ity of standard LLM benchmarks that pursue accuracy (Bubeck et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023;
073   Hendrycks et al., 2020; bench authors, 2023). In contrast, as we will show below, IRT simultane-
074   ously measures both test takers and exam questions (on the same scale). In doing so, IRT allows
075
      one to distinguish between test takers with similar CTT (accuracy) scores, but differing levels of
      true ability, by inspecting the pattern of correct or incorrect answers given. Moreover, we deploy
076
      a broader set of tools (e.g., goodness-of-fit, Fisher information, discrimination index) which enable
077
      us to evaluate which are the cases in which fitting the IRT model to the LLMs response patterns
078   gives us reliable estimates of the models’ ability. Thus, we believe that the methods of our study
079   represent a valuable step beyond the use of simple accuracy for assessing whether both current and
080   future LLMs show human-like response patterns.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
084
085   Our study connects a number of research areas, spanning benchmarking LLMs, the applications of
086
      item response theory, and the evaluation of LLMs using exams designed for humans.
087   Benchmarking LLMs. The most common strategy to evaluate LLMs is through traditional large-
088   scale NLP benchmarks (Wang et al., 2018; Talmor et al., 2019; Cobbe et al., 2021; Joshi et al.,
089   2017; Hendrycks et al., 2020; Kiela et al., 2021; bench authors, 2023). Conventionally, benchmark
090   evaluation relies on some notion of accuracy – the number of correct answers – as a proxy for
091   ability (Bubeck et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023). A key distinction of our study is to draw attention
092   to the limitations of the use of accuracy alone (Schaeffer et al., 2023) for evaluating the performance
093   of LLMs on benchmarks in understanding the similarity between the performance of models versus
094   humans.
095
      LLMs and Exams Designed for Humans. Many attempts to evaluate LLMs use exams designed
096
      for humans, e.g., at college-entrance (Achiam et al., 2023; Nunes et al., 2023) or college-level (Drori
097   et al., 2023; Silva et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Drori et al., 2022; Terwiesch, 2023; Zhong et al.,
098   2023). These exams also generally use accuracy as a metric of ability; one focus of our work is on
099   how to use IRT analysis to determine when such exams in fact perform meaningful measurement.
100
      The Brazilian nationwide college-entrance exams we use in this work (ENEM), detailed in Sec-
101
      tion 4.1, were used in previous efforts to evaluate NLP models (Silveira & Deratani Mauá, 2017;
102
      Silveira & Mauá, 2018; Nunes et al., 2023). However, those studies only used accuracy and did not
103   make use of the IRT models associated with the exam, which is a central aspect our work.
104
105   IRT in Machine Learning. Work in psychometrics (i.e., the measurement of human cognitive
106   abilities), detailed in Section 3, has shown that using accuracy as a exam score may not reflect the
107   true underlying abilities of individuals (Erguven, 2013). As a result, IRT has been advocated for
      use in machine learning (ML) as an improved tool for benchmarking. The authors in (Rodriguez


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      et al., 2021) show that it is possible to produce rankings of NLP models which are more reliable and
109   stable using IRT than accuracy. Item response theory has also been shown to help in spotting noisy
110   questions, identifying overfitting, selecting features, and designing better and smaller benchmarks
111   for ML (Polo et al., 2024; Plumed et al., 2016; Sedoc & Ungar, 2020; Kline et al., 2021; Zhuang
112   et al., 2023; Lalor et al., 2016). However, there is a critical difference between the previous uses of
113   IRT in ML and our work. Previous work uses IRT by training an IRT model on the results of ML
114   models solving question-answering or classification questions. Our method is different: we leverage
115   the fact that we have access to an IRT model trained on human responses, and we do not retrain on
116   model responses. We take this approach because a central goal of our study is to explore whether
117
      LLMs are in fact following response patterns as exhibited by human test takers.
118   Finally, we note that (Tjuatja et al., 2024) shares some goals with our work. The investigation
119   seeks to understand whether LLMs show human-like response biases in surveys. We also look at
120   the question of whether LLMs show human-like response patterns, but we study the question along
121   different dimensions: (a) patterns of correct and incorrect answers in exams; and (b) the ways in
122   which LLMs choose incorrect answers. Additionally, Xia et al. (Xia et al., 2024) recognize that
123
      accuracy as a single metric does not capture errors LLMs can make in intermediate steps when
      solving mathematical tasks, and they systematically study those errors.

126   3    BACKGROUND
127
128   In this section, we give some background of the tools we use from psychometrics.
129
130   Classical Test Theory (CTT): CTT (Allen & Yen, 2002) evaluates test takers based on the fraction
131   of questions they answer correctly. We call this score accuracy or CTT score of the test taker
132   and we use these two terms interchangeably. Inadequately, CTT does not differentiate between
133
      difficult and easy questions, nor does it take into consideration the patterns of correct answers. For
      example, the CTT score does not penalize a test taker who answers correctly difficult questions, but
134
      answers wrongly easy ones – despite the fact that such a pattern might be indicative of randomness
135
      or cheating.
136
137   Item Response Theory (IRT): IRT (De Ayala, 2013; Baker & Kim, 2004) is a model used exten-
138   sively in psychometrics to measure the ability level of the test takers and evaluate the difficulty of
139   the test questions (which are referred to as items in psychometrics). IRT takes into consideration
140   the difficulty of the questions when evaluating the test-taker’s performance and also makes use of
141   the pattern of correct and incorrect responses on the exam. The model associates with every test
142
      taker j a parameter θj , which corresponds to the ability of j. The two-parameter IRT model (2PL)
      associates every question i with two parameters ϕi = (αi , βi ). The model assumes that a test taker
143
      with ability θj answers question i associated with ϕi correctly with probability given by the logistic
144
      function:
145

148   Parameter αi is the discrimination parameter and βi is the difficulty of question i. Note that the
149

      affects pij . For fixed αi , the difficulty parameter βi is the value (on the ability scale) for which
150

151
      located at different points of the ability continuum; αi is proportional to the slope of pij = pi (θj )
152   at the point where pij = 0.5 – the steeper the slope, the higher the discriminatory power of i. All
153   the parameters of this model take values in (−∞, +∞). Note that any set of questions comprising
154   an exam spans a certain range of βi values; such a set is not appropriate to assess test takers with
155   abilities outside this range.
156

157

163   ods (Baker & Kim, 2004). In our case, the ENEM dataset came with a set of questions for which the

165   Pesquisas Educacionais Anı́sio Teixeira, 2024). Therefore, for each one of the LLMs we considered,
166   we only need to compute their ability parameters – given their response patterns. Intuitively, large
167   values of θ correspond to test takers with high ability levels and vice versa. High ability value θ of
168   an LLM implies better performance.

203   4.1   T HE ENEM E XAM
204
205   The Exame Nacional do Ensino Médio (ENEM), world’s second largest university entrance exam
206
      behind Chinese’s Gaokao exam, is taken by millions of Brazilian students each year (Silveira &
      Mauá, 2018). ENEM comprises questions requiring different levels of domain-specific knowledge
207
      and reasoning (Almeida et al., 2023).
208
209   The exam is in Brazilian Portuguese and consists of four sections, each of which has 45 multiple-
210   choice questions with five options (Instituto Nacional de Estudos e Pesquisas Educacionais Anı́sio
211   Teixeira, 2024). Each section is treated as a separate exam for the purposes of modeling via IRT.
212
      The four sections consist of the Humanities, the Languages and Codes, the Natural Sciences, and
      the Math exams. The description of these exams is given in Appendix A.11.
213
214   Since 2009, the grades assigned to ENEM test-takers have been determined using IRT. Using IRT
215   helps to penalize guessing, differentiate among students that otherwise would get the same (CTT)
      grade, and compare among students that took exams in different years. The ENEM organizers


                                                             4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      release not only the exam content and questions, but also the student (anonymized) responses and
217   their CTT and IRT scores, which enables downstream studies.
218
219   From our standpoint, there are a number of relevant aspects of the process used by the ENEM
220
      developers (Instituto Nacional de Estudos e Pesquisas Educacionais Anı́sio Teixeira, 2024). First,
      questions are given to a sample of students, whose answers are used to find inconsistencies and
221
      errors. Next, an important test of construct validity is to verify the unidimensionality of the latent
222
      trait, for which the ENEM team uses Full Information Factor Analysis (Bock et al., 1988). Finally,
223   the IRT model itself is fit using the Marginal Maximum Likelihood Estimator (Bock & Aitkin,
224   1981). Using the results, the developers may exclude questions having poor model fit.
225
226
      The exams, their solutions, and all the fitted parameters of the 3PL IRT model (θj , αi , βi , γi ) are
      publicly available at the Brazilian government website (Instituto Nacional de Estudos e Pesquisas
227
      Educacionais Anı́sio Teixeira, 2024). To the best of our knowledge, these data are the largest and
228
      most comprehensive public dataset based on item-response theory available. The datasets contain
229   questions and complete response patterns of all students taking the exams in 2022 and 2023. Ques-
230   tions for the 2023 exam were released in November 2023, minimizing the chance they are in training
231   data for most of the LLMs we considered. However, we expect fragments of the exam being in the
232   training data (e.g. poems, and any other widely available material used as part of a question) 1 . The
233   number of test takers per year ranged from 2.2M to 3.7M.
234
      The ENEM exams are initially made available as PDF files; we used the Python library PyPDF2,
235   followed by regular expressions and some manual adjustments to extract each question from its
236   exam file. In order to account for possible effects of Language, as diagnosed in previous work
237   (Ranaldi & Pucci, 2023), we translated all questions to English and run all experiments in Portuguese
238   and English. For those exam questions that incorporated images, we used the version of the exam
239   designed for blind people containing textual descriptions of the images. We manually audited all
240   questions in 2022 and 2023 exams to ensure their quality (Appendix A.1).

245
      Llama2-7B, Llama2-13B, Llama3-8B, and GPT 3.5. For the open source models, we evaluate

363   els often resorting to guessing. On the other hand, most LLMs consistently answer correctly the
364   questions in the Humanities exam, implying that this is an easy exam for them. The performance of
365   LLMs in the Natural Science exam is the most interesting as there are blocks of questions that most
366   LLMs answer consistently correctly, interleaved with blocks of questions that most LLMs answer
367   incorrectly. This suggests that there are questions that are easy for humans but difficult for LLMs
368
      and vice versa. In the next subsection, we analyze this phenomenon more closely.

370   5.3   R ELIABILITY OF IRT SCORES FOR LLM S
371
372   In this section, we investigate whether the ENEM exam is a valid test for LLMs’ ability, in the same
373   way it is for humans. Intuitively, we want to define measures that allow us to quantify to what extent
374   we trust the IRT scores we obtained for LLMs. We propose three different ways of doing this. The
375   first is the goodness-of-fit, which quantifies whether the response of LLMs fit the IRT model. The
376   second is based on Fisher information, measuring how much information the exam provides for
377   estimating the θs in a certain range. Finally, we use the discrimination index which evaluates the
      capacity of questions to accurately distinguish between high and low performing test takers.

464   Discrimination Index: To further assess the reliability of the IRT scores, we also turn into psycho-
465   metrics and use the notion of the item discrimination index (DI), which measures how well an item
466   on a test distinguishes between high and low scorers on the entire test (Chan, 2015). Let Ph (resp.
467   Pl ) be the proportion of the top 25% (resp. low 25%) LLMs (in terms of θ, including the shuffles)
468   that correctly answer the item; then DI = Ph − Pl , the difference of the two proportions. DI ranges
469   from -1 to 1, and questions with DI higher than 0.2 are considered good, while lower DI indicates
470
      flaws (Wu & Adams, 2007).
471   Figure 5 shows the distribution of the discrimination indices computed for humans and LLMs for the
472   2023 exam. Overall, we notice that discrimination indices computed for LLMs are more negative
473   compared to those of humans. We also observe that a significant fraction of Math questions have low
474   discriminative power, reinforcing the hypothesis that this exam is not well designed to measure Math
475   abilities for LLMs. Nonetheless, the Humanities and Languages have several questions with very
476
      good discriminative power. Interestingly, the Natural Sciences exam appears to follow a bimodal
      distribution, containing both informative and poorly-designed questions. This may reflect the fact
477
      that the Natural Sciences exam is a hybrid test, containing a mix of knowledge-based items and
478
      items that demand more complex reasoning over numbers and images, which can be less useful for
479   evaluating the current state-of-the-art LLMs.

484   LLMs from understanding the question properly. Our preliminary results suggest that LLMs’ abil-
485   ity to understand math questions and parse images is sub-par compared to their capacity to answer
      pure text-based questions. In Appendix A.10 we show examples of non-discriminating and highly

490   6   C ONCLUSIONS
491
492   The ongoing debate in LLM evaluation centers around whether exams designed for humans are
493
      appropriate tools for measuring the performance of LLMs. In this paper, we provide a case study
      that illustrates methods that can be used to address this question, as well as specific results for a
494
      range of current LLMs. We leverage the largest known human exam for which a public IRT model
495
      is available, and show that IRT can be leveraged to distinguish between human-like and non-human-
496   like responses under the model. We show cases where LLMs respond in non-human-like ways and
497   show how to identify those cases using a model-fit metric. Further, we show that using IRT we
498   can determine when an exam is capable of making meaningful measurement of an LLM’s ability
499   in a given subject area. Using our evaluation framework, we find that the ENEM Math exam is not
500   appropriate to make meaningful measurements of the models’ ability, for the LLMs we study. At
501   the same time, Humanities and Language exams are better suited for evaluating the LLMs’ abilities
502   on those subjects. We conclude that IRT modeling, drawing on a long history of psychometric
503   theory, provides a set of crucial tools for assessing whether exams designed for humans are actually
504
      meaningful measures of LLM ability. Our results suggest that they should be used in future studies
      when questions are raised regarding the performance of LLMs on human exams.

787   A.1.2    T RANSLATION ARTIFACTS
788   We found several issues pertaining to initial round of translation in this dataset. Mainly, independent
789   translation of question context and answer option leads to incoherence. Details are sometimes mis-
790   translated (“p.d.d” translated to “d.d.p”). There are many non-standardized translations pertaining
791   to chemical formulas, proper nouns, and mathematical formulas. In general, there are significant
792   amount of awkward phrasing, incomplete translation, and linguistic idiosyncrasies lost in transla-
793   tion.
794
795   Independent translation of context and question In a few cases, the answer options are expected
796   to complete the last sentence of the question. After translation, options do not all fit as completions of
797   the sentence (Q11). Translation without context also leads to improper translation of polysemantic
798
      terms. “Coagulation” maybe translated correctly in the question, but becomes “coagulating” as
      a stand-alone word (Q96). “Good” and “fair” (when used as survey options) gets translated to
799
      “regular” and “I will” as stand-alone options (Q171)
800
801
      Inconsistent translation details Within the same questions, there are cases where the same con-
802   cept is translated differently. In one question, the context introduces the concept “potential difference
803   (p.d.d)”, and later referred to it as “d.d.p” and “d.p.d”. Within different options, the same unit can
804   sometimes be plural and sometimes be singular (when it should be consistently plural)
805
806   Non-standard translation 1) Chemical formula translation is non-standard. “N2O3” becomes “N
807   2O3”, and “NH4+” becomes “NH4 positively charged”. 2) (Proper) nouns are sometimes capital-
808   ized when they shouldn’t. For instance, one question begins with the sentence “On the Gravitational
809   Field of a Mass Point According to Einstein’s Theory A ’Black Hole is a...” 3) Mathematical equa-
      tions are overly verbatim. This we suspect is partially due to an issue with using audio version of the

933   A.3      M ODEL ACCURACY RELATION TO MODEL PERPLEXITY
934
935
      One reason that models may error differently than humans is due to their training corpus. If models
      have encountered similar question or topics, if not identical, to those in our dataset during training,
936
      they may perform unexpectedly well, even if the questions are difficult. Recent work in data con-
937
      tamination proposed a few model intrinsic metrics that can be used to detect contamination Shi et al.
938   (2023). Mainly, the Min-k% Prob score takes the average probability of the top-k percentile tokens
939   with minimum probabilities 4 :
940
941                                                    1       X
942                          MIN-K% Prob (x) = −                           logp(xi |x1 , . . . , xi−1 )       (4)
                                                       E
943                                                        xi ∈Min-K%(x)

944
      where x = x1 , x2 , . . . , xN denotes the input sequence of N tokens, Min-K% Prob(x) represents the
945   set containing tokens with minimum k percentile probabilities, and E represents the size of such set.
946   Note here that Min-k% Prob is intrinsic to each model, and if a model has been exposed to more
947   similar training data as the questions, its Min-k% Prob would be low for that question.
948
      We do not expect any model to have unexpectedly low Min-K% Prob(x) on any of our questions,
949
      considering it is highly unlikely that the ENEM questions were parsed and translated to English, and
950
      somehow ended up in the training corpus. What we are more interested here, is whether such score
951   is correlated to model’s accuracy on the answer predictions. If they are negatively correlated (i.e.
952   high Min-K% Prob corresponds to low accuracy), this is evidence for the hypothesis that training on
953   related data leads to higher accuracy.
954
      To investigate this hypothesis, we plot 4-shot model accuracy (averaged across 31 option shuffles)
955
      against Min-20% Prob for four subjects in exam 2022 in English along with the Pearson correlations
956   5
        in Figure 6. In all except 1 model-subject pair (Llama2 chat in humanities, we investigate this
957   further) do we see a significant negative correlation (p < 0.05) between accuracy and Min-k 20%
958   Prob, indicate that model doesn’t necessarily do better if they have encountered similar data during
959   training. Another way to interpret this, is that it is not likely that these models have seen our data
960   during training.
961
962   The few negative correlation cases As seen before, we observe a significant negative correlation
963   for Llama-2 7B Chat in humanities. To get a full understanding of whether this is a stand-alone
964   phenomenon, we examine Portuguese version of the exam, as well as exam in 2023, and show
965   our findings below in Table 3. We do not see the same correlation in the Portuguese version of the

      exam. However, we additionally see Gemma-it negatively correlated with humanities section in both

      2023. The later two correlations are robust across a few other metrics we investigated from Shi et al.

1098   A.4       P ROMPTING D ETAILS
1099
1100   To administering the test to LLMs, we measure the next token logits across the 5 letter options
1101   directly (i.e. letter “A”, “B”, “C”, “D”, “E”), and take the argmax as the model’s choice (invariant
1102   to sampling temperature). We shuffle the option orders (30 runs) and take the average to calibrate
1103   model’s prior on generating each letter options. For API-based model (GPT3.5), we query for 1
1104   token generation, and obtain top-20 logits, and use that for our prediction. In the sections below we
1105
       include 0-shot (Listing 3), 1-shot (Listing 4, 5, 6, 7), and 4-shot prompts (Listing 8) we use in main
       experiments. For 1-shot, we choose the 1-shot example for each of the four subjects by selecting
1106
       the easiest question (i.e., with lowest β) from the same subject in the 2021 exam. For 4-shot, we
1107
       concatenate the 1-shots from four subjects and shuffle the options to evenly distribute the answer
1108   among five option letters.
1109
1110
       Potential limitations We ran exploratory experiments with Chain-of-Thought (CoT) like prompt-
1111
       ing (Wei et al., 2022), but and did not see significant changes. We did not include the results because
1112   CoT prompting requires generating reasoning strings and parsing answers, making 30-shuffles ex-
1113   tremely slow to run for all models. Future directions could explore how much effect more complex
1114   prompting techniques have in assimilating model behaviors. Regarding the best prompting strategy,
1115   we do acknowledge recent criticisms on first letter evaluation(Wang et al., 2024). At the time of our
1116   writing, it is still the best evaluation strategy for multiple choice question-answering data. We also
1117   acknowledge that there are more capable models than GPT3.5 that is available through API services
1118   but as our work is not trying to identify the SOTA model we did not feel the need to evaluate latest
1119   and largest models. Lastly, we assume Portuguese and Brazilian culture is present in the training data
1120
       for the language models we test. Future work could evaluate the amount of multilingual training’s
       affect on some of these IRT metric we propose.

2487   formations, as well as comprehension of social and political institutions, technological changes, and
2488   the use of historical knowledge to promote conscious engagement in society. It requires recognizing
2489   the interactions between society and nature in various historical and geographical contexts.

2491   cludes some knowledge and use of foreign languages, understanding of body language, analysis and

2493   languages, and understanding the impact of communication on personal and social life.
