011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Compositional optimization (CO) has recently gained popularity due to its appli-
014            cations in distributionally robust optimization (DRO), meta-learning, reinforce-
015            ment learning, and many other machine learning applications. The large-scale
016
               and distributed nature of data necessitates efficient federated learning (FL) algo-
               rithms for CO, but the compositional structure of the objective poses significant
017
               challenges. Current methods either rely on large batch gradients (which are im-
018
               practical) or suffer from suboptimal communication efficiency. To address these
019            challenges, we propose efficient FedAvg-type algorithms for solving non-convex
020            CO in the FL setting. We first establish that standard FedAvg fails in solving the
021            federated CO problems due to data heterogeneity, which amplifies bias in local
022            gradient estimates. Our analysis establishes that either additional communication
023            or two-sided learning rate-based algorithms are required to control this bias. To
024            this end, we develop two algorithms for solving the federated CO problem. First,
025            we propose FedDRO that utilizes the compositional problem structure to design
026            a communication strategy that allows FedAvg to control the bias in the estimation
027            of the compositional gradient, achieving O(ϵ−2 ) sample and O(ϵ−3/2 ) commu-
028
               nication complexity. Then we propose DS-FedDRO, a two-sided learning rate
               algorithm, that eliminates the need for additional communication and achieves the
029
               optimal O(ϵ−2 ) sample and O(ϵ−1 ) communication complexity, highlighting the
030
               importance of two-sided learning rate algorithms for solving federated CO prob-
031            lems. Both algorithms avoid the need for large batch gradients and achieve linear
032            speedup with the number of clients. We corroborate our theoretical findings with
033            empirical studies on large-scale DRO problems.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Compositional optimization (CO) problems deal with the minimization of the composition of func-
039   tions. A standard CO problem takes the form
040
                                minx∈Rd f (g(x)) with g(x) := Eζ∼Dg [g(x; ζ)],                           (1)
041
042   where x ∈ Rd is the optimization variable, f : Rdg → R and g : Rd → Rdg are smooth functions,
043   and ζ ∼ Dg represents a stochastic sample of g(·) from distribution Dg . CO finds applications in
044   a broad range of machine learning applications, including but not limited to distributionally robust
045   optimization (DRO) Qi et al. (2022), meta-learning Finn et al. (2017), phase retrieval Duchi & Ruan
046   (2019), portfolio optimization Shapiro et al. (2021), and reinforcement learning Wang et al. (2017).
047
      In this work, we focus on a more challenging version of the CO problem (1) that often arises in
048   the DRO formulation Haddadpour et al. (2022). Specifically, the problems that jointly minimize the
049   summation of a compositional and a non-compositional objective. DRO has recently garnered sig-
050   nificant attention because of its capability of handling noisy labels Chen et al. (2022), training fair
051   machine learning models Qi et al. (2022), imbalanced Qi et al. (2020a) and adversarial data Chen
052   & Paschalidis (2018). A standard approach to solve DRO is to utilize primal-dual algorithms Ne-
053   mirovski et al. (2009) that are inherently slow because of a large number of stochastic constraints.
      The CO formulation enables the development of faster primal-only DRO algorithms Haddadpour


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      et al. (2022). The majority of existing works to solve CO problems consider a centralized setting,
055   however, modern large-scale machine-learning applications are characterized by the distributed col-
056   lection of data by multiple clients Kairouz et al. (2021). This necessitates the development of dis-
057   tributed algorithms to solve the DRO problem.
058
      Federated learning (FL) is a distributed learning paradigm that allows clients to solve a joint problem
059
      in collaboration with a server while keeping the data of each client private McMahan et al. (2017).
060
      The clients act as computing units while the server orchestrates the parameter sharing among clients.
061   Numerous FL algorithms exist in the literature to tackle standard (non-compositional) problems Li
062   et al. (2019; 2020); Karimireddy et al. (2019); Sharma et al. (2019); Zhang et al. (2021); Khanduri
063   et al. (2021). However, there is a lack of efficient distributed implementations when it comes to CO
064   problems. The major challenges in developing FL algorithms for solving the CO problem are:
065   [C1]: Compositional structure of the problem leads to biased stochastic gradient estimates and this
066   bias is amplified during local updates, which makes the analysis intractable Chen et al. (2021).
067   [C2]: Typically, data distribution at each client is different, referred to as data heterogeneity. Hetero-
068   geneously distributed compositional objective results in client drift during local updates that lead to
069
      divergence of federated CO algorithms. This is in sharp contrast to the standard FedAvg for non-CO
      objectives where client drift can be controlled during the local updates Karimireddy et al. (2019).
070
      [C3]: A majority of algorithms for solving CO rely on accuracy-dependent large batch gradients
071
      where the batch size depends on the desired solution accuracy, which is not practical from an imple-
072   mentation point of view Huang et al. (2021); Haddadpour et al. (2022); Guo et al. (2022).
073
      These challenges naturally lead to the following question:
074
075           Can we develop FL algorithms that tackle [C1] − [C3] to solve CO in a FL setting?
076
      In this work, we address the above question and develop novel FL algorithms to solve CO problems.
077
      Although, our development focuses on the DRO problem the algorithms developed in our work have
078   wider applicability to other general CO problems. The major contributions of our work are:
079
080   • We for the first time present a negative result that establishes that the vanilla FedAvg (customized
081
      to CO) is incapable of solving the CO problems as it leads to bias amplification during the local
      updates. This shows that either additional communication/processing or non-classical aggregation
082
      procedure is required by FedAvg to mitigate the bias in the local gradient estimation.
083
084
      • We develop two novel FL algorithms FedDRO and DS-FedDRO, for solving problems with both
      compositional and non-compositional non-convex objectives. To our knowledge, such algorithms
085
      have been absent from the open literature so far. Importantly, FedDRO and DS-FedDRO address
086
      the above-mentioned challenges by developing several key innovations in the algorithm design.
087   – FedDRO addresses [C1] by designing a communication strategy that utilizes the specific
088     CO problem structure and allows us to control the gradient bias at the cost of additional low-
089     dimensional communication. On the contrary, DS-FedDRO tackles [C1] by designing 2-sided
090     learning rate CO algorithms for FL wherein the server aggregation is performed similarly as the
091     local updates.
092   – To address [C2], we design the local updates at each client so that the client drift is bounded.
093     Our analysis captures the effect of data heterogeneity on the performance of the algorithms.
094   – To address [C3], we utilize a hybrid momentum-based estimator to learn the compositional
095     embedding and combine it with a stochastic gradient (SG) estimator to conduct the local updates.
096     This construction allows us to circumvent the need to compute large accuracy-dependent batch
097     sizes for computing the gradients and the compositional function evaluations.
098   • We establish the convergence of FedDRO and DS-FedDRO and show that to achieve an ϵ-
099   stationary point both algorithms require O(ϵ−2 ) samples while achieving linear speed-up with the
100   number of clients, i.e., requiring O(K −1 ϵ−2 ) samples per client. Moreover, FedDRO achieves a
101   communication complexity of O(ϵ−3/2 ) while DS-FedDRO achieves O(ϵ−1 ).

310   low-dimensional embedding g(·) are aggregated in each iteration while the high-dimensional model
311   parameters are shared intermittently. Recall that for many practical problems (see Section 2.1 for
312   DRO) the embedding g(·) is low-dimensional (e.g., dg = 1), therefore, sharing of g(·) will be
313   relatively cheap in contrast to the high-dimensional model parameters of size d which can be very

769   A    R ELATED WORK
770
771   Centralized CO. The first non-asymptotic analysis of stochastic CO problems was performed in
772   Wang et al. (2017) where the authors proposed SCGD a two-timescale algorithm for solving the
773   problem (1). The convergence of SCGD was improved in Wang et al. (2016) where the authors pro-
774   posed an accelerated variant of SCGD. Both SCGD and its accelerated variant achieved convergence
775   rates that were strictly worse than those of SGD for solving non-CO problems. Recently, Ghadimi
776   et al. (2020) and Chen et al. (2021) developed a single time-scale algorithm for solving the CO prob-
777   lem that achieves the same convergence as SGD for solving non-CO problems. Variance-reduced
778
      algorithms for solving the CO problems have also been considered in the literature, however, a ma-
      jor drawback of such approaches is the reliance of batch size on the desired solution accuracy Lian
779
      et al. (2017); Zhang & Xiao (2019); Hu et al. (2019).
780
781   Distributed CO. There have been only a few attempts to solve non-convex CO problems in the
782   FL setting, partially, because of the challenges discussed in Section 1. The first FL algorithm to
783   solve the non-convex CO problem, Compositional Federated Learning (ComFedL), was developed
784
      in Huang et al. (2021). ComFedL required accuracy-dependent batch sizes that resulted in O(ϵ−4 )
      convergence which is significantly worse compared to FedAvg to solve standard non-compositional
785
      problems Yu et al. (2019b). In Gao et al. (2022), Local Stochastic Compositional Gradient De-
786
      scent with Momentum (Local-SCGDM) was proposed which removed the requirement of large
787   batch sizes and achieved an O(ϵ−2 ) convergence. However, Local-SCGDM utilized a non-standard
788   momentum-based update from Ghadimi et al. (2020) that does not resemble a simple SGD-based
789   update. Importantly, the CO problem solved by ComFedL Huang et al. (2021) and Local-SCGDM
790   Gao et al. (2022) is non-standard as the problem is not distributed in the compositional objective (see
791   Remark 2.1). In contrast, we consider a general setting where the compositional objective is also
792   distributed among multiple nodes. Recently, Tarzanagh et al. (2022) proposed a nested optimization
793   framework, FedNest, to solve bilevel problems in the FL setting. The proposed algorithm achieved
794   SGD rates of O(ϵ−2 ) Ghadimi & Lan (2013). Different from the simple SGD-based update rule,
795
      FedNest adopted a multi-loop variance reduction-based update. In Haddadpour et al. (2022), the
      authors proposed a Generalized Composite Incremental Variance Reduction (GCIVR) framework
796
      for solving problems of the form (2) in a distributed setting. GICVR achieved a better convergence
797
      rate of O(ϵ−1.5 ), however, it relied on a double-loop structure and accuracy-dependent large batch
798   sizes to achieve variance reduction. Importantly, none of the above works guarantee linear speedup
799   with the number of clients. Moreover, the current algorithms utilize complicated momentum or VR-
800   based update rules that require computation of accuracy-dependent batch sizes Haddadpour et al.
801   (2022), and/or consider a simple setting where the compositional objective is not distributed among
802   nodes Huang et al. (2021); Gao et al. (2022).
803
      In contrast to all the above works, our work considers a general setting (2), where the goal is to
804   jointly minimize a compositional and a non-compositional objective in the FL setting. To solve (2),
805   we develop FedDRO a FedAvg algorithm for CO problems that achieves (i). the same guarantees
806   as FedAvg for minimizing non-CO problems, (ii). linear speed-up with the number of clients, (iii).
807   improved communication complexity, (iv). performance guarantees where the batch sizes required
808   are independent of the desired solution accuracy, and (v). characterizes the performance as a function
809   of local updates at each client and the data heterogeneity in the inner and outer non-compositional
      objectives.

      DRO. DRO has been extensively studied in optimization, machine learning, and statistics literature
811   Ben-Tal et al. (2013); Bertsimas et al. (2018); Duchi et al. (2021); Namkoong & Duchi (2017);
812   Staib & Jegelka (2019) Broadly, DRO problem formulation can be divided into two classes, one is
813   a constrained formulation and the other is the regularized formulation (see (3)) Levy et al. (2020);
814   Duchi et al. (2021). A popular approach to solve the constrained DRO formulation is via primal-
815   dual formulation where algorithms developed for min-max problems can directly be applied to solve
816   constrained DRO Yan et al. (2019); Namkoong & Duchi (2017); Song et al. (2021); Alacaoglu et al.
817   (2022); Tran Dinh et al. (2020). Many algorithms under different settings, e.g., convex, non-convex
818   losses, and stochastic settings have been considered in the past to address such problems. How-
819
      ever, primal-dual algorithms suffer from computational bottlenecks, since they require maintaining
      and updating the set of dual variables equal to the size of the dataset which can become particu-
820
      larly challenging, especially for large-scale machine learning tasks. Recently, Levy et al. (2020) Qi
821
      et al. (2022) Haddadpour et al. (2022) have developed algorithms that are applicable to large-scale
822   stochastic settings. Works Levy et al. (2020) and Qi et al. (2022) consider specific formulations
823   of the DRO problem while Haddadpour et al. (2022) considers a general formulation, however, as
824   pointed out earlier the algorithms developed in Haddadpour et al. (2022) are double loop and require
825   accuracy-dependent batch sizes to guarantee convergence (see Table 1). In contrast, in this work, we
826   develop algorithms that solve general instants of CO problems that often arise in DRO formulation.
827   Importantly, the developed algorithms are amenable to large-scale distributed implementation with
828   algorithmic guarantees independent of accuracy-dependent batch sizes.
