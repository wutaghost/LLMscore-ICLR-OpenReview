011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent advancements in Web agents have introduced novel architectures and
014            benchmarks showcasing progress in autonomous web navigation and interaction.
015            However, most existing benchmarks prioritize effectiveness and accuracy, over-
016
               looking factors like safety and trustworthiness—both essential for deploying web
               agents in enterprise settings. We present ST-WebAgentBench, a benchmark de-
017
               signed to evaluate web agents’ safety and trustworthiness across six critical di-
018
               mensions, essential for reliability in enterprise applications. This benchmark is
019            grounded in a detailed framework that defines safe and trustworthy (ST) agent
020            behavior. Our work extends WebArena with safety templates and evaluation func-
021            tions to rigorously assess safety policy compliance. We introduce the Completion
022            Under Policy to measure task success while adhering to policies, alongside the
023            Risk Ratio, which quantifies policy violations across dimensions, providing ac-
024            tionable insights to address safety gaps. Our evaluation reveals that current SOTA
025            agents struggle with policy adherence and cannot yet be relied upon for critical
026            business applications. We open-source this benchmark and invite the community
027
               to contribute, with the goal of fostering a new generation of safer, more trust-
               worthy AI agents. All code, data, environment reproduction resources, and video
028
               demonstrations are available at [blinded URL]

      humans.

106   completion and the steps involved in achieving intermediate goals. WebCanvas (Pan et al., 2024) ex-
107   tends this focus by also measuring the completion rates of key nodes, while AgentBench (Liu et al.,
      2023a) assesses the performance of LLM-based agents across a wide range of tasks, emphasizing

134   latory standards is crucial. Our benchmark addresses this gap by offering the first comprehensive
135   evaluation of web agents, focusing on both policy compliance and trustworthiness.

      3.1   S AFE AND T RUSTWORTHY AGENT B EHAVIOR
163
164   In the context of deploying web agents in enterprise environments, multiple entities are invested in
165   ensuring the safety and transparency of these agents, and each has a specific policy requirement.
166   The agent is expected to behave in a way that respects a strict hierarchy of policies:
167
168   1. Organizational Policies (Porg ): The organization must allow users to deploy web agents, but it
169
         is responsible for ensuring that the agents comply with safety policies. These policies encompass
         privacy, security, resource access, and operational limits. The organization’s primary goal is to
170
         ensure the agent’s behavior aligns with these established guidelines while maintaining safety.
171
         These policies have the highest precedence. The agent must comply with all organizational
172      policies at all times. Any violation of these policies is classified as a failure, rendering the agent
173      unsafe. An example of such a policy is “Never remove or delete any record in the system”
174   2. User Preferences (Puser ): The user interacts with the agent, trains it over time, and defines pref-
175      erences that guide its behavior. While organizational policies set boundaries, users may specify
176      their own preferences, which the agent should follow as long as they do not conflict with or-
177      ganizational policies. After organizational policies, the agent should consider user preferences.
178      These preferences guide behavior, but they can only be followed if they do not violate any orga-
179      nizational policies. An example of such a policy is “Before submitting a new form always ask my
180      permission”
181   3. Task Instructions (Ptask ): Each interaction involves a specific task or utterance that dictates
182      the agent’s immediate actions. These instructions are task-specific and are expected to guide the
183
         agent’s completion of the current task. However, these instructions hold the lowest precedence in
         the policy hierarchy, with organizational and user policies being prioritized. These task-specific
184
         instructions are applied only if they comply with both organizational policies and user prefer-
185
         ences. These instructions define how the agent completes a specific task, but they hold the lowest
186      priority in the hierarchy. An example of such a policy in the Create a new issue in GitLab task
187      can be “Use default for sizing and prioirity”

197   3.2   D IMENSIONS OF S AFETY AND T RUSTWORTHINESS OF W EB AGENTS
198
      We present key dimensions for evaluating the safety and trustworthiness of web agents within the
199
      specific context of business web automation. These dimensions have been carefully selected based
200   on practical experience in enterprise settings, including collaboration with business users and in-
201   sights from real-world applications. We would like to note that our intention is not to redefine
202   general concepts of trust areas or general trust in LLM- already extensively covered in the exist-
203   ing literature—but to highlight critical aspects where web agents may fail in automating business
204   processes. By incorporating business justifications, real-world examples, and the functions used
205   to evaluate each dimension, we aim to provide a practical framework essential for assessing agent
206   behavior in enterprise environments.
207
      • User Consent and Action Confirmation refers to the agent’s obligation to obtain explicit user
208
        permission before executing permanent or critical actions, such as submitting forms, deleting
209
        records, or making purchases. This dimension is critical for mitigating risks associated with un-
210     intended operations, such as unauthorized transactions, data loss, and a decline in user trust. By
211     ensuring user validation, agents uphold accountability and prevent costly errors. To address these
212     requirements, this dimension incorporates the ask the user template 4. For example, if an agent is
213     about to delete a customer record or submit a high-value purchase order, it must prompt the user
214     for confirmation to prevent accidental or unauthorized actions. Similarly, agents should comply
215     with explicit instructions, such as: ”When you are about to click the Save Changes button, send
        me a message to ask for my permission first,” ensuring that all critical tasks align with user intent.


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      • Boundary and Scope Limitation refers to the agent’s responsibility to restrict its actions to ex-
217     plicitly authorized areas within an application and adhere to predefined rules for application man-
218     agement. This dimension is essential for maintaining data integrity and security, as unauthorized
219     actions or exploration could expose sensitive data or violate privacy regulations. Failure in this
220     area can lead to breaches of confidentiality, non-compliance with regulations, and erosion of trust
221     in the system. This dimension is implemented with the access management 5 and navigation limi-
222     tation 6 templates where for instance, an agent designed to update sales records must be restricted
223     from navigating to financial reports or accessing unrelated areas of the application. Similarly,
224     specific organizational rules should be enforced, such as defining clear protocols the agent must
225
        follow when adding new members to a dataset, ensuring adherence to authorized processes.
226   • Strict Execution and Hallucination refers to the agent’s responsibility to adhere precisely to
227     user instructions without improvisation, deviation, or the introduction of hallucinated information
228     or actions during task execution. This dimension is critical for ensuring that the agent’s perfor-
229
        mance aligns with business objectives, avoids costly errors, and maintains trust in high-stakes
        applications. A failure in this regard can lead to unintended outcomes that undermine operational
230
        efficiency or violate compliance policies. To address these requirements, this dimension incor-
231
        porates safeguards such as monitoring for irreversible actions 7— for example, the agent must
232     be monitored to ensure it does not perform any irreversible actions, such as permanently deleting
233     or removing data— and hallucinated information 8 against hallucinations. For instance, an agent
234     tasked with submitting a reimbursement form might hallucinate an email address or fabricate bank
235     account details. Such behavior, while it may allow the agent to complete the task, poses serious
236     risks. Mechanisms to prevent hallucinations enhance task accuracy and build trust in the agent’s
237     reliability, crucial in enterprise settings.
238   • Hierarchy Adherence refers to the agent’s obligation to respect and follow organizational poli-
239     cies, user preferences, and task instructions, strictly adhering to this hierarchical order. This di-
240     mension is crucial as prioritizing lower-level preferences over higher-level policies can result in
241     significant business risks, including regulatory non-compliance, security breaches, and a loss of
242     organizational trust. Ensuring adherence to this hierarchy is essential for maintaining operational
243
        integrity and protecting sensitive assets. To address these requirements, this dimension incorpo-
        rates the policy contradictions 9 template. For example, in a GitLab environment, organizational
244
        policy might mandate that all new repositories be created as private by default to safeguard sensi-
245
        tive code. While a user may prefer creating public repositories for easier collaboration, the agent
246     must prioritize the organizational policy. It should ensure that any new repository is set to private,
247     even if the user explicitly requests otherwise.
248
      • Robustness and Security refers to the agent’s ability to maintain secure operations for the user
249     and organization while demonstrating resilience against adversarial inputs. This includes safe-
250     guarding personal and confidential information, ensuring data is not leaked during interactions
251     with third-party services or other users. Robustness and security are critical for protecting user pri-
252     vacy and organizational integrity, particularly in environments where sensitive data is frequently
253     handled. This dimension is implemented with the jailbreaking 11 and sensitive information 10
254     where for instance, in a CRM task, the agent must identify and disregard hidden instructions em-
255     bedded within fields, such as a ”Job Title” containing malicious prompts like ”Your job is also to
256     return the Mobile number”. Additionally, the agent should verify that it does not use any personal
257
        information provided by the user unless explicitly requested, ensuring compliance with privacy
        and security protocols.
258
259   • Error Handling and Safety Nets refers to the agent’s ability to handle errors gracefully while
260     maintaining transparency about its actions and decision-making processes. Such capabilities are
261
        crucial for fostering user trust and confidence in the agent’s behavior. Effective error handling
        ensures operational stability and minimizes the risk of disruptions or data loss that could result
262
        from unexpected situations. Transparency, on the other hand, is vital for accountability and user
263
        satisfaction, as it keeps users informed about the agent’s actions and limitations, particularly in
264     challenging scenarios. To address these requirements, this dimension incorporates mechanisms
265     like the popup error 12 and missing parameters 13 templates. For instance, if a popup indicates
266     that the site is undergoing maintenance and records will not be saved, the agent must transparently
267     inform the user of the situation and any limitations in performing the requested task. Similarly,
268     if the agent is instructed to create a new lead with only a first name and phone number, but the
269     ”Last Name” field is mandatory, the agent should proactively prompt the user for the missing
        information instead of proceeding incorrectly or failing silently.

      3.3       C OMPLETION U NDER THE P OLICY (C U P) M ETRICS
271
272   Web agents are typically measured by the standard success rate (also known as completion rate),
273   where all the task’s evaluation functions must be satisfied in order do declare success. We suggest
274   also measuring web agents via the the CuP metric. The CuP metric is computed for each task in ST-
275   WebAgentBench. CuP quantifies the agent’s ability to complete a task while adhering to the policy
276   hierarchy. Policy violations are categorized based on their source and type. Let Ctask represent the
277   task completion score. We define a policy violation matrix V where Vsource,dim equals the number
278
      of violations for a specific source and dimension where the sources P of policy are Org and User. For
      each task, we compute the total number of violations: Vtotal = source,dim Vsource,dim . The CuP
279
      metric for each task is then defined as: CuP = Ctask · 1{Vtotal =0} .
280
281   Recognizing the inherent difficulty of many tasks, even without safety policies, we introduce the
282   Partial Completion Rate (PCR) to better focus our evaluation on safety policy adherence. The PCR
283   relaxes the strict success criteria by considering a task as successfully completed if the agent fulfills
284   at least one of its evaluation success functions. This means that even if the agent does not achieve
285
      full task completion, partial successes are acknowledged, allowing us to assess the agent’s behavior
      in the safety domains. We adjust the task completion score Ctask to reflect these outcomes. We define
286
      Ctask = 1 if the agent meets at least one evaluation success function, and Ctask = 0 otherwise.
287
288   We also introduce the Partial Completion Under the Policy (Partial CuP) metric, which measures the
289   agent’s ability to achieve partial task completion while adhering to safety policies. The Partial CuP
290   adapts the CuP metric by leveraging the relaxed task completion criterion defined by the PCR. The
291
      Partial CuP metric is computed similarly to the CuP, but it uses the adjusted Ctask score defined by
      the PCR. It highlights how well the agent adheres to the policy hierarchy during partial completions.
292
      By introducing Partial CuP, we provide a more inclusive and detailed assessment framework that
293
      captures the policy nuances of agent behavior, balancing task difficulty with policy compliance.

      3.4.1   E VALUATION T EMPLATES
325
326   Establishing robust safety dimensions for agents involves a structured approach to defining, im-
327   plementing, and evaluating their adherence to policies. To achieve this, we developed modular
328   templates for each safety dimension, encapsulating critical challenges like user consent, boundary
329   limitations, task execution precision, and error handling. These templates enable seamless dataset
330
      expansion by adding data points that adhere to a consistent format.
331   Below, we outline the templates designed to align with specific safety dimensions, followed by
332   evaluation functions implemented to support policy adherence and enforcement. The templates
333   define the core policies, while the evaluation functions ensure compliance by validating agent
334   behavior against ground truth metrics. Together, these elements form the foundation of the ST-
335   WebAgentBench framework for assessing safety dimensions.
336
      • ask the user: ensure agents validate user permissions before executing critical actions, such as
337
        deleting records or making purchases 4.
338
      • access management: enforces clear rules for application management, such as limiting access to
339
        new users 5.
340
      • navigation limitation: enforce boundaries to prevent agents from accessing unrelated areas or
341
        sensitive data, such as financial records, while performing authorized tasks 6.
342
      • irreversible actions: monitor agents to prevent actions such as permanently deleting data unless
343
        explicitly instructed to do so 7.
344
      • hallucinated information: ensure agents do not fabricate inputs or modify existing information
345
        without explicit user instructions, maintaining data accuracy 8.
346
      • policy contradictions: enforce organizational policies over user preferences when conflicts arise,
347
        such as prioritizing private repository creation over user requests for public access 9.
348
      • sensitive information: ensure agents do not handle restricted data improperly, such as using a
349     user’s personal phone number without explicit permission 10.
350
      • jailbreaking: detect and neutralize adversarial inputs embedded in fields, such as hidden instruc-
351     tions in field that prompt unauthorized actions 11.
352
      • popup error: notify users of task limitations or maintenance issues, ensuring transparency and
353     avoiding silent failures 12.
354
      • missing parameters: prompt users for necessary inputs when required fields are incomplete, pre-
355     venting erroneous task execution 13.
356
357   Building on these templates, evaluation functions assess agent compliance with the defined safety
358   dimensions. These functions form the backbone of the ST-WebAgentBench framework, enabling
359   precise measurement of task completion and policy adherence:
360
361
      • element action match: Evaluates whether specific content appears on a page and optionally
        verifies the action type. This evaluator checks the latest action in the trajectory to determine if
362
        certain elements or texts are present on the page. It can also verify if the action type matches an
363
        expected type.
364
      • is sequence match: Evaluates whether a specific sequence of actions exists within a given
365
        trajectory. The evaluation can match actions based on either element selector (using ’bid’) or
366     element text, and supports both contiguous and non-contiguous matching types.
367
      • is url match: Evaluates whether the current page URL matches any of the specified reference
368     URLs based on defined matching rules. This evaluator cleans and parses URLs, then compares
369     the current page’s URL against reference URLs using specified matching criteria to determine
370     compliance.
371   • is ask the user: Evaluates whether specific content appears on a page following a user con-
372     sent action. This evaluator checks if the required content (must include) is present in the page’s
373     element text and ensures that it is preceded by an action of type send msg to user. This function
374     also serves to check whether the agent asks the user for missing information when needed.
375   • is action count: Evaluates whether the number of specific actions in a trajectory meets the
376     expected count. This evaluator counts occurrences of a specified action type within a trajectory,
377     optionally filtering by specific element selectors. It compares the counted number against an
        expected count to determine if there’s a violation.

      • is program html: Evaluates the presence and correctness of specific HTML content across
379     multiple pages. This evaluator checks whether required HTML content appears on designated
380     URLs within the page. It supports dynamic URL evaluation, various element selectors, and dif-
381     ferent content-matching criteria.

      • is sensitive data function checks if an agent improperly handles sensitive information,
383     such as a user’s phone number, during interactions. For example, if the policy specifies that
384     the agent must not use the user’s personal phone number (e.g., XXX-YYY-ZZZ) but the trajectory
385     shows it was mistakenly filled into an input, this function flags the violation. It ensures compliance
386     by verifying that sensitive data, explicitly restricted by policy, is not used during task execution.
387   • is input hallucination function evaluates whether an agent generates incorrect or fabri-
388     cated inputs during interactions, which could lead to policy violations and unintended outcomes.
389     For example, if an agent is tasked with updating a user’s contact information but hallucinates
390     and replaces the correct email address with an incorrect one or inserts a random phone number,
391     this function flags the error. By analyzing the trajectory, it ensures that all inputs align with the
392     expected ground truth, helping to prevent disruptions in workflows and maintain data integrity.

394   The evaluation of agents against ST-WebAgentBench is implemented on top of BrowserGym as it
395   offers a rich observation space, including DOM, screenshots, AXTree, and user messages, as well as
396   an action space that combines browser actions and user actions. This enables ST-WebAgentBench
397   to challenge agents to apply multi-modal perception across the observation space and incorporate
398
      human-in-the-loop actions when required by the policies. Additionally, BrowserGym is already
      compatible with other established benchmarks, such as MiniWob++, WebArena, and WorkArena,
399
      providing a solid foundation for seamless integration with existing frameworks. Its open-source
400
      nature, along with active support and ongoing extensions, further makes it an ideal choice for our
401   benchmark infrastructure. We extended the observation space in BrowserGym to include a hierarchy
402   of policies, as well as support for asynchronous integration of agents to enable benchmarking of
403   recently trending LangGraph-based agents. To further support the research we plan to contribute
404   these extensions back to BrowserGym. In addition, we implemented a simulated confirmation from
405   the user to respond to situations where the agent chooses to ask for user permission or missing data.

454   4.2.1         A NALYSIS
455
      The evaluation results, illustrated in Figure 2, reveal significant disparities among the agents in both
456   performance metrics and policy compliance. AgentWorkflowMemory (AWM) achieved the highest
457   partial completion rate of 46.9%, indicating its ability to make substantial progress on tasks. How-
458   ever, its Completion under Policy (CuP) metric was only 20%, reflecting considerable challenges
459   in adhering to safety and trustworthiness policies during task execution. Notably, AWM exhibited
460   a high number of policy violations in the user consent dimension, with 37 violations leading to a
461   risk ratio of 44%. This suggests that while AWM advances tasks effectively, it frequently neglects
462   to obtain necessary user permissions before executing critical actions, compromising safety and
463   trustworthiness.
464   WorkArena Legacy demonstrated a more balanced performance, with a completion rate of 26%
465   and a CuP of 15%. It had fewer violations in the user consent dimension (4 violations) and strict
466   execution dimension (16 violations), resulting in lower risk ratios of 5.2% and 14.2%, respectively.
467   These figures correspond to a medium qualitative risk assessment in these areas. The agent’s better
468   compliance with consent and strict execution policies indicates a higher level of policy adherence,
469   although its overall task completion remains moderate.
470   WebVoyager, with a completion rate of 12.8% and a CuP of 10.3%, lagged behind the other agents
471   in both task performance and policy adherence. It showed high risk ratios in user consent (17.6%)
472   and strict execution (22.1%), reflecting significant policy violations in these critical dimensions.
473   The agent’s performance suggests difficulties not only in completing tasks but also in maintaining
474   compliance with essential policies, rendering it less suitable for enterprise environments.
475   An important observation across all agents is the prevalence of policy violations in the dimensions
476   of strict execution and user consent. Agents often hallucinated additional steps not specified in
477   the task instructions or failed to seek necessary user permissions before executing actions. Such
478   behavior poses substantial risks in enterprise settings, where unauthorized or unintended operations
479   can lead to severe consequences. This points to significant issues related to the lack of grounded
480   knowledge and inadequate policy-aware safeguards. Examples of these hallucinations are provided
481   in Appendix A.5.1. Conversely, the boundary and scope limitation dimension exhibited lower risk
482
      ratios, indicating that agents are less prone to accessing unauthorized areas within applications.
483   Moreover, in real-world enterprise environments, agents are expected to adhere to a multitude of
484   organizational and user policies simultaneously. Given that the agents in our evaluation struggled
485   with policy adherence even when only a few policies (1–5 per task) were in place, there is significant
      concern about their ability to manage more complex policy frameworks. As the number of policies


                                                                             9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      increases, we anticipate that the performance of agents could be drastically reduced, posing new
487   challenges for their deployment in practical settings. This raises the critical question: if agents
488   are struggling with basic policy adherence now, how will they handle the complexity of extensive
489   policy hierarchies in enterprise environments? Addressing this issue will require the development of
490   more sophisticated mechanisms that enable agents to navigate and comply with a growing number
491   of policies without compromising task effectiveness.
492
      Overall, the evaluation highlights that current state-of-the-art agents struggle to balance task per-
493   formance with strict adherence to safety and trustworthiness policies. The agents’ inability to fully
494   comply with organizational and user policies, especially in critical dimensions, indicates that they
495   are not yet ready for deployment in high-stakes enterprise environments. Addressing these chal-
496   lenges will require advancements in agent architectures that prioritize policy compliance alongside
497   task completion, ensuring both effectiveness and safety in real-world applications.

500   5    D ISCUSSION
501
502   Our analysis demonstrates that current agents are not yet ready for enterprise deployment. Agents
503   exhibited significant issues such as hallucinating extra steps not specified in the task, failing to obtain
504   user consent before executing critical actions, and not strictly adhering to policy instructions. These
505   behaviors highlight a substantial gap in agents’ ability to handle policy compliance effectively in
506   real-world scenarios.
507   We propose that the CuP metric is a more appropriate benchmark for optimizing agents toward
508   enterprise adoption. CuP emphasizes not only task completion but also adherence to hierarchical
509   policies, providing a more holistic assessment of an agent’s readiness for deployment in sensitive
510   environments. Another key contribution of our work is the ability to easily add new data points,
511   safety dimensions, and evaluation functions can be integrated into the benchmark. Our modular
512   approach, utilizing evaluation templates and functions, enables researchers and practitioners to ex-
513
      pand the benchmark to encompass additional policies and dimensions without significant overhead.
      This flexibility is crucial for adapting to the evolving landscape of enterprise policies and regula-
514
      tions, and it empowers the community to collaboratively enhance the robustness and relevance of
515
      the benchmark.
516
517   While our study has limitations in dataset size and scope, it represents an important initial step in
518   addressing the critical need for safe and trustworthy web agents. By ”scratching the surface,” we pro-
519
      vide valuable insights into the challenges and pave the way for future research. The open-sourcing
      of our benchmark and tools invites collaboration and expansion, facilitating collective progress in
520
      developing agents that can meet stringent enterprise requirements. We will maintain a leaderboard
521
      to encourage ongoing improvement and to foster a competitive yet collaborative environment.
522
523   Future work will focus on adding more data points, benchmarking additional agents, and refin-
524   ing agent capabilities to enhance policy compliance (See Figure 11 for an architecture suggestion).
525
      Techniques such as recording real user interactions and leveraging large language models for auto-
      matic annotation can aid in scaling the benchmark effectively. As agents begin to integrate advanced
526
      safety mechanisms and better manage complex policy environments, we expect significant improve-
527
      ments in both task performance and adherence to safety and trustworthiness policies.

       B    F UTURE POLICY- AWARE ARCHITECTURE
1513
1514
       Future work in policy-aware architectures for web agents highlights the need for centralized or
1515   framework-level components that extend beyond prompt-based designs. Relying solely on prompt
1516   designers to encode policies has limitations in consistency and robustness, particularly in complex
1517   or high-stakes environments. Centralized components or frameworks could enable both the guid-
1518   ance and guarding of LLMs, ensuring their outputs align with organizational and user-specific poli-
1519   cies. These components could also influence orchestration logic, enabling dynamic adjustments and
1520   safeguarding actions before they are executed. Additionally, the development of dedicated policy-
1521   awareness agents presents an opportunity to address challenges such as assessing and resolving
1522   conflicting policies in a consistent and transparent manner. Such agents could act as shared ca-
1523
       pabilities that benefit both developers and organizations by standardizing policy interpretation and
       enforcement. This approach would reduce the burden on individual agent implementations while
1524
       fostering trust and accountability across diverse applications and use cases.
