                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Multiple Choice Question Answering (MCQA) is an important problem with nu-
012            merous real-world applications, such as medicine, law, and education. The high
013            cost of building MCQA datasets makes few-shot learning pivotal in this domain.
014            While Large Language Models (LLMs) can enable few-shot learning, their direct
015            application in real-world scenarios is often hindered by their high computational
016            cost. To address this challenge, we propose a simple yet effective approach that
017            uses LLMs for data generation and scoring. Our approach utilizes LLMs to cre-
018            ate MCQA data which contains questions and choices, and to assign probability
019            scores to the generated choices. We then use the generated data and LLM-assigned
020
               scores to finetune a smaller and more efficient encoder-only model, DeBERTa-v3-
               base by leveraging distillation loss. Extensive experiments on the Massive Multi-
021
               task Language Understanding (MMLU) benchmark demonstrate that our method
022
               improves accuracy from 28.9% to 39.3%, representing a gain of over 10% com-
023            pared to a baseline finetuned directly on 5-shot examples. This shows the effec-
024            tiveness of LLM-driven data generation and knowledge distillation for few-shot
025            MCQA.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Multiple Choice Question Answering (MCQA) is a crucial task in natural language understanding
031   with wide applications across domains like medicine (Jin et al., 2021), law (Zheng et al., 2021),
032   and education (Liang et al., 2018). While transformer encoders offer high performance (Vaswani,
033
      2017; Devlin, 2018; Liu, 2019), they typically need a large amount of labeled data to achieve good
      performance, which can be both expensive and difficult to get (Welbl et al., 2017; Yu et al., 2024).
034
      While large language models (LLMs) perform well in few-shot learning (Brown, 2020; Ouyang
035
      et al., 2022), their large size makes them difficult to use in real-world scenarios with limited re-
036   sources. This work addresses the critical need for efficient few-shot MCQA methods by exploring
037   the potential of LLMs to generate training data for more compact encoder-only models.
038
039
      Despite their few-shot learning capabilities (Brown, 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022), the growing size
      of LLMs, including powerful open-source variants (Touvron et al., 2023; Team et al., 2024), makes
040
      their direct deployment for MCQA costly and impractical in many real-world settings. This moti-
041
      vates the exploration of LLMs not just as tools for answering questions directly, but for generating
042   high-quality, task-specific datasets, as demonstrated in recent studies on classification (Chung et al.,
043   2023) and instruction tuning (Li et al., 2023). While leveraging LLM-generated data for train-
044   ing smaller models shows promise, it often leads to suboptimal performance. This highlights the
045   importance of developing methods that can effectively leverage LLM-generated data to boost the
046   performance of more efficient encoder-only models.
047
      To address the challenges of few-shot MCQA, we propose a novel framework that leverages the
048   strengths of both LLMs and efficient encoder-only models. Our approach begins by generating syn-
049   thetic MCQA datasets using an open-source LLM, promoting reproducibility and reducing reliance
050   on costly labeled data. We explore two distinct data generation strategies: (1) direct generation in
051   JSON format and (2) a decomposed approach that separates question, positive answer, and negative
052   answer generation, offering flexibility and avoiding format-specific parsing issues. Critically, we
053   further enhance the student encoder-only model by employing LLM-based distillation. Specifically,
      we use the LLM to give a probability score to the generated answer choices, providing soft labels

054
      that are incorporated into the student model’s training through a distillation loss. This combined ap-
055   proach offers a simple, yet effective solution for achieving strong performance in few-shot MCQA
056   scenarios.
057
058
      Extensive experiments on the Massive Multitask Language Understanding (MMLU) benchmark
      demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed framework. Our approach significantly boosts the
059
      performance of an encoder-only baseline model, DeBERTa-base-v3 trained with only a 5-shot ex-
060
      amples, achieving a 10.4 absolute improvement in accuracy, from 28.9% to 39.3%. Remarkably,
061   applying LLM distillation enables DeBERTa-base-v3 to surpass the few-shot MCQA performance
062   of significantly larger models on the MMLU benchmark. These larger models include the LLaMA-
063   7B base, which achieved 35.1% accuracy, and Flan-T5-250M, which reached 35.9% accuracy after
064   being fine-tuned on a massive multi-task dataset. This highlights the potential of our method to
065   achieve strong MCQA performance with smaller, and more efficient models.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
068
069
      Multiple Choice Question Answering (MCQA) Data Generation. Generating synthetic data for
070   MCQA has been explored previously (Singh Bhatia et al., 2013; Araki et al., 2016), often relying
071   on external resources like Wikipedia (Rodriguez-Torrealba et al., 2022) or knowledge graphs (Yu
072   et al., 2024). While recent work has investigated using LLMs for zero-shot MCQA data genera-
073   tion (Cheung et al., 2023), these approaches typically involve human supervision to ensure quality,
074   limiting the scalability of data creation (Kıyak & Emekli, 2024). In contrast, our work focuses on
075   leveraging LLMs to generate large-scale MCQA datasets automatically, with the aim of distilling
076   their knowledge into efficient encoder-only models for few-shot learning.
077   Few-Shot Multiple Choice Question Answering (MCQA). Few-Shot MCQA remains a challeng-
078   ing problem, as achieving strong performance often requires large, computationally expensive lan-
079   guage models (Anil et al., 2023b; Touvron et al., 2023; Achiam et al., 2023; Anil et al., 2023a).
080   While efficient encoder-only models have shown promise (Sileo, 2024), they typically rely on exten-
081   sive multi-task training with hundreds of datasets. However, acquiring large-scale MCQA datasets
082   can be costly and time-consuming (Welbl et al., 2017; Yu et al., 2024). In this work, we aim to
083   enable effective few-shot MCQA with encoder-only models by leveraging LLM-generated data and
084
      knowledge distillation, addressing the limitations of both data scarcity and computational cost.
085   LLM Distillation. LLM distillation aims to transfer knowledge from large language models into
086   smaller, more efficient ones (Hinton et al., 2015; Xu et al., 2024). A common approach involves
087   generating training data with LLMs and then fine-tuning smaller models on this data. This approach
088   has proven successful in various tasks like classification (Chung et al., 2023), instruction following
089   (Li et al., 2023), and more (Chen et al., 2022; Yehudai et al., 2024; Long et al., 2024). However, most
090
      research focuses on distilling into smaller but similar language models (Gu et al., 2024), primarily
      by creating synthetic datasets (Kim & Rush, 2016; Agarwal et al., 2024). Directly distilling LLM
091
      representations is challenging (Xu et al., 2024), and distilling into different model architectures,
092
      such as encoder-only models, remains largely unexplored. This gap is particularly pronounced in
093   the context of few-shot MCQA, where the potential of distilling LLMs into encoder-only models re-
094   mains largely unexplored. While some work has investigated LLM distillation for other tasks, such
095   as semantic search (Liao et al., 2024), to our knowledge our work is the first to systematically ex-
096   plore a combined approach of data generation and probability score-based distillation for enhancing
097   encoder-only models specifically for few-shot MCQA.

      Our method addresses few-shot MCQA by leveraging the power of LLMs to generate synthetic
102   training data and then distilling their knowledge into a smaller, more efficient encoder-only model,
103   such as DeBERTa. An overview of our method can be seen in Figure 1. We first generate an MCQA
104   dataset and obtain probability scores for each answer choice using the LLM. These scores serve as
105   soft targets to guide the training of the encoder model, which learns from both the generated data
106   and the distilled LLM knowledge through distillation losses. This approach enables us to enhance
107   the performance of the encoder model in few-shot scenarios while reducing the computational cost
      associated with deploying compute-intensive LLMs.

123   3.1     LLM DATA GENERATION FOR MCQA
124
      Generating high-quality training data is crucial for effective few-shot MCQA. In this subsection,
125
      we explore two distinct strategies for leveraging LLMs to create synthetic MCQA datasets: (1)
126
      direct generation in JSON format, and (2) a decomposed approach that separates question, positive
127   answer, and negative answer generation. While the direct JSON approach can potentially yield
128   higher-quality data when successful, it can also suffer from parsing issues that reduce the amount
129   of usable data. The decomposed approach, however, avoids the potential parsing issues associated
130   with the JSON method by generating data in a simpler, unstructured format. We detail both methods
131   below and empirically evaluate their impact on the student model’s performance in Section 4. We
132   also include all the prompts we used in Appendix E.
133
134   3.1.1    JSON
135
136
      In our first approach, we attempt to directly generate MCQA data in JSON format using few-shot
      examples. The JSON structure includes the question (string), choices (array of strings), and the
137
      answer (integer representing the index of the correct choice). This format implicitly requires the
138
      LLM to generate the question first, followed by the answer choices, and finally, the index of the
139   correct answer. However, our experiments reveal that this structured generation process can be
140   challenging for LLMs. They may not consistently adhere to the strict JSON format, leading to
141   parsing errors and a reduction in the amount of usable data. To address this limitation, we propose a
142   decomposed generation method that bypasses the need for parsing JSON output.
143
144   3.1.2    D ECOMPOSE
145
146   Our second approach termed the decomposed generation method, breaks down the MCQA data
147
      generation process into three distinct stages: question generation, positive answer generation, and
      negative answer generation. For each stage, we utilize a few-shot dataset containing questions,
148
      positive answers, negative answers, and relevant topics. This decomposition eliminates the need for
149
      complex parsing of LLM output, which can be prone to errors when enforcing structured formats
150   like JSON. While this approach might potentially lead to a slight decrease in individual data point
151   quality, it significantly reduces data loss due to parsing failures, ultimately yielding a larger volume
152   of usable training data. For simplicity, we focus on generating data within a single topic, such as
153   high school programming or abstract algebra, ensuring readily available background information.
154   We leverage the few-shot examples and topic information to guide the LLM in generating new
155   MCQA instances.
156   Question Generation. The first stage of the decomposed generation method focuses on creating
157   new questions. We prompt the LLM with instructions like ”Create a question about {topic}!”, where
158   {topic} is replaced with the chosen subject (e.g., high school programming). To guide the LLM and
159   ensure the generated questions are relevant and similar in style to the target domain, we provide
160   a few-shot prompt consisting of examples randomly sampled from the few-shot dataset. We also
161   adjust the LLM’s temperature parameter during this stage to encourage diversity in the generated
      questions and prevent overfitting to the provided examples.

      positive answer generation. The second stage focuses on generating the correct answers (positive
163   examples) for the questions created in the previous stage. Similar to question generation, we employ
164   few-shot prompting to guide the LLM. We provide examples of questions and their corresponding
165   correct answers from the few-shot dataset. Then, we present the newly generated questions to the
166   LLM, prompting it to generate relevant and accurate positive answers based on the provided context
167   and examples.
168
      negative answer generation. The final stage of data generation focuses on creating plausible but
169   incorrect answer choices (negative examples) for each question. We use few-shot prompting to
170   guide the generation process. To ensure diversity, we generate N negative examples sequentially for
171   each question, prompting the LLM in each iteration to produce a distinct answer, considering all
172   previously generated ones. This iterative approach helps create a diverse set of negative examples
173   for each MCQA instance.

236   4       E XPERIMENTS
237
238   We conduct experiments to evaluate the effectiveness of our proposed framework for few-shot
239   MCQA. We use a dataset consisting of only 5 MCQA examples covering the same topic, employing
240   Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct 1 as the LLM for data generation and scoring, and DeBERTa-base-v3 (184M
241   parameters) 2 as the efficient encoder-only student model. We chose DeBERTa-base-v3 due to its
242   strong performance and relatively small size, making it suitable for resource-constrained scenarios.
243   We train the DeBERTa-base-v3 model for 500 iterations with a learning rate of 1e-5, using a batch
244   size of 4 and gradient accumulation for 2 steps, which is equal to using a batch size of 8. For
245   the decompose generation method we set the number of negative examples to be 5 for all exper-
246   iments, except explicitly mentioned. We average the results across 5 different random seeds for
247   all experiments. Unless otherwise specified, we generate 1024 MCQA examples from the initial
248   5-shot dataset for training using the temperature of 2. We first evaluate our approach on the MMLU
249   benchmark in Section 4.1 and then conduct an ablation study on the ARC datasets in Section 4.2 to
250
      analyze the impact of different components of our method.

252   4.1     MMLU BENCHMARK
253
254   We evaluate our approach on the Massive Multitask Language Understanding (MMLU) benchmark
255   (Hendrycks et al., 2020), a widely used benchmark for assessing few-shot MCQA performance
256   in LLMs. MMLU comprises 57 datasets covering diverse topics, each divided into development
257   (dev), validation, and test splits. We utilize only the 5-shot dev set for data generation in all our
258   experiments.
259   On the MMLU benchmark, we evaluate both the JSON and decomposed data generation methods,
260   both with and without knowledge distillation from the LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct model (Dubey et al.,
261   2024). Our evaluation includes comparisons against a range of baselines, including the LLaMA-3.1-
262   8B-Instruct teacher model itself, smaller LLMs like LLaMA-7B (Touvron et al., 2023) and Gemma-
263   2-2B-it (Team et al., 2024) 3 , the encoder-decoder model Flan-T5-base (Chung et al., 2024), and a
264   strong encoder-only model, Tasksource DeBERTa-base 4 , which was fine-tuned on a large multi-task
265   dataset (Sileo, 2024).

      Table 1 presents the few-shot MCQA results on the MMLU benchmark. As expected, directly
271   training DeBERTa-base-v3 on only 5 examples yields near-random accuracy because of overfit-
272   ting. Using LLM-generated data significantly improves performance, with the decomposed and
273   JSON methods achieving average gains of 4.2 and 4.3 points, respectively. However, incorporating
274   LLM-generated soft labels via distillation leads to even more substantial improvements, boosting
275   accuracy by an additional 5.3 points for the decomposed method and 6 points for the JSON method.
276   This suggests that while LLMs may generate some incorrect answers during data creation, the distil-
277   lation process allows them to effectively relabel these instances, leading to a more accurate training
278   signal for the student model. This observation aligns with findings in (Robinson & Wingate, 2023),
279
      which demonstrate that framing answer generation as a multiple-choice task can enhance LLM per-
      formance.
280
281   Our distilled DeBERTa-base-v3 model with 184M parameters achieves encouraging results. It ap-
282   proaches the performance of significantly larger models like LLaMA-7B which is over 30 times
283   larger and Flan-T5-250M, which was extensively fine-tuned on a multi-task dataset. While our
284   method does not yet reach the level of instruction-tuned LLMs like Gemma-2-2B-it and the teacher
285
      model LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct (as expected, given their substantial size, pretraining data, and in-
      struction tuning), these findings highlight the potential of our approach to achieve strong perfor-
286
      mance with smaller, more efficient models, making it particularly attractive for resource-constrained
287
      settings.
288
289   Although our method currently lags behind the Tasksource DeBERTa-base model, which was trained
290   on a massive multi-task dataset including MMLU, our data generation and distillation techniques
291
      hold the potential to further boost its performance. Fine-tuning Tasksource DeBERTa-base with our
      JSON-generated data and distillation results in a 0.5-point average improvement. Interestingly, fine-
292
      tuning with data from the decomposed method leads to a performance decrease, indicating that the
293
      pre-trained Tasksource model may be more sensitive to data quality and favors the higher-quality
294   data generated by the JSON approach, which benefits from an implicit filtering mechanism.
295
296   Considering that our approach generates only 1,024 training instances from a mere 5 initial exam-
297
      ples, the observed performance gains suggest that our method effectively distills knowledge from
      the significantly larger LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct model into the smaller DeBERTa-base-v3. While
298
      these results are promising, they also highlight opportunities for further research and improvement,
299
      such as exploring more advanced data generation and distillation techniques to further bridge the
300   gap with state-of-the-art models.

303   4.2     A BLATION S TUDY
304
305   In this section, we conduct an ablation study on the ARC-easy and ARC-Challenge benchmarks
306   (Clark et al., 2018) to analyze the impact of different components of our proposed method. We use
307   a 5-shot learning setup, randomly selecting 5 examples from the training set to generate 1024 data
308   points, which are then scored using LLaMA 3.1-8B-Instruct. We train DeBERTa-base-v3 models
309   on the generated data and scores.
310   We investigate several key aspects. First, we examine the effect of the number of generated data
311   points, as detailed in Section 4.2.1. Second, in Section 4.2.2, we analyze the impact of using smaller
312   LLMs for data generation and scoring. This section also includes a comparison with a paraphrasing
313   baseline. Finally, we explore the influence of the temperature hyperparameter during data generation
314   in Section 4.2.3.

      4.2.3    E FFECT OF G ENERATION H YPERPARAMETERS
410
411
      We now analyze the influence of the temperature hyperparameter, which controls the diversity of
      the generated data, on the performance of our approach. Table 3 presents the results for both the
412
      JSON and decomposed generation methods across different temperature settings. We observe that
413
      temperature plays a crucial role, and increasing it generally leads to improved performance. This
414   highlights the importance of data diversity for effective few-shot MCQA, demonstrating that even
415   simple techniques like temperature control can significantly impact the quality of the generated data.
416
417
      While increasing the temperature doesn’t always consistently improve performance when using only
      the generated data, the benefits become much more pronounced when combined with distillation
418
      loss. We hypothesize that this is because LLMs can introduce noise into the generated data, and dis-
419
      tillation helps mitigate this noise by encouraging the student model to learn a smoother probability
420   distribution over the answer choices, similar to label smoothing, which has been shown to improve
421   robustness to noisy labels (Szegedy et al., 2016; Lukasik et al., 2020). To further investigate this,
422   we experimented with replacing the soft labels from the LLM with hard labels (choosing the most
423   probable answer) but observed inferior performance compared to using the full probability distribu-
424   tion, we provide the results in Appendix C.2. This highlights the importance of leveraging the soft
425   labels provided by the LLM for effective knowledge distillation.
426   While the JSON generation method can yield better performance at higher temperatures, it often
427   comes at the cost of a lower usable data rate due to parsing errors. Many generated instances must be
428   discarded because they don’t adhere to the strict JSON format. In contrast, the decomposed method
429   consistently achieves competitive performance without requiring any parsing. Even when reducing
430   the temperature for JSON generation to 1 to improve the parsing success rate, its performance still
431   falls short of the decomposed method. This demonstrates that the decomposed approach offers a
      more robust and efficient alternative.

438   5   L IMITATIONS
439
440
441   Our work relies on a robust, instruction-tuned LLM, which is currently readily available in English
442   but might be less accessible in other languages. This language dependence, coupled with the re-
443
      liance on strong LLM capabilities, could limit the generalizability of our method to scenarios where
      suitable LLMs are unavailable or less powerful.
444
445   Despite significant improvements over the naive 5-shot baseline, our method still exhibits a sub-
446   stantial performance gap compared to models trained with extensive data and multi-task learning, as
447   well as the teacher LLM itself. Bridging this gap by exploring more advanced data generation tech-
448   niques, incorporating diverse knowledge sources, or developing more effective distillation strategies
449
      remains a promising direction for future research.
450   Another limitation of our approach is the potential for bias in the LLM-generated data. LLMs
451   are trained on massive text corpora, which inevitably contain societal biases. These biases can be
452   reflected in the generated questions and choices, potentially leading to a biased downstream encoder
453   model. This inherited bias could result in unfair or discriminatory outcomes when the model is
454   deployed in real-world applications. Mitigating this bias is a crucial area for future work.
455   A further limitation is that our current work focuses on MCQA tasks with relatively short question-
456   and-answer contexts, which are easier for current LLMs to generate and score effectively. We ob-
457   served increased noise in the generated data when dealing with longer contexts, evidenced by a
458   performance degradation when fine-tuning the Tasksource DeBERTa-base model on generated data
459   for longer-context MMLU tasks. This suggests that generalizing our approach to tasks involving
460   longer contexts will require further research.

463   6   C ONCLUSION
464
465   This work demonstrates the effectiveness of leveraging LLMs for both data generation and
466   probability-based distillation to enable strong few-shot MCQA performance in smaller, more ef-
467   ficient encoder-only models. Our approach achieves encouraging results on the MMLU benchmark,
468   even approaching the performance of significantly larger models like LLaMA-7B and Flan-T5-
469   250M, which benefited from more extensive training data and different training objectives. This
470   highlights the potential of our method to achieve strong performance with more compact and com-
471   putationally efficient models. However, a performance gap remains compared to models trained
472   with large-scale multi-task data, suggesting opportunities for further improvement. Future work
473
      will focus on bridging this gap by exploring more advanced data filtering techniques to enhance the
      quality of the generated data and investigating novel distillation strategies to maximize knowledge
474
      transfer from LLMs to smaller models. Additionally, extending our approach to effectively handle
475
      longer-context MCQA tasks is a crucial direction for future research.

      A     F UTURE W ORKS
649
650
      This work lays the foundation for several promising research directions, with the potential to sig-
651   nificantly advance efficient few-shot learning in multiple choice question answering and beyond.
652   Specifically, we identify the following key areas for future exploration:
653
654
      Advanced Distillation Techniques. In this work, we used a simple distillation approach to estab-
      lish a clear baseline. Exploring more sophisticated distillation techniques, such as sequence-level
655
      knowledge distillation, attention-based distillation, or other distillation approach, that could further
656
      enhance performance.
657
658   Benchmark Dataset Creation. Our findings suggest that the JSON generation method coupled
659   with LLM distillation is a promising approach for creating high-quality MCQA data. This method
660
      appears to act as an effective filter for selecting higher-quality generated examples. Combining
      our approach with automated quality filtering based on perplexity or LLM-based scoring, post-
661
      processing techniques to refine generated text, and retrieval-augmented generation to incorporate
662
      external knowledge could facilitate the creation of valuable benchmark datasets for few-shot MCQA.
663   This would require developing robust filtering and evaluation metrics to ensure the quality and di-
664   versity of the generated datasets.
665
666
      Improving Decomposed Generation. While the decomposed generation method offers advantages
      in terms of data generation efficiency, it can produce noisy data due to longer and less structured
667
      answers. Investigating more sophisticated prompting techniques could mitigate this limitation. In-
668
      corporating constraints into the prompts, specifying the desired length or format of the answers,
669   could improve the quality of the generated data. Iterative refinement, where feedback is provided
670   to the LLM to revise its responses, is another promising avenue. Additionally, using more diverse
671   and representative examples in the few-shot prompts could guide the LLM towards generating more
672   appropriate answers.
673
      Applications Beyond MCQA. Our framework has broader applicability beyond MCQA. Within
674
      NLP, it could be applied to tasks like text classification, sequence tagging, or any task where efficient
675   few-shot learning is desirable. In these applications, the LLM could generate synthetic training
676   examples and provide soft labels or confidence scores to guide the training of a smaller model.
677   Furthermore, with the advancements in Vision-Language Models (VLLMs), our approach could be
678   extended to vision tasks. For example, in Visual Question Answering (VQA), the VLLM could
679   generate image captions, which could then be used to synthesize images with a generative model.
680   The VLLM could also generate the question and possible answers. The generated VQA data, along
681   with the VLLM’s confidence scores for each answer, can then be used to distill the knowledge into
682   a smaller, more efficient vision-language model or even a specialized VQA architecture.

      B.3   M ODEL TRAINING DETAILS
739
740   We train the DeBERTa-base-v3 model, which takes a question and a choice as input, for all our
741   experiments. The model uses the pooled output of the encoder, which is then fed into a linear layer
742   to produce a scalar output. We train the model using the Adam optimizer (Kingma, 2014) for 500
743   iterations, with a batch size of 4 and gradient accumulation for 2 steps (effectively a batch size of 8).
744   This allows the model to be exposed to approximately 4000 MCQA examples during training. We
745   use a learning rate of 1e-5.
746   Before training, we filter the dataset to avoid out-of-memory errors during training. We use the
747   DeBERTa tokenizer to count the number of tokens for the concatenation of each question and its
748   corresponding choices. If the total number of tokens for any question-choice pair exceeds a prede-
749   fined maximum (max tokens), we discard that data point. For most experiments, we set max tokens
750   to 320. However, for MMLU tasks with longer sequences, we increased max tokens to 480.

997    C.7   B INARY C LASS E XTENSIONS
998
999    In real-world applications like fact verification or information retrieval, it’s often necessary to de-
1000   termine the correctness of a given answer without explicitly presenting choices. This necessitates
1001   framing the problem as binary classification. To investigate our framework’s applicability to this
1002   setting, we consider two approaches. First, we train a model with binary cross-entropy (BCE) loss
1003   and sigmoid activation on the final layer, using data generated by the LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct using
1004   JSON format. Second, we use a simple heuristic approach. We train the model exactly as in the
1005   MCQA setting. During the evaluation, we search for a constant threshold using the same data by
1006
       averaging the logits produced by the model for each question-answer pair across all choices. At
       inference, if the logit for a pair is above the threshold, the pair is classified as correct.
1007
1008   For evaluation, we use the binary F1-score, as the number of correct and incorrect pairs is not
1009   balanced. Results are presented in Table 11. As expected, using only 5-shot examples performs
1010   poorly, while training on real binary data achieves good results. Interestingly, models trained in
1011   the MCQA setting and then classified using the heuristic approach outperform the models trained
1012
       directly with BCE loss on generated data. Furthermore, models trained with distillation and then
       classified using the heuristic demonstrate smaller variance and even outperform models trained on
1013
       real binary data on the ARC-Challenge dataset. We hypothesize that the heuristic, by leveraging
1014
       the full probability distribution learned during MCQA training, allows the model to develop a more
1015   nuanced representation of correctness.

       C.8                                           I S THE RESULTS ONLY FROM LEARNING MCQA FORMAT ?
1046
1047   A potential concern is that the performance gains observed with our method might stem solely from
1048   learning the structure and format of MCQA, rather than improving actual question-answering ability.
1049   To investigate this, we conducted the following cross-evaluation experiment. We used LLaMa-3.1-
1050   8B-Instruct to generate 1024 ARC Easy examples using the JSON generation method, along with
1051   corresponding LLM-generated scores for distillation. We then trained a DeBERTa-v3-base model
1052   on this generated ARC Easy data with distillation, using the same hyperparameters as our main
1053
       experiments. We compared its performance on MMLU with a model trained directly on MMLU-
       generated data with distillation and the 5-shot baseline. Results are shown in Table 12.
1054
1055   Training on the ARC Easy-generated data significantly improved performance over the 5-shot base-
1056   line. However, the model trained on MMLU-generated data performed significantly better, achieving
1057   an average accuracy of 39.3%, compared to 37.9% for the model trained on ARC Easy generated
1058   data. This gap suggests that our method is not merely teaching the model the MCQA format, but is
1059
       also enabling it to acquire task-specific knowledge relevant to the MMLU datasets. Therefore, we
       conclude that the improvements observed from our method stem from both an improved understand-
1060
       ing of the MCQA format and, crucially, an enhanced ability to answer questions within the specific
1061
       domains covered by MMLU
