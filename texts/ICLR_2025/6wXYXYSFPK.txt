011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Olfaction—how molecules are perceived as odors to humans—remains poorly un-
014            derstood. Recently, the principal odor map (POM) was introduced to digitize
015            the olfactory properties of single compounds. However, smells in real life are
016
               not pure single molecules, but complex mixtures of molecules, whose represen-
               tations remain relatively under-explored. In this work, we introduce POMM IX,
017
               an extension of the POM to represent mixtures. Our representation builds upon
018
               the symmetries of the problem space in a hierarchical manner: (1) graph neural
019            networks for building molecular embeddings, (2) attention mechanisms for ag-
020            gregating molecular representations into mixture representations, and (3) cosine
021            prediction heads to encode olfactory perceptual distance in the mixture embedding
022            space. POMM IX achieves state-of-the-art predictive performance across multiple
023            datasets. We also evaluate the generalizability of the representation on multiple
024            splits when applied to unseen molecules and mixture sizes. Our work advances
025            the effort to digitize olfaction, and highlights the synergy of domain expertise and
026            deep learning in crafting expressive representations in low-data regimes.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   A central challenge in neuroscience is deciphering the link between the physical properties of a stim-
032   ulus and its perceptual characteristics. While this relationship is well-defined for senses like vision
033
      (wavelength to color) and audition (frequency to pitch), it remains elusive for olfaction, a chemical
      sense, where the mapping from chemical structure to odor perception is complex and not fully un-
034
      derstood (Sell, 2006; Barwich & Lloyd, 2022; Barwich, 2022). A recent advance towards digitizing
035
      olfaction came with the introduction of the Principal Odor Map (POM) by Lee et al. (2023), a high-
036   dimensional, data-driven representation of odor perceptual space learned from molecular structures.
037   This model demonstrated human-level performance in predicting odor qualities of single molecules
038   and generalized well to other olfactory tasks. However, naturally occurring olfactory stimuli are not
039   comprised of single molecules, but rather complex mixtures of molecules, whose representations
040   remain relatively unexplored within the existing literature. This work introduces POMM IX—a
041   mixture and distance-aware extension of the POM representation.
042   A searchable, rankable, and optimizable digital representation of olfactory space has potential ap-
043   plications in diverse areas (Spence et al., 2017). Such a representation could be used to develop
044   mosquito repellents (Wei et al., 2024), inform agricultural practices by enabling targeted manipu-
045   lation of insect behavior (Conchou et al., 2019), improve food quality and reduce waste through
046   enhanced spoilage detection (Jung et al., 2023), and accelerate the design of novel fragrance and
047   flavor compounds, which is particularly valuable given increasing regulatory constraints on existing
048   ingredients (Demyttenaere, 2012; IFRA, 2024).
049   Deep learning models enable the construction of task-optimized data representations, learning com-
050   plex relationships directly from data (Bengio et al., 2012). However, in low-data regimes, the success
051   of deep learning hinges on incorporating appropriate inductive biases, effectively injecting domain-
052   specific knowledge to guide the learning process and improve generalization (Tom et al., 2023).
053   Olfactory data is currently in this regime—gathering olfactory data is expensive and labor-intensive
      as it requires training human panelists, filtering potentially toxic molecules, and navigating ethi-

      cal review boards. Furthermore, probing human perception is inherently complex, necessitating
055   rigorous data collection protocols and large sample sizes to mitigate individual biases. Existing
056   work on olfactory mixture modeling remains limited, employing a small pool of compounds with
057   low coverage of chemical space (Ravia et al., 2020; Snitz et al., 2019; Sisson et al., 2023; Snitz

061   mixture characterizations (blends and perfumes) exist within the industry, but remain inaccessible
062   behind private doors.

      POMM IX is built by training a neural network to tackle the mixture similarity problem by jointly
079
      training a POM with an attention-based mixture model to predict the perceptual similarity of mix-
080   tures. This approach also allows us to combine mono-molecular datasets (up to 5,000 data points)
081   and more limited mixture data sources (up to 1,000 data points).
082
083
      At each stage of our work, we take care to respect the natural symmetries of the problem space—
      namely, the permutation invariance of molecular descriptions (introduced by the graph representa-
084
      tion of a molecule), the permutation invariance of mixture compositions (the model should not care
085
      what order the mixture ingredients are presented), and the symmetry of mixture similarities (the
086   model should predict that the similarity of mixture 1 and mixture 2 is the same as the similarity for
087   mixture 2 and mixture 1). The end result is an extension of the POM to mixtures, and a new model
088   building block, dubbed C HE M IX, for encoding mixtures of molecules.

103   1.2   R ELATED W ORKS
104
105   The modeling of molecular structure-property relationships has a rich history. Within the olfactory
106   domain, previous contributions have utilized hand-picked expert descriptors with classical machine
107   learning algorithms (e.g. tree-based models, support vector machine, and linear models), and/or
      similarity measures (e.g., cosine, angle) (Snitz et al., 2013; Keller et al., 2017; Kowalewski & Ray,


                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      2020; Vigneau et al., 2018). More recently, deep learning based models have been actively explored
109   to create a more expressive molecular representation of olfactory space (Lee et al., 2023; Tran et al.,
110   2018; Zhang et al., 2024; Sisson, 2022; Maziarka et al., 2020).
111
112
      Deep learning techniques have been explored in modeling molecular structure-property relation-
      ships, including variational autoencoders (Gómez-Bombarelli et al., 2018; Oliveira et al., 2022),
113
      large language models (Chithrananda et al., 2020; Ross et al., 2022), and graph neural networks
114
      (GNNs) (Yang et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2021). Graph neural networks and graph attention networks
115   (GANs) (Heid et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2023; Buterez et al., 2024) in particular have shown state-
116   of-the-art performance in many molecular property prediction tasks including modeling olfactory
117   space.
118
      Olfactory mixture property prediction is a much more difficult task with fewer effective attempts
119
      (Lapid et al., 2008; Khan et al., 2007; Olsson, 1998; Dhurandhar et al., 2023; Ravia et al., 2020).
120
      Molecular mixtures have been studied before for battery electrolytes by Zhang et al. (2023). The
121   work, however, uses a large dataset (10,000 mixtures), and focuses on property prediction, rather
122   than mixture representation learning. To work in the low-data regime of our olfactory mixture
123   dataset, POMM IX uses pre-training techniques (Honda et al., 2019; Shoghi et al., 2023; Goh et al.,
124   2018) and designed inductive biases to improve the expressivity of the molecular representation and
125   attention mechanisms (Wang et al., 2019; Xiong et al., 2020; Maziarka et al., 2020).

130   2.1   DATA
131
132   We combine mono-molecular datasets and multi-molecular (mixture) datasets. Mono-molecular
133   datasets list a set of odor labels ("grassy", "fishy", etc.) for a single molecule, and the most ex-
134   haustive compilation is found in the GoodScents/Leffingwell (GS-LF) dataset (Barsainyan et al.,
135   2024). We further clean the GS-LF dataset by canonicalizing SMILES (Weininger, 1988) strings
136
      with RDK IT (Landrum et al., 2022), removing duplicate entries, removing inorganic, charged or
      multi-molecular (e.g. salts) entries, removing molecules with molecular weight < 20 and > 600,
137
      and small inorganic molecules. We further removed infrequently applied odor labels that appeared
138
      for fewer than 20 molecules and subsequently removed molecules with no remaining labels (see
139   Appendix A.1 for details on dataset cleaning).
140
141   Multi-molecular datasets were compiled from previous publications, hereby referred to as Snitz
142
      (Snitz et al., 2013) (containing data from Weiss et al. (2012)), Ravia (Ravia et al., 2020), and Bush-
      did (Bushdid et al., 2014). Data for each of these publications was obtained from pyrfume (Castro
143
      et al., 2022). In aggregate, we have 743 unique mixtures, containing between 1 to 43 unique molec-
144
      ular components (Figure 2a).
145
146   These mixtures are described by 865 pairwise mixture comparisons (Figure 2b) corresponding to
147   labels from two types of experiments:
148
149
             • Explicit similarity (Snitz, Ravia): Participants are asked to explicitly rate the perceptual
               similarity of two mixtures from 0 (completely similar) to 1 (completely different). The
150
               final similarity for a mixture pair is averaged across all participants.
151
             • Triangle discrimination (Bushdid): Participants are provided three mixtures, of which
152
               two are identical, and asked to identify which mixture was different. These results are
153
               aggregated for each mixture triplet, and the percentage of correct identifications is treated
154            as the label for the two unique mixtures in the triplet.
155
156   We note that the interpretation of the triangle discrimination task is congruent with the explicit
157   similarity task, as a score of "1.0" in a triangle discrimination task shows that all tested participants
158   could identify the mixture that was different, which meant that the two unique mixtures in the triplet
159   were very perceptually distinct. Thus, in an explicit similarity test, this pair of mixtures would
160   also have a score of "1.0". While these two tests are theoretically equivalent in their extremes (0 =
161   perfect discrimination, 1 = equal to chance), calibration of intermediate scores may differ. We did
      not attempt to correct for this effect.

197   Intensity-balancing is a subtle part of mixture preparation. The naïve approach to preparing mixtures
198
      would be to use an equimolar or equivolume blend of components, but this approach tends to produce
      mixtures that are dominated by their most potent component. Snitz, Ravia, and Bushdid are instead

203   balanced.

205   2.2   M ODELING
206
207   A schematic of the POMM IX model is provided in Figure 3. The POMM IX model can be divided
208   into three hierarchical components: (1) a mono-molecular GNN POM embedding model, (2) a
209   multi-molecular C HE M IX mixture attention model, and (3) a similarity scoring function.
210   The POM is a GNN which takes in molecular graphs derived from the SMILES representations of
211   molecules. Each graph, written as G = (U, V, E), consists of a special global vertex U connected
212   to all other vertices V , and a set of edges E. The global vertex U encodes overall properties of
213   the molecule and is initialized with 200 normalized RDK IT cheminformatics molecular descriptors
214   (Landrum et al., 2022) obtained from DESCRIPTASTORUS (Kelley et al., 2024). The atoms of the
215   molecules are the vertices (nodes), with node vectors V = {vi }Ni=1 for a molecule with Nv atoms,
                                                                        v

      where vi are 85-dimensional feature vectors encoding atomic properties. Covalent bonds between

249
      The POM GNN uses the G RAPH N ETS architecture (Battaglia et al., 2018), with message-passing
      blocks for the edge, node and global properties of the molecular graphs. The architecture is designed
250
      to be lightweight in order to avoid overfitting on the limited amounts of olfactory mixture data. Edge
251
      updates use feature-wise linear modulation (FiLM) layers (Perez et al., 2017; Brockschmidt, 2019),
252   while node updates use graph attention layers (Veličković et al., 2017; Brody et al., 2021) with self-
253   attention. The global embeddings are updated through principal neighborhood aggregation (PNA)
254   (Corso et al., 2020; Zaheer et al., 2017). The GNN is composed of four of these G RAPH N ET layers,
255   and the final global embedding serves as the POM embedding.
256
      The C HE M IX model processes a set of molecular POM embeddings, and generates an embedding
257
      representing the entire mixture. Mixtures are first represented by concatenating POM embeddings
258   of constituent molecules, and mixtures with fewer molecules are padded to the length of the largest
259   mixture. C HE M IX uses molecule-wise self-attention, where each molecule attends to all other
260   molecules, followed by PNA. This ensures invariance of the mixture embeddings in the permu-
261   tation of molecules within a given mixture. This model can be viewed as isomorphic to a GAN on
262   a fully-connected graph (Joshi, 2020), with each molecule as a node, and the mixture embedding is
263   the global embedding.
264   Finally, the distances between the mixture embeddings are obtained through a similarity score. For
265   this, we use a predictive head based on cosine distance (Koch et al., 2015), commonly used for
266   distance-aware high-dimensional learned representations. A final two-parameter linear layer is used
267   to encode for human bias and experimental noise present in the dataset (see section 3), followed by
268   a HardTanh activation to enforce output in the [0,1] range, while maintaining linearity. We note that
269   this scaled cosine prediction head is invariant to the order of the mixtures due to the symmetry of
      the cosine distance operation.

      2.3   T RAINING AND OPTIMIZATION
271
272   In order to effectively train a deep learning model in a low-data regime, we adopt a transfer learning
273   strategy (Figure 3). The POM GNN is first pre-trained to predict the olfactory binary multi-labels
274   of molecules with binary cross-entropy loss on the GS-LF dataset, using a 80/20 training/validation
275   random split. All training is performed using the Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2014). To deter-
276   mine the architecture, we perform a Bayesian optimization hyperparameter search to maximize the
277   area under receiver operator curve (AUROC) metric. Early stopping is used to prevent overfitting.
278
      The best model achieves a validation AUROC 0.884, in line with previous work (Sanchez-Lengeling
      et al., 2019). We explore other graph models such as G RAPHORMER (Shi et al., 2022; Ying et al.,
279
      2021) and GPS (Rampášek et al., 2022), but we find the G RAPH N ETS architecture to be competitive
280
      with the modern state-of-the-art graph models for our dataset (Table A2).
281
282   The frozen POM embeddings from the pre-trained GNN form the vector representation of mixtures
283   for the C HE M IX model. Again, the architecture is determined through hyperparameter tuning. The
284
      training is performed with mean absolute error (MAE) loss on a 80/20 training/validation split of the
      combined mixture dataset, stratified across Snitz, Ravia, and Bushdid. The stratification process
285
      fixes the proportion of each dataset across the splits, ensuring equal representation of any experi-
286
      mental differences. To avoid vanishing gradients due to the HardTanh activation, the linear model in
287   the scaled cosine distance prediction head is initialized with bias b = 0.5, and the slope is clamped
288   to ensure m > 0 and maintain the directionality of the cosine distance. Additionally, we ablate
289   the C HE M IX prediction head, and find that the scaled cosine prediction head is optimal for learn-
290   ing mixture embedding similarities (Table A3). Early stopping terminates on maximal validation
291   Pearson correlation coefficient (ρ) between the ground truth labels and the prediction. The optimal
292   model found in the search achieved a maximal ρ = 0.794 on the validation set. Further details about
293   the hyperparameter tuning for both models are provided in Appendix A.3.
294   In the final stage of training POMM IX, the POM GNN is directly joined to the C HE M IX model,
295   and all model weights are allowed to vary. A lower learning rate is used for the POM GNN model
296   weights, as they are already well-conditioned from pre-training on the larger mono-molecular odor
297   dataset. The results following this section are based on the final POMM IX model. Models were
298   built with P Y T ORCH (Paszke et al., 2019) and P Y T ORCH G EOMETRIC (Fey & Lenssen, 2019).

      3     R ESULTS
301
302
      We evaluate our approach on the mixture dataset by training and testing on 5-fold cross-validation
303   (CV) splits, stratified across the Snitz, Ravia, and Bushdid datasets. For early stopping, a validation
304   split is randomly split from the training set, producing a final split of 70/10/20 training/validation/test
305   sets. The performances of the models are then evaluated on the test sets.
306
      We evaluate POMM IX on a progressive ladder of modeling components. For the simplest base-
307
      line, we follow the methods of Snitz et al. (2013), who performed extensive feature selection on
308
      molecular descriptors, which are then averaged together for the mixtures (see Appendix A.4). The
309   angle distance between the vector descriptors are then correlated with the experimental results. We
310   perform the same analysis using normalized RDK IT molecular features on our aggregated mixture
311   dataset. We ensure that the feature selection is only performed on the training set.
312
      We also provide comparisons with the gradient-boosted random forest XGB OOST model (Chen &
313
      Guestrin, 2016), and use features with varying levels of inductive biases (further details in section
314
      A.5). Mixture representations are created with PNA-style aggregation of molecular descriptors,
315   including RDK IT features, or the frozen POM embeddings. Additionally, we augment the training
316   data by permuting the mixture pairs, as the symmetry of the mixture similarity is not encoded in
317   XGB OOST.

416   3.3   E XPLORING OLFACTORY PHENOMENA WITH POMM IX EMBEDDINGS
417
      The white noise hypothesis states that intensity-balanced mixtures with an increasingly large num-
418
      ber of components become increasingly indistinguishable, even if they share no common molecular
419
      components, and approach a scent characterized as an "olfactory white" (Weiss et al., 2012). Using
420   the POMM IX embedding, we reproduce the "olfactory white" phenomena (Figure 6a). In our inves-
421   tigation, we observe this decrease in POMM IX embedding distances as a function of the geometric
422   mean of components in mixture pairs for our larger dataset, which includes Bushdid and Ravia.
423   This demonstrates the ability of POMM IX in capturing and explaining physiological olfaction phe-
424   nomena, allowing it to build toward an expressive odor perceptual space.
425
      It is important to note that the perceptual similarity metrics obtained across the datasets are inher-
426   ently subjective and biased as they are gathered from humans. We show a subset of the data where
427   the panelists are asked to rate the similarities of two identical mixtures, and show that a significant
428   portion of identical mixture pairs (60 out of 63) are labeled as having non-zero similarities (Figure
429   6b). While the observed bias could be descriptive of average human olfactory inaccuracies, because
430   the number of panelists sampled was low (∼300), the bias could be local to the panelists. This
431   human bias is modeled by the learned bias term of the scaled cosine similarity prediction head. In
      general, we observe that the learned bias is slightly higher than the dataset bias. When we physi-

      3.4   B UILDING INTERPRETATIONS OF MIXTURES
461
      An unanswered question relevant to mixture modeling is how the mixture components interact with
462
      each other and contribute to the prediction of mixture similarities. To probe at this question and
463   generate hypotheses for future investigation, we modified C HE M IX to be more interpretable as an
464   additive model (Agarwal et al., 2021). Specifically we express the self-attention-based mixing com-
465   ponent as a one-layer additive model by using sigmoid normalization (Ramapuram et al., 2024)
466   rather than softmax, allowing the model to attend to all components, and forcing the value vectors to
467   be positive via a ReLU activation. In a simplified way, this is a pairwise interaction model. Although
468   this modified model is simpler and more constrained, it achieves performance comparable to that of
469   our best model.
470   In Figure 7, we showcase how such sigmoidal self-attention maps can be used to analyze the in-
471   formation passing between representations of molecules within a mixture. More complex examples
472   can be found in Appendix A.9. In this simple example, when comparing the GS-LF labels asso-
473   ciated to each molecule (Figure 7a) to the attention weights attributed to each query (Figure 7b),
474   we notice that the strongest "interaction"—namely, the highest attributed attention weight—is found
475   between query molecule 1 and key molecule 3. In general, we observe that molecules that are most
476   different from the rest, either by chemical structure (e.g., presence of N or S atom) or by olfactory
477
      perception (e.g., presence of rare or numerous labels), tend to have stronger interactions. To further
      our analysis, we derive label-guided structural heuristics about molecules across the set of unique
478
      mixtures in Appendix A.10. We find that higher attention is attributed to chemical structures with
479
      strong, pungent, and unique smells. These include compounds with sulfur, nitrogen, and aromatic
480   structures. However, it is important to keep in mind that the attention map showcased here is intrin-
481   sically linked to the task of differentiating between mixtures and is therefore likely biased towards
482   attributing higher attention weights to molecular embeddings that carry discriminative power only
483   relative to this task. Careful experimentation with synthetic mixture tasks and dataset, where the
484   number of data points is not as limited, might provide guidance on the strengths and failure modes
485   of these approaches to interpretability (Sanchez-Lengeling et al., 2020). Prospective validation from
      new experimentation would also strengthen these hypotheses.

      A multi-headed (k) softmax attention mechanism can be interpreted as attending to k tokens (Joshi,
487   2020; Sanchez-Lengeling et al., 2021). In understanding interpretability, a natural question for mix-
488   tures might be: how many compounds do we need to attend to on average? Figure 7c attempts to
489   answer this by looking at the average number of interactions per compounds across the dataset. We
490   consider three attention weight cutoffs (0.3, 0.4 and 0.5) to define a significant interaction and ob-
491   serve that the number of interactions grows approximately linearly with the number of components
492   in the mixtures. For the 0.5 cutoff, this is approximately two interactions per compound for mix-
493   tures of less than 30 components. We observe an increase in average number of interactions after 30
494   components; however, we also note that data is quite sparse here, precluding the formation of firm

      conclusions about the data (Figure 2a).

      4   C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
514
515
      We introduce POMM IX, an extension of the POM for predicting olfactory similarities between
516   mixtures of molecules. Our approach combines graph neural networks for molecular representation,
517   with attention mechanisms for mixture modeling, and incorporates inductive biases by considering
518   cosine similarities between mixture embeddings to predict olfactory similarity. POMM IX demon-
519   strates state-of-the-art performance, creating meaningful representations of olfactory mixtures, and
520   we show how each component of inductive bias contributes to this performance.
521
      Our work highlights the value of incorporating domain knowledge and inductive biases, particularly
522   in low-data regimes. By respecting problem-space symmetries, we create a flexible and expres-
523   sive representation for olfactory mixtures, offering a potential solution for modeling other multi-
524   component systems. Furthermore, we provide a method towards interpretable modeling of mixture
525   components interactions studying the attention weights of mixture components in C HE M IX and
526   studying how molecular information attends to itself within a mixture.
527
      While POMM IX shows promising results, we acknowledge several limitations. The small size of
528   the available mixture dataset (< 1,000 samples) raises concerns about overfitting, despite our reg-
529   ularization efforts. Additionally, the limited coverage of chemical odorant space in current public
530   datasets (only ∼200 unique odorant compounds) constrains the model’s ability to generalize to a
531   wider range of chemical compounds. We also observed challenges in generalizing to new datasets
532   due to potential distribution shifts from varying experimental setups and human biases. Despite
533   making conscious design choices on the modeling side, the interpretability of mixture components
534   interactions in C HE M IX remains qualitative; further experimental investigations are required to val-
535   idate our conjectures.
536   We believe that the primary bottleneck in advancing olfactory modeling is the generation of high-
537   quality, diverse, and representative datasets. Future work should focus on expanding the coverage of
538   chemical space, incorporating various experimental conditions (e.g., dilution, intensity), and collect-
539   ing rich textual descriptions of odors. Such comprehensive datasets will be crucial for developing
      more robust, interpretable and generalizable models of olfactory perception.

998    A.4   P ROCEDURE FOR S NITZ BASELINE
999
1000   The Snitz baselines are reproduced following the procedure outlined in Snitz et al. (2013). There
1001   are three steps involved in optimizing the angle similarity model for the best descriptors. Prior to the
1002
       optimization campaign, we normalize the 200 RDK IT features obtained from DESCRIPTASTORUS
       (Kelley et al., 2024) and average across all the molecules in the mixture.
1003
1004   In step 1, we determine the appropriate number of descriptors by randomly sampling 20,000 times,
1005   without replacement, n ∈ [2, 200] descriptors, resulting in 199 sets of 20,000 samples each of
1006   predictions. We then evaluate the RMSE from the predictions of the similarity model for each value
1007   of n. The optimal number of descriptors was scored by minimizing µRM SE − σRM SE across all
1008
       20,000 samples for a given n. The appropriate number of descriptors was between n = 5 and n = 7,
       depending on the CV split.
1009
1010   In step 2, we evaluate the efficacy of each descriptor. We set the number of descriptors n for each CV
1011   split based on step 1, and randomly sampled n − 1 descriptors 2,000 times. Then, cycling through
1012   each individual descriptor, we appended it to each set of sampled n − 1 descriptors, producing a
1013   vector of n features, and again evaluated RMSE from the predictions of the similarity model. We
1014
       take the mean RMSE from the 2,000 samples and the most relevant descriptors are determined by
       minimizing the mean RMSE.
1015
1016   In step 3, we first calculate the score of each descriptor from step 2. The 2,000 samples of n − 1
1017   features with a specially appended feature i ∈ [1, 200] provides a score for the i-th descriptor in the
1018   representation. This score for the i-th descriptor is given by

1019                                                         RMSEi − µRMSE
                                    score(i) = max 0, −                        ,                           (1)
1020                                                              σRMSE
1021   which only provides a positive score if the feature achieves lower RMSE than the average RMSE
1022   achieved over all features. Only positive scored features are kept. We then randomly sample, 4,000
1023   times, n = 5 to n = 7 descriptors depending on the appropriate CV split out of the set of descriptors
1024   that performed better than the average RMSE value (i.e. positive score). Out of the 4,000 samples,
1025   we pick the best-performing set of descriptors (lowest RMSE) on the training set and perform a final
       evaluation on the test set. This procedure produces the values found in Section 3.

       A.10    M IXTURE SET LABEL - GUIDED STRUCTURAL INSIGHTS ON KEY MOLECULES
1243
1244   To derive structural heuristics across the entire set of unique mixtures, we analyze the key-ed
1245   molecules associated with extrema attention weight values for each query, focusing on queries that
1246   interact "strongly" with a key (an interaction is considered strong if the attention weight is above
1247   0.5). We visualize the UMAP (McInnes et al., 2018) of the POM embeddings projected by C HE M IX
1248   through one linear layer of key-ed molecules exclusively found as maximizing/minimizing attention
1249   weights (Figure A5, left). We observe a clear separation between the two classes, suggesting that cer-
1250
       tain types of molecules are prioritized (high interactions) or de-prioritized (low interactions) when
       it comes to updating the molecular embeddings within a mixture.
1251
1252   We then performed hierarchical clustering (Müllner, 2011) of these key-ed molecules with S CI P Y
1253   (Virtanen et al., 2020) based on the pairwise Jaccard similarity of the binary GS-LF odor de-
1254   scriptor labels. We selected a few representative molecules for each of the clusters and observed
1255   strong structural differences between them (Figure A5, right). This is implicitly expected from the
1256
       structure-property relationship between scent and molecular structure. More importantly, we note
       that molecules within clusters are generally either "strongly" or "weakly" interacting, suggesting
1257
       our model established a relationship between specific molecular structures and attention weight val-
1258
       ues. Through this analysis, we observe that ester/aldehydes with long alkane chains tend to have
1259   low interaction keys (cluster 2, 3 and 7; Figure A5, right), while sulfur-containing molecules and
1260   molecules containing aromatic rings tend to be highly interacting ones (cluster 1, 4 and 5; Figure
1261   A5, right). These structural insights derived from label-driven clustering confirm the idea that certain
1262   molecules receive more attention than others.
1263
       One possible explanation for why ester/aldehydes with long alkane chains are "non-interacting" keys
1264
       would be that such molecules generally have a pleasant, sweet, or fruity smell. These odor descrip-
1265   tions are highly prevalent in the mixture dataset (119 (58.62%) "sweet" molecules and 92 (45.32%)
1266   "fruity" molecules in 203 unique molecules across the 743 unique mixtures), and could therefore not
1267   be informative in distinguishing mixtures. On the other hand, sulfur-containing molecules generally
1268   have a pungent, garlicky smell and occur less in the mixture dataset dataset (10 (4.92%) "garlic"
1269   molecules).
