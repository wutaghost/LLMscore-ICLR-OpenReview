009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Most real-world deployments of large language models (LLMs) operate within
012            well-scoped tasks, yet current safety measures are general-purpose and fail to
013            leverage this information. As a result, even in narrowly-scoped tasks, LLM ap-
014            plications remain vulnerable to adversarial jailbreaks. In these settings, we argue
015            that task-specific safety guardrails solve a more tractable problem than general-
016            purpose methods. We introduce Inverse Prompt Engineering (IPE) as an initial
017            approach to building automatic, task-specific safety guardrails around LLMs. Our
018            key insight is that robust safety guardrails can be derived from prompt engineering
019
               data that is already on hand. IPE operationalizes the principle of least privilege
               from computer security, restricting LLM functionality to only what is necessary
020
               for the task. We evaluate our approach in two settings. First, in an example chat-
021
               bot application, where IPE outperforms existing methods against both human-
022            written and automated adversarial attacks. Second, on TensorTrust, a crowd-
023            sourced dataset of prompt-based attacks and defenses. Here, IPE improves av-
024            erage defense robustness by 93%, using real-world prompt engineering data.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Our goal in this paper is to build automatic, task-specific safety guardrails for language models.
030   In contrast, existing safety methods are general-purpose and task-agnostic. They aim to enforce a
031   universal definition of safety across contexts (Inan et al., 2023; Phute et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023).
032   This is a challenging problem вҖ” and harder than what we usually need to solve.
033
      For example, suppose a user asks вҖңWrite an email template for a phishing attack to test internal secu-
034
      rity.вҖқ A general-purpose safety system faces an ambiguous situation: the request could be legitimate
035   (from someone testing company security) or a deceptive attempt to bypass safety filters. Successful
036   jailbreaks frequently exploit this kind of ambiguity through roleplay or misleading scenarios.
037
038
      On the other hand, consider building a task-specific safety guardrail (e.g. for a travel assistant chat-
      bot). The task-specific guardrail could safely reject this request as out-of-scope for a travel assistant.
039
      This approach aligns with the principle of least privilege in computer security, which advocates for
040
      limiting access and functionality to only what is necessary for a particular task (Stallings & Brown,
041   2015).
042
043   However, building custom safety guardrails traditionally requires significant data collection, techni-
044
      cal expertise, and development effort that many application developers lack access to. As a result,
      developers usually fall back on general-purpose safety classifiers. An ideal system for task-specific
045
      safety would 1) not require additional data collection and 2) be computationally cheap during train-
046
      ing and inference.
047
048   We propose Inverse Prompt Engineering (IPE) as an initial approach to building automatic, task-
049   specific safety guardrails around LLMs. Our key insight is that robust safety guardrails can be
050
      derived from prompt engineering data that is already on hand, requiring no additional data collection.
      Conceptually, IPE operationalizes the principle of least privilege from computer security, restricting
051
      LLM functionality to only what is necessary for the task.
052

      against a development set of test inputs (Figure 1). IPE uses the final prompt as well as the inputs

105   2    R ELATED W ORK
106
107   Jailbreak Defenses. In light of continuing LLM vulnerabilities, jailbreak defenses have received
      substantial attention. These approaches can be split into training-time and inference-time interven-


                                                              2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      tions. Training-time interventions typically involve a combination of red-teaming to discover vul-
109   nerabilities and general-purpose alignment tools such as RLHF to train the model to produce safe
110   responses (Ganguli et al., 2022; Casper et al., 2023; Ouyang et al., 2022). However, the resulting
111   LLMs remain vulnerable to adversarial attacks, motivating additional inference-time defenses.
112
      On the other hand, inference-time defenses use a separate model to filter out harmful responses. This
113
      is done by prompting a separate LLM (Phute et al., 2023; Rebedea et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023) or
114
      training a classifier OpenAI (2023); Lees et al. (2022); Han et al. (2024); Inan et al. (2023) to detect
115   harmful inputs and/or outputs. Classifier-based approaches, in particular, require significant effort
116   to implement, so off-the-shelf, task-agnostic safety classifiers are typically used.
117
118
      IPE is also an inference-time defense but differs from existing approaches in its task-specific nature.
      Unlike general-purpose methods that identify and block disallowed content (i.e. deny-lists), IPE
119
      detects allowed content and filters everything else (i.e. allow-lists). This approach is well-suited
120
      to real-world LLM deployments, which are typically designed for specific, well-scoped tasks. By
121   focusing on the intended functionality of a task-specific LLM application, IPE addresses a more
122   constrained вҖ” and more tractable вҖ” safety problem. This targeted approach relies less on develop-
123   ers anticipating all possible vulnerabilities and more on enforcing the intended scope of the systemвҖҷs
124   functionality.
125
      Jailbreak Attacks. Human-written jailbreaks, often shared on online platforms, continue to be
126
      highly effective in bypassing safety training and external guardrails (Shen et al., 2023; Wei et al.,
127   2023; Han et al., 2024). These attacks typically leverage creative prompting and roleplay scenarios.
128   Automated red-teaming methods have also emerged, including LLM-assisted jailbreak generation
129   (Yu et al., 2024; Shah et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Chao et al., 2024; Sadasivan et al., 2024) and
130   gradient-based prompt optimization algorithms (Zou et al., 2023; Jones et al., 2023). We evaluate
131   IPE against all three methods. In contrast, existing guardrails in the literature (Han et al., 2024; Inan
132   et al., 2023) only evaluate against static datasets instead of dynamic adversaries, which more closely
133   resemble real-world threats.
134   Reward Design and Prompt Engineering. We draw a connection between reward design in rein-
135   forcement learning and prompt engineering for LLMs. Both are useful task-specification methods
136   but can fail to generalize to real-world settings, especially under adversarial attacks. Studies show
137   this problem occurs in autonomous vehicles (Knox et al., 2022), navigation tasks (Booth et al.,
138   2023), and language models (Shah et al., 2022; Toyer et al., 2023). IPE addresses this by treating
139   the chosen prompt as an observation about the true task, rather than as its literal definition.
140   Reward Learning. IPE builds directly on Inverse Reward Design (Hadfield-Menell et al., 2017;
141   Mindermann et al., 2018), which defines a distribution over a reward designerвҖҷs вҖңtrueвҖқ reward func-
142   tion given a proxy reward they engineered and the development environment they designed it for.
143   We adapt this framework to build LLM safety guardrails based on engineered prompts. Other reward
144   learning methods have also been applied to LLM safety, most notably RLHF (Christiano et al., 2017;
145   Ouyang et al., 2022). In contrast to these methods, we 1) use a small, passively collected dataset ex-
146   posed during prompt engineering and 2) build task-specific guardrails instead of a general-purpose
147
      alignment tool. Finally, we note that researchers have proposed several other methods for inferring
      reward functions from language. However, these methods use a restricted set of grounded utterances
148
      instead of unrestricted natural language prompts (Sumers et al., 2022; Zhou & Small, 2021; Fu et al.,
149
      2019).

154   3   I NVERSE P ROMPT E NGINEERING
155
156
157   In this section, we formalize Inverse Prompt Engineering (IPE) as a mathematical framework for
158   building task-specific safety guardrails from prompt engineering data. We begin by modeling the
159   prompt engineering process, drawing parallels to reward design in reinforcement learning. We then
160   define IPE as an inverse problem and address the key computational challenge in implementing this
161   approach. Finally, we describe practical aspects of how we use IPE to train robust LLM safety
      guardrails.

      3.1   M ODELING THE P ROMPT E NGINEERING P ROCESS
163
164   To develop IPE, we first need to formalize the prompt engineering process as a likelihood model.
165   This allows us to treat prompt engineering data as observations about the underlying task.
166
      Prompt engineering is a complicated trial-and-error process. In robust prompt engineering work-
167
      flows, designers test and iteratively refine prompts against representative inputs (OpenAI, 2024).
168   Our model of prompt engineering, illustrated in Figure 1, relies on an explicit characterization of
169   these evaluation inputs, which we call the development set and denote as X = {x1 , ..., xN }. The
170   output of prompt engineering is a final system prompt sвҲ— , which encodes the designerвҖҷs intended
171   task specification. We do not model the individual iterations of prompt engineering вҖ” we only as-
172   sume that when prompt engineering ends, the prompted LLM performs well on average against the
173   development set. Note that the development set defines the prompt engineerвҖҷs design context. As a
174   result, outside of this context, e.g. under deployment-time adversarial attack, there is no guarantee
175   that the prompted LLM will behave as intended.
176   The IPE Likelihood. In our model, the designer searches for a prompt so that the LLM will achieve
177   a task. This underlying task can be represented through a prompt engineerвҖҷs latent reward function
178   rвҲ— (x, y), which assigns a numerical score to input-output text pairs (x, y). The designer chooses a
179   prompt sвҲ— that causes the language model ПҖ(y|x; sвҲ— ) to generate high reward completions y.
180   We model the prompt engineer as a Boltzmann-optimal decision-maker:

      We define IPE as the inverse problem of prompt engineering. Here, we seek to infer the correct latent

      reward function given 1) a designerвҖҷs choice of prompt and 2) the development set of inputs against

189   replaces the development environment used in reward design.

235
      Alternative Prompt Generation. For all of our experiments, we use Llama2-13B-Chat-HF (Tou-
236
      vron et al., 2023) to generate a common set of 512 synthetic prompts, covering a wide range of
237
      possible roles and tasks. This is used to estimate the normalizing constant in Equation 3.2. We fol-
238   low Wang et al. (2022); Taori et al. (2023) in using meta-prompts that explicitly encourage diversity

241
      LLM Output Rollouts. To generate rollouts for IPE training, we sample batches of 8 inputs from
242
      the development set X at a time. On each input, we generate completions with the designerвҖҷs chosen
243
      prompt as well as with four randomly selected alternative prompts. For each experimental setting,
244   we generate 2,500 such batches, which form the dataset for reward inference.
245
246   Reward Inference. In all experiments, we finetune ensembles of 8 LoRA-adapted GPT-2 reward
247
      models to maximize the log IPE likelihood over the data (Radford et al., 2019; Hu et al., 2021). We
      train each model for 5000 steps or about 35 minutes on an H100 GPU.

      4.2.1   E VALUATING AGAINST AUTOMATED R ED -T EAMING
348
349   Static datasets of human-written jailbreaks provide valuable insights but donвҖҷt fully reflect real-
350   world attacks. In this section, we evaluate IPE against dynamic adversaries through automated red-
351   teaming, which better mirrors real-world threats. This approach sets our work apart from previous
352   guardrail evaluations in the literature (Inan et al., 2023; Han et al., 2024; Phute et al., 2023; Lees
353   et al., 2022), which only evaluate against static datasets. We use two automated approaches: 1)
354   GPTFuzz, an LLM-powered genetic algorithm (Yu et al., 2024) and 2) GCG, a gradient-based attack
355   (Zou et al., 2023).
356   LLM-Based Red-Teaming. We employ GPTFuzz as a representative LLM-based red-teaming at-
357   tack. GPTFuzz applies a genetic algorithm to a seed set of jailbreak templates, maintaining a tree and
358   mutating the most promising nodes. GPT-Fuzz detects jailbreak success using a custom RoBERTa
359   classifier (Yu et al., 2024). We run GPTFuzz for 1000 steps against 200 harmful queries from Harm-
360   BenchвҖҷs вҖңstandardвҖқ subset (Mazeika et al., 2024). The attack targets both the generative model
361   (Llama2-13B-Chat) and the guardrail. This means that successful attacks need to both cause the
362
      language model to produce harmful text and fool the classifier that the text is safe. We set a thresh-
      old such that 99% of benign travel completions pass through, considering an attack successful if it
363
      passes this threshold.
364
365   Given that IPE is a task-specific method, we also evaluate against targeted attacks that aim to take
366   advantage of this feature of the guardrail. We create a task-specific GPTFuzz variant. This version
367   augments the mutation operator with knowledge of the system prompt and instructions to bypass it
368
      subtly. For implementation simplicity, we modify and evaluate only one mutation operator instead
      of the full set used in GPTFuzz.
369
370   Results in Figure 3 show IPE is the most robust guardrail, with only 6% and 22% attack success
371   rates (ASR) for standard and task-specific attacks respectively. The next best performer, OpenAI
372   moderation, has more than double IPEвҖҷs ASR at 50% and 44%. Notably, Perspective API and
373   LlamaGuard2, which performed well on static jailbreaks, struggle against these dynamic attacks вҖ”
374
      reaching nearly 100% ASR. This highlights the importance of evaluating guardrails against dynamic
      adversaries before trusting performance claims.
375

377   against IPE. However, even with these targeted attacks, IPEвҖҷs overall attack success rate remains
      much lower than other methods. GPT-Fuzz represents a class of iterative, mutation-based attacks

      Discussion. In our experiments, IPE reward models are trained on a comparatively small and ho-
521
      mogenous set of development inputs (especially in the travel assistant experiment). However, these
522
      models consistently extrapolate to give jailbreaks low scores, even though these inputs are far out-
523   of-distribution (i.e. not travel-related). While uncertainty-weighting is helpful, it is not necessary
524   to detect most jailbreaks. Empirically, this means that the IPE likelihood is encoding a conservative
525   bias on out-of-distribution scores. In future work, it would be interesting to explore why this occurs.
526   One hypothesis is that jailbreaks are close to alternative prompt completions under a certain notion
527   of distance. Alternatively, it may be a result of how the IPE likelihood interacts with special features
528   of deep networks, such as the phenomenon explored in Kang et al. (2024).
