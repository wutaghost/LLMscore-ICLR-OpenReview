009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In-context learning (ICL) has demonstrated remarkable success in enabling large
012            language models (LLMs) to learn to do a downstream task by simply condi-
013            tioning on a few input-output demonstrations. Distinct from traditional learning
014            paradigms, ICL does not require model updates, thus attracting significant inter-
015            est in understanding the mechanisms behind LLMs’ ICL capabilities. Advanced
016            works aim to understand ICL through an empirical viewpoint to provide the mul-
017            tifaceted nature of ICL, while some works aim to explain how ICL can emerge
018
               theoretically. However, the current theoretical analysis exhibits a weak connec-
               tion to empirical explorations due to strong assumptions, e.g., perfect LLMs and
019
               ideal demonstrations. This work proposes an intention model, providing a novel
020
               theoretical framework for explaining ICL. With mild assumptions, we present a
021            “no-free-lunch” theorem for ICL: whether ICL emerges depends on the prediction
022            error and prediction noise, which are determined by i) LLMs’ error of next-token
023            prediction, ii) LLMs’ prediction smoothness, and iii) the quality of demonstra-
024            tions. Moreover, our intention model provides a novel explanation for the learning
025            behavior of ICL under various input-output relations, e.g., learning with flipped
026            labels. This is fortunately consistent with our experimental observations.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   In-context learning (ICL) refers to the capability of large language models (LLMs) to adapt to new
032   tasks, which is achieved through conditioning on a few input-output demonstrations without model
033
      updates (Brown et al., 2020). It shows that the striking feature of LLMs is inherently related to
      the model scale (Brown et al., 2020; Garg et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2023) and demonstrations, like
034
      the selected pairs, order of pairs, and the format of demonstrations (Min et al., 2022; Liu et al.,
035
      2022; Wang et al., 2024). According to the understanding of traditional learning paradigms, it is
036   difficult to anticipate LLMs’ ICL capacity because they are trained to predict the next token rather
037   than perform like in-context predictors (Ouyang et al., 2022). This intriguing mismatch between
038   ICL and traditional learning paradigms has attracted increasing attention in understanding LLMs’
039   ICL capability (Dong et al., 2023).
040
      Numerous outstanding works have revealed the enigmatic characteristics inherent to ICL. Specif-
041
      ically, it shows that leveraging randomly assigned labels of the inputs leads to a limited change
042   in performance (Min et al., 2022) when the number of input-output pairs is limited (Kossen et al.,
043   2024), challenging the necessary of ground-truth labels in demonstrations. Meanwhile, it shows that
044   the performance change induced by flipping labels of input demonstrations depends on the model
045   scale (Wei et al., 2023). Namely, LLMs with larger model scale can override the semantic priors (i.e.,
046   predict “positive” for samples from the positive class) by predicting the flipped label (i.e., predict
047   “negative” for samples from the positive class), where demonstrations are composed of inputs with
048   flipped labels (assigning positive labels for inputs from the negative class). These intriguing phe-
049   nomena highlight the difference between traditional learning and ICL (Kossen et al., 2024). These
050
      seminal explorations provide a fruitful guide for understanding and explaining ICL.
051   In addition to empirical investigations, recent advancements have yielded significant theoretical in-
052   sights into the mechanisms underpinning the ICL capabilities of LLMs. For instance, the semi-
053   nal work points out that ICL emerges because LLMs explicitly perform Bayesian inference (Xie
      et al., 2022), inspiring numerous explorations (Hahn & Goyal, 2023; Jiang, 2023). Meanwhile,

054
      Von Oswald et al. (2023); Akyürek et al. (2023) argue that a traditional gradient decent approach
055   can endow transformer-based models with the ICL capability, shedding light to identify model be-
056   haviors (Reddy, 2024). However, these studies often demonstrate a relatively weak connection to
057   empirical investigations, potentially due to strong assumptions such as the perfect alignment of
058   LLMs with data distributions and the ideal representation of tasks by demonstrations.
059
      Inspired by Brown et al. (2020); Xie et al. (2022), we propose a novel framework to theoretically
060
      explain LLMs’ ICL capability, sharing the same spirit as the topic model (Blei et al., 2003). Accord-
061   ing to the intention model, LLMs can infer intentions from demonstrations by filtering out unrelated
062   intentions (Eq. 5). Using inferred intentions, LLMs can predict ground-truth outputs with bounded
063   prediction errors (Eq. 7) and noise (Eqs. 8, and 9). Our main contributions are twofold. First, we
064   propose a no-free-lunch Theorem 1 for ICL to highlight that the conditions for the emergence of ICL
065   are not naturally met. Second, our intention model takes a step towards bridging the gap between
066   theoretical and empirical results by providing a novel explanation for the learning behavior of ICL
067   when LLMs are conditioned on varying input-output mappings.
068   First, whether ICL emerges depends on the prediction error and prediction noise, which are deter-
069   mined by i) LLMs’ error of next-token prediction, ii) LLMs’ prediction smoothness, and iii) the
070   quality of demonstrations. This aligns with the understanding of ICL and highlights the necessity of
071   a small error in predicting the next token for ICL. These theoretical results underscore the contribu-
072   tion of our intention model, marking the first instance of relaxing the strong assumptions regarding:
073   i) perfect alignment of LLMs, and ii) the idealized representation of tasks by demonstrations. Sec-
074   ond, the scenarios of ICL with varying input-output mappings can be modeled through an external
075
      transition matrix (Eq. 10), leading to the following conclusions.
076
             • Introducing an external mapping to modify the original outputs of demonstrations makes
077            ICL more challenging. This is because the transition matrix would increase the upper
078            bound of prediction noise (Eq. 9). This is consistent with our results shown in Table 1.
079
             • An LLM with a smaller error of next-token prediction performs better in overriding se-
080
               mantic priors under flipped label scenarios. This is because the smaller error can lead
081
               to a reduced prediction noise (Eq. 9). This conclusion aligns well with our experimental
082            observations (Table 1) and previous experimental observations (Wei et al., 2023).
083
             • Increasing the number of demonstrations under the random label scenario decreases per-
084
               formance. This is because a larger number of demonstrations would magnify the impact of
085
               demonstration shift and the LLMs’ error of next-token prediction, as shown in Eq. 9. This
086            conclusion aligns well with previous experimental observations (Kossen et al., 2024).

089   2    P RELIMINARY
090
091   In-context learning (ICL) is firstly introduced by the seminal work (Brown et al., 2020), where LLMs
092
      are conditioned on a few demonstrations of a task and can predict what comes next to complete
      the task. Although studying the special ICL can reveal the difference between traditional learning
093
      and ICL (Reddy, 2024), this paper mainly focuses on exploring what enables LLMs’ ICL ability
094
      following previous works (Brown et al., 2020; Xie et al., 2022). Thus, we mainly study the general
095   ICL ability of LLMs following Brown et al. (2020), which is defined as follows.
096
097   Definition 1 (In-context learning) In-context learning refers to the ability with which LLMs can
098   complete a task simply by conditioning on a few input-output demonstrations of the task.
099
100   According to Definition 1, ICL introduces a novel task adaptation approach that contrasts with up-
101
      dating model parameters in the traditional learning paradigm. This is achieved through task demon-
      strations, as mentioned in the definition.
102
103   Demonstration structure. The task demonstrations for ICL are constructed by natural language
104   instruction composed of input-output pairs Oi = {(xi , yi )}, which are constructed to form the
105   representation of tasks (Brown et al., 2020). A test sample xt is typically concatenated at
106   the end of these input-output pairs. Thus, the demonstration structure of ICL is, [S n , xt ] =
107   [x1 , y1 , of1 , x2 , y2 , of2 , · · · , xn , yn , ofn , xt ], where n is the number of input-output pairs, and ofi
      presents the format in ICL, e.g., delimiter tokens.

108
      Demonstration construction. Given a text sample xt , the demonstration of ICL is constructed
109   for the corresponding task descriptions. Based on the constructed demonstrations, the in-context
110   predictor fM (S n , xt ) outputs the prediction through a LLM M with the highest probability of xt :
111
                                       fM (S n , xt ) = arg max pM (y|S n , xt ),                      (1)
112                                                             y
113   where y represents a set of tokens from the vocabulary set O and pM (·|·) stands for the prediction
114   probability of an LLM. In this context, the in-context predictor is expected to minimize the pre-
115   diction risk by constructing appropriate demonstrations S n : minS n E(xt ,yt ) I(yt ̸= fM (S n , xt )),
116   where yt is the ground-truth output of the test input xt and I(·) represents the indicator function.
117   Recent works show that tuning the format, selecting different input-output pairs, and changing the
118   order of input-output pairs in the demonstrations can significantly affect ICL performance.
119
      Searching for demonstrations to promote ICL performance is similar to prompt learning (Shin et al.,
120   2020; Zhou et al., 2022), i.e., tuning prompts to adapt large-scale models to downstream tasks.
121   However, the mechanism involved in ICL could be beyond the feature alignment between modal-
122   ities (Brown et al., 2020). Moreover, optimizing prompts in the embedding space is challenging
123   for ICL due to the large and discrete search space (Zou et al., 2023). Thus, the current methods
124   mainly focus on hand-crafted demonstrations. In this context, ICL performance heavily relies on
125   two factors: i) the construction of demonstrations and ii) the performance of LLMs.

127   3       S ETUP OF INTENTION MODEL
128
129   Our intention model for explaining ICL of LLMs is mainly inspired by the seminal works (Blei
130   et al., 2003; Brown et al., 2020; Xie et al., 2022).

250   4.1   TARGET OVERVIEW
251
252   LLMs are mainly trained with a simple objective function that encourages models to predict the next
253   token over a large corpus. As LLMs scale up, ICL emerges as a surprising capability. ICL enables
254   LLMs to learn to do a new task by simply conditioning on a few input-output demonstrations of the
255   task. This exciting and newly discovered capability introduces several intriguing questions: When
256   does ICL emerge? Why does ICL emerge despite the absence of explicit training for such behavior?
257   How does ICL remain effective when demonstrations differ from LLMs’ training distributions?
258   Let us start with a general understanding of ICL. It is known that ICL performance mainly relies on
259   two factors: i) LLMs’s performance and ii) demonstration quality. This is intuitive because the dis-
260   crepancy between the predicted and ground-truth distribution stems from these two factors. Namely,
261   the predicted distribution obtained by LLMs through ICL can be written as qM (y|S n (θd ), xt ), where
262   the corresponding ground-truth distribution is q(y|S n (θg ), xt ). Thus, it would benefit to fulfill these
263   questions about when, why, and how ICL emerges if we can model the discrepancy between these
264   two distributions and identify conditions when the discrepancy is bounded.
      With the prediction expansion pM (y|S n (θd ), xt ) = Θ pM (y|S n (θd ) , xt , θ) pM (θ|S n (θd ) , xt ) dθ,

355
356   It shows that ICL can achieve good performance with randomly assigned labels (Min et al., 2022),
357   implying that ground-truth input-output mappings are not required. In contrast, a novel perspec-
358   tive (Kossen et al., 2024) is then proposed: ICL predictions depend on the input-output mapping of
359   demonstrations. Moreover, it is shown that larger LLMs can override semantic priors, i.e., following
360   the flipped labels in demonstrations (Wei et al., 2023). In the flipped label scenario, demonstra-
361   tions are constructed to describe a binary classification task in which all labels of input samples are
362   flipped. These correct but differing views make understanding and explaining ICL difficult. Fortu-
363
      nately, arming with the intention model, we will find that these outstanding observations are correct
      and predictable under specific conditions.
364
365   Our intention model provides a novel view of explaining the input-output mapping in ICL. In these
366   scenarios, the original outputs can be wrapped in the way of multiplying a transition matrix by the
367   original transition matrix, e.g., applying an external mapping to the original outputs,

422   5.1   P OSSIBLE MECHANISM OF INTENTION INFERENCE .
423
424   The basic idea of our intention model is that LLMs can infer task intentions from the demonstrations
425   and filter out unrelated intention. A natural question is raised: what is the instantiation of intentions
426   in LLMs? According to Olsson et al. (2022), induction heads are shown to be closely related to
427   general ICL in LLMs. Namely, induction heads could be the mechanistic source of general ICL in
428
      LLMs Olsson et al. (2022). This motivates us to identify a set of induction heads for a given inten-
      tion. Thus, we aim to design experiments to verify the impact of these intentions on the generation
429
      process under a specific intention.
430
431   Intuitively, some specific induction heads mainly correspond to a specific intention. Therefore,
      we aim to identify a set of induction heads for a given intention and verify the impact of these


                                                         8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      intentions on the generation process under a specific intention. For the algorithm to locate induction
433   heads, we draw inspiration from the work Goldowsky-Dill et al. (2023). We construct counterfactual
434   examples to compare the induction heads when the intention of interest is activated and when it is
435   not. Specifically, given a reference sample used for activating a certain intention, we construct
436   a corresponding counterfactual example to deactivate the intention with minimal changes to the
437   reference sample. Subsequently, we replace the induction heads of the reference sample with those
438   of the counterfactual example. Thus, we can record the output changes when replacing each head.
439   Consequently, induction heads that cause drastic output changes are located as the candidate heads
440   related to the current intention. Following previous work, we employ the model LLaMA-2-7B and
441
      the dataset SST-2 for the employed models and datasets. More details can be found in Appendix F.
      Fig. 1 shows the map of identified induction heads. It can be seen that only a small fraction of
442
      heads are frequently involved in generation for a specific intention. To verify whether these heads
443
      are related to the same intention, we perturb these identified heads of samples, which are different
444   from those used in the intention-locating process. For instance, replacing values of these heads
445   with zeros (Goldowsky-Dill et al., 2023). We also randomly sample an equal number of induction
446   heads for perturbation as a control experiment. The effect of perturbing induction heads on LLMs’
447   performance is shown in Fig. 1. We can see that the heads identified with distinct samples can
448   significantly reduce prediction accuracy when they are perturbed, a phenomenon not observed on
449   randomly selected heads. Thus, these identified induction heads are highly related to the intention.

451   5.2   S TUDY OF L EARNING B EHAVIOR
452
453   Our theorem shows a no-free-lunch nature of ICL, involving prediction error and noise. A straight-
454   forward approach to validate the theorem is to calculate the prediction error and noise. However, it
455   is challenging to calculate or estimate these values, i.e. the error of next-token prediction δ3 , LLM’s
456   prediction smoothness, and demonstration shift ϵ. Therefore, it is challenging to provide a quantitive
457   analysis to verify the theorem. Fortunately, some related factors can be controlled implicitly. Thus,
458   to validate our theorem, we design experiments to test the impact of these factors implicitly. For
459
      instance, the error of next-token prediction δ3 could be related to the LLMs’ performance under
      general tasks. In this context, we could conclude that δ3 of GPT4 is less than that of LLaMa-7B.
460
461   Eq. 10 generalizes the flipped-label scenario to the problem of capturing complex mapping rela-
462   tions. We can then validate our theoretical insights by experimenting on flipped label questions. In
463   this regard, we instantiate the mapping using a multiple-choice question sampled from the CSQA
464   dataset (Saha et al., 2018), where the ground-truth outputs of the original questions are changed
465
      with a mode addition operation, i.e., i) y = (y + 1) mod 5, and ii) y = (3y + 1) mod 5. According
      to our theoretical results, larger LLMs are able to follow the instructions shown in the demonstra-
466
      tions. For instance, if the answer to a certain question is “(a): Yes” but labeled as “(b)”, larger
467
      LLMs can predict “(b): No” as the answer, even though the answer in the option “(b)” does not
468   match the actual answer. As a simple corollary to our theory, learning behaviors under a fliped-
469   label scenario can be modeled by multiplying a transition matrix T by the original transition ma-
470   trix θ, as shown in Eq 10. Introducing an external T to modify original outputs would lead to
471   KL(qM (x, y|T θd )||qM (x, y|T θg )) = δ ′ > δ = KL(qM (x, y|θd )||qM (x, y|θg )), making the task
472   more challenging. This is becuase large ϵ′ results in 1) larger prediction errors as shown in Eq. (7);
473   and 2) larger prediction noise as shown in Eq. (13). Thus, introducing an external matrix T will
474   degrade ICL performance, which is consistent with the results shown in Table 1, i.e., changing y to
475   (y + 1) mod 5. Our theoretical result also shows that LLMs with smaller errors of next-token pre-
476
      diction δ3 perform better in overriding semantic priors under flipped label scenarios. This is because
      a smaller prediction error δ3 can reduce the prediction noise, as shown in Eq. 10.
477
478   We leverage three LLMs: LLaMa-7B (Touvron et al., 2023), Mistral-7B (Jiang et al., 2023), and

481   shares the same spirit with the flipped label scenario (Wei et al., 2023). Thus, these experimental
482
      results align with our conclusion: an LLM with a minor error of next-token prediction δ3 performs

      6    R ELATED WORK
499   Language modeling stands as a pivotal technology in the vast domain of natural language process-
500   ing (NLP) (Radford et al., 2019; Lewis et al., 2020). Scaling models often exhibit distinct behaviors
501   from their smaller counterparts and display surprising capabilities. Trained with immense text data
502   collections, LLMs (Brown et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023) have revolution-
503   ized various NLP tasks. Unlike traditional transfer learning, LLMs showcase a remarkable ICL
504   capability, a framework that empowers LLMs to grasp tasks through conditioning on some demon-
505
      strations (Dong et al., 2023). Namely, LLMs can do a downstream task by simply conditioning on
      a few input-output demonstrations without model updates. Advanced works (Liu et al., 2022; Wang
506
      et al., 2024) show that the label space presented, the overall format of the presentation, and the selec-
507
      tion of demonstrations play a crucial role in ICL (Min et al., 2022). Theoretical explorations on the
508   learnability of ICL mainly focused on the analysis of demonstration format in ICL (Xie et al., 2022;
509   Wies et al., 2023). Meanwhile, Lin & Lee (2024) consider a specific regression task and assess the
510   performance of ICL with the focus on context length’s impact on ICL and explains two Real-World
511   phenomena. In contrast, our work not only considers the possible errors of the demonstration itself
512   but also considers the model’s general forecasting ability. Some empirical understanding (Kossen
513   et al., 2024; Pan et al., 2023) on the mechanism of ICL. We defer detailed discussions in Appendix E.
514
515   7    L IMITATION .
516
517   Demonstration order is not covered by our work, while recent works show that the order of demon-
518   strations also plays a crucial role in ICL (Lu et al., 2022; Perez et al., 2021). One possible approach
519   is to introduce the mechanism of ICL like Reddy (2024), which is left as our future work.
520
      Mixed intention is not considered in our work. Although generating all input-output pairs with
521
      the same intention is intuitive, studying mixed intentions is also a promising direction. Specifically,
522
      what would happen when an input-output pair sampled from a different intention is injected into the
523   demonstration is unclear. Thus, we leave it as our future work.
524
525   Explicit intention is a possible approach to contribute to training LLMs, as our intention model
526
      shows that inferring intention plays a crucial role in ICL. Training an LLM is out of the scope of
      this work, and we hope to verify the point in our future work. Moreover, exploring ICL through the
527
      lens of novel tasks is a promising direction not discussed in our work. Learning with in-distribution
528
      data often struggles to achieve robust generalization performance on out-of-distribution data, which
529   usually requires further exploration regarding the learnability and generalizability Fang et al. (2022).
530   Thus, we will explore the novel intentions and novel tasks in our future work.

      8    C ONCLUSION
533
534   We propose a novel intention model framework to investigate the in-context learning (ICL) capabili-
535   ties of large language models. In particular, we give a no-free-lunch theorem: whether ICL emerges
536   depends on the prediction error and prediction noise, which are determined by i) LLMs’ error of
537
      next-token prediction, ii) LLMs’ prediction smoothness, and iii) demonstration shift. Note that our
      theoretical results connect LLMs’ error of next-token prediction to the ICL capability. Moreover,
538
      our theoretical results bridge the gap between theoretical explanations and empirical observations.

       E    R ELATED WORK
1152
1153   Large Language Models. Based on highly parallelizable Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al.,
1154   2017), which incorporates self-attention mechanisms, BERT (Devlin et al., 2019) emerged as a
1155   groundbreaking bidirectional language model through pre-training. This study has inspired a large
1156
       number of follow-up work, establishing the “pre-training and fine-tuning” learning paradigm, e.g.,
       GPT-2 (Radford et al., 2019) and BART (Lewis et al., 2020)). Researchers have further observed that
1157
       scaling models often exhibit distinct behaviors from their smaller counterparts and display surpris-
1158
       ing capabilities. Consequently, the term ”large language models (LLMs)” have gained significant
1159   traction. Typically, LLMs refer to Transformer-based language models encompassing hundreds of
1160   billions or more parameters, trained on immense text data collections(Shanahan, 2023), such as
1161   GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020), Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023) and LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023). LLMs
1162   have revolutionized various NLP tasks, such as text summarization, question answering, and transla-
1163   tion, with their explosive growth in size significantly enhancing their capacity to comprehend human
1164   language (Zhao et al., 2023).
1165   Adapting LLMs through fine-tuning. Similar to traditional transfer learning, fine-tuning is the
1166   straightforward approach to adapting pre-trained models to specific downstream tasks, involving ad-
1167   justing the model parameters to optimize performance on a given dataset. In this regard, parameter-
1168   efficient fine-tuning has become a preferred choice, which focuses on updating only a subset of
1169   parameters (Hu et al., 2021; Houlsby et al., 2019; Ouyang et al., 2022). These methods significantly
1170   enhance LLMs’ performance on downstream tasks.
1171   Adapting LLMs through in-context learning. Different from traditional transfer learning, LLMs
1172   showcase a remarkable in-context learning (ICL) capability, which is a framework that empowers
1173   LLMs to grasp tasks through conditioning on some demonstrations (Dong et al., 2023). Namely,
1174   LLMs can do a downstream task by simply conditioning on a few input-output demonstrations with-
1175   out the need to update model parameters for novel tasks. Similar to transfer learning to search for
1176   optimal model parameters, advanced works aim to search for good demonstrations (Liu et al., 2022;
1177   Wang et al., 2024), where it shows that the label space presented, the overall format of the presen-
1178   tation, and the selection of demonstrations play a crucial role in ICL (Min et al., 2022). Besides
1179
       practical applications, there have been some theoretical explorations on the mechanism of ICL. Xie
       et al. (2022) conceptualize in-context learning as a language model capable of conducting implicit
1180
       Bayesian inference, theoretically proving that LLMs can identify and learn latent conceptual vari-
1181
       ables embedded within examples. Recent work points out that ICL is not conventional learning
1182   when learning label relationships (Kossen et al., 2024). Pan et al. (2023) decouple the ICL ability
1183   into task recognition ability and task learning ability, and further show how they utilize demonstra-
1184   tions. Wies et al. (2023) segment the ICL framework into two distinct stages: pre-training and
1185   context learning, and leverage the PAC framework to provide a theoretical analysis of ICL. The
1186   existing works make a great contribution to explaining ICL, while their connections to empirical
1187   explorations are relatively weak. Our work mainly focuses on making theoretical explanations align
       well with empirical understanding.

       The intuition is related to the basic idea of our intention model: LLMs can infer intentions from
1206
       the demonstrations. This implies that intentions could be recognized. Thus, we collect prompts
1207   with distinct intentions and extract the features of these prompts using different LLMs, leading
1208   to numerous pairs of features and labels representing intentions. Subsequently, we train models
1209   on the training set and evaluate them on the test set. The results are given in Table 2, indicating
1210   that intentions can be accurately recognized. However, leveraging low-level features achieves a
1211   low prediction accuracy. We further explore the relationship between accuracy and the number
1212   of words from the response, i.e., how many words from the output are leveraged as features for
1213   intention prediction. We observe a decrease in accuracy with increased input numbers of words
1214   for all language models and layers. We find that merely using the input prompt achieves the best
1215
       prediction accuracy.
1216   Text description. The collection methodology involves collaboration between humans and LLMs.
1217   We generate initial prompts and group them into 2 to 5 related categories per batch to encourage
1218   LLMs to expand the prompt set, where ChatGPT 4 (OpenAI, 2023) and LLaMA-2 70B (Touvron
1219   et al., 2023) are leveraged. We employed specific strategies to guide ChatGPT in generating high-
1220   quality prompts that are challenging for LLMs to distinguish. This strategy makes verbs in the
1221
       prompts vague, creating hybrid prompts. Specifically, the hybrid prompts allow the keyword “social
       media” to appear across various categories within the same batch, not just limited to the intention
1222
       of “Social Media Content Retrieval.” Moreover, the prompts in the same batch are thematically
1223
       connected by similar topics, e.g., the Olympics, enhancing their contextual relevance. These strate-
1224   gies ensure the prompts are challenging to categorize, maintaining their intended thought without
1225   compromising distinctiveness. All prompts generated by LLMs are subject to manual screening and
1226   verification by two human annotators to ensure quality and relevance.
1227
       Dataset description. We collect 10, 150 samples from 50 distinct intentions to construct a novel
1228
       dataset, where each intention class comprises about 200 samples. As we merely consider the sce-
1229
       nario with non-mixed intention, each sample is devised to elicit a specific intention. In this context,
1230   we can train a model to classify LLMs’ features of these samples. To ensure that the classifier
1231   recognizes intuitions based on intention patterns of features extracted by LLMs rather than specific
1232   keywords in the prompts, we avoid cases where all that is needed to infer the “translation” intention
1233   is to see the word translation. In this context, the development of the dataset should be a detailed
1234   process that integrates both linguistic and cognitive insights. Thus, the dataset is designed to accu-
1235   rately reflect human intuitions by analyzing various language usages and cognitive patterns (Talmy,
1236   2019), where it categorizes LLM intuitions into facets such as retrieval, communication, creativity,
1237   and imitation, among others, providing a human-centric perspective. More details can be found in
1238
       the Appendix F.1.
1239   Experimental setting. In our experiments, we select three 7-billion parameter LLMs to extract
1240   features of prompts: LLaMA-2 (Touvron et al., 2023), Vicuna (Chiang et al., 2023), and Mistral
1241   (Jiang et al., 2023). These models are chosen for their robustness and diversity in architecture,
       allowing for a comprehensive analysis of intuitions across different systems. This selection enables

1243   models are leveraged for intention recognition, where we utilize low-level, mid-level, and high-level
1244   features to explore whether LLMs’ intentions are influenced by deeper or shallower transformer
1245   blocks. For a 32-layer LLM, we define the low-level feature as originating from the 1st layer, the
1246   mid-level feature as originating from the 16th layer, and the high-level feature as originating from
1247   the last (32nd) layer. This allows us to dissect and analyze the contributions of different layers to the
1248   overall intention of the LLMs, providing insights into how various levels of abstraction within the

1250   recognition accuracy. Each layer outputs a hidden state with a dimensionality of 4, 096. We employ

1257   Besides recognizing intention, we further locate intentions in LLMs at an induction heads level,

       Locating strategy. The strategy for locating a certain intention is straightforward. We just need to
       compare the induction heads when the intention of interest is activated and when it is not. This is in-
1271
       herent to the construction of counterfactual examples through causal intervention, which has shown
1272
       outstanding potential in ICL (Goldowsky-Dill et al., 2023; Madaan & Yazdanbakhsh, 2022). Specif-
1273   ically, we can leverage a sample to activate a certain intention while slightly modifying the sample
1274   so that it is unable to activate the intention of interest. Subsequently, we can locate the intention
1275   through the difference in induction heads under the activated and non-activated conditions. To this
1276   end, we employ the elaborate design proposed in Goldowsky-Dill et al. (2023) to realize the strat-
1277   egy. Specifically, given a reference sample, xr , used for activating a certain intention, we construct
1278   a corresponding counterfactual example xc to deactivate the intention with minimal changes to xr .
1279   Subsequently, we replace the induction heads of xr with those of xc , where the heads of xc cannot
1280   activate intentions. Accordingly, we can record the output changes when replacing each head. Thus,
1281
       induction heads that cause drastic changes in outputs are located as the candidate heads related to the
       current intention. Note that it is unclear whether pM (θ|S n (θd ), xt ) or pM (y|S n (θd ), xt , θ) causes
1282
       the change, thus, a fine-grained exploration would be promising, This is related to the exploration
1283
       of internal mechanisms of LLMs (Geva et al., 2021; 2022; Belrose et al., 2023) and out-of-scope of
1284   this work.
