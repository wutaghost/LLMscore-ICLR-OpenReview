009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Causal concept effect estimation is gaining increasing interest in the field of inter-
012            pretable machine learning. This general approach explains the behaviors of ma-
013            chine learning models by estimating the causal effect of human-understandable
014            concepts, which represent high-level knowledge more comprehensibly than raw
015            inputs like tokens. However, existing causal concept effect explanation methods
016            assume complete observation of all concepts involved within the dataset, which
017            can fail in practice due to incomplete annotations or missing concept data. We
018
               theoretically demonstrate that unobserved concepts can bias the estimation of the
               causal effects of observed concepts. To address this limitation, we introduce
019
               the Missingness-aware Causal Concept Explainer (MCCE), a novel framework
020
               specifically designed to estimate causal concept effects when not all concepts are
021            observable. Our framework learns to account for residual bias resulting from miss-
022            ing concepts and utilizes a linear predictor to model the relationships between
023            these concepts and the outputs of black-box machine learning models. It can of-
024            fer explanations on both local and global levels. We conduct validations using
025            a real-world dataset, demonstrating that MCCE achieves promising performance
026            compared to state-of-the-art explanation methods in causal concept effect estima-
027            tion.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Machine learning models explained through concept-based methods are often more intuitive than
033
      those based solely on raw inputs like tokens or pixels (Poeta et al., 2023). Unlike traditional ap-
      proaches that attribute model decisions to low-level features, such as individual pixels in an image
034
      or tokens in text, concept-based methods leverage high-level semantic knowledge derived from these
035
      inputs. These methods facilitate a deeper understanding of how models make decisions by align-
036   ing their internal representations with concepts that are comprehensible to humans. By focusing on
037   high-level concepts, stakeholders can better assess the model’s reasoning process. This is especially
038   significant in areas like healthcare (Cutillo et al., 2020; Rasheed et al., 2022) and finance (Giudici &
039   Raffinetti, 2023; Zhou et al., 2022), where trust and transparency are critical.
040
      The gold standard for assessing a concept-based explanation is comparing its output to the causal
041
      effect of concepts (Wu et al., 2023). Causal effect estimation measures the direct impact of changing
042   a specific concept on the outcome, while holding all other concepts constant. This approach goes
043   beyond simple associations, which are prone to confounding effects, by identifying how altering
044   a specific concept causally influences the model’s predictions (Moraffah et al., 2020). However,
045   current concept-based causal explanation methods usually assume that the entire set of involved
046   concepts is completely observed in the dataset. In reality, the identification of concepts from data
047   can vary between experts or automated systems, and one or many concepts may not be annotated
048   in the entire dataset (Ghorbani et al., 2019). As a result, complete observation and annotation of all
049   relevant concepts are not guaranteed in real-world applications, highlighting the need for methods
050
      that can handle incomplete or missing concept data.
051   In this paper, we conduct a mathematical analysis showing that the presence of unobserved concepts
052   hinders the unbiased estimation of concepts’ causal effects. To address this challenge, we propose
053   a framework called Missingness-aware Causal Concept Explainer (MCCE). MCCE captures the
      impact of unobserved concepts by constructing pseudo-concepts that are orthogonal to observed

082   We summarize our main contributions as follows:
083
084          • We demonstrate that violating the assumption of complete observation of concepts, which
085            is commonly imposed in existing research, can lead to biased estimation of causal concept
086            effects.
087          • We propose the Missingness-aware Causal Concept Explainer (MCCE), to our best knowl-
088            edge, the first concept-based causal effect estimation framework that takes the existence of
089            unobserved concepts into consideration.
090          • Empirical results show that our proposed MCCE achieves promising performance in esti-

092            Meanwhile, it can provide global interpretations of a model and can act as an interpretable
093            white-box prediction model.

095   2   R ELATED WORK
096
097   Explaining the behaviors of black-box machine learning models has been drawing interest from
098   researchers in the past decade. Various methods have been proposed to estimate the contribution
099   of input to models’ output. Learned weights can be used to denote the importance of features
100   (Olden & Jackson, 2002; Zhou et al., 2016; Molnar, 2020). Permutation-based methods evaluate
101   feature importance by measuring how the model’s prediction performance changes when the values
102   of a single feature are randomly shuffled (Altmann et al., 2010; Lundberg, 2017; Smith et al., 2020).
103   Gradient-based methods interpret machine learning models by analyzing the gradients of the model’s
104
      output with respect to its input (Sundararajan et al., 2017; Kim et al., 2018; Srinivas & Fleuret, 2020).
      However, these approaches focus on individual input features instead of summerizing the effect of
105
      high-level semantic concepts.
106
107   The concept-based explanation uses high-level semantic concepts to interpret a black-box machine
      learning model’s behaviors. Koh et al. (2020) introduced the Concept Bottleneck Model, which


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      predicts human-interpretable concepts as intermediate variables first and then uses these predicted
109   concepts to make final predictions with an interpretable model such as a linear regression. Since
110   then, variants of approaches have been developed to build concept-based interpretation frameworks,
111   such as Concept Transformer (Rigotti et al., 2021), Post-hoc Concept Bottleneck Models (Yuksek-
112   gonul et al., 2022), Concept Embedding Models (Zarlenga et al., 2022), Logic Explained Networks
113   (Ciravegna et al., 2023), Probabilistic Concept Bottleneck Models (Kim et al., 2023), Enerby-based
114   Concept Bottleneck Models (Xu et al., 2024), among others.
115   Recent years have witnessed the rising interests in causal concepts effect estimation. Feder et al.
116   (2021) proposed CausalLM to estimate concept-based causal effects by learning counterfactual rep-
117   resentations via adversarial tasks. Ravfogel et al. (2020) introduced Iterative Nullspace Projection
118   to learn the causal effect of a concept, which removes a concept from a representation vector by
119   iteratively training linear classifiers to predict the attribute and projecting it onto the null space.
120   Abraham et al. (2022) not only built a human-validated concept-based dataset with counterfactuals
121   called Causal Estimation-Based Benchmark (CEBaB) but also found that many popular explanation
122   methods, including those described above, can fail to accurately estimate the causal effects of models
123
      on their developed dataset. Wu et al. (2023) developed the Causal Proxy Model (CPM) which mim-
      ics the counterfactual behaviors of a model by creating representations that allow for intervention,
124
      achieving state-of-the-art performance on the CEBaB dataset. However, their approach assumes all
125
      the involved concepts are observed during the development process. Our work provides a theoretical
126   analysis of the impact of unobserved concepts and, motivated by this analysis, proposes a solution
127   to reduce the resulting bias. We also compare the accuracy of causal effect estimation using our
128   proposed method against existing approaches on the CEBaB dataset.

218   Missingness-aware Concept-based Causal Explainer (MCCE)
219   The core idea behind MCCE is to compensate for information missed in observed concepts Cob
220   by harnessing the raw input data. We create vectors, termed pseudo-concepts (denoted as Cpseud ),
221   that are orthogonal to the observed concept vectors using linear transformations from encoded input
222   data. These pseudo-concepts are then combined with the observed concepts to train a linear model
223   that approximates the output of a black-box model. The orthogonality of the pseudo-concepts to the
224   observed concepts prevents collinearity, ensuring that these pseudo-concepts contribute information
225   absent in the observed data.

      4     E XPERIMENTS

272   4.1   DATASET
273
274   We use the CEBaB dataset Abraham et al. (2022) to validate our proposed MCCE. CEBaB contains
275   restaurant reviews from OpenTable for sentiment analysis. Each text in CEBaB received a 5-star sen-
276   timent score from crowd workers and was annotated on four concept levels-ambiance, food, noise,
277   and service, with the labels negative, unknown, and positive. It started with 2,299 original reviews
278   and was expanded to 15,089 texts through modifications by human annotators. These annotators
279
      edited the reviews to reflect specific interventions such as changing food evaluations from positive
      to negative. As far as we know, it is the only dataset with human-verified approximate counterfac-
280
      tual text. The resulting dataset is divided into training, development, and testing partitions. The
281
      development and test sets serve to evaluate explanation methods. The ICaCE-Error of MCCE and
282   the baselines are validated using the test set.
283
284   4.2   MCCE CONSTRUCTION
285
286   We finetune three different types of publicly available models for the multiclass semantic classifica-
287   tion tasks of the CEBaB dataset: the base BERT (Devlin, 2018), the base RoBERTa (Liu, 2019), and
288   Llama-3 (Dubey et al., 2024). These three models are different generations of transformer-based
289   language models designed to capture contextual relationships within text and have been widely used
290   in a wide range of NLP tasks. We use the last hidden states of the cls token for BERT and RoBERTa
291
      as the H vector in Equation 7, and for Llama-3, we use the last hidden states of the last token. To
      explore how unobserved concepts influence the ICaCE-Error across different explainers, we omit
292
      each of the four attributes individually during the construction of MCCE and the baseline models.
293
      Additionally, we exclude every possible pair of concepts from these four attributes.

386   MCCE not only provides causal explanations but also can function as an interpretable predictor.
387   Table 2 displays MCCE’s performance, measured by macro-F1 score, when it is used to directly
388   learn sentimental outcomes instead of interpreting a black-box model’s output. MCCE predictor
389   achieves comparable performance when leveraging BERT and RoBERTa’s hidden states compared

392   remains robust, showing only a marginal decrease, when two concepts are unobserved compared to

      6   D ISCUSSION
433
434
      In this work, we address a critical but previously unexplored question: How can we effectively mea-
435   sure the causal effect of concepts when some are unobserved? We introduce the Missingness-aware
436   Concept-based Causal Explainer (MCCE), the first framework specifically designed to estimate the
437   causal effects of concepts while accounting for the impact of those that are unobserved. MCCE
438   innovatively constructs pseudo-concepts that are column-wise orthogonal to the observed concepts,
439   enriching the model with complementary information that captures the influence of the missing
440   concepts. Our experimental results on the CEBaB dataset demonstrate that MCCE achieves supe-
441   rior or, at the very least, comparable performance to existing baseline methods in scenarios where
442   unobserved concepts are present.
443   Among the baseline methods, the CPM approach is the only one that matches MCCE’s performance
444   on some of the metrics. However, CPM methods depend on labeled counterfactual data for training,
445   which may limit their practical applicability. In contrast, MCCE effectively utilizes only factual
446   training data and does not require counterfactual data. Additionally, the coefficients derived from
447   MCCE’s linear predictor offer a direct, global interpretation of black-box models, a character absent
448   in CPM.
449   The number of pseudo-concepts in MCCE is a hyperparameter that needs to be pre-selected. Em-
450   pirically, we observe that a number of pseudo-concepts comparable to or slightly greater than the
451   number of observed concepts tends to yield the best results. For example, in a scenario where one of
452   four attributes in CEBaB is unobserved, with the remaining three attributes encoded into nine con-
453   cepts (”negative”, ”unknown”, ”positive” for each attribute). MCCE shows optimal performance
454   with nine or twelve pseudo-concepts. The theoretical rationale for the choice of the number of
455
      pseudo-concepts remains a subject for further investigation.
456   One limitation of our work is that MCCE is validated on one dataset. The CEBaB dataset, which,
457   while comprehensive, contains only a limited number of labeled concepts. Though the construc-
458   tion of the orthogonal pseudo-concepts and the linear predictor allows MCCE to cope with a large
459   number of concepts through straightforward modifications according to Fan et al. (2024), further
460   empirical validation is necessary to fully establish its effectiveness across varied datasets. In ad-
461
      dition, MCCE is designed to be modal-agnostic but needs further validation on modalities beyond
      text. Unfortunately, to our knowledge, CEBaB is the sole publicly available dataset that includes the
462
      labeled counterfactual data necessary for assessing causal concept effects. Further validation will be
463
      available only when more benchmark datasets for causal concept effect estimation are available.
