                                                        A BSTRACT
008
009
               We present GenPlan, a novel deep learning architecture for generating architectural
010
               floor plans. GenPlan provides flexibility and precision in room placement, offering
011            architects and developers new avenues for creative exploration. We adapted an
012            autoencoder-like structure comprising of two encoders and four specialized de-
013            coders that predict the centers of different rooms. These predictions are converted
014            into graph along with the other constraints and used as inputs for a Transformer-
015            based graph neural network (GNN), which is responsible for delineating room
016            boundaries and refining the predicted room centers. The Graph Transformer Net-
017            work ensures that the generated floor plans are realistic and executable in real-life.
018            GenPlan’s methodological innovation provides heightened control during the de-
019
               sign phase, serving as a valuable tool for automating and refining the architectural
               design process.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
040
041
      The generation of architectural floor plans is a foundational step in building design, directly impact-
042   ing not only aesthetics but also the functionality and sustainability of living spaces. Traditionally,
043   this process has been time-consuming and prone to human error, particularly as design complexity
044   increases. However, recent advancements in computational design, especially through deep learning,
045   have begun to transform this landscape. Research by Chaillou (2020), Hu et al. (2020), Sun et al.
046   (2022), Wang et al. (2023), Upadhyay et al. (2023), demonstrates the effectiveness of generative ad-
047   versarial networks (GANs) and deep learning models in automating and optimizing layout generation.
048   These advanced methods eliminate the need for brute-force strategies and traditional optimization
049   algorithms, which often struggle to find solutions in a reasonable timeframe. Moreover, traditional
050
      approaches face challenges in achieving optimal metrics, such as golden ratios in spatial design.
      This limitation further highlights the superiority of deep learning models in efficiently generating
051
      optimized designs.
052
053   Moreover, most prior work has incorrectly classified balconies as part of the interior area. In fact,
      balconies are exterior spaces and should not be included in the carpet area or floor area Chow (2002).

054
      Considering the balcony as part of the floor area not only misrepresents the true use of space but also
055   limits the design process, as architects typically do not include balconies in the carpet area when
056   designing a floor plan.
057
058
      Unlike previous methods that primarily focus on generating basic layouts, GenPlan integrates state-
      of-the-art techniques from both convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and graph neural networks
059
      (GNNs) in a sequential manner to ensure conflict-free designs.
060
061   GenPlan consists of two sequential modules: GenCenter and GenLayout. GenCenter utilizes shared
062   ResNet101 encoders and specialized decoders to predict the locations of various room types, including
063   bedrooms, restrooms, and kitchens, based on the given floor plan boundary. These predictions are
064
      then converted into a graph structure that incorporates additional constraints, which serves as input
      for GenLayout, our Transformer-based Graph Neural Network (GNN). This module is responsible
065
      for delineating room boundaries and refining the predicted room centers.
066
067   The GenLayout module ensures that the generated floor plans are both realistic and executable in
068   real-world scenarios. By innovating this methodological approach, GenPlan enhances control during
069   the design phase, positioning itself as a valuable tool for automating and refining the architectural
070
      design process.
071   To illustrate GenPlan’s functionality, consider an architect exploring specific design ideas. They
072   input various constraints, and GenPlan generates multiple layouts with different room counts and
073   configurations. Additionally, the architect can interact with the system during the process, modifying
074   aspects like room location or removing rooms entirely, offering a more flexible and adaptable
075   interactive design experience.

079
080
081
      Researchers have extensively applied deep learning models, such as generative adversarial networks
      (GANs) and variational autoencoders (VAEs) to generate floor plans based on the shape of the floor
082
      and the position of the front door, as demonstrated by Sun et al. (2022), Chaillou (2020), Hu et al.
083
      (2020), Nauata et al. (2021), Wang et al. (2023), Upadhyay et al. (2023), Chen (2022), Ślusarczyk
084
      et al. (2023), Huang & Zheng (2018). However, these models often face challenges in adhering
085   to specific architectural standards or functional requirements. For instance, many previous works
086   incorrectly include balconies as part of the interior area, misclassifying them as floor or carpet areas.
087   This approach limits the design process, as architects typically do not consider balconies in the carpet
088   area when designing a floor plan.
089
      This area of research faces several challenges, such as the need for extensive and diverse datasets to
090
      train more robust models and the integration of user-specific preferences and constraints. Additionally,
091   the development of comprehensive evaluation metrics that effectively assess the usability and quality
092   of generated designs remains crucial.

      3    M ETHODOLOGY
097
098   Once a room center is predicted, a Transformer-Convolutional GNN is employed to predict the
099   rectangular dimensions of the room. This is done using the boundary polygon, front door position,
100   and the predicted room centers from the first phase; the GNN processes this information and outputs
101   the appropriate dimensions for each room, ensuring they fit within the overall floor plan layout and
102   respect spatial relationships between adjacent rooms and boundaries.
103   After the room layout is generated, doors and windows are placed using a similar architecture to
104   the one used for generating room centers, but this time they are trained specifically to generate
105   the positions of doors and windows. Then, all elements—walls, rooms, doors, and windows—are
106   overlaid on top of each other. We apply geometric operations to adjust the layout, including cutting
107   out parts of the room rectangles that extend beyond the floor boundary. This process can result in
      non-rectangular room shapes similar to those commonly seen in real-world floor plans.

      3.1   DATA P REPARATION
109
110   In the development of GenPlan, we created the ResPlan dataset, which comprises 17,000 diverse
111   real-world floor plans sourced from various real estate websites. These floor plans are represented
112   as geometric shapes, which not only significantly reduce data size but also facilitate the seamless
113   conversion of floor plans into various formats, such as graphs and images. By addressing common
114   dataset challenges, such as overly simplistic floor plans and a lack of diversity—where most designs
115   in the RPlan dataset features only a single restroom, and it is rare to encounter a floor plan with
116
      three restrooms or more than three bedrooms, as well as issues with incorrect room connections—the
      ResPlan dataset not only enhances the training process for GenPlan but also establishes a standard
117
      for comparison in this field.
118
119   For the convolutional neural network (CNN), the floor plan boundaries and front door positions are
120   converted into binary masks (see Figure 3). These inputs are critical as they define the minimum
121   constraints required to generate a floor plan. Other inputs, such as room counts, are encoded as
122
      a one-hot encoded 8-channel image representing up to four bedrooms and four bathrooms, which
      are the limits of our dataset and align with most residential floor plan requirements. The area is
123
      encoded in a single channel as a solid binary square, where its length is linearly scaled from 0 to
124
      255, corresponding to a range of 20 to 400 square meters. These inputs are concatenated into an
125   11-channel input for one of the two encoders used in the room center generation component.
126
127   For the graph input version, all elements are converted into a graph structure. The boundary is
128
      represented using nodes for corners and edges for walls. The front door is added as a node that
      intersects one of the wall edges, with each node encoded using one-hot encoding based on its type.
129
      After generating the room centers, they are also included in the graph as nodes, with distinct one-
130
      hot encodings corresponding to room types. Each room node is then connected to the five closest
131   boundary corner nodes with edges. Additionally, all room center nodes are interconnected to facilitate
132   learning about each other’s positions and to avoid conflicts in acquiring areas.
133
134
      3.2   I NITIAL ROOM C OUNT P REDICTION
135
136   A preliminary convolutional neural network (CNN), specifically a pre-trained ResNet18, is utilized
137   to analyze the boundary, front door position, and the total area of the plot to estimate the required
138   number of bedrooms and restrooms. This model leverages historical data and trained parameters to
139   predict an optimal distribution of rooms based on spatial dimensions and entry points. The output
140   guides the spatial distribution within the floor plan:
141
142                  Ncounts = RoomCounter(Boundary, Front Door Position, Area; θcounts )                 (1)
143
144   where Nrooms is the suggested number of bedrooms and restrooms that could fit in this floor area,
145   Boundary is the the floor plan boundary mask, Front Door Position is the designated entry point, and
146   Area is the encoded area of the floor plan boundary.
147   Moreover, while the model provides an automated estimate, users can override these suggestions
148   to exert greater control over the design. This feature is crucial for accommodating specific client
149   requests or adapting to unique architectural challenges, allowing designers to adjust the number of
150   Bedrooms and Restrooms, and hence providing greater diversity in design outcomes.

      predicting the center coordinates of various room types. This shared encoder architecture significantly
      reduces computational redundancy and enhances consistency across the output from the specialized
158
      decoders. The use of a shared encoder ensures that the feature extraction process is uniform and only
159
      needs to be executed once, thus speeding up the prediction process:

168   room centers into a compact feature representation. These images, illustrating centers as circles with

      Initially, the ResNet101-based Layout Encoder was trained with a single general decoder to optimize

      3.5   B LOB D ETECTION FOR C ENTER S EGMENTATION
204
205   After obtaining the single-channel image outputs (Figure 2) from the specialized decoders, we apply
206   the Laplacian of Gaussian (LoG) blob detection technique to identify potential room centers. This
207   method effectively distinguishes multiple blobs in the output images, with each blob representing
208   a potential room center. While all detected blobs are valid candidates, we select the one with the
209   highest intensity as the predicted center. This selection is crucial because, although nearly all these
210   blobs could serve as valid room centers, they are not necessarily designed to coexist in the same
211   layout. Therefore, we choose only one blob to ensure clarity in the design.
212   Once we select a center, we feed it back into the prediction system along with the other generated
213   centers. This iterative process allows the model to determine the most suitable location for the next
214   center based on the context of the existing design. Moreover, by choosing a random center from
215   the produced blobs, we can generate numerous valid designs. Each of these variations remains
      architecturally sound, as demonstrated in Figure 4.

231   3.6   G RAPH -BASED ROOM B OUNDARY D ETERMINATION (G EN L AYOUT )
232
233   This crucial stage in the GenPlan system involves using GenLayout, a graph neural networks
234   (GNN) to precisely delineate and refine room boundaries. The method leverages a composite
235   representation of architectural elements, emphasizing the dynamic integration of spatial data and the
236   adaptability of GNNs to complex layouts. After generating the room centers (x, y) using 3.3, the
237
      Graph Transformer Network is used to determine the actual room shape by predicting the diagonal
      coordinates (x1 , y1 , x2 , y2 ), which are then used to form a rectangle, representing the room’s shape.
238

254   Graph Transformer Convolution is chosen for its advanced capabilities in handling spatial data, which

256

264
265   c) Multi-Head Attention: Although not explicitly shown in the equations, multi-head attention
266
      typically involves computing multiple sets of attention coefficients and aggregating the results. This
      allows the model to capture diverse aspects of the spatial relationships between nodes.

290   3.7   T RAINING
291
      GenPlan was trained in two phases, employing Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss, as the task is
292
      inherently regression-based. The Adam optimizer was utilized with a batch size of 20 and an initial
293
      learning rate of 10−2 , which decayed by a factor of 0.1 every 30 epochs, reaching a minimum learning
294   rate of 10−4 .
295
296   In the first phase, we trained the GenCenter to simultaneously generate centers for all room types.
297
      Random samples and tasks were input into the model, ensuring that each batch contained examples
      from all tasks. This phase spanned a duration of 150 epochs.
298
299   In the second phase, we replicated the decoder four times, with each instance dedicated to a specific
300   room type: bedrooms, restrooms, kitchens, and balconies. After freezing the parameters of the two
301   encoders, each decoder was trained independently for 50 epochs on its corresponding task.
302   A similar architecture was employed for the placement of doors and windows, utilizing only two
303   specialized decoders. This distinction was crucial to ensure the precise positioning of doors and
304   windows, as their locations depend on the room boundaries established earlier. The door and window
305   model was trained using the same two-phase strategy as the primary architecture.
306
      We trained GenLayout, a Transformer-based convolutional GNN, on the graph representation of
307   the input constraints, applying various augmentation methods to slightly shift the centers of rooms.
308   This enabled the model to learn to adjust the center of the generated rectangle if it deviates from the
309   optimal position. GenLayout was trained for 300 epochs, using the same learning rate and batch size
310   as GenCenter.
311
312   3.8   P OST- PROCESSING
313
314   The final layout is assembled by integrating room boundaries within the plot. This process involves
315   aligning and refining boundaries to create clear partitions. Geometric buffering, a key technique
316   used here, extends or contracts room boundaries by a predefined wall width to model the physical
317   dimensions of walls, ensuring clear delineation and structural integrity.
318   The algorithm sets a predefined wall width and adjusts the floor plan layout to accommodate wall
319   thickness using geometric buffering. Rooms are sorted by size, and each is buffered to prevent
320   overlaps, maintaining clear boundaries. The main living area is adjusted similarly, optimizing space
321   usage. Leftover spaces are efficiently integrated back into the layout. Finally, room positions are
322   finalized, and visible wall lines are defined based on buffered placements, ensuring a precise and
323   functional architectural layout. This approach effectively combines advanced machine learning with
      architectural principles, enhancing both the functional aspects of the design.

      4.1   D ISCUSSION
474
475   The GenPlan architecture significantly advances the field of automated architectural design by
476   integrating convolutional and graph neural networks to enhance the generation and delineation of
477   floor plans. This methodology not only accelerates the design process but also introduces precision in
478   handling complex spatial relationships through the use of Transformer Convolution within the GNNs.
479   This system offers a notable improvement over other published methods so far.
480   GenPlan has broad implications beyond architectural design. In urban planning, it can enhance
481   the accuracy and efficiency of safety simulations and city layouts. Robotics applications could
482   benefit from more precise environment mapping for better navigation and task performance. In the
483   gaming industry, GenPlan can add realism and variety to game environments, improving player
484   immersion. The film industry could use it to design set layouts and virtual environments more
485   efficiently, potentially reducing production costs and time. These applications demonstrate GenPlan’s
      potential to transform various industries by providing advanced design and simulation capabilities.

      As future work, we plan to introduce additional dimensional constraints to make the system more
487   capable of addressing real-life situations. This includes considering the orientation of the floor
488   boundary and the geographical location to better utilize natural elements, such as sunlight for
489   illumination and wind for cooling. We aim to achieve this by reverse engineering the ResPlan dataset
490   to determine the optimal wind direction for each floor plan. The same applies to neighboring walls,
491   as many complex floor plans arise from fitting multiple layouts within a single story, resulting in
492   shared walls that cannot accommodate windows or doors. While ResPlan already contains initial data
493   on neighboring walls, we are still in the process of refining this feature, so it has not been included in
494   the current version.

496   4.2   C ONCLUSION
497
      In this work, we introduce GenPlan, a deep learning framework for the generation of realistic floor
498
      plans, tailored for use by architects, game designers, and developers. GenPlan harnesses the power
499
      of Transformer-based Graph Neural Networks (GNN) to enhance the precision of design outputs.
500   The system is architected in a modular fashion, allowing for mid-process interaction to ensure the
501   validity of the floor plan at each step prior to further progression. Moreover, GenPlan offers the ability
502   to generate multiple valid designs for the same set of input constraints, representing a significant
503   advancement in floor plan generation.
