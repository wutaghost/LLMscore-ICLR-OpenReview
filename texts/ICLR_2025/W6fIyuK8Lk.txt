009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            A change in the input data stream of a machine-learning model is referred to as a
012            data drift and may impact the model’s accuracy. This paper proposes a framework
013            to detect data drifts, identify the type of drift, and estimate the drift magnitude
014            that occur in the input data stream of image classification neural networks due to
015            various effects. It applies to any type of drift that occurs in images due to various
016
               factors such as noise, weather, etc. A novel statistical method is proposed for drift
               magnitude estimation. The method relies on the change in the prediction proba-
017
               bility distributions of the predicted classes in the classification network caused by
018
               the data drift. The drift magnitude is estimated by applying a set of thresholds to
019            the prediction probabilities. The drift type is identified using a classification neu-
020            ral network. Experimental results obtained using various datasets, drift types, and
021            neural network architectures show that the proposed framework can accurately de-
022            tect data drifts, accurately identify the drift type, and estimate the drift magnitude
023            with a very low quantization error.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      As a result of the rapid advancement in artificial intelligence and computing power, the adoption of
028
      machine intelligence in modern applications is continually growing. Computer vision stands out as
029
      one of the prominent areas where machine intelligence is widely applied. Image classification is a
030   fundamental computer vision task accomplished through machine learning techniques. There are
031   advanced Deep Neural Network (DNN) models for image classification that have achieved remark-
032   able success, such as AlexNet (Krizhevsky et al. (2012)), VGG (Simonyan & Zisserman (2014)),
033   GoogleNet (Szegedy et al. (2015)), and ResNet (He et al. (2016)). Utilization of image classification
034   DNNs has become more prevalent across a broad range of applications. For instance, self-driving
035   cars (Ni et al. (2020)), mobile computing devices (Zhang et al. (2018)), medical imaging (Sarva-
036   mangala & Kulkarni (2022)), surveillance (Kaljahi et al. (2019)), defect detection (Czimmermann
037   et al. (2020)), etc.
038   Machine learning models such as DNNs are trained using data-driven statistical learning schemes.
039   During the operation time, a model is expected to exhibit similar accuracy as in the training phase
040   assuming a static distribution of the input data. However, the real-world application environments
041   are profoundly dynamic and continuously changing. The assumption of a static distribution of the
042   data is not valid in many of the applications, making it challenging for the models to maintain a
043   high accuracy during their operation. A change in the distribution of the input data that impacts the
044
      accuracy of a machine learning model is referred to as data drift. Such data drifts occur over time
      due to various reasons and the model accuracy may drop below the safety margin.
045
046   Image data streams are highly susceptible to data drifts due to noise effects (such as Gaussian,
047   Poisson, Salt & Pepper, Speckle), weather effects (such as Snow, Fog, Rain, Shadow), degradation
048   of the camera, change in lighting, etc. Such data drifts impact the accuracy and may compromise the
049   system reliability. The accuracy degradation of the image classification neural networks due to data
050
      drifts is discussed by Hashmani et al. (2019). Diagnosing data drifts occur in image classification
      neural networks is an essential aspect of maintaining the system reliability.
051
052   Detection and magnitude estimation are two main aspects of the drift diagnosis process (Webb et al.
053   (2016), Webb et al. (2017)). There exist many approaches for the detection of data drifts in various
      application domains (Yang et al. (2020), Suprem et al. (2020), Dube & Farchi (2020), Ackerman


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      et al. (2021), Senarathna et al. (2023)). It is important to know the drift magnitude for tasks such
055   as determining the detection threshold, appropriate resource allocation, model performance assess-
056   ment, model adaptation, risk management, etc. The detection and magnitude estimation of drifts in
057   one-dimensional data can be done by computing the distance between the distribution of the training
058   data and the production data (Goldenberg & Webb (2019)). Various distance matrices are used to
059   identify data drifts in one-dimensional data. For instance, Kullback-Leibler Divergence (Kullback
060   & Leibler (1951)) and Hellinger Distance (Hoens et al. (2011)) are used by Webb et al. (2016), To-
061   tal Variation Distance (Levin & Peres (2017)) is used by Webb et al. (2017), and Energy Distance
062   (Rizzo & Székely (2016)) is used by Yang et al. (2020). However, these matrices cannot be directly
063
      applied to higher dimensional data with spatial correlations, such as images.
064   There are methods in the literature for the detection of data drifts in image data streams (Suprem
065   et al. (2020), Dube & Farchi (2020), Ackerman et al. (2021)). Some methods use a low-dimensional
066   feature vector extracted from the image to address the challenge of processing the high-dimensional
067   image data (Suprem et al. (2020)). The method by Suprem et al. (2020) maps the images into a low-
068   dimensional feature vector using a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) and detects data drifts
069
      by applying clustering on the low-dimensional feature vector. The method by Dube & Farchi (2020)
      uses the divergence score between the average feature vectors of the training dataset and the pro-
070
      duction dataset to detect data drifts in image classification neural networks. Ackerman et al. (2021)
071
      proposed a method in to detect data drifts using the prediction probability i.e. the maximum class
072   probability. The change in the prediction probability distribution resulting from the data drift was
073   employed to detect a data drift by Ackerman et al. (2021). However, the methods by Suprem et al.
074   (2020), Dube & Farchi (2020), Ackerman et al. (2021) do not estimate the drift magnitude, and they
075   only consider the drifts due to the appearance of unseen classes in classification neural networks.
076   There exist methods that estimate the magnitude of various noise effects in images (Pyatykh et al.
077   (2012), Chuah et al. (2017), Wang et al. (2021)). However, those methods rely on the statistical
078   properties of the noise model, and therefore they only apply to a particular noise type.
079   The method proposed by Senarathna et al. (2023) also relies on the change in the prediction prob-
080   ability distribution due to the data drift and estimates the drift magnitude by applying thresholds
081   to the prediction probabilities. However, Senarathna et al. (2023) assumed that the distribution of
082   the images among the classes, referred to as the class distribution, remains unchanged in training
083   and production data. This assumption often does not hold in real-world application environments.
084   In this paper, we address this limitation and propose a method that can cope with varying class
085   distributions.
086   To address the problem of varying class distribution of the images in the input data stream, we com-
087   bine the thresholding criterion proposed by Senarathna et al. (2023) with an approach that estimates
088   the number of images from each class in the input data stream. The process of estimating the num-
089   ber of samples from each class in an input data stream of a machine learning model is referred to as
090   Quantification (Forman (2005)). With that, we derive a generalized method to estimate the magni-
091   tude of data drifts due to any type of effect under varying class distributions. The proposed method
092
      relies on the change in the prediction probabilities of the neural network caused by the data drift.
      It is a statistical method that operates on a batch of images and estimates a discrete magnitude for
093
      the batch. Using the proposed drift magnitude estimation method, we developed a comprehensive
094
      drift detection framework that detect data drifts, identify the type of drift, and estimate the drift
095   magnitude in the input data stream of image classification neural networks due to various effects.
096   Drift type is identified using a secondary classification neural network that runs in parallel with the
097   primary classification network. The drift type is detected from a set of potential drift types and the
098   drift magnitude is estimated from a set of potential drift magnitudes.
099
      The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 presents preliminaries and Section 3 de-
100
      scribes the proposed method. Section 4 discusses the experimental results and conclusions are pre-
101   sented in Section 5.

104   2   P RELIMINARIES
105
106   Changes in the input data distribution of a machine learning model are called concept drifts. A con-
107   cept drift is a change that occurs in the relationship between the input features and the target variable
      of a machine learning model over time in an unforeseen manner. Concept drifts are categorized as


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      real concept drifts, covariate drifts, and novel class appearance (Hoens et al. (2012), Gama et al.
109   (2014), Webb et al. (2016), Webb et al. (2017)). A real concept drift, which is also referred to as
110   class drift or prior probability shift, is a change in the relation between the input and output. A co-
111   variate drift, which is also referred to as virtual drift, is a change in the input data distribution. The
112   novel class appearance is a previously unseen new class appears in the input. This paper specifically
113   focuses on covariate drifts that occur in image classification neural networks due to various effects
114   such as noise effects and weather effects among others. Furthermore, we consider “full concept
115   drifts” where every input in the input data stream is equally affected with the same drift magnitude
116   (Webb et al. (2016)).
117   To understand the impact of data drifts on image classification neural networks, let us consider the
118   impact of Gaussian noise on a classification network trained on the MNIST handwritten digit image
119   dataset, detailed in Section 4, under the effect of Gaussian noise. Drift magnitude is represented by
120   the variance σ 2 of the Gaussian noise. The network had an accuracy of 99.10% on clean images
121   i.e. σ 2 = 0. Accuracy gradually declined as the drift magnitude increased and dropped below 80%
122   when the images were affected by the Gaussian noise of σ 2 = 0.20. See also Figure A.1 in the
123
      Appendix.
124   Softmax is the activation function used in the output layer of classification neural networks. The
125   softmax activation provides a normalized distribution of probabilities among the output classes and
126   the predicted class for a given input is the class that has the maximum softmax probability. The
127   maximum softmax class probability is referred to as the prediction probability and it is an indication
128   of the confidence of the prediction made by the classifier. The distribution of the prediction prob-
129
      abilities changes when a data drift occurs. Hendrycks & Gimpel (2016) showed that the average
      prediction probability decreases when out-of-distribution data is fed into a neural network classifier.
130

      in Figure 1b are different. The thresholding-based criterion proposed by Senarathna et al. (2023) to

      The proposed drift detection approach considers a batch of images at a time. A set of potential
      drift types and a set of potential discrete drift magnitudes per each drift type are considered. The
184
      approach detects the presence of a data drift, identifies the drift type (i.e. effect type) and estimates
185
      the drift magnitude for a batch of images. First, a drift magnitude is estimated for each potential
186   drift type. Based on the estimated magnitudes for each type, it is determined whether a data drift has
187   occurred or not. If a data drift has occurred, then the drift type is identified using the type detection
188   criterion. The drift magnitude corresponds to the estimated magnitude of the detected drift type.
189
      For a given drift type, the magnitude is estimated based on the percentage of images in each predicted
190
      class with a prediction probability above a predetermined threshold. The thresholds are computed
191
      using the method proposed by Senarathna et al. (2023). We first describe the threshold computation
192   criterion and the application of the thresholds assuming a static class distribution in the image data
193   stream.
194
195
      The percentage of images with prediction probability higher than an appropriately selected thresh-
      old differs at different drift magnitudes. Consider the CDFs shown in Figure 1a of the prediction
196
      probabilities of the images predicted as class 1 in the MNIST classifier. The noticeable difference in
197
      the cumulative sum at different drift magnitudes indicates the possibility to distinguish among the
198   different drift magnitudes by choosing an appropriate threshold. For instance, let us consider the
199   number of class 1 predictions with prediction probability ≥ 0.9 at different Gaussian noise magni-
200   tudes in Figure 1a. The corresponding percentage for σ 2 = 0, σ 2 = 0.04, σ 2 = 0.08, and σ 2 = 0.12
201   were 99.3%, 95.4%, 86.0%, and 71.1% respectively. The drift level is recognized based on these
202   differences in the percentages above some threshold probability.
203
      Let T denote the drift type and M denote the drift magnitude. Let m be the number of non-zero
204   drift magnitudes. For a potential drift type T , multiple threshold probability values are computed.
205   In particular, a different threshold is computed for each predicted class C, for each drift magnitude
206   M of the drift type T . Let us consider the CDFs shown in Figure 1. It can be observed that the
207   probability that has the highest contrast among the CDFs of consecutive drift levels varies based on
208   the drift magnitude and the predicted class. Therefore, a different threshold is computed per each
209   predicted class C and per each drift magnitude M such that the difference among the percentages
210   above the thresholds of consecutive drift levels is maximized. Hence, a better distinguishability
211   among the adjacent drift levels is achieved.
212   Let τC,M denote the threshold probability for the predicted class C, drift magnitude M where 1 ≤
213   C ≤ n and 0 ≤ M ≤ m. For some drift type T , the thresholds τC,M for each predicted class C
214   and each drift magnitude M are computed as follows. First, the CDFs of the prediction probability
215   are obtained for each predicted class C considering each drift magnitude M . Let FC,M denote the
      CDF of the predicted class C for drift magnitude M . FC,M is a discrete function of the prediction

      As was mentioned in Section 2, the number of images predicted as a particular class and the pre-
239   diction probability distribution of a class depends on the class distribution in the input data stream.
240   Equation (1) indicates that the expected percentage Pi,M above the threshold τi,M for the predicted
241   class C = i also depends on the number of images from each class i.e. the class distribution. When
242   the class distribution changes, the expected percentage PC,M and the observed percentage P̂C,M
243   are not comparable. To address this challenge, we apply quantification to estimate the number of
244   images from each class.
245
      The proposed quantification method is a modified version of the readme method by Hopkins &
246
      King (2010). The readme method estimates the class distribution using the number of samples
247   observed in each predicted class. In contrast, the proposed quantification method estimates the class
248   distribution using the number of samples observed in each predicted class before and after applying
249   the thresholds. The method considers the relation between the number of images predicted as a
250   particular class and the number of images from each class, before and after thresholds are applied.
251   The drift magnitude is estimated based on the quantification output.

297   For an accurate estimation of the drift magnitude, the drift type needs to be correctly identified when
298   there is more than one potential drift type. First, we apply the magnitude estimation considering
299   every potential drift type. If a majority of the drift types indicate a non-zero magnitude, we use a
300
      structured approach to detect the drift type. The approach relies on a classification neural network
      trained to detect the drift type, referred to as the type detection network, and the minimum residual
301
      value of the magnitude estimation in each drift type. The type detection network classifies every
302
      image in the input stream into one of the N potential drift types. It is trained to identify the drift
303   type present in an image, regardless of the drift magnitude. For better accuracy, it is trained using
304   only the drifted images without including the clean images. Therefore, the type detection network
305   output is used only when the magnitude estimation indicates a non-zero drift magnitude.
306
      The complete flow of the proposed drift detection framework is as follows. The first step is to
307
      determine if a data drift has occurred in the input image stream. For a given batch of images, it is
308
      determined as a data drift has occurred if more than α types indicate a non-zero magnitude after
309   applying the magnitude estimation. If a data drift is present, a scoring criterion is used to identify
310   the drift type, and the drift type with the highest score is selected. The score is computed based
311   the output of the type detection network and the minimum residual of each type. The percentage
312   of the images detected as a particular drift type by the type detection network is used as one score
313   value. Let st,T denote the type detection network percentage for type T . The minimum residual
314   value of each drift type is normalized with respect to the lowest residual value among all drift types.
315   The percentage above the lowest residual is added as a negative score. sr,T denote the residual
316   score for type T . The total score sT of the drift type T is the summation of st,T and sr,T , i.e.,
317
      sT = st,T + sr,T . The drift type corresponds to the type with the highest score and the magnitude
      corresponds to the magnitude with the minimum residual value of the detected drift type. See also
318
      Algorithm 1 in the Appendix.

322
323   The proposed drift detection framework was validated by considering the data drifts that commonly
      occur in image data streams due to various types of effects. We experimented with three noise

      effects, namely Gaussian, Poisson, and Salt & Pepper, and three weather effects, namely Snow,
325   Fog, and Rain. Experimental results are presented considering the six drift types on the MNIST,
326   CIFAR10, and CIFAR100 datasets. Clean images without any data drift and images of different
327   drift magnitudes were considered for each type.
328
      For the MNIST dataset, we used a lightweight CNN that resembles the VGG architecture by (Si-
329
      monyan & Zisserman (2014)). The network consisted of two convolutional layers, followed by a
330
      maxpooling layer, followed by a fully connected layer, followed by the output layer. It was trained
331   using 60,000 images. For the CIFAR10 dataset, we use the ResNet18 architecture (He et al. (2016)).
332   For the CIFAR100 dataset, we use the network was trained using 50,000 images. The ResNet50
333   architecture (He et al. (2016)). The network was trained using 60,000 images. CIFAR100 vali-
334   dation dataset only has 100 images per-class. Such a small number of samples does not provide
335   sufficient statistical confidence, resulting in instability in the linear equation system. Therefore, a
336   shadow network of the primary classifier was trained on the twenty super-classes of the CIFAR100
337   dataset, and drift detection was performed using the shadow network output. The accuracy of the
338   MNIST, CIFAR10, and CIFAR100 (shadow network) classifiers were 99.1%, 86.7%, and 90.4%
339
      respectively.
340   The validation datasets consisting of 10,000 images in each were used in the drift magnitude estima-
341   tions. 60% of the validation dataset was used to compute the thresholds (τC,M ), expected percent-
342   ages above the thresholds (PC,M ), and the coefficient matrices (A1 and A2 ). Magnitude estimations
343   were done using the remaining 40%. The architecture of the type detection network was the same as
344   the architecture of the image classification network of each dataset. They were trained with transfer
345
      learning using the weights of the primary classification network.
346   The Drift magnitude of each noise effect was represented by the corresponding model parameter
347   of the noise type as follows. In Gaussian with σ 2 , in Poisson with 1/λ, and in Salt & Pepper with
348   σ 2 . Figure A.4 shows an example MNIST image affected by the noise effects compared with the
349   original clean image.
350   The drift magnitude of each weather effect was represented using the values within the range [0, 1].
351   Weather effects were added to the images using the Albumentations library Buslaev et al. (2020).
352   Figure A.5 shows an example CIFAR10 image affected by the weather effects compared with the
353   original clean image.

      In the first experiment, a higher number of drift magnitudes were considered for each drift type
355   within a selected range. In the second experiment, a lesser number of drift magnitudes compared to
356   the first experiment were considered for each drift type within the same range.

      The magnitude estimation method was evaluated considering twenty different random class distri-
      butions for each drift magnitude. A random class distribution was created as follows for a given
359
      magnitude. A total of 400 images from each class was considered, and a random percentage of the
360
      total images in each class was chosen to create a batch of images with a random class distribution.
361   twenty such random distributions were considered for each magnitude. The class distribution of

517   5   C ONCLUSION
518
519   A novel framework is proposed in this paper for detecting data drifts occur in the input stream of
520   image classification neural networks due to various effects. The framework can detect data drifts,
521   identify the type of drift, and estimate the drift magnitude in occur in image data streams. It is
522   consisting of a classification network that detects the drift type and a novel statistical method that
523   estimates the drift magnitude. The magnitude estimation method relies on the change in the predic-
524
      tion probability distribution caused by the data drift. The drift magnitude is estimated by applying
      a set of thresholds to the prediction probabilities. Thresholds are applied based on the predicted
525
      class, and the magnitude is estimated using the percentage of predictions above the threshold in
526
      each class. Experimental evaluation was conducted considering three different classification neural
527   networks trained on MNIST, CIFAR10, and CIFAR100 datasets. Data drifts of different magnitudes
528   that occur in images caused by various types of noise effects and weather effects were considered
529   in the evaluation. Results indicated that the proposed framework can detect data drifts with a high
530   accuracy, while accurately identifying the drift type and estimating the drift magnitude with a very
531   low quantization error.
