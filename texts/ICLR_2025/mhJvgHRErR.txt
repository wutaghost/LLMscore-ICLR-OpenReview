009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Quantitative analysis of animal behavior and biomechanics requires accurate ani-
012            mal pose and shape estimation across species, and is important for animal welfare
013            and biological research. However, the small network capacity of previous meth-
014            ods and limited multi-species dataset leave this problem underexplored. To this
015            end, this paper presents AniMer to estimate animal pose and shape using Trans-
016
               former, enhancing the reconstruction accuracy of diverse quadrupedal species.
               AniMer aims to unify the understanding of various quadrupedal forms within
017
               a single framework, overcoming the limitations of traditional methods that fo-
018
               cus on narrow specific species. A key feature of AniMer is its integration of
019            a high-capacity Transformer-based backbone, which significantly boosts perfor-
020            mance. To effectively train AniMer, we aggregate most available open-sourced
021            quadrupedal datasets, either with 3D or 2D labels, Further, we introduce Ctr-
022            lAni3D, a novel large-scale synthetic dataset created through a diffusion-based
023            conditional image generation pipeline, consisting of about 10k images with pixel-
024            aligned SMAL labels. In total, we get 41.3k annotated images for training and val-
025            idation. The combination of a robust backbone and an expansive dataset enables
026            AniMer to outperform existing methods on the multi-species Animal3D dataset
027
               and several single-species dog benchmarks. Experiments on the unseen Animal
               Kingdom dataset further demonstrate the effectiveness of CtrlAni3D in enhancing
028
               the generalization ability of AniMer. Our study, through the development of Ani-
029
               Mer and CtrlAni3D, underscores the significance of a large-capacity backbone and
030            AI-driven synthetic data generation in advancing animal pose estimation research.
031            Code and data will be released upon publication.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Animal pose and shape estimation from images is essential for capturing animal behavior, biome-
037   chanics, and interactions with their environment, thereby yielding vital insights for animal welfare,
038   agricultural practices and life sciences. The integration of geometric and appearance information
039
      from diverse species into a unified deep neural network represents a significant step to compre-
      hensively interpret the intricate and dynamic animal world. A notable endeavor in this domain
040
      involves the estimation of pose and shape parameters from an articulated animal template known
041
      as the SMAL model (Zuffi et al., 2017), primarily targeting general quadrupeds. While extensive
042   researches have been conducted regarding single species or families, such as horses (Zuffi et al.,
043   2024; Li et al., 2021; Zuffi et al., 2019) and dogs (Sabathier et al., 2024; Rüegg et al., 2023; Rueegg
044   et al., 2022; Li & Lee, 2021; Biggs et al., 2020; 2019), investigations into other quadrupedal species,
045   including cats, cows or hippos, remain relatively underexplored.
046
      The reconstruction of multiple species within a single network is hampered by the limited capacity
047
      of backbones and the scarcity of multi-species datasets annotated with SMAL labels. Recent ad-
048   vancements in human mesh recovery via the SMPL model (Loper et al., 2015) have demonstrated
049   that using a high-capacity backbone in conjunction with large-scale datasets significantly enhances
050   the accuracy of human pose and shape estimation in diverse settings (Goel et al., 2023; Pavlakos
051   et al., 2024; Cai et al., 2024). However, this simple and effective paradigm remains untested within
052   the context of animal studies. Xu et al. (2023a) introduced the first large-scale dataset named An-
053   imal3D featuring SMAL mesh annotations; nevertheless, their study predominantly utilized tradi-
      tional CNN-based networks such as HMR (Kanazawa et al., 2018) and WLDO (Biggs et al., 2020).

054
      In this paper, we present AniMer, a systematic approach pursuing accurate animal pose and shape
055   estimation using Transformer. AniMer surpasses existing methodologies on both the multi-species
056   Animal3D dataset and several single-species dog benchmarks. This success rely on two key scaling
057   factors: scaled backbone and scaled dataset. In previous animal researches, the well-known scaled
058   Transformer backbone proposed by ViT (Xu et al., 2023b) has only been used for 2D animal pose
059   estimation. We go beyond it by connecting ViT with a Transformer-based decoder to yield SMAL
060   parameters, following the practice of HMR2.0 (Goel et al., 2023). To make such framework work for
061   animals whose available data space and prior knowledge are limited compared to human, we modify
062   the residual parameter decoding commonly used for human mesh recovery (Kanazawa et al., 2018;
063
      Goel et al., 2023) to direct parameter decoding, and adopt a two-stage training scheme to train on
      3D dataset first instead of training on full dataset directly.

      To obtain scaled dataset, we aggregate most available open-sourced quadrupedal datasets, resulting
088
      in a comprehensive set of 41.3k images annotated with either 3D mesh or 2D keypoints. Within
089
      the full expansive dataset, an important part is our newly proposed multi-species 3D animal dataset
090   termed CtrlAni3D. It is a synthetic dataset generated by a novel rendering pipeline that extracts
091   AI knowledge regarding animal geometry and appearance from a diffusion-based conditional im-
092   age generation methodology. Specifically, we prompt ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023) with textual
093   descriptions of animal behaviors and rendered SMAL mask and depth images, resulting in highly
094   realistic visual outputs, as illustrated in Fig. 1. To ensure dataset quality, we use SAM2.0 (Ravi et al.,
095   2024) together with manual verification to filter implausible generated images. Finally, CtrlAni3D
096   comprises 9711 images annotated with pixel-aligned SMAL mesh, and its scalable nature allows for
097   further expansion. In contrast to traditional CG pipelines for synthetic data generation (Cao et al.,
098
      2019; Xu et al., 2023a), CtrlAni3D exhibits superior rendering quality and reduced labor intensity.
099   To rigorously evaluate the efficacy of the AniMer model and the CtrlAni3D dataset, we conduct
100   extensive empirical studies. Our findings indicate that AniMer, when trained on the same full multi-
101   species datasets, significantly outperforms CNN-based methods such as HMR and WLDO (Biggs
102   et al., 2020), which serve as baselines in the Animal3D research. Notably, AniMer demonstrates
103   improved performance over previous methods on dog pose and shape estimation, emphasizing its
104
      representation ability on single species tasks. Through comprehensive ablation studies, we verify
      that CtrlAni3D enhances the generalization abilities of AniMer on the unseen Animal Kingdom
105
      dataset (Ng et al., 2022).
106
107   In conclusion, AniMer represents a substantial advancement toward a generalized 3D understand-
      ing of animals, characterized by its simple yet effective design principles. Our contributions are

      Animal Pose and Shape Estimation. In this paper, we concentrate on template-based methods
      instead of template-free methods (Yang et al., 2022a; Yao et al., 2022; Jakab et al., 2024; Li et al.,
120
      2024b). Reconstructing the shape and pose of animals has been studied on various kinds of animal
121
      families such as birds (Wang et al., 2021), mouse (An et al., 2023), non-human primates (Neverova
122   et al., 2020) and quadrupeds (Zuffi et al., 2018). Although we focus on quadrupeds in this paper, the
123   proposed strategy could apply to other animal families. For quadrupeds, Zuffi et al. (2017) proposes
124   a well-known parametric model SMAL, which is built upon 41 scans of animal toys. Due to the
125   limited geometry accuracy of SMAL for representing specific species, most previous methods stride
126   over predicting parametric SMAL parameters only and further enhance the geometry of horses and
127   dogs. For example, Li et al. (2021) designs the hSMAL model specific to horse and applies it
128   to the problem of lameness detection from video. VAREN (Zuffi et al., 2024) further improves
129   hSMAL with super high quality horse scans. Similarly, using SMAL for dog mesh recovery is
130
      addressed by adding bone lengths control (Biggs et al., 2020), by adding per-vertex deformation (Li
      & Lee, 2021), by introducing breed loss (Rueegg et al., 2022), by ground contact constraints (Rüegg
131
      et al., 2023), or by temporal avatar optimization (Sabathier et al., 2024). Though super high quality
132
      reconstruction has been achieved on horses and dogs, end-to-end SMAL estimation has not been
133   fully investigated on other challenging species. Animal3D (Xu et al., 2023a) provides the first large
134   scale 3D benchmark for general quadruped SMAL estimation, yet neglects in-depth research on the
135   network itself. Therefore, our proposed AniMer delves into the network design choice of SMAL
136   recovery, and could be integrated with different geometry enhancing methods summarized above to
137   further reduce geometric errors.
138
      Transformer Based Human Mesh Recovery. The Transformer architecture (Vaswani, 2017) has
139   revolutionized the field of Natural Language Processing (NLP) by enabling unprecedented accuracy
140   and efficiency in a wide range of tasks. Inspired by its success in NLP, one of the core part of Trans-
141   former, i.e. self-attention, has been widely used for human mesh recovery (Kocabas et al., 2021;
142   Wan et al., 2021; Shen et al., 2023; Shin et al., 2024). Dosovitskiy (2020) proposes Vision Trans-
143   former(ViT), which divides an image into patches as the input to Transformer. ViT has achieved
144   state-of-the-art performance on several computer vision tasks, including human mesh recovery us-
145   ing SMPL (Kocabas et al., 2021; Wan et al., 2021; Shen et al., 2023; Goel et al., 2023; Shin et al.,
146   2024). Among all these works, HMR2.0 (Goel et al., 2023) is a milestone which demonstrates the
147
      effectiveness of simply using ViT backbone and large scale datasets to achieve accurate mesh recov-
      ery and in-the-wild generalization ability. Inspired by this, HaMeR (Pavlakos et al., 2024) employs
148
      ViT backbone to achieve highly accurate hand mesh recovery, which is further extended to interact-
149
      ing hands (Lin et al., 2024). Similarly, SMPLer-X (Cai et al., 2024) scales up expressive human pose
150   and shape estimation using ViT backbone and the combination of 32 datasets. Although impressive
151   results have been achieved in human mesh recovery, the effect of ViT backbone for animal pose and
152   shape estimation remains unexplored.
153
      Synthetic Animal Training. Compared to human pose estimation which benefits from large-scale
154
      datasets, acquiring annotated images of animals is significantly more difficult. Therefore, synthetic
155
      dataset would alleviate this problem by rendering the input and output simultaneously. However, all
156   previous methods only attempt to render RGB images or depth images using traditional computer
157   graphics pipelines, ignoring the image hallucination ability of generative AI models such as stable
158   diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) or ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023). Specifically, Mu et al. (2020)
159   utilizes animal CAD models to generate animal images with 2D keypoints and part segmentation
160   for training. RGBD-dog (Kearney et al., 2020) synthesizes depth images with labels to train depth-
161   based 3D skeleton prediction network. BITE (Rüegg et al., 2023) builds semi-synthetic 3D test
      dataset based on scans of real dogs. DigiDogs (Shooter et al., 2024) generates outdoor dog videos


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      together with GT labels using Grand Theft Auto (GTA) game engine. Different from above methods
163   which only focus on single species or family, (Xu et al., 2023a) renders multi-species images using
164   textured SMAL meshes with vertex deformations obtained by SMALR (Zuffi et al., 2018). Although
165   above traditional CG rendering achieves success in assisting network training, the plausibility of
166   rendered images is hindered by the coarse texture quality and sophisticated lighting and shadow
167   control.

184   3.1   PRELIMINARIES
185
186   The SMAL Model. The SMAL model, denoted as M(β, θ, γ), is a 3D parametric shape model
187   designed for quadrupeds. The shape parameter β ∈ R41 is derived from 41 3D scans of various
188   animal figurines, including cats, dogs, horses, cows, and hippos (Zuffi et al., 2017). The pose
189   parameter θ ∈ R35×3 represents the rotation of each joint relative to its parent joint, expressed
190
      in terms of axis-angle. Controlled by β and θ, the SMAL model outputs a mesh with vertices
      V ∈ R3889×3 and faces F ∈ N7774×3 through linear blend skinning (LBS) process. The animal
191
      body joints are regressed from vertices by J ∈ R35×3 = W · V , where W ∈ R35×3889 represents a
192
      linear mapping matrix.
193
194   Camera Projection. Following Goel et al. (2023), π(·) represents the projection process of a
195   weak-perspective camera model, which is determined by a translation vector T ∈ R3 , a fixed focal
196
      length f = 1000 and thereby a fixed intrinsic matrix K. The global orientation R is the rotation of
      root joint, therefore we ignore it here. Consequently, a 3D point X is projected as 2D point x by
197
      x = π(X) = Π(K(X + T )), where Π is typical perspective projection procedure.

      3.2   T HE ARCHITECTURE OF A NI M ER
201
      The full architecture of AniMer is shown in Fig. 2. Specifically, given an image I, we first uti-
202   lize a ViT encoder to extract image feature tokens F. We then feed the feature tokens F into a
203   SMAL transformer decoder to obtain a feature vector f . Finally, independent multi-layer percep-
204   trons (MLPs) are used to predict the shape parameter β̂, pose parameter θ̂, and the camera parameter
205   π̂, where ˆ· means estimated parameters. Note that the weights of ViT encoder are pre-trained on Im-
206   ageNet (Deng et al., 2009) using Masked Autoencoders (MAE) (He et al., 2022), which significantly
207   enhances mesh recovery performance.
208
      To make such Transformer-based structure works well for animals, AniMer features two key dif-
209
      ferences compared with previous Transformer-based human or hand mesh recovery methods. First,
210   different from HMR2.0 (Goel et al., 2023) and HaMeR (Pavlakos et al., 2024) which regress the
211   residual SMPL/MANO parameters with respect to the non-zero mean parameters computed from
212   large scale motion databases, we choose to directly decode the final SMAL parameters due to lim-
213   ited SMAL pose prior. Second, both HMR2.0 and HaMeR train on the whole datasets in single
214   stage. Instead, we train AniMer in two stages. In the first stage, we train AniMer using only 3D data
215   to ensure the network could predict feasible shapes and poses. In the second stage, all 3D and 2D
      data are introduced for training. The insight is that the size of 3D datasets for animal is much smaller


                                                                 4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      than that of human at present, resulting in an imbalanced 3D and 2D data scale. Unless otherwise
217   specified, we train two stages for 500 and 700 epochs respectively.

221   To align animal images with reconstruction results, we train our model using a comprehensive loss
222   function that incorporates various 2D and 3D annotations. We define the main loss function Ltotal
223   as a weighted sum of several loss components, each focusing on different aspects of the model’s
224   performance. The main loss function is given by
225

228   data, all losses are used. For samples without 3D annotations, the 3D loss L3D is disabled.
229   3D Loss. For images annotated with SMAL model parameters β and θ, we supervise these parame-
230   ters directly to enable fast convergence. Additionally, we also supervise the estimated 3D keypoints

232   defined as
233                          L3D = λβ ||β̂ − β||22 + λθ ||θ̂ − θ||22 + ||K̂3D − K3D ||1 ,              (2)
234
      where λβ = 0.01 and λθ = 0.2 are loss weights. The term || · ||22 denotes squared L2 norm, while
235   || · ||1 represents L1 norm.
236
237   2D Loss. Because most training data only contain 2D-level annotations (2D keypoints or masks,
238
      yet masks are for evaluation only). For these data, we supervise 2D keypoints during training using

      L2D = ||π(K̂3D ) − K2D ||1 .

      Motivation. Acquiring a large number of animal images with full 3D annotations is difficult, and
271   synthetic datasets can supplement the shortcomings of real data. Previous methods use traditional
272   graphics pipeline to render either deformed SMAL models obtained by fitting images (Xu et al.,
273   2023a) or manually created CG assets (Shooter et al., 2024) to obtain image-label pairs. However,
274   the plausibility of rendered images is hindered by the coarse texture quality and sophisticated light-
275   ing and shadow control. These limitations motivate our usage of ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023) to
276   create high quality AI hallucinated images conditioned on SMAL structures. This principle effec-
277   tively bridges the domain gap between parametric labels and images, and help us to create a novel
278   large scale dataset named CtrlAni3D with minimal human labor involved.
279   SMAL Structure Condition. The dataset generation pipeline is illustrated in Fig. 3. Given a
280   posed SMAL mesh and viewpoint, we render it into mask map and depth map as the condition
281   images cv to guide the structure of images x generated by a pre-trained ControlNet. To guarantee
282   the diversity of the poses and shapes used for synthesis, we randomly sample β from the Gaussian
283   distributed shape space provided by original SMAL (Zuffi et al., 2017), and sample a more diverse
284   range of θ from a combined pose space presented by dog models (Rüegg et al., 2023; Li et al.,
285
      2024a; Biggs et al., 2020). This is reasonable because the quadrupeds expressed by SMAL share
      similar anatomical structures. About the viewpoint for rendering, each dimension of the global
286
      rotation vector is uniformly sampled from (−π, π) while the position is uniformly sampled between
287
      [−0.5, −0.5, 4] and [0.5, 0.5, 8].
288
289   Text Condition. To further control the style of generated images x, we seek to use text prompt.
290   We first manually classify the sampled 3D meshes into 10 species: cat, tiger, lion, cheetah, dog,
291
      wolf, horse, zebra, cow and hippo, and use species name as one keyword. The second keyword is
      the pose description, e.g. ”stands” in Fig. 3, which is assigned by human annotator according to
292
      the 3D mesh. Based on these keywords, ChatGPT (Achiam et al., 2023) is employed to complete a
293
      prompt sentence ct depicting possible animal behaviors. Finally, both cv and ct act as the prompts
294   of ControlNet for realistic and rich image generation.
295
296   Semi-Automated Filtering. Note that, not all generated images are perfectly aligned with condi-
297
      tions. To address this, we design a semi-automated filtering strategy to lower the burden of anno-
      tators. First, SAM2 (Ravi et al., 2024) is utilized to extract the foreground mask of the generated
298
      images, enabling cycle-consistency checking by comparing to conditioned mask. Further, we man-
299
      ually filter out images that do not match the mesh poses to ensure good data quality. Each synthetic
300   image has a resolution of 512 × 512 pixels and includes well-aligned annotations for β, θ, γ and
301   3D keypoints. By comparing rendered depth image the projected keypoint depths, we also obtain
302   visible 2D keypoints as annotation.

380   Following (Xu et al., 2023a), we utilize HMR (Kanazawa et al., 2018) and WLDO (Biggs et al.,

389   MPJPE and PA-MPVPE metrics. AniMer performs well not only on 3D datasets, but also on in-the-
390   wild 2D dataset that is never seen during training. Specifically on Animal Kingdom dataset, AniMer
391   demonstrates strong robustness and improves over WLDO by 16.2% for AUC. Qualitative results in

425   without ttopt for BITE and our method are shown in Fig. 5.
426   Tab. 2 also shows that our method outperforms all existing methods on 2D datasets Stanford Extra
427   and Animal Pose, yet performs competitive to BITE on Animal3D dataset. This indicates that our
428   method generalizes better to datasets without 3D supervision. On the challenging pet video dataset
429   Cop3D, our method achieves the best performance among per-frame approaches, but Animal Avatar
430   performs better on IOUw5 metric. The reason is that IOUw5 accounts for the worst frames while
431   Cop3D contains frames with extreme viewpoints and highly truncated targets. The joint optimization
      across the entire time series of Animal Avatar makes it more robust to these extreme cases.

530   6   C ONCLUSION
531
532
      This paper presents AniMer, a simple yet effective method for accurate animal pose and shape
533
      estimation. The key to the success of AniMer is a large capacity Transformer backbone together
534   with an aggregated large scale dataset. Within the aggregated dataset, we propose a novel synthetic
535   general quadruped dataset CtrlAni3D, which is rendered by prompting a controllable text-to-image
536   generation model ControlNet. Benefiting from our design philosophy, AniMer not only outperforms
537   previous methods for general quadruped mesh recovery, but also beats state-of-the-art single-species
538   reconstruction methods such as BITE for dog. We believe the principles behind AniMer would
539   inspire the mesh recovery tasks of the whole animal kingdom, and enable several down-stream
      applications such as avatar creation and behavioral analysis.

730
731   Animal Pose dataset. The Animal Pose dataset (Cao et al., 2019) includes five categories: dog, cat,
732
      cow, horse and sheep, comprising a total of over 6,000 instances across more than 4,000 images.
      Each animal instance in Animal Pose dataset is annotated with 20 keypoints.
733
734   APT-36k dataset. The APT-36k dataset (Yang et al., 2022b) contains 36000 images covering 30
735   different animal species from different scenes. There are typically 17 keypoints labeled for each
736   animal instance.
737   AwA Pose dataset. The AwA Pose dataset (Banik et al., 2021) is introduced for 2D quadruped
738   animal pose estimation. AwA contains 10064 images of 35 quadruped animal species and each
739   image is annotated with 39 keypoints.
740
      Stanford Extra dataset. The Stanford Extra dataset (Biggs et al., 2020) consists of 20,580 images
741
      and covers 120 dog breeds. Each image is annotated with 20 2D keypoints and silhouette.
742
743   Zebra synthetic dataset. The Zebra synthetic dataset (Zuffi et al., 2019) consists of 12850 images.
744   Each image is randomly generated that differs in background, shape, pose, camera, and appearance.
745   Animal Kingdom dataset. The Animal Kingdom dataset (Ng et al., 2022) includes a diverse range
746   of animal species across 8 major animal classes. We only use the part of pose estimation dataset to
747   evaluate our method.
748
      Animal3D dataset. Animal3D dataset (Xu et al., 2023a) contains a total of 3379 images, which are
749   classified into 40 classes. Each image is annotated with SMAL (Zuffi et al., 2017) parameters, 2d
750   keypoints, 3d keypoints and mask.
751
