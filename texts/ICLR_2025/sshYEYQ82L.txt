011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013
               Text-to-Image (T2I) models have revolutionized the synthesis of visual content
               from textual descriptions. However, their potential misuse for generating Not-
014
               Safe-For-Work (NSFW) content presents significant risks. While developers have
015
               implemented prompt filters and safety checkers, these defense mechanisms have
016            proven inadequate against determined adversaries. In this paper, we introduce U3-
017            Attack, a novel multimodal jailbreak attack against T2I models that effectively
018            circumvents existing safeguards to generate NSFW images. To achieve a univer-
019            sal attack, U3-Attack constructs a context-independent paraphrase candidate set
020            for each sensitive word in the text modality. This approach enables practical at-
021            tacks against prompt filters with minimal perturbation. In the image modality, we
022            propose a two-stage adversarial patch generation strategy that does not require ac-
023            cess to the T2I model’s internal architecture or parameters. This design makes our
024
               attack applicable to both open-source models and online T2I platforms. Exten-
               sive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of our method across various T2I
025
               models, including Stable Diffusion, Leonardo.Ai, and Runway. Our work exposes
026
               critical vulnerabilities in current T2I model defenses and underscores the urgent
027            need for more robust safety measures in this rapidly evolving field.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Text-to-Image (T2I) models have revolutionized the synthesis of high-quality images from textual
034   descriptions, bridging the gap between natural language and visual content (Rombach et al., 2022a;
035   Zhou et al., 2022; Shi et al., 2024). Their remarkable ability to generate realistic images has led
036
      to unprecedented popularity in various applications* . However, concerns have emerged regard-
      ing the potential misuse of these models for generating Not-Safe-for-Work (NSFW) content (Qu
037
      et al., 2023). The proliferation of unsafe images generated by T2I models, encompassing ele-
038
      ments of pornography, violence, and politically sensitive themes, has been observed across vari-
039   ous online platforms† . To mitigate these risks, T2I model developers have implemented preemptive
040   prompt filters and post-hoc safety checkers (CompVis, 2024) (Fig. 1). Nevertheless, these measures
041   have demonstrated limited efficacy, as adversaries can successfully jailbreak T2I models to produce
042   NSFW images.
043
      Identifying underlying vulnerabilities is crucial for addressing this issue. Our work focuses on jail-
044
      break attacks against current T2I models. Building upon the pioneering work of Zou et al. (2023),
045   who introduced the Greedy Coordinate Gradient (GCG) for guiding large language models (LLM) to
046   generate harmful content, jailbreak attacks have gained significant attention (Wei et al., 2024a; Liu
047   et al., 2024) and have been extended to T2I models. Qu et al. (2023) conducted a comprehensive se-
048   curity assessment of several popular T2I models, highlighting substantial risks. Subsequently, Yang
049   et al. (2024) developed MMA-Diffusion, a multimodal attack capable of bypassing both prompt
050   filters and safety checkers.

066   The primary challenge in jailbreak attacks on T2I models lies in circumventing prompt filters and
067   safety checkers. MMA-Diffusion addresses this challenge by employing a text modality attack
068   mechanism to generate unrestricted adversarial prompts, effectively bypassing prompt filters. How-
069   ever, this approach results in significant perturbations compared to the original text prompt due to the
070   lack of restrictions on text modifications. To evade safety checkers, MMA-Diffusion utilizes adver-
071
      sarial attacks by adding perturbations to images. While effective, MMA-Diffusion operates under a
      white-box setting, requiring access to model details, which is impractical for attacking online T2I
072
      APIs. Moreover, its case-by-case design necessitates unique perturbations for each text prompt and
073
      image, rendering the attack computationally expensive in practice.
074
075   To address these limitations, we propose U3-Attack, a jailbreak attack for T2I models that effec-
076   tively bypasses both prompt filters and safety checkers. U3 is Universal, applicable across diverse
077
      images and different prompts containing the same sensitive word; Unfiltered, capable of evading
      prompt filters; and Unseen, able to generate content that circumvents safety checkers. In the text
078
      modality, we achieve a universal attack through a context-independent paraphrase candidate set for
079
      each sensitive word. By replacing sensitive words with optimal paraphrases from corresponding
080   candidate sets, we attain a highly transferable attack against prompt filters. For the image modality,
081   we employ adversarial patches to enable a universal attack. Unlike global perturbations, adversarial
082   patches are easily applied and removed, and a single patch can be utilized across different images,
083   demonstrating robustness against artifacts introduced by T2I models.
084
      Experimentally, we have effectively explored the security risks of multiple popular T2I models
085
      (SDv1.5, SDv2.0, SDXLv1.0, SLD) and two T2I services (Leonardo.Ai, Runway). The main con-
086   tributions of this paper are as follows:
087
088            1. We propose a universal jailbreak attack which simultaneously launches attacks through
089               both the text and image modalities to bypass the prompt filters and safety checkers deployed
090               in T2I models. This attack further exposes the security vulnerabilities in current defense
091               mechanisms, highlighting the potential risks of existing safeguards being compromised.
092            2. We introduce a paraphrase candidate set generation framework in text modality, which
093               enables bypass of prompt filters with minimal perturbation. In image modality, we deploy a
094               universal adversarial patch to evade safety checkers, utilizing a novel two-stage generation
095               strategy for efficient patch discovery without requiring internal model details.
096
               3. We comprehensively evaluate the effectiveness of our universal jailbreak attack across var-
097               ious T2I models, including state-of-the-art open-source models like Stable Diffusion, as
098               well as online platforms such as Leonardo.Ai and Runway.

128   Semantic Similarity-Driven Loss Oriented Towards Text Encoder. We choose SD (Stable Dif-
129   fusion) as the target victim model in the T2I models. In the SD model, the diffusion model denoises
130   the image in the latent space, and the denoising process is guided by the text embeddings which is
131
      obtained by encoding the text input P with the text encoder Tθ of CLIP (Radford et al., 2021). Our
      goal is to ensure that the target prompt does not contain any sensitive word, while still allowing the
132
      semantic information associated with the sensitive word to appear in the final synthesized image. To
133
      achieve this, we shift our focus away from the context where  the sensitive word wsen appears and
134   instead construct a universal paraphrase candidate set S = s1 , s2 , ..., s|S| corresponding to the
135   sensitive word. |S| represents the size of the candidate set. By ensuring the identical latent features
136   produced by the Tθ , given by i.e., Tθ (wsen ) ≈ Tθ (si ), we select the paraphrase si for the candidate
137   set. By setting the number of iterations to |S|, we can ultimately obtain a paraphrase candidate set S
138   containing |S| paraphrases corresponding to the sensitive word. We ensure the semantic consistency
139   between wsen and si ∈ S by maximizing the cosine similarity between the latent feature Tθ (wsen )
140   and Tθ (si ). We formalize the attack objective as follows:
141
142                                          max cos(Tθ (wsen ), Tθ (si )).                                               (1)

      Gradient-Based Optimization. To optimize the attack objective more effectively, we follow the
145
      approach in MMA-Diffusion (Yang et al., 2024) by utilizing gradients to guide the optimization pro-
146
      cess. We begin by initializing the paraphrase si with M random tokens, si = [si1 , ..., sij , ..., siM ].
147   At each token position j in the paraphrase si , every token in the vocabulary V is considered a po-
148   tential candidate. We perform backpropagation on the attack objective to construct a token-level
149   gradient matrix G ∈ RM ×|V | for the paraphrase si . |V | is the vocabulary size. Gjk indicates the
150   influence of the k th candidate token in the vocabulary V at token position j of the paraphrase si .
151   Based on the gradient matrix G, we rank every token in the vocabulary V and select the top v tokens
152   for each token position in si . Finally, we construct a paraphrase candidate pool P ∈ N M ×v . We
153   randomly sample t paraphrases from the candidate pool P . The paraphrase copt with the highest
154   loss value in Equation (1) is selected as the final value for si . This process is repeated |S| times,
155   ultimately resulting in a paraphrase candidate set S corresponding to the sensitive word. Notably,
156   paraphrase candidate set is designed to be universal. For different prompts containing the same sen-
157   sitive word, we simply select the optimal paraphrase from the corresponding paraphrase candidate
158
      set, rather than retraining from scratch for each prompt like MMA-Diffusion.
159   To prevent sensitive words from appearing at any token position in the paraphrase si , we set the gra-
160   dients corresponding to the sensitive words to −inf in the gradient matrix G based on the sensitive
161   word list constructed by MMA-Diffusion. This ensures that sensitive words are excluded from the
      candidate pool P , and the paraphrase si will not contain any sensitive words.

      2.2     U NIVERSAL I MAGE -M ODAL ATTACK
180
181   In common T2I models, post-hoc safety checker is typically deployed to further review and fil-
182   ter out images containing NSFW content. Inspired by adversarial attack technique in the image
183   domain (Brown et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2023a; Wei et al., 2023), we propose a universal image-
184   modal attack using adversarial patch. In this attack, we primarily focus on image editing task in T2I
185   (Text-to-Image) scenario. By adding adversarial patch to the non-edited region of the original input
186   image xinput (see Fig. 2b), we can ultimately bypass the post-hoc safety checker even if the syn-
187   thesized image xsyn contains NSFW content. Considering that image editing models focus on the
188   regions of the original input image that require editing while striving to maintain consistency in the
189
      non-edited region between the synthesized image and original input image, we propose a two-stage
      strategy for generating adversarial patch.
190
191   Safety-Driven Loss Oriented Towards safety Checker. Image editing model of T2I receives two
192   types of inputs simultaneously. The first type is an image pair, consisting of the image to be edited
193   xinput and the mask image Medi that indicates the regions to be edited . The second type of input
194   is a text prompt P , which describes the content that needs to be modified and provides additional
195
      guidance for the editing process. When the T2I model generates a synthesized image xsyn , the
      image encoder Ven of post-hoc safety checker will map this image into a latent vector O. The safety
196
      checker then sequentially calculates the cosine distances between the latent vector O and each of the
197
      N built-in default NSFW concept embeddings, denoted as Ci for i = 1, ..., N . If any cosine distance
198   exceeds the threshold Ti associated with a specific concept embedding, the synthesized image will
199   be flagged as corresponding to that NSFW concept. Considering that optimizing adversarial patch
200   across the entire pipeline of T2I is highly time-consuming, and the synthesized image xsyn maintains
201   visible consistency with the original input image xinput in non-edited regions, we strategically place
202   adversarial patch in the non-edited regions of the synthesized image xsyn containing NSFW content,
203   which allows us to directly optimize adversarial patch against the safety checker. Our expectation
204   is that when adversarial patch is present in the non-edited regions of the synthesized image xsyn ,
205   adversarial example xadvsyn will bypass the safety checker. Our objective is formalized as follows:

300   Compared Methods. We select MMA-Diffusion and QF-Attack as our baseline methods. This
301   is mainly because QF-Attack is conceptually consistent with our approach, while MMA-Diffusion
302   aligns with our objective of simultaneously bypassing prompt filter and safety checker.
303
304    • MMA-Diffusion (Yang et al., 2024): MMA-Diffusion bypasses prompt filter by generating un-
305      constrained adversarial prompts and evades safety checker by adding imperceptible perturbations
306      to images.
307    • QF-Attack (Zhuang et al., 2023): We adapt QF-Attack by first aligning its attack objective with
308      Equation (1), ensuring that the generated images contain the semantic information corresponding
309      to the sensitive word. Next, we mask the sensitive word in each prompt and apply perturbation
310      at the position where the sensitive word previously appeared.
311
312   Evaluation Metrics. We utilize attack success rate ASR-N-M (M ⩽ N) as a metric to evaluate the
313   effectiveness of our attack method. We generate N images for each prompt using T2I model, and if
314   at least M of these images successfully jailbreak and display unsafe content, we deem the attack to
315   be successful. A larger M indicates a greater attack difficulty. ASR-N-M represents the proportion
316   of prompts that achieve successful attacks out of the total prompts evaluated. For the attacks on both
317   text and image modalities, we deploy three NSFW detectors, including Q16 (Schramowski et al.,
318   2022), MHSC (Qu et al., 2023), and built-in safety checker SDSC (CompVis, 2024) of SD, to assess
319   the attack success rate. For the attacks on online T2I platforms, we engage six human evaluators to
320
      report the final average attack success rate. It is important to note that a higher ASR signifies greater
      attack effectiveness.

524   4               C ONCLUSION
525
526   In this paper, we introduced U3-Attack, a universal jailbreak attack designed to circumvent both
527   prompt filters and safety checkers in Text-to-Image (T2I) models. Our approach achieves universal-
528   ity through the generation of context-independent paraphrase candidate sets for sensitive words, and
529
      robustness in image modal attacks by employing adversarial patches. Our experiments validated
      the effectiveness of U3-Attack across several state-of-the-art T2I models, including open-source
530
      models such as Stable Diffusion and widely-used online platforms like Leonardo.Ai and Runway.
531
      The results highlight the limitations of current safeguards in place, underscoring the importance of
532   reevaluating defense strategies to better protect against such adversarial threats.
533
534   Ethics Statement. This research explores vulnerabilities in Text-to-Image (T2I) models with the
535
      sole purpose of enhancing the security and safety of these systems. We acknowledge the sensitive
      nature of our work and its potential for misuse. Our intention is not to facilitate the generation of
536
      harmful or Not-Safe-For-Work (NSFW) content, but rather to expose critical weaknesses in current
537
      defense mechanisms, thereby contributing to the development of more robust safety measures. It is
538   our hope that this work will ultimately lead to safer and more reliable T2I systems that can be used
539   responsibly for creative and beneficial purposes.

      B      R ELATED W ORK
748
749
      Jailbreak Attack. Jailbreak attacks aim to induce the generative model to produce Not-Safe-for-
750   Work (NSFW) content, which are typically achieved by carefully crafted inputs that cause the model
751   to deviate from its predefined constraints and safeguards. Given the potential security risks posed by
752   jailbreak attacks, research in this area is highly active, with continuous advancements in attack meth-
753   ods to uncover potential risks. Wei et al. (2024a) suggested that aligned LLMs remain vulnerable to
754   jailbreak attacks due to competing objectives and mismatched generalization. Zou et al. (2023) pro-
755   posed a universal and transferable adversarial attack against aligned language models. Specifically,
      they appended an adversarial suffix to queries, prompting the model to generate harmful content.


                                                                  14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      Experiments demonstrated that this attack could induce aligned language models to produce nearly
757   any offensive content. Qu et al. (2023) evaluated four popular open-source T2I models using a
758   harmful prompt dataset. The results showed that a significant portion (14.56%) of the generated
759   images were unsafe. Zhang et al. (2023b) leverages the inherent classification capabilities of Dif-
760   fusion Models to simplify the generation of adversarial prompts by eliminating the dependence on
761   auxiliary models. Zhang et al. (2023b) primarily examines concept-erased models (Gandikota et al.,
762   2023; Kumari et al., 2023; Schramowski et al., 2023) that employ internal safety mechanisms and
763   does not extend to external defense mechanisms. Tsai et al. (2024) obtains the holistic representa-
764   tions of sensitive and inappropriate concepts through concept extraction, automatically identifying
765
      problematic prompts that generate unsafe content. However, it lacks precise control over the details
      of the generated content. MMA-Diffusion (Yang et al., 2024) proposes a novel multimodal system-
766
      atic attack that adds adversarial perturbations to both text and images, bypassing prompt filters and
767
      safety checkers, and guiding T2I models to generate NSFW content.
768
769   Adversarial Attack. Adversarial attack techniques, which modify only input data without altering
770   model parameters, can deceive models and induce incorrect predictions, exposing vulnerabilities in
771
      various DNN-based models (Wei et al., 2024b). Szegedy et al. (2014) first introduced adversarial
      examples, demonstrating that slight image perturbations could cause complete misclassification by
772
      models. Subsequently, numerous works, including FGSM (Goodfellow et al., 2015), PGD (Madry
773
      et al., 2018), and C&W attacks (Carlini & Wagner, 2017), have explored and analyzed adversarial
774   attacks. In this work, we leverage the vulnerability of DNNs to adversarial attacks to design jailbreak
775   methods for T2I models.
776
777
      The Security Defense Mechanisms Possessed by T2I Models. To prevent T2I models from being
      misused to generate images containing NSFW content, both open-source and online T2I models
778
      have implemented certain defense mechanisms to mitigate the risk of abuse. The existing safety
779
      mechanisms can be primarily divided into two aspects: internal safety mechanisms and external
780   safety mechanisms. External safety mechanisms primarily consist of two strategies: prompt fil-
781   ters (Leonardo.AI, 2023; Runway, Inc., 2023) and post-hoc safety checkers (Rombach et al., 2022b;
782   Runway, Inc., 2023). The key distinction between two security mechanisms lies in their timing;
783   prompt filters aim to prevent the generation of unsafe content during the input phase, whereas post-
784   hoc safety checkers conduct additional evaluations on the synthesized images during the output
785   phase. Internal safety mechanisms primarily focus on concept-erasing models, which operate di-
786   rectly on the diffusion model by modifying the inference process (Schramowski et al., 2023) or
787   fine-tuning the model’s parameters (Gandikota et al., 2023; Kumari et al., 2023) to suppress the
788
      generation of unsafe content.

      Due to page limitations, we move the experiments verifying the robustness of the universal image

885   quantify the robustness of our attack method against unknown T2I models and unknown post-hoc
886   safety checkers, we transfer the generated adversarial patch to two black-box T2I models and two
887   black-box safety checkers. Table 5 reportes the robustness of our attack across different security
888   checkers and various T2I models.
889

890
      of 36.066% and 54.098% on two black-box safety checkers, Q16 (Schramowski et al., 2022) and
891   MHSC (Qu et al., 2023), respectively. This demonstrates that the adversarial patch generated by
892   our method exhibit good robustness across different safety checkers, effectively deceiving unknown
893   safety checkers without requiring additional effort. A possible reason for this is that the adversarial
894   patch we generate captures higher-level semantic features, and the detection results of different
895   safety checkers may rely on similar feature space.
896
      For the attack robustness in different T2I models, our adversarial patch exhibit strong robustness
897   across different T2I models. We achieve ASR-4-1 rates of 92.982% and 90.385% on the editing
898   models corresponding to the SDXLv1.0 and SDv2.0, respectively. This indicates that, even when
899   faced with T2I models of different architectures, our adversarial patch can withstand the effects of
900   variations caused by the T2I models, demonstrating that our method generates more robust adver-
901   sarial patch. The primary reason for this is that our residual modeling strategy effectively captures
902   the distribution of variation. In the Stage 2 of adversarial patch generation, we anticipate poten-
903   tial variations and integrate them into the optimization process, ultimately producing more robust
904   adversarial patch.
