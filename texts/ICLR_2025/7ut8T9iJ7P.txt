011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent studies have empirically demonstrated the feasibility of directly incorpo-
014            rating classification regularizers into Deep Imbalanced Regression (DIR). By seg-
015
               menting the entire dataset into distinct groups and performing classification reg-
               ularization on these groups, previous works primarily focused on maintaining the
016
               ordinal consistency between the feature space and the label space to capture the
017
               continuity of data in DIR. However, this direct integration would also lead the
018            model to focus merely on learning discriminative features during representation
019            learning and potentially distort the geometrical structure of the feature space due
020            to the label imbalance during the fine-tuning phase in DIR. As a result, semantic
021            similarity, namely, instances with similar labels would also be close to each other,
022            can be leveraged to address the imbalance in DIR but has always been ignored.
023            Consequently, the effectiveness of these classification-based approaches would be
024            significantly undermined in DIR. To tackle this problem, we investigate the sim-
025            ilarity characteristics of the data in DIR and propose an end-to-end solution to
026
               unleash the potential of classification in helping DIR. Specifically, we first split
               the objective of DIR into a combination of a global inter-group imbalance group
027
               classification task and a local intra-group imbalance instance regression task. To
028
               fully exploit the potential of classification under the DIR task, we propose both
029            a symmetric and asymmetric soft labeling strategy to capture the global semantic
030            similarity to handle the cross-group imbalance. In the meantime, we employ label
031            distribution smoothing to leverage the instance semantic similarity in addressing
032            the intra-group instance imbalance with a multi-head regressor. Furthermore, we
033            link up the group classification to guide the learning of the multi-head regressor,
034            which can further harness the classification to help the DIR from end to end. Ex-
035            tensive experiments in real-world datasets also validate the effectiveness of our
036            proposed method. The code can be found in https://anonymous.4open.
037
               science/r/ICLR2025submission-9415/README.md.

040   1   I NTRODUCTION
041
042   Deep imbalanced regression (DIR) aims to perform regression tasks with deep neural networks on
043   particular datasets where certain labels are much less observed than others Yang et al. (2021). While
044   the goal of classification tasks is to predict discrete class labels to model the training distribution,
045   in contrast, the label space in regression is always continuous and infinite. To tackle this problem,
046   recent research focused on capturing the label continuity in DIR.
047
      By segmenting the whole dataset into distinct and continuous groups, previous works incorporated
048   classification regularizers (e.g. representation learning regularizers) to maintain the continuity of
049   the labels in the feature space. Specifically, these researches has explored extensively in preserving
050   the ordinal nature of the feature space. For instance, Gong et al. (2022) proposed a ranking regular-
051   ization to align the sorting of features with their corresponding labels. Zhang et al. (2023a) used an
052   ordinal entropy regularizer to maintain the ordinal relationships between the feature and the label.
053   Zha et al. (2023) proposed a contrastive regularization to learn both ordinal and discriminative fea-
      ture representation. Similarly, Keramati et al. (2024) introduced a novel pair selection strategy into


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      contrastive learning to pull the positive pairs together and push away the negative pairs given their
055   corresponding label distance.
056
057   However, these methods primarily focus on learning the ordinal characteristics of the data in the
058
      feature space. In the meantime, the integration of classification regularizers would lead the model
      to concentrate solely on learning discriminative features Zha et al. (2023); Keramati et al. (2024)
059
      in representation learning and potentially alter the geometrical structure of the feature space due
060
      to the label imbalance during the fine-tuning phase in DIR (e.g. in Fig.1). Therefore, semantic
061   similarity, another aspect of the continuity in DIR where the similarity across labels would also
062   reflect the similarity of their features, is always overlooked by the previous works. For example, in
063   the age regression task, images of age 20 would have similar features to those of ages 15 and 25.
064   Consequently, the knowledge learned from age 20 can be leveraged to approach the age of 25 or 15
065   if either of them is less observed in training.
066
      Nevertheless, direct incorporation of the classification regularizers would obliterate this semantic
067   similarity (e.g. push away effect of the feature representationsKeramati et al. (2024)), which limits
068   the feasibility of leveraging semantic similarity for tackling the DIR problem. Additionally, these
069   previous works often treated DIR as merely classification tasks. As the label boundaries in regression
070   tasks become more fine-grained (with smaller bin sizes Yang et al. (2021)), these solutions would
071   inevitably lead to a heavy computational burden and eventually become infeasible for DIR.
072
      In this paper, we investigate the semantic similarity in DIR to exploit the potential of classification
073   in helping DIR. Instead of directly incorporating classification regularizers in the feature space as
074   that of previous works Zhang et al. (2023a); Zha et al. (2023); Keramati et al. (2024), we propose
075   an end-to-end solution that tackles the DIR in the combination of 1) a global inter-group imbalance
076   group classification task and 2) a local intra-group imbalance instance (data sample) regression task.
077   We leverage the semantic similarity from both global and local perspectives to unleash the potential
078   of classification to address the imbalance in global and local respectively.
079   Specifically, we first propose a symmetric descending soft labeling strategy to capture the semantic
080   similarity across the groups in the group classification task. Meanwhile, considering the imbal-
081   ance across the groups, we also propose an asymmetric soft labeling strategy that incorporates the
082   imbalance priors of the groups into the symmetric soft labeling to tackle the global imbalance classi-
083   fication. These soft labeling strategies leverage the semantic similarity between the groups to tackle
084   the imbalance across the groups, which can effectively capture the intrinsic characteristics of the
085   data in DIR from a global perspective.
086   Furthermore, we associate the group predictions from the group classification with a multi-head
087   regressor to guide each instance forwarding to its corresponding regressor head in an end-to-end
088   manner. Additionally, to address the imbalance between the instances in each group, we introduce
089   the local label distribution smoothing to capture the intra-group semantic similarity for each instance
090   from a local perspective. Hereby, we unleash the potential of the classification in helping DIR by
091   leveraging the semantic similarity from global to local. We also conduct comprehensive experiments
092   over three real-world DIR benchmarks to validate the effectiveness of our proposed method.
093   In summary, our contribution can be concluded as the following:
094
095          • We divide the objective of DIR into the combination of 1) a global group imbalance classi-
096            fication task and 2) a local instance imbalance regression task.
097          • We leverage the semantic similarity to unleash the potential of classification in helping
098            regression by proposing a symmetric and asymmetric descending soft labeling strategy and
099            introducing label distribution smoothing to tackle the imbalance from global to local.
100          • We associate the global group classification with the local instance regression to address
101            the DIR from end to end.

130   2.2      D ISCUSSION ON DIRECT INCORPORATION OF CLASSIFICATION FOR DIR.
131
132   Inspired by Pintea et al. (2023), we decompose the objective of DIR into the combination of the
133   group classification globally over the whole dataset and instance regression locally within each
                                                         PG
134   group from a Bayesian perspective: p(y|x) =           g p(g|x)p(y|x, g), where g denotes the group
135   index and G denotes the total number of groups. Therefore, we can model the p(g|x) as the global
136   group classification and p(y|x, g) as the local instance regression. Consequently, the imbalance of
137   our regression task has also been divided into the global inter-group classification imbalance and
138   local intra-group instance imbalance.
139   When we take the negative log-likelihood of the objective of DIR, we can have the following decom-
140
                                  PG
      position: − log p(y|x) = g − log p(g|x) − log p(y|x, g), where the − log p(g|x) can be regarded
141   as the group classification loss and − log p(y|x, g) can be regarded as the regression loss given g. As
142   most of the previous works Zha et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023a) which incorporated the classifica-
143   tion regularizers in the feature space are actually modeling the posterior of the feature representation
144   p(z|x), there exists a gap between modeling the p(z|x) and the p(g|x) in our decomposition.
145
      Furthermore, at the fine-tuning phase Zha et al. (2023), the data dependence Yang et al. (2021)
146
      and the label imbalance would also affect the mapping process (from p(z|x) → p(y|z) in DIR).
147   Consequently, the geometrical structure of the feature space would be distorted this fine-tuning
148   phase. As we can observe from Fig.1, the structure of the feature space in (a) has been modified by
149   the fine-tuning phase and differs a lot compared to (a) in both (b) and (c) given the label imbalance in
150   DIR. Therefore, the effectiveness of incorporating classification regularizers would be significantly
151   limited in addressing the DIR.
152
      Meanwhile, as evident from the clear clustering boundaries in (b) and (c) compared to Fig.1 (a) (red
153   arrows), it is feasible to leverage the classification in helping the DIR. To fully exploit the classifi-
154   cation in helping DIR, as we can observe from the above decomposition, the classification objective
155   of the p(g|x) can be regarded as the re-weight of the regression objective p(y|x, g). Therefore, ac-
156   curate estimating of the groups would be crucial as the local intra-group instance regression is also
157   dependent on the estimated group (g as the prior in p(y|x, g)) in our decomposed objective func-
158   tion. Since we divide the objective of DIR into the global group imbalance classification p(g|x) and
159   local instance imbalance regression p(y|x, g), a straightforward way to solve this imbalance is to
160   re-weight the group and instance based on their empirical label density distribution respectively.

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
170
171   In this section, we propose both the symmetric and asymmetric soft labeling strategy to capture the
172   semantic similarity and leverage the imbalance information globally across the divided groups to
173   tackle the group imbalance. In the meantime, we introduce a label distribution smoothing to acquire
174   the semantic similarity locally in each divided group to address the local instance imbalance.
175
176   3.1   S YMMETRIC D ESCENDING SOFT LABELING FOR GLOBAL GROUP CLASSIFICATION
177
178   We first address the inter-group imbalance by leveraging the semantic similarity at a group level.
179   We propose a symmetric descending soft labeling strategy to capture the semantic similarity across
180
      the groups. For a group label g, in the classification with cross-entropy (CE) loss, the group label
      is encoded into hard labels as ghard = [0, . . . , 1g , . . . , 0] where 1g denotes 1 at g-th index of the
181
      ghard list. Meanwhile, the loss function is defined as LCE = −1g log og , where og is the output
182
      logit of the deep model at index g after soft-max (opred = {. . . , og , . . . }). As we can observe
183   from the CE, the information from the other group labels is overlooked. Consequently, only the
184   discriminative information is learned to distinguish the groups from each other while the semantic
185   similarity characteristics between the groups are ignored.
186
      To tackle this problem, we propose a soft labeling strategy to capture the semantic similarity across
187
      the groups. We first define the symmetric descending soft labeling strategy to convert the group
188
      label g into the soft label gsof t :

460   4.6   A BLATION STUDY
461
462   To further explain the effectiveness of our proposed method, we conduct the ablation study on dif-
463   ferent numbers of groups in Fig.3. Compared to the CE loss, our proposed method can achieve a
464
      better performance over the group classification. As we stated in our methodology, CE can provide
      no other information when calculating the group classification loss and ignore the semantic similar-
465
      ity across the groups. Therefore, as we can observe from Fig.3(a), the classification performance
466
      of CE would always be worse than the symmetric and asymmetric strategy. Moreover, when we
467   observe Fig.3(b), the MAE of CE is also a lot worse than the symmetric and asymmetric strategy.
468   In Fig.3(c), the G-Mean performance of CE is also worse than the symmetric and asymmetric soft
469   labeling. showcasing that semantic similarity is a crucial aspect of data continuity in DIR.
470
      Furthermore, when we compare the group numbers across these three classification losses, as we can
471
      observe from Fig.3(a), the group classification accuracy drops. This is because with the increasing
472
      of the group numbers, the nearby groups would be more similar, which makes the classifier harder
473   and harder to distinguish. However, when we leverage the semantic similarity for the group classifi-
474   cation, our proposed solution would smooth the discrepancy between the groups. Consequently, our
475   method can outperform the CE and perform steadily over the different number of groups (Fig.3(b)),
476   which further validates the effectiveness of our proposed method.

508   5.2   D EEP I MBALANCED REGRESSION
509
510   Deep imbalanced regression (DIR) has been proposed by Yang et al. (2021) and has attracted tremen-
511   dous interests in the recent machine learning studies. Similarly, re-weighting Torgo et al. (2013);
512   Steininger et al. (2021); Branco et al. (2018); Stocksieker et al. (2023) has also been adopted in
513   the regression tasks. Yang et al. (2021) proposed a label distribution smoothing and feature distri-

      bution smoothing to redeem the imbalance. Ren et al. (2022); Silva et al. (2022) revised the MSE
      loss for accommodating the imbalance distribution. Jiang et al. (2023) used a mixture-of-experts on
515
      the outputs while Wu et al. (2023); Yao et al. (2022) proposed a mix-up strategy for dealing with
516
      the regression tasks. Wang & Wang (2023) used the variational inference for addressing the DIR.
517   Moreover, integrating classification regularizers with the mean squre error loss (MSE) Gong et al.
518   (2022); Zha et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023b;a); Keramati et al. (2024); Pintea et al. (2023) has
519   been empirically shown to be effective in tackling the DIR. Different from previous works, instead
520   of directly incorporating the classification regularizers, our work aims to unleash the power of the
521   classification for better helping the DIR by capturing the semantic similarity.
522
523   6     C ONCLUSION
524
525   In this paper, we investigate the semantic similarity, a characteristic which has been always over-
526   looked in previous works, to unleash the potential of the classification in helping DIR. Specifically,
527   we decompose the imbalance of DIR into global and local imbalances. We propose a symmetric
528   and asymmetric soft labeling strategy that captures the semantic similarity to tackle the global group
529   imbalance. Furthermore, we use the label distribution smoothing to handle the local instance imbal-
530   ance. By linking up the global group classification with the local instance regression, we unleash
531   the potential of the classification and solve the DIR from end-to-end. Extensive experiments over
532   real-world datasets also validate the effectiveness of our proposed method.
