009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               The integration of prompt tuning with multimodal learning has shown significant
012
               generalization abilities for various downstream tasks. Despite advancements, exist-
013
               ing methods heavily depend on massive modality-specific labeled data (e.g., video,
014            audio, and image), or are customized for a single modality. In this study, we present
015            Text as Any-Modality by Consistent Prompt Tuning (TaAM-CPT), a scalable ap-
016            proach for constructing a general representation model toward unlimited modalities
017            using solely text data. TaAM-CPT comprises modality prompt pools, text construc-
018            tion, and modality-aligned text encoders from pre-trained models, which allows
019            for extending new modalities by adding prompt pools and modality-aligned text
020            encoders. To harmonize the learning across different modalities, TaAM-CPT de-
021            signs intra- and inter-modal learning objectives, which can capture category details
022
               within modalities while maintaining semantic consistency across different modali-
               ties. Benefiting from its scalable architecture and pre-trained models, TaAM-CPT
023
               can be seamlessly extended to accommodate unlimited modalities. Remarkably,
024
               without any modality-specific labeled data, TaAM-CPT achieves leading results
025            on diverse datasets spanning various modalities, including video classification
026            (Kinetic-400/600/700), image classification (MSCOCO, VOC2007, NUSWIDE,
027            VOC2012, Objects365), and audio classification (ESC50, US8K). The code is avail-
028            able at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/TaAM-CPT-0EA6.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   As unified architectures (Vaswani et al., 2017; Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Arnab et al., 2021; Gong
035   et al., 2021) and multimodal pre-training models (Devlin et al., 2019; Radford et al., 2021; Tong
036   et al., 2022) progress, recent works have exhibited impressive representation abilities in multimodal
037   learning (Li et al., 2023b; Zhang et al., 2023a; Zhu et al., 2024; Yeh et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023b).
038   In scenarios restricted by either labeled data or computational resources, owing to the aligned pre-
039
      trained models (Radford et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2024b; Wu et al., 2023b), prompt tuning (Zhu et al.,
      2023a; Yao et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023a) showcases robust generalization capabilities across various
040
      downstream tasks by adjusting a negligible number of parameters, such as video classificatiot (Li
041
      et al., 2023a; Wasim et al., 2023), image classification (Zhou et al., 2022b; Hu et al., 2023; Guo et al.,
042   2023), and audio classification (Duan et al., 2024; Chang et al., 2023).
043
044   Despite prompt tuning emerging as a novel paradigm for adjusting large-scale pre-trained mod-
045
      els (Radford et al., 2021; Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2023b), current techniques still rely
      heavily on massive modality-specific labeled data (e.g. video, audio, and image). For instance, as
046
      illustrated in Figure 1 (a) and Figure 1 (e), image supervised methods (Zhou et al., 2022b;a; Sun
047
      et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2023) design text prompt that is combined with the textual labels to align with
048   labeled image data for image classification tasks. Likewise, for video and audio classification tasks,
049   previous methods (Ju et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2024; Kushwaha & Fuentes, 2023)
050   primarily focus on adapting pre-trained multimodal models to video and audio understanding tasks
051   supervised with labeled video and audio data. However, sufficient modality-specific labeled data
052   necessitates considerable manual effort, which, in the face of labeled data limits, can impede the
053   development of robust object classification networks. In the absence of labeled data altogether, these
      techniques may even fail outright.

      To address the above issue, some studies advocate using the well-aligned embedding space, achieved
071
      by contrastive learning (e.g.,CLIP (Cherti et al., 2023)) for prompt tuning. For example, TAI-
072
      DPT (Guo et al., 2023), as a pioneering work depicted in Figure 1 (b) and Figure 1 (e), proposes
073   to enable labeled text data (e.g., coco-caption (Lin et al., 2014)) instead of labeled image data for
074   training text prompt, while testing with images and learned text prompt. Similarly, PT-Text (Li
075   et al., 2024) pioneers the approach of audio-free prompt tuning by pre-trained audio-language
076   model (Wu et al., 2023b), where the prompt is learned from text rather than audio for zero-shot audio
077   classification. To further reduce the cost of obtaining labeled text data, PVP (Wu et al., 2024) and
078   TAI-Adapter (Zhu et al., 2023b) recommend using synthetic text data, generated by large language
079   models (LLMs) (Touvron et al., 2023a), as a substitute for labeled text data. However, these strategies
080   require the design of sophisticated text prompt, visual prompt, or adapter frameworks, as well as the
081
      deployment of a text encoder to encode the prompts. Additionally, these approaches focus solely on a
      single modality (e.g., video classification, image classification, or audio classification), and for more
082
      modalities, multiple independent models need to be trained additionally.
083
084   In this paper, we explore a universal representation model capable of scaling to unlimited modalities
085   without any modality-specific labeled data. This necessitates the following conditions: 1) The model
086   exclusively relies on easy-collected text data for training, eliminating the need for any labeled data. 2)
087
      The model architecture needs to be flexible enough to accommodate new categories or modalities and
      simplify the design of prompt, thereby reducing the complexity of prompt encoding. 3) The model
088
      must ensure learning across different modalities does not mutually affect each other, and appropriate
089
      training objectives should be designed to enhance the representational capabilities of all modalities.
090
091   Motivated by these factors, as shown in Figure 1 (c) and Figure 1 (d), we propose Text as Any-
092   Modality for Consistent Prompt Tuning (TaAM-CPT), a general representation model toward
093
      unlimited modalities solely using text data generated by LLMs. Unlike TAI-DPT (Guo et al.,
      2023) and PVP (Wu et al., 2024), which require intricate, multi-grained text prompt designs, our
094
      method simplifies the design by characterizing any modality category as a randomly initialized
095
      vector. Leveraging the instruction following ability of LLMs (Touvron et al., 2023a), we can
096   comfortably obtain text training data for any category. By directly optimizing the vectors within the
097   aligned space of pre-trained models (Radford et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2024b), we
098   eliminate intermediate encoding processes. Since the initialization way for each category is identical,
099   TaAM-CPT ensures the flexible addition of any category from any modality without retraining the
100   already learned class-specific prompt. Moreover, we design uni-directional contrastive loss, which
101   uses modalities with stronger representational abilities to guide the learning of those weaker ones.
102   Surprisingly, not only does it enhance the representational abilities of weaker modalities but also
103   further improves the representational abilities of stronger modalities.
104   We conduct extensive experiments across multiple modalities, including video, audio, and image
105   classification tasks. Without any labeled data, TaAM-CPT achieves superior performance to pre-
106   trained models and recent SOTAs (Guo et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024). Notably, in image
107   classification, TaAM-CPT outperforms CLIP (Cherti et al., 2023) by 12.5% on MSCOCO (Lin et al.,
      2014), 8.0~12.0% on Object365 (Shao et al., 2019) and NUSWIDE (Chua et al., 2009). For video

      2     R ELATED W ORK
115
116   Video, Image, and Audio Classification. Video classification involves identifying actions in the
117   video. Early works (Wang et al., 2016b; Tran et al., 2018; Feichtenhofer, 2020) focus on designing
118   two-stream networks and 3D CNNs for action recognition. Building on the success of transformers in
119   the image, recent works (Yan et al., 2022b; Xue et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Li et al.,
120   2023d) explores effective objectives for adapting pre-trained image models to video understanding.
121
      To handle the problem of local video redundancy, UniFormerV2 (Li et al., 2022) introduces local and
      global relation aggregators to learn discriminative representations.
122
123   Image classification aims to recognize all the categories in an image. To explore the correlations
124   among labels, some works propose to incorporate semantic dependencies via object proposals (Wang
125   et al., 2016a; Liu et al., 2018), semantic graph (Zhang et al., 2023b; Zhu et al., 2023c), and transformer-
126   based architecture (Bhatti et al., 2023; Scheibenreif et al., 2023). When labeled data is limited,
127
      another line of works (Liu et al., 2022b; Simon et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023b) attempts to solve more
      challenging scenarios, including zero-shot, few-shot, and partial-label tasks. DualCoOp (Sun et al.,
128
      2022) and DualCoOp++ (Hu et al., 2023) learn multiple prompts for each class, resulting in improved
129
      performance for both zero-shot and partial-label image classification.
130
131   Audio classification involves tagging audio signals into different categories. Traditional works (Henaff
132   et al., 2011; Nanni et al., 2017) mainly rely on machine learning technology and manual feature
133
      extraction. In recent years, driven by advancements in deep learning, some works (Xu et al., 2023;
      Sarkar & Etemad, 2023) have begun to explore the application of neural networks. Additionally,
134
      some efforts (Liu et al., 2023a; Garg et al., 2024) attempt to apply the transformer to the audio
135
      classification, thereby capturing the long-term dependencies.
136
137   Prompt Tuning in Multimodal Learning. Prompt tuning (Zhou et al., 2022b; Li et al., 2023a; Duan
138   et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a) has emerged for rapidly adapting to downstream tasks by adjusting
139
      a minimal number of parameters. For instance, some works (Zhou et al., 2022b; Nie et al., 2023)
      introduce learnable context vectors to align with images via frozen CLIP encoders. When labeled
140
      data is limited, TAI-DPT (Guo et al., 2023) and PT-Text (Li et al., 2024) introduced multi-grained
141
      text prompts, surpassing pre-trained multimodal models in image and audio classification tasks solely
142   training text data. PVP (Wu et al., 2024) further enhances image classification performance by
143   co-learning pseudo-visual prompt and text prompt.
144
145
      Different from the above prompt learning methods, which require a massive of labeled data, complex
      prompt design, and are limited to single modality design. Our work eliminates the prompt encoder,
146
      scales to unlimited modalities, initializes any categories of any modality with an identical vector, and
147
      only uses text data generated by LLMs for prompt learning.

196   modality, we maintain a modality-specific prompt pool, defined as follows:
197

201

202

203

205
206   Modality Text Encoding. According to previous methods (Guo et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Wu
207
      et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2023b; Yang et al., 2024), text is treated as a surrogate for other modalities(e.g.
      image and audio) for zero-shot classification. Such a paradigm potentially assumes that pre-trained
208
      models have aligned text with other modalities into a shared embedding space, thereby making
209
      it feasible to extract text features as substitutes for other modalities. However, these methods are
210   designed for individual modalities and fail to utilize complementary information among multiple
211   modalities. Hence, as shown in Figure 2, we adopt a parallel architecture and obtain modality-aligned
212   text encoders (Textv , Texta , Texti Encoder) from pre-trained models ViCLIP (Wang et al., 2024b),
213   CLAP (Wu et al., 2023b), and CLIP (Cherti et al., 2023), to extract text features. Furthermore, we
214   find CLIP (Cherti et al., 2023) and CLAP (Wu et al., 2023b) have superior representation abilities
215   for image and audio, compared to ViCLIP (Wang et al., 2024b) for video, specifically reflected in
      the zero-shot classification performance. Inspired by the discovery, we design an uni-directional

219   3.3   I NTRA - AND I NTER - MODAL L EARNING
220   To learn the modality prompt pool for each modality, our work is to design two learning objectives:
221   a) intra-modal learning aims to optimize the prompt pool for each modality using global text
222   features extracted by modality-aligned text encoders. b) inter-modal learning aims to improve the
223   representational abilities of weaker modalities based on stronger ones.

330   3.5   M ODEL T ESTING
331   After learning the prompt pool of each modality, each prompt uniquely represents a specific class.
332   We take the video modality as an example to showcase the video classification. Given an input video,
333   we replace the video modality-specific text encoder in Figure 2 with the video encoder of ViCLIP
334   to obtain the video feature. Then, we directly calculate the similarity between the video feature
335   and each prompt in the video prompt pool, and the prediction of the input video is the prompt with
336
      the highest similarity. It can be seen that each prompt is calculated directly with the video features
      without any encoding processing, which significantly improves the inference speed of the model. For
337
      image classification and audio classification, we adopt the same approach, calculating the similarity
338
      between image or audio and their corresponding prompt pools to obtain the predictions.

533
534   In this paper, we explore a scalable way of constructing a universal representation model for various
535   modalities. Based on a flexible architecture and aligned pre-trained models, we develop TaAM-CPT,
536   treating any category as a learnable vector and optimizing it directly through aligned pre-trained
537   models. In addition, uni-directional contrastive learning also improves the classification performance
538   of all modalities. The experimental results on 13 datasets show that TaAM-CPT achieves leading
539   results in various classification tasks, including zero-shot video classification, image classification,
      audio classification, and partial-label image classification.

817   Our TaAM-CPT is built upon multimodal pre-trained models, including video-language model,
818   image-language model, and audio-language model, and uses frozen text encoders for prompt tuning,
819   as well as frozen modality encoders for object recognition predicting. In our work, we choose the
820   pretrained multimodal models, open-sourced by the LAION (Schuhmann et al., 2022) organization,
821
      as the modality-aligned text and modality encoders. For a total of 300k text sentences on a single
      Tesla V100 for the Kinetic-400, MSCOCO, and ESC50 datasets, each epoch takes 12 minutes and
822
      the total training cost for 10 epochs is about 2 hours.
823
824   ViCLIP. ViCLIP is a video-language pretraining model, building upon the open-source CLIP of
825   OpenAI. The model consists of a video encoder and corresponding text encoder, which is pretrained
826   on the InternVid dataset containing 7 million videos, each with detailed text descriptions. We use the
827
      BASE architecture as our baseline model with 12 attention layers and 512 encoding dimensions.
828   CLIP. We select the open-source image-language pretraining model released by the LAION organi-
829   zation as our baseline model. The model comprises an image encoder and corresponding transformer-
830   based text encoder, each with 12 attention layers and an encoding dimension of 512. The size of the
831   input image is 224 × 224, with the patch size being 32. For image modality, CLIP-ViT-B-32 (Cherti
832   et al., 2023) is selected as the image encoder and image-text encoder.
833   CLAP. For the audio-language pretraining model, likewise, we select CLAP released by the LAION
834   organization as our baseline model. The audio encoder is a transformer-based model with 4 groups of
835   swin-transformer blocks, while the text encoder is RoBERTa. Two-layer MLPs with ReLU activation
836   are applied to mAP both audio and text outputs into 512 dimensions. For audio modality, we select
837   CLAP (Wu et al., 2023b) from LAION (Schuhmann et al., 2022) as the audio encoder and the built-in
838   Robert as the audio-text encoder.

      Here, we discuss the text training data construction for different modalities. We construct the
