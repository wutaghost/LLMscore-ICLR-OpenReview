009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011                Despite the rapid advancements in domain generalization (DG), the majority of
012                DG studies center on establishing theoretical guarantee for generalization under
013                the assumption of sufficient, diverse or even infinite domains. This assumption
014                however is unrealistic, thus there remains no conclusive evidence as to whether the
015                existing DG algorithms can truly generalize in practical settings where domains
016                are limited. This paper aims to elucidate this matter. We first study the conditions
017
                   for the existence and learnability of an optimal hypothesis. As the sufficient condi-
                   tions are non-verifiable, our identified two necessary conditions become critical to
018
                   guaranteeing the chance of finding the global optimal hypothesis in finite domain
019
                   settings. In light of the theoretical insights, we provide a comprehensive review
020                of DG algorithms explaining to what extent they can generalize effectively. We
021                finally introduce a practical approach that leverages the joint effect of the two sets
022                of conditions to boost generalization. Our proposed method demonstrates superior
023                performance on well-established DG benchmarks.

026   1       I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Domain generalization (DG) aims to train a machine learning model on multiple data distributions
029   so that it can generalize to unseen data distributions. Although challenging, DG is crucial for prac-
030   tical scenarios where there is a need to quickly deploy a prediction model on a new target domain
031
      without access to target data. Various approaches have been proposed to address the DG problem,
      which can be broadly categorized into 3 families: representation alignment, invariant prediction, and
032
      data augmentation. Representation alignment focuses on learning domain-invariant representations
033
      by reducing the divergence between latent marginal distributions (Long et al., 2017; Ganin et al.,
034   2016; Li et al., 2018b; Nguyen et al., 2021; Shen et al., 2018; Xie et al., 2017; Ilse et al., 2020)
035   or aligning conditional distributions (Gong et al., 2016; Li et al., 2018d; Tachet des Combes et al.,
036   2020). Invariant prediction ensures stable performance regardless of the domain by learning a con-
037   sistently optimal classifier (Arjovsky et al., 2020; Ahuja et al., 2020; Krueger et al., 2021; Rosenfeld
038   et al., 2020; Li et al., 2022a). Data augmentation applies predefined or learnable transformations on
039   the original samples or their features to create augmented data, thereby enhancing the model’s gen-
040   eralization capabilities (Mitrovic et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2022b; Shankar et al., 2018; Zhou et al.,
041   2020; 2021; Xu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2020b; Zhao et al., 2020; Yao et al.,
042
      2022a; Carlucci et al., 2019; Yao et al., 2022b). Despite these developments, these methods have
      not consistently outperformed Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) on fair model selection criteria
043
      (Gulrajani & Lopez-Paz, 2021; Idrissi et al., 2022; Ye et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022a).
044
045   Several studies have sought to elucidate this phenomenon. In one line of research, the prevailing
046   theoretical models in DG are typically established based on domain adaptation (Ben-David et al.,
047   2010; Ben-Hur et al., 2001; Phung et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2020; Johansson et al., 2019), which
048
      mainly discuss the differences between source and target domains. In other approaches grounded in
      causality, namely (Arjovsky et al., 2020; Mitrovic et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2023), there is typically
049
      an assumption of having prior knowledge of target domains. There are also studies on the optimality
050
      conditions for generalization. Specifically, Ruan et al. (2021)1 require the optimal representation to
051   be discriminative for the task and the representation’s marginal support to be same across source and
052   target, which theoretically, (Ruan et al., 2021) also require knowledge of target domains.
053

064   While these analyses offer insights about DG from various perspectives, we argue that these conclu-
065   sions do not fully contribute to our understanding of generalization in practice where only a finite
066   number of training domains are available. Indeed, these frameworks establish generalization either
067   under the condition that target domains are known or diverse, or when sufficient number of training
068   domains are given. Consequently, it remains largely unknown regarding the extent to which domain
069   generalization can be attained in limited and finite number of domains, as well as the nature of the
070
      representation required to achieve this. Our work seeks to fill in this gap with a comprehensive study
      of DG landscape in light of the following aspects:
071
072   1. Conditions for Generalization. We first systematically develop a set of necessary and sufficient
073   conditions for generalization. We reaffirm that although existing DG methods strive to achieve the
074   sufficient conditions, these conditions remain non-verifiable, thus cannot guarantee the chance of
075   reaching a global optimal hypothesis when training domains are only finite (See Section 3).
076   2. DG through the lens of Necessity and Sufficiency. We then shed light on how the DG dynamics
077   is greatly reshaped in limited domain settings. Our analysis reveals that when a sufficient condition
078   (3.5 or 3.4 in Table 1) is met, it automatically results in the fulfillment of both necessary conditions
079   (3.3 and 3.7 in Table 1). DG literature thus tends to overlook the role of the necessary conditions
080   in real-world scenarios, particularly the condition of sufficient representation function (3.7). When
081   the sufficient conditions cannot be guaranteed, the necessary conditions in fact hold greater practical
082
      value in determining how to maximize the likelihood of achieving generalization (See Section 4.1).
      This licenses a new view to understanding why DG algorithms fail to outperform the fundamental
083
      approach of empirical risk minimization (ERM) on standard benchmarks (See Section 4.2).
084
085   3. Learning Sufficient Invariant Representation. Finally, we empirically validate our theories
086   by proposing a practical method that promotes the sufficient representation constraint via ensemble
087   learning, while maintains the necessary conditions via a novel representation alignment strategy.
088
      Our method demonstrates superior performance across all experimental settings (See Section 5).

      While attempting to restrict the set of feasible solutions, a DG algorithm, with its extra constraints,
325   may as well reduce the grey area, by restricting the global optimal set to only solutions that also meet
326   the constraints. With arbitrary constraints, there is a possibility that the grey area shrinks to null.
327   Interestingly, a key insight from Theorem 3.8 is that, under the Condition 3.3, as long as the solution
328   of an algorithm fulfills the sufficient representation function constraint (Condition 3.7), there exists
329   a non-empty F ∗ ⊆ FEtr ; otherwise F ∗ = ∅. In fact, that an algorithm meets a sufficient condition
330   implies the satisfaction of Condition 3.7 by default.
331   In summary, an algorithm should be effectively designed to minimize the space FEtr while main-
332   taining the coverage of F ∗ . Condition 3.3 ensures the green area is non-empty i.e., FEtr ̸= ∅ while
333   Condition 3.7 ensures the grey one is non-empty i.e., F ∗ ̸= ∅. Satisfying both conditions further
334   guarantees the existence of the global optimal solutions in F ∗ ⊆ FEtr . In contrast, if both conditions
335   are violated, the algorithm has zero chance of achieving generalization. Despite its significance, ex-
336   isting DG algorithms tends to overlook Condition 3.7. From finite training domains, they thus cannot
337   guarantee the possibility of searching for global optimal hypotheses.

339   4.2   U NDERSTANDING DG L ITERATURE VIA N ECESSITY
340
      It is clear that in order to be considered a globally optimal candidate, a learned hypothesis from finite
341
      domains must meet the necessary conditions 3.3 and 3.7. Following the previous analysis, we here
342
      review the popular classes of DG methods and discuss when they meet or fail these conditions.
343
344   Representation Alignment. These approaches aim to learn a representation function g for data X
345   such that g(X) is invariant or consistent across different domains. Key studies like (Long et al.,
346
      2017; Ganin et al., 2016; Li et al., 2018b; Nguyen et al., 2021; Shen et al., 2018; Xie et al., 2017;
      Ilse et al., 2020) focus on learning such domain-invariant representations by reducing the divergence
347
      between latent marginal distributions E[g(X)|E] where E represents a domain environment. Other
348
      methods seek to align the conditional distributions E[g(X)|Y = y, E] across domains as seen in (Li
349   et al., 2018c; Tachet des Combes et al., 2020). However, achieving true invariance is challenging

      Invariant Prediction. These methods aim to learn a consistent optimal classifier across domains.
      For example, Invariant Risk Minimization (IRM) (Arjovsky et al., 2020) seeks to learn a represen-
370
      tation function g(x) with invariant predictors E[Y |g(x), E]. This goal aligns with Condition 3.7

373   domains, assuming that the optimal risks are similar across domains. If, however, the optimal solu-
374   tions exhibit large loss variations, balancing risks could result in suboptimal performance for some
375   domains, violating Condition 3.3. Furthermore, with a limited number of training domains, both
376   IRM and VREx may struggle to identify the optimal invariant predictor, as discussed by Rosenfeld
377   et al. (2020) and may not offer advantages over ERM, especially when representations from differ-
      ent domains occupy distinct regions in the representation space, as noted by (Ahuja et al., 2020).

385   is to utilize predefined or learnable transformations T on the original sample X or its features g(x)
386   to create augmented data T (X) or T (g(x)). Applying various transformations during training effec-
387   tively increases the training dataset, which, according to Theorem 3.4, should narrow the hypothesis
388   space. However, it’s crucial that transformation T maintains the integrity of the causal factors. This
389   implies a necessity for some knowledge of the target domain to ensure the transformations do not
390   alter the causal/invariant information (Gao et al., 2023), otherwise it risks violating Condition 3.7
391   (e.g., augmentation possibly introduces misleading information (Zhang & Ma, 2022)).
392   Ensemble Learning. Ensemble learning (Zhou, 2012) refers to training multiple copies of the
393   same architecture with different initializations or splits of the training data, then ensembling the
394   individual models for prediction. This straightforward technique has been shown to outperform a
395   single model across various applications, including DG (Zhou et al., 2021; Ding & Fu, 2017; Zhou
396   et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2020a; Mancini et al., 2018; Cha et al., 2021; Arpit et al., 2022). Unlike
397   explicit ensemble methods where multiple models (or model components) need to be trained, Cha
398   et al. (2021); Rame et al. (2022); Wortsman et al. (2022) demonstrate that averaging model weights
399
      (WA) at different time steps during training to form a single model at test time (Izmailov et al.,
      2018) can significantly enhance robustness under domain shift. Different from the previous works,
400
      our analysis in Section 5.1) provides a new insight that ensemble-based methods can also encourage
401
      the learning of sufficient representation (Condition 3.7) to promote generalizability.

404   5     S UFFICIENT I NVARIANT R EPRESENTATION L EARNING
405
406   Section 4.2 highlights that existing DG strategies attempt to maximize the likelihood of seeking a
407   global optimal hypothesis from different directions yet with several drawbacks. Furthermore, that
408
      they all overlook Condition 3.7 poses a risk of landing in regions with empty solution set. Generally,
      an effective DG algorithm is one that strives to attain the sufficient conditions while guarantees the
409
      necessary conditions. Here we propose a method that exploits the joint effect of the two sets of
410
      conditions to boost generalization.
411
412   In the following, we explain how to incorporate the sufficient representation constraint via ensemble
413   learning and present a novel representation alignment strategy that can enforce the necessary condi-
414
      tions. We particularly do not consider invariant prediction since it cannot substantiate its superiority
      over ERM with a potential of violating both necessary conditions. Meanwhile, data augmentation
415
      typically provides significant benefits and can be integrated in a plug-and-play fashion. Since it
416
      requires prior knowledge, users should apply it carefully based on their expertise.

1247   Optimal Representation (Ruan et al., 2021) While at first glance, (Ruan et al., 2021) and our
1248
       work share the same goal of identifying the necessary and sufficient conditions for generalization,
       the two studies fundamentally differ in the following aspects:
1249
1250   Ruan et al. (2021) aim to identify the set of conditions that are both necessary and sufficient, which
1251   provide theoretical guarantee essentially by assuming some knowledge of target domains. Without
1252   accessing target information, generalization is provably impossible. Meanwhile, we focus on ana-
1253   lyzing generalizability from limited domains without assuming any additional information from the
1254
       target.
1255   More concretely, Ruan et al. (2021) propose the idealized domain generalization hypothesis (IDG),
1256   which is the expected worst-case target risk over source risk minimizers:
1257

1258

       RIDG is an expectation over all possible pairs of domains (ei , ej ) ∼ P where P is the distribution
1262   over domain space E. During training, they sample any two domains from the domain distribution,
1263   assigning one as the source and the other as the target, to determine the worst-case target risk.
1264
1265
       The representation Z = g(X) deemed optimal for IDG must satisfy two conditions (by Theorem 1
       therein):
1266
1267          • Sufficient representation: the representation needs to be task-discriminative, allowing a
1268            predictor to minimize risk across all domains. In the presence of all domains, this condition
1269            can be simply satisfied by learning a hypothesis optimal for all training domains.
1270
              • The representation’s marginal support must be consistent across all pairs of source and
1271            target domains. This condition generally coincides with our assumption of causal support,
1272            which is a common assumption across DG literature.
1273
1274   It is clear from the formulation RIDG that all possible domains should be known to achieve gen-
1275   eralization. Ruan et al. (2021) also point out the challenge in generalization without data from the
1276   target domain and recommends incorporating data augmentation from pre-trained models such as
1277   CLIP. To our best knowledge, using augmentation in DG is not new. Various studies have shown
1278
       that access to all label-preserving augmentations (which is generally unfeasible) would reveal true
       causal factors (Mitrovic et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2023). To satisfy this condition, Ruan et al. (2021)
1279
       assume augmentation is Bayes-preserving augmentation (Assumption 10 therein).

1502            dataset contains photographs of wild animals taken by camera traps; camera trap locations
1503            are different across domains.
1504          • DomainNet (Peng et al., 2019) contains 596,006 images of dimension (3, 224, 224) and
1505            345 classes, over 6 domains clipart, infograph, painting, quickdraw, real and sketch. This
1506            is the biggest dataset in terms of the number of samples and classes.
