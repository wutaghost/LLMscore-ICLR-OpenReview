010                                                                  A BSTRACT
011
012                  Recently, multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have received much atten-
013                  tion for their impressive capabilities. The evaluation of MLLMs is becoming crit-
014                  ical to analyzing attributes of MLLMs and providing valuable insights. However,
015                  current benchmarks overlook the problem of prompt sensitivity - minor prompt
016                  variations may lead to significant performance fluctuations. Thus, inappropri-
017                  ate prompts may obscure the models’ capabilities, underestimating the models’
018                  performance. Moreover, different models have different preferences for different
019
                     prompts, and thus, using the same prompt for all models will cause evaluation
                     bias. This paper analyzes this deficiency in existing benchmarks and further in-
020
                     troduces a new evaluation framework named TP-Eval, which introduces a prompt
021
                     customization method to reduce evaluation biases and tap models’ potential. TP-
022                  Eval will rewrite the original prompts to different customized prompts for different
023                  models. In particular, we propose some well-designed modules for prompt cus-
024                  tomization tailored to the scenario of MLLM evaluation. Extensive experiments
025                  demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach to uncovering models’ capabilities,
026                  and TP-Eval should benefit the community in developing more comprehensive
027                  and convincing MLLM evaluation benchmarks.

051   Large language models (LLMs), such as ChatGPT, and Claude, are becoming a milestone in achiev-
052   ing artificial general intelligence (AGI). Recently, beyond text conversation, multimodal large lan-
053   guage models (MLLMs), like GPT-4o (Achiam et al. (2023)), Deepseek (Lu et al. (2024)), InternVL
      (Chen et al. (2024)) and LLaVA (Liu et al. (2024a)), have received much attention for their im-

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      pressive capabilities to understand multimodal inputs (this paper focuses on image and text). Subse-
055   quently, researchers present various benchmarks to evaluate their performance in different scenarios.
056   Most apply prompt-based benchmarking approaches to ask models multimodal questions and assess
057   their responses. For instance, MMT-Bench by Ying et al. (2024) comprehensively evaluates per-
058   formance in 162 general tasks spanning 32 categories. Meanwhile, MMMU by Yue et al. (2024)
059   encompasses six core disciplines drawn from university curricula and assesses performance on mul-
060   tidisciplinary tasks requiring domain-specific knowledge and meticulous reasoning. Convincing
061   benchmarking is crucial to analyze the attributes of models, provide valuable insights, and guide the
062   development of MLLMs.
063   Nevertheless, recent research (Zhan et al. (2022; 2023; 2024)) found that LLMs and MLLMs exhibit
064   pronounced sensitivity to prompt variations. Thus, minor modifications to questions in benchmarks
065   may lead to significant output differences. This makes prompt-based benchmarking unreliable since
066   models’ low accuracy may be owed to unsuitable prompts, not their inner capability. Furthermore,
067   many MLLMs’ benchmarks use simple and uniform prompts for all samples in a specific task,
068   which aggravates the problem and causes general underestimation. Additionally, different models
069
      show various sensitivity to the same prompt changes, and existing evaluation frameworks fail to
      consider such prompt-induced bias and may not be able to conduct a convincing comparison.
070
071   To address the aforementioned deficiencies, this paper introduces TP-Eval, a novel evaluation frame-
072   work for MLLMs that customizes optimal prompts for different models to fully tap their potential
073   during evaluation while mitigating the effects leading to performance underestimation by prompt
074   sensitivity. We posit that this framework enables researchers to assess the strengths and weaknesses
075
      of various models more accurately. To ensure fairness across models while also managing labor
      costs, it is essential for the prompt customization process to be automated. A relevant technique
076
      is automatic prompt optimization, as exemplified by recent methods such as ProTeGi Pryzant et al.
077
      (2023) and OPRO Yang et al. (2023), which employ an optimizer-scorer architecture. These methods
078   generate multiple candidate prompts and score them on a training set to identify the most effective
079   option.
080
081
      Inspired by this, TP-Eval implements prompt customization through automatic prompt optimization
      tailored to MLLMs’ evaluation. In particular, related prompt optimization methods consider text
082
      only, while our prompt customization incorporates text with images. Moreover, the data scale of the
083
      MLLM benchmark is usually limited (e.g., 20 validation samples per task in MMT-Bench) due to
084   the high construction cost, while related prompt optimization methods did not consider this few-shot
085   scenario and easily caused overfitting. Thus, our method introduces a novel error introspection from
086   wrong responses and employs some designs to limit the prompt semantic change. They significantly
087   improve the performance of our method.
088
      We conduct extensive experiments to reveal the presence of prompt-induced underestimation and
089
      bias in MLLM evaluation and demonstrate that the TP-Eval framework effectively mitigates these
090   issues. The primary contributions of this paper can be outlined as follows:
091
092          • We identify and analyze prompt design deficiencies in existing MLLMs’ benchmarks that
093            lead to underestimation and evaluation bias due to prompt sensitivity in MLLMs.
094          • We propose TP-Eval, a novel evaluation framework for MLLMs that customizes optimal
095            prompts for distinct models and makes it practical through automatic prompt optimization
096            tailored to MLLMs’ benchmarks.
097          • We conducted extensive experiments on advanced MLLM benchmarks and various
098            MLLMs to demonstrate the effectiveness of our method in alleviating the underestimation
099            bias in evaluation.

      2.2     I DEAL E VALUATION
155
156   The ideal evaluation should be able to evaluate the true capabilities of the model. However, due to the
157   significant performance influence caused by prompt design, how do we define the true capabilities
158   during evaluation? We argue that models’ true capabilities are performance under optimal prompts,
159   considering that users will also refine the prompts to get desirable responses when using MLLMs.
160   The optimal prompts should be derived from slight modifications from the benchmarks’ original
161   prompts while maintaining the semantic integrity of the task instructions. The optimal prompts
      for different models may be identical or different. Therefore, we propose TP-Eval, an evaluation


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      framework that customizes the best prompts for each model in each task, thereby tapping their
163   potential and uncovering their true capabilities.
164
165   Manual exploration of optimal prompts during evaluation is time-consuming and impractical. In-
166
      spired by existing works on automatic prompt optimization for LLMs, we propose to use an au-
      tomated prompt customizer to leverage original prompts from benchmarks and a few examples to
167
      customize specific prompts for each MLLM under evaluation, thereby tapping their potential.
168
169   However, existing text-only prompt optimization methods are not applicable. On the one hand, the
170   data scale for multi-modal tasks is relatively small, especially for evaluation data, which necessitates
171   that the prompt customizer possesses a strong few-shot capability, which is overlooked by existing
172
      methods. On the other hand, the desirable prompt customization requires a new framework to utilize
      visual information beyond text, and the cost associated with calling MLLM APIs is prohibitively
173
      high, making extensive calls impractical. Therefore, a novel prompt customization method tailored
174
      specifically for multi-modal benchmarks is needed.

177   3     R ELATED WORKS
178
179   3.1   R ESEARCH ON P ROMPT S ENSITIVITY
180
      Some studies have revealed that even minor prompt modifications, which have negligible impact
181
      on human semantic understanding, can lead to significant shifts in the output of LLMs (Zhan et al.
182
      (2022; 2023)). This property has been widely exploited in the creation of adversarial examples,
183   where small perturbations to the embeddings or input text can induce the model to generate in-
184   correct or misleading answers (Zhan et al. (2024)). This sensitivity allows minor adjustments to
185   questions in LLM benchmarks to significantly impact the final evaluation performance. Recent re-
186   search has begun exploring variations in prompt formatting to achieve better results (Sclar et al.
187   (2023)). Similar phenomena also occur for MLLM. However, addressing this deficiency in MLLM
188   benchmark design remains relatively underexplored. In this work, we provide a detailed analysis of
189   prompt design issues and introduce an effective evaluation framework with prompt customization to
190   avoid the above problems and bias from prompts.
191
192   3.2   P ROMPT E NGINEERING & O PTIMIZATION
193
      Prompt engineering seeks to identify effective prompts for LLMs to optimize their task performance.
194
      To minimize manual effort, researchers have explored automatic prompt optimization, broadly cate-
195
      gorized into continuous and discrete methods. Discrete methods directly optimize natural language
196   prompts using techniques such as reinforcement learning (Zhang et al. (2022)) or prompt editing
197   (Prasad et al. (2022)). In contrast, continuous methods (Lester et al. (2021); Li & Liang (2021)) per-
198   form optimization within the LLMs’ embedding space, enabling gradient-based approaches. Given
199   the unprecedented capabilities of LLMs, recent research has started leveraging them as prompt op-
200   timizers. For example, Yang & Li (2023) integrates LLMs with evolutionary algorithms to enhance
201   prompt optimization, while Yang et al. (2023); Pryzant et al. (2023) focuses on adapting concepts
202   and techniques from gradient-based model optimizers, including gradient descent (Pryzant et al.
203   (2023)) and momentum methods (Yang et al. (2023)), for LLM-based prompt optimization.
204   Our work follows discrete methods and employs MLLM as prompt optimizers. In particular, we
205   combine error introspection, semantic change, and accuracy as “pseudo-gradients” proposed by
206   Tang et al. (2024) to guide the MLLM optimizer in the multimodal scenario. We also introduce a
207   final re-ranking scheme for better performance.

      We show the overall framework of our customization method in Fig. 2. Starting from the initial text
237
      prompt p0 for a task from the multimodal evaluation dataset, we utilize GPT-4o mini as an optimizer
238   MO and a few examples Df ew (questions and answers) from the evaluation dataset to obtain an
239   optimal prompt p∗ for the MLLM MT . Specifically, we first feed p0 to a scorer, which consists
240   of the MT and an answer analyzer MA (GPT-4o mini), to output the scores and introspection.
241   Then we use these results to construct a well-designed meta-prompt for the optimizer MO to obtain
242   optimized prompts P1 = {p1 , p2 , · · · , pn }. We feed them to the scorer and iteratively run this
243   framework to collect N sets of optimized prompts {P1 , P2 , · · · , PN } with their scores. Finally, we
244   select the optimal prompt p∗ according to the scores. Please note that we will feed the corresponding
245   images to MT and corresponding images and answers to MA . We will introduce the details of the
246
      prompt customization method in the following.
247
248   4.2     S CORER
249
      In the i-th iteration, we feed the prompt set Pi (using p0 in the first iteration) to the scorer to obtain
250
      the corresponding scores and introspection (i.e., pseudo gradient) of each prompt.
251
252
      4.2.1       S CORE G ENERATION
253
254   We first feed these prompts with corresponding images to MT to obtain models’ responses. Then
255   considering the variations of answers and most benchmarks apply choice questions, we use MA
256   (GPT4o-mini) extract choices and then compute the accuracy api on Df ew for pi .
257   Using accuracy as a reward only may lead to drastic changes in the new prompt and destroy the
258   optimization. Thus we utilize a semantic similarity metric as proposed by Tang et al. (2024) to
259   limit the changes in each iteration. Specifically, we use BERT by Kenton & Toutanova (2019) to
260   extract the embedding of the current prompt pi and the original prompt p0 , then calculate their cosine
261   similarity as spi .
262
      We combine api and spi as the final score cpi = αapi +(1−α)spi , where α is a weighting coefficient
263   to make a trade-off between optimization and original semantic meaning maintain.
264
265
      4.2.2       I NTROSPECTION G ENERATION
266
267   We argue that scores are quantitative and not informative enough, especially in the few-shot exam-
268   ples, and thus, we introduce to employ additional introspection during optimization. Specifically, we
269   aim to help the optimizer better understand the deficiencies in the current prompt. To achieve this,
      we represent introspection Ii on the incorrect responses in Df ew of MT under pi , allowing MO to

275
      We use the optimizer MO (GPT4o-mini) to generate a new prompt set Pi+1 from all history prompts
276   {P0 , · · · , Pi }. Specifically, we design a meta-prompt as shown in Fig. 2 and complete it using K
277   prompts with Top-K scores from {P0 , · · · , Pi }. We also feed their scores and introspection to the
278   optimizer. The meta prompt is composed of four parts: description, pseudo gradient (i.e., prompts
279   with their scores and introspection), examples (questions with ground-truth answers from Df ew ),
280   and instruction. The description is used to describe the prompt optimization tasks. The pseudo
281   gradient and examples are used to provide information for the optimization. The instruction is
282   used to generate new prompts. In particular, to ensure smooth optimization and not overlook op-
283   timal prompts, we use a decaying edit distance You can only edit at most {counter}

      we will add an initialized meaningless phrase and optimize it rather than the whole prompt, see the

288   4.4   I TERATION
289
      We use the above scorer-optimizer framework iterative to obtain N prompt set {P1 , P2 , · · · , PN }

      with scores for each prompt. Then we select the optimal prompt from all history prompts.

292   In contrast to related prompt optimization methods using large-scale training data to obtain candidate
293   prompts and selecting the optimal prompt with the highest accuracy, MLLM evaluation can provide
294   only limited examples in the optimization. Thus, we have to consider the problem of overfitting
295
      and bias in the few examples. The introduced semantic cosine similarity and decaying edit distance
      can alleviate this problem. Moreover, in the selection of the optimal prompts, we employ a higher
296
      weighting coefficient α∗ > α to re-compute each prompt’s score and select the prompt with the
297
      highest score.

304   Models. The MLLMs to be evaluated (i.e., MT ) are LLaVA-1.5-7B, DeepSeek-VL-7B,
305   Mini-InternVL-Chat-4B-V1-5. We use GPT-4o-mini for optimizer (MO ) and answer
306   analyzer (MA ).
307   Benchmarks. We use MMT-Bench and MMMU as the evaluation benchmarks. MMT-Bench is
308   designed for the evaluation of general capabilities, while MMMU is designed for multi-discipline
309   evaluation. Considering our limited resources, we select a subset of MMT-Bench as MMT-S, which
310   contains 83 tasks (19 categories). We use the development set and validation set of MMMU.
311
      Settings of prompt optimization We evaluate our method in two settings: optimizing the
312   whole prompt or optimizing the newly added phrase. MMT-Bench follows the most prevalent
313   MLLM benchmark format, which uses the same prompt template within a task (e.g., How many
314   <object> are there in the image? for the task of object counting). Thus, we opti-
315   mize the whole prompt for each task in MMT-S. In MMMU, each question is identical, and thus we
316   add an initialized meaningless phrase Answer the questions about {task name} as the
317   prompt to be optimized and move the original prompt to <QUESTION> in the meta prompt.
318   Implementation details. For MMT-S, we utilize the officially designated validation set as Df ew ,
319   which comprises approximately 10% of the total data, with roughly 20 samples per task. For
320   MMMU, we combine the development and validation sets and allocate half of the data as Df ew .
321   We follow VLMEvalKit by Duan et al. (2024) to implement the answer extraction module in MA .
322   The total optimization iteration N = 16, with each round generating three new prompts. In each
323   iteration, we select the top eight (i.e., K = 8) prompts for the meta prompt. We set the temperature
      to 1.0 when generating new prompts. During the optimization phase, we set α to 0.8 to encourage

430      Similar to many text-only prompt optimizations, our method, while ensuring an overall enhance-
431      ment in performance and a closer alignment with the true capability for evaluated models, may still
         encounter optimization failures for a limited number of tasks. This can, in turn, result in a slight

         performance deterioration when using optimized prompts. For instance, although LLaVA has an
433      overall improvement of 25% across 32 tasks, it also experiences an approximate 6% performance

435      the validation set currently designated by the official multi-modal benchmarks, which may cause
436      overfitting on the training set. Despite our efforts to incorporate introspection mechanisms for more

501   We investigated MLLM benchmarks and found that overly simplistic or unsuitable textual prompts
502
      may lead to an underestimation of models’ capabilities. To address this issue, we propose an ideal
      evaluation framework, TP-Eval, which customizes the most suitable task prompt for each model to
503
      mitigate prompt-induced biases and tap the models’ potential. To achieve this goal, we drew on the
504
      successful experiences of automatic prompt optimization on text-only LLMs and designed a prompt
505   optimization method tailored to the few-shot scenario of MLLM benchmarks. Our experiment re-
506   sults for three models on the MMT and MMMU indicate the effectiveness of our method.
