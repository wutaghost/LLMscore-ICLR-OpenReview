008                                                A BSTRACT
009
010            In this work, we focus on the alignment problem of diffusion models with a contin-
011            uous reward function, which represents specific objectives for downstream tasks,
012            such as increasing darkness or improving the aesthetics of images. The central
013
               goal of the alignment problem is to adjust the distribution learned by diffusion
               models such that the generated samples maximize the target reward function. We
014
               propose a novel alignment approach, named Direct Noise Optimization (DNO),
015
               that optimizes the injected noise during the sampling process of diffusion models.
016            By design, DNO operates at inference-time, and thus is tuning-free and prompt-
017            agnostic, with the alignment occurring in an online fashion during generation.
018            We rigorously study the theoretical properties of DNO and also propose variants
019            to deal with non-differentiable reward functions. Furthermore, we identify that
020            naive implementation of DNO occasionally suffers from the out-of-distribution
021            reward hacking problem, where optimized samples have high rewards but are
022            no longer in the support of the pretrained distribution. To remedy this issue, we
023            leverage classical high-dimensional statistics theory to an effective probability
024
               regularization technique. We conduct extensive experiments on several important
               reward functions and demonstrate that the proposed DNO approach can achieve
025
               state-of-the-art reward scores within a reasonable time budget for generation.

057   A common mathematical formulation in RL-based methods is to maximize the expected reward

059

061
      on the data used. In the online method, the algorithm has the capacity to query the reward function
      throughout the entire optimization process. Two notable online RL methods are DDPO (Black et al.,
062
      2023) and DPOK (Fan et al., 2023), which have been shown to improve downstream objectives
063
      such as aesthetics and compressibility. Alternatively, research has also delved into the offline RL
064   optimization setting, where an explicit reward function is not accessible and only a fixed preference
065   dataset is utilized. Noteworthy works in this category include Diffusion-DPO (Wallace et al., 2023a)
066   and SPIN-Diffusion (Yuan et al., 2024).

      Before delving into the formal description of this method, it is useful to revisit the sampling process
      of diffusion models, which also serves as the foundation for the rest of this work. Solving the ODE
070
      equation 1 or the SDE equation 2 typically involves discretizing the time steps into T steps. By
071
      starting with the initial noise xT ∼ N (0, I), the solution process for the ODE equation 1 can be
072   viewed as a mapping that transforms the initial noise xT into less noisy data through the following
073   process: xt−1 = ODE_solver(xt ), for t = T, ..., 1. After T steps, the output will be the generated
074   sample x0 . Similarly, solving the SDE equation 2 can be seen as a mapping that gradually converts
075   both the initial noise xT and the entire extra random noise vectors zT , ..., z1 into the generated sample
076   x0 , e.g., through the following process: xt−1 = SDE_solver(xt , zt ), for t = T, ..., 1, where zt is
077   also drawn from standard Gaussian distribution.
078   Remark 1. Throughout this work, for simplicity, we adopt only the DDIM sampling algorithm (Song
079   et al., 2020a) for our experiments, as it remains one of the most popular choices for diffusion sampling
080   and, more importantly, supports both ODE-style and SDE-style sampling. To be self-contained, we
081
      summarize the notations and procedure of the DDIM sampling method in Appendix A.
082   Diffusion Sampling as a Noise-to-Sample Mapping. From the diffusion sampling process described
083
      above, we observe that the sampling process can be conceptualized as an end-to-end mapping Mθ (z),
      which translates noise vectors z, sampled from the standard Gaussian distribution, into generated
084
      samples. Here, the noise vectors z serve as a unified abstraction for both the xT in the ODE-based
085
      sampling process and the (xT , {z1 , ..., zT }) in the SDE-based sampling process. As we can see, the
086   noise vector z uniquely determines the generated sample from the diffusion models.
087   Two recent studies, AlignProp (Prabhudesai et al., 2023) and DRaFT (Clark et al., 2023), have
088   proposed to directly fine-tune diffusion models using differentiable rewards. Specifically, their opti-
089   mization objective is formulated as: maxθ Ez∼N (0,I) [r(Mθ (z))]. Both the AlignProp (Prabhudesai
090   et al., 2023) and DRaFT (Clark et al., 2023) methods utilize the ODE-type DDIM solver for the
091   sampling process, specifically employing Algorithm 1 with η = 0.
092   1.1.3 L OSS -G UIDED D IFFUSION
093   A recent work focusing on loss-guided diffusion models (LGD) (Song et al., 2023a) also examines
094   the concept of aligning diffusion models with differentiable rewards. Unlike the methods mentioned
095   previously, LGD is an Inference-Time alignment method, meaning it does not necessitate modifica-
096   tions to the pretrained model θ and only works by modifying the inference process. In essence, the
097   core idea of LGD is that, during the sampling process for the ODE equation 1, it considers a modified
098   version of the ODE by introducing a new term that guides the generation toward areas  of higher
099   reward. Specifically, the new ODE is: dxt = f (t)xt − 12 g 2 (t)ϵ(xt , t) + ∇xt r(x0 (xt )) dt, where
100   x0 (xt ) denotes the solution of this ODE starting from xt . However, the gradient term ∇xt r(x0 (xt ))
101
      is not readily available during generation. To address this, the authors suggest utilizing Monte Carlo
      estimation to approximate the gradient. Nevertheless, this estimation tends to be noisy and imprecise,
102
      leading to suboptimal performance, particularly with complex reward functions.

104   1.2 C OMPARING E XISTING M ETHODS
105   Existing works can be generally categorized based on two criteria: whether it requires fine-tuning
106   and whether the reward function needs to be differentiable.
107   Inference-Time or Tuning-based Methods. All RL-based methods and the direct fine-tuning
      method are tuning-based, meaning they necessitate adjustments to the network models θ. There


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      are two main disadvantages associated with tuning-based methods. The first is that they require
109   fine-tuning for new reward functions, a process that can consume considerable resources, especially
110   when faced with extensive choices for reward functions. The second drawback is that the fine-tuning
111   process typically relies on a limited set of input prompts, which challenges the model to generalize
112   to new and unseen prompts. In contrast, methods such as LGD (Song et al., 2023a) belong to the
113   inference-time category. The main advantage of the inference-time approach is its elimination of
114   the need for fine-tuning, as well as its ability to avoid the prompt generalization issues associated
115   with tuning-based methods. This makes it prompt-agnostic, as the inference-time method optimizes
116   the sample specifically for the given prompt during the inference process. Further, inference-time
117
      methods require significantly fewer computing resources than tuning-based methods. However, the
      major drawback of inference-time methods is the substantial increase in the time required for the
118
      generation process.
119
      Differentiable or Non-Differentiable Rewards. Current RL-based methods can work by utilizing
120   solely the value or preference information of the reward functions, therefore they can still work even
121   when the reward function is non-differentiable. In contrast, the existing direct fine-tuning methods
122   and LGD require the reward function to be differentiable. In practice, working with non-differentiable
123   reward functions is important due to their prevalence. This non-differentiable property can arise from
124   the simulation-based procedures used to compute the reward, or the reward function itself may be a
125   black box.

128
129   In this work, we focus on inference-time alignment of diffusion models, as we believe that flexibility
130   with the choices of the reward functions, generalization on unseen prompts, and low computing
131
      requirements are critical for a broad range of real-world applications. Our primary goal is to design
      an inference-time alignment method that can match the performance of tuning-based methods by
132
      incurring a reasonable additional time cost, and is capable of handling both differentiable and non-
133
      differentiable objective functions. To this end, we focus on an under-explored technique for achieving
134   inference-time alignment of diffusion models—Direct Noise Optimization (DNO). Specifically, we
135   make the following contributions:
136
             • We conduct a self-contained and comprehensive study for DNO, and demonstrate that noise
137
               optimization can be theoretically interpreted as sampling from an improved distribution.
138
             • We identify out-of-distribution reward-hacking as a critical issue in DNO. To address this
139
               issue, we introduce a novel probability-regularized noise optimization method designed to

               ensure the generated samples remain within the support of pretrained distribution.

             • By developing a novel and highly efficient hybrid gradient approximation strategy, we

143
             • Through the experiments on several important image reward functions, we demonstrate that
144            our proposed method can achieve state-of-the-art scores in comparison to existing alignment
145            methods, without any fine-tuning on the parameters of diffusion models.

243   3     O UT-O F -D ISTRIBUTION R EWARD -H ACKING IN N OISE O PTIMIZATION
244   It has been observed that when aligning generative models (e.g., including autoregressive language
245   models or diffusion models) with reward functions, one can experience the so-called reward-hacking,
246   i.e., the optimized samples yield high rewards but do not possess the desirable properties (Miao
247   et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024a). Generally, there are two different types of reward-hacking. In the
248   first type, the reward function admits some shortcuts, so the optimized samples score high rewards
249   but remain barely distinguishable from the samples of the pretrained distribution. The second type
250   is related to the generative model used – the optimized samples no longer fall within the support
251   of the pretrained distribution after optimization. We denote this second type of reward-hacking as
252
      Out-Of-Distribution (OOD) Reward-Hacking. In this work, we will focus on this second type
      and reveal that OOD reward-hacking is a common issue in DNO. In Appendix B.2, we provide
253
      two visualized examples of the phenomenon of OOD Reward-Hacking using both a 2-dimensional
254
      diffusion model and an image diffusion model.
255
256   One of our key contributions in this work is to identify one critical cause of OOD reward-hacking in
257   noise optimization. That is, the optimized noise vectors stray towards the low-probability regions
258
      of the high-dimensional standard Gaussian distribution; in other words, there is an extremely low
      chance of such noise vectors being sampled from the Gaussian distribution. As diffusion models are
259
      originally trained with Gaussian noise, when the noise vectors originate from these low-probability
260
      areas—such as vectors comprised entirely of zeros—the neural network within the diffusion models
261   may incur significant approximation errors for these particular inputs. This error, in turn, leads to the
262   generation of out-of-distribution samples. In the subsequent section, we introduce a novel method to
263   measure the extent to which noise is part of the low-probability region by leveraging the classical
264   concentration inequalities for high-dimensional Gaussian distributions.
265

312   term, we use the expectation of the log probabilities over the permutation matrices, rather than the
313   minimum probability P (z). This is because the expectation is smoother for optimization purposes.
314
315   4    TACKLING N ON -D IFFERENTIABLE R EWARD F UNCTIONS
316
      In the previous section, DNO method has been applied to optimize differentiable reward functions.
317
      However, in many applications the ground-truth gradient of the reward function is unavailable. Such
318   non-differentiable properties can arise from various scenarios; here we present two representative
319   cases. Firstly, the reward may be computed through simulation-based procedures, such as the JPEG-
320   compressibility employed in DDPO (Black et al., 2023), which calculates the size of an image
321   in bits after running a JPEG compression algorithm. Additionally, the reward function itself may
322   be a black box provided through online API providers, as in the setting considered in (Sun et al.,
323   2022). This scenario is common when the reward function is a large neural network model, like
      those in (Wang et al., 2024a; Lin et al., 2024), making it impossible to directly obtain the gradient


                                                          6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      for optimization. To address these scenarios, we explore adapting the noise optimization approach
325   to handle the optimization of non-differentiable reward functions by estimating the gradient with
326   function values. Specifically, we explore three methods under this setting.
327
328
      Method 1. Concerning optimization with only function value, a major family of optimization
      approaches is zeroth-order optimization algorithms, including ZO-SGD (Nesterov & Spokoiny, 2017).
329
      This method treats the entire mapping r ◦ Mθ (·) as a black-box function and seeks to estimate the
330
      gradient of r ◦ Mθ (·) via function value queries.
331
332   Method 2. It is worth noting that for the mapping r ◦ Mθ (·), only the gradient of the reward
333   function r(x) is not available, and we are still able to compute the gradient of Mθ (z). Therefore, a
334
      straightforward idea is to adopt a hybrid gradient approach—only to estimate the gradient of r, while
      using the ground truth gradient for Mθ (z). Specifically, we denote that the initial noise is z, and the
335
      generated sample is x = Mθ (z). Firstly, we can estimate the gradient of ∇r(x) in a similar fashion
336
      with the ZO-SGD (Nesterov & Spokoiny, 2017):
337
338
                            H1 (x) = Eξ∼N (0,I) [(r(x + µξ) − r(x)) ξ] ≈ C1 ∇r(x),                       (10)
339   where µ is the coefficient for perturbation, and C1 is some constant. With the estimated gradient H1 (x)
340   for the reward function r(x), we can use the following estimated gradient G1 (z) for optimization:
341                                    def.
                                G1 (z) = H1 (x) · ∇z Mθ (z) ≈ C1 ∇z r ◦ Mθ (z),                          (11)
342
      where the main idea is to replace the ground truth ∇r(x) in the chain-rule of differentiating r(Mθ (z)).
343
      We refer this method as Hybrid-1 in the following sections.
344   Remark 3. As one can observe, the computation of equation 11 involves the Jacobian ∇z Mθ (z).
345   However, it is important to note that when we only require the vector-Jacobian product H1 (x) ·
346   ∇z Mθ (z), it is unnecessary to compute the full Jacobian ∇z Mθ (z). In Appendix E, we describe an
347   elegant and efficient way to implement equation 11 using an auto-differentiation technique.
348   Method 3. There is a crucial drawback in the gradient estimator equation 10, that one needs to query
349   the reward function r(·) with noisy input x + µξ. When the reward function is only defined on some
350   manifold M, e.g., defined on the image manifold, rather than the whole space Rn , this can lead to
351   severe problems, because, for some x ∈ M, the noisy sample x + µξ may no longer stay within M.
352   To remedy this issue, we propose to perturb the sample through the latent noise, rather than directly
353   in the sample space. Specifically, our proposed new gradient estimator for ∇r(x) is
354                   H2 (x) = Eξ∼N (0,I) [(r(Mθ (z + µξ)) − r(x)) (Mθ (z + µξ) − x)] .                  (12)
355   Following a similar proof in (Nesterov & Spokoiny, 2017), we can also show that H2 (x) ≈ C2 ∇r(x)
356   for some constants C2 . As we can see, when computing the gradient equation 12, we ensure that
357   we query the reward function r(·) with only samples that are within the manifold of the pretrained
358   distribution. Similar to Hybrid-1, we can adopt a gradient estimator G2 (z) with H2 (x):
359                                                 def.
                                              G2 (z) = H2 (x) · ∇z Mθ (z).                               (13)
360
361   We refer this method equation 13 as Hybrid-2. As we will see in Section 5.3, this Hybrid-2 method
362
      is significantly faster than the other two in terms of optimization speed.

528   6     C ONCLUSIONS
529
530   In this work, we present a comprehensive study on Direct Noise Optimization (DNO) for align-
531
      ing diffusion generative models at inference-time. We introduce variants of DNO designed to
      efficiently address challenges such as out-of-distribution reward-hacking and the optimization of
532
      non-differentiable reward functions. More significantly, we demonstrate the exceptional efficacy of
533
      DNO, underscoring its capacity to rival tuning-based methods. The primary limitation of DNO lies
534   in its integration with the sampling process of diffusion models, leading to a substantial increase in
535   processing time compared to direct sampling. Nonetheless, we argue that the additional time cost,
536   being within a reasonable and acceptable range, is a worthwhile trade-off for attaining high-reward
537   generated samples in a wide range of real-world applications. We anticipate DNO gaining greater
538   attention in future research and applications due to its flexibility to accommodate any reward func-
539   tion—or even a combination of different reward functions—while demanding only modest computing
      resources, which positions it as an accessible tool for many practitioners.
