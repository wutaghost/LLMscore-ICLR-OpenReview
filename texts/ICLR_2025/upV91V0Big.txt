009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Training a generalizable agent to continually learn a sequence of tasks from of-
012            fline trajectories is a natural requirement for long-lived agents, yet remains a
013            significant challenge for current offline reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms.
014            Specifically, an agent must be able to rapidly adapt to new tasks using newly col-
015            lected trajectories (plasticity), while retaining knowledge from previously learned
016
               tasks (stability). However, systematic analyses of this setting are scarce, and it
               remains unclear whether conventional continual learning (CL) methods are ef-
017
               fective in continual offline RL (CORL) scenarios. In this study, we develop the
018
               Offline Continual World benchmark and demonstrate that traditional CL meth-
019            ods struggle with catastrophic forgetting, primarily due to the unique distribution
020            shifts inherent to CORL scenarios. To address this challenge, we introduce Com-
021            poFormer, a structure-based continual transformer model that adaptively com-
022            poses previous policies via a meta-policy network. Upon encountering a new
023            task, CompoFormer leverages semantic correlations to selectively integrate rele-
024            vant prior policies alongside newly trained parameters, thereby enhancing knowl-
025            edge sharing and accelerating the learning process. Our experiments reveal that
026            CompoFormer outperforms conventional CL methods, particularly in longer task
027
               sequences, showcasing a promising balance between plasticity and stability.
028
029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Similar to human cognition, a general-purpose intelligent agent is expected to continually acquire
032   new tasks. These sequential tasks can be learned either through online exploration (Malagon et al.,
033   2024; Yang et al., 2023; Wolczyk et al., 2021) or offline from pre-collected datasets (Huang et al.,
034   2024; Gai et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2024a), with the latter being equally critical but having received
035   comparatively less attention. Furthermore, online learning is not always feasible due to the need for
036   direct interaction with the environment, which can be prohibitively expensive in real-world settings.
037   Thus, the study of continual offline reinforcement learning (CORL) is both crucial and valuable for
038   advancing general-purpose intelligence, which integrates offline RL and continual learning (CL).
039   Although offline RL has demonstrated strong performance in learning single tasks (Chen et al., 2021;
040   Hu et al., 2024b;c), it remains prone to catastrophic forgetting and cross-task interference when ap-
041   plied to sequential task learning (Bengio et al., 2020; Fang et al., 2019). Moreover, there is a lack
042   of systematic analysis in this setting, and it remains unclear whether conventional CL methods are
043   effective in CORL settings. CORL faces unique challenges, including distribution shifts between the
044   behavior and learned policies, across offline data from different tasks, and between the learned pol-
045
      icy and saved replay buffers (Gai et al., 2023). As a result, managing the stability-plasticity tradeoff
      in CL and addressing the challenges posed by distribution shifts in offline RL remains a critical issue
046
      (Yue et al., 2024). To tackle this, we leverage sequence modeling using the Transformer architecture
047
      (Hu et al., 2024d; Vaswani, 2017), which reformulates temporal difference (TD) learning into a be-
048   havior cloning framework, enabling a supervised learning approach that aligns better with traditional
049   CL methods. While Transformer-based methods help reduce distribution shift between the behav-
050   ior and learned policies and facilitate knowledge transfer between similar tasks due to the model’s
051   strong memory capabilities (Chen et al., 2021), they may exacerbate shifts between time-evolving
052   datasets and between the learned policy and replay buffer (Huang et al., 2024). Consequently, these
053   methods still face significant stability-plasticity tradeoff problems, even when combined with CL
      approaches for learning sequences of new tasks (see Section 5.2 for details).

073
      To overcome the limitations of previous approaches and better address the plasticity-stability trade-
074
      off, we explore how to train a meta-policy network in CORL. Structure-based methods typically
075
      introduce new sub-networks for each task while preserving parameters from previous tasks, thereby
076   mitigating forgetting and interference (Mallya & Lazebnik, 2018). These methods transfer knowl-
077   edge between modules by transferring relevant knowledge through task-shared parameters. How-
078   ever, as the number of tasks increases, distinguishing valuable information from shared knowledge
079   becomes increasingly challenging (Malagon et al., 2024). In such cases, knowledge sharing may
080   offer limited benefits and could even impede learning on the current task. Therefore, in this paper,
081   we propose leveraging semantic correlations to selectively compose relevant prior learned policy
082   modules, thereby enhancing knowledge sharing and accelerating the learning process.
083   As illustrated in Figure 1, when a new task is introduced, our model, CompoFormer, first utilizes its
084   textual description to compute attention scores with descriptions of previous tasks using the frozen
085   Sentence-BERT (S-BERT) (Reimers, 2019) module and a trainable attention module. After several
086   update iterations based on the final output action loss, if the composed policy output is sufficient
087   for the current task, it is applied directly. Otherwise, new parameters are integrated alongside the
088   composed policy to construct a new policy for the new task. This process naturally forms a cascading
089   structure of policies, growing in depth as new tasks are introduced, with each policy able to access
090
      and compose outputs from prior policies to address the current task (Malagon et al., 2024). This
      approach could effectively manage the plasticity-stability trade-off. Specifically, relevant tasks share
091
      a greater amount of knowledge, facilitating a faster learning process, while less knowledge is shared
092
      between unrelated tasks to minimize cross-task interference. This mechanism enhances plasticity
093   and accelerates the learning process. Simultaneously, the parameters of previously learned tasks
094   remain fixed, preserving stability and reducing the risk of forgetting.
095
096
      In our experiments, we extend the Continual World benchmark (Wolczyk et al., 2021) to the Of-
      fline Continual World benchmark and conduct a comprehensive evaluation of existing CL methods.
097
      Our approach consistently outperforms these baselines across different benchmarks, particularly in
098
      longer task sequences, achieving a marked reduction in forgetting (Table 1). This demonstrates its
099   ability to effectively leverage prior knowledge and accelerate the learning process by adaptively se-
100   lecting relevant policies. Additionally, we highlight the critical contributions of each component
101   in our framework (Figure 4) and show the generalizability of our method across varying task or-
102   der sequences (Table 2). Compared to state-of-the-art approaches, CompoFormer achieves the best
103   trade-off between plasticity and stability (Figure 5).

107   Offline RL. Offline RL focuses on learning policies solely from static, offline datasets D, without
      requiring any further interaction with the environment (Levine et al., 2020). This paradigm signif-

108
      icantly enhances the sample efficiency of RL, particularly in scenarios where interacting with the
109   environment is costly or involves considerable risk, such as safety-critical applications. However,
110   a key challenge in offline RL arises from the distribution shift between the learned policy and the
111   behavior policy that generated the dataset, which can lead to substantial performance degradation
112   (Fujimoto et al., 2019). To address this issue, various offline RL algorithms employ constrained or
113   regularized dynamic programming techniques to minimize deviations from the behavior policy and
114   mitigate the impact of distribution shift (Fujimoto & Gu, 2021; Kumar et al., 2020; Kostrikov et al.,
115   2021). Another promising approach for offline RL is conditional sequence modeling (Hu et al.,
116   2024d; Chen et al., 2021), which predicts future actions based on sequences of past experiences,
117
      represented by state-action-reward triplets. This approach naturally aligns with supervised learning,
      as it restricts the learned policy to remain within the distribution of the behavior policy, while be-
118
      ing conditioned on specific metrics for future trajectories (Hu et al., 2023a; 2024b; Yamagata et al.,
119
      2023; Hu et al., 2023b; 2024e; Meng et al., 2023). Given that most continual learning methods fol-
120   low a supervised learning framework, we adopt the Decision Transformer (Chen et al., 2021) as the
121   base model and implement various continual learning techniques in conjunction with it.
122
123
      Continual RL. Continual learning is a critical and challenging problem in machine learning, aiming
      to enable models to learn from a continuous stream of tasks without forgetting previously acquired
124
      knowledge. Generally, continual learning methods can be categorized into three main approaches
125
      (Masana et al., 2022): (i) Regularization-based approaches (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Aljundi et al.,
126   2018), which introduce regularization terms to prevent model parameters from drifting too far from
127   those learned from prior tasks; (ii) Structure-based methods (Mallya & Lazebnik, 2018; Huang et al.,
128   2024), which allocate fixed subsets of parameters to specific tasks; (iii) Rehearsal-based methods
129   (Chaudhry et al., 2018b; Wolczyk et al., 2021), which retrain the model by merging a small amount
130   of data from previously learned tasks with data from the current task. Most of these methods have
131   been extensively investigated within the context of online RL (Yang et al., 2023; Malagon et al.,
132   2024), whereas there is a paucity of research focused on adapting them to offline RL settings (Huang
133   et al., 2024; Gai et al., 2023). Furthermore, these efforts often differ in their experimental settings,
134
      evaluation metrics, and primarily focus on rehearsal-based methods, often leveraging diffusion mod-
      els (Hu et al., 2024a). Nonetheless, they continue to grapple with the significant stability-plasticity
135
      dilemma (Khetarpal et al., 2022). In this paper, we introduce the Offline Continual World bench-
136
      mark to perform a comprehensive empirical evaluation of existing continual learning methods. Our
137   approach demonstrates a promising balance between plasticity and stability.

188   In this work, given a sequence of previously learned policies {π (i) }i=1,...,k−1 , where each policy
189   π (i) corresponds to task M(i) , we identify two scenarios for the current task M(k) : (i) M(k) can be

191   learning a new policy that leverages knowledge from previous tasks to accelerate learning without

193   task and freezing its weights after training, effectively preventing forgetting. There are two main
194
      approaches to sub-network representation: (i) adding new parameters to construct the sub-network
      (Czarnecki et al., 2018; Malagon et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024), or (ii) applying binary masks
195
      to neurons’ outputs (Serra et al., 2018; Ke et al., 2021; Mallya & Lazebnik, 2018). We provide
196
      solutions for both approaches. The detailed pipeline is summarized in Algorithm 1.

363
364   Table 1 and Figure 3 present the results of all methods evaluated under two settings, OCW10 and
365   OCW20, using the metrics outlined in Section 5.1.
366   Both regularization-based and rehearsal-based methods struggle to perform well, in contrast to their
367   success in supervised learning settings. This underperformance stems from the unique distribution
368   shifts inherent to offline RL, which increase the sensitivity of parameters and negatively impact per-
369   formance. Regularization-based methods impose additional constraints to limit parameter updates
370   and maintain stability, as evidenced by their lower forgetting rates compared to Finetuning baseline.
371   However, these methods still experience significant catastrophic forgetting, often exceeding 0.50.
372   Increasing the weight of the regularization loss can reduce forgetting further, but it also hampers the
373   learning of new tasks, exacerbating the stability-plasticity tradeoff (Appendix G). Moreover, tuning
374
      these hyperparameters is often challenging and time-consuming, particularly in offline settings.
375   For rehearsal-based methods, despite their effectiveness in supervised learning, methods like Perfect
376   Memory and A-GEM exhibit poor performance in offline RL, even when allowed a generous replay
377   buffer. We hypothesize that this is due to the additional distribution shift introduced between the
      replay buffer and the learned policy, as discussed in Gai et al. (2023).

       6                  C ONCLUSION
529
530
      We introduce CompoFormer, a modular growing architecture designed to mitigate catastrophic for-
531   getting and task interference while leveraging knowledge from previous tasks to address new ones.
532   In our experiments, we develop the Offline Continual World benchmark to perform a comprehen-
533   sive empirical evaluation of existing continual learning methods. CompoFormer consistently out-
534   performs these approaches, offering a promising balance between plasticity and stability, all without
535   the need for experience replay or storage of past task data.
536
      We believe this work represents a significant step forward in the development of continual offline
537   reinforcement learning agents capable of learning from numerous sequential tasks. However, chal-
538   lenges remain, particularly concerning the computational cost, which is critical in never-ending
539   continual learning scenarios, and thus warrants further research.

      B.2   S TRUCTURE -BASED M ETHODS
820
      Structure-based methods, also referred to as modularity-based methods, preserve previously ac-
821   quired knowledge by keeping specific sets of parameters fixed. This approach imposes a hard con-
822   straint on the network, in contrast to the soft regularization penalties used in regularization-based
823   methods.
824
      LoRA. As introduced by Huang et al. (2024), this method adds a new LoRA-Linear module when
825
      a new task is introduced. According to Lawson & Qureshi (2024), in sequential decision-making
826
      tasks, Decision Transformers rely more heavily on MLP layers than on attention mechanisms. LoRA
827   leverages this by merging weights that contribute minimally to knowledge sharing and fine-tuning
828   the decisive MLP layers in DT blocks with LoRA to adapt to the current task.
829
830
      PackNet. Introduced by Mallya & Lazebnik (2018), this method iteratively applies pruning tech-
      niques after each task is trained, effectively ”packing” the task into a subset of the neural network
831
      parameters, while leaving the remaining parameters available for future tasks. The parameters as-
832
      sociated with previous tasks are frozen, thus preventing forgetting. Unlike earlier methods, such as
833   progressive networks (Rusu et al., 2016), PackNet maintains a fixed model size throughout learning.
834   However, the number of available parameters decreases with each new task. Pruning in PackNet is
835   a two-stage process. First, a fixed subset of the most important parameters for the task is selected,
836   typically comprising 70% of the total. In the second stage, the network formed by this subset is
837   fine-tuned over a specified number of steps.

839   B.3   R EHEARSAL -BASED M ETHODS
840
841   Rehearsal-based methods mitigate forgetting by maintaining a buffer of samples from previous tasks,
842   which are replayed during training.
843   Perfect Memory. This method assumes an unrealistic scenario of an unlimited buffer capacity,
844   allowing all data from previous tasks to be stored and replayed.
845

846
      ing as a constrained optimization problem. Specifically, the objective is to minimize the loss for
847

848
      ℓ(θ, M(i) ) ≤ ℓi , where ℓi represents the previously observed minimum, and M(i) contains buffer
849
      samples from task i for 1 ≤ i ≤ k−1. However, this constraint is intractable for neural networks. To
850   address this, Chaudhry et al. (2018b) propose an approximation using a first-order Taylor expansion:

890   In this section, we analyze the computational cost of inference in the CompoFormer architecture
891   with respect to the number of tasks, expressing the complexity in big-O notation.
892

893   the self-composing policy module. In the worst-case scenario, where no task can be solved by

936   The computational cost remains a primary limitation in growing neural network architectures, with
937   memory and inference costs being key considerations in this study. The self-composing policy
938   module in CompoFormer is designed to address memory complexity by ensuring that the model
939   grows linearly in terms of parameters as the number of tasks increases, while still retaining the
940   knowledge from all previously learned modules. Furthermore, when a new task can be solved by
941   reusing previous policies, no additional computational cost for training is incurred, significantly
942   reducing the computational burden.

      E    D ETAILED SINGLE PERFORMANCE IN OUR BASELINES
944
945   In Section 5.3, we visualized the single-task performance of five methods; here, we present the per-
946
      formance of all methods in Figure 8. As shown in Figure 8, the MT method, which has access to data
      from all tasks throughout the learning process, achieves high performance on every task, comparable
947
      to the single-task method. In contrast, regularization-based and rehearsal-based methods introduce
948
      additional loss terms to mitigate catastrophic forgetting. However, during training, these methods
949   still experience forgetting, particularly for the initial tasks in the sequence, and only show improved
950   performance towards the end of the learning sequence. Increasing the regularization strength to pre-
951   vent forgetting results in impaired learning of new tasks, leading to a pronounced stability-plasticity
952   trade-off. This issue is particularly significant in the offline RL setting, where policy optimization is
953   more sensitive to parameter changes compared to supervised learning in classification tasks(Masana
954   et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023).
955   Structure-based methods maintain good stability by freezing task-specific parameters, but their abil-
956   ity to learn new tasks diminishes as the training progresses. This is due to the decreasing number
957   of available parameters and cross-task interference from the frozen parameters. Consequently, the
958   performance on later tasks is often lower than that of regularization- and rehearsal-based methods.
959   Our self-composing policy module addresses this issue by reducing cross-task interference and en-
960   hancing the plasticity of structure-based methods, allowing for more efficient learning of new tasks
961   without compromising stability.
