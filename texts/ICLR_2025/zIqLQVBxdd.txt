011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Coarse-grained(CG) molecular dynamics simulations offer computational effi-
014            ciency for exploring protein conformational ensembles and thermodynamic prop-
015
               erties. Though coarse representations enable large-scale simulations across ex-
               tended temporal and spatial ranges, the sacrifice of atomic-level details limits
016
               their utility in tasks such as ligand docking and protein-protein interaction predic-
017
               tion. Backmapping, the process of reconstructing all-atom structures from coarse-
018            grained representations, is crucial for recovering these fine details. While recent
019            machine learning methods have made strides in protein structure generation, chal-
020            lenges persist in reconstructing diverse atomistic conformations that maintain ge-
021            ometric accuracy and chemical validity. In this paper, we present Latent Diffusion
022            Backmapping (LDB), a novel approach leveraging denoising diffusion within la-
023            tent space to address these challenges. By combining discrete latent encoding
024            with diffusion, LDB bypasses the need for equivariant and internal coordinate ma-
025            nipulation, significantly simplifying the training and sampling processes as well
026
               as facilitating better and wider exploration in configuration space. We evaluate
               LDB’s state-of-the-art performance on three distinct protein datasets, demonstrat-
027
               ing its ability to efficiently reconstruct structures with high structural accuracy
028
               and chemical validity. Moreover, LDB shows exceptional versatility in capturing
029            diverse protein ensembles, highlighting its capability to explore intricate confor-
030            mational spaces. Our results position LDB as a powerful and scalable approach for
031            backmapping, effectively bridging the gap between CG simulations and atomic-
032            level analyses in computational biology.

      Coarse-Grained Molecular Dynamics (CG-MD) simulation has become an indispensable tool in
      computational biology for simulating large biomolecular systems (Das & Baker, 2008; Liwo et al.,
038
      2014; Kmiecik et al., 2016; Souza et al., 2021; Majewski et al., 2023; Arts et al., 2023). Through
039
      grouping atoms into super-atoms or beads, CG models significantly decrease computational require-
040   ments and allow the observation of long-time processes such as folding, aggregation, and self-
041   assembly (Lequieu et al., 2019; Shmilovich et al., 2020; Mohr et al., 2022). However, CG rep-
042   resentations inherently sacrifice atomistic details of protein structures, limiting their application to
043   a bunch of important downstream tasks in drug discovery, such as molecular recognition, signaling
044   pathways deciphering, and allosteric sites prediction (Badaczewska-Dawid et al., 2020; Vickery &
045   Stansfeld, 2021; Zambaldi et al., 2024). Under such circumstances, backmapping, i.e., reconstruct-
046   ing all-atom structures from CG representations, is essential for a comprehensive understanding and
047   wider applications of CG-MD (Huang et al., 2016; Śledź & Caflisch, 2018; Peng et al., 2019; Kim,
048   2023).
049   Two primary challenges are faced with backmapping coarse-grained protein representations to
050   all-atom structures. The first challenge is the high dimensionality involved in modeling large
051   biomolecules. Proteins, in particular, consist of thousands of atoms and intricate structural patterns,
052   making it difficult for models to learn and extract relevant features effectively (Rogers et al., 2023;
053   Fu et al., 2024; Wuyun et al., 2024). This complexity also leads to issues during sampling, where
      directly generating 3D coordinates for numerous atoms can result in chemically invalid structures,

078   The second challenge is the vast and dynamic conformational space that proteins can occupy. Such
079   dynamics result in unique conformational changes, which play a critical role in enabling the di-
080   verse functions of proteins and are essential for maintaining the proper physiological functions of
081
      living organisms (Miller & Phillips, 2021). Though CG simulations allow us to observe and study
      these special conformations across temporal and spatial scales, they make an obstacle for struc-
082
      ture backmapping. When generative models are provided with multiple CG representations that are
083

084   also reconstruct the all-atom variations in the 3D conformational space with structural and chemical
085   accuracy (Yang & Gómez-Bombarelli, 2023).

087
      Traditional backmapping methods often rely on heuristics to generate initial structures, but these
      approaches frequently result in non-physical artifacts and fail to capture the thermodynamic diversity
088
      of protein conformations (Nicholson & Greene, 2020). Early machine learning approaches, such as
089
      generative adversarial networks (Li et al., 2020; Stieffenhofer et al., 2020; 2021; Shmilovich et al.,
090   2022) and variational autoencoders (Wang & Gómez-Bombarelli, 2019; Wang et al., 2022; Yang
091   & Gómez-Bombarelli, 2023), align all-atom structures with the prior distribution of coarse-grained
092   models. However, such methods typically approximate only the most probable conformations and
093   struggle to capture the complex dynamics of structural distributions (Murphy, 2012; Yang & Gómez-
094   Bombarelli, 2023).
095
      Denoising diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020) offer a stochastic approach for sampling protein en-
096   sembles. Diffusion over local structural relationships, like bond angles, often requires complex
097   approximations and post-processing for structural validity (Jing et al., 2022; Yim et al., 2023). La-
098   tent space methods, while promising, handle both node and edge features, constraining network
099   design and limiting their use to small molecules or backbone-only structures (Xu et al., 2023; Fu
100   et al., 2024). DiAMONDBack (Jones et al., 2023) uses an autoregressive approach to backmap
101   atom coordinates sequentially, but this complicates sampling, reducing both efficiency and quality,
102   particularly with large biomolecules.
103   In this paper, we propose Latent Diffusion Backmapping (LDB) to address the above challenges.
104   LDB begins by encoding the all-atom structures into a node-level latent representation, captur-
105   ing equivariance and local structural relationships. By applying physical constraints such as bond
106   lengths and angles, the method ensures chemical validity, thereby eliminating the need for extensive
107   post-processing. Furthermore, the node-level representation allows for greater flexibility in the de-
      noising network architecture, removing the requirement for explicit edge modeling. Finally, LDB

      translates these embeddings into discrete, low-dimensional codes, reducing the dimensionality of
109   the generative task and enabling a more efficient and stable training process.

115   We evaluate LDB on widely-used protein dynamics datasets PED (Ghafouri et al., 2024), demon-

134

      Traditional Methods. Traditional backmapping methods utilize rule-based heuristics to generate
136   initial atomic structures (Lombardi et al., 2016), which are subsequently refined through geometric
137   optimization or energy minimization (Vickery & Stansfeld, 2021). However, these approaches often
138   result in non-physical imperfections, such as atomic clashes and abnormal bond angles (Xu et al.,
139   2019), and the refinement process can be computationally expensive and biased toward specific min-
140   imization schemes (Badaczewska-Dawid et al., 2020). Additionally, these methods are deterministic
141   and do not capture the thermodynamic diversity of atomic structures that correspond to a single CG
142   representation (Yang & Gómez-Bombarelli, 2023).
143
144   Data-driven Methods. Data-driven approaches aim to overcome these limitations by predicting
145   atomic structures from CG representations. While deterministic models like MLPs (An & Desh-
146   mukh, 2020) and SE(3)-Transformers (Heo & Feig, 2023) offer high precision, they struggle with
147   the one-to-many nature of backmapping, leading to reduced structural diversity. Chennakesavalu
148   & Rotskoff (2024) uses Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) for local rotamer states and a predic-
149   tion model for global coupling to generate protein conformations. Compared to direct distribution-
150   learning models, it relies more on physical constraints and statistical models, lacking end-to-end
151   optimization of the target distribution, resulting in lower accuracy.
152   Generative models, including GANs (Li et al., 2020; Stieffenhofer et al., 2020; 2021; Shmilovich
153   et al., 2022) and VAEs (Wang & Gómez-Bombarelli, 2019; Wang et al., 2022; Yang & Gómez-
154   Bombarelli, 2023), address these challenges by learning multimodal distributions of atomic struc-
155   tures. However, GANs are often ineffective at modeling complex distributions, and VAEs tend to
156   prioritize common structures, limiting their ability to generate diverse conformations.
157   Recent work has shown that diffusion models, such as those proposed by Li et al. (2024) and Jones
158   et al. (2023), are particularly effective for backmapping. These models condition on CG inputs to
159   generate diverse and detailed atomic structures. However, diffusion in atomic space suffers from
160   high computational cost and limited flexibility, particularly for large systems. Moreover, the exces-
161   sive freedom in exploration can lead to generated structures that deviate from the target conforma-
      tions.

      In this section, we introduce the proposed Latent Diffusion Backmapping (LDB) framework. Our
219
      work is inspired by the success of Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022), which has demonstrated
220   the effectiveness of generating high-resolution images in latent space. However, extending this
221   concept to complex protein structures presents unique challenges (Winter et al., 2022; Xu et al.,
222   2023; Hayes et al., 2024). We address these challenges by first compress the complex all-atom
223   protein structure into discrete latent codes, and then apply conditional diffusion in the latent space.
224
      In the following sections, we detail the design of the discrete latent encoding and latent diffusion in
225
      Sections 4.1 and 4.2, respectively. An overview of the framework is provided in Figure 1.

267   4.2   G RAPH L ATENT D IFFUSION
268
269   In this section, we describe the noise addition and removal processes in the latent space, as derived
      earlier. Unlike traditional diffusion models that operate in high-dimensional coordinate space, our


                                                         5

270
      approach simplifies diffusion by leveraging a lower-dimensional discrete latent codes, avoiding the
271   complexity of geometric parameters, as shown in Figure 1.
272
273   Traditional diffusion methods face challenges when applied to protein structures, as they often op-
274
      erate in three-dimensional space or rely on relative distances and angles. This increases complexity
      and makes it harder to capture the symmetry and physical constraints inherent in protein structures.
275
      Moreover, performing diffusion in high-dimensional spaces complicates the multi-step denoising
276
      process, making it difficult to accurately model subtle conformational differences.
277
278   To overcome these challenges, we focus on node-level latent representations, which embed the nec-
279   essary structural information. This eliminates the need for explicit geometric constraints, simpli-
280
      fying the noise addition and removal processes. By performing diffusion in the latent space, our
      method avoids the intricacies of handling node and edge features, resulting in a more streamlined
281
      and efficient model.
282
283   Additionally, by compressing the latent representation into a discrete code, we mitigate the compu-
284   tational complexity associated with large protein structures. This compact representation allows for
285   efficient diffusion, reducing noise accumulation and improving overall computational efficiency.
286   We build our denoising network ϵθ on the ProteinMPNN framework Dauparas et al. (2022), focus-
287   ing on CG discrete latent codes without modeling edge information, which enhances flexibility. The
288   network processes three inputs: coarse-grained graph node coordinates, residue types, and an initial
289   noise vector. The node coordinates and residue types represent the coarse-grained protein struc-
290   ture and serve as conditional information to refine the noise vector during the denoising process
291   iteratively.
292   To account for varying noise levels, we modify the LayerNorm layer of ProteinMPNN to adaptive
293   layer norm (adaLN) (Perez et al., 2018), allowing dynamic adjustments during the denoising process.
294   This ensures consistent, physically plausible protein structures across all time steps.
295
      The denoising objective minimizes the difference between predicted and actual noise, as described
296   by the following loss function:

302
303   By embedding symmetry and equivariance in the node-level latent space, our method avoids han-
304   dling complex physical constraints explicitly, significantly enhancing both the simplicity and com-

      Model Implementations For the autoencoder in LDB, we adopted the parameter settings from
351   Genzprot, setting the dimensionality of the output node features to 36. The vector quantization
352   employs a codebook size of 4096 with an embedding dimension of 3. Learning rate reduction and
353   early stopping were controlled based on validation loss. The network was trained with a batch size
354   of 4 and an initial learning rate of 0.001 for the PED and PDB datasets, while using 0.0005 for the
355   ATLAS dataset
356
      For the diffusion framework, we used a linear variance schedule, setting tmax = 1000, with the
357   variance ranging from 1 × 10−4 to 2 × 10−2 . A learned covariance Σθ was utilized as described by
358   (Peebles & Xie, 2023). During sampling, 100 steps were used to balance computational efficiency
359   and output quality. The denoising neural network employs a 3-layer encoder-decoder architecture
360   with a hidden layer size of 128. The training process utilized a learning rate of 3 × 10−4 with a batch
361   size of 128, followed by a warmup period of 20,000 steps and a linear schedule up to 300,000 steps,
362   with the final learning rate set to 1 × 10−5 . We implemented LDB using PyTorch 2.3.0 with CUDA
363   12.1 and Python 3.11. All models were trained and evaluated on 1 NVIDIA A100 GPUs, each with
364   40GB of memory.

470   5.5    A BLATION S TUDIES
471
472   To evaluate the contributions of key model components, we conducted ablation studies on the PED
473   dataset, comparing our discrete latent space approach (VQ-VAE+diffusion) with two alternatives:
474   a continuous latent space model (VAE+diffusion) and a flow-based variant (VQ-VAE+flow). Flow
475   matching, known for balancing stochasticity and structure in recent tasks, offers efficient probability
476
      flows (Irwin et al., 2024; Jing et al., 2024), but for protein backmapping tasks with large confor-
      mational spaces, diffusion’s ability to explore diverse conformations proves more effective. Each
477
      component plays a distinct role in improving structural accuracy and validity.
478
479   Firstly, our discrete latent space (VQ-VAE) shows clear advantages over the continuous VAE-based
480   method. By discretizing the latent space, our model can better preserve the bond graph consistency,
481   which is crucial for maintaining accurate internal structures. This is reflected in the significantly
482
      lower GED scores as shown in Table 3. The discrete space effectively reduces errors related to bond
      lengths and angles, which leads to better structural precision.
483
484   Secondly, the diffusion process proves superior to the flow-based approach (VQ-VAE+flow) in han-
485   dling large conformational spaces. Diffusion leverages stochastic noise, which allows for explo-
      ration across diverse conformations while maintaining structure validity. This is evident in the

514   6   C ONCLUSION
515
516   In this paper, we introduced LDB, a denoising diffusion backmapping method operating in la-
517   tent space. By implicitly incorporating equivariance and internal coordinates into a discrete low-
518   dimensional node-level latent representation, we effectively preserved structural information while
519   simplifying the diffusion process, thereby enhancing both efficiency and performance. This method
520   addresses the inefficiencies and accuracy challenges of direct diffusion in coordinate space, as well
521   as the difficulties in learning simple prior distributions that struggle to capture diverse conforma-
522
      tional spaces. Our experiments demonstrate that LDB achieves SOTA accuracy across various
      datasets while maintaining higher structural validity. For future work, we aim to extend this frame-
523
      work to model continuous time trajectories, which will allow better prediction of dynamic protein
524
      behaviors. Additionally, this versatile framework can be adapted for other tasks in protein design
525   and beyond.

      A.3    R EPRESENTATION FOR P ROTEIN S TRUCTURE
841
842   Protein structure representations are essential for tasks such as protein design, folding prediction,
843   and structural backmapping. Numerous approaches have been developed to represent protein struc-
844   tures in a computationally efficient manner. Below, we discuss several common methods of repre-
845   sentation.
846
847   Voxel Representation Voxel representations divide 3D space into a grid, where each voxel in-
848   dicates the presence or absence of atoms. This method provides a clear way to capture spatial
849   information, but it can be computationally demanding due to the high dimensionality of the voxel
850   grid, especially when applied to large macromolecules. It is mainly utilized in tasks that require
851
      explicit spatial reasoning, such as molecular docking simulations. Several studies (Masuda et al.,
      2020; Stieffenhofer et al., 2020; 2021; Shmilovich et al., 2022) have implemented atomic density
852
      grids, allowing for the entire molecule to be generated in one step by producing a density over the
853
      voxelized 3D space. However, these grids lack the desirable property of equivariance and often
854   necessitate separate fitting algorithms, which adds complexity to the modeling process.
855
856
      Coordinate Representation Coordinate representation captures the precise spatial arrangement
857
      of each atom in a protein using Cartesian coordinates, making it a standard approach in many molec-
858   ular modeling techniques. This method effectively preserves the geometric properties of protein
859   structures, facilitating accurate modeling tasks. However, directly integrating Cartesian coordinates
860   into deep learning models presents challenges, particularly the need for translational and rotational
861   invariance, which necessitates specific constraints within the network. Furthermore, the high dimen-
862   sionality of coordinate data increases computational complexity, especially in large-scale datasets,
863   while uneven data distribution can impede learning efficiency. Consequently, advanced learning
      strategies are often required to address these challenges (Hoogeboom et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2022).


                                                         16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      Internal Coordinate Representation The internal coordinate representation utilizes bond
865   lengths, bond angles, and dihedral angles to reduce the degrees of freedom compared to Carte-
866   sian coordinates, resulting in a more compact and efficient representation (Jing et al., 2022; Eguchi
867   et al., 2022). This approach inherently encodes the geometric constraints of molecular structures,
868   enhancing computational efficiency while eliminating redundant spatial information. It is particu-
869   larly well-suited for backmapping tasks, where known reference points facilitate the reconstruction
870   of full-atom coordinates. By relying on internal coordinates, the process conforms to the physical
871   and chemical constraints of the system, enabling the accurate and efficient generation of all-atom
872   structures.
873
874   Latent Representation Latent diffusion models have demonstrated significant success across var-
875
      ious generative tasks, including image (Vahdat et al., 2021), point cloud (Zeng et al., 2022), text (Li
      et al., 2022), audio (Liu et al., 2023), and molecular generation (Xu et al., 2023). In the context
876
      of protein structures, latent representations offer a compact and efficient method for modeling by
877
      embedding them into lower-dimensional spaces, thereby simplifying both the generation and de-
878   sign processes. (Xu et al., 2023) introduced a geometric latent diffusion model for 3D molecular
879   generation that ensures roto-translational equivariance within the latent space, enhancing the mod-
880   eling of small molecular geometries. (Fu et al., 2024) proposed a latent diffusion model that adeptly
881   captures protein geometry, facilitating the efficient generation of novel protein backbones through
882   latent node and edge features. Similarly, (Hayes et al., 2024) employed latent space modeling to
883   simulate protein evolution, showcasing its capability to co-design protein sequences and structures.
884   Collectively, these methods reduce computational complexity while preserving high-quality protein
885   generation and designability.
886
887   A.4   BASELINE M ODELS
888
      DiAMoNDBack reconstructs full-atom structures from CG representations by directly performing
889
      diffusion on atomic coordinates. The method introduces Gaussian noise to atomic coordinates in
890
      a forward diffusion process, transforming the data into a noise distribution. During inference, the
891   reverse process iteratively removes the noise to recover the original coordinates. To ensure invari-
892   ance to global rotations and translations, each residue is represented in a canonical reference frame
893   defined by its neighboring residues.
894
      The model employs a U-Net-based denoising network to predict clean coordinates at each diffusion
895
      step. It follows an autoregressive approach, generating the structure residue by residue, starting from
896
      the N-terminus. Each residue is predicted conditionally based on the previously generated residues,
897   ensuring both local accuracy and global consistency.
898
899   DiAMoNDBack focuses on directly modeling in Cartesian coordinate space, allowing it to generate
900
      diverse conformations while maintaining structural integrity. However, its autoregressive nature
      significantly increases inference time compared to non-autoregressive models.
901
902   GenZProt reconstructs full-atom structures from CG representations using a VAE framework. In-
903   stead of predicting Cartesian coordinates directly, it predicts internal coordinates, including bond
904   lengths, bond angles, and torsion angles, ensuring chemical and physical validity while avoiding
905   steric clashes.
906   The model uses a hierarchical architecture to process input data. The encoder captures geometric
907   information at three levels: atomic interactions within 9 Å, residue-level interactions, and long-range
908   residue interactions within 21 Å. The decoder generates internal coordinates for each residue, which
909   are converted to Cartesian coordinates using a Z-matrix formulation. This representation reduces
910   the complexity of direct Cartesian prediction while preserving physical constraints.
911   Training is guided by physics-informed loss functions, focusing on bond lengths, bond angles, tor-
912   sion angles, and steric clash avoidance. GenZProt learns a prior distribution of protein structures
913   in the latent space, capturing the most plausible conformations for a given CG representation. This
914   allows it to efficiently reconstruct full-atom structures with high chemical accuracy.
