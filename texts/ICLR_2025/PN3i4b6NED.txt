                                                    Abstract
 1            Metagenomics is essential for exploring the vast diversity and intricate interactions
 2            of microbes that impact health, agriculture, and environmental sciences. Despite
 3            the surge of machine learning-based metagenomic models addressing these ques-
 4            tions, evaluating their respective benefits is challenging due to the use of distinct,
 5            experimental datasets, partly contrived, and varying model performance across
 6            different tasks. To this end, we introduce OpenMeta, the first comprehensive
 7            benchmark tailored for metagenomic function prediction, which integrates diverse
 8            datasets ranging from 1,000 to 213,000 sequences and incorporates hierarchical
 9            data. We highlight the inadequacies of current genomic models and the superior
10            performance of metagenomic pre-trained models for handling complex metage-
11            nomic data. Furthermore, we identify a critical research gap: the lack of unified
12            models that process both sequence and hierarchical data. Addressing this could
13            significantly advance metagenomic analyses. OpenMeta sets a new standard for
14            metagenomic analysis, offering insights that could enhance the understanding and
15            application of microbial ecology in biotechnology and environmental science.


16   1   Introduction
17   Metagenomics is a discipline that studies the genetic composition and functional dynamics of all
18   microorganisms in environmental samples [41, 44]. By directly extracting the entire DNA from these
19   microorganisms, metagenomics captures a broad spectrum of life forms, including viruses, viroids,
20   and free DNA present in diverse habitats such as soil, seawater, and human microbiomes [53, 40].
21   Unlike traditional genomics, which focuses on sequencing DNA from single species in isolation [57,
22   74], metagenomics eliminates the need for isolating each organism, allowing research of uncultivable
23   microorganisms [83, 48, 23]. Consequently, metagenomics unveils the vast diversity of microbial
24   communities, enabling the interpretation of gene interactions and essential biological processes within
25   ecosystems [3, 37, 39].
26   Deep learning techniques have significantly advanced metagenomics research, enabling more pre-
27   cise function prediction and complex relationship elucidation in metagenomics [1, 60, 45, 77, 22,
28   38](Sec. 2). However, the field lacks standardized benchmarks, making it difficult to evaluate the
29   efficacy of various metagenomic models that often rely on distinct, artificially constructed datasets.
30   Existing genomic benchmarks primarily focus on single-species genomic analysis. The GUE bench-
31   mark [84], built upon DNABERT2, encompasses multiple datasets ranging from humans to viruses
32   and includes 7 binary classification tasks like promoter detection and transcription factor prediction.
33   GenomicBenchmarks [49] address gene regulation and chromatin accessibility tasks. Nucleotide

34   Transformer (NT) [9], pre-trained on multi-species genomic data, concentrates on transcription factor
35   binding and enhancer-promoter interactions within the human genome [32] (Sec. 3.1).
36   However, these methods often fail to address the unique challenges in metagenomics. Firstly,
37   metagenomic data is distinct as it comprises genomic collections from multiple organisms sequenced
38   simultaneously from numerous individuals, unlike genomic studies that focus on single organisms.
39   Secondly, metagenomic tasks are unique, involving the prediction of complex interactions among
40   various microorganisms, including bacteria and viruses, influenced by dynamic environments –
41   a complexity that surpasses single-species genomic analysis. Lastly, existing genomic models
42   often perform poorly on metagenomic tasks, as models trained on genomic data cannot be directly
43   transferred to metagenomic data. Hence, there is an urgent need to develop a metagenomic reference
44   to deepen our understanding of microbial ecology and provide a powerful tool for biotechnology and
45   environmental science research (Sec. 3.2).
46   To this end, we develop OpenMeta, the first comprehensive benchmark specifically designed for
47   metagenomics based on the FGBERT model [13]. OpenMeta integrates a wide range of tasks across
48   genetic, functional, bacterial, and environmental levels and can handle datasets with sequences
49   ranging from 1,000 to 213,000, as well as richer hierarchical data of phylogenetic tree structures,
50   reflecting the diversity and complexity of metagenomic data. We compare metagenomic pre-trained
51   models with genomic pre-trained models (Sec. 5.2), and although the latter provided new insights,
52   their performance on metagenome data tended to decrease, highlighting the need for the development
53   of metagenome pre-trained models. Our main contributions are as follows:
54      i. We establish OpenMeta, the first comprehensive benchmark for metagenomic research en-
55         compassing 23 representative models. OpenMeta sets a new standard in the evaluation of
56         metagenomic models using various collected standardized datasets and metrics across three di-
57         mensions: pre-trained vs. not pre-trained models, general vs. specialized models, and sequence
58         data-based vs. hierarchical data-based models.
59     ii. We conduct extensive experiments on various tasks ranging from metagenomic sequences to
60         hierarchical data, covering small-scale, large-scale, and fine-grained scopes.
61    iii. Our findings lead us to reconsider the potential of metagenomic pre-trained models, advocat-
62         ing for architectures like FGBERT that better accommodate the diversity and complexity of
63         metagenomic data.
64    iv. We identify a significant research gap: the lack of a unified model capable of simultaneously
65         processing sequence and hierarchical data. Addressing this could significantly advance compre-
66         hensive metagenomic analyses and represent a promising direction for future research.


67   2   Related Work

68   Gene Representation Learning. For sequence metagenomic data, while the K-mer method [17]
69   efficiently captures characteristics of short sequences, it struggles with longer sequences due to
70   its inherent limitations. Alternatively, one-hot encoding, despite its high-dimensional and sparse
71   nature, restricts its utility for large-scale applications. In contrast, deep learning-based embeddings,
72   such as those from Transformer models, enhance sequence representation by capturing contextual
73   and global features, offering biologically meaningful insights. For hierarchical metagenomic
74   data, constructing phylogenetic trees provides an effective framework for delineating hierarchical
75   and evolutionary relationships among microbial taxa [2, 79]. This process begins with analyzing


                                                        2
76    microbial genomes through multiple sequence alignment, organizing them into a phylogenetic
77    structure. Microbial taxa abundances are then mapped to corresponding nodes on the tree. By
78    aggregating abundance values from child nodes to their respective parent nodes and transforming this
79    phylogenetic tree into a matrix format [18, 58], the structure is adapted for input into CNN models
80    for disease phenotype prediction.
81    Metagenomic Methods. Traditional alignment-based methods like MetaPhlAn5 [66] aim to match
82    similarities between query sequences and known reference genomes and are common for taxonomic
83    profiling. Advancements in deep learning have led to new methods like DeepVirFinder [61], which
84    use CNNs for viral classifications with one-hot encoding. K-mer tokenization [17], employed in
85    approaches like MDL4Microbiome [33], is a standard for DNA sequence characterization. Addi-
86    tionally, Virtifier [45] maps a nucleotide sequence using a codon dictionary combined with LSTM
87    to predict viral genes. DeepMicrobes [35] employs a self-attention mechanism, while DeepTE [81]
88    uses K-mer inputs with CNNs for element classification, and Genomic-nlp [46] applies word2vec
89    for gene function analysis. MetaTransformer [77] uses K-mer embedding for species classification
90    with Transformer. For pre-training models, LookingGlass [24] uses a three-layer LSTM model for
91    functional prediction in short DNA reads. ViBE [22] employs a K-mer token-based BERT model
92    pre-trained with Masked Language Modeling for virus identification.
93    Genomic Benchmark. To our knowledge, there is no benchmark in the field of metagenomics.
94    Due to the scarcity of specialized benchmarks in metagenomics and the inherent similarities in data
95    structure and content between genomic and metagenomic datasets, comparing the two allows us
96    to leverage advancements in genomics to address metagenomics’ unique needs. Existing genomic
97    benchmarks, such as GUE, GenomicBenchmarks, and Nucleotide Transformer [9], each have their
98    distinct focus but primarily address single-species genome analysis. GUE, as part of DNABERT2 [9],
99    addresses challenges in genome tokenization and pre-training, covering multi-species datasets from
100   humans to viruses and involving tasks like promoter detection and transcription factor prediction.
101   GenomicBenchmarks, through HyenaDNA [49], focuses on improving long-sequence genome
102   modeling and handling ultra-long sequences, including gene regulation and chromatin accessibility
103   analysis. NT is pre-trained on multi-species genomic data, emphasizing transcription factor binding
104   and enhancer-promoter interaction in the human genome.

156   4     OpenMeta
157   4.1   Supported Methods

158   OpenMeta supports 23 methods for comprehensive analysis and performance evaluation across
159   various tasks and data types, detailed in Tab. 3 with their respective publication years. To present
160   them systematically, we categorize them along three dimensions: General vs. Specialized Models,
161   Pre-trained vs. Not Pre-Trained Models, and Sequence data-based vs. Hierarchical data-based
162   Models, acknowledging that some methods may span multiple categories.
163   General vs. Specialized Models: General models include widely used machine learning and
164   deep learning models, such as SVM [69], Random Forest (RF) [64], CNN, LSTM [25], and Trans-
165   former [76], providing robust foundations for various tasks. Moreover, models like DNABERT2 [84]
166   will be discussed in detail as pre-trained models in the next part. Conversely, specialized models
167   are designed for specific metagenomic tasks such as functional gene prediction (PLM-ARG [78],
168   DeepARG [5], RGI [4], ViBE [22], CLEAN [82]), and prototype prediction (PopPhy-CNN [59]).
169   Pre-trained vs. Not Pre-trained Models: OpenMeta includes DNABERT2 [84], HyenaDNA [49],
170   and NT [9] genomic pre-trained models, alongside FGBERT [13] metagenomic pre-trained model.
171   Although ViBE [22] and PLM-ARG [78] are not pre-trained from scratch, they use BERT model [11]
172   and protein language model [36], respectively, to enhance their functional prediction capabilities.

230   5     Results and Insights

231   5.1   Results

232   Small-Scale Benchmarks. Appendix Tab. A5 and A6 show M.F1 for gene operon and pathogen
233   prediction on two small-scale datasets, E-K12 and PATRIC (sequence length less than 5000). General
234   models (RF to VT) test K-mer (K=3), one-hot, and w2v data encoding methods, and BiLSTM
235   (one-hot) and LSTM (w2v) performed best. FGBERT achieves superior results, far exceeding
236   other methods, highlighting the necessity of metagenomic pre-training. Models like CNN-MGP [1],
237   PlasGUN [16], and DeepMicrobes [35], designed for binary classification, are unsuitable for our
238   multi-classification benchmark. Tab. 6 evaluates various models on three ARG prediction datasets:
239   CARD-A, CARD-D, and CARD-R. In General models, the word2vec data encoding method performs

321   (B) Sequence Data vs. Hierarchical Data:
322   Why Use Hierarchical Data? Hierarchical
323   data introduce an additional dimension by pro-
324   viding interrelationships and evolutionary con-
325   text among microbial communities, enriching
326   metagenomic research. Unlike traditional abun-
327   dance data, hierarchical data offer not only
328   quantitative information but also capture the Figure 4: Phylogenetic Tree Representation of Mi-
329   complex hierarchical relationships between mi- crobial Communities for Hierarchical Data.
330   crobes, which is crucial for exploring host-microbe interactions [68].
331   Why Use Phylogenetic Tree Structures for Hierarchical Data? Tree structures naturally represent
332   the hierarchical and phylogenetic relationships among microbial taxa. Each node represents a
333   microbial taxon, and the connections between nodes reflect their evolutionary relationships. This
334   helps to reveal the evolutionary links between different microbial taxa, integrating complex biological
335   information (such as abundance and hierarchical data) into a unified data structure. By accumulating
336   the abundance values from child nodes to their parent nodes and converting the phylogenetic tree into
337   a matrix format, each row represents a level in the tree, and columns represent different microbes
338   or attributes. As shown in Fig. 4, this matrix-based representation effectively combines abundance
339   and hierarchical information. This approach is particularly useful in disease prediction tasks, such
340   as studies on Cirrhosis and T2D, demonstrating how understanding the hierarchical structure of
341   microbial communities can elucidate the complexity of host-microbe interactions. This hierarchical
342   method provides powerful tools for the precise identification and functional analysis of disease-related
343   microbial communities. Our benchmark framework underscores the importance and benefits of using
344   hierarchical data to enhance the accuracy and depth of metagenomic analysis.

345   6   Conclusion and Future Work

346   Conclusion. In this paper, we introduce OpenMeta, the first comprehensive benchmark tailored for
347   metagenomic function prediction. This benchmark standardizes the evaluation process across various
348   metagenomic tasks and facilitates the design of metagenomic models through a unified approach.
349   Our extensive analysis includes comparisons between pre-trained and not pre-trained models, general
350   versus specialized models, and sequence data-based versus hierarchical data-based models. Inspired
351   by OpenMeta, we emphasize the necessity of pre-trained metagenomic models in this field and
352   advocate for the community’s engagement with metagenomic models trained on hierarchical data
353   such as phylogenetic trees. This approach can profoundly enhance our understanding of the complex
354   relationships and interactions within microbial communities.
355   Limitations. It is crucial to note that OpenMeta primarily serves as an evaluative tool that aggregates
356   and assesses a wide array of multi-class datasets, including both sequence and hierarchical data.
357   While this benchmark significantly contributes to the field, it does not involve the development of
358   new models but focuses on the assessment of existing methodologies. This limitation underscores the
359   necessity for further research and development in creating comprehensive models that can process
360   both sequence and hierarchical inputs simultaneously.
361   Future Work. We identify a significant gap in the current landscape: the absence of a unified
362   metagenomic model capable of simultaneously processing sequence and hierarchical data from
363   phylogenetic trees. Addressing this gap represents a promising direction for future work and could
364   significantly advance our holistic understanding of metagenomics.

634   A     Social Impacts
635   Metagenomics offers significant benefits in fields like medicine and environmental science, yet it also
636   poses dual-use concerns. For instance, technologies designed to minimize disease can theoretically
637   be repurposed for harmful uses, such as biological weapons. The advancement of metagenomic
638   benchmarks could inadvertently facilitate such misuse. Additionally, while these technologies can
639   accelerate experimental processes, the need for traditional wet lab experimentation remains crucial.
640   Thus, developing robust, clinically validated benchmarks is essential for integrating metagenomic
641   methods into medical practice responsibly. This approach will ensure technological advances support
642   health and environmental management without replacing foundational experimental techniques.

667   different microbial communities and predict functions across various environments, covering diverse
668   tasks such as gene structure analysis, functional gene prediction, pathogenicity assessment, and
669   nitrogen cycle prediction. These tasks span gene, functional, bacterial, and environmental levels,
670   with input sizes ranging from 1,000 to 213,000 sequences, reflecting the diversity and complexity
671   of metagenomic data. Therefore, incorporating FGBERT’s multi-species genomic datasets into our
672   OpenMeta benchmark not only substantiates its proficiency in deciphering complex microbial func-

736   F.1   Genomic and Metagenomic Benchmark

737   Relation between Genomic Benchmark and Metagenomic Benchmark. While these benchmarks
738   excel in single-species genome analysis, they often fail to capture the complex interactions among
739   multiple species inherent in metagenomics. In contrast, metagenomic benchmarks aim to address
740   interactions within diverse microbial communities and functional predictions in various environments,


                                                          20
741   covering tasks such as gene structure analysis, functional gene prediction, pathogenicity assessment,
742   and nitrogen cycle prediction. Specifically, the limitations of genomic benchmarks include (1) their
743   reliance on single-species data, which misses the complex interactions in metagenomics; (2) lack of
744   data diversity, as their datasets are typically structured and uniform, lacking the environmental diver-
745   sity needed for metagenomic studies; (3) limited functional prediction, focusing on sequence-based
746   predictions without integrating crucial functional annotations; and (4) insufficient model adaptabil-
747   ity, as models trained on single-species genomic data struggle with multi-species metagenomic data.
748   These deficiencies underscore the urgent need to develop metagenomic benchmarks that can integrate
749   multi-species interactions and complex environmental factors.
750   (A) Metagenomic Pre-trained Models vs. Genomic Pre-trained Models:
751   Table 14 compares genomic and metagenomic pre-trained models, including pre-training datasets,
752   token embedding methods, network architectures, application tasks, and benchmarks. In terms of Pre-
753   Training Datasets, DNABERT2 [84] utilizes human and multi-species genomes for its foundational
754   model pre-training, covering a vast dataset of 27.5 billion nucleotide bases from the Human reference
755   genome [32] and 135 species genomes across seven categories. HyenaDNA [49], on the other hand,
756   is pre-trained solely on a single human reference genome. Nucleotide Transformer (NT) [9] pre-trains
757   on three datasets: the human reference genome [32], 3,202 diverse human genomes, and 850 genomes
758   from several species. In contrast, FGBERT [13] employs the MGnify database (updated February
759   2023) [62], comprising 2,973,257,435 metagenomic sequences from various microbial communities.
760   For Token Embedding, DNABERT2 applies Byte Pair Encoding (BPE) [67], while both HyenaDNA
761   and NT use 6-mer tokenization. FGBERT utilizes a unique protein-based genomic representation
762   tailored for metagenomic sequences.
763   Regarding Network Architecture, both DNABERT2 and FGBERT adopt BERT-like structures [11];
764   DNABERT2 enhances its predecessor by replacing learned positional embeddings with Attention with
765   Linear Biases (ALiBi) [54] to eliminate input length limitations and incorporates Flash Attention [10]
766   to boost computational efficiency. FGBERT introduces contrastive learning to strengthen the intricate
767   relationships between metagenomic sequences and functions, proposing two pre-training tasks:
768   Masked Gene Modeling (MGM) and Triplet Enhanced Metagenomic Contrastive Learning (TMC)
769   to enhance co-representation learning of metagenomic gene sequences and functions. HyenaDNA
770   employs a simple stack of Hyena operators for next token prediction, while NT uses an encoder-only
771   Transformer architecture with Rotary Positional Embeddings (RoPE) [70] to enable reasoning over
772   longer sequences during training.
773   For Application and Benchmark, Table 2 provides a detailed comparison of downstream tasks for
774   genomic and metagenomic pre-trained models, highlighting the datasets, tasks, sequence numbers,
775   and class counts. DNABERT2 focuses primarily on binary classifications of promoters and splice
776   site detection tasks across human, mouse, yeast, and virus datasets, with the number in parentheses
777   indicating the number of independent sub-datasets for each task and the sequence count reflecting
778   the size of the first sub-dataset. Notably, Transcription Factor Prediction task recurs for both
779   human and mouse species with identical dataset numbers, class numbers, and sequence lengths,
780   suggesting a uniform level of difficulty that may not present significant challenges due to its repetitive
781   nature across similar species settings. HyenaDNA’s downstream tasks are divided into two parts:
782   GenomicBenchmarks, consisting of 8 regulatory element classification datasets with sequence lengths
783   ranging from 200 to 500, and NT’s 18 prediction tasks. In addition to the human datasets, HyenaDNA
784   includes Demo and Dummy datasets. The inclusion of Demo and Dummy datasets, which are
785   typically used for initial testing and validation purposes. Additionally, NT covers 18 downstream
786   tasks primarily centered around transcription factor binding, promoter prediction, and chromatin
787   accessibility analysis, underscoring its detailed engagement with gene regulation mechanisms. FG-
788   BERT engages with various downstream tasks that address multi-species metagenomic sequences
789   through multi-class classification challenges. These tasks span across gene, functional, bacterial,
790   and environmental levels, accommodating input sizes that range from 1,000 to 219,000 sequences,
791   reflecting the diversity and complexity of metagenomic data.
