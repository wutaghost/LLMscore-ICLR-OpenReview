009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011            Recently, leveraging big data in deep learning has led to significant performance improve-
012            ments, as confirmed in applications like mental state decoding using fMRI data. However,
013            fMRI datasets remain relatively small in scale, and the inherent issue of low signal-to-noise
014            ratios (SNR) in fMRI data further exacerbates these challenges. To address this, we apply
015            compression techniques as a preprocessing step for fMRI data. We propose BrainCodec, a
016            novel fMRI codec inspired by the neural audio codec. We evaluated BrainCodec’s com-
017
               pression capability in mental state decoding, demonstrating further improvements over
               previous methods. Furthermore, we analyzed the latent representations obtained through
018
               BrainCodec, elucidating the similarities and differences between task and resting state
019
               fMRI, highlighting the interpretability of BrainCodec. Additionally, we demonstrated that
020            fMRI reconstructions using BrainCodec can enhance the visibility of brain activity by
021            achieving higher SNR, suggesting its potential as a novel denoising method. Our study
022            shows that BrainCodec not only enhances performance over previous methods but also
023            offers new analytical possibilities for neuroscience. Our codes, dataset, and model weights
024            are available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/BrainCodec.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
      In recent years, the scaling up of deep learning has significantly boosted performance in Natural Language
029
      Processing (NLP). Research on Scaling laws (Kaplan et al. (2020)) has shown that both parameters and data
030   scale contribute to performance improvements. This finding has led to the release of numerous large-scale
031   models and has influenced various research fields, including computer vision (Dosovitskiy et al. (2020);
032   Radford et al. (2021)), speech processing (Wang et al. (2023); Le et al. (2024)), and neuroscience, which is
033   the focus of this study.
034
      Deep learning is actively employed in the field of neuroscience. For instance, numerous studies have proposed
035
      the use of deep learning in the analysis of fMRI data (Mozafari et al. (2020); Ozcelik et al. (2022); Takagi
036   and Nishimoto (2023)). Recently, researchers have begun to publish their collected fMRI datasets, and efforts
037   are underway to standardize the structure and preprocessing of neuroimaging data (Horien et al. (2021);
038   Markiewicz et al. (2021); Gorgolewski et al. (2016); Esteban et al. (2019)). Against this backdrop, research
039   on large-scale models using fMRI datasets has been increasing (Liu et al. (2023); Ortega Caro et al. (2023)).
040
      Thomas et al. (2022) conducted the first study applying NLP modeling techniques to large-scale fMRI datasets.
041
      They utilized methods such as BERT (Devlin et al. (2018)) and Causal Sequence Modeling (CSM) (Brown
042   et al. (2020)), proposing self-supervised learning (SSL) using large-scale raw fMRI data. By utilizing such
043   pretrained models for fMRI feature extraction and applying them to downstream tasks, specifically for mental
044   state decoding using the HCP dataset (Van Essen et al. (2013)), they demonstrated significant improvements
045   in accuracy over baseline models. Studies of this nature, leveraging big data and SSL for fMRI feature
046   extraction, have become increasingly prevalent in recent years (Asadi et al. (2023); Ortega Caro et al. (2023)).

047
      Despite the advent of the big data era, the scale of fMRI data remains significantly smaller compared to fields
048
      such as NLP and computer vision, thus necessitating cleaner data. However, fMRI data are known for their
049   low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) due to the presence of multiple sources of noise (Geenjaar et al. (2022)).
050   Thus, applying preprocessing that enhances the SNR of fMRI data will be crucial not only for these SSL
051   methods but also for improving the performance of general analyses using fMRI in future research.
052
      Here, we develop a codec model as a preprocessing method for fMRI data by importing the signal compression
053
      techniques from the field of audio, specifically known as the neural audio codec (NAC) (Kumar et al. (2024)).
054
      The NAC uses the Residual Vector Quantization (RVQ) method (Zeghidour et al. (2021)) to hierarchically
055   discretize audio by tokenizing, achieving state-of-the-art performance in compression efficiency and quality
056   (Wang et al. (2023); Song et al. (2024); Ju et al. (2024)).
057
058
      In cognitive neuroscience, finding discrete representations is a natural approach because different functional
      signals are distributed across various brain areas or networks. For instance, DiFuMo (Dadi et al. (2020)) is a
059
      compression method that employs dictionary learning to obtain sparse representations. One of our motivations
060
      is to leverage the multi-scale discrete information inherent in BOLD signals. Multi-scale information is
061   critical because it allows for capturing the hierarchical organization of brain functional signals, such as
062   hierarchical properties of functional connectivities (Vidaurre et al. (2017)). RVQ is suited for this purpose.
063   Furthermore, signal compression methods that incorporate spatiotemporal relationships can enhance the
064   interpretability of noisy data (Descombes et al. (1998); Woolrich et al. (2004)). These findings encourage us
065   to employ spatiotemporal compression techniques, such as NAC, to improve the SNR of fMRI data, leading
066   to further performance enhancements.
067   Several data compression methods for fMRI data have been proposed. For instance, AutoEncoder-based com-
068   pression methods using VAE or ViT (Dosovitskiy et al. (2020)) are also commonly seen (Chen et al. (2023);
069   Kim et al. (2021)). While these methods can be used for the same purpose, they still have low compression
070   rates due to continuous value-based compression methods, and they suffer from low interpretability.
071
      Our contributions are summarized as follows:
072
      • We propose BrainCodec, an RVQ-based codec model as a preprocessing method for fMRI data, inspired
073   by the latest neural audio codec model.
074   • We evaluate the compression capability of BrainCodec as a preprocessing step within the framework of
075   Thomas et al. (2022) and show that our method can further improve the classification performance of mental
076   state decoding beyond conventional methods (Section 4.1).
077   • We analyze the codebooks, which are the latent representations obtained from the RVQ in BrainCodec. We
078   propose obtaining these codebooks separately in two different domains: task fMRI and resting state fMRI,
079   and comparing them. As a result, we elucidate their similarities and differences, confirming their validity
080   from a neuroscientific perspective (Section 4.2.1). This result shows the high interpretability of BrainCodec.
081
      • We analyze the performance changes when modifying the number of codebooks and find that competitive
      results are achieved even with half the codebooks, emphasizing the effectiveness of hierarchical modeling in
082
      RVQ. Additionally, we show that the reconstructions by BrainCodec provide clearer visualizations of brain
083
      activity, i.e., higher SNR, suggesting potential applications as a novel denoising technique (Section 4.2.2).
084   • We publish the codes, dataset, and model weights at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/
085   BrainCodec to further development.

104   (i) Direct use of raw fMRI data
105
      fMRI data, which consist of four-dimensional BOLD signals over time, pose challenges in machine learning
      due to their high dimensionality (Lemm et al. (2011)). Brain activity exhibits strong spatial correlations
106
      (Smith and Kohn (2008)), allowing the aggregation of correlated voxels. The Dictionaries of Functional
107
      Modes (DiFuMo) (Dadi et al. (2020)) technique addresses this by defining a sparse dictionary matrix learned
108   from millions of fMRI data. By using the pseudo-inverse of the DiFuMo matrix, the high-dimensional
109   BOLD signals can be compressed into a lower-dimensional representation, allowing them to use fMRI data
110   in NLP methods. Thus, they transform fMRI data into a form X ∈ RT ×n , where n = 1024, using the
111   DiFuMo matrix, then pass it through a single Linear layer to serve as input to the CSM. They consider this the
112   embedded representation of fMRI data, drawing an analogy to NLP. In this study, we also utilize DiFuMo.
113   (ii) Introduction of TR embeddings
114   While CSM employs positional embeddings to model temporal relationships, fMRI data may have varying
115   repetition times (TRs), meaning the temporal relationship between each time-step is not constant. They
116
      propose explicitly including TR in the CSM input. Specifically, they prepare embeddings correspond-
      ing to 0, 0.2, 0.4, . . . , 300s. If the TR is 0.7s with a time length of 10, each time-step corresponds to
117
      0, 0.7, 1.4, . . . , 6.3s. Each time-step is then matched with the nearest embedding, in this case, embeddings
118
      corresponding to 0, 0.6, 1.4, . . . , 6.2s. The resulting TR embeddings are added to the positional embeddings
119   to model variations in TR. We also adopt this architecture and further introduce TR embeddings into our
120   proposed method (See 2.2.1).

122   2.2     P ROPOSED METHOD
123
124   In this study, we propose BrainCodec, a codec model that considers spatiotemporal relationships (Fig
125   1). By compressing fMRI data using such a codec model, the training of models like CSM is facilitated,
126   potentially improving performance. First, this section will detail BrainCodec. Then, it will describe how to
127   use BrainCodec within the framework of SSL.
128
129   2.2.1    B RAIN C ODEC
130   BrainCodec features an autoencoder architecture consisting of an Encoder, a Residual Vector Quantization
131   (RVQ) module (Zeghidour et al. (2021)), and a Decoder (Fig 1). First, the fMRI data transformed by DiFuMo
132   is input into the Encoder, whose output (i.e. latent representation) is then discretized by the RVQ module.
133   The discretized latent representation is subsequently fed into the Decoder, which outputs the fMRI data
134   post-DiFuMo application. All modules are trained end-to-end to minimize reconstruction error. Below, we
135   detail each module and the objective function.
136   Encoder & Decoder modules. The Encoder and Decoder are primarily composed of convolutional layers
137   and are designed to be symmetrical. The Encoder takes the DiFuMo-transformed fMRI data X ∈ RT ×1024
138   as input and produces a latent representation Z ∈ R3T ×128 . This involves spatial compression by a factor
139   of 1024/128 = 8 while expanding the temporal dimension by a factor of 3 through transposed convolution
140   layers (Dumoulin and Visin (2016)). This results in an overall compression ratio of approximately 2.6,

202   2.2.2    CSM WITH B RAIN C ODEC
203
204   In this study, following the approach by Thomas et al. (2022), we train the CSM in two phases: (i) upstream
205
      learning and (ii) downstream learning. First, we perform self-supervised learning of CSM in the upstream
      learning. This enables the extraction of features from fMRI data. Then, using the model, we perform
206
      fine-tuning to solve the target classification task in the downstream learning.
207
208   (i) Upstream learning. The CSM undergoes self-supervised learning, meaning it is pretrained without
209   specific labels. We reconstruct fMRI data using BrainCodec, and this reconstructed data is then used as both
210   input and output for CSM (see Fig 2). The training objective is the reconstruction error, using the L1 loss.
211
      Except for processing fMRI data with BrainCodec, this method follows Thomas et al. (2022) without any
      modifications to the CSM architecture. This ensures a fair comparison with previous research.
212
213   (ii) Downstream learning. The pretrained CSM is fine-tuned for classification task. Specifically, we
214   prepared a trainable class embedding (CLS) and concatenated it at the end of the input data (see Fig 3). This
215   concatenated input is then processed by CSM, and the resultant CLS embedding is treated as a feature vector.
216   This vector was input into a linear and softmax layer to produce outputs for the classification task.

      3.2     DATASETS
239
240   The datasets utilized in this study differ between upstream and downstream learning. For upstream learning,
241   34 datasets were used, comprising 11,980 fMRI runs from 1,726 subjects, all of which are available on
242   OpenNeuro.org (Markiewicz et al. (2021)). Specific identifiers for these data can be found in Appendix C.1.
243   This dataset spans many acquisition sites and covers a diverse set of experimental conditions and domains.
244   Notably, the data allocation for validation and testing was 4% and 1% respectively, selected randomly not
245   from the total data but specifically within each dataset.
246   In downstream learning, three datasets were employed as benchmark mental state decoding datasets: the
247   Human Connectome Project (HCP (Van Essen et al. (2013))), the Multi-Domain Task Battery (MDTB (King
248   et al. (2019))) and Over 100 Task fMRI Dataset (Over100 (Tomoya Nakai (2020))). HCP includes task-fMRI
249   data across 20 tasks from 100 subjects. The tasks include those related to working memory and emotion.
250   MDTB contains data from 24 subjects who performed 26 tasks across two sessions, with each session
251   including 8 fMRI runs. The tasks include concrete permuted rules operations (CPRO) and action observation.
252   Over100 consists of 6 participants, each performing 103 cognitive tasks. Each participant completes 18
253   runs. The tasks include natural actions such as PressRight, where the participant presses a button with their
254
      right hand, and RestOpen, where they are asked to keep their eyes open. Additional details are available in
      Appendix C.2. The HCP and MDTB datasets were split by subject, with HCP divided into 48, 10, and 20 for
255
      train, validation, and test, respectively, and MDTB into 11, 3, and 9. In contrast, the Over100 dataset had only
256
      6 subjects, so it was randomly split into 5,428, 904, and 1,810 runs.
257
258   It is important to note that both upstream and downstream datasets have been previously utilized and made
259   publicly accessible by Thomas et al. (2022). These datasets were already preprocessed (details in Appendix
260
      C.4), and no further preprocessing was performed in our study. The data splitting ratios were also maintained
      as per the original study to ensure accurate comparisons.

      4.2     C ODEBOOK ANALYSIS
340
      In this section, we analyze the codebooks obtained through BrainCodec training, discussing their latent
341   representations and potential applications. Through these discussions, we aim to confirm the neuroscientific
342   validity of the obtained codebooks and demonstrate their useful applications, which are challenging with
343   conventional methods such as VAE. This highlights the novelty and importance of BrainCodec.
344

      5    C ONCLUSION
456
457
      In this study, we proposed a compression method for fMRI, BrainCodec. As a result, we demonstrated that
      combining BrainCodec with SOTA models such as Linear and CSM can achieve further improvements in
458
      accuracy of mental state decoding. Additionally, we quantitatively and qualitatively compared the codebooks
459
      obtained from task fMRI and resting state fMRI, elucidating common and different aspects in representations
460   for each codebook. We confirmed that these aspects are consistent with neuroscientific findings. Also, we
461   focused on the performance changes resulting from varying the number of codebooks used. We demonstrated
462   that using only half of the layers achieves performance comparable to prior research, highlighting the high
463   compression capability of BrainCodec. Furthermore, comparisons of fMRI reconstructions with other methods
464   showed that BrainCodec can clearly extract brain activity, suggesting its potential as a novel denoising method.
465
      Through this study, we believe the necessity for large-scale fMRI data will only increase. One major obstacle
466   in collecting such data is the issue of privacy. However, by using this compression method to exchange data
467   in a compressed state, we may see a potential solution to this problem. Therefore, this paper could contribute
468   not only to neuroscience but also to the field of deep learning itself.

682   In this section, we delineate the architecture of the model, focusing on the parameters without delving into
683   the specifics of parameter selection. For further details on parameter choices, refer to A.2.
684
      The critical hyperparameters are summarized in Table 5. As illustrated in Figure 1, BrainCodec is an
685
      AutoEncoder-based model consisting of three primary components: the Encoder, the Decoder, and the
686   Residual Vector Quantization (RVQ).
687
688   The Encoder is primarily composed of ConvTransposedBlocks. Each ConvTransposedBlock consists of
689
      a Transposed Convolution Layer (Dumoulin and Visin (2016)) and a Residual Layer. In the Transposed
      Convolution Layer, operations are performed to expand in the temporal direction when the stride is greater
690
      than one. Additionally, a standard Convolution Layer is inserted to match the input and output dimensions,
691
      and an LSTM is applied to the output of the ConvTransposedBlock. Summarizing the transformations, the
692   shape of the input fMRI data after applying DiFuMo (1024, 50) undergoes the following changes:
693
694
                                (1024, 50)     →Convolution layer              →(1024, 50)
695
696                            →(1024, 50)     →ConvTransposedBlock1           →(1024, 50)
697                            →(1024, 50)     →ConvTransposedBlock2           →(512, 50)
698                            →(512, 50)      →ConvTransposedBlock3           →(256, 150)
699                            →(256, 150)     →LSTM                           →(256, 150)
700
                               →(256, 150)     →Convolution layer              →(128, 150)
701
702   The Decoder, on the other hand, mirrors the Encoder’s structure. Each module is designed symmetrically with
703   reversed filter sizes and strides, and the LSTM is applied first. Unlike the Encoder, the Decoder compresses
704   in the temporal direction using standard Convolution Layers instead of Transposed Convolution Layers.

                                                       15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


705
      Finally, the RVQ is an extension of the widely used Vector Quantization (VQ) module, concatenating multiple
706
      VQ modules (eight in this case). Each VQ’s input is the difference between the input and output of the
707   previous VQ. This hierarchical modeling allows different codebooks to capture distinct features, akin to
708   frequency decomposition. In the audio domain, the first VQ module tends to capture low-frequency semantic
709   features, enabling techniques like teacher forcing to explicitly learn these semantic features, as proposed
710   in codecs such as the SpeechTokenizer (Zhang et al. (2023)). Similar trends are observed in our study, as
711   demonstrated in Section 4.2.
712
713   A.2   H YPER -PARAMETER SELECTIONS
714
715   In this section, we present the important hyperparameters considered in our study, accompanied by loss
716   graph plots. It is important to note that while each individual experiment offers a valid comparison, the
717   overall comparisons are not consistent across all experiments. Therefore, direct comparisons between separate
718   experiments should be approached with caution. Additionally, since the trajectory of the loss typically
719   stabilizes early in the training process, some experiments were halted at the initial stages.
720   Figure 6a: Strides
721   Our experiments revealed that increasing the temporal dimension in the encoder leads to a greater reduction
722   in reconstruction error. This is a logical outcome, as it essentially corresponds to a reduction in effective
723   compression rate, making reconstruction easier. Considering this trade-off, we opted for a factor of 3 for
724   temporal expansion. This choice is analogous to the approach used in Encodec, where the temporal dimension
725   is compressed by a factor of 320, while the spatial dimension is compressed by a factor of 128, resulting in an
726
      effective compression rate of approximately 2.5. In BrainCodec, we used the spatial dimension compression
      to 128, as in Encodec, yielding an 8-fold compression. To achieve an effective compression rate closest to
727
      2.5, we chose to expand the temporal dimension by a factor of 3.
728
729   Figure 6b: Loss function
730   By default, Encodec employs L1 loss, which is a very common setting in the audio domain. However, this
731   setting is not self-evident for fMRI data. In fact, in reconstructions using VAE, L2 loss is also often employed
732
      (Kim et al. (2021)). Therefore, we conducted a comparison and found that using L2 loss resulted in a lower
      reconstruction error for L2 loss, while L1 loss increased correspondingly, and vice versa. This outcome is
733
      quite natural. Given that it is unclear which loss function is better based on reconstruction error alone, we
734
      compared them in terms of downstream performance. As shown in the figure, L2 loss contributed slightly
735   more to improvement. L2 loss imposes a heavier penalty on outliers, which could be beneficial for the noisy
736   nature of fMRI data that may contain many outliers.
737
738
      Figure 6c: Discriminator
      This is likely the most significant factor contributing to the differences observed. We concluded not to use
739
      the traditionally well-utilized discriminator. The reason, as illustrated in the figure, is that it exacerbated
740
      the reconstruction error. This issue was not resolved by simply adjusting the coefficients between the loss
741   functions. This instability is believed to stem from the inherently noisy nature of fMRI data. Reconstruction
742   error, by definition, calculates loss based on the difference across all data points between input and output.
743   Consequently, when inputs like fMRI data, which are significantly affected by noise, are processed, the
744   learning progresses towards replicating that noise in the output. While discriminators generally aim to enhance
745   fidelity, employing them leads to learning that replicates a similar noise output from noise inputs, essentially
746   attempting to reproduce the noise distribution. Therefore, this compatibility issue between the reconstruction
747   error, which aims to fit to one realization of noise distribution, and the use of discriminators led to unstable
748   learning outcomes. To improve upon this, approaches such as the introduction of noise-robust GANs (Kaneko
749
      and Harada (2020)) might be considered, though this remains a topic for future research.
750   Additionally, as shown in Figure 6d, changing the number of ConvBlocks confirms that with fewer layers,
751   overfitting occurs rapidly. Furthermore, altering the dimensions of the codebook (Figure 6e) did not result

      Here, we describe how to train CSM in a token-based manner by leveraging BrainCodec’s ability to tokenize
894
      fMRI data. This technique is well-known for handling raw data that is difficult to work with directly, such as
895   audio (Wang et al. (2023); Łajszczak et al. (2024)).
896
897   Using the Encoder and RVQ of BrainCodec, DiFuMo-transformed fMRI data with a shape of (1024, 50) can
898
      be converted into a token sequence with a shape of (8, 150). Typically, in Transformer training with text, the
      token sequence shape is a one-dimensional array like (150,). The hierarchical tokenization introduced by RVQ
899
      must be managed appropriately. MusicGen (Copet et al. (2024)) discusses how to input these hierarchical
900
      token sequences into a Transformer. It shows that the "delay pattern", which inputs the token sequences offset
901   by one token per hierarchy, best balances efficiency and performance. However, our experiments found that
902   the "flatten pattern", which simply arranges the token sequences in a single line, yielded the best results (see
903   Fig 10b).
904
      Figure 9 illustrates the overall process of TokenCSM’s upstream learning. Note that, since the input and
905
      output are token sequences, the training objective is cross-entropy loss rather than reconstruction error. For
906
      downstream learning, as described in Section 2.2.2, we append a CLS token at the end and use this CLS token
907   as the feature for solving the classification task.
908
909   For TokenCSM training, the parameters for upstream learning were the same as CSM (see Section 3.1), except
910
      the batch size was 32 and the learning rate was 7 × 10−4 . This training lasted about 56 hours. For downstream
      learning, the only change made compared to the first method was the batch size, which was decreased to 64.
911
      This phase of training took approximately 8 hours. This configuration results in approximately 100 million
912
      parameters. Compared to the tens of billions of parameters commonly used in NLP (Touvron et al. (2023))
913   and the hundreds of millions to billions used in speech models (Łajszczak et al. (2024)), this is still small.

916   B.3   H YPER -PARAMETER SELECTIONS
917
918   Here, we introduce the loss curves for TokenCSM when hyperparameters are adjusted. Specifically, we
919   illustrate the effects of varying the number of layers and token rearrangement patterns.
920
      Figure 10a shows the loss curves when the number of layers is varied. It was observed that increasing the
921   number of layers consistently reduces the loss. Naturally, increasing the number of layers, and hence the
922   parameter size, necessitates a corresponding increase in data volume. Since the data volume was fixed in this
923   study, we selected 24 layers, the maximum value shown here.
924
      Figure 10b depicts the changes in loss when the token rearrangement patterns "delay" and "flatten" are applied.
925
      As shown, the "flatten" pattern significantly outperforms the "delay" pattern. However, it is important to note
926
      that the "flatten" pattern uses approximately 8 times more token length than "delay," resulting in increased
927   computational resources and time.
928
929   Moreover, regardless of the method, the absolute loss value remains around 6. Initially, the loss was
930
      approximately 6.8, so this represents only a reduction of 0.8. In text-to-speech systems, which operate on
      similar principles, the cross-entropy loss typically ranges from 3 to 4. This indicates that the current method
931
      has considerable room for improvement. The most effective and reliable way to achieve this improvement
932
      is to increase both the data volume and parameter size. Collecting more data remains a significant future
933   challenge for this field.

1145
       deviates from the ground truth.

1148   truth data (Figure 12d) is already reconstructed data. At the initial step following the prompt, the generated
1149
       data closely matches the ground truth. However, as the sequence progresses, the generated data increasingly
       diverges from the ground truth. This divergence is likely due to the autoregressive nature of the CSM, where
1150

1151

1158   from a single example. For instance, generating data for downstream learning and performing classification
1159   on this generated data could objectively compare the quality of the generated sequences. This remains a
