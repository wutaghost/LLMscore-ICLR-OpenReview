037                                                     A BSTRACT
038
039           We introduce a new paradigm for AutoRegressive (AR) image generation, termed
040           Set AutoRegressive Modeling (SAR). SAR generalizes the conventional AR to the
041           next-set setting, i.e., splitting the sequence into arbitrary sets containing multiple
042
              tokens, rather than outputting each token in a fixed raster order. To accommo-
              date SAR, we develop a straightforward architecture termed Fully Masked Trans-
043
              former. We reveal that existing AR variants correspond to specific design choices
044
              of sequence order and output intervals within the SAR framework, with AR and
045           Masked AR (MAR) as two extreme instances. Notably, SAR facilitates a seamless
046           transition from AR to MAR, where intermediate states allow for training a causal
047           model that benefits from both few-step inference and KV cache acceleration, thus
048           leveraging the advantages of both AR and MAR. On the ImageNet benchmark, we
049           carefully explore the properties of SAR by analyzing the impact of sequence order
050           and output intervals on performance, as well as the generalization ability regard-
051           ing inference order and steps. We further validate the potential of SAR by training
052           a 900M text-to-image model capable of synthesizing photo-realistic images with
053
              any resolution. We hope our work may inspire more exploration and application
              of AR-based modeling across diverse modalities. Code will be available.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
055
056
      The success of AutoRegressive (AR) models in Large Language Models (LLMs) (Radford, 2018;
057   Radford et al., 2019; Brown, 2020; Raffel et al., 2020; Yang, 2019; Touvron et al., 2023) has also
058   driven their development in image generation, where some recent work (Ramesh et al., 2021; Yu
059   et al., 2021; 2022; Tian et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024) has demon-
060   strated that the generative capabilities of AR models can rival or even surpass those of diffusion
061   models (Song & Ermon, 2019; Song et al., 2020b; Ho et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Rom-
062   bach et al., 2022; Song et al., 2020a; Lipman et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022; Karras et al., 2022; Peebles
063   & Xie, 2023; Esser et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2024; Zhuo et al., 2024).
064   Despite their strong performance, the large number of inference steps in AR models due to the ‘next-
065   token prediction’ manner has become a bottleneck. This limitation has inspired explorations on
066   more efficient AR approaches, with the idea of outputting multiple tokens simultaneously. Existing
067   work (Chang et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2023a; Chang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; 2024; Ni et al.,
068   2024) usually adopts BERT-like (Devlin, 2018) masked modeling approaches to exchange the cost
069   of always performing global computations (thus KV cache is not allowed) for fewer inference steps.
070   Another stream of work designs proper sequence orders and arranges multiple tokens with similar
071
      properties into one group, to predict these tokens at once, e.g., the scale-aware order (Tian et al.,
      2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2024). We conclude that, in the training phase, these approaches
072
      pay attention to two aspects: one is the sequence order, the other is the output intervals. The
073
      defined order and intervals split the sequence into token sets. AR splits the sequence into sets of
074   single tokens, VAR (Tian et al., 2024) builds several multi-scale sets for an image, and Masked AR
075   (MAR) (Chang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023; 2024) randomly divides the sequence into a masked set
076   and an unmasked set. Fig. 2 (a1, a2) illustrates examples for AR with intervals of length 1, while
077   (d1, d2) demonstrates MAR with 2 output intervals.
078
      In this work, we present Set AutoRegressive Modeling (SAR), extending causal learning by gener-
079
      alizing sequence order and output intervals to arbitrary configurations. Specifically, compared with
080   AR that splits the training process into sub-processes that output one single token in fixed raster
081   order, SAR is able to input token sequence in any order (some examples are illustrated in Fig. 3 and
082   Fig. 5), and splits it into any number of token sets, each as a sub-process that output multiple to-
083   kens. In order to represent the sequential relationship of token sets, we introduce generalized causal
084   masks. As shown in Fig. 2, the classical causal mask (a1) is a lower triangular matrix; when the set
085   contains more than one token (b1, c1, d1), the matrix becomes block-wise and is called a generalized
086   causal mask. Within our framework, we show that AR, VAR (analogously), and MAR emerge as
087   special cases of SAR, with AR and MAR representing two extreme instances. Refer to the left side
088
      of Fig.2 and Table1 for conceptual illustrations. Moreover, by the new formulation, we offer a path
      for smoothly transiting between AR and MAR. The intermediate states of SAR enables one to train
089
      a few-step causal model in support of KV cache acceleration that inherits both the advantages of AR
090
      and MAR models. Given that classical AR models, such as the decoder-only transformer, fails in
091   the SAR setting, we propose a simple model architecture termed Fully Masked Transformer (FMT).
092   FMT adopts the encoder-decoder structure proposed in the original transformer (Vaswani, 2017) to
093   enable both recording the output position and facilitating position-aware interaction between seen
094   and output tokens. It incorporates generalized causal masks into each attention process to keep the
095   causal manner, and the details can be referred to Fig. 4.
096
      Under the SAR framework with FMT, we conduct experiments to explore the properties of SAR
097   on the ImageNet 256 × 256 benchmark. We examine the relationship between the two hyper-
098   parameters—sequence order and output intervals—and their impact on model performance, few-step
099   generalization ability, and inference order generalization ability, discussing the associated trade-offs.
100   Then, we train a text-to-image model on 20 million high-aesthetic images to further validate the gen-
101   eration capability of the transition states in SAR. Using limited computational resources and data,
102   our model demonstrates the capability to generate photo-realistic images of arbitrary aspect ratios
103   that adhere to the text descriptions.
104   Our main contributions are:
105
106         i) We propose Set AutoRegressive Modeling, that unifies existing AR variants and offers new
107            states between the two extremes, AR and MAR. The new states enables the training of
               few-step causal generation models.

      2.1    AUTOREGRESSIVE AND M ASKED M ODELING
143
144   Originated in language processing, GPT series (Radford, 2018; Radford et al., 2019; Brown, 2020)
145   and BERT (Devlin, 2018) are representative works in autoregressive and masked modeling respec-
146   tively. During the AR training, the current output token can only be observed by the preceding
147   tokens. At inference tokens remain unchanged once output, facilitating the use of KV cache accel-
148   eration. Recently some work (Cai et al., 2024; Gloeckle et al., 2024) studies to reduce the inference
149   steps by training multiple prediction heads and conducting speculative decoding (Leviathan et al.,
150   2023; Chen et al., 2023) at inference. In contrast, BERT (Devlin, 2018) employs a bidirectional
151
      modeling approach known as masked modeling, to capture contextual information. It randomly
      masks a portion of tokens at a high masking ratio and trains the model to predict these masked to-
152
      kens. At inference, BERT models can iteratively generate the output sequence with fewer steps than
153
      AR methods, at the cost of global calculation. Additionally, some works have introduced context
154   perception into AR models. For example, XLNet (Yang, 2019) integrates insights from BERT by
155   permuting the input sequence to enable bidirectional training with AR models. On image modality,
156   our work not only provides further unification of AR and BERT models but also builds a smooth
157   path connecting AR and BERT, where one can train models with both their merits.

234   3.2   S ET AUTO R EGRESSIVE M ODELING
235
236   Sequence order and output intervals characterize autoregressive paradigms. Actually, the to-
237   ken sequence in any output order can be rearranged into a causal one. AR is the simplest case
238   whose input sequence is inherently causal. The other two instances with respect to an 8 × 8 image
239   token grid are shown in Fig. 3. The left order is derived by downsampling the tokens using nearest
240
      neighbor interpolation (so the token value stays unchanged after interpolation). We make the model
      progressively output tokens downsampled with a scale factor of 1/8, 1/4, and 1/2, and finally the
241
      rest of the tokens in a scale-aware order. It shares a similar spirit with VAR (Tian et al., 2024), so we
242
      call it a ‘next-scale’ variant. In this case, we can rearrange the tokens in the scale order. The right
243   subfigure corresponds to mask modeling. By putting the unmasked tokens at the front and masked
244   ones as the rest, we also derive a causal sequence.
245
246
      Next, we consider the output intervals. For example, the output intervals of the ‘next-scale’ variant
      in Fig. 3 are 1, 4, 16, 43, while those of the masked variant are the number of masked tokens and
247
      unmasked tokens. Since these variants output multiple tokens in each interval, they should be paired
248
      with generalized causal masks in training. Some conceptual instances are shown in Fig. 2 (b1, c1,
249   d1), where generalized causal masks extend the classical causal mask (a1) to a block-wise format.
250   The generalized causal mask can be uniquely determined by the output intervals.
251
252
      SAR generalizes AR by extending the sequence order and the output intervals to any possible sce-
      narios. In Fig. 2 (a1, d1) we can see that the causal mask of AR and MAR are two extreme case. In
253
      the intermediate states of SAR, one can train causal models with few-step inference enabled, which
254
      do not appear in either AR or MAR families. For example, if a 8-token sequence is split into 4
255   sets with 1, 2, 2, 3 tokens, the causal mask should be like that in Fig. 2 (b1). In short, SAR extends
256   ‘next-set prediction’ in Eq. 2 to the training phase.
257
258
      The model implementation—Fully Masked Transformer. The realization of SAR is not straight-
      forward, though. Classical AR models, e.g., the decoder-only transformer fails in three aspects. i)
259
      When AR shifts the sequence to align the current set with the previous set, it will find the number
260
      of tokens may not be equal. ii) AR models can only model the output-seen relations with fixed and
261   simple ‘next token’ forms of relative positional relationships, rendering them ineffective in complex
262   scenarios involving arbitrary sets. iii) Given a token at a specific position, AR models output it
263   based on its relative steps to the first token, leading to failure when outputting arbitrary sets. These
264   drawbacks inspire the design philosophy: i) the model should have perception of absolute positions
265   for outputting arbitrary token sets, and ii) the output tokens and the seen tokens should be placed
266   into two containers, each with positional encoding, to facilitate their position-aware interaction.
267   Hence, we split the decoder-only transformer into two parts, an encoder and an decoder. The
268   encoder takes in the image tokens and extract the semantic features. The decoder records
269   the output position with position embeddings and models the interaction between output tokens
      and seen tokens from the encoder, at the cost of adding cross-attention in each decoder layer.

514   adopt the training strategy with multiple aspect ratios enabled in Gao et al. (2024); Zhuo et al. (2024)

518   each training stage, we group images with different aspect ratios but similar resolutions, which are

526   8 steps. With 64 to 128 steps, it can deliver high-quality outputs, requiring a processing time of only
527
      3 to 6 seconds. Typically, the full 4096 steps take 56 times longer than that required for 64 steps.

529   5     C ONCLUSION
530
531   In this work, we propose Set AutoRegressive Modeling (SAR), a new AR paradigm that enables
532   users to freely customize the AR training and inference processes. For SAR, we also develop a
533   preliminary model architecture called the Fully Masked Transformer. We carefully explore the
534   properties of SAR, with a particular focus on the intermediate states, which facilitate training models
535   capable of both few-step generation and KV cache acceleration. Additionally, we train a T2I model
536
      under the SAR paradigm to validate the generation capabilities at the transition states of SAR.
537   Limitation. As a newly emerging paradigm, the exploration of SAR in this paper is limited, partic-
538   ularly concerning the performance of SAR intermediate states on ImageNet. Future work may focus
539   on developing better training and inference schedules, designing model architectures that are more
      compatible with SAR, and exploring the application of SAR across additional modalities.
