011                                                    A BSTRACT
012
013            In recent years, Large Language Models (LLMs) have become fundamental to a broad
014            spectrum of artificial intelligence applications. As the use of LLMs expands, precisely
015            estimating the uncertainty in their predictions has become crucial. Current methods often
016            struggle to accurately identify, measure, and address the true uncertainty, with many fo-
017
               cusing primarily on estimating model confidence. This discrepancy is largely due to an
               incomplete understanding of where, when, and how uncertainties are injected into mod-
018
               els. This paper introduces a comprehensive framework specifically designed to identify
019
               and understand the types and sources of uncertainty, aligned with the unique character-
020            istics of LLMs. Our framework enhances the understanding of the diverse landscape of
021            uncertainties by systematically categorizing and defining each type, establishing a solid
022            foundation for developing targeted methods that can precisely quantify these uncertain-
023            ties. We also provide a detailed introduction to key related concepts and examine the
024            limitations of current methods in mission-critical and safety-sensitive applications. The
025            paper concludes with a perspective on future directions aimed at enhancing the reliability
026            and practical adoption of these methods in real-world scenarios.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large Language Models (LLMs) have recently demonstrated remarkable capabilities in various complex
032   reasoning and question-answering tasks (Zhao et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024c; Liang et al., 2022). However,
033
      despite their potential, LLMs still face significant challenges in generating erroneous answers (Ji et al.,
      2023a; Li et al., 2023a; Huang et al., 2023), which can have serious consequences, particularly in domains
034
      where high levels of accuracy and reliability are critical. A key issue undermining trust in LLM outputs is the
035
      models’ lack of transparency and expressiveness in their decision-making processes (Zhou et al., 2023; Lin
036   et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2023; Xiao & Wang, 2018; Hüllermeier & Waegeman, 2021), where comprehensively
037   understanding and estimating the model’s uncertainty plays a vital role. For example, in the medical field, a
038   physician diagnosing a critical condition like cancer would not only require a high predictive accuracy from
039   the model but also need to understand the uncertainty associated with the case (Gawlikowski et al., 2022a).
040
      While the need for quantifying uncertainty in LLMs is widely recognized, there still lacks a consensus on
041
      the interpretation of uncertainty in this new context (Gawlikowski et al., 2022a; Mena et al., 2021; Guo
042   et al., 2022; Hüllermeier & Waegeman, 2021; Malinin & Gales, 2018), which in turn further complicates its
043   estimation. Terms such as “uncertainty”, “confidence”, and “reliability” are often used interchangeably, yet
044   they refer to distinct concepts that require careful distinction (Gawlikowski et al., 2021). For instance, an
045   LLM can exhibit a high-confidence response to an inherently uncertain and unanswerable question. How-
046   ever, this response could be contextually inappropriate or factually incorrect, illustrating that high confidence

                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


047
      does not necessarily correspond to low uncertainty (Gawlikowski et al., 2022b). Thus, the first challenge
048
      that remains in the literature is to explicate the definition of uncertainty in the context of LLMs and explore
049   the nuanced differences between these intertwined concepts.
050
051   Traditionally, uncertainty in deep neural networks (DNNs) is categorized into two types: aleatoric, arising
052
      from data randomness such as sensor noise, and epistemic, stemming from limitations in model knowledge
      due to insufficient data or unmodeled complexities (Gawlikowski et al., 2022a; Mena et al., 2021; Guo et al.,
053
      2022; Hüllermeier & Waegeman, 2021; Malinin & Gales, 2018). Although these categories are widely
054
      used in deep learning, they do not fully address the unique challenges of LLMs, which include process-
055   ing complex text data, managing extremely large parameters, and dealing with often inaccessible training
056   data. Furthermore, the entire lifecycle of LLMs—from pre-training through inference—introduces unique
057   uncertainties, as does the interaction between users and these models. Understanding these different sources
058   of uncertainty is critical, particularly from the perspective of making LLMs more interpretable and robust.
059   Achieving this understanding, however, is not possible without an inclusive and fine-grained framework that
060   systematically identifies and analyzes the various sources of uncertainty in LLMs.
061   Recently, numerous studies have been proposed, aiming to estimate the uncertainty in LLMs (Manakul
062   et al., 2023; Beigi et al., 2024; Azaria & Mitchell, 2023a; Kadavath et al., 2022; Kuhn et al., 2023), and
063   can be broadly divided into four main categories based on their underlying mechanisms: logit-based (Lin
064   et al., 2022b; Mielke et al., 2022a; Jiang et al., 2021; Kuhn et al., 2023), self-evaluation (Kadavath et al.,
065   2022; Manakul et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024a), consistency-based (Portillo Wightman et al., 2023; Wang
066   et al., 2023), and internal-based (Beigi et al., 2024). However, given the unique characteristics and nuanced
067   aspects of uncertainty in LLMs, critical questions arise regarding the effectiveness of each type of method
068   in truly capturing uncertainly or other related aspects in the context of LLMs, and which specific sources of
069
      uncertainty are being detected across the various stages of an LLM’s lifecycle. Answering these questions
      is vital for developing more reliable and comprehensive approaches to uncertainty estimation in LLMs.
070
071   To address the aforementioned challenges and questions, we conduct a critical review and analysis of stud-
072   ies related to uncertainty and other related concepts, aiming to present a comprehensive survey covering the
073   full spectrum of uncertainty in LLMs, particularly focusing on the interplay between uncertainty concepts,
074   sources, estimation methods, and text data characteristics, which, to the best of our knowledge, is still lack-
075
      ing in this field. In summary, our contributions in this survey are manifold and pioneering: (1) We have
      standardized definitions for uncertainty and explore related concepts, enhancing communication across the
076
      field (Section 2). (2) We are the first to propose a comprehensive framework that analyzes all sources of
077
      uncertainty throughout the lifecycle of LLMs, providing deep insights into their origins and effective man-
078   agement strategies (Section 3). (3) We evaluate and compare current methods for estimating and evaluating
079   LLM uncertainty, discussing their strengths and limitations (Section 4). (4) Finally, we identify future re-
080   search directions to enhance uncertainty estimation in LLMs, addressing critical gaps and emerging trends
081   for improved reliability and accuracy in critical applications (Section 5).

110   2021), inappropriate model selection (Gawlikowski et al., 2022a; Mena et al., 2021; Battaglia et al., 2018),
111   or factors like noise and inherent data ambiguity (Kendall & Gal, 2017; Mena et al., 2021; Guo et al., 2022).
112   These factors collectively delineate a model’s understanding—or misunderstanding—of its operational en-
113   vironment, influencing the reliability of its outputs.
114   Confidence, often expressed as a “predicted probability score”, quantifies the likelihood that a model’s
115   prediction is correct. Derived from the softmax output applied to logits, this score assigns each class a
116   probability between 0 and 1, with the highest probability indicating the model’s chosen prediction (He et al.,
117   2024; Guo et al., 2017; Nandy et al., 2021). However, confidence scores can be misleading; they may exhibit
118   “overconfidence”, where the score is high despite inaccurate predictions, or “underconfidence”, where scores
119   are low even when predictions are correct (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017; Wang, 2023; Chen et al., 2022;
120   Guo et al., 2017).
121   Reliability. Merely estimating confidence scores is insufficient for safe decision-making. It’s vital to align
122   confidence scores with the actual probabilities of correct predictions, a process known as calibration (Guo
123   et al., 2017; Wang, 2023). Re-calibration techniques have been developed to enhance this alignment, en-
124   suring that confidence estimates are accurately calibrated and reliable for practical applications (Guo et al.,
125   2017; Wang, 2023; Nixon et al., 2019; Mukhoti et al., 2020).
126   Does High Confidence Score Always Mean Low Uncertainty? Confidence scores are often interpreted
127   as indicators of uncertainty, leading to significant challenges. DNNs frequently assign high confidence to
128   inputs far removed from their training data, resulting in misleading confidence levels for incorrect classifi-
129   cations (Hein et al., 2019). For instance, a network trained on images of cats and dogs might confidently,
130   but incorrectly, classify a bird as one of these categories (Gawlikowski et al., 2022a; Malinin & Gales,
131   2019). Therefore, high confidence does not necessarily indicate low uncertainty, making such an assump-
132   tion problematic. LLMs also exhibit high confidence even when uncertainty is substantial. For example,
133
      models might confidently answer ambiguous or unanswerable questions like “How many planets are in the
      universe?” (known unknowns), “What will be the top performing stock in 10 years?” (future unknowns),
134
      “What is consciousness and how does it work?” (controversial unknowns), or “What is the exact weight
135
      of a watermelon?” (ambiguous questions), despite the inherent uncertainty of such questions. Similarly,
136   models may express high certainty in speculative or hypothetical scenarios, like “What would happen if the
137   US had lost the Independence War?”, even when no definitive answer exists. These examples highlight that
138   a high confidence score which derived through various computational methods, often imply as zero uncer-
139   tainty, does not necessarily indicate the correctness of an answer. It is crucial, therefore, to approach high
140   confidence scores with caution and to develop methods to measure the true uncertainty.

144   3.1   A C OMPREHENSIVE F RAMEWORK FOR U NDERSTANDING U NCERTAINTY IN LLM S
145
146   There are three traditional categories of uncertainty commonly used in deep learning, including (1) Model
147   (epistemic) Uncertainty, which pertains to uncertainties in estimating model parameters, reflecting model
148   fit and its limitations in generalizing to unseen data (Der Kiureghian & Ditlevsen, 2009; Lahlou et al., 2023;
149   Hüllermeier & Waegeman, 2021; Malinin & Gales, 2018); (2) Data (or aleoteric) Uncertainty that stems
150   from complexities within the data itself, such as class overlap and various types of noise (Der Kiureghian &
151
      Ditlevsen, 2009; Rahaman & Thiery, 2020; Wang et al., 2019; Malinin & Gales, 2018); and (3) Distribu-
      tional Uncertainty, which often dues to dataset shift and occurs when training and testing data distributions
152
      differ, leading to potential generalization issues during real-world applications where the model faces data
153
      markedly different from what it was trained on (Malinin & Gales, 2018; Nandy et al., 2021; Gawlikowski
154   et al., 2022a; Chen et al., 2019; Mena et al., 2020).
155
156   These traditional uncertainty categories,
157
      though prevalent in deep learning, fail
      to fully address the unique challenges
158
      of LLMs. LLMs are characterized by
159
      extensive parameters, complex text data
160   processing, and often limited access to
161   training data, introducing specific uncer-
162   tainties in their outputs. Moreover, in-
163   teractions with users in dynamic envi-
164   ronments and human biases in data an-
165   notation or model alignment complicate
166   the uncertainty landscape. Unlike gen-
167   eral deep learning models that primar-
168
      ily predict numerical outputs or classes,
      LLMs generate knowledge-based out-
169
      puts which may include inconsistent or
170
      outdated information (Lin et al., 2024b).
171   These features cannot be adequately ad-
172   dressed by simply categorizing uncer-
173   tainty into three traditional types. These
174   distinctive aspects necessitate a com-
175   prehensive framework to better under-
176   stand the diverse sources of uncertainty
177   in LLMs.
178   To address these challenges, we intro-
179   duce a new framework to categorize un-
180   certainty in LLMs, as illustrated in Fig-
181   ure 2. This framework distinguishes be- Figure 2: A comprehensive framework to categorize the sources
182   tween operational uncertainty, which of uncertainty through LLM’s life-cycle.
183   pertains to model and data processing,
184   and output uncertainty, focusing on the quality of the generated content. Specifically:
185   Operational uncertainty in LLMs arise from pre-training to inference, encompassing data acquisition,
186   model and architecture design, training and optimization processes, alignment, and inference activities.
187   These uncertainties stem from how LLMs are trained on extensive datasets, process inputs, and generate

188
      text. In essence, operational uncertainties arise when the model is unable to capture the full complexity of
189
      the data it has been trained on, or when the input data itself introduces ambiguities or noise.
190
191   Output uncertainty in LLMs stems from challenges in analyzing and interpreting the generated text, relat-
192   ing to the quality and reliability of information used for decision-making. For example, in medical scenar-
193
      ios, where an LLM is tasked with providing diagnostic suggestions based on patient symptoms, the model
      may generate multiple possible diagnoses. However, if these suggestions lack justification or supporting
194
      evidence, or contain contradictory information, significant uncertainty arises for the physician who must
195
      determine the credibility of these diagnoses. The physician may face substantial challenges in deciding
196   which diagnosis to investigate further, highlighting the critical need for LLMs to provide well-supported,
197   consistent, and reliable outputs to ensure their practical utility in decision-making processes.
198
199
      By distinguishing between operational and output uncertainties, our framework offers several benefits: first,
      a fine-grained approach that captures the unique features of LLMs, providing a precise reflection of uncer-
200
      tainty for better modeling and understanding. Second, it establishes a foundation for identifying uncertainty
201
      sources, essential for developing targeted methods to accurately quantify them. Third, it offers stakeholder-
202   specific insights, helping developers, users, and administrators address uncertainties relevant to their roles,
203   enhancing model robustness, user interaction, and governance. Lastly, by aggregating beliefs and evaluat-
204   ing output evidence, the framework builds trust in LLM outputs, particularly in critical fields like medical
205   diagnosis or legal reasoning.

207   3.2     O PERATIONAL U NCERTAINTY IN LLM S
208
209   3.2.1    O PERATIONAL U NCERTAINTY IN P RE -T RAINING AND T RAINING S TAGE OF LLM S
210
      Data Uncertainty This phase involves collecting and organizing the pre-training corpus, which is critical
211   as the quality, diversity, and representativeness of the data directly affect the model’s understanding and
212   ability to generalize. The sources of uncertainty at this stage include:
213
214
      (1) Semantic Ambiguity: Textual data inherently contain semantic complexities, leading to significant un-
      certainties in training and inference processes for language models. For example, the word ’bank’ can mean
215
      a financial institution or a river’s edge, and ’lead’ can refer to the act of leading or the metal, depending
216
      on the context (Anand & Kumar, 2022; Ott et al., 2018; Dreyer & Marcu, 2012; Blodgett et al., 2020).
217   These semantic ambiguities pose challenges in maintaining meaning across different contexts and highlight
218   the difficulty of achieving consistent semantic understanding. Such ambiguities are a primary source of
219   uncertainty, complicating the model’s understanding (Anand & Kumar, 2022; Piantadosi et al., 2011).
220
      (2) Linguistic Variability: Textual environments are dynamic and subject to contextual and cultural shifts
221
      that significantly alter data interpretation and relevance (Kutuzov et al., 2018; Levy, 2008). For example,
222
      word meanings and usages evolve, new slang emerges, and topics range from casual conversations to spe-
223   cialized discussions, each with distinct linguistic nuances (Levy, 2008; Liu et al., 2018; Kutuzov et al.,
224   2018). This variability requires language models to continually interpret context to determine meaning,
225   greatly increasing the uncertainty in their knowledge and response.
226
      (3) Errors: The data collection process can introduce errors like typographical mistakes, incorrect tagging,
227
      or grammatical errors. These inconsistencies can significantly mislead the learning process, impairing the
228
      LLM’s ability to model and generate text accurately (Wang et al., 2024a; Liu et al., 2018).
229
230   (4) Insufficient Coverage: This refers to situations where incomplete coverage in the training dataset leads
231   to uncertainty. Mitigating this requires acquiring more extensive and diverse data that encompasses various
232
      viewpoints (Gawlikowski et al., 2022a).
233   (5) Reliability and Contamination of Data: Training data for LLMs often contains inaccuracies or outdated
234   content (Lin et al., 2024b), which can lead these models to perpetuate and amplify such errors (Lin et al.,


                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


235
      2024b). Misrepresented facts and contaminated data—incorrect or misleading information included in the
236
      training set—introduce significant uncertainty and hinder the models’ reliability as decision-making tools
237   (Jiang et al., 2024).
238
239   (6) Human Biases: In training data related to gender, race, socio-economic status, age, or disability are
240
      primary sources of uncertainty in LLM predictions (Bender & Friedman, 2018). These biases skew the
      model’s understanding and responses, resulting in outputs that may not be universally valid or appropriate,
241
      thus increasing uncertainty about the model’s performance and reliability in diverse real-world scenarios
242
      (Kirk et al., 2021).
243
244   Model Uncertainty This type primarily arises from the model’s fit to the data, highlighting its ability to
245   generalize from the training data to unseen data (Malinin & Gales, 2018). The design of the architecture
246   and the training process of an LLM are crucial in shaping its capabilities and effectiveness. This process
247   involves the strategic configuration of the neural network, where each decision reflects an inductive bias—the
248   underlying assumptions embedded in the model through choices in network structure. These biases influence
249   how the model interprets and processes information (Battaglia et al., 2018). The sources of uncertainty
250   pertain to model uncertainty are:
251   (1) Model Architecture and Hypothesis Space: The architecture of an LLM and the hypothesis space it
252   explores are crucial to its performance and error susceptibility. Architectural decisions, such as the number
253   of layers and network types, significantly influence model effectiveness across various tasks. These choices
254   determine the hypothesis space—what the model can learn and predict—thereby introducing uncertainty in
255   the model’s ability to understand and generate language under different conditions. Variability in architec-
256   tural setup can cause performance discrepancies when applied to new or varied datasets (Fedus et al., 2022;
257   Abdar et al., 2021b; He et al., 2024; Gawlikowski et al., 2022a; Dodge et al., 2020).
258   (2) Parameter Estimation and Optimization: Parameter estimation and optimization methods are critical
259   sources of variability and uncertainty in LLMs. Choices in optimization techniques (e.g., SGD, Adam),
260   learning rates, loss functions, and regularization methods (e.g., dropout, L2 regularization) significantly
261   impact the model’s generalization capabilities and robustness (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017). These fac-
262   tors contribute to uncertainty in the model’s ability to consistently replicate results across different runs or
263   datasets, affecting its adaptability to new data and stability across various operational environments (Payzan-
264
      LeNestour & Bossaerts, 2011).
265
      3.2.2   O PERATIONAL U NCERTAINTY IN I NSTRUCTION T UNING AND A LIGNMENT S TAGE OF LLM S
266
267   Instruction-tuning and Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) are advanced techniques
268   that enhance LLMs’ adaptability and responsiveness to specific tasks or user preferences (Ouyang et al.,
269   2022; Rafailov et al., 2024). These techniques refine model responses to align more closely with expected
270   outcomes using predefined instructions or guidelines (Bai et al., 2022; Askell et al., 2021). This process
271   introduces uncertainty through two main sources:
272   (1) Inconsistency and Bias in Data Labeling and Human Annotation: Human labeling and annotation
273   are fundamental sources of uncertainty in the training LLMs (Ghandeharioun et al., 2019; Abdar et al.,
274   2021a; Zhou et al., 2024). The subjective nature of human judgment introduces variability and biases,
275   affecting learning outcomes from reward models (Zhang et al., 2023a). Individual differences in perception
276   and decision-making can lead to cognitive biases and inconsistency in labeled data, which RLHF reward
277   modeling algorithms may exacerbate (Wang et al., 2024b; Denison et al., 2024).
278   (2) Interpretation of Guidelines and Feedback:The interpretation of instructions can vary, depending on
279   the clarity of the guidelines and the model’s ability to interpret them contextually Wang et al. (2024a).
280   Discrepancies in understanding or applying these instructions can lead to variability in the model’s outputs
281   (Siththaranjan et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024; Chidambaram et al., 2024; Park et al., 2024). The subjective

282
      nature of feedback and its interpretation by the model can introduce additional layers of uncertainty. This
283
      is particularly evident when there is a lack of consensus among human reviewers, leading to challenges in
284   achieving stable and predictable model behavior (Ghandeharioun et al., 2019; Abdar et al., 2021a; Zhou
285   et al., 2024).

287   3.2.3    O PERATIONAL U NCERTAINTY IN I NFERENCE S TAGE OF LLM S
288
289   Distributional Uncertainty occurs when there are discrepancies between the training data distributions and
290   those encountered during testing, a phenomenon known as dataset shift. This uncertainty is prevalent in
291
      real-world applications where models face data significantly different from their training sets. Distribu-
      tional uncertainty indicates a lack of model familiarity with new data, posing challenges in making accurate
292
      predictions. This uncertainty is categorized into in-domain and out-of-domain types.
293
294   (1) In-Domain Uncertainty: This type of uncertainty occurs when LLMs operate within their training en-
295   vironments and inputs closely resemble the training data distribution. Such scenarios often present interpo-
296   lation challenges or deal with ’unknown knowns’ (Ashukha et al., 2021; Hüllermeier & Waegeman, 2021).
297
      Despite the similarity to training datasets, subtle nuances and variations within seemingly familiar data can
      still provoke uncertainties if not fully captured during training (Kim et al., 2023; Kong et al., 2020).
298
299   (2) Out-of-Domain Uncertainty: This occurs when LLMs face queries or data points outside their training
300   distribution, leading to ’unknown knowns’ and ’unknown unknowns,’ where the model lacks the necessary
301   data or precedent to generate well-founded responses, often resulting in overly generic or shallow outputs
302   (Xu & Ding, 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Kong et al., 2020). ’Unknown knowns’ are situations where the model
303
      has indirect knowledge but encounters data that, while potentially interpolatable from known data, still lies
      outside its direct experience, leading to uncertain responses despite possible correctness (Amayuelas et al.,
304
      2023). Conversely, ’unknown unknowns’ refer to entirely unfamiliar data types or topics that the model has
305
      never encountered, typically producing speculative, erroneous, or hallucination.
306
307   Sampling and Decoding Strategy is another importatn sources of uncertainty in inference stage originate
308   from the . The configuration of LLMs at inference time, including temperature scaling, context length (Anil
309
      et al., 2022), and decoding strategies such as beam search or nucleus sampling, significantly affects the un-
      certainty of model outputs. Temperature scaling adjusts the randomness of predictions by modifying the
310
      probability distribution, with lower values resulting in more deterministic outputs and higher values increas-
311
      ing diversity and variability. The choice of context length can influence the extent of uncertainty in outputs,
312   with longer generations potentially introducing more uncertainty than shorter ones. Decoding strategies like
313   beam search enhance output coherence by considering multiple possibilities, yet may reduce variability and
314   creativity. These configurations are crucial for the model’s generalization across tasks, impacting the co-
315   herence and consistency of predictions, thus playing a key role in balancing performance with uncertainty
316   (Renze & Guven, 2024; Xie et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2021; Ott et al., 2018; Hashimoto et al., 2024; Stahlberg
317   & Byrne, 2019; Eikema & Aziz, 2020; Meister et al., 2020; Fan et al., 2018; Holtzman et al., 2020; Hewitt
318   et al., 2022).

320   3.3     O UTPUT U NCERTAINTY IN LLM S
321
      In contrast to operational uncertainties, which stem from the mechanics of how LLMs as deep neural net-
322
      works generate text, output uncertainties focus on the outputs these models produce when used as knowledge
323
      generation tools. This capability can release a vast flow of information useful for decision-making in various
324   downstream tasks. Here, the challenge shifts from a shortage of information to the risks of poorly under-
325   standing and managing inherent uncertainties, which may arise from unreliable, incomplete, deceptive, or
326   conflicting information. The topic of reasoning and decision-making under uncertainty has been extensively
327   explored in various AI domains, such as belief/evidence theory and game theory. This extensive knowledge
328   base is crucial for enhancing our understanding of LLM outputs and identifying their inherent uncertainties,

329
      important for tasks relying on this knowledge. Building on insights from a recent survey on uncertainty and
330
      belief theory (Guo et al., 2022), we adapted their framework to classify uncertainties better suited to the
331   characteristics of LLM outputs. As a result, we categorize the sources and causes of output uncertainties
332   based on the ambiguity in the output itself, which can stem from various causes and sources as:
333
334
      (1) Lack of supporting evidences and incomplete knowledge: An LLM may produce outputs with uncertain-
      ties due to a lack of supporting evidence in the responses, a common occurrence even in well-trained models
335
      addressing complex or nuanced topics. This uncertainty stems from the model generating conclusions with-
336
      out sufficient evidence to substantiate its answers, and from its inability to provide sufficient theoretical
337   understanding or reliable information. The connections between claims and supporting information may
338   not be clearly established or detailed, which hampers confident and reliable reasoning and decision-making.
339   To mitigate this, the model’s output can be enriched by incorporating more robust evidence or discarding
340   unreliable evidence. Additionally, the complexity of model-generated information can overwhelm users due
341   to limited cognitive capacity to process dense or intricate data. Simplifying the data into more manageable
342   chunks with coarser granularity or focusing on key features while omitting less critical details can help.
343   Effectively managing this uncertainty involves concentrating on leveraging the most relevant information
344   available, thus enhancing the confidence and trust in the accuracy and relevance of the outputs.
345   (2) Multiple knowledge frames and contradicting knowledge : These sources of uncertainty arise in scenar-
346   ios where the same information—such as evidence or opinions—can be interpreted in various ways, leading
347   to conflicting views. Multiple, valid beliefs about certain knowledge or information may coexist, often due
348   to conflicting evidence. Conflicts can occur when parts of the information are incorrect, irrelevant, or when
349   the model interpreting the data is not suitable for the current context. Additionally, conflicts may arise where
350   there is no definitive ground truth, or in cases of controversial debate. Differing opinions from users, based
351
      on subjective perspectives, further complicate understanding and increase the layers of uncertainty.
352

      4    A PPROACHES FOR E STIMATING AND E VALUATING U NCERTAINTY IN LLM S
354
355
356
      Existing approaches to assess how well a model understands and is certain about its predictions in LLMs
      can be summarized into four major categories:
357
358   Logit-based approaches (Lin et al., 2022b; Mielke et al., 2022a; Jiang et al., 2021; Kuhn et al., 2023) assess
359   model confidence by analyzing the probability distributions or entropy of outputs, providing clear measures
360   of confidence. Although straightforward to implement, a fundamental issue with using logits as confidence
361   indicators is that they reflect the probability distribution across potential tokens (vocabulary space), capturing
362
      linguistic forms rather than verifying the truthfulness or correctness of statements (Lin et al., 2022b; Si
      et al., 2022; Tian et al., 2023). Logit probabilities, irrespective of their magnitude, predominantly represent
363
      distribution over vocabulary space. Therefore, logit probabilities do not directly indicate model uncertainty
364
      but also reveal various linguistic factors that influence the model’s output. This nuanced perspective is in
365   stark contrast to human expressions of certainty, which typically reflect a belief in the accuracy or truth of a
366   claim, based on information processing and decision-making processes, and are not influenced by phrasing
367   (Koriat et al., 1980; Fischhoff et al., 1977). Additionally, another significant limitation is that logit-based
368   methods do not identify or measure any types of uncertainty, limiting their applicability in scenarios where
369   understanding the sources and degrees of uncertainty is crucial.
370
      Consistency-based approaches (Vazhentsev et al., 2023; Portillo Wightman et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023;
371   Shi et al., 2022; Manakul et al., 2023; Agrawal et al., 2023) assess confidence by evaluating the agreement
372   among various model responses, identifying potential inconsistencies. However, these methods encounter
373   significant challenges, especially due to the diversity of potential paraphrases and formatting variations in
374   textual data, complicating their use in real-time scenarios (Xiong et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2021; Fadaee
375   et al., 2017; Li et al., 2022; Ding et al., 2024; Kuhn et al., 2023). Additionally, a non-trivial challenge is the

                                                          8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


376
      effective measurement of consistency among responses, a persisting issue that hinders accurate confidence
377
      assessment (Manakul et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023b).
378
379   Self-evaluation methods (Kadavath et al., 2022; Manakul et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024a) enable models to
380   internally assess the correctness of their answers by leveraging their introspective capabilities. These meth-
381
      ods employ various prompts that encourage models to express their confidence through numerical values or
      verbalized terms. Recent studies have refined these approaches, utilizing strategies like Chain of Thought
382
      (CoT) to enhance how models calibrate and articulate linguistic confidence (Xiong et al., 2023). Research
383
      has also explored expressing confidence with linguistics qualifiers to better align verbal expressions with the
384   model’s actual confidence levels (Mielke et al., 2022b; Zhou et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2022a), making model
385   outputs more understandable for users. Despite their potential, these methods are constrained by the model’s
386   limited self-awareness, which can lead to circular reasoning and overconfident inaccuracies (Ji et al., 2023b;
387   Chen et al., 2023). Another challenge is the interpretability and validity of obtained probabilities that align
388   with specific linguistic and psychological interpretations, including expressions like ’I think,’ ’undoubtedly,’
389   or ’high confidence.’
390   Internal-Based Approach Recently, Beigi et al. (2024) used a mutual information framework to theo-
391   retically demonstrate that the internal states of large language models provide additional insights into the
392   correctness of their answers. Burns et al. (2023) introduced an innovative unsupervised method that maps
393   hidden states to probabilities. This approach involves responding to ”Yes” or ”No” questions, extracting
394   model activations, converting these activations into probabilities. Furthering this research, studies have em-
395   ployed linear probes (Li et al., 2023b; Azaria & Mitchell, 2023b) and contrastive learning (Beigi et al.,
396   2024) to assess whether the internal states across various layers can distinguish between correct and incor-
397   rect answers. Empirical results suggest that certain middle layers and specific attention heads show strong
398
      discriminative abilities. Beigi et al. (2024) expanded these findings by illustrating that for tasks requiring
      contextual processing, such as reading comprehension, the outputs of multi-head self-attention (MHSA)
399
      components are crucial for assessing response correctness. However, current methodologies exhibit lim-
400
      itations. Each task and dataset requires training a specific “confidence estimator” model, which restricts
401   their generalizability. This limitation is evident as the performance of these methods often declines when
402   trained on one task and dataset and tested on another, highlighting their limited transferability across differ-
403   ent applications (Bashkansky et al., 2023). Additionally, the computational resources required to train these
404   confidence estimators pose challenges for their deployment in real-time applications, further complicating
405   their practical utility.

      5    D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE D IRECTION
424
425
426   Go beyond Confidence Estimation: As discussed, the literature on uncertainty estimation in LLMs pri-
427   marily interprets confidence scores as measures of uncertainty. Such a prevalent method oversimplifies the
428   nuanced and complex nature of uncertainty inherent in model predictions, which is crucial for accurate in-
429   terpretation and reliability of model outputs. The limitations inherent in current methodologies necessitate
430   the development of a more advanced framework for categorizing uncertainty estimation in large language
431
      models that surpasses the reliance on simple confidence scores.
432   Lack of Explainability: Current methods of confidence estimation provide certainty predictions without
433   elucidating the underlying causes of potential uncertainties. While these scores may seem reasonable to
434   human observers, the absence of insight into the sources of uncertainty complicates trust in the model’s
435   outputs, particularly in safety-critical contexts where explainability is essential. Current confidence quantifi-
436   cation techniques struggle to pinpoint specific weaknesses or improvement areas in the model. Additionally,
437
      these methods lack the necessary transparency to clarify the reasons for model uncertainty, whether due to
      input ambiguity from users, insufficient knowledge, or conflicting information in the training data.
438
439   Lack of Ground Truth for Uncertainty Estimation: Current methods for estimating uncertainty are em-
440   pirically evaluated and assess how accurately they predict the correctness of an answer. However, there
441   is generally no established ground truth for validation, particularly regarding the sources of uncertainty,
442   meaning currently there is no metric and method to determine the contribution of different uncertainties for
443
      specific models and tasks.
444   Lack of Transferability of Uncertainty Estimation Methods Across Different Applications and
445   Datasets: Current uncertainty estimation methods often struggle with adaptability and generalizability when
446   applied to new applications or datasets. Although effective within their specific domains, these methods fre-
447   quently fail to yield reliable results in different settings due to factors like data distribution differences and
448   unique domain-specific requirements. To overcome these limitations, it is crucial to develop more robust and
449
      flexible uncertainty estimation techniques that can adjust to the varied conditions and demands of diverse
      applications.
450
451   Lack of Standardized Evaluation Protocol & Comprehensive Benchmarks for Confidence Estimation:
452   Current methods for evaluating uncertainty estimation methods are typically used to compare uncertainty
453   quantification techniques through metrics like accuracy, calibration, or performance in out-of-distribution
454   detection, using standardized datasets common within the LLM community. Despite this, variations in
455
      experimental settings across studies highlight the need for a comprehensive benchmark across tasks and
      domains to assess their robustness. The absence of a standardized testing protocol poses challenges for
456
      researchers from different downstream tasks, making it difficult to identify the most advanced methods
457
      or choose a specific sub-field of uncertainty quantification to pursue. This lack of uniformity hinders the
458   direct comparison of emerging techniques and impedes the broader acceptance and integration of established
459   uncertainty quantification methods.

463   6    C ONCLUSION
464
465   In this paper, we have reviewed and analyzed the uncertainty inherent in LLMs. We clarified the definition of
466   uncertainty and related concepts, enhancing understanding across various domains. A comprehensive frame-
467   work was proposed to categorize and identify sources of uncertainty throughout the lifecycle of LLMs. We
468   also reviewed current approaches in the literature, discussed their challenges and limitations, and highlighted
469   future directions to enhance the practicality of LLMs in real-life applications.
