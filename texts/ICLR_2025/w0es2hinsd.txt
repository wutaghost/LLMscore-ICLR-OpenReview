                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               The progress of humanity is driven by those successful discoveries accompanied
012
               by countless failed experiments. Researchers often seek potential solutions de-
013            scribed in related literature (raw information) and verify them through experi-
014            ments. With the explosive growth of deep learning literature and methods, such
015            a process imposes a more significant burden on researchers and renders success-
016            ful discoveries veiled. Therefore, automating such a research and development
017            (R&D) process is an urgent need. In this paper, we serve as the first effort
018            to formalize the goal by proposing a Real-world Data-centric automatic R&D
019            Benchmark, namely RD2 Bench. RD2 Bench benchmarks the whole data-centric
020            automatic R&D (D-CARD) process, including extracting methods (formulas and
021
               models) from raw information (reports and papers) and implementing methods
               through codes. Specifically, to investigate the capability boundaries of the state-
022
               of-the-art (SOTA) large language models (LLMs) in the unexplored D-CARD, we
023
               conduct exhausting and expensive human annotations and experiments. We eval-
024            uate the performance of SOTA LLMs on our identified 27 formulas and 6 models
025            across various difficulty levels from financial reports and ML papers. We find
026            that although RD2 Bench is very challenging, SOTA LLMs possess promising po-
027            tential to bring more significant development to D-CARD. We appeal to research
028            teams with various domain expertise to consider constructing domain-specific D-
029            CARD benchmarks, contributing to both a cross-domain D-CARD platform and
030            the potential revolutionary upgrade to human productivity.

      In this paper, we serve as the first effort to investigate the capabilities of the SOTA LLMs in tackling
      automatic R&D and propose a Real-world Data-centric automatic R&D Benchmark (RD2 Bench).
088
      The scenario studied by RD2 Bench possesses two unique and distinct characteristics that fundamen-
089
      tally differentiate it from others. First, RD2 Bench focuses on studying the real-world scenario where
090   all the operations in R&D are automatic and evaluated as a whole, thus navigating the related future
091   research efforts toward the goal of developing human technology more effectively. The real-world
092   scenario requires more comprehensive and advanced model capabilities and exhibits new challenges.
093   Second, we study the real-world automatic R&D in data-centric settings to navigate future work to-
094   ward the urgent experimental exploration need brought by black-box data-driven models. Compared
095   with existing benchmarks, RD2 Bench possesses two significant advantages:
096   (1) RD2 Bench evaluates the interaction and synergistic effects of various model capabilities
097   instead of focusing on a single aspect of ability, which not only captures the frontier of SOTA LLMs
098   but also bridges the gap between studying “individual ability” and “real-world synergistic effects
099   of abilities”. In automatic R&D, an ML system fails to complete the task even if it possesses both
100   the strong information extraction ability and the strong programming or tool-using ability: While
101   it succeeds in extracting methods and implementing them, it fails in selecting the appropriate data
102   from the datasets or misunderstanding either the descriptions of data features or the requirements
103   expressed by prompts. Additionally, exhaustively enumerating all the aspects for benchmarking is
104
      extremely challenging, which is overcome by RD2 Bench.
105   (2) RD2 Bench tends to select well-performing trustworthy models instead of those models that
106   fail to learn rationales and causality yet possess outstanding performance. Specifically, ML systems
107   easily achieve SOTA performance on previous benchmarks by shortcut learning or learning spuri-
      ous correlations instead of learning rationales or causality (Mudrakarta et al., 2018; Geirhos et al.,


                                                             2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      2020; Cui & Athey, 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023). This renders a benchmark ineffec-
109   tive and misleading as it fails to accurately identify the well-performing trustworthy methods. For
110   example, an ML system achieves SOTA performance on dog classification by merely recognizing
111   grass (Zhang et al., 2021). RD2 Bench, on the contrary, eliminates such models by its high difficulty
112   and large scope. The decision rules of models have to simultaneously satisfy at least four major
113   requirements: (1) accurately and comprehensively extracting the implementable methods; (2) pre-
114   cisely selecting the method-specific data for computation; (3) correctly writing the code according
115   to the logic expressed by methods and prompts; (4) successfully storing the correct results in a pre-
116   defined format. Therefore, the decision rules of models selected by this benchmark are stable (work
117
      well in various situations), and thus getting closer to rationales and causality (Cui & Athey, 2022).
118   We evaluate the existing SOTA LLMs on RD2 Bench to expose their bottleneck and characterize the
119   future research direction. RD2 Bench reveals new insights: (1) Among the popular LLMs, GPT-
120   4 exhibits promising potency in dealing with the D-CARD task; (2) Detailed information of data
121   descriptions significantly improves the performance of GPT-4; (3) The ability to query domain-
122   specific knowledge is a basic requirement of D-CARD methods; (4) The more complex the method
123
      is, the more unstable the model’s performance is.

127   2.1   LLM AS AUTONOMOUS AGENT
128
129   In the past few years, LLM has made great achievements in both academia and industry (OpenAI,
130   2023a; Touvron et al., 2023), and has achieved results that surpass the previous level in a number of
131   classic tasks (Zhao et al., 2023). Research has shown that with the growth of data volume and model
132
      size (Zoph et al., 2022), LLM has emerged with stronger reasoning and other capabilities (Ouyang
      et al., 2022). These capabilities enable LLM to exhibit certain agent-like behaviors in some tasks
133
      such as using or creating tools (Qin et al., 2023b; Qian et al., 2023), planning (Yao et al., 2023;
134
      Brown et al., 2020a), and memory. Therefore, more and more researchers have expressed their ex-
135   pectations for its human-like and overall capabilities, and have made preliminary explorations of it
136   as an independent agent (Wang et al., 2023a; Shinn et al., 2023b). Multi-agent collaboration (Wu
137   et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023) is also introduced to LLM for better accuracy and generalizability. More-
138   over, for reducing human efforts and automatically exploring, previous work focuses on autonomous
139   LLM agents for general purpose are purposed (Yang et al., 2023b; Shen et al., 2023). Positive views
140   further believe that the realization of AGI may come from the evolution of autonomous LLM and
141   some inspiring examples have been released (Penov et al., 2024).
142   However, most research still focuses on limited scenarios that are given with clear and fixed ques-
143   tions and backgrounds. A recent work (Yang et al., 2023d) has attempted to introduce LLM to the
144   R&D field and formalize the R&D process as a sequence of tasks. However, there is no easy-to-use
145   benchmark for the community and current R&D tasks may be too general and can’t reveal signifi-
146   cant signals. In this work, we propose a benchmark for LLM in data-centric R&D tasks and provide
147   a comprehensive evaluation.
148
149   2.2   S EMI -AUTOMATIC R&D WITH AGENTS
150
151   Scientific research and development (R&D) is a time-consuming and important process. In the past,
152   R&D has been mainly conducted by human researchers with countless failed experimental explo-
153
      rations and creative observation conclusions. Agents have been introduced to R&D to reduce human
      efforts and automatically explore. Recently, there have been attempts to partly automate R&D, in-
154
      cluding the automatic chemical synthesis planning (Boiko et al., 2023), automatic molecular design
155
      (Joshi & Kumar, 2021; Schneider, 2017; Boiko et al., 2023), automatic theorem proving (Wang
156   et al., 2023b; Yang et al., 2023c). However, these attempts mainly focus on automatic searching for
157   possible solutions and optimizations with symbolic representation (Lu et al., 2023) and heuristic
158   techniques (Whalen, 2016), but less addressing long-horizon planning, implementation, and rea-
159   soning for the next step idea exploration. Moreover, the data-centric R&D tasks currently have not
160   been explored in the community, and no benchmark has been proposed for the community. Pre-
161   vious works have applied LLM to real-world R&D tasks such as debugging issues (Tian et al.,
      2024; Jimenez et al., 2023a) or only focus on data-centric but not real-world R&D tasks (Liu et al.,


                                                         3

162
      2023a). In this work, we propose a benchmark for LLMs in data-centric R&D tasks and evaluate
163   the performance of LLMs.

166   3     RD2 B ENCH
167
168   Overall, our benchmark focuses on evaluating the finally implemented results according to the given
169
      raw information (e.g., papers, reports, websites, etc.). Moreover, we also provide human-annotated
      ground-truth information corresponding to the intermediate steps for debugging and more compre-
170
      hensive evaluation. RD2 Bench selects well-performing models that follow human operations and
171
      accurately calculate the final results. We introduce the details of our proposed RD2 Bench in the fol-
172   lowing sections. In section 3.1 and section 3.2, we introduce how we collect data and perform human
173   annotation to form RD2 Bench. Then, we elaborate on the two necessary steps, namely method ex-
174   traction and method implementation, to perform R&D in section 3.3 and section 3.4. Finally, we
175   detail our adopted evaluation metrics in section 3.5.
176
      As an initial step toward data-centric automatic R&D, our study focuses on the financial domain as
177
      a starting point. Our motivations are as follows: (1) The financial domain is representative. It
178   heavily relies on data and has high scalability to be extended to academic research with minimal
179   adjustments. In the future, we plan to expand the reports in the current dataset to include research
180   papers (e.g., papers scraped from OpenReview). The methods will include models and formulas
181   from papers, and our current manually implemented code could be replaced by GitHub code from
182   open-source papers. At that point, we could benchmark a model’s capability to conduct ML research.
183   (2) The financial domain is well-defined. We can establish well-defined academic questions in this
184   scenario, with clear evaluation metrics and an analytical, streamlined process. The F1 score and
185   accuracy for method extraction and implementation are core metrics indicating the development of
186
      data-driven automatic R&D. The whole process is fully traceable, making it easy to explain how
      each final result is achieved.

      3.1   DATA C OLLECTION
189
190   We consider the raw information that contains formulas and models, which represent a wide range
191   of methods proposed in the AI domain.
192
193
      Data Collection with Formulas. We prepare raw information that contains formulas as the input
      of R&D. Raw information is presented as publicly available financial reports and stock trading data.
194
      Formulas are usually mathematical formulas that take complex numeric input data about stock, com-
195
      pany, and market as input and output a series of values with the time series. We collect financial
196   reports with 27 implementable formulas distributed in three difficulty levels: easy, medium, and
197   hard. Domain experts manually label the difficulty levels according to the complexity of implemen-
198   tation. To obtain their implementation results, an agent is expected to accurately select the features
199   from three types of trading data scattered across 2010 to 2022, namely fundamental, price-volume,
200   and high-frequency data. We denote the three types of data as Data I, II, and III, respectively.
201
      Data Collection with Models. We collect papers with six open-sourced models (Gravina et al.,
202   2023; Rossi et al., 2023; Rampášek et al., 2022; Lim et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2023a; Wang et al.,
203   2024). The implementation of models adopts Pytorch (Paszke et al., 2019) and torch gemometric
204   framework (Fey & Lenssen, 2019) to perform deep learning. All the papers and models are publicly
205   available. We manually label the difficulty level (easy, medium, hard) of the task based on the
206   complexity of implementation (computational graphs and tensor operations). We refer the readers
207   to the appendix for more details about the dataset and the task.
208
209   3.2   H UMAN A NNOTATION
210
211   To provide a more comprehensive evaluation for debugging and analyzing, we conduct human an-
212
      notation to provide the ground-truth results of our collected data, namely method extraction results
      and method implementation results.
213
214   Challenges. We confront five main challenges in the human annotation process. First, we need
215   to identify the difficulty levels of methods to ensure the diversity of our benchmark and expose
      the bottleneck of current models. Second, we have to identify and discard the raw information if


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      its presented methods demand unavailable data: The computation of some formulas can require
217   confidential information that is not publicly available. Third, since the definitions or descriptions
218   of some methods can be vague, leading them to be unimplementable, we have to filter out these
219   methods. Fourth, some domain-specific methods containing factual errors should be filtered out
220   since they are not implementable. Fifth, we should distinguish the domains and types of the methods
221   according to their descriptions. To sum up, all the challenges imply the fact that human annotation
222   of RD2 Bench requires expensive time cost and the expertise of annotators. Therefore, we commit
223   more effort to designing the annotation guidelines, process, and quality control to ensure the dataset
224   quality.
225   Annotation Guidelines. The annotation guidelines are discussed and formulated after the trial
226   phase, where each annotator completes 3-5 trial annotations. Our goal is to identify the described
227   methods (formulas and models) in publicly licensed raw information and then implement them.
228   In the method identification (extraction) process, a method is identified if: (1) all required data
229   features for its computation are present in our predefined dataset, and (2) all necessary information
230   for reproducing its code is explicitly available in the report. For example, negative examples include
231
      instances where variables used in the formula are not declared in the report; descriptions are vague
      or lack critical information, making reproduction infeasible; required data features are too rare or
232
      costly to obtain, thus lacking general applicability. Methods are extracted following a predefined
233
      schema (details are described in Appendix D), and corresponding code implementations are created
234   to reproduce the results. To enhance reproducibility, generalization, and verification, we also define
235   the scope of data features: In a specific domain, most data features are publicly accessible while
236   a small portion may be costly and difficult to obtain. In the code implementation process, if the
237   original report provides source code that can be successfully executed, it is executed by annotators
238   and marked as the successfully implemented code for the method. If source code is absent or not
239   executable, annotators write and execute code for reproduction. Manually written code must follow
240   these specifications: (1) Use Python in Jupyter Notebook; (2) Import all required packages at the
241   beginning of the notebook; (3) Include the method information in the form of the predefined schema
242
      as comments at the start; (4) Begin with a data loading step; (5) Treat each block of code involving
      input data transformation or format changes as a separate step; (6) End with an output data storage
243
      step, saving results in folders named after the method; (7) Display the intermediate results of each
244
      step in the notebook; (8) Store completed notebooks in the “check” folder.
245
246   Annotation Process. We build an annotation team where members possess varying levels of ex-
247   pertise in both ML and financial domains, categorized as follows: undergraduate students, master
248
      students, doctoral students, postdoctoral researchers/general researchers in the Financial AI indus-
      try, and senior researchers in the Financial AI industry. A 2-3 hour offline training session was held,
249
      including live demonstrations and interaction, with a recorded session provided for later reference.
250
      The session covered the guidelines and a full annotation workflow demonstration by a senior re-
251   searcher. After the training stage, each annotator completes 3-5 trial annotations. The trial serves
252   two purposes: (1) refining the schema for method reproduction and (2) evaluating the quality and
253   expertise of annotators. Trial results are rigorously reviewed by senior researchers and authors. The
254   obtained results were applied to real financial scenarios (e.g., backtesting) for validation. The anno-
255   tators who pass the trial will compose our annotation team. Special cases (e.g., whether a method
256   can be included if parameters are inferred by annotators) are discussed and decided collectively
257   by the team. FAQs are documented and shared with all members. More details are presented in
258   Appendix E.
259   Annotation Quality. To ensure clarity of guidelines, as well as accuracy in results, multiple anno-
260   tators annotate and implement methods for the same report. Discrepancies are analyzed to verify
261   the robustness of the guidelines and the reliability of the results. Both the extraction and implemen-
262   tation results in our “ground truth” folder show their consistency across the multiple annotations,
263   which demonstrates the quality of both our annotation guideline and annotation results. During the
264   annotation process, the tasks of annotators vary according to their annotating status. Specifically,
265
      method extraction and implementation tasks are assigned by senior researchers based on profiles of
      annotators and their trial performance. For annotators exceeding the expected annotation time, task
266
      complexity is adjusted (becomes easier) accordingly.

      3.3   M ETHOD E XTRACTION S TEP
271
272   We evaluate the ability of models to recognize and extract information from raw information (e.g.,
273   R&D context). A qualified model is expected to discern feasible methods (formulas and models)
274   from extensive research materials and extract all necessary information for implementing these for-
275   mulas. The ability serves as the foundational premise for subsequent code implementation.
276   We expect the model to accurately and comprehensively extract the methods mentioned in the re-
277   search materials it reviews, including all essential conditions required to implement the method.
278   For methods with incomplete information, further implementation is not required; for methods with
279   complete conditions, a model is expected to correctly comprehend the semantic meaning of these
280   conditions stated in natural language and generate corresponding code. Specifically, we have prede-
281   fined the extraction format (key-value pair) for the model. We employ the F1 score to measure the
282   comprehensiveness and accuracy of method identification and extraction.
283   Note that some methods in the original materials might only imply their function, effect, or origin
284   through their names without explicitly presenting their formulas, definitions, or other details. In such
285   cases, the model may choose not to extract them or opt to autonomously complete them based on
286   the semantics of the original materials. We expect the latter approach in future work, as it showcases
287   the creativity of models by proposing new formulas and generating brand-new, informative, and
288   reliable information. In the current version of the benchmark, only methods mentioned by name
289   are evaluated in this manner; future iterations will explore and assess the model’s ability to generate
290
      new names and formulas when none are explicitly mentioned.

      for the evaluation of the implementation of methods. We set the base models as GPT-4-turbo (Ope-

461   Sources of Errors from GPT-4. Based on the well-performed GPT-4, as an example, the first is-
462   sue stems from the agents’ lack of domain knowledge, which leads to erroneous operations. For
463   instance, the model is unfamiliar with the concept of market value neutralization in the financial
464   domain. At times, it merely defines a function without providing any content, or it directly applies
465   a normalization operation. However, the industry-standard approach is not a simple one-step nor-
466   malization but rather a multi-step data processing procedure. Through further prompting, we found
467   that the agent knows how to do it inherently but fails to think it through during the implementation
468   process. The second observed error occurs when the input query is lengthy. In such cases, the model
469
      often ignores specific requirements of what it should or should not do. Repeating the requirements
      three times usually ensures the model follows the instructions. The third observed issue is that when
470
      writing code, the model often fails to anticipate the state of the data after each step of processing.
471
      This leads to a situation where the code written for step t + 1 assumes the data is in the state it was
472   at step t − 1, without accounting for the processing done in step t.

515   5   L IMITATION
516
517   The RD2 Bench framework, while innovative, only evaluates the most representative base LLM, such
518   as GPT4, GPT-4o, LLaMa-3.1, LLama3, LLama2, and GPT35, without further evaluating more
519   open source models. Meanwhile, this paper only includesthe most representative R&D domains and
520   problems and focuses on data-driven scenarios, which can be extended more in the future to show
521   its generalizability. We believe that the benchmark will be a valuable tool for the community to
522
      evaluate the performance of the models in the data-centric R&D tasks and to develop new models
      and techniques to address the challenges and opportunities in the domain. To obtain a general cross-
523
      domain automatic R&D benchmark, we need the help of more domain experts and the participation
524
      of more research teams due to its prohibitively expensive cost.

527   6   C ONCLUSION
528
529
      In this paper, we serve as the first effort to tackle the real-world data-centric automatic R&D scenario
      in the hope of significantly improving the research efficiency of scientists and thus contributing to
530
      the revolution of human productivity. Specifically, we first propose RD2 Bench that benchmarks all
531
      the operations in D-CARD as a whole to navigate future work toward the ultimate goal of automating
532   data-centric R&D directly. RD2 Bench focuses on evaluating the interaction and synergistic effects
533   of various model capabilities and aiding in selecting the well-performing trustworthy models. Based
534   on RD2 Bench, we find that although the most SOTA GPT-4 shows its promising potency in tackling
535   D-CARD, there remains ample room for future work.
