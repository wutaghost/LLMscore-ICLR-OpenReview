009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion models have achieved great success in generating samples from complex
012            distributions, notably in the domains of images and videos. Beyond the experi-
013            mental success, theoretical insights into their performance have been illuminated,
014            particularly concerning the convergence of diffusion models when applied with
015            discretization methods such as Euler-Maruyama (EM) and Exponential Integrator
016            (EI). This paper embarks on analyzing the convergence of the higher-order dis-
017            cretization method (SDE-DPM-2) under L2 -accurate score estimate. Our findings
018            reveal that to attain OŐÉ(ŌĶ20 ) Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the target
019
               and the sampled distributions, the sampling complexity - or the required number of
020
               discretization steps - for SDE-DPM-2 is OŐÉ(1/ŌĶ0 ), which is better than the currently
021            known sample complexity of EI given by OŐÉ(1/ŌĶ20 ). We further extend our analysis
022
               to the Runge-Kutta-2 (RK-2) method, which demands a sampling complexity of
023
               OŐÉ(1/ŌĶ20 ), indicating that SDE-DPM-2 is more efficient than RK-2. Our study
               also demonstrates that the convergence of SDE-DPM-2 under Variance Exploding
024
               (VE) SDEs aligns with that of Variance Preserving (VP) SDEs, highlighting the
025
               adaptability of SDE-DPM-2 across various diffusion models frameworks.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Diffusion models , also known as Score-based Generative Models (SGMs), are a powerful generative
031   model which is widely used in image synthesis (Li et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022; Saharia et al.,
032   2022), video generation (Harvey et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023) and molecular design (Anand & Achim,
033   2022; Xu et al., 2022).
034
      Diffusion models operate through two primary processes: the forward process and the backward
035
      (reverse) process. The forward process involves transforming the original data distribution into
036   Gaussian noise via a Stochastic Differential Equation (SDE). During this process, the gradient of the
037   log density function, known as the score function, is estimated by denoising score matching (Vincent,
038   2011) and sliced score matching (Song et al., 2020). The backward process, on the other hand, is
039   capable of generating samples from the target distribution using the estimated score function. This
040   is accomplished through an equivalent reverse SDE or a probability Ordinary Differential Equation
041   (ODE). Specifically, the reverse SDE usually can generate more diverse and high-quality samples
042   than the reverse ODE (Tachibana et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2022b), while the reverse ODE is faster than
043   the reverse SDE (Li et al., 2024).
044   Anderson (1982) showed that a continuous backward process could converge to a target distribution
045   using the ground truth score function. However, in real-world applications, we‚Äôre often limited to
046   estimating this score function based on the available data. Moreover, to implement the backward
047   process in practice, we need to discretize the reverse SDE or reverse ODE.
048   Commonly used discretization methods include Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM)
049   (Ho et al., 2020), Denoising Diffusion Implicit Models (DDIM) (Song et al., 2021a), Exponential
050   Integrator (EI) (Zhang & Chen, 2023) and DPM-2 (Lu et al., 2022a). Song et al. (2021b) illustrated
051   that DDPM is essentially the Euler-Maruyama (EM) discretization of the reverse SDE, a first-order
052   discretization method. The Euler-Maruyama method directly discretizes the reverse SDE‚Äôs drift
053   term, leading to a high discretization error. To address this issue, Lu et al. (2022a); Zhang & Chen
      (2023) designed a new discretization method named DPM or EI, which utilizes the linear part of

      2     P RELIMINARY

110   Song et al. (2021b) delineates two principal types of forward processes: Variance Preserving (VP)
111   SDE and Variance Exploding (VE) SDE. The VP-SDE maintains a bounded variance throughout
112   its evolution, culminating in a distribution that resembles white noise, denoted as N (0, Id ). A
113   distinguished example of VP-SDE is the DDPM, pioneered by Ho et al. (2020). In contrast, the
114   VE-SDE is characterized by its variance which incrementally increases over time, a concept vividly
115   illustrated through the Score Matching and Langevin Dynamics (SMLD) framework by Song &
116   Ermon (2019). The focus of our discussion will be predominantly on the VP-SDE, owing to its
117   widespread application in the theoretical exploration of diffusion models, as evidenced by the works
118
      of Yang & Wibisono (2022); Chen et al. (2023a); Li et al. (2024); Chen et al. (2024a). Furthermore,
      we will demonstrate the applicability of our findings to VE-SDE, broadening the scope of our analysis.
