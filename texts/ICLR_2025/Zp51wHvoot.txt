009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Autoregressive models (ARMs) and diffusion models (DMs) represent two lead-
012            ing paradigms in generative modeling, each excelling in distinct areas: ARMs in
013            global context modeling and long-sequence generation, and DMs in generating
014            high-quality local contexts, especially for continuous data such as images and short
015            videos. However, ARMs often suffer from exponential error accumulation over
016            long sequences, leading to physically implausible results, while DMs are limited
017            by their local context generation capabilities. In this work, we introduce Autore-
018
               gressive Coherent multimodal generation with Diffusion Correction (ACDC), a
               zero-shot approach that combines the strengths of both ARMs and DMs at the
019
               inference stage without the need for additional fine-tuning. ACDC leverages ARMs
020
               for global context generation and memory-conditioned DMs for local correction,
021            ensuring high-quality outputs by correcting artifacts in generated multimodal to-
022            kens. In particular, we propose a memory module based on large language models
023            (LLMs) that dynamically adjusts the conditioning texts for the DMs, preserving
024            crucial global context information. Our experiments on multimodal tasks, includ-
025            ing coherent multi-frame story generation and autoregressive video generation,
026            demonstrate that ACDC effectively mitigates the accumulation of errors and signif-
027            icantly enhances the quality of generated outputs, achieving superior performance
028            while remaining agnostic to specific ARM and DM architectures. Project page:

               https://acdc2025.github.io/

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Autoregressive models (ARM) (Brown, 2020) and diffusion models (DM) (Ho et al., 2020) are
035   the two prominent paradigms in modern generative modeling, each with its strengths, weaknesses,
036   and areas where they excel. ARMs excel at modeling discrete token sequences, leading to their
037   domination of the language domain (Dubey et al., 2024; Abdin et al., 2024), and more recently,
038   multimodal modeling (Kondratyuk et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2024; Jin et al., 2024),
039
      as vector quantized (VQ) autoencoder architectures become advanced (Esser et al., 2021; Yu et al.,
      2023b). ARMs are useful for 1) modeling the global context, as it attends causally to all previous
040
      tokens, and 2) long-sequence generation, as they are not limited to fixed-length tokens (Liu et al.,
041
      2023; 2024). These advantages render ARMs excellent for high-level long-term planners (Du et al.,
042   2023; Huang et al., 2024a) and world models (Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018; Hao et al., 2023; Ge et al.,
043   2024b). However, the “plans” generated by ARMs cannot be corrected, often leading to physically
044   implausible results (Kambhampati et al., 2024) and low quality images/videos. To mitigate this
045   issue, one often needs an external verifier to locally correct for the mistakes (Kambhampati et al.,
046   2024) and rely on vision-specific decoder fine-tuning (Ge et al., 2024b;a). The inability to correct
047   for the errors exacerbate as the length of the generated sequences becomes longer, inherently due to
048   the exponential error accumulating nature of these models. The accumulated errors are especially
049   noticeable in autoregressive video generation, with videos often diverging when generating over a
050
      few tens of frames (Liu et al., 2024).
051   On the other hand, DMs excel at modeling continuous vectors, especially capable of generating high-
052   quality images (Rombach et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022) and short video clips (Brooks et al., 2024).
053   The generative process in diffusion models, which transitions from noise to the data space, inherently
      creates a Gaussian scale-space representation of the data distribution. This approach allows the model

      Another intriguing feature of diffusion models (DMs) is their high degree of controllability. A
102   pre-trained DM can be repurposed for a variety of tasks, such as solving inverse problems (Chung
103   et al., 2023), image editing (Meng et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2022), adversarial purification (Nie et al.,
104   2022), and more, by reconstructing the inference path and applying appropriate guidance. Since DMs
105   function as score estimators, they implicitly serve as priors for the data distribution (Song & Ermon,
106   2019), ensuring that generated or modified samples remain close to the high-density regions of the
107   distribution. However, DMs typically generate fixed-length vectors, which limits their suitability for
      tasks involving long sequence generation or planning.

      Combining the advantages of ARMs and DMs, researchers have investigated ways to combine the
109   two to by using ARM as the global model and DM as the local vision generator. This led to the
110   construction of multimodal generators (Ge et al., 2024a; Li et al., 2024), robot planners (Du et al.,
111   2023), and world models (Xiang et al., 2024). However, all these models require re-purposing of the
112   base models, and require expensive modality-specific fine-tuning or instruction tuning. Moreover, to
113   the best of our knowledge, using DMs as a way to avoid exponential error accumulation of ARMs
114   has not been investigated in the literature.
115   To fill in this gap, in this work, leveraging the intriguing strengths of both pre-trained ARMs
116   and DMs, we present Autoregressive coherent multimodal generation with Diffusion Correction
117   (ACDC), a flexible zero-shot combination method of existing models applicable at inference stage,
118   regardless of the specific design of the ARMs and DMs. In ACDC, the ARM is the main workhorse,
119   responsible for generating multimodal tokens that respect the global context. For every chunk of
120   vision tokens generated (e.g. a single image, 16 video frames), the tokens are decoded into the
121   continuous space with the visual detokenizer, and locally corrected with a memory-conditioned DM
122   through the SDEdit (Meng et al., 2021) process. Specifically, to distill the global context of the
123
      generated sequence into the DM without any fine-tuning, we additionally propose a memory module
      implemented with a large language model (LLM), which causally corrects the conditioning texts of
124
      the DM.
125
126   Through extensive experiments with various multimodal ARMs including Large World Models (Liu
127   et al., 2024), Unified-IO-2 (Lu et al., 2024), and Show-o (Xie et al., 2024) on two distinct tasks:
128   coherent multi-frame story generation and autoregressive video generation, we show that ACDC
129
      significantly improves the quality of the generated results and can correct for the exponential accumu-
      lation in errors (See Fig. 1 for representative results), achieving all this while being training-free and
130
      agnostic to the model class.

135   2.1   U NIFYING AUTOREGRESSIVE MODELS WITH DIFFUSION MODELS
136
137
      Diffusion models as vision decoder for autoregressive models Instead of the standard way of
      using VQGAN decoder as the visual detokenizer, works such as SEED-X (Ge et al., 2024a) and
138
      Mini-Gemini (Li et al., 2024) propose to leverage Stable Diffusion XL (SDXL) (Podell et al., 2023)
139
      as the visual decoder by fine-tuning a pre-trained SDXL to take in the visual tokens as the condition
140   through attention. While the image generation quality of these methods exceed the ones that only use
141   ARMs since the pre-trained diffusion model guarantees high-quality outputs, consistency is hard to
142   control. Pandora (Xiang et al., 2024) is a work that shares a goal that is similar to ours, which uses
143   two Q-formers (Li et al., 2023a) as adapters to connect ARMs and DMs, repurposing them as a world
144   model.
145
146   Proposals in unified architecture For methods that use DMs as vision decoders, the ARM
147   component is still responsible for generating the multimodal tokens, and hence the methods can be
148   considered as late-fusion methods. Recently, several methods have been proposed to combine the two
149   model classes in the earlier pre-training stage in an early-fusion manner. Transfusion (Zhou et al.,
150
      2024) trains the LMM with the next token prediction loss for language and with the diffusion (i.e.
      denoising) loss for image patches. The sampling modes are switched between next token prediction
151
      and continuous diffusion when the special token is sampled. Show-o (Xie et al., 2024) is similar to
152
      Transfusion in that it uses both next token prediction and diffusion, but differs in that the discrete
153   tokens are generated through discrete diffusion (Austin et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2023a). Diffusion
154   forcing (Chen et al., 2024a) generalizes ARMs so that it can take perform noisy autoregression, as
155   well as iteratively refine the current token by denoising it.
156
      While promising, all of the methods discussed require altering the architecture of the DM1 or the
157
      ARM, as well as extensive fine-tuning and aligning. Our goal, on the other hand, is to leverage the
158
      pre-trained model components as is, without any fine-tuning. For example, later we show that Show-o
159   also benefits from the use of the proposed ACDC.

184   2.2       D IFFUSION MODELS FOR IMAGE / VIDEO SEQUENCE GENERATION
185
186   Diffusion models as world models Recently, several works aimed to repurpose diffusion models
187   as world models (Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018), which estimate the next state (i.e. image or video) given
188   the current state and action. GenHowto (Damen et al., 2024) fine-tuned a DM to predict the next
189   image frame on the previous frame and a text instruction on a targetted dataset (Souček et al., 2022).
190   SEED-story (Yang et al., 2024) achieves a similar goal on another targetted story dataset but on a
191   longer sequence. Genie (Bruce et al., 2024) and GameNGen (Valevski et al., 2024) take latent actions
192
      (e.g. control signal) as input to output the game frame. Going further, world models that can generate
      chunks of video frames (Xing et al., 2024; Xiang et al., 2024) were also proposed. While promising
193
      as world models, these models do not possess the understanding and planning abilities of LMM, and
194
      again, deviate from a general-purpose DM.
195
196
197
      Diffusion models as long sequence generators Standard video diffusion models (Ho et al., 2022;
      Blattmann et al., 2023; Brooks et al., 2024) generate data of fixed length. One can repurpose
198
      video DMs for arbitrary-length video generation by using diagonal denoising proposed in FIFO-
199
      diffusion (Kim et al., 2024), or train a video DM from scratch using a similar strategy (Ruhe et al.,
200   2024). Unfortunately, even the models that are repurposed for long sequence generation can only
201   attend to a local context, hampering the consistency of the generated videos.

204   3         AUTOREGRESSIVE COHERENT MODELING WITH DIFFUSION CORRECTION
205
206   In this section, we explore the use of Multimodal ARMs as world models used in the task of coherent
207   story and video generation, where the key lies in the fidelity of the model’s predictions, as well as
208   controlling the exponential accumulation of errors. Specifically, we assume a Partially Observable
209   Markov Decision Process (POMDP) setup (Sutton, 2018) where the actions are described by texts
210   and the states are rendered as image frames.
211
212   Autoregressive multimodal modeling for world models For predictive planning (Chang et al.,
213   2020; Zhao et al., 2022; Damen et al., 2024), given the initial state and the goal, both a policy and a
214   world model is required. The policy is responsible for predicting the next action from the current
215   state. The world model, on the other hand, should be capable of modeling the next state from the
      current state and action pair.

238   Diffusion correction The decoded i-th image xi0 often contains visual artifacts. The problem is
239   less significant in the initial frame but exacerbates for later predicted frames. To correct the visual
240   artifacts, our goal is to (1) bring xi0 closer to the clean data manifold, yet (2) proximal to the starting
241   point. This can be effectively implemented with a text-conditional SDEdit (Meng et al., 2021), where
242   one can use any text-to-image (T2I) DM, such as stable diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) that operates
243
      in the latent space, or DeepFloyd IF (Team, 2023) that operates in the pixel space. As illustrated in
      Fig. 2, the process reads

      Incorporating physical constraints When generating images with multimodal ARMs, the degra-
280   dation in the quality of images is not the only issue. Specifically, we observe that the ARMs are
281   less sensitive to the physical feasibility of the rendered image. For instance, we often observe cases
282   where rabbits have three ears, or where there is more than one moon or sun in the background,
283   as can be seen in Fig. 5. Fortunately, incorporating physical constraints to diffusion models has
284   been widely explored in the context of inverse problems (Chung et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2023) and
285   conditional diffusion models (Zhang et al., 2023). Thanks to the versatility of diffusion inference,
286   one can additionally incorporate user feedback (e.g. inpainting masks, depth constraints) to explicitly
287   correct for the errors, or automatically project the image to the manifold of feasible data (Gillman
288
      et al., 2024).

309   Unlike standard image diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022), which are limited in their ability to

311   temporal coherence throughout the video. When utilizing diffusion correction, selecting the time step

356   Benchmark dataset generation The goal of this section is to study whether ACDC can truly
357   reduce error accumulation and improve the coherent story generation quality. As the base ARM, we
358   choose UIO-2XXL (Lu et al., 2024) and Show-o (Xie et al., 2024), as they are two representative
359   open-sourced models. As the base DM, we choose Stable Diffusion v1.5 (Rombach et al., 2022). We
360   note that other choices such as DeepFloyd IF for DM can also be utilized, without any modifications to
361   the algorithm. To test our hypothesis, it is crucial to select the set of examples that are approximately
362
      in-distributed to both the base ARM and DM. We found that existing open-sourced benchmarks such
      as ChangeIT (Souček et al., 2022) does not meet this criterion, and hence, we decided to generate 1k
363
      stories with an LLM. Following the practices in Alpaca (Taori et al., 2023) and self-instruct (Wang
364
      et al., 2022), we manually construct 10 examples of the stories and randomly select 3 examples

      5    C ONCLUSION
510
511
      We presented Autoregressive Coherent multimodal generation with Diffusion Correction (ACDC), a
      flexible, zero-shot approach that leverages the complementary strengths of autoregressive models
512
      (ARMs) and diffusion models (DMs) for multimodal generation tasks. By employing DMs as local
513
      correctors through SDEdit and incorporating a memory module with large language models (LLMs),
514   ACDC addresses the exponential error accumulation inherent in ARMs, resulting in consistent and
515   high-quality outputs across a range of tasks. Our experiments on coherent story generation and
516   autoregressive video generation validate that ACDC effectively integrates pre-trained ARMs and
517   DMs without requiring additional fine-tuning or architectural modifications, thus establishing a robust
518   and scalable framework for multimodal generation. Future work could explore further enhancements
519   in the memory module and investigate the application of ACDC to other complex multimodal tasks,
520   pushing the boundaries of generative modeling.
