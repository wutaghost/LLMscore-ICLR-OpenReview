009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               The Tsetlin Machine (TM) is an innovative machine learning algorithm rooted
012            in propositional logic, achieving state-of-the-art performance in various pattern
013            recognition tasks. While previous studies analyzed its convergence properties for
014            the 1-bit and XOR operators, this work extends the analysis to the AND and OR
015            operators, completing the study of fundamental digital operations. Our findings
016            demonstrate that the TM almost surely converges to reproduce the AND and OR
017            operators when trained on noise-free data over an infinite time horizon. Notably,
018            the analysis of the OR operator uncovers a distinct property: the ability of the
019            TM to represent two sub-patterns jointly within a single clause, contrasting with
020
               its behavior in the XOR case. Furthermore, we investigate the TM’s behavior for
               AND/OR/XOR operators with noisy training samples, including mislabeled sam-
021
               ples and irrelevant inputs. With wrong labels, the TM does not converge to the
022
               intended operators but can still learn efficiently. With irrelevant variables, the TM
023            converges to the intended operators almost surely. Together, these analyses pro-
024            vide a comprehensive theoretical foundation for the TM’s convergence properties
025            across basic Boolean operators.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   A Tsetlin Machine (TM) (Granmo, 2018) organizes clauses, each of which is associated with a
032   team of Tsetlin Automata (TAs) (Tsetlin, 1961), to collaboratively capture distinct sub-patterns for
033
      a certain class. A TA, which is the core learning entity of TM, is a kind of learning automata (Zhang
      et al., 2020; Yazidi et al., 2019; Omslandseter et al., 2022) that selects the current action based on
034
      past experiences learned from the environment in order to obtain the maximum reward. In a TM, a
035
      clause is a conjunction of literals, where a literal is a Boolean input variable or its negation. A clause
036   is used to represent a sub-pattern. Once distinct sub-patterns are learned by a number of clauses,
037   the overall pattern recognition task is completed by a voting scheme from the clauses. The TM has
038   several advantages, such as transparent inference and learning (Bhattarai et al., 2024; Abeyrathna
039   et al., 2023; Rafiev et al., 2022), and hardware friendliness (Maheshwari et al., 2023; Rahman et al.,
040   2022; Morris et al., 2022).
041
      The TM, together with its variations (Granmo et al., 2019; Abeyrathna et al., 2021; Dar-
042   shana Abeyrathna et al., 2020; Sharma et al., 2023), has been employed in many applications, such
043   as word sense disambiguation (Yadav et al., 2021c), aspect-based sentiment analysis (Yadav et al.,
044   2021b), novelty detection (Bhattarai et al., 2021), text classification (Yadav et al., 2021a) with en-
045   hanced interpretability (Yadav et al., 2022), and solving contextual bandit problems (Seraj et al.,
046   2022). These studies indicate that TMs obtain better or competitive performance compared with
047   most of the state-of-the-art techniques. At the same time, the transparency of learning is maintained
048   with smaller memory footprint and higher computational efficiency.
049   The TM convergence properties of the 1-bit operator and XOR operator were analyzed in (Zhang
050   et al., 2022) and (Jiao et al., 2022), respectively. In (Zhang et al., 2022), TM’s almost surely con-
051   vergence to the identity/NOT operator with 1-bit input was confirmed, revealing the role of the
052   hyperparameter s. In (Jiao et al., 2022), TM’s convergence to the XOR operator with 2-bit input
053   was proven, highlighting the functionality of the hyperparameter T . In this paper, we first focus on
      analyzing the AND and OR operators in the noise-free training samples, followed by an examina-

054
      tion of the convergence properties of AND, OR, and XOR with noisy training samples, including
055   the presence of wrong labels and irrelevant input variables.
056
057   This paper differs from prior work in several key aspects. While (Zhang et al., 2022) used stationary
058
      distribution analysis of discrete-time Markov chains (DTMC), the current study focuses on absorb-
      ing states. For XOR (Jiao et al., 2022), where sub-patterns are bit-wise exclusive, TM learns and
059
      converges to sub-patterns individually. In contrast, the OR operator’s sub-patterns share features
060
      (e.g., [x1 = 1, x2 = 1] and [x1 = 1, x2 = 0] share x1 = 1), allowing joint representation. We show
061   that TM can effectively learn and represent these shared features, making the convergence process
062   distinct. Additionally, this paper examines the role of Type II feedback, omitted in the prior work,
063   and analyzes convergence property under noise.
064
      It is worth noting that learning 2-bit operators, both with and without noise, is a well-solved prob-
065
      lem with transparent solutions. Since the 1980s, numerous studies in concept learning and probably
066
      approximately correct (PAC) learning have extensively explored this topic. For instance, it has
067   been shown in Valiant (1984); Haussler et al. (1994) that 2-DNF formulas are both properly and
068   efficiently PAC learnable, with sample complexity scaling logarithmically in the input dimension.
069   More generally, transparent algorithms for learning k-DNF formulas have been proposed in Marc-
070   hand & Shawe-Taylor (2002), and the problem of learning conjunctions under noise has been studied
071   in Mansour & Parnas (1998). While many elegant methods exist for learning conjunctions or dis-
072   junctions, their existence does not necessarily imply that the TM converges to such operators in
073   the same manner. TM employs a unique approach, learning from samples to construct conjunctive
074   expressions and coordinating these expressions across various sub-patterns, which merits its own
075
      dedicated investigation.

      3   C ONVERGENCE A NALYSIS OF THE AND O PERATOR
172
173
      A TM has converged when the transitions among the states of its TAs do not happen any
174
      longer. We assume that the training samples are noise free, i.e., P (y = 1|x1 = 1, x2 = 1) =
175   1, P (y = 0|x1 = 0, x2 = 1) = 1, P (y = 0|x1 = 1, x2 = 0) = 1, P (y = 0|x1 = 0, x2 = 0) = 1.
176   We also assume the training samples are independently drawn at random, and the above four cases
177   will appear with non-zero probability, which means that all of the four types of samples will appear
178   for infinite number of times given infinite time horizon.
179
      Because the considered AND operator has only one pattern of input, i.e., x1 = 1, x2 = 1, that will
180
      trigger a true output, we employ one clause in this TM, and we thus can ignore the indices of the
181
      classes and the clauses in our notation in the proof. After simplification, TAi,j
                                                                                    k becomes TAk , and
182
      C11 becomes C. Since there are two input variables, namely x1 and x2 , we implement four TAs in
183   the clause, i.e., TA1 , TA2 , TA3 , and TA4 . TA1 has two actions, i.e., including or excluding x1 .
184   Similarly, TA2 corresponds to including or excluding ¬x1 . TA3 and TA4 determine the behavior of
185   x2 and ¬x2 , respectively. Once the TM can converge correctly to the intended operation, the actions
186   of TA1 , TA2 , TA3 , and TA4 should be I, E, I, and E. Here we use “I” and “E” as abbreviations for
187   include and exclude respectively.
188   Theorem 1. Any clause will converge almost surely to x1 ∧x2 given noise free AND training samples
189   in infinite time when u1 > 0 and u2 > 0.
190
191   Due to page limit, the complete proof of Theorem 1 can be found in Appendix B. We here outline
192   the concept and the main steps of the proof.
193   The condition u1 > 0 and u2 > 0 guarantees that all types of samples are always given and no
194   specific type is blocked by Eqs. (3) and (4) during training. The goal of the proof is to show that the
195   system transitions will guarantee that there is a unique absorbing state of the TM and the absorbing
196   state has the actions of TA1 , TA2 , TA3 , and TA4 to be I, E, I, E, respectively, corresponding to the
197   propositional expression x1 ∧ x2 .
198   To simplify the complex analysis of joint TA transitions, we use quasi-stationary analysis by freezing
199   the transitions of the TAs for the first input bit and focusing on the transitions of the second input
200   bit. Clearly, there are four possibilities for the first bit x1 . We name them as cases, as: Case 1:
201   TA1 = E, TA2 = I, i.e., include ¬x1 . Case 2: TA1 = I, TA2 = E, i.e., include x1 . Case 3:
202   TA1 = E, TA2 = E, i.e., exclude both x1 and ¬x1 . Case 4: TA1 = I, TA2 = I, i.e., include both
203   x1 and ¬x1 .
204   In each of the above four cases, we analyze individually the transition of TA3 with a given current
205   action, for different actions of TA4 , and vice versa. We index the possibilities as situations: Situ-
206   ation 1. We study the transition of TA3 when it has “Include” as its current action, given different
207   actions of TA4 (i.e., when the action of TA4 is frozen as “Include” or “Exclude”). Situation 2. We
208   study the transition of TA3 when it has “Exclude” as its current action, given different actions of
209   TA4 (i.e., when the action of TA4 is frozen as “Include” or “Exclude”). Situation 3. We study the
210   transition of TA4 when it has “Include” as its current action, given different actions of TA3 (i.e.,
211   when the action of TA3 is frozen as “Include” or “Exclude”). Situation 4. We study the transition
212
      of TA4 when it has “Exclude” as its current action, given different actions of TA3 (i.e., when the
      action of TA3 is frozen as “Include” or “Exclude”).
213

215   of the input variables of x1 and x2 , and the two possible TA actions, Include and Exclude. As an

337   Understandably, because of the newly added sub-patterns, the absorbing states in Eqs. (6) and (8)
338   disappear and no new absorbing states are generated. In other words, the TM trained based on
339   samples from Eq. (9) becomes recurrent.
340   Following the same concept, we can show that the system becomes recurrent for Eqs. (5), (10), and
341   (11) as well. For the sake of conciseness, we will not provide the details here. In general, any newly
342   added sub-pattern will involve a probability for the learnt sub-pattern to move outside the learnt
343   state, making the system recurrent.                                                                 ■
344   Lemma 2 tells us that if we always give TM the training samples from all sub-patterns without
345   blocking the learnt patterns by using T via Eqs. (3) and (4), the system is recurrent. In other words,
346   if we want to have the TM converge to the OR operator in an absorbing state, it is critical to utilize
347   the feature of T to block any incoming training samples from updating the learnt sub-patterns.
348   Specifically, we need to configure T (1) so that the absorbing states exist and (2) confirm that the
349   absorbing states follows the OR operator. In what follows, we will, through Lemmas 3-5, show how
350   T via Eqs. (3) and (4) can guarantee the convergence and how the value of T should be configured.
351   Let’s revisit the functionality of T . T can block the training samples from updating a learnt sub-
352   pattern (clauses that have converged to one of the absorbing states) so that the clauses that have not
353   converged can be guided to learn the other unlearned sub-patterns. More specifically, if the vote sum
354   of the clauses reaches T for a certain sub-pattern, the new training samples of this sub-pattern will
355   be blocked by the TM. There are three sub-patterns in OR operator. When the sum of clauses for
356   each of the three sub-patterns reaches T , all training samples for Type I feedback are blocked. At
357   the same time, if all samples for Type II feedback will not trigger any update to the states of TAs,
358
      the TM is absorbed. In Lemma 3, we detail the necessity and sufficiency of the absorbing state.
359
      Lemma 3. The system is absorbed if and only if (1) the vote sum of any sample from intended
360   sub-patterns reaches T , i.e., fΣ (Ci (X)) = T , ∀X = [x1 = 0, x2 = 1] or [x1 = 0, x2 = 1] or
361   [x1 = 0, x2 = 1], and (2) no clause is formed only by a negated literal or negated literals.
362
363
      Proof of Lemma 3: In Lemma 2, the TM is recurrent if the functionality of T is disabled (i.e.,
364
      u1 > 0, u2 > 0). Therefore, for the OR operator to converge, the functionality of T is critical to
365   block any feedback in order to form an absorbing state.
366
367   By design, TM will either be updated via Type I feedback or Type II feedback. We show via (1)
368
      the condition when Type I feedback is blocked and then show via (2) when any update from Type II
      feedback is not given. When both types of feedback are blocked, the system will not be updated
369
      anymore and thus absorbed.
370
371   To prove (1) in Lemma 3, we show that the system is not absorbed when 0 or 1 intended sub-pattern
372   is blocked by T . When 2 intended sub-patterns are blocked, the system will guide the clauses to
373   learning the remaining intended sub-pattern. Only when all 3 intended sub-patterns are blocked by
374
      T , the system will stop updating based on Type I feedback.
375   Clearly, when no intended sub-pattern is blocked by T , the training samples given to the system
376   follow Eq. (5). Following this type of training samples, it has already been shown in Lemma 2 that
377   the TM is recurrent. When only 1 intended sub-pattern is blocked by T , the system is updated based
      on Eqs. (9), (10), or (11), which is also recurrent.

379   In other words, the vote sum for any two intended sub-patterns reaches at least T , and the sum for

      with the action “excluding x2 ” will be penalized. In other words, the actions of the TAs for x1 and

403   update the system any longer. Following the same concept, for C = ¬x2 , the Type II feedback will
404   encourage the excluded x1 to be included so that the clause becomes C = x1 ∧ ¬x2 . The same
405   applies to C = ¬x1 , which will eventually become C = ¬x1 ∧ x2 upon Type II feedback. When all
406   clauses in C = ¬x2 or C = ¬x1 are also updated to C = x1 ∧ ¬x2 or C = ¬x1 ∧ x2 , no Type II

      have 2T ≤ m, giving T ≤ ⌊m/2⌋ for any integer. Note that the fact that two sub-patterns can be

      cannot obtain a sum of T clauses. For this reason, the maximum integer value is T = ⌊m/2⌋.      ■

      6    C ONVERGENCE A NALYSIS UNDER R ANDOM N OISE
494
      We studied the convergence properties of AND, OR, and XOR operators under training samples
495
      with noise following the noise type named noisy completely at random Frénay & Verleysen (2013),
496
      categorized as wrong labels (in Appendix D) and irrelevant input variables (in Appendix E). A
497   wrong label refers to an input that should be labeled as 1 but is instead labeled as 0, or vice versa.
498   An irrelevant input variable, on the other hand, is one that does not contribute to the classification.
499   We demonstrate that, with wrong labels, the TM does not converge to the intended operators but
500   can still learn efficiently. With irrelevant variables, the TM converges to the intended operators
501   almost surely. Experimental results confirmed these findings (Appendix G). We summarize the main
502   findings below. The proof and the experiment results can be found in the corresponding appendices.
503   Theorem 3. The TM is recurrent given training samples with wrong labels for the AND, OR, and
504   XOR operators.
505
506   Remark 3. The recurrent property of TM indicates that there is a non-zero probability that it cannot
507   learn the intended operator. The primary reason for the recurrent behavior when wrong labels are
508
      present is the statistically conflicting labels for the same input samples. These inconsistency causes
      the TAs within a clause to learn conflicting outcomes for the same input. When a clause learns to
509
      evaluate an input as 1 based on Type I feedback, samples with a label of 0 for the same input prompt
510
      it to learn the input as 0 during Type II feedback. This conflict in labels confuses the TM, leading to
511   back-and-forth learning.
512
      Remark 4. Note that although wrong labels will make the TM not converge (not absorbing with
513
      100% accuracy for the intended logic), via experiments, we can still find that the TM are able to
514
      learn the operators efficiently, as shown in Appendix G. This property aligns with the concept of
515   PAC learnable Mansour & Parnas (1998) or ϵ-optimality Zhang et al. (2020), although a formal
516   proof remains an open question.
517
      Theorem 4. The clauses in a TM can almost surely learn the 2-bit AND logic given training samples
518
      with k irrelevant input variables in infinite time, 0 < k < ∞, when T ≤ m.
519
520   Theorem 5. The clauses in a TM can almost surely learn the 2-bit XOR and OR logic given training
521
      samples with k irrelevant input variables in infinite time, 0 < k < ∞, when T ≤ ⌊m/2⌋.
522
      When the number of irrelevant variables is large, the training set may not cover all possible examples
523   due to the required exponential space. Although not yet theoretically proven, polynomial space for
524   training samples seems feasible for TM, which has been observed by experiments (Appendix G.3).
525   This is because the TM can independently update the actions of a TA within a clause, as long as the
526   clause value and the literal value are determined by the training sample. In other words, once the
527   clause value and the literal value are known, the transitions triggered by Type I and Type II feedback
528   are fully determined. As a result, the TM does not need to observe all possible combinations of
529   irrelevant input variables to learn effectively. Instead, as long as the statistical irrelevance of certain
530   variables is demonstrated in the training samples, the corresponding TA transitions will be triggered
531
      accordingly. This enables the TM to learn without requiring exhaustive coverage of the input space.
532
533   7    C ONCLUSIONS
534
535   In this article, we prove the convergence of the TM for the AND and OR operators with noise free
536   training samples. Our proof for the OR operator highlights the TM’s ability to learn joint sub-
537   patterns, showcasing the efficiency of its learning process. Additionally, we analyze the behavior of
538   the TM for the AND, OR, XOR operators in the presence of random noise within the training data.
539   Combined with the convergence proofs in (Zhang et al., 2022) and (Jiao et al., 2022), this work
      concludes the analysis of TM convergence for fundamental digital operators.

655
      The input of a TM is denoted as X = [x1 , x2 , . . . , xo ], where xk ∈ {0, 1}, k = 1, 2, . . . , o, and o is
656   the number of features. A literal is either the xk in the original form or its negation ¬xk . A clause
657   is a conjunction of literals, and each literal is associated with a TA. The TA is a 2-action learning
658   automaton whose job is to decide whether to Include/Exclude its literal in/from the clause, and the
659   decision is determined by the current state of the TA.
660

661

662   between 0 to N − 1, the action “Include” is selected. The action becomes “Exclude” when the TA
663   is in any state between N to 2N − 1. The transitions among the states are triggered by a reward or
664   a penalty that the TA receives from the environment, which, in this case, is determined by different
665   types of feedback defined in the TM (to be explained later).
666
      A clause is associated with 2o TAs, forming a TA team. A TA team is denoted in general as Gji =
667

      Note that for the voting architecture, the TM can assign a polarity to each TA team (Granmo, 2018).
      Specifically, TA teams with odd indices have positive polarity, learning from training samples with
761
      label 1, while those with even indices have negative polarity, learning from training samples with

      label 0. The only difference between these polarities is that the output of a clause associated with an

764   clause outputs, and the threshold T h is set to zero. For example, for the XOR operator with four
765   clauses, the learned clauses with positive polarity can be C1 = x1 ∧ ¬x2 and C3 = ¬x1 ∧ x2 , while
766   the ones with negative polarity can be C2 = x1 ∧ x2 and C4 = ¬x1 ∧ ¬x2 . In this case, when the
767   testing sample [x1 = 1, x2 = 0] arrives, the sum of the clause values is 1. On the contrary, when
768   the testing sample [x1 = 0, x2 = 0] arrives, the sum of the clause values is −1. In this way, with
769   T h = 0, the system’s decision range and tolerance is expected to be larger.
770   In this study, we consider only positive polarity clauses. The reason is two-folds: firstly, in the
771   AND/OR case, once the TM has learned out the pattern that outputs 1, it also has learned the pattern
772   that outputs 0, as they are complementary. Therefore, the learning/reasoning process of TM can be
773   explained from the perspective of learning the pattern that outputs 1. Secondly, for the sake of easy
774   analysis and better understanding.

1930         VARIABLES
1931
       In the previous subsections, we demonstrated that if a single irrelevant bit is present in the training
1932
       samples, the system will almost surely converge to the intended operators. This conclusion can
1933
       be readily extended to scenarios involving multiple irrelevant variables. Here, “multiple irrelevant
1934   variables” refers to the presence of additional variables, beyond x3 , in the training samples that do
1935   not contribute to the classification.
1936
       Theorem 10. The clauses in a TM can almost surely learn the 2-bit AND logic given training
1937   samples with k irrelevant input variables in infinite time, 0 < k < ∞, when T ≤ m.
1938
       Theorem 11. The clauses in a TM can almost surely learn the 2-bit XOR and OR logic given
1939
       training samples with k irrelevant input variables in infinite time, 0 < k < ∞, when T ≤ ⌊m/2⌋.
1940
1941   Proof: The proofs of Theorems 10 and 11 are straightforward. We must verify whether the condi-
1942   tions for blocking Type I feedback and resulting no transitions upon Type II feedback remain valid
1943   when multiple irrelevant variables are present. In addition, we show that the system is still recurrent
       when those conditions do not fulfill.


                                                          36
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1944
       The condition for blocking Type I feedback remains valid because Type I feedback is only deter-
1945   mined by the first two input bits and is not a function of the irrelevant variables. For Type II feedback,
1946   its effect depends on whether the literals for the irrelevant input variables are present in the clause.
1947   In cases where the literals of the irrelevant bits are not included in the clause, the analysis holds, as
1948   those literals are absent. When the literals of the irrelevant bits are included, their number does not
1949   impact the analysis. This is because the clause value is entirely determined by the first two bits, and
1950   the clause value remains C = 0, regardless of the number of irrelevant variables.
1951   The system is recurrent when these conditions for absorbing do not fulfill. We have shown that the
1952   system is recurrent when one irrelevant label x3 exists, and the same analysis applies for the transi-
1953   tions with more irrelevant variables. In addition, any extra irrelevant variable does not eliminate any
1954   transitions in the original system before it is added. Therefore, the system is still recurrent before the
1955   absorbing conditions fulfill. We constrain the number of irrelevant variables to an infinite number to
1956   avoid the analysis on a system with infinite states.                                                    ■
