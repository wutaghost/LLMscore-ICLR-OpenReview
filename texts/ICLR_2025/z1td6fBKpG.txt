                                                 A BSTRACT
008
009
010            The semantic similarity between sample expressions measures the distance be-
011            tween their latent ‘meaning’. Such meanings are themselves typically represented
012
               by textual expressions, often insufficient to differentiate concepts at fine granu-
               larity. We propose a novel approach whereby the semantic similarity among tex-
013
               tual expressions is based not on other expressions they can be rephrased as, but
014
               rather based on the imagery they evoke. While this is not possible with humans,
015            generative models allow us to easily visualize and compare generated images, or
016            their distribution, evoked by a textual prompt. Therefore, we characterize the
017            semantic similarity between two textual expressions simply as the distance be-
018            tween image distributions they induce, or ‘conjure.’ We show that by choosing
019            the Jensen-Shannon divergence between the reverse-time diffusion stochastic dif-
020            ferential equations (SDEs) induced by each textual expression, this can be directly
021            computed via Monte-Carlo sampling. Our method contributes a novel perspective
022            on semantic similarity that not only aligns with human-annotated scores, but also
023
               opens up new avenues for the evaluation of text-conditioned generative models
               while offering better interpretability of their learnt representations.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Semantic similarity is about comparing data not directly, but based on their underlying ‘concepts’
030   or ‘meanings’. Since meanings are most commonly expressed through natural language, various
031   methods have attempted to compute them in this space. Words have been often compared based
032   on the occurrences of other words that surround them, and images have likewise been compared
033   ‘semantically’ by using the text captions that describe them.
034
      While measuring semantic similarity comes natural to humans who share significant knowledge and
035   experience, defining semantic similarity for trained models is non-trivial since text can often be
036   ambiguous or open to multiple (subjective) interpretations. On the other hand, visual elements often
037   transcend communication barriers and allow for comparison at finer granularity. Comparing images
038   is also relatively simple – unlike words, pixel values do not depend on distant knowledge or context.
039
      Hence, instead of comparing images by the captions which describe them, we propose the converse:
040
      comparing textual expressions in terms of the images they conjure. In other words, we propose
041   an expanded notion of meaning that is purely “visually-grounded”. This would be hard if not im-
042   possible for humans, since the process requires visualizing and comparing ‘mental images’ each
043   individual can conceive, but it is straightforward for trained models.
044
      We propose to leverage modern image generative models, in particular the class of text-conditioned
045
      diffusion models, for this purpose. By doing so, the semantic similarity between two text passages
046
      can simply be measured by the similarity of image distributions generated by a model conditioned
047   on those passages.
048
049   There is a technical nugget that needs to be developed for the method to be viable, which is how
050
      to compare diffusions in the space of images. To address this, we propose to leverage the Jensen
      Shannon Divergence between the stochastic differential equations (SDEs) that govern the flow of
051
      the diffusion model, which we will show to be computable using a Monte-Carlo sampling approach.
052
053   We will show that our simple choice of distance already leads to results comparable to zero-shot
      approaches based on large language models. To validate our proposed definitions, we further run ab-

093   To summarize the contributions of this work, we propose an approach for evaluating semantic sim-
094   ilarity between text expressions that is grounded in the space of visual images. Our method has a
095   unique advantage over traditional language-based methods that, in addition to providing a numerical
096
      score, it also provides a visual expression, or ‘explanation’, for comparison, enabling better inter-
      pretability of the learnt representations (Figure 1). Additionally, our method is the first to enable
097
      quantifying the alignment of semantic representations learnt by diffusion models compared to that
098
      of humans, which can open up new avenues for the evaluation text-conditioned diffusion models.
099   Finally, we note that our approach can be used to derive many possible variants based on the metric
100   used to compare images, which we leave for future exploration.

104
105   Text-Conditioned Image Generative Models. We begin by briefly surveying the literature on
106   text-conditioned image generation. Goodfellow et al. (2020) proposed the Generative Adversarial
107   Network (GAN), a deep learning-based approach for image generation. Mirza & Osindero (2014)
      proposed a method for conditioning GANs based on specified labels. Works such as VQ-VAE

      (Van Den Oord et al., 2017) and VQ-VAE-2 (Razavi et al., 2019) also built upon the foundational
109   works of Variational Auto Encoders (VAEs) (Kingma, 2013) to learn discrete representations used
110   to generate high quality images, among other outputs, when paired with an autoregressive prior.
111   DALL-E (Ramesh et al., 2021) has also been developed as an effective text-to-image generator
112   leveraging autoregressive Transformers (Vaswani, 2017).
113
      Our work focuses on diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015), which have achieved state-
114
      of-the-art results among modern image generation models. These models can be viewed from the
115   perspective of score-based generative models (Song et al., 2020b), which represent the distribution of
116   data via gradients, and are sampled from using Langevin dynamics (Welling & Teh, 2011). Diffusion
117   models have been trained by optimizing the variational bound on data likelihood (Ho et al., 2020).
118   Modeled as stochastic differential equations (Song et al., 2020b), the same training objective has also
119   been shown to enable maximum-likelihood training under specific weighing schemes (Song et al.,
120   2021). Diffusion models have also demonstrated strong results on conditional image generation
121   tasks. Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015) and Song et al. (2020b) showed that gradients of a classifier can
122   be used to condition a pre-trained diffusion model. Dhariwal & Nichol (2021) introduced Classifier-
123
      guidance, which achieved state-of-the-art results in image synthesis at the time it was released.
      Song et al. (2020a) introduced Denoising Diffusion Implicit Models (DDIM), greatly accelerating
124
      the process of sampling from trained diffusion models as compared to DDPM (Ho et al., 2020).
125
      Karras et al. (2022) presented a unified view of existing diffusion models from a practical standpoint,
126   enabling develop improved sampling and training techniques to obtain greatly improved results.
127
128
129   Semantic Space of Generative Models. The Distributional Hypothesis (Harris, 1954) forms the
130   basis for statistical semantics, characterizing the meaning of linguistic items based on their usage
131   distributions. This is also closely related to Wittgenstein’s use theory of meaning (Wittgenstein,
132   1953), often popularized as “meaning is use”. Many methods have been developed in machine learn-
133   ing and Natural Language Processing (NLP) literature to compute these semantic spaces, including
134   Word2Vec (Mikolov et al., 2013). In light of modern Large Language Models (LLMs), Liu et al.
135
      (2023) defined the space of meanings for autoregressive models to be the distribution over model
      continuations for any input sequence. This has been used to define notions of semantic distances and
136
      semantic containment between textual inputs. Achille et al. (2024) further defined conceptual simi-
137
      larity between images by projecting them into the space of distributions over complexity-constrained
138   captions, producing similarity scores that strongly correlate with human annotations. Soatto et al.
139   (2023) generalizes these definitions by considering meanings as equivalence classes, where parti-
140   tions can be induced by either an external agent or the model itself. Vector-based representations
141   such as those obtained from CLIP Radford et al. (2021), or in general any sentence embedding model
142   (Devlin et al., 2018; Opitz & Frank, 2022) have also been defined specifically for the computation
143   of semantic distances. Such representations, however, are often difficult to interpret.
144   In contrast to these works, Bender & Koller (2020) argues that training on language alone is insuf-
145   ficient to capture semantics, which they argue requires a notion of “communicative intents” that are
146   external to language. In this paper, we explore a notion of meaning that is grounded in the distribu-
147   tion of evoked imageries, and present a simple algorithm to compute interpretable distances in this
148   space for the class of text-conditioned diffusion models.
149
150

152   models are, among many others, the widely-used FID (Heusel et al., 2017) score, Kernel Inception
153
      Distance (Bińkowski et al., 2018), and the CLIP score (Hessel et al., 2021). Despite these choices,
      Stein et al. (2024) recently discovered that no existing metric used to evaluate diffusion models
154
      correlates strongly with human evaluations. While existing evaluations often focus on the quality
155
      and diversity of generations, our method is the first to evaluate semantic alignment of the repre-
156   sentations learnt by diffusion models. Several techniques have also been developed to interpret the
157   generation of diffusion models. Kwon et al. (2022); Park et al. (2023) edit the bottleneck representa-
158   tions within the U-Net architecture of diffusion models for semantic image manipulation. Gandikota
159   et al. (2023) identifies low-rank directions corresponding to various semantic concepts, and Li et al.
160   (2023) shows that diffusion models can be used for image classification. Kong et al. (2023a;b)
161   frame diffusion models using information theory, improving interpretability of their learnt semantic
      relations. Orthogonal to these works, our method enables visualizing the semantic relations be-

184
185   Our derivations will leverage Song et al. (2020b)’s the SDE formulation of diffusion models when
186   viewed from the lens of score-based generaive modeling. In particular, a (forward) diffusion process
187   {x(t)}Tt=0 can be modeled as the solution to the following SDE:
188
189

190   with drift coefficient f : Rd × R 7→ Rd and (scalar) diffusion coefficient g : R 7→ R, where wt is
191   standard Brownian motion. We constrain the timesteps t such that t ∈ [0, T ], where x(0) represents
192   the distribution of “fully-denoised” images generated by the diffusion model. We also assume that
193   by construction, the prior at time T is known and distributed according to x(T ) ∼ π, where often
194   π = N (0, I).
195

196   θ and conditioned on a textual prompt y ∈ Y that is used to approximate the score function (Song

198   conditional model sθ (x, t|y) defines a reverse-time SDE given by:

      4.3   E MPIRICAL VALIDATION
300
301   While our work defines a notion of semantic distance grounded in evoked imagery, the validity of
302   this definition hinges on its use as a measure of similarity that aligns with humans’. In particular,
303   we should expect our definition to produce measurements of similarity that agree often with human
304   annotators (which can be viewed as “ground-truth”).
305
      To quantify this, we use the Semantic Textual Similarity (STS) (Agirre et al., 2012; 2013; 2014;
306   2015; 2016; Cer et al., 2017) and Sentences Involving Compositional Knowledge (SICK-R) (Marelli
307   et al., 2014) datasets, containing pairs of sentences each labelled by human annotators with a se-
308   mantic similarity score ranging from 0-5. We then use our method to compute the image-grounded
309   similarity score, and measure their resulting Spearman Correlation with the annotations.
310
      Interestingly, our experiments in Table 1 show that our visually-grounded similarity scores exhibit
311
      significant degrees of correlation with that annotated by humans. While, expectedly, our method
312   presently lags behind embedding models trained specifically for semantic comparison tasks, we
313   show that our visually-grounded similarity scores can rival that produced by existing large language
314   models up to 33B in size. Since our work is the first to formalize and evaluate semantic alignment for
315   this class of conditioned diffusion models, we have also included results from baselines with which
316   our approach is directly comparable against. Our method convincingly outperforms all baseline
317   methods for evaluating semantic similarity in diffusion models. As a remark, we note the large
318   standard deviations in these scores across datasets for all methods, suggesting that what matters
319   for validation purposes is that our distances correlate significantly with that of annotators, rather
320
      than obtaining “state-of-the-art” alignment scores since ‘ground truth’ is fundamentally subjective
      in each of these benchmarks.
321
322   We further remark that in most existing text-conditioned diffusion models, the representation struc-
323   tures that can be captured by our method are limited by those learnt by text-encoder models such
      as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), since these encoders are often used to pre-process textual prompts.

      5   D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATIONS
460
      Our method has several limitations. First, imageries might indeed not be sufficient to fully capture
461
      the meaning of certain expressions, such as mathematical abstractions (like ‘imaginary numbers’)
462
      and metaphysical concepts (like ‘conscience’). Furthermore, many modern diffusion models use a
463   pre-trained text-encoder to pre-process textual prompts. This means that representation structures
464   obtained from the diffusion model outputs would be bottle-necked by those learnt by the text en-
465   coder. However, this limitation can be mitigated by the development of better encoders, such as
466   those based on LLMs (BehnamGhader et al., 2024). Additionally, these encoder-based (vector) rep-
467   resentations are often difficult to interpret, while our proposed method offers a way to visualize and
468   interpret the learnt semantic similarities between textual expressions. Lastly, computation costs also
469   remain a key limitation of our method, since it requires several inference passes through the diffu-
470   sion model to compute a single semantic similarity score, mitigated only partially by the conclusions
471
      of our ablation study on required number of iterations.
472   Nevertheless, our method is the first to show that textual representations can be meaningfully com-
473   pared for diffusion models by “grounding” them in the space of conjured images. We introduce
474   the first method for evaluating semantic alignment of text-conditioned diffusion models. In par-
475   ticular, our work enables not only qualifying (via visual ‘explanations’), but also quantifying the
476   alignment of this resulting semantic space with that of human annotators. Our method also enable
477
      fine-grained analysis of the failure modes of existing diffusion models, pinpointing specific areas
      where they align poorly with human annotators. Our general framework of conjuring semantic sim-
478
      ilarity is applicable to the broader class of image generative models, and we leave the exploration of
479
      applications beyond diffusion models to future work.
