009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Improving the alignment of modalities has proven effective across various down-
012            stream tasks in multimodal models. Currently, modality alignment follows two
013            main research directions: aligning all modalities simultaneously or binding the
014            others by aligning to a core modality. The first ensures direct alignment, but it is
015            difficult to extend to new modalities. The second is scalable but weak in emer-
016            gent ability due to needing more direct inter-modality alignment. To address these
017            problems, we propose the TangentBind. Specifically, we first align all modalities
018
               to a core modality, e.g., image or text. Then, we introduce a generative network
               that generates the embeddings of the second modality, e.g., text or image, based
019
               on the core modality embedding. Thirdly, other modalities, such as audio, are
020
               aligned to the core modality and generative embedding, improving emergent abil-
021            ity while retaining alignment with the core modality. During training, in addition
022            to infoNCE, the Tangent Term is introduced to align the new modalities with the
023            generated embeddings. This addresses accuracy issues caused by using generated
024            vectors as representations for modalities. With VISION and TEXT as the core
025            modality, our experiments include other modalities such as AUDIO, DEPTH, and
026            INFRARED. Eventually, our experiments show that the emergent ability of Tan-
027            gentBind significantly outperforms the original benchmark on 9 datasets.

030   1   INTRODUCTION
031
032   Unifying multimodal representation aims to learn a shared semantic representation space across
033
      various modalities, such as audio, RGB images, text, depth images, and heatmaps(Wang et al.,
      2023a; Girdhar et al., 2023; Guzhov et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2021; 2022; Liu et al., 2023). A unified
034
      multimodal space is essential as a critical foundation for multimodal understanding and generation
035
      (Karpathy et al., 2014; Mithun et al., 2018; Lu, 2023). However, the previous multimodal requires
036   all modes to coexist, which is challenging and labor-intensive(Guzhov et al., 2021; Radford et al.,
037   2021; Moon et al., 2022).
038
039
      Due to the limitations of existing datasets, recent approaches only utilize pairs of modalities or a
      few visual modalities. Consequently, the resulting embeddings are confined to the modality pairs
040
      used during training and lack alignment for other modalities (Zhu et al., 2024). Recent works have
041
      introduced more flexible alignment strategies to address this issue.(Gao et al., 2024; Wang et al.,
042   2024a; Zhu et al., 2024; Dhakal et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b; Lyu et al., 2024). Among them,
043   ImageBind was the first work in this direction, introducing a core modality alignment framework to
044   reduce paired data requirements, where only the image as core modality is directly aligned with other
045   modalities. The concept of emergent alignment pertains to the indirect alignment performance, and
046   it also facilitates a degree of alignment between modalities that are not directly trained together.
047   However, when other modalities are indirectly aligned through text, the emergent effect exists only
048   in limited form, which typically results in suboptimal zero-shot performance.
049   To address the weak zero-shot performance issue of ImageBind, LanguageBind (Zhu et al., 2024)
050   selects text as the core modality and employs generative data to enhance text-related zero-shot ca-
051   pabilities. However, LanguageBind struggles with retrieval tasks involving non-text modalities due
052   to the limitations of the binding method. Specifically, text needs more of the fine-grained details
053   presented in images. Using text as the core modality significantly reduces retrieval performance
      across various image-based modalities. Besides, generating large amounts of data requires much

      2.2   C ONTRASTIVE L EARNING
145
146   Contrastive learning has been remarkably successful in learning representations from multimodal
147   data pairs (Logeswaran & Lee, 2018; He et al., 2020). The primary motivation behind these work
148   is maximizing the mutual information between two views (Tian et al., 2020; Bachman et al., 2019;
149   Tamkin et al., 2020). The loss functions, such as NCE (Gutmann & Hyvärinen, 2010), infoNCE
150   (van den Oord et al., 2018) and MIL-NCE (Miech et al., 2020), have been proposed for contrastive
151   learning. However, these loss functions focus on the alignment of two modalities. To extend the
152   model to multiple modalities, (Guzhov et al., 2021; Alayrac et al., 2020) propose a simple summa-
153   tion of loss functions for joint learning across various modalities. According to Wang & Isola (2020),
154
      the loss function of contrastive learning can be split into the alignment and uniformity parts. The
      alignment part is responsible for the alignment when using loss function summation for multimodal
155
      alignment. We analyze the direct summation of two loss functions, and then the two alignment parts
156
      will interfere in appendix A.2.

1052   C.2   M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
1053
1054
       Diffusion Model We deployed two symmetric decode-only diffusion models, one for generating
       text embeddings from image embeddings and another for the reverse. Both models use the same
1055
       transformer architecture with a 1024-dimensional embedding, 8 attention heads of 128 dimensions
1056
       each, and 6 layers and the number of training parameters is about 30M. Training involves 100
1057   timesteps, with a 5% embedding dropout to facilitate classifier-free guidance, optimized using an
1058   AdamW optimizer, a learning rate of 1e-4, and a batch size of 128, following the loss function1 of
1059   our methodology documentation. For the model where the image is the core modality, the image
1060   embedding serves as the input, and the text embedding is the output to generate. Conversely, when
1061   text is the core modality, text embedding is used as input to generate the image embedding. This
1062   approach allows us to efficiently handle and transform data within the latent space, reducing com-
1063   putational demands. This setup not only ensures the capability of model in cross-modal generation
1064   but also enhances its performance in generating high-quality, contextually accurate outputs across
1065
       different modalities.
1066
1067   Multimodal Encoders For image core model and language core model, we use the same structure
1068
       of encoder on the same modality. Following Girdhar et al. (2023) we use 12-layer, 1024-dimensional
       vision transformer with a patch size of 16 and a stride of 10 for the VISION, AUDIO, DEPTH, and
1069
       INFRARED modalities. For video data, our strategy includes capturing two frames every two sec-
1070
       onds to optimize processing efficiency. Besides, we used 128 mel-spectrogram partitions to convert
1071   2 seconds of audio sampled at 16kHz into a spectrogram. Similarly, thermal and depth images are
1072   treated as single-channel inputs and encoded using the same ViT architecture, facilitating consistent
1073   handling across these modalities. The encoders for both the image and language core models are
1074   initialized with weights from the ImageBind-Huge and LanguageBind pre-trained models respec-
1075   tively for enhancing learning efficiency and demonstrating the transferability of our TangentBind
1076   approach. This leverages their advanced pre-trained features to accelerate convergence and improve
1077   generalization across varied multimodal applications.

1087   ResNet We employed the standard ResNet50(He et al., 2015) architecture provided by PyTorch.
1088
       The ResNet50 model has a nearly number of training parameters with the diffusion model employed,
       suggesting that both architectures share comparable complexity and computational demands, which
1089
       allows for an equitable comparison of their performance in analogous tasks. To tailor the model for
1090
       our embedding-based task, the output dimension of the final fully connected layer was modified to
1091   produce embeddings with a dimensionality of 1024. These embeddings are subsequently normal-
1092   ized to lie on the unit hypersphere to facilitate the use of cosine similarity measures in subsequent
1093   analyses. For training the modified ResNet50, we utilized the L2 loss function, which is well-suited
1094   for embedding normalization by encouraging the model to minimize the Euclidean distance between
1095   the predicted and target embeddings. The optimizer of choice was AdamW, the learning rate was
1096   set to 1e-4. The training was conducted with a batch size of 128. Our training procedure mirrored
1097   that of the diffusion model in terms of dataset usage; specifically, we trained on the train split of the
1098   ImageNet-1K-VL-Enriched dataset. During training, the core modality embeddings were utilized as
1099
       inputs, while the embeddings of an alternative modality served as labels.
