011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Knowledge tracing aims to predict students’ future performance based on their
014            past interactions, helping online learning platforms and teachers assess learners’
015
               knowledge levels. This technology plays a critical role in achieving large-scale
               cognitive diagnosis. Recently, deep learning-based knowledge tracing models
016
               have demonstrated impressive results, with most research focusing on designing
017
               customized network architectures and novel optimization objectives. However,
018            redundant parameters and overly complex loss functions can complicate model
019            training and make it harder to maintain prediction accuracy.To further investigate
020            the effectiveness of simple recurrent neural networks in this field, and to lever-
021            age their advantages in handling sequential exercise representation, this paper
022            introduces a GRU-based knowledge tracing model named ExerCAKT (Exercise
023            Context-Aware Knowledge Tracing). This model effectively captures contextual
024            features of exercises and achieves robust knowledge state modeling through the
025            use of a GRU-based knowledge state feature extractor and a GRU-based exer-
026
               cise feature extractor—without relying on additional optimization objectives.The
               model’s superior performance is validated through comparisons with baseline
027
               models, such as AKT and SIMPLEKT, on three public datasets in the knowledge
028
               tracing domain. Evaluations are conducted using AUC and ACC metrics at both
029            the Knowledge Concept level and the question level. We validated that relying
030            solely on simple recurrent neural networks, combined with appropriate represen-
031            tation methods, can still achieve excellent performance in this field. Our code will
032            be available at xxx (Anonymous URL).

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Knowledge tracing is a foundational technology for achieving large-scale personalized education,
038   helping educators diagnose learners’ cognitive levels to provide more targeted teaching. In recent
039
      years, knowledge tracing has seen widespread application in online learning platforms (Abdelrah-
      man et al., 2023; Song et al., 2022a; Käser et al., 2017). The task of knowledge tracing is illustrated
040
      in Figure 1. Students complete exercises in an online learning system, and each student’s responses
041
      to these exercises are referred to as interactions in knowledge tracing. Each interaction generates at
042   least the following data: exercise ID, knowledge concept ID, and whether the student answered the
043   question correctly. All exercises are associated with knowledge concepts, and an exercise may be
044   linked to multiple knowledge concepts, as shown with Q4, Q5, and Q8 in the figure. To predict a
045   student’s performance in the next interaction, the input to the knowledge tracing algorithm typically
046   includes the student’s historical interaction information and the information of the exercise to be
047   predicted. Therefore, better modeling of interaction information and representation of the exercises
048   to be predicted are crucial strategies for achieving good performance.
049   Recently, Liu et al. (2022) introduced a tool for standardized experiments in the knowledge tracing
050   field called pyKT, which implemented multiple advanced knowledge tracing models under various
051   experimental conditions and published the optimal results. The benchmark provided by pyKT holds
052   significant reference value. We meticulously reviewed this work and the publicly available code,
053   and replicated their experiments. Based on these results, we obtained the same and surprising con-
      clusion: the first model to apply deep learning to the knowledge tracing field, DKT (Piech et al.,

078   to see DKT achieve outstanding results, as it is an extremely simple model, suggesting that we may
079   not need overly complex models to achieve excellent performance in knowledge tracing tasks.
080   The success of ChatGPT has generated significant attention (Wu et al., 2023), and its underlying
081   Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) had already demonstrated excellent performance in the field of
082   natural language processing with the advent of language models like BERT (Devlin et al., 2019)
083   and T5 (Raffel et al., 2020). Subsequently, Transformer models have been applied in computer
084   vision and recommendation systems, with representative works such as ViT (Dosovitskiy et al.,
085   2020), which has cross-modal modeling capabilities for multimodal learning, and SASRec (Kang &
086   McAuley, 2018), a Transformer-based model that effectively improves the accuracy of recommen-
087
      dation algorithms.
088   However, Transformer models have not achieved breakthrough success in the field of knowledge
089   tracing. The representative method, SAINT (Choi et al., 2020), uses a Transformer-based encoder
090   and decoder to capture features of student interaction sequences, but the improvements have been
091   limited. In the comparative experiments of pyKT (Liu et al., 2022), evaluations on seven different
092   datasets under the KC Level evaluation mode showed poor performance in more than half of the
093
      datasets. This somewhat supports our view that in the field of knowledge tracing, the complex-
      ity of model structures does not necessarily lead to better results. By analyzing the similarities and
094
      differences between the field of knowledge tracing and other fields such as natural language process-
095
      ing, computer vision, and recommendation systems, we believe that the reasons Transformer-based
096   knowledge tracing models fail to achieve good results may include: (1) The knowledge tracing
097   field is more sensitive to sequence positions, and the ”token embedding + positional encoding”
098   method disrupts the underlying semantic information of interaction encoding and reduces the ben-
099   efits brought by sequence information. (2) The classic knowledge tracing datasets generally have
100   fewer interactions, and too many parameters may lead to overfitting issues. Therefore, we believe
101   that using recurrent neural models, which can naturally capture temporal relationships, along with
102   appropriate knowledge state and exercise representations, are two ways to achieve better results in
103   the field of knowledge tracing.
104   In this paper, we move away from the current obsession with ”Attention is all you need” and design
105   our model ExerCAKT based on gated recurrent networks (Chung et al., 2014). The model includes
106   an embedding layer, a knowledge state feature extractor, an exercise feature extractor, and a pre-
107   diction layer used to predict the student’s performance in the next exercise. The contributions of
      this paper are as follows: (1)Design of a GRU-based Exercise Feature Extractor: We have designed

123   2     R ELATED WORK
124
125   2.1     P ROBLEM D EFINITION
126
      The goal of knowledge tracing is to predict a student’s performance in future interactions based
127
      on their interaction sequence, which can be viewed as a sequence modeling task. In this task, the
128
      student’s learning history is considered as an interaction sequence, typically representing the history
129   of the student’s responses to exercises. The model’s objective is to predict whether the student will
130   answer the next question correctly based on the data in this sequence. Formally, let the interaction
131   sequence be denoted as S, and the interactions that have occurred up to time t be represented as
132   S = [R0 , R1 , . . . , Rt ] = [(q0 , c0 , r0 ), (q1 , c1 , r1 ), . . . , (qt , ct , rt )], where each interaction records
133   the exercise ID qt , the concept ct , and whether the response was correct at (i.e., 0 or 1). The goal of
134   knowledge tracing is to predict the student’s response rt+1 given the exercise et+1 and concept ct+1
135   at the next time step.
136
137   2.2     D EEP L EARNING -BASED K NOWLEDGE T RACING
138
139   Currently, deep learning has been widely applied in the field of knowledge tracing. The work in this
140
      area can be roughly categorized into methods based on recurrent neural networks, memory networks,
      self-attention mechanisms, and other neural network methods. Subsequent work based on these four
141
      categories primarily focuses on improving methods through three aspects: optimizing the network
142
      architecture, adding additional features, and simulating the real learning processes of humans.
143
144
      2.2.1    M ETHODS BASED ON R ECURRENT N EURAL N ETWORKS
145
146   DKT (Piech et al., 2015) is the first model to use deep learning techniques in the field of knowledge
147   tracing. It directly employs Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks to model students’ inter-
148   action sequences and predict their performance in subsequent interactions. Subsequent work such
149   as DKT+ (Yeung & Yeung, 2018) and KQN (Lee & Yeung, 2019) builds on DKT by improving the
150   training objectives and network architecture for methods based on recurrent neural networks.
151
152   2.2.2    M ETHODS BASED ON M EMORY N ETWORKS
153
      Methods based on memory networks still use a recurrent structure. Compared to LSTM, mem-
154
      ory networks use matrices to store historical information, which provides better long-term memory
155   capabilities. A representative work in this category is the Dynamic Key-Value Memory Network
156   (DKVMN) (Zhang et al., 2017).
157
158   2.2.3    M ETHODS BASED ON S ELF -ATTENTION M ECHANISMS
159
160   Self-attention mechanisms involve calculating the importance of each input element relative to other
161   elements, thereby assigning different weights to each element. This allows the model to dynam-
      ically adjust attention based on the internal relationships of the data when processing sequence


                                                                 3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      data, thereby focusing on information from different positions with strong expressive capability.
163   SAKT (Pandey & Karypis, 2019) is the first knowledge tracing framework based on self-attention
164   mechanisms. Other notable works in this area include SAINT (Choi et al., 2020) and AKT (Ghosh
165   et al., 2020).
166
167   2.2.4    M ETHODS BASED ON OTHER N EURAL N ETWORKS
168
169   In addition to the three basic methods mentioned above, some works have introduced other network
170   structures or training methods. For example, GKT (Nakagawa et al., 2019)and Bi-CLKT (Song
171
      et al., 2022b) use graph neural networks to model the dependencies between knowledge concepts,
      ATKT (Guo et al., 2021) employs adversarial learning to enhance generalization capabilities, and
172
      CL4KT (Lee et al., 2022) utilizes contrastive learning. Currently, these four types of methods have
173
      established stable research communities, with relatively mature research directions. Introducing
174   other networks or training methods is an important avenue for future exploration in the field of
175   knowledge tracing.

      Our proposed method is illustrated in Figure 2 and includes the Embedding layer, Knowledge State
      Feature Extractor(Knowledge Characterizer), Exercise Feature Extractor(Specific Exercise Char-
204
      acterizer), and Prediction layer. The Knowledge State Feature Extractor is used to represent the
205
      learner’s knowledge state, while the Exercise Feature Extractor extracts contextual information of
206   the exercises to reflect the specificity of the exercises in different interaction sequences.

      3.1     E MBEDDING L AYER
209
210   Similar to other KT models, each interaction of the learner includes the following information: (1)qt ,
211   the exercise answered by the learner at time t; (2) rt , whether the learner answered correctly; (3)
212   ct , the knowledge concept corresponding to the answered exercise. First, embeddings are created
213   for all knowledge concepts and exercises, resulting in embedding matrices Ec ∈ RC×d and Eq ∈
214   RQ×d , where C and Q denote the number of knowledge concepts and exercises, respectively, and
215   d represents the embedding dimension, which is consistent across all embedding matrices. For
      example, at time t, the representations of qt and ct are eqt and ect . Next, the learner’s response


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      to interactions is embedded; since the response can only be ”correct” or ”incorrect”, Er is set to
217   Er ∈ R2×d . Additionally, the parameter matrix Eu ∈ RQ×1 is used to represent the specificity
218   sensitivity of the exercises, with details on specificity provided in subsequent section.
219
220
      3.2   K NOWLEDGE S TATE F EATURE E XTRACTOR
221
222   The purpose of the knowledge state feature extractor is to determine the student’s knowledge mastery
223   at different time steps based on the student’s interaction sequence. To represent the learner’s learning
224   process, the interaction at time t is denoted as:
225
226
                                               e(qt ,ct rt ) = ect + ert ∈ Rd                                      (1)
227   This means that modeling the learner’s knowledge state is independent of the exercises qt answered
228   at a given time step t. From a cognitive perspective, the reason for not using the exercise is that
229   a learner’s knowledge is primarily constructed around knowledge concepts. During the learning
230   process, the learner mainly acquires and understands knowledge concepts rather than memorizing
231   specific exercises. From a data perspective, the number of exercises is typically greater than the
232   number of knowledge concepts, i.e., Q > C. The same knowledge concept often appears multiple
233   times in the sequence, while an exercise typically does not reappear after being completed by the
234
      student. Therefore, the sparsity of sequence [q0 , q1 , . . . , qt ] is much higher than that of sequence
      [c0 , c1 , . . . , ct ]. Incorporating eqt into e(qt ,ct ,rt ) could increase the learning difficulty due to high
235
      sparsity and affect the representation effectiveness.
236
237   ExerCAKT uses standard Gated Recurrent Units (GRU) (Chung et al., 2014) as the sequence feature
238   extractor. The reason for this choice is that the structure of GRU is similar to human memory. After
239   each interaction occurs, GRU updates the knowledge state and reorganizes the knowledge structure.
240
      Additionally, GRU is simpler than LSTM. it combines the forget gate and input gate in LSTM into
      a single ”update gate.” This makes GRU have fewer parameters than LSTM, which generally results
241
      in faster training and inference. When dealing with shorter sequences, fewer parameters mean the
242
      network can learn important features more quickly, potentially leading to better performance. The
243   formal description of GRU is as follows:
244
245
246                                             Zt = σ (WZ · [ht−1 , xt ])                                         (2)
247                                             rt = σ (Wr · [ht−1 , xt ])                                         (3)
248
249                                         h̃t = tanh (W · [rt ∗ ht−1 , xt ])                                     (4)
250
                                            ht = (1 − Zt ) ∗ ht−1 + Zt ∗ h̃t                                       (5)
251
252
      In GRU,Zt and rt are the update gate and reset gate weights, respectively, used for the update and
253
      reset operations. ExerCAKT uses e(qt ,ct ,rt ) as the input xt for GRU and then applies a multi-layer
254   perceptron for feature transformation to enhance the model’s non-linear processing capability, that
255   is:
                                                                              ∈ Rd

256                             ht = W1 σ W2 GRU [. . . , e(qt ,ct ,rt ) ]                              (6)
257
      In this context,W1 and W2 represent neural network parameters, and σ represent the GELU non-
258
      linearity (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016). Ultimately, the output ht at each time step contains infor-
259
      mation from the sequence over time [0, t], which is used to represent the knowledge state features.

      3.3   E XERCISE F EATURE E XTRACTOR
263
      Although exercises are not used to describe the learning interaction sequence, learners interact based
264   on the exercises they answer, so the representation of exercises is also important. The exercise fea-
265   ture extractor is used to generate the representation for the exercise at time t + 1. ExerCAKT
266   does not directly use eqt+1 as the representation for the exercise at t + 1 for the following rea-
267   sons:(1)When modeling knowledge states, only knowledge concept ct and answer correctness rt
268   are used without specific exercises. This inconsistency might affect the model’s performance.(2)As
269   mentioned earlier, Q > C, and the high sparsity and long-tail distribution of exercises will lead
      to poor model performance when predicting exercises with lower frequency.(3)Directly using eqt+1

      as the question representation does not consider the contextual relationships of exercises, thus ne-
271   glecting non-knowledge factors such as related exercises and changes in difficulty when students are
272   answering.

274
      Through a review of the literature (Wright, 1977), the Rasch method treats exercises as ”specific
      manifestations of knowledge concepts.” Therefore, some studies (Ghosh et al., 2020; Liu et al.,

276
      cept difficulty vector dct , plus the knowledge concept embeddingect , i.e., eqt = ect + dct ∗ uqt .

278   multi-layer perceptron to extract features from the exercise sequence [q0 , q1 , . . . , qt , qt+1 ]. This fea-
279   ture is used as the specificity vector of the exercise concerning the knowledge concept (this vector
280   represents the offset of the exercise with respect to the knowledge concept, indicating the exercise’s
281   specificity). This approach captures the contextual relationships of exercises to reflect the specificity
282   of the exercises. Specifically,

424   cise representation. (IV) Use both context-aware and Rasch, i.e., the complete method described in

522   This paper presents the ExerCAKT model, which effectively models knowledge states and provides
523   high-quality representations of exercises through GRU-based knowledge state and exercise feature
524   extractors. The paper demonstrates the competitive performance of ExerCAKT through comparative
525   experiments on three datasets, two evaluation methods, and two evaluation metrics. Additionally,

      framework’s scalability. Future research could focus on optimizing knowledge state modeling and

      exercise representation in ExerCAKT, such as: (1) proposing new RNN structures to replace GRU
528
      units; (2) optimizing exercise representation by considering characteristics such as exercise difficulty
529   and multi-knowledge-concept associations based on historical data; (3) incorporating additional op-
530   timization objectives to improve model accuracy and generalization.
