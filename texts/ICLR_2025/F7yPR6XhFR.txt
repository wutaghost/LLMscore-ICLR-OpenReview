009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Latent diffusion models (LDMs) have made significant advancements in the field
012            of image generation in recent years. One major advantage of LDMs is their ability
013            to operate in a compressed latent space, allowing for more efficient training and
014            deployment. However, despite these advantages, challenges with LDMs still re-
015            main. For example, it has been observed that LDMs often generate high-frequency
016            details and complex compositions imperfectly. We hypothesize that one reason
017            for these flaws is due to the fact that all pre- and post-training of LDMs are done
018
               in latent space, which is typically 8 × 8 lower spatial-resolution than the output
               images. To address this issue, we propose adding pixel-space supervision in the
019
               post-training process to better preserve high-frequency details. Experimentally,
020
               we show that adding a pixel-space objective significantly improves both super-
021            vised quality fine-tuning and preference-based post-training by a large margin on
022            a state-of-the-art DiT transformer and U-Net diffusion models in both visual qual-
023            ity and visual flaw metrics, while maintaining the same text alignment quality.
024

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Diffusion models learn to sequentially denoise from random Gaussian noise to sharp images and
029   have revolutionized the field of media generation and editing in recent years. Latent diffusion mod-
030   els represent the most popular type of diffusion model because of their efficiency and simplicity.
031   State-of-the-art LDMs are typically pretrained on webscale data, resulting in “foundation mod-
032
      els” (Rombach et al., 2022; Podell et al., 2023; Esser et al., 2024; Saharia et al., 2022; Imagen
      3 Team, 2024; Dai et al., 2023; Ramesh et al., 2021; 2022; Betker et al., 2023).
033
034   These foundation models are then post-trained on a smaller, carefully curated dataset to improve
035   quality through either supervised quality fine-tuning (SFT) (Dai et al., 2023) or human-in-the-loop
036   preference modeling (Rafailov et al., 2024; Wallace et al., 2024; Meng et al., 2024). Post-training of
037   image foundation models is also utilized to create new models for a variety of applications, including
038
      controllable generation (Zhang et al., 2023), editing (Sheynin et al., 2024), 3D generation (Poole
      et al., 2022), video generation (Singer et al., 2022; Girdhar et al., 2023), and many others.
039
040   To achieve efficiency and simplicity, LDMs use a pretrained variational autoencoder (VAE) to com-
041   press images into latent representations. For example, in the original LDM paper (Rombach et al.,
042   2022), the authors used a conv-based VAE to compress a 512 × 512 × 3 image to 64 × 64 × 4.
043   This representation significantly speeds up training and reduces computational cost as the denois-
044
      ing diffusion model now operates in the 64 × 64 × 4 space instead of the original 512 × 512 × 3
      space (48× compression). However, this comes at the cost of lossy compression, which can result
045
      in inaccuracies in or loss of high-frequency details.
046
047   The research community has invested considerable effort in improving fine details, including scaling
048   up the model, carefully curating fine-tuning datasets, increasing the latent channel dimension (Dai
049   et al., 2023), and designing more powerful decoders (Betker et al., 2023).
050   In this paper, we take a step back and hypothesize that the artifacts in LDMs are partially caused
051   by the fact that all pretraining, post-training, and inference steps are done on a lower-resolution
052   latent space. With this assumption, we propose adding an additional supervision term in the original
053   pixel space during post-training by decoding the latent representation back and combining it with
      the original latent loss term. This approach aims to improve the quality of generated images by

115   2.1   T EXT- TO - IMAGE DIFFUSION MODEL
116
117
      Researchers have explored a variety of representations to train text-to-image diffusion models, in-
      cluding pixel-diffusion models (Ramesh et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022; Balaji et al., 2022), latent
118
      diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022; Dai et al., 2023), and token-based generative transform-
119
      ers (Chang et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024). Pixel-diffusion models directly generate
120   images in the pixel space, but due to computational constraints, they typically first generate images
121   at a lower resolution (e.g., 64 × 64) and then upsample them (sometimes multiple times) to achieve
122   the target resolution in a cascade fashion (Saharia et al., 2022).
123
      Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs), on the other hand, employ a pretrained autoencoder (Rombach
124
      et al., 2022) to compress the spatial dimensions of the image to be generated, typically by a factor of
125
      8 × 8, while moderately increasing the channel dimension from 3 (RGB) to 4. This approach signif-
126   icantly enhances training efficiency compared to pixel diffusion models, thereby facilitating various
127   applications such as high-resolution (Chen et al., 2023) and real-time image generation (Kohler
128   et al., 2024; Wimbauer et al., 2024). Early LDM models use convolutional U-Nets as the backbone
129   diffusion model, such as LDM1.5 (Rombach et al., 2022) and Emu (Dai et al., 2023). Recently,
130   the field has been dominated by diffusion transformers (DiTs), such as SD3 (Esser et al., 2024) and
131   PixArt-α (Chen et al., 2023). PixArt-α incorporates cross-attention modules into DiT and trained
132   the model on high-aesthetic data in its final training stage. However, all of these LDM methods are
133   still trained in latent space, which might suffer from loss of details and artifacts due to low spatial
134
      resolution.
135   In this paper, we propose a novel approach to refining image quality in diffusion models by deploy-
136   ing a pixel-space objective function in the post-training stage. Our method does not depend on a
137   particular diffusion model type and works equally well for both U-Nets and DiTs.
138
139   2.2   S UPERVISED QUALITY FINE - TUNING (SFT)
140
141   Supervised fine-tuning is crucial to the success of modern LLMs (Zhou et al., 2024; Touvron et al.,
142   2023; Achiam et al., 2023). In image and vision, Dai et al. (2023) proposes using a small set of ex-
143   tremely high-quality images to fine-tune a pretrained LDM model, resulting in significant improve-
144   ments to the visual quality of generated images without sacrificing text-image alignment. Betker
145   et al. (2023) and Segalis et al. (2023) propose using captions rewritten by vision language models to
146
      facilitate better learning, including during SFT. However, none of these proposed methods explored
      the representation space in which the model was fine-tuned. In this paper, we propose supplement-
147
      ing the regular supervised fine-tuning loss with a pixel-space objective function. We experimented
148
      with two different models: a replacement-trained U-Net LDM-1.5 (Rombach et al., 2022) and a DiT
149   model. Our results show that when fine-tuning on a small high-quality dataset, our proposed method
150   can significantly improve generation quality and visual flaws.

      2.3   H UMAN P REFERENCE BASED P OST T RAINING
154
      Reinforcement learning represents another popular type of post-training technique. The seminal
155
      work of Schulman et al. (2017) makes the policy gradient method practical. Rafailov et al. (2024)
156   proposes doing direct preference optimization (DPO) with a reference model to improve the model
157   quality on language models. Wallace et al. (2024) and Black et al. (2023) extend DPO to diffusion
158   models. DPO optimizes diffusion models on paired human preference data by implicitly estimating
159   a reward model. Liang et al. (2024) proposes doing step-aware DPO. Meng et al. (2024), on the
160   other hand, remove the reference model to make reinforcement learning more direct and effective.
161   In this paper, we show that our proposed pixel-space objective also works well for reward-based
      post-training.

232
233   We conduct a comprehensive qualitative and quantitative analysis, as well as ablation studies to
234   show that our proposed loss function outperforms the regular latent space loss in both supervised
235   fine-tuning and preference-based post-training.

237   4.1   H UMAN EVALUATION
238
239
      Like many recent studies, we found that a rigorous and independent human evaluation process is the
      most reliable way to evaluate different models. Commonly used metrics such as the FID score do
240
      not correlate well with human preference (Dai et al., 2023; Podell et al., 2023; Kirstain et al., 2023).
241
242   We contracted a team of paid and independent annotators who do not have contexts of our project to
243   evaluate the generated images. We conducted A/B comparisons on visual flaws and visual appeal,
244   as well as standalone evaluations on text alignment. We use a 600-prompt list in the GenAI MAGIC
245
      challenge for the evaluation (Tsai et al., 2024), where each example is annotated by at least 5 people
      and the majority decision is used.
246
247   Visual flaws. The annotators were presented with two images side-by-side, generated by two differ-
248   ent models, without the prompt. The annotators were trained to identify major flaws (e.g., displaced
249   body parts) and minor flaws (distorted eyes), and are asked to choose from “left wins”, “tie” and
250   “right wins”.
251   Visual appeal. Similar to the visual flaws task, but the annotators are asked to compare which image
252   is more aesthetically pleasing. Annotators were instructed to reject any examples where one image
253   was photo-realistic and the other was stylized (e.g., a cartoon).
254
      Text alignment. We predefined a list of binary questions for each prompt and asked annotators
255   to answer yes or no. We calculated the text alignment rate by aggregating the results across all
256   questions. For example, for the prompt “a cat and a dog”, the annotators are asked “is there one
257   dog”, “is there one cat”, and “are there other animals present”.

      5      L IMITATIONS
517
518
      Limitations of Baseline Models. Fine-tuning improvements are dependent on the quality of the
519
      original baseline model, and thus fine-tuning using pixel space loss may not always produce signifi-
520   cant improvements. For example, if the original baseline model already has minimal flaws and high
521   visual appeal, fine-tuning may not achieve many improvements. In contrast, if the original baseline
522   foundation model generates significant structural flaws that require the global understanding of the
523   image composition, fine-tuning with our loss alone may not help eliminate them.
524
      Limitations of Fine-tuning Dataset. Fine-tuning is also dependent on the quality and composition
525
      of the dataset used. Our dataset was hand-curated and consisted of images that followed our def-
526   inition of high quality and style. Changing the composition of this dataset would lead to different
527   results.
528
529
      Limitations of Human Evaluation. The images generated by the different models were evaluated
      by independent annotators. Although the annotators were trained on standards for visual flaws,
530
      visual appeal, and text alignment, these results may not fully reflect the real-world use of the models.
531
      Human evaluation is also inherently subjective and noisy in terms of aesthetics.
532
533
534   6      C ONCLUSIONS
535
536   In this paper, we proposed a novel post-training objective function for latent diffusion models by
537   incorporating a pixel-space loss with the commonly used latent-space fine-tuning loss. The resulting
538   model shows noticeable improvement in visual flaws and visual appeal metrics in both supervised
539   fine-tuning and preference-based post-training through rigorous human evaluations. The proposed
      objective function is simple and can be easily plugged into existing models such as DiT and U-Net.
