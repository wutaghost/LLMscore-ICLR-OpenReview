011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Monocular depth estimation has been extensively studied over the past few
014            decades, yet achieving robust depth estimation in real-world scenes remains a
015            challenge, particularly in the presence of reflections, shadow occlusions, and low-
016
               texture regions. Existing methods typically rely on extracting front-view 2D
               features for depth estimation, which often fail to capture those complex physi-
017
               cal factors present in real-world scenes, leading to discontinuous, incomplete, or
018
               inconsistent depth maps. To address these issues, we turn to learning a more
019            powerful 3D representation for robust monocular depth estimation, and propose a
020            novel self-supervised monocular depth estimation framework based on the Three-
021            dimensional Scene Field representation, or TSF-Depth for short. Specifically, we
022            build our TSF-Depth framework upon an encoder-decoder architecture. The en-
023            coder extracts scene features from the input 2D image, and subsequently reshapes
024            it as a tri-plane feature field by incorporating scene prior encoding. This tri-plane
025            feature field is designed to implicitly model the structure and appearance of the
026            continuous 3D scene. We then estimate a high-quality depth map from the tri-
027
               plane feature field by simulating the camera imaging process. To do this, we
               construct a 2D feature map with 3D geometry by sampling from the tri-plane fea-
028
               ture field using the coordinates of points where the line of sight intersects with the
029
               scene. The aggregated multi-view geometric features are subsequently fed into
030            the decoder for depth estimation. Extensive experiments on KITTI and NYUv2
031            datasets show that TSF-Depth achieves state-of-the-art performance. We also val-
032            idate the generalization capability of our model on Make3D and ScanNet datasets.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Monocular depth estimation is an essential computer vision task and has wide applications in au-
038   tonomous driving (Geiger et al., 2013; Menze & Geiger, 2015), robot navigation (Dudek & Jenkin,
039   2024), and 3D reconstruction (Lyu et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2022), etc. This task aims to infer the
040   depth of each pixel in a single image, thereby recovering the 3D scene structure. Yet, estimating
041   depth from a single image is indeed ill-posed and inherently ambiguous, since a 3D scene can be
042   back-projected from an infinite number of 2D images (Shao et al., 2023). Thus, the lack of sufficient
043   3D geometric cues in a 2D image poses a substantial challenge for monocular depth estimation.
044   Early monocular depth estimation (Yuan et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2024) worked in
045   a supervised manner and yielded relatively accurate depth. However, depth labels are expensive to
046   obtain. Moreover, existing physical devices can only capture sparse scene depth (Moon et al., 2023).
047   Consequently, the sparse supervision and data scale hinder their application in practical scenarios.
048   Recently, self-supervised monocular depth estimation (Zhang et al., 2023a; Han et al., 2023) has
049   attracted widespread attention. The core of such approaches is to synthesize a 2D image using the
050   estimated depth map and minimize the photometric loss between the synthesized image and the tar-
051   get image (Zhao et al., 2023a). Previous efforts focused on mining effective 2D features for depth
052   estimation by designing advanced network architectures (Lyu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023a),
053   developing more suitable loss functions (Godard et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2024), using semantic in-
      formation (Casser et al., 2019), or leveraging geometric priors (Zhao et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024).

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Supervised Monocular Depth Estimation. Eigen et al. (2014) first used a coarse-to-fine network
111
      for monocular depth estimation. Subsequently, numerous supervised works have been proposed.
112   These works can be functionally classified into regression-based methods (Ranftl et al., 2021; Zhao
113   et al., 2021; Shao et al., 2023) and classification-based methods (Bhat et al., 2021; Hu et al., 2022;
114   Shao et al., 2024). Regression-based works use convolutional neural networks to directly learn the
115   depth value of each pixel by minimizing the error between the prediction and ground-truth depths.
116   However, these methods usually suffer from slow convergence and local solutions (He et al., 2022).
117   Classification-based methods divide the depth range into different bins, and predict the probability
118   of falling in each bin to obtain the final depth by weighted summation, which is easier to optimize.
119   However, the high cost of data collection for training limits the wide application of these methods.
120   Self-Supervised Monocular Depth Estimation. Self-supervised depth estimation approaches that
121   avoid the need for ground-truth depth during training phase have gained attention. Zhou et al.
122   (2017) proposed a pioneering work that utilized depth network and pose network to jointly estimate
123   depth map and camera pose, and only adopted monocular video as training data. Following this
124   classical joint training pipeline, subsequent works improve the performance by designing robust
125   losses (Gordon et al., 2019; Shu et al., 2020; Zhan et al., 2018), using auxiliary information during
126
      training (Watson et al., 2019; Klodt & Vedaldi, 2018), dealing with moving objects (Godard et al.,
      2019; Klingner et al., 2020), and adding extra geometric constraints (Yang et al., 2018; Li et al.,
127
      2021). Yet, these methods also have limitations as they infer depth from the fron-view 2D feature
128
      space, which often do not contain sufficient 3D geometric cues (Han et al., 2023), and ignore the
129   value of additional scene geometry priors in depth estimation. Instead, our TSF-Depth models 3D
130   scene field using scene feature and scene priors to recover the multi-view representation and then
131   capture sufficient 3D geometric features from it for robust depth estimation.
132
      3D Scene Representation. Depth estimation using only 2D representation is a well-known ill-
133
      posed problem. To this end, learning-based multi-view stereo (MVS) methods (Yao et al., 2018;
134
      2019; Yang et al., 2022) use the cost volume as spatial representation of the scene and then utilize
135   3D CNNs to continuously learn full-space 3D features. Considering the point cloud structure makes
136   3D feature learning more flexible compared to the cost volume, some point cloud-based MVS (Chen
137   et al., 2019; 2020; Zhao et al., 2023b) propose to replace the cost volume with the point cloud as the
138   spatial representation of the scene. Although these methods establish the spatial structure feature of
139   the scene or learn the global feature of the scene, the structural attributes are not further perceived
140   and learned. Additionally, 3D CNNs require memory cubic to the model resolution, which can be
141   a hindrance to achieving optimal performance. Unlike existing MVS methods require complex cost
142   volume, multi-view images and 3D CNNs, TSF-Depth models a single-view image into a tri-plane
143
      feature field as a 3D scene representation using only a 2D encoder-decoder architecture.

147   3.1   OVERVIEW
148
149   Previous studies relied on extracting front-view 2D features for depth estimation. Although these
150   methods are effective in conventional scenes, as discussed in Section 1, they often fail to represent
151   those complex physical factors present in real-world scenes due to the limitations of front-view
152   2D representations. To this end, we propose TSF-Depth, a novel self-supervised depth estimation
153   framework based on the 3D scene representation. The TSF-Depth is designed to model a multi-view
154
      representation of the continuous 3D scene with a tri-plane feature field. Then, we can achieve robust
      depth estimation using 2D features with 3D geometry sampled from the 3D scene field.

310   Following monodepth2 (Godard et al., 2019), we use a target frame It and two adjacent frames Ia
311   (a ∈ {t − 1, t + 1}) to jointly train a DepthNet (θE , θI , θF ) and a PoseNet θp . During training, It
312   is fed into the DepthNet to get the depth DF,t , and (It , Ia ) are put into PoseNet to get the relative
313   camera pose T t→a . Then, we can synthesize a target frame IF,a→t by warping the source frame
314   It : IF,a→t = Ia ⟨proj(DF,t , T t→a , K)⟩, where proj(·) is the coordinate projection operation (Zhou
315
      et al., 2017). The photometric error between IF,t and It , consisting of L1 and SSIM weighted by α,
      is calculated as: pe(It , IF,a→t ) = α2 (1 − SSIM (It , IF,a→t )) + (1 − α)∥It − IF,a→t ∥1 . Following
316
      Monodepth2, we adopt the per-pixel minimum photometric loss as our reprojection loss:
317
                                       Lph (It , IF,a→t ) = min pe(It , IF,a→t ).                         (10)
318                                                            a
319   We also use the edge-aware smoothness loss to encourage locally smooth depth maps:
320                           Lsm (DF,t , It ) = ∂x D∗F,t e−|∂x It | + ∂y D∗F,t e−|∂y It | .       (11)
321   where ∂x and ∂y are the gradients in the horizontal and vertical direction respectively. Besides,
322   D∗F,t = DF,t /D̄F,t is the mean-normalized inverse depth from Monodepth2 to discourage shrinking
323   of the estimated depth. Therefore, the total loss for final depth map is defined as:
                                   LF = βLph (It , IF,a→t ) + γLsm (DF,t , It ).                   (12)

      We implement TSF-Depth in PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2017), traning it for 20 epochs on the outdoor

      5   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we propose a novel self-supervised monocular depth estimation framework based on
534   the Three-dimensional Scene Field representation (TSF-Depth). Unlike previous methods typically
535   rely on extracting front-view 2D features, we turn to learning a more powerful 3D representation for
536   robust depth estimation. We design a tri-plane feature field that is reshaped from hybrid features of
537   scene content and scene prior to implicitly model the multi-view representation of the continuous
538   3D scene. Then, we attentively design a 3D-to-2D mapping strategy to sample 2D features with 3D
539   geometry for depth estimation. Extensive experiments on widely-used outdoor datasets (KITTI and
      Make3D) and indoor datasets (NYUv2 and ScanNet) show the robustness and generalization ability.

804   When humans understand the real-world or infer the depth from the 3D scene including indoor or
805   outdoor scene, they will employ specific prior knowledge about the physical world. For the indoor
806   scene, the floor and celling are located in the lower and the upper parts respectively, and the room
807   is surrounded by flat walls perpendicular to the floor and ceiling. As for the outdoor scene such as
808   driving scene, the road and sky appear in the lower and upper parts respectively, and other objects
809   such as people, vehicles, houses, etc. are connected and located above the road. Besides, objects
      in the middle area of the image often have greater depth than objects in other areas. Inspired by

1061   depth quality was achieved when combining with multi-scale tri-plane feature fields, as shown in 8
1062   (f). Based on the above analysis, the scene prior we introduce is effective for depth estimation even
1063   for monocular challenging indoor scenes.

1065   scene field using multi-scale tri-plane feature fields. The results are shown in Table 8 (c) and (e).
