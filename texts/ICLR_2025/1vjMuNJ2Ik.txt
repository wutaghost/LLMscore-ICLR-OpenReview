009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Sketching is both a fundamental artistic expression and a crucial aspect of art.
012            The significance of sketching has increased alongside the development of sketch-
013            based generative and editing models. To enable individuals to use these sketch-
014            based generative models effectively, personalizing sketch extraction is crucial. In
015            response, we introduce DiffSketch, a novel method capable of generating vari-
016            ous geometrically aligned sketches from text or images, using a single manual
017            drawing for training the style. Our method exploits rich information available
018
               in features from a pretrained Stable Diffusion model to achieve effective domain
               adaptation. To further streamline the process of sketch extraction, we further re-
019
               fine our approach by distilling the knowledge from the trained generator into the
020
               image-to-sketch network, which is termed as DiffSketchdistilled . Through a series
021            of comparisons, we verify that our method not only outperforms existing state-
022            of-the-art sketch extraction methods but also surpasses diffusion-based stylization
023            methods in the task of extracting sketches.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Sketching, as an initial stage in artistic creation, serves as a foundational process for conceptual-
028
      izing and conveying artistic intentions while visualizing the core structure and content of the final
029   artwork. As sketches can exhibit distinct styles despite their basic form composed of simple lines,
030   many studies in computer vision and graphics have attempted to train models for automatically ex-
031   tracting geometric sketches Winnemöller (2011); lllyasviel (2017); Ashtari et al. (2022); Chan et al.
032   (2022); Seo et al. (2023). The majority of previous sketch extraction approaches utilize image-to-
033   image translation techniques to produce high-quality results. These approaches typically require a
034   large dataset when training an image translation model from scratch, making it difficult to person-
035   alize applications such as sketch auto-colorization, sketch-based editing, or conditional generation.
036   Recently, advancements in abstract curve optimization have been made as an alternative that does
037
      not require training Mo et al. (2021); Vinker et al. (2022); Willett et al. (2023); Vinker et al. (2023).
      While these methods can effectively optimize curves based on a given text or image, they cannot
038
      follow the target style image, making it challenging to generate personalized sketches.
039
040   Meanwhile, recent research has explored the utilization of diffusion model Rombach et al. (2022);
041   Saharia et al. (2022) features for downstream tasks Xu et al. (2023); Khani et al. (2023); Zhang et al.
042   (2023a); Tumanyan et al. (2023). Features derived from pretrained diffusion models are known to
043
      contain rich semantics and spatial information Tumanyan et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2023), which can
      help train networks for various tasks using a small number of data. Previous studies have utilized
044
      these features extracted from a subset of layers Baranchuk et al. (2021), certain timesteps Zhang
045
      et al. (2023a); Xu et al. (2023), or every specific interval Luo et al. (2023). Unfortunately, these
046   selected features often do not contain most of the information generated during the entire diffusion
047   process.
048
049
      To this end, we propose Diffsketch, a new method that can extract representative features from a
      pretrained Stable Diffusion (SD) Rombach et al. (2022) and train the sketch generator with one
050
      manual drawing. For feature extraction from the denoising process, we statistically analyze the
051
      features and select those that can represent the whole feature information from the denoising process.
052   Our new generator aggregates the features from multiple timesteps, fuses them with Variational

095   2.1   S KETCH E XTRACTION
096   At its core, sketch extraction utilizes edge detection. Edge detection serves as the foundation not
097   only for sketch extraction but also for tasks like object detection and segmentation Zhang et al.
098   (2015); Arbelaez et al. (2010). Initial edge detection studies primarily focused on identifying edges
099   based on abrupt variations in color or brightness Canny (1986); Winnemöller (2011). Although
100   these techniques are direct and efficient without requiring extensive datasets to train on, they often
101   produce outputs with artifacts, like scattered dots or lines.
102   To make extracted sketches authentic, learning-based strategies have been introduced. These strate-
103   gies excel in identifying object borders or rendering lines in distinct styles Xiang et al. (2021); Xie
104   & Tu (2015a); lllyasviel (2017); Li et al. (2019; 2017). Informative drawing Chan et al. (2022)
105   took a step forward from prior techniques by incorporating the depth and semantic information of
106   images to procure superior-quality sketches. In a more recent development, Ref2sketch Ashtari et al.
107   (2022) permits to extract stylized sketches using reference sketches through paired training. Semi-
      Ref2sketch Seo et al. (2023) adopted contrastive learning for semi-supervised training. All of these

      methods share the same limitation; they require a large amount of sketch data for training, which
109   is hard to gather. Due to data scarcity, training a sketch extraction model is generally challenging.
110   To address this challenge, our method is designed to train a sketch generator using just one manual
111   drawing.

      2.2   D IFFUSION F EATURES FOR D OWNSTREAM TASK
114
115   Diffusion models Ho et al. (2020); Nichol & Dhariwal (2021) have shown cutting-edge results in
116   tasks related to generating images conditioned on text prompt Rombach et al. (2022); Saharia et al.
117   (2022); Ramesh et al. (2021). There have been attempts to analyze the features for utilization in
118   downstream tasks such as segmentation Baranchuk et al. (2021); Xu et al. (2023); Khani et al.
119   (2023), image editing Tumanyan et al. (2023), and finding dense semantic correspondence Luo
120   et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023a); Tang et al. (2023). Most earlier studies chose a specific subset
121
      of features for their own downstream tasks. Recently, Diffusion Hyperfeature Luo et al. (2023)
      proposed an aggregator that learns features from all layers and that uses equally sampled time steps.
122
      We advance a step further by analyzing and selecting the features from multiple timesteps, which
123
      represent the overall features. We also propose a two-stage aggregation network and feature-fusing
124   decoder utilizing additional information from VAE to generate finer details.

126
127   2.3   D EEP F EATURES FOR S KETCH E XTRACTION
128   Most of recent sketch extraction methods utilize the deep features of a pretrained model for sketch
129   extraction training Ashtari et al. (2022); Seo et al. (2023); Yi et al. (2019; 2020). These approaches
130   utilize deep features from a pretrained classifier Johnson et al. (2016); Zhang et al. (2018) or vision-
131   language models such as CLIP Radford et al. (2021) to measure semantic similarity Chan et al.
132   (2022); Vinker et al. (2022). They indirectly use the features by comparing them for the loss cal-
133   culation during the training process instead of using them to generate a sketch. DiffSketcher Xing
134   et al. (2023) utilizes a diffusion model to perform curve optimization from text. StyleSketch Yun
135   et al. (2024) utilizes GAN features to extract a facial sketch with a few data. These recent models
136
      have successfully demonstrated that generative features can be used to create sketches. However,
      neither method can extract a sketch from a single example because DiffSketcher cannot take a style
137
      or content image as input, while StyleSketch requires 16 data for training in a single domain. To fa-
138
      cilitate translating an image to a sketch in a provided style, we directly use the diffusion features that
139   contain rich information and generate geometric sketches using a network trained with one example
140   pair.

143   3     D IFFUSION F EATURES
144
145   During the backward diffusion process, UNet Ronneberger et al. (2015) produces several inter-
146   mediate features with different shapes while reducing noise. This collection of features contains
147   rich information about texture and semantics, which can be used to generate an image in various
148
      domains. For instance, features from the lower to intermediate layers of the UNet reveal global
      structures and semantic regions, while features from higher layers exhibit fine and high-frequency
149
      information Tumanyan et al. (2023); Luo et al. (2023). Furthermore, features become more fine-
150
      grained over time steps Hertz et al. (2022). As these features have different information depending
151   on their embedded layers and processed timesteps, it is important to select diverse features to fully
152   utilize the information they provide.
153

160   Diffusion Implicit Model (DDIM) sampling, with a total of 50 steps Song et al. (2020). For an
161   experiment, features from a total of 50,000 data (50 UNet features with varying timesteps from
      1,000 randomly generated images) were gathered.

302   4.3 S AMPLING S CHEME FOR T RAINING
303   Our method uses one source image and its corresponding sketch as the only ground truth when
304   guiding the sketch style, using the direction of CLIP embeddings. Therefore, our losses rely on well-
305   constructed CLIP manifold. We found that when the domains of two images Isource and Isamp differ
306   largely, the confidence in the directional CLIP loss becomes lower (explanation and experiment are
307   provided in Sec. 5.2). To fully utilize the capacity of the diffusion model and produce sketches in
308   diverse domains, however, it is important to train the model on diverse examples.
309   To ensure learning from diverse examples without decreasing the confidence of directional CLIP
310   losses, we propose a novel sampling scheme, condition diffusion sampling for training (CDST) in
311   which the condition is diffused from a single point to whole sampling space of SD. We envision
312   that this sampling can be useful when training a model with a conditional generator. CDST initially
313   samples a data Isamp from one known condition encoded from prompt C and gradually changes
314   the sampling distribution to the distribution of pretrained SD by using a diffusion algorithm when
315   training the network (see Fig. 5).
316   Here, to estimate the distribution of SD, we randomly sampled 100k prompts from LAION-
317   400M Schuhmann et al. (2021) and used them as a subset of the trained text-image pairs of the
318   SD model. We then tokenized and embedded these prompts for preprocessing, following the pro-
319   cess of the pretrained SD model. We then conducted Shapiro-Wilk test Shapiro & Wilk (1965),
320   followed by Mardia test Mardia (1970; 1974) with a significance level of α = 5% and found that
321   the distribution of SD follows a multivariate normal distribution. The detailed process is stated in
322
      Sec. D of the Appendix. The condition on the iteration i (0 ≤ i ≤ S) can be described as follows:
323
              \label {eq:CDST} \small \begin {aligned} \alpha _{i} = \sqrt {(1 - \frac {i}{S})},\quad \beta _{i} = \sqrt {\frac {i}{S}}, \quad C_{i}^{samp} \sim \mathcal {N}(\boldsymbol {\mu }, \boldsymbol {\Sigma }) ,\quad C_{i} = \frac {\alpha _{i}}{\alpha _{i} +\beta _{i}} C + \frac {\beta _{i}}{\alpha _{i} + \beta _{i}} C_{i}^{samp} \end {aligned}    (5)

328   4.4 D ISTILLATION
329
      Once the sketch generator Gsketch is trained, DiffSketch can generate pairs of images and sketches
330
      in the trained style. This generation can be performed either randomly or with a specific condi-
331
      tion. Due to the nature of the denoising diffusion model, in which the result is refined through the
332   denoising process, long processing time and high memory usage are required. Moreover, when ex-
333   tracting sketches from images, the quality can be degraded because of the inversion process. There-
334   fore, to perform image-to-sketch extraction efficiently while ensuring high-quality results, we train
335   DiffSketchdistilled using Pix2PixHD Wang et al. (2018).
336
      To train DiffSketchdistilled , we extract 30k pairs of image and sketch samples using our
337
      trained DiffSketch, adhering to CDST. Additionally, we employ regularization to ensure that the
338   ground truth sketch Isketch can be generated and discriminated effectively during the training of
339   DiffSketchdistilled . With this trained model, images can be extracted in a given style much more
340   quickly than with the original DiffSketch.

      6   L IMITATION AND C ONCLUSION
522
523   We proposed DiffSketch, a novel method to extract sketches in given styles by training a sketch gen-
524   erator using representative features. For the first time, we conducted the task of extracting sketches
525   from the features of a diffusion model and demonstrated that our method outperforms previous state-
526   of-the-art methods. The ability to extract sketches in input style, trained with one example, will have
527   various use cases not only for artistic purposes but also for personalizing sketch-to-image retrieval
528   and sketch-based image editing.
529   We built our generator network specialized for producing sketches by fusing aggregated features
530   with the features from a VAE decoder. Consequently, our method works well with diverse sketches
531   including dense sketches and outlines. However, because our method utilizes features during gener-
532   ation, it requires the user to draw a sketch, making it impossible to use existing sketch pairs. One
533   possible future research direction could involve utilizing features from inversion. To help under-
534   stand future research in this direction, we visualize the features from inversion to show that their
535   characteristics are similar to the features from generation in Sec. B.2 of the Appendix.
536   Although we focused on sketch extraction, our analysis of selecting representative features and
537   the proposed training scheme are not limited to the domain of sketches. Extracting representative
538   features holds potential to improve applications leveraging diffusion features, including semantic
539   segmentation, visual correspondence, and depth estimation. We believe that this research direction
      promises to broaden the impact and utility of diffusion feature-based applications.

       D.1 A DDITIONAL D ETAILS ON CDST
1083
1084   As stated in the main paper, we randomly sampled 100k prompts to estimate the distribution of
1085   SD. Specifically, we tokenized and embedded these 100k prompts in the space of the CLIP model.
1086   With this embedding, we conducted PCA to extract 512 principal components. We then checked the
1087   normality of the sampled embeddings with all 512 principal component axes using the Shapiro-Wilk
1088   test Shapiro & Wilk (1965) with a significance level of α = 5%.
1089
       As a result, 214 components rejected the null hypothesis of normality. This indicates that each of its
1090   marginals cannot be assumed to be univariate normal. Next, we conducted the Mardia test Mardia
1091   (1970; 1974) with the same 100k samples, taking into account skewness and kurtosis to check if
1092   the distribution is multivariate. The results failed to reject the null hypothesis of normality with a
1093   significance level of α = 5%. Therefore, we assumed DSD as a multivariate normal distribution for
1094   our sampling during training. In addition, we calculated the Earth Moving Distance (EMD) Levina
1095   & Bickel (2001) with 100k samples from LAION- 400M, which were not used for our analysis.
1096   For comparison, we used the normal distribution for each axis, and the uniform distribution to find
1097   that our N (244.22) is lower than the normal distribution for each axis (244.31) and the uniform
1098
       distribution (1480.57).
