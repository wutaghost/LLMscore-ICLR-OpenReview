009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Chinese character recognition has been a longstanding research topic and remains
012            essential in visual tasks like ancient manuscript recognition. Chinese character
013            recognition faces numerous challenges, particularly the issue of zero-shot char-
014            acters. Existing Chinese zero-shot character recognition methods primarily fo-
015            cus on the radical or stroke decomposition. However, radical-based methods
016            still struggle to solve zero-shot radicals, while stroke-based ones are hard to per-
017            ceive fine-grained information. Besides, previous methods can hardly general-
018
               ize to characters of other languages. In this paper, we propose a novel Self-
               learning Compositional Representation method for zero-shot Chinese Character
019
               Recognition (SCR-CCR). SCR-CCR learns compositional components automat-
020
               ically from the data, which are not aligned with human-defined radical or stroke
021            decomposition methods. SCR-CCR follows the pretraining-inference paradigm.
022            First, we train a Character Slot Attention (ChSA) via pure feature reconstruction
023            loss to parse appropriate components from character images. Then we recognize
024            zero-shot characters without finetuning or retraining in the inference stage by com-
025            paring components between input and example images. To evaluate the proposed
026            method, we conduct experiments of zero-shot character recognition. The exper-
027            iments illustrate that SCR-CCR outperforms previous methods in most cases of
028            character and radical zero-shot settings. In particular, visualization experiments
029
               indicate that the components learned by SCR-CCR reflect the structure of char-
               acters in an interpretable way, and can be used to recognize Japanese and Korean
030
               characters.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Optical Character Recognition (OCR) plays a crucial role in various downstream tasks, such as docu-
036   ment understanding (Francois et al., 2022; Singh & Sachan, 2018) and traffic sign recognition (Jain
037   & Gianchandani, 2018; Greenhalgh & Mirmehdi, 2014). Thus, this field continues to attract the
038   attention of researchers. Unlike Latin characters, Chinese characters possess complex internal struc-
039   tures. Consequently, the multitude of Chinese character categories often leads to the prevalence of
040   zero-shot learning problems in practical applications (Yu et al., 2023).
041
      As Figure 1 illustrates, previous zero-shot Chinese Character Recognition (CCR) methods can be
042   broadly categorized into character-based, radical-based and stroke-based approaches. To solve the
043   zero-shot problem, the character-based approach (Li et al., 2020; Xiao et al., 2019) extracts mono-
044   lithic representation for images and typically requires additional printed character images during
045   training. Differently, the radical- or stroke-based approaches (Wang et al., 2019; 2018; Chen et al.,
046   2021) recognize Chinese characters through radical and stroke decomposition, which may cost
047   considerable inference time due to the existence of auto-regressive decoders. Recently, based on
048   CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), Yu et al. (2023) proposed an efficient image-IDS matching method
049   for zero-shot CCR. Although existing methods have achieved certain performance improvements in
050
      zero-shot CCR, the human-defined representations used in these methods may lack the flexibility to
      adapt to different scenarios and have poor generalization in practical applications.
051
052   Compositionality is a fundamental way in which humans understand and interpret the world (Lake
053   et al., 2017). In contrast to monolithic representations of entire scenes, compositional representa-
      tions describe the visual world by discovering objects in scenes, capturing attributes of objects, and

079

      catello et al., 2020; Dedhia et al., 2023). For example, if one can recognize a car and a tree as two

      independent objects, it can understand a new scene where a car parks next to a tree, even though
081
      it has never seen such combination. In zero-shot CCR, we can decompose unseen characters into
082   acquired objects, and transform the task of character recognition into comparing object sequences
083   (Lake et al., 2011; 2015). Although radical-based or stroke-based approaches are similar in the
084   motivation of character decomposition, the object-centric representations are automatically learned
085   from data and can handle different types of data without annotations of human-defined radical or
086   stroke categories.
087
      Inspired by the compositionality of visual scenes, we propose a novel Self-learning Compositional
088   Representation method for zero-shot Chinese Character Recognition (SCR-CCR). As Figure 1d
089   shows, SCR-CCR parses slots (i.e., compositional objects) from Chinese characters automatically,
090   which are not aligned with human-defined structures such as radicals and strokes, allowing it to
091   generalize effectively to unseen characters in a zero-shot setting. SCR-CCR realizes zero-shot CCR
092   via a pretraining-inference paradigm. In the first pretraining stage, we train an encoder, decoder,
093   and Character Slot Attention (ChSA) to parse appropriate slots from input character images by
094   reconstructing features of a frozen pre-trained encoder (Locatello et al., 2020). In the second in-
095   ference stage, SCR-CCR recognizes zero-shot characters without finetuning by matching slots of
096
      input and example images. We conduct experiments of character and radical zero-shot CCR. SCR-
      CCR outperforms previous methods on all datasets in both zero-shot settings and surpasses previous
097
      methods by about 50% in the radical zero-shot setting. Visualization experiments indicate that the
098
      slots learned by SCR-CCR can reflect the structure of Chinese characters in an interpretable way.
099   In particular, trained with only Chinese characters, SCR-CCR can recognize Japanese and Korean
100   characters, achieving an accuracy of 89% and 62%.

105   2.1   Z ERO - SHOT C HINESE C HARACTER R ECOGNITION
106
107   Due to the significantly larger number of Chinese characters compared to Latin characters, character
      recognition in Chinese inevitably encounters zero-shot problems, i.e., the characters in the test set are


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      excluded in the training set. Early works in Chinese character recognition can be broadly categorized
109   into three types: character-based, radical-based, and stroke-based approaches.
110
111   Character-based. Before the era of deep learning, the character-based methods usually utilize the
112
      hand-crafted features to represent Chinese characters (Jin et al., 2001; Su & Wang, 2003; Chang,
      2006). With deep learning achieving a great success, MCDNN (Cireşan & Meier, 2015) takes the
113
      first attempt to use CNN for extracting robust features of Chinese characters while approaching
114
      the human performance on handwritten CCR in the ICDAR 2013 competition (Yin et al., 2013).
115   Although the character-based methods, treating each character as one class, have a higher time
116   efficiency, they are prone to suffer from the character zero-shot problem in practice.
117
118
      Radical-based. To solve the character zero-shot problem, some methods propose to predict the
      radical sequence of the input character image. In Wang et al. (2018), character images are first
119
      fed into a DenseNet-based encoder (Huang et al., 2017) to extract the character features, which are
120
      subsequently decoded into the corresponding radical sequences through an attention-based decoder.
121   FewShotRAN (Wang et al., 2019) proposes a radical aggregation module to introduce the deep
122   prototype learning for robust radical feature representation. These radical-based methods can indeed
123   alleviate the character zero-shot problem to a certain extent, but the prediction of radical sequences
124   takes longer time than the character-based methods. Although HDE (Cao et al., 2020) adopts a
125   matching-based method to avoid the time-consuming radical sequence prediction, this method needs
126   to manually design a unique vector for each Chinese character. Meanwhile, it does not achieve ideal
127   performance in the zero-shot settings.
128   Stroke-based. To fundamentally solve the zero-shot problem, some methods decompose Chinese
129   characters into stroke sequences. The early stroke-based methods usually extract strokes by tradi-
130   tional strategies. For example, in Kim et al. (1999), the authors employed mathematical morphology
131   to extract each stroke in characters. The proposed method in (Liu et al., 2001) describes each Chi-
132   nese character as an attributed relational graph. Recently, a deep-learning-based method (Chen et al.,
133   2021) is proposed to decompose each Chinese character into a sequence of strokes and employs a
134   feature-matching strategy to solve the one-to-many problem (i.e., there is a one-to-many relationship
135
      between stroke sequences and Chinese characters). This stroke-based method can indeed alleviate
      the zero-shot problem and achieve higher performance than radical-based methods. However, it
136
      costs more time in inference, resulting from that the predicted stroke sequences of Chinese charac-
137
      ters are longer than the corresponding radical sequences.
138
139   Recently, Yu et al. (2023) introduced CCR-CLIP, which aligns character images with their rad-
140   ical sequences to recognize zero-shot characters, achieving comparable inference efficiency with
141
      the character-based approach. All previous methods focus on learning Chinese character features
      through human-defined representations but struggle to achieve high generalization capabilities.

      2.2   OBJECT- CENTRIC REPRESENTATION LEARNING
146
147
148   Object-centric representation methods interpret the world in terms of objects and their relationships.
149   They capture structured representations that are more interpretable, compositional, and generaliz-
150   able, which has become increasingly popular in computer vision, as it aligns with how humans
151   perceive and interact with the world. One class of models extracts object-centric representations
152   with feedforward processes. For example, SPACE and GNM (Lin et al., 2020; Jiang & Ahn, 2020)
153
      attempt to divide images into small patches for parallel computation while modeling layouts of
      scenes. Another class of models initializes and updates object-centric representations by iterative
154
      processes (Greff et al., 2017; 2019; Emami et al., 2021). A representative method is Slot Attention,
155
      which assigns visual features to initialized slots via iterative cross-attention mechanism (Locatello
156   et al., 2020). Based on Slot Attention, many methods have been proposed to improve the quality of
157   object-centric representations in different scenarios (Seitzer et al., 2023). Recently, some models
158   have aimed at parsing object-centric scene representations in videos. SAVi++ (Elsayed et al., 2022)
159   uses Slot Attention to extract a set of temporally consistent latent variables while discovering and
160   segmenting objects with additional visual cues of the first video frame. STEVE (Singh et al., 2022)
161   combines the transformer-based decoder of SLATE (Singh et al., 2021) with a standard slot-level
      recurrence module to extract object-centric representations.

194   3     M ETHODOLOGY
195
196   In this paper, we propose SCR-CCR, an object-centric representation method for CCR. As shown in
197   Figure 2, SCR-CCR recognizes characters through two separate stages: the pre-training stage and
198   the slot-matching stage. In the pre-training stage, we train an encoder, ChSA, and decoder that can
199
      extract slots (i.e., object-centric representations) from the input character images. And in the slot-
      matching stage, we use the pre-trained ChSA to extract slots from the input and example images,
200
      and assign a category to the input by comparing their component slots.
201
202
203   3.1       P ROBLEM S ETTING
204
      Given an input character image, a CCR model is required to provide the class of the input. In most
205
      cases, the training and testing splits of datasets have similar distributions of characters. However, all
206   testing samples will not appear in the training stage in a more challenging zero-shot setting. The key
207   to zero-shot character recognition is transferring the ability of character recognition to novel cases,
208   which are not available in the model training. In the paper, a sample is a tuple (X, y) where X is
209   the input character image and y is the corresponding input type. We keep the training charset and
210   the testing charset disjoint for the zero-shot setting.
211

      3.3   P RE - TRAINING S TAGE
249
250   Since SCR-CCR performs recognize characters based on object-centric representations, we train an
251   encoder, ChSA, and decoder to parse slots from input images in the pre-training stage. For an input
252   image, the encoder extracts its visual features; the ChSA parser aggregates features that belong to
253   the same object based on visual clues to form slots; the decoder reconstructs the features of slots and
254   composes them into a complete feature map. The training objective of SCR-CCR is to reconstruct
255   the DenseRAN features, allowing the ChSA to output slots that can reflect meaningful components
256
      of the character.
257   Image Encoding. The encoder is responsible for downsampling the input character image to extract
258   visual features. Since the slot parser needs to assign each feature to one slot, the number of features
259   is an important factor influencing the computational efficiency of SCR-CCR. On the other hand, the
260   input character image may contain details that are not critical for recognition (e.g., stroke thickness
261   and color). By downsampling input images through the encoder, we can control the number of
262
      features and obtain more representative features for recognition. The encoder is randomly initialized
      and trained from scratch. Assuming that the input images X, the process of image encoding is:
263
264                                         F = Encoder(Scale(X)).                                         (5)
265
266

268   the complete character. ChSA is built upon the Slot Attention mechanism, where the features in
269   F are iteratively assigned to different slots through update aggregation. Most character recognition
      datasets provide additional auxiliary information besides character images and the corresponding

314   3.4   S LOT- MATCHING S TAGE
315
      SCR-CCR uses the pre-trained encoder and ChSA to extract test slots Ŝ from the test image X̂. A
316
      straightforward idea is that the distance between slots can reflect the similarity between the images.
317
      But as Figure 3 displays, even if we input the same character image, ChSA may output slots with
318   different orders due to the randomness of slot initialization. In this case, although the input images
319   belong to the same category, the distance between their slots can be quite large. To solve this
320   problem, SCR-CCR uses a fixed set of vectors sampled from standard Gaussian to initialize slots.
321   We find that the region focused by each slot is related to its initial state. If all input images share
322   the same initial states of slots, the acquired components will tend to have the same order. SCR-CCR
323   estimates the similarity of two images by calculating the distance (e.g., L2 distance) between the
      ordered slots.

353   In this section, we first introduce the experimental settings, including data construction and training
354   details. Then, we show some results of conducted experiments (additional experimental results are
355   shown in Appendix A.3). Finally, we conduct evaluation on Japanese and Korean characters to

358   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
359
360   Dataset Construction. In this paper, we mainly conduct experiments on two datasets: HWDB1.0-
361   1.1 (Liu et al., 2013) and Printed artistic characters (Chen et al., 2021). HWDB1.0-1.1 (Liu et al.,
362   2013) contains 2,678,424 handwritten Chinese character images with 3,881 classes, which is col-
363
      lected from 720 writers and covers 3,755 commonly-used Level-1 Chinese characters. Printed artis-
      tic characters (Chen et al., 2021) are generated in 105 font files and contains 394,275 samples for
364
      3,755 Level-1 Chinese characters. Some examples of each dataset are shown in Appendix A.1. We
365
      follow (Chen et al., 2021) to construct the corresponding datasets for the character zero-shot and
366   radical zero-shot settings. For the character zero-shot settings, we collect samples with labels falling
367   in the first m classes as the training set and the last k classes as the test set. For the handwritten
368   character dataset HWDB and printed artistic character dataset, m ranges in {500, 1000, 1500, 2000,
369   2755} and k is set to 1000. For the radical zero-shot settings, we first calculate the frequency of
370   each radical in the lexicon. Then the samples of characters that have one or more radicals appearing
371   less than n times are collected as the test set, otherwise, collected as the training set, where n ranges
372   in {10, 20, 30, 40, 50} in the radical zero-shot settings.
373   Training Details. SCR-CCR is trained using the Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2014) where the
374   momentums β1 and β2 are set to 0.9 and 0.99. For the encoder and ChSA, we increase the learning
375   rate from 0 to 10−4 in the first 30K steps and then halve the learning rate every 250K steps. For the
376   decoder, we increase the learning rate from 0 to 3 × 10−4 in the first 30K steps and then halve the
377   learning rate every 250K steps. The training batch size is 32, and the input image of SCR-CCR will
      be scaled to 80 × 80. We set the maximum number of slots as K = 3 in the slot-matching stage.

459
460   Previous radical-based or stroke-based methods rely on human-defined representations, such as rad-

462   ferent characters, which requires the model to extract different features from the same visual charac-
463   teristics, thereby hindering performance. In addition, stroke-based methods require perceiving fine-
464   grained stroke information, which is challenging for Chinese characters with complex structures.
465   In this section, we attempt to visualize the compositional representations learned by the proposed
466
      method. As shown in Figure 4, we visualize the regions attended by different slots for both printed
      and handwritten character samples. The visualization results reveal that each slot focuses on distinct
467
      and independent components of the characters. It is satisfying to note that despite the absence of any
468
      fine-grained supervision information (such as radicals or strokes), different slots can still effectively
469   distinguish different character regions. Therefore, the compositional representations learned in an
470   unsupervised manner from the training set characters can possess stronger generalization capabili-
471   ties, thus being more robust to zero-shot characters.

475
476   In experiments, we observe that there are two factors that affect the performance of SCR-CCR in
477
      inference: the number of slots and that of template character images used for matching. As shown in
      Figure 5, we have evaluated the performance of SCR-CCR with different slot and template character
478
      image quantities in the character zero-shot settings. On both HWDB and Printed characters, SCR-
479
      CCR exhibits better performance when the number of slots is set to 2 or 3. This also conforms to
480   the way that native Chinese speakers recognize Chinese characters, i.e., they tend to only focus on

482   the more template characters used in inference, the better the performance of SCR-CCR. Therefore,

484   the features of character images used for matching can be extracted in advance, there will be no
485   additional inference time when more template character images are used. More experimental results
      in the radical zero-shot setting are displayed in Appendix A.3.2.

      5     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we introduce the Self-learning Compositional Representation method for zero-shot
      Chinese Character Recognition (SCR-CCR) to address challenges in Chinese character recog-
532
      nition, particularly zero-shot recognition. SCR-CCR offers a unique solution by autonomously
533
      learning compositional components from the data, distinct from traditional radical or stroke-based
534   approaches. By following a pretraining-inference paradigm and leveraging Character Slot Atten-
535   tion, SCR-CCR excels in extracting relevant components for recognition. The experimental results
536   demonstrate that SCR-CCR outperforms previous methods in most scenarios of character and radi-
537   cal zero-shot settings. Particularly, visualization experiments reveal that the components learned by
538   SCR-CCR reflect the structure of characters in an interpretable manner and can be applied to recog-
539   nize Japanese and Korean characters. In essence, SCR-CCR not only advances the field of Chinese
      character recognition but also offers insights into broader applications across languages.
