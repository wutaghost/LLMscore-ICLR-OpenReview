011                                                  A BSTRACT
012
013            Distributed high dimensional mean estimation is a common aggregation routine used
014            often in distributed optimization methods (e.g. federated learning). Most of these ap-
015
               plications call for a communication-constrained setting where vectors, whose mean
               is to be estimated, have to be compressed before sharing. One could independently
016
               encode and decode these to achieve compression, but that overlooks the fact that these
017
               vectors are often similar to each other. To exploit these similarities, recently Suresh et
018            al., 2022, Jhunjhunwala et al., 2021, Jiang et al, 2023, proposed multiple correlation-
019            aware compression schemes. However, in most cases, the correlations have to be
020            known for these schemes to work. Moreover, a theoretical analysis of graceful degra-
021            dation of these correlation-aware compression schemes with increasing dissimilarity
022            is limited to only the ℓ2 -error in the literature. In this paper, we propose four different
023            collaborative compression schemes that agnostically exploit the similarities among
024            vectors in a distributed setting. Our schemes are all simple to implement and computa-
025            tionally efficient, while resulting in big savings in communication. We do a rigorous
026
               theoretical analysis of our proposed schemes to show how the ℓ2 , ℓ∞ and cosine
               estimation error varies with the degree of similarity among vectors. In the process,
027
               we come up with appropriate dissimilarity-measures for these applications as well.

032   We study the problem of estimating the empirical mean, or average, of a set of high-dimensional
033   vectors in a communication constrained setup. We assume a distributed problem setting, where m
034   clients, each with a vector gi ∈ Rd , are connected to a single server (see, Fig. 1a). Our goal is to
035   estimate their mean g on the server, where

039   We use [m] to denote the set {1, 2, ... , m}. The clients can communicate with the server via a
040   communication channel which allows limited communication. The server does not have access to
041   data but has relatively more computational power than individual clients.
042   This problem, referred to as distributed mean estimation (DME), is an important subroutine in several
043   distributed learning applications. Two common scenarios for these applications are distributed training,
044   when different clients correspond to different processors inside a datacenter or federated learning McMa-
045   han et al. (2016); McMahan & Ramage (2017), when different clients correspond to different edge
046   devices, for instance mobile phones. In distributed training, the communication channel is the network
047   inside the datacenter, while in federated learning, the communication channel can be the internet.
048   The typical learning task for DME is supervised learning via gradient-based methods Bottou &
049   Bousquet (2007); Robbins & Monro (1951). The vectors gi then correspond to the gradient updates
050   for each client i computed on its local training data and g is the average gradient over all clients. On
051   the other hand, distributed mean estimation is also used in unsupervised learning problems such as
052   distributed KMeans Liang et al. (2013) and distributed PCA Liang et al. (2014) or distributed power
053   iteration Li et al. (2021). In distributed KMeans and distributed power iteration, gi corresponds to
      estimates of cluster center and the top eigenvector respectively, on the ith client.

068   The naive strategy of clients sending their vectors gi to the server for DME incurs no error, however, has
069   a high communication cost, rendering it useless in most of the real-world network applications. A princi-
070
      pled way to tackle this is to use compression: each client i ∈ [m] compresses its vector gi into an efficient
071   encoding b̃i ∈ Bi which can then be sent to the server; The server forms an estimate g̃ of the mean g using
072   the encodings {b̃i }i∈[m] . We can then compute the error of the estimate g̃ and the number of bits required
073   to communicate b̃i (i.e., log2 |Bi |) to analyze the efficiency of the compression scheme. As opposed to
074   distributed statistical inference Braverman et al. (2016); Garg et al. (2014), we do not assume that gi are
075   sampled from a distribution, and instead the estimation error of these schemes is computed in terms of gi .
076   One way to approach this compression paradigm is when each client compresses its vector oblivious
077   to others, and the server separately decodes the vectors before aggregating (Figure 1a). We call this
078   independent compression and several existing works Konečný & Richtárik (2018); Suresh et al. (2017);
079   Safaryan et al. (2021); Gandikota et al. (2022); Vargaftik et al. (2021) use such a compression scheme.
080   The simplest example of this scheme is RandK Konečný & Richtárik (2018), where each client sends
081   only K ∈ N coordinates as b̃i , and the server estimates g̃ as the average of K-sparse vectors from each
082   client. As K < d, this scheme requires less communication than sending the full vector gi from each
083
      client i ∈ [m]. Note that independent compressors are a specific class among the more general possible
      compressors.
084
085   However, independent compressors suffer from a significant drawback, especially when the vectors to be
086   aggregated are similar/not-too-far, which is often the case for gradient aggregation in distributed learn-
087   ing. Consider the case when two distinct clients i,j ∈ [m] have different vectors gi ̸= gj , but they differ in
088   only one coordinate. Then, independent compressors like RandK will end up sending b̃i and b̃j which are
089   very similar (in fact, same with high probability) to each other, and therefore wasting communication.
090   Collaborative compressors Suresh et al. (2022); Szlendak et al. (2021); Jhunjhunwala et al. (2021);
091   Jiang et al. (2023) can alleviate this problem. Figure 1b describes a collaborative compressor, where
092   the encodings {g̃i }i∈[m] may not be independent of each other and a decoding function jointly decodes
093   all encodings to obtain the mean estimate g̃. Clearly, this opens up more possibilities to reduce
094   communication - but also the error of collaborative compressors can be made to scale as the variance
095   of the vectors instead of their norms. Whereas, in independent compression a lot of communication
096   is also spent in figuring out their norms separately.
097   The amount of required communication also depends on the metric for estimation error. Among
098   the existing schemes for collaborative compressors, most provide guarantees on the ℓ2 error
099   ||g̃ − g||22 Suresh et al. (2022); Szlendak et al. (2021); Jhunjhunwala et al. (2021); Jiang et al.
100   (2023). Also, in collaborative compressors, the error must ideally be dependent on some measure
101   of correlation/distance among the vectors, which is indeed the case for all of these schemes. In this
102   paper, the measure of such a distance is denoted with ∆, with some subscript signifying the exact
103   measure; the vectors in question have high similarity as ∆ → 0. The estimation error naturally grows
104
      with the dimension d, and decays with the number of clients m (due to an averaging). One of our major
      contributions is to design a compression scheme that has significantly improved dependence on the
105
      number of clients m to counter the effect of growing dimension d.

      Our contributions. We provide four different collaborative compressors, which are communication-
138
      efficient, give error guarantees for different error metrics (ℓ2 error, ℓ∞ error and cosine distance), and
139
      exhibit optimal dependence on the number of clients m and the diameter of ambient space B. To
140   see the advantage of collaboration, we define few natural similarity metrics. All our schemes show
141   graceful degradation of error with the similarity metric between different clients. Our schemes have
142   three subroutines: Init which corresponds to initial steps, Encode which is performed individually
143   at each client to obtain their encoding b̃i and Decode which is performed at the server on all the
144   encodings to obtain estimate of mean g̃.

531   5    C ONCLUSION
532
533
      We proposed four communication-efficient collaborative compression schemes to obtain error
534   guarantees in ℓ2 -error (SparseReg), ℓ∞ -error (NoisySign, HadamardMultiDim) and cosine distance
535   (OneBitAvg). The estimation error of our schemes improves with number of clients, and degrades with
536   dissimilarity between clients. Our schemes are biased and our dissimilarity metrics (∆reg , ∆Hadamard )
537   depend on the quantization levels. However, these can be improved by using existing techniques for
538   converting biased compressors to unbiased ones Beznosikov et al. (2022) and adding noise before
539   quantization Tang et al. (2023); Chzhen & Schechtman (2023). Lower bounds for collaborative
      compressors in terms of their dissimilarity metrics will allow us to assess the optimality of our schemes.
