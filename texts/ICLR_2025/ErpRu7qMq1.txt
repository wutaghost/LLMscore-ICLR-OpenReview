011                                                   A BSTRACT
012
013             Symbolic music generation aims to create musical notes, which can help users
014             compose music, such as generating target instrument tracks based on provided
015
                source tracks. In practical scenarios where there’s a predefined ensemble of tracks
                and various composition needs, an efficient and effective generative model that
016
                can generate any target tracks based on the other tracks becomes crucial. However,
017
                previous efforts have fallen short in addressing this necessity due to limitations in
018             their music representations and models. In this paper, we introduce a framework
019             known as GETMusic, with “GET” standing for “GEnerate music Tracks.” This
020             framework encompasses a novel music representation “GETScore” and a diffusion
021             model “GETDiff.” GETScore represents musical notes as tokens and organizes
022             tokens in a 2D structure, with tracks stacked vertically and progressing horizontally
023             over time. At a training step, each track of a music piece is randomly selected as
024             either the target or source. The training involves two processes: In the forward
025             process, target tracks are corrupted by masking their tokens, while source tracks
026
                remain as the ground truth; in the denoising process, GETDiff is trained to pre-
                dict the masked target tokens conditioning on the source tracks. Our proposed
027
                representation, coupled with the non-autoregressive generative model, empowers
028
                GETMusic to generate music with any arbitrary source-target track combinations.
029             Our experiments demonstrate that the versatile GETMusic outperforms prior works
030             proposed for certain specific composition tasks. Readers are invited to listen to our
031             music demos.1 We promise to open our code.2

034   1       I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Symbolic music generation aims to create musical notes, which can help users in music composition.
037   Due to the practical need for flexible and diverse music composition, the need for an efficient and
038   unified approach capable of generating arbitrary tracks based on the others is high.3 However,
039   current research falls short of meeting this demand due to inherent limitations imposed by their
040   representations and models. Consequently, these approaches are confined to specific source-target
041   combinations, such as generating piano accompaniments based on melodies.
042   Current research can be categorized into two primary approaches based on music representation:
043   sequence-based and image-based. On one hand, sequence-based works (Huang & Yang, 2020; Zeng
044   et al., 2021; Christopher, 2011) represent music as a sequence of discrete tokens, where a musical
045   note requires multiple tokens to describe attributes such as onset, pitch, duration, and instrument.
046   These tokens are arranged chronologically, resulting in the interleaving of notes from different tracks,
047   and are usually predicted by autoregressive models sequentially. The interleaving of tracks poses a
048   challenge of precise target generation because the autoregressive model implicitly determines when
049   to output a target-track token and avoids generating tokens from other tracks. It also complicates the

092

      source. Our co-designed representation and diffusion model in GETMusic offer several advantages

095

097

098    • GETScore is a compact multi-track music representation while effectively preserving interde-
099   pendencies among simultaneous notes both within and across tracks, fostering harmonious music
100   generation.
101    • Beyond track-wise generation, the mask and denoising mechanism of GETDiff enable the zero-shot
102   generation (i.e., denoising masked tokens at any arbitrary locations in GETScore), further enhancing

104
      We demonstrate that our proposed versatile GETMusic surpasses approaches proposed for specific
105
      tasks such as conditional accompaniment or melody generation, as well as generation from scratch.

130   2     BACKGROUND
131
132   2.1   S YMBOLIC MUSIC GENERATION
133
134   Symbolic music generation aims to generate musical notes, whether from scratch (Mittal et al., 2021;
135   Yu et al., 2022) or based on given conditions such as chords, tracks (Shuyu & Sung, 2023; Huang &
136   Yang, 2020; Dong et al., 2017), lyrics (Lv et al., 2022; Ju et al., 2021; Sheng et al., 2020), or other
137   musical properties (Zhang et al., 2022), which can assist users in composing music. In practical
138   music composition, a common user need is to create instrumental tracks from scratch or conditioning
139   on existing ones. Given a predefined ensemble of tracks and considering flexible composition needs
140   in practice, a generative model capable of handling arbitrary source-target combination is crucial.
141
      However, neither of the existing approaches can integrate generation across multiple source-target
      combinations, primarily due to inherent limitations in their representations and models.
142
143   Current approaches can be broadly categorized into two main categories with respect to adopted
144   representation: sequence-based and image-based. In sequence-based methods (Huang & Yang, 2020;
145   Hsiao et al., 2021; Zeng et al., 2021; Ren et al., 2020), music is represented as a sequence of discrete
146   tokens. A token corresponds to a specific attribute of a musical note, such as onset (the beginning
147
      time of a note), pitch (note frequency), duration, and instrument, and tokens are usually arranged
      chronologically. Consequently, notes that represent different tracks usually interleave, as shown in
148
      Figure 2(b) where the tracks are differentiated by colors. Typically, an autoregressive model is applied
149
      to processes the sequence, predicting tokens one by one. The interwove tracks and the autoregressive
150   generation force the model to implicitly determine when to output tokens of desired target tracks
151   and avoid incorporating tokens belonging to other tracks, which poses a challenge to the precise
152   generation of the desired tracks; the sequential representation and modeling do not explicitly preserve
153   the interdependencies among simultaneous notes, which impact the harmony of the generated music;
154   furthermore, the model is required to be highly capable of learning long-term dependencies (Bengio
155   et al., 1994) given the lengthy sequences. Some unconventional methods (Ens & Pasquier, 2020)
156   organize tokens according to the track order in order to eliminate track interleaving. However, it
157   comes with a trade-off, as it results in weaker dependencies both in the long term and across tracks.
158   Image-based methods mainly employ pianoroll representations which depict notes as horizontal lines
159   in 2D images, with the vertical position denoting pitch and the length signifying duration. However,
160   pianorolls need to include the entire pitch range of the instrument, resulting in images that are both
161   large and sparse. For instance, Figure 2(c) illustrates a pianoroll representation of a three-track music
      piece, which spans a width of hundreds of pixels, yet only the bold lines within it carry musical

      information. Most works focus on conditional composition involving only a single source/target
163   track (Dong et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2017; Shuyu & Sung, 2023) or unconditional generation (Mittal
164   et al., 2021) because generating a sparse and high-resolution image is challenging.

      Our proposed GETMusic addresses above limitations with a co-designed representation and a discrete
      diffusion model which together provide an effective solution to versatile track generation.

195    Mittal et al. (2021) first applied continuous diffusion models to music generation. However, due

206

207
      models. Meanwhile, our novel representation combines the strengths of conventional representations
208   while circumventing their shortcomings, thereby overcoming the obstacles associated with generating
209   high-quality content.

236   3.1   GETS CORE
237
238   Our goal is to design an efficient and effective representation for modeling multi-track music, which
239   allows for flexible specification of source and target tracks and thereby laying the foundation of the
240   diverse track generation tasks. Our novel representation GETScore involves two core ideas: (1) the
241   2D track arrangement and (2) the musical note tokenization.
242
243   Track arrangement We derive inspiration from music scores to arrange tracks vertically, with each
244   track progressing horizontally over time. The horizontal axis is divided into fine-grained temporal
245
      units, with each unit equivalent to the duration of a 16th note. This level of temporal detail is sufficient
      to the majority of our training data. This arrangement of tracks brings several benefits:
246
247    • It prevents content of different tracks from interleaving, which simplifies the specification of
248   source and target tracks, and facilitates the precise generation of desired tracks.
249    • Because tracks are temporally aligned like music scores, their interdependencies are well preserved.
250
251
      Note tokenization To represent musical notes, we focus on two attributes: pitch and duration,
252   which are directly associated with composition. Some dynamic factors like velocity and tempo
253   variation fall outside the scope of our study. We use two distinct tokens to denote a note’s pitch and
254   duration, respectively. These paired pitch-duration tokens are placed in accordance with the onset
255   time and track within GETScore. Some positions within GETScore may remain unoccupied by any
256   tokens; in such instances, we employ padding tokens to fill them, as illustrated by the blank blocks in
257   Figure 2(d). Each track has its own pitch token vocabulary but shares a common duration vocabulary,
258   considering pitch characteristics are instrument-dependent, whereas duration is a universal feature
259   across all tracks. To broaden the applicability of GETScore, we need to address two more problems:
260   (1) How to use single pitch and duration tokens to represent a group of notes played simultaneously
261   within a track? We propose merging pitch tokens of a group of simultaneous notes into a single
262   compound pitch token. Furthermore, we identify the most frequently occurring duration token within
263   the group as the final duration token. This simplification of duration representation is supported by
264   our observation from the entire training data, in only 0.5% groups, the maximum duration difference
265   among notes exceeds a temporal unit. These findings suggest that this simplification has minimal
266
      impact on the expressive quality of GETScore. Figure 2(d) illustrates the compound token: in the
      piano track, we combine three simultaneous 1/8 notes played at the first beat, namely, “La”, “Do”,
267
      and “Fa,” into a single pitch token indexed with “147” alongside a duration token “2.”
268
269   (2) How to represent percussive instruments, such as drums, which do not involve the concepts of
      ”pitch” and ”duration?” We treat individual drum actions (e.g., kick, snare, hats, toms, and cymbals)


                                                                                 5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      as pitch tokens and align them with a special duration token. The drum track in Figure 2(d) illustrates
271   our approach.
272
273   In conclusion, besides the benefits from track arrangement, our GETScore also gains advantages
274
      through this note tokenization:
275    • Each track requires only two rows to accommodate the pitch and duration tokens, significantly
276   enhancing the efficiency of GETScore.
277
      • The compound token preserves the interdependecies within a track. When a token is generated, the
278   harmony is guaranteed because the corresponding note group is derived from real-world data.

308   The denoising process Figure 3 provides an overview of GETDiff denoising a three-track training
309   sample of a length of L time units. GETDiff has three main components: an embedding module,
310   Roformer (Su et al., 2021) layers, and a decoding module. Roformer is a Transformer (Vaswani et al.,
311   2017) variant that incorporates relative position information into the attention matrix, which enhances
312   the model’s ability to length extrapolation during inference.
313   During training, GETDiff needs to cover the various source-target combinations for a music piece
314   with I tracks, represented as a GETScore with 2I rows. To achieve this, m tracks (resulting in
315   2m rows in GETScore) are randomly chosen as the source, while n tracks (resulting in 2n rows in
316   GETScore) are selected as the target, m ≥ 0, n > 0, and m + n ≤ I.
317   At a randomly sampled time t, to obtain xt from the original GETScore x0 , tokens in target tracks are
318   transitioned according to Qt , tokens in the source tracks remain as the ground truth, and uninvolved
319   tracks are emptied. GETDiff denoises xt in four steps, as shown in Figure 3:
320
      (1) All tokens in GETScore are embedded into d-dimensional embeddings, forming an embedding
321
      matrix of size 2Id × L.
322
323   (2) Two types of learnable embeddings, named condition flags, are added to the matrix. These flags
      differentiate whether a token originates from the provided source (“True”) or is generated by the


                                                                 6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      model (“False”). The rationale behind this design is as follows: GETScore is designed to mimic a
325   music score where each token, including paddings that signify rests or cadences, carries information.
326   Because the tokens predicted at the current time step can act as conditions in the subsequent time
327   step, any inaccuracies in these tokens can lead to deviations from the intended denoising direction
328   during inference, especially in the first few steps. Condition flags explicitly indicates the reliability of
329   the tokens upon which the model is conditioned, thereby enhancing the quality of generation.
330
      (3) The embedding matrix is resized to GETDiff’s input dimension dmodel using an MLP, and then
331   fed into the Roformer model.
332
333   (4) The output matrix passes through a classification head to obtain the token distribution over the
334
      vocabulary of size K and we obtain the final tokens using the gumbel-softmax technique.
335   GETDiff employs the x0 -parameterization (Austin et al., 2021) (see §2.2) and thus the training
336   objective is defined as Eq.2. The posterior computation for the Lvlb term in Eq.2 is provided by Eq.5.
337
338   Inference During inference, users can specify any target and source tracks. The resulting GETScore
339   representation is then constructed accordingly, denoted as xT , which contains the ground truth of
340   source tracks, masked target tracks, and emptied tracks (if any). GETDiff then denoises xT step by
341
      step to obtain x0 . As GETDiff generates all tokens simultaneously in a non-autoregressive manner,
      potentially modifying source tokens in the output, we need to ensure the consistent guidance from
342
      source tracks: when xt−1 is acquired, tokens in source tracks are recovered to their ground truth
343
      values, while tokens in uninvolved tracks are once again emptied.

      Training details We set diffusion timesteps T = 100 and the auxiliary loss scale λ = 0.001. For
379   the transition matrix Qt , we linearly increase γ t (cumulative γt ) from 0 to 1 and decrease αt from 1
380   to 0. GETDiff has 12 Roformer layers with d = 96 and dmodel = 768, where there are about 86M
381   trainable parameters. During training, we use AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of 1e − 4, β1 =
382   0.9, β2 = 0.999. The learning rate warmups first 1000 steps and then linearly decays. The training is

397   this metric is not suitable for the third task. Instead, melodic evaluation for the third task relies on

399

400   musical features in generated and ground truth tracks: note pitch, duration (Dur) and Inter-Onset
401   Interval (IOI) that measures the temporal interval between two consecutive notes within a bar.
402   First, we quantize the duration and IOI into 16 classes, then convert the histograms into probability
403   density functions (PDFs) using Gaussian kernel density estimation. Finally, we compute the KL-
404   divergence (Kullback & Leibler, 1951) KL{P itch,Dur,IOI} between PDFs of generated target tracks
405   and ground truth. For task 3, we compute KL{P itch,Dur,IOI} between PDFs of generated target
406   tracks and the corresponding distribution of training data. In this definition, KLP itch reflects the
407   melody consistency, whereas KLDur and KLIOI are indicative of rhythm consistency.

461   each musical piece using an Nvidia A100-80G GPU. Due to the considerably fewer denoising steps
462   (T = 100) compared to the extensive prediction steps required by an autoregressive model, GETDiff
463   exhibits notable speed advantages.
464
      While it would be more informative to evaluate diffusion models trained with traditional sequence
465
      representations, this approach is intractable. Firstly, due to the inherently higher computational
466
      resource requirements of training a diffusion model compared to an autoregressive model, coupled
467   with the fact that traditional sequence representations are usually orders of magnitude longer than
468   GETScore when representing the same musical piece, the training cost becomes unaffordable for our
469   source. Furthermore, diffusion models require the specification of the generation length in advance.
470   Yet, the length of traditional sequences representing the same number of bars can vary in a wide
471   range, leading to uncontrollable variations in the generated music’s length and structure.
472
      Based on above results and analyses, we contend that our GETScore and GETDiff together provide
473   an efficient and effective solution for versatile and diverse symbolic music generation.

509   We have discovered that certain learned features within GETScore closely align with principles of
510   music theory. For instance, although C and Cm share two same notes out of their total three, the

516
      boasting a similarity score of 0.43. These learned features within our GETScore closely adhere to

660   A       DATA DETAILS
661
662   Cleansing data We perform a data cleansing process by four steps. Firstly, we employ MIDI
663   Miner (Guo et al., 2019) to identify the melody track. Secondly, we condense the remaining tracks
664   into five instrument types: bass, drum, guitar, piano, and string. Thirdly, we apply filtering criteria to
665   exclude data that contains a minimal number of notes, has less than 2 tracks, exhibits multiple tempos,
666   or lacks the melody track. Fourthly, for all the data, we utilize the Viterbi algorithm implemented
667   by Magenta6 to infer the corresponding chord progression for each music piece. This serves as an
668   additional guide for composition. Further details are discussed below. Lastly, we convert these data
669
      into GETScores and segment them into fragments of up to 512 time units.
670
671   Chord progression A chord progression in music refers to a specific sequence of chords that are
672   played or harmonized in a particular order. Chords, which are combinations of two or more notes
673   played simultaneously, serve as the fundamental elements of harmony in music. The use of chord
674
      progressions is crucial in defining the harmony and structure of a musical composition. They play a
      significant role in determining the mood, emotional impact, and overall direction of the music. For
675
      instance, “C - Am - F - G” usually evokes an atmosphere of sadness and nostalgia.7 Consequently,
676
      chord progressions are commonly used as a guiding framework in the creation of music (Zhu et al.,
677   2018; Lv et al., 2022; Ju et al., 2021).
678
679   In GETScore, we arrange the chord progression as an extra track and it differs from regular instru-
680
      mental tracks in some aspects: Although certain commonly used chords may appear in specific
      instrumental tracks and have been represented as pitch tokens, we do not reuse these tokens to ensure
681
      that the chord progression track provides equitable guidance for each individual track. GETMusic
682
      incorporates 12 chord roots: C, C#, D, D#, E, F, F#, G, G#, A, A#, B and 8 chord
683   qualities: major, minor, diminished, augmented, major7, minor7, dominant, and
684   half-diminished. In the chord progression track, we allocate the chord root in the first row and
685   the quality in the second row. The chord track is entirely filled, without any paddings. Figure 5 is an
686   illustrative configuration of GETScore used for training.
687
688   Vocabulary In the last step of the cleansing process mentioned above, the construction of the
689   vocabulary is essential before converting music fragments into GETScores. In GETScore, each track
690   has its own pitch vocabulary, while the duration vocabulary is shared among all tracks. The maximum
691   duration considered in this paper is 16 time units, resulting in a total of 17 duration tokens ranging
692   from 0 (the special duration token for drums) to 16 time units. The final vocabulary consists of
693   17 duration tokens, 20 chord tokens, a padding token, a [MASK] token, an [EMPTY] token, and
694   specific pitch tokens for each track: 128 for lead, 853 for bass, 4,369 for drums, 1,555 for piano,
695   3,568 for guitar, and 1,370 for strings. In total, the vocabulary consists of 11,883 tokens.
696   After implementing rigorous cleansing and filtering procedures, we produced approximately 140,000
697   high-quality GETScores, equivalent to approximately 3,000 hours, with the maximum L as 512. We
698   sampled 1,000 GETScores for validation, 1,000 for testing, and the rest for training.
