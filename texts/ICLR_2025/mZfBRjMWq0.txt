009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Recent literature has effectively leveraged diffusion models trained on continu-
012            ous variables as priors for solving inverse problems. Notably, discrete diffusion
013            models with discrete latent codes have shown strong performance, particularly
014            in modalities suited for discrete compressed representations, such as image and
015            motion generation. However, their discrete and non-differentiable nature has lim-
016            ited their application to inverse problems formulated in continuous spaces. This
017            paper presents a novel method for addressing linear inverse problems by leverag-
018            ing image-generation models based on discrete diffusion as priors. We overcome
019            these limitations by approximating the true posterior distribution with a variational
020
               distribution constructed from categorical distributions and continuous relaxation
               techniques. Furthermore, we employ a star-shaped noise process to mitigate the
021
               drawbacks of traditional discrete diffusion models with absorbing states, demon-
022
               strating that our method performs comparably to continuous diffusion techniques.
023            To the best of our knowledge, this is the first approach to use discrete diffusion
024            model-based priors for solving image inverse problems.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Diffusion models have gained significant attention as deep generative models, achieving remarkable
031   success in image (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021b; Dhariwal & Nichol,
032   2021; Esser et al., 2024), audio (Liu et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024a), and video generation (Ho
033
      et al., 2022b;a).    These models operate by iteratively corrupting data then learning to reverse
      this corruption process, ultimately generating high-quality samples from noise. In parallel with
034
      continuous diffusion models, discrete diffusion models have emerged as a compelling alternative.
035
      These models have gained traction by demonstrating notable results not only in image (Gu et al.,
036   2022), audio (Yang et al., 2023), and text generation (Austin et al., 2021; Lou et al., 2023a) but also
037   in more specialized areas such as motion data (Lou et al., 2023b; Pinyoanuntapong et al., 2024),
038   protein synthesis (Gruver et al., 2024), and graph generation (Vignac et al., 2023).
039
      Building on these advancements, researchers have made significant progress in expanding the ap-
040
      plication of diffusion models. They have explored using diffusion models, trained either directly on
041
      pixel space or on latent representations derived from variational autoencoders (VAEs), to address
042   inverse problems (Kawar et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2023) and carry out vari-
043   ous conditional-generation tasks (Yu et al., 2023; Bansal et al., 2024; He et al., 2024) without the
044   need for additional training. These efforts aim to use the powerful generative capabilities of diffu-
045   sion models to tackle intricate problems and generate conditional outputs, all while preserving the
046   models’ original trained parameters.
047
      This line of work has been primarily restricted to diffusion models trained in continuous spaces, and
048   methods using trained discrete diffusion models as priors remain limited Gruver et al. (2024); Chen
049   et al. (2024b); Li et al. (2024). The inherent nature of the generation process in discrete diffusion
050   models involves non-differentiable operations, posing a challenge for their application to inverse
051   problems formulated in continuous spaces. Therefore, controlling discrete diffusion models often
052   necessitates an additional trained network (Gruver et al., 2024; Nisonoff et al., 2024; Klarner et al.,
053   2024; Vignac et al., 2023). Training-free methods have been confined to relatively low-dimensional
      data (Chen et al., 2024b) or to specific tasks such as image inpainting (Gu et al., 2022).

073   This limitation constrains the utilization of the powerful generative capabilities in domains such as
074   motion data, where generative models with discrete latent spaces have demonstrated remarkable
075
      success. This constraint motivates the exploration of discrete diffusion models as priors, given their
      potential advantages in representing complex data distributions and generating high-fidelity samples.
076
      To the best of our knowledge, in the context of inverse problems for motion data and image data,
077
      there exists no prior work that uses discrete diffusion models as priors.
078
079   Controlling the generation of diffusion models without additional training generally involves manip-
080   ulating the generation trajectory using gradients in continuous space. The primary objective here is
081
      to generate samples from the prior model that have high likelihood with respect to the measurement
      equation of the inverse problem or the guidance target. A specific approach to achieve this involves
082
      adjusting samples during the diffusion model’s generation process by using gradients of a loss func-
083
      tion that computes the likelihood. This methodology has been demonstrated in the works of Chung
084   et al. (2023b) and Yu et al. (2023).
085
086   We propose Gradient-guided Discrete Diffusion (G2D2), an inverse problem solving method that
087
      overcomes the aforementioned limitations while using a discrete diffusion model as a prior. Our
      focus is on solving image inverse problems using a generative model based on a discrete diffusion
088
      model specifically designed for images with discrete latent variables such as those found in vector-
089
      quantized (VQ)-VAE models. G2D2 overcomes the limitations of previous methods by using a
090   continuous relaxation technique to optimize the parameters of a variational distribution, effectively
091   bridging the gap between continuous and discrete domains.
092
093
      Discrete diffusion models for image data often use a variant called “mask-absorbing” due to its
      efficiency. However, this model has a significant drawback in inverse-problem solving. While a
094
      substantial portion of the image structure is determined in the initial stages of generation in discrete
095
      diffusion models (i.e., when only a few tokens are determined), the mask-absorbing type does not
096   allow transitions from an unmasked state to either a masked state or another unmasked state. Our
097   experiments show that this restriction imposes a significant limitation on performance.
098
099
      To address this issue, we use the star-shaped noise process previously proposed in the context of
      continuous diffusion models (Okhotin et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024). This process removes the
100
      dependency between consecutive sampling steps, thus expanding the solution space that can be
101
      explored. It therefore enables the correction of errors introduced in the early phases of sampling.
102   Originally proposed to enhance the performance of diffusion models (Okhotin et al., 2024), it was
103   later introduced as a decoupled noise annealing process in the context of inverse problems using
104   continuous diffusion models, demonstrating its effectiveness (Zhang et al., 2024). In this study, we
105   not only demonstrate that this process can be effectively applied to discrete diffusion models, but also
106   find that it uniquely addresses potential issues inherent in mask-absorbing-type discrete diffusion
107   processes, specifically the inability to correct errors introduced in the early stages of sampling during
      later steps.

      We conduct an experimental investigation to evaluate the performance of G2D2 by comparing it to
109   current methods using standard benchmark datasets. We consider methods that use both pixel-
110   domain and latent diffusion models. We also explore the application of a discrete prior-based
111   motion-data-generation model to solve an inverse problem, specifically path-conditioned genera-
112   tion, without requiring further training. The results of our study indicate that G2D2 shows promise
113   in tackling various inverse problems by leveraging pre-trained discrete diffusion models.

117   2.1   D ISCRETE DIFFUSION MODELS FOR IMAGE GENERATION
118
119   We first provide a brief overview of VQ-Diffusion (Gu et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2022), an image-
120   generation model based on discrete diffusion processes. VQ-Diffusion generates images in a two-
121   step process. It first produces discrete latent representations z0 using a discrete diffusion model
122   trained on representations obtained from a pre-trained VQ-VAE model (Van Den Oord et al., 2017).
123
      It then transforms these representations into the continuous image space using a decoder. Each
      element of z0 ∈ {1, . . . , K}dz corresponds to one of the embedding vectors from the codebook,
124
      denoted as B := {b1 , . . . , bK }, bk ∈ Rdb . During decoding, a variable Z ∈ Bdz is constructed
125
      through codebook assignment, where (Z)i = bz0,i and z0,i denotes the i-th element of z0 . This
126
      variable is then fed into a continuous decoder D : Rdb ×dz → Rdx0 to obtain the final image:
127
      x0 = D(Z).
128

130   resentation z0 , and a reverse process is learned to invert this process. A single step of the forward

      2.2   L INEAR - INVERSE - PROBLEM SETTINGS
163
164   Inverse problems involve estimating unknown data from measurement data. We specifically focus
165   on linear inverse problems in the image domain. The relationship between the measurement image
166   y ∈ Rdy and unknown ground-truth image x0 ∈ Rdx0 can be represented as
167
                                                y = Ax0 + η,                                            (4)
168
169   where A ∈ Rdy ×dx0 is referred to as the forward linear operator, which describes the process by
170   which the measurement data y are obtained from data x0 . We assume this operator is known. The
171   term η represents measurement noise, which we assume follows an isotropic Gaussian distribution
172   with a known variance ση2 . Consequently, the likelihood function q(y|x0 ) can be described as
173   N (y; Ax0 , ση2 I).
174
      One of the primary challenges in inverse problems is their ill-posed nature. This means that for any
175
      given measurement y, multiple candidate solutions may exist. To address this issue and determine
176   x0 , a common approach is to assume a prior distribution for x0 , such as a Laplace distribution.
177   Diffusion models have been utilized as more powerful and expressive priors, offering enhanced
178   capabilities in solving these inverse problems (Kawar et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2023b; Wang et al.,
179   2023; Rout et al., 2023). These methods are able to produce images that not only fit the measurement
180   data but also exhibit high likelihood for the prior model. Given a prior q(x0 ), the objective in the
181   inverse problem is to sample from the posterior distribution q(x0 |y), which, according to Bayes’
182   theorem, is proportional to q(y|x0 )q(x0 ).
183   These methods can be categorized based on how they incorporate the information from the mea-
184   surement data y into the generation trajectory of diffusion models. Methods such as denoising
185   diffusion restoration models (DDRM) (Kawar et al., 2022) and denoising diffusion null-space mod-
186   els (DDNM) (Wang et al., 2023) leverage the assumption of linear operators, using singular value
187   decomposition of the forward process to control the generative process. In contrast, methods such
188   as diffusion posterior sampling (DPS) (Chung et al., 2023b) and posterior sampling with latent dif-
189   fusion (PSLD) (Rout et al., 2023) operate by propagating the gradient of a loss term through the
190
      generative process. This loss term is designed to maximize the measurement likelihood, specifically
      by minimizing the term ∥y − Ax0 ∥22 .
191
192   However, the application of these methods to generative models that use discrete diffusion models
193   as priors is not straightforward. This limitation stems from two primary factors. First, with the
194   former methods, diffusion models are assumed trained in the pixel domain. Second, while the
195   latter methods can be extended to latent diffusion-type models, they encounter difficulties when
196
      handling discrete diffusion models, in which the generative process is inherently discrete. The core
      challenge lies in the lack of a direct mechanism to propagate gradients of the loss function through
197
      the generative process in discrete diffusion models. In such models, after generating discrete data,
198
      a non-differentiable operation (i.e., codebook assignment) is followed by a decoding operation into
199   continuous space, which prevents the application of conventional gradient-based guidance.

219   This observation highlights a problem for commonly used mask-absorbing discrete diffusion models
220
      in the context of inverse problems. The issue arises from the definition of Qt in (2), where βt is
      usually set to be extremely small. In the forward process, therefore, unmasked tokens are highly
221

222

223
224   This characteristic indicates that in the reverse process, the probability of unmasked tokens reverting
225   to masked tokens or transitioning to different unmasked tokens is negligible. Consequently, when
226
      sampling to satisfy the measurement model, errors occurring in the early stages of sampling become
      nearly impossible to correct in the later phases.
227
228   One solution to this problem is the “re-masking” operation, which reverts unmasked tokens back
229   to masked tokens. Similar approaches have been used with discrete predictor-corrector meth-
230   ods (Lezama et al., 2023) and predictor-corrector techniques for continuous-time discrete diffu-
231   sion (Campbell et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2024) to improve image-generation quality. However, those
232
      that involve reversing time steps can increase computational complexity. To address this, we demon-
      strate that by considering a noise process that is independent at each time step and different from the
233
      one used during the training of the prior, we can naturally resolve the inherent issues associated with
234
      mask-absorbing discrete diffusion models. We also show that despite this difference in the noise
235   process, the prior model can still be used within our framework.

      Gumbel-Softmax dequantization We use the Gumbel-Softmax trick (Jang et al., 2016; Maddi-
360   son et al., 2016) to make the computation of the second term in (7) differentiable. At time step
361   t, this process begins by generating Gumbel-Softmax samples using parameters of p̃α as follows:
362   ẑ0,i,k = softmax ((log αt,i,k + gi,k ) /τ ), where gi,k represents samples drawn from the Gumbel
363   distribution, and τ is the temperature parameter. This procedure generates a “soft” categorical
364   sample for each dimension in z0 , indicating the proportional selection of each codebook element.
365   As these proportions correspond to the contribution rate of each codebook element, we construct
                                                                  PK
366   ZGumbel ∈ Rdz ×db as their weighted sum: (ZGumbel )i = k=1 ẑ0,i,k bk . Finally, we pass ZGumbel
367   through the decoder to obtain the image x0 = D(ZGumbel ). By substituting this image into the
368   likelihood function q(y|x0 ), we have the differentiable objective with respect to the variational pa-
369   rameter αt , enabling continuous optimization. For linear inverse problems, the objective function
370
      will include the term ∥y − Ax0 (αt )∥22 , excluding the constant term derived from measurement
      noise.
371
372
373   Optimization initialization strategy At time step t, we are required to optimize the variational
374   parameter αt . To expedite this process, we can leverage the optimized values from the previous time
375   step as the initialization for the optimization process, effectively reducing the number of required
376   optimization steps. To achieve this, we introduce a forgetting coefficient γ and initialize αt through
377   a weighted sum of the previous optimized variables and the prior model’s output in the logarithm
      domain, given by αt = exp(γ log αt+1 + (1 − γ) log p̃θ (z0 |zt )).

521   5     C ONCLUSION
522
523   We proposed G2D2 for solving inverse problems using discrete diffusion models as priors. We
524   demonstrated that G2D2 effectively addresses the limitation of discrete diffusion in inverse problem-
525   solving by using a continuous relaxation technique and star-shaped noise process. Specifically,
526   G2D2 approximates the posterior in inverse problems by optimizing the parameters of a variational
527
      distribution, composed of parameterized categorical distributions, at each time step of the diffusion
      process. Our experiments show that G2D2 performs comparable to its continuous counterparts,
528
      opening up possibilities for training-free applications of discrete diffusion models across a wide
529
      range of tasks.
530
531   Limitations and future works G2D2 does not significantly surpass its continuous counterparts in
532   terms of computational speed or performance. We anticipate that these limitations can be mitigated
533   through the optimization of efficiency and the enhancement of prior models. The application to
534   more complex problem settings, including nonlinear inverse problems, as well as to other domains
535   such as audio and video, constitutes future work.

      A.1   L EVERAGING D IFFUSION M ODELS AS P RIOR M ODELS FOR I NVERSE P ROBLEMS
757
758   Pixel-Domain Diffusion Models for Inverse Problems Several methods have been proposed that
759   utilize pixel-domain diffusion models for solving inverse problems. DDRM and DDNM (Kawar
760   et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023) assume linear operators and known noise levels, leveraging the
761   singular value decomposition (SVD) of these operators. ΠGDM (Song et al., 2023a) can handle
762   certain classes of non-linear operators, such as low dynamic range, where a pseudo-inverse operator
763   can be defined. Notably, ΠGDM does not require SVD or gradient computations for such a case.
764   DPS (Chung et al., 2023b) broadens the applicability to cases where operator gradients can be com-
765   puted, enabling it to handle both linear and non-linear operators like phase retrieval and non-linear
766   blur. Other notable methods in this category include RePaint (Lugmayr et al., 2022) and RED-
767   Diff (Mardani et al., 2024).
768
769   Latent Diffusion Models for Inverse Problems Recent work has also explored the use of latent
770   diffusion models for inverse problems. PSLD (Rout et al., 2023) extends the ideas of DPS to latent
771   diffusion models, demonstrating provable sample recovery for linear inverse problems. ReSam-
772   ple (Song et al., 2024) achieves data consistency by solving an optimization problem at each step
773   during sampling.
774   Of particular relevance to our work is DAPS (Zhang et al., 2024), which, like our approach, adopts
775   a graphical model during sampling that differs from the one used during training of the prior model.
776   This approach, known as the noise decoupling scheme, offers new possibilities for adapting diffusion
777   models to various inverse problems.
778
779   Application of Inverse Problem Solving in Various Domains Solving inverse problems using
780   diffusion models has enabled various real-world applications. In the image domain, diffusion mod-
781   els have been extensively studied and applied to tasks such as image deblurring, super-resolution,
782   and inpainting (Lugmayr et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2023a; Zhu et al., 2023). In the audio domain,
783   methods such as those proposed by Song et al. (2021a), Chung et al. (2023c), and Bian et al. (2024)
784   have been developed to address tasks like dereverberation and audio restoration. Similarly, in the
785   medical imaging domain, approaches like those introduced by Song et al. (2021a), Chung et al.
786   (2023c), and Bian et al. (2024) have been used to improve image reconstruction and enhance di-
787
      agnostic accuracy. These advancements demonstrate the versatility and effectiveness of diffusion
      models across different domains.

790   A.2   C ONDITIONAL G ENERATION U SING D ISCRETE D IFFUSION M ODELS AS P RIORS
791
792   While our work focuses on inverse problems, it is important to consider related approaches in con-
793   ditional generation tasks using discrete diffusion models as priors. These methods, primarily devel-
794
      oped in the context of graph generation and protein design, introduce new conditioning to pre-trained
      models rather than directly addressing inverse problems.
795
796   The predominant strategy in this field involves training additional guidance networks. For instance,
797   in protein sequence generation, LaMBO-2 (Gruver et al., 2024) and Cemri et al. (2024) learn net-
798   works that evaluate how intermediate features of samples during generation achieve the desired
799   objectives. Similarly, CGD (Klarner et al., 2024) learns a guidance model for corrupted data. Other
800
      examples requiring additional training include Nisonoff et al. (2024) and DiGress (Vignac et al.,
      2023) for graph generation.
801
802   In contrast, Chen et al. (2024b) proposes a training-free approach to guide discrete diffusion models
803   for generating Electronic Health Record data. This method employs Langevin dynamics sampling
804   to minimize a given loss function by adjusting the parameters of the final layer of the prior model’s
805   transformer output. However, this approach faces scalability issues with models having large dis-
806
      crete latent spaces, such as VQ-Diffusion, as it requires evaluating all possible discrete states to
      compute the loss function.
807
808   Another training-free method for guiding generative models with discrete latents is proposed by Li
809   et al. (2024). This approach avoids gradient computation of the loss function, instead evaluating the
      loss on multiple generated samples and conducting sampling based on these values. However, like

       C.8   FAILURE MODES OF G2D2
1235
1236   We conduct an analysis of failure modes. Figure 8 shows the results of G2D2 and the images during
1237   inference for the Gaussian deblurring task on FFHQ. When the ground truth image is a relatively
1238   young (child’s) face, the generated face images appear to be drawn towards a distribution of more
1239   adult faces. This is likely due to the use of the prompt “a high-quality headshot of a person”. As a
1240   result, there is a consistent bias towards adult face images throughout the generation process, leading
1241   to artifacts in the final image. In the absence of a prompt, the intermediate generated images are not
       influenced by any specific textual guidance. As a result, the final image tends to have fewer artifacts.
