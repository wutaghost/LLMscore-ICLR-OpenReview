011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Efficient public transportation management is essential for the development of
014            large urban centers, providing several benefits such as comprehensive coverage
015            of population mobility, improvement of the local economy with the offer of new
016
               jobs and the decrease of transport costs, better control of traffic congestion, and
               significant reduction of environmental impact limiting gas emissions and pollution.
017
               Realizing these benefits requires carefully pursuing two essential pathways: (i)
018
               deeply understanding the population and transit patterns and (ii) using intelligent
019            approaches to model multiple relations and characteristics efficiently. This work
020            addresses these challenges by providing a novel dataset that includes various
021            public transportation components alongside machine learning models trained to
022            understand and predict different real-world behaviors. Our dataset comprises daily
023            information from about 710,000 passengers in Salvador, one of Brazil’s largest
024            cities, and local public transportation data with approximately 2,000 vehicles
025            operating across nearly 400 lines, connecting almost 3,000 stops and stations.
026            As benchmarks, we have fine-tuned diverse Graph Neural Networks to perform
027
               inference on vertices and edges, undertaking both regression and classification
               tasks. These models leverage temporal and spatial features concerning passengers
028
               and transportation data. We emphasize the greatest advantage of using our dataset
029
               lies in different possibilities of modeling a real-world urban mobility dataset,
030            reproducing our results, overcoming selected models, and investigating several
031            other open-problem situations listed in this manuscript as future work, which
032            include the designing of new methods, optimization strategies, and environmental
033            approaches. Our dataset, codes, and models are available at https://github.
034            com/suntdataset/sunt.git.

037   1    I NTRODUCTION
038
039   Efficient urban mobility requires a branch of strategies to manage traffic, delivering improvements,
040   such as increasing safety, reducing travel time, decreasing costs, and supporting environmental
041   issues. Each strategy has guided researchers to explore different proposals like vehicle-to-vehicle
042   communication, route optimization, adoption of the Internet of Things for connecting transit devices,
043   and effective scheduling of the public transportation system [Zhang et al., 2011, Rahmani et al.,
044
      2023].
045   In this work, we focus our investigation on the public transportation system due to its importance to the
046   population. Moreover, any decision regarding this system directly impacts urban mobility, especially
047   in developing countries, where it is often the only means of transport available to low-income
048   populations. When poorly planned, it delivers low-quality services with delayed and overloaded
049   vehicles, concentrates traffic in specific regions while leaving others unattended, and aggravates
050
      pollution with higher gas emission rates.
051   Intelligent Transportation Systems address these issues by incorporating data monitoring, heuristics
052   to extract new information, and Artificial Intelligence (AI) to support decision-making tasks. In
053   summary, such systems collect and analyze data from passengers and vehicles to uncover implicit
      patterns that can identify bottlenecks, sudden changes, and areas for improvement.


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      As discussed by Ceder [2016], achieving a successful public transportation system depends on three
055   key elements. Firstly, it requires gathering and understanding adequate data. Next, the data must
056   be utilized for intelligent planning and decision-making. Finally, the plans and decisions must be
057   effectively implemented to ensure smooth operations and control.
058
      To exemplify the adoption of such keys in real-world scenarios, researchers have collected data from
059
      passengers’ fares and vehicle locations to create a heuristic that estimates boarding and alighting
060
      times and locations in London (England) [Gordon et al., 2013, Wang et al., 2011]. Similarly,
061   researchers collected passenger and vehicle data from Harbin (China), which was later modeled using
062   unsupervised machine learning methods to understand public transit riders’ travel patterns better
063   [An et al., 2017]. Researchers from Seoul (the Republic of Korea) also developed a methodology
064   for estimating non-tagged alighting stop information gradually, by considering the characteristics of
065   trip types and utilizing transportation card data [Lee et al., 2021]. In New York (USA), researchers
066   analyzed data from the transit system, where riders swipe a fare card only when entering a station
067   or boarding a bus. They used this information to estimate alighting stops based on the bus boarding
068   locations [Barry et al., 2009]. The similar problem was addressed in Southeast Queensland (Australia)
069
      by using Deep Neural Network. This network was used to predict unknown alighting locations after
      being trained in a dataset with a combination of transactional and public transit network attributes
070
      [Assemi et al., 2020]. Although these aforementioned citations are more related to our work, further
071
      research on the problem of inferring boarding-alighting locals in public transportation systems is
072   detailed in a review published by [Mohammed & Oke, 2023].
073
074   After an in-depth investigation on published manuscripts focused on public transportation, we noticed
075
      a limitation on the availability of totally public dataset containing a comprehensive quantitative,
      spatial, and temporal information about passengers, vehicles, lines, stops, and stations. In this paper,
076
      we overcome this issue by making available a massive dataset with all the data from Salvador (Brazil)
077
      collected for five months in 2024. Salvador is the capital of Bahia, a state located in the northeast
078   region of Brazil. Salvador is situated in an area of approximately 694 km2 with a population of
079   around 3 million and a Gross Domestic Product (GDP) per capita near USD 4,220.18.
080
081
      Our dataset, referred to as Salvador Urban Network Transportation (SUNT), is organized into two
      parts: (i) a raw set of data that the reader can process according to their needs, and (ii) a graph
082
      connecting all these data, respecting their geospatial and temporal restrictions. In addition, we
083
      also shared adjusted models as benchmarks, since classical time series analyses to Graph Neural
084   Networks, to perform classification and regression tasks on vertices and edges. Benchmarking
085   public transportation datasets forms the foundation for developing effective, efficient, and sustainable
086   transportation systems that meet the evolving needs of society.
087
      In summary, the main contributions of this work are as follows:
088
089
             • A set of four raw datasets collected over five months with information about vehicles,
090
               passengers, and stops/stations;
091
092          • A preprocessed dataset as a complex network with several attributes derived from the bus
093            velocities, time and distance between stops, boarding-alighting information, and so on;
094          • A variety of models using time series and Graph Neural Networks, along with their parame-
095            ters, to ensure the reproducibility and improvements of our results;
096
097
             • A list of future investigations that can inspire researchers to use our dataset and increase the
               current features and information.

100   2    R ELATED W ORK
101
102   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have been widely considered in Intelligent Transportation Systems
103   (ITS) [Zhang et al., 2011] for addressing challenges such as more precisely representing complex
104   relationships between vehicles, stations, and passengers. This natural capability to model such
105   relationships and handle irregularly structured data has boosted their advancement in the field. Real-
106   world graph datasets of public transportation are vital in advancing research, innovation, and real-time
107   decision-making in route planning, scheduling, and resource allocation [Rahmani et al., 2023,
      Iliopoulou & Kepaptsoglou, 2019]. Constructing datasets is a significant challenge in developing


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      models for ITS due to the difficulty of collecting and integrating comprehensive data while also
109   respecting privacy when gathering information from traffic sensors, users, and GPS.
110
111   In addition, public transportation datasets play an important role in modeling spatiotemporal depen-
112
      dencies, enabling adaptation to dynamical change in network structures, identifying significant agents
      affecting trajectories, and accurately representing long-term dependencies. Currently, only a few
113
      publicly available ITS datasets exist with some of these features as, for example, TaxiBJ [Zhang et al.,
114
      2017, Bai et al., 2019], BikeNYC [Zhang et al., 2017, Bai et al., 2019], Shanghai Metro dataset [Xie
115   et al., 2023], Hangzhou Metro dataset [Liu et al., 2022, Ren et al., 2023, Xie et al., 2023], Beijing
116   Metro dataset [Zhang et al., 2020, Xie et al., 2023], Chongqing Metro [Xie et al., 2023], Stockholm
117   County [Klar & Rubensson, 2024], METR-LA [Jiang et al., 2023, Wu et al., 2019, Cini et al.,
118   2023, Du et al., 2021], PEMS-BAY [Wu et al., 2019, Chen et al., 2020, Shao et al., 2022, Oreshkin
119   et al., 2021, Cini et al., 2023, Du et al., 2021], and UVDS [Bui et al., 2021, Rahmani et al., 2023].
120   Therefore, most available datasets for graph-based transportation representation lack spatiotemporal
121   features and integration of multifaceted data necessary for effective adaptation in dynamic urban
122   environments [Hu et al., 2020, Poursafaei et al., 2022, Huang et al., 2024]. Moreover, recent studies
123
      on GNN advancement have underscored deficiencies in current benchmark datasets [Shchur et al.,
      2018], including limited graph availability, smaller scale in vertices and edges, and constrained
124
      class diversity. As a result, creating large, high-quality, and comprehensive graph datasets remains a
125
      significant challenge both for GNN research and for ITS applications [Li et al., 2024]. In addition
126   to providing a comprehensive dataset, this work offers a significant advantage by including a set of
127   pre-trained models as benchmarks. These benchmarks range from traditional time series models to
128   varied GNN architectures, as discussed in the following sections.

131   3     SUNT DATASET C ONSTRUCTION
132
133   The transportation used by the local population in Salvador comprises three systems: regular buses,
134   subway, and BRT (Bus Rapid Transit). The regular bus system is the most extensive transportation
135   in Salvador, serving most of the population. Currently, there are about 1,900 buses distributed on
136   approximately 400 lines with around 3,000 stops and stations, supporting roughly 470,000 passengers
137   daily. The subway system spans about 35 km across 2 lines with 20 stations. Approximately 210,000
138   passengers use this system daily. The BRT (Bus Rapid Transit) system was recently inaugurated,
139   further enhancing urban mobility and serving about 30,000 passengers daily. Currently, about 40
140   buses are operating on 3 lines and 20 stations.
141   Our dataset, referred to as Salvador Urban Network Transportation (SUNT), contains information
142   from all systems organized into raw and processed data. The processing steps considered in this work
143   explore concepts based on trip chaining, as detailed next.
144
145
146
      3.1   T RIP C HAINING
147
      In this study, we utilized an Automated Data Collection System (ADCS) to gather data from multiple
148
      sources [Mohammed & Oke, 2023], resulting in two distinct raw datasets. The first dataset was
149
      obtained from the Automatic Vehicle Location (AVL) system, which monitors all regular and BRT
150   buses, providing details about their geospatial positions over time. The second dataset, the Automatic
151   Fare Collection (AFC) system, contains information from the ticketing systems, recording the time
152   when users’ contactless cards are used for payments. In addition to the exact time of card usage, it
153   also includes details on the vehicles and their respective lines.
154
      Additionally, we used static data based on the General Transit Feed Specification (GTFS) format,
155
      which defines a standard format for public transportation schedules associated with geographic
156   information (http://gtfs.org/). Using this format, we provided geospatial details about
157   stations and stops along with their sequential order, lines, and directions. Finally, we also provide a
158   dataset containing Local Trip Information (LTI), which includes details about the expected and actual
159   departure and arrival times for all vehicles on every line and in each direction. Due to the dynamic
160   nature of data collected from the AVL system, missing data may occur, resulting in random loss of
161   information about vehicle activities. This issue can be easily addressed by combining redundant
      vehicle information from GTFS and LTI.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

      After organizing these four datasets (AVL, AFC, GTFS, and LTI), the first challenge is to find out
163   the boarding locations for all users. As illustrated in Figure 1(a), this information is computed by
164   integrating AVL and AFC, and retrieving the exact latitude and longitude positions when the users’
165   cards performed the payments. Using these positions, we can estimate the closest stop or station that
166   indicates the boarding local. Next, we merge multiple boarding locations to classify the users’ trips
167   as initial, intermediate, and final. Such a classification is relevant to map all possible connections that
168   compose a complete user’s trip. Finally, all boarding positions with their respective time instants are
169   used to organize trip chains describing the passengers’ behavior.

192   In the next phase, Figure 1(b), we assess the validity of the boarding registration by checking two
193   specific conditions. Firstly, a user’s boarding is discarded if the time difference between the AFC-
194   recorded fare payment and the AVL-recorded bus arrival at the stop exceeds a certain threshold. This
195   threshold has two possible values: (i) 20 minutes for bus stations; and (ii) 5 minutes for regular stops.
196   This differentiation is necessary because buses typically remain longer at stations. Secondly, another
197   discarding possibility happens when there is no direct connection between AVL and AFC records,
198   which is considered in this figure as “out of trip”.
199   In the subsequent phase, Figure 1(c), we analyzed user types to determine the feasibility of estimating
200   their alighting points. In Salvador, there is no device to validate the passengers’ alighting; therefore,
201   the main challenge is to estimate it by analyzing the following boarding. Moreover, it is impossible
202   to track older people because they are not individually identified. According to local policies, the
203   fares for such passengers are recorded as general users without identification. Consequently, we are
204   unable to estimate their alighting points. Another particular case that prevents us from identifying
205   users’ alighting points occurs when there is only a single trip registration on a given day. In such
206
      cases, we can only determine the boarding point, with no information available about the alighting
      point. Therefore, we cannot consider such situations in our analyses.

      3.2   G RAPH M ODELING
271
272   The organization of the OD dataset with passengers’ boarding and alighting allowed us to create
273   the SUNT dataset, embedding a set of quantitative, temporal, and geospatial variables as a complex
274   network. Formally, we have used information on latitude, longitude, and time to create a spatial-
275   temporal graph G = {G1 , G2 , ..., GT }. For all t = 1, ..., T , Gt = (V, E) stands for an attributed
276   and directed graph at time t, where V = {v1 , v2 , ..., vN } is the set of N vertices corresponding to
277   the bus stops and stations, and E is the set of edges corresponding to feasible routes. A directed edge
278
      (vi , vj ) ∈ E connects vertices vi , vj ∈ V if, and only if, there is a feasible route for the bus traffic
      from the corresponding station vi to vj in the network. Gt is a fixed graph structure since sets V and
279
      E do not change over time.
280
281   Figure 3(a) shows the map of Salvador with all vertices stored in our SUNT dataset, i.e., stops and
282   stations used by regular and BRT buses, as well as subways. The geospatial information allows us to
283   place them on the map, respecting their actual geographic position and the distances connecting them
284
      by the physical streets.

      In our context, spatial data do not depend on time t, i.e., their information is time-invariant. Specif-
305
      ically, in every vertex vi ∈ V , we store the following features: geographical position, number of
306   boarding and alighting per vehicle, and passenger load. The features specifically concerning edges
307   (vi , vj ) ∈ E include the distance between stops and stations, the trip duration, the mean velocity, and
308   the Renovation Factor (RF). The RF is a well-known metric used in transportation research to assess
309   the total demand in a line, i.e., it is computed on a set of edges that belong to the line [ITDP, 2016].
310   Formally, this metric is the ratio of the total demand of a line to the load on its critical link. Higher
311   renovation factors occur when there are many short trips along the line. Corridors with very high
312   renovation factor rates are more profitable because they handle the same number of paying customers
313   with fewer vehicles [ITDP, 2016]. Besides the individual features, there is relevant information
314
      shared by both vertices and edges, such as the number of passengers per vehicle, lines and directions,
      vehicle characteristics, altitude, and trips.
315
316   The black bounding box in Figure 3(a) represents an essential region of the city, which gathers
317   different lines and connections. Figure 3(b) zooms in this region with a portion of the full graph,
318   illustrating some bus stops as vertices and lines connecting them as edges. The red explaining box
319   contains some features related to that bus stop (vertex) such as its latitude and longitude position, and
320
      the amount of boarding and alighting passengers. In the green explaining box, we illustrate some
      features related to a line (edge), such as the distance connecting two stops, the mean velocity and trip
321
      duration among the buses in that section, and the total amount of traveling passengers.
322

      5-minute intervals. This interval allows the data to be represented as a temporal graph, in addition to

330   4    E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
331
332   Besides sharing SUNT, we have also trained models as benchmarks to demonstrate that valuable
333   insights can be derived from different learning tasks. In this manuscript, we focused on three particular
334   learning tasks: node classification, edge classification, and node regression.
335
      In our experiments, we created a temporal graph by selecting data from a work day (March 1st, 2024)
336   whose observations on passenger loading were collected every 5 minutes. To train the classification
337   models, we have selected the following features: passenger loading, mean velocity and distance
338   between stops/stations, total boarding and alighting, and number of lines, vehicles, and trips. The
339   labels were defined by computing the average loading in every node (stop/station). Next, we have
340   selected a single graph from another day (March 8th, 2024 at 7 PM) to test the performances of our
341   models. In this case, every node was encoded as “high” if the loading was greater than its average;
342   otherwise, we considered it “low”. The majority class is 60% for high loading.
343   Similarly, in the edge classification, we have considered the same data interval but labeled edges
344   according to the following rule: if the mean velocity (bus speed) in a route is greater than its average,
345   the label is “high”; otherwise, it was considered low. The majority class is 51% for low velocity.
346   We highlight that these data intervals were designed to address local demands and showcase the
347   capabilities of our dataset. Alternative configurations can easily be adjusted using our provided codes.
348   We have designed our experiments for node and edge classification using 10-fold cross-validation.
349   We considered the same validation metrics in both tasks to assess the obtained models: Accuracy, F1-
350   score, Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC), Precision, and Recall. Aiming to keep the manuscript
351   concise, other relevant metrics, such as the ROC curve and AUC, are shown in Appendix D, along
352   with their mathematical details and interpretations.
353
      Regarding the node regression, we have organized a set of experiments using minimal features,
354   thus making it possible to use traditional univariate time series analyses. Therefore, we predict the
355   passenger load in nodes and only use the distances between stops/stations to weigh edges. Due
356   to the temporal dependencies, we trained the models using a sliding window strategy by fitting
357   the model with 3-hour observations and validating the result with the subsequent one hour. As
358   previously mentioned, each observation summarizes 5-minute data. Further details on the process
359   of creating the time series are shown in Appendix A. The prediction results were assessed by using
360   traditional regression metrics (MSE, MAE, RMSE, MAPE, and R2 ), whose details are also discussed
361   in Appendix D.
362   For the node and edge classification, we have trained the following models: CHEB Defferrard
363   et al. [2016], GAT [Veličković et al., 2017] GCN [Kipf & Welling, 2016], SAGE [Hamilton et al.,
364   2017], S2 GC [Zhu & Koniusz, 2021], EGC [Tailor et al., 2021], A-DGN [Gravina et al., 2023],
365   LEConv [Ranjan et al., 2020], SuperGAT [Kim & Oh, 2022], and PAN [Ma et al., 2020].
366   In relation to the node regression, besides such models, we also trained other focused on data sequence:
367   SARIMA [Box et al., 2015], LSTM [Sak et al., 2014], GRU [Cho et al., 2014b], GConvLSTM [Seo
368   et al., 2018], GConvGRU [Seo et al., 2018], TGCN [Zhao et al., 2019], DCRNN [Li et al., 2017],
369   AT3GCN [Bai et al., 2021], CHRONOS [Ansari et al., 2024], and SOFTS [Han et al., 2024]. In
370   Appendix C, we provide more details about all models considered in our experiments.

466   6    C ONCLUSION
467
468   This paper introduced SUNT, a novel dataset collected from public transportation in Salvador,
469   Brazil. This dataset is notably relevant to the scientific community for supporting investigations
470   in several domains, such as planning public transportation, designing computational approaches,
471   and managing environmental impact. As previously mentioned, other researchers have published
472   related datasets, ratifying the importance of this subject. However, our dataset stands out due to its
473   massive information and complete availability. Unlike manuscripts that only share outcomes, we
474   have fully shared collections of raw and graph-based details of vehicles, passengers, stations, time,
475   and geographic properties.
476   We also analyzed SUNT using diverse time series and machine learning models to demonstrate the
477   feasibility of the learning process, showing that it is possible to derive valuable insights from our
478   data. To ensure the entire reproducibility of our results, along with all datasets, we have shared our
479   pre-trained models with hyper-parameters, architecture details, sources, notebooks, and experiments.
480   Potential Positive and Negative Impacts: Analyses from SUNT pave new ways to provide positive
481   social impacts, such as better planning the allocation of buses to lines, reasonably defining regular
482   and express trips, thus reducing traffic jams and carbon emissions, and offering better trip experiences.
483   About negative impacts, SUNT is not automatically updated, neither allowing the inclusion of new
484   data nor detecting general changes. However, we have locally designed a protocol to maintain and
485   evolve the datasets and benchmarks over time to attract new users and citations. Details about how
      we plan to keep and update SUNT are listed in Appendix E.

486
      Limitations: Although we have sought the best-known models in the literature, their architectures
487   and parametrization can be individually analyzed to improve the results. Secondly, we explored
488   a limited variation of attributes available in our dataset. Learning from other attributes or broad
489   combinations of them is also possible. Thirdly, we have analyzed three learning tasks based on
490   node and edge classification and regression. Researchers can also use different attributes and their
491   transformations as targets. Fourthly, other graph structures may provide important information,
492   mainly varying the edges’ weights. Finally, we have predefined some parameters related to the
493   application, such as 5-minute intervals and 1.1km walking distance, due to the particularities of our
494   local scenario. Such definitions may not attend other research. However, by operating our shared
495
      scripts used to create SUNT, readers can use the raw datasets to redefine them according to their
      needs.
496
497   Future work: By sharing SUNT and the pre-trained models, we expect to provide a robust dataset
498   along with baselines for the community, supporting the advancement of several investigation possibil-
499   ities like time-based models, graph algorithms, spatial approaches, deep neural networks, routing
500   simulations, and search heuristics. To illustrate such possibilities, we have listed future work that is
501
      worth investigating from our perspective: (i) GNN approaches designed to pass messages using both
      temporal and spatial information; (ii) Multi-objective optimization approaches to find the shortest
502
      path based on edges weighted by distance and time considering traffic jam; (iii) Multimodal GNN
503
      combines different features (e.g., temporal, spatial, numerical, and categorical data) with varying
504   encoding approaches as message passing; (iv) Queue theory to address the problem of attending
505   passengers from a stop A to B; (v) Concept Drift methods designed to identify when passengers’ pat-
506   tern changes in real-world automatically; and (vi) GNN to guide search processes in meta-heuristics,
507   e.g., in a multi-agent evolutionary algorithm, each agent handles a part of the search and, in each
508   generation, GNN could help to select the most suitable agent at each step of the evolutionary process.
509   In Appendices A and A.5, we present preliminary experiments that demonstrate the investigation
510   discussed in (v) and the integration with other urban datasets for various applications, respectively.

893
894   Passenger’s pattern behavior changes can be predicted via Concept Drift (CD) [Gama et al., 2014,
895   Bifet & Gavalda, 2007, Koh et al., 2024]. This technique models unforeseen changes in prediction
896   variables or relationships among input features over time to ensure machine learning models’ accuracy
897   and robustness in prediction. These changes can arise from various factors such as human behavior,
898   environment, or adaptations in the system being modeled. Concept Drift is particularly suitable for
899   dynamic environments, such as transportation systems. For instance, social or extraordinary events
900   can impact urban traffic and consequently influence passenger behavior during specific periods of the
901   day.
902   In Figure 10, we illustrate using black-dashed lines the usage of a CD method to detect when the
903   system changes its behavior (blue line) in relation to the prediction performances (red and yellow
904   lines). The CD method considered in this experiment was the Page-Hinkley test [Page, 1954].
905
      Another concept detection example is illustrated in Figure 11. In this case, rather than using CD to
906   monitor the learning performance of classification algorithm, we have directly assessed the passenger
907   load (referred to as “Amplitude”) between March, 2024 and June, 2024. To illustrate the benefits of
908   using such detectors, we selected a bus stop located near the Federal University of Bahia, the largest
909   university in Salvador. In the figure below, the red vertical dashed lines indicate the moments when
910   concept drifts were detected. The first drift corresponds to the start of the academic semester, marked
911   by an increase in student use of public transportation. The second drift captures the onset of a strike,
912   which disrupted classes and led to a decrease in passenger numbers.

914   as described in Algorithm 1. The first three lines perform a spectral analysis, extracting components

      observations are filtered based on whether the changes exceed a specified threshold.

960   Another important task in managing public transportation is the identification of outliers. There is a
961   conceptual distinction between CD and outlier detection [Gama et al., 2014]. CD refers to an actual

963   example, when the strike began, student behavior changed, leading to a decrease in the number of
964   passengers using public transportation, signaling a shift in the system. In contrast, outliers do not
965   signify a change in system behavior; they simply represent abnormal events that temporarily disturb
966   the system.

971   red dots). This example underscores the importance of outlier detection for automatically identifying
      abnormal events, thereby supporting policymakers in making more informed decisions.

1120   A.6   E XPLORING F UTURE W ORK
1121
1122   Multi-objective optimization problems aim at simultaneously optimizing two or more conflicting
1123   objective functions. It means that improving one objective implies worsening another. Several
1124   characteristics add to the difficulty of multi-objective problems. Firstly, these problems entail finding
1125   a set of optimal solutions, i.e., those that yield the best compromise among objectives. Secondly,
1126   the distribution of the efficient points in the objective space may make the intensification and
1127   diversification balance difficult. Thirdly, the difficulty increases with the number of objectives.
1128   Several graph-based multi-objective optimization problems have been investigated over the last
1129   decades, including spanning trees, shortest path, and maximum flow. The edges are assigned to two
1130   weights: the distance and the travel time between adjacent vertices, which may be anticorrelated
1131   values. The distance is geospatial information from the dataset, and the travel time is temporal
1132   information computed from the traffic jam. The multi-objective shortest path problem consists of
1133   finding the set of optimal paths from S to T (starting and target points) by simultaneously optimizing
       both the total distance and travel time.

1274   B.2       AUTOMATIC FARE C OLLECTION (AFC)
1275
1276   Automated Fare Collection (AFC) is a fare payment system through smart cards or smartphones [Am-
1277   pelas, 2001]. This process can occur either at the boarding or alighting, depending on the type of
1278   AFC system the vehicle operator implements. In addition, AFC also collects personal information
1279   and, in some scenarios, boarding and/or alighting locations [Mohammed & Oke, 2023].
1280   Salvador’s public transportation system collects data from buses, BRT, and subway passengers via
1281   AFC system. Concerning the buses, the collection occurs at two moments: when the passenger
1282   validates the ticket at the vehicle’s turnstile or a mobile turnstile. AFC collects subway data from a
1283   turnstile installed at the station entrance. AFC system in BRT combines the collection methods used
1284   in the buses and subway.
1285   Despite AFC operation in all transport systems, some limitations exist in collecting crucial data

       C.1        T IME S ERIES A NALYSES
1560
1561   The modeling process using univariate time series was performed considering two well-known
1562   methods: Seasonal Auto-Regressive Integrated Moving Average (SARIMA), Gated Recurrent Units
1563   (GRU), and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM). Although there are several methods designed to reach
1564   this objective, we have selected those as benchmarks for representing classical statistic-based and
1565   ML-based methods. In addition, recognizing the growing importance of foundation models in time
       series analysis, specifically those that can perform tasks without explicit training (zero-shot learning),


                                                                             29
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1566
       we also considered a language model adapted for time series forecasting, named CHRONOS. Finally,
1567   we used multivariate time series forecasting to make predictions on our dataset. This method is
1568   important for public transportation because it considers multiple factors at each moment, like different
1569   types of transportation (subway, BRT, and bus), to make more accurate predictions. For this, we used
1570   the Series-cOre Fused Time Series forecaster (SOFTS).
1571
1572   C.1.1    S EASONAL AUTO -R EGRESSIVE I NTEGRATED M OVING AVERAGE (SARIMA)
1573
1574   SARIMA comprises the combination of Auto-Regressive (AR) and Moving Average (MA) meth-
1575   ods [Shumway & Stoffer, 2011]. To understand these methods better, let Xt = {x0 , x1 , x2 , . . . , xt }
1576
       be a time series composed of observations collected over time (t). We can also express time series
       as a sum of non-observable components Xt = Tt + St + εt , such that Tt represents the trend, St
1577
       the seasonality, and εt the noise produced by a random process. Typically, Tt and St are seen as
1578
       deterministic, i.e., a given observation strictly depends on past ones. On the other hand, εt represents
1579   the stochasticity, being determined by probability density functions.
1580
1581   By considering the deterministic and stochastic components, one can model time series in terms
1582
       of q past random observations as Moving Average process, MA(q), xt = xt−1 + θ1 · εt−1 + θ2 ·
       εt−2 + · · · + θq · εt−q , such that {θq } are constants and {εt } are values produced by a purely random
1583
       process with mean E(Xt ) = 0 and variance var(Xt ) = σ 2 [Box et al., 2015, Shumway & Stoffer,
1584
       2011]. Time series can also be modeled by p past observations as an Autoregressive process, AR(p),
1585
       xt = ϕ1 · xt−1 + ϕ2 · xt−2 + · · · + ϕp · xt−p + εt , such that {ϕp } are constants and εt is value
1586
       produced by a purely random process with mean E(Xt ) = 0 and variance var(Xt ) = σ 2 [Box et al.,
1587   2015, Shumway & Stoffer, 2011].
1588
1589   These processes can also be combined to model time series according to q past noise values and
1590
       p past observations using an Autoregressive and Moving Average (ARMA) process, as shown in
       Equation 2. Formally, this process is represented by ARMA(p, q), in which p is the autoregressive
1591
       part AR(p), and q is the moving average one MA(q) [Box et al., 2015, Shumway & Stoffer, 2011].
1592
1593
1594           xt = c + ϕ1 xt−1 + ϕ2 xt−2 + . . . + ϕp xt−p + εt + θ1 εt−1 + θ2 εt−2 + . . . + θq εt−q      (2)
1595
1596   The ARMA process was designed to model stationary time series once their mean and variance do
1597   not vary over time [Box et al., 2015, Shumway & Stoffer, 2011]. In situations where the stationary
1598   restriction cannot be ensured, one may use an autoregressive integrated moving average process,
1599
       ARIMA(p, d, q), defined in Equation 3. In summary, the non-stationary source is removed by
       replacing xt with wt , such that wt = ∇d xt , thus providing a model for originally non-stationary time

1764   C.1.5    S ERIES - C O RE F USED T IME S ERIES FORECASTER (SOFTS)
1765
1766
       SOFTS [Han et al., 2024] is a model designed for forecasting multivariate time series, which are time
       series data that include multiple variables, or channels, at each time step. It works by combining
1767
       all series into a global representation and then redistributing this to improve channel interactions
1768
       efficiently while reducing reliance on the quality of individual channels.
1769
1770   Given historical values Xt ∈ RC×L where L represents the length of the lookback window, and C is
1771   the number of channels. The goal of SOFTS is to predict the future values Y ∈ RC×H , where H > 0
1772
       is the forecast horizon.
1773   SOFTS consists of the following main components. First, it normalizes the time series by centering
1774   them to zero mean and scaling them to unit variance. After forecasting, it reverses the normalization
1775   on the predicted series. Next, the model performs embedding on the lookback window, capturing
1776   the essential features of the data using a linear projection to embed the series of each channel to
1777   S0 = RC×d , where d is the hidden dimension.
1778   The embedded series then undergoes interaction through multiple layers of the STar Aggregate-
1779   Redistribute (STAR) module Si = STAR (Si−1 ), i = 1, 2, · · · , N . This module uses a star-shaped
1780   structure that allows information to flow between different channels, improving how they interact. The
1781   STAR module differs from other common methods like attention [Vaswani et al., 2017], GNN [Kipf
       & Welling, 2016], and Mixer [Tolstikhin et al., 2021], which use a distributed structure that depends


                                                          33
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1782
       on the quality of each channel. In contrast, the STAR module uses a centralized structure. It first
1783   aggregates information from all channels into a global core representation and then sends this core
1784   information to each channel. This approach reduces the complexity of interactions and decreases the
1785   reliance on the quality of individual channels.
1786
       After passing through N layers of STAR, a linear predictor generates the forecast. Considering the
1787
       output series representation as SN , the prediction is computed as Y = Linear(SN ).

1791
       The organization of the Origin-Destination (OD) dataset with passengers’ boarding and alighting
1792
       allowed us to create the SUNT dataset, embedding a set of quantitative, temporal, and geospatial
1793   variables as complex network. Formally, we have used information on latitude, longitude, and time
1794   to create a spatial-temporal graph G = {G1 , G2 , ..., GT }. For all t = 1, ..., T , Gt = (V, E) stands
1795   for an attributed and directed graph at time t, where V = {v1 , v2 , ..., vN } is the set of N vertices
1796   corresponding to the bus stops and stations, and E is the set of edges corresponding to feasible routes.
1797   A directed edge (vi , vj ) ∈ E, i = 1...N , j = 1...N , connects vertices vi , vj ∈ V if, and only if,
1798   there is a feasible route for the bus traffic from the corresponding station vi to vj in the network. Gt
1799   is a fixed graph structure since sets V and E do not change over time.
1800   In our context, spatial data do not depend on time t, i.e., their information is time-invariant. Specif-
1801   ically, in every vertex vi ∈ V , we store the following features: geographical position, number of
1802   boarding and alighting per vehicle, and passenger load. The features specifically concerning edges
1803   (vi , vj ) ∈ E include the distance between stops and stations, the trip duration, the mean velocity, and
1804   the Renovation Factor (RF). The RF is a well-known metric used in transportation research to assess
1805   the total demand in a line, i.e., it is computed on a set of edges that belong to the line [ITDP, 2016].
1806   Formally, this metric is the ratio of the total demand of a line to the load on its critical link. Higher
1807
       renovation factors occur when there are many short trips along the line. Corridors with very high
       renovation factor rates are more profitable because they handle the same number of paying customers
1808
       with fewer vehicles [ITDP, 2016]. Besides the individual features, there is relevant information
1809
       shared by both vertices and edges, such as the number of passengers per vehicle, lines and directions,
1810   vehicle characteristics, altitude, and trips.
