009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Optimizing objective functions under constraints is a fundamental problem in many
012            real-world applications. However, constraints are often not explicitly provided
013            and must be inferred from the observed behavior of expert agents. The problem
014            is known as Inverse Constraint Inference (ICI). A common solver, Inverse Con-
015            strained Reinforcement Learning (ICRL) seeks to recover the optimal constraints
016
               in complex environments in a data-driven manner. Existing ICRL algorithms col-
               lect training samples from an interactive environment. However, the efficacy and
017
               efficiency of these sampling strategies remain unknown. To bridge this gap, we in-
018
               troduce a strategic exploration framework with guaranteed efficiency. Specifically,
019            we define a feasible constraint set for ICRL problems and investigate how expert
020            policy and environmental dynamics influence the optimality of constraints. Moti-
021            vated by our findings, we propose two exploratory algorithms to achieve efficient
022            constraint inference via 1) dynamically reducing the bounded aggregate error of
023            cost estimation and 2) strategically constraining the exploration policy. Both algo-
024            rithms are theoretically grounded with tractable sample complexity. We empirically
025            demonstrate the performance of our algorithms under various environments.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Constrained Reinforcement Learning (CRL) addresses sequential decision-making problems within
030   safety constraints and achieves considerable success in various safety-critical applications (Gu et al.,
031   2022). However, in many real-world environments, such as robot control (GarcГӯa & Shafie, 2020;
032   Thomas et al., 2021) and autonomous driving (Krasowski et al., 2020), specifying the exact constraint
033   that can consistently guarantee the safe control is challenging, which is further exacerbated when the
034   ground-truth constraint is time-varying and context-dependent.
035
      Instead of utilizing a pre-defined constraint, an alternative approach, Inverse Constrained Reinforce-
036   ment Learning (ICRL) (Malik et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2024a), seeks to learn the constraint signals
037   from the demonstrations of expert agents and imitate their behaviors by adopting the inferred con-
038   straint. ICRL effectively incorporates expert experience into the online CRL paradigm and thus better
039   explains how expert agents optimize cumulative rewards under their empirical constraints. Under this
040   framework, existing ICRL algorithms often assume the presence of a known dynamics model (Scobee
041   & Sastry, 2020; McPherson et al., 2021), or a generative transition model that responds to queries
042   for any state-action pair (Papadimitriou et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023). However, this setting has
043   a considerable gap with scenarios in practice where the transition models are often not available,
044
      or even time-varying, necessitating agents to physically navigate to new states to learn about them
      through exploration.
045
046   To mitigate the gap, some recent studies (Malik et al., 2021; Qiao et al., 2023; Baert et al., 2023)
047   explicitly maximized the policy entropy throughout the learning process, yielding soft-optimal policy
048   representations that favor less-selected actions. Unfortunately, such an uncertainty-driven exploration
049   largely ignores the potential estimation errors in dynamic models or policies. To date, it still lacks
050
      a theoretical framework to demonstrate how well the maximum entropy approaches facilitate the
      accurate estimation of constraints.
051
052   In this paper, we introduce a strategic exploration framework to solve ICRL problems with guaranteed
053   efficiency. Recognizing the inherent challenge in uniquely identifying the exact constraint from expert
      demonstration, the objective of our framework is to recover the set of feasible constraints where each


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      element can accurately align with expert preferences, rather than to identify an exact constraint. By
055   explicitly representing these constraint sets with the reward advantages and the transition model, we
056   manage to confine the constraint estimation error with the discrepancy by comparing the estimated
057   environmental dynamics and expert policy with the ground-truth ones. This strategy provides a
058   quantifiable measure of error for our constraint estimation, linking it directly to a computationally
059   tractable upper bound.
060
      Under our framework, we design two strategic exploration algorithms for solving ICRL problems:
061   1) A Bounded Error Aggregate Reduction (BEAR) strategy, which guides the exploration policy
062   to minimize the upper bound of discounted cumulative constraint estimation error; and 2) Policy-
063   Constrained Strategic Exploration (PCSE), which diminishes the estimation error by selecting an
064   exploration policy from a predefined set of candidate policies. This collection of policies is rigorously
065   established to encompass the optimal policy, thereby promising to accelerate the training process
066   significantly. For both algorithms, we provide a rigorous sample complexity analysis, furnishing a
067   deeper understanding of the training efficiency of these algorithms.
068   To empirically study how well our method captures the accurate constraint, we conduct evaluations
069   under different environments. The experimental results show that PCSE significantly outperforms
070   other exploration strategies and is applicable to continuous environments.

073   2    R ELATED W ORK
074
075
      In this section, we introduce previous works that are most related to our algorithms. Additional
076
      discussions can be found in Appendix B.
077
078   Exploration in Inverse Reinforcement Learning (IRL). Compared with the exploration strategies
079   in RL for forward control (Amin et al., 2021; Ladosz et al., 2022), the exploration algorithms in IRL
080
      have relatively limited studies. Balakrishnan et al. (2020) utilized Bayesian optimization to identify
      multiple IRL solutions by efficiently exploring the reward function space. To learn a transferable
081
      reward function, Metelli et al. (2021) introduced an active sampling methodology that is designed to
082
      target the most informative regions with a generative model to facilitate effective approximations of
083   the transition model and the expert policy. A subsequent research (Lindner et al., 2022) expanded
084   this concept to finite-horizon MDPs with non-stationary policies, crafting innovative strategies to
085   accelerate the exploration process. To better quantify the precision of recovered feasible rewards,
086   Metelli et al. (2023) recently provided a lower bound on the sample complexity for estimating the
087   feasible reward set in the finite-horizon setting with a generative model. However, these methods
088   study only reward functions under a regular MDP without considering the safety of control or the
089   constraints in the environments.
090   Inverse Constrained Reinforcement Learning (ICRL). Unlike IRL which solely focuses on the re-
091   covery of reward functions, ICRL seeks to elucidate the preference of expert agents by inferring which
092   constraints they follow. The majority of ICRL algorithms update the cost functions by maximizing the
093   likelihood of generating the expert dataset under the maximum (causal) entropy framework (Scobee
094   & Sastry, 2020). This method can be efficiently scaled to both discrete (McPherson et al., 2021)
095   and continuous state-action space (Malik et al., 2021; Baert et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Qiao et al.,
096   2023; Xu & Liu, 2024). To improve training efficiency, recent studies combined ICRL with bi-level
097
      optimization techniques (Liu & Zhu, 2022; Gaurav et al., 2023). However, current ICRL methods
      have not explored exploration strategies or conducted theoretical studies about the sample complexity
098
      of their algorithms.

165   feasible cost functions based on two intuitions: 1) if a policy achieves higher rewards than the expert
166   policy (shorter path in Figure 1, left), the underlying constraints must be violated, and we can detect
167   unsafe state-action pairs by examining these infeasible trajectories; 2) if a policy achieves the same or
168   lower rewards than the expert policy (equal or longer path in Figure 1, middle & right), this suggests

170
      may not be violated. To minimize the impact of constraints on the reward-maximizing policy, ICRL

171
      Sastry, 2020). In this sense, policies in case 2 are not employed to expand the cost set.

529   7                                      C ONCLUSIONS
530
      This paper introduces a strategically efficient exploration framework for ICRL problems. We
531
      conduct theoretical analysis to investigate the influence of estimation errors in expert policy and
532   environmental dynamics on the estimation of constraints. Building upon this, we propose two
533   exploration strategies, namely BEAR and PCSE. Both algorithms actively explore the environment to
534   minimize the aggregated bounded error of cost estimation. Moreover, PCSE goes a step further by
535   constraining the exploration policies to plausibly optimal ones, thus enhancing the overall efficiency.
536   We provide tractable sample complexity analyses for both algorithms. To validate the effectiveness
537   of our method, we perform empirical evaluations in various environments. Several future research
538   directions deserve attention to address the limitations of this paper: 1) extending this work to
539   finite-horizon settings and deriving lower bounds for sample complexities, and 2) analyzing the
      transferability of constraint information.

847   B    A DDITIONAL R ELATED W ORKS
848
849   Sample Efficiency. Sample-efficient algorithms have been explored across various RL directions,
850   yielding significant advancements. To find the minimal structural assumptions that empower sample-
851   efficient learning, Jin et al. (2021) introduced the Bellman Eluder (BE) dimension and proposed a
852   sample-efficient algorithm for problems with low BE dimension. Liu et al. (2024b) introduced a
853
      sample-efficient RL framework called Maximize to Explore (MEX), which reduces computational
      cost and enhances compatibility. In the field of imitation learning, Liu et al. (2022b) addressed
854
      both online and offline settings, proposing optimistic and pessimistic generative adversarial policy
855
      imitation algorithms with tractable regret bounds. In the realm of model-free RL, Jin et al. (2018)
856   developed a Q-learning     algorithm with Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) exploration, achieving a
857
                        вҲҡ
      regret bound of T in episodic MDPs. Wachi et al. (2018) modeled state safety values using a
858   Gaussian Process (GP) and proposed a more efficient approach to balance the trade-off between
859   exploring the safety function, exploring the reward function, and exploiting knowledge to maximize
860   rewards. In the context of constrained reinforcement learning (CRL), Miryoosefi & Jin (2022) bridged
861   reward-free RL and CRL, providing sharp sample complexity results for CRL in tabular Markov
862   Decision Processes (MDPs). Focusing on episodic finite-horizon Constrained MDPs (CMDPs),
863   Kalagarla et al. (2021) established a probably approximately correct (PAC) guarantee on the number
      of episodes required to find a near-optimal policy, with a linear dependence on the state and action


                                                       16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      spaces and a quadratic dependence on the time horizon. From a meta-learning perspective, Liu
865   & Zhu (2023) framed the problem of learning an expertвҖҷs reward function and constraints from
866   few demonstrations as a bi-level optimization, introducing a provably efficient algorithm to learn
867   a meta-prior over reward functions and constraints. In terms of sample efficiency in IRL, (Lazzati
868   et al., 2024a) redefines offline IRL by introducing the feasible reward set to address limited data
869   coverage, proposing approaches to ensure inclusion monotonicity through pessimism. (Lazzati
870   & Metelli, 2024) extends IRL to Utility Learning (UL), introducing a framework for capturing
871   agentsвҖҷ risk attitudes via utility functions. (Lazzati et al., 2024b) tackles scalability in online IRL by
872   introducing reward compatibility and a state-space-independent algorithm for Linear MDPs, bridging
873
      IRL and Reward-Free Exploration (RFE). For misspecification in IRL, (Skalse & Abate, 2023)
      provides a framework and tools to evaluate the robustness of standard IRL models (e.g., optimality,
874
      Boltzmann rationality) to misspecification, ensuring reliable inferences from real-world data. (Skalse
875
      & Abate, 2024) quantifies IRLвҖҷs sensitivity to behavioral model inaccuracies, showing that even small
876   misspecifications can result in significant errors in inferred reward functions.
877
878   Constraint Inference. Constraint learning in reinforcement learning has advanced significantly
879
      to address shared safety requirements and improve scalability and efficiency. Chou et al. (2018)
      introduced a method to infer shared constraints across tasks using safe and unsafe trajectories,
880
      leveraging hit-and-run sampling and integer programming with theoretical guarantees. Kim & Oh
881
      (2022) proposed Off-Policy TRC, a sample-efficient RL method with CVaR constraints that addresses
882   distributional shift via surrogate functions and trust-region constraints, achieving high returns and
883   safety in complex tasks. To ensure stable convergence, Moskovitz et al. (2023) developed ReLOAD,
884   which guarantees last-iterate convergence and overcomes limitations of gradient-based methods in
885   CRL. For scenarios with unknown rewards and dynamics, Lindner et al. (2024) introduced a CMDP
886   method that constructs a convex safe set from safe demonstrations, enabling task transferability
887   and outperforming IRL-based approaches. Kim et al. (2024) extended IRL framework to infer
888   tighter safety constraints from diverse expert demonstrations, addressing the ill-posed nature of
889   constraint learning and enhancing multi-task generalization. Our approach infers a feasible cost set
890
      encompassing all cost functions consistent with the provided demonstrations, eliminating reliance
      on additional information to address the inherent ill-posedness of inverse problems. In contrast,
891
      prior works either require multiple demonstrations across diverse environments or rely on additional
892
      settings to ensure the uniqueness of the recovered constraints. This feasible set approach can focus
893   on analyzing the intrinsic complexity of the ICRL problem only, without being obfuscated by other
894   factors, resulting in solid theoretical guarantees (Lazzati et al., 2024b).

       Methods. We first train a Deep Q Network (DQN) in advance that stores the Q values of the
2007   constrained Point Maze environment. This DQN induces the expert policy at any given state. We
2008   also train a density model that accounts for calculating the pseudo-count of any given state-action
2009   pairs. The agent then collects samples from an unconstrained Point Maze environment where it could
2010   violate constraints. For algorithm BEAR, Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) is utilized to obtain

2012   policy, the action that has a high estimated cost or a high reward is assigned with more probability to
2013   choose from. After a rollout of this exploration policy, the density model and accuracy are updated

2015

2016   Point Maze. In this environment, we create a map of 5m Г— 5m, where the area of each cell is
2017   1m Г— 1m. The center of the map is the original point, i.e. (0, 0). The constraint is initially set at the
2018   cell centered at (вҲ’1, 0). The agent is a 2-DoF ball, force-actuated in the cartesian directions x and y.
2019   The reward obtained by the agent depends on where the agent reaches a target goal in a closed maze.
2020   The ball is considered to have reached the goal if the Euclidean distance between the ball and the goal
2021
       is smaller than 0.5m. The reward in the reward state cell is 1, while all other cells have a 0 reward.
       The cost in a constrained location is also 1. The game terminates when a maximum time step of 500
2022
       is reached. The state space dimension is continuous and consists of 4 dimensions (two as x and y
2023
       coordinates of the agent and two as the linear velocity in the x and y direction). The action space is
2024   discrete and at each state there are 8 permissible actions (8 directions to add a linear force), similar
2025   to the action space of Gridworld environment. The environment has certain degree of stochasticity
2026   because there is a sampled noise from a uniform distribution to the cellвҖҷs (x, y) coordinates.

2379   F    D ISCUSSION ON S CALING TO P RACTICAL E NVIRONMENTS
2380
2381   Sample complexity analysis has primarily focused on discrete state-action spaces (Agarwal et al.,
2382   2019). Existing algorithms for learning feasible sets (Metelli et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Lazzati
2383
       et al., 2024a) struggle to scale effectively to problems with large or continuous state spaces. This
       limitation arises because their sample complexity depends directly on the size of the state space, and
2384
       real-world problems frequently involve large or continuous spaces. Scaling feasible set learning to
2385
       practical problems with large state spaces remains a pressing challenge in the field (Lazzati et al.,
2386   2024b). One key difficulty is the estimation of the ground-truth expert policy, which is hard to
2387   obtain in an online setting. A potential solution involves extracting the expert policy from offline
2388   datasets of expert demonstrations. However, these datasets often contain a mix of optimal and
2389   sub-optimal demonstrations, leading to sub-optimal expert policies. Addressing this issue could
2390   involve: 1) treating the dataset as noisy and applying robust learning algorithms designed to handle
2391   noisy demonstrations, or 2) combining offline demonstrations with online fine-tuning, where feasible,
2392   to refine the learned policy. Finally, the scalability of learning in continuous spaces is frequently
2393   hindered by the curse of dimensionality. Dimensionality reduction techniques can mitigate this
2394
       challenge by simplifying state and action representations while retaining the features essential for
       effective policy learning.
2395
2396
       To enable our complexity analyses scalable to practical environments, linear Markov Deci-
2397   sion Processes (MDPs) (Jin et al., 2020; Yang & Wang, 2019) offer a straightforward yet robust
2398   framework by assuming that the reward function and transition dynamics can be represented as linear
2399   combinations of predefined features. This assumption allows for theoretical exploration of sample
2400   complexity. In future work, we plan to leverage the Linear MDP framework and its extensions (Jin
2401   et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2020; Du et al., 2021) as a foundation to design scalable methods for
2402   inferring feasible cost sets within the ICRL framework.
