011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Learning the spatial context of cells through pre-training may enable us to sys-
014            tematically decipher tissue organization and cellular interactions in multicellular
015
               organisms. Yet, existing models often focus on individual cells, neglecting the
               intricate spatial dynamics between them. We develop GeST, a deep generative
016
               transformer model that is pre-trained on the task of using information from neigh-
017
               boring cells to iteratively generate cellular profiles in spatial contexts. In GeST,
018            we propose a novel serialization strategy to convert spatial data into sequences, a
019            robust cell quantization method to tokenize continuous gene expression profiles,
020            and a specialized attention mechanism in the transformer to enable efficient train-
021            ing. We pre-trained GeST on a large-scale spatial transcriptomics dataset from the
022            mouse brain and demonstrated its performance in unseen cell generation. Our re-
023            sults also show that the pre-trained model can extract spatial niche embeddings in
024            a zero-shot way and can be further fine-tuned for spatial annotation tasks. Further-
025            more, GeST can simulate gene expression changes in response to spatial pertur-
026
               bations, closely matching experimental results. Overall, GeST offers a powerful
               framework for generative pre-training on spatial transcriptomics.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   In recent years, pre-training transformer-based models on large-scale scientific data have emerged
032   as a new paradigm in AI for biology (Webb et al., 2018; Bunne et al., 2024; Szałata et al., 2024), en-
033   abling the development of foundation models tailored to specific modalities such as DNA sequences
034   (Nguyen et al., 2024), proteins (Abramson et al., 2024), and single-cell gene expression (Theodoris
035   et al., 2023; Hao et al., 2024; Cui et al., 2024; Bian et al., 2024). However, most of these models fo-
036   cus on gene-gene relationships or products within isolated cellular contexts, neglecting the intricate
037   cell communications in spatial that is fundamental in multicellular organisms. As a result, current
038   models struggle to handle spatial tasks or understand spatial patterns, which limits their ability to
039
      fully comprehend and model cellular behaviors in complex tissue environments.
040   Spatial transcriptomic (ST) is an emerging technology that combines high-throughput gene expres-
041   sion profiling with spatial localization of cells within tissue sections (Moses & Pachter, 2022).
042   Beyond scRNA-seq data, where a cell is analogous to a sentence composed of gene tokens, in
043   spatial transcriptomics data, a tissue is a document consisting of many cell sentences. Rich ST
044   datasets enable us to learn cell-cell relationships in a data-driven manner. Previous studies such as
045
      GraphST(Long et al., 2023) and SpaGCN (Hu et al., 2021) often trained graph neural network to in-
      tegrate spatial and gene expression information. These models were trained independently for each
046
      dataset, leaving the paradigms of pretraining or generative modeling unexplored. A recent study
047
      called CellPLM (Wen et al., 2023) built a BERT-style (Devlin, 2018) pre-trained model by using
048   partial gene expression data from a target cell and information from its neighboring cells to predict
049   the remaining gene expression. However, since CellPLM needs to know the expression of a subset
050   of genes in a cell before predicting the cell’s overall gene expression, it cannot generate brand new
051   cells in unseen locations. This limitation restricts its ability to explicitly study how spatial context
052   alone influences a cell’s characteristics, which is crucial to understand the pattern of tissue function-
053   ality. In addition, constrained by the BERT modeling, its predictions are based on the existing input
      all at once, lacking the ability to iteratively generate new cells or adapt to dynamic spatial contexts.

054
      Inspired by the advancements of GPT models (Achiam et al., 2023; Radford et al., 2019; Brown,
055   2020), we endeavor to develop a generative pre-trained model on ST data to overcome these lim-
056   itations. Such a model can iteratively generate cells at unseen positions. It can further investigate
057   perturbation effects in spatial contexts by manipulating the given neighborhood information, provid-
058   ing an in-silico extension of current single-cell perturbation studies. However, GPT modeling on ST
059   data faces several unique challenges. First, there is no inherent order of cells within two-dimensional
060   tissue sections. While one solution can involve serializing spatial data into a fixed sequence, this ap-
061   proach fails to accommodate scenarios requiring different orders during inference. Therefore, a
062   flexible serialization strategy is essential. Second, cells in spatial transcriptomics data have continu-
063
      ous gene expression profiles. Unlike the discrete tokens in natural language, these continuous values
      may introduce error accumulation during the autoregressive generation (Figure A.1).
064
065   To address these challenges and support new applications such as in-silico spatial perturbation, we
066   present GeST, a deep generative pre-trained transformer that iteratively generates cells by leveraging
067   the neighbor information. To the best of our knowledge, GeST is the first generative pre-trained
068   transformer to understand cell-cell relationships and advance cell modeling in spatial context. Our
069
      experiments showed its superior performance across several downstream tasks. Our work makes the
      following key contributions:
070
071          • Spatial Serialization Strategy: We introduce a novel method for serializing spatial tran-
072            scriptomics data, coupled with a specialized attention mechanism called Spatial Attention
073            and a designed input sequence for the transformer. This ensures high computational effi-
074            ciency during pre-training and provides flexibility during inference.
075
             • Robust Cell Tokenization: We develop a cell quantization method to tokenize cells’ ex-
076            pression profiles, alongside a hierarchical pre-training loss designed to mitigate error accu-
077            mulation in autoregressive generation.
078
             • Transferable Performance: We pre-train a GeST model with 1 million parameters and
079
               demonstrated that the pre-trained model can achieve superior performance after being
080
               transferred to clustering and annotation tasks.
081
082
             • Pioneering Spatial Perturbation Analysis: We establish GeST as a pioneering model
               for in-silico spatial perturbation analysis, achieving substantial alignment with results from
083
               real spatial experiments.

088   Given a spatial omics dataset, we denote it as a set {x1 , x2 , x3 , . . . , xn }, encompassing all n cells

090   retrieval function, and s(·), the spatial information retrieval function. For any given cell x, the set
091   N (x) = {xN 1 , xN 2 , . . . , xN k } includes all k neighboring cells.

093   of a target cell x based on its spatial location. Instead of making a direct prediction based on the
094   spatial coordinate like P (g(x)|s(x)), we aim to predict the gene expression of a target cell x using
095   the spatial locations and gene expressions of its neighboring cells:

140   3     G ENERATIVE PRE - TRAINING AND FINE - TUNING METHODOLOGY
141
142   We introduce GeST, a spatial cell language model, with the following basic components: a cell ex-
143   pression quantization module and a transformer decoder. During pre-training, only the neighboring
144
      cells have complete information (both gene expression and spatial position tokens), while the target
      cells are provided only with their spatial position token, compelling the model to predict the gene
145
      expression at that specific location(Figure 1). Fine-tuning extends GeST to other downstream tasks
146
      with niche embeddings. We introduced these in detail in the following sections.
147
148
      3.1   S ERIALIZATION STRATEGY
149
150   At each training step, we first crop a square from the training tissue section, and all n cells X =
151   {xo1 , xo2 , xo1 , ..., xoN } in this square will be used to constitute a training sequence. We serialize
152   them by sampling along diagonal paths. Specifically, we first randomly select an anchor point p from
153   four vertexes of the square. Then we calculate all cells’ Euclidean distances from p and use them
154   as the sampling weights: {wo1 , wo2 , wo3 , ..., woN }, where woi = ||s(xoi ) − p||2 . We do sampling
155   without replacement in N times and thus get a sequence [x1 , x2 , x3 , ...xN ]. At each sampling time
156
      t, the probability of selecting cell xoi is:
                                                                    woi
157                                           P (xt = xoi ) = P                                             (5)
158                                                              j∈X \D woj
159   where D contains cells that have been selected into the sequence. This strategy allows the adjacent
160   cells in spatial to have similar indexes in sequence but still retain randomness to prevent the model’s
161   overfitting. Based on this, we design a novel attention mechanism to enhance the computational
      efficiency of the pre-training, as shown in section 3.3.

177   3.2   C ELL TOKENIZATION
178
179   Given a training sequence, we tokenize both gene expression and spatial position for each cell. For
180   the spatial position, we take the cell that is centered in the original tissue region as the origin of the
181   coordinate system. Then we normalized each cell’s coordinates by calculating the relative coordi-
182   nates to the origin. We tokenize the coordinate values by a two-dimensional sinusoidal positional
183   encoding (Detailed in A.3).
184   For gene expression, we found in preliminary experiments that directly generating continuous
185   single-cell expressions would cause error accumulation in the iterative generation process, even-
186   tually leading to model failure (refer to ablation study “w/o quantization” in Table 4). Therefore, we
187   propose to build a “meta cell vocabulary” to quantize cells’ continuous expression to discrete cell
188   states. Formally, given a training spatial dataset X = {x1 , x2 , x3 , . . . , xn } with n cells and T genes,
189   we first perform PCA reduction to p dimensions and categorize them into K clusters by K-means.
190
      The center point of each cluster contributes a “meta cell”, and there are two attributes of the meta
      cell vocabulary: the mean expression Cexpr ∈ RK×T and the mean PCs Cpca ∈ RK×p (Algorithm
191
      1). We note that this quantization of continuous value loses distance relationship in the expression
192
      space, i.e., two different meta cell labels may stand for either two very similar or two totally differ-
193   ent meta cells. Thus, we further perform K-means clustering on the meta cell vocabulary with few
194   cluster numbers to obtain hierarchical labels L1 , L2 , L3 at various levels. After that, for any input
195   continuous single-cell expression y ∈ RT , we can project its expression vector to PCA space and
196   retrieve the nearest meta cell. Then, we substitute the original expression with its corresponding
197   meta cell’s mean expression c ∈ RT as the actual input to the model (Algorithm 2).

      3.3   S PATIAL C ONTEXT-AWARE D ECODER
200
201
      Our main model is a transformer decoder (Vaswani, 2017) modified for the spatial generation task.
202
      During pre-training, the model’s input is divided into two contiguous sequences after tokenization:
203   the neighbor cell sequence and the target cell position sequence, constituting a sequence of N +
204   (N − 1) tokens. The output is a sequence of N − 1 gene expression (Figure 2a).
205
206
      Neighbor Cell Sequence. This part consists of the complete tokens of the first to the (N − 1)-
      th cells, totaling N − 1 cells. Each token combines both gene expression and spatial information:
207
      [gs(x1 ), gs(x2 ), . . . , gs(xN −1 )], where gs(xi ) = g(xi )+s(xi ) represents the combined embedding
208
      of gene expression and spatial position for cell xi .
209
210   Target Cell Position Sequence. This part has the spatial position tokens of the second
211   to the N -th cells, totaling N − 1 cells, formulating a target cell position token sequence,
212
      [s(x2 ), s(x3 ), . . . , s(xN )].
213   As illustrated in Figure 2b, for each target cell position s(xi+1 ), we use the transformer de-
214   coder’s parallel training capability to predict the gene expression of the next neighbor cell
215   g(xi+1 ). Prediction of target cell xi+1 is conditioned on the complete tokens of the neighbor cells
      {gs(x1 ), gs(x2 ), . . . , gs(xi )} and the spatial position token s(xi+1 ).

      To achieve this, we design a special attention matrix called Spatial Attention. Unlike the causal
217   attention used in language models, which employs a lower triangular mask to ensure that each to-
218   ken can only attend to previous tokens in the sequence, our Spatial Attention allows each position
219   to attend to specific relevant tokens, enabling the model to capture spatial dependencies more ef-
220   fectively (Figure 2c). Specifically, for a sequence length of 2L (where L = N − 1), the attention
221   mask M is a 2L × 2L matrix. For the token at position i + L (corresponding to predicting the gene
222   expression of cell xi+1 ), we allow attention to 1) The neighbor cell tokens at positions 1 to i (i.e.,
223   {gs(x1 ), gs(x2 ), . . . , gs(xi )}). and 2) The target cell position token at position i + L (i.e., s(xi+1 )).
224   Formally, for i ∈ [1, L], the attention mask M is defined as:
225
                                                   1, if t ∈ {1, 2, . . . , i} ∪ {L + i}
226                                  Mi+L,t =                                                                    (6)
                                                   0, otherwise
227
228
229   This design leverages the transformer decoder’s capability for parallel computation while effectively
230
      modeling spatial relationships (Radford et al., 2019). By allowing each prediction to attend to the
      relevant neighbor cells and the spatial position of the target cell, the model learns to generate gene
231
      expressions conditioned on spatial context. After the decoder, a multilayer perceptron is used to con-
232
      vert the hidden embedding h ∈ RD to gene expression space ŷ ∈ RT . Each element of prediction
233   ŷ represents the expression level of a specific gene, where T is the total number of genes.

283   3.5   N ICHE EMBEDDING EXTRACTION
284
285   GeST can be used for niche clustering in a zero-shot manner and can be fine-tuned for niche anno-
286   tation, both of which require the extraction of a niche embedding. Given a niche comprising a target
287   cell x and its N − 1 neighboring cells, we follow the pre-training setup to generate both position
288
      tokens and cell tokens as input. We include the target cell’s position token twice: once at the begin-
      ning and once at the end of all position tokens, resulting in a total of N + 1 position tokens. For
289
      the cell sequence, we incorporate the content tokens of all cells, yielding N tokens, as illustrated
290
      in Figure 3a. Under this configuration, the last output token of the model is generated based on the
291   information from all cells, and we define this as the niche embedding, consistent with Equation 4.
292   We use the hidden embedding h obtained here for the zero-shot niche clustering task.
293
294
      For fine-tuning, we input the sequence in the same setting and apply a pooling operation to aggregate
      these embeddings into a single vector (Figure 3b) hp = Pool({hx } ∪ {hn | n ∈ N (x)}). Mean
295
      pooling is default but we also compare it with max pooling in downstream tasks. The embedding
296
      hp is then passed through a linear layer to obtain the logit for the niche classification task.

299   4     E XPERIMENTS
300
301   We conducted four experiments to demonstrate our model’s capabilities: unseen region gene expres-
302   sion generation, zero-shot niche embedding clustering, fine-tuning based niche label annotation, and
303   in-silico spatial perturbation. In generations, we validated our model on three different datasets: a
304   large-scale MERFISH spatial dataset of the whole mouse brain (Zhang et al., 2023), a Visium hu-
305
      man primary liver cancer (PLC) dataset (Wu et al., 2021), and a stereo-seq brain dataset (Cheng
      et al., 2022). The MERFISH dataset contains more than 3 million cells and has two replicates, and
306
      we used the first Mouse1 and second Mouse2 replicate as the training and test set, respectively. The
307
      rest two PLC dataset and the Stereo-seq dataset are used to show GeST ability to handle irregular
308   spatial patterns and various resolutions. For the in-silico spatial perturbation experiment, we ap-
309   plied our pre-trained model to simulate the mouse brain ischemic process. We used a real ischemic
310   spatial dataset (Han et al., 2024) as a reference, providing a basis for ischemia-induced genes as
311   ground truth. Due to differences in resolution and gene panel between our training and the reference
312   datasets, instead of applying our model to this dataset, we performed in-situ in-silico gene pertur-
313   bation on a section of our training dataset that closely matched the spatial location of this ischemic
314   dataset. This allowed us to identify proposed ischemia-induced genes and compare our findings with
315   established results. More details can be found in Section 4.4.
316   We implemented GeST with 8 Transformer layers and 8 heads per layer, having about 1 million
317   parameters. Each training sequence comes from a sampled square region with a side length of
318   600µm. We used PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019) and trained the model on four NVIDIA A800 GPUs.
319   The pre-training process took approximately 3 hours.

      4.2   U NSUPERVISED NICHE CLUSTERING
386
387   A key feature of our model is its ability to learn the spatial context of neighboring cells during pre-
388   training. We evaluated this capability on spatial clustering and annotation tasks. We used two levels
389   of anatomical labels, “Division” and “Region”, provided by the Mouse Brain Common Coordinate
390   Framework (CCF) v3 (Wang et al., 2020), as ground truth. For the niche clustering task, we used
391   niche-level embeddings of each cell for clustering. We compared our method with three methods:
392   NicheCompass (Birk et al., 2024), STAGATE (Dong & Zhang, 2022), GraphST (Long et al., 2023)
393   and SpaGCN(Hu et al., 2021). We also included a baseline that gets clusters based solely on the cell’s
394   own gene expression data (Raw). All spatial clustering methods outperformed the raw baseline,
395
      with our model achieving the highest adjusted mutual information (AMI) scores at both resolutions
      (Table 1 and Figure A.6). These results demonstrate that our pre-trained model can be effectively
396
      transferred to new tissues in a zero-shot manner. We also noted that after we continued training our
397
      model on the test data in the same generative way, the model showed an even higher performance.
398
399   Unlike previous methods, our model allows control over the scope of niche information used to
400   generate niche embeddings. To investigate this, we used the same pre-trained model but varied the
401
      neighborhood window size during inference to 200µm, 400µm, and 600µm. As shown in Table A.2,
      increasing the window size improved clustering performance, a trend observed in both zero-shot
402
      and fine-tuning scenarios. These findings indicate that our pre-trained model is an effective niche
403
      embedding extractor and benefits from incorporating larger neighborhood information.

417   4.4   I N - SILICO SPATIAL PERTURBATION
418
      GeST is a pioneer model for predicting cell response of in-silico perturbation in spatial transcrip-
419
      tomic. Inspired by single-cell large models (Theodoris et al., 2023), we proposed a design for
420   in-silico spatial perturbations: Firstly, select a region of interest (ROI) and generate the surrounding
421   cell expression as an in-silico control group. Next, simulate the perturbation by modifying specific
422   gene expression of cells in that ROI, and predict the expression profiles at the same surrounding
423   positions to obtain an in-silico perturbation group (Figure 5a). By analyzing these simulation data,
424   we could study the impact of the perturbations in spatial context.
425
      Here, we demonstrated an in-silico spatial perturbation experiment of an ischemic condition of the
426   mouse brain. Recently, Han et al. (2024) measured gene expression and cell distribution in the
427   ischemic mouse brain and identified several ischemic regions including the infarct core area (ICA)
428   and the proximal region of the peri-infarct area (PIA P). In our experiment, we selected an area with
429   a similar location of ICA from sample 49 from Mouse1 as ROI and generated the surrounding cell
430   expression as the control group, representing the normal gene expression around the ROI. In order
431   to simulate the ischemic effect, we manually altered gene expression in this ROI according to the
      differentially expressed genes (DEGs) of ICA through in-silico activation and in-silico inhibition


                                                         8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      (Detailed in A.5). We then fed the model with the same surrounding positions and obtained the gene
433   expression prediction as the perturbation group, representing the predicted PIA P.
434
435   To validate the results, we calculated the Pearson correlation coefficients (PCCs) between gene
436
      expression of the perturbation group and the average expression of PIA P, and compared them with
      those between control group and PIA P. The PCCs are significantly higher in the perturbation group
437
      (Figure 5b). Taking all 87 high and low DEGs in PIA P as ground truth, we correctly classified
438
      70.11% of them by our in-silico perturbation experiment (Detailed in A.3). This is higher than the
439   baseline accuracy of 44.8%, which is obtained by simply adopting the DEGs from ICA (i.e. a naive
440   model that believes changes in ROI are the same in the neighbor). For example, Rnh1, a gene for
441   normal homeostasis of the brain (Hedberg-Oldfors et al., 2023), and Neurod6, a key gene in the
442   development and function of the central nervous system (Tutukova et al., 2021), were not modified
443   in ROI, but we predicted them as the high/low DEG in the neighbor area (Figure 5c,d). These
444   findings are consistent with Han et al. (2024). Taken together, GeST demonstrates the ability to
445   simulate perturbation in spatial transcriptomics.

      6   C ONCLUSION
524
525
      Understanding the spatial context of cells is critical for deciphering tissue organization mechanisms
526
      (Palla et al., 2022) and has the potential to facilitate the identification of therapeutic biomarkers
527
      (Zhang et al., 2022). In this work, we introduced GeST, a novel deep generative pre-trained trans-
528   former model that leverages spatial neighbor information, marking the first generative model in the
529   spatial transcriptomics field. GeST employs a cell quantization module to overcome the challenge
530   of error accumulation during generation and utilizes an ordinal serialization strategy with an effi-
531   cient attention mask design to model two-dimensional data in a sequential generative pretraining
532   framework. Our results demonstrate that this generative task enables the model to learn underlying
533   spatial contexts, thereby enhancing performance on niche-level tasks. To the best of our knowledge,
534   GeST is the first data-driven model to explore perturbation effects in spatial transcriptomic, laying
535   the groundwork for building more comprehensive foundation models for spatial biology.
536   Despite its advancements, GeST has certain limitations that warrant further investigation. The short-
537   age of large-scale reference ST data may restrict the model’s ability generalizability across diverse
538   tissue types. Besides, our current design does not fully account for dynamic gene-gene interac-
539   tions which similfies the biological mechanism. Future work could integrate GeST with single-cell
      foundation models to capture both intrinsic and extrinsic cellular characteristics.

661
      CCF, and the z coordinates are estimated. Then the multi-tissue annotation can be obtained based
      on the locations. Since the spatial coordinates value is an important guidance to our model, we only
662
      used the measured x and y coordinates in our training. And we selected the first coronal replicate
663
      which has the most number of cells as the training data, and used another one as the test data. The
664   first Mouse1 dataset encompasses 2.8 million cells across 147 coronal sections, each annotated with
665   a panel of 1122 genes. The Mouse2 dataset is from a separate mouse brain replicate, comprising 1.2
666   million cells across 66 coronal sections.

668
      (HCC-1 to HCC-5), one case of intrahepatic cholangiocarcinoma (ICC-1) and one case of combined
669
      hepatocellular and cholangiocarcinoma (cHC-1), containing 84,823 spots in total. We selected one
670   slice (HCC-1L, where L represents the leading-edge section) as the test set, and took the other 20
671   slices as the training set. Since the data volume of PLC by Visium is much less than the mouse brain
672   datasets by MERFISH, we trained a GeST model with fewer layers and heads (4 transformer layers
673   and 4 heads per layer). The slice for evaluation, HCC-1L, measured the spatial gene expression
674   from tumor to normal tissue of one patient. We cropped an area of 100 spots containing the edge of
675   the tumor as unseen spots (labeled as “Test”), and took all the other spots as seen spots (labeled as
676   “Ref”) (Figure A.3). After pretraining on 20 slices, we applied GeST to generate gene expression
677   at the location of unseen spots based on the information of the rest seen spots. We visualized the
678
      meta-cell on the UMAP and results showed that it can well preserve the original data space (Figure
      A.9).

681   spots. Each spot is bin50 (25 µm), a typical resolution used for analyzing stereo-seq data. We
682   segmented the cortex region from the right top corner as the test data, and used the rest of the tissue
683   as the training data. We train GeST with the default model size.

      the MERFISH dataset, we used the intersection of both dataset in the in-silico spatial perturbation
      experiment.

693   A.2   I TERATIVE CELL GENERATION
694
      Our model generated unseen cells based on the information from seen cells within a given win-
695
      dow size. If the unseen region is larger than the given window size, an iterative generation is
696   needed (as shown in Figure A.10). Given the spatial location of all unseen cells Xunseen =
697   {s(xu1 ), s(xu2 ), ..., s(xuN )}, in each iterative round, we first find a subset of the seen cells, Xs
698   from all seen cells Xseen . Each cell in Xs has at least one unseen cell in its available window. These
699   cells will be used as the reference in this round. For each cell xs in Xs , we generate expression for

713   A.3    2D SINUSOID POSITIONAL ENCODING
714
715   We first unified the spatial coordinates to millimeters based on the CCF information. Since the abso-
716   lute values of spatial coordinates in different slices are not comparable, for each training sequence,
717   we converted the coordinates of each cell into relative coordinates. Specifically, the coordinate value
718   of the central cell is subtracted from each cell. Then we anchored each coordinate into integers in
719   a fixed range. In our experiments, we use [0,200) as default. Then we use 2D sinusoidal posi-
720
      tional encodings to encode the two-dimensional coordinates into high dimension embeddings. Our
      approach is inspired by the method proposed in CellPLM, which employs sinusoidal functions to
721
      encode spatial coordinates in two dimensions. The encoding for a cell located at coordinates (x, y)
722
      is formulated as:
