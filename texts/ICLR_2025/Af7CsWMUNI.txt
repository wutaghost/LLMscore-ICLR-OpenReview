009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
                   Large language models (LLMs) have exhibited impressive capability of In-Context
012                Learning (ICL), where LLMs perform relatively complicated tasks beyond the
013                pre-training objective by conditioning on the given demonstrations. Nevertheless,
014                ICL introduces two gaps between pre-training and inference: label appearance
015                (presence of inserted labels in the demonstrations) and weak semantic relevance
016                (independently sampled demonstrations exhibit less semantic coherence compared
017                to consecutive text segments in pretraining corpora). We propose a new inference
018                method that only use unlabeled inputs from the test set and label space. In this
019                method, we extract the representations of the demonstrations inputs independently
020
                   and fuse them to reshape the representation of the test input for inference. Inter-
                   estingly, without access to labels, our method outperforms traditional ICL with
021
                   extra information of gold labels. Furthermore, our method allows small models
022
                   to outperform the zero-shot performance of models that are twice their size (e.g.,
023                GPT-Neo-2.7B surpasses Llama2-7B, and Llama2-7B outperforms Llama2-13B).
024                Our code will be available at this 1 .

      1       I NTRODUCTION
028
029
030
      One of the representative characteristics of generative large language models (LLMs), e.g., GPT-
      3 (Brown et al., 2020), Llama2 (Touvron et al., 2023), and Gemini (Team et al., 2023) is their
031
      in-context learning (ICL) capabilities. Through task-specific input-output examples, large language
032
      models can “learn” to accomplish various tasks beyond the pre-training objective (Dong et al., 2022).
033   Despite the improved down-stream performance compared to zero-shot inference, ICL introduces
034   gaps between pre-training and inference in different aspects. The goal of LLM pre-training is to
035   maximize the likelihood of each next token given its preceding context tokens, while ICL inference
036   forces LLMs to predict the output of downstream tasks conditional on the given demonstrations that
037   are not involved in pre-training.
038
      Existing works have recognized the above target discrimination between pre-training and ICL
039   and presented a few strategies accordingly. Chen et al. (2022) first propose meta-learning to learn
040   in-context examples with task instructions. Min et al. (2022a) expand the scope of the experiment
041   by covering more diverse tasks without task instructions. However, there are still two gaps between
042   pre-training and ICL that have not been fully discussed. Label appearance: Compared to the texts
043   that are not related to a specific task during pre-training, the input-label mapping in ICL inserts
044   additional task information. Weak semantic relevance: Unlike the coherent texts used in pre-training,
045   the ICL demonstration examples are not necessarily semantically relevant. Nevertheless, previous
046   ICL research focuses on what and how input-label mapping information is utilized (Kossen et al.,
047
      2023; Pan et al., 2023), but neglects when the positive effect brought by ICL exceeds the negative
      influence of pretraining-inference gaps.
048
049   Given this, we first explore the two gaps caused by demonstration examples, i.e., the aforementioned
050   label appearance, and the weak semantic relevance. We then calibrate when bridging these two gaps
051   surpasses the improvement from ICL. To eliminate the negative effects of different gaps, we propose
052   a new ICL paradigm: conducting in-context learning at the representation level via unlabeled texts.

      Contributions Throughout this paper, we mainly (1) propose a new ICL paradigm that conducts
065
      in-context learning at the representation level via unlabeled texts from the test set. (2) demonstrate
066   that our method outperforms zero-shot significantly over eight datasets from multiple sources and
067   surpasses traditional in-context learning. (3) disclose the limitations along with the potential reasons
068   and solutions for further performance improvement.
069
      Important Observations Despite being at its preliminary stages, this work offers several vital
070
      observations regarding in-context learning, which can serve as valuable references for future research.
071
      (1) When working with specific-domain datasets, the positive impact of task-related labels outweighs
072   the negative effects of the label appearance gap. However, the opposite is true for general-domain
073   datasets. (2) The input-label mapping information provided by demonstrations is considerably more
074   beneficial for specific-domain datasets than general-domain datasets. (3) Conditioning in-context
075   learning on the independent representations of demonstration inputs proves more effective in bridging
076   weak semantic relevance than conditioning it on the concatenation.
077
      In the rest of this paper, we first launch a preliminary study of the two gaps: label appearance and
078   weak semantic relevance in Section 2. Building on the analysis, we propose a new in-context learning
079   paradigm in Section 3 and extensive experiments for our method in Section 4. Section 5 provides the
080   background for in-context learning and the efforts put into understanding it.

130   2.2      T HE E FFECT OF L ABEL -A PPEARANCE
131
132   When processing specific-domain datasets, the positive effect of task-related labels exceeds the
133
      negative influence of the label-appearance. However, for general-domain datasets, it is just the
      opposite. We conduct a controlled experiment with/without labels in top-ICL to analyze the possible
134
      effect of label-appearance. Table 2 reveals that eight datasets exhibit varying performance trends
135
      when labels are removed from demonstrations. For instance, some datasets like MRPC benefit from
136   the change while others like PHRASE suffer greatly. However, when viewing datasets in groups, the
137   trend is clear: removing labels boosts the performance of general-domain datasets while conversely
138   reducing the performance of specific-domain datasets. This discovery shows that labels are far more
139   critical in specific-domain datasets than in general-domain.
140
      The input-label mapping information provided by demonstrations benefits specific-domain datasets
141
      much more than general-domain. The above analysis reveals that labels play a vital role in specific-
142   domain datasets while are less critical in general-domain datasets. We suppose that ICL benefits
143   from the input-label mapping information when processing specific-domain datasets. Following
144   the previous work (Min et al., 2022b; Pan et al., 2023), we study the performance difference when
145   shuffling labels in demonstrations to analyze the effect of the correct input-label mapping information.
146   According to Figure 1, when modeling random labels, the performance of four language models in all
147   the datasets decreases. The reduction in performance indicates that the correct input-label mapping
148   information benefits all the models and datasets. However, the performance drops much more in
149   specific-domain datasets than general-domain datasets, implying that correct input-label mapping is
150
      much more needed in specific-domain datasets.

154   Unlike accepting single coherent text in language models pretraining, in-context learning takes in
155   the concatenation of multiple demonstrations which are not necessarily relevant. This observation
156
      leads to a new design of ICL, as we illustrate below.

158   input-output pairs, the inference process of a LLM can be expressed as follows:

      The gap between pretraining and inference. We observed a poential weakness of the “unlabled
185
      ICL” in equation 2. LLMs are pre-trained on “coherent text” (u1 , u2 , . . . , us ; us+1 ) extracted from an
186   article or a file, where the inputs u1 , u2 , . . . , us , us+1 have strong semantic dependence. For instance,
187   the sentence “Apples are juicy and delicious, and many kids like to eat them” may appear in the
188   pre-trained corpus, and the words exhibit strong semantic dependence. In contrast, in unlabled ICL
189   equation 2, the inputs z1 , z2 , . . . , zm , xtest are (independently) sampled from a certain distribution,
190   instead of from an article, thus their semantic dependence is weak. For instance, when m = 1, z and
191   xtest can be z = “apple”, xtest = “car”, which exhibits weaker semantic dependence.
192   To design a better way to utilize the demonstration inputs, we briefly analyze the mechanism of
193   unlabeled ICL. In the equation equation 2, we suspect that the demonstration inputs (z1 , . . . , zm ) serve
194   as the contextual information that helps LLM better “understand” the query input xtest . Nevertheless,
195   processing the concatenation (z1 , . . . , zm ; xtest ) by LLM may not be the best way to utilize the
196   context information of z1 , . . . , zm since LLMs are not trained to handle m consecutive samples
197   drawn from an independent distribution. One possible path for design is to consider various ways
198   of combining demonstration inputs in the prompt (i.e., prompt design). In this paper, we aim to
199   explore the representation space, and develop better methods to manipulate the representations of
200
      z1 , . . . , zm , xtest , hoping that this may provide some improvement.
201   New Idea: Processing Representations of Demonstration Inputs and Test Input Independently
202   How to improve unlabled ICL? Our idea is the folloiwng: Since the demo inputs z1 , . . . , zm and
203   xtest are not from a coherent text, they do not necessarily need to be processed as a whole by the
204   LLM. Instead, they can be processed indepedently by the LLM and then combined for inference.
205   To illustrate the idea and analyze its validity, we use an example of m = 1. When m = 1, we
206   consider two samples z and xtest , which are independently drawn from a certain distribution. The
207   unlabled ICL can be expressed as ytest = LLM(z; xtest ). For notation simplicity, we denote A = xtest ,

498   5   R ELATED W ORK
499
500   Large language models (LLMs) such as GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020) exhibit the ability to do in-context
501
      learning (ICL), where the model performs a downstream task simply by conditioning on a prompt
      made up of input-output examples.
502
503   Understanding How ICL Works Xie et al. (2021); Jiang (2023); Wang et al. (2023); Zhang et al.
504   (2023); Han et al. (2023) propose that ICL can be formulated as the Bayesian inference. Min et al.
505   (2022b); Wies et al. (2023) observe ICL is more about identifying the task than learning it, recovering
506   the capacity obtained in pretraining. However, Kossen et al. (2023) argue that ICL almost always
507
      depends on in-context labels, and can learn novel semantics about tasks. Chan et al. (2022); Hahn &
      Goyal (2023); Raventos et al. (2023) investigate the factors affecting the emergence of ICL. Razeghi
508
      et al. (2022) discover that term frequencies in the pretraining data affect the performance of ICL.
509
      Some studies explore the relationship between gradient descent and conducting ICL (Dai et al., 2023;
510   Akyurek et al., 2022; Von Oswald et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2023). Yan et al. (2023) empirically
511   establish a principle that strengthens the relationship between two tokens based on their contextual
512   co-occurrences by investigating the role of surface features in text generation.
513
      ICL Free of Demonstrations at Instance Level It is hard to get access to the demonstrations pool for
514
      ICL in real-world scenarios. Kim et al. (2022); Chen et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023a) bootstrap LLMs
515
      to generate pseudo demonstrations. This approach does alleviate the dependency on demonstrations.
516   However, generation may be uncontrollable and unstable, easily accumulating biases when generating
517   multiple demonstrations. Also, generating pseudo demonstrations is often expensive and slow. In
518   the study by Lyu et al. (2023), the approach involves initially retrieving k unlabeled test instances,
519   assigning random labels to them, and subsequently conducting in-context learning. Both Kossen et al.
520   (2023) and our finding demonstrate that ICL indeed depends on in-context labels, thus assigning
521   random labels can be risky, especially for datasets coming from specific-domain.
522
523
      6   L IMITATIONS & C ONCLUSION
524
525
      Limitations To begin with, every step requires prediction in text generation problems, which
526
      accumulates latency in adopting our method. Therefore, our work currently only involves text
527
      classification problems in the experiment, leaving a gap in text generation. Second, our method is
528   partly worse than traditional in-context learning in specific-domain datasets, showing there is still
529   room for improvement in exploring the relationship between unlabeled texts and the label space
530   information. Last but not least, our method does not incorporate any training; thus the potential of
531   our approach has not been fully explored. We leave all the above for future work.
532
      Conclusion We first analyze the effects of label appearance and weak semantic relevance in traditional
533
      in-context learning. Building on the analysis, we propose a new ICL paradigm, which conducts
534   in-context learning at the representation level via unlabeled texts. Results over eight datasets coming
535   from general and specific domain and four language models demonstrate that our method exhibits
536   broad and significant improvements compared to zero-shot. Besides, our method with unlabeled texts
537   from the test set surpasses traditional in-context learning with demonstrations from the training set.
538   Furthermore, our method enables small models to perform even better than larger models that are
539   two times their size. Also, our method boosts specific-domain scenarios only with unlabeled texts,
      showing the potential in real-world problems, which deserves more attention in the future.
