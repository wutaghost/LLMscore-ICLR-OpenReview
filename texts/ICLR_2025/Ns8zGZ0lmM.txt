008                                                A BSTRACT
009
010            Automated evaluation leveraging large language models (LLMs), commonly re-
011            ferred to as LLM evaluators or LLM-as-a-judge, has been widely used in measuring
012
               the performance of dialogue systems. However, the self-preference bias in LLMs
               has posed significant risks, including promoting specific styles or policies intrinsic
013
               to the LLMs. Despite the importance of this issue, there is a lack of established
014
               methods to measure the self-preference bias quantitatively, and its underlying
015            causes are poorly understood. In this paper, we introduce a novel quantitative
016            metric to measure the self-preference bias. Our experimental results demonstrate
017            that GPT-4 exhibits a significant degree of self-preference bias. To explore the
018            causes, we hypothesize that LLMs may favor outputs that are more familiar to
019            them, as indicated by lower perplexity. We analyze the relationship between LLM
020            evaluations and the perplexities of outputs. Our findings reveal that LLMs assign
021            significantly higher evaluations to outputs with lower perplexity than human evalu-
022            ators, regardless of whether the outputs were self-generated. This suggests that the
023
               essence of the bias lies in perplexity and that the self-preference bias exists because
               LLMs prefer texts more familiar to them.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Measuring the quality of responses in dialogue systems presents unique challenges due to the diverse
029
      range of response strategies. Nowadays, thanks to the strong text understanding of large language
030   models (LLMs), various automatic evaluations can be easily implemented by employing LLMs as
031   evaluators (often referred to as LLM evaluators or LLM-as-a-judge). MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2024)
032   is an example of a benchmark utilizing this approach to score dialogue systems.
033
      However, as Deutsch et al. (2022) reported, LLM evaluators are inherently biased. The inherent bias
034
      might cause inappropriate testing and inject biased preferences into the target dialogue systems (Zheng
035
      et al., 2024). One of the most significant biases is self-preference bias, which refers to the tendency
036   of LLMs to overestimate the quality of their own outputs, as demonstrated in Figure 1a. The self-
037   preference bias poses potential risks, including the promotion of specific ideologies or response styles
038   intrinsic to the LLM evaluator.
039
      Several studies have addressed the issue of self-preference bias; however, there is a lack of reliable
040
      metrics to quantify the extent of self-preference bias, and the fundamental causes of this phenomenon
041
      remain unclear. Koo et al. (2024) proposed a benchmark incorporating self-preference bias. Pan-
042   ickssery et al. (2024) investigated its relationship with self-recognition capabilities. However, neither
043   study has quantitatively assessed self-preference bias while considering its disparity from human
044   evaluations. Xu et al. (2024) and Stureborg et al. (2024) addressed quantifying self-preference bias
045   within an evaluation approach where LLMs assign an absolute score to a single generated text. In
046   this approach, gold-standard evaluators are required to assign scores based on abstract criteria while
047   ensuring consistency with prior evaluations, making it challenging to obtain accurate assessments.
048   As a result, these studies often restricted their scope to specific tasks, such as text summarization or
049   machine translation, and relied on reference-based metrics like BLEURT (Sellam et al., 2020), which
050
      does not reflect the diversity of real-world use cases.
051   By contrast, a pairwise evaluation approach that involves direct comparison between two texts
052   enables evaluators to recognize specific differences more readily, resulting in more consistent human
053   judgments. Consequently, such pairwise evaluation methods are particularly suitable for analyzing
      biases related to discrepancies with human evaluations.

078   In this paper, we measure the self-preference biases of LLMs in the pairwise evaluation. To ac-
079   complish this, we propose a new metric to quantify self-preference bias on the basis of algorithmic
080   fairness concepts, thereby enabling discussions within the existing frameworks of fairness. In our
081   experiment, we measured self-preference bias in eight LLMs. The results indicated that GPT-4
082
      exhibited a significant self-preference bias (Figure 1b). This finding suggests a potential concern:
      using GPT-4 as a judge may lead to excessive influence from GPT-4’s unique styles and policies.
083
084   Furthermore, we investigated the underlying causes of self-preference bias. Although LLM evaluators
085   are not explicitly informed whether a given text is their own, they still exhibit self-preference bias.
086   We hypothesized that LLM evaluators might be affected by the perplexity of the text, which tends
087   to be lower perplexity when it is generated by themselves. To test this hypothesis, we analyzed the
088
      relationship between the perplexities of the texts to be evaluated and their corresponding evaluations.
      Our analysis revealed that LLMs assign significantly higher evaluations to texts with lower perplexity
089
      than human evaluators, regardless of whether the texts were self-generated. This suggests that the
090
      fundamental cause of self-preference bias may be the familiarity of the texts to the LLM evaluators,
091   specifically how likely they are to generate the same response.

096

      LLM-as-a-judge has been studied in various directions. The use of large language models (LLMs) for
102   benchmarking has been advancing steadily. LLMs are increasingly employed in dialogue evaluation
103   due to their ability to provide flexible assessments from perspectives such as utility and safety (Zheng
104   et al., 2024; Sottana et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Schick et al., 2021). Furthermore, numerous
105   studies have explored the use of feedback from LLMs to enhance the learning of the LLMs them-
106   selves (Madaan et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024). In particular, pairs of texts annotated with their
107   relative quality have been proven to be useful training signals (Sun et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2024),
      combined with various learning methods (Ouyang et al., 2022; Rafailov et al., 2023).

      The self-preference bias is only one of several limitations inherent in the LLM-as-a-judge paradigm.
109   Zheng et al. (2024) identified other biases such as position bias, where specific positions within
110   a prompt are preferentially selected, and verbosity bias, which favors longer responses. Position
111   bias can be addressed relatively easily by rearranging the order of comparative options within the
112   prompt, and alignment-based methods to mitigate the bias have also been proposed (Li et al., 2024).
113   Additionally, Saito et al. (2023) investigated the presence of verbosity bias in GPT-4 and GPT-3.5-
114   Turbo, focusing on the quantification of bias by examining the error rates associated with longer
115   versus shorter text options.

117   3     P RELIMINARIES : FAIRNESS AND B IAS
118
119   The measurement of bias in classifiers has been widely discussed within the framework of fair-
120   ness (Calders et al., 2009; Hardt et al., 2016). In this section, we focus on a representative definition
121   of fairness, Equal Opportunity, as the preparation for quantifying the self-preference bias in LLM
122   evaluators.
123   Equal Opportunity (Hardt et al., 2016) is a fairness definition that requires the classifier to achieve
124   equal recall across groups with different sensitive attributes (e.g. gender or race). Specifically, let the
125   sensitive attribute be S ∈ {0, 1}, the prediction of the classifier be Y ′ ∈ {0, 1}, and the ground truth
126   label be Y ∈ {0, 1}. The classifier satisfies Equal Opportunity if the following condition holds:
127
128
                              P (Y ′ = 1|S = 1, Y = 1) = P (Y ′ = 1|S = 0, Y = 1)                              (1)
129
130   When quantifying bias in the classifier, the difference or ratio between both sides of the equation are
131   often used. The amount of bias derived from this definition is the difference in how well the classifier
132   matches the ground truth between groups with different sensitive attributes. Therefore, if there is
133   already bias towards sensitive attributes during the creation of the ground truth, Equation 1 cannot be
134   considered an ideal definition of fairness.
135   To address this drawback, the fairness definition known as Demographic Parity (Calders et al., 2009),
136   which does not rely on ground truth, is also widely used. Demographic Parity requires that the
137   predictive distribution of the classifier be consistent across groups with different sensitive attributes. It
138   is based on the assumption that there is no inherent causal relationship between the sensitive attributes
139   and the predicted labels.
140   LLM-as-a-judge is a technique aimed at replacing human evaluators with LLMs, and more specifically,
141   it assumes that dialogue system are aligned based on human preferences. Therefore, in this study, we
142   employ the concept of Equal Opportunity to quantify self-preference bias by treating LLM evaluators
143   as classifiers.

145   4     Q UANTIFYING S ELF -P REFERENCE B IAS
146
147   In this section, we propose a new metric to quantify self-preference bias. In particular, we focus on a
148   setting where evaluators compare two texts and select the one higher quality, allowing comparison
149   with reliable human evaluations.
150
151   4.1   S ELF - PREFERENCE B IAS M ETRIC
152
153   To measure the extent to which an LLM’s evaluation deviates from human evaluations across its
154   own responses and those generated by others, we employ the concept of Equal Opportunity (Hardt
155   et al., 2016). The bias is quantified by calculating the difference between both sides of Equation 1. A
156
      rigorous definition is provided below.
157   Definition 4.1. (self-preference bias of the evaluator f .) Let f be the evaluator assessing the quality
158   of dialogue responses, capable of generating its own responses. For a pair of responses y0 and y1 in
159
      a dialogue, define: Y ∈ 0, 1 as the index of the human-preferred response, Y ′ ∈ 0, 1 as the index
      of the f -preferred response, and S ∈ 0, 1 as the index of the f -generated response. We define the
160
      self-preference bias of the evaluator f in dialogue comparison evaluation as follows:
161
                         Bias = P (Y ′ = 1|S = 1, Y = 1) − P (Y ′ = 1|S = 0, Y = 1).                           (2)


                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      In this definition, the amount of bias is represented by the difference between the conditional
163   probability of the evaluator f rating itself favorably given that the human evaluator has rated it
164   favorably, and the conditional probability of the evaluator f rating itself unfavorably given that the
165   human evaluator has rated it unfavorably. A value of 0 indicates the absence of bias, while a value
166   close to 1 suggests a high degree of bias. Conversely, a value of −1 would indicate the presence of
167   a reverse bias, where the evaluator f tends to undervalue its own responses. In this definition, the
168   case where y1 is preferred is explicitly considered, but this does not result in a loss of generality by
169   treating the preferred response as y1 .

172   4.2   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTING
173
174   The aim of our experiment is to quantify the self-preference bias in various LLMs. To address a wide
175   range of tasks and topics, we have LLMs evaluate responses in open-ended dialogues and measure
176   the bias using the Definition 4.1.
177   First, we provide the LLM evaluator with a user query written by a human and two responses
178   generated by different LLMs. We then ask the LLM evaluator to determine which of the two
179   responses demonstrates higher quality. For clarity and ease of reference during evaluation, we
180   designate the two responses as “response A” and “response B”. Finally, using the probabilities that
181   the LLM evaluator outputs for “A” and “B”, denoted as p(A|context) and p(B|context), we calculate
182   the score for response A using the following equation.
183
184
185                                                    p(A|context)
186                                    scoreA = P                                                          (3)
                                                     w∈{A, B} p(w|context)
187
188
189
      The score for response B is calculated in the same manner. This post-processing, as shown in
190
      Equation 3 is designed to enhance the interpretability of analyses across LLMs with different
191
      probability distributions, following the approach of Schick et al. (2021). Additionally, according to
192   Zheng et al. (2024), LLMs may exhibit position bias, which refers to the tendency to prefer responses
193   located in specific positions within the prompt. To mitigate this bias, we swap the positions of
194   responses A and B, and the evaluation scores are averaged over two iterations. Using the scores
195   obtained through the above process, we examine the extent to which there is a difference in evaluation
196   scores between the LLM’s own generated response and responses generated by other LLMs.
197
      The experimental setup may include biases other than position bias, such as verbosity bias. Position
198   bias stems from the design of the experimental setup, whereas other biases are intrinsic to the model.
199   To comprehensively measure self-preference bias specific to the model, we have not attempted to
200   mitigate biases other than position bias.
201
      Empirical evaluation uses Chatbot Arena dataset (Zheng et al., 2024), which contains 33,000 dialogues
202
      and each consisting of a user query and a pair of responses generated by two different LLMs. We
203
      calculate evaluation scores for the pre-existing response pairs stored in the dataset. In this dataset,
204   every response pair is labeled with either ”model a”, ”model b”, or ”tie” as a result of a human
205   evaluation comparing the quality of the responses.
206
207
      As the LLM evaluators, we employed the following eight LLMs, which also used in Chatbot Arena
      dataset: the closed-source GPT-3.5-Turbo and GPT-4 (Josh et al., 2024), and the open-source Vicuna-
208
      7b, Vicuna-13b (Chiang et al., 2023), oasst-pythia-12b (Biderman et al., 2023), dolly-v2-12b (Conover
209
      et al., 2023), Koala-13b (Geng et al., 2023), and stablelm-tuned-alpha-7b (Jonathan Tow, 2023). We
210   used the same prompt as Zheng et al. (2024) to calculate the evaluation scores of responses A and B
211   for all LLM evaluators. The prompt instructs the LLM to output either “[[A]]”, “[[B]]”, or “[[C]]”
212   after providing an explanation. In the implementation, we used the output probability distribution
213   following the token corresponding to “[[” in the generated text to compute the score using Equation 3.
214   We excluded 1.07% of the responses that failed to comply with the prompt instructions, such as not
215   providing a response in the required format (e.g., “[[A]]” or a similar structure). The temperature
      value used in the experiment was set to 0.7, following the reference provided in Zheng et al. (2024).

304   We have shown that certain LLM evaluators tend to assign disproportionately high scores based on
305   whether the output being generated from themselves, even though LLM evaluators are not explicitly
306   provided with labels indicating whether a given output is their own. We hypothesized that LLM
307   evaluators might be affected by the similarity of the response to their own output. To investigate this
308
      further, we focus on how LLM evaluators change their evaluation depending on the perplexities of
      responses.
309
310   As in previous experiments, we employed the pairwise evaluation framework where evaluators com-
311   pare two responses, response A and B. First, we computed the perplexities of responses conditioned
312   on the prompt, and took the difference between response A and B for all samples. Next, we divided
313   the perplexity differences into bins and calculated the probabilities that each LLM evaluator judged
314
      response A as the winner in each subset. Additionally, we computed the winning judgment rate of the
      human for response A within each bin. In this experiment, we excluded GPT-4 and GPT-3.5-Turbo,
315
      as perplexity values could not be obtained for these models.
316

      tendency was stronger in vicuna-13b, vicuna-7b, koala-13b, and oasst-pythia-12b than in humans.

413   6   D ISCUSSION
414
415   To reduce self-preference bias, one possible approach is ensemble evaluation using multiple models.
416   This method is expected to provide a more equitable evaluation by avoiding reliance on a single model.
417   Specifically, when a model exhibits low perplexity on a sample, decreasing the weight assigned to that
418   model’s evaluation for that sample may contribute to bias mitigation. To evaluate the effectiveness of
419   bias reduction strategies, our proposed new metric can be utilized. Therefore, we believe that our
420   research makes a significant contribution to the understanding of self-preference bias and will greatly
421   facilitate the development of future research in this area.
422   Our experimental results reveal that LLM evaluators tend to assign higher scores to texts with lower
423   perplexity. We further discuss the reasons behind this phenomenon. First, LLMs are trained during
424   the pretraining phase to reduce perplexity on large-scale text corpora. Moreover, when aligning with
425   human preferences, the models are also trained to minimize perplexity on the given dialogue data.
426   Therefore, high-perplexity texts are likely those that the LLM has not frequently encountered during
427   training, suggesting that such texts may be related to domains that the LLM evaluators do not fully
428
      comprehend.
429   This observation may seem contradicted by the fact that GPT-4, which is well-versed across various
430   domains due to a wide range of benchmarks, exhibits a high degree of self-preference bias. However,
431   by investigating specific cases of self-preference bias, as shown in Figure 1a and Table 1, we
      found that the bias was often not related to clear factual errors but rather to differences in response


                                                        8

432
      styles, such as the handling of specialized domains or the description of premises before answering.
433   This suggests that, advanced models like GPT-4, which thoroughly understand and adhere to their
434   predefined policies, may use the degree of alignment with these policies as a deciding factor when
435   evaluating responses of comparable quality.
436
      The proposed metric in Definition 4.1 is based on the fairness definition known as Equal Opportunity.
437
      Demographic Parity Calders et al. (2009) is also a prominent definition of fairness. Demographic
438
      Parity requires that the predictive distribution of a classifier be consistent across sensitive groups,
439   regardless of the ground truth. In the context of this study, the focus is on whether the evaluations by
440   LLMs align with human preferences, which is why Demographic Parity was not the focal point. While
441   the bias metric based on Demographic Parity cannot demonstrate the unfairness of an evaluation,
442   it is useful for analyzing how highly each LLM evaluator rates its own outputs within the given
443   experimental setup. Similar to the Definition 4.1, the self-preference bias based on Demographic
444   Parity also can be quantified as follows:
445
446                             Bias = P (Y ′ = 1|S = 1) − P (Y ′ = 1|S = 0).                              (4)

      7    C ONCLUSION
470
471   In this study, we propose a metric to quantify the self-preference bias in LLM-as-a-judge and
472
      measured the self-preference bias of eight LLMs. Experimental results confirmed that GPT-4, in
      particular, exhibits a high self-preference bias. This finding suggests a risk that GPT-4 as a judge may
473
      inadvertently reinforce its own style and policies.
474
475   Furthermore, we hypothesized that the self-preference bias is related to the perplexity of the texts,
476   and showed that, compared to human evaluators, LLM evaluators assigned higher evaluations to
477   texts with lower perplexity, and this tendency was observed regardless of whether the text was
478
      generated by themselves or not. This suggests that the essence of the bias lies in perplexity and that
      the self-preference bias exists because LLMs prefer texts more familiar to them.
479

484   have. The primary objective is to investigate the current tendency of LLM evaluators to prefer their
485   own outputs and to deepen the understanding of this issue in order to prevent unfair evaluations and
      the reinforcement of a single policy in the future. For the experiments, we use a publicly available
