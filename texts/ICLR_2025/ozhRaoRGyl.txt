008                                               A BSTRACT
009
010
               Imitation learning (IL) has shown great potential in various applications, such as
               robot control. However, traditional IL methods are usually designed to learn only
011
               one specific type of behavior since demonstrations typically correspond to a single
012
               expert. In this work, we introduce the first generic framework for Quality Diver-
013            sity Imitation Learning (QD-IL), which enables the agent to learn a broad range
014            of skills from limited demonstrations. Our framework integrates the principles
015            of quality diversity with adversarial imitation learning (AIL) methods, and can
016            potentially improve any inverse reinforcement learning (IRL) method. Empirically,
017            our framework significantly improves the QD performance of GAIL and VAIL
018            on the challenging continuous control tasks derived from Mujoco environments.
019            Moreover, our method even achieves 2x expert performance in the Humanoid
020            environment.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
023

      Imitation learning (IL) enables intelligent systems to quickly learn complex tasks by learning from
025   demonstrations, which is particularly useful when manually designing a reward function is difficult.
026   IL has been applied to many real-world scenarios such as autonomous driving (Bojarski, 2016),
027   robotic manipulation (Zhu et al., 2018), surgical skill learning (Gao et al., 2014), and drone control
028   (Ross et al., 2013). The concept of IL relies on the idea that experts can showcase desired behaviors,
029   when they are unable to directly code them into a pre-defined program. This makes IL applicable to
030   any system requiring autonomous behavior that mirrors expertise (Zare et al., 2024).
031   However, traditional IL methods tend to replicate only the specific strategies demonstrated by the
032   expert. If the expert demonstrations cover a narrow range of scenarios, the model may struggle when
033   faced with new or unseen situations. Additionally, IL faces challenges in stochastic environments
034   where outcomes are uncertain or highly variable. Since the expert’s actions may not capture all
035   possible states or contingencies, IL often struggles to learn an optimal strategy for every scenario
036   (Zare et al., 2024). These limitations are further exacerbated when the demonstration data is limited,
037   as the IL algorithm will only learn specific expert behavior patterns. Hence, traditional IL methods
038   are significantly constrained due to the lack of ability to learn diverse behavior patterns to adapt to
039
      stochastic and dynamic environments.
040   On the other hand, the Quality Diversity (QD) algorithm is designed to find diverse (defined by
041   measure m) solutions to optimization problems while maximizing each solution’s fitness value (fitness
042   refers to the problem’s objective) (Pugh et al., 2016). For instance, QD algorithms can generate
043   diverse human faces resembling “Elon Musk” with various features, such as different eye colors
044   (Fontaine & Nikolaidis, 2021). In robot control, the QD algorithm excels at training policies with
045
      diverse behaviors. This enhances the agent’s robustness in handling stochastic situations (Tjanaka
      et al., 2022). For example, if an agent’s leg is damaged, it can adapt by switching to a policy that
046
      uses the other undamaged leg to hop forward. Different ways of moving forward represent diverse
047
      behavior patterns (Fontaine & Nikolaidis, 2021). Traditional QD algorithms often use evolutionary
048   strategies (ES). They have been successful in exploring solution space but suffer from lower fitness
049   due to the large solution spaces, especially when the solution is parameterized by neural networks
050   (Hansen, 2006; Salimans et al., 2017). Recent works combining ES with gradient approximations
051   in differentiable QD (DQD) have significantly improved the ability to discover high-performing
052   and diverse solutions (Fontaine & Nikolaidis, 2021). Naturally, one valuable question is raised:
053   can we design a novel IL framework that can combine the respective strengths of traditional IL
      and QD algorithms, enabling the agent to learn a broad set of high-performing skills from limited

      2.1   Q UALITY D IVERSITY O PTIMIZATION
123
124   Distinct from traditional optimization which aims to find a single solution to maximize the objective,
125   Quality Diversity (QD) optimization aims to find a set of high-quality and diverse solutions in an
126   n-dimensional continuous space Rn . Given an objective function f : Rn → R and k-dimensional
127   measure function m : Rn → Rk , the goal is to find solutions θ ∈ Rn for each local region in the
128   behavior space B = m(Rn ). Two canonical algorithms of QD are MAP-Elites Mouret & Clune
129   (2015a) and Novelty Search with Local Competition Lehman & Stanley (2011), which differ in terms
130
      of how they structure the behavior space into an archive of solutions and how local competition
      and replacement of solutions is performed (see also Cully & Demiris (2018) for an overview of QD
131
      algorithm classifications). We focus on grid-based archives as in the MAP-Elites algorithm, which
132
      discretize B into M cells, where each cell i = 1, . . . , M represents a small hypercube [ai , bi ] within a
133   multi-dimensional grid of the behavioral measure space. A new solution replaces an existing solution
134   in the same cell if it outperforms it and falls within the same hypercube. Formally, the objective
135   is to find a set of solutions {θi }M
                                         i=1 which maximises f (θi ) for each i = 1, . . . , M. Each solution θi
136   corresponds to a cell in A via its measure m(θi ), forming an archive of high-quality and diverse
137   solutions (Chatzilygeroudis et al., 2021; Pugh et al., 2016).
138
      Some traditional Quality Diversity optimization methods integrate Evolution Strategies (ES) with
139   MAP-Elites (Mouret & Clune, 2015b), such as Covariance Matrix Adaptation MAP-Elites (CMA-
140   ME) (Fontaine et al., 2020). CMA-ME uses CMA-ES (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001) as ES algorithm
141   generating new solutions that are inserted into the archive, and uses MAP-Elites to retains the
142   highest-performing solution in each cell. CMA-ES adapts its sampling distribution based on archive
143   improvements from offspring solutions. However, traditional ES faces low sample efficiency, espe-
144   cially for high-dimensional parameters such as neural networks.
145   Differentiable Quality Diversity (DQD) improves exploration and fitness by leveraging the gradients
146   of both objective and measure functions. Covariance Matrix Adaptation MAP-Elites via Gradient
147   Arborescence (CMA-MEGA) (Fontaine & Nikolaidis, 2021) optimizes both objective function f
148   and measure functions m using gradients with respect to policy parameters: ∇ f = ∂∂ θf and ∇m =
149

        ∂ m1           ∂ mk                                                              k
150      ∂ θ , . . . , ∂ θ . The objective of CMA-MEGA is g(θ ) = |c0 | f (θ ) + ∑ j=1 c j m j (θ ), where the
151   coefficients c j are sampled from a search distribution. CMA-MEGA maintains a search policy πθµ in
152   policy parameter space, corresponding to a specific cell in the archive. CMA-MEGA generates local
153
      gradients by combining gradient vectors with coefficient samples from CMA-ES, creating branched
      policies πθ1 , . . . , πθλ . These branched policies are ranked based on their archive improvement, which
154
      measures how much they improve the QD-score (one QD metric, which will be discussed in the
155
      experiment section) of the archive. The ranking guides CMA-ES to update the search distribution,
156   and yields a weighted linear recombination of gradients to step the search policy in the direction
157   of greatest archive improvement. The latest DQD algorithm, Covariance Matrix Adaptation MAP-
158   Annealing via Gradient Arborescence (CMA-MAEGA) (Fontaine & Nikolaidis, 2023), introduces
159   soft archives, which maintain a dynamic threshold te for each cell. This threshold is updated by
160   te ← (1 − α)te + α f (πθi ) when new policies exceed the cell’s threshold, where α balances the time
161   spent on exploring one region before exploring another region. This adaptive mechanism allows more
      flexible optimization by balancing exploration and exploitation.

174

176   by comparing the policy parameters before and after multiple PPO updates. These gradients are
177   then passed to the modified CMA-MAEGA to update the policy archive. We recommend readers to
178   explore prior works in depth (Batra et al., 2023) or refer to Appendix F for further details on PPGA

      2.3   I MITATION L EARNING
183
      In Imitation learning (IL) (Zare et al., 2024), an agent learns high-performing policies from demon-
184
      stration data. A traditional approach to solve this challenge is Behavior Cloning (BC), which uses
185
      supervised learning to learn the policy from demonstrations, a technique which unfortunately suffers
186   from severe error accumulation (Ross et al., 2011). More recent techniques include inverse reinforce-
187   ment learning (IRL), where one seeks to learn a reward function from the demonstrations and then
188   use RL to train a policy based on that reward function (Abbeel & Ng, 2004).
189
      Early IRL methods estimate rewards using the principle of maximum entropy (Ziebart et al., 2008;
190
      Wulfmeier et al., 2015; Finn et al., 2016). Recent adversarial IL methods treat IRL as a distribution-
191
      matching problem. For instance, Generative Adversarial Imitation Learning (GAIL) (Ho & Ermon,
192   2016) trains a discriminator to differentiate between the state-action distribution of the demonstrations
193   and the state-action distribution induced by the agent’s policy, and output a reward to guide policy
194   improvement. Improving on GAIL, Variational Adversarial Imitation Learning (VAIL) (Peng et al.,
195   2018b) applies a variational information bottleneck (VIB) (Alemi et al., 2016) to the discriminator,
196   improving the stability of adversarial learning. Another technique for adversarial IL is Adversarial
197   Inverse Reinforcement Learning (AIRL) (Fu et al., 2017), which learns a robust reward function by
198   training the discriminator via logistic regression to distinguish expert data from policy data.
199   Recently, one can also observe a variety of techniques for non-adversarial imitation learning. For
200   instance, Primal Wasserstein Imitation Learning (PWIL) (Dadashi et al., 2021) formulates the reward
201   function based on an upper bound of the Wasserstein distance between the expert and agent’s
202   state-action distributions, avoiding the instability of adversarial IL methods. Generative Intrinsic
203   Reward-driven Imitation Learning (GIRIL) (Yu et al., 2020) computes rewards offline by pretraining
204   a reward model using a conditional VAE (Sohn et al., 2015), which combines a backward action
205   encoding model with a forward dynamics model. The reward is then derived from the prediction error
206
      between the actual next state and its reconstruction. GIRIL has demonstrated superior performance
      even with limited demonstrations.
207
208   Our paper primarily focuses on IRL with limited demonstrations, and we compare our proposed
209   approach to adversarial and non-adversarial techniques as baselines. More details about these
210   baselines are provided in Appendix C. In addition to the IRL setting, we also investigate how our
211   results translate to the related setting of Imitation From Observation (IFO), where only the experts’
212
      state sequences, rather than full state-action sequences, are available (Liu et al., 2018).
213   While we are the first to explore quality diversity imitation learning, related work uses diverse
214   demonstrations to design policies that have the behavioral measure as one of the inputs (Justesen
215   et al., 2020) rather than designing an archive of diverse policies. Subsequent to our work, WQDIL Yu
      et al. (2024) introduces a technique closely related to ours in terms of recognising behavior-space

269   exploration. Therefore, it will explore less in other areas, as illustrated in Figure 1(a). Additionally,

      4.2.1     MC ONBO -GAIL
309
310   The GAIL discriminator receives a state-action pair (s, a) and outputs how closely the agent’s behavior
311   resembles that of the expert, serving as a reward function. However, GAIL tends to overfit specific
312   behaviors with limited demonstrations. In large state spaces, the discriminator struggles to generalize
313   to unseen states (Kostrikov et al., 2018). This results in localized and sparse rewards, hindering
314   quality diversity. Therefore, the core question in QD-IL is how to generalize knowledge from limited
315   demonstrations to the entire policy archive while avoiding localized rewards.
316   To address this, we use the Markovian Measure Proxy (Batra et al., 2023). It decomposes trajectory-
317   based measures into individual steps: mi (θ ) = T1 ∑t=0

360   tation using the Brax simulator (Freeman et al., 2021), enhanced with QDax wrappers for measure
361   calculation (Lim et al., 2022). We leverage pyribs (Tjanaka et al., 2023) and CleanRL’s PPO (Huang
362   et al., 2020) for implementing the PPGA algorithm. The observation space sizes for these environ-
363   ments are 17, 18, and 227, with corresponding action space sizes of 6, 6, and 17. The measure
364   function is a vector where each dimension indicates the proportion of time a leg touches the ground.

501    5.4             A BLATION S TUDY
502
503    In this section, we examine the effect of the measure bonus by comparing the performance of
504
       MConbo-GAIL with Measure-Conditional-GAIL (without the measure bonus) on Walker2d. The
       results in Figure 7 show a significant performance drop across all metrics without the measure bonus.
505
       This highlights the synergy between the measure bonus and CMA-MEGA. Without the exploration
506
       bonus, the algorithm struggles with highly localized rewards and the inherent local optima of policy
507    gradient approach. As a result, the search policy fails to explore new behavior patterns, leading to
508    lower coverage. Furthermore, since the reward function learned by IL is biased and especially with
509    limited demonstrations, PPGA’s search policy may miss opportunities to explore rewarding behavior
510    patterns. This results in lower average and best rewards. The measure bonus directly encourages the
511    exploration of new behaviors, addressing this issue. To view a full ablation study, including the effect
512    of both the measure bonus and measure conditioning, please refer to Appendix E.

       6               C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
526
527
       In this work, we proposed MConbo-IRL which can potentially improve any IRL method in QD task.
528    Additionally, our framework opened the possibility of QD-IFO, providing the first generic QD-IL
529    framework for future research. Our framework follows the paradigm of IRL to learn a QD-enhanced
530    reward function, and use a QD-RL algorithm to optimize policy archive. By encouraging behavior-
531    level exploration and facilitating knowledge generalization from limited expert demonstrations, our
532    framework addresses the key challenges of QD-IL. Extensive experiments show that our framework
533    achieves near-expert or beyond-expert performance, and significantly outperforms baselines.
534    To establish our framework as a generic QD-IL solution, we focused on improving the two widely
535    used IRL algorithms in this paper to make our framework as simple and effective as possible. However,
536    we believe that our framework has the potential to be compatible with more IRL algorithm backbones.
537    Additionally, exploring the development of new architectures for QD-IL and exciting applications
538    such as behavior adaptation, for instance with context-conditioned policies (Seyed Ghasemipour
539    et al., 2019), remain important avenues for future research. We also discuss the potential limitations
       of our work in Appendix K.

      • GAIL (Ho & Ermon, 2016). In GAIL, the objective of the discriminator Dψ is to differentiate
        between the state-action distribution of expert demonstration D and the state-action distribution
835
        induced by the agent’s policy π:

1042   In this context, m j (θ ) represents the j-th measure of the solution θ , and k is the dimension of the
1043   measure space. The objective function of CMA-MEGA is dynamic because the coefficient for each
1044   measure, c j , is updated adaptively to encourage diversity in m. For instance, if the algorithm has
1045   already found many solutions with high m1 values, it may favor new solutions with low m1 values
1046   by making c1 negative, thus minimizing m1 . However, the coefficient for the fitness function f will
1047   always be positive, as the algorithm always seeks to maximize fitness. This objective function ensures
1048   that CMA-MEGA simultaneously maximizes fitness f and encourages diversity across the measures
1049
       m. We update θ by differentiating objective (16) and use gradient-descend-based optimization
       approaches, since DQD assumes f and m are differentiable.
1050
1051   Furthermore, the coefficients c j are sampled from a distribution, which is maintained using Covariance
1052   Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) (Hansen, 2016). Specifically, CMA-ES updates the
1053   coefficient distribution by iteratively adapting the mean µ and covariance matrix Σ of the multivariate
1054   Gaussian distribution N(µ, Σ), from which the coefficients c j are sampled. At each iteration, CMA-
1055
       MEGA ranks the solutions based on their archive improvement (i.e. How much they improve the
       existing solutions of occupied cell). The top-performing solutions are used to update µ, while Σ is
1056
       adjusted to capture the direction and magnitude of successful steps in the solution space, thereby
1057
       refining the search distribution over time.
1058
1059   In CMA-MAEGA (Fontaine & Nikolaidis, 2023), the concept of soft archives is introduced to
1060   improve upon CMA-MEGA. Instead of maintaining the best policy in each cell, the archive employs
1061
       a dynamic threshold, denoted as te . This threshold is updated using the following rule whenever a
       new policy πθi surpasses the current threshold of its corresponding cell e:
1062
1063
1064                                         te ← (1 − α)te + α f (πθi )
1065
1066
       Here, α is a hyperparameter called the archive learning rate, with 0 ≤ α ≤ 1. The value of α
1067
       controls how much time is spent optimizing within a specific region of the archive before moving to
1068   explore a new region. Lower values of α result in slower threshold updates, emphasizing exploration
1069   in a particular region, while higher values promote quicker transitions to different areas. The concept
1070   of soft archives offers several theoretical and practical advantages, as highlighted in previous studies.
1071
       PPGA (Batra et al., 2023) is directly built upon CMA-MAEGA. We summarize the key synergies
1072
       between PPGA and CMA-MAEGA as follows:(1) In reinforcement learning (RL), the objective
1073
       functions f and m in Equation 16 are not directly differentiable. To address this, PPGA employs
1074   Markovian Measure Proxies (MMP), where a single-step proxy δ (st ) is treated as the reward
1075   function of an MDP. PPGA utilizes k + 1 parallel PPO instances to approximate the gradients of f
1076   and each measure m, where k is the number of measures. Specifically, the gradient for each i-MDP
1077   is computed as the difference between the parameters θi,new after multi-step PPO optimization and
1078   the previous parameters θi,old . (2) Once the gradients are approximated, the problem is transformed
1079   into a standard DQD problem. PPGA then applies a modified version of CMA-MAEGA to perform
       quality diversity optimization. The key modifications include:

1220   I            I MPROVE IMITATION FROM OBSERVATION
1221
1222   Imitation from Observation (IFO) is a type of imitation learning where agents learn behaviors by
1223   observing state trajectories, without needing access to the actions that generated them. Unlike
1224   traditional methods that require both states and actions, IFO mimics behavior solely from state
1225   sequences, making it ideal for situations like video demonstrations. This approach aligns more
1226   naturally with how humans and animals learn, as we often imitate behaviors by observation without
1227   knowing the exact actions involved (e.g., muscle movements) (Zare et al., 2024). IFO is particularly
1228   useful in scenarios where action data is unavailable, using techniques like inverse reinforcement
1229   learning to infer the underlying policy.
1230   We further observe the potential of our MConbo framework to handle IFO problem, as illustrated in
