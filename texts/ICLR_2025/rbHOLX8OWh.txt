009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We investigate feature universality in large language models (LLMs), a research
012            field that aims to understand how different models similarly represent concepts in
013            the latent spaces of their intermediate layers. Demonstrating feature universality
014            allows discoveries about latent representations to generalize across several models.
015            However, comparing features across LLMs is challenging due to polysemanticity,
016            in which individual neurons often correspond to multiple features rather than
017            distinct ones. This makes it difficult to disentangle and match features across
018
               different models. To address this issue, we employ a method known as dictionary
               learning by using sparse autoencoders (SAEs) to transform LLM activations into
019
               more interpretable spaces spanned by neurons corresponding to individual features.
020
               After matching feature neurons across models via activation correlation, we apply
021            representational space similarity metrics like Singular Value Canonical Correlation
022            Analysis to analyze these SAE features across different LLMs. Our experiments
023            reveal significant similarities in SAE feature spaces across various LLMs, providing
024            new evidence for feature universality.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across a wide range
030   of natural language tasks (Bubeck et al., 2023; Naveed et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023). However,
031   understanding how these models represent and process information internally remains a significant
032   challenge (Bereska and Gavves, 2024). A key question in this domain is whether different LLMs
033
      learn similar internal representations, or if each model develops its own unique way of encoding
      linguistic knowledge. This question of feature universality is crucial for several reasons: it impacts
034
      the generalizability of interpretability findings across models, it could accelerate the development of
035
      more efficient training techniques, and it may provide insights into safer and more controllable AI
036   systems (Chughtai et al., 2023; Gurnee et al., 2024; Sharkey et al., 2024; Bricken et al., 2023).
037
038   Comparing features across LLMs is inherently difficult due to the phenomenon of polysemanticity,
039
      where individual neurons often correspond to multiple features rather than distinct ones Elhage et al.
      (2022). This superposition of features makes it challenging to disentangle and match representations
040
      across different models. To address these challenges, we propose a novel approach leveraging sparse
041
      autoencoders (SAEs) to transform LLM activations into more interpretable spaces. SAEs allow us to
042   decompose the complex, superposed representations within LLMs into distinct features that are easier
043   to analyze and compare across models (Makhzani and Frey, 2013; Cunningham et al., 2023; Bricken
044   et al., 2023). We then apply specific representational space similarity metrics to these SAE features,
045   enabling a rigorous quantitative analysis of feature universality across different LLMs (Klabunde
046   et al., 2023). Our methodology involves several key steps: obtaining SAEs trained on the activations
047   of multiple LLMs to extract interpretable features, developing and applying representational similarity
048   metrics tailored for high-dimensional SAE feature spaces, and conducting extensive experiments
049   comparing SAE features across models with varying architectures, sizes, and training regimes.
050   To verify the effectiveness of our approach, we present a comprehensive set of experiments and
051   results. Our experimental approach involves solving two issues for representational space similarity
052   measurement: the permutation issue, which involves aligning individual neurons, and the rotational
053   issue, which involves comparing two spaces which have different basis that are rotationally-invariant.
      Additionally, we present an approach to filter out feature matches with low semantically-meaningful

054
      similarity. We demonstrate high correlations between individual SAE features extracted from
055   distinct models, providing evidence for feature universality. Furthermore, we show that semantically
056   meaningful subspaces (e.g., features related to specific concepts, such as Emotions) exhibit even
057   higher similarity across models. We also analyze how feature similarity varies across different layers
058   of LLMs, revealing patterns in how representations evolve through the model depth.
059
      This work opens up several exciting avenues for future research. Exploring whether universal features
060
      can be leveraged to improve transfer learning or model compression techniques may lead to more
061   efficient training paradigms (Yosinski et al., 2014; Hinton et al., 2015). Additionally, examining
062   the implications of feature universality for AI safety and control, particularly in identifying and
063   mitigating potentially harmful representations, could contribute to the development of safer AI
064   systems (Hendrycks et al., 2023). By shedding light on the internal representations of LLMs, our
065   research contributes to the broader goal of developing more interpretable, efficient, and reliable AI
066   systems (Barez et al., 2023). Understanding the commonalities and differences in how various models
067   encode information is a crucial step toward building a more comprehensive theory of how large
068   language models process and generate human language. Our key contributions include:
069
            1. A novel framework for comparing LLM representations using SAEs and similarity metrics.
070
071         2. Empirical evidence supporting the existence of universal features across diverse LLMs.
072         3. A quantitative analysis of how feature similarity varies across layers and semantic domains.
073         4. A step-by-step approach to compare feature weight representations by paired features that
074            differs from previous methods which compare feature activations.

089   2   BACKGROUND
090
091   Sparse Autoencoders. Sparse autoencoders (SAEs) are a type of autoencoder designed to learn
092   efficient, sparse representations of input data by imposing a sparsity constraint on the hidden layer
093   (Makhzani and Frey, 2013). The network takes in an input x ∈ Rn and reconstructs it as an output
094   x̂ using the equation x̂ = W′ σ(Wx + b), where W ∈ Rh×n is the encoder weight matrix, b is a
095   bias term, σ is a nonlinear activation function, and W′ is the decoder matrix, which often uses the
096
      transpose of the encoder weights. SAE training aims to both encourage sparsity in the activations
                                                                          2
097   h = σ(Wx + b) and to minimize the reconstruction loss ∥x − x̂∥2 .
098   The sparsity constraint encourages a large number of the neuron activations to remain inactive (close
099   to zero) for any given input, while a few neurons, called feature neurons, are highly active. Recent
100   work has replaced the regularization term by using the TopK activation function as the nonlinear
101   activation function, which selects only the top K feature neurons with the highest activations (Gao
102   et al., 2024), zeroing out the other activations. The active feature neurons tend to activate only for
103   specific concepts in the data, promoting monosemanticity. Therefore, since there is a mapping from
104
      LLM neurons to SAE feature neurons that "translates" LLM activations to features, this method is a
      type of dictionary learning (Olshausen and Field, 1997).
105
106   SAE Feature Weight Spaces. By analyzing UMAPs of feature weights from an SAE trained
107   on a middle residual stream layer of Claude 3 Sonnet, researchers discovered SAE feature spaces
      organized in neighborhoods of semantic concepts, allowing them to identify subspaces corresponding


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      to concepts such as biology and conflict (Templeton et al., 2024). Additionally, these concepts appear
109   to be organized in hierarchical clusters, such as "disease" clusters that contain sub-clusters of specific
110   diseases like flus. In Section §4.3, we measure the extent of similarity of these semantic feature
111   spaces across LLMs.
112
      Representational Space Similarity: Representational similarity measures typically compare neural
113
      network activations by assessing the similarity between activations from a consistent set of inputs
114
      (Klabunde et al., 2023). In this paper, we take a different approach by comparing the representations
115   using the decoder weight matrices W ′ of SAEs, where the columns correspond to the feature neurons.
116   We calculate a similarity score m(W, W ′ ) for pairs of representations W in SAE A and W ′ from
117   SAE B. We obtain two scores via the following two representational similarity metrics:
118
      Singular Value Canonical Correlation Analysis (SVCCA). Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA)
119
      seeks to identify pairs of canonical variables, ui and vi , from two sets of variables, X ∈ Rn×d1
120
      and Y ∈ Rn×d2 , that are maximally correlated with each other (Hotelling, 1936). Singular Value
121   Canonical Correlation Analysis (SVCCA) (Raghu et al., 2017) enhances CCA by first applying
122   Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) to both X and Y, which can be expressed as:
123
124
                                                 T
125                                   X = UX SX VX ,          Y = UY SY VYT
126
127   where UX and UY are the matrices containing the left singular vectors (informative directions), and
128   SX and SY are diagonal matrices containing the singular values. By projecting the original data onto
129   these informative directions, noise is reduced. SVCCA then applies CCA on the transformed datasets,
130   UX and UY , resulting in correlation scores between the most informative components. These scores
131   are averaged to obtain a similarity score.
132
      Representational Similarity Analysis (RSA). Representational Similarity Analysis (RSA)
133   (Kriegeskorte et al., 2008) works by first computing, for each space, a Representational Dissimi-
134   larity Matrix (RDM) D ∈ Rn×n , where each element of the matrix represents the dissimilarity (or
135   similarity) between every pair of data points within a space. The RDM essentially summarizes the
136   pairwise distances between all possible data pairs in the feature space. A common distance metric
137   used is the Euclidean distance. After an RDM is computed for each space, a correlation measurement
138   like Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient is applied to the pair of RDMs to obtain a similarity
139   score. RSA has been applied to measure relational similarity of stimuli across brain regions and of
140   activations across neural network layers (Klabunde et al., 2023).
141   SVCCA vs RSA. SVCCA focuses on the comparison of entire vector spaces by aligning the principal
142   components (singular vectors) of two representation spaces and then computing the canonical
143   correlations between them. In other words, it identifies directions in the space that carry the most
144   variance and checks how well these align between two models or layers. On the other hand, RSA is
145   primarily concerned with the structure of relationships, or relational structure, between different data
146   points (like stimuli) within a representation space. It allows us to measure how well relations such as
147
      king-queen transfer across models. Thus, SVCCA focuses on comparing the aligned subspaces of
      two representation spaces, while RSA is more suitable for analyzing the structural similarity between
148
      representations at the level of pairwise relationships.

151   3    M ETHODOLOGY
152
153
      Representational similarity is important for capturing the across-model generalizations of both: 1)
154
      Feature Spaces, which are spaces spanned by groups of features, and 2) Feature Relations. We
155
      compare SAEs trained on layer Ai from LLM A and layer Bj from LLM B. This is done for every
156   layer pair. To compare latent spaces using our selected metrics, we have to solve both permutation and
157   rotational alignment issues. For the permutation issue, we have to find a way to pair neuron weights.
158   Typically, these metrics are performed by pairing activation vectors from two models according to
159   common input instances. For example, input Ik is passed through models A and B, and then a vector
160   of N1 neuron activations from model A is compared to a paired vector of N2 activations from model
161   B. The common input instance creates individual mappings from one space to another, which is
      required for metrics like SVCCA and RSA (Klabunde et al., 2023).


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      In our paper, we do not pair activation vectors by input instances, but we pair the feature neuron
163   weights themselves. This means we have to find individual pairings of "similar" neurons in SAE
164   A to SAE B. However, we do not know which features map to which features; the order of these
165   features are permuted in the weight matrices, and some features may not have a corresponding
166   partner. Therefore, we have to pairwise match them via a correlation metric. For the rotational issue,
167   models typically develop their own basis for latent space representations, so features may be similar
168   relation-wise across spaces, but not rotation-wise due to differing basis. To solve this issue, we
169   employ rotation-invariant metrics.
170   Assessing Scores with Baseline. We follow the approach of Kriegeskorte et al. (2008) to randomly
171   pair features to obtain a baseline score. We compare the score of the features paired by correlation
172   (which we refer to as the "paired features") with the average score of N runs of randomly paired
173   features to obtain a p-value score. If the p-value is less than 0.05, the metric suggests that the
174   similarity is statistically meaningful.
175   In summary, the steps to carry out our similarity comparison experiments are given below, and steps
176   1 to 3 are illustrated in Figure 8:
177
178         1. For the permutation issue: Get mean activation correlation of two decoder SAEs for similar
179            LLMs. Pair each feature A with its max activation correlated feature from model B.
180         2. Rearrange the order of features in each matrix to pair them row by row.
181         3. For the rotational issue: Apply various rotation-invariant metrics to get a Paired Score.
182
            4. Using the same metrics, obtain the similarity scores of N random pairings to estimate a
183
               null distribution. Obtain a p-value of where the paired score falls in the null distribution to
184            determine statistical significance.
185
186   Semantic Subspace Matching Experiments. For these experiments, we first identify semantically
187   similar subspaces, and then compare their similarity. This is summarized by the following steps:
188
189
            1. We find a group of related features in each model by searching for features which activate
               highly on the top 5 samples’ tokens that belong to a concept category. For example,
190
               the concept of "emotions" contains keywords such as "rage". Since the correlations are
191
               calculated by tokens, we use single-token keywords. If a keyword from a concept appears at
192            least once, that feature is included. The keywords in each category are given in Appendix D.
193
            2. We obtain mappings between these subsets of features using max activation correlation.
194
195         3. We characterize the relationships of features within these groups (eg. pairwise differences
196
               between features), and compare these relationships across models using similarity metrics.
197   Assessing Scores with Baseline. We use two types of tests which compare the paired score to a null
198   distribution. Each test examines that the score is rare under a certain null distribution assumption:
199
200         1. Test 1: We compare the score of a semantic subspace mapping to the mean score of randomly
201            shuffled pairings of the same subspaces. This test shows just comparing the subspace of
202            features is not enough; the features must be paired.
203         2. Test 2: We compare the score of a semantic subspace mapping to a mean score of mappings
204            between randomly selected feature subsets of the same sizes as the semantic subspaces. This
205            uses a correlation matrix only between the two subsets, not between the entire space. This
206            shows that the high similarity score does not hold for any two subspaces of the same size.
207
      Metrics for the Permutation Issue: These metrics measure individual, local similarity.
208
209   Mean Activation Correlation. We take the mean activation correlation between neurons, following
210   the approach of Bricken et al. (2023). We pass a dataset of samples through both models. Then, for
211   each feature in model A, we find its activation correlation with every feature in model B, using tokens
212
      as the common instance pairing. For each feature in model A, we find its highest correlated feature
      in model B. We pair these features together when conducting our experiments. We refer to scores
213
      obtained by pairing via mean activation correlation as Paired Scores.
214
215   Filter by Top Tokens. We notice that there are many "non-concept mappings" with "non-concept
      features" that have top 5 token activations on end-of-text / padding tokens, spaces, new lines, and


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      punctuation; these non-concept mappings greatly bring our scores down as they are not accurately
217   pairing features representing concepts, as we describe further in Appendix B. By filtering max
218   activation correlation mappings to remove non-concept mapping features, we significantly raise the
219   similarity scores. We also only keep mappings that share at least one keyword in their top 5 token
220   activations. The list of non-concept keywords is given in the Appendix B.
221
      Filter Many-to-1 Mappings. We find that some of these mappings are many-to-1, which means
222
      that more than one feature maps to the same feature. We found that removing many-to-1 mappings
223   slightly increased the scores, and we discuss possible reasons why this may occur in Appendix A.
224   However, the scores still show high similarity even with the inclusion of many-to-1 mappings, as seen
225   in Figures 2. We show only scores of 1-to-1 mappings (filtering out the many-to-1 mappings) in the
226   main text, and show scores of many-to-1 mappings in Appendix A. We use features from the SAEs
227   of Pythia-70m and Gemma-1-2B as "many" features which map onto "one" feature in the SAEs of
228   Pythia-160m and Gemma-2-2B.
229   Metrics for the Rotational Issue: Rotation-invariant metrics measure global similarity after feature
230   pairing. We apply metrics which measure: 1) How well subspaces align using SVCCA, and 2) How
231   similar feature relations like king-queen are using RSA. We set the inner similarity function using
232   Pearson correlation the outer similarity function using Spearman correlation, and compute the RDM
233   using Euclidean Distance.

259
      In summary, for almost all layer pairs after layer 0, we find the p-value of SAE experiments for
260   SVCCA, which measures rotationally-invariant alignment, to be low; all of them are between 0.00 to
261   0.01 using 100 samples.2 While RSA also passes many p-value tests, their values are much lower,
262   suggesting a relatively weaker similarity in terms of pairwise differences. It is possible that many
263   features are not represented in just one layer; hence, a layer from one model can have similarities to
264   multiple layers in another model. We also note that the first layer of every model, layer 0, has a very
265   high p-value when compared to every other layer, including when comparing layer 0 from another
266   model. This may be because layer 0 contains few discernible, meaningful, and comparable features.

      5    R ELATED W ORK
392
393
394   Superposition and Sparse Autoencoders. Superposition is a phenomenon in which a model, in
395   response to the issue of having to represent more features than it has parameters, learns feature
396   representations distributed across many parameters (Elhage et al., 2022). This causes its parameters,
397   or neurons, to be polysemantic, which means that each neuron is involved in representing more than
398   one feature. This leads to issues for interpretability, which aims to disentangle and cleanly identify
399
      features in models in order to manipulate them, such as by steering a model away from deception
      (Templeton et al., 2024). To address the issue of polysemanticity, Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs)
400
      have been applied to disentangle an LLM’s polysemantic neuron activations into monosemantic
401
      "feature neurons", which are encouraged to represent an isolated concept (Makhzani and Frey, 2013;
402   Cunningham et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2024; Rajamanoharan et al., 2024a;b). Features interactions in
403   terms of circuits have also been studied (Marks et al., 2024).
404
405
      Feature Universality. To the best of our knowledge, only Bricken et al. (2023) has done a quantitative
      study on individual SAE feature similarity for two 1-layer toy models, finding that individual SAE
406
      feature correlations are stronger than individual LLM neuron correlations; however, this study did
407
      not analyze the global properties of feature spaces. Gurnee et al. (2024) found evidence of universal
408   neurons across language models by applying pairwise correlation metrics, and taxonimized families
409   of neurons based on patterns of downstream, functional effects. O’Neill et al. (2024) discover "feature
410   families", which represent related hierarchical concepts, in SAEs across two different datasets. Recent
411   work has shown that as vision and language models are trained with more parameters and with better
412   methods, their representational spaces converge to more similar representations (Huh et al., 2024).
413
      Representational Space Similarity. Previous work has studied neuron activation spaces by utilizing
414   metrics to compare the geometric similarities of representational spaces (Raghu et al., 2017; Wang
415   et al., 2018; Kornblith et al., 2019; Klabunde et al., 2024; 2023; Kriegeskorte et al., 2008). It was
416   found that even models with different architectures may share similar representation spaces, hinting
417   at feature universality. However, these techniques have yet to be applied to the feature spaces of
418   sparse autoencoders trained on LLMs. Moreover, these techniques compare the similarity of paired
419   input activations. Our work differs as it compares the similarity of paired feature weights. Essentially,
420   previous works do not show that one can match LLMs by features that both LLMs find in common.
421   Feature Manifolds. In real world data, features may lie on a manifold, where nearby features respond
422   to similar data. (Olah and Batson, 2023; Bricken et al., 2023). Previous work has discovered the
423   existence of features corresponding to months and days of the week arranged in a circular structure
424   in SAE feature spaces across models (Engels et al., 2024). Studying how feature arrangements
425   generalize across models may shed light on how features lie on a manifold.
426   Mechanistic Interpretability. Recent work has made notable progress in neuron interpretation
427   (Foote et al., 2023; Garde et al., 2023) and interpreting the interactions between Attention Heads
428   and MLPs (Neo et al., 2024). Other work in mechanistic interpretability has traditionally focused on
429   ’circuits’-style analysis (Elhage et al., 2021), as well as the use of steering vectors to direct models at
430   inference using identified representations (Zou et al., 2023; Turner et al., 2023; Bereska and Gavves,
431   2024). These approaches have been combined with SAEs to steer models in the more interpretable
      SAE feature space (Nanda and Conmy, 2024). Steering vectors have been found across models;


                                                          8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      for instance, Arditi et al. (2024) found "Refusal Vectors" that steered a model to refuse harmful
433   instructions across 13 different models, including Gemma and Llama-2.
434
435   AI Safety. Advanced AI systems may not be well aligned with the values of their designers (Ngo
436
      et al., 2022), due to phenomena such as goal misgeneralization (Shah et al., 2022; Langosco et al.,
      2022), deceptive alignment (Hubinger et al., 2024; 2021), sycophancy (Sharma et al., 2023), and
437
      reward tampering (Denison et al., 2024). SAEs may be able to find AI safety-related features across
438
      models; using SAEs to reliably find universal capabilities, especially in combination with control
439   methods such as steering, may help provide solutions to the above pheonomena.
440

      6    C ONCLUSION
443
      In this study, we address the underexplored domain of feature universality in sparse autoencoders
444
      (SAEs) trained on LLMs with multiple layers and across multiple model pairs. To achieve these
445
      insights, we developed a novel methodology to pair features with high mean activation correlations,
446   and then assess their global similarity of the resulting subspaces using SVCCA and RSA. Our findings
447   reveal a high degree of similarity in SAE feature spaces across various models. Furthermore, our
448   research reveals that subspaces of features associated with specific semantic concepts, such as calendar
449   or people tokens, demonstrate remarkably high similarity across different models. This suggests that
450   certain semantic feature subspaces are universally encoded across varied LLM architectures. Our
451   work enhances understanding of how LLMs represent information at a fundamental level, opening
452   new avenues for further research into model interpretability and the transferability of learned features.
453
454   L IMITATIONS
455
456   We perform analysis on available pre-trained SAEs for similar LLMs that use the same tokenizer, and
457   preferrably on SAEs with the same number of features to match them. However, there are a limited
458   number of pre-trained SAEs on large models. Our study is also limited by the inherent challenges
459   in interpreting and comparing high-dimensional feature spaces. The methods we used to measure
460   similarity, while robust, may not capture all nuances of feature interactions and representations within
461
      the SAEs. Additionally, our analysis is constrained by the computational resources available, which
      may have limited the scale and depth of our comparisons. The generalizability of our findings to SAEs
462
      trained on more diverse datasets or specialized domain-specific LLMs remains an open question.
463
      Furthermore, the static nature of our analysis does not account for potential temporal dynamics in
464   feature representation that might occur during the training process of both LLMs and SAEs.
465
466
467
      F UTURE W ORK
468
      We performed similar analysis on the Pythia and Gemma LLMs that the SAEs in this paper were
469
      trained on, and found that they have some similarity, but much lower than the SAEs (around 0.1-0.2
470
      for all layer pairs) with no patterns showing that the middle layers were the most similar; however,
471   the LLMs did not have corresponding weights for the "residual stream", which is an addition of
472   activations from the MLP and attention head layers, so we compared the MLPs of LLMs. Additionally,
473   unlike the SAE dimensions, the LLM layers they were trained on had different dimensions (eg. 512
474   for Pythia-70m vs 768 Pythia-160m), making feature matching less trivial. Thus, future work could
475   study the comparison of LLM similarity by feature weights to SAEs. Additional experiments could
476   include using ground-truth feature experiments for LLMs that partially share ground-truth features to
477   see if SAEs are modeling the same features, and performing functional similarity experiments.

849
       We define "non-concept features" as features which are not modeling specific concepts that can be
850
       mapped well across models. Their highest activations are on new lines, spaces, punctuation, and
851    EOS tokens. As such, they may introduce noise when computing similarity scores, as their removal
852    appears to greatly improve similarity scores. We hypothesize that one reason they introduce noise
853    is that these could be tokens that aren’t specific to concepts, but multiple concepts; thus, they are
854    mixing concept spaces up when matching is done.
855
        For instance, say feature X from model A fires highly on "!" in the sample “I am BOB!”, while
856
        feature Y from model B fires highly on "!" in the sample, “that cat ate!”
857
858    A feature X that activates on "!" in "BOB!" may be firing for names, while a feature Y that activates
859    on "!" in "ate!" may fire on food-related tokens. As such, features activating on non-concept features
860
       are not concept specific, and we could map a feature firing on names to a feature firing on food.
       We call these possibly "incoherent" mappings "non-concept mappings". By removing non-concept
861
       mappings, we show that SAEs reveal highly similar feature spaces.
862
863    We filter "non-concept features" that fire, for at least one of their top 5 dataset examples, on a
       "non-concept" keyword. The list of "non-concept" keywords we use is given below:
