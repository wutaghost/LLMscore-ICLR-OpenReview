009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep neural networks (DNNs) have proven powerful for denoising individual im-
012            ages, but there is a limit to the noise level they can handle. In applications like
013            cryogenic electron microscopy (cryo-EM), the noise level is extremely high but
014            datasets contain hundreds of thousands of projections of the same molecule, each
015            taken a different viewing direction. This redundancy of information is useful in
016
               traditional denoising techniques known as class averaging methods, where images
               are clustered, aligned, and then averaged to reduce the noise level. We present a
017
               neural network architecture based on polar representation of images and trans-
018
               formers that simultaneously clusters, aligns, and denoises cryo-EM projection im-
019            ages. Results on synthetic data show accurate denoising performance using this
020            architecture, with a relative mean squared error of 0.06 at signal-to-noise (SNR)
021            level of 0.05, outperforming traditional filter-based methods by a factor of 2×.
022

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Many imaging problems amount to reconstructing some unknown quantity (often two-dimensional
027
      images or three-dimensional density maps) from several measurements obtained through a lossy,
      noisy forward process. In the simplest case where the forward process is identical for all measure-
028
      ments, information from these measurements can be combined by simple averaging, which reduces
029
      the relative noise level. This suggests using similar strategies also for the general case where there
030   is some difference in setup for each measurement.
031
032   Cryo-EM (Dubochet et al., 1982) is a molecular imaging technique that uses transmission electron
033
      microscopes to analyze the structure of molecules, typically proteins of interest for biological or
      medical research. In its single-particle reconstruction (SPR) form, this involves freezing a sample
034
      containing millions of copies of a single molecule in a thin layer of vitreous ice, imaging the frozen
035
      sample in an electron microscope, and then reconstructing the 3D structure of the molecule from the
036   resulting 2D projection images, known as micrographs. This process involves first extracting images
037   of individual particles from the micrographs, denoising these images, estimating their corresponding
038   viewing directions, and then reconstructing a 3D density map (Frank, 1996).
039
      Apart from the unknown viewing directions, a main challenge of cryo-EM imaging is the high
040
      noise level. Indeed, since the electron dose needs to be kept low enough to avoid radiation damage
041
      during the imaging process, each projection contains only a weak signal besides a large amount of
042   shot noise (Bendory et al., 2020). Denoising is therefore a central task in any cryo-EM pipeline,
043   such as for visual inspection (Singer & Sigworth, 2020), detecting contamination (outliers) (Bhamre
044   et al., 2016), generating templates for particle picking (Singer & Sigworth, 2020), identifying high-
045   quality projection images (Scheres, 2015), and as preprocessing for certain ab-initio reconstruction
046   methods (Singer & Shkolnisky, 2011). However, despite the large number of molecules in the
047   sample, averaging is not directly applicable because of the unknown viewing directions.
048   Traditional denoising methods can be divided into two categories. The first is based on applying
049   a Wiener filter to the images (Frank et al., 1996; Tang et al., 2007; Sindelar & Grigorieff, 2011;
050   Bhamre et al., 2016). While this approach exploits prior information on the content of the images and
051   is optimal in the class of linear estimators, it often results in a high level of blurring for the relevant
052   noise levels. The second group of denoising methods is known collectively as class averaging
053   methods. These rely on the geometry of the problem described above – given the large number of
      images, some will be taken from the same viewing direction and only differ by an in-plane rotation.

054
      Class averaging methods therefore first classify the images, where each cluster consists of images
055   that are similar up to some in-plane rotation, then align those images and average them (van Heel,
056   1984; Park & Chirikjian, 2014; Zhao & Singer, 2014; Scheres, 2015). This average has less noise,
057   no bias introduced by a filter, and can then be used for subsequent reconstruction tasks. However,
058   the high noise level often makes both classification and alignment very challenging (Jensen, 2001).
059
      Like many other disciplines, cryo-EM processing has been improved in several ways by the introduc-
060
      tion of methods from deep learning. This ranges from particle picking (Wang et al., 2016; Wagner
061   et al., 2019) and postprocessing of 3D densities (Sanchez-Garcia et al., 2021) to using implicit neu-
062   ral representations to represent 2D images (Bibas et al., 2021; Nasiri & Bepler, 2022; Kwon et al.,
063   2023) and 3D structures (Zhong et al., 2021; Nashed et al., 2021; Levy et al., 2022; Schwab et al.,
064   2024). DNNs have also been used for denoising, but in the setting of entire micrographs (Bepler
065   et al., 2020; Buchholz et al., 2019; Palovcak et al., 2020). This problem is different from the denois-
066   ing of projection images in several respects, such only being able to leverage information within a
067   single micrograph. These methods also do not explicitly incorporate rotational symmetries of the
068   data distribution, limiting their performance.
069   This work proposes to solve the denoising problem by simultaneously denoising multiple images
070   in a manner that extends traditional class averaging by leveraging prior information on the content
071   of the projection images. The novel architecture rotationally aligns and integrates information from
072   multiple cryo-EM projection images using a new convolutional attention mechanism that also guar-
073   antees the overall rotational equivariance of the system. This network, known as polar transformer,
074   is shown to successfully cluster, align, and denoise sets of cryo-EM images on simulated datasets,
075
      yielding a reduction of more than 2× compared to classical Wiener filter methods on images of SNR
      as low as 0.05, or 1.5× compared to deep neural networks operating on single projections.
076
077   The rest of this paper is structured as follows. Section 2 describes the three main denoising tasks:
078   single-image denoising, aligned denoising, and class averaged denoising. Then Section 3 reviews
079   some of the existing literature on the subject of cryo-EM denoising. Sections 4 and 5 describe the
080   construction of the polar transformer and some of its properties. Finally, section 6 gives details of
081
      concrete models and and how we trained and tested them.

083   2     P ROBLEM S ETUP
084
085   The denoising problem in cryo-EM aims at recovering a clean image x ∈ X from a noisy version
086   y ∈ X. We represent all images through their pixel values on an L × L raster, i.e., X = RL×L .
087
      The clean images are assumed to be tomographic projections of a 3D potential density from a bio-
088   logical macromolecule. These are calculated using line integrals of that density along some chosen
089   viewing direction. In the full model, there is also the application of a contrast transfer function (CTF)
090   to simulate the optical properties of the microscope, but we will omit this in the present work to sim-
091   plify the discussion. We shall also assume that these are images of individual projection images
092   (i.e., we are past the particle-picking stage) and that images have been centered, but not rotationally
093   aligned. While simple, the above model has proven to be relatively accurate (Frank, 1996; Vulović
094   et al., 2013) and is used in a across range of cryo-EM reconstruction methods (Barnett et al., 2017;
095   Scheres, 2012; Punjani et al., 2017; Zhong et al., 2021). To simulate the noisy projections y from the
096
      clean image x, we then use additive Gaussian noise, a common assumption in many reconstruction
      methods (Punjani et al., 2017).
097
098   We will now consider three distinct denoising tasks.
099
100   2.1   I NDIVIDUAL P ROJECTIONS
101
102   The simplest setting is to consider each projection separately, potentially combining with informa-
103   tion from other projections later on. We thus have a mapping f : X → X such that x ≈ f (y). We
104
      call this the individual image denoising task. This is the setting of Wiener filters, which are trained
      on a large set of images to calculate their statistics and then applied separately to each noisy image.
105
106   One property that we would like f to exhibit is equivariance to rotations. This follows from the
107   fact that the probability distribution of the images (both clean and noisy) is invariant to in-plane
      rotation. Indeed, one image is as likely to appear in the data as a rotated copy of that image. Letting

      2.3   G ENERAL S ETS

127   In the general case, we cannot assume that our images have already been clustered by viewing
128   direction. Instead, we rely on the fact that any sufficiently large set already contains such clusters of
129   images that are the same up to rotation. This is the underlying idea of class averaging (see below)
130   and represents the most typical denoising setting. We will refer to this as the full set denoising task
131   and the challenge here is identify the similar images in the set (up to rotation) and denoise them
132   jointly. Such a denoiser again takes the form f : X K → X K and satisfies the same equivariance
133   property equation 2 as in the directional set case.

135   3     R ELATED W ORK
136
137
      Existing approaches to image denoising can be categorized into shallow (non-neural) approaches and
138   DNNs. While the former often rely on geometrical properties of the images (Dabov et al., 2007),
139   they also exploit (often implicitly) some prior knowledge about those images. DNNs have typically
140   extended these methods by enriching the latter component, providing stronger priors encoded in the
141   neural networks by training on a large set of images. In contrast, these networks are often less able
142   to exploit geometrical properties of the images, such as those found in cryo-EM.
143
      The simplest way to denoise cryo-EM images is to filter them. These can either be fixed low-pass
144   filters (referred to as “binning”) (Bartesaghi et al., 2018), stationary Wiener filters (Frank et al.,
145   1996; Tang et al., 2007; Sindelar & Grigorieff, 2011) or generalized Wiener filters calculated in a
146   steerable basis Bhamre et al. (2016). While these have achieved a certain degree of success, they are
147   fundamentally limited in that they are linear denoisers, and can therefore only achieve optimality
148   with respect to a Gaussian prior distribution. Since the prior distribution of clean cryo-EM images
149   is necessarily non-Gaussian, these methods will typically result in clean, but overly blurred images.
150   Another method for denoising in cryo-EM is class averaging (van Heel, 1984; Park & Chirikjian,
151   2014; Zhao & Singer, 2014; Scheres, 2015). The central idea here is to use the fact that all the images
152   in a cryo-EM dataset cluster naturally by viewing direction. Indeed, given a particular projection
153   image, it is typically possible to find another set of images taken from the same viewing direction,
154   but subject to a different in-plane rotation. The goal of these class averaging methods is therefore
155   to cluster the images, align them, and then average. This results in images that have less blurring
156   compared to filter-based methods, but often a large number of images (on the order of 100) are
157   needed to achieve a low reconstruction error. Furthermore, the classification and alignment steps are
158
      quite sensitive to noise and therefore fail at low SNR.
159   Another way to improve over the linear denoisers is to consider non-linear denoisers. This is the
160   basis of the DnCNN architecture (Zhang et al., 2017), where a standard residual CNN is trained to
161   recreate the clean image from a noisy version of that image. While the work focused on denoising
      of photos, the technique can be applied to any set of images.

182   More generally, DNNs have been used in the cryo-EM reconstruction pipeline to provide stronger

      has been extended by Kimanius et al. (2024), where the method was used to reconstruct very small
186
      molecules (of molecular weight 40 kDa).
187
188   In a separate line of research, DNNs have found widespread use in cryo-EM as a general-purpose
189   function approximation tool. Here, DNNs are used in an unsupervised manner and optimized to fit
190
      a particular cryo-EM dataset, for example with the goal of fitting a 3D model (Zhong et al., 2021;
      Gupta et al., 2021; Schwab et al., 2024; Nashed et al., 2021; Levy et al., 2022) or the set of 2D
191
      projections (Bibas et al., 2021; Kwon et al., 2023; Nasiri & Bepler, 2022). Consequently, the neural
192
      networks typically do not impose a specific prior on the reconstructions beyond the inductive bias
193   provided by the particular architecture. This is in contrast with the approach of Bepler et al. (2020);
194   Kimanius et al. (2021; 2024) and the method proposed in this work, which train DNNs to form
195   universal estimators by encoding prior information extracted from publicly available datasets.

      4     P OLAR D ECOMPOSITION AND CNN S
199
      For the task of denoising images, a natural approach is to train a convolutional neural network for the
200
      purpose, with noisy images as input and clean images as targets (Zhang et al., 2017; Gurrola-Ramos
201
      et al., 2021). While these have been successful in various domains, such as natural and medical
202   images, they do not apply directly to cryo-EM projection images.
203
204   The main reason for this is that a single cryo-EM image has a much higher level of noise compared
205
      to other modalities. We thus need to denoise multiple images jointly by classifying, aligning, and
      averaging them in the manner of class averaging (van Heel, 1984; Park & Chirikjian, 2014; Zhao
206
      & Singer, 2014; Scheres, 2015) To achieve this using a neural network architecture, we need a
207
      representation that allows us to rotate images in an efficient and stable manner. As it turns out, this
208   representation will also enforce rotational equivariance, which is desirable in a cryo-EM denoiser
209   since the distribution of images is invariant to rotation.
210

      of a radial and an angular grid.

312   a DNN. Since the polar representation is essentially a remapping of the original Cartesian image,
313   standard layers from CNNs can be applied to the output of the angular convolutions, such as Re-
314   LUs (Goodfellow et al., 2016), avoiding less natural spectral non-linearities required for other equiv-

318   Singer, 2013; Langfield et al., 2022). Finally, we note that the above construction can be formulated
319   as an SO(2)-CNN in the formalism of Kondor & Trivedi (2018).
320   We shall refer to a DNN whose linear layers consist of the angular convolutions described above
321   as polar CNNs. As constructed, they are equivariant to shifts along the angular axis and may be
322   converted to a rotationally equivariant DNN operating on Cartesian images using the Cartesian-to-
323   polar and polar-to-Cartesian mappings discussed above. We shall abuse terminology slightly and
      also refer to these as polar CNNs.

      5     P OLAR T RANSFORMER
325
326
      To go beyond single-image denoising, we must find a way to combine information from multiple
327
      images. This is particularly relevant in the cryo-EM setting, where copies of the same molecule are
328   imaged from different directions. Indeed, this observation is the basis for one of the most common
329   denoising methods in cryo-EM, class averaging (van Heel, 1984; Park & Chirikjian, 2014; Zhao &
330   Singer, 2014; Scheres, 2015).
331
      One direct extension of the single-image denoiser would be to use its output in a class averaging
332
      method, where the denoised images are clustered and aligned. However, since training would not be
333
      end-to-end, the results would be suboptimal. In particular, the single-image denoiser is optimized
334   to reproduce the original image, potentially discarding information relevant for clustering and align-
335   ment. We will instead combine denoising and class averaging into an end-to-end neural network
336   that learns how to optimally cluster and align the images.
337
338   5.1   ATTENTION M ECHANISM
339
340   A natural architecture for aggregating disparate sources of information is the transformer, originally
341   introduced in the field of language models (Vaswani et al., 2017; Devlin et al., 2018; Brown et al.,
342   2020), but which has since seen applications in image processing (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), molec-
343   ular modeling (Jumper et al., 2021), and other areas (Peebles & Xie, 2023; Ma et al., 2024). Here, a
344   sequence of tokens are used to generate a key, query, and value vector that are combined using what
345
      is known as an attention mechanism (Vaswani et al., 2017). In language models, tokens are parts of
      words, but these can be any information carrier, such as an image.
346
347   Let us consider a multi-channel polar image z[c, n, m], where c = 0, 1, . . . , C − 1 is the channel
348   index, n = 0, 1, . . . , N − 1 is the radial index, and m = 0, 1, . . . , M − 1 is the angular index. Now
349   we consider three polar CNNs, denoted fθkey , fθquery , and fθvalue , corresponding to the key, query, and
350   value networks, respectively. Here, θ is a vector of weights shared between the different networks.
351   Now suppose we have a set z of K such images z0 , z1 , . . . , zK−1 , which we process using the above
352   networks to key, query, and value vectors. Combining the key and query images, we obtain the
353   scaled dot-product attention coefficient

402   6     E XPERIMENTS AND R ESULTS
403
404   To evaluate the architectures proposed above, we conduct numerical experiments on simulated data.
405   These show how the polar CNN and polar transformer are able to achieve very low denoising errors
406   despite high noise levels, significantly outperforming baseline methods.
407
408   6.1   N EURAL NETWORKS
409
      Two neural network architectures are used in the experiments. The first is a simple polar CNN
410
      consisting of a Cartesian-to-polar layer, 25 angular convolutional layers with 8 channels each, each
411
      followed by a GroupNorm layer (with 4 groups) (Wu & He, 2018) and a ReLU nonlinearity (Good-
412   fellow et al., 2016). Finally, the polar representation was converted back to the Cartesian domain.
413   The convolutional layers had an angular kernel width of 5 and a radial kernel width of 3.
414
415
      The other network used was a polar transformer, which first consisted of a Cartesian-to-polar layer
      followed by a polar CNN preprocessing network of depth 5 applied individually to each image in
416
      the sequence. This was followed by an angular attention module where the key and query networks
417
      consisted of the same polar CNN of depth 3 with shared weights (the value network was set to the
418   identity). Finally, the output of the attention module was postprocessed in a 9-layer polar CNN
419   and the result converted back to the Cartesian domain. The convolutional layers in the CNNs had
420   the same configuration as in the first architecture, but the number of channels in the pre- and post-
421   processing networks was set to 8 while that number was 16 for the key- and query-network.
422
423   6.2   DATA

428
      repeated for another set of 100 molecules, resulting in a testing set of 500 000 clean images. Both
      of these were then used to create three different datasets for training and testing, in accordance with
429
      the three denoising tasks described in Section 2.

      Each model was trained using the training sets described above for a specific SNR (defined as the

535
536         In this work, we have presented a new powerful architecture for joint denoising of cryo-EM projec-
537         tion images: the polar transformer. While this model has significant potential, more work remains
538         before it can be applied to practical problems. Notably, they must be extended to incorporate point

            in Appendix E), and most importantly, scale to larger datasets.

958   D      ATTENTION FOR C LUSTERING
959
960   The polar transformer architecture is designed to combine information from precisely those image
961   in an input set which match up to plane rotation. It is the process of finding out which ones these are
962   that we call clustering.
963
      How it occurs can be observed by studying the activations in the activation coefficient matrix as a
964
      set with known split of two directions is processed. Without loss of generality (because the attention
965
      mechanism is equivariant under permutations of the images), this can be realized by taking the
966   first 8 projections in one directions and the remaining 8 projections in another direction. Ideally,
967   the attention (summed over the angular direction) should then have block-matrix structure, as only
968   the images corresponding to the same direction interact with each other. Indeed this matches the
969   observations in the case of relatively high SNR (see Figure 4). Consequently, the model can then
970   also combine information for denoising purposes just as well as if the directions had already been
971   known a priori, as is the case of a directional set. This manifests in MSE scores that are almost as
      good with two directions as with a single direction (Figure 3).

1034
       Although this paper works with synthetic data throughout, this is for practical reasons – separation
1035
       of concerns, comparability with ground truth, possibility to systematically study different SNRs and
1036   noise distribution regimes.
