011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Learning conditional distributions π ∗ (·|x) is a central problem in machine learn-
014            ing, which is typically approached via supervised methods with paired data
015
               (x, y) ∼ π ∗ . However, acquiring paired data samples is often challenging, espe-
               cially in problems such as domain translation. This necessitates the development
016
               of semi-supervised models that utilize both limited paired data and additional un-
017
               paired i.i.d. samples x ∼ πx∗ and y ∼ πy∗ from the marginal distributions. The us-
018            age of such combined data is complex and often relies on heuristic approaches. To
019            tackle this issue, we propose a new learning paradigm that integrates both paired
020            and unpaired data seamlessly using data likelihood maximization techniques. We
021            demonstrate that our approach also connects intriguingly with inverse entropic
022            optimal transport (OT). This finding allows us to apply recent advances in com-
023            putational OT to establish a light learning algorithm to get π ∗ (·|x). In addition,
024            we derive the universal approximation property demonstrating that our approach
025            can theoretically recover true conditional distributions with arbitrarily small error.
026
               Furthermore, we demonstrate through empirical tests that our method effectively
               learns conditional distributions using paired and unpaired data simultaneously.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Recovering conditional distributions π ∗ (y|x) from data is one of the fundamental problems in ma-
032   chine learning, which appears both in predictive and generative modeling. In predictive modeling,
033   the standard examples of such tasks are the classification, where x ∈ RDx is a feature vector and
034   y ∈ {0, 1, . . . , K} is a class label, and regression, in which case x is also a feature vector and
035   y ∈ RD is a real number. In generative modeling, both x and y are feature vectors in RDx , RDy ,
036   respectively, representing complex objects, and the goal is to find a transformation between them.
037   In our paper, we focus on the case when x and y are multi-dimensional real-value vectors and
038   the true joint data distribution π ∗ (x, y) is a continuous data distribution on RDx × RDy , i.e., we
039   exclude the problems when, e.g., y is a discrete object such as the class label. That is, the scope
040   of our paper is the multi-dimensional probabilistic regression problems, which can be referred to
041   as domain translation problems, as usually x and y are feature vectors representing data from
042   different domains. In turn, the goal is to make a (probabilistic) prediction, where for a new object
043   xnew from the input domain, we aim to predict the corresponding data y(xnew ) from the target
044
      domain, according to the conditional distribution π ∗ (y|x).
045   It is very natural that to learn the conditional distributions π ∗ (y|x) of data one requires input-target
046   data pairs (x, y) ∼ π ∗ , where π ∗ is the true joint distribution of data. In this case, π ∗ (y|x) can be
047   modeled via standard supervised learning approaches starting from a simple regression and ending
048   with conditional generative models (Mirza & Osindero, 2014; Winkler et al., 2019). However, ac-
049   quiring paired data may be costly, while getting unpaired samples x ∼ πx∗ or y ∼ πy∗ from two
050
      domains may be much easier and cheaper. This fact inspired the development of unsupervised (or
      unpaired) learning methods, e.g., (Zhu et al., 2017; Wu et al., 2020) among many others, which aim
051
      to somehow reconstruct the dependencies π ∗ (y|x) with access to unpaired data only.
052
053   While both paired (supervised) and unpaired (unsupervised) domain translation approaches are be-
      ing extremely well developed nowadays, surprisingly, the semi-supervised setup when both paired


                                                          1

054
      and unpaired data is available is much less explored. This is due to the challenge of designing learn-
055   ing objective (loss) which can simultaneously take into account both paired and unpaired data. For
056   example, one potential strategy here is to heuristically combine typical paired and unpaired losses.
057   However, such a strategy leads to complex training objectives, see (Tripathy et al., 2019, M3.5), (Jin
058   et al., 2019, M3.3), (Yang & Chen, 2020, MC), (Vasluianu et al., 2021, M3), (Panda et al., 2023, Eq.
059   8), (Tang et al., 2024, Eq. 8). Therefore, it is reasonable to raise a question: is it possible to design
060   a simple loss to learn π ∗ (y|x) which naturally takes into account both paired and unpaired data?
061   Contributions. In our paper, we positively answer the above-raised question. Namely,
062
063   1. We introduce a novel loss function (optimization objective) designed to facilitate the learning of
064      conditional distributions π ∗ (·|x) using both paired and unpaired training samples derived from π ∗
065      (M3.1). This loss function is based on the well-established principle of likelihood maximization.
066      Our approach’s notable advantage lies in its capacity to support end-to-end learning, thereby
067      seamlessly integrating both paired and unpaired data into the training process.
068   2. We demonstrate the theoretical equivalence between our proposed loss function and the inverse
069      entropic optimal transport problem (M3.2). This finding enables to leverage established compu-
070      tational OT methods to address challenges encountered in semi-supervised learning.
071   3. Building upon recent advancements in the field of computational optimal transport, we provide
072      a light and end-to-end algorithm exploiting the Gaussian mixture parameterization specifically
073      tailored to optimize our proposed likelihood-based loss function (in M3.3).
074   4. We prove that our proposed parameterization satisfies the universal approximation property. This
075      finding theoretically allows our algorithm to recover π ∗ arbitrarily well (M3.4).
076
077   Our empirical validation in M5 shows the impact of both unpaired and paired data on overall per-
078   formance. In particular, our findings reveal that conditional distributions π ∗ (·|x) can be effectively
079   learned even with a modest quantity of paired data (x, y) ∼ π ∗ , provided that a sufficient amount of
080
      auxiliary unpaired data x ∼ πx∗ , y ∼ πy∗ is available.

096   2.1   D OMAIN T RANSLATION P ROBLEMS
097
098   The goal of domain translation (DT) task is to transform data samples from the source domain to
099   the target domain while maintaining the essential content or structure. This approach is widely used
100   in applications like computer vision (Zhu et al., 2017; Lin et al., 2018; Peng et al., 2023), natural
101
      language processing (Jiang et al., 2021; Morishita et al., 2022), and audio processing (Du et al.,
      2022), etc. Domain translation task setups can be classified into supervised (or paired), unsupervised
102
      (or unpaired), and semi-supervised approaches based on the data used for training (see Figure 1).
103
104   Supervised (Paired) Domain Translation relies on matched examples from both the source and
105   target domains, where each input corresponds to a specific output, enabling direct supervision during
                                                                                                          def
106   the learning process. Formally, this setup assumes access to a set of P empirical pairs XYpaired =
                                          ∗

      the conditional distributions π ∗ (·|x) to generate samples y|xnew for new inputs xnew that are not

      present in the training data. While this task is relatively straightforward to solve, obtaining such
109   paired training datasets can be challenging, as it often involves significant time, cost, and effort.
110
111   Unsupervised (Unpaired) Domain Translation, in contrast, does not require direct correspon-
112
      dences between the source and target domains (Zhu et al., 2017, Figure 2). Instead, it involves
      learning to translate between domains using unpaired data, which offers greater flexibility but de-
113
      mands advanced techniques to achieve accurate translation. Formally, we are given Q unpaired

118   respectively. Clearly, the primary challenge in unpaired setup is that the task is inherently ill-posed,
119   leading to multiple potential solutions, many of which may be ambiguous or even not meaningful
120
      (Moriakov et al., 2020). Ensuring the translation’s accuracy and relevance requires careful consid-
      eration of constraints and regularization strategies to guide the learning process (Yuan et al., 2018).

      Overall, the unpaired setup is very important because of large amounts of unpaired data in the wild.

355   4     R ELATED W ORKS
356
357   We review below the most related semi-supervised models and OT-based approaches to our work.
358
      Semi-supervised models. As mentioned in M1, many existing semi-supervised domain translation
359   methods combine paired and unpaired data by incorporating multiple loss terms into complex opti-
360   mization objectives (Jin et al., 2019, M3.3), (Tripathy et al., 2019, M3.5), (Mustafa & Mantiuk, 2020,
361   M3.2), (Paavilainen et al., 2021, M2), (Panda et al., 2023, Eq. 8), (Tang et al., 2024, Eq. 8). However,
362   these approaches often require careful tuning of hyperparameters to balance the various loss terms.
363
      We also note that there exist works addressing the question of incorpotating unpaired data to the
364
      log-likelihood training (8) by adding an extra likelihood terms, see CNFs-related works (Atanov
365   et al., 2019; Izmailov et al., 2020). However, they rely on x being a discrete object (e.g., a class
366   label) and does not easily generalize to the continuous case, see Appendix B.2.2 for details.
367
368
      The recent work by (Gu et al., 2023) utilizes both paired and unpaired data to build a transport plan
      based on key-point guided OT, initially introduced in (Gu et al., 2022). This transport plan is used
369
      as a heuristic to train a conditional score-based model on unpaired or semi-paired data. Overall, we
370
      note that the idea of applying OT in a semi-supervised manner traces back to the seminal work by
371   (Courty et al., 2016), although their focus was on classification, not domain translation.
372
373   Another recent work by (Asadulaev et al., 2024) introduces a neural network-based OT framework
374
      for semi-supervised scenarios, utilizing general cost functionals for OT. However, their method re-
      quires manually constructing cost functions which can incorporate class labels or predefined pairs.
375
      In contrast, our approach adjusts the cost dynamically during training.
376
377   (Inverse) OT solvers. Our approach builds upon the light OT methods proposed by (Korotin et al.,
      2024; Gushchin et al., 2024), which introduce a forward solver for the entropic OT problem with the


                                                               7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      quadratic cost function c∗ (x, y) = 21 ∥x−y∥22 using the Gaussian Mixture parametrization. However,
379   we consider a more general cost function (16) and incorporate cost function learning directly into
380   the objective (20), in fact, producing an inverse OT (5) solver.
381
382
      As highlighted in M2.2, the task of inverse optimal transport (IOT) implies learning the cost function
      from samples drawn from an optimal coupling π ∗ . Existing IOT solvers (Dupuy & Galichon, 2014;
383
      Dupuy et al., 2016; Li et al., 2019; Stuart & Wolfram, 2020; Ma et al., 2020; Chiu et al., 2022; Gali-
384
      chon & Salanié, 2022) focus on reconstructing cost functions from discrete marginal distributions, in
385   particular, using the log-likelihood maximization techniques (Dupuy et al., 2016), see the introduc-
386   tion of (Andrade et al., 2023) for a review. In contrast, we develop a log-likelihood based approach
387   aimed at learning a conditional distribution π θ (·|x) ≈ π ∗ (·|x) that incorporates both paired and
388   unpaired data but not the cost function itself.
389
      Recent work by (Howard et al., 2024) proposes a framework for learning cost functions to improve
390
      the mapping between the domains. However, it is limited by the use of deterministic mappings, i.e.,
391   does not have the ability to model non-degenerate conditional distributions.

522   6    D ISCUSSION
523
524
      Limitations. A limitation of our approach is that it uses the Gaussian Mixture parameterization
      for conditional distributions. This may limit its scalability. As a promising avenue for future work
525
      is incorporation of the more general parameterizations, such as neural networks, which are already
526
      well-studied in the context of forward entropic OT, see (Mokrov et al., 2024). In Appendix A, for
527   completeness of the exposition, we showcase one possible way to use the neural parameterization
528   for both cost and potential in our method via the energy-based modeling (LeCun et al., 2006, EBM).
529
530
      Potential impact. Our framework has a simple and non-minimax optimization objective that seam-
      lessly incorporates both unpaired and paired samples into the training. We expect that these ad-
531
      vantages will encourage the use of our framework to develop other max-likelihood-based semi-
532
      supervised approaches based on more advanced (than Gaussian mixtures) techniques, e.g., energy-
533   based models (LeCun et al., 2006; Du & Mordatch, 2019), diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020), etc.
534
535   Broader impact. This paper presents work whose goal is to advance the field of Machine Learn-
536
      ing. There are many potential societal consequences of our work, none of which we feel must be
      specifically highlighted here.
