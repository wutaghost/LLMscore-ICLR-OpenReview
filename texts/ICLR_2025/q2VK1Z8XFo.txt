                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               We revisit FedExProx вҖ“ a recently proposed distributed optimization method de-
010
               signed to enhance convergence properties of parallel proximal algorithms via ex-
011            trapolation. In the process, we uncover a surprising flaw: its known theoretical
012            guarantees on quadratic optimization tasks are no better than those offered by the
013            vanilla Gradient Descent (GD) method. Motivated by this observation, we develop
014            a novel analysis framework, establishing a tighter linear convergence rate for non-
015            strongly convex quadratic problems. By incorporating both computation and com-
016            munication costs, we demonstrate that FedExProx can indeed provably outperform
017            GD, in stark contrast to the original analysis. Furthermore, we consider partial par-
018            ticipation scenarios and analyze two adaptive extrapolation strategies вҖ“ based on
019
               gradient diversity and Polyak stepsizes вҖ” again significantly outperforming previ-
               ous results. Moving beyond quadratics, we extend the applicability of our analysis
020
               to general functions satisfying the Polyak-ЕҒojasiewicz condition, outperforming
021
               the previous strongly convex analysis while operating under weaker assumptions.
022            Backed by empirical results, our findings point to a new and stronger potential of
023            FedExProx, paving the way for further exploration of the benefits of extrapolation
024            in federated learning.

033

034   et al., 2020) and recommendation systems (Hard et al., 2018). Formally, FL solves the following

      Partial Participation. In real-world FL scenarios, not all clients remain active for the entire duration
      of training. Instead, a subset of clients is chosen at each iteration, typically based on practical factors
068
      such as device availability (e.g., battery life or network conditions) or statistical considerations (e.g.,

070
071   Communication bottleneck. Another key problem in distributed training is the communication
072   bottleneck (Ramesh et al., 2021; Kairouz et al., 2021). Since the overall performance of a distributed
073
      algorithm is the product of the number of communication rounds K needed to find a solution and the
      cost C of one such round, there exist two main strategies to addressing this issue: (i) minimizing C
074
      and/or (ii) minimizing K. Objective (i) is typically achieved via compression of the information
075
      transmitted between the clients and the server (Beznosikov et al., 2020; Gruntkowska et al., 2024).
076   Objective (ii) can be accomplished by increasing the amount of local computation performed by
077   clients between rounds, allowing for less frequent communication.
078
079
      FedAvg and modern local methods. A common strategy for ensuring communication efficiency
      that falls into category (ii) is local training. The idea is simple: allow clients to do more work before
080
      transmitting the results to the server. If executed with care, this approach leads to each communica-
081
      tion round providing more вҖңinformativeвҖқ updates, ultimately resulting in more effective changes to
082   the global model. One of the most popular methods in this class is Federated Averaging (FedAvg),
083   introduced by McMahan et al. (2017). Within this framework, a subset of clients is selected in
084   each round to perform local training using a gradient-based method of their choice (e.g., GD). This
085   is followed by an averaging step on the server, where the local updates are aggregated. Despite its
086   popularity, the theoretical properties of FedAvg are somewhat limited (Khaled et al., 2019; Koloskova
087   et al., 2020). A major issue is client drift, which can lead to slower convergence (Karimireddy et al.,
088   2020). This problem can be mitigated using modern drift correction techniques (Karimireddy et al.,
089   2020; Gorbunov et al., 2021; Mishchenko et al., 2022). One notable advancement in this area is Fed-
090
      ExP, introduced by Jhunjhunwala et al. (2023), which extends FedAvg by leveraging extrapolation
      to better control server updates.

146   2       C ONTRIBUTIONS
147
148   1. Although the iteration complexity (8) of FedExProx outperforms both FedProx and FedExP, we
149   find that it is not superior to that of the simplest baseline, GD, on quadratic optimization tasks (see
150
      Remark 3.3). This raises a critical question regarding the utility of employing the proximal oracle
      in the first place.
151
152   2. Motivated by these pessimistic findings, we revisit the analysis of FedProx and prove a signifi-
153   cantly more optimistic iteration complexity result. We establish a linear convergence rate for solving
154   non-strongly convex quadratic distributed optimization problems of the form (1) (see Theorem 4.1)
155   and demonstrate that our new result does indeed lead to the conclusion that FedExProx can signif-
156
      icantly outperform GD. Our analysis assumes a realistic model that accounts for both computation
      and communication times вҖ“ critical factors in real-world distributed optimization. We show that the
157
      total time complexity of FedExProx is never worse than that of GD, and can be strictly better when
158
      communication time dominates computation time (see Theorem 4.3), which is typically the case in
159   FL scenarios. This stands in stark contrast to the previous analysis by Li et al. (2024), underscoring
160   the superiority of our new findings.

416   7.1   W HY DO WE GET A TIGHTER ANALYSIS ?
417
418   To understand why the new analysis yields stronger guarantees, we need to examine the reasoning
419   behind Theorem 3.2. When proving the result, we noticed that the main reason why (9) was true
420   was the dependence of the complexity on the Lmax factor. At the same time, the complexity of GD
421   depends only on L, and there exist many examples when Lmax вү« L. The question was: why does
422   the original analysis of FedExProx involve Lmax , and can one circumvent this dependency? The
423
      reason behind it is the reliance of the proofs by Li et al. (2024) on Lemma A.9, which establishes
                                                 1
      the inequality M Оі (x)вҲ’M Оі (xвҲ— ) вүҘ 1+ОіL          (f (x) вҲ’ f (xвҲ— )) for all x вҲҲ Rd . This inequality appears
424                                                max

425   to be essential for deriving the convergence result E[f (xМ„)] вҲ’ f (xвҲ— ) вүӨ Оө (where xМ„ вҲҲ Rd is the output
426
      of FedExProx). Our main idea is to instead obtain convergence in terms of the distance between xМ„
                                                   2
427   and xвҲ— , i.e., to establish that E[вҲҘxМ„ вҲ’ xвҲ— вҲҘ ] вүӨ Оө, and then translate it to E[f (xМ„)] вҲ’ f (xвҲ— ) вүӨ Оө using
428
      the LвҖ“smoothness of the function f . In this way, one can avoid using Lemma A.9 when dealing
      with quadratic functions or problems satisfying the PЕҒ condition, ultimately obtaining much more
429
      favourable convergence guarantees.

      selves, as it offers valuable insights and can be instructive for future research.

531
      In this work, we revisit an empirically promising extrapolated parallel proximal method FedExProx.
532
      Motivated by the limitations of its original overly pessimistic analysis, we develop a novel analytical
533
      framework for non-strongly convex quadratic and PЕҒ cases. Our analysis significantly improves
534   upon the previous results, offering insights into the true methodвҖҷs performance. While our work
535   focuses on the relatively simple case of quadratic problems, the experimental results indicate that
536   the phenomena we describe extend beyond this setting. These findings suggest that the benefits of
537   extrapolation in FL hold in broader optimization scenarios, motivating future research to rigorously
538   establish such extensions. We believe that our work provides a foundational stepping stone toward
539   better understanding the role of extrapolation in FL and can inspire further exploration to relax the
      assumptions made here, ultimately advancing the theory and practice of federated optimization.
