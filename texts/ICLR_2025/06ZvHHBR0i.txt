009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               This paper explores optimal architectures for evaluating the outputs of large lan-
012
               guage models (LLMs) using LLMs themselves. We propose a novel framework
013            that interprets LLMs as advocates within an ensemble of interacting agents, al-
014            lowing them to defend their answers and reach conclusions through a judge and
015            jury system. This approach offers a more dynamic and comprehensive evaluation
016            process compared to traditional human-based assessments or automated metrics.
017            We discuss the motivation behind this framework, its key components, and com-
018            parative advantages. We also present a probabilistic model to evaluate the error
019            reduction achieved by iterative advocate systems. Finally, we outline experiments
020            to validate the effectiveness of multi-advocate architectures and discuss future re-
021
               search directions.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   The rapid advancement of large language models (LLMs) has revolutionized the field of natural
027   language processing, enabling the development of increasingly sophisticated AI systems capable of
028   generating human-like text, engaging in dialogue, and performing complex language tasks (5). As
029   these models grow in size and capability, the challenge of accurately evaluating their performance
030   and aligning their outputs with human preferences has become increasingly critical (3; 15; 49).
031   Traditional evaluation methods, such as human assessments and automated metrics, often struggle to
032   capture the nuances and complexities of LLM outputs, leading to a gap between model performance
033   and user expectations (7; 17; 24). Human evaluations are time-consuming, expensive, and prone
034   to inconsistency and bias (12; 27), while automated metrics frequently fail to align with human
035   judgments, particularly in open-ended generation tasks (29; 13; 22).
036
      To address these challenges, we propose a novel framework for evaluating LLM outputs using LLMs
037   themselves as interacting agents in a courtroom-inspired, multi-agent system. Our approach draws
038   inspiration from various fields, including decision theory, economics, psychology, legal theory, and
039   voting theory, to develop a more dynamic, contextual, and comprehensive assessment process.

      1.1   M OTIVATION FROM D ECISION T HEORY AND L EGAL T HEORY
055
056   Our approach is motivated by various approaches proposed in literature on designing systems with
057   agents of varying capabilities and incentives. In what follows, we review a few motivating frame-
058   works.
059
060   D ECISION T HEORY AND B OUNDED R ATIONALITY
061
062   Decision theory provides a foundation for understanding how agents make choices under uncer-
063
      tainty and constraints (44; 21). The concept of bounded rationality, introduced by Herbert A. Simon
      (39; 40), acknowledges that decision-makers often operate with limited information, cognitive re-
064
      sources, and time, leading to satisficing rather than optimizing behavior. In the context of LLM
065
      evaluation, our LLM advocates framework addresses bounded rationality by distributing the cog-
066   nitive load across multiple specialized agents, each focusing on a specific aspect of the evaluation
067   process. This division of labor allows for a more efficient and targeted assessment, mitigating the
068   constraints faced by individual agents and enabling the system to converge on more accurate and
069   informative evaluations.
070
071   P SYCHOLOGICAL T HEORIES OF P ERSUASION AND A RGUMENTATION
072
073   Psychological theories of persuasion and argumentation, such as the Elaboration Likelihood Model
074   (36) and the Heuristic-Systematic Model (8), provide valuable insights into the factors that influence
075   the effectiveness of arguments and the formation of judgments. These theories highlight the impor-
076
      tance of central and peripheral routes to persuasion, as well as the role of heuristics and biases in
      shaping perceptions and decisions.
077
078   Our LLM advocates framework incorporates elements of persuasion and argumentation theory by
079   encouraging LLMs to present well-structured, compelling arguments that appeal to both central and
080   peripheral routes of persuasion. By exposing the outputs to scrutiny from opposing advocates and
081   subjecting them to the judgment of impartial LLM juries, our system helps to identify and mitigate
082
      the influence of heuristics and biases, leading to more robust and reliable evaluations.
083
084   L EGAL T HEORIES OF A DVERSARIAL P ROCESS AND J URISPRUDENCE
085
      Legal theories of adversarial process and jurisprudence emphasize the importance of structured de-
086   bate, cross-examination, and impartial judgment in uncovering truth and reaching fair outcomes
087   (46; 16; 41). The adversarial system, which lies at the heart of many legal traditions, relies on the
088   clash of opposing arguments to test the strength of evidence and reasoning, while the role of neu-
089   tral judges and juries ensures that decisions are based on a balanced consideration of the facts and
090   arguments presented.
091
      Our LLM advocates framework draws inspiration from the adversarial legal process, casting LLMs
092   as advocates tasked with presenting and defending competing arguments, while other LLMs serve
093   as impartial judges and juries. This structure promotes a thorough and rigorous examination of LLM
094   outputs, exposing weaknesses and inconsistencies that may be overlooked by traditional evaluation
095   methods. By emulating the checks and balances of the legal system, our framework aims to produce
096   more accurate, unbiased, and trustworthy assessments of LLM performance.
097
      Furthermore, we also draw inspiration from Voting and social choice theories, which study the
098   design of collective decision-making systems, considering factors such as preference aggregation,
099   strategic behavior, and fairness (2; 18; 38). Our LLM advocates framework incorporates recom-
100   mendations of voting theory and social choice by employing multi-layer jury systems to aggregate
101   the judgments of multiple LLM agents. By exploring different voting schemes (e.g., majority rule,
102   Borda count, or pairwise comparisons), our framework can adapt to the specific requirements and
103   constraints of different evaluation contexts.

131   2     R ELATED W ORK
132
133   The evaluation of language models has been a longstanding challenge in the field of natural lan-
134   guage processing, with the rapid growth of LLMs in recent years bringing this issue to the forefront.
135   As these models have increased in size and capability, the need for robust, comprehensive, and
136   theoretically grounded evaluation methods has become increasingly apparent. This section reviews
137   relevant literature on LLM evaluation, drawing on insights from human-computer interaction, psy-
138
      chometrics, multi-agent systems, and social choice theory to highlight the limitations of existing
      approaches and the potential for more sophisticated assessment frameworks.
139
140
141
      2.1   H UMAN -BASED E VALUATIONS AND THE C HALLENGE OF S UBJECTIVITY
142   Human judgments have long been considered the gold standard for evaluating the quality of lan-
143   guage model outputs, with platforms like LMSYS Chatbot Arena (10) and others (47; 23) providing
144   structured environments for collecting human ratings and preferences. However, the subjectivity and
145   variability inherent in human evaluations pose significant challenges for the reliable and consistent
146   assessment of LLMs (12; 27).
147
      Research in human-computer interaction and cognitive psychology has shown that factors such as
148   individual differences, task framing, and cognitive biases can significantly influence human judg-
149   ments of AI systems (20; 14; 6). For example, the anchoring effect (42) and the halo effect (33)
150   can lead to over- or under-estimation of LLM performance based on initial impressions or salient
151   features, while the confirmation bias (32) can cause evaluators to seek out information that supports
152   their preconceptions.
153
      Moreover, the reliance on reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) (4; 11; 50) for
154   aligning LLMs with user expectations introduces additional challenges, as the pool of reinforcers
155   may not be representative of the general user population (31). This can lead to models that are
156   optimized for the preferences of a narrow subset of users, potentially exacerbating issues of bias,
157   fairness, and accountability (30; 19).
158
159   2.2   AUTOMATED M ETRICS AND THE L IMITS OF R EFERENCE -BASED E VALUATION
160
161   To address the scalability and consistency issues of human evaluations, researchers have developed
      various automated metrics for assessing LLM performance across different tasks, such as BLEU


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      (35) for machine translation, ROUGE (28) for summarization, and exact match (EM) and F1 scores
163   (37) for question answering. These metrics provide a standardized and efficient means of evaluating
164   LLMs, enabling the comparison of different models and the tracking of progress over time.
165
166
      However, the reliance of these metrics on reference-based evaluation, where model outputs are com-
      pared against a fixed set of ground-truth answers, has been shown to have significant limitations
167
      (29; 13; 22). In open-ended generation tasks, such as dialogue and creative writing, there may be
168
      a wide range of acceptable responses that differ in content, style, and format from the reference
169   answers, leading to both false positive and false negative errors in the evaluation.
170
171
172
      2.3   LLM-BASED E VALUATION AND THE P ROMISE OF M ULTI -AGENT F RAMEWORKS
173
      To overcome the limitations of human evaluations and automated metrics, recent research has ex-
174
      plored the use of LLMs themselves as evaluators. This approach leverages the linguistic knowledge
175   and reasoning capabilities of LLMs to provide more nuanced and contextually aware assessments
176   of model outputs. Initial studies have employed single LLMs as judges (34; 45), demonstrating the
177   potential of this approach to capture aspects of quality that are missed by traditional metrics.
178
      However, the use of single LLMs as evaluators has been shown to suffer from issues of bias and
179
      limited generalizability (34). To address these concerns, researchers have proposed using multiple
180
      LLMs as evaluators, drawing on insights from multi-agent systems and ensemble learning (43; 26).
181
182   Our work builds on these ideas by proposing a novel LLM advocates framework that interprets
183   LLMs as interacting agents within a courtroom-inspired setting. This framework draws on princi-
184
      ples from adversarial legal systems (46; 16; 41), where the clash of opposing arguments and the
      judgment of impartial decision-makers are used to uncover the truth and reach fair outcomes. By
185
      casting LLMs as advocates, judges, and juries, our approach enables a more dynamic and compre-
186
      hensive evaluation process that captures the nuances and complexities of language understanding
187   and generation.
188
189
190
      2.4   S CORING , R ANKING , AND AGGREGATION M ETHODS IN M ULTI -AGENT E VALUATION
191
      A critical consideration in the design of multi-agent evaluation frameworks is the choice of scoring,
192
      ranking, and aggregation methods for combining the judgments of individual LLM evaluators. Var-
193   ious approaches have been explored in the literature, drawing on insights from social choice theory,
194   voting theory, and decision analysis (1; 25; 26).
195
196
      Scoring methods, where evaluators assign numerical ratings to model outputs, have been shown to
      provide a more granular and expressive means of assessment compared to ranking methods, which
197
      only capture relative preferences (26; 45). However, the use of scoring requires careful design
198
      of the rating scales and anchors to ensure inter-rater reliability and comparability across different
199   evaluators and tasks (9).
200
201   Ranking methods, on the other hand, have been shown to be more robust to individual biases and
202
      scale differences, as they only require evaluators to make pairwise comparisons between model
      outputs (25). This can be particularly useful in settings where the absolute quality of the outputs is
203
      difficult to assess or where the evaluators have different standards or expectations. However, ranking
204
      methods may provide less information about the magnitude of the differences between the outputs
205   and can be more computationally expensive to aggregate.

292   A NALYSIS AND F ORMULATION
293
294   We begin by formally defining the key components of our comparative analysis. Let Q denote the
295   space of possible questions, A the space of candidate answers, and D the space of debate arguments
296   or defenses. We consider a setting where, for a given question q ∈ Q, we have two candidate
297   answers a1 , a2 ∈ A that need to be evaluated and compared.
298   In the iterative debate framework, we have two advocate functions, f1 and f2 , that take the question
299   q and the two answers a1 and a2 as input, and produce debate arguments in D:
300
301
                                      fi : Q × A × A → D,        i ∈ {1, 2}
302
303   These advocate functions represent the LLMs responsible for defending and refining the arguments
304   for each candidate answer.
305
      In the multi-advocate framework, we have k advocate functions for each candidate answer, denoted
306   by f1j and f2j for j ∈ {1, . . . , k}. These functions take the same inputs as in the iterative debate
307   framework, but produce a set of k debate arguments for each answer:
308
309
                             fij : Q × A × A → D,        i ∈ {1, 2}, j ∈ {1, . . . , k}
310
311   To compare and evaluate the debate arguments, we introduce a scoring function g : D → [0, 1]
312   that assigns a score between 0 and 1 to each argument. This scoring function represents the judge
313   LLM responsible for assessing the quality and persuasiveness of the arguments presented by the
314   advocates.
315

316

317   g(f1agg ) and g(f2agg ), respectively.

323   g. In other words, we assume that under appropriate prompting, the internal scoring function g ′
      behaves similarly to the external scoring function g.

      3.4   E FFICIENCY C ONSIDERATIONS
359
360   In addition to the score differentiation property, the multi-advocate framework also offers potential
361   efficiency advantages over the iterative debate framework. In the iterative debate setting, the advo-
362   cates engage in multiple rounds of argument and rebuttal to refine and improve their defenses of the
363   candidate answers. This process can be time-consuming and resource-intensive, especially if a large
364   number of iterations are required to achieve a satisfactory level of differentiation between the scores.
365
      In contrast, the multi-advocate framework allows for a parallel exploration of different argument
366   strategies and perspectives, enabling a more efficient search for strong and persuasive defenses.
367   By leveraging the collective knowledge and creativity of multiple advocates, the multi-advocate
368   framework can potentially achieve the same level of score differentiation as the iterative debate
369   framework in fewer rounds of interaction.
370
      To formalize this efficiency advantage, we introduce the concept of iteration complexity, defined as
371
      the number of rounds of argument and rebuttal required to achieve a desired level of score differ-
372   entiation. Let Iid (ε) denote the iteration complexity of the iterative debate framework for a given
373   tolerance level ε > 0, and let Ima (ε) denote the iteration complexity of the multi-advocate frame-
374   work for the same tolerance level.
375
      We have the following result regarding the relationship between Iid (ε) and Ima (ε):
376
377   Theorem 2 (Iteration Complexity). For any given tolerance level ε > 0, the iteration complex-
      ity of the multi-advocate framework is lower than the iteration complexity of the iterative debate

384
      the multi-advocate framework allows for a more efficient exploration of the argument space by
385
      parallelizing the search for strong defenses across multiple advocates. This parallelization leads
386   to faster convergence to high-quality arguments compared to the sequential nature of the iterative
387   debate framework. The aggregation process in the multi-advocate framework selects the strongest

389   the individual advocates. This maximum operation amplifies the score differentiation achieved by

699         3. Despite the potential for occasional setbacks, where an iteration may result in a temporary
700            increase in error or a widening of the gap between competing positions, well-structured
701            advocate systems exhibit a general trend towards error reduction and convergence on the
