011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We present ClearSR, a new method that can better take advantage of latent low-
014            resolution image (LR) embeddings for diffusion-based real-world image super-
015            resolution (Real-ISR). Previous Real-ISR models mostly focus on how to activate
016
               more generative priors of text-to-image diffusion models to make the output high-
               resolution (HR) images look better. However, since these methods rely too much
017
               on the generative priors, the content of the output images is often inconsistent with
018
               the input LR ones. To mitigate the above issue, in this work, we explore using
019            latent LR embeddings to constrain the control signals from ControlNet, and extract
020            LR information at both detail and structure levels. We show that the proper use of
021            latent LR embeddings can produce higher-quality control signals, which enables
022            the super-resolution results to be more consistent with the LR image and leads to
023            clearer visual results. In addition, we also show that latent LR embeddings can be
024            used to control the inference stage, allowing for the improvement of fidelity and
025            generation ability simultaneously. Experiments demonstrate that our model can
026            achieve better performance across multiple metrics on several test sets and generate
027
               more consistent SR results with LR images than existing methods. Our code will
               be made publicly available.

095   the structure and detail levels.

097   LR embeddings from the pre-trained VAE encoder preserve rich LR information that is beneficial for
098   controlling generative priors from SD. Compared to semantic information, latent LR embeddings can
099   provide more precise controls, an effective way to mitigate the issue of inconsistent SR results. We
100   take advantage of the latent LR embeddings by designing two new modules, called Detail Preserving
101   Module (DPM) and Global Structure Preserving Module (GSPM), which aim to embed the latent LR
102   embeddings through window-based cross-attention into different layers of ControlNet to enhance
103   details, and preserve LR structural information, respectively.
104   Moreover, we show that the use of latent LR embeddings in the inference stage is also able to address

106   capability. We achieve this by introducing the Latent Space Adjustment (LSA) strategy. This strategy

      2.1   I MAGE S UPER -R ESOLUTION
127
128   Image Super-Resolution (ISR) aims to restore a high-resolution (HR) image from its low-resolution
129   (LR) version. Traditional ISR works are usually based on stacked CNN or transformer layers and
130   are learned under a known degradation. Since SRCNN (Dong et al., 2015) introduced CNN into the
131   field of image super-resolution and achieved better results than traditional methods, many excellent
132   works have emerged (Dong et al., 2015; Zhang et al., 2018a; Dai et al., 2019; Niu et al., 2020; Tong
133   et al., 2017; Kim et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2018b; Shi et al., 2016; Ahn et al., 2018; Lim et al.,
134
      2017; Mei et al., 2021; Dong et al., 2016; Li et al., 2020). After that, some researchers applied
      Swin Transformer (Liu et al., 2021) to the image super-resolution task and achieved impressive
135
      success (Liang et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2023a; Zhou et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b; Zhang et al.,
136
      2022). However, as the degradation is usually simple and known, the application scope of this task
137   is limited. In recent years, attention has shifted toward more practically valuable topics, such as
138   Real-world Image Super-Resolution (Zhang et al., 2021; Liang et al., 2022a; Wang et al., 2021;
139   2024a; Lin et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023; Xie et al., 2023).
140
141   2.2   R EAL -W ORLD I MAGE S UPER -R ESOLUTION
142
143   Real-world Image Super-Resolution (Real-ISR) has become a popular topic in recent years. Compared
144   to traditional ISR, Real-ISR requires modeling complex degradations in the real world, which further
145   tests the generative capabilities of models and offers greater practical values. Many studies have
146   used GANs (Wang et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021; Liang et al., 2022b) for Real-ISR tasks due to its
147
      excellent detail generation capabilities, demonstrating competitive results (Zhang et al., 2021; Wang
      et al., 2021; Liang et al., 2022b;a). However, GAN-based methods often produce unnatural artifacts,
148
      limiting their applications in Real-ISR tasks. Recently, since the introduction of DDPM (Ho et al.,
149
      2020), Diffusion Models (DMs) have secured a significant position in the field of image synthesis.
150   After some exploration (Lu et al., 2022; Kong & Ping, 2021; San-Roman et al., 2021), Rombach et al.
151   (2022) reduced the computational cost of DMs, broadening its application range.
152
153
      Due to the outstanding success of DMs in various computer vision tasks, some researchers have
      begun to use them for Real-ISR tasks (Yue et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b; Xia et al., 2023), but the
154
      generative capabilities of these models are still limited. As the pre-trained text-to-image (T2I) DMs,
155
      such as Stable Diffusion (SD) have powerful generative priors, which can help generate details needed
156   for Real-ISR. StableSR (Wang et al., 2024a) has used SD for the first time to conduct Real-ISR tasks
157   and demonstrates impressive detail generation capabilities. However, the overly strong generative
158   ability of the pre-trained T2I models often leads to inconsistent super-resolution results. Therefore,
159   how to effectively utilize LR information becomes a challenge in this topic. DiffBIR (Lin et al., 2023)
160   has used ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023) to provide appropriate control signals for SD, improving
161   the generation effect of the model. On this basis, PASD (Yang et al., 2023) focuses on the control
      signals provided by ControlNet, making more efficient use of them. SeeSR (Wu et al., 2024) uses


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      a reasonable method to extract the semantic signals of the LR image to activate more generative
163   abilities of models.
164
165   Our work also focuses on how to utilize LR information better. Unlike previous methods (Yang
166
      et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024), we find PASD (Yang et al., 2023) did not improve the control signals
      themselves, and the usage of semantic information Wu et al. (2024) is coarse and leads to inconsistent
167
      SR results. As a result, we focus on latent LR embeddings provided by the pre-trained VAE encoder
168
      and use it to constrain the control signals and extract more LR information at both detail and structure
169   levels.

174   3.1   OVERALL A RCHITECTURE OF C LEAR SR
175
      As mentioned in Sec. 1, the inefficient LR image utilization may lead to inconsistency with the
176
      input image. Therefore, our intention is to dig how to better take advantage of the LR information
177   to improve the control signals. Figure 3 shows the overall pipeline of our ClearSR. The cores of
178   our method are two new modules, named Detail Preserving Module (DPM) and Global Structure
179   Preserving Module (GSPM). DPM is based on ControlNet, which aims to preserve essential detailed
180   information from the latent LR embeddings for the control signals. GSPM on the other hand, is a
181   ResBlock-based module without attention layers, allowing it to retain more LR structure information
182   and enhance fidelity (Yu et al., 2024b).
183   During the training process, the objective of the Diffusion Model (DM) is to learn the probability
184   distribution of the reverse denoising process. Specifically, denote the LoRA finetuned SD UNet, the
185   LoRA finetuned VAE encoder, the pre-trained VAE encoder, and the pre-trained VAE decoder as ϵθ′ ,
186   Eθ′ , Eθ , and Dθ , respectively. Denote DPM and GSPM as dϕ and sϕ . For a randomly sampled time
187   step t and a high-quality image Ihq , let Ihq pass through Eθ and perform the noise addition process
188   to obtain xt . Sending the low-quality image Ilr into Eθ′ yields the latent LR embeddings xlr . Then,
189   we can collect the control signals xc = {c1 , c2 , . . . } by inputting xlr into dϕ and sϕ and summing
190   their outputs. Similar to PASD (Yang et al., 2023) and CoSeR (Sun et al., 2024), we let the LR image
191
      pass through the CLIP image encoder to obtain the image-level feature p and replace the null-text
      prompt in the UNet decoder. The optimization objective can be formulated as:

      As mentioned in a previous work (Yu et al., 2024a), the pre-trained VAE encoder is unsuitable for
197
      encoding LR images, because it was not trained on LR images. During training, unlike previous
198
      works, such as SUPIR (Yu et al., 2024a) and SeeSR (Wu et al., 2024), that introduce a new loss or

201   add LoRA layers to the SD UNet decoder to adapt the model to the mixed control signals. Readers
202   may refer to Appendix A for more details.

207   LR images. We achieve this by adjusting the control signals at both the detail and structure levels,

213   the latent LR embeddings into different layers of ControlNet. These cross-attention layers are placed
214   after text cross-attention layers. Specifically, let the newly added cross-attention layer be denoted as
215   CA. Given the intermediate feature xd ∈ RL×C , we let it pass through the linear layer and window

      3.3                 L ATENT S PACE A DJUSTMENT STRATEGY IN INFERENCE STAGE
309
310
      Previous work has pointed out that adding additional LR information during the inference stage
311
      can help improve fidelity (Yu et al., 2024a; Wu et al., 2024). However, the improvement in fidelity
312   comes at the expense of reducing the generative capability of the models. This type of unidirectional
313   adjustment strategy has a negative impact on the image details after super-resolution, affecting the
314   visual effect. Unlike previous works, we propose the Latent Space Adjustment (LSA) strategy,
315   which can improve either fidelity or generation. Moreover, our strategy can improve the fidelity and
316   generation simultaneously through appropriate settings.
317
      As shown in Figure 5(a), during the inference stage, the PSNR score increases first and then decreases
318   as the number of steps increases. This is because the model performs structural refinement in the early
319   steps while generation in the later steps (Sun et al., 2023). For the middle steps of the inference stage,
320   the model focuses on content generation. As shown in Figure 5(b), at around 40th step, the model
321   can already determine most of the information in the image, but it is difficult to generate realistic
322   textures. This indicates that detail enhancement is mainly in the last few steps. This motivates us to
323   divide the whole inference stage into three parts: structure refinement, content generation, and detail
      enhancement.


                                                                                                                6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      Based on the analysis above, we propose the Latent Space Adjustment (LSA) strategy. We notice that
325   an inherent property of LR images is that it mainly contains structural information and has less details
326   compared to HR images. We take advantage of this property and move the output of each inference
327   step away from the latent LR embeddings in the latent space in the later steps of the inference stage
328   so that the model can focus more on generating details (Lin et al., 2023). In contrast, in the early
329   steps, we let the output close to the latent LR embeddings, similar to previous work, to enhance the
330   fidelity. As shown in Figure 5(a), we statistically select the highest point of the NIQE curve and the
331   point where NIQE starts to fall below the HR image to split the inference stage into three parts. The
332   LR adjustments in the structure refinement and detail enhancement are referred to as Early-step LR
333
      Adjustment (ELA) and Later-step LR Adjustment (LLA), respectively. We use two factors α and β
      to determine the control level. Figure 5(c) shows our LSA strategy. The xi is the predicted latent
334
      embeddings of i-th step. The formulation can be written as follows:
335
336                                          ELA(xi ) = (1 − α)xi + αxlr ,                                         (3)
337
338
                                             LLA(xi ) = (1 + β)xi − βxlr .                                         (4)
339
340   The experimental results show that using ELA can improve the fidelity of the model, and using
341   LLA can improve the generation, solving the problem that previous methods (Wu et al., 2024; Yu
342   et al., 2024a) can only adjust in one direction. Moreover, the fidelity and generation of the model
343   can be improved simultaneously through appropriate α and β settings. (See Section 4.3 for more
344   discussions.)

379
380   4.1    E XPERIMENT S ETTINGS
381
382   Following previous works (Wu et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023), for training, we train ClearSR on
383   DIV2K (Agustsson & Timofte, 2017), Flickr2K (Timofte et al., 2017), DIV8K (Gu et al., 2019),
384   OST (Wang et al., 2018), and the first 10K face images from FFHQ (Karras et al., 2019). We use the
385   degradation pipeline of Real-ESRGAN (Wang et al., 2021) to obtain LR/HR pairs. For testing, we
386   test ClearSR on DRealSR (Wei et al., 2020), RealSR (Cai et al., 2019), and DIV2K-Val (Agustsson
387
      & Timofte, 2017) using the same configurations as (Wu et al., 2024). For evaluation, we employ 7
      widely used metrics, including PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS, NIQE, MUSIQ, MANIQA, and CLIPIQA. We
388
      use PSNR, SSIM (calculated on the Y channel from the YCbCr space) to evaluate fidelity, LPIPS to
389
      evaluate perceptual quality, and NIQE, MUSIQ, MANIQA, and CLIPIQA to evaluate the generation
390   ability of the model.
391
392   For implementation details, we use SD 2.1-base as our pre-trained T2I model. We use the Adam
393
      optimizer to train ClearSR. The total iteration, batch size, learning rate, inference step are set to 150K,
      8, 5 × 10−5 , and 50, respectively. α and β are set to 0.01 and 0.01 for ClearSR and 0.03 and 0.01
394
      for ClearSR*, respectively. We also use the LR embeddings proposed by (Wu et al., 2024) in the
395
      inference stage to improve the fidelity. The training process is conducted on 512 × 512 resolution
396   with 4 NVIDIA A40 GPUs. For inference, we use a spaced DDPM sampling schedule (Nichol &
397   Dhariwal, 2021).

      4.3   A BLATION A NALYSIS
452
453   In this subsection, we conduct extensive experiments to show the effectiveness of our method. For
454   ablation study, the total iteration, batch size, and learning rate are set to 50K, 8, and 1 × 10−4 ,
455   respectively. α and β are set to 0.01 and 0.01. We use DRealSR (Wei et al., 2020) for testing and
456   PSNR, SSIM, NIQE, MANIQA metrics for evaluation.
457   Effectiveness of Model Design. We first conduct the ablation study on our model design. As
458   shown in Table 2, we compare the full model with several modified versions. As discussed above,
459   GSPM preserves the structural information and DPM preserves the detailed information. We can
460   see the model without GSPM results in a drop in PSNR, which means a loss of fidelity. Moreover,
461   removing the extra cross-attention layers in DPM leads to a drop in MANIQA, which means a loss
462   for generation. We also testing the window partition strategy. We can see that not using the window
463   partition strategy in DPM weakens the fidelity (PSNR and SSIM metrics). These results prove the
464   effectiveness of our model design.
465   Effectiveness of LoRA. Next, we demonstrate the effectiveness of LR information adaptation. As
466   shown in Table 3, the model without VAE LoRA layers achieves a very high PSNR and SSIM, but its
467   generative capability decreases significantly. This is because the pre-trained VAE encoder cannot
468   correctly map the LR image to the latent space. The model with VAE LoRA layers and without UNet
469   LoRA layers exhibits higher MANIQA but lower fidelity (PSNR and SSIM metrics). This may be
470   due to the fact that the UNet fails to adapt to the output of the mixed control signals, leading to the
471
      misapplication of the provided structural information in generating details.

517   5     C ONCLUSIONS
518
519   We propose ClearSR, a new method that can better take advantage of latent LR embeddings for
520   diffusion-based Real-ISR tasks. We constrain the ControlNet in latent space through latent LR
521   embeddings, and propose DPM and GSPM to extract LR information at the detail and structure levels.
522   We propose a new LSA strategy in inference stage, which can improve the fidelity and generation
523
      ability simultaneously. Extensive experimental results show that the super-resolution results of our
      ClearSR are more consistent with the LR images and can also generate rich details for better visual
524
      effects.

      A             M ORE DETAILS OF LR INFORMATION ADAPTATION
757
758
      As mentioned in a previous work (Yu et al., 2024a), the pre-trained VAE encoder is unsuitable for
759
      encoding LR images, because it was not trained on LR images. As a result, the pre-trained VAE
760   encoder is unable to map LR images to the correct latent space. Besides, the mixed control signals
761   are also unfamiliar to SD UNet, which is a similar problem. We simply add LoRA layers to the VAE
762   encoder and SD Unet to adapt the LR information to our model. For the VAE encoder, we add LoRA
763   layers to each convolution layer and attention layer, and for UNet, we only use LoRA layers in the
764   attention layer of the UNet decoder. Our method is simple, requires fewer training parameters, and
765   does not need an additional training phase. The LoRA rank is set to 16.
