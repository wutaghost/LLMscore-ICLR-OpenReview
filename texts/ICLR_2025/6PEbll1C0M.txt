                                                      A BSTRACT
010
011
               Using generative models for protein design is gaining interest for their potential scientific
012
               impact. However, biological processes are mediated by many modalities, and simulta-
013            neous generating multiple biological modalities is a continued challenge. We propose
014            PLAID (Protein Latent Induced Diffusion), whereby multimodal biological generation
015            is achieved by learning and sampling from the latent space of a predictor from a more
016            abundant data modality (e.g. sequence) to a less abundant data modality (e.g. crystallized
017            structure). Specifically, we examine the all-atom structure generation setting, which re-
018            quires producing both the 3D structure and 1D sequence, to specify how to place sidechain
019            atoms that are critcial to function. Crucially, since PLAID only requires sequence inputs
020            to obtain the latent representation during training, it allows us to use sequence databases
021
               when training the generative model, thus augmenting the sampleable data distribution by
               102 × to 104 × compared to experimental structure databases. Using sequence-only training
022
               further unlocks more annotations that can be used to conditioning model generation. As
023
               a demonstration, we use two conditioning variables: 2219 function keywords from Gene
024            Ontology, and 3617 organisms across the tree of life. Despite not receiving structure inputs
025            during training, model generations nonetheless exhibit strong performance on structure
026            quality, diversity, novelty, and cross-modal consistency metrics. Analysis of function-
027            conditioned samples show that generated structures preserve non-adjacent catalytic residues
028            at active sites, and learn the hydrophobicity pattern of transmembrane proteins, while
029            exhibiting overall sequence diversity. Model weights and code are publicly accessible at
030            [redacted].

071   Contributions Towards resolving these challenges, we introduce PLAID (Protein Latent Induced
072   Diffusion). Our principal demonstration is that multimodal generation in biology can be achieved by
073   learning the latent space of a predictor from a more abundant data modality (e.g. sequence) to a less abundant
074   data modality (e.g. crystallized structure). In particular, we focus on ESMFold [3] and all-atom structure
075   generation, and present a controllable diffusion model capable of simultaneous sequence and all-atom
076   structure generation, while requiring only sequence inputs during training. Because training dataset can
077   be defined by sequence databases rather than structural ones, one can obtain better coverage of the viable
078
      protein space traversed by evolution. It furthermore allows us to leverage structural information encoded

079
      controllable generation. As a motivating demonstration, we examine compositional control across the axes
080
      of function and organism, though sequence databases also offer far more annotation types, such as natural
081   language abstracts. The method is designed to be easily adaptable to expanding sequence datasets, capitalize
082   on improved inference and training infrastructure for Transformer-based models [4, 5, 6], and increasingly
083   multimodal protein folding models, such as nucleic acids and molecular ligand binding [7, 8].

      Generative Modeling for Proteins State-of-the-art diffusion models for designing protein structure have
088
      thus far focused on generating novel backbone folds, with conditioning controllability typically governed by
089   secondary structure, or for generating scaffolding for a known motif [13, 14, 15, 16]. Evaluation and design
090   of these models focus on fold stability and novelty, and often involve using oracle models [17, 3, 18, 19] for
091   folding or inverse folding. However, to synthesize the protein, the sequence is required, and not all sampled
092   structures might have a corresponding sequence. To address this, “designability” has been posited as a metric,
093   which assesses the correspondence between the original structure and the sequence predicted for that structure.

113   joint embedding of sequence and structure. (B) Latent diffusion training. Our goal is to learn and sample
114   from pθ (x), following the diffusion [9] formulation. To improve learning efficiency, we use the CHEAP
115   [10] encoder he (·) to obtain compressed embedding x′ = he (x), such that the actual diffusion objective
116   becomes sampling from pθ (he (x)). (C) Inference. To obtain both sequence and structure at inference time,
117   we use the trained model to sample x̃′ ∼ pθ (x′ ), then uncompress using the CHEAP decoder to obtain

      these generations using a protein folding model, but models do not explicitly produce atomic positions.

130   Multimodal Sequence-Structure and All-Atom Generation All-atom generation can thus be viewed as a
131   multimodal generation problem, where the 1D protein sequence and 3D protein structure are jointly produced.
132   Existing works [25, 26] often generate only one of structure or sequence at each diffusion step, and rely on an
133
      external predictor to produce the other modality. Multiflow [27] performs co-generation without an external
      tool, but does not produce side chain positions. Some works have focused on specific protein subclasses,
134
      such as antibody design [28, 29]. While these models achieve success within their specialized domains,
135
      antibodies represent a narrow subset of protein space and such models often struggle with out-of-distribution
136   generalization to all protein families. Concurrently developed with this work, ESM3 [30] also uses generates
137   in the shared sequence-structure space, and is conditioned on Interpro [31] (many of which are derived from
138   GO terms [32]) for controllability. However, the ESM3 tokenizer is trained on structure datasets, rather than
139   sequence databases, and cannot perform all-atom generation. It is easy to extend PLAID to the tokenized
140   setting, since CHEAP [10] embeddings also includes a tokenized variant.

      Latent Generation Our goal is to learn pθ (x) ≈ p(x), where θ are parameters of the model learned through
      diffusion training (Figure 3C). Then, after training, we can sample x̃ ∼ pθ (x) (Figure 3C). To do so, we
213
      use diffusion models [9, 35], with some modifications (described in ablation Table 1). To obtain structure
214
      from the sampled latent embedding, we can simply use frozen ESMFold structure module weights to obtain
215
      Ω̃ = ϕStructure Module (x̃) (Figure 3C). Since the output of ϕStructure Module is all-atom, the sampled Ω̃ is also
216   all-atom.
217
218
219
      Sequence Decoder To obtain the sequence, we need an “inverse mapping of ESM2" to get s̃ = ϕ−1            ESM (x̃).
      This inverse mapping is straightforward to train, since x is a linearly projected version of the ESM2 embedding,
220
      which was trained via the MLM loss. This sequence decoder ϕ−1        ESM is also trained and provided in Lu et al.
221
      [10], with validation accuracy on a heldout partition of UniRef [36] reaching 99.7% [10]. Note that s̃ must be
222
      decoded first, which determines the side-chain atoms to be placed in Ω̃.
223
224
225   Latent Space Compression In initial experiments, we found that directly learning p(s) performed poorly
226
      (results shown in Appendix Figure 10). We suspected that this might be due to the dimensions of x ∈ RL× 1024 .
      For proteins with length L = 512 (the length cut-off used in this work), this maps to a high-resolution synthesis
227
      problem in image diffusion literature. We therefore mirror works in this literature, and perform diffusion in
228
      the latent space of an autoencoder s′ = he (x) such that the array dimensions of x′ is much smaller [37]. We
229   use the CHEAP autoencoder [10], such that diffusion training becomes pθ (x′ ) = he (x). Noise is added and
230   denoised from p(x′ ). At inference time, we first sample the compressed latent x̃′ ∼ pθ (x′ ), then “uncompress”
231   it to x̃ = hd (x′ ), followed by using frozen decoders to obtain s̃ = ϕ−1   ESM (x̃) and Ω̃ = ϕStructure Module (x̃).
232   Figure 4 illustrates how noised added in the latent space manifests in the sequence and structure spaces, and
233   Appendix Figure 10 shows results without compression. More information on CHEAP can be found in Lu
234   et al. [10] and Appendix B.

      Computational evaluation of function- and organism-conditioned generative models presents a conundrum:
      lower similarity is a favorable heuristic in machine learning, since it is indicates that the generative model
410
      did not merely memorize the training data. From a bioinformatics perspective, however, conservation is key
411
      to function; taxonomic membership can be difficult to validate, given the high degree of similarity between
412   homologs. In our case study experiments, we look for high structural similarity to evaluate for function
413   conditioning, and low sequence similarity to penalize exact memorization. Case studies shown in Figure 7
414   show that function-conditioned proteins possess known biological characteristics, such as conserved active
415   site motifs, and membrane hydrophobicity patterns. Global sequence diversity is low despite high levels of
416   conservation at catalytic sites, suggesting the the model has learned key biochemical features associated with
417   the function prompt without direct memorization. Appendix Figure 14 considers how conditioning scale
418   might affect sample quality, and possible GO term characteristics that might be influencing the difference in
419   Sinkhorn Distance between function-conditioned generations and random proteins.

446   6   D ISCUSSION
447
448   We proposed PLAID, a paradigm for multi-modal, controllable generation of proteins by diffusing in the
449   latent space of a prediction model that maps single sequences to the desired modality. Our method is
450
      designed to adhere to progress in data availability, model scalability, and sequence-to-structure prediction
      capabilities. To this end, we chose an implementation that can make use of fast attention kernels [42] for
451
      Transformer-based architectures, and chose GO terms as a proxy for the vast quantities of language annotation
452
      that are paired with sequence databases.
453
454   It is straightforward to expand PLAID to many downstream capabilities. First, though we do not examine
455   motif scaffolding or binder design explicitly in this current work, this is easy to build by holding some input
456
      residues constant. Second, though we examine ESMFold [3] in this work, the method can be applied to
      any prediction model. There is rapid progress [8, 7, 58, 59, 60] in predicting complexes from structure, and
457
      diffusing in the latent space of such models enables us to use the frozen decoder to obtain more modalities
458
      than just all-atom structure.
459
460   A limitation of PLAID is that performance can be bottlenecked by the prediction model from which the
461   frozen decoders are derived. Here, we rely on the optimism that such models will continue to improve. With
462
      explicit finetuning for latent generation (e.g. training CHEAP and the structure decoder end-to-end), model
      performance can likely be improved. Furthermore, since the current structure decoder is deterministic, it
463
      does not sample alternative conformations. A solution is to use a decoder that returns a distribution over
464
      structural conformations instead; such a model might naturally be developed with progress in the field, or be
465   explicitly finetuned for. Additionally, the GO term one-hot encoding used here does not take into account the
466   hierarchical nature of the Gene Ontology vocabulary, nor that a protein might have several relevant GO terms.
467   Finally, the classifier-free guidance scale can be separated for the organism and function conditions, since the
468   two may require different guidance strengths in real-world use cases. These limitation will be examined in
469   future work.

661   A    A DDITIONAL T RAINING D ETAILS
662
663   We train two variants of the model: a 2B version and a 100M version, both with the memory efficient attention
664   implementation in xFormers, with float32 precision. A learning rate of 1e-4 was used, with a cosine annealing
665   applied over 800,000 steps. The xFormers memory efficient attention kernel requires input lengths to be a
666   multiple of 4. Since we also apply an upsampling factor of 2, the actual inference length must be a multiple
667
      of 4. During training, the maximum sequence length we use is 512, based on the distribution of sequences in
      Pfam and a shorten factor of 2 based on results in Lu et al. [10].
668
669   Following Ho and Salimans [12], with puncond = 0.3, the class label is replaced with the ∅ unconditional
670   token. This is sampled separately for both function and organism. Note that not all data samples will have
671   an associated GO term; we use the ∅ token for those cases as well. At inference time, to perform generate
672   unconditionally (for either or both of function and/or organism), we use the ∅ token for conditioning.

      B    CHEAP C OMPRESSION D ETAILS
675
676
      Briefly, the CHEAP encoder and decoder uses an Hourglass Transformer [61] architecture that downsamples
677
      lengthwise, as well as downprojects the channel dimension, to create a bottleneck layer, the output of
678   which is our compressed embedding. The entire model is trained with the reconstruction loss M SE(x, x̂).
679   Results in Lu et al. [10] show that structural and sequence information in ESMFold latent spaces are in fact
680   highly compressible, and despite using very small bottleneck dimensions, reconstruction performance can be
681   nonetheless maintained when evaluated in sequence or structure space.
682                                                                          L

683
      Based on reconstruction results in Lu et al. [10], we choose x′ ∈ R 2 ×32 with L = 512, which balances
      reconstruction quality at a resolution comparable to the size of latent spaces in image diffusion models [37].
684
      Dividing the length in half allows us to better leverage the scalability and performance of Transformers, while
685
      managing its O(L) memory needs.
686
687   The CHEAP module involves a channel normalization step prior to the forward pass through the autoencoder.
688   We find that the distribution of embedding values is fairly “smooth" here (Figure 9). Though the original
689
      Rombach et al. [37] paper was trained with a KL constraint to a Gaussian distribution, we use the embedding
      output as is. CHEAP embeddings were also trained with a tanh layer at the output of the bottleneck; this
690
      allows us to clip our samples between [−1, 1] at each diffusion iteration, as was done in original image
691
      diffusion works [9, 12, 33, 62]. We found in early experiments being able to clip the output values were
692   very helpful for improving performance. Without using the CHEAP compression prior to diffusion, sample
693   quality was poor, even on short (L = 128) generations, shown in Figure 10.

758   D    S AMPLING
759
760
761   Inference-time sampling hyperparameters provides the user with additional control over quality and sampling
762   speed trade-off. PLAID supports the DDPM sampler [9] and the DDIM sampler [33], as well as the improved
763   speed samplers from DPM++ [52]. We find that using the DDIM sampler with 500 timesteps using either the
764   sigmoid or cosine schedulers works best during inference, and reasonable samples can be obtained using the
765
      DPM++2M-SDE sampler with only 20 steps. Experiments shown here uses DDIM sampler with the sigmoid
      noise schedule at 500 timesteps.
766
767   Note that the performance bottleneck is found mostly during the latent sampling and structure decoding
768   (which depends on the number of recycling iterations [17, 3] used); however, these two processes can be
769   easily decoupled and parallelized, which cannot be done in existing protein diffusion methods. Furthermore,
770   it allows us to prefilter which latents to decode using heuristic methods, and decode only those latents to
771
      structure, which would boost performance for nearly the same computational cost. We do not empirically
      explore this in this paper to provide a fair comparison, and because the filtering criteria would vary greatly by
772
      downstream use.

776   E    E VALUATION D ETAILS
777
778
779   For all benchmarks and models, we use default settings provided in their open-source code. For ProteinMPNN
780   [18], we use the v_48_002 model with a sampling temperature of 0.1 and generate 8 sequences for protein,
781   from which the best performing sequence use chosen. To calculate self-consistency, we fold sequences using
782   OmegaFold [53] rather than ESMFold.
783   Though our models generate all-atom structure, we examine Cα RMSD rather than all-atom RMSD, to avoid
784   mis-attributing sequence generation under-performance to structure generation failures. Also, since there are
785   usually differences in the sequence that is generated, different number of atoms make it difficult to assess
786   all-atom RMSD.
787
      For the hold-out natural reference dataset, we use sequences from Pfam and keep length distributions similar
788   to that of the sampled proteins. Specifically, for each sequence bin between {64, 72, ..., 504, 512}, we take
789   64 natural sequences of that length. For the experiment in Figure 14D, we use the Sinkhorn Distance rather
790   than the Frechet Distance used commonly in images and video. Since not all functions have a large number
791   of samples, we elected to use a metric that works better in low sample settings.
792
      Structure novelty is obtained by searching samples to PDB100 using Foldseek [55] easy-search. We
793
      examine the TM-score to the closest neighbor. For Foldseek and MMseqs experiments, all clustering
794   experiments are performed by length. We use default settings for both tools. Though we report the average
795   TMScore to top neighbor for Foldseek, we run easy-search in 3Di mode. For sequences, we use
796   MMseqs2 [54] to see if sequences have a homolog in UniRef50, using default sensitivity settings. For samples
797   with homologs, we further calculate the average sequence identity to the closet neighbor to assess novelty
798   (Seq ID %).
