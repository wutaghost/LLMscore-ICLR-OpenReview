011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Advancements in open-ended and embodied AI require highly adaptable and com-
014
               putationally efficient environments. Yet, existing platforms often lack the flexibil-
               ity, efficiency, or richness necessary to drive progress in these areas. Research
015
               in fields related to open-endedness, such as unsupervised environment design and
016
               continual reinforcement learning, usually defaults to simplistic 2D environments,
017            as alternatives are either too rigid or computationally expensive. Conversely, in
018            embodied AI, the field relies on fully featured video games like Minecraft, which
019            are rich in content but computationally inefficient, single-agent only, and hinder
020            the creation of new tasks. This paper introduces Craftium, a framework based on
021            the open-source Minetest game engine, providing a highly customizable, easy-to-
022            use, and efficient platform for building rich single and multi-agent Minecraft-like
023            3D environments. We showcase environments of different complexity and na-
024            ture: from single and multi-agent reinforcement learning tasks to vast worlds with
025
               many creatures and biomes and customizable procedural task generators. Con-
               ducted benchmarks show that Craftium substantially improves the computational
026
               cost of Minecraft-based frameworks, achieving +2K steps per second more.

041   1   I NTRODUCTION
042
043   Progress in open-ended (Hughes et al., 2024) and embodied AI (Paolo et al., 2024), as well as in
044   Reinforcement Learning (RL) (Sutton & Barto, 2018) is inherently tied to the environments where
045   agents are trained, evaluated, and analyzed. Each new insight or advancement in the field is sup-
046   ported by an environment that enables its emergence and study. A well-known example is the Atari
047   Learning Environments (ALE) (Bellemare et al., 2013), which undoubtedly contributed to the ad-
048   vancement of the RL field marking many of its most important milestones. To name a few: the
049   introduction of the Deep Q-Networks (Mnih et al., 2013), the “infamously difficult Montezuma’s
050
      Revenge” (Bellemare et al., 2016) that inspired many exploration strategies (Ostrovski et al., 2017;
      Burda et al., 2019; Badia et al., 2020b), and the first time an agent outperformed humans in all Atari
051
      benchmarks (Badia et al., 2020a).
052
053   However, the research in these areas is bound to challenges introduced by the employed environ-
      ments, as largely observed throughout the literature. The researcher often faces a dilemma between


                                                        1

054
      computationally efficient but simplistic, or substantially slower but rich environments. For instance,
055   Continual Reinforcement Learning (CRL) (Abel et al., 2023), Unsupervised Environment Design
056   (UED) (Garcin et al., 2024), and Multi-Agent RL (MARL) (Ying et al., 2023), are greatly affected
057   by the efficiency of the employed environments as require learning from many tasks or agents. Thus,
058   in these works, experiments are often limited to simple environments as a consequence of the com-
059   putational cost of employing more complex alternatives (Rigter et al., 2024; Malagon et al., 2024;
060   Beukman et al., 2024; Rutherford et al., 2024). For example, Craftax relies on 2D grids (Matthews
061   et al., 2024), while OMNI-EPIC (Faldor et al., 2024) employs 3D environments of substantially lim-
062   ited diversity compared to alternatives like MineDojo (Fan et al., 2022) or Habitat 3.0 (Puig et al.,
063
      2024).
064   Conversely, works on rich and complex environments (Grbic et al., 2021; Earle et al., 2024; Prasanna
065   et al., 2024; Raad et al., 2024) rely on fully featured video games that have a high computational
066   cost and are close-source. The best-known of such platforms is Minecraft, which has inspired sev-
067   eral single-agent environments and benchmarks over the years (Johnson et al., 2016; Guss et al.,
068   2019; Fan et al., 2022). However, Minecraft is a fully featured and complex 3D game, which makes
069
      it substantially more inefficient than simpler alternatives (Wydmuch et al., 2019; Matthews et al.,
      2024). Furthermore, its closed source greatly limits its flexibility, hindering its application to prob-
070
      lems beyond classic RL, like UED, CRL, and MARL scenarios.
071
072   Another important issue that specially affects research in these areas is the lack of flexibility of
073   the environments. Commonly used environments offer no customization or limited possibilities
074   often restricted to a set of predefined parameters, such as difficulty level or the number of ene-
075
      mies. Among others, these environments include: ALE (Machado et al., 2018), MineRL (Guss
      et al., 2019), ProcGen (Cobbe et al., 2020), MineDojo (Fan et al., 2022), Crafter (Hafner, 2022),
076
      and Craftax (Matthews et al., 2024). The lack of flexibility hinders the ability to analyze specific
077
      behavior of agents, obstructing algorithmic comparison beyond pure performance benchmarking,
078   which has been shown insufficient for RL (Jordan et al., 2024). Although flexible platforms that
079   allow the creation of new and diverse environments exist, these fall into 2D worlds (Bamford et al.,
080   2020; Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2023; Matthews et al., 2024) or depend on complex Domain Spe-
081   cific Languages (DSL) that difficult their implementation, while still not being 3D, as it is the case
082   of VizDoom (Wydmuch et al., 2019) and MiniHack (Samvelyan et al., 2021).
083
      In this paper, we present Craftium, a platform for easily creating rich 3D environments for single
084   and multi-agent embodied and open-ended AI research, thought to be highly customizable and effi-
085   cient. Unlike most complex environment platforms, that are based on video games (e.g., VizDoom
086   is based on ZDoom and MiniHack in NetHack), Craftium is based on a game engine: Minetest
087   (Minetest Team, 2024b). This allows easy creation of rich single and multi-agent voxel environ-
088   ments1 using a powerful and greatly documented Lua API (Minetest Team, 2024a), instead of much
089   less popular DSLs. Lua (Ierusalimschy, 2006) is a Python-like, easy-to-use and understand, mature,
090   and efficient programming language used in many popular tools and projects (e.g., Roblox, World of
091   Warcraft, and Neovim). In Craftium, Lua is used to expose a complete game engine (i.e., Minetest)
092
      to develop environments. Moreover, Minetest is open-source and has a vibrant community that has
      created many games and assets that can be used in Craftium environments (Ward, 2023a), signif-
093
      icantly reducing the development cost of complex scenarios. For instance, all the environments
094
      shown in Figure 1 have been implemented in less than 160 lines of code, comments and whites-
095   pace included. These environments, later described in Section 14, showcase the versatility of the
096   presented framework, from RL and MARL tasks of various levels of complexity and different na-
097   ture, customizable procedural environment generators for UED, CRL, and meta-RL (Yu et al., 2020;
098   Rimon et al., 2024) to gigantic procedurally generated open worlds (64K×64K×64K blocks) for
099   research on open-ended (Hughes et al., 2024) and embodied AI (Paolo et al., 2024). Beyond being
100   flexible, feature-rich, and developer-friendly, we show that Craftium environments run 38× faster
101   than alternatives based on the original Minecraft game, the only platforms that offer a similar level
102   of complexity and richness. Furthermore, Craftium implements the popular Gymnasium (Towers
103
      et al., 2024) and PettingZoo (Terry et al., 2021) interfaces the modern standard for RL and MARL
      research2 respectively, making it compatible with many other libraries and projects (Raffin et al.,
104
      2021; Huang et al., 2022b; Serrano-Muñoz et al., 2023). Finally, Craftium is fully open source and

      2       BACKGROUND : M INETEST AND M INECRAFT
128
129
      Minecraft is a very popular (Gerken, 2023) sandbox game4 where players explore a voxel-based,
130
      procedurally generated 3D world, gather resources, craft tools, and build structures. Although
131   Minecraft supports partially extending the original game (user extensions are referred to as mods),
132   this is limited by its close source nature and restrictions to access its underlying logic. Additionally,
133   the game and its mods are implemented in Java, a programming language not tailored for high-
134   performance applications and usually not commonly accessible in HPC clusters.
135
      In contrast, Minetest is a voxel-based game engine inspired by Minecraft, serving as a platform for
136
      creating games rather than being a game itself. Unlike Minecraft, Minetest supports modding at
137   its core, allowing fine-grained real-time access and modification of the internal state of the game
138   engine. This enables extensive customization of its behavior, facilitating the creation, modification,
139   and extension of existing games using its powerful Lua API (Minetest Team, 2024a; Ward, 2023b).
140   In turn, Minetest is implemented in C++, a widely adopted programming language and known for
141   its high efficiency. Moreover, Minetest is open source and is supported by an active community that
142   has created hundreds of open free-to-use games and mods (Ward, 2023a), that are seamlessly loaded
143   in Craftium (as employed in all environments from Section 3.5).

          3.1   O BSERVATIONS , ACTIONS , AND REWARDS
181
182       Observations. In Craftium, observations are images from the agent’s point of view. An example
183       observation is provided in Figure 3. Observations are highly customizable (e.g., size, number of
184       channels, etc.) and can vary between environments. Moreover, Craftium supports many popular
185       techniques such as, frame skipping and frame stacking that are commonly used throughout the
186       literature (Huang et al., 2022a).
187
188       Actions. By default, actions are composed of a combination of 21 keyboard actions and a tuple
189       that defines the movement of the mouse, which is mainly used to control the camera. Keyboard-
190       related actions are binary variables with a value of 1 if the key is pressed, and 0 otherwise. The
191       movement of the mouse is defined with the tuple (∆x , ∆y ) ∈ [−1, 1]2 , where ∆x < 0 moves the
192       mouse to the left in the horizontal axis and ∆x > 0 to the right, similarly, ∆y < 0 moves the mouse
193
          downwards in the vertical axis and ∆y > 0 moves it upwards. Thus, if ∆x = ∆y = 0, the mouse
          is not moved. See Appendix B.1 for a detailed description of all the possible actions supported in
194
          Craftium.
195
196       The default action space is designed to be versatile, covering as many use cases as possible: from
197       tasks with complex action sequences (e.g., manual inventory control) to simple navigation environ-
198       ments with a couple of actions (e.g., forward and lateral movement). However, the default action
199
          space is overly complex for most tasks: the number of possible keyboard action combinations in the
          default space is 221 . Therefore, Craftium allows reducing the action space to the minimal subset
200
          required to solve the task at hand substantially simplifying the learning process of the agent (see
201
          Appendix B.2).
202
203
          Rewards. In Craftium, reward functions are defined using Lua scripts (mods are discussed in the
204
          next section). The framework provides a comprehensive set of tools for this purpose, including an
205
          extended version of the Minetest Lua API. This functionality is implemented in a modified version
206       of the game engine developed specifically for this work, which incorporates additional functions for
207       setting and retrieving reward values and episode termination flags. An example of these functions
208       is presented in Section 3.2. A complete list of modifications to the original Minetest game engine
209       can be found in Appendix A, while additional functions for defining RL environments are detailed
210       in Appendix C.

465   4     R ELATED WORK
466
467   Table 1 includes a comparative overview of popular environment frameworks of the RL, open-ended,
468   and embodied AI literature. The following lines provide a more extensive discussion of this analysis.
469
      As stated in the introduction, a wide range of environments have been proposed for developing
470
      and evaluating autonomous agents. However, many of these environments are adaptations of video
471
      games (Bellemare et al., 2013; Wydmuch et al., 2019; Guss et al., 2019; Küttler et al., 2020) not
472   originally designed for research. As a result, they offer limited customization, often restricted to
473   predefined parameters (e.g., number of enemies). Examples include ALE (Machado et al., 2018),
474   MineRL (Guss et al., 2019), and NLE (Küttler et al., 2020). The lack of flexibility hinders their use in
475   various research scenarios, such as designing custom environments to study catastrophic forgetting
476   or analyzing specific behaviors of open-ended learning systems.
477
      These limitations have long been recognized, and several frameworks have been proposed that al-
478   low the creation of completely new environments. For example, VizDoom (Wydmuch et al., 2019)
479   allows defining environments using ZScript, and MiniHack (Samvelyan et al., 2021) employs the
480   des-file format for the same purpose. Both, ZScript and the des-file format are Do-
481   main Specific Langauges (DSL) tailored to the games they originate from (ZDoom and NetHack
482   respectively). However, DSLs are often purpose-specific and lack the flexibility and functionality of
483   general-purpose programming languages. For instance, the des-file format is not a program-
484

      https://github.com/Farama-Foundation/Procgen2.

      ming language per se, just a language to define NetHack levels. Additionally, DSLs often differ
487   significantly from mainstream programming languages, which limits their usability and adoption.
488
489   Some frameworks offer customization through the programming languages they are implemented
490
      in, avoiding the limitations of DSLs. For example, Griddly (Bamford et al., 2020) and MiniGrid
      (Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2023) offer Python APIs for creating 2D grid-like environment. While
491
      grid environments are fast to simulate, they lack the complexity and diversity of more advanced
492
      environments like MineRL and VizDoom. Although more complex tasks could be implemented in
493   these frameworks, it would require significant development effort for researchers. Regarding 3D
494   environments, MiniWorld (Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2023) offers a similar API to MiniGrid, but
495   suffers from the same issues regarding the implementation of richer environments.
496
      The field of embodied AI for robotics has long recognized the importance of visually complex sce-
497
      narios, which include popular frameworks such as AI2-THOR (Kolve et al., 2017) and Habitat 3.0
498
      (Puig et al., 2024). However, these works focus on accurate physical modeling and photorealism
499   while having limited diversity (mostly including indoor household scenarios) and a lack of open-
500   world environments. For higher-level cognitive tasks that do not require accurate physics modeling
501   or photorealism, the field has popularly adopted Minecraft: an extremely popular game, with rich
502   content, diverse open worlds, and complex game mechanics. Some examples are the Malmo (John-
503   son et al., 2016) project and MineRL (Guss et al., 2019) that wraps Minecraft in a Python interface.
504   However, they lack support for task customization or the creation of new environments. More re-
505   cently, MineDojo (Fan et al., 2022) has greatly improved customization within Minecraft-based
506   environments. Nevertheless, environment creation is constrained by predefined parameters, making
507
      scenarios like those in Section 3.5 infeasible to implement (see Appendix G for details) and lacking
      multi-agent support, which hinders its adoption in this growing field.

      5   C ONCLUSION
513
514
      Designing new environments and modifying existing ones is crucial for advancing research in open-
515
      ended and embodied AI, RL, MARL, and autonomous agents in general. However, many established
516
      environments provide limited or no options for customization (Bellemare et al., 2013; Johnson et al.,
517   2016; Guss et al., 2019; Küttler et al., 2020; Matthews et al., 2024). Although some works offer tools
518   for developing environments, they rely on restrictive DSLs (Wydmuch et al., 2019; Samvelyan et al.,
519   2021) or simplistic 2D worlds (Bamford et al., 2020; Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2023). Conversely,
520   rich and complex 3D environments like MineDojo (Fan et al., 2022) allow limited customization,
521   have no multi-agent support, and are built on closed-source and computationally expensive games
522   like Minecraft.
523   This work presents Craftium, an easy-to-use and flexible framework for creating rich 3D environ-
524   ments. Craftium’s versatility is showcased in Section 3.5, which shows its application to train and
525   analyze single and multi-agent RL algorithms, implement open-world environments for complex
526   embodied agent tasks, and used to procedurally generate environments for CRL. Unlike many al-
527   ternatives built on top of existing video games, Craftium is based on Minetest, a fully-featured
528   open-source game engine. This analogy is also translated to the presented framework, as it is not
529   a benchmark but a general-purpose tool for creating environments. By leveraging the extensive
530   and well-documented Minetest Lua API (Minetest Team, 2024a; Ward, 2023b), Craftium enables
531
      nearly limitless possibilities for the development of custom single and multi-agent environments.
      Additionally, Minetest has a vibrant community that has produced numerous games and extensions
532
      (Ward, 2023a), which can be easily integrated into Craftium environments (see Section 3.5.3). More-
533
      over, its efficient implementation significantly reduces the computational cost of other alternatives.
534   As shown in Section 3.4, Craftium achieves over 2K timesteps per second more than MineDojo,
535   and performs competitively with VizDoom, even though VizDoom is not fully 3D. Craftium also
536   implements the widely-adopted Gymnasium (Towers et al., 2024) and Petting Zoo (Terry et al.,
537   2021) interfaces, making it compatible with numerous existing tools and projects, such as, Moritz
538   et al. (2018); Huang et al. (2022b); Serrano-Muñoz et al. (2023) and Raffin et al. (2021). Finally,
539   Craftium is open source and provides extensive documentation, including many practical examples
      from which users can build environments for their particular research needs.

783   A    M ODIFICATIONS TO MINETEST
784
785   Although Minetest is an extremely flexible game engine with extensibility built into its core, adapt-
786   ing it to be a platform for RL environment creation required modifying the game engine’s source
787   code. As Minetest is a large C++ project with thousands of files, modifications have been thought-
788   fully limited to as few changes as possible to ensure seamless updates to new versions of Minetest
789   and improve maintainability. Most of the introduced code is limited to a single craftium.h and a
790   few modifications to the client.cpp file. These modifications have allowed the implementation
791
      of features required for training RL agents in Minetest:
792
            • Implementation of a client that connects to the Python process with the agent’s implemen-
793
              tation. This is the communication channel from which Minetest sends RGB frames and
794           other timestep data to Python, and Python sends the next actions to be executed.
795
796         • Executing agent’s actions as keyboard and mouse commands in Minetest. All actions are
797           translated as virtual keyboard keypresses or mouse movements (for moving the camera and
798
              controlling the inventory).
799         • Extensions to the Minetest Lua API to incorporate vital functionalities for RL environ-
800           ments. Extensions include 5 new Lua functions that implement functions such as setting
801           the episode termination flag or sending reward values.
802
            • Minetest has a client/server architecture, where the server runs the world’s logic and the
803
              client interfaces with the player (e.g., game control and rendering). However, the asyn-
804           chronous nature of this architecture introduced many problems to most RL agent training
805           scenarios, as the server could update the world many times while the client was waiting for
806           the agent to return an action. This causes many reproducibility issues and behaviors such
807           as monsters attacking the player while the client is waiting for the agent’s response. For
808           this purpose, Craftium introduces synchronous client/server updates. This ensures that the
809           server waits for the client to be updated before continuing with the next update, preventing
              the mentioned issues.

841   B.2     ACTION WRAPPERS
842
843   By default, Craftium environments have a large action space with discrete (binary) and continu-
844   ous values (see Section 3). However, many tasks do not require the complete default action space
845
      and can be greatly simplified by considering only the relevant actions to solve the specific task
      that the environment defines. Consequently, Craftium provides tools for customizing the action
846
      space of environments by using Gymnasium Wrappers.9 Specifically, Craftium implements two
847
      wrappers:BinaryActionWrapperand DiscreteActionWrapper.
848
849   BinaryActionWrapper allows selecting the subset of keyboard actions (see Table 2 for the
850   complete list) to use in the new action space. This wrapper also simplifies the continuous mouse
851
      movement actions by discretizing them into four binary actions: move the mouse left, right, up,
      and down. The magnitude of these movements can be chosen by the developer. For example, this
852
      wrapper allows simplifying the default {0, 1}21 ∪ [0, 1]2 action space into a {0, 1}3 space where
853
      binary values correspond to: move forward, move mouse right, and move mouse left.
854
855   DiscreteActionWrapper allows selecting the subset of keyboard actions and discretizes the
856   mouse movement similarly to the previous wrapper. However, in this case, actions are not binary
857
      vectors but a single discrete value. Thus, actions can not be combined as in the case of the previ-
      ous wrapper. Following the previous example, instead of simplifying the default action space into
858
      {0, 1}3 this wrapper defines the new space as {0, 1, 2}, where 0 corresponds to move forward, 1
859
      moves the mouse to the right, and 2 moves it to the left.

925   E    D ETAILS ON THE PERFORMANCE BENCHMARK
926
927   Due to the page limit constraint of the paper, this section provides additional details on the environ-
928   ment performance comparison presented in Section 3.4.
929   To complement the results illustrated in Figure 7, Table 4 provides the exact average and stan-
930   dard deviation values. The measurements aggregate the results of 5 different runs of 1K steps in
931   3 environments per framework. Note that all environments considered for this experiment were
932   single-agent, as MineDojo does not support multi-agent scenarios12 and VizDoom does not provide
933   multi-agent environments (although technically supports this setting).13 The environments were:
934   speleo, room, and spiders attack for Craftium (see Appendix F.1); VizdoomHealthGathering-v0,
935
      VizdoomCorridor-v0, and VizdoomDefendCenter-v0 for VizDoom; and harvest milk, creative:255,
      and Harvest for MineDojo. In all cases, observations were RGB images, without frameskip, and
936
      actions were selected uniformly at random. In the case of MineDojo and Craftium environments
937
      observation size was set to 64 × 64 pixels, and to 320 × 240, as the latter resolution is not available
938   for VizDoom environments.
939
940   As can be observed in Table 4, Craftium achieves substantially higher, +38%, steps per second
941
      than the Minecraft alternative, MineDojo. The reasons for such a significant performance gap are
      many, as both frameworks are complicated systems with many interacting components. One of
942
      the most significant differences is the choice of implementation language: MineDojo is based on
943
      Minecraft, which is implemented in Java 8, while Craftium relies on Minetest, implemented in C++
944   and known to perform significantly higher than Java.14 Another relevant aspect is that Minecraft is
945   a complete game, that has grown in complexity over the years, and this complexity directly affects
946   to the environments implemented on top of it. As it is a close source game, the developer is not
947   allowed to modify its source code to remove irrelevant parts of the game for the environment at
948   hand for the sake of computational efficiency. Contrarily, Minetest is open source and exposes
949   a highly flexible Lua API to modify its behavior. This allows building environments with only
950   the relevenat components for the task at hand. Along the same line, the open-source nature of
951   Minetest allowed its modification to tightly integrate it with the proposed framework. For example,
952
      to incorporate a system to execute the actions sent from the Python interface as keyboard and mouse
      commands. Conversely, Minecraft does not allow modifications to its source code, which requires
953
      MineRL and MineDojo15 to include many layers of complexity to adapt the Minecraft game to the
954
      RL setting. Most notably, Minecraft is a game and is not intended to run on a server without a
955   monitor. Therefore, MineRL and MineDojo use an external tool, Xvfb16 . to emulate a monitor
956   without showing any screen output, which causes significant performance drawbacks. This also
957   implies that the X11 windowing system17 is installed, which is not often the case in HPC clusters.

1099   F.3     O PEN WORLD
1100
1101   In Section 3.5.3 we introduce an open-world environment. In this environment, the agent has to
1102
       survive and gather resources in an open world based on the open-source VoxeLibre (Fleckenstein
       et al., 2024) game for Minetest. The environment is designed to have three different tracks: tools,
1103
       hunt, and defend.
1104
1105   The first, the Tools track, consists of 4 different milestones: collect two wood blocks, three stone
1106   blocks, three iron blocks, and finally, a diamond block. When the agent unlocks one of the stages
1107   (i.e. tasks) it receives a reward and a new set of tools to employ to solve the next task. The reward for
1108
       completing each of the stages is 128, 256, 1024, and 2048 respectively. Moreover, when the agent
       unlocks a new stage, it receives a sword and a pickaxe of the material of the completed stage. For
1109
       example, if the agent unlocks the wood stage (collect two wood blocks), a wood sword and pickaxe
1110
       are automatically added to its inventory. To simplify solving the first stage of this track, the initial
1111   inventory of the agent is composed of a stone axe and 256 torches. The stone axe allows the agent to
1112   more easily chop trees to collect wood, while it also serves to defend from enemies (i.e. monsters)
1113   and hunt animals.
1114
       Conversely, the Hunt and Defend tracks are non-sequential. The agent is expected to develop skills
1115
       to handle increasingly complex scenarios rather than progressing linearly (although this could also
1116
       be the case). In these tracks, a reward is provided to the agent every time it puches an enemy or
1117   an animal. In the case of enemies, the reward value is equal to the damage caused by the tool,
1118   while in the case of the animals, this value is reduced to half. The motivation behind this particular
1119   reward function is the following. If the agent defeats an enemy or hunts an animal, the episodic
1120   return obtained by the agent is linear to the life of the enemy or animal. Moreover, the agent is also
1121   encouraged to use the correct tool for these tasks. For example, using a sword to fight a monster will
1122   provide more reward than using a torch or pickaxe for the same task.
1123   In Minetest, the time of day of the game is linked to the real clock time, where the day/night cycle
1124   lasts for 20 minutes by default.19 In consequence, in this environment the time of day is set ac-
1125   cording to the global timestep to maintain consistency and avoid relaying in real clock time while
1126   training agents. If the latter is not considered, the time of day experienced by the agents could vary
1127   depending on the time required by the agent to select an action, which greatly varies depending on
1128   its implementation and architecture.
1129   The following lines provide details on the methods used in the experiment from Section 3.5.3. Note
1130   that in both cases, the action space of the agents was composed of 18 discrete actions, defined
1131   using DiscreteActionWrapper from Appendix B.2. The actions are: nop, move forward,
1132   backward, left, and right, jump, sneak, dig, place, slot 1, slot 2, slot 3, slot 4, slot 5, move the mouse

1146   Liu et al. (2024a), specifically version 1.6 (Liu et al., 2024b). This agent is not intended as a new
1147   proposal for LMM for embodied AI, but just as an example of how LMMs can be employed within

1149   LLaVa has been directly employed with no fine-tuning for the open-world environment. Specifically,
1150

1152
