011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Language Models (LLMs) are becoming increasingly pivotal in applications
014            that depend on extensive personalized context, such as conversational agents
015            and specialized task-oriented systems. In these scenarios, effective long-context
016
               handling is essential to support agentic tasks and enhance in-context learning
               capabilities. To address this challenge, we propose a novel integration of Retrieval-
017
               Augmented Generation (RAG) techniques with LLMs, designed to enhance their
018
               ability to effectively manage and utilize large contextual information only available
019            at test time. Our methodology, Test-Time RAG (TTRAG), enriches LLMs by
020            dynamically generating novel conditional embeddings coupled with query rewriting
021            and utilizing semantic search to retrieve the most relevant document chunks at
022            test time. This process preserves the context’s meaning and enhances the model’s
023            responsiveness and accuracy in knowledge-intensive Question Answering (QA)
024            tasks. Our evaluations demonstrate our system’s ability synthesize and retrieve
025            information across extensive texts: HotpotQA (+17.29%), QASPER (+4.39%), and
026            Natural Questions (+8.73%), demonstrating the effectiveness of TTRAG across
027
               varied context lengths from 1 million to 9.6 million tokens.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The ability for LLMs to incorporate a detailed history of a user’s experiences and actions could
033
      unlock a new era of personalized intelligence. For example, an LLM helping a user respond to
      an email could take into account much more than just the text in the email: it could also take
034
      into account the user’s documents, calendar schedule, and other related emails or shared data.
035
      However, capturing this scale of rich context for a user presents several challenges. First, because
036   the information is diverse and sparse, naively retrieving related documents by keyword or vector
037   similarity alone is insufficient (Karpukhin et al., 2020; Khattab & Zaharia, 2020). Second, the
038   potentially relevant context could be orders of magnitude larger than what can fit within existing
039   long-context models (Anthropic, 2023; Reid et al., 2024). Finally, when the information does fit in
040   context, the use of long-context and the presence of distracting information can lead to significant
041   quality loss and increased latency (Liu et al., 2023c).
042   Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) systems have been increasingly employed for enabling
043   LLMs to effectively access large collections of data (Lewis et al., 2021; Borgeaud et al., 2022; Wang
044   et al., 2023; Patil et al., 2023). RAG operates by first computing document embeddings for each
045   document in the collection and then computes a similarity score with a given prompt to determine
046   a set of documents that should be included as context in a prompt to an LLM. RAG systems must
047   pre-compute document embeddings, limiting their application to scenarios where documents cannot
048   be indexed in advance. Such situations are common in our personalized applications including chat-
049   based interactions (Peng et al., 2023), code analysis, database joins, and privacy-centric systems where
050
      personal data is not available offline. To overcome these constraints and unlock new possibilities
      in long-context processing, this paper presents a systems that extends classic RAG techniques of
051
      embedding and filtering to a test-time setting, which we refer to as Test-time RAG (TTRAG).
052
053   As illustrated in fig. 1, TTRAG is built upon three critical components that address the challenges
      posed by dynamic knowledge bases and enhance the efficiency of long-context processing: Con-

131   2   M OTIVATION
132
133   While in-context learning has proved to be a powerful tool for providing the model with relevant
134   context (Wies et al., 2024), LLMs that support long-contexts face three challenges: the computational
135   overhead required for training, the degradation in performance at longer-context, and the latency
136   overhead at inference time. The self-attention mechanism in auto-regressive transformer-based archi-
137   tectures suffers from quadratic increase in complexity as context length increases (Vaswani, 2017).
138   This computational burden poses a substantial challenges in training larger context models Dubey et al.
139   (2024). Moreover, even when long context capabilities are implemented, models often experience
140   performance degradation (Li et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023c), as well as increased latency (Agrawal
141
      et al., 2024), as the context window expands, limiting their practical effectiveness.
142   Classically, to address these limitations, two techniques are adopted: retrieval augmented gener-
143   ation (RAG) and recursive summarization. These approaches aim to filter content, and compress
144   information to fit within the limited context windows of current models respectively. Recursive
145   summarization (Ren et al., 2017), while effective in some scenarios, inherently results in lossy
146   compression of previous context, often sacrificing important information as the context grows.
147
      RAG (Lewis et al., 2020), on the other hand, pre-indexes a domain of context into an external
      database of documents and employs information retrieval mechanisms to fetch relevant context at
148
      prompt time. While RAG has shown promise in many applications, its effectiveness is heavily
149
      dependent on having prior knowledge of the information set required to answer prospective queries.
150   None of the solutions mentioned before provide an effective technique to handle large amounts of
151   data introduced at test time and do not exploit the availability of intent present in the prompt.
152
153
      Other efforts have looked at advancements in long context processing driven by architectural mod-
      ifications such as State-space models (Gu et al., 2021), ring attention (Liu et al., 2023a), sparse
154
      attention (Chen et al., 2021; Zoph, 2022), etc. These advancements are complementary and benefit
155
      from Test-time RAG.

      3.1   C ONDITIONAL E MBEDDINGS
173
174
      We present a novel approach to generate conditional embeddings at test time that capture detailed
175   semantic relationships between context and query. The fundamental mechanics which power con-
176   ditional embeddings rely on the premise enforced in test-time settings: access to the user query.
177   Traditional approaches to compute document embeddings are done offline and independent of the
178   queries themselves. We instead propose a method to compute document embeddings on a per-query
179   basis which leads to selecting documents that are more related to the query during retrieval. Our
180   approach contains two components that utilize query information: conditional embedding generation
181   and filtering. During the embedding generation step, we employ the query to tailor granular repre-
182   sentations of text such that the filtering portion can efficiently retrieve relevant context over large
183
      contexts. To accomplish this, we leverage the contextual embedding capabilities that are implicitly
      present in the transformer architecture, and utilize encoder-decoder variants which jointly condition
184
      context on text appearing before and after a given token in a sequence (Devlin et al., 2019).
185
186   Decoder-only architectures where masked, causal attention is utilized, only allow tokens to attend to
187   previous tokens in the sequence, with next token prediction as the training objective (Radford et al.,
188   2019). In contrast, bidirectional encoders allow each token to attend to all other tokens, creating
189
      token representations that have contextual awareness of other tokens in the sequence regardless
      of their ordering as they pass through the encoder (Devlin et al., 2019). Bidirectional attention is
190
      a critical component of conditional embeddings generation to enable proper conditioning on the
191
      query. Conditional document embeddings are constructed by first prefixing each document with the
192   query text. This augmented document is passed through a bidirectional encoder (DistilBERT), where
193   we extract the final hidden state embeddings, mask the tokens corresponding to the prefixed query
194   (visualized in fig. 4), then take an average across the sequence length dimension of the unmasked
195   token embeddings. From our ablations, we find that the performance of embeddings generated
196   from pooling the last hidden state is agnostic of the presence of the <EOS> token, which we leave
197   unmasked. We are left with a fixed length embedding that captures fine grained relationships between
198   the query and context, which can be used in similarity search without the prefixed query inflating the
199   similarity scores. Conditional Embeddings (Filtering only) applies cosine similarity search between
200
      query and document embeddings to only retain semantically similar context documents.
201
202   3.2   I TERATIVE Q UERY R EWRITING
203
204   Drawing inspiration from query reformulation techniques in recommendation systems (Bhandari
205   et al., 2023), we introduce a framework for automatic query rewriting to improve retrieval and
206   generation quality. Questions over long context often have vague subjects and directives, as these
207
      aspects are meant to be derived from context. However, in retrieval augmented paradigms, this
      poses as a bottleneck during filtering and similarity search, as the standard embeddings struggle with
208
      capturing the fine-grained semantics of a vague query with respect to a set of documents. We leverage
209
      reasoning capabilties of LLMs to make the query more conducive for similarity search. We first pass
210   the query along with the top three (hyperparameter) documents retrieved from similarity search to
211   the LLM. Rather than immediately moving to the answer generation step, we prompt the LLM to
212   determine whether the answer to the query is found in the provided context. If the answer is not found,
213   the LLM is tasked with rewriting the query to be a better proxy for retrieving relevant context given
214   the documents provided. During this step, the LLM has freedom to incorporate additional information
215   from the documents into the rewritten question to dispel ambiguity and reword the question structure
      so the question directive is less vague. If the answer is found, the LLM is instructed to just return the

      3.3   C ONDITIONAL C OMPUTE
262
263
      Access to the query and documents at test time allows our pipeline to incorporate a conditional
264   compute feature designed to minimize compute while maintaining output quality based on the nature
265   of the user’s query, which greatly enhances its versatility across various tasks such as summarization,
266   question answering and key-value retrieval. Categorizing different user questions into various
267   "compute categories" is built on the idea that different types of information require distinct strategies
268   for document retrieval and processing. Adding this level of granularity results in context retrieval that
269   is more cost-effective and compute-efficient. In this paper, conditional compute refers to the number
      of tokens processed at test time conditioned on the query, consistent with literature (Brown, 2021)


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      Task Classification and Response Generation: Each user query is classified into four predefined
271   categories: summarization tasks, KV retrieval tasks, aggregate statistics queries, and question-
272   answering tasks by an LLM classifier. Depending on the determined category, the pipeline adjusts its
273   subsequent processing steps to tailor the retrieval and response generation specifically to the task at
274   hand.
275
      Summary Tasks: Retrieval based approaches historically struggle with creating summaries due to
276
      the lack of whole context comprehension. For queries categorized under summarization, we split the
277   large context block into smaller batches that fit into the context window of the base LLM and then ask
278   the LLM to generate summaries for these smaller chunks and concantenate them to form the output.
279
280
      Aggregate Statistics Tasks: We adopt a selective filtering-based implementation that identifies the
      target block of text and calculate relevant aggregate statistics across the entire document set.
281
282   KV Retrieval Tasks: Key-value pair (KV) retrieval challenges the LLM with accurately retrieving
283   a value given a key from a large block of JSON objects (Liu et al., 2023c). The complexity of this
284   task is lies in the large retrieval base and the indistinguishable format of relevant and irrelevant
285   information, making the actual textual content the only distinct attribute. To tackle these challenges,
286
      we employ a sparse BM25 retriever to fetch the most similar document from the context (top k = 1).
287   Question-Answering Tasks: For queries that do not fall into any of the other categories, the pipeline
288   defaults to our standard Test-time RAG retrieval process, leveraging the configured embedding model
289   to fetch documents that best match the query’s semantic content.

303   4.1   DATASETS , M ODELS
304
      We center our findings around the end-to-end evaluation of TTRAG and bolster them by presenting
305
      ablation studies to quantify individual component performance, using a common base of models and
306
      datasets. All datasets have long documents as context, either by nature of the dataset or by artificially
307   augmenting context during preprocessing.
308
309
      Conditional Embeddings Conditional embedding performance is evaluated on two different
310   categories of datasets: general question-answering spanning multiple domains and domain-specific
311   datasets. For the first group of datasets, we use popular knowledge intensive question-answer datasets:
312   HotpotQA, Natural Questions, and PubMedQA (Yang et al., 2018; Kwiatkowski et al., 2019; Jin
313   et al., 2019).
314
      For HotpotQA and Natural Questions, we extend the model-provided context to millions of tokens by
315
      artificially concatenating documents across all questions in their respective datasets. We additionally
316   evaluate conditional embeddings on a second group of datasets with domain-specific context to test if
317   conditional embeddings are proficient at creating granular representations of text from context blocks
318   that are all relatively similar to each other. QASPER is a dataset consisting of question and answering
319   over NLP research papers, while QuALITY requires comprehension over whole text passages Dasigi
320   et al. (2021); Pang et al. (2022). Both datasets contain approximately 5k tokens of context on average
321   per question, all pertaining to the same body of text.
322
323   Iterative Query Rewriting CRAG, a comprehensive RAG-specific benchmark, presents a unique
      challenge for query rewriting by including 300k tokens of simulated search results per question (Yang

325   contained in CRAG question documents is related. The inherent similarity between documents can

      5   C ONCLUSION
524
      In conclusion, this paper presents Test-time RAG, a novel system incorporating conditional embed-
525
      dings, iterative query rewriting, and conditional compute. As a novel extension to classical RAG,
526
      TTRAG addresses the challenges of incorporating vast, dynamic knowledge bases in personalized AI
527   applications. Test-time RAG enables more flexible and efficient use of long contexts, overcoming
528   the limitations of pre-indexing and static retrieval mechanisms. Our evaluation of TTRAG is two-
529   fold: we measure the performance of the system as a whole, then present ablations exploring the
530   individual contributions of each component across various knowledge-intensive, domain-specific, and
531   long-context benchmarks, including HotpotQA, Natural Questions, PubMed, QASPER, QuALITY,
532   and CRAG. Test-time RAG demonstrates consistent and substantial improvements in accuracy and
533   context-handling capabilities with varied base models in diverse settings, showcasing TTRAG’s
534   potential in scaling long-context processing to millions of tokens. By dynamically adapting at test
535
      time, Test-time RAG empowers large language models to personalize and refine their responses based
      on evolving user contexts, enhancing their utility in real-world, user-focused scenarios.

902   Another implication of our pipeline is its cost effectiveness. One implicit feature of Test-Time RAG
903
      is that context processing is targeted as only relevant chunks are shown to the LLM, especially for
      Question-Answering tasks. As a result, the average amount of tokens processed per generation is
904
      significantly less than passing in large blocks of context. Because LLM API providers charge usage
905
      based on tokens in and tokens out, large context tasks can incur compounding charges very quickly.
906   This is mitigated by the reduced token processing done in at all components of Test-Time RAG. We
907   display example costs for running our evaluations in 9, and demonstrate that Test-Time RAG attains
908   superior performance at a fraction of the cost.
