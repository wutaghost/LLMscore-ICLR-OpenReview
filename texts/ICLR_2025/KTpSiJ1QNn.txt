009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Graphs play a central role in modeling complex relationships across various do-
012            mains. Most graph learning methods rely heavily on neighborhood information,
013            raising the question of how to handle cold-start nodes — nodes with no known
014            connections within the graph. These models often overlook the cold-start nodes,
015            making them ineffective for real-world scenarios. To tackle this, we propose G-
016
               SPARC, a novel framework addressing cold-start nodes, that leverages general-
               izable spectral embedding. This framework enables extension to state-of-the-art
017
               methods making them suitable for practical applications. By utilizing a key idea of
018
               transitioning from graph representation to spectral representation, our approach is
019            generalizable to cold-start nodes, capturing the global structure of the graph with-
020            out relying on adjacency data. Experimental results demonstrate that our method
021            outperforms existing models on cold-start nodes across various tasks like node
022            classification, node clustering, and link prediction. G-SPARC provides a break-
023            through built-in solution to the cold-start problem in graph learning. Our code will
024            be publicly available upon acceptance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Graphs have advanced deep learning techniques across various domains, enabling tasks such as
029
      node classification, node clustering, and link prediction (Kipf & Welling, 2016a; Battaglia et al.,
030   2018; LeCun et al., 1989; Yao et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2019). By incorporating both node features
031   and structural relationships, graph-based models address complex relational patterns that traditional
032   methods struggle to capture, positioning graph learning at the center of modern machine learning.
033
      A major, yet often overlooked, challenge in graph learning is generalizing to nodes that emerge
034
      without initial connections. This scenario frequently occurs in real-world applications but is poorly
035
      addressed by existing methods. This issue, commonly referred to as the cold-start problem, is
036   particularly crucial in dynamic environments where new nodes regularly appear without any links.
037   For instance, on social media platforms, new users often join without any initial connections or
038   followers. Despite having detailed profiles, these users do not have the connections that graph-
039   based methods rely on to make predictions. As real-world graphs are not static; they are constantly
040   evolving as new nodes may appear without connections, necessitating models that can adapt to these
041   changes.
042   While state-of-the-art methods, such as message-passing (Gilmer et al., 2017; Wu et al., 2020),
043   graph convolutional networks (Kipf & Welling, 2016b; Chiang et al., 2019), and graph transformers
044   (Chen et al., 2022; Fu et al., 2024), excel on benchmark datasets, they fall short when applied to
045   real-world scenarios that involve cold-start nodes. These models heavily rely on the neighborhood
046   information, leaving a significant gap in their ability to make accurate predictions for cold-start
047   nodes. This limitation represents a major obstacle to deploying graph-based solutions in practical
048   settings.
049   To address the limitations of traditional graph learning methods, we leverage a fundamental con-
050   cept in graph theory by transitioning from a graph representation defined by the adjacency matrix to
051   its spectral representation captured through the eigenvectors of the Laplacian matrix. Spectral em-
052   bedding represents the location of the node in the manifold coordinate system (Lafon et al., 2006;
053   Belkin & Niyogi, 2003). We infer neighborhood from the spectral embedding for cold-start nodes,
      bypassing the need for explicit adjacency information.

054
      Our approach uses a generalizable spectral embedding framework that provides spectral embedding
055   for the cold-start nodes allowing isolated nodes to be seamlessly integrated into the graph struc-
056   ture. Our approach involves training a neural network to map node features to their corresponding
057   spectral embeddings. During training, adjacency information is used to guide this mapping, but the
058   model is generalizable to cold-start nodes during inference when no connections are available. This
059   parametric mapping enables us to compute spectral embeddings that reflect the graph’s underlying
060   structure, even in the absence of adjacency data for the cold-start nodes.
061   In this paper, we propose a novel spectral-based framework specifically designed to handle cold-
062   start node predictions across multiple downstream tasks. Rooted in spectral theory, our architectures
063   leverage generalizable spectral embeddings to support a wide range of graph learning applications.
064   We demonstrate the effectiveness of our framework across three key applications: node classifica-
065   tion, node clustering, and link prediction. Our contributions address a critical gap in the current
066   landscape of graph learning and offer a key extension for current methods to handle cold-start nodes
067   making them suitable for real-world applications. Our framework is adaptable and can be seamlessly
068   integrated into existing and future graph learning models to support cold-start nodes. Additionally,
069
      we show that our generalizable spectral embedding can be used for graph partitioning, enabling an
      effective mini-batching strategy that is well-suited for GCN methods.

072   2   R ELATED W ORK
073
074   In graph machine learning, most state-of-the-art algorithms assume the graph structure is fixed (e.g.
075   Kipf & Welling (2016b); Veličković et al. (2017); Hamilton et al. (2017a); Xu et al. (2018); Gasteiger
076   et al. (2018); Wu et al. (2019); Chiang et al. (2019); Chen et al. (2022); Thorpe et al. (2022); Mo
077   et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2023)), thereby bypassing the cold-start problem. While these algorithms are
078
      effective on benchmark datasets, they are limited to real-world applications, where new cold-start
      nodes are common, making it crucial to develop models that can handle evolving graphs.
079
080   While the issue of cold-start nodes is often overlooked, few methods have been developed to ad-
081   dress similar problems (Liu et al., 2020; 2021; Rong et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2022),
082   focusing on tail nodes - nodes with low-connectivity. A few methods specifically developed for han-
083   dling cold-start scenarios include GraphSAGE (Hamilton et al., 2017a) and Cold-Brew (Zheng et al.,
084
      2021). Cold-Brew introduces a distillation technique where a trained ”teacher” GCN model imparts
      knowledge to a ”student” model, enabling the student to predict the low-dimensional embedding
085
      learned by the teacher. GraphSAGE learns a cluster-based representation of the graph by aggre-
086
      gating features from neighboring nodes to create an inductive representation. Unlike these methods
087   that focus on representing the graph’s structure, our approach learns a representation of the manifold
088   from which the graph is drawn, utilizing spectral embeddings. Grounded in spectral graph theory,
089   which is based on sound mathematical principles, these embeddings preserve both global and local
090   structures, enabling the generation of meaningful representations for cold-start nodes while main-
091   taining the overall topology. As a result, our method enhances the ability to capture the graph’s
092   structure, leading to improved performance and more accurate predictions in downstream tasks.

102   SpectralNet. SpectralNet (Shaham et al., 2018) is a deep-learning approach designed for spectral
103   clustering. It maps data points to the approximate k eigenvectors of the Laplacian matrix, which is
104   constructed from the similarities between the data points. By leveraging the convergence proper-
105   ties of the Laplacian operator applied in mini-batches (Belkin & Niyogi, 2001; 2003), SpectralNet
106   enables efficient spectral clustering even for large-scale datasets by utilizing SGD to approximate
107   the eigenvectors of the Laplacian. One key advantage of SpectralNet is that it learns a parametric
      mapping, approximating the eigenfunction of the Laplace-Beltrami operator. This allows a general-

      ization to out-of-sample data (OOSE). This makes it possible to compute spectral embeddings for
109   unseen data points.  The learning process involves minimizing a Rayleigh-quotient loss function:

110   trace Y T LY , s.t. Y T Y = I, where Y ∈ Rn×k represents the network outputs, and L is the
111   graph Laplacian. This method serves as the foundation for our generalizable spectral embedding,
112   extending spectral techniques to settings of traditional graph-structured data.
113
114   Spectral-GCNs. Spectral Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) represent a significant advance-
115   ment in graph-based machine learning by enabling convolution-like operations directly on graph-
116   structured data. These networks adapt traditional convolutional operations from Euclidean domains
117   to non-Euclidean spaces, such as graphs, making them highly effective for tasks involving relational
118   data.
119   Spectral convolution (Kipf & Welling, 2016b) operates in the spectral domain of the graph, defining
120   convolution as the multiplication of a signal x ∈ RN by a filter gθ = diag(θ), parameterized by
121   θ ∈ RN , in the Fourier domain. Specifically, this operation can be expressed as applying the Fourier
122   transform, followed by its inverse. In this context, U is the matrix of eigenvectors of the graph
123   Laplacian, which serves as the basis for the graph’s Fourier transform. First, x is transformed into
124   the spectral domain using U T x. Then, it is multiplied by gθ , a filter defined in the spectral domain.
125
      Finally, the inverse Fourier transform is applied by multiplying with U , transforming the filtered
      signal back into the original graph domain. The primary limitation of spectral GCNs is the need to
126
      compute the eigenvectors U of the graph Laplacian. This requires the entire Laplacian matrix to be
127
      available, making the approach unsuitable for incorporating new nodes into the graph. Moreover,
128   for cold-start nodes, recomputing the Laplacian eigenvectors is not only computationally expensive
129   but also futile. Since the purpose of the Laplacian in GCNs is to enable convolution over connected
130   nodes, nodes without any connections will not benefit from this operation. Making spectral-GCNs
131   unsuitable for the generalization of cold-start nodes.
132
133   Graph Transformers. Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017), originally developed for natural lan-
134   guage processing, have been successfully adapted to handle graph data. A key development in this
135   area is the Graph Transformer (Dwivedi & Bresson, 2020), which extends the transformer architec-
136   ture to graph structures. This approach incorporates spectral embeddings for positional encoding,
137
      enabling the model to capture the relative positions of nodes within a graph.
138   However, standard transformer architectures face scalability issues when applied to large graphs due
139   to the high computational complexity of the attention mechanism. This challenge has highlighted
140   the need for more efficient methods that can handle large-scale graph data while maintaining the ad-
141   vantages of transformer-based models. State-of-the-art methods such as NAGphormer (Chen et al.,
142   2022) and VCR-Graphormer (Fu et al., 2024) address this by creating token lists for each node,
143
      which aggregate key information from their neighborhoods. By focusing only on the most relevant
      data for each node, these approaches significantly reduce the computational load of the attention
144
      mechanism. While these methods excel in performance and scalability, they rely on adjacency in-
145
      formation during inference, limiting their applicability in real-world environments where new nodes
146   lack initial connections. Our work builds on these advancements by introducing a generalizable so-
147   lution that supports cold-start nodes, enabling predictions without requiring adjacency information.

      4     G-SPARC
151
      In this section, we present the proposed G-SPARC framework in detail. To address the cold-start
152
      problem in graph learning, we begin by introducing our generalizable spectral embedding frame-
153
      work. Sections 4.2 and 4.3 then demonstrate how we adapt state-of-the-art architectures to handle
154   cold-start nodes by integrating our generalizable spectral embeddings for the node classification
155   task. Finally, we highlight additional applications of our approach for other graph-based down-
156   stream tasks.

158   4.1   SPARC - G ENERALIZABLE E MBEDDING
159
160   It is reasonable to assume that graph data is drawn from an unknown manifold M, with the graph
161   G serving as a discrete approximation of this manifold. Spectral embeddings map the manifold
      M by computing the first k eigenvectors of the graph’s Laplacian matrix. These eigenvectors

                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      (u1 , u2 , . . . , uk ), corresponding to the smallest eigenvalues, offer a low-dimensional representation
163   of the graph. In this space, Euclidean distances approximate the diffusion distance of the underlying
164   manifold M (Lafon et al., 2006).
165
166
      Cold-start nodes—those without any existing connections in the graph—still lie on the manifold M
      despite their missing adjacency information. Let vcold represent a cold-start node. While identifying
167
      neighbors based solely on the graph structure is impossible for such nodes, one straightforward
168
      approach is to estimate their neighborhood purely by feature similarity. However, this naive method
169   fails to capture the intrinsic geometry of the graph. As shown in Section 5.1, the feature space does
170   not naturally align with the graph’s manifold, resulting in suboptimal performance.
171
172
      We address the cold-start challenge by introducing a parametric mapping, Fθ : Rd → Rk , which ap-
      proximates the first k eigenfunctions of the Laplace-Beltrami operator, thereby forming the spectral
173
      embeddings of the nodes. This mapping takes node features as input and projects cold-start nodes
174
      onto the graph’s manifold, enabling the identification of relevant neighbors through Euclidean dis-
175   tance in the spectral embedding space.
176
177   Crucially, the graph structure is only used during training. This enables the learned spectral embed-
178
      ding to generalize to cold-start nodes. During inference, for cold-start nodes with only node features,
      we compute approximate spectral embeddings. This allows us to identify their nearest neighbors in
179
      the graph spectral space (see Figure 1). This generalizable spectral embedding framework enables
180
      cold-start nodes to be integrated into various downstream tasks, such as node classification and clus-
181   tering, even in the absence of adjacency information. In this paper, we incorporate our generalizable
182   spectral embedding into two state-of-the-art architectures for node classification and demonstrate its
183   straightforward application to link prediction and node clustering. This approach enables accurate
184   predictions for cold-start nodes across these tasks, even in the absence of adjacency information.

      4.2   SPARC-GCN
206
207   Spectral-GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2016b) applies graph convolutional networks in the spectral domain,
208   utilizing spectral embeddings to perform convolution-like operations on graph-structured data. The
209   convolution is formally defined as:
210                                           Xl+1 = U gϕ U T Xl
211
212
      Where U denotes the matrix of eigenvectors of the graph Laplacian, gϕ is the spectral filter, and X
      represents the nodes’ features. This method enables effective feature aggregation across the graph.
213
214   A significant limitation arises for cold-start nodes. For a cold-start node v with features x, traditional
215   convolution becomes impossible. Furthermore, the computing of U with cold start nodes is futile as
      the convolution applies only to connected nodes.

217   replace the spectral embedding U with the output of a parametric map Fθ denoted as Uθ , a function
218   on the feature space. Our modified convolution is defined as:

221

      Where Uθ (X) is the approximated spectral embedding using features X, and gϕ is a learnable diago-

227
      the cold-start node, denoted as Uθ (x). (2) Perform convolution over the new set X̂ = [X; x] that

      Clustering and Mini-Batching. When applying Euclidean methods like k-means on spectral em-
      beddings to partition the graph C = k-means(Fθ (X), k) —a process known as spectral cluster-
280
      ing—nodes are partitioned into k clusters with small intra-cluster diffusion distances and large
281
      inter-cluster distances. This technique effectively partitions the graph into sub-graphs, which of-
282   fer valuable insights into the graph’s structure. The partitioning retains essential, close connections
283   between nodes, while minimizing the loss of less significant, distant connections, making it ideal
284   for tasks that require dividing the graph into meaningful substructures while preserving as much
285   information as possible.
286
      We found that spectral partitioning is particularly useful for mini-batching in GCNs, where maintain-
287
      ing local neighborhoods and graph structure during training is essential. GCNs rely on aggregating
288   information from neighboring nodes within each batch, and splitting important connections across
289   different mini-batches can hinder the network’s ability to learn meaningful node representations.
290   Spectral partitioning overcomes this issue by clustering highly connected nodes into the same mini-
291   batch, preserving local structural information and improving the efficiency of the learning process.

      Most graph-based methods utilize both node features and graph structure to make node-level predic-
455
      tions. The SPARC embedding, developed to address cold-start nodes, approximates the eigenvectors
456
      of the graph Laplacian captures only the graph structure. However, incorporating node affinities, de-
457   noted as W , introduces a distinct form of a Laplacian that for some tasks may be beneficial.
458
459   Similarity solely on the feature space in graphs often fails to capture the true underlying structure,
460
      leading to misleading results when used for graph analysis tasks. While node features can reflect
      shared characteristics or common properties, they do not necessarily correspond to the actual con-
461
      nectivity patterns within the graph. This arises because features may be unrelated to the graph
462
      topology, resulting in nodes with high feature similarity that are not directly linked or nodes that are
463   closely connected but have divergent features. Consequently, relying on feature similarities alone
464   can obscure critical structural information, undermining the effectiveness of algorithms that depend
465   on understanding the graph’s inherent organization. Therefore, integrating structural information is
466   essential to accurately capture the relationships and dependencies in graph-based models.

519
      In this work, we introduced G-SPARC, a novel spectral-based method designed to address the cold-
520
      start problem in graph learning. Our approach effectively integrates cold-start nodes into state-of-
521   the-art graph learning methods, enabling accurate predictions for cold-start nodes. Experimental
522   results demonstrate that G-SPARC outperforms existing models in handling cold-start nodes, pro-
523   viding a solution for real-world applications where new nodes frequently appear. The adaptability
524   of our method allows it to be seamlessly integrated into existing and future graph learning frame-
525   works, enhancing their capability to manage evolving graphs. In this paper, we introduced two novel
526   adaptations, SPARC-GCN and SPARCphormer, to existing state-of-the-art methods, enabling them
527   to effectively manage cold-start nodes and making them suitable for real-world applications.
528   A limitation of our method is its dependency on the meaningful node features. G-SPARC may
529   not perform optimally if the node features are random or lack any relationship with the graph’s
530   manifold. However, it is generally safe to assume that in practical scenarios, the features will have
531   some correlation with the graph structure, which supports the applicability of our method.
532
      Our research has primarily focused on the common real-world scenario of homophilous graphs,
533   where the graph structure plays a crucial role in node-level predictions. We also explored the fea-
534   ture weighting factor, which becomes particularly significant in heterophilous graphs where node
535   features drive the predictions. Moving forward, we plan to continue our research to include het-
536   erophilous graphs, aiming to adapt and refine our approaches.

682   The generalization process borrows key ideas from SpectralNet (Shaham et al., 2018) and spectral
683   clustering to achieve a scalable and generalizable method for the first k eigenvectors of the graph
684   Laplacian. A key idea in spectral clustering is that embedding of the first k eigenvectors (where
685
      k << n) captures the most significant variations in the graph structure.
686   We computes the Laplacian matrix for a mini-batch using the graph adjacencies to find the paramet-
687   ric map using a neural network with orthogonal enforcement in the last layer. The training process is
688   in a coordinated descent fashion, where we alternate between orthogonalization and gradient steps.
689   Each of these steps uses a different mini-batch (possibly of different sizes), sampled from the train-
690   ing set X.

      Spectral eigendecompositions are typically performed solely on the graph structure, oblivious to any
736   additional information associated with the graph. However, in most deep-learning models, differ-
737   ent Laplacian matrices can yield better results for specific tasks to enhance unsupervised spectral
738   embeddings. In our study, we explored two key approaches:
739
740   K-Power Random Walk. The k-power random walk method captures context from the k-hop
741   neighbors within a graph by leveraging the k-power of the normalized adjacency matrix. The key
742   idea lies in summing up these normalized matrices for each step, resulting in a new matrix de-
                         Pk
743   noted as Ak-power = i normalized(Ai ), where A is the graph adjacency matrix and normalization
744   defined as D−1/2 AD−1/2 . This operation effectively simulates multiple convolutions, leading to
745   smoother graphs with tighter clusters. In essence, the k-power random walk bridges local and global
746   information, enhancing the expressive power of the graph.
747
      Figure 8b evaluates the effect of increasing the power k of the adjacency matrix, which integrates
748   increasingly distant neighborhood information into the graph representation. Initially, as k in-
749   creases, performance improves, reflecting the benefits of incorporating broader contextual infor-
750   mation. However, beyond a certain point, further increases in k lead to performance degradation,
751   evident from the sharp declines for all datasets at higher k values. This phenomenon, known as
752   oversmoothing, occurs because the node representations begin to lose their distinctive characteris-
753   tics, converging to a similar state that dilutes the useful signals for the learning tasks.
754
755   Features-Edges View. In graph-based data, we construct simulated graphs with both node features
      and connections. In the context of node clustering, nodes may have defining features that indicate
