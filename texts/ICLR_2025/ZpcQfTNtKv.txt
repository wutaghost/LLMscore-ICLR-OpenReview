009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            An established failure mode for machine learning models occurs when the same
012            features are equally likely to belong to class 0 and class 1. In such cases, existing
013            ML models cannot correctly classify the sample. However, a solvable case emerges
014            when the probabilities of class 0 and 1 vary with the context distribution. To the
015            best of our knowledge, standard neural network architectures like MLPs or CNNs
016            are not equipped to handle this.
017            In this article, we propose a simple activation function, quantile activation (QACT),
018            that addresses this problem without significantly increasing computational costs.
019            The core idea is to “adapt” the outputs of each neuron to its context distribution.
020
               The proposed quantile activation, QACT, produces the relative quantile of the
               sample in its context distribution, rather than the actual values, as in traditional
021
               networks.
022
               A practical example where the same sample can have different labels arises in
023
               cases of inherent distribution shift. We validate the proposed activation func-
024
               tion under such shifts, using datasets designed to test robustness against distor-
025            tions—CIFAR10C, CIFAR100C, MNISTC, TinyImagenetC. Our results demon-
026            strate significantly better generalization across distortions compared to conventional
027            classifiers, across various architectures. Although this paper presents a proof of
028            concept, we find that this approach unexpectedly outperforms DINOv2 (small)
029            under large distortions, despite DINOv2 being trained with a much larger network
030            and dataset.

      Thanks to deep learning approaches, machine learning has been adopted across wide variety of
      domains. However, there is a significant failure mode within the standard framework of machine
036
      learning – A specific sample can potentially be labelled 0 or 1 depending on the context. Hence,
037
      standard ML models cannot handle these conflicts effectively.

      Fixing the failure mode and defining the “context”: The only way to fix the above failure
      mode is to infer the µ1 , µ2 from the batch of samples. We define context as the batch of samples
083
      according to which each sample is processed. To our knowledge current neural network architectures
084
      such as MLP/CNN do not consider this. While transformers considers this to some extent via the
085   self-attention module, it is still sample specific and is very expensive computationally to obtain
086   self-attention for the entire batch. Moreover, vision transformers do not consider attention across
087   different samples in the batch.
088
089   Quantile Activation can identify the context distribution from each batch: To rectify this
090   shortcoming, we propose quantile activation, QACT, in this article. The key idea is that – at each
091   neuron, the final activation value depends on the activations of the entire batch of samples. Specifically,
092   we use the relative quantile of the pre-activation 1 with respect to other pre-activations in the batch.
093   (Details in section 2). Figure 1b shows that this simple change can allow the neural network to learn
094   in spite of the contradictory labels.

097   distribution shift is a manifestation of the failure mode discussed above. For instance consider the
098
      above distribution (figure 1a), where, if the pair of Gaussian distributions are rotated by 30◦ (a.k.a
      distribution shift), the class 0 and class 1 overlap. We validate the proposed quantile activation on the
099
      standard distribution shift dataset - CIFAR10C. Figure 2 illustrates the results obtained using ReLU
100
      activations and QACT. As severity increases (w.r.t Gaussian Noise), we observe that ReLU activation
101   loses the class structure. On the other hand, the proposed QACT framework does not suffer from this
102   and the class structure is preserved with QACT.

176   Rethinking Outputs from a Neuron: To recall – if x denotes the input, a typical neuron does the
177   following – (i) Applies a linear transformation with parameters w, b, giving wt x + b as the output,
178   and (ii) applies a rectifier g, returning g(wt x + b). Typically, g is taken to be the ReLU activation -
179   grelu (x) = max(0, x). Intuitively, we expect that each neuron captures an “abstract” feature, usually
180   not understood by a human observer.
181   An alternate way to model a neuron is to consider it as predicting a latent variable y, where y = 1
182   if the feature is present and y = 0 if the feature is absent. Mathematically, we have the following
183   model:
184                                  z = wt x + b + ϵ and y = I[z ≥ 0]                                 (2)
185
      This is very similar to the standard latent variable model for logistic regression, with the main
186
      exception being, the outputs y are not known for each neuron beforehand. If y is known, it is rather
187
      easy to obtain the probabilities – P (z ≥ 0). Can we still predict the probabilities, even when y itself
188   is a latent variable?
189

193         2. Assign y = 1 whenever z > (1 − τ )th quantile of z, and 0 otherwise. For a specific sample,
194            we have yi = 1 if zi > (1 − τ )th quantile of {zi }
195         3. Fit the model Q(x, τ ; θ) to the dataset {((xi , τ ), yi )}, and estimate the probability as,

322   In the previous section, we described the procedure to adapt a single neuron to its context distribution.
323   In this section we discuss how this extends to the Dense/Convolution layers, the loss functions to
      train the network and the inference aspect.

      Extending to standard layers: The extension of equation 4 to dense outputs is straightforward.
325   A typical output of the dense layer would be of the shape (B, Nc ) - B denotes the batch size, Nc
326   denotes the width of the network. The principle is - The context distribution of a neuron is all the
327   values which are obtained using the same parameters. In this case, each of the values across the ‘B’
328   dimension are considered to be samples from the context distribution.
329
      For a convolution layer, the typical outputs are of the form - (B, Nc , H, W ) - B denotes the size of
330
      the batch, Nc denotes the number of channels, H, W denotes the sizes of the images. In this case we
331   should consider all values across the 1st,3rd and 4th dimension to be from the context distribution,
332   since all these values are obtained using the same parameters. So, the number of samples would be
333   B × H × W.
334
335   Loss Functions: One can use any differentiable loss function to train with quantile activation.
336   We specifically experiment with the standard Cross-Entropy Loss, Triplet Loss, and the recently
337   proposed Watershed Loss in (Challa et al., 2024) (see section 4). However, if one requires that the
338   boundaries between classes adapt to the distribution, then learning similarities instead of boundaries
339   can be beneficial. Both Triplet Loss and Watershed Loss fall into this category. We see that learning
340   similarities does have slight benefits when considering the embedding quality.
341
342   Inference with QACT: As stated before, we want to assign a label for classification based on the
343   context of the sample. There exist two approaches for this – (1) One way is to keep track of the
344   quantiles and the estimated densities for all neurons and use it for inference. This allows inference
345
      for a single sample in the traditional sense. However, this also implies that one would not be able
      to assign classes based on the context at evaluation. (2) Another way is to make sure that, even for
346
      inference on a single sample, we include several samples from the context distribution, but only use
347
      the output for a specific sample. This allows one to assign classes based on the context. In this article,
348   we follow the latter approach.
349
350
      Quantile Classifier: Observe that the proposed QACT (without normalization) returns the values in
351
      [0, 1] which can be interpreted as probabilities. Hence, one can also use this for the classification layer.
352   Nonetheless, two changes are required – (i) Traditional softmax used in conjunction with negative-
353   log-likelihood loss already considers “relative” activations of the classification in normalization.
354   However, QACT does not. Hence, one should use Binary-Cross-Entropy loss with QACT, which
355   amounts to one-vs-rest classification. (ii) Also, unlike a neuron in the middle layers, the bias of the
356   neuron in the classification layer depends on the class imbalance. For instance, with 10 classes, one
357   would have only 1/10 of the samples labelled 1 and 9/10 of the samples labelled 0. To address this,
358   we require that the median of the outputs be at 0.9, and hence weight the positive class with 0.9 and
359   the negative class with 0.1 respectively. In this article, whenever QACT is used, we use this approach
360
      for inference.
361   We observe that (figures 14 and 15) using quantile classifier on the learned features in general
362   improves the consistency of the calibration error and also leads to the reducing the calibration error.
363   In this article, for all networks trained with quantile activation, we use quantile classifier to compute
364   the accuracy/calibration errors.

368   To summarize, we make the following changes to the existing classification pipeline – (i) Replace
369   the usual ReLU activation with QACT and (ii) Use triplet or watershed loss instead of standard
370   cross-entropy loss. We expect this framework to learn context dependent features, and hence be
371   robust to distortions. (iii) Also, use quantile classifier to train the classifier on the embedding for
372   better calibrated probabilities.

415   Calibration error measures the reliability of predicted probabilities. In simple words, if one predicts
416   100 samples with (say) probability 0.7, then we expect 70 of the samples to belong to class 1 and
417   the rest to class 0. This is measured using either the marginal or top-label calibration error. We refer
418   the reader to (Kumar et al., 2019) for details, which also provides an implementation to estimate the
419
      calibration error.

      otherwise stated, for training. For inference, we train the Quantile Classifier on the train embedding

      5    C ONCLUSION A ND F UTURE W ORK
487
488
      To summarize, traditional classification systems do not consider the “context distributions” when
489   assigning labels. In this article, we propose a framework to achieve this by – (i) Making the activation
490   adaptive by using quantiles and (ii) Learning a kernel instead of the boundary for the last layer. We
491   show that our method is more robust to distortions by considering MNISTC, CIFAR10C, CIFAR100C,
492   TinyImagenetC datasets across varying architectures.
493
      The scope of this article is to provide a proof of concept and a framework for performing inference in
494
      a context-dependent manner. We outline several potential directions for future research:
495
496         I. The key idea in our proposed approach is that the quantiles capture the distribution of each
497            neuron from the batch of samples, providing outputs accordingly. This poses a challenge for
498
               large datasets, and we have discussed two potential solutions: (i) remember the quantiles
               and density estimates for single sample evaluation, or (ii) ensure that a batch of samples
499
               from the same distribution is processed together. We adopt the latter method in this article.
500
               An alternative approach would be to learn the distribution of each neuron using auxiliary
501            loss functions, adjusting these distributions to fit the domain at test time. This gives us more
502            control over the network at test time compared to current workflows. If the networks are very
503            large, where batch sizes cannot be big – there exists several strategies such as checkpointing
504            to implicitly increase the batch size.
505        II. Since the aim of the article was to establish a proof-of-concept, we did not focus on scaling,
506            and use only a single GPU for all the experiments. To extend it to multi-GPU training,
507            one needs to synchronize the quantiles across GPU, in a similar manner as that for Batch-
508            Normalization. We expect this to improve the statistics, and to allow considerably larger
509
               batches of training.
          III. On the theoretical side, there is an interesting analogy between our quantile activation and
510
               how a biological neuron behaves. It is known that when the inputs to a biological neuron
511
               change, the neuron adapts to these changes (Clifford et al., 2007). Quantile activation does
512            something very similar, which leads to an open question – can we establish a formal link
513            between the adaptability of a biological neuron and the accuracy of classification systems?
514       IV. Another theoretical direction to explore involves considering distributions not just at the
515            neuron level, but at the layer level, introducing a high-dimensional aspect to the problem.
516            The main challenge here is defining and utilizing high dimensional quantiles, which remains
517            an open question (Koenker, 2005).

987    D    WATERSHED L OSS
988
989    The authors in (Challa et al., 2024) proposed a novel classifier – watershed classifier, which works by
990    learning similarities instead of the boundaries. Below we give the brief idea of the loss function, and
991
       refer the reader to the original paper for further details.
992          1. Let (xi , yi ) denote the samples in each batch, and let fθ denote the embedding network.
993             fθ (xi ) denotes the corresponding embedding.
994          2. Starting from randomly selected seeds in the batch, propagate the labels to all the samples.

       Why Watershed Loss?: Observe that the loss in equation 11 implicitly learns representations
       consistent with the RBF kernel, which is known to be translation invariant. Minimizing this loss
1008
       function, hence, will learn translation invariant kernels. This is important for obtaining networks
1009

1012   linearly separable. Contrast this with watershed, which instead learns a similarity between two points
