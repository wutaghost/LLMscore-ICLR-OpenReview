030                                                A BSTRACT
031
032            We empirically study the scaling properties of various Diffusion Transformers
033            (DiTs) for text-to-image generation by performing extensive and rigorous ablations,
034
               including training scaled DiTs ranging from 0.3B upto 8B parameters on datasets
               up to 600M images. We find that U-ViT, a pure self-attention based DiT model
035
               provides a simpler design and scales more effectively in comparison with cross-
036
               attention based DiT variants, which allows straightforward expansion for extra
037            conditions and other modalities. We identify a 2.3B U-ViT model can get better
038            performance than SDXL UNet and other DiT variants in controlled setting. On the
039            data scaling side, we investigate how increasing dataset size and enhanced long
040            caption improve the text-image alignment performance and the learning efficiency.
041
042
043   1    I NTRODUCTION
044
045   Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017)’s straightforward design and ability to scale efficiently has driven
046   significant advancements in large language models (LLMs) (Kaplan et al., 2020). Its inherent
047   simplicity and ease of parallelization makes it well-suited for hardware acceleration. Diffusion
048   Transformers (DiTs) (Peebles & Xie, 2023; Bao et al., 2023) initially replaces UNet with transformers
049   for diffusion-based image generation results in the proposal of numerous variants (Chen et al., 2024b;
050
      Esser et al., 2024b; Crowson et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b) and has since successfully
      expanded into video generation (Brooks et al., 2024).
051
052   Despite the rapid evolution of DiT models, a comprehensive comparison between various DiT
053   architectures and UNet-based models for text-to-image generation (T2I) is still lacking. The impact
      of model design on DiT’s ability to accurately translate text descriptions into images (text-to-image


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      alignment) remains unclear. Furthermore, the optimal scaling strategy for transformer models in T2I
055   tasks compared to UNet is yet to be determined. The challenge of establishing a fair comparison is
056   further compounded by the variation in training settings and the significant computational resources
057   required to train these models.
058
      In this work, we empirically study the scaling properties of several representative DiT architectures
059
      for T2I by performing rigors ablations, including training scaled DiTs ranging from 0.3B to 8B
060
      parameters on datasets up to 600M images in controlled settings. Specifically, we ablate and scale
061   three DiT variants, i.e., PixArt-α (Chen et al., 2024b), LargeDiT (Gao et al., 2024), and U-ViT (Bao
062   et al., 2023). We train them from scratch on large-scale datasets without using pre-trained DiT
063   initialization or ImageNet pre-training. All DiT variants are trained in a controlled setting with
064   the same autoencoder, text encoder and training settings for fair comparison. We find that U-ViT’s
065   simpler architecture design facilitates efficient model scaling and supports image editing by simply
066   expanding condition tokens. Finally, we explore the impact of dataset scaling, considering both
067   dataset size and caption density. The main contributions of our work include:
068
069
      • We compare the architecture design of three text conditioned DiT models including scaled PixArt-α,
        LargeDiT and U-ViT variants in controlled settings, allowing a fair comparison of recent DiT
070
        variants for real-world text-to-image generation.
071
072   • We scale the three DiT variants along depth and width dimensions and verify their scalability with
073     model size as large as 8B. We find that the U-ViT architecture, a full self-attention based ViT with
074
        skip connections, has competitive performance with other DiT designs. The scaled 2.3B U-ViT can
        outperform SDXL’s UNet and much larger PixArt-α and LargeDiTs.
075
076   • We verified that the full self-attention design of U-ViT allows training image editing model
077     by simply concatenating masks or condition image as condition tokens, which shows better
078     performance than traditional channel concatenation approach.
079   • We examined why long caption enhancement and dataset scaling help to improve the text-image
080     alignment performance. We find captions with higher information density can yield better text-
081     image alignment performance.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
084
085
      Transformers for T2I U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015) was the de facto standard backbone for
086
      diffusion based image generation since (Ho et al., 2020) and is widely used in text-to-image models
087   including LDM (Rombach et al., 2021), SDXL (Podell et al., 2023), DALL-E (Ramesh et al., 2022)
088   and Imagen (Saharia et al., 2022). U-ViT (Bao et al., 2023) treats all inputs including the time,
089   condition and noisy image patches as tokens and employs ViT equipped with long skip connections
090   between shallow and deep layers, suggesting the long skip connection is crucial while the downsam-
091   pling and up-sampling operators in CNN-based U-Net are not always necessary. DiT (Peebles & Xie,
092   2023) replaces U-Net with Transformers for class-conditioned image generation and identify there
093   is a strong correlation between the network complexity and sample quality. PixArt-α (Chen et al.,
094   2024b) extends DiT (Peebles & Xie, 2023) for text-conditioned image generation by initializing from
095
      DiT pre-trained weights. PixArt-Σ (Chen et al., 2024a) upgrades PixArt-α with stronger VAE, larger
      dataset and longer text token limit. It introduces token compression to support 4K image generation.
096
      Those DiT variants are mostly around 0.6B and focus on showing comparable results with U-Net.
097
098
099
      Scaling DiTs HourglassDiT (Crowson et al., 2024) introduces hierarchical design with down/up
      sampling in DiT and reduces the computation complexity for high resolution image generation.
100
      SD3 (Esser et al., 2024b) presents a transformer-based architecture that uses separate weights for
101
      the image and text modalities and enables a bidirectional flow of information between image and
102   text tokens. They parameterize the size of the model in terms of the model’s depth and scale up
103   the backbone to 8B. Large-DiT (Gao et al., 2024) incorporates LLaMA’s text embedding (Touvron
104   et al., 2023) and scales the DiT backbone. Specifically, they modify the causal attention of LLaMA
105   to a bidirectional attention mechanism. They normalize the key and query within the attention
106   mechanism. They show scaling-up parameters up to 7B can improve the convergence speed. They
107   further extend it for generating multiple modalities in flow-based Lumnia-T2X (Gao et al., 2024) and
      Lumina-Next (Zhuo et al., 2024).

122   We ablate and scale three DiT variants, i.e., PixArt-α (Chen et al., 2024b), LargeDiT (Gao et al.,

128   3.1                                      A BLATION S ETTINGS
129
      Model Design Space We ablate the transformer-based diffusion model design in the following
130
      dimensions: 1) hidden dimension h: we scale it from 1024 to 3072. 2) transformer depth d: we scale
131
      the transformer blocks from 28 to 80. 3) number of heads n, we keep it fixed to 16 or 32.
132
133
      Training Settings We mainly train models and perform ablations on our curated dataset LensArt,
134
      which contains 250M text-image pairs. For additional data scaling experiment in later sections, we
135
      also use our curated dataset SSTK, which contains 350M text-image pairs. We train all models at
136   256×256 resolution with batch size 2048 up to 600K steps. We follow the setup of LDM (Rombach
137   et al., 2021) for DDPM schedules. We use AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) optimizer with 10K
138   steps warmup and then constant learning rate 8e−5 . We employ mixed precision training with BF16
139   precision and enable FSDP (Zhao et al., 2023) for large models.
140
141   Evaluation We use the DDIM sampler (Song et al., 2020) in 50 steps with fixed seed and CFG
142   scale (7.5) for inference. We follow the setting of (Li et al., 2024a) for evaluation on composition
143   ability and image quality with: 1) TIFA (Hu et al., 2023) measures the faithfulness of a generated
144   image to its text input via VQA. It contains 4K collected prompts and corresponding question-answer
145   pairs generated by a language model. Image faithfulness is calculated by checking whether existing
146   VQA models can answer these questions using the generated image. 2) ImageReward (Xu et al.,
147   2023) was learned to approximates human preference. We calculate the average ImageReward score
148
      over images generated with sampled 10K MSCOCO (Lin et al., 2014) prompts. More evaluation
      metrics can be found in Appendix D.

160   ImageNet pre-training tends to generate distorted images in comparison to models initialized from
161   pre-trained DiT weights, which is trained 7M steps on ImageNet. Though Chen et al. (2024b)
      proves that U-Net is not a must for diffusion models, PixArt-α variants do take longer iterations and

      4               A BLATING U-V I T S
271
272
      To understand why scaled U-ViT outperforms U-Net, PixArt-α and LargeDiT variants, we first
273   analyze the design of U-Net and U-ViT, and then ablate the effect of text encoder fine-tuning.
274
275
      4.1                  C OMPARING U-N ET AND U-V I T

277   The major difference between U-Net and U-ViT lies at that: 1) U-Net has down/up sampling layers,

360   5.2                   S CALING CONDITION TOKENS FOR IMAGE EDITING
361
362   In image generation, conditioning can be applied using both global (e.g., text embeddings, CLIP
363
      image embeddings) and local factors (e.g., edge maps, masks). Recent works (Zhao et al., 2024;
      Ye et al., 2023) have trained models that handle multiple conditions simultaneously; however, these
364
      models differentiate between global and local conditions in their handling. Global conditions are
365
      typically cross-attended by the noise latents, while local conditions are concatenated with them. This
366   distinction is often a result of the limitations imposed by widely used diffusion backbones like UNet
367   and DiTs. In this section, we show that we can adapt U-ViT to any new condition by just scaling up
368   the tokens - we tokenize the condition and concatenate it with noise and text tokens, without having
369   to incorporate any specialized logic to handle different types of conditions. In contrast to methods
370   that concatenate the condition with noise latents along the channel dimension, our approach does
371   not require the local condition to have the same resolution as the noise latents. We use 2.3B U-ViT
372   pretrained on 1K resolution data for all experiments in this section.

376   is trained in the standard manner, optimizing the L2 loss. We use the same datasets as those for
377   text-to-image training, generating masks randomly. We train inpainting models using both methods
      and evaluate them on ImageReward (for image fidelity) and a modified version of TIFA metric (for

517
518   Since both scaling up long caption and data size can improve text-image alignment, we aspire to
519
      unertand the common factor contributing to the improvement. We hypothesize that both LensArt-long
      and SSTK captions provide more information that can be measured by TIFA. TIFA leverages VQA
520
      to answer questions on generated images and measures answer accuracy. Specifically, given an image
521
      caption, several pairs of question and answers are generated. Each question corresponds to one
522   element in the caption. Each element belongs to a type (e.g., surfer belongs to the animal/human
523   element type). TIFA comprehensively evaluates the following element types: animal/human, object,
524   location, activity, color, spatial, attribute, food, counting, material, shape, other. Based on element
525   phrases (note that each element can contain more than one English word) in TIFA, we do phrase
526   matching for (1) LensArt original caption, (2) LensArt long caption, and (3) SSTK captions. In
527   Fig. 14, we show the percentage of captions with matched element phrases in each element type.
528   The results show that both LensArt long captions and SSTK have higher percentage of captions with
529   matched TIFA element phrases compared to LensArt caption in almost all TIFA element types, which
530
      explains the relative performance difference in Fig. 12. Thus, we conclude that captions with higher
      information density (i.e., not necessarily long caption length) is key to improve text-image alignment.

535   We performed large-scale ablation of DiT variants and showed the potential scaling ability of different
536   backbone designs. We observe that the architecture like U-ViT that self attends on both condition

      C.1    B ENCHMARKS
738
739   We outline the evaluation benchmarks used to assess the performance of our image inpainting and
740   canny edge conditioning models.
741
742   TIFA TIFA benchmark has a set of prompts, and a set of question-answer pairs associated with
743   each prompt. Given a prompt, the generated image is processed by a VQA model and the answers
744   generated by it are matched against GT answers to assign a score. The scores for 4,081 prompts are
745   averaged to obtain the final score.
746
747
              • TIFA for image inpainting: Unlike text-to-image (T2I), the image inpainting task requires
                a ground truth image, a mask, and a text prompt as input. Out of the 4,081 text prompts in
748
                TIFA benchmark, 2000 are taken from the MSCOCO dataset (Lin et al., 2014), and have
749
                a corresponding image and bounding boxes associated with it. We evaluate the inpainting
750             model on this subset of TIFA benchmark. We will refer to this modified metric as TIFA-
751             COCO. For each prompt, we select the bounding box with largest area and convert it to a
752             mask. Given this input, image inpainting model generates an output which is processed
753             by the VQA to get a TIFA score. An inpainting model that can keep the unmasked details
754             preserved while generating the masked details reliably will have a higher TIFA score, since
755             the images generated by it will answer most questions correctly. From our findings, we
                observe that TIFA is a good metric for text-image alignment for image inpainting.


                                                       14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
              • TIFA for canny edge conditioned generation: Similar to inpainting, we evaluate canny
757             edge conditioned model on the 2000 MSCOCO images part of the TIFA benchmark. We
758             extract the edges from these images, and generate outputs, which are then processed by
759             VQA to get a TIFA score. We will refer to this modified metric as TIFA-COCO.
760
761   BrushBench BrushBench has 600 images with corresponding inside-inpainting and outside-
762   inpainting masks. The authors of BreshBench propose evaluating image inpainting on following
763   metrics:
764
765           • Image Generation Quality: ImageReward (IR) (Xu et al., 2023), HPS v2 (HPS) (Wu
766             et al., 2023), and Aesthetic Score (AS) (Schuhmann et al., 2022) as they align with human
767
                perception.
768           • Masked Region Preservation: Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR) (contributors, 2024),
769             Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity (LPIPS) (Zhang et al., 2018), and Mean Squared
770             Error (MSE) (Wikipedia contributors, 2024) in the unmasked region among the generated
771             image and the original image.
772           • Text Alignment: CLIP Similarity (CLIP Sim) (Wu et al., 2021) to evaluate text-image
773             consistency between the generated images and corresponding text prompts.
