009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The pretraining-finetuning paradigm has achieved remarkable success in natural
012            language processing and computer vision, becoming the dominant approach in
013            many downstream tasks. However, its application in the event camera domain has
014            encountered significant challenges. First, the scarcity and sparsity of large-scale
015            event datasets lead to issues like overfitting during extensive pretraining. Second,
016
               event data inherently contains both temporal and spatial information, making it dif-
               ficult to directly transfer knowledge from image-based pretraining to event camera
017
               tasks. In this paper, we propose a low-parameter-cost SpatioTemporal Information
018
               Fusion Prompting (STP) method to address these challenges. This method enables
019            bidirectional fusion of event and image data while mitigating the risk of overfitting.
020            Specifically, the key innovation lies in effectively integrating the spatio-temporal
021            information of event data to align with pre-trained image models and reduce the
022            impact of data sparsity. To achieve this, we designed an Overlap Patch Embedding
023            module within the STP, which employs wide receptive field to capture more local
024            information and reduce the influence of sparse regions. Additionally, we intro-
025            duce a Temporal Transformer that integrates both global and local information,
026            facilitating the fusion of temporal and spatial data. Our approach significantly
027
               outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods across multiple downstream tasks,
               including classification, semantic segmentation, and optical flow estimation. For in-
028
               stance, it achieves a top-1 accuracy of 68.83% on N-ImageNet with fewer trainable
029
               parameters. Our code is available in the Supplement.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Event cameras are dynamic vision sensors inspired by the perceptual mechanism of the human retinas
035   Lichtensteiner et al. (2008); Taverni et al. (2018); Schuman et al. (2022). They asynchronously capture
036   the event stream by comparing the intensity changes of each pixel Taverni et al. (2018); Gallego et al.
037   (2020); Brandli et al. (2014). These events are sorted as positive or negative depending on whether
038   the light intensity increases or decreases Lichtensteiner et al. (2008); Gallego et al. (2020); Chen &
039
      Guo (2019). This triggering mechanism enables event cameras to efficiently record information in
      high-dynamic-range (HDR, 120dB) or high-speed motion scenes, while offering advantages such as
040
      low power consumption and low redundancy Lichtensteiner et al. (2008). Currently, event cameras
041
      are widely used in novel computer vision and robotics tasks, including video interpolation Tulyakov
042   et al. (2022); Yu et al. (2021); Gao et al. (2022); Sun et al. (2023), image or video reconstruction
043   Rebecq et al. (2019); Paredes-Vallés & De Croon (2021); Munda et al. (2018); Simon Chane et al.
044   (2016), optical flow estimation Zhu & Yuan (2018); Lee et al. (2020), depth estimation Zhu et al.
045   (2019); Gallego et al. (2018), detection Ramesh & Yang (2020); Ramesh et al. (2020), and SLAM
046   Vidal et al. (2018); Mueggler et al. (2017); Jiao et al. (2021).
047
      However, due to the high cost of acquiring event camera data and the difficulty of labeling, there
048   is still a lack of pretrained models based on large-scale event camera datasets. This has hindered
049   the adoption of the pretraining-finetuning paradigm for event-based vision tasks and limited the
050   development of corresponding deep learning methods and models. Given the emergence of large-scale
051   RGB image datasets (e.g., ImageNet-21k Deng et al. (2009), JFT-300M Sun et al. (2017)) and the
052   development of pretrained models He et al. (2016); Dosovitskiy et al. (2020); Radford et al. (2021);
053   He et al. (2022) based on these datasets, researchers have attempted to use transfer learning Hu
      et al. (2020); Sun et al. (2022); Messikommer et al. (2022) or knowledge distillation Wang et al.

078   JFT-300M. Coupled with the sparsity of event data, this imbalance between the extensive number of
079   trainable parameters and the limited data can lead to overfitting in pretrained models.
080
      In this paper, we identify three key challenges for event camera data pretraining: (i). The need
081
      to account for both temporal and spatial information in event data. (ii). The need to handle the
082   sparsity and noise inherent in event data. (iii). The need for sufficient prior knowledge to mitigate the
083   imbalance between the large number of trainable parameters and the limited available data.
084
085
      To address these challenges, we propose a method that transfers knowledge from the image domain to
      the event data domain through appropriate prompting engineering. This approach not only aggregates
086
      the temporal and spatial information of event data and reduces the impact of sparsity, but also
087
      effectively transfers knowledge from the image domain to the event data domain, mitigating the issue
088   of overfitting. Specifically, we design a SpatioTemporal Information Fusion Prompting (STP) method,
089   which uses wide-receptive-field overlapping convolutions combined with a temporal transformer to
090   gradually fuse the temporal and spatial information of event streams. This reduces the impact of
091   data sparsity and produces a spatiotemporal representation of the event data. Then the representation
092   is fed into a pretrained image model, where the model’s weights are frozen to extract high-level
093   features from the event data for classification. Through end-to-end optimization, the image-domain
094   prior knowledge guides the training of the prompting module, enhancing spatiotemporal information
095   fusion and facilitating knowledge transfer. Finally, STP can be combined with the pretrained image
096
      model, forms a pretraining model for event camera data, which is finetuned together on downstream
      tasks. This enables bidirectional knowledge flow between event data and image data and generalizes
097
      well to a variety of downstream tasks.
098
099   In summary, our work makes the following contributions:
100
101              • We propose a novel event camera pretraining method based on prompt-tuning, which
102                facilitates bidirectional knowledge fusion between event data and image data through
103                spatiotemporal feature fusion prompting. This approach drives a new pre-training paradigm
104
                   for event-based vision tasks.
105              • We propose a representation that preserves both the spatial and temporal information of event
106                streams and introduce a Spatiotemporal Information Fusion Prompting method, specifically
107                designed to gradually integrate the spatial and temporal features of event data, effectively
                   addressing its unique characteristics.

      2.1   V ISUAL P RE - TRAINING
116
      With the rapid development of deep learning and computer vision, pretrained large models have
117   become an important topic and research method Hendrycks et al. (2019); Raghu et al. (2021); He et al.
118   (2019), driven by the continuous evolution of visual models. From the perspective of training, these
119   methods mainly include supervised pre-training on large-scale datasets Carreira & Zisserman (2017);
120   Dosovitskiy et al. (2020); Zhai et al. (2022); Dehghani et al. (2023), weakly supervised pre-training
121   requiring less data Berthelot et al. (2019); Pham et al. (2021); Xie et al. (2020); Zheng et al. (2021);
122   Ramanathan et al. (2021), and unsupervised pre-training that exploits intrinsic features of data for
123   learning without relying on any labels Bao et al. (2021); Chen et al. (2020a;b); He et al. (2022); Grill
124   et al. (2020). These models can efficiently transfer knowledge to downstream tasks through tuning.
125   In contrast to the rapid development of image-based pre-training, event-based pre-training is still in its
126   early stages. There are two main challenges in pre-training with event camera data: first, the difficulty
127   in acquiring event stream data and the lack of large-scale datasets; second, the sparsity of event
128   stream data, which easily leads to overfitting or training collapse during large-scale training. Previous
129   pre-training methods have been primarily relied on self-supervised learning. Yang et al. Yang et al.
130   (2023), were the first to propose a contrastive learning-based method for large-scale event camera data
131
      pre-training. Klenk et al. Klenk et al. (2024), drew inspiration from VQVAE’s discrete encoding Van
      Den Oord et al. (2017) and BERT’s masked reconstruction Devlin et al. (2018) to propose Masked
132
      Event Modeling (MEM). Huang et al. Huang et al. (2024), introduced an efficient self-supervised
133
      learning method based on voxel-based data, enabling rapid convergence with a small amount of
134   pre-training data. However, these methods are still limited by the lack of large-scale datasets and the
135   sparsity of event data. Additionally, they do not fully consider the temporal characteristics of event
136   data, which hinders the model’s ability to fully leverage its learning and representation capabilities.
137
138   2.2   P ROMPT T UNING
139
140   Prompt tuning is an important paradigm that leverages pretrained large models Lester et al. (2021);
141   Liu et al. (2023). As a lightweight tuning method, its principle is to adapt downstream tasks to the
142   original training task at minimal cost, thereby utilizing the knowledge embedded in pretrained models
143   to address problems. Prompting was initially introduced in natural language processing (NLP) Liu
144
      et al. (2023), where additional tokens are added to token sequences to help the pretrained model
      better ”understand” the task Li & Liang (2021); Lester et al. (2021). Initially, the values of prompt
145
      engineering were heuristically selected Brown et al. (2020). Subsequently, prompt methods based on
146
      learnable parameters gradually became mainstream due to their efficiency and flexibility Lester et al.
147   (2021); Li & Liang (2021); Liu et al. (2021a); Vu et al. (2021). Due to its simplicity and effectiveness,
148   prompt tuning has also been applied to some visual tasks such as image classification Bahng et al.
149   (2022); Jia et al. (2022), segmentation Nie et al. (2023), and 3D point clouds Wang et al. (2022);
150   Tang et al. (2024); Zhu et al. (2023). To the best of our knowledge, there has been no prior work
151   utilizing prompt tuning in the field of event cameras. We explore the use of prompt tuning for the first
152   time in the task of event camera data pre-training, aiming to achieve an efficient transfer of image
153   pre-training knowledge to event camera data.

191   3.2     E VENT R EPRESENTATION
192
193   Combining event streams with deep learning typically requires converting event streams into planar
194   representations. There are currently three main representation methods: Event Count Image (ECM)
195   Maqueda et al. (2018); Zhu & Yuan (2018), Voxel grid Zhu et al. (2019), and Event Spike Tensor
196   (EST) Gehrig et al. (2019), with their characteristics and descriptions detailed in Table 1. For
197   event camera pretraining, we aim for a representation that preserves as much of the event stream’s
198   characteristics and information as possible, without introducing additional information that could
199   affect its generalizability. However, as observed, none of the current mainstream methods fully retain
200
      all the key features of event streams, including temporal information, spatial count information, and
      event polarity, while avoiding the introduction of extraneous information.
201
202   To address this, we propose a novel representation method called the SpatioTemporal Event Count
203   Image (STECM), which incorporates temporal information into the existing ECM representation.
204   This effectively resolves the issue of discarding time information. Specifically, following the approach
205   of the Voxel grid, we divide the event stream into T temporal segments. For each segment of the
206
      event stream ET = {ek }nk=1 , where n is the number of events, each event ek is represented by a
      tuple (xi , yi , ti , pi ), where xi and yi denote the pixel coordinates, ti represents the timestamp of the
207
      event, and pi = ±1 represents the polarity. As event cameras asynchronously report changes in pixel
208
      intensity, the output is a series of independent events Brandli et al. (2014); Gallego et al. (2020). To
209   obtain a 2D image structure with visible edges, we accumulate events of different polarities separately
210   onto a 2D plane, resulting in a ECM ∈ R2×H×W . As illustrated in Figure 2, by concatenating these
211   T ECM, we obtain a temporal ECM representation (tECM) of the event stream. To preserve the
212   spatial characteristics, we also convert the entire event stream E into a single ECM and concatenate it
213   with the tECM, resulting in the ST ECM ∈ R2×(T +1)×H×W . This representation method retains the
214   complete spatial structure and effective temporal information of the event stream without introducing
215   additional information or constraints. As a result, it can be effectively transferred to a variety of
      downstream tasks.

226   3.3     S PATIOT EMPORAL I NFORMATION F USION P ROMPTING
227
228   The design of SpatioTemporal Information Fusion Prompting (STP) aims to achieve two key objec-
229   tives: (i) to compress and integrate the temporal information of event data into spatial information as
230   much as possible, allowing it to align with pretrained image models. Accorading to the characteristics
231   of event cameras, event streams from different time segments contain non-overlapping yet similar
232
      spatial information, providing a foundation for compressing them onto the same plane. (ii) When
      encoding local information into embeddings, a larger receptive field is needed to reduce sparse areas
233
      and avoid overfitting caused by sparsity. Additionally, we must adhere to the principles of prompt
234
      tuning by minimizing computational complexity and parameter counts.
235
236   Therefore, our proposed STP consists of two components: first, an Overlap Patch Embedding
237   module utilizing overlap convolution with a larger patch window, allowing adjacent patches to share
238
      information with the current patch’s embedding. This enhances local information exchange and
      effectively reduces sparse regions. Second, a Temporal Transformer facilitates the interaction and
239
      fusion of global and local temporal information. To further minimize computational costs while
240
      achieving gradual integration of spatial local information, we employ a pyramid structure design
241   based on pVIT Wang et al. (2021b). This approach progressively enlarges the patch’s receptive field
242   and continuously merges information across different temporal dimensions, ensuring thorough fusion
243   of spatiotemporal information.
244
245   3.3.1     OVERLAP PATCH E MBEDDING
246
247   In the Overlap Patch Embedding, the kernel size of the convolution must be larger than the stride
248   to utilize information from neighboring patches and fill in sparse regions within the current patch.
249
      Specifically, given an input event image with dimensions (BT ) × H × W × C, where B represents
      batch size, we apply a convolution layer with a stride of P and a kernel size K, where K>P . The
250
      size of the output feature map is (BT ) × H        W        2
                                                   P × P × CP . Next, we apply a 3 × 3 convolution to
251
      extract finer local details, while keeping the feature map size unchanged. Finally, the feature map is
252                                                  2
      reshaped to size B × H         W
                                P × P × T × CP and fed into the Temporal Transformer, which fuses
253   temporal information within each patch. The Overlap Patch Embedding is applied three times within
254   the STP, with the kernel size {k1 , k2 , k3 } being a set of hyperparameters. The strides are set to
255   {4, 2, 2}. By progressively increasing the receptive field, each patch is enriched with information
256   from neighboring patches, thereby enhancing its embedding representation and mitigating the risk of
257   overfitting due to sparsity.

      Pretraining. In the pretraining phase, we utilize ViT Dosovitskiy et al. (2020) as the backbone of

286
      the embedding has already been completed in the STP process. The embedding features ẑ ∈

296   adapt efficiently to various downstream tasks.

303   Pre-training Dataset. We utilize the N-ImageNet Kim et al. (2021) and ImageNet-1K Deng et al.

      (2009) datasets for pre-training. N-ImageNet is currently the largest dataset for event camera
      classification. It is constructed based on the ImageNet-1K dataset, capturing RGB images displayed
305
      on a monitor using a moving event camera. This dataset comprises 1,781,167 samples covering 1,000

      object categories. Each event data sample has a resolution of 480 × 640. To align with the pretraining

      during the pretraining phase. After pretraining, the STP and ViT classification models together form

      further implementation details and training hyperparameters, please refer to the Appendix A.1.

530   5     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we propose a Spatiotemporal Information Fusion Prompting (STP) method that effec-
533
      tively bridges the gap between event stream data and pretrained image models, facilitating efficient
534   knowledge transfer. Our approach progressively fuses the spatiotemporal information in event data
535   to generate representations compatible with image pretrained models, enabling seamless transfer
536   of knowledge from images to events. Additionally, by expanding the receptive field and enhancing
537   local information fusion, we address the sparsity issue inherent in event data, achieving bidirectional
538   knowledge transfer between event streams and images. Experimental results demonstrate the superi-
539   ority and potential of our pretraining method, offering a novel perspective for pretraining models on
      event camera data.
