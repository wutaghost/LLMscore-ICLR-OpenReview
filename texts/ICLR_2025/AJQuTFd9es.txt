009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            How can we predict future interaction trajectories of human hands in a scene given
012            high-level colloquial task specifications in the form of natural language? In this
013            paper, we extend the classic hand trajectory prediction task to two tasks involving
014            explicit or implicit language queries. Our proposed tasks require extensive under-
015            standing of human daily activities and reasoning abilities about what is happening
016
               next given cues from the current scene. We also develop new benchmarks to eval-
               uate the proposed two tasks, Vanilla Hand Prediction (VHP) and Reasoning-Based
017
               Hand Prediction (RBHP). We enable solving these tasks by integrating high-level
018
               world knowledge and reasoning capabilities of Vision-Language Models (VLMs)
019            with the auto-regressive nature of low-level ego-centric hand trajectories. Our
020            model, HandsOnVLM is a novel VLM that can generate textual responses and pro-
021            duce future hand trajectories through natural-language conversations. Our exper-
022            iments show that HandsOnVLM outperforms existing task-specific methods and
023            other VLM baselines on proposed tasks, and demonstrates its ability to effectively
024            utilize world knowledge for reasoning about low-level human hand trajectories
025            based on the provided context.

042   Humans interact with the everyday world and express themselves with informal and oftentimes
043   vague language descriptions. Consider the example in Fig. 1 - when we try to open the jar, we might
044   think, “Ah, I need something to help open this slippery jar more easily.” We are uncertain about
045   what we want exactly as well as about how to come up with a solution. To build a computational
046   system for addressing this need, we would require a good understanding of what tools we have lying
047   around (visual scene understanding), general apriori experience of opening jars (reasoning ability
048   and world knowledge priors), and the ability to actually execute the necessary actions for opening the
049   jar (low-level trajectory). In this paper, we develop two language-conditioned tasks for tackling this
050
      problem, propose benchmarks for evaluating progress on these tasks, and build a vision-language
      model (VLM) for predicting low-level hand trajectories in a user’s egocentric view of a scene given
051
      colloquial language queries.
052
053   Towards a similar goal, some prior works have focused on identifying human intentions based on
      egocentric human videos of daily activities (high-level intentions of the form “cutting pepper”,


                                                         1
054
      “washing plates”) (Krishna et al., 2017; Grauman et al., 2022; Kahatapitiya et al., 2024), while
055   others have focused on predicting low-level actions such as hand trajectories given human action
056   clips (Liu et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024b) without conditioning the predictions on detailed lan-
057   guage descriptions of the task to be performed. Both these scenarios are a bit restrictive since for
058   most everyday tasks (e.g. in Fig. 1) we need a combination of high-level reasoning of what to do in
059   a scene and low-level understanding of how to interact with the relevant objects in the scene.
060
      By drawing on the recent successes of VLMs for high-level reasoning (Liu et al., 2024; Lai et al.,
061   2024; Cheng et al., 2024) and advancements in hand reconstructions from generic web videos (Shan
062   et al., 2020b; Rong et al., 2020; Pavlakos et al., 2024), we develop a system for future hand trajectory
063   prediction given conversation-style language instructions. Current best multimodal VLMs are good
064   at predicting semantic actions in the form of what is happening at a certain point in a video (Maaz
065   et al. (2023); Huang et al. (2024)), interpreting what objects are in a scene (Achiam et al., 2023) and
066   natively support free-form language conversations for conditioning. However, they are not good at
067   directly predicting low-level actions (in the future) of the form of hand-object trajectories. At the
068   same time, recovering low-level interactions in videos, like hand meshes (Pavlakos et al., 2024),
069
      object meshes (Fan et al., 2024), and regions of interactions (Shan et al., 2020b; Goyal et al., 2022)
      has independently become very reliable in recent years. Our key insight is to fine-tune a pre-trained
070
      VLM with auto-regressive trajectory predictions of human hand positions, given a few seconds of
071
      video and a language description of the task.
072
073   Our approach HandsOnVLM casts hand trajectory prediction as an auto-regressive next token predic-
074   tion conditioned on fused video and language tokens. We develop HandsOnVLM as an interactive
075
      chat assistant that we can query with informal instructions of the form, “Where should my hand
      move if I want to open the refrigerator?” and a video (or an image) of a scene, and obtain outputs
076
      of the form, “To open the refrigerator, the predicted hand trajectory is <HAND> ,.... <HAND> ”
077
      The HandsOnVLM model first converts the RGB video context to visual tokens and fuses them with
078   the language tokens through slow-fast pooling (Huang et al., 2024) for capturing temporal informa-
079   tion from the context video at a fine resolution. We extend the vocabulary to add a new <HAND> to-
080   ken, and output a sequence of text and and hand tokens. We finally have a trajectory decoder to
081   convert the hand tokens to a sequence of 2D positions of the left and right hands over the prediction
082   horizon.
083
      In summary, our paper has the following contributions:
084
085
             • We develop HandsOnVLM, a novel VLM that can generate textual responses and produce
086
               future hand trajectories through conversations by expanding the original vocabulary with
087
               hand tokens and having iterative position encodings for auto-regressive predictions during
088            inference.
089
090
             • We extend existing traditional hand prediction tasks to two new tasks, Vanilla Hand Pre-
091            diction (VHP) and Reasoning-based Hand Prediction (RBHP), to predict hand trajectories
092            from ego-centric human videos conditioned on language queries of different forms.
093
094          • We develop benchmarks for evaluating progress on the VHP and RBHP tasks which we
095            will open-source to the community, in addition to our trained models on the benchmarks.
096
097   Our results on diverse real-world datasets of human videos and zero-shot evaluations on completely
098   unseen datasets demonstrate strong generalization and reasoning capabilities of HandsOnVLM for
099   hand trajectory prediction given colloquial language instructions. Furthermore, the model outper-
100   forms most baselines on the Reasoning-based Hand Prediction (RBHP) task, showcasing its capa-
101   bility to reason and leverage world knowledge of VLMs.

      2.1   H UMAN M OTION R ECONSTRUCTION AND F ORECASTING
109
110   Several prior works have attempted to recover hand meshes and full body meshes from human
111   videos Rong et al. (2020); Pavlakos et al. (2024). Going beyond reconstruction, other works have
112   also investigated forecasting motions of humans in the future. Early works used RNNs Bütepage
113   et al. (2017; 2018); Honda et al. (2020) for anticipating future human poses, and recent approaches
114   include Transformer architectures for more diverse and plausible future predictions Ding et al.
115   (2023). More directly related to our work, some approaches predict egocentric hand-trajectories
116
      in the form of 2D waypoints (Liu et al., 2020), and others also predict object affordances jointly
      with hand trajectories (Liu et al., 2022). Some predict hand trajectories in a 3D space conditioned
117
      on a few RGB observations from an egocentric view Bao et al. (2023). Architectures for such ego-
118
      centric predictions have ranged from transformers (Liu et al., 2022; Bao et al., 2023) to diffusion
119   models Ma et al. (2024b;a) trained specifically for this prediction task. Our work extends this line of
120   low-level egocentric trajectory prediction by enabling reasoning capabilities through augmentation
121   and joint training with a pre-trained VLM.
122
123   2.2   M ULTIMODAL L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
124
125   Our work is enabled by developments in multimodal Large Language Models that augment vi-
126   sion and language reasoning in a unified model. Such models like LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024) and
127   Video-ChatGPT (Maaz et al., 2023) have enabled large-scale video understanding and localization
128
      of temporal events (semantic actions) in videos (Huang et al., 2024). Adjacently, other works have
      sought to make the inputs to the VLMs more flexible and informal through automatic segmentations
129
      of language instructions (Lai et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023) and visual grounding allowing flexibil-
130
      ity to process both image and region inputs (Rasheed et al., 2024). Recent works have extended the
131   capabilities of VLMs to diverse domains including robotic navigation (Zhang et al., 2024a), robotic
132   manipulation (Kim et al., 2024; Brohan et al., 2023), spatial reasoning Cheng et al. (2024), and
133   reasoning about 3D human poses from images and text Feng et al. (2024). While these approaches
134   are orthogonal to our task of egocentric hand trajectory prediction, they serve as evidence of the
135   potential of VLMs for downstream applications.
136
137   2.3   ACTION R ECOGNITION AND P REDICTION FROM V IDEOS
138
139   Understanding actions in the form of what is happening in a video segment has a long history in
140   computer vision (Sigurdsson et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2021; Kovashka & Grauman, 2010; Feichten-
141
      hofer et al., 2019). Several benchmarks and datasets containing human videos and action labels for
      tasks have also been proposed for related problems (Grauman et al., 2022; Caba Heilbron et al.,
142
      2015; Goyal et al., 2017). Our work leverages such datasets and goes beyond recognition of actions
143
      in videos to prediction of low-level actions in the future by first reasoning about future high-level
144   actions through a VLM. As such our work can have potential applications in robotics for learning
145   motion from web videos for manipulation by complementing prior works in this space (Bharadhwaj
146   et al., 2024a; Bahl et al., 2023; Bharadhwaj et al., 2024b; Nair et al., 2022).

      3     A PPROACH
150
      HandsOnVLM is a video-based VLM with the capability of predicting future hand trajectories given
151
      a video context and language instructions. There are three key components of HandsOnVLM’s ar-
152
      chitecture: (1) SlowFast tokens to capture temporal information at fine temporal resolution, (2) hand
153   representation using an augmented vocabulary of <HAND> token, and (3) iterative hand decod-
154   ing to enable auto-regressive trajectory training and inference. In the training stage, we fine-tune a
155   pre-trained VLM by combining next-token prediction loss and trajectory loss.
156

181   SlowFast Token Compression. To obtain a capable video-conditioned VLM we need to be able to
182   interpret temporal information at a fine resolution. Following Huang et al. (2024), given Xv , we
183   embed them into T × M visual tokens using a visual backbone, where M is the number of tokens
184   in each frame. Then we apply slow-fast pooling to get T + M visual tokens. In the fast path, we
185   average all the tokens within each frame to get T tokens overall. We also uniformly select s frames
186   among all T frames and perform s × s spatial average pooling to get M slow frames in total. These
187
      slow tokens will help preserve spatial information during the encoding process. Then we embed and
      align T + M visual tokens to the language space through a vision-to-language projector f (·).
188
189   Hand as Embedding. To represent hand in the language space, we extend the existing vocabulary
190   with a new <HAND> token. However, a typical embedding layer would encode each <HAND> to-
191   ken identically, resulting in individual <HAND> token being indistinguishable from one another.
192   To overcome this limitation, we embed ground truth hand positions into the <HAND> tokens dur-
193
      ing the tokenization process. We feed them into the Large Language Model backbone and get the
      embedding of the last layer H, where H = F(Xq , f (Fenc (Xv ))).
194
195   Iterative Hand Decoding. For i-th token in the sequence, let Hi be the last-layer embedding of this
196   token from the Large Language Model. HandsOnVLM decode it to predict the (i + 1)-th token as
197   LLMs do. When (i + 1)-th token is a <HAND> token, we input Hi into a hand trajectory decoder
198   Fdec to predict the hand position of the (i + 1)-th token hi+1 = Fdec (Hi ). During inference, this
199
      decoded position is then encoded into the corresponding <HAND> token embedding for following
      prediction rounds. In this way, we ensure that each subsequent prediction is conditioned on all previ-
200
      ously predicted hand positions, maintaining temporal consistency and spatial awareness throughout
201
      the inference process and mitigating compounding errors.

222   4     R EASONING AND P REDICTING H AND T RAJECTORIES
223
224   In this section, we introduce two tasks: the Vanilla Hand Prediction (VHP) task, which extends
225   the classic hand motion prediction (Liu et al., 2022), and the proposed Reasoning-based Hand Pre-
226   diction (RBHP) task. Finally, we describe a two-step annotation-generating pipeline to build the
227   corresponding RBHP dataset.
228
229   4.1   VANILLA H AND P REDICTION TASK
230
231   In this task, explicit action narration is required to predict the next hand motion. Here explicit means
232   the action narration directly specifies the action and the target object without ambiguity, such as “cut
233   the paper” or “open the microwave”. We choose Epic-Kitchen (Damen et al., 2018; 2022), H2O
234
      (Kwon et al., 2021) and FPHA (Garcia-Hernando et al., 2018) as datasets for this task. To generate
      the hand labels for all the datasets, following Liu et al. (2022), we first run an off-the-shelf active
235
      hand-object detector (Shan et al., 2020a) to get the hand bounding box in each frame. To get the
236
      ground truth of each future hand trajectory, we first compute pairwise homographies by matching
237   SURF (Bay, 2006) features of masked regions through RANSAC and project each future hand po-
238   sition into the last observation frame. Then, we apply cubic Hermite spline interpolation to smooth
239   the projected trajectories and fill any missing points. Finally, we filter the resulting trajectories with
240   multiple criteria, including confidence thresholds, highest-score detection selection, feature match-
241   ing thresholds, trajectory completeness checks, and boundary constraints.
242
      To reformat these datasets for visual question answering, we structure them in a question-answer
243   format using the following template:
244
245   “USER:<images>,can you give me the future hand trajectory for {explicit action narra-
246
      tion}?ASSISTANT: Sure, it is<HAND><HAND><HAND><HAND>.”,
247   where <images>represents a placeholder of visual tokens of the input frames. Note that the action
248   is optional because we can also generate general templates without specifying the action, and in this
249   case the task reduces to that in prior works Liu et al. (2022); Bao et al. (2023); Ma et al. (2024b).
250
251   4.2   R EASONING - BASED H AND P REDICTION TASK
252
253   In addition to the Vanilla Hand Prediction Task, we introduce the Reasoning-based Hand Prediction
254   (RBHP) task. Instead of utilizing explicit instructions to directly predict the hand motion, here the
255   system is required to reason about it with implicit instructions. We define implicit instructions as
256   colloquial language instructions that provide sufficient information for inferring the intended human
257   hand action through reasoning, without explicitly naming the target object or action.
258   To construct a dataset for this task, we implement a two-step annotation-generating pipeline (Fig.
259   3) powered by GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023). This pipeline extracts implicit instructions from the
260   Epic-Kitchens-100 dataset (Damen et al., 2022). Prompt templates for these two steps are provided
261   in the Appendix A.5.
262
      Action-aware Image Description. To get the implicit instructions, the first step is to generate a
263   detailed description of the scene including all the objects in the foreground. We prompt GPT-4 with
264   the ground truth action to capture action-related information, such as the physical properties of the
265   target object or the spatial relationship with other objects.
266
      Implicit Action Generation. Using the action-aware description of the scene, we are able to gen-
267
      erate the implicit instructions using GPT-4 in a text template as follows:
268
269   ”USER:<images>,can you give me the future hand trajectory for {action implicit descrip-
      tion}?ASSISTANT: Sure, it is<HAND><HAND><HAND><HAND>.”.

      5.2   M ETRICS AND BASELINES
342
343   Following previous works (Liu et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2024b) we use Average Displacement Error
344   (ADE), Final Displacement Error (FDE) and Weighted Displacement Error (WDE) as metrics to
345   evaluate VHP and RBHP tasks.
346
      Vanilla Hand Prediction. For the VHP task, we choose Kalman Filter(KF) and Object-centric
347   Transformer(OCT) (Liu et al., 2022) as the baselines. Since OCT still requires the bounding box
348   feature of the hand and object as input, to get a fairer comparison with other end-to-end methods,
349   we implement a version without the requirement of the bounding box, which we call OCT-global.
350
      Reasoning-based Hand Prediction. To evaluate HandsOnVLM’s performance on the RBHP task,
351
      we perform baseline comparisons with several VLM-based methods. We describe these basleines
352
      below:
353
354     • LLaVA-Traj. Note that the hand trajectories are a sequence of pixel positions, we can rep-
355       resent them in text directly. In this case, we can directly fine-tune the LLaVA without any
356       modification.
357
        • LLaVA-Pixel2Seq. An alternative approach to representing hand positions involves quantizing
358       the image into discrete spatial bins (Chen et al., 2021), each corresponding to a unique token.
359       We can extend the existing vocabulary with those discrete tokens.
360
        • Language-conditioned Image-to-video Models.              We also compare our model to base-
361
          lines of the language-conditioned image-to-video generation followed by hand-tracking. We
362
          use commercial state-of-the-art language-conditioned image-to-video systems such as LumaL-
363       abs (LumaLabs, 2024), Kling 1.5 (KlingAI, 2024) and generate videos conditioned on the last
364       observation frame and the language description. Following the hand label generation process
365       in Sec. 4.1, we track and extract the hand trajectories of the generated video.

      6   C ONCLUSION
494
495
      Summary. In this work, we propose HandsOnVLM, a novel video-based VLM to predict hand
496
      motion from ego-centric videos. We also proposed two tasks, Vanilla Hand Prediction(VHP) task
497
      and Reasoning-based Hand Prediction(RBHP) task to benchmark the hand motion prediction as
498   well as the reasoning ability. We demonstrate its effectiveness through extensive quantitative and
499   qualitative results. We believe this research represents a promising initial step towards integrating
500   egocentric hand-object video understanding with the powerful capabilities of VLMs.
501
      Limitations. While we enabled hand-trajectory prediction from colloquial language instructions,
502
      the quality of our predictions are bottle-necked by the limitations of ground-truth hand location
503
      extraction from videos, the models for which often fail when the hand is occluded or moving too
504   fast. In addition, the 2D locations of hand we predict are not rich enough for directly being adapted
505   for downstream applications like robotics and augmented reality.
506
507
      Future Work. An interesting direction of future work would be to predict trajectories of full hand
      meshes in the future including orientation and articulation and also include depth in the predictions.
508
      Another exciting direction would be to adapt our model for long-horizon predictions for activities
509
      like “making coffee” which would consist of several steps and require reasoning over an extended
510   period. Since video clips on the web have significant camera motion over time, a viable strategy for
511   this could be chaining the model sequentially for different sub-tasks.

525   egocentric frame of reference.
