011                                                    A BSTRACT
012
013            Distributional reinforcement learning improves performance by effectively capturing envi-
014            ronmental stochasticity. However, existing research on its regret analysis has relied heavily
015            on structural assumptions that are difficult to implement in practice. In particular, there has
016            been little attention to the infeasibility issue of dealing with the infinite-dimensionality of
017            a distribution. To overcome this infeasibility, we present a regret analysis of distributional
018            reinforcement learning with general value function approximation in a finite episodic
019            Markov decision process scenario through statistical functional dynamic programming.
020
               We first introduce a key notion of Bellman unbiasedness which is essential for exactly learn-
               able and provably efficient online updates. Among all types of statistical functionals for
021
               representing infinite-dimensional return distributions, our theoretical results demonstrate
022
               that only moment functionals can exactly capture the statistical information. Secondly, we
023            propose a√  provably efficient algorithm, SF-LSVI, that achieves a tight regret bound of
024                      3
               Õ(dE H 2 K) where H is the horizon, K is the number of episodes, and dE is the eluder
025            dimension of a function class.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Distributional reinforcement learning (DistRL) (Bellemare et al., 2017; Rowland et al., 2019; Choi et al.,
032   2019; Kim et al., 2024b; Peng et al., 2024; Wiltzer et al., 2024) is an advanced approach to reinforcement
033   learning (RL) that focuses on the entire probability distribution of returns rather than solely on the expected
034   return. By considering the full distribution of returns, distRL provides deeper insight into the uncertainty
035   of each action, such as the mode or median. This framework enables us to make safer and more effective
036   decisions that account for various risks (Chow et al., 2015; Son et al., 2021; Greenberg et al., 2022; Kim et al.,
037   2024a), particularly in complex real-world situations, such as robotic manipulation (Bodnar et al., 2019),
038   neural response (Muller et al., 2024), stratospheric balloon navigation (Bellemare et al., 2020), algorithm
039
      discovery (Fawzi et al., 2022), and several game benchmarks (Bellemare et al., 2013; Machado et al., 2018).
040   In practice, a probability distribution contains an infinite amount of information, and we must inevitably
041   resort to approximations using a finite number of parameters or statistical functionals, such as categorical
042   (Bellemare et al., 2017) and quantile representations (Dabney et al., 2018b). However, previous works have
043   often conducted analyses while overlooking the infeasibility of dealing with the infinite-dimensionality of a
044   distribution. Additionally, not all statistical functionals can be exactly learned through the Bellman operator,
045
      as the meaning of statistical functionals is not typically preserved after updates. For example, the median is
      not preserved under Bellman updates, as the median of a mixture of two distributions does not generally
046
      correspond to the mixture of their medians. Hence, a distinct analysis is needed to determine whether there
047
      exists a corresponding Bellman operator for each statistical functional that guarantees exactness.
048
049   To address this issue, Rowland et al. (2019) introduced the concept of Bellman closedness – a property of
050   statistical functionals that can be exactly learned by the presence of a corresponding Bellman operator. At this
051
      point, we take a closer look at the distributional Bellman update. We reconsider what additional properties of
      statistical functionals beyond Bellman closedness are necessary for constructing online RL algorithms that
052
      are not only exactly learnable but also provably efficient in terms of regret. Simply, we identify two critical
053
      issues when using statistical functionals in lieu of a distribution, which motivated us to address them:

085   In this paper, we introduce a key concept, Bellman unbiasedness, for precise information learnability of a

089   defined structural assumption known as Distributional Bellman Completeness (DistBC) (Wang et al., 2023;
090   Chen et al., 2024). We then investigate the benefits of redesigning this concept using a collection of statistical
091   functionals. Finally, we propose a provably efficient statistical functional reinforcement learning algorithm
092   with general value function approximation, called SF-LSVI.

122   Distributional RL. In classical RL, the Bellman equation, which is based on expected returns, has a
123   closed-form expression. However, it remains unclear whether any statistical functionals of return distribution
124   always have corresponding closed-form expressions. Rowland et al. (2019) introduced the notion of Bellman
125   Closedness for collections of statistical functionals that can be updated in a closed form via Bellman update.
126
      It was shown that the only Bellman-closed statistical functionals in the discounted setting are the moments
      EZ∼η [Z k ]. More recently, Marthe et al. (2023) proposed a general framework for distRL where the agent
127
      aims to maximize its own utility functionals instead of the expected return, formalizing this property as
128
      Bellman Optimizability. They further demonstrated that in the undiscounted setting, the only W1 -continuous
129   and linear Bellman optimizable statistical functionals are exponential utilities λ1 log EZ∼η [exp(λZ)].
130
131   In practice, C51 (Bellemare et al., 2017) and QR-DQN (Dabney et al., 2018b) are notable distributional RL
132
      algorithms where the convergence guarantees of sampled-based algorithms are proved (Rowland et al., 2018;
      2023). Dabney et al. (2018a) expanded the class of policies on arbitrary distortion risk measures by taking
133
      the based distribution non-uniformly and improve the sample efficiency from their implicit representation.
134
      Cho et al. (2023) highlighted limitations of optimistic exploration in distRL and introduced a randomized
135   exploration method that perturbs the return distribution when the agent selects the next action.
136
137   RL with General Value Function Approximation. Regret bounds have been extensively studied in online
138   RL, across various domains, such as bandit (Lattimore and Szepesvári, 2020; Abbasi-Yadkori et al., 2011;
139   Russo and Van Roy, 2013), tabular RL (Kakade, 2003; Auer et al., 2008; Osband and Van Roy, 2016; Osband
140   et al., 2019; Jin et al., 2018), and linear function approximation (Jin et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2019; Zanette
141   et al., 2020). In recent years, deep RL has shown significant performance using deep neural networks as
142   function approximators, and there have been attempts to analyze in general function approximation (GVFA)
143   (Jin et al., 2021; Agarwal et al., 2023). Wang et al. (2020) established a provably efficient RL algorithm
144   with general value function approximation based on √     the eluder dimension dE (Russo and Van Roy, 2013)
145   and achieves a regret upper bound of Õ(poly(dE H) K). To circumvent the intractability of computing
146   the upper confidence bound, Ishfaq et al. (2021) injected the stochasticity on the training data and get
147
      the optimistic value function instead, improving computationally efficiency. Beyond risk-neutral setting,
      several prior works have shown regret bounds under risk-sensitive objectives (e.g., entropic risk (Fei et al.,
148
      2021; Liang and Luo, 2022), CVaR (Bastani et al., 2022)), which partially align with our approach in
149
      that they arepbuilt on a distribution framework. Liang and Luo   p (2022) achieved a regret upper bound of
150
      Õ(exp(H) |S|2 |A|H 2 K) and a lower bound of Ω(exp(H) |S||A|HK) in the tabular setting.
151
152
      DistRL with General Value Function Approximation. Recently, only a few studies have attempted to
153   bridge the gap between distRL and GVFA. Wang et al. (2023) proposed a distRL algorithm, O-DISCO,
154   which enjoys small-loss bound by using a log-likelihood objective. Similarly, Chen et al. (2024) provided
155   a risk-sensitive reinforcement learning framework with static Lipschitz risk measure. While these studies
156   investigate the benefits of distributional framework, their reliance on the distBC assumption raises several
157   potential issues, which we discuss in Section 4.3. In contrast, our approach is grounded in a statistical
158   functional framework. A detailed comparison with other distRL methods is provided in Table 1.

230   4       S TATISTICAL F UNCTIONALS IN D ISTRIBUTIONAL RL
231
232   In this section, we begin by defining two key concepts commonly used in the distRL framework: the statistical
233   functional and the sketch. We also illustrate Bellman closedness, a crucial property defined by Bellemare
234   et al. (2023). Next, we introduce a novel concept called Bellman unbiasedness which complements Bellman
235   closedness and is essential for achieving provable efficiency. As illustrated in Figure 2, quantile functionals
236   cannot be updated in an unbiased manner (as proven in Theorem 4.3), demonstrating that only certain
237
      sketches can be updated exactly. Building on this, we show that the moment functional is the only sketch
      that satisfies both properties, making it unique among the broader class of statistical functionals. Finally,
238
      we address the limitations of the previous structural assumption, distributional Bellman Completeness,
239
      particularly its tendency to result in linear regret. To overcome this, we introduce a relaxed structural
240   assumption, statistical functional Bellman Completeness.

347   4.3   S TATISTICAL F UNCTIONAL B ELLMAN C OMPLETENESS
348
349   We consider distRL with general value function approximation. For successful temporal difference learning,
350
      the GVFA framework for classical RL (Wang et al., 2020; Ayoub et al., 2020; Ishfaq et al., 2021) commonly
      requires the assumption of Bellman Completeness. This assumption states that, after applying the Bellman
351
      operator, the output lies in the function class F (i.e., if f ∈ F, then Thπ f ∈ F for all π, h).
352
353
      DistBC inevitably leads to linear regret. In seminal works, Wang et al. (2023) and Chen et al. (2024)
354   assumed that the function class H ⊆ {η : S × A → P([0, H])} follows the distributional Bellman
355   Completeness (distBC) assumption (i.e., if η ∈ H, then Thπ η ∈ H for all π, h ∈ [H]). While this assumption
356   seems natural, constructing a finite-dimensional subspace H that satisfies distBC is quite challenging. Since
357   the distributional Bellman operator is a composition of translation and mixing distributions for the next state,
358   it implies that the function class H must be closed under translation and mixture. However, when representing
359   infinite-dimensional distributions using a finite number of parameters or statistical functionals, it is not
360   trivial to ensure that a mixture of distributions can also be represented with the same number of parameters
361   or statistical functionals. For example, while a single Gaussian distribution can be represented using two
362
      parameters (µ, σ 2 ), a mixture of K Gaussians generally requires 2K parameters for exact representation.
363   To address the issue of closedness under mixture, both previous studies assumed a discretized reward MDP
364   where all outcomes of the return distribution are discretized into a uniform grid of finite points. Unfortunately,
365   the approximation error introduced by this discretization is not negligible with respect to regret. This is
366   because the approximation error is, in fact, a form of model misspecification – an error arising when the
367   model fails to represent the target – which makes it difficult to achieve provable efficiency.
368   Definition 4.7 (Model Misspecification in distBC). For a given distribution class H, which is a finite-
369   dimensional subspace of the space of all distribution F ∞ , we define ζ as the model misspecification

374   Note that ζ is strictly positive unless the function approximator fη̄ can represent any distribution in the finite-
375
      dimensional subspace H generated by translation and mixture. In the classical linear bandit setting (Zanette
      et al., 2020), a lower bound with model misspecification ζ is known to yield linear regret Ω(ζK). Therefore,
376
      redefining Bellman Completeness within the infinite-dimensional distribution space is not appropriate, as it
377
      either imposes overly restrictive constraints on the MDP structure or leads to linear regret.

508   7   C ONCLUSIONS
509
510   To overcome the infeasibility of dealing with the infinite-dimensionality of a distribution, we analyzed
511   the approximation error (model misspecification) inherent in distribution-based updates. We introduced a
512   new concept of Bellman unbiasedness, which enables to exactly learn a distribution with a sample efficient
513   manner. Based on this, we present a provably efficient online distRL algorithm, SF-LSVI, with general √
                                                                                                            value
                                                                                                          3
514   function approximation. Notably, our algorithm achieves a near-optimal regret bound of Õ(dE H 2 K),
515   matching the tightest upper bound achieved by non-distributional frameworks (Zhou et al., 2021; He et al.,
516   2023).
517   One might wonder whether representing a distribution with a finite number of moments still introduces
518   approximation errors. Indeed, a reconstructed distribution using only finite information cannot perfectly match
519   the ground truth within infinite-dimensional distribution space. However, when measuring discrepancies
520   between two distributions within a finite-dimensional representation space, moment sketches provide a
521   unique characteristic of being the only representation that introduces no approximation error for these
522   distributions. Leveraging this property, an interesting future direction would be to reformulate regret in terms
523   of moment discrepancies rather than expected returns and to demonstrate the sample efficiency of distRL.
524
      It is hoped that this work triggers new insights and ideas for future research in establishing the provable
      efficiency in practical distRL settings.
