011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Graph Neural Networks have emerged as the most popular architecture for graph-
014            level learning, including graph classification and regression tasks, which fre-
015            quently arise in areas such as biochemistry and drug discovery. Achieving good
016
               performance in practice requires careful model design. Due to gaps in our un-
               derstanding of the relationship between model and data characteristics, this often
017
               requires manual architecture and hyperparameter tuning. This is particularly pro-
018
               nounced in graph-level tasks, due to much higher variation in the input data than
019            in node-level tasks. To work towards closing these gaps, we begin with a system-
020            atic analysis of individual performance in graph-level tasks. Our results establish
021            significant performance heterogeneity in both message-passing and transformer-
022            based architectures. We then investigate the interplay of model and data charac-
023            teristics as drivers of the observed heterogeneity. Our results suggest that graph
024            topology alone cannot explain heterogeneity. Using the Tree Mover’s Distance,
025            which jointly evaluates topological and feature information, we establish a link be-
026            tween class-distance ratios and performance heterogeneity in graph classification.
027
               These insights motivate model and data preprocessing choices that account for
               heterogeneity between graphs. We propose a selective rewiring approach, which
028
               only targets graphs whose individual performance benefits from rewiring. We fur-
029
               ther show that the optimal network depth depends on the graph’s spectrum, which
030            motivates a heuristic for choosing the number of GNN layers. Our experiments
031            demonstrate the utility of both design choices in practice.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have found widespread applications in the social, natural and engi-
037   neering sciences (Zitnik et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2022; Shlomi et al., 2020). Notable examples include
038   graph classification and regression tasks, which arise in drug discovery (Zitnik et al., 2018), protein
039   function prediction (Gligorijević et al., 2021), and the study of chemical reactions (Jin et al., 2017;
040   Coley et al., 2019), among others.
041
      Most state of the art GNNs are based on message-passing or transformer-type architectures. In both
042   cases, careful model design and parameter choices are crucial for competitive performance in down-
043   stream tasks. A growing body of literature studies the relationship of model and data characteristics
044   in graph learning. This includes the study of challenges in encoding long-range dependencies, which
045   arise in shallow architectures due to under-reaching (Barceló et al., 2020) and in deep architectures
046   due to over-smoothing and over-squashing effects (Alon & Yahav, 2021; Li et al., 2018). A second
047   perspective evaluates a model’s (in)ability to encode certain structural functions due to limitations in
048   representational power (Xu et al., 2018). Some recent works have studied model and data character-
049   istics through classical complexity lenses, such as generalization (Garg et al., 2020; Le & Jegelka,
050
      2024; Franks et al., 2024) and trainability (Kiani et al., 2024). While these results offer valuable
      theoretical insights, their ability to directly guide design choices for specific datasets and tasks is
051
      often limited. As a result, model design usually relies on manual hyperparameter tuning in practise.
052
053   In this work, we study the interplay of model and data characteristics from a different perspective.
      We analyze the performance of a model on individual graphs with the goal of understanding varia-


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      tions in optimal model design within data sets. We introduce heterogeneity profiles as a tool for sys-
055   tematically evaluating individual performance across graphs in a dataset. The analysis of the profiles
056   of several classification and regression benchmarks for both message-passing and transformer-based
057   architectures reveals significant performance heterogeneity in graph-level learning. We then investi-
058   gate how data and model characteristics drive this heterogeneity. A natural idea in this context is that
059   the topological properties of the graphs, along with their variation across the dataset, might explain
060   the observed heterogeneity. However, our results indicate that graph topology alone cannot explain
061   heterogeneity. We then analyze the datasets with the Tree Mover’s Distance (Chuang & Jegelka,
062   2022), a similarity measure that compares graphs using both topological and feature information.
063
      Using this lens, we show that common graph-level classification benchmarks contain examples that
      are more “similar” to graphs of a different label than to graphs with the same label. The prediction
064
      stability of typical GNN architectures therefore makes it hard to classify these examples correctly.
065
066   Using these insights, we study data pre-processing and model design choices with an eye towards
067   heterogeneous graph-level effects. We first revisit graph rewiring, a pre-processing technique that
068   perturbs the edges of input graphs with the goal of mitigating over-smoothing and over-squashing.
069
      We find that while some graphs benefit, the individual performance of others drops significantly
      as a result of rewiring. Although we observe notable differences between rewiring techniques, the
070
      general observation is consistent across approaches and data sets. Motivated by this observation, we
071
      introduce a new “selective” rewiring approach that rewires only graphs that based on their topology
072   are likely to benefit. We further show that the optimal GNN depth varies between the graphs in a
073   dataset and depends on the spectrum of the input graph; hence, aligning spectra across graphs allows
074   for choosing a GNN depth that is close to optimal across the data set. We illustrate the utility of both
075   intervention techniques in experiments on several common graph benchmarks.

077   1.1   R ELATED WORK
078
079   Performance heterogeneity The interplay of model and data characteristics in graph learning was
080   previously studied by Li et al. (2023); Liang et al. (2023), albeit only for node-level tasks. Li et al.
081   (2023) establish a link between a graph’s topological characteristics and performance heterogeneity.
082
      To the best of our knowledge, no such study has been conducted for graph-level tasks. As we discuss
      below, performance heterogeneity is linked to model generalization. Size generalization in GNNs
083
      has been studied in (Yehudai et al., 2021; Maskey et al., 2022; Le & Jegelka, 2024). For a more
084
      comprehensive overview of generalization results for GNNs, see also (Jegelka, 2022). Beyond graph
085   learning, performance heterogeneity has been studied through the lens of example difficulty (Kaplun
086   et al.) by analyzing a model’s performance on individual instances in the test data.
087
088   Graph Rewiring Several rewiring approaches have been studied in the context of mitigating over-
089   smoothing and over-squashing effects, most of which are motivated by topological graph character-
090   istics that can be used to characterize both effects. Notable examples include rewiring based on the
091   spectrum of the Graph Laplacian (Karhadkar et al., 2023; Arnaiz-Rodrı́guez et al., 2022), discrete
092   Ricci curvature (Topping et al., 2022; Nguyen et al., 2023; Fesser & Weber, 2024a), effective resis-
093   tance (Black et al., 2023), and random sparsification (Rong et al., 2019). When applied in graph-level
094
      tasks, these preprocessing routines are applied to all input graphs. In contrast, Barbero et al. (2023)
      propose a rewiring approach that balances mitigating over-smoothing and over-squashing and pre-
095
      serving the structure of the input graph. A more nuanced analysis of the effectiveness of standard
096
      rewiring approaches has been given in (Tortorella & Micheli, 2022; Tori et al., 2024).
097

      1.2   S UMMARY OF CONTRIBUTIONS
099
100   Our main contributions are as follows:
101
102         1. We introduce graph-level heterogeneity profiles for analyzing performance variations of
103            GNNs on individual graphs in graph-level tasks. Our analysis suggests that both message-
104            passing and transformer-based GNNs display performance heterogeneity in classification
105            and regression tasks.
106         2. We provide evidence that topological properties alone cannot explain graph-level hetero-
107            geneity. Instead, we use the notion of the Tree Mover’s Distance to establish a link between
               class-distance ratios and performance heterogeneity.

            3. We use these insights to derive lessons for architecture choices and data preprocessing.
109            Specifically, we show that the optimal GNN depth for individual graphs depends on their

122   Message-Passing Graph Neural Networks Message-Passing (MP) (Gori et al., 2005; Hamilton

124   of the art GNN architectures, such as GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2017), GIN (Xu et al., 2018) and
125
      GAT (Veličković et al., 2018), implement MP by iteratively updating their nodes’ representation
      based on the representations of their neighbors. Formally, let xlv denote the representation of node

151   Graph Rewiring The topology of the input graph(s) has significant influence on the training dy-
152   namics of GNNs. Two notable phenomena in this context are over-smoothing and over-squashing.
153
      Over-smoothing (Li et al., 2018) arises when the representations of dissimilar nodes become indis-
      tinguishable as the number of layers increases. In contrast, over-squashing (Alon & Yahav, 2021)
154
      is induced by “bottlenecks” in the information flow between distant nodes as the number of layers
155
      increases. Both effects can limit the GNN’s ability to accurately encode long-range dependen-
156   cies in the learned node representations, which can negatively impact downstream performance.
157   Graph rewiring was introduced as a pre-processing routine for mitigating over-smoothing and over-
158   squashing by perturbing the edges of the input graph(s). A plethora of rewiring approaches, which
159   leverage a variety of topological graph characteristics, have been introduced. In this paper we con-
160   sider two rewiring approaches: FOSR (Karhadkar et al., 2023), which leverages the spectrum of the
161   Graph Laplacian, and BORF (Nguyen et al., 2023; Fesser & Weber, 2024a), which utilizes discrete
      Ricci curvature. We defer a more detailed description of both approaches to Appendix A.2.

      2.2   T REE M OVER ’ S D ISTANCE
163
164   The Tree Mover’s Distance (short: TMD) is a similarity measure on graphs that jointly evaluates
165   feature and topological information Chuang & Jegelka (2022). Like an MPGNN, it views a graph as
166   a set of computation trees. A node’s computation tree is constructed by adding the node’s neighbors
167   to the tree level by level. TMD compares graphs by characterizing the similarity of their computation
168   trees via hierarchical optimal transport: The similarity of two trees Tv , Tu is computed by comparing
169   their roots fv , fu and then recursively comparing their subtrees. We provide a formal definition of
170
      the TMD in Appendix A.4.

172   3     E STABLISHING G RAPH - LEVEL H ETEROGENEITY
173
174   In this section, we establish the existence of performance heterogeneity in common graph classifica-
175   tion and regression tasks in both message-passing and transformer-based GNNs. We further provide
176   empirical evidence that topological features alone are not sufficient to explain these observations.
177
178   3.1   H ETEROGENEITY P ROFILES
179
180   To analyze performance heterogeneity, we compute heterogeneity profiles that show the average
181   individual test accuracy over 100 trials for each graph in the dataset. For each dataset considered,
182   we apply a random train/val/test split of 50/25/25 percent. We train the model for 300 epochs on the
183   training dataset and keep the model checkpoint with the highest validation accuracy (see Appendix
184   D for additional training details and hyperparameter choices). We then record this model’s error on
185
      each of the graphs in the test dataset in an external file. We repeat this procedure until each graph has
      been in the test dataset at least 100 times. For the graph classification tasks, we compute the average
186
      graph-level accuracy (higher is better, denoted as ↑), and for each regression task the graph-level
187
      MAE (lower is better, denoted as ↓).

221   3.3   E XPLAINING PERFORMANCE HETEROGENEITY
222
223   As mentioned earlier, performance heterogeneity can also be found between individual nodes in
224   node classification tasks (Li et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2023). They find that node-level heterogeneity
225   can be explained well based on topological features alone: Nodes with higher degrees are provably
226   easier to classify correctly than nodes with lower degrees Li et al. (2023). A natural question is
227
      whether this extends to graph-level tasks.
228   We test this with the following experiment, which is inspired by the analysis presented in Li et al.
229   (2023) for node-level heterogeneity. For each dataset considered, we record the graph-level GCN
230   accuracy averaged over 100 runs as above and compute several classical topological graph char-
231   acteristics, such as assortativity, the clustering coefficient, and the spectral gap (see Appendix B.2
232   for details). We then train an MLP to predict the accuracy of a GCN on a given graph based on
233
      its topological features. While the MLP is able to fit the training data, it does not generalize well
      with test MSEs ranging from 0.1 to 0.27. Figure 11 in the Appendix visualizes this for the Enzymes
234
      dataset. Additional experiments in Appendix B.4 support the same message: Unlike in the node-
235
      level setting, topological features alone are insufficient to explain performance heterogeneity in the
236   graph-level setting.

      3.4   H ETEROGENEITY APPEARS DURING GNN TRAINING
292
293   To better understand how performance heterogeneity arises during training, we analyze the predic-
294   tions on individual graphs in the test dataset after each epoch. Figure 3 shows the mean accuracy and
295   variance for GIN (message-passing) and GraphGPS (transformer-based) on Mutag. As we can see,
296   both models have an initial increase in heterogeneity, followed by a steady decline over the rest of
297   the training duration. Both models learn to correctly classify some graphs almost immediately, while
298   others are only classified correctly much later. We should point out that these “difficult” graphs, i.e.
299   graphs that are either learned during later epochs or never learned at all, are nearly identical for GIN
300
      and GraphGPS. GraphGPS, being a much more powerful model, learns to correctly classify more of
      these “difficult” graphs during later epochs. The (few) graphs which it cannot learn are also graphs
301
      on which GIN fails. Additional experiments on other datasets can be found in Appendix C.2. Across
302
      datasets and models, we witness this “learning in stages”, where learning easy examples leads to an
303   initial increase in heterogeneity, followed by a steady decrease.

307
308   In this section, we use heterogeneity profiles to show that preprocessing techniques can reinforce
309   heterogeneity in graph-level learning. We focus primarily on rewiring techniques, but we also pro-
310   vide some experimental results with encodings in Appendix C.3. We find that while preprocessing
311
      methods such as rewiring are usually beneficial when averaged over all graphs, GNN performance
      on individual graphs can in fact drop by as much as 90 percent after rewiring. Deciding which graphs
312
      to preprocess is therefore crucial. We take a first step in this direction and propose a topology-aware
313
      selective rewiring method. As for encodings, our results indicate that investigating a similar, se-
314   lective approach is a potentially fruitful direction (though unfortunately beyond the scope of this
315   paper). We further note that existing theoretical results on improved expressivity when using encod-
316   ings cannot explain the oftentimes detrimental effects observed in the heterogeneity analysis.
317

      4.2   T OPOLOGY- AWARE SELECTIVE REWIRING
349
350
      The large differences in performance benefits from FoSR on individual graphs reveal a fundamental
351   shortcoming of almost all existing (spectral) rewiring methods: They rewire all graphs in a dataset,
352   irrespective of whether individual graphs actually benefit. To overcome this limitation, we propose
353   to rewire selectively, where a topological criterion is used to decide whether an individual graph’s
354   performance will benefit from rewiring. The resulting topology-aware selective rewiring chooses a
355   threshold spectral gap λ∗ from the initial distribution of Fiedler values in a given dataset. Empiri-
356   cally, we find the median to work well. Using standard FoSR, we then add edges to all graphs whose
357   spectral gap is below this threshold. The resulting approach, which we term Selective-FOSR, leaves
358   graphs with an already large spectral gap unchanged. This “alignment” results in a larger degree of
359
      (spectral) similarity between the graphs in a dataset.
360   The effect on the spectral gaps of graphs in Enzymes and Proteins is plotted in Figure 5. Both
361   datasets originally have a large number of graphs whose spectral gap is close to zero, i.e. which
362   are almost disconnected and hence likely to suffer from over-squashing. Standard FoSR mitigates
363   this, but simultaneously creates graphs with much larger spectral gaps than in the original dataset
364   while more than doubling the spread of the distribution. Our selective rewiring approach avoids both
365
      of these undesirable effects. This also shows in significantly increased performance on all datasets
      considered when compared to standard FoSR, as can be seen in Table 1.

368   5     H ETEROGENEITY- INFORMED M ODEL S ELECTION
369
370
      So far, we have compared the heterogeneity profiles of different architectures, such as GCN and
371
      GraphGPS, on the same datasets, or compared profiles of datasets with and without rewiring. In this
372   section, we now fix the preprocessing technique and base layer and focus on heterogeneity profiles
373   at different GNN depths. This allows us to define a graph-level optimal depth. We show that the
374   optimal depth varies widely within datasets, an observation that, we argue, is related to the graphs’
375   spectral gap. We show that decreasing the variation in spectral gaps within a dataset via selective
376   rewiring makes it easier to find a depth that works well on all graphs. At the same time, those
377   insights motivate a heuristic for choosing the GNN depth in practise, which reduces the need for
      (potentially expensive) tuning of this crucial hyperparameter.

459   autonomous differential equation

468

482   5.2   S PECTRAL ALIGNMENT ALLOWS FOR PRINCIPLED DEPTH CHOICES
483
484   Our discussion on average consensus dynamics suggests that individual graphs might require dif-
485   ferent GNN depths because approaching a global consensus takes about 1/λ2 time steps – in our
      case layers – where λ2 is the graph’s spectral gap. We also saw in the previous section that selective


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      (spectral) rewiring lifts the spectral gaps of (almost) all graphs in a dataset above a predetermined
487   threshold λ∗2 without creating graphs with very large spectral gaps. Motivated by these insights, we
488   propose to use 1/λ∗2 as a heuristic for the GNN depth.
489
490
      As Figure 7 shows, we empirically find that this approach works very well. On all three datasets
      depicted here (Mutag, Enzymes and Peptides-Struct), the ideal GCN depth turned out to be the
491
      integer closest to 1/λ∗2 : 7 layers for both Mutag and Enzymes, and 12 layers for Peptides-struct.
492
      Using grid-search, we previously determined 4 layers to be optimal on both datasets after applying
493   standard FoSR or no rewiring at all.
494
495   We now find that the accuracy obtained with a 7-layer GCN after applying selective FoSR is 3
496
      percent higher than the accuracy obtained with selective FoSR and 4 layers on Mutag (5 percent
      on Enzymes). We also note that using 7 layers after applying FoSR or no rewiring is far from
497
      optimal on both datasets, highlighting the fact that the optimal depth changes with rewiring. Our
498
      observations on Peptides-struct are similar: Computing 1/λ∗2 suggests that we should use 12 layers
499   on the selectively rewired dataset, which indeed turns out to be the ideal depth. This is far deeper
500   than the 8 layers that are optimal with standard FoSR or no rewiring at all. Overall, we find that
501   deeper networks generally perform better than shallow ones after selective rewiring. This might be
502   surprising given that spectral rewiring methods are meant to improve the connectivity of the graph
503   and hence allow for shallower models. We note that there is no such clear trend on the original
504   datasets. For example, 8 layers perform almost as well on Mutag as 4. This further supports the
505   notion that selective rewiring results in a spectral alignment between the graphs in a dataset.

519   6   D ISCUSSION
520
521   In this paper we analyzed performance heterogeneity in graph-level learning in both message-
522   passing and transformer-based GNN architectures. Our results showed that unlike in the node-level
523
      setting, topological properties alone cannot explain graph-level heterogeneity. Instead, we identify
      large class-distance ratios as the main driver of heterogeneity within data sets. Our analysis suggests
524
      several lessons for architecture choice and data preprocessing, including a selective rewiring ap-
525
      proach that optimizes the benefits of rewiring and a heuristic for choosing the optimal GNN depths.
526   We corroborate our findings with computational experiments.
527
528   Our study of data preprocessing routines has focused on rewiring. However, recent literature has
529
      shown that encodings too can lead to significant performance gains in downstream tasks. While we
      include a preliminary study of heterogeneity in the context of encodings in Appendix C.3, a more
530
      detailed analysis is left for future work. We believe that a principled approach for selective encod-
531
      ings is a promising direction for extending the present work. Similarly, our theoretical understanding
532   of common encodings such as LAPE and RWPE needs to be reassessed: Recent results argue that
533   adding encodings makes GNNs more expressive, which does not explain the substantial detrimental
534   effects on some graphs observed in our preliminary experiments. While our results suggest con-
535   nections between performance heterogeneity and generalization, a detailed theoretical analysis of
536   this link is beyond the scope of the present paper. We believe that in particular a detailed study of
537   similarity measures in the style of the Tree Mover’s Distance could provide valuable insights into
538   transformer-based architectures. Lastly, this study aimed to work towards automating model choices
539   in GNNs. We believe that the heterogeneity perspective could provide insights beyond GNN depth
      and preprocessing routines.

      A.2   G RAPH R EWIRING
757
758   In this study we focus on two approaches that are representatives of the two most frequently consid-
759   ered classes of rewiring techniques.
760
761   FOSR Introduced by Karhadkar et al. (2023), this rewiring approach leverages a characterization
762   of over-squashing effects using the spectrum of the Graph Laplacian. It adds synthetic edges to a
763   given graph to expand its spectral gap which can mitigate over-squashing.
764
765   BORF Introduced by (Nguyen et al., 2023), BORF leverages a connection between discrete Ricci
766
      curvature and over-smoothing and over-squashing effects. Regions in the graph that suffer from
      over-smoothing have high curvature, where as edges that induce over-squashing have low curvature.
767
      BORF adds and removes edges to mitigate extremal curvature values. Fesser & Weber (2024a)
768
      show that the optimal number of edges relates to the curvature gap, a global curvature-based graph
769   characteristic, providing a heuristic for choosing this hyperparameter.
770
771
      A.3   P OSITIONAL AND STRUCTURAL ENCODINGS
772
773   Structural (SE) and Positional (PE) encodings endow GNNs with structural information that they
774   cannot learn on their own, but which is crucial for downstream performance. Encodings are often
775   based on classical topological graph characteristics. Typical examples of positional encodings in-
776   clude spectral information, such as the eigenvectors of the Graph Laplacian (Dwivedi et al., 2023) or
777   random-walk based node similarities (Dwivedi et al., 2022). Structural encodings include substruc-
778
      ture counts (Bouritsas et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2022), as well as graph characteristics or summary
      statistics that GNNs cannot learn on their own, e.g., its diameter, girth, the number of connected
779
      components (Loukas, 2019), or summary statistics of node degrees (Cai & Wang, 2018) or the
780
      Ricci curvature of its edges (Fesser & Weber, 2024b). The effectiveness of encodings has been
781   demonstrated on numerous graph benchmarks and across message-passing and transformer-based
782   architectures.
