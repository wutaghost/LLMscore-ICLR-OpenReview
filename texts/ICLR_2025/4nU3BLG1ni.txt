009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Multi-player multi-armed bandits have been researched for a long time due to their
012            application in cognitive radio networks. In this setting, multiple players select
013            arms at each time and instantly receive the feedback. Most research on this prob-
014            lem focuses on the content of the immediate feedback, whether it includes both
015            the reward and collision information or the reward alone. However, delay is com-
016
               mon in cognitive networks when users perform spectrum sensing. In this paper,
               we design an algorithm DDSE (Decentralized Delayed Successive Elimination)
017
               in multi-player multi-armed bandits with stochastic delay feedback and establish
018
               a regret bound. Compared with existing algorithms that fail to address this prob-
019            lem, our algorithm enables players to adapt to delayed feedback and avoid colli-
020            sion. We also derive a lower bound in centralized setting to prove the algorithm
021            achieves near-optimal. Numerical experiments on both synthetic and real-world
022            datasets validate the effectiveness of our algorithm.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Multi-armed Bandits (MAB) is a classic framework widely applied in diverse fields such as on-
      line advertising, clinical trials, and recommendation systems. In this framework, a single player
028
      sequentially selects an arm k from a finite set [K] := {1, ..., K} and receives a random reward
029
      Xk (t). However, in many real-world scenarios, the standard MAB framework may not adequately
030   capture the complexities involved. Considering cognitive radio systems which aim that spectrum
031   resources are shared efficiently to users, a key difference from the traditional MAB problem is that
032   when users select the same channel, they collide and no message is transmitted. This situation mo-
033   tivates multi-player multi-armed bandits (MMAB) framework in which M players simultaneously
034   pull arms. If two or more players pull the same arm, their rewards turn to zero which represents
035   failed transmission.
036   In multi-player bandits, the problem is categorized into centralized and decentralized settings. In
037   the centralized setting, players can freely share their rewards without any loss. Whereas this di-
038   rect communication would consume substantial energy in cognitive networks, recent studies have
039   primarily focused on the decentralized problem, where players cannot communicate directly. This
040   setting is more complex than centralized MMAB because it requires additional techniques to sim-
041   ulate communication between players. Most recent studies on decentralized MMAB (Boursier &
042   Perchet, 2019; Wang et al., 2020) simulate communication between players by forcing collisions, as
043   the occurrence or absence of a collision provides binary information on optimal arms.
044   However, in practical cognitive radio networks, a more realistic scenario involves users experiencing
045   delays in signal reception due to various inherent factors. These delays arise from spectrum analysis,
046   where different link layer protocols are needed for different spectrum bands to handle path loss
047   and wireless link errors, leading to different packet transmission delays at the link layer (Akyildiz
048   et al., 2006; Ahmad et al., 2020). Although these delays are common in real-world cognitive radio
049   networks, current research on decentralized MMAB (Xiong & Li, 2023; Xu et al., 2023; Richard
050
      et al., 2024) largely overlooks this issue and most existing works discuss the setting that rewards are
      immediately revealed after players pull arms. Actually, this setting does not align with the practical
051
      challenges faced by users, where delays significantly alter the effectiveness of algorithms.
052

      et al., 2013; Lancewicki et al., 2021; Tang et al., 2024). In their model, a player selects an arm but

      1.1      C ONTRIBUTION
087
088   Motivated by the pressing challenge of delay in cognitive radio networks, we propose a novel ban-
089   dit framework where multiple players engage in a multi-armed bandit and if two or more players
090   select the same arm, none of them receive the reward. Crucially, in our framework, players receive
091   feedback after a period of stochastic delay, which complicates their ability to learn and adapt in real
092   time, making it exceedingly difficult to avoid collisions and optimize performance.
093
      For this problem, we introduce an algorithm DDSE (Decentralized Delayed Successive Elimination
094   ), where players are divided into a leader and several followers. The leader explores all arms and
095   gradually eliminates sub-optimal arms, while followers pull arms only from the set of best empirical
096   arms. Before each exploration phase begins, players coordinate to use the same best empirical arm
097   set based on the estimation of delay, ensuring that no collision occurs. At regular intervals, the
098   leader communicates the update to followers also using the coordinated set so that followers stay
099   synchronized and receive correct information.

531
532
      In this paper, we proposed the algorithm DDSE for multi-player multi-armed bandits with delayed
533
      feedback. We demonstrated that a decentralized MMAB algorithm can avoid collisions and achieve
534   performance close to its centralized counterpart, even when player feedback is delayed. Rather than
535   allowing players to update blindly, introducing appropriate waiting significantly improves perfor-
536   mance and reduces the regret. The lower bound in the centralized setting further confirms that our
537   algorithm is near-optimal. Additionally, practical simulations have validated the superiority of our
538   algorithm. A promising direction for future work would be to study player-dependent delays in
539   multi-player bandits, as delays in cognitive networks often depend on user-specific factors, such as
      location and device capability.

      A    R ELATED W ORK
703
704   The problem of multi-player multi-armed bandits has recently been studied in different settings in the
705   existing literature, where most of the efforts have concentrated on the decentralized setting. Bour-
706   sier & Perchet (2019) propose an implicit communication mechanism where players intentionally
707   collide to signal information, achieving performance comparable to centralized approaches. Wang
708   et al. (2020) improve this communication phase by electing a leader and only allowing the leader to
709   communicate with followers. Research also has focused on heterogeneous reward settings (Besson
710   & Kaufmann, 2018; Bistritz & Leshem, 2018; Tibrewal et al., 2019; Shi et al., 2021) and adversarial
711   collision scenarios (Mahesh et al., 2022). The challenge of incomplete feedback is another promi-
712
      nent topic (Boursier & Perchet, 2019; Shi et al., 2020; Lugosi & Mehrabian, 2022). Notably, Huang
      et al. (2022) present near-optimal results under incomplete feedback setting. Wang et al. (2022);
713
      Xu et al. (2023) explore the scenario of shareable arms. Recently, Richard et al. (2024) consider
714
      asynchronous multi-player bandits in the centralized setting and derive a constant or logarithmic
715   regret.
716
717   There has been growing interest in stochastic delay in multi-armed bandits. Vernade et al. (2017)
718
      investigate delayed Bernoulli bandits, although their approach requires knowledge of the delay dis-
      tribution. Pike-Burke et al. (2018) consider scenarios where a sum of observations is received after
719
      some stochastic delay. Zhou et al. (2019) explore contextual bandits with stochastic delay. Arm-
720
      dependent delay is discussed by Gael et al. (2020), and Lancewicki et al. (2021) later remove the
721   restriction on delay distribution. Tang et al. (2024) focus on strongly reward-dependent delay and
722   achieve near-optimal results. Yang et al. (2024) propose a reduction-based framework to handle
723   delays with sub-exponential distributions.
724
      A similar setting to ours is multi-agent bandits with delay. Existing literature has focused on de-
725
      centralized cooperative bandits (Cesa-Bianchi et al., 2016; Martı́nez-Rubio et al., 2019), while non-
726
      cooperative game with delay is discussed in Bistritz et al. (2019; 2022). Zhang et al. (2023) consider
727   multi-agent reinforcement learning with both finite and infinite delay. Li & Guo (2023) discuss ad-
728   versarial bandit problem with delayed feedback from multiple users. Hanna et al. (2024) propose
729   an algorithm in multi-agent bandits with delay and reach a sub-linear regret. However, none of
730   these works consider collisions between players. Since collisions result in a loss of reward, current
731   algorithms in multi-agent bandits cannot be directly applied to our problem.
