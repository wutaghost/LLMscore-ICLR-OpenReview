009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Existing diffusion-based methods for inverse problems sample from the posterior
012            using score functions and accept the generated random samples as solutions. In
013            applications that posterior mean is preferred, we have to generate multiple samples
014            from the posterior which is time-consuming. In this work, by analyzing the
015            probability density evolution of the conditional reverse diffusion process, we prove
016            that the posterior mean can be achieved by tracking the mean of each reverse
017            diffusion step. Based on that, we establish a framework termed reverse mean
018            propagation (RMP) that targets the posterior mean directly. We show that RMP can
019
               be implemented by solving a variational inference problem, which can be further
               decomposed as minimizing a reverse KL divergence at each reverse step. We
020
               further develop an algorithm that optimizes the reverse KL divergence with natural
021
               gradient descent using score functions and propagates the mean at each reverse
022            step. Experiments demonstrate the validity of the theory of our framework and
023            show that our algorithm outperforms state-of-the-art algorithms on reconstruction
024            performance with lower computational complexity in various inverse problems.

029
      Diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Song & Ermon, 2019; Ho et al., 2020; Song et al.,
030
      2020a; Rombach et al., 2022) have shown impressive performance for image generation. For diffusion
031   models such as diffusion denoising probability model (DDPM) (Ho et al., 2020) and denoising score
032   matching with Langevin dynamics (SMLD) (Song & Ermon, 2019), the essential part is the learning
033   of score functions of data distributions with large datasets. By approximating score functions with
034   neural networks such as U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015; Song & Ermon, 2019), the prior of complex
035   data distributions can be learned implicitly which encourages many applications. Inverse problems
036   aim to recover an unknown state x0 from observation y, which is fundamental to various research
037   areas such as wireless communication, image processing and natural language processing. Recent
038   works (Jalal et al., 2020; Kawar et al., 2021; Song et al., 2020a; 2021; Chung et al., 2022b; Kawar
039
      et al., 2022; Meng & Kabashima, 2022; Chung et al., 2022a; Laumont et al., 2022; Mardani et al.,
      2023) have shown that diffusion models can be used for solving inverse problems since the prior of
040
      data distribution is learned implicitly with score functions and score-based priors are more efficient
041
      to train (Song & Ermon, 2020).
042
043   Based on Bayes’ rule, diffusion models are used for the generation of data from the posterior
044   distribution with score functions of data and likelihood, and thus can be applied in solving reverse
045
      problems. The main difficulty of applying diffusion models to solving inverse problems is the
      calculation of likelihood score. SNIPS (Kawar et al., 2021) and DDRM (Kawar et al., 2022) are
046
      proposed to solve noisy linear inverse problems with diffusion process in the spectral domain. In
047
      these methods, the measurement and data to be estimated are transformed into the spectral domain
048   via singular value decomposition (SVD), and the conditional score can be calculated with SVD
049   explicitly. In Meng & Kabashima (2022), the authors propose to approximate the likelihood score by
050   a noise-perturbed pseudo-likelihood score which has a closed form under certain assumptions and
051   can be efficiently calculated using SVD for noisy linear inverse problems. MCG (Chung et al., 2022b)
052   circumvents the calculation of likelihood score by projections onto the measurement constrained
053   manifold. In DPS (Chung et al., 2022a), the Laplace method is used for the approximation of the
      likelihood score for general inverse problems. Instead of directly approximating the likelihood

075   The Fokker-Planck equation (Risken, 1996; Jordan et al., 1998) describes the evolution of the
076   probability density of a stochastic process. In this work, we present an evolution analysis of the
077   probability density for the conditional reverse diffusion process, as described by the Fokker-Planck
078   equation. Based on the analysis, we propose a variational inference framework that minimizes the
079
      reverse KL divergence, referred to as Reverse Mean Propagation (RMP), since it propagates the mean
      at each reverse step, as illustrated in Fig. 1. Different from the previous works that draw samples
080
      from the posterior with a reverse diffusion process, our proposed RMP algorithm is essentially
081
      an approximation of the Fokker-Planck equation for a conditional reverse process that tracks the
082   evolution of the probability density which is deterministic. This represents a fundamental difference
083   between our method and previous methods. Unlike sampling-based methods that have to sample
084   from the posterior multiple times and average out to get the posterior mean which is time-consuming,
085   RMP converges to the posterior mean, i.e. the MMSE estimation in Bayesian inference, which is
086   preferred in many applications (Kay, 1993). RMP simplifies the application of variational inference
087   to a broader range of problems in a plug-and-play manner by adopting a score-based prior that can be
088   learned using neural networks (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020b) and performs effectively when the
089   score networks are available. This is contrary to traditional variational inference methods (Maestrini
090
      et al., 2025; Wand, 2017) for inverse problems that require complex models of prior. The main
      contributions of our work are summarized as follows:
091
092          • We characterize the evolution of the transaction probability density of the conditional reverse
093            diffusion process in terms of mean and covariance, based on which we propose the RMP
094            framework that approaches the posterior mean.
095
             • We connect variational inference with conditional diffusion process and propose an imple-
096
               mentation of the RMP framework based on stochastic natural gradient descent, incorporating
097            a score-based prior and suitable approximations that reduces complexity.
098
             • We conduct extensive experiments to demonstrate the validity of the RMP framework theory
099
               and show that RMP outperforms state-of-the-art algorithms in reconstruction performance
100
               with lower computational complexity across various problems.

523   5.3           C OMPLEXITY VS P ERFORMANCE
524
      The complexity of diffusion-based methods dependent on the number of neural function evaluations
525   (NFE). RMP consists of T outer loops and each outer loop has Tin inner loops. The operations in
526   the outer loops can be ignored since only variable assignments and scalar calculation are involved.
527   In each inner loop, two main operations are involved: denoising with score network sθ (xk , σk )
528   and stochastic natural gradient descent step which requires the likelihood score. Thus, the NFE of
529   VE/VP-RMP is T Tin . To show the performance of RMPs with different NFE, we compare the curves
530   of NFE versus the performance of VP-RMP and DPS on SR task in Fig. 4. From the figure, we see
531   that RMP outperforms DPS significantly on all metrics for all NFEs. We also note that for the PSNR
532   metric, VP-RMP with 50 NFEs achieves a higher PSNR than DPS with 1000 NFEs.

534   6     C ONCLUSIONS
535
536   In this paper, we propose a score-based variational inference framework for inverse problems. By
537   optimizing the reverse KL divergence at each step of the reverse process and tracking the evolution
538   of reverse mean at each reverse step, a practical algorithm for general inverse problems is proposed.
539   Extensive experiments validate our theoretical results and demonstrate that our proposed algorithm
      achieves superior performance on various image reconstruction tasks.

1106   G    L IMITATIONS OF RMP
1107
1108   Although RMP achieves better performance with lower computational complexity compared to
1109   existing methods across a variety of inverse problems, as demonstrated by experiments, some
1110   limitations may restrict its application to general settings. As a plug and play method, RMP requires a
1111
       pre-trained score network for the data. In our experiments, the pre-trained score networks were taken
       from previous works. Training the score network can be challenging for certain data distributions,
1112
       and obtaining clean datasets can also be difficult in some fields. This is a common limitation shared
1113
       by all score-based methods for inverse problems, such as DPS, DDRM, and ΠGDM. Additionally, the
1114   complexity of solving the variational problems using stochastic NGD, which is involved in updating
1115   the mean parameter in RMP, may hinder its application in real-time scenarios.
