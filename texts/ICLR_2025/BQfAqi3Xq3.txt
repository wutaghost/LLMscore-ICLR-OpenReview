009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Indoor image geolocation, the task of determining the location of an indoor scene
012            based on visual content, presents unique challenges due to the constrained and
013            repetitive nature of indoor spaces. Current geolocation methods, while advanced
014            in outdoor contexts, struggle to perform accurately in indoor environments due to
015            the lack of diverse and representative indoor datasets. To address this gap, we in-
016            troduce INDOOR-3.6M, a large-scale dataset of geotagged indoor imagery span-
017
               ning various residential, commercial, and public spaces from around the world.
               In addition to the dataset, we propose a new sampling methodology to ensure ge-
018
               ographic diversity and balance. We also introduce INDOOR-15K, a benchmark
019
               for evaluating indoor-specific geolocation models. Finally, we demonstrate the
020            dataset’s utility by finetuning GeoCLIP using our dataset, which shows significant
021            improvements over the GeoCLIP baseline on our test set and other benchmark test
022            sets.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Image geolocation, which involves determining the geographic origin of a photograph based on its
028   visual content (Hays & Efros, 2008), is a critical vision task with a wide range of applications,
029   including forensic investigations and fraud detection. Current approaches to geolocation typically
030   follow either a retrieval-based or classification-based framework. Retrieval-based methods depend
031   on extensive geotagged image databases, employing similarity metrics to match query images with
032   known locations (Hays & Efros, 2008; Vo et al., 2017). In contrast, classification-based approaches
033
      discretize the Earth’s surface into geocells, treating geolocation as a multi-class classification task,
      requiring substantial training data per geocell to achieve high accuracy (Seo et al., 2018; Weyand
034
      et al., 2016).
035
036   As with other core computer vision tasks—such as object detection, semantic segmentation, scene
037   recognition and image classification—-the performance of geolocation models is closely tied to
038   the availability of large, diverse, and high-quality datasets. Datasets such as ImageNet (Krizhevsky
039
      et al., 2017), MS COCO (Lin et al., 2014), and Places (Zhou et al., 2017) have been pivotal in driving
      progress in their respective domains. However, for image geolocation, the need for comprehensive
040
      datasets is even more pronounced due to the task’s inherent complexity and global scope. The visual
041
      appearance of locations can vary dramatically depending on factors such as seasonal changes, time
042   of day, weather conditions, and human-induced modificationsPramanick et al. (2022). Additionally,
043   the global nature of the task requires representation across a wide variety of geographic regions, each

057   To support the training and evaluation of geolocation models, high-quality geotagged images an-

      geographic balance of these datasets directly influence the generalizability and accuracy of geolo-

060
      social media, curating datasets that are comprehensive, balanced, and representative of global geo-
061   graphic diversity remains challenging. Urban areas and popular tourist destinations are often over-
062   represented, while rural or less-frequented regions suffer from data scarcity, leading to models that
063   are less effective in underrepresented areas.
064
      While significant advances have been made in outdoor and mixed-environment (or hybrid) image
065
      geolocation, indoor geolocation remains under-explored and presents a unique set of challenges.
066
      Unlike outdoor environments, where landmarks, street signs, skylines, and natural features offer
067   rich contextual cues, indoor spaces are more constrained and visually repetitive. The interiors of
068   buildings, rooms, and enclosed areas typically lack the expansive contextual markers found in out-
069   door settings. Moreover, variations in design, layout, and lighting across indoor spaces introduce
070   additional layers of complexity. These factors highlight the need for datasets specifically tailored to
071   indoor environments.
072
      An indoor image typically depicts a scene from an enclosed or semi-enclosed space, such as a home,
073   office, or public building, and is defined by elements like furniture, walls, artificial lighting, and in-
074   terior structural elements. These spaces can range from small rooms to vast halls, each with distinct
075   characteristics. The line between indoor and outdoor environments can also blur in transitional
076   spaces like covered patios or parking garages, where structural openness is combined with indoor
077   elements such as artificial lighting and furniture, producing environments that straddle the boundary
078   between the enclosed and the open.
079   The feasibility of indoor image geolocation lies in the distinctive visual markers inherent in the
080   design, utilization and layout of interior spaces. Regional, cultural, religious, economic, and political
081   factors shape architectural styles, materials, decor, and spatial layouts, resulting in distinct visual
082   characteristics that vary geographically. Furniture, decor, artwork, religious symbols, and fixtures
083   like electrical outlets provide valuable locational clues. Additionally, the layout of indoor spaces
084   is often tailored to human needs and influenced by local aesthetics, making their visual structure
085   identifiable and learnable. Despite lacking the prominent landmarks typical of outdoor settings,
086   indoor environments offer a rich array of details that can support effective geolocation.
087   Given the current emphasis on outdoor geolocation and the limited focus on indoor environments, it
088   is evident that a geographically diverse dataset dedicated to indoor image geolocation is crucial for
089   advancing this field. Such a dataset would capture the unique characteristics of indoor spaces across
090   a broad range of geographic locations and functional areas. Its development represents a critical step
091   toward addressing the existing gap in indoor geolocation research and enables the creation of models
092   capable of fine-grained localization of complex, enclosed environments. To empower research into
093
      indoor image geolocation we make the following contributions:
      • We introduce INDOOR-3.6M, a dataset of geotagged indoor imagery featuring diverse living
094
         spaces, functional areas, leisure and public facilities. This extensive collection, enriched with
095
         comprehensive multimodal metadata, will empower indoor-specific geolocation research, address-
096      ing a critical gap in the current literature.
097   • We propose a sampling strategy that offers a method for obtaining geographically representative
098      samples from geographically biased datasets. Our approach considers both land area and popula-
099      tion distribution, ensuring a balanced representation of images.
100   • We present INDOOR-15K, a geographically representative benchmark test set designed to eval-
101      uate the performance of both indoor-specific and hybrid geolocation models on diverse indoor
102
         scenes. This benchmark provides a standardized evaluation framework for fairly evaluating and
         comparing advancements in indoor geolocation research.
103
      • Finally, we finetune GeoCLIP–yielding a specialized GeoCLIP model that outperforms the Geo-
104      CLIP baseline across all levels of geographic granularity, establishing a benchmark for indoor
105      image geolocation and paving the way for future innovations in this field.
106

      In recent years, image geolocation has seen remarkable advancements, driven by a convergence of
111
      cutting-edge computer vision techniques, deep learning architectures, and the availability of large-
112   scale geotagged image datasets. These innovations have significantly improved the ability of models
113   to accurately predict the geographic origin of images. The evolution of geolocation techniques has
114   largely been defined by two primary paradigms: retrieval-based approaches and classification-based
115   approaches. While retrieval-based methods rely on matching query images with similar images
116   in a large geotagged database, classification-based methods divide the Earth’s surface into discrete
117   regions or geocells (Weyand et al., 2016), treating geolocation as a multi-class classification prob-
118   lem. More recently, hybrid approaches have emerged, combining the strengths of both paradigms to
119   enhance geolocation accuracy.
120   State-of-the-art systems like PIGEON/PIGEOTTO (Haas et al., 2023) and Geoclip (Vivanco Cepeda
121   et al., 2024) exemplify this advancement. These models utilize CLIP Vision Transformers (ViTs)
122   (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020; Radford et al., 2021) and leverage large-scale geotagged image datasets
123   to infer geographic locations based on visual content. The success of these systems highlights
124   the effectiveness of modern neural architectures in capturing complex visual features tied to spe-
125   cific geographic locations, and the importance of combining such architectures with comprehensive,
126
      high-quality datasets.

194
      cessor IM2GPS3k (Vo et al., 2017), along with subsets of YFCC100M like YFCC4K (Vo et al.,
      2017) and YFCC26K (Muller-Budack et al., 2018), have been instrumental in evaluating geoloca-
195
      tion systems. However, these datasets predominantly comprise outdoor imagery, rendering them in-
196
      adequate for assessing indoor-specific geolocation tasks. Indoor environments present unique chal-
197   lenges, necessitating the interpretation of more complex and nuanced visual features, including vari-
198   ations in room layout, furniture arrangements, lighting conditions, and decorative elements. Con-
199   sequently, to facilitate accurate indoor geolocation, it is imperative to develop specialized indoor-
200   specific datasets for both training and benchmarking purposes.

203   3    DATASET OVERVIEW
204
205   To achieve accurate and reliable indoor image geolocation, a large and diverse dataset covering var-
206   ious indoor environments is essential. The INDOOR-3.6M dataset addresses this need by being
207   agnostic to specific indoor scenes, enabling generalization across a wide range of locations, includ-
208   ing residential, office, shopping, leisure, and public spaces. Geolocation data, provided either as
209
      GPS coordinates or text-based location labels, is included alongside textual information such as de-
      scriptions and metadata as supplementary features. This multimodal approach enhances the dataset’s
210
      versatility, particularly for tasks that benefit from both visual and textual data. It is important to note
211
      that the dataset does not explicitly identify specific locations in the manner typical of place recog-
212   nition tasks. However, the accompanying text, and descriptions may contain useful information that
213   could inform place recognition applications.

      3.1   DATA SOURCES AND COLLECTION METHODS
217
218   The INDOOR-3.6Mdataset was constructed using three primary sources: Flickr (flickr.com, 2024),
219   a popular photo-sharing platform where users upload and tag images with metadata; Wikidata
220   (wikidata.org, 2024), a free, collaborative knowledge base that provides structured data to support
221   Wikipedia and other Wikimedia projects; and Booking.com (booking.com, 2024), a popular hotel
222   booking website. For the image repositories (Flickr and Wikidata), we formulated search terms
223   based on indoor scene categories from the Places365 dataset1 , and appended ”indoor” to categories
224
      typically associated with outdoor environments. To ensure usability and proper attribution, we re-
      stricted our search to images with Creative Commons licenses and included only those with latitude
225
      and longitude coordinates. However, the initial search terms yielded few results because of these
226
      constraints. To address this, we manually refined the search terms, generalizing specific categories
227   like ”ski resort” to broader terms such as ”resort” and expanding our vocabulary with synonyms and
228   colloquial terms. Additionally, we introduced new categories that seemed relevant but were absent
229   from the original list. Productive search terms included ”living room”, ”indoor”, ”villa”, ”cottage”,
230   ”diner”, ”office space”, and ”beach house”. For the web scraping component, we employed country
231   labels to initialize a crawler that retrieved images from search results for each country.
232
      This collection process yielded approximately 10 million candidate images combined. In addition
233   to visual data, we also collected associated textual metadata from these platforms, such as user-
234   generated tags, descriptions, and captions. The textual data varies significantly in length, language,
235   and detail, ranging from brief labels to detailed narratives or contextual information.
236
      To ensure the dataset’s focus remained on indoor scenes, we filtered the candidate images using
237
      the Places365 ResNet indoor/outdoor image classifier (Zhou et al., 2017). Recognizing that the
238
      distinction between indoor and outdoor scenes can sometimes be blurred, we used the classifier to
239   quantify the ”indoorness” of each image. We retained only those images with a probability of being
240   indoor, P (indoor) ≥ 0.5. Additionally, we recorded this likelihood score for each image in the
241   metadata, placing images on a continuum between relatively indoor spaces (P (indoor) = 0.5) and
242   purely indoor spaces (P (indoor) = 1.0).
243
244   3.2   S CALE AND D ISTRIBUTION
245
246   The INDOOR-3.6Mdataset comprises 3.6 million images spanning a wide variety of scenes from
247   223 countries worldwide, uploaded between 1978 and 2024. While the dataset aims to be represen-
248   tative of indoor environments, it is not entirely geographically representative due to its reliance on
249   online sources (See Figure 2a). This dependence introduces inherent biases in geographic distribu-
250
      tion, resulting in over-representation of regions with a strong digital footprint and larger populations
      such as United States (which represent 30% of the data), and under-representation of areas with less
251
      online activity or smaller populations. Figure 3a illustrates the dataset’s distribution according to the
252
      MIT indoor scenes label set. A significant portion of the images are labeled as ”tv studio”, which
253   predominantly corresponds to spaces where a TV is present—-commonly living rooms.

343   4.1   S AMPLING S TRATEGY
344
345   Our sampling methodology integrates both population density and land area to ensure a repre-
346   sentative distribution of GPS points across countries. This approach accounts for the fact that
347   countries with larger populations should receive proportionally more sampling points, while also
348   considering the spatial diversity inherent in nations with expansive land areas. We account for
349
      population in our sampling strategy because it serves as a proxy for the density of human-made
      structures and indoor environments. Highly populated areas are more likely to contain a di-
350
      verse range of indoor spaces, such as residential buildings, commercial centers, and public facil-
351
      ities. The allocation  of GPS points foroeach country is determined using the following formula:
352

354   the population of country i, Ai denotes the land area of country i in square kilometers, α is the
355   weighting factor assigned to population, β is the weighting factor assigned to land area, N is the
356
      total number of GPS points to be sampled across all countries, n is the total number of countries in
      the study, and Nmin is the minimum number of samples per country i, to prevent under-sampling
357
358   This formulation allows for the calibration of sample sizes based on the relative importance of
359   population and land area through the parameters α and β. For example, setting α = 1 and β = 0
360   results in a sampling strategy driven exclusively by population, while setting α = 0 and β = 1
361   yields a distribution solely based on land area.
362   In constructing our test set, we chose Nmin = 3, α = 0.3 and β = 0.7, favoring land area over
363   population in determining the sample size for each country. Population and land area data were
364   obtained from publicly available sources provided by the World Bank (The World Bank, 2024).
365   Once the number of points per country was determined, we sample uniformly within each country’s
366   available data points. The weighting factors of 0.7 for land area and 0.3 for population were chosen
367   to prioritize geographic diversity, as represented by land area. As a result, our approach prevents
368
      the over representation of small, densely populated countries and the under representation of large,
      sparsely populated nations.
369
370   The choice of population and land area as proxies for scene visual diversity reflects the idea that
371   highly populated countries tend to feature a broader range of indoor environments, shaped by di-
372   verse cultural, economic, and other social. Similarly, larger countries encompass varied geographic
373   regions, often translating into more diverse architectural and interior styles. These provide a practi-
374
      cal heuristic for achieving geographic balance without requiring additional data collection.
375   Our sampling strategy resulted in a dataset containing 15,025 GPS points, offering improved spa-
376   tial representation of indoor imagery compared to existing benchmark datasets such as IM2GPS3K
377   (3,000 points) and YFCC26k (26,000 points). Figure 6 illustrates the improved spatial distribution
      achieved through our methodology.

391   4.2    E XPERIMENTS
392
393   In this study, we fine-tune GeoCLIP to establish a baseline for indoor geolocation using a subset
394   of the INDOOR-3.6M dataset, following the sampling strategy described. GeoCLIP was selected
395   for its state-of-the-art performance in environment-agnostic geolocation. We retained most of the
396   training parameters from Vivanco Cepeda et al. (2024), including a constant learning rate of 1e-6 and
397   a batch size of 256. The model converged after 10 epochs and outperformed the original GeoCLIP
398   on our test set. Table 3 highlights the improved performance across all levels of granularity.
399   We also assessed the zero-shot classification performance of CLIP on a location classification task.
400   For this, using the INDOOR-3.6M dataset, we divided the Earth into semantic geocells based on
401   the approach in Haas et al., ensuring each geocell contained between 1,000 and 2,000 images. This
402   resulted in approximately 1,300 geocells. We utilized the image encoder from the clip-vit-large-
403   patch14 Radford et al. (2021) architecture to perform zero-shot classification of geocells. The en-
404   coder extracted visual embeddings, which were then used to predict geocells without additional
405
      training. For GPS prediction, the latitude and longitude of an image were approximated by averag-
      ing the GPS coordinates of all images within the predicted geocell. The results of these experiments
406
      are presented in Table 3.
407
408   The study underscores the potential of domain-specific training in enhancing geolocation mod-
409   els, particularly for indoor environments. Our experiments with GeoCLIP on the INDOOR-
410   3.6M dataset reveal critical insights into model performance across various geographic scales, with
411
      the fine-tuned GeoCLIP consistently outperforming its counterparts. The reduction in mean dis-
      tance error from 4089.11 km for the baseline GeoCLIP to 3598.02 km for the fine-tuned version
412
      is especially remarkable given the inherent complexity of indoor geolocation. The most striking
413
      observations emerge at broader scales, where fine-tuned GeoCLIP demonstrates pronounced gains,
414   such as improving continent-level accuracy from 53% to 61% and country-level accuracy from 25%
415   to 35%. These results highlight the ability of the model to leverage the diversity and richness of
416   INDOOR-3.6M to capture geographically meaningful features. While the gains at finer scales, such
417   as street and city levels, are more modest, the consistent improvements across all levels reinforce the
418   importance of domain-specific datasets in overcoming the unique challenges of indoor geolocation.
419
      To evaluate the impact of the proposed sampling strategy, we conducted ablation studies using
420   datasets prepared with random sampling and the strategic sampling methodology described in the
421   Appendix. The model finetuned on the dataset created using our sampling strategy yields better
422   performance on geolocating both over represented classes and underrepresented classes.

      5        C HALLENGES
475
476
477
      The development and utilization of large-scale indoor image datasets for geolocation present several
      challenges. Firstly, the INDOOR-3.6M dataset, like many large-scale datasets sourced from online
478
      platforms, exhibits significant geographic and demographic biases. This bias arises from the
479
      over representation of regions with higher internet penetration, tourism, and socioeconomic status,
480   leading to under-representation of areas with limited digital footprints. This imbalance hinders
481   the performance of geolocation models in underrepresented regions. Another critical issue is the
482   validation of GPS data. In datasets sourced from user-uploaded images on photo-sharing sites, the
483   accuracy of geotags could be unreliable. This can stem from a variety of factors, including device
484   limitations, poor satellite coverage, or user errors in manually tagging locations. Since the GPS data
485   in these platforms cannot be easily verified or cross-checked for accuracy, this remains a pervasive
      problem across geolocation datasets which rely on user-generated content, potentially leading to


                                                                  9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      discrepancies between model predictions and the true locations. Hotel booking and rental platforms
487   provide more reliable and verifiable GPS information, but are limited to residential scenes.
488
489   The emergence of large vision models like Vision Transformers (ViTs) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) and
490
      CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) introduces challenges related to potential data leakage. These mod-
      els are pretrained on vast datasets scraped from the internet, including Flickr-sourced collections
491
      like YFCC100M. Consequently, there is no guarantee that new publicly sourced datasets, such as
492
      INDOOR-3.6M, do not introduce a data leak when fine-tuning such models. This overlap could arti-
493   ficially inflate performance metrics during model evaluation. To mitigate this risk for our benchmark
494   test set, we deliberately selected images captured after 2017, the publication year of YFCC100M,
495   reducing the likelihood of overlap with this widely used dataset.
496
      Indoor geolocation datasets introduce additional difficulties for geolocation systems due to intra-
497
      class variation. Unlike outdoor environments, where variations are often limited to views in the
498
      four cardinal directions (North, South, East, and West), indoor spaces exhibit far more complexity.
499   In settings like hotels, different floors and rooms have distinct layouts, styles, and views, making it
500   harder for models to establish consistent visual cues. This issue is exacerbated by the absence of
501   clear landmarks, necessitating more nuanced feature extraction. Moreover, indoor environments are
502   more subject to temporal dynamics. Frequent renovations, redecorations, and repurposing result
503   in visual instability, which can quickly render models obsolete. Continuous updating or adaptive
504   learning is required to ensure that models remain effective over time. To truly advance the field
505   of indoor geolocation, it is crucial for future work to actively confront these issues, ensuring that
506   models are both reliable and adaptable across diverse and evolving environments.

508   6   E THICS
509
510   The INDOOR-3.6M dataset has been developed with careful attention to ethical considerations.
511   The dataset contains geotagged indoor images sourced from public platforms, without the intention
512   of identifying specific individuals or private spaces. We provides URLs and metadata information,
513   rather than the raw image files, to prevent direct misuse, protect privacy, and avoid unauthorized
514   redistribution of sensitive content. License and owner information from included to allow proper
515   attribution. Geographic bias is acknowledged, particularly the over-representation of urban areas,
516
      and researchers are encouraged to apply sampling strategies and imbalance mitigation techniques
      to achieve fairer regional representation in model training. The dataset is strictly for research pur-
517
      poses, and misuse for purposes such as unauthorized surveillance or invasive applications is strongly
518
      discouraged. Researchers are urged to handle the data responsibly, especially during algorithm de-
519   velopment and when implementing public-facing technologies.
520
521   There are concerns about the harmful applications of this dataset for geolocation technology, includ-
522
      ing privacy violations and unauthorized surveillance. We encourage researchers to remain mindful
      of the societal impact of their work, implementing safeguards to prevent abuse and adhering to pri-
523
      vacy laws and ethical standards. It is essential that the research community stays actively engaged in
524
      discussions about the ethical development and use of indoor geolocation technologies, ensuring that
525   advancements prioritize individual privacy and security. Misuse for invasive purposes is explicitly
526   discouraged.

      A.1   S AMPLING S TRATEGY

652   To evaluate the impact of the proposed strategic sampling methodology, we conducted experiments
653   comparing its performance with that of random sampling. Both sampling methods were used to pre-
654   pare three datasets for fine-tuning the GeoCLIP model. Performance was assessed across multiple
655   geographic granularities, ranging from street-level (1 km) to continent-level (2500 km), and further
656   analyzed for regions with high and low data representation to highlight the strengths and limitations
657
      of each approach. The averaged results are presented in Tables 4 and 5.
658   The results indicate that the proposed sampling method outperforms random sampling at finer gran-
659   ularities, such as street and city levels, in high-representation regions. For example, the proposed
660   method achieves a street-level accuracy of 0.05 compared to 0.03 for random sampling and a city-
661   level accuracy of 0.13 compared to 0.11. This improvement suggests that the proposed method’s
662   balanced geographic representation allows the model to capture features more effectively even in
