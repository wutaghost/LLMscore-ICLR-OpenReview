009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011             The use of synthetic data has been crucial in achieving recent state-of-the-art
012             breakthroughs. However, relying solely on a single oracle teacher model for data
013             generation can lead to issues such as model collapse and bias propagation. These
014             problems are particularly pronounced in multilingual contexts, where no single
015             teacher model performs optimally across all languages. In this study, we pro-
016             pose a solution through multilingual arbitrage, which exploits performance varia-
017
                tions among multiple models for each language. By strategically routing samples
                through a diverse set of models, each possessing unique strengths in different
018
                languages, we address these challenges. Our extensive experiments with state-
019
                of-the-art models demonstrate that our arbitrage techniques significantly enhance
020             performance compared to relying on a single teacher model. Our multilingual
021             arbitrage techniques result in large gains of up to 80% win-rates over state-of-art
022             proprietary and widely adopted open weight models such as Gemma 2, Llama 3.1,
023             Mistral v0.3. These gains, achieved through multilingual arbitrage and averaged
024             across all languages, were most substantial in the less-resourced languages within
025             our pool.

029
      Throughout our lives, we are guided by many teachers, each contributing distinct insights and ex-
030
      pertise to our personal and professional growth. For specialized skills, such as becoming a doctor or
031
      mastering culinary arts, we seek out experts who provide targeted guidance. In contrast, synthetic
032   data generation often relies on a single teacher model to impart knowledge to a student model. This
033   approach can lead to the passive transfer of both the strengths and limitations inherent in the teacher
034   model, as highlighted in various studies (Shumailov et al., 2023; Magister et al., 2023; Shimabucoro
035   et al., 2024; Gerstgrasser et al., 2024). Moreover, it assumes that a single model can effectively teach
036   all relevant skills, which may not always be the case.

055   scape of multilingual model development has resulted in a variety of models: large-scale models
056   that support multiple languages (Xue et al., 2020; Scao et al., 2022; Shliazhko et al., 2022; Li et al.,
057   2023; Üstün et al., 2024), frontier models with some multilingual capabilities that are not specifically
058   optimized (Armengol-Estapé et al., 2021; Chowdhery et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022; Team et al.,
059   2024), and models focused on regional language families (Adelani et al., 2021; Mirzakhalov et al.,
060   2021; Cahyawijaya et al., 2022). As a result, it is often unclear how to determine which model to use
061   to maximize performance for a given language. Relying on a single model can also further amplify
062   disparities in treatments between languages, as models may perform well on some language but not
063
      have coverage for others. Performance tends to be critical for the quality of synthetic data, which
      can enable further progress in those languages by making data more ubiquitous over time (Alaa
064
      et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2023; Bukharin & Zhao, 2023; Li et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024).
065
066   In this work, we take a wider view of synthetic data generation. Instead of viewing model
067   distillation as simply transferring data from a single oracle to a student, we reframe the problem
068   within this heterogeneous landscape as learning how to optimize sampling for a desired part of the
069
      data distribution from an ensemble of teachers. Multilingual settings serve as an ideal case study for
      this approach due to the distinct boundaries between languages compared to tasks. We anticipate
070
      that our arbitrage techniques will enhance performance in scenarios where it is uncommon for a
071
      single model to be state-of-the-art across all tasks.
072
073   We introduce the concept of multilingual arbitrage, which leverages performance differences among
074   models for a given language. For each language, we utilize a pool of models as potential teachers
075
      and evaluate strategic sampling methods by routing to different models. This optimized distribution
      is then used to instruction fine-tune a new multilingual model, aiming to surpass the performance of
076
      a single multilingual model across all languages. This approach raises the question: Can strategic
077
      sampling from multiple models outperform individual models?
078
079   We conducted exhaustive experiments across 15 languages using 9 state-of-the-art multilingual mod-
080   els to evaluate our method. Our extensive evaluations included LLM-as-an-evaluator win rates,
081
      discriminative tasks, and textual characteristics. Our key findings and contributions are as follows:
082
083          • We introduce the concept of “multilingual arbitrage” which significantly outper-
084            forms traditional single teacher distillation. Our experiments demonstrate that arbitrage
085            methods surpass single-teacher models. Specifically, our reward-based routing technique
086            achieved an average improvement of 56.5% in generative win rates and a 28.1% improve-
087
               ment over the best single-teacher model. Additionally, student models trained using this
               technique exhibited an average absolute gain in win rates of 32.02% (a relative gain of
088
               153.5%) over various state-of-the-art models, and 6.9% absolute improvement (15.9% rel-
089
               ative improvement) over the best model highlighting the significant performance advantage
090            of our approach.
091
092          • Not all arbitrage techniques are equal. We evaluate the performance of various arbi-
093            trage techniques against a lower bound baseline of random routing. Reward-based routing,
094            fixed routing with predefined set of expert teachers, and learned routing improved abso-
095            lute performance by 30.6%, 22.9% and 13.4% (relative performance by 119.5%, 76.8%,
096            and 40.6%) respectively. While reward-based routing, though resource-intensive, was the
097            most effective, our results show that the more efficient reward-guided learned routing can
098
               achieve impressive performance gains without needing to generate all completions from
               each model.
099
100
             • Arbitrage improves or maintains textual characteristics. We analyze the textual charac-
101
               teristics of student model generations by calculating various statistics scores, examining the
102            effects of instruction fine-tuning (IFT) with multilingual arbitrage on text verbosity, read-
103            ability, and lexical diversity. Our findings show that reward-based routing results in a 14.1%
104            increase in the number of tokens in generated text, while learned routing leads to a 68.4%
105            increase compared to both single-teacher generations (averaged across all single teachers)
106            and random routing. Additionally, we observe increases in lexical diversity scores: reward-
107            based routing improves scores by 6%, and learned routing by 4.2% compared to single
               teachers, and by 13.4% and 11.5% compared to random routing, respectively.

      2       M ETHODOLOGY
116
117
      Our primary goal is to train a high-performing multilingual student model S. Given a set of input
118   prompts P = {pi }N                                                                  N
                          i=1 , we generate a corresponding set of completions C = {ci }i=1 using a pool
119                                          M
      of potential teacher models T = {Tj }j=1 . These prompt-completion pairs (pi , ci ) will then be used
120   to fine-tune S. For each prompt pi ∈ P , we aim to identify the specific teacher model Tj ∈ T that
121   produces the highest quality completion ci .
122
      We consider that each teacher model Tj may not perform uniformly across all regions of interest R
123
      in the data distribution. Therefore, we aim to minimize the empirical error E[Pj (R)], where Pj (R)
124
      represents the performance of teacher model Tj in region R, over the broader distribution D. This
125   ensures robustness and generalization beyond the i.i.d. training sample Diid . This approach allows
126   us to select the most suitable teacher model for each prompt, optimizing the training of our student
127   model S. We note that this amounts to optimization in the data space and allows for on-the-fly
128   creation of dataset properties to minimize sensitivity to distribution drift.

      2.1      ROUTING M ETHODS
147
148   The crux of the problem of multilingual arbitrage is: how do you route prompts to the most calibrated
149   teacher model for each prompt? We exhaustively benchmark different routing strategies which we
150   introduce briefly below:
151
      Fixed Routing. In practice, one might choose a fixed model, such as T2 , to process all input prompts
152   in P . This can be reasonable if T2 demonstrates significantly better overall performance for a ma-
153   jority of the prompts. In the multilingual case, this setting is one in which we can pre-determine
154   the best model for each language based on their known strengths, enabling us to use a fixed rout-
155   ing strategy for each prompt deterministically by choosing the appropriate teacher model according
156   to the prompt’s language. However, in real-world settings it is not always possible to know what
157   models are relatively strong at different languages in advance.
158   Reward-based Routing. Next we consider the more realistic setting which assumes that we cannot
159   pre-determine a fixed routing strategy. Instead, we rely on a reward model for routing. For each
160   pi we generate a completion from each of the teacher models in T and then select ci to be the
161

      completion with the highest score given by some ranking method. In our case, we use a proprietary
163   reward model (Cohere May 2024) which is competitive with top-scoring state-of-the-art reward
164   models on the RewardBench Leaderboard (Lambert et al., 2024)2 . We intentionally use a separate
165   reward model for routing from the model that we use for our LLM-as-a-judge evaluation (GPT-4-
166   Turbo3 ) given the known biases incurred by using the same model for both (Bansal et al., 2023;
167   Verga et al., 2024; Shimabucoro et al., 2024).
168
      Learned-Routing. The disadvantage of reward-based routing is that it requires generating a full set
169   of M completions for each prompt where M = |T |. As a more efficient alternative, we explore
170   the merits of a learned router which instead trains a router model based on scores produced by the
171   reward model which is proposed by (Lu et al., 2024). In this method, the router model learns to
172   predict the reward conditioned only on the prompt pi , thereby determining the most suitable teacher
173   model Tj without the need to generate multiple completions based upon historical routing trends.
174   The router R(pi ) is defined to select the teacher model Tj that maximizes the expected reward for a
175   given prompt pi . Formally, for each pi ∈ P , the selected model Tj is given by:
176                                         Tj = arg max R(pi , T ).
177                                                   T ∈T

178   This approach leverages the complementary strengths of the models in T and ensures that each
179   prompt is routed to the model most likely to produce the highest quality completion. By integrating
180   reward model ranking with query routing, reward-guided Learned-Routing enhances the efficiency
181   of the LLM ensemble, reducing computational overhead while ensuring effective training of the
182   student model S.
183   To train our learned-routing model, we collect a training dataset of diverse prompts and then generate
184   completions from each of the candidate models in the teacher pool. Given a prompt from our training
185   set, we obtain a scalar reward for each candidate model generation as in the following:
186
                                                            |T |
187                              ri = {RM (pi , Tj (pi ))}j=1 ,    i = 1, . . . , N                      (1)
188
      where ri ∈ R|T | . We then train our router R on the training data with Kullback-Leibler (KL)
189
      divergence as the loss function:
190
191                                  L(pi , ri ) = KL(R(pi ), softmax(ri )).                             (2)
192
      This approach improves the quality of synthetic data while maintaining computational efficiency
193   during inference, introducing only minimal overhead compared to traditional reward model ranking
194   methods, which is training the router model. However, this overhead is well compensated during
195   inference because learned routing only generates samples from the routed model, rather than from
196   each model in the pool. As a result, the generation cost is reduced to 1/M , where M is the number
197   of models in the pool.

212
      et al., 2024), and Gemma 2 (Team et al., 2024). For larger-scale experiments with expanded language
213   coverage, we choose top-performing open-weight models: CommandR+, Gemma2 27B (Team et al.,

234   Fixed Router Model Pool. In our fixed router experiments, we assume prior knowledge of which

239

243

244

      3.4      E VALUATIONS
282
283   Open-ended Generation Win rates. Beyond traditional NLP tasks, we aim to evaluate the open-
284   ended generation capabilities of the student models, focusing on their ability to produce unstruc-
285   tured and long-form responses. For this evaluation, we use GPT-4 as an LLM-judge to measure
286   pairwise win rates between two model generations. We evaluate on the target language subset of
287   the Multilingual Dolly-200 Eval dataset (Singh et al., 2024; Üstün et al., 2024). This 200 instance
288   evaluation dataset is a held-out curated sample from the Dolly-15k dataset (Conover et al., 2023).
289   These prompts are open-ended and capture general-purpose non-code use cases. Hence, evaluation
290   using this dataset is a valuable proxy for how multilingual arbitrage impacts more fluid and often
291
      open-ended asks.
292   Discriminative Tasks. To evaluate our models on completely unseen tasks, we follow Muen-
293   nighoff et al. (2023) and use XNLI (Conneau et al., 2018), XCOPA (Ponti et al., 2020), and XSto-
294   ryCloze (Lin et al., 2021) datasets targeting natural language inference, commonsense reasoning and
295   sentence completion respectively. These unseen tasks are crucial for evaluating the effectiveness of
296   IFT in improving a model’s reasoning and comprehension capabilities as they test the model’s abil-
297
      ity to discriminate between different possible interpretations or outcomes. For all unseen tasks, we
      report zero-shot performance.

488
      To gain a holistic view of how multilingual arbitrage affects model generation characteristics, we
489   use the TextDescriptives framework from Hansen et al. (2023) to calculate various textual features.
490   We report average statistics, including token count, readability, and lexical diversity scores. Detailed
491   analyses of the textual characteristics of generations are provided in Appendix A.7.

494   5     R ELATED W ORK
495
496
497
      LLM circularity. The issue of LLM circularity, where models influence others through distilled
      data, has gained attention, focusing on model degradation and self-preference (Dohmatob et al.,
498
      2024; Briesch et al., 2023; Shumailov et al., 2023). Recursive training impairs performance by
499
      neglecting long-tail knowledge (Briesch et al., 2023; Bertrand et al., 2024; Shumailov et al., 2024),
500   leading to a loss of diversity (Guo et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2024). (Shimabucoro et al., 2024) explore
501   how the transfer of characteristics via passive inheritance occurs when synthetic data generated
502   by different LLMs is involved. By considering the issues highlighted in these studies, we aim to
503   optimize synthetic data generation by selecting the most calibrated teacher model from a pool of
504   LLMs in a multilingual setting.
505
      Instruction Fine-tuning (IFT) and Multilingual Synthetic Data. IFT enhances LLM performance
506   and generalization (Sanh et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2021; Mishra et al., 2021; Min et al., 2021; Ouyang
507   et al., 2022), relying on task diversity (Longpre et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b; Chung et al., 2022),
508   complexity (Xu et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023), and quality (Zhou et al., 2023; Taori et al., 2023).
509   While validated mainly for English tasks, there is a growing focus on multilingual contexts (Üstün
510   et al., 2024). Efforts address multilingual instruction dataset scarcity (Singh et al., 2024). Research
511   on English synthetic data generation is extensive (Gao et al., 2023; Anaby-Tavor et al., 2019), but its
512   multilingual impact is less understood (Kaddour & Liu, 2023). Recent studies explore multilingual
513   data with a single teacher model (Aryabumi et al., 2024) and for preference training (Aakanksha
514
      et al., 2024). In this work, we strategically sample from a diverse pool of models, each with unique
      strengths across different languages, to generate high-quality synthetic instruction data. Our research
515
      diverges by concentrating on multilingual synthetic instruction data generation from an ecosystem
516
      view rather than a single teacher.
517
518   Large Language Model Ensemble. Ensembling LLMs leverages individual strengths, but limited
519   research exists on these effective strategies. Frameworks combine LLMs using pairwise ranking
520
      and generative fusion (Jiang et al., 2023), sequential inference (Chen et al., 2023), and supervised
      learning for output fusion (Wang et al., 2023a). Routers select the best LLM candidate based on
521
      benchmarks (Shnitzer et al., 2023). Relevant work proposes reward model-guided routing for task
522
      strengths (Lu et al., 2024). Our work explores various routing strategies beyond reward-based rout-
523   ing, in multilingual contexts.

      6     C ONCLUSION
527
528
529   In this work, we introduce the concept of multilingual arbitrage, which strategically utilizes perfor-
530   mance variations across different models for a given language to sample from a pool of teacher mod-
531
      els, thereby generating a superior dataset for training effective student models. Our extensive experi-
      ments across 23 languages demonstrate the efficacy of our routing strategies, significantly enhancing
532
      student models’ performance across all benchmarks. We observed notable gains in both open-ended
533
      generation tasks and discriminative benchmarks compared to the traditional single-teacher data gen-
534   eration and training method. Furthermore, additional analysis of textual characteristics and evalua-
535   tion on unseen discriminative tasks confirm that our instruction fine-tuned students not only retain
536   their initial capabilities but also improve their multilingual generation skills. Our findings under-
537   score the value of strategic sampling, particularly in scenarios where a diverse pool of models can
538   excel at different parts of the data distribution. We expect arbitrage techniques will yield substantial
539   gains in addressing out-of-distribution challenges and in handling rare or underrepresented parts of
      the data distribution.

1135
1136         1. Gunning Fog Index (Gunning, 1968) is a readability test that estimates the years of formal
1137            education required to understand a piece of text on the first reading. Gunning-Fog uses
1138            sentence length and prevalence of complex words to estimate the complexity of the text
1139            and assign a grade level between 0 and 20. A score of 17-18 indicates college graduate-
1140            level text.
1141         2. Rix (Anderson, 1983) calculates readability based on the number of words with more than
1142            six characters divided by the number of sentences in the text. A score of 5 corresponds
1143            to a grade level of around 10, while a score of 7 or higher indicates the need for a higher
1144            educational level to comprehend.
1145         3. Measure of Textual Lexical Diversity (MTLD) score (Shen, 2022) helps tracking
1146            changes in vocabulary by reflecting the average number of words in a sequence that main-
1147            tains a certain type-token ratio (TTR), a measure of vocabulary variety (McCarthy & Jarvis,
1148            2010). An MLTD score of 50 can be considered as moderate lexical diversity.
