                                                                 A BSTRACT
008
009
              Traditionally, players in first-person shooter (FPS) games have been limited to
010
              communicating with AI companions using simple commands like “attack,” “de-
011
              fend,” or “retreat” due to the constraints of existing input methods such as hotkeys
012           and command wheels. One major limitation of these simple commands is the
013           lack of target specificity, as the numerous targets in a 3D virtual environment are
014           difficult to specify using existing input methods. This limitation hinders players’
015           ability to issue complex tactical instructions such as “clear the second floor,” “take
016           cover behind that tree,” or “retreat to the river.” To overcome this limitation, this
017           paper introduces the AI Companion with Voice Interaction (ACVI), the first-ever
018           AI system that allows players to interact with FPS AI companions through natu-
019           ral language. Deployed in the popular FPS game Arena Breakout: Infinite, this
020
              revolutionary feature creates the most immersive experience for players, enabling
              them to work with human-like AI. ACVI is not confined to executing limited com-
021
              mands through simple rule-based systems. Instead, it allows players to engage
022
              in real-time voice interactions with AI teammates. By integrating various natural
023           language processing techniques within a confidence-based selection framework, it
024           achieves rapid and accurate decomposition of complex commands and intent rea-
025           soning. Moreover, ACVI employs a multi-modal dynamic entity retrieval method
026           for environmental perception, aligning human intentions with decision-making
027           elements. It can accurately comprehend complex voice commands and delivers
028           real-time behavioral responses and vocal feedback to provide close tactical col-
029           laboration to players. Additionally, it can identify more than 17,000 objects in the
030           game, including buildings, vehicles, grasslands, and collectible items, and has the
031
              ability to accurately distinguish different colors and materials.

      Over the past few decades, video games have attracted billions of players, and the role of game AI
055   has become increasingly important (Ontanón et al., 2013). Game AI in board games like Go (Silver
056   et al., 2016) and real-time strategy games like StarCraft II (Vinyals et al., 2019) have already reached
057   expert player levels. However, most game AI systems focus on competing with players, and re-
058   search on AI companion systems in games has been largely overlooked (Gao et al., 2023; Ashktorab
059   et al., 2020). In complex Multi-player Online Battle Arena (MOBA) (Berner et al., 2019; do Nasci-
060   mento Silva & Chaimowicz, 2017) or First-person Shooter (FPS) games (Lample & Chaplot, 2017),
061   players need the companionship of teammates, and meticulous cooperation between players and
062   teammates is crucial for achieving goals and an immersive experience. Therefore, attaining reliable
063
      human-AI collaboration in games and creating an AI companionship system that can understand
      human instructions and provide quick and accurate assistance is a promising research field.
064
065   In traditional FPS games (Tong et al., 2011), players typically use simple commands such as hotkeys
066   and command wheels to interact with AI companions, for example, by sending quick commands to
067   attack, defend, or retreat. The primary limitation of these simple commands is the lack of target
068   specificity, as the numerous targets in a 3D virtual environment are difficult to specify using existing
069
      input methods. This limitation hinders the player’s ability to issue complex tactical commands such
      as “clear the second floor”, “take cover behind that tree”, or “retreat to the river”, creating a gap in
070
      human-AI collaboration and a poor gaming experience.
071
072   To address this problem, we propose AI Companion with Voice Interaction (ACVI), the first-ever
073   FPS AI companion that can interact with players through natural language, provide tactical collabo-
074   ration, understand the game environment, and identify in-game objects with precision. Deployed in
075
      the popular FPS game Arena Breakout: Infinite (Tencent, 2024), this revolutionary feature aims to
      create the most immersive human-AI collaboration experience for its players. ACVI is not limited
076
      to executing a finite set of commands through a simple rule system but allows players to inter-
077
      act with the AI teammate in real-time voice interactions, executing a large number of open-ended
078   commands. The ACVI system follows a three-part pipeline: instruction reasoning, environmental
079   perception, and decision execution. First, we proposed BERT-FID (BERT designed for Fuzzy Intent
080   Detection) to handle the decomposition of complex player instructions and intent reasoning. It offers
081   a confidence-based selection mechanism that integrates the capabilities of a smaller model (BERT)
082   and a more powerful large language model (LLM) for switching inference. This enables the system
083   to respond quickly to simple commands while distributing fuzzy commands to the LLM for further
084   understanding and analysis, achieving accurate comprehension. This approach effectively reduces
085   the system’s deployment costs and inference latency while maintaining comprehension accuracy.
086
      Then, the player’s commands are converted into action and target description templates. We intro-
      duce a novel multi-modal dynamic entity retrieval framework for environment perception, match-
087
      ing the player’s commands with real-time game observations and assets. Ultimately, the player’s
088
      complex commands are broken down into multiple atomic instructions, and integrated with vari-
089   ous environmental asset information that needs to be considered. The AI companion can instantly
090   execute strategies through behavior trees and provide feedback to the player. Fig. 1 demonstrates
091   the capability of ACVI in completing tasks. A full match visualization video is released on our
092   homepage.
093
      We summarize the contributions of our work as follows:
094
095   • We introduce BERT-FID, a confidence-based selection mechanism specifically designed for fuzzy
096     intention detection which integrates the advantages of both small models (BERT) and more pow-
097     erful models (LLM), achieving competitive accuracy about 88.6% close to solely LLMs with a
098     4-fold increase in inference concurrency.
099   • We present a multi-modal dynamic entity retrieval method for environmental perception and game
100     asset recognition, which can accurately retrieve over 17,000 objects with different colors and ma-
101     terials in the game scene, enabling fine-grained control over the companions. Our enhancements
102     method can identify 96.6% dynamic entities in the game with a 5-fold increase in inference con-
103     currency.
104   • By integrating instruction reasoning, environmental perception, and decision execution pipelines,
105     we develop ACVI system, the first voice-interactive FPS game AI companion in the Arena Break-
106     out: Infinite, revolutionizing AI companions in commercial FPS games. The flexible design of
107     ACVI enables a low-cost, real-time inference execution process, enables open-ended voice inter-
        actions, capable of comprehending users’ open-ended commands. The flexible design of ACVI

114   2     BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
115
116
117   Arena Breakout: Infinite is a tactical extraction FPS game developed and globally published by
118   Tencent Games. Its core gameplay offers players the freedom to choose their own playstyle. Ag-
119   gressive players with high-quality equipment may actively engage in combat and loot the bodies of
120   fallen enemies for weapons and armor. In contrast, passive players can focus on looting the map and
121   avoiding crossfire to complete in-game quests or gather more collectibles before extraction. How-
122   ever, the struggle to reach a consensus on whether to engage in combat can make it challenging to
123   achieve a positive cooperative experience with randomly matched human teammates. This scenario
124
      presents opportunities for teaming up with AI companions.
125   The integration of AI companions in gaming has recently attracted significant attention (Gallotta
126   et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024; Park et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2023), particularly concerning roles involv-
127   ing non-player characters (NPCs) and player assistance. Shao et al. (2023) introduced Character-
128   LLM, a framework designed to train LLMs to simulate specific characters, thereby enhancing role-
129   playing experiences. Zhang et al. (2024) developed a text-to-game engine that utilizes LLMs to
130
      transform text inputs into dynamic RPGs, automatically generating game content such as story-
      lines and mechanics in real time. Rao et al. (2024) explored the application of LLMs in powering
131
      NPCs in 3D games like Minecraft, where players collaborate with LLM-driven agents to complete
132
      tasks. While existing studies primarily focus on conversational interactions and the generation of
133   narrative text (Gursesli et al., 2023), they often overlook the enhancement of game-state and visual
134   understanding necessary for improving in-game collaboration. In commercial games, NetEast &
135   Entertainment (2020); Interactive (2023) are also working on developing more complex AI compan-
136   ion systems, but they are struggle to decomposing a wider range of player instructions and a precise
137   ability to perceive game scenarios. AI companions frequently struggle with spatial reasoning (Team
138   et al., 2022) and planning—crucial elements (Wang et al., 2024) in many games, especially FPS
139   games, which depend on precise spatial reasoning and strategic planning (Ontanón et al., 2024).
140   Intent recognition and slot filling are critical tasks for AI companion system. Early methods (Huang
141   et al., 2015; Krizhevsky et al., 2017; Liu & Lane, 2016) relied on traditional machine learning,
142   which limited in handling complex linguistic phenomena. With the advent of pre-trained language
143   models, BERT-based methods (Vaswani et al., 2017; Devlin, 2018; Chen et al., 2019; Comi et al.,
144   2023) has shown outstanding performance in handling downstream tasks. Recently, LLMs have
145   achieved remarkable generalization capabilities. Brown et al. (2020) and Patel et al. (2023) possess
146   powerful generative and comprehension abilities, making zero-shot learning feasible, greatly reduc-
147
      ing the dependency on labeled data. The work by (Zhang et al., 2021b; 2022; 2021a) discusses the
      classification of open intents in real dialogue scenarios, aiming to discover the decision boundaries
148
      required in real-world applications. Some recent works (Leviathan et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023;
149
      Chen et al., 2023; 2024) explore a collaborative paradigm between small and large models to ac-
150   celerate inference and reduce deployment costs across various text-related tasks. Inspired by these
151   works, we have equipped BERT with the capability to perform fuzzy intent detection. By leveraging
152   the stronger generalization capabilities of large language models (LLMs), we can infer these new
153   intents more effectively.
154
      Image-text retrieval (ITR) is a crucial cross-modal task aimed at bridging visual and textual infor-
155
      mation. Encoding image and text features independently is a straightforward approach. Frome et al.
156   (2013) and Faghri et al. (2018) employ classical vision and text encoder models and subsequently
157   align them through similarity calculations.The emergence of large-scale cross-modal pre-training
158   techniques leverage large-scale web data for the feature extractors, demonstrating impressive per-
159   formance on ITR tasks. CLIP (Radford et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2022a) by OpenAI leverages large-
160   scale image-text pairs for contrastive learning, greatly enhancing image-text retrieval performance.
161   Dual-stream models are well-suited for this application scenario because they allow for the offline
      storage of decoded images, resulting in higher computational efficiency during real-time inference.

      3     OVERVIEW OF ACVI
177
178
      As shown in Fig. 2, players can cooperate with the ACVI agent in combat and freely speak into the
      microphone. After processing by the general Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) (Malik et al.,
179
      2021; Wang et al., 2017) module, the spoken content is converted into textual information. Players
180
      can cooperate with the ACVI agent in combat and freely speak into the microphone. We implement
181   a confidence-based method for the instruction reasoning, which can hand over some intricate in-
182   structions that the BERT-FID classifier cannot handle to the domain-aligned LLM. After parsing the
183   player’s action and target description, the multi-modal dynamic entity retrieval module is activated
184   to obtain specific 3D coordinate information within the game engine. Once the parsed command
185   sequence is sent to the game, the AI companion can immediately begin executing actions based
186   on behavior trees and generate voice feedback through the real-time text-to-speech (TTS) (Kumar
187   et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2021). These modules are interconnected to form the complete ACVI sys-
188   tem, enhancing interpretability and facilitating individual optimization, analysis, and upgrades of
189
      each component. As detailed in the subsequent sections, the enhancements in instruction reasoning
      and scene recognition are core technologies that contribute to the success of ACVI.

      4.2   D OMAIN -A LIGNED LLM
295
296   To enable the ACVI agents to accurately execute complex and ambiguous commands that BERT-
297   FID achieve low confidence, we have fine-tuned a lightweight LLM using our in-house SFT dataset
298   to better align with the preferences of real-life tactical companions.
299
      As shown in Fig. 5, we adopt a code generation approach with LLMs to control the actions of
300
      ACVI agents. We find that Python is more suitable for manipulating the ACVI agents due to its
301
      alignment with the training corpora of most existing LLMs. Moreover, compared to having LLMs
302   output in JSON format, Python code structures offer more flexible expressive capabilities, enabling
303   the representation of common code constructs such as conditional and loop structures, which are
304   not inherently supported by the JSON format. We designed a series of atomic tasks (implemented
305   by behavior trees, e.g. move, attack, open door), which are represented as Python functions in
306   LLM. The LLM Planner is trained to generate Python code that invokes these functions, enabling
307   the agents to reason and act in order to address complex tactical tasks.
308   We obtain the expected actions of FPS game agents in response to various player commands us-
309   ing a state-of-the-art general-purpose LLMs. These actions are then further validated by human
310   annotators. The command-actions pairwise dataset constructed in this manner is
311   used for the supervised fine-tuning of the lightweight LLM.

368   Evaluation Dataset. To continuously assess the capabilities of ACVI and gather player data, we
369   have established a human testing and evaluation system that leverages real-time feedback from play-
370   ers (ref to Appendix A.2). During gameplay, players can provide authentic feedback on their inter-
371   actions with AI teammates using thumbs-up and thumbs-down buttons. The data collected from the
372   thumbs-up button, which reflects the model’s predicted outcomes, can be used directly as part of
373   the evaluation dataset. The data from the thumbs-down button were annotated and incorporated into
374
      the evaluation dataset. In each game session, we can collect between 10 and 30 pieces of authentic
      data, and after 100 sessions with human players, we have gathered a total of 2,550 data points for
375
      our evaluation dataset.

495   7   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
496
497   This paper introduces ACVI, a low-cost, high-precision solution for AI companions with advanced
498   natural language understanding and visual perception capabilities. It serves as a revolutionary ex-
499   ample of how AI companions can assist players in achieving their goals and enhancing the gaming
500   experience, offering a valuable and practical solution for commercial FPS games. We developed
501   BERT-FID, which incorporates the uncertainty of complex and fuzzy samples during training, to
502   achieve effective collaborative inference with LLM in both accuracy and response performance. For
503   scene recognition, we implemented a multi-modal dynamic entity retrieval scheme for 3D game as-
504
      sets, which effectively aligns human intentions with decision-making elements. A real-world user
      study demonstrated that ACVI can effectively understand 87.2% natural language commands and
505
      identify dynamic entities in the game. The improvements in instruction reasoning and scene recog-
506
      nition reduce inference deployment costs by approximately 77%. ACVI offers a comprehensive
507   pipeline reference for designing AI teammates in commercial games, which is also applicable to
508   a variety of virtual games, including RPGs (Role-Playing Games) like The Legend of Zelda and
509   MOBA (Multiplayer Online Battle Arena) games such as Honor of Kings. Developers only need to
510   prepare the BERT dataset, the LLM prompts, and the game asset dataset. This approach will facil-
511   itate more engaging and realistic interactions between players and AI characters. In the future, we
512   will explore the relationship between audio signals and player intentions to enhance voice processing
513   and reduce reasoning latency. Additionally, we aim to equip AI companions with distinct personality
514   traits to influence their autonomous behavior, thereby further enhancing player enjoyment.

895   repeated posture changes and executes this action iteratively. In cases where the confidence level of

       A.5   FAILURE C ASES
1034
       Despite its strengths, ACVI has its limitations. The following examples illustrate scenarios where
1035   the system may struggle to accurately interpret or execute commands, potentially leading to failures:
1036
1037   Unresolvable Commands. Due to the limitations of the underlying implementation logic, the re-
1038
       sponse actions in the behavior tree are finite. For example, if a player asks the AI to help with a
       countdown, but the countdown function is not pre-defined in the behavior tree interface, the AI can
1039
       only refuse the command.
1040
1041   Spatial Awareness in Architectural Contexts. Current text-image retrieval models struggle with
1042   the three-dimensional perception of space. For example, if a player‘‘s target is ”the second window
1043   from the left on the second floor,” we cannot effectively retrieve this based on spatial relationships.
1044
       We hope to establish a deeper connection between 3D models and text matching.
1045   These failure cases highlight the critical opportunities and challenges for AI teammate systems. By
1046   identifying and analyzing these issues, we can focus efforts on enhancing ACVI’s performance and
1047   increasing user satisfaction in future iterations.
