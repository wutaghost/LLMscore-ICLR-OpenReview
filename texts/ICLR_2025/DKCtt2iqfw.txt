009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               The influence function, a robust statistics technique, is an effective post-hoc method
012
               that measures the impact of modifying or removing training data on model parame-
013            ters, offering valuable insights into model interpretability without requiring costly
014            retraining. It would provide extensions like increasing model performance, improv-
015            ing model generalization, and offering interpretability. Recently, Multivariate Time
016            Series (MTS) analysis has become an important yet challenging task, attracting
017            significant attention. However, there is no preceding research on the influence
018            functions of MTS to shed light on the effects of modifying the channel of MTS.
019            Given that each channel in an MTS plays a crucial role in its analysis, it is essential
020            to characterize the influence of different channels. To fill this gap, we propose a
021
               channel-wise influence function, which is the first method that can estimate the in-
               fluence of different channels in MTS, utilizing a first-order gradient approximation.
022
               Additionally, we demonstrate how this influence function can be used to estimate
023
               the influence of a channel in MTS. Finally, we validated the accuracy and effective-
024            ness of our influence estimation function in critical MTS analysis tasks, such as
025            MTS anomaly detection and MTS forecasting. According to abundant experiments
026            on real-world datasets, the original influence function performs worse than our
027            method and even fails for the channel pruning problem, which demonstrates the
028            superiority and necessity of the channel-wise influence function in MTS analysis.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Multivariate time series (MTS) plays an important role in a wide variety of domains, including internet
034   services (Dai et al., 2021) , industrial devices (Finn et al., 2016; Oh et al., 2015) , health care (Choi
035   et al., 2016b;a), finance (Maeda et al., 2019; Gu et al., 2020) , and so on. Thus, MTS modeling is
036   crucial across a wide array of applications, including disease forecasting, traffic forecasting, anomaly
037   detection, and action recognition. In recent years, researchers have focused on deep learning-based
038   MTS analysis methods (Zhou et al., 2021; Tuli et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Xu
039
      et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2022). Due to the large number of different channels in MTS, numerous
      studies aim to analyze the importance of these channels (Liu et al., 2024; Zhang & Yan, 2022; Nie
040
      et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024). Some of them concentrate on using graph or attention structure to
041
      capture the channel dependencies (Liu et al., 2024; Deng & Hooi, 2021), while some of them try to
042   use Channel Independence to enhance the generalization ability on different channels of time series
043   model (Nie et al., 2022; Zeng et al., 2023). Although these deep learning methods have achieved
044   state-of-the-art performance, most of these methods focus on understanding the MTS by refining the
045   model architecture to improve their models’ performance.
046
      Different from previous work, we try to better understand MTS from a data-centric perspective-
047
      influence function (Hampel, 1974; Koh & Liang, 2017). The influence function is proposed to
048   study the counterfactual effect between training data and model performance. For independent and
049   identically distributed (i.i.d.) data, influence functions estimate the model’s change when there is
050   an infinitesimal perturbation added to the training distribution, e.g., a reweighing on some training
051   instances and dataset pruning, which has been widely used in computer vision and natural language
052   processing tasks, achieving promising results (Yang et al., 2023; Tan et al., 2024; Thakkar et al.,
053   2023; Cohen et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2020; Pruthi et al., 2020). Considering that, it is essential
      to develop an appropriate influence function for MTS. It would provide extensions like increasing


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      model performance, improving model generalization, and offering interpretability of the interactions
055   between the channels and the time series models.
056
057   To the best of our knowledge, the influence of MTS in deep learning has not been studied, and
058
      it is nontrivial to apply the original influence function in Koh & Liang (2017) to this scenario.
      Since different channels of MTS usually include different kinds of information and have various
059
      relationships (Wu et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2024), the original influence function can not distinguish the
060
      influence of different channels in MTS because it is designed for a whole data sample, according
061   to the definition of the original influence function. In addition, our experiments also demonstrate
062   that the original influence function does not support anomaly detection effectively and fails to solve
063   the forecasting generalization problem in MTS, while it performs well on computer vision and
064   natural language process tasks (Yang et al., 2023; Thakkar et al., 2023). Thus, how to estimate the
065   influence of different channels in MTS is a critical problem. Considering a well-designed influence
066   function should be able to distinguish the influence between different channels, we propose a channel-
067   wise influence function, a first-order gradient approximation (Pruthi et al., 2020), to characterize
068   the influence of different channels. Then, we introduce how to use this function in MTS anomaly
069
      detection (Saquib Sarfraz et al., 2024) and MTS forecasting (Liu et al., 2024) tasks effectively. Finally,
      we use various kinds of experiments on real-world datasets to demonstrate the characteristics of our
070
      novel influence function and prove it can be widely used in the MTS analysis tasks.
071
072   The main contributions of our work are summarized as follows:
073
074
             • We developed a novel channel-wise influence function, a first-order gradient approximation,
               which is the first of its kind to effectively estimate the channel-wise influence of MTS.
075
076          • We designed two channel-wise influence function-based algorithms for MTS anomaly
077            detection and MTS forecasting tasks, and validated its superiority and necessity.
078          • We discovered that the original functions do not perform well on MTS anomaly detection
079            tasks and cannot solve the forecasting generalization problem.
080          • Experiments on various real-world datasets illustrate the superiority of our method on the
081            MTS anomaly detection and forecasting tasks compared with original influence function.
082            Specifically, our influence-based methods rank top-1 among all methods for comparison.

100   2.2   BACKGROUND OF M ULTIVARIATE T IME S ERIES
101
102   There are various types of MTS analysis tasks. In this paper, we mainly focus on unsupervised
103   anomaly detection and preliminarily explore the value of our method in MTS forecasting.
104   MTS Anomaly detection: MTS anomaly detection has been extensively studied, including complex
105   deep learning models (Su et al., 2021; Tuli et al., 2022; Deng & Hooi, 2021; Xu et al., 2022). These
106   models are trained to forecast or reconstruct presumed normal system states and then deployed to
107   detect anomalies in unseen test datasets. The anomaly score, defined as the magnitude of predic-
      tion or reconstruction errors, serves as an indicator of abnormality at each timestamp. However,

      Saquib Sarfraz et al. (2024) have demonstrated that these methods create an illusion of progress due
109   to flaws in the datasets (Wu & Keogh, 2021) and evaluation metrics (Kim et al., 2022), and they
110   provide a more fair and reliable benchmark.
111
112
      MTS Forecasting: In MTS forecasting, many methods try to model the temporal dynamics
      and channel dependencies effectively. An important issue in MTS forecasting is how to better
113
      generalize to unseen channels with a limited number of channels (Liu et al., 2024). This places high
114
      demands on the model architecture, as the model must capture representative information across
115   different channels and effectively utilize this information. There are two popular state-of-the-art
116   methods to achieve this. One is iTransformer (Liu et al., 2024), which uses attention mechanisms to
117   capture channel correlations. The other is PatchTST (Nie et al., 2022), which enhances the model’s
118   generalization ability by sharing the same model parameters across different channels through a
119   Channel-Independence strategy. However, both of these methods are model-centric methods, which
120   cannot identify the most informative channels in the training data for the model.
121   Although MTS forecasting and anomaly detection are two different kinds of tasks, both of their
122   state-of-the-art methods have fully utilized the channel information in the MTS through model-centric
123   methods. Different from previous model-centric methods, we propose a data-centric method to
124   improve the model’s performance on MTS downstream tasks and identify practical techniques to
125   improve the analysis of the training data by leveraging channel-wise information.

129
      The influence function (Koh & Liang, 2017) requires the inversion of a Hessian matrix, which is
130
      quadratic in the number of model parameters. Additionally, the representer point method necessitates
131   a complex, memory-intensive line search or the use of a second-order solver such as LBFGS.
132   Fortunately, the original influence function can be accelerated and approximated by TracIn (Pruthi
133   et al., 2020) effectively. TracIn is inspired by the fundamental theorem of calculus. The fundamental
134   theorem of calculus decomposes the difference between a function at two points using the gradients
135   along the path between the two points. Analogously, TracIn decomposes the difference between the
136   loss of the test point at the end of training versus at the beginning of training along the path taken by
137   the training process. The specific definition can be derived as follows:
138                                                                                 ⊤
                       TracIn (z ′ , z) = L (z; θ) − L(z; θ ′ ) ≈ η∇θ L (z ′ ; θ) ∇θ L(z; θ)                   (1)
139
140   where z ′ is the training example, z is the testing example, θ is the model parameter, θ ′ is the updated
141   parameter after training with z ′ , L(·) is the loss function and η is the learning rate during the training
142   process, which defines the influence of training z ′ on z.
143   However, in the MTS analysis, the data sample z, z ′ are MTS, which means TracIn can only calculate

200   estimates the anomaly score. When it is larger than the threshold, the data is predicted anomaly.
201
      Relationship between self-influence and anomaly score: According to the conclusion in Section 4.1
202
      of (Pruthi et al., 2020), influence can be an effective way to detect the anomaly sample. Specifically,
203
      the idea is to measure self-influence, i.e., the influence of a training point on its own loss, i.e., the
204   training point z ′ and the test point z in TracIn are identical. From an intuitive perspective, self-
205   influence reflects how much a model can reduce the loss during testing by training on sample z ′ itself.
206   Therefore, anomalous samples, due to their distribution being inconsistent with normal training data,
207   tend to reduce more loss, resulting in a greater self-influence. Therefore, when we sort test examples
208   by decreasing self-influence, an effective influence computation method would tend to rank anomaly
209   samples at the beginning of the ranking.

214   each channel. Since, according to the Remark 3.2 and the nature of self-influence, the diagonal
215   elements reflect the channel-wise self-influence, it is an effective method to reflect the anomaly level

       6            C ONCLUSIONS
518
519
       In this paper, we propose a novel influence function that is the first influence function that can estimate
520    the influence of each channel in MTS, which is a concise data-centric method, distinguishing it
521    from previously proposed model-centric methods. In addition, according to abundant experiments
522    on real-world datasets, the original influence function performs worse than our method in anomaly
523    detection and cannot solve the channel pruning problem. This limitation arises from its inability to
524    differentiate the influence across various channels. In contrast, our channel-wise influence function
525    serves as a more universal and effective tool for addressing a wide range of MTS analysis tasks. In
526    conclusion, we believe that our method has significant potential for application and can serve as an
527    effective post-hoc approach for MTS analysis, helping us to better understand the characteristics of
528
       MTS and helping us develop more effective MTS models.
529    Limitation: While we have successfully applied our method to two fundamental MTS tasks and
530    demonstrated its effectiveness, there remains a vast landscape of MTS-related tasks that are yet to
531    be explored and understood. Looking ahead, a primary focus of our research will be the further
532    application of the channel-wise influence function. We believe that delving deeper into this area will
533    yield valuable insights and contribute significantly to advancing the field.
534    Broader Impact: Our model is well-suited for multivariate time series analysis tasks, offering
535    practical and positive impacts across various domains, including disease forecasting, traffic prediction,
536    internet services, content delivery networks, wearable devices, and action recognition. However, we
537    emphatically discourage its application in activities related to financial crimes or any other endeavors
538    that could lead to negative societal consequences.
