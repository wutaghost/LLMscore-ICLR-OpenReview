009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recently, the Kolmogorov-Arnold Network (KAN) has been proposed, signifi-
012            cantly outperforming MLP in terms of interpretability and symbolic representa-
013            tion. In practice, KANs are required to fit data to extremely high precision. For
014            instance, in typical applications of KAN like inferring precise equations from data
015            and serving as solvers for partial differential equations, high accuracy is an intrin-
016            sic requirement. In the current architecture of KAN, cubic B-spline basis func-
017            tions were selected as the approximate tools. However, the inflexibility of fixed
018
               degree and knots in B-splines restricts the adaptability of the activation functions.
               Due to these inherent limitations of B-spline functions, especially low-order and
019
               homogeneity, KAN still has room for improvement in accuracy. In this paper,
020
               we propose the Legendre-KAN that can enhance the degrees of freedom of the
021            basis functions in the KAN. Compared to the traditional Spline-KAN, Legendre-
022            KAN utilizes parameterized Legendre basis functions and normalization layers
023            at the edges of the KAN. Benefiting from higher-order orthogonal polynomials,
024            Legendre-KAN significantly outperforms the Spline-KAN in terms of accuracy.
025            Extensive experiments demonstrate that Legendre-KAN achieves higher accuracy
026            and parameter efficiency, of which accuracy reaches 10 times that of Spline-KAN
027            in some cases. For those functions which can be symbolized, this leads to more
028            correct results as opposed to Spline-KAN. Our approach effectively improves the
029
               accuracy of the mathematical relationships in KANs, providing a better solution
               for approximating and analyzing complex nonlinear functions.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Traditional neural networks have demonstrated outstanding performance in many typical fields, in-
035
      cluding image processing (Krizhevsky et al., 2012), speech recognition (Hinton et al., 2012), and
036   natural language processing (Nadkarni et al., 2011). During the past decade, they have also become
037   important tools for solving scientific research problems, in which the interpretability and accuracy of
038   the network is very important. However, neural networks based on multilayer perceptrons (MLPs)
039   are often viewed as black-box models, significantly limiting their utility in scientific research (Makke
040   & Chawla, 2024). On the other hand, traditional activation functions, such as ReLUs, are lack of de-
041   grees of freedom and that the determination of their number and positioning is also part of the prob-
042   lems. A substantial number of redundant parameters are often required to achieve high-precision
043   approximations of scientific formulas.
044   Recently, a new interpretable Kolmogorov-Arnold network (KAN) has been proposed. As a promis-
045   ing alternative to the MLP in neural networks, KAN uses parameterized B-spline basis functions
046   at the edges of network, known as Spline-KAN. In Spline-KAN, complex multi-dimensional func-
047   tions are first decomposed into the sum and composition of one-dimensional functions. These one-
048   dimensional functions are then fitted by B-spline basis functions through training neural network
049   and are represented as symbolic functions wherever possible. Compared to symbolic regression in
050
      traditional machine learning, Spline-KAN provides more continuous and stable results in symbolic
      representation tasks (Liu et al., 2024).
051
052   In scientific research based on AI, equations inferred from noise-free data demand extremely high
053   precision. For example, the physics-informed neural networks (PINNs), which serve as numerical
      solvers for partial differential equations (PDEs), inherently require high accuracy (Cuomo et al.,


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2022). More importantly, the accuracy of network directly impacts its reliability, which severely
055   limits its practical applications (Korns, 2011). Especially in KAN, inaccurate predictions lead to
056   mathematical expressions that significantly differ from the true function. The initial results of the
057   activation functions in KAN are typically obtained through a pre-training approach. Subsequently,
058   the functions in the set are translated and scaled to identify a symbolic function that closely aligns
059   with the training outcomes, which serves as the mathematical expression of this activation function.
060   However, low-precision pre-training results of Spline-KAN hinder the network’s ability to find the
061   correct symbolic representation, severely impacting the subsequent training phase and leading to a
062   decrease in the network’s accuracy. Furthermore, this may result in an analytical expression that is
063
      entirely different from the original function.
064   The low accuracy of Spline-KAN can be attributed to the use of B-spline basis functions. As a
065   fundamental approximate tools, B-splines endow the Spline-KAN with superior interpretability and
066   performance on scientific tasks compared with MLP. In traditional function approximation, low-
067   degree piecewise polynomials in B-spline functions often fail to achieve effective fitting in certain
068   regions of one-dimensional functions due to lower degrees of freedom. Unfortunately, the same
069
      issue persists for Spline-KAN when dealing with multidimensional functions. This implies that, in
      the field of symbolic representation, Spline-KAN struggles to achieve high-accuracy results with a
070
      limited number of parameters in some cases.
071
072   As a system of orthogonal polynomials defined over a finite interval, Legendre polynomials are
073   widely used in solving differential equations, physical problems and computer graphics (Mall &
074   Chakraverty, 2016; Parand & Razzaghi, 2004; Wu et al., 2020). When used as activation func-
075
      tions, Legendre polynomials can flexibly approximate complex signals with high-order and global
      polynomials. To improve the accuracy of traditional KAN, we proposed Legendre-KAN. This net-
076
      work significantly enhance the accuracy of the pre-training results, allowing for more precise sym-
077
      bolic approximations. In cases where certain Spline-KAN fits yield erroneous outcomes, the use of
078   Legendre basis functions enables the Legendre-KAN to still produce relatively accurate analytical
079   expressions. To alleviate the issue of gradient explosion commonly encountered with polynomial
080   activation functions in neural networks, as well as the limitations imposed by the domain of Legen-
081   dre polynomials, we draw inspiration from Spline-KAN and employ a periodic Min-Max function
082   to constrain the range of input node data. This normalization layer dynamically normalizes the in-
083   put data by periodically assessing the range of each input node’s values. Furthermore, we identify
084   that the gradient explosion in KAN arises from excessively large coefficients during affine trans-
085   formations of the basis functions. To address this, we utilize smaller initialization coefficients in
086
      Legendre-KAN to ensure that the activation function transitions from a more stable state to the tar-
      get function. Additionally, we enhance the loss function in Spline-KAN to restrict the coefficients
087
      of the basis functions within a specified range.
088
089   This paper proposes the Legendre-KAN based on the Legendre polynomials and the KA theorem.
090   By using parameterized Legendre basis functions on the edges of the KAN, the advantages of Legen-
091   dre polynomials are well integrated into the KAN. With the same number of parameters and greater
092
      time efficiency, Legendre-KAN outperforms both Spline-KAN and MLP in most functions, as il-
      lustrated in Figure 1, especially excelling in fitting complex signals. This significantly enhances
093
      the reliability and interpretability of mathematical relationships for KAN in symbolic representation
094
      tasks, which has important implications for the application and development of KA networks in
095   natural science research.

112   The Kolmogorov-Arnold representation theorem (Arnold, 2009; Kolmogorov, 1957; Braun &
113   Griebel, 2009) has the following general form:

120   Hieber, 2021). Recently, as a breakthrough application of the KA theorem in neural networks,
121   Kolmogorov-Arnold Network(KAN) was proposed to implement a multi-level deep KA neural net-
122   work, effectively combining the strengths of MLP and the KA theorem. By utilizing parameterizable
123   activation functions, the KAN has demonstrated outstanding performance across various domains.

128
      critically, the KAN leverages the KA theorem to decompose the traditional multivariate function
129   approximation problem into a composition of univariate function approximations. This implies that
130   the accuracy or interpretability of the KAN directly depends on the univariate basis functions it

198   From a mathematical perspective, the optimal parametrized activation function trained in KAN can
199   be interpreted as a univariate function approximation problem. As the foundation of Spline-KAN, B-
200   spline basis functions exhibit piecewise smooth characteristics, but they are only suitable for fitting
201   smooth, simple functions. For more complex functions, spline functions perform well in smooth
202   regions but may introduce significant errors in jump areas, as illustrated in Figure 3. The core issue
203   is the low degree of freedom of piecewise basis in the function approximation space of splines. The
204   jump areas of data usually require many and higher-order basis functions to achieve high accuracy
205   fitting, but in Spline-KAN, only a very small number of lower-order polynomials are used to fit
206
      each areas, which are not sufficient to fit the drastic changes in the jump region. This problem
      can severely impact the accuracy of the activation function approximation between certain nodes.
207
      Furthermore, it can degrade the overall fitting accuracy of the entire network, as shown in Figure 6.
208
      In order to improve the accuracy of the network, it is crucial to choose a basis function that has
209   global approximation space and is higher-order stable.
210

233   for jump regions where B-spline fitting exhibits lower accuracy, Legendre polynomials, with their

235   as shown in Figure 3. Based on these advantages, we propose the Legendre-KAN. Experimental
236   results demonstrate that the aforementioned characteristics of Legendre polynomials in function
237   fitting are well integrated into KAN, enabling Legendre-KAN to achieve 10 to 100 times higher
238   fitting accuracy than Spline-KAN in many complex functions in Table 7. Moreover, the degree of
239
      the polynomial can be adjusted, which provides flexibility in the capacity of model.

241   3     M ETHODOLOGY
242
243   In this work, we propose Legendre-KAN as improvement over the accuracy of Spline-KAN. Build-
244   ing on the strengths of the KAN, particularly its interpretability and symbolic representation capa-
245   bilities, Legendre-KAN enhance the accuracy of KAN through input normalization, reduced initial-
246   ization parameters, and the incorporation of orthogonal high-order Legendre bases. We first explain
247
      the advantage of Legendre-KAN in Section 3.1. On the other hand, the structure of Legendre-KAN
      is presented in Section 3.2.

      3.1   L EGENDRE -KOLMOGOROV-A RNOLD NETWORK (L EGENDRE -KAN).
251
      As discussed below, the activation functions with lower degrees of freedom prevents Spline-KAN
252   from producing accurate results. Local low-order basis functions struggle to achieve high-accuracy
253   results, but as we increases the order of B-spline, test loss get worse in Appendix B. In some cases
254   this may cause the network to choose the wrong symbolic function. In Table 2, we use KAN to
255   approximate F (x) = (x0 − 10e−5 )−1 + (x1 − 10e−5 )−1 . Due to the lower accuracy of the pre-
256   training step, Spline-KAN selects the wrong sign function on edge 1, which ultimately leads to the
257   result which has nothing to do with the original function.
258   To improve the degrees of freedom of basis functions, we initially used power basis functions.
259   Within the polynomial function space, this approach offers higher-order and global polynomials
260   compared to the spline function space. However, these functions exhibits instability when dealing
261   with complex tasks in Table 7, which means that power basis is not suitable for KAN. Naturally,
262   we consider the orthogonalized version of xn , the Legendre polynomials. Legendre polynomials
263   mitigate the numerical instability inherent in power bases by applying appropriate orthogonalization.
264   For given B-spline basis functions with G knots and Kth order, the (K + G − 1)th order Legendre
265   polynomials achieve significantly higher accuracy in global function fitting compared to B-splines,
266
      as the function space contains global higher-order polynomials. With normalization and smaller
      initial coefficients, Legendre-KAN reduces the impact of the increase of function’s order on the
267
      results of KAN. Motivated by this, we propose the Legendre-KAN.
268

      polynomials are effectively embedded into Legendre-KAN. With the same number of parameters

535   This paper proposes the Legendre-KAN that combines the advantages of Legendre polynomials
536   and the KA theorem. This network leverages the global and orthogonal properties of the Legendre
537   basis functions, effectively addressing the numerical approximation accuracy issues associated with
538   low-order spline bases. Extensive experiments demonstrate that Legendre-KAN achieves higher
539   accuracy and interpretability in symbolic representation, compared to the Spline-KAN, while using
      less time and parameters.

692   Some explanations of indicators used in this article. All the ratios represent the ratio of the
693
      indicator of a certain network to this indicator of Legendre.
      For each tasks, we test the network with different parameter quantity. The parameter quantity for
694
      the lowest error of the result of the Legendre-KAN is assumed to be kl . We divide all the results of
695
      networks into two parts. The first part is the part where the parameter quantity is less than or equal
696   to or slightly greater than kl . The other part is the part where the number of parameters is less
697   than or equal to the maximum number of basis functions specified. The former is to compare the
698   situation when the two networks’ parameter quantities are equal. The second part is used to compare
699   the optimal fitting accuracy of the network under a certain number of parameters. For the result with
700   the lowest error in the first part, the number of basis functions is described as Equal params. For
701   the result with the lowest error in the second part, the number of basis functions is described as best
      k/grid in Spline-KAN and best k in other networks.
