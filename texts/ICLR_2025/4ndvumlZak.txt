011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Despite the historical successes of neural networks, the rigour of logical reasoning
014            is still beyond their reach. Taking syllogistic reasoning as a subset of logical rea-
015            soning, we show supervised neural networks cannot reach the rigour of syllogistic
016
               reasoning, mainly because they use composition tables, which are coarse to distin-
               guish each valid type of syllogistic reasoning and because end-to-end supervised
017
               learning may change the premises. As Transformer’s Key-Query-Value structure
018
               is a combination table, we conclude that neural networks built upon Transformers
019            cannot reach the rigour of syllogistic reasoning and, thus, cannot reach the rigour
020            of logical reasoning. We logically prove that oversmoothing, in the setting of
021            part-whole relations, can be avoided, if neural networks use region embeddings,
022            and propose the method of reasoning through explicit constructing and inspecting
023            region configurations, to achieve the rigour of logical reasoning.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   The power of neural networks is witnessed by its superior performance in simulating a variety of hu-
029   man behaviours, for example, human-like question-answering (Biever, 2023), playing games (Silver
030   et al., 2017; Schrittwieser et al., 2020), predicting gene structures (Abramson et al., 2024), helping
031   mathematical discovery (Davies et al., 2021), solving IMO tasks (Trinh et al., 2024). The success of
032   neural networks may ascribe to their strong power to represent closed-world tasks (e.g., Chess, Go).
033
      RNNs and Transformers are even proven to be Turing complete (given unbounded time) (Nowak
      et al., 2023; Strobl et al., 2024).
034
035   However, accompanied by these exciting successes are LLMs’ unpredictable behaviours (Park et al.,
036   2024), errors in simple abstract reasoning (Eisape et al., 2024; Lampinen et al., 2024), and the
037   irrationality of making correct answers with incorrect explanations (Creswell et al., 2022; Zelikman
038   et al., 2022). This brings unpredictable risks to our society (Bengio et al., 2024), for example, in
039
      clinical decision-making (Hager et al., 2024). Although breaking a complex reasoning task into
      multiple steps may improve their reasoning performance (Creswell et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2023;
040
      Lightman et al., 2023), it remains unclear whether they reason at all (Biever, 2023; Melanie, 2023)
041
      and how far can neural networks go with the scaling law (Kaplan et al., 2020). Neural networks are
042   invented to simulate the functions of our minds. The design is inspired by the biological structure of
043   neurons (Anderson, 1995). Here, we show that supervised neural networks cannot achieve the rigour
044   of syllogistic reasoning and thus cannot achieve the rigour of logical reasoning. We further show,
045   that to achieve the rigour, neural networks shall promote vector embeddings to region embeddings
046   and adopt the method of reasoning through model construction and inspection (Johnson-Laird &
047   Byrne, 1991; Knauff et al., 2003; Goodwin & Johnson-Laird, 2005; Knauff, 2009), as demonstrated
048   in (Dong et al., 2024).
049   The supervised learning syllogistic reasoning falls in the paradigm of statistic learning, with the
050   stable-world assumption that the training data and the testing data share the same distribution (Goyal
051   & Bengio, 2022; Gigerenzer, 2022). A well-trained neural network for syllogistic reasoning may
052   reach 100% confidence for the reasoning All Greeks are human. All humans are mortal. ∴ All
053   Greeks are mortal., but will be less confident, if we replace Greeks with Dani people, or a Papuan
      ethnic group. Experiments also show that LLMs may mimic syllogistic errors in the training data,

      though they can perform very well with syllogistic reasoning (Lampinen et al., 2024). Thus, using
055   symbolic terms, neural networks cannot achieve the rigour of syllogistic reasoning. Considering
056   the finite set-theoretic relations behind all syllogistic statements, we may first translate a syllogistic
057   statement into a set-theoretic relation that can be represented by Euler diagram. In this way, Greeks,

      In Section 2, we introduce syllogistic reasoning and the criteria of rigorous syllogistic reasoning
060
      for neural networks as follows: (1) being able to correctly reason with out-of-distribution data, (2)
061   being able to correctly reason with all valid syllogistic reasoning, and (3) not changing premises.
062   In Section 3, we visit Euler Net, a supervised neural network that learns syllogistic reasoning by
063   utilising a combination table of its set-theoretic semantics and achieves 99.8% accuracy. However,
064   the performance drops to 56%, when we fed with new randomly generated data. In Section 4, we
065   introduce a method that automatically identifies and removes out-of-distribution data. We conclude
066   that given the reasoning types as represented by the combination table, increasing training data will
067   improve the reasoning performance.
068   In Section 5, we show that Euler Net’s composition table cannot distinguish all valid types of syllo-
069   gistic reasoning and, thus, cannot help Euler Net to achieve the rigour of syllogistic reasoning. We
070   improve the composition table and propose to separate the end-to-end mapping between premises
071   (network inputs) and conclusions (network outputs) into two processes: firstly, constructing a latent
072   model from inputs, and secondly, inspecting this model to draw conclusions. Though with sufficient
073   data, the improved version of Euler Net may achieve nearly 100% accuracy for all valid types of syl-
074   logistic reasoning, it may need an unbounded number of inspecting models and may draw incorrect
075
      conclusions by automatically changing premises.
076   In Section 6, we introduce a contradictory design feature for object recognition and logical reason-
077   ing. It is a desirable feature for object recognition networks that they can recognise objects only
078   by observing parts; however, it is an undesirable feature for reasoning networks that they will insert
079   new concepts into the premises. Thus, if an end-to-end supervised neural network for reasoning uses
080   supervised neural networks for recognising inputs, this reasoning neural network will not achieve
081
      the rigour of logical reasoning. Because its object recognition networks may automatically insert
      new concepts by recovering the whole from parts. We demonstrate this phenomenon through exper-
082
      iments with Euler Net and show that this problem can be relieved by defining unintended inputs and
083
      creating the training data, but cannot be solved entirely.
084
085   Syllogistic reasoning is the microcosm of human rationality. Neural reasoning for syllogistic rela-
086   tions are embedded in scenarios with complex objects that needs sophisticated neural architectures,
087
      e.g. Visual Transformers (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021). In Section 7, we prove that if all the outputs of
      neural networks oversmooth (the same embedding), the output embedding will be a single point. The
088
      contraposition of this statement is that if the output embedding is not a point, neural networks will
089
      not oversmooth. The simplest region embedding would be sphere embeddings that can be obtained
090   by adding a non-zero radius to a vector embedding. We briefly visit Sphere Neural Network (Dong
091   et al., 2024) and illustrate the method of reasoning through explicitly constructing and inspecting
092   sphere configurations as Euler diagrams, and achieves the rigour of syllogistic reasoning.

      A syllogistic reasoning can be satisfiable, unsatisfiable, valid, or invalid, as listed in Table 1. For
      example, being valid means the conclusion is true in every case its premises are true (Jeffrey, 1981);
154
      being satisfiable means there is a case that both premises and the conclusion are true. For a valid
155
      reasoning, the negation of its conclusion is unsatisfiable; for an invalid reasoning, the negation of
156   its conclusion is satisfiable. Geometrically, a syllogistic reasoning is satisfiable, if and only if we
157   can construct an Euler diagram, e.g., three circles satisfying the spatial relations of the premises
158   and conclusion; otherwise, this reasoning will be unsatisfiable. In Figure 1(g), we successfully
159   constructed an Euler diagram of the premises and the conclusion some lawyers are not scientists,
160   so this reasoning is satisfiable. But, we cannot construct an Euler diagram of the premises and
161   the conclusion all lawyers are scientists, so this conclusion is unsatisfiable. Therefore, its negation
      (some lawyers are not scientists) is valid.

      statement, there will be 256 different forms of Aristotelian syllogistic reasoning, among which 24
171
      types are valid (Khemlani & Johnson-Laird, 2012). A reasoning network reaches the rigour of
172
      syllogistic reasoning, if it can correctly determine all valid syllogistic reasoning for all datasets in
173   bounded computing time. This criterion requires the system to achieve 100% accuracy on out-of-
174   distribution data or to classify them as unintended input. To be rigorous also requires the system
175   not to change the premises. It is hard or unrealistic to develop supervised neural networks that meet
176   these criteria. However, we may believe that supervised neural networks can infinitely close to the
177   rigour and meet the criteria, as long as we have more and more data (the scaling law). We show that
178   increasing the amount of data will improve reasoning performance but will not infinitely close to the
179   rigour of logical reasoning – there is a gap, and to close this gap, we need to abandon training data
180   and switch to the method of reasoning through model construction and inspection.

204   Syllogistic reasoning is not a challenging task for symbolic approaches (Vukmirovic et al., 2019;
205   Bentkamp et al., 2021), but very challenging for neural modelling and developing neural syllogistic
206   models was once regarded a Utopian (Khemlani & Johnson-Laird, 2012). A recent comparative
207
      analysis found even the largest LLM may make mistakes in syllogistic reasoning (Eisape et al.,

208

209

213   output is a vector representing the set-theoretic relation(s) between the subject and the predicate.
214   The mapping from two premises to possible conclusions is enumerated in the combination table,
215   where possible conclusions are symbolised as a vector, as illustrated in Figure 2(b). The structure
      of a piece of training data is ((image, image), vector). The benchmark dataset consists of 96000

308   The reason for the performance drop is Euler Net’s combination table fails to distinguish each valid
309
      type of syllogistic reasoning. The same premises can have different valid syllogistic conclusions.

310
      the diagram that “V circle is inside U circle”. This is one valid type of syllogistic reasoning (the
311

315   three relations between V and U as follows: “V circle is inside from U circle”, “U circle is inside from

317   logical conclusion and “some V are U” its logical consistency. The combination table only partially

409
410   We use the method of maximization activation (Samek et al., 2019) to examine whether Euler Net
411
      will change premises. Concretely, we fix the output of the Euler Net and search two input images,
      each with only one green circle, with the target that inputs shall maximize the confidence score of
412
      the fixed output. Our experiments showed that there were input images with only one green circle
413
      that could lead Euler Net to give syllogistic conclusions between a blue circle and a red circle. For
414   example, when each input image only has one green circle, Euler Net might output the red circle is
415   inside the blue circle ([0, 1, 0, 0]). This is an amazing ability to predict/complete a single object, but
416   not a desirable capability for logical reasoning, as this ability will let Euler Net change the premise
417   of logical reasoning.
418
      The rigour of syllogistic reasoning requires Euler Net to determine that single-circled images are
419
      invalid input. We created a new output vector [0, 0, 0, 0] representing invalid inputs and trained
420   Euler Net to be capable of classifying single-circled inputs into [0, 0, 0, 0], reaching 100% accuracy.
421   The improved Euler Net can classify single-circle inputs as invalid and perform syllogistic reasoning
422   for normal inputs. However, this does not follow that it immediately reaches 100% accuracy for any
423   inputs. We report our experiments to show that training data automatically creates unindented inputs
424   as follows.

      7   B RIDGING THE GAP
446
447   Because combination tables are coarse and because end-to-end mapping from premises to conclu-
448   sions, Euler Net cannot distinguish logical conclusions from their weaker version statements and
449
      may change premises, thus can not achieve the rigour of symbolic-level reasoning. These two limi-
      tations in representation and in methodology cause neural networks using the same or similar repre-
450
      sentation and methodology not to reach the rigour of syllogistic reasoning, for example, Transform-
451
      ers. With the self-attention mechanism, Transformers automatically learn Key-Query-Value struc-
452   tures that capture association relations between/among concepts within or across sentences, texts, or
453   patches. A Key-Query-Value structure is a special combination table: given a Key (a row of a table)
454   and a Query (a column of a table), the table returns a Value (a cell of the table). Thus, the best per-
455   formance of Visual Transformers can reach is by learning the new combination table, as illustrated
456   in Figure 5, with which Transformers will not achieve rigorous syllogistic reasoning. Multilayered
457   visual transformers may be used to reason with syllogistic relations in complex objects, e.g., spatial
458   relations among cars in street scenes. However, Transformers suffer from oversmoothing when their
459   depth increases (outputs converge to the same feature embedding) (Park & Kim, 2022; Wang et al.,
460
      2022; Guo et al., 2023). It may be possible to avoid oversmoothing by artful reparameterization of
      the weights, as suggested by an ongoing research (Dovonon et al., 2024), here, we show that in the
461
      setting of part-whole relations, for any neural network that outputs region embeddings, if all outputs
462
      converge to the same feature embedding (oversmoothing), these embeddings must be a single point.
463
464   Theorem 1. for any neural network that outputs region embeddings, if over-smoothing (all output
465
      feature embeddings are the same), these output embeddings will be a point.
466
      The proof is sketched in the supplementary material. This theorem is consistent with Wang et al.
467
      (2022)’s findings, which applied discrete Fourier analysis to ViTs and found that ViTs are low-pass
468   filters – they lose all feature expressive power (only waves with the frequency of 0 can pass, which
469   is a static point) after ViT networks reach 12-th depth. The contraposition of Theorem 1 is that if the
470   output embeddings are not points, they will not over-smooth.
471
      This suggests a method to completely avoid oversmoothing and, meanwhile, a necessary feature
472
      for logical reasoning, namely, neural networks using non-vector region embedding, e.g., Gaussian
473
      distribution (Athiwaratkun & Wilson, 2017), boxes (Ren et al., 2020), Beta distribution (Ren &
474   Leskovec, 2020), cones (Zhang et al., 2021), sphere embeddings (Dong et al., 2024).
475
476   Configurations of regions may explicitly represent explicit human-like semantic representations,
477
      such as Euler diagrams in the vector space. By introducing the method of reasoning through explicit
      model construction and inspection (Johnson-Laird & Byrne, 1991; Knauff et al., 2003; Goodwin &
478
      Johnson-Laird, 2005; Knauff, 2009), we can separate the neural process of object recognition from
479
      the neural reasoning process. Models can be constructed without training data, as follows: The con-
480   struction process initialises a simple configuration, e.g., all objects coincide, then, the configuration
481   will be repeatedly optimised – the next model will be revised following the principle of minimal

516   8   C ONCLUSIONS AND D ISCUSSIONS
517
518
519   By taking syllogistic reasoning, a subset of logical reasoning, we show the representation limitation
520
      of using composition tables and the method limitation of reasoning through direct mapping between
      premises and conclusion – the training data may not consider all situations and may only superfi-
521
      cially capture literal meanings of training data (Bengio et al., 2024). We show that when a cell of
522
      the composition table exactly matches one valid syllogistic reasoning, the scaling law may work
523   well. By using sphere embedding and the method of reasoning by model construction and inspec-
524   tion, the Sphere Neural Network achieves the rigour of syllogistic reasoning. This encourages us to
525   re-design a new Euler Net as follows: it takes the same input as the Sphere Neural Network. Two
526   premises are translated into Euler diagram images in the composition shown in Figure 5. Its model
527   construction process will map the two images into the image in the table cell. Then, we train an in-
528   spection network for each valid conclusion, totalling 24 inspection networks. This new architecture
529   may satisfy all three criteria of achieving the rigour of syllogistic reasoning: Euler diagram images,
530   being translated by a fixed method from syllogistic statements, will follow the same distribution.
531
      So, no out-of-distribution images; Premise statements will construct one model. Each valid type
      will be identified by its well-trained neural network; there is no chance to change premises. Given
532
      enough training data, this new Euler Net will approach to near 100% accuracy for all syllogistic
533
      reasoning. However, given “all Greeks are human. all humans are mortal.”, the inspection network
534   that confirms “all Greeks are mortal.” will deny “some Greeks are mortal.” If we introduce more
535   qualifiers into syllogistic reasoning (Khemlani, 2021), such as “most”, “half”, and “quarter”, we
536   must train new inspection neural networks for each. If we allow quantifiers having numbers, such
537   as “at least three”, “exactly five”, “at most ten” or allow particular instances, such as “Socrates is
538   mortal”, “Aristotle is mortal”, we may need an unbounded number of inspection networks. Instead
539   of asking, to which degree it achieves syllogistic reasoning, we may doubt whether such a system
      truly learns syllogistic reasoning.
