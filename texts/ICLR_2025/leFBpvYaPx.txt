009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Existing studies have shown that Message-Passing Graph Neural Networks
012            (MPNNs) are highly susceptible to adversarial attacks. In contrast, despite the
013            increasing importance of Graph Transformers (GTs), their robustness properties
014            are unexplored. Thus, for the purpose of robustness evaluation, we design the
015            first adaptive attacks for GTs. We provide general design principles for strong
016
               gradient-based attacks on GTs w.r.t. structure perturbations and instantiate our at-
               tack framework for five representative and popular GT architectures. Specifically,
017
               we study GTs with specialized attention mechanisms and Positional Encodings
018
               (PEs) based on random walks, pair-wise shortest paths, and the Laplacian spec-
019            trum. We evaluate our attacks on multiple tasks and threat models, including
020            structure perturbations on node and graph classification and node injection for
021            graph classification. Our results reveal that GTs can be catastrophically fragile
022            in many cases. Consequently, we show how to leverage our adaptive attacks for
023            adversarial training, substantially improving robustness.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
      Graphs are versatile data structures that have applications in a wide range of different domains,
028
      and Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have become the tool of choice for many learning tasks on
029
      graphs. Given the increasing popularity of GNNs, multiple studies in the past years have devel-
030   oped adversarial attacks for GNNs and analyzed their robustness (Zügner et al., 2018; Zügner &
031   Günnemann, 2019; Zügner & Günnemann, 2020). These studies mostly focus on Message-Passing
032   GNNs (MPNNs), such as the Graph Convolutional Network (GCN) (Kipf & Welling, 2017) and
033   show that GNNs are vulnerable to even slight graph structure perturbations (Zügner et al., 2018).
034
      Recently, Graph Transformers (GTs) have gained popularity (Müller et al., 2024), addressing inher-
035
      ent limitations such as over-smoothing, over-squashing, and limited receptive fields (Müller et al.,
036   2024). Yet, the adversarial robustness of GTs is entirely unexplored. The unknown stability of GTs
037   poses a substantial risk in practical applications, where robustness is crucial. However, evaluating
038   the robustness of GTs is non-trivial. GTs’ employ modified attention mechanisms and Positional
039   Encodings (PEs) that often include parts that are not differentiable w.r.t. the graph structure. This
040   renders the application of gradient-based adaptive attacks difficult, even though the gradient is key
041   for crafting adversarial attacks efficiently. Adaptive attacks can use the model-specific gradient to
042   adjust to all architecture details and are vital for realistic robustness estimates (Athalye et al., 2018;
043   Carlini & Wagner, 2017; Tramèr et al., 2020), also in the GNN domain (Mujkanovic et al., 2022).
044   We provide the first analysis of the robustness of GTs. To obtain precise robustness estimates,
045   we propose continuous relaxations and perturbation approximations for the most widely used GT
046   components including (a) Shortest Path, (b) Random Walk, and (c) Spectral PEs. These enable
047   us to apply adaptive gradient-based attacks to five popular and representative GT architectures: 1)
048   Graph Inductive bias Transformer (GRIT) (Ma et al., 2023), 2) Graphormer (Ying et al., 2021),
049   3) Spectral Attention Network (SAN) (Kreuzer et al., 2021), 4) General, Powerful, Scalable (GPS)
050
      GT (Rampášek et al., 2022), and 5) Polynormer (Deng et al., 2024). Moreover, we provide guiding
      principles to develop relaxations for other discrete or non-differentiable components in GTs.
051
052   Our study reveals that GTs can be catastrophically fragile if evaluated with our adaptive attacks
053   (Fig. 1). For example, on the proposed node injection attacks (NIAs) for fake news detection (Fig. 1c
      & 1d), perturbing 2.5% of edges suffices to essentially halve the accuracy. Consequently, we use

      3   ATTACKING GRAPH TRANSFORMERS
169
170
      The main obstacles for gradient-based structure attacks on GTs fθ are PEs and attention mechanisms
      that are designed for a discrete graph structure. As a result, the model output is often a discontinuous
171
172
      function of the continuous input adjacency matrix Ã ∈ [0, 1]n×n , rendering continuous optimization
      ineffective. Thus, to obtain useful gradients we need to relax the structure aware components such
173
      as PEs. For designing the continuous relaxations f˜θ , we identify three main principles:
174
175   Principle I: Relaxed and target models coincide for discrete inputs. The prediction should equal
176   f˜θ (A) = fθ (A) for any discrete adjacency A ∈ {0, 1}n×n .
177
178
      Principle II: f˜θ can interpolate “smoothly” between any different discrete graphs. In other
179
      words, f˜θ (Ã) should be continuous w.r.t. Ã but does not need to be continuously differentiable.
180   Principle III: The relaxed model f˜θ must be efficient. It is a critical property that the relaxation
181   does not excessively increase memory and runtime complexity.
182
      While Principle II might appear surprising at first glance, we argue that we do not need to enforce
183
      stronger standards on f˜θ than perhaps the most widely used activation function ReLU. We expect
184
      these principles to be sensible defaults for the design of adaptive attacks on future GTs.
185

253   4     N ODE INJECTION ATTACK
254
255   We also consider the relevant case of inserting nodes into an existing graph structure. In contrast to
256   the usual framing of Node Injection Attack (NIA), where the attacker also chooses the node features
257   for the new vicious nodes (Wang et al., 2020), we connect existing nodes from other graphs of an in-
258   ductive graph dataset. Therefore, the nodes’ features are fixed but physically realizable even if, e.g.,
259   they represent embeddings of natural language. This alleviates us from a somewhat subjective def-
260
      inition of imperceptibility required to craft the node features in the existing NIA. Hence, our attack
      solely focuses on ‘structure’ perturbations and their influence on the PEs, which are of particular
261
      interest for attacking GTs.
262

264   both the original nodes and the set of potential injection nodes. This formulation enables the use of
265   the same PRBCD attack optimization, where the edge flip budget constraint also serves as an upper
266
      bound for the number of nodes that can be injected. We provide details in D.

290
291   Datasets. We first evaluate our structure attacks on CLUSTER (Dwivedi et al., 2023). It contains
292   SBM-generated graphs with 6 clusters, where each cluster has one labeled node. The average num-
293   ber of nodes is 117.2. The task is to predict which node belongs to which cluster, i.e., inductive node
294   classification. For training, we use the standard PyG train/val/test split of 10000/1000/1000 graphs,
295   respectively. Additionally, we evaluate on the graph classification dataset Reddit Threads (Rozem-
296   berczki et al., 2020). It contains 203 088 small graphs without any features and with an average of
297
      23.9 nodes. The graphs represent users that are connected if they directly reply to each other in the
      thread. The task is binary classification of whether the thread is disscussion-based. We use stratified
298
      random train/val/test split of 75%/12.5%/12.5%.
299
300   We use the UPFD Twitter fake news detection datasets from (Dou et al., 2021) to evaluate our node
301   injection attacks. There are 2 datasets: politifact, with political; and gossipcop with celebrity fake
302   news. The average number of nodes is 131 and 58, respectively. The graphs consist of retweet
303
      trees, where the root node has features concerning both the news content and the user who posted
      the news. All other nodes’ features are related to the users that retweeted the news. We add all
304
      dataset nodes except for the graph roots into the candidate set of injection nodes, since the roots are
305
      special. Moreover, we do not allow for perturbations of the original tree structure and if the discretely
306   sampled injection perturbations do not have a tree structure, we take the maximum spanning tree
307   (using the edge probabilities) to ensure all perturbations are valid retweet trees. The task is binary
308   classification whether the graph contains fake news or not. We use the standard PyG train/val/test
309   split of 20/10/70% of the 314 and 5464 graphs for politifact and gossipcop, respectively.
310
      Due to the quadratic scaling in the number of nodes of the three chosen GTs, their application is lim-
311
      ited to smaller graphs. This renders evaluation on larger graph datasets commonly used in robustness
312   studies impractical. While GTs are most widely applied to molecule data, adversarial attacks are of
313   little practical relevance in that domain. Thus, we omit molecule data from our evaluations.

474   7                    A DVERSARIAL T RAINING
475
476   We based our implementation of the adversarial training on the ‘Free’ adversarial training of Shafahi
477   et al. (2019). The main idea is to couple the attack and training updates by ‘replaying’ the same
478   mini-batch k times for each of which both an attack and a training optimization step is made. This
479   enables finding stronger perturbations whithout the large overhead of only performing a single model
480   update for every k attack steps of ‘traditional’ adversarial training. Note that we make some slight
481   modifications to fit the limitations of our setting which are described in § F.
482   We evaluate our advarsarial training using Graphormer, which was one of the least robust models in
483   our attack evaluation and thus has a large potential for robustness gains. As a baseline comparison
484   we also include GCN. We evaluate node injection attacks on the UPFD datasets, the results for
485   politifact are shown in Figs. 8a & 8b and for gossipcop in Figs. 8c & 8d. We show results for
      training configurations with different attack budgets and number of replay steps.

      8                   R ELATED WORK
509
510
      Triggered by the seminal works of Zügner et al. (2018); Dai et al. (2018), a research area
      emerged spanning attacks, defenses, and certification of GNNs (Jin et al., 2021; Günnemann, 2022;
511
      Schuchardt et al., 2021; Scholten et al., 2022; Guerranti et al., 2023; Gosch et al., 2023b). However,
512
      GTs have been entirely neglected despite their success on common benchmarks. Zhu et al. (2024)
513   is the sole exception acknowledging this gap. However, they solely propose a robust and sparse
514   transformer and evaluate it with non-adaptive poisoning attacks. Thus, they do not shine light on the
515   robustness of the diverse set of graph transformers nor do they study adaptive attacks.
516
      Our attack is rooted in the GNN robustness literature. Xu et al. (2019) proposes the first Pro-
517
      jected Gradient Descent attack for discrete L0 perturbations of the graph structure, with a focus
518
      on message-passing architectures. Geisler et al. (2021) extend this PGD with a randomization
519   scheme to obtain the efficient Projected Randomized Block Coordinate (PRBCD) attack. Gosch
520   et al. (2023a) extend PRBCD with local constraints, which is comparable with our relaxed GTs.
521   While highlighting the general nature of our framework, we leave the empirical evaluation for fu-
522   ture work. Further important related works are Lin et al. (2022); Zhu et al. (2018); Bojchevski
523   & Günnemann (2019), where the authors study similar approximations for perturbations on the
524   eigen-decomposition of the graph Laplacian. Moreover, Wang et al. (2023) attack message-passing
525   architectures on the UPFD fake news detection using reinforcement learning. As an entry to Node
526   Insertion Attacks (NIA), we refer to Wang et al. (2020); Zou et al. (2021).
527
528   9                   C ONCLUSION
529
530   We are the first to study the adversarial robustness of GTs and we provide the guiding principles
531   for designing adaptive attacks. We study five representative GTs which use three of the most com-
532   monly used PEs: random-walk-based PEs; distance-based PEs; and spectral PEs. We empirically
533   demonstrate that GTs can be catastrophically fragile in some settings and remarkably robust w.r.t.
534   the studied attack in other settings. This diverse picture underlines the importance and need for
535   adaptive attacks to reveal nuanced robustness properties. Similarly, also the comparison of GT’s and
536
      GNN’s robustness w.r.t. the studied attacks does not allow for a conclusion about which approach is
      superior in terms of robustness. Nevertheless, our work sets the important cornerstone for empirical
537
      research in answering this very question. Finally, we leverage our adaptive attacks to obtain the first
538
      results for adversarial training with GTs. These results are promising and show that GTs have the
539   potential to become very robust against graph structure perturbations.

      A    L IMITATIONS
703
704
      All our adversarial attacks do neither provide a guarantee nor insights about how close they are to the
705   optimal adversarial example. We note that this is common practice in the adversarial learning com-
706   munity. Nevertheless, if we are able to find an adversarial example, this proves the non-robustness
707   of the studied model. We conducted the experiment with utmost care, s.t. we expect the results to be
708   reasonable upper bounds that will be challenging to beat for future work. We still state clearly that
709   due to the high computational cost of dense GTs, we cannot certify that these efforts are sufficient
710   (e.g., our hyperparameter searches were comprehensive yet not exhaustive).
711
712   B    B ROADER IMPACT
713
714   While the threat model of attacking fake news detection could have a negative societal impact, our
715   methods are applicable mostly in a white-box setting and, therefore, are much more useful to those
716   who are developing fake news detection to probe and improve the robustness of their models. If a
717   model developer has access to the right tools, we expect its information advantage to outweigh the
718   availability of attacks by far.

       F    A DVERSARIAL TRAINING ALGORITHM
1027
1028
       We based our implementation of the adversarial training on the ‘Free’ adversarial training of Shafahi
1029   et al. (2019). Pseudocode for our adversarial training is given in Alg. 1. The main modifications
1030   are that we do two separate forward and backwards passes for the attack and model respectively.
1031   This is because: (1) the attack and model often have distinct loss functions that they optimize for,
1032   and (2) we sample a discrete structure perturbation for the model, such that the perturbed graph is
1033   included in the original valid sample space. Another difference is that we need to iterate over the
1034   graphs in the minibatch separately. This is a limitation caused by: (1) The attack optimization steps
1035   are not trivial to parallelize, especially for node injection attacks, and (2) the PE computations (e.g.
1036   Laplacian eigen-decomposition) are also not easily parallelizable and need to re-computed for each
1037
       new perturbed graph.
1038   Given these limitations, our adversarial training is much less efficient. It requires at least 2·|B| times
1039   more model evaluations than normal training. Furthermore, for many GTs the PE computation is
1040   one of the most computationally expensive steps. Therefore, PEs are usually precomputed in a pre-
1041   processing step. During adversarial training, we need to compute PEs for new unseen perturbations
1042   at each step, which further increases the overhead. Nonetheless, following the main idea of Shafahi
1043
       et al. (2019) alleviates some of the overhead and makes it somewhat practically feasible.
1044

       H.2   BACKPROPAGATION : BREAKING UP REPEATED EIGENVALUES
1182
1183   The only thing preventing the use of auto-differentiation to compute gradients through the eigen-
1184   decomposition is the presence of repeated eigenvalues. As a workaround, Lin et al. (2022) propose
1185   adding small amplitude random noise to the entire adjacency matrix. While this usually separates the
1186   repeated eigenvalues, it is not guaranteed to. We propose a different approach in which the smallest
1187   possible perturbation term is added to the Laplacian matrix, such that the repeated eigenvalues are
       guaranteed to be separated while the eigenvectors remain unchanged.
