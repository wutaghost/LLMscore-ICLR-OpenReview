009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Simulating object deformations is a critical challenge in many scientific domains,
012            with applications ranging from robotics to materials science. Learned Graph
013            Network Simulators (GNSs) are an efficient alternative to traditional mesh-based
014            physics simulators. Their speed and inherent differentiability make them par-
015            ticularly well-suited for inverse design problems such as process optimization.
016            However, these applications typically offer limited available data, making GNSs
017            difficult to use in real-world scenarios. We frame mesh-based simulation as a
018
               meta-learning problem and apply conditional Neural Processes to adapt to new
               simulation scenarios with little data. In addition, we address the problem of
019
               error accumulation common in previous step-based methods by combining this
020
               approach with movement primitives, allowing efficient predictions of full trajec-
021            tories. We validate the effectiveness of our approach, called Movement-primitive
022            Meta-MeshGraphNet (M3GN), through a variety of experiments, outperforming
023            state-of-the-art step-based baseline GNSs and step-based meta-learning methods.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   The simulation of complex physical systems is of paramount importance in a wide variety of
029   engineering disciplines, including structural mechanics (Yazid et al., 2009; Zienkiewicz & Taylor,
030   2005; Stanova et al., 2015), fluid dynamics (Chung, 1978; Zienkiewicz et al., 2013; Connor & Brebbia,
031   2013), and electromagnetism (Jin, 2015; Polycarpou, 2022; Reddy, 1994). In particular, the simulation
032   of object deformations under external forces is crucial for, e.g., robotic applications (Scheikl et al.,
033
      2022; Wang & Zhu, 2023; Linkerhägner et al., 2023). Mesh-based simulations are appealing for
      such problems due to the computational efficiency and accuracy of the underlying finite element
034
      method (Brenner & Scott, 2008; Reddy, 2019). However, the diversity of the problems to be
035
      modeled usually necessitates the development of task-specific simulators to accurately capture the
036   relevant physical quantities (Reddy & Gartling, 2010). Such specialized simulators can be slow and
037   cumbersome to use, especially for large-scale simulations (Paszynski, 2016; Hughes et al., 2005).
038
039
      Thus, data-driven models trained on reference simulations have gained attention as an appealing alter-
      native (Guo et al., 2016; Da Wang et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022). Among them, general-purpose Graph
040
      Network Simulators (GNSs) have recently become increasingly popular (Battaglia et al., 2018; Pfaff
041
      et al., 2021; Allen et al., 2022b; 2023; Linkerhägner et al., 2023). GNSs encode the simulated
042   system as a graph of interacting entities whose dynamics are predicted using Graph Neural Networks
043   (GNNs) (Bronstein et al., 2021). These models are one to two orders of magnitude faster than
044   classical simulators (Pfaff et al., 2021) while being fully differentiable, which makes them highly
045   effective for, e.g., inverse design problems (Allen et al., 2022b; Xu et al., 2021).
046
      GNSs are typically trained through simple next-step supervision (Battaglia et al., 2018; Pfaff et al.,
047
      2021; Allen et al., 2023). During inference, entire trajectories are simulated by iteratively predicting
048   per-node dynamics from an initial system state in an autoregressive manner. This approach is
049   prone to error accumulation over time, especially as the input distribution diverges from the training
050   set (Brandstetter et al., 2022; Han et al., 2022). To mitigate this issue, existing approaches add noise
051   to the input during training and predict the dynamics based on the original inputs, thus following an
052   implicit de-noising objective (Pfaff et al., 2021; Brandstetter et al., 2022). While this significantly
053   improves simulation stability over longer rollouts, the auto-regressive inference of next-step GNSs
      still propagates and accumulates errors made in earlier prediction steps. These methods also struggle

079   To address these limitations, we reformulate learned mesh-based simulation as a trajectory-level
080   meta-learning problem. Here, the initial mesh states of a trajectory serve as a context set on
081   which to condition future predictions. We employ Conditional Neural Processs (CNPs) (Garnelo
082   et al., 2018a) to aggregate these context sets and the dynamics inferred from them into a latent
083
      descriptor, which is then used to predict the rest of the trajectory. This method enables efficient
      training and rapid adaptation to trajectory-specific simulation parameters, such as unknown object
084
      material properties, during inference. In addition, we mitigate the problem of error accumulation
085
      by directly predicting full simulation trajectories of the mesh nodes instead of iteratively predicting
086   their next-step dynamics. To this end, we represent the simulation using node-level Probabilistic
087   Dynamic Movement Primitives (ProDMPs) (Schaal, 2006; Paraschos et al., 2013; Li et al., 2023),
088   which allows for an efficient encoding of higher-order dynamics at arbitrary temporal resolution.
089   The resulting method, called Movement-Primitive Meta-MeshGraphNet (M3GN), allows efficient
090   generation of context-dependent simulation trajectories that accurately infer and integrate unknown
091   system properties. Figure 1 shows examples for different tasks, while Figure 2 provides an overview
092   of our approach.
093   To validate the effectiveness of M3GN, we introduce three novel tasks based on challenging de-
094   formable object simulations with varying object materials. These include a planar plate bending under
095   stress, and a variety of falling objects that collide with the ground. In addition to these new tasks, we
096   evaluate M3GN on an existing suite of experiments (Linkerhägner et al., 2023).Our results show that
097   our method provides superior simulation accuracy compared to several variants of MeshGraphNet
098   (MGN) Pfaff et al. (2021), the state-of-the-art GNS.1 Further, the ProDMPs trajectory representation
099   of M3GN reduces the number of required model calls, improving inference runtime by up to 32 times
100
      compared to MGN.
101   In summary, we (i) propose M3GN, a novel GNS that combines meta-learning and movement
102   primitives to predict node-level simulation trajectories based on initial system state contexts;
103   (ii) introduce two challenging deformation prediction tasks involving varying object materials;
104   (iii) evaluate and compare our method to state-of-the-art GNSs, demonstrating superior prediction
105   performance.

129   Graph Network Simulators. Deep neural networks for physical simulations can provide signifi-
130   cant speedups over traditional simulators while being fully differentiable (Pfaff et al., 2021; Allen
131   et al., 2022a), making them a natural choice for applications like model-based Reinforcement Learn-
132   ing (Mora et al., 2021) and Inverse Design problems (Baqué et al., 2018; Durasov et al., 2021;
133   Allen et al., 2022a). A popular class of learned neural simulators are Graph Network Simulators
134   (GNSs) (Battaglia et al., 2016; Sanchez-Gonzalez et al., 2020). GNSs utilize Message Passing
135   Networks (MPNs), a special type of GNN (Scarselli et al., 2009; Bronstein et al., 2021) that repre-
136
      sentationally encompasses the function class of many classical solvers (Brandstetter et al., 2022).
      GNS handle physical data by modeling arbitrary entities and their relations as a graph. Applications
137
      of GNSs include particle-based simulations (Li et al., 2019; Sanchez-Gonzalez et al., 2020; Whitney
138
      et al., 2023), atomic force prediction (Hu et al., 2021), and fluid dynamic problems (Brandstetter et al.,
139   2022). These models have additionally been applied to the mesh-based prediction of deformable
140   objects (Pfaff et al., 2021; Weng et al., 2021; Han et al., 2022; Fortunato et al., 2022; Linkerhägner
141   et al., 2023). Recent extensions handle rigid objects (Allen et al., 2022b; 2023; Lopez-Guevara et al.,
142   2024) and integrate learned adaptive mesh refinement strategies (Plewa et al., 2005; Freymuth et al.,
143   2023) into the simulator (Wu et al., 2023). Existing work that considers unknown material properties
144   in simulations of deformable objects combines the GNS prediction with point cloud information to
145   improve long-term predictions Linkerhägner et al. (2023). This method requires a constant stream of
146   point clouds to ground the simulation in, but can not aggregate this information into a description of
147
      the material properties. Additionally, the DEL method (Wang et al., 2024) integrates physical priors
      from the Discrete Element Analysis (DEA) framework with learnable graph kernels, addressing
148
      the challenges of simulating 3D particle dynamics from 2D images. In the context of larger-scale
149
      simulations, foundation models are gaining traction in neural simulation tasks, as exemplified by
150   Aurora (Bodnar et al., 2024), a large-scale model trained on extensive climate data. While Aurora
151   demonstrates impressive performance on atmospheric predictions, including global air pollution
152   and weather forecasts, it requires significantly more data for fine-tuning compared to our approach,
153   which focuses on efficient adaptation with fewer data points. Notably, all previously mentioned
154   GNSs predict system dynamics iteratively from a given state, whereas we directly estimate entire
155   trajectories, improving rollout stability and reducing function calls. Related to our approach is the
156   Equivariant Graph Neural Operator (EGNO) (Xu et al., 2024), which also predicts full trajectories
157   using SE(3) equivariance to model 3D dynamics and capture spatial and temporal correlations. In
158
      contrast to the related work, which rely on supervised learning or fine-tuning a foundation model, we
      employ meta-learning for efficient adaptation to new trajectories.
159
160   Meta-Learning. Meta-learning (Schmidhuber, 1992; Thrun & Pratt, 1998; Vilalta & Drissi, 2005;
161   Hospedales et al., 2022) extracts inductive biases from a training set of related tasks in order to
      increase data efficiency on unseen tasks drawn from the same task distribution. In contrast to other


                                                                       3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      multi-task learning methods, such as transfer learning (Krizhevsky et al., 2012; Golovin et al., 2017;
163   Zhuang et al., 2020), which merely fine-tune or combine standard single-task models, meta-learning
164   makes the multi-task setting explicit in the model architecture (Bengio et al., 1991; Ravi & Larochelle,
165   2017; Andrychowicz et al., 2016; Volpp et al., 2019; Santoro et al., 2016; Snell et al., 2017). This
166   explicit architecture allows the resulting meta-models to learn how to learn new tasks from a small
167   number of example contexts. A popular variant is Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning (MAML) (Finn
168   et al., 2017; Grant et al., 2018; Finn et al., 2018; Kim et al., 2018), which employs standard single-task
169   models and formulates a multi-task optimization procedure. Neural Processes (NPs) (Garnelo et al.,
170   2018a;b; Kim et al., 2019; Gordon et al., 2019; Louizos et al., 2019; Volpp et al., 2021; 2023) instead
171
      build on a multi-task model architecture (Heskes, 2000; Bakker & Heskes, 2003) but employ standard
      gradient based optimization algorithms (Kingma & Ba, 2015; Kingma & Welling, 2014; Rezende
172
      et al., 2014; Zaheer et al., 2017). Here, we use Conditional Neural Processes (CNPs) (Garnelo
173
      et al., 2018a), which aggregate learned features over a variable-sized context set to yield a latent task
174   description that our downstream GNS is conditioned on. Compared to regular NPs, CNPs assume
175   a deterministic task description, eliminating the need for a distribution over latent variables. This
176   assumption simplifies and accelerates the training process, as our objective is to predict a single
177   precise simulation trajectory from the context set.

      Conventional GNSs encode the state of the simulated system as a graph, feed it through the MPN,
197
      and interpret the per-node outputs as velocities or accelerations (Pfaff et al., 2021). These dynamics
198   are used to forward the simulation in time using, e.g., a forward-Euler integrator (Sanchez-Gonzalez
199   et al., 2020). The graph encodes relative distances and velocities between entities instead of absolute
200   ones, as the resulting equivariance to translation improves generalization (Sanchez-Gonzalez et al.,
201   2020). GNSs usually minimize a next-step Mean Squared Error (MSE) per node during training,
202   adding carefully tuned implicit denoising strategies (Pfaff et al., 2021; Brandstetter et al., 2022) to
203   stabilize long-term predictions. During inference, they compute trajectories by iteratively predicting
204   and integrating their output in an autoregressive fashion. If some simulated objects, like the col-
205   lider, are known, only the remaining nodes are predicted. Our method instead uses a ProDMP to
206
      predict a compact representation of a whole trajectory per system node, reducing the effect of error
      accumulation, similar to temporal bundling (Brandstetter et al., 2022).

      Meta-Learning and Graph Network Simulators. To enable generalization across tasks with
      varying properties, we frame GNS as a meta-learning problem. In this setup, each task corresponds
243
      to a simulation of a deformable object with unknown material properties. The goal is to learn
244
      a simulator that can adapt quickly to a specific scenario using a limited amount of context data.
245   Following the notation of Volpp et al. (2021), the meta-dataset D = D1:L consists of simulation
246   trajectories Dl = {Gl,1 . . . Gl,T } of length T , where T is the trajectory length. Each simulation step
247   Gl,t = (ml,t , ul,t ) represents a graph capturing both the deformable object mesh ml,t (describing
248   its position and topology) and an optional rigid collider ul,t . Physical proximity is used to define
249   graph edges that model interactions between the deformable object and the collider. At test time,
250   the first T c ≪ T simulation frames, Gl,1:T c , are observed as a context set to predict the remaining
251   trajectory. Following prior work (Pfaff et al., 2021), we assume access to the full collider trajectory
252   during prediction, resulting in the complete context set:
253                                             Dlc = {Gl,1 , . . . Gl,T c } ∪ {ul,T c +1 , . . . , ul,T }.                          (2)

      To provide a clear reference point for discussion, we define the anchor time step as the final time step
      T c of the context set. The corresponding anchor graph, Gl,T c , represents the system’s state at this
256
      point and serves as the starting state for trajectory prediction by the GNSs. Notably, the anchor graph
257
      Gl,T c alone does not capture the complete system state, as the material properties of the deformable
258   object remain unknown. These properties must be inferred from the prior simulation steps, Gl,1:T c , to
259   enable accurate trajectory predictions. Figure 3 illustrates this setup.

      Model Architecture. Our model architecture, M3GN, is designed to learn from context data and
      predict future simulation steps by leveraging a combination of graph network simulation and meta-
262
      learning techniques. The architecture consists of two parts: the computation of the latent task
263
      description from the context data and the actual graph network simulation of future simulation steps.

268   by using each graph as an input, and setting its labels as the node-wise velocities. This approach
269   allows the model to focus on dynamics rather than absolute positions, which are more task-specific.

344   Setup. Our experimental setup largely follows previous work (Linkerhägner et al., 2023). The graph
345
      representation views the mesh vertices as nodes and adds edges according to the mesh topology within
      an object, and based on Euclidean distance between different objects. We employ one-hot encoding
346
      to differentiate between deformable objects and colliders, omitting explicit world edges (Pfaff et al.,
347
      2021). The edges additionally contain the relative distances between their nodes.
348
349   For both the context MPN as well as the simulator MPN, we use 15 message passing steps. Each
350   message passing step uses separate 1-layer MLPs with a latent dimension of 128 and LeakyReLU
351
      activations for its node and edge updates. We evaluate the Full Rollout MSE, which is calculated
      as the average of all simulation MSEs following the anchor time step, and the 10-step MSE, which
352
      is the average performance for the next 10 simulation steps following the anchor time step. Both
353
      metrics are averaged over all test set trajectories. We report the interquartile mean and bootstrapped
354   confidence intervals (Agarwal et al., 2021) over 8 random seeds for each experiment. We evaluate the
355   metrics for various context sizes ranging from 2 to 30 steps, always setting the anchor time step to
356   the last context mesh position. Appendix C provides additional details on our experimental setup.
357
      Datasets. We validate our method on five different simulation datasets based on three different
358

518   5    C ONCLUSION
519
520
      We introduce Movement-Primitive Meta-MeshGraphNet (M3GN), a novel Graph Network Simulator
521
      that combines movement primitives and trajectory-level meta-learning for efficient and accurate
522   long-term predictions in physical simulations. Our method dynamically adapts to provided context
523   information during inference, allowing for an accurate prediction of deformations under unknown
524   object properties. Additionally, it effectively addresses the issue of error accumulation while reducing
525   the number of required simulator function calls. To validate the effectiveness of M3GN, we propose
526   three novel deformation prediction tasks with uncertain material properties. Results on these tasks and
527   existing datasets show that our method consistently outperforms a strong Graph Network Simulators
528   baseline, even when providing the baseline with oracle information about the material property.
529   Limitations and Future Work. We currently consider each trajectory as a task, and require initial
530   states of this trajectory as a context set during inference. As generating such data is often impractical,
531   we plan to group simulations with the same system properties into the same task. This adaptation
532   will enable data-efficient generalization to unseen properties regardless of the underlying simulated
533   objects. We also plan to integrate online re-planning of trajectories, predicting trajectory segments
534   with every model forward pass. This process may increase coordination between simulated nodes
535   across segments, while maintaining the benefits of a compact multi-step trajectory representation.
536   Broader Impact Statement Our proposed Graph Network Simulator can positively impact various
537   fields relying on computational modeling and simulation by significantly reducing computational
538   cost compared to traditional simulators while providing accurate simulations. However, efficient and
539   accurate simulation of physical systems also comes with potential negative impacts, such as, e.g., the
      development of advanced weapon models.

      We provide an overview of the probabilistic dynamic movement primitives (ProDMP) formulations
867   utilized in this paper, starting with the foundational methods: Dynamic Movement Primitives (DMPs)
868   and Probabilistic Movement Primitives (ProMPs).
869
870   A.1    DMP S
871
872    Schaal (2006) introduced Dynamic Movement Primitives (DMPs), which integrate a forcing term
873   into a dynamical system to generate smooth trajectories from given initial conditions3 , such as a
874   robot’s position and velocity at a particular time. A DMP trajectory is governed by a second-order
875   linear ordinary differential equation (ODE) as follows:

880   system at a specific time t, respectively. Constants α and β are spring-damper parameters, g is the
881   goal attractor, and τ is a time constant modulating the speed of trajectory execution.
882   The functions φi (x) represent the basis functions for the forcing term, as shown in Fig. 8a, while the
883   phase variable x = x(t) ∈ [0, 1] captures the execution progress. The trajectory’s shape is determined
884   by the weight parameters wi ∈ w for i = 1, . . . , N and the goal term g. The trajectory [yt ]t=0:T is
885   typically computed by numerically integrating the dynamical system from the start to the endpoint.
886   However, this numerical process is computationally expensive (Bahl et al., 2020; Li et al., 2023), as
887   its cost scales with the trajectory length and the resolution of the numerical integration.

900

902   limiting their ability to specify a given initial condition for a trajectory or predict smooth transitions
903   between two ProMP trajectories with differing parameter vectors.

991    B     A RCHITECTURE AND M ETHOD D ETAILS
992
993    This section offers detailed insights into our methodology and the architectural decisions guiding our
994    approach.
995
996    B.1    G RAPH E NCODINGS
997
998    In processing the initial graph G∗,T c , we create edges between the mesh and the collider based on
999    a radius graph. Specifically, we connect mesh and collider nodes for Deformable Plate and Tissue
1000   Manipulation if their euclidean distance is smaller than 0.3. In the Tissue Manipulation task, the
1001
       collider is given as a single node which is connected to the tip of the tissue. It marks the grasping
       point of a gripper. In the Planar Bending task, we add an additional node feature to the nodes which
1002
       get directly influenced by the external force. Therefore, no collider is used in this task. For the
1003
       Falling Teddy Bear and the Mixed Objects Falling task, we implicitly model the ground as a collider
1004   by adding the current z position of every node to its node features (Sanchez-Gonzalez et al., 2018).
1005   This quantity gets updated for the step-based methods.
1006
1007   B.2    P RO DMP D ETAILS
1008
1009   Initialization of ProDMPs necessitates node velocities for the anchor time step T c . We employ a
1010   linear approximation, leveraging data from the previous time step T c − 1.
1011   Similar to the relative encoding of node positions in the MPN, we employ a technique in ProDMP
1012   to derive relative trajectories. Initially, we integrate a relative goal position as part of the node
1013   weights wv . Utilizing this approach, trajectories commence from the origin and traverse towards their
1014   respective relative goals. Subsequently, we adjust all positions by the initial position. This strategy
1015   fosters model generalization across various nodes.
1016
       The parameter τ , as described in Equation 7, is learned globally across all tasks using a compact
1017   MLP. The model’s final layer employs a scaled sigmoid function for parameter estimation.

       other hyperparameters as the latent task description dimension. In general, we optimized all methods
1033   on all tasks separately, however, we noticed that over different tasks and methods some parameters
1034   had the same best configuration. We did not use the test data for this, but tuned all hyperparameters
1035   on a separate validation split. This split was also used to determine the best epoch checkpoint to
1036   mitigate any overfitting effects.
1037
       In the end, all methods worked well with a learning rate of 5.0 × 10−4 except in the Planar Bending
1038
       task. Here, our hyperparameter optimization indicated that the trajectory based methods benefit from
1039   a smaller learning rate of 1.0 × 10−5 . For MGN, we experimented with different input noise scales.
1040   Notably, for the Deformable Plate and the Planar Bending task, a smaller noise scale improved
1041   performance significantly. In the falling objects tasks, we also explored second-order predictions,
1042   such as node accelerations, instead of velocity predictions. Following the approach in Pfaff et al.
1043   (2021), we adjusted the labels accordingly and conducted preliminary evaluations. However, since
1044   direct velocity predictions yielded superior results, we opted for them as our final approach, as
1045   presented in the main paper.

1048   C.1   EGNO T RAINING
1049
1050   For the Equivariant Graph Neural Operator (EGNO) method, we used the original code from Xu et al.
1051   (2024) for the model implementation. Since EGNO can only predict for a fixed next horizon, we
1052   cut the remaining prediction when using a later anchor time step. This is done during training and
1053   evaluation.
1054
1055   C.2   MGN T RAINING
1056
       We mainly follow Pfaff et al. (2021) for the training of the MGN baseline. The only difference is
1057
       the incorporation of current and historic velocity node features. Pfaff et al. (2021) consider this in
1058   their experiments but they show in their experiment suite that it does not improve the results and can
1059   lead to overfitting. This is different to our results. For us, on all tasks except Mixed Objects Falling,
1060   adding the current and historic velocities of nodes improves the results. We follow the Gaussian
1061   random walk noise injection for the velocity features from Sanchez-Gonzalez et al. (2020).
