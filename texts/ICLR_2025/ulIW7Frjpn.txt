009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) have shown promising advancements in lossless
012            compression due to their excellent next-token prediction capabilities. However,
013            there is a gap between LLM-based compressors and classical transform-based
014            codecs. Existing LLM-based compressors function solely as entropy coders, fo-
015            cusing on compressing redundant data in the raw domain. In contrast, classical
016            codecs typically transform raw data into more compact features in the latent do-
017            main before applying entropy coding. But LLM-based compressors have not dis-
018
               cussed this case. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work to introduce an
               LLM-based entropy model for transform coding. Specifically, we propose a sim-
019
               ple yet effective fine-tuning strategy, tested across various codecs for both images
020
               and speeches. With less than 2% parameters are fine-tuned, the LLMs can serve as
021            highly effective entropy models for well-established transform-based compression
022            codecs. For instance, LLaMA3-8B paired with arithmetic coding compresses la-
023            tent image codes on Kodak to 4.62% and speech codes on LibriTTS to 42.53% of
024            their transformed sizes after fine-tuning. Our proposed methods achieve notable
025            BD-rate improvements of 54.07% over JPEG, 17.61% over VQGAN, and 34.61%
026            over SpeechTokenizer. These findings highlight the great potential of integrating
027            LLMs into codecs to significantly improve coding efficiency. Source codes will
028            be released upon acceptance.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Large language models (LLMs) have achieved great success on various tasks and are highly-efficient
      for probabilistic prediction. According to source information theory (Shannon, 1948), probability
034
      prediction combined with entropy coding is equivalent to lossless data compression, as the minimum
035
      bit length required to represent data is determined by its − log2 probability likelihood (MacKay,
036   2003). Therefore, by combining LLM’s predictive capabilities with entropy coding methods like
037   Huffman coding (Huffman, 1952) and arithmetic coding (Pasco, 1977; Rissanen, 1976), LLMs can
038   function as highly effective lossless compressors. Some works have already validated their compet-
039   itive compression performance by feeding the text, images and audio data directly into LLMs, such
040   as Valmeekam et al. (2023) and Deletang et al. (2024).
041
      However, there is a clear gap between LLM-based compressors and classical transform-based
042   codecs. Existing LLM-based compressors (Deletang et al., 2024; Valmeekam et al., 2023) solely
043   play a role of entropy coders and process redundant data in its raw domain, while classical codecs
044   (Wallace, 1992; Esser et al., 2021; Cheng et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2023b; Zhang et al., 2024b; Du et al.,
045   2023) typically transform the data into latent feature domain, achieving more compact representa-
046   tions and then apply entropy coding to further improve compression efficiency. Hence in this paper,
047   we bridge the gap by integrating LLM-based entropy models with transform coding frameworks.
048   We first transform the data into compact latent domain and perform quantization. The quantized
049   discrete codes are then fed into LLMs for context probability prediction, followed by entropy cod-
050
      ing to generate the binary bitstream, see Fig. 1. Leveraging the powerful prediction capabilities of
      LLMs, we can further enhance the compression performance of classical transform-based codecs.
051
052   Although quantized latent codes can be treated as tokens for LLMs’ input, two key challenges still
053   remain. First, the latent codes are typically two- or multi-dimensional, which is incompatible with
      the sequential one-dimensional input format required by LLMs. A reasonable flatten method needs

      In this paper, we demonstrate that large language models are stronger entropy models for transform-

      based codecs. We integrate LLMs into transform coding frameworks and apply simple fine-tuning

      to adapt them for compression across various modalities. Experiments are conducted on three types

080   (DCT) and quantization like JPEG (Wallace, 1992), codecs using the neural network-based trans-
081   form and vector quantization (VQ) like VQGAN (Esser et al., 2021), and codecs using network-
082   based transform and residual vector quantization (RVQ), such as SpeechTokenizer (Zhang et al.,
083   2024b). Our method demonstrates satisfactory performance across all these codecs, notably im-
084   proving their compression efficiency.
085   Generally speaking, our contributions can be summarized as
086
087          • We extensively evaluate recently-released LLMs in lossless text compression and select
088            Llama3-8B (AI@Meta, 2024) as the backbone for our proposed entropy model.
089          • We propose a latent-codes arrangement module to align the dimension between LLMs and
090            latent codes, along with a simple yet effective fine-tuning strategy to adapt LLM-based
091            entropy models to various compressors.
092          • Experiments show that our LLM-based entropy model can significantly improve the com-
093            pression efficiency of image codecs like JPEG and VQGAN by 54.07% and 17.61%, and
094            enhance speech codecs like SpeechTokenizer by 34.61%.

      3.1    BACKGROUND
136
      Given a sequence of data x1:n with distribution p1:n , lossless compression methods aim to encode
137   the data into binary streams C(x1:n ), minimizing its bit length lc (x1:n ) while preserving the original
138   information. According to the source-coding theorem, the expected minimal lc (x1:n ) is given by
139   its entropy Ex∼p [− log2 p]. In existing LLM-based compressors, x represents words, pixels, and
140   speech samples for text, image, and speech compression, respectively.
141
      However, the actual data distribution is usually unknown during compression. Therefore, we au-
142
      toregressively predict the distribution p̂1:n using LLMs during encoding and decoding, where the
143   probability space of is confined to the LLMs’ vocabulary size, requiring that both the input and
144   output layers of LLMs must match this dimensionality for accurate prediction. Upon applying arith-
145   metic coding, the generated bit length can be approximated via cross-entropy as

184
185   This section presents our LLM-based transform coding framework, demonstrating its effectiveness
186   across three types of codecs: (1) codecs using the classic DCT and quantization approach, such as
187   JPEG; (2) codecs using the neural network-based transform and vector quantization, like VQGAN;
188   (3) codecs using network-based transform and residual vector quantization, like SpeechTokenizer.
189
190   4.1         OVERALL F RAMEWORK
191
192   Workflow Fig. 2 illustrates the proposed LLM-based transform coding framework. On the encod-
193   ing side, raw data x is transformed by T (·) into latent features y. Then, Q(·) quantizes these features
194   into discrete latent codes ŷ with a distribution p. The process is described as:
195                                                        ŷ = Q(T (x))                                                                                                  (2)
196
197   The latent codes are discrete integer values and can be directly treated as tokens for LLMs’ in-
198   put. However, the latent codes are typically multi-dimensional and not compatible with the one-
199
      dimensional input format required by LLMs. Hence we first flatten the codes in raster order to
      preserve spatial correlations. Due to LLMs’ context limitations, we split the flattened codes into
200
      several chunks ŷ1:n of size n. Each chunk is fed into the LLMs sequentially, where the conditional
201
      probability p̂(ŷi | ŷ1:i−1 ) is calculated based on its context. Arithmetic coding is then applied to
202   compress the latent codes to their entropy limit and optimize coding efficiency. The expected bit
203   length is determined by cross-entropy like eq. (1) and can be formulated as eq. (3).
204                                                   " n                             #

211   between quantization and LLM-based entropy model. This module maps the quantized latent codes

235   Latent-Codes Arrangement To align the LLMs’ input and output dimension (i.e., the vocabulary
236   size) with the latent codes’ value range, we design a latent-codes arrangement module between
237   quantization and proposed LLM-based entropy module. JPEG’s latent codes include both positive
238   and negative values, hence we add an offset to ensure all values are non-negative and generate the
239   LLMs’ vocabulary. For VQGAN, which uses VQ and has a predefined codebook, we directly use
240   the codebook as LLMs’ vocabulary. SpeechTokenizer employs RVQ with layers having distinct
241   codebooks, we assign a specific vocabulary for each layer.
242
243   Fine-tuning of LLMs Upon determining the vocabulary size based on latent codes’ value ranges,
244   we modify the LLMs’ input and output dimensions accordingly. To better adapt the modified LLMs
245
      to each codec, we then freeze its backbone and fine-tune only the input and output layers. During
      fine-tuning, the prediction likelihood is maximized by minimizing the cross-entropy loss, which
246
      aligns with the goal of compression. Experiments demonstrate that with less than 1% parameters
247
      updated, the fine-tuned LLMs can effectively capture the latent codes’ characteristics and achieve
248   satisfactory performance across various datasets.

      4.2       I MAGE C OMPRESSION WITH LLM- BASED E NTROPY M ODEL
251
252   For image compression, we utilize the highly representative traditional codec JPEG and the widely
253   adopted image generation method VQGAN as anchor codecs, replacing their entropy models with
254   our proposed LLM-based entropy model, as shown in Fig. 3.
255
256   JPEG As shown in Fig. 3 (a), with JPEG as the anchor codec, raw images are divided into 8 × 8
257   patches and transformed into DCT domain. After scalar quantization, the latent codes are flattened
258   in raster order and an offset is added to ensure all values are non-negative before being fed into the
259
      LLMs. Though JPEG’s latent codes do not have a fixed maximum value, tests on several datasets
      reveal that most values fall between -127 and 128. Hence we empirically set the offset to 127 and the
260
      maximum value to 255, with all exceeding values truncated to 255, which means that the generated
261
      vocabulary size is 256. By altering the quality factors, variable bitrate compression can be achieved.
262
263
      VQGAN When using VQGAN as the anchor codec, raw images are transformed into the latent
264   domain via a neural network without being divided into patches, as shown in Fig. 3 (b). Then, they
265   are mapped to discrete integer values using a fixed codebook through vector quantization. There-
266   fore, the LLMs’ vocabulary size is directly determined by VQGAN’s codebook. VQGAN supports
267   several architectures with different codebook sizes and corresponding different bitrates, hence we
268   also conduct experiments to compare the compression performance using different codebook sizes.
269   During fine-tuning, 256 × 256 input images are quantized into latent codes of size 16 × 16 and
      flattened to length of 256 before being fed into LLMs. During inference, latent codes of different

      4.3   S PEECH C ODING WITH LLM- BASED E NTROPY M ODEL
293
294   When using speech codecs like SpeechTokenizer as anchor codecs, the raw speech is downsampled
295   and transformed by neural networks and then quantized hierarchically using residual vector quanti-
296   zation. The quantization layers share the same codebook but are mutually independent. Hence we
297   perform a separate latent-codes arrangement for each layer, as shown in Fig. 4.
298   Speech of length L is quantized into 8 equal-sized latent codes using eight-layer RVQ, with each
299   code containing N/320 values. We train separate LLMs for each layer, processing latent codes
300   sequentially. Typically, a single quantization layer produces fewer than 1024 latent codes, which
301   is situated within almost all LLMs’ context length. Hence it is unnecessary to chunk the input
302   speeches. During decoding, speeches are reconstructed by concatenating RVQ layers from the first
303   to the n-th. Changing the number of layers allows for variable speech quality, with the bitrate
304   adjusted accordingly. During inference, multiple speech inputs can be concatenated to maximize
305
      the GPU utilization and enable simultaneous compression.

311   Implementation Details During LLMs’ training process, we freeze the LLMs’ backbone and fine-
312   tune only the input and output layers. We use AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) with
313
      an initial learning rate of 2.5×10−4 and a cosine annealing scheduler with warm restarts. The β1 and
      β2 values are set to 0.9 and 0.99, respectively, with a weight decay of 1×10−2 to prevent overfitting.
314
      Automatic mixed precision training is employed to reduce GPU memory usage and accelerate both
315
      training and inference. Fine-tuning runs for 5 epochs per dataset, with quantized latent codes pre-
316   extracted to further save GPU memory. All experiments are conducted on an NVIDIA GTX 4090
317   GPU with 24GB of memory.
318
319   Datasets We fine-tune the LLMs for image compression using a subset of ImageNet
320   database (Russakovsky et al., 2015) and cropped them into 13,830 samples with the size of 256
321   × 256. We evaluate performance on a validation subset of ImageNet, Kodak (Kodak, 1999) and
322   CLIC (CLIC, 2021) datasets. For speech compression, LLMs are fine-tuned on the LibriTTS train-
323   ing dataset (Zen et al., 2019), with compression performance assessed using the LibriTTS clean-test
      and other-test dataset, high-quality LJSpeech (Ito & Johnson, 2017) dataset.

       Effect of Chunk Size on Compression Ratio According to the latent size, our anchor codecs
464
       fall into two categories. The first, like JPEG, generates latent codes matching the original image
465
       size, requiring images to be split into multiple chunks due to LLMs’ context limitation. The second
466    type, such as VQGAN and SpeechTokenizer, transforms raw data into dowm-sampled latent codes,
467    allowing multiple tokens to be combined and compressed in a single input.
468
469
       Fig. 7 illustrates the compression performance across chunk sizes from 256 to 8192. The boxplots
       ”PP”, ”IS”, and ”SL” in (a), (b), and (c) represent processed pixels per image chunk for JPEG, the
470
       number of image samples in VQGAN, and speech length in SpeechTokenizer, respectively. Larger
471
       chunk sizes allow LLMs to utilize more contextual information, significantly improving the com-
472    pression ratio of latent codes (shown on the right y-axis). For JPEG, the latent’s compression ratio
473    improves from 10.78% to 5.3% on Kodak and 9.56% to 5.02% on CLIC. For VQGAN and Speech-
474    Tokenizer, whose latent compression ratio are relatively higher, increasing the chunk size boosts the
475    compression ratio by about 1% and 5%, respectively.
476
477    Limitations in Compression Complexity Our LLM-based entropy model requires autoregressive
478    probability predictions during both encoding and decoding. Fig. 8 provides the inference time per
479    latent code for different vocabulary sizes and chunk sizes. As the vocabulary size increases, the
480    probability space for each latent code expands. resulting in significant increases in inference time.
481    Similarly, when the chunk size grows, each code’s prediction context becomes longer, which also
482
       slows down the inference time. To ensure an acceptable encoding and decoding time, we typically
       use chunk sizes less than 2048. Recent developments in lightweight LLMs (Abdin et al., 2024; Team
483
       et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a) also provide more options for our future work.
484

       anchor codecs and chunk sizes. The running time here refers to the sum of entropy encoding and

      6    C ONCLUSION
530
531
      To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work to explore the potential of large language models
      as powerful entropy models in widely-used transform coding frameworks for images and speeches.
532
      We introduce a latent code arrangement module, and propose a simple yet effective fine-tuning
533
      strategy that adjusts less than 2% of the model parameters. We test our proposed method on various
534   types of image and speech codecs, including JPEG with traditional DCTs and scalar quantization,
535   VQGAN with neural transform and vector quantization, and SpeechTokenizer with neural transform
536   and residual vector quantization. Experiments show that LLaMA3-8B, combined with arithmetic
537   coding, reduces the latent size of image and speech codecs up to 4.62% on Kodak and 42.53% on
538   LibriTTS, respectively. Consequently, our approach achieves significant BD-Rate improvements,
539   outperforming classical codecs like JPEG, VQGAN, and SpeechTokenizer by 54.07%, 17.61%, and
      36.61%, respectively.
