011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent advances have shown that statistical tests for the rank of cross-covariance
014
               matrices play an important role in causal discovery. These rank tests include
               partial correlation tests as special cases and provide further graphical information
015
               about latent variables. Existing rank tests typically assume that all the continuous
016
               variables can be perfectly measured, and yet, in practice many variables can
017            only be measured after discretization. For example, in psychometric studies,
018            the continuous level of certain personality dimensions of a person can only be
019            measured after being discretized into order-preserving options such as disagree,
020            neutral, and agree. Motivated by this, we propose Mixed data Permutation-based
021            Rank Test (MPRT), which properly controls the statistical errors even when some
022            or all variables are discretized. Theoretically, we establish the exchangeability
023            and estimate the asymptotic null distribution by permutations; as a consequence,
024            MPRT can effectively control the Type I error in the presence of discretization
025
               while previous methods cannot. Empirically, our method is validated by extensive
               experiments on synthetic data and real-world data to demonstrate its effectiveness
026
               as well as applicability in causal discovery (our code will be available).

029   1    I NTRODUCTION AND R ELATED W ORK
030
031   Recent advances have shown that the rank of a cross-covariance matrix and its statistical test play
032   essential roles in multiple fields of statistics especially in causal discovery (Sullivant et al., 2010;
033
      Spirtes, 2013). From one perspective, Independence and Conditional Independence (CI) are crucial
      concepts in causal discovery and Bayesian network learning (Pearl et al., 2000; Spirtes et al., 2000;
034
      Koller & Friedman, 2009) due to its relation to d-separations (Pearl, 1988), and it has been shown
035
      that rank tests take those linear CI tests as special cases (Sullivant et al., 2010; Di, 2009; Dong et al.,
036   2024). From another point of view, rank of a cross-covariance matrix corresponds to t-separations
037   in a graph (Sullivant et al., 2010), which contain graphical information that can be used to identify
038   latent variables (Huang et al., 2022; Dong et al., 2024). A more detailed discussion about related
039   work can be found in Appendix D.
040
      Existing statistical rank tests (Anderson, 1984) are often built upon Canonical Correlation Analysis
041
      (CCA) (Jordan, 1875; Hotelling, 1992), with a likelihood ratio based test statistics. Despite their
042   effectiveness, existing methods rely on the strong assumption that all the variables concerned can be
043   perfectly measured. However, in many fields, it is often the case that the best available data are just
044   discretized approximations of some underlying continuous variable (formally defined in Eq. 1). For
045   example, in mental health, anxiety levels are often categorized into levels such as mild, moderate,
046   or severe, according to some latent thresholds (Johnson et al., 2019). Examples can be found in
047   multiple fields such as finance (Changsheng & Yongfeng, 2012), psychology (Lord & Novick, 2008),
048   biometrics (Finney, 1952) and econometrics (Nerlove & Press, 1973), where continuous variables are
049   often assumed to be observed as discretized values.
050   When discretization is present, existing rank tests can hardly work. The main reason lies is that
051   the discretized values only reflect the order of the data, leading to cross-covariance estimates that
052   may differ significantly from the underlying cross-covariance matrix (also illustrated in Figure 1).
053   Furthermore, even though the true underlying cross-covariance matrix can be estimated by maximum
      likelihood-based methods such as polychoric and polyserial correlations (Olsson et al., 1982; Olsson,


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      1979), they cannot be directly plugged into existing rank tests. This is because the involved discretiza-
055   tion and maximum likelihood processes change the distribution of test statistics to a considerable
056   extent and thus the p-values cannot be correctly calculated. As a consequence, Type I errors of
057   existing methods cannot be effectively controlled. Both of these points are elaborated in Section 2.2.
058
      To properly address the issue of discretization, in this paper, we propose a novel statistic rank test
059
      based on permutation, i.e., Mixed data Permutation-based Rank Test (MPRT) that can accommodate
060
      continuous, partially discretized, or fully discretized observations. Specifically, in the presence of
061   discretization, the underlying cross-covariance can be estimated by maximum likelihood estimator,
062   but the information loss resulting from discretization and the additional estimation steps make the
063   derivation of the null distribution highly non-trivial. To this end, we start with the continuous case and
064   establish exchangeability of linear projections of concerned variables (captured by Theorem 4), based
065   on which the null distribution can be empirically estimated by permutations. When some observations
066   are discretized, the exchangeability still holds but we do not have direct access to permutable data.
067   Fortunately, we show that the concerned statistic distribution can still be consistently estimated by
068   properly using permuted discretized observations (captured by Theorem 5). We summarize our major
069
      contributions as follows.
070
      • To our best knowledge, we propose the first statistic rank test i.e., Mixed data Permutation-based
071
        Rank Test (MPRT), that properly deals with the problem of discretization. Rank test takes partial
072     correlation CI test as a special case and thus the problem is crucial to many scientific fields such as
073     psychology, biometrics, and econometrics, where discretizations are ubiquitous.
074   • Theoretically, we estimate the asymptotic null distribution by effectively making use of data
075     permutations, and thus properly controls the Type I error. The setting considered is rather general:
076     for the test of rank(ΣX,Y ), both X and Y are allowed to be either fully continuous, partially
077     discretized, or fully discretized. Therefore, our method also includes the fully-continuous rank test
078     as a special case.
079   • Empirically, we validate our novel rank test under multiple synthetic settings where our method is
080     shown to control Type I error properly and Type II error effectively, while existing methods cannot.
081     We also use a real-world dataset to show the practicability of the proposed rank test and illustrate
082     its application in causal discovery.

      2.2   W HY THIS P ROBLEM IS I MPORTANT ?
109
110   In this section we will briefly discuss why rank test is important in the context of causal discovery as
111   well as why it is crucial to deal with discretization.
112
      (i) Rank Test Takses Linear CI Test as a Special Case
113
114   In causal discovery, we aim to find the underlying causal graph among variables given observational
115   data. The most classical approach is to use conditional independence (CI) relationships to identify
116   d-separations in a graph; see, e.g., the PC algorithm (Spirtes et al., 2000). This idea is captured by the
117
      following theorem.
118   Theorem 1 (Conditional Independence and D-separation (Pearl, 1988)). Under the Markov and
119   faithfulness assumption, for disjoint sets of variables A, B and C, C d-separates A and B in graph
120
      G, iff A ⊥⊥ B|C holds for every distribution in the graphical model associated to G.
121   In practice, we often consider linear causal models where the CI test can be done by e.g., Fisher-Z
122   (Fisher et al., 1921). It has been shown that, for linear causal models, d-separations between variables
123   can also be uncovered by rank tests, which is summarized in the following theorem.
124
      Theorem 2 (D-separation by Rank Test (Dong et al., 2024)). Suppose a linear causal model with
125   graph G and assume rank faithfulness (Spirtes, 2013). For disjoint variable sets A, B, and C, we
126   have C d-separates A and B in graph G, if and only if rank(ΣA∪C,B∪C ) = |C|.
127
128   The above Theorem 2 says that d-separations can also be inferred from rank of a cross-covariance
129   matrix, and thus for causal discovery of linear causal models, partial correlation test / linear CI test
130   can be substituted by rank test.
131   (ii) Rank Relates to T-separation that Indicates Latent Variables
132
      Next, we show that rank of cross-covariance informs something beyond d-separations. Specifically,
133
      t-separations (Sullivant et al., 2010) can be inferred from rank, and t-separations can be used to
134
      identify latent variables. The relation between rank and t-separations is given as follows.
135
      Theorem 3 (Rank and T-separation (Sullivant et al., 2010)). Given two sets of variables A and B
136
      from a linear model with graph G and assume rank faithfulness. We have:
137
138               rank(ΣA,B ) = min{|CA | + |CB | : (CA , CB ) t-separates A from B in G},                  (2)
139   where ΣA,B is the cross-covariance over A and B.
140
141   The left-hand side of Equation 2 is about properties of the observational distribution, while the right-
142   hand side describes properties of the graph. An example highlighting the greater informativeness of
143   rank compared to CI is as follows. Consider the graph G in Figure 5, where {X1 , X2 } and {X3 , X4 }
144   are d-separated by L1 , but we can never infer that from any CI test, i.e., we can never check whether
145
      {X1 , X2 } ⊥⊥ {X3 , X4 }|L1 holds, as L1 is not observed. In contrast, using rank information, we can
      infer that rank(Σ{X1 X2 },{X3 X4 } ) = 1, which implies {X1 , X2 } and {X3 , X4 } are t-separated by one
146
      latent variable. The rationale behind is that the t-separation of two set of variables A, B by (CA , CB )
147
      can be inferred through rank information, without actually observing any element in (CA , CB ). A
148
      more detailed discussion can be found in (Dong et al., 2024).
149
150   (iii) Discretization is Ubiquitous and Needs to be Handled
151   Discretization is ubiquitous in many scientific fields. For instance, it is common to come across
152   concepts that cannot be measured directly, such as depression, anxiety, attitude, and the observations
153   of such variables are often the result of coarse-grained measurement of the underlying continuous
154   ones. More examples can be found in fields like psychology (Lord & Novick, 2008), biometrics
155   (Finney, 1952) and econometrics (Nerlove & Press, 1973), where it is widely accepted to assume a
156   continuous variable underlies a dichotomous or polychotomous observed one.
157   In the context of rank test, what should we do to deal with such a ubiquitous discretization problem?

161   example, assume that the original continuous observations are discretized into three levels represented

412

421     control the Type I errors given a significance level α, while the Type II errors should be as small as
422     possible. We consider different sample sizes, and for each comparison, we consider 3000 random
423     trials. For MPRT, we randomly generated 200 permutations to calculate the p-value. The ground

      In this section we analyze the performance of each method in terms of Type I and Type II errors under

465   Type I errors of them are both very close to the significance level α = 0.05; in contrast, CCART-D

470   input while MPRT takes mixed data as input. We show the performance of CCART-C just in order to
471
      show the minimal possible Type II errors that one can achieve in the presence of discretization.

477
      the empirical CDF to calculate the p-value; the two results are expected to be exactly the same
      asymptotically.

774   C.1   B RIEF I NTRODUCTION TO P ERMUTATION T EST
775
776   Permutation tests aim to empirically estimate the CDF of the null distribution of a test statistic. The
777   core of such an CDF estimation is the exchangeability, under which we can make use of permuted
778   data to serve as additional samples from the same distribution.
779   Take Figure 6 as an example. The left figure in Figure 6 refer to N i.i.d. samples from P (X, Y).
780   After random permutation on Y, the permutated data can be considered as random i.i.d. samples from
781   P (X) and P (Y). If the exchangeability holds under the null hypothesis, i.e., random vectors X and
782   Y are independent, then we have P (X, Y) = P (X)P (Y), and thus the permuted data can serve as
783   another N i.i.d. samples from P (X, Y). Now we know how to generate additional N i.i.d. samples.
784   As a test statistic is just a deterministic function of the N i.i.d., samples. For each randomly permuted
785
      data, we can calculate the value of the test statistic, and thus all these calculated test statistics can
      be considered as sampled from the distribution of the test statistic. Given these samples, we can
786
      construct the empirical CDF of the null distribution, and consequently correctly calculate the p-value.

799   C.3   N UMBER OF C ATEGORIES AND A NALYSIS OF T YPE -I ERROR AND P OWER
800
801   The proposed method can handle any level of discretization, as long as it is greater than 1, with Type-I
802   errors properly controlled. At the same time, more levels are always beneficial, because it leads to
803   less information loss during the discretization process, and thus the correlation matrix can be more
804   efficiently estimated for building the test.
805   Regarding Type-I errors, as we establish the exchangeability even in the discretized scenario, the
806   asymptotic null distribution can be estimated by random permutations. Consequently, Type-I errors
807   can be properly controlled at any significance level. At the same time, we do not have theoretical result
808   on the analysis of the power yet. To be specific, even without considering discretization, the analysis
809   of power involves tools from advanced random matrix theories and is highly nontrivial. Furthermore,
      in our setting with discretized variables, the involved maximum likelihood step makes such an

816   Conditional independence and rank test. A line of conditional independence tests imposes
817
      simplifying assumptions on the distributions. For instance, when the variables have linear relations
      with additive Gaussian noise, the Fisher’s classical z-test based on partial correlations can be used
818
      (Fisher, 1924; Baba et al., 2004). Ramsey (2014) developed an approach that separately regresses X
819
      and Y on Z, and further perform independence test on the corresponding residuals. Fukumizu et al.
820   (2007) proposed a conditional independence test method based on Hilbert-Schmidt independence
821   criterion (HSIC) (Gretton et al., 2007). Zhang et al. (2012) further provided a kernel-based conditional
822   test that yields pointwise asymptotic level control. Shah & Peters (2018) investigated the hardness
823   of conditional independence test, and developed a method based on kernel-ridge regression and
824   generalised covariance measure. On the other hand, existing statistical tests for rank of a cross-
825   covariance matrix (Anderson, 1984) often rely on CCA (Jordan, 1875; Hotelling, 1992), with a
826   likelihood ratio based test statistics.
827   Permutation test. Research and applications related to permutation tests have addressed increased
828   attention in recent years (David, 2008; Pesarin & Salmaso, 2010; Welch, 1990). These tests lead
829   to valid inferences while requiring weak assumptions that are commonly satisfied, base on the
830   exchangeability of observations under the null hypothesis. Recently, a permutation-based CI test
831   was proposed (Doran et al., 2014) and more recently a permutation-based rank test (Winkler et al.,
832   2020). However, they cannot deal with the discretization problem. In contrast, our MPRT can take all
833   continuous, partially discretized, or all discretized data as input, and our Type I errors can be properly
834
      controlled.
835   Constraint-based causal discovery. Constraint-based methods leverage statistical tests, such
836   as conditional independence tests, to estimate the causal structure. Spirtes & Glymour (1991)
837   proposed the PC algorithm that estimates the skeleton and orient certain edges to identify the Markov
838   equivalence class. FCI (Spirtes et al., 1995; Colombo et al., 2012) was developed to allow for latent
839   and selection variables, while the CCD algorithm (Richardson, 1996) can accommodate cycles.
840
      Furthermore, Huang et al. (2020) developed a constraint-based method that allows for heterogeneity
      or non-stationarity in the data distribution, while Silva et al. (2006); Huang et al. (2022); Dong
841
      et al. (2024) proposed algorithms based on rank test that recover the causal structure involving latent
842
      confounders.
