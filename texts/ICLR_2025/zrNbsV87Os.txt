009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011             Existing digital sensors capture images at fixed spatial and spectral resolutions (e.g.,
012             RGB, multispectral, and hyperspectral images), and generating super-resolution
013             images with different resolution settings requires bespoke machine learning models.
014             Spatial Implicit Functions (SIFs) partially overcome the spatial resolution challenge
015             by representing an image in a spatial-resolution-independent way. However, they
016             still operate at fixed, pre-defined spectral resolutions. To address this challenge,
017             we propose Spatial-Spectral Implicit Function (SSIF), a neural implicit model
018
                that represents an image as a function of both continuous pixel coordinates in
                the spatial domain and continuous wavelengths in the spectral domain. This
019
                continuous representation across spatial and spectral domains enables a single
020
                model to learn from a diverse set of resolution settings, which leads to better
021             generalizability. This representation also allows the physical principle of spectral
022             imaging and the spectral response functions of sensors to be easily incorporated
023             during training and inference. Moreover, SSIF does not have the equal spectral
024             wavelength interval requirement for both input and output images which leads to
025             much better applicability. We empirically demonstrate the effectiveness of SSIF on
026             two challenging spatial-spectral super-resolution benchmarks. We observe that
027             SSIF consistently outperforms state-of-the-art baselines even when the baselines
028             are allowed to train separate models at each spatial or spectral resolution. We
029
                show that SSIF generalizes well to both unseen spatial and spectral resolutions.
                Moreover, due to its physics-inspired design, SSIF performs significantly better at
030
                low data regime and converges faster during training compared with other strong
031
                neural implicit function-based baselines.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   While the physical world is continuous, most digital sensors (e.g., cell phone cameras, multispectral
037   or hyperspectral sensors in satellites) can only capture a discrete representation of continuous signals
038   in both spatial and spectral domains (i.e., with a fixed number of spectral bands, such as red, green,
039   and blue). Due to the limited energy of incident photons, fundamental limitations in achievable signal-
040   to-noise ratios (SNR), and time constraints, there is always a trade-off between spatial and spectral
041   resolution (Mei et al., 2020; Ma et al., 2021)1 . High spatial resolution and high spectral resolution
042
      can not be achieved at the same time, leading to a variety of spatial and spectral resolutions used
      in practice for different sensors. However, ML models are typically bespoke to certain resolutions,
043
      and models typically do not generalize to spatial or spectral resolutions they have not been trained
044
      on. This calls for image super-resolution (SR) methods, which are capable of increasing the spatial
045   or spectral resolution of a given single low-resolution image (Galliani et al., 2017). It has become
046   increasingly important for a wide range of tasks including object recognition and tracking (Pan et al.,
047   2003; Uzair et al., 2015; Xiong et al., 2020), medical image processing (Lu & Fei, 2014; Johnson
048   et al., 2007), remote sensing (He et al., 2021b; Bioucas-Dias et al., 2013; Melgani & Bruzzone, 2004;
049   Zhong et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2022a; Liu et al., 2023), and astronomy (Ball et al., 2019).
050
      The diversity in input-output image resolutions (both spatial and spectral) significantly increases the
051   complexity of deep neural network (DNN) based SR model development. Most SR research develops

093   images should have equal-spaced spectral wavelength intervals which most RGB and multispectral
094   images do not satisfy. This significantly limits its applicability in most spatial-spectral SR problems.

      Image Super Resolution As an ill-posed single image-to-image translation problem, super-
      resolution (SR) aims at increasing the spatial or spectral resolution of a given image such that
124
      it can be used for different downstream tasks. It has been widely used on natural images(Zhang et al.,
125
      2018a; Hu et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2020b; Saharia et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021), screen-shot
126   images (Yang et al., 2021), omnidirectional images (Deng et al., 2021; Yoon et al., 2021) medical
127   images (Isaac & Kulkarni, 2015), as well as multispectral (He et al., 2021b; Liu et al., 2023) and
128   hyperspectral remote sensing images(Mei et al., 2017; Ma et al., 2021; Mei et al., 2020; Wang et al.,
129   2022b). Traditionally image SR (Ledig et al., 2017; Lim et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2018b; Haris et al.,
130   2018; Zhang et al., 2020c; Yao et al., 2020; Mei et al., 2020; Saharia et al., 2021; Ma et al., 2021; He
131   et al., 2021b; Ma et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2023) has been classified into three tasks according to the
132   input and output image resolutions:3 Spatial Super-Resolution (spatial SR), Spectral Super-Resolution
133   (spectral SR) and Spatio-Spectral Super-Resolution (SSSR). Spatial SR (Zhang et al., 2018a; Hu
134
      et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2020a; Niu et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2021b; Chen et al., 2021; He et al., 2021b)
      focuses on increasing the spatial resolution of the input images (e.g., from h √ó w pixels to H √ó W
135
      pixels) while keeping the spectral resolution (i.e., number of spectral bands/channels) unchanged.
136
      In contrast, spectral SR (Galliani et al., 2017; Fu et al., 2018; Arad et al., 2018; Kaya et al., 2019;
137   Fu et al., 2020; He et al., 2021a; Sun et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2021; Zhang, 2021; Mei et al., 2022;
138   Zhang et al., 2022; He et al., 2023) focuses on increasing the spectral resolution of the input images
139   (e.g., from c to C channels) while keeping the spatial resolution fixed4 . SSSR (Mei et al., 2020; Ma
140   et al., 2021; 2022) focuses on increasing both the spatial and spectral resolution of the input images.
141   Here, h, w (or H, W ) indicates the height and width of the low-resolution, LR, (or high-resolution,
142   HR) images while c and C indicate the number of bands/channels of the low/high spectral resolution
143   images. For video signal, SR can also be done along the time dimension, but we don‚Äôt consider it
144   here and leave it as future work.
145

147
      implicit neural representations for different tasks such as image regression (Tancik et al., 2020)

157   frequencies), which converts the coordinates into multi-scale features. Then a multi-layer perceptron

      The spatial-spectral image super-resolution (SSSR) problem over various spatial and spectral reso-
179   lutions can be conceptualized as follows. Given an input low spatial/spectral resolution (LR-MSI)
180   image Ilr‚àím ‚àà Rh√ów√óc , we want to generate a high spatial and spectral resolution (HR-HSI) image
181   Ihr‚àíh ‚àà RH√óW √óC . Here, h, w, c and H, W, C are the height, width and channel dimension of
182   image Ilr‚àím and Ihr‚àíh , and H > h, W > w, C > c. The spatial upsampling scale p is defined
183   as p = H/h = W/w. Without loss of generality, let Œõhr‚àíh = [ŒõT0 , ŒõT1 , ..., ŒõTC ] ‚àà RC√ó2 be the
184   wavelength interval matrix, which defines the spectral bands in the target HR-HSI image Ihr‚àíh . Here,
185   Œõi = [Œªi,s , Œªi,e ] ‚àà R2 is the wavelength interval for the ith band of Ihr‚àíh where Œªi,s , Œªi,e are the
186   start and end wavelength of this band. Œõhr‚àíh can be used to fully express the spectral resolution of
187   the target HR-HSI image Ihr‚àíh . In this work, we do not use C/c to represent the spectral upsampling
188   scale because bands/channels of image Ilr‚àím and Ihr‚àíh might not be equally spaced (See Figure
189
      1a). So Œõhr‚àíh is a very flexible representation for the spectral resolution, capable of representing
      situations when different bands have different spectral widths or their wavelength intervals overlap
190
      with each other. When Ihr‚àíh has equally spaced wavelength intervals, such as those of most of the
191
      hyperspectral images, we use its band number C to represent the spectral scale.
192
193   The spatial-spectral super-resolution (SSSR) can be represented as a function

249   4.2      L IGHT S OURCE P RINCIPLE
250   On the light source side, SSIF model design leverages the ‚Äúspectral signature‚Äù principle that
251   the spectral intensity curve (radiation as a function of wavelength) of any pixel Œ≥ I (x, Œª) can be
252   decomposed as a weighted sum of k pretrained spectral signature functions. This constraint enforces
253   a useful regularity that different surface types such as water, bare ground, and vegetation reflect
254   radiation differently in various wavelengths and have their unique spectral signatures6 . With the
255   decomposition of the pixel spectral intensity curve as a weighted sum of learnable spectral signature
256   functions, it is possible to learn them from raw data, which often contains mixed surface types.

449   SSIF. We also conduct an ablation study for K on Pavia Centre dataset (see Figure 15 in Appendix
450   A.9.3) and find out that a larger K usually leads to better performance and better generalizability on

452   C > 102 just like what we see in the CAVE experiments while bigger Ks will mitigate this problem.

472   6     C ONCLUSION
473
      In this work, we propose Spatial-Spectral Implicit Function (SSIF), a physics-inspired neural implicit
474   model that represents an image as a continuous function of both pixel coordinates in the spatial
475   domain and wavelengths in the spectral domain. This enables SSIF to handle SSSR tasks with
476   different output spatial and spectral resolutions simultaneously with one model. In contrast, all
477   previous works have to train separate SR models for different spectral resolutions. We demonstrate
478   the effectiveness of SSIF on the SSSR task with two datasets ‚Äì CAVE and Pavia Centre. We show
479   that SSIF can outperform all baselines across different spatial and spectral scales even when the
480   baselines are allowed to be trained separately at each spectral resolution, thus solving an easier task.
481   We demonstrate that SSIF generalizes well to unseen spatial and spectral resolutions. Moreover, we
482
      show that compared with other SIFs, due to its physics-inspired nature, SSIF is much more data
      efficient, parameter efficient, and training efficient.
483
484   In this study, the effectiveness of SSIF is mainly shown on hyperspectral image SR, while SSIF is
485   flexible enough to handle multispectral images with irregular wavelength intervals. This will be
      studied in future work. Moreover, the data limitation of the hyperspectral images poses a significant


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      challenge to SR model training. We also plan to construct a large dataset for hyperspectral image SR.
487   SSIF also has the risk of generating Deepfakes. Therefore, a holistic evaluation of SSIF on various
488   downstream tasks is one of our future works.

946
      We consider 10 baselines in our SSSR task on two benchmark datasets:
947
948          1. RCAN + AWAN uses RCAN (Zhang et al., 2018a) for spatial SR and then AWAN (Li et al.,
949             2020) for spectral SR in a sequential manner.
950
             2. AWAN + RCAN simply reverses the order of RCAN and AWAN.
951
952          3. AWAN + SSPSR uses AWAN and SSPSR (Mei et al., 2020) for spectral SR and spatial SR.
953          4. RC/AW + MoG-DCN first separately uses RCAN (Zhang et al., 2018a) to do spatial SR
954             to obtain HR-MSI images and uses AWAN (Li et al., 2020) to do spectral SR to obtain
955             LR-HSI images, and then uses MoG-DCN (Dong et al., 2021) to do hyperspectral image
956             fusion based on the previously generated HR-MSI and LR-HSI images.
957          5. SSJSR (Mei et al., 2020) uses a fully convolution-based deep neural network to do SSSR.
958
             6. US3RN (Ma et al., 2021) uses a deep unfolding network to solve the SSSR problem with a
959             closed-form solution.
960
             7. SSFIN (Ma et al., 2022) follows the multi-task structure, first decoupling the SSSR into
961
                two tasks: spatial SR and spectral SR. Then it implements SSSR by feature fusion. It is the
962
                current state-of-the-art model for the SSSR task.
963
964          8. LIIF (Chen et al., 2021) is a spatial implicit function which was initially designed for spatial
965
                SR on multispectral data. We increase the output dimension of LIIF‚Äôs final MLP to allow it
                to work on hyperspectral images.
966
967          9. CiaoSR (Cao et al., 2023) modifies the LIIF‚Äôs nearest neighbor interpolation query feature
968             into a self-attention-like architecture. We also change the output dimension of its final MLP
969
                to allow it to work on hyperspectral images.
970         10. LISSF (Zhang et al., 2024) is an implicit neural representation for joint SSSR of multi-
971             spectral images in arbitrary scales. However, the input image encoder of LISSF utilizes 3D
                CNN layers, based on the assumption that the bands of the input images should have equal

993
994    The CAVE dataset (Yasuma et al., 2010b) consists of 32 indoor hyperspectral (HSI) images captured
995    under controlled illumination. Each image has a spatial size of 512 √ó 512 and 31 spectral bands
996    covering the wavelength from 400nm to 700nm. Each HSI image is associated with an RGB image
997    with the same spatial size. There are a lot of studies using the CAVE dataset for hyperspectral image
998
       super-resolution (Yao et al., 2020; Mei et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2020c; Zhang, 2021; Han et al.,
       2021; Qu et al., 2021; Ma et al., 2021; 2022). However, these works focus on different SR tasks. In
999
       this work, we focus on the most challenging task ‚Äì SSSR. The train/test split on the CAVE dataset
1000
       varies from paper to paper. In order to keep a fair comparison to the previous study, we adopt the
1001   train/test split from SSFIN (Ma et al., 2022), the latest work on this dataset, and use the first 22
1002   samples as the training dataset and the rest 10 samples as testing. The limited number of samples
1003   poses a significant challenge on modeling training. So similar to the previous work (Ma et al., 2021;
1004   Chen et al., 2021), given a HR-HSI and HR-MSI image pair (Ihr‚àíh    max , I
                                                                                  hr‚àím
                                                                                       ), we first do random
                                                                          hr‚àím
1005   cropping for a 64p √ó 64p image patch from both images. Then I            is spatially downsampled to
1006   a 64 √ó 64 image patch which serves as the input LR-MSI image Ilr‚àím . We choose pmin = 1 and
1007   pmax = 8 for spatial downsampling, Cmin = 8 and Cmax = 31 for spectral downsampling (See
1008   Appendix A.3).
1009   The Pavia Centre (PC) dataset is taken by ROSIS, a widely used hyperspectral sensor. The images
1010   were collected over the center area of Pavia, northern Italy, in 2001. It contains 102 spectral bands
1011   covering a spectrum from 430nm to 860nm. Figure 1a shows the spectral signature of one pixel
1012   A when C = 102. It has 1095 √ó 715 effective pixels. Similarly, we also adopt the train/test split

1014   the rest for training. The PC dataset does not come with a multispectral image counterpart. So we
1015

       based on the sensor specification of the multispectral sensor of IKONOS. The resulted image has 4

1279   A.10     S TUDIES ON THE A DVANTAGES OF P HYSICS -I NSPIRED NATURE OF SSIF
1280
1281
       Compared with existing SIF models such as LIIF (Chen et al., 2021) and CiaoSR (Cao et al., 2023),
       SSIF has one big difference ‚Äì it incorporates the physical principles of spectral imaging into the
1282
       neural implicit function model design. We hypothesize that the physics-inspired nature of SSIF can
1283
       lead to three advantages:
1284
1285          1. Data efficiency: Compared with other SIF models, SSIF will require less training data to
1286             achieve the same level of model performance. In other words, when trained with different
1287             proportions of training data, SSIF will always outperform other SIF models.
1288
              2. Parameter efficiency: Compared with other SIF models, SSIF requires a much smaller
1289             number of learnable parameters to achieve the same set of tasks.
1290
1291          3. Training efficiency: During model training, SSIF converges faster than other SIF models.
1292
       To validate our hypotheses, we conduct a series of experiments on the CAVE dataset (Yasuma et al.,
1293
       2010a) by comparing our strongest baseline CiaoSR (Cao et al., 2023) with our SSIF-RF-* and
1294
       SSIF-M.
1295
       We summarize our findings in Figure 16, Table 4, and Figure 17. We can see that:


                                                           24
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1296
              1. SSIF-RF-* is indeed more data efficient than CiaoSR and SSIF‚Äôs simple variant, SSIF-M, as
1297             shown in Figure 16. Since SSIF explicitly embeds the physical principles of spectral imaging
1298             into its model design, SSIF is less data-dependent and more robust. When trained with
1299             different proportions of training data, SSIF-RF-* can consistently outperform CiaoSR. In
1300             particular, SSIF shows great performance gains when trained with only 25% of the train set
1301             (at least 3.14 PSNR gain). Moreover, SSIF-RF-* can also consistently outperform SSIF-M
1302             which indicates that simply performing spectral encoding without considering the nature of
1303             sensors‚Äô response functions (as SSIF-M does) will lead to significant model performance
1304             degradation.
1305          2. SSIF is also parameter efficient. It has similar numbers of learnable parameters as CiaoSR
1306             (see Table 4). However, we need to train separate CiaoSR models for different target spectral
1307             resolutions while one SSIF can handle all these tasks simultaneously.
1308          3. SSIF is also training efficient as shown in Figure 17. As discussed above, since SSIF
1309             explicitly embeds the physics principles, it can converge faster as a result. This phenomenon
1310             is particularly evident in early epochs, as shown in Figure 17.
