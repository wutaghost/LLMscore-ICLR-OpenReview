009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Protein-protein interaction (PPI) represents a central challenge within the biology
012            field, and accurately predicting the consequences of mutations in this context is
013            crucial for drug design and protein engineering. Deep learning (DL) has shown
014            promise in forecasting the effects of such mutations but is hindered by two pri-
015            mary constraints. First, the structures of mutant proteins are often elusive to ac-
016            quire. Secondly, PPI takes place dynamically, which is rarely integrated into the
017            DL architecture design. To address these obstacles, we present a novel framework
018
               named Refine-PPI with two key enhancements. First, we introduce a structure re-
               finement module trained by a mask mutation modeling (MMM) task on available
019
               wild-type structures, which is then transferred to hallucinate the inaccessible mu-
020
               tant structures. Second, we employ a new kind of geometric network, called the
021            probability density cloud network (PDC-Net), to capture 3D dynamic variations
022            and encode the atomic uncertainty associated with PPI. Comprehensive experi-
023            ments on SKEMPI.v2 substantiate the superiority of Refine-PPI over all existing
024            tools for predicting free energy change. These findings underscore the effective-
025            ness of our hallucination strategy and the PDC module in addressing the absence
026            of mutant protein structure and modeling geometric uncertainty.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Proteins seldom act in isolation and typically engage in interactions with others to perform a wide
032   array of biological functions (Phizicky & Fields, 1995; Du et al., 2016). One illustrative instance
033
      involves antibodies, which belong to a protein category within the immune system. They identify
      and attach to proteins found on pathogen surfaces and trigger immune responses by interacting with
034
      receptor proteins in immune cells (Lu et al., 2018). Accordingly, it is crucial to devise approaches
035
      to modulate these interactions, and a prevalent strategy is to introduce amino acid mutations at the
036   interface (see Fig. 1). However, the space of possible mutations is vast, making it impractical or
037   prohibitive to conduct experimental tests on all viable modifications in a laboratory setting (Li et al.,
038   2023). Thus, computational techniques are required to guide the recognition of desirable mutations
039   by forecasting their mutational effects on binding strength, commonly measured by the change in
040   binding free energy termed ∆∆G.
041
      The past decade has witnessed the great potential of deep learning (DL) techniques (Rives et al.,
042   2021; Min et al., 2022) in biological science, such as protein design (Jing et al., 2020), folding
043   classification (Hermosilla et al., 2020), model quality assessment (Wu et al., 2023), and function
044   prediction (Gligorijević et al., 2021). These DL algorithms also surpass conventional approaches
045   in computing ∆∆G and can be roughly divided into biophysics- and statistics-based kinds. In
046   particular, the former depends on sampling from energy functions and consequently faces a trade-
047   off between efficiency and accuracy (Schymkowitz et al., 2005; Leman et al., 2020). Meanwhile,
048   statistical-based methods are limited by the selection of descriptors and cannot take advantage of the
049   growing availability of protein structures (Alford et al., 2017).
050   Despite DL’s fruitful progress in identifying ∆∆G, their efficacy encounters various obstacles. First
051   is the absence of the mutant complex structure. Due to the long-standing consensus that protein
052   function is intricately related to its structure (Jumper et al., 2021), an emerging line seeks to encode
053   protein structures using 3D-CNNs or GNNs (Jing et al., 2020; Satorras et al., 2021), but typically
      relies on experimental structures like Protein Data Bank (PDB). Their performance deteriorates

075   with various structures to identify metastable states.

077   Fig. 3) with two key innovations for the mutation effect
078   prediction problem. First, we devise a masked muta-
079   tion modeling (MMM) strategy and propose to predict
080   the mutant structure and ∆∆G simultaneously. Refine-
081   PPI combines the prediction of structure and the predic-
082   tion of free energy change into a joint training objective
083   rather than relying on external software to sample mutant
084   structures. This offers several distinct advantages. On the
085
      one hand, the hallucinated mutant structure exhibits sig-
      nificant differences from the wild-type structure, provid-
086
      ing crucial geometric information related to the change in
087
      binding free energy. On the other hand, MMM not only Figure 2: Performance of Refine-PPI on

091   et al. (2021). Besides, ∆∆G implicitly conveys extra information about the structural difference
092   before and after the mutation. Collective training with ∆∆G would promote the efficiency of struc-
093   ture prediction. Second, we introduce a new kind of geometric GNN called PDC-Net to capture the
094   flexibility and dynamics of conformations during the binding process. Specifically, each particle in
095   a complex is represented as a probability density cloud (PDC) that illustrates the scale and strength
096
      of their motion throughout the interaction procedure. Then, an aligned network is used to propa-
      gate the distributions of the equilibrium of molecular systems. A comprehensive evaluation in the
097
      SKEMPI.v2 dataset (Jankauskaitė et al., 2019) proves that our Refine-PPI outperforms all present
098
      methodologies by a significant margin (see Fig. 1) and it is promising to generate absent mutant
099   structures via a multi-task training scheme.

151   Overview. Refine-PPI (see Fig. 3) has three constituents parameterized by ρ, θ, τ , respectively.
152
      The backbone module hρ (.) encodes the input 3D complex structure, the structure refinement mod-
      ule fθ (.) hallucinates the unseen mutant structure, and the predictor gτ (.) estimates the final ∆∆G.
153
      The whole pipeline is described below. To begin with, the wild-type structure G WT and a well-
154
      initialized mutant structure G̃ MT (the initialization details will be elucidated later) are fed into hρ (.)
155
156   to gain their corresponding features ZWT ∈ Rn×ψ1 and Z˜MT ∈ Rn×ψ1 , respectively. Then, the
157   imperfect mutant structure G̃ MT along with its first-round representation Z˜MT is forwarded into fθ (.)
158   for several cycles and acquires the ultimate structure Ĝ MT with more robust coordinates x̂MT . Sub-
159   sequently, the hallucinated mutant structure Ĝ MT is encoded by hρ (.) again, and we can retrieve its
160   second-round updated representation ZMT ∈ Rn×ψ1 . As last, a pooling layer and gτ (.) are appended
161   to aggregate graph-level representations of both wild-type and mutation-type noted as HWT ∈ Rψ2
      and HMT ∈ Rψ2 based on ZWT and ZMT , and output the predicted free energy change ŷ.

210   Discussion. Previous studies exemplified by Google’s DeepDream (Mordvintsev et al., 2015) train
211   networks to recognize faces and other patterns in images, and invert and adjust arbitrary input images
212   to draw more strongly resemble patterns perceived by the network. The generated images are often
213   referred to as hallucinations because they may not faithfully represent any actual face, but what DL
214   models view as an ideal face. This mechanism has also demonstrated success in macromolecules,
215   where information stored in parameters of trained networks can be harnessed to design new protein
      structures featuring new sequences (Anishchenko et al., 2021). Refine-PPI uses a similar method to

218   hallucination capacity to resolve the inevitable dilemma of the missing mutant structures.

238   Probability Density Cloud. Atoms are never at rest, even at extremely low temperatures (Clerk-
239   Maxwell, 1873), and exhibit translational, rotational, or vibrational motion. In quantum mecha-
240   nisms, electrons do not follow well-defined paths like planets around the Sun in classical physics
241   but exist at specific energy levels and are described by wave functions, the mathematical functions
242   on the probability of finding an electron in various locations around the nucleus (Schumaker, 1986).
243
      Physicists commonly envision an electron or other quantum particle by depicting their probability
      distribution around a specific region of space within an atom or molecule, where the shape and size
244
      of orbitals depend on the quantum numbers.
245
246   Inspired by this phenomenon, we portray particles as PDC showing regions with a higher probability
247   of finding them. xi are assumed to follow Gaussian as N (µi , Σi ). µi ∈ R3 is the place where node
248   i is most likely to be located, and Σi ∈ R3×3 is an isotropic (or spherical) covariance matrix
249
      signifying the independence upon the coordinate system. Given this premise, we can derive many
      invariant geometries that emphasize molecular structural information. The primary variable is the
250
      distance dij = ||xi − xj ||2 . As xi and xj are are statistically independent, their difference follows
251
      a normal distribution as xi − xj ∼ N (µi − µj , Σi + Σj ) (Lemons, 2003), and its squared norm
252
      denoted as d2ij exhibits a generalized chi-squared distribution χ2 (.) with a set of natural parameters,
253
      comprising (µi − µj , Σi + Σj ). The mean and variance of χ2 (.), denoted as µdij and σdij , are:

293
294   Data Evaluation is carried out in SKEMPI.v2 (Jankauskaitė et al., 2019). It contains data on
295   changes in the thermodynamic parameters and kinetic rate constants after mutation for structurally
296   resolved PPIs. The latest version contains manually curated binding data for 7,085 mutations. The
297
      dataset is split into 3 folds by structure, each containing unique protein complexes that do not appear
      in other folds. Two folds are used for train and validation, and the remaining fold is used for test.
298
      This yields 3 different sets of parameters and ensures that every data point in SKEMPI.v2 is tested
299
      once. The pretraining data is derived from PDB-REDO, a database that contains refined X-ray
300   structures in PDB. The protein chains are clustered based on 50% sequence identity, leading to
301   38,413 chain clusters, which are randomly divided into the training, validation, and test sets by
302   95%/0.5%/4.5% respectively.
303
304   Baselines and Metrics. We evaluate PDC-Net against various categories of techniques. The initial
305   kind encompasses conventional empirical energy functions such as Rossetta Cartesian ∆∆G Park
306   et al. (2016); Alford et al. (2017) and FoldX. The second grouping comprises sequence/evolution-
307   based methodologies, exemplified by ESM-1v Meier et al. (2021), PSSM, MSA Transformer Rao
308   et al. (2021), and Tranception Notin et al. (2022). The third category includes end-to-end learn-
309
      ing models such as DDGPred Shan et al. (2022) and another End-to-End model that adopts Graph
      Transformer (GT) Luo et al. (2023) as the encoder architecture, but employs an MLP to directly fore-
310
      cast ∆∆G. The fourth grouping encompasses unsupervised/semi-supervised learning approaches,
311
      consisting of ESM-IF Hsu et al. (2022) and MIF Yang et al. (2022). They pretrain networks on
312   structural data and then employ the pretrained representations to predict ∆∆G. MIF also utilizes
313   GT as an encoder for comparative purposes with two variations: MIF-∆logit uses the disparity in
314   log-probabilities of amino acid types to attain ∆∆G, and MIF-Network predicts ∆∆G based on
315   acquired representations. Besides, B-factors is the network that anticipates the B-factor of residues
316   and incorporates the projected B-factor in lieu of entropy for ∆∆G prediction. Lastly, Rotamer
317   Density Estimator (RDE) Luo et al. (2023) uses a flow-based generative model to estimate the prob-
318   ability distribution of rotamers and uses entropy to measure flexibility with two variants containing
319   RDE-Linear and RDE-Network. PPIFormer (Bushuiev et al., 2023) is pretrained on a newly col-
320
      lected non-redundant 3D PPI interface dataset PPIRef through the mask language modeling (MLM)
      technique. More details are in the Appendix A.

      4.3     C ORRELATION BETWEEN VARIANCE AND ATOMIC U NCERTAINTY
383
384   PDC posits that atoms adhere to Gaussian and derives geometric attributes such as distance and
385   angles as distributions, where Σ determines the magnitude of 3D atomic uncertainty. Here, we jus-
386   tify the correspondence between Σ and positional uncertainty. Notably, experimentally observing
387   and documenting particle uncertainty within macromolecules, such as proteins, is challenging. All
388   data in PDB or SKEMPI.v2 are deterministic and uncertainty-free conformations. As a solution, we
389   resort to molecular dynamics (MD) simulations to simulate atomic motions. Notably, MD approxi-
390   mates atomic motions by Newtonian physics and can capture the sequential behavior of molecules
391   in full atomic details at a very fine temporal resolution. We run short-time MD for all complexes in
392
      SKEMPI.v2 and calculate the Root Mean Square Fluctuation (RMSF) alongside the entire trajectory,
      which numerically indicates positional differences between entire structures over time. It calculates
393
      individual residue flexibility, or how much a particular residue fluctuates during a simulation.
394

      5   C ONCLUSION
494
495
      This work proposes a new framework named Refine-PPI to predict the mutation effect. Given that
496   mutant structures are always absent, we introduce an additional structure refinement module to re-
497   cover the masked regions around the mutations. This module is trained simultaneously via mask
498   geometric modeling. In addition, we notice that protein-protein interactions are a dynamic process,
499   but few prior studies have taken this characteristic into account in a deep learning design. To bridge
500   the gap, we present a probability density cloud (PDC)-Network to capture the dynamics in atomic
501   resolution. Our results highlight the necessity to adopt a more robust mutant structure and consider
502   dynamics for molecular modeling. A statement regarding limitations and future work is elaborated
503   in Appendix E.

      A     E XPERIMENTAL D ETAILS
703
704
      We implement all experiments on 4 A100 GPUs, each with 80G memory. Refine-PPI is trained
705
      with an Adam optimizer without weight decay and with β1 = 0.9 and β2 = 0.999. A ReduceL-
706
      ROnPlateau scheduler is employed to automatically adjust the learning rate with a patience of 10
707   epochs and a minimum learning rate of 1.e − 6. The batch size is set to 64 and an initial learning
708   rate of 1.e − 4. The maximum iterations are 50K and the validation frequency is 1K iterations. The
709   node dimension is 128, and no dropout is conducted. As for the structure refinement, the recycle
710   number is set as 3, and the balance weight is tuned as 1.0. We performed a grid search to find the
711   optimal length of the masked region and found that l = r = 5 is a good choice. However, different
712   initializations require different optimal hyperparameters, and typically we can mask longer regions
713   for denoising-based MMM. The pretraining follows a similar training scheme with a batch size of
714   32. During pretraining, the data loader randomly selects a cluster and then randomly chooses a chain
715
      from the cluster to ensure balanced sampling. Since there is no mutant residue in PDB-REDO, we
      randomly select a seed residue from the chosen chain and adopt the same MMM strategy.
716
717   As for the specific model architecture, the backbone module hρ (.) can take the form of any conven-
718   tional geometric neural network (e.g., GVP-GNN, EGNN, SE(3)-Transformer, Graph Transformer).
719   Here, we adopt a one-layer Graph Transformer (Luo et al., 2023) to extract general representations
720   of proteins. The refinement module fθ (.) needs to output both updated features and coordinates,
721
      and therefore we use PDC-EGNN as fθ (.) in our experiments. Lastly, the head predictor gτ (.) is a
      simple linear layer that accepts the concatenation of representations of both wide and mutation types
722
      and forecasts the change in free energy. The total model size of our Refine-PPI is approximately 6M.

725   A.1    BASELINES I MPLEMENTATIONS
726
727   Baselines that require training and calibration using the SKEMPI.v2 dataset (DDGPred, End-to-
728
      End, B-factor, MIF-∆logit, MIF-Network, RDE-Linear, and RDE-Net) are trained independently
      using the 3 different splits of the dataset as described in Section 4.1. This is to ensure that every data
729
      point in the SKEMPI.v2 dataset is tested once. Below are descriptions of the implementation of the
730
      baseline methods, which follow the same scheme as Luo et al. (2023) and Bushuiev et al. (2023).
731
732   Rosetta (Alford et al., 2017; Leman et al., 2020): The Rosetta version is 2021.16, and the scor-
733   ing function is ref2015 cart. Every protein structure in the SKEMPI.v2 dataset is first pre-
734
      processed using the relax application. The mutant structure is built by cartesian ddg. The
      binding free energies of both wild-type and mutant structures are predicted by interface energy
735
      (dG separated/dSASAx100). Finally, the binding ∆∆G is calculated by subtracting the binding
736
      energy of the wild-type structure from the binding energy of the mutant.
737
738   FoldX (Delgado et al., 2019): Structures are first relaxed by the RepairPDB command. Mutant
739   structures are built with the BuildModel command based on the repaired structure. The change in
740
      binding free energy ∆∆G is calculated by subtracting the wild-type energy from the mutant energy.
741   ESM-1v (Meier et al., 2021): We use the implementation provided in the ESM open-source reposi-
742   tory. Protein language models can only predict the effect of mutations for single protein sequences.
743   Therefore, the cases where mutations occur in multiple sequences are ignored. The sequence of the
744   mutated protein chain is extracted from the SEQRES entry in the PDB file. A masked marginal
745   mode is used to score both wild-type and mutant sequences and use their difference as an estimate
746
      of ∆∆G.
747   PSSM MSAs are constructed from the Uniref90 database for chains with mutation annotations in the
748   SKEMPI.v2 dataset. Jackhmmer version 3.3.1 is used following the setting in Meier et al. (2021).
749   The MSAs are filtered using HHfilter with coverage 75 and sequence identity 90. This HHfilter
750   parameter is reported to have the best performance for MSA Transformer according to Meier et al.
751   (2021). Position-specific scoring matrices (PSSM) is calculated and the change in probability is
752
      used as a prediction of ∆∆G.
753   MSA Transformer (Rao et al., 2021): We use the implementation provided in the ESM open-
754   source repository. We input the MSAs constructed during the evaluation of the PSSM to the MSA
755   Transformer. We used the mask-marginal mode to score both wild-type and mutant sequences and
      use their difference as the prediction of ∆∆G.


                                                         14
756
      Tranception (Notin et al., 2022): We use the implementation provided in the Tranception open-
757   source repository. We predict mutation effects using the large model checkpoint. Previously built
758   MSAs (not filtered by HHfilter) are used for inference-time retrieval.
759
760
      DDGPred (Shan et al., 2022): We use the implementation that follows the paper by Shan et al.
      (2022). Since this model requires predicted sidechain structures of the mutant, we use mutant struc-
761
      tures packed during our evaluation of Rosetta to train the model and run prediction.
762
763   End-to-End: The end-to-end model shares the same encoder architecture as RDE (Luo et al., 2023).
764   The difference is that in the RDE normalizing flows follow the encoder to model rotamer distribu-
765   tions, but in the end-to-end model, the embeddings are directly fed to an MLP to predict ∆∆G.
766   B-factor: This model predicts per-atom b-factors for proteins. It has the same encoder architecture
767   as RDE (Luo et al., 2023). The encoder is followed by an MLP that predicts a vector for each amino
768   acid, where each dimension is the predicted b-factor of different atoms in the amino acid. The amino
769   acid-level b-factor is calculated by averaging the atom-level b-factors. The predicted b-factors are
770   used as a measurement of conformational flexibility. They are used to predict ∆∆G using the linear
771   model same as RDE-Linear (Luo et al., 2023).
772   ESM-IF (Hsu et al., 2022): ESM-IF can score protein sequences using the log-likelihood. Im-
773   plementation of the scoring function is provided in the ESM repository. We enable the –
774   multichain backbone flag to let the model see the whole protein-protein complex. We subtract the
775   log-likelihood of the wild-type from the mutant to predict ∆∆G.
776   MIF Architecture: The masked inverse folding (MIF) network uses the same encoder architecture
777   as RDE (Luo et al., 2023). Following the encoder is a per-amino-acid 20-category classifier that
778   predicts the type of masked amino acids. We use the same PDB-REDO train-test split to train the
779   model. At training time, we randomly crop a patch consisting of 128 residues and randomly mask
780   10% amino acids. The model learns to recover the type of masked amino acids with the standard
781   cross entropy loss.
782   MIF-∆logit: To score mutations, we first mask the type of mutated amino acids. Then, we use the
783   log probability of the amino acid type as the score. Analogously, we have the score of the wild-
784   type bound ligand, wild-type bound receptor, wild-type unbound ligand, unbound receptor, mutated
785   bound ligand, mutated bound receptor, and mutated unbound ligand. Therefore, we use the identical
786   linear model to RDE-Linear (Luo et al., 2023) to predict ∆∆G from the scores.
787
      MIF-Network: This is similar to RDE-Network (Luo et al., 2023). The difference is that we use
788   the pre-trained encoder of MIF rather than the encoder of RDE. We also freeze the MIF encoder as
789   we aim to utilize the unsupervised representations.
790
791
      PPIFormer: We use EquiFormer as the backbone and pretrain it on PPIRef. Then the effects of
      mutations are predicted via the log odds ratio.

1059   E    L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
1060
1061   Despite the success of Refine-PPI in estimating the mutation effect, there is still room left for im-
1062   provement. First, Refine-PPI keeps most of the complex stable and merely restores a region around
1063   the mutant site. It is possible that the entire complex can be significantly different upon mutation.
1064   Therefore, a promising future direction would be to enlarge the mask region. Furthermore, previous
1065   studies demonstrate the benefit of structural pretraining to dramatically expand the representation
1066   space of DL models. We expect to implement MMM with more experimental structures other than
1067   PDB (e.g., Alphafold-Database) and transfer the knowledge to predict free energy change.
