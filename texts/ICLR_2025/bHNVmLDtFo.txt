009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In the realm of recommender systems (RS), Top-K metrics such as NDCG@K
012            are the gold standard for evaluating performance. Nonetheless, during the training
013            of recommendation models, optimizing NDCG@K poses significant challenges
014            due to its inherent discontinuous nature and the intricacies of the Top-K truncation
015            mechanism. Recent efforts to optimize NDCG@K have either neglected the Top-
016            K truncation or suffered from low computational efficiency. To overcome these
017            limitations, we propose SoftmaxLoss@K (SL@K), a new loss function designed
018            as a surrogate for optimizing NDCG@K in RS. SL@K integrates a quantile-
019
               based technique to handle the complex truncation term; and derives a smooth
               approximation of NDCG@K to address discontinuity. Our theoretical analysis
020
               confirms the close bounded relationship between NDCG@K and SL@K. Besides,
021
               SL@K also exhibits several desirable properties including concise formulation,
022            computational efficiency, and noisy robustness. Extensive experiments on four
023            real-world datasets and three recommendation backbones demonstrate that SL@K
024            outperforms existing loss functions with a notable average improvement of 6.19%.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Recommender systems (RS) (Ko et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2019) have been widely applied in various
030   personalized services (Nie et al., 2019; Ren et al., 2017). The primary goal of RS is to model users’
031   preferences (scores) on items and subsequently retrieve a few items that users are most likely to
032   interact with (Liu et al., 2009; Li et al., 2020; Hurley & Zhang, 2011). In practice, RS typically
033   display only the Top-K items to users. Therefore, Top-K ranking metrics, e.g., NDCG@K (He
034   et al., 2017b), are commonly used to evaluate recommendation performance. These metrics focuses
035
      on the quality of the items ranked within the Top-K positions, as opposed to full-ranking metrics
      (e.g., NDCG) (Järvelin & Kekäläinen, 2017), which assess the entire ranking list.
036
037   Despite the widespread adoption of the NDCG@K metric, optimizing this metric remains highly
038   challenging: 1) The loss function is discontinuous and flat across most regions, rendering gradient-
039   based optimization ineffective; 2) The loss computation involves truncating the ranking list, requiring
040   the identification of whether an item appears in the Top-K positions, which is difficult to manage.
041   Recent efforts have proposed surrogate losses (Lapin et al., 2016; 2017) to optimize NDCG@K, yet
042   these approaches exhibit significant limitations:
043
      • Some studies have focused on optimizing full-ranking metrics such as NDCG, without accounting
044
        for Top-K truncation (Rashed et al., 2021; Chapelle & Wu, 2010; Taylor et al., 2008). A notable
045     and successful example is the Softmax Loss (SL) (Wu et al., 2024a), which is easily implemented
046     and serves as an upper bound for optimizing NDCG (Bruch et al., 2019). SL has been widely
047     applied in practice and usually yield state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance (Wu et al., 2024b).
048     However, NDCG and NDCG@K are not always aligned — NDCG@K focuses on the quality
049     of a few top-ranked items, while NDCG evaluates the entire list. This discrepancy makes that
050     optimizing NDCG does not always yield improvements in NDCG@K and sometimes may even
051     lead to performance degradation, as illustrated in Figure 1a.
052   • Other approaches have sought to optimize NDCG@K by incorporating lambda weights (Burges
053     et al., 2006; Wang et al., 2018) for each training instance in their LambdaLoss@K (Jagerman
        et al., 2022). While this method has proven effective in document retrieval tasks (Liu et al., 2009),

072       user at every iteration. This is computationally prohibitive in real-world RS given the immense

076   Given the critical importance of optimizing NDCG@K and the limitations of existing approaches,
077   there is a pressing need to develop a more effective surrogate loss for NDCG@K. In this work, we
078   propose SoftmaxLoss@K (SL@K), incorporating the following strategies:
079
      • To address the challenge of Top-K truncation, we introduce a quantile-based technique (Koenker,
080
        2005; Hao & Naiman, 2007; Shao, 2008). Specifically, we define a Top-K quantile as a threshold
081     score that separates the Top-K items from the rest. This quantile can be efficiently estimated, and
082     the complex top-K truncation term can be reformulated as a simple comparison between an item’s
083     score and the quantile. This transformation makes the truncation both computationally efficient
084     and tractable for optimization.
085   • To overcome the issue of discontinuity, we analyzes an upper bound for optimizing NDCG@K and
086     relax it into a fully continuous function. Our theoretical analysis proved that SL@K serves as a
087     tight upper bound for − log NDCG@K, ensuring both theoretical rigor and practical applicability.
088
      Beyond its theoretical merits, SL@K is concise in form and easy to implement. Compared to the
089   conventional SL, SL@K introduces only a quantile-based weight for each positive instance, which
090   adds minimal computational overhead (cf. Figure 1b). Furthermore, our analysis reveals that SL@K
091   demonstrates enhanced robustness to false positive noise (Chen et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2021; Wen
092   et al., 2019) — a common issue in RS, where some positive interactions may result from factors other
093   than true user preference (e.g., misclicks).
094
      To empirically validate the effectiveness of SL@K, we conduct extensive experiments across four real-
095   world recommendation datasets using three typical recommendation backbones. The experimental
096   results demonstrate that SL@K achieves impressive performance improvements, with an average
097   gain of 6.19% in NDCG@K. Additional experiments, including an exploration of the hyperparameter
098   K and robustness evaluations, confirm that SL@K is not only well-aligned with NDCG@K but also
099   exhibits superior resistance to false positive noise.

106   recommender systems (RS) (Su, 2009; Zhu et al., 2019). Given a RS with a user set U and an item
107   set I, let D = {yui : u ∈ U, i ∈ I} denote the historical interactions between users and items, where
      yui = 1 indicates that user u has interacted with item i, and yui = 0 indicates has not. For each user

      u, we denote Pu = {i ∈ I : yui = 1} as the set of positive items for u, and Nu = I \ Pu as the set
109   of negative items. The recommendation task can be formulated as follows: learning user preference
110   from D and recommending the Top-K items that users are most likely to interact with.
111
112
      Formally, modern RS typically infer user preferences for items with a learnable recommendation
      model sui = fΘ (u, i), where fΘ (u, i) : U × I → R can be any flexible model architecture with
113
      parameters Θ, mapping user/item features (e.g., IDs) into their preference scores sui . Subsequently,
114

115   focus not on model architecture design but instead on exploring the loss function. Given that the loss
116   function guides the optimization direction of models, its importance cannot be overemphasized.

      3.1      S OFTMAX L OSS @K: A S UPERIOR S URROGATE L OSS FOR NDCG@K
217
218   The primary challenges in optimizing NDCG@K stem from the Top-K truncation and the discon-
219   tinuity. To address these challenges, we propose a novel surrogate loss, named SoftmaxLoss@K
220   (SL@K), leveraging the following strategies:
221
      Leveraging quantile technique. The original truncation term I(πui ≤ K) involves estimating the
222   ranking position πui and determining whether it is less than K, which is computationally difficult to
223   handle efficiently. To overcome this, we introduce the Top-K quantile βuK of the preference scores
224   for each user u, which is defined as:
225
226                                          βuK := inf{sui : πui ≤ K}                                    (3.1)
227   This quantile acts as a threshold score that separates the Top-K items from the remainder. Specifically,
228   if an item’s score sui ≥ βuK , it indicates that the item belongs to the Top-K positions; conversely,
229   sui ≤ βuK implies that it does not. Using this quantile, the truncation term can be simplified as:
230
231                                          I(πui ≤ K) = I(sui ≥ βuK )                                   (3.2)
232
      This transformation reduces the problem to a simple comparison between the item’s score sui and the
233
      quantile βuK , thus avoiding the need to directly estimate the ranking position πui . This makes the
234   Top-K truncation both computationally efficient and easily optimizable.
235
236   Some may express concerns regarding the computational cost of estimating the Top-K quantile. In
237
      fact, this quantile can be estimated efficiently and accurately using a sampling-based method with
      theoretical guarantees. We will discuss this in detail in Section 3.3.
238
239   Deriving a continuous surrogate. To tackle the discontinuity issue, we turn to relax NDCG@K
240   into a fully smooth function. Specifically, we aim to derive a smooth upper bound of − log DCG@K,
241   since optimizing this upper bound is equivalent to lifting NDCG@K 1 . To ensure well-definedness
242   and rigor, we simply assume that DCG@K is non-zero. In fact, this assumption is practical note
243
      that DCG@K = 0 is the worst result. During training, the scores of positive instances would be fast
      lifted and typically larger than those of negative instances. As a result, there is almost always at least
244
      one positive item in the Top-K positions, ensuring that DCG@K > 0.
245

302
      Our proposed SoftmaxLoss@K (SL@K) offers several advantages:
303
304   Concise and efficient. The proposed SL@K has a concise form (3.5). Compared to conventional
305   SL, SL@K only introduces an additional quantile-based weight wui for each instance, which just
306   involves a simple difference between the scores sui and the quantiles βuK . SL@K inherits the benefits
307
      of SL, while the introduction of wui can be intuitively understood: it assigns larger weights to positive
      instances with higher scores sui , emphasizing those within the Top-K positions during optimization.
308
      This aligns with the principles of Top-K ranking metric for recommendation.
309
310   The introduction of wui does not incur significantly computational overhead. The quantile estimation
311   and weight calculation in SL@K are efficient and do not require the time-consuming estimation
312   of ranking positions, as in LambdaLoss@K. Moreover, similar to SL, SL@K supports negative
313
      sampling, leading to further acceleration during training.
314   The time complexity of SL@K changes from O(|U|P̄ N ) of SL to O(|U|P̄ N + |U|N log N ), where
315   P̄ denotes the average number of positive items per user; and N denotes the size of sampled negative
316   items satisfying N ≪ |I|. The additional complexity O(|U|N log N ) arises from the quantile
317   estimation (cf. Section 3.3), which remains efficient, as log N is typically smaller than P̄ . Our
318   experiments also confirm the computational efficiency of SL@K (cf. Table 3 in Section 4.2).

351   performance (Wen et al., 2019). Interestingly, the introduction of the weight wui in SL@K helps
352   mitigate this issue. False positives, which often resemble negative instances, tend to have lower
353   prediction scores sui than true positives. As a result, they receive smaller weights wui and contribute
354   less in model training, which enhances the robustness of model, as analyzed in Appendix C.3.

358   Quantile estimation has been extensively studied in the field of statistics (Koenker, 2005; Hao &
359
      Naiman, 2007; Bickel & Doksum, 2015). In this work, we develop a simple Monte Carlo sampling-

360
      sample a small set of N items and estimate the Top-K quantile from this sampled set. The complexity
361
      of this method is O(|U|N log N ), as it only requires sorting the items in the sample set. Despite its
362   simplicity, this method comes with theoretical guarantees:

527   5        C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
528
529   This work introduces a novel loss function, SoftmaxLoss@K (SL@K), designed for optimizing
530   NDCG@K. SL@K leverages a quantile-based technique to handle the truncation challenge and
531   derives a smooth approximation to tackle the discontinuity problem. Our theoretical analysis confirms
532   the close bounded relationship between NDCG@K and SL@K. Beyond its theoretical strengths,
533   SL@K offers a concise formulation, introducing only quantile-based weights on top of the conven-
534   tional Softmax Loss, making it both easy to implement and computationally efficient.
535   Looking ahead, a promising direction for future work would be the development of incremental
536   quantile estimation methods, which could further enhance the efficiency of SL@K and support
537   the incremental learning of recommendation models. Additionally, investigating the application of
538   SL@K in other domains would be valuable, as Top-K metrics are widely utilized in tasks such as
539   multimedia retrieval, question answering, link prediction, and anomaly detection.

      A    R ELATED W ORK
757
758
759   Recommendation models. As a fundamental component of recommender systems, recommendation
760   models aim to predict the user-item interactions. One of the most popular paradigms is collaborative
761   filtering (CF) (Su, 2009; Zhu et al., 2019). CF-based models assume that users with similar preferences
762   will have similar interactions with items. Therefore, a common practice to implement CF models is
763   to parameterize the user and item embeddings and predict the interactions by the vector similarity
764   between user and item embeddings.
765   The earliest works stem from the idea of Matrix Factorization (MF) (Koren et al., 2009), which
766   factorizes the user-item interaction matrix into user and item embedding vectors, such as MF (Koren
767   et al., 2009), SVD (Deerwester et al., 1990; Bell et al., 2007), SVD++ (Koren, 2008), NCF (He et al.,
768   2017a), etc. However, MF-based models have limitations in capturing high-order relations, since
769   they only consider the first-order interactions. To address this issue, some works have proposed to
770   incorporate the graph structure of user-item interactions, using Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) (Wu
771
      et al., 2022; Kipf & Welling, 2016; Wang et al., 2019). GNN-based models, such as LightGCN
      (He et al., 2020), NGCF (Wang et al., 2019), and APDA (Zhou et al., 2023), have achieved great
772
      success in recommendation. Moreover, the most recent works, including SGL (Wu et al., 2021) and
773
      XSimGCL (Yu et al., 2023), introduce contrastive learning (Liu et al., 2021; Oord et al., 2018) for
774   graph data augmentation, achieving state-of-the-art performance in recommendation.
775
776   Recommendation losses. Recommendation loss, which significantly impacts the effectiveness
777
      of recommendation models, is gaining increasing attention from researchers in the field. The
      earliest works treat recommendation as a simple regression or binary classification problem, utilizing
778
      pointwise losses such as MSE (He & Chua, 2017) and BCE (He et al., 2017a). However, due to
779
      neglecting the ranking essence in recommendation, these pointwise losses usually result in inferior
780   recommendation performance.
781
782   To address the limitations of pointwise losses, pairwise losses such as BPR (Rendle et al., 2012)
783
      have been proposed. BPR aims to learn a partial order between positive and negative items, which
      is a surrogate loss for AUC metric and achieves significant improvements over pointwise losses.
784
      Following BPR, listwise losses (Cao et al., 2007) such as Softmax Loss (SL) (Wu et al., 2024a)
785
      extends the pairwise ranking to listwise, i.e., maximizing the likelihood of the entire list of items
786   consisting of one positive item and multiple negative items. SL has been proven as a NDCG surrogate
787   loss and achieves state-of-the-art performance in recommendation (Wu et al., 2024a; Bruch et al.,
788   2019).
789
      Given the success of ranking losses, recent works have attempted to further improve ranking per-
790
      formance from different perspectives. For instance, some works have proposed to further improve
791
      the robustness of SL by introducing Distributional Robust Optimization (DRO) (Shapiro, 2017),
792   e.g., AdvInfoNCE (Zhang et al., 2024) and BSL (Wu et al., 2024b). Other works try to directly
793   optimize the ranking metrics including NDCG (Järvelin & Kekäläinen, 2017) and MRR (Lu et al.,
794   2023). Among them, LambdaRank (Burges et al., 2006) and LambdaLoss (Wang et al., 2018) are
795   the most representative works, which serve as the NDCG surrogate losses with a different form
796   compared to SL. There are also some works focusing on optimizing NDCG from other approaches,
797   e.g., GuidedRec (Rashed et al., 2021) uses neural networks, Smooth-NDCG (Chapelle & Wu, 2010)
798   designs a smooth ranking position indicator, SoftNDCG (Taylor et al., 2008) considers the rank
799   distribution, NeuralSort (Grover et al., 2019) leverages Gumbel-Softmax trick for optimization, etc.
800   Despite the success of the aforementioned ranking losses, they still have limitations in practice, as
801   real-world recommender systems only retrieve a small subset of items for users, i.e., Top-K recom-
802   mendation (Li et al., 2020; Hurley & Zhang, 2011). The Top-K ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG@K),
803   which consider solely the top-ranked items, could be inconsistent with the full ranking metrics
804   (e.g., NDCG). Therefore, the NDCG surrogate losses like SL and LambdaLoss may obtain subopti-
805   mal performance in practical recommendation scenarios. To address this issue, directly optimizing
806
      the Top-K ranking metrics has become increasingly important.
807   Several existing works focus on Top-K metrics optimization. For example, LLPAUC (Shi et al., 2024)
808   optimizes the lower-left part of AUC, which is a surrogate loss for Recall@K and Precision@K.
809   Prec@K (Lu et al., 2019) directly optimize the Precision@K in deep image embedding task.
      LambdaLoss@K (Jagerman et al., 2022), which is a reweighted LambdaLoss, achieves a NDCG@K


                                                        15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      surrogate loss in document retrieval tasks. However, LLPAUC and Prec@K are not designed for
811   optimizing NDCG@K. Besides, LLPAUC involves complex adversarial training, hinders its effec-
812   tiveness and applicable. Moreover, Prec@K and LambdaLoss@K are not specifically designed
813   for recommendation, would suffer from serious inefficiency issue when transferred to recommen-
814   dation scenarios. The skewed lambda weight in LambdaLoss@K also hinders its effective training.
815   Therefore, it is still an open problem to design an efficient and effective surrogate loss for optimizing
816   NDCG@K in recommendation.

1062
1063
       In Section 3.3, we introduce a sampling trick to estimate the Top-K quantile βuK in RS. Specifically,
1064   our sampled items will include all positive items Pu and N (≪ I) sampled negative items N̂u =
            i.i.d.
1065   {jk ∼ Nu }N     k=1 . Since the Top-K quantile is usually located within the score range of positive
1066   items, this trick can estimate the quantile more effectively than directly i.i.d. sampling from all items,
1067   as shown in Figure D.2.
1068   However, applying this sampling trick leads to a theoretical gap. Since the sampled items Îu =
1069   Pu ∪ N̂u are not i.i.d. sampled from the whole item set I, we should not directly sample the (K/I)-th
1070
       quantile of Îu as the estimated quantile β̂uK , which may introduce serious bias. Instead, under a
1071   reasonable assumption that all Top-min(K, Pu ) items are positive items, we should set the estimated
1072
       quantile β̂uK as:
1073
1074   • If K ≤ Pu , β̂uK should be set as the Top-K score of {sui }, where i ∈ Pu .
1075   • If K > Pu , β̂uK should be set as the ((K − Pu )/I)-th quantile of {suj }, where j ∈ N̂u .
1076
1077   The sampling trick above can be seen as non-bias. Nevertheless, this sampling setting is still not
1078
       practical in RS. In the case of K > Pu , the quantile ratio (K − Pu )/I can be too small and even
1079   less than 1/N (e.g., K = 20, I = 105 , N = 103 ). Therefore, the estimated quantile β̂uK could be
       theoretically higher than all the negative item scores and can not be estimated by sampling N̂u .

1276   In this paper, we evaluate the performance of each method mainly under the following two Top-K

1279     where the training and test sets are i.i.d. split from the whole dataset and have the same distributions.

1282     evaluate the denoising capabilities. Our false positive noise setting is similar to the false negative
1283     noise setting in Wu et al. (2024b). Specifically, for each user u, we randomly sample ⌈r × Pu ⌉
1284     negative items and flip them to positive items as false positive noise. The range of noise ratios r is
1285     {5%, 10%, 15%, 20%}.

1303

1309     between the final layer and the l∗ -th layer, i.e. adding a auxiliary InfoNCE (Oord et al., 2018) loss
1310     between these two layers. Following the original Yu et al. (2023)’s setting, the modulus of random
1311     noise between each layer is set as 0.1, the contrastive layer l∗ is set as 1 (where the embedding layer
1312     is 0-th layer), the temperature of InfoNCE is set as 0.1, and the weight of the auxiliary InfoNCE
1313     loss is searching from {0.05, 0.1, 0.2}.
