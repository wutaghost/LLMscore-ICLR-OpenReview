009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Variational Autoencoder (VAE) aims to compress pixel data into low-dimensional
012            latent space, playing an important role in OpenAI’s Sora and other latent video dif-
013            fusion generation models. While most of existing video VAEs inflate a pretrained
014            image VAE into the 3D causal structure for temporal-spatial compression, this pa-
015            per presents two astonishing findings: (1) The initialization from a well-trained
016
               image VAE with the same latent dimensions suppresses the improvement of sub-
               sequent temporal compression capabilities. (2) The adoption of causal reasoning
017
               leads to unequal information interactions and unbalanced performance between
018
               frames. To alleviate these problems, we propose a keyframe-based temporal com-
019            pression (KTC) architecture and a group causal convolution (GCConv) module to
020            further improve video VAE (IV-VAE). Specifically, the KTC architecture divides
021            the latent space into two branches, in which one half completely inherits the com-
022            pression prior of keyframes from lower-dimension image VAEs while the other
023            half involves temporal compression into the 3D group causal convolution, reduc-
024            ing temporal-spatial conflicts and accelerating the convergence speed of video
025            VAE. The GCConv in above 3D half uses standard convolution within each frame
026            group to ensure inter-frame equivalence, and employs causal logical padding be-
027
               tween groups to maintain flexibility in processing variable frame video. Extensive
               experiments on five benchmarks demonstrate the SOTA video reconstruction and
028
               generation capabilities of the proposed IV-VAE. The source code and weights will
029
               be made available to the public.

032   1   INTRODUCTION
033
034   In recent years, video generation has made significant advances in both academia and industry,
035   showcasing cinematic-level visuals and the potential of world simulator (e.g., OpenAI’s Sora (Ope-
036   nAI, 2024)). In particular, diffusion optimization methods on latent space have garnered widespread
037   attention and dominated the video generation field due to their high efficiency and stability, result-
038
      ing in a series of outstanding Latent Video Diffusion Models (LVDMs), e.g., Stable Video Diffusion
      (SVD) (Blattmann et al., 2023), Open-Sora (hpcaitech, 2024), Open-Sora-Plan (Chen et al., 2024).
039
040   To achieve a bidirectional mapping between the high-dimensional pixel space and the low-
041   dimensional latent space, Variational Autoencoder (VAE) (Kingma, 2013) are employed to encode
042   the original video continuously into the latent space, along with spatial and even temporal compres-
043   sion. The compression rate and reconstruction quality of VAE directly determines the information
044
      effectiveness of the video corpus, which plays an important role in the exploration of LVDMs.
045   As the most commonly used video VAE, SVD-VAE (Blattmann et al., 2023) freezes the weights of
046   the 2D encoder based on the image VAE (Rombach et al., 2022) and retrains a temporal decoder
047   containing 3D convolutional blocks, which enables the feature interaction across different frames
048   and significantly reduces flicker artifacts. Unfortunately, this method struggles to effectively elim-
049   inate redundancy in the temporal dimension, limiting the LVDMs to perceive and generate long
050
      sequences of video content.
051   To address the aforementioned issues, several works (Yang et al., 2024; hpcaitech, 2024; Chen et al.,
052   2024; Qin et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024) have proposed to construct a 3D VAE with both spatial
053   and temporal compression capabilities, which can be summarized in the following three points. In
      terms of model structure, the UNet network (Ronneberger et al., 2015) with strong pixel-perception

      capabilities is still adopted, consistent with the classic image VAE series (i.e., Stable Diffusion). For
055   the operator implementation, most of the methods (Yang et al., 2024; hpcaitech, 2024; Chen et al.,

062   from scratch (Yu et al., 2023a). Although existing methods achieve video reconstruction under 4x

      the improvement of the video quality and continuity in reconstruction.

      First, initialization from a well-trained image VAE with the same latent dimension cannot support

      further 4x time compression, leading to a sharp decline in initial spatial compression performance

      and slow improvement in subsequent temporal compression. As shown in Fig. 1(a), we calculate the

086   Appendix A.1). We note that: (1) As the latent channel number Z increases, the spatial compres-
087   sion gain produced by the image VAE gradually diminishes (blue dashed lines), and even the spatial
088   compression capability provided by an image VAE with dimension Z/2 is sufficient for a temporal-
089   spatial compressed video VAE with dimension Z. (2) The video VAE that inherits image weights of
090   a lower dimension Z/2 and uses the remaining latent channels to store the spatial information of an
091   extra frame, i.e., acquiring the initial 2× time compression capacity (red curves), converges even bet-
092   ter and faster than one that inherits weights of the same dimension Z (green curves). This inspires us
093
      to start from an image prior with a lower number of latent channels and leave the remaining features
      for temporal compression, promoting a more balanced temporal-spatial compression initialization.
094
      To this end, we propose a dual-branch keyframe-based temporal compression architecture, where a
095
      2D branch inheriting the low-dimensional image VAE prior focuses on keyframe compression, and
096   a 3D branch faciliates temporal compression.
097
098   Second, the unidirectional information flow of 3D causal convolution prevents the interaction be-
099
      tween video frames, exacerbating inter-frame unbalanced performance under temporal compression.
      For M frames (a frame group) upsampled from the same compressed frame, the front frame can-
100
      not interact with other frames in the group due to the forward flow of causal convolution, while
101
      the last frame can acquire the information of the whole frame group, resulting in the preference of
102   later frames in the optimization process. As shown in Fig. 1(b), the methods (Yang et al., 2024;
103   hpcaitech, 2024; Chen et al., 2024) based on causal convolution suffer from significant inter-frame
104   flicker, which is indicated by the fluctuating SSIM values across different frames. This inspires us to
105   propose group causal convolution, which groups the input frames based on the temporal compres-
106   sion rate. Padding operations with causal logic are applied between groups to ensure the continuity
107   in processing variable frame videos, and standard convolution operations are used within each group
      of M frames to share equivalent interactive information, achieving smoother reconstruction results.

      In addition, the local receptive field struggles to meet the same temporal compression demand at
109   high resolutions. For the same video, the higher the resolution, the more pixels the same motion
110   spans, making it more challenging for the model to capture motion patterns. To this end, we intro-
111   duce dilated convolutions and added multiple attention modules to expand the receptive field, thus
112   improving the temporal motion perception capability of the model at high resolutions.
113
      Finally, considering the increasing attention to content diversity and resolution in the video genera-
114
      tion field, there is a lack of a suitable open source benchmark for comprehensive evaluation of video
115   VAE reconstruction capabilities. Most of the datasets (e.g., Kinetics-600 (Carreira et al., 2018) and
116   UCF-101 (Soomro et al., 2012)) are 720P or lower, and cannot be used for higher resolution (e.g.,
117   1080P) evaluations. In addition, high-resolution datasets usually consist mainly of slow-motion and
118   fixed-shot videos, which hardly reflects the overall performance of video VAEs under varying mo-
119   tion speeds. Therefore, we re-collect a subset of 2000 videos with 1080P containing various motion
120   speeds from the OpenVid-0.4M (Nan et al., 2024) dataset, named MotionHD, as a supplement to
121   the overall evaluation. Experimental results show that our method achieves state-of-the-art video
122   reconstruction at various resolutions and motion rates.
123   In summary, the contributions of this paper include: (1) We propose a keyframe-based temporal
124   compression architecture to accelerate convergence speed by providing more balanced and power-
125   ful temporal-spatial compression initialization. (2) We introduce group causal convolution as a re-
126   placement for causal convolution to achieve better and balanced performance between frames, while
127   maintaining causal logic. (3) Extensive experiments on five benchmark demonstrate the SOTA video
128   reconstruction and generation capabilities of the proposed IV-VAE.

      2   R ELATED WORK
131
132
      Variational Autoencoder. Variational Autoencoder (VAE), as a widely used model in the gener-
133
      ation domain, adept at learning complex distributions from high-dimensional data. VAEs can be
134
      categorized into two types based on the class of tokens, including discrete and continuous. Clas-
135   sical work of the former type includes VQ-VAE (Van Den Oord et al., 2017), Magvit (Yu et al.,
136   2023a) et al, where continuous latent features are discretized by quantization techniques for autore-
137   gression generation. The latter type of continuous VAE, which can achieve spatial or even temporal
138   compression by mapping high-dimensional data distributions to a low-dimensional latent space, is
139   widely used in latent diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022; Blattmann et al., 2023).
140
      Video VAE. Earlier video generation works (Zhou et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024; Blattmann et al.,
141   2023) usually use image VAE to compress video frames, or fine-tune the image VAE with additional
142   time-dimensional convolutions to reduce flickering artifacts, but this approach cannot effectively
143   reduce the redundancy in the time dimension. With the emergence of OpenAI’s Sora (OpenAI,
144   2024), to allow video generation models to learn and generate longer videos, there have been some
145   recent works (hpcaitech, 2024; Qin et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Zhao et al.,
146   2024) that attempt to train a video VAE with a larger time compression ratios (e.g., 4x). Open-
147   Sora (hpcaitech, 2024) adopts a stacked approach, first compressing the video frames in the spatial
148   dimension based on SDXL image VAE (Podell et al., 2023), and then introducing 3D VAE to com-
149
      press the temporal dimension, this practice dramatically reduces the GPU memory usage of video
      reconstruction. OD-VAE (Chen et al., 2024) uses tail initialization to inflate the 2D convolutions of
150
      SD image VAE (Rombach et al., 2022) into 3D causal convolutions and explores a more efficient
151
      model variant. CV-VAE (Zhao et al., 2024) uses latent space regularization to avoid latent space
152   distribution shift while compressing time. CogVideoX (Yang et al., 2024) constructs a multi-GPU
153   parallel computing approach to process long videos based on causal video VAE.

      3.1   3D C AUSAL VAE
184
      In the beginning, we introduce a causal video VAE as the baseline method. We first train an im-
185   age VAE based on the architecture of the SD VAE with a few structural modifications (see sub-
186   section 3.5). Following the thought of Magvitv2 (Yu et al., 2023b), we choose to use 3D causal
187   convolution in the construction of video VAE. Drawing on the idea of central initialization (Carreira
188   & Zisserman, 2017), we inflate the 2D convolution in the image VAE to the 3D causal convolution.
189   For example, noting the 2D convolutional weights as W2D , when inflating W2D to be W3D with
190   convolutional kernel time dimension of 3, W3D is formulated as [0, 0, W2D ]. In this way, we can
191   obtain high image quality reconstruction results after extending the image VAE to a causal video
192   VAE. Lastly, in addition to the first image frame being processed separately, the causal video VAE
193
      achieves 4x temporal compression on the input, following Magvitv2 (Yu et al., 2023b).
194   Adopting a causal VAE as the baseline approach has the following two advantages: (1) The first
195   frame is always independent of other frames. According to Magvitv2 (Yu et al., 2023b), using a
196   temporal causal VAE instead of a regular VAE makes the model perform better in processing a
197   single image. (2) Each frame depends only on the previous frame. Thus, the temporal causal video
198   VAE can use cache mechanism in the inference, which can split the reconstruction process of a long
199
      video into a series of shorter videos with exactly the same result. We consider this a better operation
      compared to the regular overleap approach, more details are displayed in subsection 4.5.2.

      3.2   G ROUP C AUSAL C ONVOLUTION
203
      Based on the above baseline, we note that it is practically impossible and non-optimal to ensure
204   causal logic for all frames. For example, the baseline approach compresses time by a factor of 4,
205   then the input 4 frames will be compressed into 1 frame by the encoder and then recovered into
206   4 frames through the decoder. Obviously, these 4 frames recovered from the same compressed
207   frame do not satisfy the temporal causal correlation. In addition, for these 4 reconstructed frames,
208   the performance of the first frame is usually worse than that of the following frames as shown in
209   Fig. 1(b), resulting in frequent flickering in the reconstructed video. We attribute this problem to the
210   insufficient and unbalanced interaction between neighboring frames due to the causal convolution.
211   Therefore, to alleviate this problem while being able to maintain the advantages of causal VAE
212   (subsection 3.1), we propose group causal convolution (GCConv). As shown in Fig. 2(a), for a
213   video VAE with a temporal compression rate of tc (tc = 4 in our method), the proposed group
214   causal convolution sequentially groups every tc frames of the input video frames into one frame
215   group. In particular, the first image frame is a separate frame group. For a frame group, we aim
      to make the features between frames visible and interactable with each other, thus increasing the


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      reconstruction smoothness between frames and reducing the flicker. For different frame groups,
217   we maintain the property of causal convolution that the current frame group only depends on the
218   previous frame group. Therefore, the time padding scheme for group causal convolution is designed
219   in Fig. 2(a). We pad the features of the previous frame before each frame group, in particular, the
220   first image frame is padded using its own features. And we use zero padding after each frame group.
221   After causal logic padding, each group is operated separately using the same regular convolution.
222   Notably, the number of frames in the frame group is not fixed during the forward process, and
223   for each temporal downsampling/upsampling operation of the feature map, the number of frames
224   contained in the frame group decreases/increases by the same factor.

226   3.3   K EYFRAME - BASED TEMPORAL COMPRESSION
227
228
      Building on the observation from Fig. 1(a), to obtain better temporal-spatial compression capability
      at the initialization of video VAE, it is feasible to utilize pre-trained weights of images with a low
229
      latent channel number. To this end, we propose a dual-branch keyframe-based temporal compression
230
      (KTC) architecture that decouples the compression of a frame group into the spatial compression of
231   key frame and the temporal compression of the overall motion. The basic unit is shown in Fig. 2(b).
232   For the input feature map F with Cin channels, it is fed into a 2D convolution to extract the image
233   information and a 3D group causal convolution to extract the overall temporal motion information,
234   respectively. The two convolutions each output feature maps with channel number Cout /2, which
235   are then separately normalized by RMS-Norm and concatenated together. In the initialization phase,
236   the 2D and 3D branches of the network are initialized using pre-trained image weights with the
237   number of latent channels Z/2 respectively. In the 3d branch, the 2D image weights are inflated to 3d
238   weights based on the center initialization (Carreira & Zisserman, 2017). In addition, by elaborately
239
      setting the weights of the newly added temporal upsampling and downsampling layers of the two
      branches, the network is initially able to independently process two different frames within a frame
240
      group to achieve 2× temporal compression with strong image compression capability as well.
241
242   The overall structure of the network is based on UNet (Ronneberger et al., 2015) and adopts the
243   proposed KTC as the base unit, as shown in Fig. 2(d). After two temporal downsampling, a frame
244   group is merged into a compressed frame, so we replace the GCConv3D in KTC Unit with a 2D
245
      convolution at this stage to reduce the computation. In the output of the model, each of the two
      branches produces a sequence of video frames with the same shape as the input, where the recon-
246
      struction results of keyframes and remaining frames are selected from the 2D branch and 3D branch
247
      outputs, respectively, and compose the final reconstruction result.

      3.4   T EMPORAL M OTION P ERCEPTION E NHANCEMENT
250
251   For the same video, as the video resolution increases, the pixel span generated by object motion
252   or camera motion increases, and the more difficult it is for the video VAE to capture the motion
253   patterns due to the limitations of the receptive field. To this end, we make two improvements to
254   increase the receptive field of the video VAE to enhance the perception of temporal motion at high
255   resolution. As shown in Fig. 2(c), we introduce multiple parallel atrous convolutions (PAC) with
256   different atrous rates in the last layer of the encoder. The generated features of different branches
257   are concatenated together and then pass through a 1×1 convolution to adjust the number of channels,
258
      borrowing from the ASPP in DeepLabV3+ (Chen et al., 2018). In addition, we expand the number
      of attention modules from 2 to 7, and all attention modules are implemented after full temporal
259
      and spatial compression to reduce the computational effort. At this time, the compressed frame
260
      contains all the information of the frame group, and the global receptive field of attention modules
261   can effectively facilitate the perception of the temporal motion especially at high resolution.
262

      3.5   OTHER STRUCTURAL MODIFICATIONS
264
265   Based on the architecture of the SD image VAE, we make some structural modifications to better
266   adapt to the 3D causal VAE. First, we replace all GroupNorms with RMSNorms to ensure tempo-
267   ral causality following Magvitv2, since the standard GroupNorm involves temporal dimension when
268   calculating the mean and variance of the features within each group, which destroys temporal causal-
269   ity. Second, we shrink the channel number during spatial upsampling instead of in the later residual
      block, which allows IV-VAE to reduce max memory usage by 29% in 480P video reconstruction.

509   4.5.2    C ACHE MECHANISM vs OVERLEAP MECHANISM .
510
      For the reconstruction of a long video, it is usually impractical to encode and decode it in a single
511
      operation due to the limitation of the memory, therefore it is necessary to reconstruct the original
512
      video by splitting it into a number of clips. Overlap is the most commonly used approach (Chen
513   et al., 2024), where each input video clip has a one-frame overlap with the previous clip as a history
514   frame, and the overlapped frame is discarded after reconstruction. However, encoding through over-
515   lap is not equivalent to single-step encoding, which may introduce semantic bias in the latent space
516   due to short history frame. And the existence of overlapped frame leads to extra computation.
517
      To address this problem, we introduce the cache mechanism, which is based on the property that the
518
      causal network depends only on the previous frames in temporal order. According to the length Pt
519   needed for 3D convolutional temporal padding, the last Pt frames of the current video clip’s feature
520   maps are saved at each layer of the network, which are used for the convolutional padding of the
521   next video clip. Therefore, this mechanism can be fully equivalent to one-step reconstruction in
522   terms of results. In addition, as shown in Table 7, the use of the caching mechanism achieves less
523   time usage and video memory usage compared to the overlap mechanism.
524

532   5     L IMITATION .
533
534   The overall architecture of the proposed method is still based on UNet following SD image VAE
535   without exploring on other architectures. Video VAE faces more and unique difficulties compared
536   to image VAE, e.g., as the video resolution increases, the need for receptive field increases for
537   video VAE. While the classical UNet lacks a global receptive field, in addition, the number of
538   spatial downsampling of video VAE is usually aligned with the spatial compression ratio, which
539   also limits the receptive field of video VAE. Therefore, it is worthwhile to consider introducing new
      architectures such as Dit or Mamba into video VAE in future work.
