009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Current interactive segmentation approaches, inspired by the success of META’s
012            Segment Anything model, have achieved notable advancements, however they
013            come with substantial limitations that hinder their practical application in real clin-
014            ical scenarios. These include unrealistic human interaction requirements, such as
015            slice-by-slice operations for 2D models on 3D data, a lack of iterative refinement,
016            and insufficient evaluation experiments. These shortcomings prevent accurate as-
017            sessment of model performance and lead to inconsistent outcomes across studies.
018            IntRaBench overcomes these challenges by offering a comprehensive and re-
019            producible framework for evaluating interactive segmentation methods in realis-
020            tic, clinically relevant scenarios. It includes diverse datasets, target structures,
021            and segmentation models, and provides a flexible codebase that allows seamless
022            integration of new models and prompting strategies. Additionally, we introduce
023
               advanced techniques to minimize clinician interaction, ensuring fair comparisons
               between 2D and 3D models. By open-sourcing IntRaBench, we invite the research
024
               community to integrate their models and prompting techniques, ensuring continu-
025
               ous and transparent evaluation of interactive segmentation models in 3D medical
026            imaging.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Accurate segmentation of anatomical structures or pathological areas is crucial in fields like ra-
033
      diology, oncology, and surgery to isolate affected regions, monitor disease progression, treatment
      planning and guide therapeutic procedures. Traditional supervised medical segmentation models
034
      have demonstrated strong performance across a range of anatomies and pathologies (Isensee et al.,
035
      2020; 2023; Huang et al., 2023; Ulrich et al., 2023). However, their effectiveness remains heavily
036   constrained by the amount and diversity of available training data, with the quality of human label
037   annotations serving as a critical limiting factor. Consequently, fully autonomous AI solutions have
038   not yet reached performance needed for widespread autonomous clinical applications.
039
      On the other hand, numerous semi-automatic segmentation techniques, not reliant on AI, are already
040
      in clinical practice to expedite manual annotation processes Hemalatha et al. (2018). These current
041
      ad hoc methods do not tap into the potential of AI-based automation to drastically reduce annotation
042   time. A method that allows clinicians to segment any target with just a single click within the image
043   could greatly enhance the efficiency of clinical workflows.
044   The release of META’s Segment Anything (SAM) model represents a big leap towards making this
045   potential a reality (Kirillov et al., 2023). ”SAM” is designed to segment any target through different
046   user interaction methods, including point-based and bounding box prompts. This allows users to
047   easily specify the area of interest by clicking on it or drawing a bounding box around it, making the
048   segmentation process both flexible and intuitive. A particularly powerful feature is the ability for
049   users to iteratively refine initial predictions by adding more positive or negative prompts.
050   This advanced functionality, in contrast to traditional supervised segmentation methods, has at-
051   tracted a lot of attention in the medical domain, and led to many studies evaluating and adapting
052   SAM for 3D medical image segmentation (Roy et al., 2023; Deng et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2023; Zhou
053   et al., 2023; Mohapatra et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024; Gong et al., 2023). More-
      over, several researchers have been inspired by SAM’s capabilities to develop their own methods,

      Although these domain-specific adaptations on medical data have shown promising progress, many
      published methods are plagued by pitfalls which obfuscate the efficacy of the models and prevent
082
      clinicians and researchers from determining the best methods for their use-cases:
083
084   Applying interactive 2D models to 3D data on a slice-by-slice basis (P1): Assuming clinicians
085   will interact with each slice individually is unrealistic and undermines the efficiency improvements
086   these methods aim for. Moreover, a slice-by-slice approach introduces an unfair bias when compar-
087
      ing 2D and 3D models, as 3D models typically require only a few interaction per image, leading to
      significantly fewer interactions and less supervision Cheng et al. (2023); Ma et al. (2024); Zhang &
088
      Liu (2023); Wu et al. (2024); Wong et al. (2024).
089
090   Neglecting refinement (P2): Many studies assess interactive segmentation methods based on a
091   single interaction step, overlooking the inherent ambiguities in radiological images (Ma et al., 2024;
092   Du et al., 2024; Gong et al., 2023; Bui et al., 2024). Often, a second interaction may be necessary
093
      to specify which specific substructure the clinician wants to segment. This could be, e.g. a vessel
      within the liver, or the necrosis within a tumor, as exemplified in the well-known BraTs segmentation
094
      challenge (de Verdier et al., 2024). Furthermore, clinicians often want to adapt the segmentations
095
      to their clinic’s local protocol or refine them particularly for targets with high inter-rater variability,
096   like pathological structures (Fu et al., 2014; Benchoufi et al., 2020; Hesamian et al., 2019). Overall,
097   there is a notable lack of research exploring realistic, iterative interaction methods for 2D models
098   applied to 3D volumes.
099
      Obfuscated and insufficient evaluation (P3): With promptable models only recently garnering
100
      great attention, there is a lack of a standardised approach to evaluation, which has led to disparate
101
      and incomparable methods, which are at times even obfuscated or insufficient. (i) Not specifying
102   whether predictions were interactively refined or based on a single prompt with multiple points
103   (Cheng et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024). (ii) Being intransparent on the number of initial prompts
104   given (Du et al., 2024). (iii) Using the best mask rather than the final one after interactive refinement
105   (Wang et al., 2024). (iv) Evaluating predictions slice by slice or on sub-patches of a 3D volume
106   instead of the full image (Roy et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 2023; He et al., 2024;
107   Li et al., 2024). (v) Excluding targets considered ’too small’ neglecting valid targets such as small
      lesions that are neither tested nor trained on Ma et al. (2024); Cheng et al. (2023); Wang et al.

135
      mentation as well as the best prompting methods in the radiological domain. In this paper, we
136

137   the following key contributions:
138
139        1. IntRaBench, for the first time, enables a fair comparison of the most influential 2D and 3D
140           interactive segmentation methods. By measuring the number of simulated interactions, a
141           proxy for the ”Human Effort”, we test different prompting strategies that do not require a
142           slice-wise interaction (P1).
143        2. We propose effective interaction strategies for refinement of predictions in a 3D volume,
144           without requiring clinicians to interact with each individual slice (P2).
145        3. We provide a standardized evaluation protocol to generate prompts, select model outputs
146           and compute the segmentation metrics on the entire image across eight datasets, covering
147           various modalities and target structures, including small lesions (P3). Our benchmarking
148           efforts includes a performance comparison against leading interactive segmentation meth-
149           ods in the medical domain.

151           method in a fair manner against established methods and b) easily develop and investigate
152           new prompting strategies.

155
      work, promoting continuous and equitable assessment that allows to track the overall progress in the
      field of interactive 3D medical image segmentation reproducibly and transparently.

160   The Interactive Radiology Benchmark is designed to easily enable a fair and reproducible evaluation
161   of 2D and 3D interactive segmentation methods for 3D radiological image segmentation for the very
      first time. While prompting 3D models is generally straightforward, we introduce specific prompting

175   2.1   I NITIAL P ROMPTING
176
177   Prompts are a key component of any interactive segmentation method and can highly influence over-
178   all performance of the underlying method. IntRaBenchdistinguishes between 2 visual prompting
179   types. Point prompts correspond to a click of a user in the image, and box prompts refer to a box
180   around the target structure. While there is no difference in the action of clicking for 2D and 3D
181   methods, a 3D box requires an additional dimension compared to a 2D box. Notably, some meth-
182
      ods also enable a distinction between foreground and background point prompts. While 3D models
      allow segmenting a 3D volume natively, 2D based models require an interaction for each slice, re-
183
      sulting in excessive effort, which is prohibitive for clinicians as it would take too much time in daily
184
      clinical practice. Hence, any meaningful performance comparison must account for this difference
185   in prompting effort.
186
187   To increase the feasibility of 2D models for 3D application, it is essential to reduce this effort. We
188
      propose two straightforward methods, for both point and box prompts, to explore their performance
      and provide a proxy for measuring the effort of human interaction.
189
190   Point interpolation: Let I ⊂ N be a set of axial indices of all foreground slices. We simulate a user
191   by selecting n foreground points, specifically the center of the largest connected component of slice
192   i1 , ...in ∈ I where the ij are equally spaced within I and i1 = min(I) and in = max(I). Then, we
193   interpolate linearly between each point and the next one and use the intersections of the resulting
194
      lines with the axial slices as positive point prompts, as visualized in Fig. 3 c).
195   Point propagation: We simulate a user providing min(I), max(I), and a 2D point prompt within
196   the median slice corresponding to the median axial index im . Given this point, the model generates
197   a segmentation Sm for the median slice. We then select a ’central point,’ specifically the center of
198   mass of the largest connected component of Sm , to use as a prompt for the slice indexed by im−1 .
199   We generate a segmentation Sm−1 of this slice, and sample from it similarly to generate a prompt
200
      for the slice indexed by im−2 . This process continues downwards until we segment the slice with
      the axial index min(I). The propagation is then repeated upwards, starting from im+1 and using the
201
      center of mass of Sm , continuing until we segment the slice with the axial index max(I). This is
202
      shown in Fig. 3 e).
203
204   Box interpolation: We simulate a user providing n 2D bounding boxes, one in each of i1 , ...., in ∈
205   I, the ij as in point interpolation. Since the boxes are uniquely defined by their minimum and
206
      maximum vertices, we can interpolate between the minimum vertices as in point propagation to get
      a minimum vertex in each axial slice, and similarly get a maximum vertex in each axial slice, this
207
      providing a box prompt in each slice. This is shown in Fig. 3 d).
208
209   Box propagation: We simulate a user providing min(I), max(I), and a 2D box prompt within the
210   slice corresponding to the axial index im , m as in point propagation. The model then generates a
211   segmentation Sm for the median slice. We take a bounding box of Sm and use this as a prompt for
212
      the slice indexed by im − 1. We continue propagating down to min(I) and then start again from the
      center and propagate up to max(I) as in point propagation, but using box prompts instead of point
213
      prompts. Fig. 3 f).
214
215   While one cares about realistic prompting behavior, IntRaBench also supports the previously
      mentioned slice-wise prompting styles for completeness.

250   2.2   R EFINEMENT P ROMPTING
251
252   Refinement of previous segmentations is an important aspect of interactive segmentation models, as
253   it allows iteratively improving the segmentation until the desired structure is segmented to a user’s
254   demands. Some interactive segmentation models allow for the refinement of initial segmentations by
255   providing the model with the previous prediction along with a new prompt to correct errors, either
256   through foreground clicks on false negative pixels or background clicks on false positive pixels.
257   While this process is straightforward for 3D models, 2D models naively only allow for refinement
258
      of one slice at a time, which again places an unrealistic burden on clinicians. Therefore, we present
      refinement strategies that require a manageable level of effort from the user.
259
260
261
      Scribble refinement: To represent a user-centric refinement strategy we introduce an algorithm
      simulating user-created scribble prompts: At each refinement step, our proposed algorithm generates
262
      either positive or negative additional prompts. The decision to generate positive prompts follows a
263
      Bernoulli trial with success probability p = nf n /(nf n +nf p ), where nf n , nf p represent the number
264   of false negatives and false positive voxels, respectively.
265   If positive prompts are selected, we perform a connected component analysis on the false negative
266   voxels. Let L be the largest connected component, we generate a scribble from the bottom to the
267   top of L by taking the centroid of L in each slice to simulate drawing a vertical scribble through the
268   ’middle’ of L. This simulates a clinician annotating regions that were falsely not segmented.
269   For 2D models, we then individually feed all slices i ∈ I where the voxel along the scribble was not
      predicted, along with the new positive prompt derived from the scribble and the previous prediction


                                                          5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      s ⊂ S, back into the model. For 3D models, we feed the whole 3D patch, together with the previous
271   prediction S and all new positive points derived from the scribble into the network in one step.
272   If negative prompts are selected, we identify a non-axial slice Sf p of S that contains the most false
273   positives. Then we generate a contour curve around the ground truth target object at a distance of 2
274   pixels. We then select a subpart C with a length of 60% of the full curve and sample all pixels c ∈ C
275   that are false positives to obtain a set of points D, simulating a user drawing a few scribbles in areas
276   where the model over-segmented the target. For 2D models, we then generate new slice predictions
277   for each slice containing a point in D by providing the model with the previous prediction as well
278   as new negative prompts: all d ∈ D which belong to that slice. For 3D models, we again feed the
279
      whole 3D patch, together with the previous prediction S and a negative prompt sampled from D.
280
281   2.3   H UMAN E FFORT P ROXY
282
      The models’ performance is highly dependent on the effort a human puts into initial prompting and
283
      refinement of the predicted masks. Generally, the effort required for 3D methods is less than that
284   for 2D methods, although the strategies mentioned above significantly reduce the effort of 2D meth-
285   ods substantially. We aimed to establish a general measure of the effort a method would require
286   from a human user. A more formalized mathematical approach involves assigning degrees of free-
287   dom (DoF) to each interaction. For instance, a point corresponds to 3 DoF, a 2D box has 5 DoF
288   (requiring selection of the z-axis and two 2D points), and a 3D box consists of 6 DoF. However,
289   point interpolation has 9 DoF, whereas point propagation only has 5 DoF, since it requires just the
290   axial coordinate rather than both minimum and maximum points with 3 DoF each. From the user’s
291   perspective, however, identifying the z-coordinate demands the same level of effort as selecting a
292
      3D coordinate by clicking at the target structure’s endpoint along the z-axis. Similarly, an arbitrary
      scribble has significantly more DoF than a straight or parabolic line, yet the difference in effort for
293
      the user is minimal. Therefore, we define user effort in terms of the number of interactions required
294
      for a specific task. While not an exact measure, this method offers the most practical estimation of
295   the actual effort involved from the user’s perspective.

298
299   In our comprehensive benchmark, we include various interactive segmentation methods. Fig. 1
300   illustrates the types of prompts each method supports. Iterative refinement is only possible for
301   methods that allow a (previously predicted) mask as a prompt.
302

303
      to evaluate and adapt it to the domain of radiological medical images. It was trained on iteratively
304   generated and curated 1B masks and 11M images, but not explicitly on radiological images. META’s

307

308

      2.5     DATASETS
344
345
      Dataset selection was a non-trivial problem for this benchmark: While models that were originally
346   introduced in the natural image domain rarely see any radiological 3D data, the medical counter-
347   parts were often trained on all publicly available data that the authors could obtain. For example,
348   MedSAM was trained using more than 60 publicly available datasets (Ma et al., 2024). Although
349   these methods conducted their final validation on excluded datasets or at least on separate test sub-
350   sets of images, the test data varies between models. As a result, identifying annotated datasets with
351   interesting target structures that were not part of any methods training set has proven challenging.
352   Nevertheless, we assembled a diverse collection of ten lesser known or recently released public
353   datasets featuring various pathologies and organs, including CT and MRI images. Specific details
354   of these are provided in Table 1. To enhance reproducibility and eliminate barriers of entry for non
355   domain experts, we automated the dataset download and preprocessing, minimizing any required
356   domain knowledge to use the benchmark. However, due to the sparsitity of labeled datasets we urge
357   developers to exclude these datasets from their train dataset selection, as this would compromise the
358   integrity of a clean evaluation.

      2.6     E VALUATION
361
362
      All interactive segmentation methods identify their target structure based on a spatial prompt, in-
363   herently resulting in instance segmentation. As a result, we perform the evaluation on an instance-
364   by-instance basis. Unlike in object detection, each prompt already provides information on the
365   localization of the target structure, hence detection metrics like F1-Score are not relevant, hence
366   we rely solely on the Dice Similarity Coefficient (DSC) score as a metric. The instance-wise DSC
367   metric is then averaged per case (i.e. per image volume), and further aggregated across all cases in
368   the dataset, as recommended by Maier-Hein et al. (2024). For better presentation, we averaged the
369   dice across all classes of a dataset and also specify how many human interactions are simulated.

446   observed that for 2D models, it is crucial to provide the initial prompts during the refinement process.
447   2D models tend to over segment the target, filling the entire slice foreground. The absence of the
448   initial prompt leads to a complete loss of target location information, as the initial predicted mask
449   is highly inaccurate. Our refinement likely generates negative additional prompts due to the large
450   number of false positive pixels. In Table 5, we present refinement results from initial predictions
451   produced by 3 Box Interpolation. In this case, we did not include the previous point in the iterative
452
      prompts, which resulted in a performance decline during refinement.
453   For 3D models, iterative refinement also led to consistent performance gains. Both randomly sam-
454   pled prompts and those derived from refinement scribbles improved performance with each refine-
455   ment iteration. Although SegVol initially performs best without iterative refinement, it lacks support
456   for further refinement. In contrast, SamMed 3D Turbo surpasses SegVol after several refinement
457   steps.

477   3.3   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
478
479   In this paper we introduced IntRaBench and with it, compared the performance of 2D and 3D
480   interactive segmentation models in 3D medical imaging. We provide a holistic and transparent
481   overview of the current state-of-the-art and highlight key findings that offer practical insights:
482
483         1. Bounding Boxes Outperform Points: Bounding boxes consistently outperform point-
484            based inputs by providing better spatial context, which leads to improved segmentation
485            accuracy, especially for complex structures in radiological images. Point-based prompts
               lack this context, resulting in poorer performance.


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
            2. Iterative Refinement is Essential: The ability to iteratively refine segmentations signifi-
487            cantly enhances model performance, particularly in challenging cases. Models that allow
488            multiple rounds of corrections show better accuracy, making this feature crucial for clinical
489            applications. For example SegVol reached highest performance in a static setting, how-
490            ever SamMed 3D Turbo is able to exceed SegVol given a few interactions, highlighting the
491            importance of refinement.
492         3. Realistic 2D prompting can match unrealistic prompting: Our introduced realistic
493            prompting styles are able to reach ?? and match unrealistic prompting 2D prompting meth-
494            ods. This unlocks 2D methods for actual clinical workflows without any performance
495            penalties.
496         4. Points Underperform Compared to Literature: Contrary to claims in previous literature,
497            point-based methods underperformed, likely due to previous work training and evaluating
498            their methods on simpler target structures. Previous work mostly focused largely on high-
499            contrast tasks, and excluded small target objects inflating expectations for these methods.
500
501   Implications IntRaBench suggests that bounding boxes and iterative refinement should be
502   prioritized in the design of segmentation models for medical imaging, particularly when addressing
503   complex radiological images. Furthermore, it underscores the importance of including diverse, dif-
504   ficult tasks in training data to improve model generalization for clinical use. It is also crucial to test
505
      2D models in scenarios that simulate real human interaction, ensuring that segmenting a volumetric
      image does not require unreasonable effort by prompting the model slice by slice.
506
507   A key limitation of this work is that it only simulates real clinical settings. While this approach pro-
508   vides valuable insights into model performance providing a proxy for the simulated human effort, it
509   falls short of capturing the full complexity and practical challenges of actual clinical workflows. As
510   future work, a comprehensive study involving clinicians is essential to assess different prompting
511
      strategies in real-world environments. Such a study should not only evaluate segmentation perfor-
      mance but also measure the time required for annotation, offering critical insights into the practical
512
      feasibility and efficiency of these models in clinical practice.
513
514   To conclude, our proposed IntRaBench benchmark presents a powerful tool for the future of inter-
515   active segmentation research in medical imaging, serving as a catalyst for innovative solutions by
516   enabling a fair and reproducible comparison between leading methods. One of the standout poten-
517
      tials is its ability to streamline the evaluation of both 2D and 3D segmentation models, allowing
      for more realistic and clinically relevant testing conditions. By focusing on human interaction and
518
      the efficiency of iterative refinement, IntRaBench opens new avenues for research, including un-
519
      derstanding the impact of different interaction strategies and how they reduce clinician effort. Not
520   only does this benchmark address the existing gaps in evaluation standardization, but it also of-
521   fers a unique opportunity to refine segmentation performance on pathologies often overlooked, such
522   as small lesions. The open-source nature of the benchmark further encourages continuous contri-
523   butions, allowing researchers to test new methods and prompting strategies seamlessly within this
524   framework. Future work using IntRaBench can reveal novel insights into the balance between per-
525   formance and clinician involvement, fostering advancements that may lead to improved medical
526   workflows. This potential to improve real-world clinical applications, especially by reducing the la-
527   bor intensity of medical professionals, marks IntRaBench as a crucial tool in catalyzing meaningful
528
      research progress.

705   B     M ODEL SPECIFICATIONS
706
707   B.1   SAM
708
      SAM is compatible with multiple image encoders, particularly the ViT family from Dosovitskiy
709
      et al. (2021). We used the default and best-performing model with ViT-Huge. To ensure high-
710   quality inputs for the model, we performed slice-wise inference by slicing through the valong the
711   through-plane. Each slice was normalized by first clipping 0.5th and 99.5th percentile of the vol-
712   ume’s intensity distribution and then scaling the values between volume minimum and maximum
713   to [0,1], followed by a rescaling to [0,255]. The image was repeated three times along the channel
714   axis to produce an RGB-like image. The images were resized so their longest side was 1024 pixels,
715   padding the shorter side to 1024 pixels if needed to maintain square dimensions. Finally, the images
716   were again normalized using the model’s pre-stored mean and standard deviation as suggested by the
717   original implementation. Inference was restricted to slices containing foreground. After prediction,
718
      the slices were reassembled into a volume, inverse transformed to the original coordinate system,
      and metrics were computed in the native image space.
719
720
721   B.2   SAM2
722
      SAM2 supports multiple image encoders, specifically the Hiera family of Ryali et al. (2023). We
723
      used the best-performing model, Hiera-L. We clip the volumes, slice along the through-plane and
724
      make the images RGB-like just as with SAM. The images are then rescaled to 1024×1024 pixels and
725   again normalized using mean and standard deviation provided together with the pretrained weights.
726   Aggregation and inverse transformation are then performed similarly to SAM.
727
728
      B.3   M ED SAM
729
730   To apply the model slice-wise, we slice the input volume as with SAM, and then clip each slice
731   based on their 0.5th and 99.5th percentile values. The images are then made RGB-like by repeating
732   thrice along a new channel-dimension, rescaled to 1024 × 1024 pixels and then normalised to [0,1].
733   Aggregation and inverse transformation is performed similarly as with SAM.
734
735   B.4   SAM-M ED 2D
736
737   To apply the model slice-wise, we slice the input volume as with SAM, and then clip each slice based
738   on their 0.5th and 99.5th percentile values same as with MedSAM. The slices are then made RGB-
739   like and converted to a [0,255] scale as in SAM’s preprocessing. The slices are then standardised
740
      using a mean and standard deviation provided along with the model, and resized to 256 × 256 pixels.
      Aggregation and inverse transformation is performed similarly as with SAM.
741
742
743   B.5   SAM-M ED 3D
744
      The model is not applied slice-wise so no reorientation is needed. The volume is respaced to
745
      1.5×1.5×1.5 mm and then clipped based on its 0.5th and 99.5th percentiles. SAMMed3D performs
746   inference on a 128x128x128 pixel crop, which in a first inference pass we take centered around our
747   point prompt, if there is only one point prompt passed, and around the centroid of our prompts
748   if multiple points are passed simulatenously. For subsequent passes (in the case of iterative refine-
749   ment), the crop remains unchanged. The predicted crop is inserted back in its correct position within
750   the wider coordinate system, and then respaced back to the original spacing so that evaluation takes
751   place in the coresponding native image space.
752
753   B.6   SAM-M ED 3D T URBO
754
755   SAM-Med3D Turbo is an updated checkpoint for SAMMed-3D and so we perform the same pre-
      and postprocessing.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      B.7   S EG VOL
757
758   SegVol uses all voxels with value exceeding the volume mean to calculate the 0.5th and 99.5th
759   percentile of these voxels. Intensity values are clipped by these percentiles, and the mean and
760   standard deviation of the foreground voxels are used for normalization. The values are then rescaled
761   to a [0,1]. Finally, the volume is cropped to its foreground. A first ’zoom-out’ inference is performed
762   on this image, followed by a ’zoom-in’ sliding window inference. The predicted volume is then
763   transformed back to the original space and compared with the unprocessed ground truth to calculate
764
      metrics.
