032                                                                             A BSTRACT
033
034                     Large-scale diffusion models have achieved remarkable success in generating
035                     high-quality images from textual descriptions, gaining popularity across various
036                     applications. However, the generation of layered content, such as transparent im-
037                     ages with foreground and background layers, remains an under-explored area.
038                     Layered content generation is crucial for creative workflows in fields like graphic
039                     design, animation, and digital art, where layer-based approaches are fundamen-
040                     tal for flexible editing and composition. In this paper, we propose a novel im-
041
                        age generation pipeline based on Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs) that generates
                        images with two layers: a foreground layer (RGBA) with transparency informa-
042
                        tion and a background layer (RGB). Unlike existing methods that generate these
043
                        layers sequentially, our approach introduces a harmonized generation mechanism
044                     that enables dynamic interactions between the layers for more coherent outputs.
045                     We demonstrate the effectiveness of our method through extensive qualitative and
046                     quantitative experiments, showing significant improvements in visual coherence,
047                     image quality, and layer consistency compared to baseline methods.

050   1       I NTRODUCTION
051
052   Large-scale diffusion models have recently emerged as powerful tools in the domain of generative
053   AI, achieving remarkable success in generating high-quality, diverse, and realistic images from tex-
      tual prompts. These models, such as DALL-E (Ramesh et al. (2021)), Stable Diffusion (Rombach


                                                                                     1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      et al. (2022)), and Imagen (Saharia et al. (2022)), have gained immense popularity due to their abil-
055   ity to create complex visual content with impressive fidelity and versatility. As a result, they have
056   become integral to various applications, from digital art and entertainment to data augmentation and
057   scientific visualization. However, despite the significant advancements in these models, the problem
058   of layered content generation has only recently been explored by works such as Zhang et al. (2024);
059   Quattrini et al. (2024), which exhibits the potential of enabling creative workflows.
060
      Layered content generation, especially the creation of transparent images, plays a vital role in cre-
061   ative industries such as graphic design, animation, video editing, and digital art. These workflows
062   are predominantly layer-based, where different visual elements are composed and manipulated on
063   separate layers to achieve the desired artistic effects. Transparent image generation, where the fore-
064   ground content is isolated with an alpha channel (RGBA), is essential for blending different visual
065   elements seamlessly, enhancing flexibility, and ensuring coherence in complex visual compositions.
066   The lack of research on generating such layered content highlights an important gap considering
067   the application of diffusion models in practical and creative context, in addition to the usefulness of
068   layer-based content creation tools such as Adobe Photoshop and Canva.
069   To address this gap, we propose a novel image generation pipeline based on Latent Diffusion Models
070   (LDMs) (Rombach et al. (2022)) that focuses on generating layered content. Our method produces
071   images with two distinct layers: a foreground layer in RGBA format, containing transparency infor-
072   mation, and a background layer in RGB format. This approach contrasts with traditional methods
073   that are either based on generating layered content in a sequential manner (Quattrini et al. (2024)), or
074   empowered by sequentially generated synthetic data in less satisfying quality (Zhang et al. (2024)),
075
      which often leads to inconsistencies and lack of harmony between generated layers. Instead, our
      proposed method introduces a harmonized generation mechanism that enables interactions between
076
      these two layers, resulting in more coherent and appealing outputs and supporting flexible spatial
077
      edits for manipulation, as shown in Fig. 1.
078
079   In our framework, the harmonization between foreground and background layers is achieved through
080   the utilization of cross-attention and self-attention masks extracted from the foreground generation
081
      model. These masks play a critical role in guiding the generation process by identifying and focus-
      ing on the relevant features needed to create both layers in a unified manner. By leveraging these
082
      attention mechanisms, our approach allows for a fine-grained control over the generation process,
083
      ensuring that the generated foreground and background elements interact naturally, enhancing the
084   overall visual quality and coherence. Following this, we introduce an innovative attention-level
085   blending mechanism that utilizes the extracted attention masks to generate the background and fore-
086   ground pair in a harmonized manner. Unlike previous methods that handle each layer separately and
087   rely on training data that involves sequentially generated layers (Zhang et al. (2024)), our blending
088   scheme integrates information from both layers at the attention level, allowing for dynamic inter-
089   actions and adjustments that reflect the underlying relationships between the elements of the scene.
090   This not only improves the realism of the generated images but also provides users with enhanced
091   control over the final composition. In summary, our contributions are threefold:
092
             • We propose a new image generation pipeline that generates images with two lay-
093
               ers—foreground (RGBA) and background (RGB)—in a harmonized manner, allowing for
094
               natural interactions between the layers.
095
096
             • We develop a novel attention-level blending scheme that uses the extracted masks to per-
               form seamless blending between the foreground and background layers. This mechanism
097
               ensures that the two layers interact cohesively, leading to more natural and aesthetically
098
               pleasing compositions.
099
100          • We perform extensive qualitative and quantitative experiments that demonstrate the ef-
101
               fectiveness of our method in generating high-quality, harmonized layered images. Our
               approach outperforms baseline methods in terms of visual coherence, image quality, and
102
               layer consistency across several evaluation metrics.

107   Denoising Probabilistic Diffusion Models. Diffusion models contributed significantly in the field
      of image generation, specifically for the task of text-to-image generation. In early efforts, Ho et al.

      (2020); Song et al. (2020a;b) made significant contributions to the area, where significant improve-
109   ments on generation performance has been experienced with diffusion models on pixel level. In
110   another paradigm Rombach et al. (2022) proposed operating in a latent space, which enabled the
111   generation of high-quality images with a lower computation cost compared to models operating on
112   pixel-level, which built the foundation of the state-of-the-art image generation models Podell et al.
113   (2023); Esser et al. (2023); Peebles & Xie (2023). Even though such approaches differ in terms
114   of their architecture designs, they all follow a paradigm that prioritizes building blocks relying on
115   attention blocks Vaswani (2017).
116   Transparent Image Layer Processing. In terms of obtaining single foreground layer, the work of
117   Chen et al. (2022) presents PP-Matting, a trimap-free natural image matting method that achieves
118   high accuracy without requiring auxiliary inputs like user-supplied trimaps. Meanwhile, Quattrini
119   et al. (2024) propose Alfie, a method for generating high-quality RGBA images using a pre-trained
120   Diffusion Transformer model, designed to provide fully-automated, prompt-driven illustrations for
121   seamless integration into design projects or artistic scenes. It modifies the inference-time behavior
122   of a diffusion model to ensure that the generated subjects are centered and fully contained without
123
      sharp cropping. It utilizes cross-attention and self-attention maps to estimate the alpha channel,
      enhancing the flexibility of integrating generated illustrations into complex scenes. In terms of multi-
124
      layer, Tudosiu et al. (2024) recently introduce MuLAn, a novel dataset comprising over 44,000
125
      multi-layer RGBA decompositions of RGB images, designed to provide a resource for controllable
126   text-to-image generation. MuLAn is constructed using a training-free pipeline that decomposes a
127   monocular RGB image into a stack of RGBA layers, including background and isolated instances.
128   While these methods have made significant progress, precise control over image layers and their
129   harmonization remain challenging.
130
      The most related effort for layered content synthesis is done by Zhang et al. (2024). This approach is
131
      notable for its capability to generate both single and multiple transparent image layers with minimal
132   alteration to the original latent space of a pretrained diffusion model. The method utilizes a “latent
133   transparency” that encodes the alpha channel transparency into the latent manifold of the model. It
134   offers two main workflows. One is jointly generating foreground and background layers by attention
135   sharing. The other one is a sequential approach that generates one layer first and then another layer
136   based on previous layer. Both requires heavy model training relying on synthetic training data in less
137   satisfying quality (obtained by a pretrained inpainting model). In contrast, our framework provides
138   a training-free solution that offers generation of layered content in a simultaneous manner, which
139   both benefits from layer transparency and achieves harmony between layers.

145
      For the foreground generation, we rely on the LayerDiffuse framework proposed by Zhang et al.
146   (2024). As a preliminary step to achieve foreground transparency, it initially introduces a latent
147   transparency offset xϵ , which adjusts the latents x decoded by the VAE of the latent diffusion model,
148   to obtain a latent distribution modelling foreground objects as xa = x+xϵ . Following this step, they
149   train a transparent VAE D(I,  ˆ xa ), that predicts the α channel of the RGB image involving a single
150   foreground image, which is referred to as the pre-multiplied image I.  ˆ Note that our framework only
151   benefits from their foreground generation model, proposing a training-free solution of generating
152   blended and background images without needing any additional training, without disturbing the
153   output distribution of neither the foreground or the original pretrained diffusion model. A visual
154   overview of our framework is provided in Fig. 2.

245   3.3      B LENDING S CHEME
246
247   Given the formulations for the structure prior and the content confidence maps, extracted from the
248   foreground generator, ϵθ,F G , we propose a blending scheme on the attention level to achieve full har-
249   monization. Since the content of the generated image is constructed gradually in every consecutive
250   attention layer, where self-attention focuses more on the structure details and cross-attention focuses
251   more on the content of the image, we introduce a blending scheme that targets both, with the help of
252
      generative priors extracted from these targeted layers. For the blending scheme, we first introduce
      a mask extraction algorithm where we extract soft and hard blending masks for the given attention
253
      block. Given the structure prior s and content confidence prior c, we initially extract masksof t as
254
      s ∗ c, followed by a min-max normalization to be able to use it as a blending mask. Then, to identify
255   the regions that are affected by the soft blending, we extract our hard mask maskhard by using the
256   soft decision boundary σ(d ∗ (masksof t − 0.5)), where σ is the sigmoid operator. During blending,
257   we select the decision boundary coefficient d as 10, where we provide ablations in Sec. 4.1.4. We
258   provide visualizations of prior masks s, c and blending masks masksof t and maskhard in Fig. 3.
259
      After extracting the soft and hard blending masks, we perform blending in attention level. Given an
260
      image generation procedure where one aims to generate an image triplet consisting a foreground,
261   background and blended image, we introduce a blending approach involving the attention outputs of
262   the blended image, aBlended , and the foreground image, aF G . As the soft mask masksof t encodes
263   the structure and content information related with the foreground, we initially perform soft attention
264   blending between the blended and foreground attention outputs, to reflect the foreground content on
265   the blended image. We formulate the blending equation in Eq. 1.

526
527   In this paper, we presented a novel image generation pipeline based on LDMs that addresses the
528   challenge of generating layered content, specifically focusing on creating harmonized foreground
529   and background layers. Unlike traditional approaches that rely on consecutive layer generation, our
530   method introduces a harmonized generation process that enables dynamic interactions between the
531   layers, leading to more coherent and aesthetically pleasing outputs. This is achieved by leverag-
532   ing cross-attention and self-attention masks extracted from the foreground generation model, which
533   guide the generation of both layers in a unified and context-aware manner. It is noted that since our
534   pipeline is built on top of pre-trained LDMs and LayerDiffuse Zhang et al. (2024) which may carry
535
      inherent biases from their training data, these biases can affect the generated content, potentially
      leading to outputs that are not entirely aligned with user expectations or specific requirements. Nev-
536
      ertheless, the findings highlight the potential of our approach to transform creative workflows that
537
      rely on layered generation, providing more intuitive and powerful tools for artists and designers.

      A.2   L IMITATIONS
657
658   While our proposed image generation pipeline based on Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs) demon-
659   strates significant advancements in generating harmonized foreground (RGBA) and background
660   (RGB) layers, there are several limitations that warrant discussion. Our current approach focuses on
661   generating images with two distinct layers—a foreground and a background. While this is suitable
662   for many creative workflows, it does not extend to more complex scenarios involving multiple layers
663   or hierarchical relationships among multiple visual elements, which we intent to explore for future
664   work. Moreover, the harmonization between foreground and background layers in our framework
665   relies heavily on the quality of the cross-attention and self-attention masks extracted from the gener-
666
      ation model. In cases where these masks are suboptimal or noisy, the blending of layers may not be
      as effective, leading to artifacts or less coherent outputs. Finally, our method depends on pre-trained
667
      Latent Diffusion Models both for foreground and background generation, which may carry inherent
668
      biases from their training data (such as generating centered foregrounds for the RGBA component).
669   These biases can affect the generated content, potentially leading to outputs that are not entirely
670   aligned with user expectations or specific requirements in diverse applications. Nevertheless, our
671   method provides a structured framework for generating transparent images and layered composi-
672   tions, which are crucial for many creative tasks.
