009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            Hyperparameter tuning, particularly the selection of an appropriate learning rate
012            in adaptive gradient training methods, remains a challenge. To tackle this chal-
013            lenge, in this paper, we propose a novel parameter-free optimizer, A DAM G (Adam
014            with the golden step size), designed to automatically adapt to diverse optimization
015            problems without manual tuning. The core technique underlying A DAM G is our
016
               golden step size derived for the AdaGrad-Norm algorithm, which is expected to
               help AdaGrad-Norm preserve the tuning-free convergence and approximate the
017
               optimal step size in expectation w.r.t. various optimization scenarios. To bet-
018
               ter evaluate tuning-free performance, we propose a novel evaluation criterion,
019            reliability, to comprehensively assess the efficacy of parameter-free optimizers
020            in addition to classical performance criteria. Empirical results demonstrate that
021            compared with other parameter-free baselines, A DAM G achieves superior perfor-
022            mance, which is consistently on par with Adam using a manually tuned learning
023            rate across various optimization tasks.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
      Optimization serves as a foundation technique underpinning modern deep learning, which finds
028
      applications in various domains such as computer vision, AI for science, and natural language pro-
029
      cessing (Voulodimos et al., 2018; Redmon et al., 2016; Paul et al., 2021; Devlin et al., 2018; Radford
030   et al., 2019). Some of the recent optimization approaches include embedding momentum mecha-
031   nisms (Sutskever et al., 2013) and Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014). Among them, adaptive gradient
032   methods play an important role (Duchi et al., 2011; Kingma & Ba, 2014; Liu et al., 2023) due to
033   their attractive performance across diverse problem structures, encompassing deep model architec-
034   tures, data characteristics, and running hyper-parameters. Hyperparameter tuning associated with
035   those optimization algorithms has a significant impact on the practical performance (Wilson et al.,
036   2017). Especially, the Learning Rate (LR) tuning holds primary importance, since the precision
037   of LR of popular (adaptive gradient) optimization methods is closely related to unknown problem
038
      properties, such as smoothness, error of estimated gradient, and the initial optimality gap. The close
      relationship between LR and these properties makes it a necessity to choose LR. Manual tuning is a
039
      commonly adopted practice for parameter selection, which requires computational resources and is
040
      prohibitive in large-scale machine-learning tasks.
041
042   Recently, there has been a growing interest in parameter-free training methods 1 due to their practical
043   training efficiency and satisfactory performance. These methods are designed to eliminate the need
044
      for manual tuning parameters, achieving performance levels close to the best manually tuned train-
      ing methods. Pioneering works in the realm of parameter-free training, incorporating mechanisms
045
      like subroutine&bisection subroutine mechanisms (Nesterov, 2015; Carmon & Hinder, 2022), are
046
      prohibitively expensive in the context of large-scale deep learning problems. This study directs its
047   focus toward identifying parameter-free training methods that maintain comparable training costs
048   to the most standard training algorithms, such as Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014), for deep learning
049   problems.
050
      Current trends in parameter-free training methodologies are standard practices of embedding initial
051
      optimality gap into step size or drawing insights from the recently proposed DoG (Ivgi et al., 2023),

066   To tackle this problem, we first explore how to systematically evaluate the effectiveness of

      In this paper, we design a novel algorithm that leverages the ability of tuning-free convergence of
090   AdaGrad-Norm (Duchi et al., 2011; Ward et al., 2020; McMahan & Streeter, 2010; Wang et al.,
091   2023). Specifically, we formally define a golden step size of AdaGrad-Norm, drawing insights to
092   preserve the ability of tuning-free convergence and approximate the optimal step size in expectation
093   across various optimization problems. Subsequently, we derive the solution for the golden step size,
094   which is independent of problem-specific properties, and integrate it into AdaGrad-Norm, resulting
095   in our first parameter-free optimizer (Algorithm 1). By deeply integrating the derived golden step
096   size with the Adam, we further introduce an Adam-like parameter-free method named A DAM G
097   (Algorithm 2). Compared to existing parameter-free optimization methods, our proposed A DAM G
098
      stably outperforms all the baselines across various optimization tasks and achieves performance that
      closely aligns with the best performance achieved by manually tuning Adam.
099
100   We highlight the following contributions of the paper:
101
             • We introduce a novel evaluation criterion, namely reliability, for assessing parameter-free
102            training methods. Practical results show that this criterion reasonably validates the adaptabil-
103            ity of parameter-free optimizers to diverse optimization problems.
104          • Based on our analysis of the classical AdaGrad-Norm algorithm, we propose the golden step
105            size for AdaGrad-Norm, which is expected to preserve the ability of tuning-free convergence
106            and approximate the optimal step size in expectation w.r.t. various optimization problems,
107            resulting in an AdaGrad-Norm version parameter-free optimizer. Furthermore, we extend
               this concept to devise an Adam-like parameter-free method named A DAM G.

115   Adaptive gradient methods have emerged as predominant choices for training deep learning opti-
116   mization problems (Kingma & Ba, 2014; Balles & Hennig, 2018; Zhuang et al., 2020; Chen et al.,
117   2023). Concurrently, there is a rising popularity of adaptive parameter-free approaches in the opti-
118   mization landscape (Ivgi et al., 2023; Defazio & Mishchenko, 2023; Khaled et al., 2023; Mishchenko
119   & Defazio, 2023).
120   The search mechanism is the natural avenue for achieving parameter-free capability. As we men-
121   tioned earlier, several works are the practice of search mechanism (Nesterov, 2015; Feurer & Hutter,
122   2019; Carmon & Hinder, 2022). Pioneering efforts without search mechanisms usually estimate the
123   problem properties and are more concerned with guarantees for convex optimization problems. For
124   instance, the Polyak step size schedule incorporates f (xk ) − f ⋆ into gradient descent for convex op-
125   timization problems (Polyak, 1987). The subsequent adaptations of this approach demonstrate fair
126
      performance in handling nonconvex problems (Loizou et al., 2021; Malitsky & Mishchenko, 2019;
      Latafat et al., 2023). Contrary to problem properties estimation, approaches adopted from online
127
      learning with theoretical guarantees, such as coin betting schemes, have been applied in deep learn-
128
      ing optimization problems (Orabona & Pál, 2016; Orabona & Tommasi, 2017; Chen et al., 2022).
129   A more recent trend involves the utilization of the initial optimality gap and the sum of gradient

      loss curve) and solution quality (E.g., test accuracy), which are common in classical optimizer
337   designs (Kingma & Ba, 2014; Liu et al., 2023) and parameter-free optimizer designs (Ivgi et al.,
338   2023; Defazio & Mishchenko, 2023; Khaled et al., 2023; Mishchenko & Defazio, 2023). A good
339   parameter-free optimizer consistently performs well across various optimization tasks, which is not
340   covered by these criteria. We hereby introduce a novel criterion, reliability, to assess parameter-free
341   optimizers.
342
      Following, we formally introduce the definition of reliability to illustrate how to systematically
343   evaluate the adaptability of a parameter-free training method to diverse optimization tasks, which is
344   hard to achieve by observing the performance of single or independent tasks.
345
      Definition 4.1 (Reliability). Given a set of optimization tasks, we initially group all the conducted
346
      optimization tasks into four categories based on the optimizers among Adam(1e-2), Adam(1e-3),
347   Adam(1e-4), and Adam(1e-5), whichever yields the best performance measure, such as test accu-
348   racy. Reliability is calculated by averaging the practical ratio in each category, where the ratio is
349   the statistical information about specific parameter-free optimizers achieving less than a 5% perfor-
350   mance drop compared to the corresponding best Adam on all tasks in that category.

410   5    C ONCLUSION
411
412   In this work, we introduced a new mechanism for achieving parameter-free in adaptive gradient
413   training methods by proposing the golden step size. This step size aims to preserve the tuning-
414   free convergence and approximate the optimal step size in expectation w.r.t. various optimization
415   problems. The resulting optimizer, A DAM G, demonstrates improved reliability and solution quality
416   compared to previous methods, closely matching the performance of manually tuned Adam and
417   facilitating deployment readiness.
418   Limitation 1). Despite the practical success, understanding the theoretical guarantees of the pro-
419   posed approach is crucial. We discuss that while the proof framework for the convergence of
420   AdaGrad-Norm in Wang et al. (2023) served as inspiration for our approach, it relies on the col-
421   lective behavior of AdaGrad-Norm step size throughout the entire training trajectory. The diverging
422   step size for AdaGrad-Norm is not directly connected to the one derived from the progressive up-
423   dating formula, (Wang et al., 2023; Faw et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023), paving the way for future
424
      research. 2). While this work verifies that the proposed optimizer has a wide adaptability range
      through a broad spectrum of optimization tasks, it fails on some tail tasks possibly due to its ex-
425
      pectation mechanism. Further investigations of embedding extra (approximated) problem properties
426
      such as the optimality gap may mitigate the issue but also lead to further work.
