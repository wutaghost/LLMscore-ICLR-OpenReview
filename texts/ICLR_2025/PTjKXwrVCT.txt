               Shocks and sudden spikes are common characteristics of real-world time series
               data. For example, demand surges or electricity outages often occur in time
011
               series data, manifesting as spikes (“Needles”) added to the regular time series
012
               (“Haystack”). Despite their importance, it is surprising to find their absence
013            in the benchmarking protocol at the frontier of time series research—Time Se-
014            ries Foundation Models (TSFM). To address this gap, we present the Needle-in-
015            a-Time-Series-Haystack (N ITH) Benchmark, which includes both synthetic and
016            real-world spiky time series data from diverse domains like traffic, energy, and
017            biomedical systems. For synthetic data, we develop a flexible framework using
018            Poisson-based modeling to generate spiky time series, allowing us to evaluate
019            forecast models under various conditions. To accurately assess model perfor-
020            mance, we introduce a new metric based on Dynamic Time Warping, specifically
021
               designed for spiky data. We evaluate the zero-shot forecasting capabilities of six
               popular TSFMs over 64 million observations, identifying their limitations related
022
               to architecture, tokenization, and loss functions. Furthermore, we demonstrate
023
               that the incorporation of the proposed N ITH dataset, due to its diversity compared
024            to the common pre-training corpus of TSFMs, results in improved performance.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Time series forecasting, where future events are predicted given the past, is a critical task with appli-
030   cations in many industries, such as climate science, traffic engineering, and advertising. Forecasting
031   spiky time series is particularly important, because rare events, such as solar flares, traffic accidents
032   and holiday sales, are numerically represented as narrow spikes occurring sparsely across a long
033   time series. Due to their rarity and short duration, we refer to these spikes as ’needles’ and the
034   base time series without spikes as the ’haystack’. The ability to accurately forecast needles in a
035   times-series haystack gives practitioners the foresight to plan around rare and significant events.
036   Recently, foundation models for time series forecasting (TSFM S) (Woo et al., 2024; Goswami et al.,
037   2024; Das et al., 2023; Ansari et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b) have garnered significant attention. Un-
038   like traditional time series forecasting models (Rangapuram et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2019; Salinas
039   et al., 2020; Rasul et al., 2023; Lim et al., 2021), foundation models do not require training on
040   downstream datasets to perform effective inference. Instead, they first pretrain on a large corpus of
041   general time series, then perform zero-shot forecasting on any downstream univariate time series.
042   The development of foundation models has democratized time series forecasting from only individ-
043   uals with large training datasets to the general public. Given the success of TSFM S on standard
044
      benchmarks and the significance of spiky time series forecasting, our work studies the zero-shot
      effectiveness of TSFM S on spiky time series specifically.
045
046   Although forecasting spikes is known to be challenging (Ansari et al., 2024), existing zero-shot
047   forecasting model only measure model performance in aggregate and not specific classes of time
048   series. Hence, it is unclear whether such models can reliably forecast spikes and when it cannot.
049   In contrast, our study systematically identifies some problem areas current TSFM S struggle with,
050
      offering suggestions for developing the next generation of TSFM S. To the best of our knowledge,
      we are the first to study TSFM S’ performance on spiky time series data.
051
052   Our work introduces two times series benchmarks, a Real-world Needle in a Time-series Haystack
053   dataset (N ITH -R EAL), and a Synthetic Needle in a Time-series Haystack dataset (N ITH -S YNTH).
      For the real-world datasets, we collect multiple spiky time series datasets, across various do-

055   foundation models can immediately be used in practice for spiky time series forecasting. For the

088   2.1   T IME S ERIES F OUNDATION M ODELS
089
      Recently, TSFM S have been proposed for zero-shot forecasting on numerical time series, outper-
090
      forming traditional time series forecasting models such as ETS, ARIMA (Hyndman et al., 2008),
091   and Theta (Assimakopoulos & Nikolopoulos, 2000) under univariate settings. Early TSFM S used
092   prompting to query language models for forecasting tasks (Jin et al., 2023; Gruver et al., 2024;
093   Chang et al., 2023). Instead of prompting, PatchTST (Nie et al., 2022) uses patching (Alexey,
094   2020) to directly tokenize and finetune on the time series. Later works experiment with different
095   tokenization schemes, such as LagLlama’s (Rasul et al., 2023) lag-based covariate tokenizer and
096   Chronos’ (Ansari et al., 2024) quantile-based sample tokenizer. By pretraining on larger datasets,
097   these models exhibit effective zero-shot abilities. Other works utilize updated model architectures,
098   such as TimeFM’s (Das et al., 2023) decoder-only architecture and Moment’s (Goswami et al.,
099
      2024) encoder-decoder architecture. Certain works, like Moment (Goswami et al., 2024), require
      finetuning to adapt masked token reconstruction pretraining to the forecasting task. Moirai (Woo
100
      et al., 2024) open-sourced a large real-world pretraining dataset, LOTSA, which were then used
101
      by later works such as Timer (Liu et al., 2024b) to perform tasks beyond univariate forecast-
102   ing. TinyTMS (Ekambaram et al., 2024) allows efficient forecasting though smaller transformers.
103   TimeGPT (Garza & Mergenthaler-Canseco, 2023) provides commercial API access to a closed-
104   source model for zero-shot forecasting. In this work, we test 6 recent open-source time series fore-
105   casting models (Rasul et al., 2023; Ansari et al., 2024; Das et al., 2023; Woo et al., 2024; Liu et al.,
106   2024b; Ekambaram et al., 2024). While newer TSFM S tackle tasks beyond univariate forecasting
107   or improve inference efficiency, our work questions whether TSFM can really perform effective
      zero-shot forecasting on a particularly important class of univariate time series, spiky time series.

      3.1      T IME S ERIES F ORECASTING
127
128   We study the forecasting problem on univariate spiky time series. Specifically, given a time series
129   with a context of C steps, ux = [u0 , u1 , ..., uC−1 ], our goal is to forecast the next F steps, uy =
130   [uC , uC+1 , ..., uC+F −1 ]. We benchmark time series forecasting models, M, which predict the next
131   F steps by tokenizing the context, passing it through a transformer, then detokenizing the time series.
132   We denote inference by ũy = M(ux ), where ũy is the predicted forecast.
133
134   3.1.1      S PIKY T IME S ERIES
135   Spiky time series are time series with repeated spikes. When these spikes follow some underlying
136   pattern, it is possible for forecasting models to forecast them1 . Compared to standard time series,
137   forecasting spiky time series induces 3 new challenges: (1) spikes occur infrequently within the
138   context, thus the model has limited information, (2) spikes occur only for a short duration, thus the
139   model must memorize past occurrences, and (3) the average duration between spikes is subject to
140   some noise, thus the model must be robust. We call these challenges the “difficulty dimensions.”
141   Specifically, given Nspikes spikes that occur at indices, J = [τ̂0 , τ̂1 , ..., τ̂Nspikes ], we quantify

      3.3   N ITH : S YNTHETIC S PIKY T IME S ERIES
188
      We motivate our synthetic dataset, N ITH -S YNTH, by the limited size and scope of real-world
189
      datasets and by observing that real-world spikes may either be the product of some underlying pat-
190   tern or noise. To train and evaluate on more N ITH-style data, we provide a mathematical framework
191   for generating diverse spiky time series. To disentangle the contribution of noise from underlying
192   patterns, our framework follows the most simple pattern, periodicity, where a spike occurs every µT
193   time steps “in-expectation” with standard deviation of σT . Note, although this pattern is seemingly
194   simple, no TSFM S could accurately forecast needles across all settings.
195
      Specifically, we define spiky time series in N ITH -S YNTH benchmark by enforcing the following
196   properties: (1) “outlier constraint”: the needle must have larger amplitude than the haystack, (2)
197   “rarity constraint”: the needle must be near-nonzero only for a small fraction of the time series, and
198   (3) “periodic-in-expectation”: a spike occurs every µT time steps “in-expectation” with standard
199   deviation of σT . The summarized generation algorithm can be found at Alg 1.

      4.4   O N M ODEL D ESIGN
461
      Finally, we provide further insight into tokenizer design decision for TSFM, by evaluating
462   C HRONOS pretrained from scratch on synthetic N ITH with σT = 0, as motivated in Section 4.1,
463   with different tokenizer settings. Specifically, we benchmark TSFM performance with different
464   patch-lengths and model architectures. As a reference, T IMES FM uses a patch length of 32. Given
465   the positive performance of C HRONOS, we ablate smaller patch lengths of 8, 16, and 32. In this
466   study, we consider when stride is equal to the patch length. We leave further analysis to future work.
467   We consider encoder, encoder-decoder, and decoder only models with fixed number of layers and
468   dimension size. Our results hold with fixed number of layers and larger dimension size as well.
469   Architecture. We begin our investigation by testing the architecture across the most popular to-
470   kenizer setup, patch-based tokenizers. By evaluating SDTW performance on the synthetic N ITH
471   benchmark (Figure 7), we find that encoder-decoder outperform both encoder-only and decoder-only
472   architectures. We hypothesize this is because encoder-decoder models combine bidirectional atten-
473   tion, extracting more information from the context, with autoregressive generation, which generate
474   tokens conditioned on past predictions. Patch length has a smaller overall impact than architecture
475   on SDTW performance. Hence, it is worthwhile to first ablate architecture-level decisions when
476
      training new TSFM.
477   Patch Length. As shown in Figure 7, shorter patch lengths outperform longer patch lengths when
478   forecasting needles. Because patch-based tokenizers are convolutional layers, they low-pass filter
479   the data with a kernel-size equal to the patch-length. Because, the larger patch-length correlates
480   with more aggressive filtering, smaller patch lengths learn narrower needles better. We believe auto-
481   matically choosing the correct kernel-size for downstream problems could greatly improve efficacy
482
      of TSFM. Some existing TSFM, such as M OIRAI, accomplish this by pretraining with multiple
      patch-based tokenizers.

      5   C ONCLUSION
534
535   In conclusion, while existing time series foundation models made remarkable progress in zero-
536   shot univariate time series forecasting on most workloads, our analysis has identified substantial
537   challenges these models face when forecasting spiky time series. Specifically, we have identified
538   three core issues that existing foundation models encounter: 1) the average duration between spikes,
539   2) the width of the spikes, and 3) the randomness in duration between spikes. To systematically study
      each weakness and real-world uses, we proposed N ITH -S YNTH and N ITH -R EAL benchmarks.

      K.1    N EEDLE - IN - A -H AYSTACK
892
893   Needle-in-a-haystack search can be approached using various techniques, broadly categorized into
894   classical methods and machine learning-based approaches. Classical methods include heuristic
895   search techniques, such as brute-force search, which examines every element in the dataset, and
896   index-based methods, which improve efficiency by pre-processing the dataset to quickly locate
897   potential matches (Chauhan & Vig, 2015). However, these methods often struggle with high-
898   dimensional data and complex patterns (Pang et al., 2021).
899   Specifically, machine learning models such as convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and recurrent
900   neural networks (RNNs) have been applied to rare object detection in images, and RNNs have been
901   used for anomaly detection in sequential data. Additionally, transformer-based models, which excel
902   at handling long-range dependencies in data, have been applied to this problem, offering improved
903   performance in tasks such as DNA sequence analysis (Beltagy et al., 2020; Zaheer et al., 2020;
904   Kitaev et al., 2020; Gu & Dao, 2023). To characterize the language recalling ability of large language
905   models (Tay et al., 2020; Gu et al., 2021; 2022), many needle-in-a-haystack benchmarks have been
906   proposed (Hengle et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a;b; Kuratov et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024; Gu &
907
      Dao, 2023). In general, recalling information that occurs infrequently in the dataset is a tough but
      reliable challenge to measure the strengths of different foundation models. Here we propose a new
908
      needle-in-a-haystack dataset in the time series domain. In addition to that, our work investigates
909
      how to forecast needles instead of detecting needles in a retrieving manner.
