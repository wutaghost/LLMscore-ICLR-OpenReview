011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The rapid evolution of drug-resistant (DR) microbial has become a severe issue
014            for human health. Antimicrobial peptides (AMPs) are powerful therapeutic drugs
015
               for treating DR microbial, but their clinical application is limited by activity and
               toxicity. Recently, AI has shown its power in discovering the high-activity AMPs,
016
               relying on the database of the AMP’s wet-lab activity data. However, the activity
017
               data from this database are collected from thousands of papers, with their differ-
018            ent wet lab experiments setting on one or few types of DR bacteria, have further
019            limits the development of AI methods for AMP identification. Moreover, recently
020            AlphaFold has revolutionized the field of drug discovery, but how can we ben-
021            efit from the predicted structure for AMP discovery still remains unknown. To
022            address the above challenges, we make two contributions. a) We construct the
023            DRAMPAtlas 1.0 that contains the training set collected from the public and the
024            testing set from our wet lab experiment. Each AMP sequence is equipped with
025            its 3D structure, activity data, and toxicity, where the activity is about six types
026
               of DR bacteria. b) We conduct extensive experiments for AMP identification,
               by modeling the 3D structure as voxels or graphs, in conjugate with its sequence
027
               information or solely with the structure or sequence. We have made many inter-
028
               esting findings. We hope that our benchmark and findings can benefit the research
029            community to better design the algorithms for high-activity AMP discovery. All
030            code and data associated with the work will be made publicly available after ac-
031            ceptance.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Antimicrobial peptides (AMPs) are short amino acid (AA) sequences that can kill bacteria, fungi,
037   tumor cells, and viruses with the length typically from 6 to 50 (Fjell et al., 2012; Wan et al., 2024).
038   Although people have been discovering AMPs for over 80 years, fewer than 50 AMPs have been
039
      under clinical experiment or approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) (Chen &
      Lu, 2020). Drug resistance (DR) has become a serious issue for humans, with an increasing number
040
      of deaths of over 1.2 million a year (Murray et al., 2022). The membrane disruption and another
041
      mechanism of AMPs (Silva et al., 2020), have given the AMPs the potential to deal with the DR issue
042   of conventional antibiotics. Over the past decades, especially the revolution of deep learning (LeCun
043   et al., 2015), scientists have found new ways to effectively find the potential clinically useful AMPs.
044   Still, there is a long way to find the AMPs with high activity like antibiotics with the low toxin, high
045   stability, etc (Chen & Lu, 2020).
046
      The performance of deep learning-based methods highly relies on the data (Li et al., 2024b; Wan
047
      et al., 2024), specifically, the ground truth (GT) value and input data pairs. a) For the GT values,
048   in the real of the discovery of AMPs, the wet lab data like minimal inhibitory concentration (MIC)
049   values are collected from papers under different experimental setting (Wan et al., 2024). For exam-
050   ple, some AMP experiments are conducted under water while another AMP is conducted under the
051   plasma protein solution (Moretta et al., 2021). Besides, according to the classification of DR bacteria
052   from WHO (Willyard, 2017), there is more than one type of DR bacteria. One AMP may perform
053   differently under different types of DR bacteria (Szymczak et al., 2023). Current studies (de Breij
      et al., 2018; Das et al., 2021; Ma et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2023; Pandi et al.,

104   in Section 4.2. We rigorously evaluate the performance of the different types of input data, such as
105   sequence, structure, sequence & structure, etc. We answer the second question in Section 4.3 by
106   evaluating different types of backbone neural networks and different re-balancing strategies. We also
107   validate the scale-up ability of network parameters. All these trained models will be made available
      to the public.

127   2.1   R EPRESENTATIONS OF A NTIMICROBIAL P EPTIDES (AMP S )
128
129   From a global to a local perspective, the features of AMPs can be categorized into global descriptors,
130   sequence descriptors, and structure-based descriptors.
131
      Global descriptors summarize properties of peptides using fixed-size vectors, capturing aspects
132   such as sequence composition, structural features, and physicochemical properties (Xu & et al.,
133   2021). These descriptors have been extensively studied (Osorio et al., 2015; van Westen & et al.,
134   2013; Müller et al., 2017; Romero-Molina et al., 2019; Barigye et al., 2021; Chen & et al., 2018);
135   however, employing all available descriptors can lead to high-dimensional and redundant informa-
136   tion. Feature-selection algorithms (Saeys et al., 2007) help generate low-dimensional representa-
137   tions better suited for machine learning (ML) models. Constructing global descriptors requires
138   substantial effort and domain knowledge but is useful for capturing specific information when train-
139   ing data is limited. It is worth noting that global features can be derived from both sequence and
140
      structural information.
141   Sequence-based representations capture primary amino acid sequences using an n × L matrix,
142   where n is the number of features per amino acid and L is the sequence length. Amino acids are
143   often represented using one-hot encoding (Chen & et al., 2021), where each amino acid is uniquely
144   identified. However, one-hot encoding does not capture additional amino acid properties, which
145   can be addressed by incorporating physicochemical and evolutionary features (Kawashima, 2000).
146
      Deep learning (DL) techniques can learn amino acid embeddings in a data-driven manner (ElAbd
      & et al., 2020). Sequence-based representations are useful for ML models like recurrent neural
147
      networks (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Chung et al., 2014) and have been applied to peptide
148
      sequence generation (Wan et al., 2022) and property prediction (Chen & et al., 2021).
149
150   For structural representations, there are two prevailing methods: voxel representation and graph
151   representation.
152   Voxel representations involve voxelization of peptides’ 3D structures, with each voxel storing in-
153   formation about atom occupancies and properties (Jiménez et al., 2017). Three-dimensional convo-
154   lutional neural networks (3D-CNNs) (Maturana & Scherer, 2015) process these structures and have
155   been applied to tasks like protein binding site prediction (Jiménez et al., 2017) and protein-ligand
156   binding affinity prediction (Jones & et al., 2021).
157   Graph representations model peptides by using nodes (atoms or residues) and edges (chemical
158   bonds or spatial distances) to form graphs (Wang et al., 2023; Gong et al., 2023). These graphs are
159   encoded using features such as one-hot encodings of atom or residue types and geometric properties.
160   Graph-based inputs are suitable for geometry-related ML tasks and have been used in protein struc-
161   ture prediction (Jumper et al., 2021; Baek et al., 2021), AMP prediction (Yan et al., 2023), molecular
      conformation generation (Ganea & et al., 2021), and antibody design (Jin et al., 2022).

      2.2   L EARNING TO I DENTIFY AMP S
184
      Machine learning-based approaches (Garcı́a-Jacas et al., 2022; Sidorczuk & et al., 2022) trained
185   on public AMP databases (Pirtskhalava et al., 2021; Waghu et al., 2016; Witten & Witten, 2019;
186   Wang et al., 2016) have been utilized to predict antimicrobial activity from amino acid sequences, a
187   crucial step in AMP development. These approaches employ traditional ML methods like random
188   forests and support vector machines (Meher et al., 2017; Xiao et al., 2013; Fingerhut et al., 2021;
189   Santos-Júnior et al., 2020; Burdukiewicz & et al., 2020; Lawrence & et al., 2021; Bhadra et al.,
190   2018; Pane & et al., 2017), as well as DL-based methods (Yan & et al., 2020; Veltri et al., 2018;
191   Wang et al., 2024). However, variations in input representations, classification strategies for AMPs,
192   and evaluation metrics across studies complicate the determination of the most effective features
193
      and models. To ensure fair model comparisons, feature selection and classification of training data
      require standardized benchmarking (Garcı́a-Jacas et al., 2022; Sidorczuk & et al., 2022). Improving
194
      data quality is also vital, as public AMP databases often contain data from diverse experimental
195
      conditions and organisms (Wan et al., 2024). Therefore, in this work, we build a high-quality testing
196   set for AMP identification through extensive wet lab experiments.

201   3.1   G ROUND T RUTH C ONSTRUCTION FOR THE T ESTING S ET
202
      We constructed a multi-label test set through extensive wet lab experiments on a series of peptides.
203
      This test set includes the Minimum Inhibitory Concentration (MIC) performance of each peptide
204   against six high-risk Drug-Resistant (DR) bacterial classes listed by WHO. The MIC values for each
205   DR bacterium are represented by the average MICs of three different strains. The specific names of
206   these bacteria are: A. baumannii, P. aeruginosa, E. coli, K. pneumoniae, S. enterica, and S. aureus.
207   All peptides were tested under uniform wet-lab conditions, and each strain was tested twice to ob-
208   tain an average MIC value as the assessment result. The entire process spanned 18 months and cost
209   over $100,000, with detailed specifics provided in Appendix Table 1. The template peptides, from
210   which these similar peptides were derived, were generated using a conditional variational autoen-
211   coder (Wang et al., 2024). Through manual expertise and various methods, 151 peptide sequences
212
      were modified from this template.
213   In Table 1, we present a comparison between our new test set with previous works, highlighting that
214   our method currently involves the largest dataset of peptides verified through extensive wet-lab ex-
215   periments under uniform experimental conditions for MIC testing. Unlike previous studies (de Breij
      et al., 2018; Huang et al., 2023) that often focused on a single type of bacteria, such as Staphylococ-

217   different types of microorganisms and includes 18 different strains, most of which are DR strains.
218   This not only increases the diversity and representativeness of the data but also allows our method
219   to be evaluated in more complex and realistic environments. Furthermore, previous datasets like
220   SAAP (de Breij et al., 2018), CLaSS (Das et al., 2021), and SearchAMP (Huang et al., 2023),

233   3.2   G ROUND T RUTH A SSEMBLY FOR THE T RAINING S ET
234
235   The Ground Truth (GT) for our training data, namely the MIC values for the six classes of bacteria,
236   was sourced from the DBAASP and PDB databases. Given that longer sequences require biolog-
237   ically more expensive synthesis methods compared to cheaper chemical synthesis, only peptides
238   ranging from 6 to 30 amino acids in length were included. For the DBAASP database, a series
239   of data cleansing algorithms were employed to obtain average MIC values for each class of DR
240
      bacteria, while peptides from the PDB database were designated as negative samples (Wang et al.,
      2024). The peptides consist exclusively of the 20 standard amino acids. Considering the challenge
241
      of obtaining a comprehensive dataset from existing databases, we employed the Multiple Imputation
242
      by Chained Equations (MICE) (van Buuren & Groothuis-Oudshoorn, 2011) to impute missing MIC
243   values. This imputation method demonstrated superior results on the wet-lab dataset compared to
244   the direct imputation of ineffective MIC values.
245
246
      Table 2 presents a comparison of various datasets used for antimicrobial peptide (AMP) identifica-
      tion, emphasizing their contributions in terms of sequence count, species diversity, structural data
247
      availability, and data cleanliness. Notably, our proposed dataset, DRAMPAtlas 1.0, introduced in
248
      this work, comprises 23,673 sequences and includes structures for all entries, totaling 47,346 struc-
249   tures due to multiple conformations per peptide. It also covers six microbial species and has been
250   thoroughly cleaned to ensure high data quality. Compared to other datasets like LAMPv2 (Ye et al.,
251   2020), dbAMP 2.0 (Jhong et al., 2022), and DRAMPv3 (Shi et al., 2022), which either lack struc-
252   tural information or data cleaning, DRAMPAtlas 1.0 provides a more comprehensive and reliable
253   resource for AMP research.
254
      Furthermore, while QLAPD (Wang et al., 2024) also offers cleaned data with structures for 12,914
255   sequences across six species, DRAMPAtlas 1.0 significantly expands on the structural annotations
256   and sequence count. The availability of extensive structural data in our dataset facilitates advanced
257   modeling approaches that leverage three-dimensional information, potentially leading to more accu-
258   rate AMP identification. This comprehensive dataset addresses the limitations of previous resources
259   by providing high-quality, well-annotated data, thus serving as a valuable asset for the research
260   community in developing and benchmarking AMP prediction models.

      3.3   R EPRESENTATION AND L EARNING OF A NTIMICROBIAL P EPTIDES
263
264   In the domain of antimicrobial peptide (AMP) identification, the representation of peptides is a
265   pivotal factor influencing the performance of machine learning models. Different representations
266   encapsulate varying levels of biological and chemical information, affecting the models’ ability to
267   learn discriminative features. In this study, we considered three primary types of representations for
268   AMPs: sequence representations, structural representations, and global descriptor representations.
269   Each representation offers unique insights into the peptides’ properties, and understanding their
      impact on model learning is essential for advancing AMP identification.

      3.3.1     S EQUENCE R EPRESENTATION
271
272   Amino acids in the peptides were encoded using integers ranging from 1 to 20, corresponding to
273   the 20 standard amino acids. Sequences were padded with 0 to handle varying lengths. This integer
274   encoding was often transformed into a one-hot encoding, resulting in a binary vector representation
275   for each amino acid residue. One-hot encoding preserves the categorical nature of amino acids and
276
      allows models to learn from the positional information within sequences.
277   Sequence representations focus on the primary structure of peptides, providing foundational infor-
278   mation for models to identify motifs and patterns associated with AMPs.
279
280   3.3.2     S TRUCTURAL R EPRESENTATION
281
      To incorporate three-dimensional structural information, we employed structural representations in-
282
      volving two key steps: structural prediction and structural encoding.
283
284
      Structural Prediction      We predicted the tertiary structures of peptides using advanced algorithms:
285
286            • AlphaFold (Jumper et al., 2021): A state-of-the-art protein structure prediction tool utiliz-
287              ing multiple sequence alignments and transformer architectures to predict protein folding.
288            • HelixFold (Fang et al., 2023): A BERT-like encoder-decoder architecture that predicts
289              structures directly from sequences without requiring homologous sequences.
290
291   Since many peptides lack sufficient homologous sequences, especially short AMPs, we adapted the
292
      input sequences for AlphaFold by repeating them to meet the algorithm’s requirements. Detailed
      hyperparameters for structure prediction are provided in the Appendix.
293
294
      Structural Encoding Structural representations aim to provide models with comprehensive spa-
295
      tial and physicochemical information, potentially enhancing the identification of structural motifs
296   relevant to antimicrobial activity. We encoded them using:
297
298           1. Graph Neural Networks (GNNs): Each amino acid residue was represented as a node in a
299              graph, with edges representing spatial proximity and sequential relationships. In our graph
300              representation (Gong et al., 2023), each node features a 43-dimensional vector comprising:
301              1. Amino Acid Embedding (20 dimensions): A one-hot encoding of the amino acid type. 2.
302
                 Energy Information (20 dimensions): Energy-related features pertinent to the amino acid.
                 3. 3D Position (3 dimensions): Cartesian coordinates representing the spatial location of the
303
                 amino acid residue. This representation captures both the structural and physicochemical
304
                 properties of the peptides within the graph framework.
305
              2. Voxel Representation: We encoded molecular characteristics into a three-dimensional
306
                 voxel grid (Wang et al., 2024). The voxel representation had dimensions of 64 × 64 × 64
307
                 with four channels: 1. Atom Weight: Encoding the atomic mass of the amino acids. 2.
308              Amino Acid Charge: Representing the electric charge properties. 3. Amino Acid Cate-
309              gory: Categorical classification of amino acids (e.g., hydrophobic, polar). 4. Hydrophilic
310              Information: Indicating the hydrophilicity of amino acids.
311              Voxel representations allow neural networks to process 3D spatial data, capturing intricate
312              structural details of peptides.
313
314   3.3.3     G LOBAL D ESCRIPTOR R EPRESENTATION
315
316
      Global descriptors offer aggregated properties of peptides derived from sequences or structures.
      Each peptide was represented by a 10-dimensional vector encompassing features such as charge,
317
      and hydrophobicity. These descriptors provide holistic insights into the peptides’ biochemical prop-
318
      erties, which can be crucial for distinguishing AMPs from non-AMPs. The detailed list of global
319   descriptors used is available in Appendix Table 2.

      All bacterial strains were initially streaked onto Nutrient Broth (NB) agar medium and incubated
921   overnight at 37°C to ensure proper growth. For antimicrobial activity assessments, Minimum In-
922   hibitory Concentration (MIC) assays of antimicrobial peptides were conducted using Mueller Hin-
923   ton broth in accordance with Clinical and Laboratory Standards Institute (CLSI) guidelines. The
924   bacterial colonies were first suspended in a saline solution, and the turbidity was adjusted to a Mc-
925   Farland standard of 0.5 to achieve a bacterial concentration of 108 CFU/mL, followed by a 100-fold
926   dilution for the inhibition test. Subsequently, 50µL of the bacterial suspension at 1 × 106 CFU/mL
927   were incubated with an equal volume of different concentrations of peptide solutions (serial 2-fold
928   dilutions in Mueller-Hinton Broth from Hopebio). After incubation for 18–20 hours at 37°C, the
929
      MIC was recorded as the lowest concentration at which no obvious bacterial growth was observed.
      Fifty microliters (50µL) of the mixture without observed bacteria were added to Mueller-Hinton
930
      (MH) agar plates. After further incubation for 18 hours at 37°C, the Minimum Bactericidal Concen-
931
      tration (MBC) was recorded as the lowest concentration with no bacterial growth. All experiments
932   were performed in biological triplicates to ensure reproducibility and reliability of the results. The
933   detailed bacterial species used in our studies are shown in Tab. 1.

       B     A PPENDIX OF S TRUCTURE P REDICTION D ETAILS
973
974
       In this study, we employed advanced computational frameworks to predict the three-dimensional
975    structures of antimicrobial peptides (AMPs). Specifically, we utilized ColabFold (Mirdita et al.,
976    2022), which integrates AlphaFold2 (Jumper et al., 2021), and HelixFold (Fang et al., 2023) for
977    structure prediction. Additionally, we refined the predicted structures using Rosetta (Leaver-Fay
978    et al., 2011) to enhance structural accuracy.
979
980    B.1   S TRUCTURE P REDICTION USING C OLAB F OLD AND A LPHA F OLD 2
981
982    We adopted the ColabFold framework, which leverages the AlphaFold2 algorithm for protein struc-
983    ture prediction. ColabFold accelerates the prediction process through optimized implementations
984    and allows for easy execution on cloud-based platforms. The detailed command used for structure
985
       prediction is as follows:
986
       colabfold_batch ./a3ms/ ./results/ --amber --use-gpu-relax
987
                       --num-relax 1 --num-models 3 --model-order 3,4,5
988
989    In this command:
990
991           • ./a3ms/ specifies the directory containing input multiple sequence alignments (MSAs)
992             in A3M format.
993           • ./results/ designates the directory where predicted structures and related outputs are
994             saved.
995
              • --amber enables relaxation using the AMBER force field for improved structural refine-
996             ment.
997
              • --use-gpu-relax allows the relaxation to be performed using GPU acceleration, en-
998
                hancing computational efficiency.
999
1000          • --num-relax 1 sets the number to 1 for how many of the top ranked structures to relax.
1001          • --num-models 3 specifies that three models will be predicted for each input sequence.
1002          • --model-order 3,4,5 indicates that AlphaFold2 models 3, 4, and 5 will be used in
1003            the prediction process.
1004
1005   The use of the AMBER force field and multiple models aims to improve the accuracy of the predicted
1006   structures by providing optimized energy states and accounting for model variability (Jumper et al.,
1007   2021).
1008
1009   B.2   S TRUCTURE P REDICTION USING H ELIX F OLD AND R EFINEMENT WITH ROSETTA
1010
       For additional validation, we employed HelixFold (Fang et al., 2023), a protein structure prediction
1011
       framework designed for high-throughput and high-accuracy modeling. We used HelixFold to predict
1012
       structures for the AMPs, focusing on capturing intricate structural features that might not be fully
1013   resolved by other methods.
1014
1015   Post-prediction, we refined the structures using the Rosetta software suite (Leaver-Fay et al., 2011),
1016
       a widely used tool for protein structure prediction and refinement. Rosetta facilitates energy mini-
       mization and conformational sampling, improving stereochemical properties and reducing potential
1017
       errors in side-chain orientations and backbone conformations. The refinement process involves:
1018
1019         1. Relaxing the protein structure to find the lowest energy conformation.
1020
             2. Fine-tuning side-chain packing to enhance hydrophobic interactions and eliminate steric
1021
                clashes.
1022
1023         3. Optimizing hydrogen-bond networks to stabilize secondary and tertiary structures.
1024
       The integration of HelixFold and Rosetta refinement ensures that the predicted structures are as
1025
       accurate and reliable as possible for downstream analyses.
