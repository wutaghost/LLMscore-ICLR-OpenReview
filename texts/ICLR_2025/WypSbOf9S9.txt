011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Extensive research has shown that deep neural networks (DNNs) are vulnerable
014            to slight adversarial perturbations—small changes to the input data that appear
015
               insignificant but cause the model to produce drastically different outputs. In ad-
               dition to augmenting training data with adversarial examples generated from a
016
               specific attack method, most of the current defense strategies necessitate modi-
017
               fying the original model architecture components to improve robustness or per-
018            forming test-time data purification to handle adversarial attacks. In this work,
019            we demonstrate that strong feature representation learning during training can
020            significantly enhance the original model’s robustness. We propose MOREL, a
021            multi-objective feature representation learning approach, encouraging classifica-
022            tion models to produce similar features for inputs within the same class, despite
023            perturbations. Our training method involves an embedding space where cosine
024            similarity loss and multi-positive contrastive loss are used to align natural and
025            adversarial features from the model encoder and ensure tight clustering. Concur-
026
               rently, the classifier is motivated to achieve accurate predictions. Through ex-
               tensive experiments, we demonstrate that our approach significantly enhances the
027
               robustness of DNNs against white-box and black-box adversarial attacks, outper-
028
               forming other methods that similarly require no architectural changes or test-time
029            data purification.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   The deployment of deep neural networks (DNNs) in critical vision applications such as autonomous
035   driving and medical diagnosis (Bojarski et al., 2016; Miotto et al., 2018) underscores the need for
036   robust models capable of reliably handling real-world scenarios. However, extensive research has
037   demonstrated that DNNs are vulnerable to adversarial examples—inputs crafted by adding imper-
038   ceptible perturbations that can cause the model to make incorrect predictions with high confidence
039
      (Nguyen et al., 2015; Szegedy et al., 2013; Goodfellow et al., 2014). This vulnerability poses sig-
      nificant challenges to the security and reliability of AI systems, especially in safety-critical envi-
040
      ronments. To mitigate the risks posed by adversarial attacks, various defense strategies have been
041
      proposed. A common approach is adversarial training (Madry et al., 2017), where models are trained
042   on adversarial examples generated from a specific attack method. In addition, to improve robustness,
043   most existing defenses require modifications to the original model architecture (Panousis et al., 2021;
044   Liu et al., 2024; Mohammed et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2023), introducing additional complexity and
045   often being architecture-dependent. Some approaches also involve test-time data purification (Meng
046   & Chen, 2017; Song et al., 2017; Cohen & Giryes, 2024; Tang & Zhang, 2024), which increases
047   latency, limiting their practical applicability.
048   In this paper, we propose a novel method named Multi-Objective REpresentation Learning
049   (MOREL) that addresses these challenges by focusing on robust feature representation learning.
050   MOREL encourages the model to produce consistent features for inputs within the same class, de-
051   spite adversarial perturbations. By enhancing the robustness of feature representations, MOREL
052   strengthens the model’s inherent ability to differentiate between classes, making it more resilient to
053   adversarial attacks. The core of our approach is a multi-objective optimization framework that si-
      multaneously optimizes two key objectives: enhancing adversarial robustness and maintaining high


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      classification accuracy. We achieve this by embedding natural and adversarial features into a lower-
055   dimensional space, where cosine similarity and contrastive loss functions are applied to align and
056   tightly cluster these features. The classifier is concurrently motivated to achieve accurate predictions.
057   This approach ensures that the model’s learned representations are robust to adversarial perturbations
058   while preserving the information necessary for accurate classification. The embedding space used
059   during training is discarded, allowing the model to retain its original structure and computational
060   efficiency during inference. This characteristic distinguishes MOREL from many existing defense
061   strategies that either involve architectural changes or rely on additional modules during inference.
062   Through extensive experiments (Sec. 4), we demonstrate that our approach significantly enhances
063
      the robustness of DNN models against white-box and black-box adversarial attacks, outperforming
      existing adversarial training methods that similarly require no architectural changes or test-time data
064
      purification, in terms of the accuracy-robustness trade-off. In summary, our key contributions are:
065
066          • We propose Multi-Objective REpresentation Learning (MOREL), a framework that en-
067            hances the robustness of deep neural networks by aligning natural and adversarial features
068            in a shared embedding space during training while preserving the model’s original structure
069            for practical deployment.
070          • We approach the challenge of improving adversarial robustness and maintaining high ac-
071            curacy as a multi-objective optimization task, effectively balancing these objectives to en-
072            hance the accuracy-robustness trade-off.
073          • We demonstrate through extensive experiments that models trained with MOREL outper-
074            form those trained with existing adversarial training methods, supporting our hypothesis
075            that strong feature representation learning enhances model robustness.

      2.1   A DVERSARIAL T RAINING
080
081   Adversarial training, introduced by Madry et al. (2017), has emerged as one of the most effective
082   defenses against adversarial attacks. The core idea involves augmenting the training data with ad-
083   versarial examples generated using methods like Projected Gradient Descent (PGD). While standard
084   adversarial training has proven effective against known attacks, it often results in models becoming
085   overly specialized to the specific types of adversarial examples used during training (Tsipras et al.,
086
      2018). To address this limitation, several variants of adversarial training have been proposed. Kan-
      nan et al. (2018) introduced Adversarial Logit Pairing (ALP), which enhances robustness by pairing
087
      logits from adversarial and clean examples during training. Building on this, they proposed Clean
088
      Logit Pairing (CLP), which further refines the approach by focusing specifically on randomly se-
089   lected clean training examples. Ding et al. (2018) advanced the field with Max-Margin Adversarial
090   (MMA) training, which pushes decision boundaries further from data points, thereby offering en-
091   hanced robustness. The TRADES method by Zhang et al. (2019) marked a significant leap forward
092   by explicitly balancing the trade-off between robustness and accuracy through a regularized loss
093   function that minimizes the Kullback-Leibler divergence between predictions on natural and adver-
094   sarial examples. This was further refined by MART Wang et al. (2019), which focuses on the robust-
095   ness of misclassified examples, addressing vulnerabilities near the decision boundary. Despite these
096   advancements, common limitations persist, including the challenge of maintaining strong robustness
097
      while achieving high accuracy on clean data. Building on these state-of-the-art adversarial training
      methods, our approach, MOREL, addresses these challenges by strengthening the robustness of
098
      DNNs through a robust feature representation learning technique. By considering a multi-objective
099
      optimization framework, MOREL aims to achieve the best possible trade-offs between robustness
100   and accuracy—an aspect that, to our knowledge, has not been fully explored in previous work.
101
102
      2.2   I NSIGHTS FROM C ONTRASTIVE L EARNING
103
104   To enhance the learning of robust features in the context of adversarial training, our method also
105   draws insights from recent advances in contrastive learning. Contrastive learning has been shown to
106   be effective in producing robust and well-structured feature representations by encouraging similar
107   samples to be closer in the embedding space while pushing dissimilar samples apart (Chen et al.,
      2020; Gidaris et al., 2018; He et al., 2020). Specifically, Khosla et al. (2020) extend the principles of


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      contrastive learning to a supervised setting. This method leverages label information to group similar
109   examples (i.e., those sharing the same class label) closer together in the feature space. This work
110   informs the design of our embedding space in MOREL, where we apply a multi-positive contrastive
111   loss function (Khosla et al., 2020; Tian et al., 2024) to align natural and adversarial features. By
112   doing so, MOREL not only enhances robustness against adversarial attacks but also ensures that
113   the learned features are tightly clustered and well-separated across different classes, improving both
114   robustness and accuracy.
115
116   2.3   D OMAIN A DAPTATION AND C ONTRASTIVE A DVERSARIAL T RAINING
117
118   Domain adaptation seeks to enhance model performance on a target domain by utilizing knowledge
119
      from a related source domain. Approaches such as those by Song et al. (2018) and Bashivan et al.
      (2021) primarily concentrate on aligning output distributions between domains to enhance gener-
120
      alization. In contrast, recent research has highlighted the effectiveness of focusing on the feature
121
      space, leveraging contrastive learning to achieve more robust domain adaptation. Contrastive ad-
122   versarial training, particularly in unsupervised and self-supervised contexts, has gained attention
123   for its ability to learn invariant feature representations resilient to adversarial perturbations. Kim
124   et al. (2020) proposed a self-supervised adversarial contrastive learning framework that enhances
125   robustness without relying on labeled data. Similarly, Chen et al. (2024) introduced a contrastive
126   adversarial training method for unsupervised domain adaptation, demonstrating the value of align-
127   ing feature representations across domains. Our method aligns with these recent advancements by
128   emphasizing robust feature representation learning.

356   conducted using MOREL(← MART) with a ResNet18 model trained on the CIFAR-10 dataset. All
357   experiments are conducted on an NVIDIA A100 80GB GPU.
358
      For testing, we use various attack methods, including FGSM (Goodfellow et al., 2014), PGD-20,
359
      PGD-100, with a step size of ϵ/10, as well as CW∞ attack (Carlini & Wagner, 2017), using the
360
      Python library Adversarial Robustness Toolbox (ART) (Nicolae et al., 2018). For the CW∞ attack,
361   we set the maximum number of iterations to 10, with an initial learning rate of 10−2 . We use a
362   confidence level of 1 and initialize the constant c at 15. The experimental results for AutoAttack
363   (Croce & Hein, 2020) and the query-based black-box attack, SquareAttack (Andriushchenko et al.,
364   2020), are provided in Appendix A. All attack methods are evaluated under the non-targeted setting,
365   with adversarial perturbation strength constrained by the l∞ −norm. We evaluate both the “best” and

461
      We explore the Pareto front by varying the values of the preference vector k for the MOREL frame-
462
      work (MOREL(← MART)) with a ResNet18 model trained on the CIFAR-10 dataset. Figure 2
463   provides a visualization of how the loss terms and performance against PGD-20 evolve as we adjust
464   the values of k1 (the weight assigned to the robustness loss L1 ) and k2 (the weight assigned to the
465   clean accuracy loss L2 ). As the preference shifts from prioritizing robustness (k1 = 0.9) to accu-
466   racy (k1 = 0.1), we observe a clear trade-off between the two objectives (Figure 2a). This behavior
467   clearly illustrates the multi-objective nature of the problem, where optimizing for one objective (ac-
468   curacy or robustness) leads to a trade-off with the other. Figure 2b shows the relationship between
469   robust accuracy and the values of k1 . As k1 decreases towards 0.1, robust accuracy improves, reach-
470   ing its peak at k1 = 0.3 . This emphasizes the importance of appropriately weighting the robustness
471
      loss to improve robustness.
472   In addition, we analyze the impact of varying batch sizes during training and the presence of the
473   Me module (with Lcsl ) in the embedding space on the model’s robust accuracy. Figure 2c illustrates
474   the overall robust accuracy under PGD-20 attacks as a function of batch size, with values plotted
475   for batch sizes of 8, 32, 128, and 512. While larger batch sizes are commonly used in contrastive
476   learning to leverage a diverse set of negative samples, our analysis revealed a different dynamic in
477
      MOREL. As the batch size increases, the model’s robustness declines. This trend can be attributed
      to the differences in training paradigms. In standard contrastive learning (Khosla et al., 2020; Chen
478
      et al., 2020), training typically involves two distinct steps: first, the encoder is trained to cluster
479
      features in the embedding space, and then the classifier is trained on top of the frozen encoder.
480   This separation allows larger batch sizes to enhance feature learning by providing a rich diversity
481   of negative samples, with little interference from downstream classification. In contrast, MOREL
482   considers a simultaneous learning approach, optimizing both feature alignment and classification
483   objectives through multi-objective optimization. As these objectives can sometimes conflict, smaller
484   batch sizes seem to focus the optimization process on a narrower subset of samples, reducing the
485   diversity and complexity of competing gradients in each step. This allows the model to resolve
      conflicts more effectively, maintaining a better balance between the objectives.

      5   C ONCLUSION
503
504
      In this paper, we introduced MOREL, a multi-objective feature representation learning framework
505   aimed at enhancing the adversarial robustness of deep neural networks. MOREL encourages the
506   alignment of natural and adversarial features through the use of cosine similarity and contrastive
507   losses during training, promoting the learning of robust feature representations. Our approach con-
508   sistently outperformed existing methods that similarly require no architectural changes or test-time
509   data purification, such as TRADES and MART, in terms of robustness against a wide range of adver-
510   sarial attacks, while maintaining high clean accuracy. Moreover, the ability of our multi-objective
511   optimization approach to generalize across various datasets and attack types, without requiring ar-
512   chitectural modifications, makes it a practical and scalable solution for real-world applications. For
513
      future work, we plan to investigate the transferability of robust features learned by MOREL across
      different tasks and domains, which could unlock new possibilities for applying adversarially ro-
514
      bust models in areas like transfer learning and domain adaptation. Additionally, we aim to explore
515
      grouping techniques to extend MOREL’s application to scenarios with limited labeled data, such as
516   semi-supervised or few-shot learning settings.
