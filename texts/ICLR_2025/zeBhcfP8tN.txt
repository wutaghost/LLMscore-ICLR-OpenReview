009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Vision-Language Models (VLMs) often generate plausible but incorrect responses
012            to visual queries. However, reliably quantifying the effect of such hallucinations in
013            free-form responses to open-ended queries is challenging as it requires visually ver-
014            ifying each claim within the response. We propose Programmatic VLM Evaluation
015            (PROVE), a new benchmarking paradigm for evaluating VLM responses to open-
016            ended queries. To construct PROVE, we provide a large language model (LLM)
017
               with a high-fidelity scene-graph representation constructed from a hyper-detailed
               image caption, and prompt it to generate diverse question-answer (QA) pairs, as
018
               well as programs that can be executed over the scene graph object to verify each QA
019
               pair. We thus construct a benchmark of 10.5k challenging but visually grounded
020            QA pairs. Next, to evaluate free-form model responses to queries in PROVE, we
021            propose a programmatic evaluation strategy that measures both the helpfulness
022            and truthfulness of a response within a unified scene graph-based framework. We
023            benchmark the helpfulness-truthfulness trade-offs of a range of VLMs on PROVE,
024            finding that very few are in-fact able to achieve a good balance between the two.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
      Vision-language models (VLMs) have emerged as an effective solution for generating responses
029   to queries about visual content. However, despite impressive progress (and much like their LLM-
030   counterparts), VLMs are still known to hallucinate – to generate plausible but incorrect answers that
031   are either inconsistent or unverifiable against the provided visual context 1 . This crucial shortcoming
032   has the potential to erode trust in such systems and has already begun to attract significant research (Yu
033   et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023a; Gunjal et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024) and regulatory (Biden, 2023)
034   interest, particularly as using such models as the “foundation” of various high-stakes applications
035   becomes imminent (Bommasani et al., 2021).
036   This has led to a flurry of research on reliably benchmarking VLM performance Liu et al. (2024a), by
037   measuring not just the helpfulness but also the truthfulness of their responses. Existing benchmarks
038   fall into two categories – discriminative (Hu et al., 2023; Lovenia et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023),
039   which evaluate the model’s responses to close-ended, existence-based queries (“Is there a man in this
040   image?”), and generative (Rohrbach et al., 2018; Sun et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b;a; Gunjal et al.,
041   2024), which evaluate responses to free-form, open-ended questions (“Describe this image.”). While
042   discriminative benchmarks ease evaluation, they do not realistically simulate in-the-wild usage. On
043
      the other hand, generative benchmarks, while realistic, are extremely challenging to reliably evaluate,
      as they require verifying both that the model response fully answers the question (i.e. is helpful) and
044
      does not make any false claims (i.e. is truthful).
045
046   Evaluating such free-form responses typically relies on external models (usually, a proprietary LLM)
047   to score responses given some image context (typically ground-truth annotations). However, we
048   find that in several such benchmarks, the context provided is completely insufficient to judge if the
049
      response contains hallucinations. Consider Fig. 1: an VLM may respond to the query “How many
      puppies are in the image?” (correct answer = “four”), with “There are four labradoodle puppies”.
050

051
      (<count == four> and <breed == labradoodle>); however, an LLM judge provided

      We propose Programmatic VLM Evaluation (PROVE), a new evaluation paradigm that performs
      reliable and interpretable programmatic evaluation of free-form VLM responses to challenging,
082
      diverse, and grounded questions. To build this dataset, we first use hyper-detailed image captions to
083
      construct a high-recall scene graph image representation. We then use an LLM to generate a diverse
084   set of open-ended question-answer (QA) pairs along with accompanying verification programs. While
085   the QA pairs are meant to test a range of model capabilities under real-world use, the verification
086   programs can be executed over a given scene graph object to verify the correctness and groundedness
087   of its corresponding QA pair. We thus only retain the QA pairs that we can programmatically verify
088   and construct a benchmark of 10.5k diverse and challenging examples which are visually grounded
089   by design, that we can use to reliably benchmark VLM responses.
090   Next, we benchmark VLM responses to queries in PROVE by comparing scene graph representations.
091   First, we measure the helpfulness of a response by computing its scene graph-based recall against
092   the ground truth answer. Next, we measure response truthfulness as its scene graph-based precision
093   against both the scene-graph constructed from the full caption or the image itself. We benchmark a
094   range of VLM responses using this approach, and study their respective trade-offs between helpfulness
095   and truthfulness. Our findings suggest that much of the recent progress in training “better” VLMs
096   also translate to improved helpfulness on our benchmark, but often at the cost of reduced truthfulness.

      Most recently, a few benchmarks with truly open-ended queries have been proposed (Sun et al.,
      2023; Liu et al., 2023a; Jing et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b), which either hand-design or use an
142

143
      However, these too have limitations: MMHal (Sun et al., 2023) and HallusionBench (Liu et al.,
144   2023a) rely on a series of off-the-shelf models at various stages which introduce noise (see Fig. 2,
145   col 3). GAVIE’s (Liu et al., 2023b) reliance on dense captions and bounding boxes leads to a
146   majority of questions querying localized image regions and spatial relationships, many of which
147   have unnatural-sounding responses (eg. mentioning image coordinates, see Fig. 2, col 4). Finally,
148   GPT-4-based evaluation is both expensive and inherits the model’s own limitations.
149
      Understanding and mitigating VLM hallucination. Several works have sought to better understand
150   why VLMs hallucinate. One prevalent theory is the model learning spurious correlations between the
151   input and the output: either due to overly strong text priors learned by the LLM backbone (Huang
152   et al., 2024; Leng et al., 2024), or due to distilling synthetic outputs generated by stronger models
153   (such as GPT-4V) that may themselves contain confabulation (Liu et al., 2024b). This is often
154   exacerbated by the predominant training recipe (Liu et al., 2024b; Abdin et al., 2024) that learns a
155   shallow projection from the visual input to the text embedding space which limits the expressivity of
156   the model to learn visually grounded representations.
157   Recent work has proposed training-based and training-free strategies for mitigating hallucinations.
158   The former involves finetuning (Liu et al., 2023b; Yan et al., 2024) or preference optimization (Yu
159   et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2023) of “preferred” ground truth responses against dis-preferred synthet-
160   ically generated “hallucinations”. Training-free methods instead focus on specialized decoding
161   strategies (Huang et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2024; Leng et al., 2024) that seek to correct for potential
      statistical bias that may lead to hallucination.

      3     A PPROACH
197
198   Vision-language models are trained to respond to a question Q about an image I with a ground-truth
199   answer A. Let mθ (.) denote a VLM model trained on a large dataset of such (I, Q, A) triplets.
200   At test time, we wish to evaluate the model response Â=mθ (Q, I). Specifically, while prior work
201   typically evaluates either the response’s correctness (is Â=A) or truthfulness (is p(Â|I) > threshold),
202   we propose a unified framework that jointly evaluates both.
203
204   3.1   G ENERATING VERIFIABLE V ISUAL Q UESTION -A NSWERS
205   To build PROVE , we first download image-caption pairs (I, C) from the test set of the recently
206   proposed DOCCI (Onoe et al., 2024) dataset, containing 5k manually curated images with com-
207   prehensive human-annotated descriptions. DOCCI is particularly well-suited for VLM evaluation
208   because: i) its captions are extremely detailed, with a higher median caption length than competing
209   datasets, which correlates with high image recall ii) its comprehensive and rigorous 3-stage human
210   annotation protocol leads to high-fidelity captions that are suitable to test a range of image under-
211   standing challenges including spatial reasoning, counting, text rendering, and compositionality, and
212
      iii) its images are newly curated and so more likely to be truly held-out for existing models.
213   Building a robust scene-graph representation.              Scene-graphs are comprised of en-
214   tity (<entity>), attribute (<entity, attribute>), and relationship (<entity_1,
215   attribute, entity_2>) tuples that describe a scene. We use the tuples included with the
      DOCCI test set that were automatically extracted from its captions using an LLM (Cho et al., 2024),

216
      and use it to construct a scene graph representation g(C) as a directed graph with attributed entities as
217   nodes and relationships as edges. The scene graph is implemented as a Python class with methods
218   to query the graph for its entities, attributes, and relationships, as well as to extract and describe
219   subgraphs in natural language (full API in Lst. 1).
220
      Generating open-ended questions with verifiable answers. Next, for each image, we prompt a
221
      pre-trained LLM to generate 10-15 challenging, diverse, and unambiguous question-answer (QA)
222
      pairs given a caption and scene graph, along with an accompanying Python program that accepts the
223   scene graph as input and can be executed to verify the generated QA pair (Gupta & Kembhavi, 2023;
224   Surís et al., 2023). We include a few in-context examples of such scene-graph and QA+program
225   input/output pairs in the prompt (see Fig. 9). We repeat this procedure to generate a large dataset of
226   open-ended image+QA pairs {(Ii , Qi , Ai )}N i=1 and their verification programs.
227
      Filtering QA pairs. Next, we perform two rounds of filtering:
228
229   1. Programmatic: First, we execute the generated program with the scene graph as input to verify the
230   QA pair. We discard pairs for which the program either fails or returns an answer that is semantically
231   different (Reimers, 2019) from the ground truth answer.
232   2. Text-based: Next, we perform a few additional post-processing steps to exclude low-quality QA
233   pairs which are i) trivial, ungrammatical, ambiguous, or incomplete (using an LLM, see Fig. 10),
234   ii) not entailed by the image (using a visual entailment model (Wang et al., 2022)), iii) include one
235   or more words from a manually curated list of taboo words that we find to result in low-quality
236   questions, or iv) semantic duplicates for the same image (using SemDeDup (Abbas et al., 2023)).
237   Our final dataset after filtering contains 10.5k high-quality visual question answers – see Fig. 3.
238   Dataset statistics. We now present some statistics about PROVE, which comprises of 10.5k QA
239   pairs generated from 5k image-caption pairs from the DOCCI test set. These are obtained after
240   applying both programmatic filtering i.e. either the unit test fails (18.3%) or returns the wrong answer
241   (9.8%), and text-based filtering (∼ 50% of the total from the previous stage). Note that we opt to
242   filter out such a large percentage of QA pairs in the interest of ensuring high-quality evaluation data.
243   Further, our benchmark curation process is fully automatic and so can be readily scaled to a larger
244
      image-caption source. Questions in PROVE average 10.3 words in length whereas answers average
      13.4 words (see Fig. 6, right). In Fig. 6, left we present a sunburst visualization of the first 4 words in
245
      the questions, highlighting the diversity of questions in our benchmark.

293   4      E XPERIMENTS
294
295   We now present our benchmarking experiments on PROVE. We include a broad set of models spanning
296   a range of sizes and learning strategies and extensively analyze their performance, including their
297   performance trade-offs. We also conduct a human study to validate both the quality of our benchmark
298   and how well our proposed metrics correlate with human judgement.
299
300   4.1     S ETUP
301
302   Baselines. We include VLMs of three sizes – small (<5B parameters), medium (5-10B parameters),
303   and large (>10B parameters) – and include both open-source and proprietary models. We also include
304   two additional LLM-based methods – a Blind baseline (is not provided the image), and an “oracle”
305
      model as an upper bound (a blind model that is provided with the ground truth caption image). Both
      LLM-based methods use a LLaMA-3.1-8B backbone (Dubey et al., 2024) with in-context prompting.
306
307   Data. PROVE is constructed from images, tuples, and captions released under a CC by 4.0 license as
308   the test split of the DOCCI (Onoe et al., 2024) dataset. DOCCI images were reviewed both by human
309   and automatic methods to remove or obfuscate PII (faces, phone numbers, and URLs) and unsafe
310   content. Images underwent a rigorous 3-stage human annotation phase resulting in hyper-detailed
311
      and high-recall captions averaging 136 words.
312   Implementation details. We use GPT-4o (Achiam et al., 2023) for generating structured question,
313   answers, and verification programs using the batch API and prompting it with a detailed task
314   description, examples, and a Python definition of the SceneGraph class. We also use GPT-4o for
315   the first round of text-based post-processing described in Sec. 3.1. We use OFA (Wang et al., 2022)
316   fine-tuned for visual entailment for both post-processing and measuring image-tuple entailment
317
      (Eq. 2), and Sentence-Bert (Reimers, 2019) to extract text embeddings.

      5        D ISCUSSION
408
409   Our work takes a step towards reliably evaluating the helpfulness-truthfulness trade-offs of vision-
410   language models. Our design leverages an LLM prompted with a robust scene graph representation
411   and API to construct “in-the-wild” visual question answer pairs that are grounded by design. Further,
412
      these QA pairs lend themselves to programmatic evaluation via comparing scene-graph representa-
      tions. The reliability of our benchmark comes from three factors: i) high-recall human-annotations
413
      image captions that seed the scene graphs, which make it possible to (almost) exhaustively validate
414
      the veracity of any claim ii) programmatic verification of the generated QA pairs that ensure that both
415   the question and answer are indeed grounded in the visual input, and iii) evaluation metrics that are
416   both holistic (i.e. consider all the provided context) and interpretable (i.e. provide a concrete scoring
417   rubric based on scene graph-based matching). We will fully open-source our benchmark code and
418   data for research use.
419
      Limitations. While we hope that PROVE will serve as a useful test-bed for reliable VLM evaluation
420
      and spur future research on the topic, it is not without limitations. While we try to ensure high
421   precision in QA pairs retained in our benchmark (via programmatic verification), this naturally
422   comes at some cost to recall (i.e. some hard-to-verify question types may be excluded from the
423   benchmark). Next, even high-recall image captions may not capture every aspect of an image, and so
424   our evaluation may not be able to catch all model hallucinations. Further, our evaluation relies on
425   off-the-shelf models for computing text-embeddings, scene graph tuples, and image-text entailment,
426   and so almost certainly inherits some of their own limitations. Finally, we hope future work will study
427   the effectiveness of recent fine-tuning (Liu et al., 2023b), preference-tuning (Yu et al., 2024; Sun
428   et al., 2023), and training-free (Huang et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2024; Leng et al., 2024) hallucination
429
      mitigation strategies on PROVE, as well as agentic models that can plan (Surís et al., 2023; Gupta
      & Kembhavi, 2023), reason, and self-reflect (Valmeekam et al., 2024), towards the elusive goal of
430
      achieving Pareto improvements in both helpfulness and truthfulness.

769   Consider the SceneGraph class defined below, which takes as input an image caption and a dictionary
770   of tuples (entities, attributes, and relations) parsed from the image caption, and builds a directed
771   graph representation with attributed entities as nodes and relations as edges.
772
773   You will be provided with such an image caption, tuple dictionary, and corresponding SceneGraph
774   object. Your task is to generate a set of:
775         1. Free-form question-answer pairs that test non-trivial image understanding and reasoning
776            capabilities.
777         2. A Python function that receives as input the SceneGraph object and can be executed to
778            answer the query by reasoning over the scene graph.
779
      Guidelines. The generated questions should be:
780
781          • Clear and conversational, in the tone of a person who is asking another person about the
782
               scene. You may paraphrase where appropriate to improve clarity (eg. “Can you describe the
               dog?” is better than “What is the state of the dog?”). Note that the tuples in the scene graph
783
               are generally accurate but not necessarily precise, and so may require rephrasing to generate
784
               meaningful questions from.
785
786
             • Diverse, both in question type (e.g. starting with “is”, “where”, “what”, “when”, “how”,
               “which”, “why”, etc.) and length.
787
788          • Non-trivial (eg. avoid “What color are the green trees?”) and unambiguous (eg. avoid
789            “What is the color of the puppy?” for an image with multiple puppies).
790   The generated Python functions should be:
791          • Executable: The code should run without requiring modifications.
792
             • General: The code should generalize to similar scene graphs as the one provided. Do NOT
793
               hard-code specific attributes or relations.
794
795
      For each image, generate 10-15 such question-answer pairs and corresponding Python functions.

      You will be provided with a list of question-answer pairs about an image. Your task is to identify
814
      whether each pair has any of the following issues:
815
816         1. Trivial question. A trivial question can be answered directly from information provided in
817
               the question or using common-sense, without requiring looking at the image. Examples:
818            Question: What is the material of the stadium’s horizontal concrete bar?
819
               Answer: The stadium’s horizontal concrete bar is made of concrete.
               Judgement: Trivial (The question already mentions that the bar is made of concrete)
820
821
               Question: What text rendering is found on the stop sign?
822
               Answer: The stop sign has white text rendering of the word "STOP".
823
               Judgement: Trivial (Stop signs almost always have the word "STOP" on them)
824
825
               Question: What feature of the scene reflects sunlight?
826            Answer: The hard surfaces reflect sunlight.
827            Judgement: Trivial (Hard surfaces are known to reflect sunlight)
828
829
            2. Incomplete answer. An incomplete answer does not completely answer the question. It
830            may be missing key details or may not provide a full description, or may also be entirely
831            irrelevant. Examples:
832            Question: What is between the red neon light and the frame?
833            Answer: The red neon light is behind the metal construction frame.
834            Judgement: Incomplete (does not answer the question)
835
836            Question: How would you describe the trees surrounding the green lake?
837            Answer: The trees surrounding the green lake are large in size.
838            Judgement: Incomplete (“large” is not a sufficiently detailed description)
839
840            Question: In which part of the image is there no visible cloud coverage?
841            Answer: The rest of the image has the clear blue sky with no visible cloud coverage.
842            Judgement: Incomplete (“the rest of the image” is meaningless without context)
843
844         3. Unnatural-sounding. The question-answer pair may sound awkward, ambiguous, or
845            unnatural. This could be due to its phrasing, structure, or grammar. Examples:
846            Question: Can you describe the role of the stones in relation to the anemones?
847            Answer: The stones are covered with anemones and line the bottom of the tank and go up
848            its left side.
849            Judgement: Unnatural (“role” of stones is odd phrasing and the overall question is
850
               ambiguous)
851
852
               Question: What is the overall shape of the section of grass?
               Answer: The section of grass is small in shape.
853
               Judgement: Unnatural (“small” is not a shape)
854
855
               Question: What kind of state is the sign experiencing due to the brightness?
856
               Answer: Due to the brightness, the sign is experiencing a duller state.
857            Judgement: Unnatural (“experiencing a state” is awkward phrasing)
