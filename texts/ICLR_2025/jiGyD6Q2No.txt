009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               We address the problem of offline learning a policy that avoids undesirable demon-
012            strations. Unlike conventional offline imitation learning approaches that aim to
013            imitate expert or near-optimal demonstrations, our setting involves avoiding un-
014            desirable behavior (specified using undesirable demonstrations). To tackle this
015            problem, unlike standard imitation learning where the aim is to minimize the dis-
016            tance between learning policy and expert demonstrations, we formulate the learning
017            task as maximizing a statistical distance, in the space of state-action stationary
018            distributions, between the learning policy and the undesirable policy. This sig-
019            nificantly different approach results in a novel training objective that necessitates
020
               a new algorithm to address it. Our algorithm, UNIQ, tackles these challenges
               by building on the inverse Q-learning framework, framing the learning problem
021
               as a cooperative (non-adversarial) task. We then demonstrate how to efficiently
022
               leverage unlabeled data for practical training. Our method is evaluated on stan-
023            dard benchmark environments, where it consistently outperforms state-of-the-art
024            baselines.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Reinforcement learning (RL) is a powerful framework for learning to maximize expected returns
031   and has achieved remarkable success across various domains. However, applying reinforcement
032   learning to real-world problems is challenging due to difficulties in designing reward functions and
033
      the requirement for extensive online interactions with the environment. While some approaches have
      addressed these challenges, they often rely on costly datasets, requiring either accurate labeling or
034
      clean, consistent data, which is often impractical. Imitation learning (Abbeel & Ng, 2004; Ziebart
035
      et al., 2008; Kelly et al., 2019) offers a more feasible alternative, enabling agents to learn directly
036   from expert demonstrations without the need for explicit reward signals. It has proven effective in
037   several tasks, even with limited expert data, and is particularly useful in capturing human preferences.
038
039
      Most existing imitation learning approaches prioritize maximizing task performance (i.e., expected
      return) by closely mimicking expert demonstrations (Ho & Ermon, 2016; Fu et al., 2018; Kostrikov
040
      et al., 2019; Garg et al., 2021). However, in practice, expert or near-expert demonstrations may
041
      be unavailable or insufficient. In many scenarios, instead of high-quality examples, there may be
042   collections of undesirable (or suboptimal) demonstrations that should be avoided. For example,
043   in a large dataset of user conversations used to train a chatbot, the system must learn to avoid
044   inappropriate or sensitive content that is present in the data (Duan et al., 2024). Similarly, in the
045   development of self-driving cars, while companies may collect user driving data to train their models,
046   the system must ensure it does not replicate faulty behavior, such as traffic violations or unsafe driving
047   practices (Bansal et al., 2018). In the field of treatment optimization, data may include actions that led
048   to bad patient outcomes and the system must learn to avoid such behaviors. In these cases, avoiding
049   undesirable behaviors is essential to training a safe and effective model. These types of undesirable
050
      demonstrations are common in real-world applications and are crucial for shaping the desired policy.
051   Although this is an important and interesting problem setting, there has been limited research
052   addressing it effectively. Here, we formulate this challenge as an Offline Reverse Imitation Learning
053   problem: given a dataset containing undesired demonstrations we wish to avoid, alongside a much
      larger unlabelled dataset consisting of both desired and undesired demonstrations, the goal is to learn

070   To the best of our knowledge, only SafeDICE (Jang et al., 2024) directly addresses this setting

072   then mimicking the mixed policy. This approach, however, may suffer from the fact that the mixed
073   policy is not necessarily a desirable one to follow, which is often the case in practice. There are
074   existing methods that, while not specifically designed to address this problem, can be adapted to

076   & Sadigh, 2024) can be modified by creating pairwise comparisons between the undesirable and

      approach is indirect and may not be effective. Similar to SafeDICE, this approach will also suffer
078
      as the unlabeled dataset may contain undesirable demonstrations that are not necessarily preferable

      to those in the undesirable dataset. Another approach, Discriminator-Weighted Behavioral Cloning
080   (DWBC) (Xu et al., 2022), can be adapted to train a discriminator to distinguish between desirable
081   and undesirable demonstrations, allowing the agent to avoid learning from undesirable samples.
082   Our experiments later will show that our method significantly outperforms the above baselines on
083   benchmark problems.
084
      In this paper, we develop a principled framework for learning from undesirable demonstrations,
085
      based on the well-known MaxEnt RL framework (Ziebart et al., 2008) and inverse Q-learning (Garg
086   et al., 2021) — a state-of-the-art imitation learning method. In our work, unlike traditional imitation
087   learning approaches where the main goal is to minimize the distance between the learning policy and
088   expert (or near-optimal) demonstrations, we instead aim to maximize a statistical distance between
089   the learning policy and the undesirable policy (represented by undesirable demonstrations) in the
090   state-action stationary distribution space. To address this novel learning objective, we adopt the
091   inverse Q-learning framework. In our context, since this objective requires optimization in the
092   opposite direction as compared to the standard IQ-learn method (Garg et al., 2021), it demands
093   significantly novel investigations. Specifically, our contributions can be summarized as follows:
094
               • We demonstrate that maximizing the statistical distance between the learning policy and the
095
                 undesirable policy can be formulated as a cooperative training task, in contrast to previous
096              imitation learning approaches where the objective is adversarial Ho & Ermon (2016); Fu
097              et al. (2018); Kostrikov et al. (2019). This new cooperative training objective poses new
098              challenges that existing algorithms cannot readily solve. To tackle this, we show that certain
099              beneficial characteristics of the standard IQ-learn algorithm can be brought over in our
100              context – the training problem can be equivalently reformulated as a minimization problem
101              over the Q-space, where the optimal policy is recovered as a soft-max of the Q-function.
102            • To efficiently solve the training problem in the Q-space using limited undesirable demon-
103              strations, we introduce an occupancy correction to recast the training objective so that
104              the expectation over the undesirable policy can be empirically approximated by unlabeled
105              trajectories. This approach offers three main advantages: (i) the unlabeled data contains
106              many more samples than the undesired data set, so using unlabeled demonstrations would
107              greatly improve the accuracy of the empirical approximations, (ii) the training outcome is
                 theoretically independent of the quality of the unknown random policy (represented by the

               unlabeled data), and (iii) it does not require an additional hyper-parameter since we do not
109            combine the two datasets (undesired and unlabeled) as in most prior approaches. Moreover,
110            we show that the occupancy correction can be estimated by solving a convex optimization
111            problem using samples from both undesirable and unlabeled data.
112
             • For policy extraction, we employ a weighted behavior cloning (WBC) formulation. This
113
               WBC approach theoretically ensures to recover the optimal policy derived from the Q-
114
               learning process while providing more stable offline training outcomes, compared to directly
115            extracting the policy from the Q-function. While our algorithm, UNIQ, tackles a significantly
116            different and more challenging setting compared to the original IQ-learn, it only requires a
117            minimal adaptation from the IQ-learn algorithm, with two additional steps — computing
118            the occupancy correction and extracting the policy via WBC — which can be carried out
119            quickly and efficiently.
120
             • We evaluate our method on the Safety-Gym and Mujoco-velocity benchmarks (Ray et al.,
121            2019; Ji et al., 2023), two of the most challenging environments in constrained reinforce-
122            ment learning, and demonstrate superior performance compared to several state-of-the-art
123            baselines.

126   2    R ELATED W ORK
127
128   Imitation learning. Imitation learning is a key technique for learning from demonstrations. Behav-
129   ioral Cloning (BC) maximizes the likelihood of expert demonstrations but often struggles due to
130
      distributional shift (Ross et al., 2011). To improve, Generative Adversarial Imitation Learning (Ho &
      Ermon, 2016; Fu et al., 2018) aligns the learner’s policy with the expert’s using GANs (Goodfellow
131
      et al., 2014), while SQIL (Reddy et al., 2019) assigns simple rewards to expert and non-expert
132
      demonstrations to learn a value function. PWIL (Dadashi et al., 2021) uses the Wasserstein dis-
133   tance (Vaserstein, 1969) to compute rewards. While these methods show promise, they rely on online
134   interaction, which can be impractical. For offline learning, AlgaeDICE (Nachum et al., 2019) and
135   ValueDICE (Kostrikov et al., 2020) use Stationary Distribution Correction Estimation (DICE) but face
136   stability issues. Inspired by ValueDICE, O-NAIL (Arenz & Neumann, 2020) introduced an offline
137   method without adversarial training. IQ-learn (Garg et al., 2021), a popular approach, supports both
138   online and offline learning and offers a state-of-the-art framework with several variants developed
139   based on it (Al-Hafez et al., 2023; Hoang et al., 2024b). Unlike the above mentioned works, our
140   focus in this paper is on offline reverse imitation learning that aims to avoid undesirable trajectories,
141
      as opposed to imitating expert trajectories. As indicated earlier, this requires fundamentally different
      methods due to the nature of the problem.
142
143   Imitation Learning from Sub-optimal Demonstrations. There are two main research directions in
144   this area. The first focuses on online and offline preference-based imitation learning methods. Online
145   approaches, such as T-REX (Brown et al., 2019), PrefPPO (Lee et al., 2021a), and PEBBLE (Lee
146   et al., 2021b), leverage ranked sub-optimal demonstrations to learn a preference-based reward
147
      function using the Bradley-Terry model (Bradley & Terry, 1952). While these methods achieve
      strong performance, they rely on interaction with the environment. In contrast, offline methods,
148
      such as those proposed by (Kim et al., 2023; Kang et al., 2023; Hejna & Sadigh, 2024), rely heavily
149
      on extensive pairwise trajectory comparisons. SPRINQL (Hoang et al., 2024b) addressed this
150   limitation by utilizing demonstrations categorized into different levels of expertise, resulting in
151   better performance with fewer comparison demands. While these methods concentrate on imitating
152   preferred (or expert) trajectories, our focus is on avoiding non-preferred (or undesired) trajectories.
153   This important distinction necessitates significant changes in methodology.
154
      The second direction focuses the use of additional unlabeled datasets to enhance learning from
155
      expert data. Beginning with DemoDICE (Kim et al., 2021), several DICE-based methods (Ma
156   et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2023) have been developed to utilize small sets of expert
157   demonstrations, supplemented by larger unlabeled datasets. In addition to these DICE-based methods,
158   DWBC (Xu et al., 2022) propose a simple and efficient method based on training a classifier using
159   positive-unlabeled learning (Kiryo et al., 2017). SafeDICE (Jang et al., 2024) presents a DICE-
160   based framework capable of learning from undesirable demonstrations. This method combines an
161   undesirable policy (represented by an undesirable dataset) with a random policy (represented by
      a larger unlabeled dataset), assigns negative weights to the undesirable policy, and then applies a


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      standard DICE-based approach (Kim et al., 2021; 2022) to mimic the combined policy. In our paper,
163   we advance this line of work by developing an new inverse Q-learning algorithm (Garg et al., 2021)
164   where the primary goal is to maximize the statistical distance between the learning policy and the
165   undesirable policy. Our method offers several advantages over prior approaches, including minimal
166   hyper-parameter tuning and reduced sensitivity to the quality of the unlabeled data.

      6     E XPERIMENTS
379
380
      We assess our algorithm in the context of safe RL (or constrained RL) problems, where each state-
381   action pair is associated with a cost value, and the objective is to learn a policy that satisfies certain
382   cost constraints. We define an undesirable trajectory as one where the accumulated cost exceeds a
383   specified threshold. This setting is similar to the one used in SafeDICE (Jang et al., 2024), one of the
384   SOTA algorithms in the context of learning from undesirable demonstrations.
385
386   6.1    E XPERIMENT SETUP
387
388   Baselines. We compare our algorithm against several baseline methods. First, we benchmark UNIQ
389   against standard imitation learning algorithms that utilize the entire unlabelled demonstration dataset,
390
      specifically BC and IQ-learn, which we refer to as BC-mix and IQ-mix, respectively. Additionally,
      we benchmark our approach against the SOTA preference-based reinforcement learning algorithm,
391
      IPL (Hejna & Sadigh, 2024). Lastly, we include comparisons with DWBC (Xu et al., 2022) and
392
      SafeDICE (Jang et al., 2024), both of which are recognized for their ability to learn from undesired
393   demonstrations. More details are provided in the Appendix B.3.
394
395   Environments and Data Generation. We evaluate our method in four Safety-Gym and two Mujoco
396
      environments (Ray et al., 2019; Ji et al., 2023). Following the setup from (Jang et al., 2024),
      the undesired policy1 is trained using unconstrained PPO, while the safe policy is trained with
397
      SIM (Hoang et al., 2024a). The unlabeled dataset is constructed by combining trajectories from both
398
      safe and undesired policies in a specified ratio. The undesired data consists of trajectories that violate
399   the constraint. Detailed information about the policies and datasets is provided in the Appendix B.2.
400
401   Metrics. The main goal is to train a policy that is safe in the context of constrained RL. Therefore,
402
      an ideal outcome is achieving the lowest possible cost without significantly sacrificing return.
      We report the accumulated return and cost for the trained policies, computed based on the last 20
403
      evaluations, with all results summarized across at least 5 training seeds.
404
405   Experimental Concerns. Throughout the experiments, we aim to address several key questions:
406   (Q1) How does UNIQ perform compared to other baseline methods? (Q2) How does the presence of
407   undesired demonstrations impact the performance of UNIQ and other baselines? (Q3) What happens
408
      if the policy is directly extracted from the Q-function, rather than by solving the WBC? (Q4) How
      does the policy learned by UNIQ compare to a policy trained purely from expert demonstrations?
409
      (Q5) How do UNIQ and other baselines perform when being trained without access to the unlabeled
410
      dataset? While (Q1) and (Q2) are addressed in the main paper, the experiments for the remaining
411   questions are provided in the appendix. The appendix also includes proofs of the theoretical claims
412   made in the main paper, additional details about our experimental setup, and further results such as
413   CVaR cost comparisons, the complete set of experiments for the MuJoCo tasks, comparisons using
414   datasets from the SafeDICE paper, and detailed learning curves.

      7             C ONCLUSION , F UTURE W ORK , AND B ROADER I MPACTS
518
      Conclusion. We have developed UNIQ, a principled framework based on inverse Q-learning to
519
      facilitate learning from undesirable demonstrations. Our algorithm can be seen as a reverse version
520
      of standard imitation learning frameworks, where the goal is to maximize the statistical distance
521   between the learning policy and the undesirable policy. UNIQ requires minimal hyper-parameter
522   tuning, as it does not introduce any additional hyperparameters beyond those typically used in inverse
523   Q-learning algorithms. Moreover, it demonstrates superior performance in producing safe policies in
524   several safe reinforcement learning experiments, outperforming other baseline methods.
525   Limitations and Future work. There are several aspects that have not been addressed in this paper,
526   as they are too significant to be fully explored here. For instance, we generally assume the presence
527   of only one set of undesirable demonstrations, whereas in practice, multiple datasets of varying
528   quality could be leveraged to enhance the training. Additionally, each undesirable trajectory may
529   not be undesirable in its entirety, as it could contain some good actions. Extracting the good parts
530
      from undesirable demonstrations could improve sample efficiency but introduces new challenges that
      warrant further investigation. Another open question is how to extend the framework to multi-agent
531
      settings, which would be both relevant and interesting to explore in future research.
532
       Broader Impact. Beyond the standard impacts of imitation learning, our work is particularly useful
533
      in scenarios where undesirable demonstrations are richer, clearer, or more reliable than desirable
534   ones. This is especially valuable in critical applications like healthcare and autonomous driving,
535   where avoiding harmful actions is essential. However, our approach also carries potential negative
536   impacts. If not carefully designed, the system might unintentionally reinforce undesirable behaviors
537   or learn harmful actions from poorly curated data. Misinterpreting bad demonstrations could result
538   in unintended consequences, particularly in safety-critical contexts. Additionally, there is a risk
539   that malicious actors could misuse this framework to deliberately train AI systems with harmful or
      unethical behaviors.

      E THICAL S TATEMENT
541
542   Our work on the UNIQ framework addresses learning from undesirable demonstrations. This research
543   holds potential for impactful real-world applications, including areas such as autonomous systems,
544   healthcare, and robotics, where avoiding harmful behaviors is crucial. However, it is important to
545   acknowledge the ethical implications and risks associated with our work.
546
      While the UNIQ framework aims to learn safe policies by avoiding undesirable behaviors, there is a
547
      potential risk that the algorithm could be misused in ways that reinforce unintended or harmful actions
548   if trained on poorly curated data. For example, in scenarios where undesirable demonstrations are not
549   clearly defined, the model could inadvertently reinforce biased or harmful behaviors. Furthermore,
550   there is the risk of deploying the framework in environments where safety-critical decisions are made
551   without sufficient validation or human oversight, leading to unintended consequences.
552
      To mitigate these risks, we emphasize the importance of using well-curated datasets that accurately
553
      represent undesirable behaviors and conducting thorough testing in controlled environments before
554   applying the system to real-world scenarios. Moreover, we encourage transparency and collaboration
555   with domain experts to ensure the framework is used responsibly, particularly in safety-critical
556   applications such as healthcare and autonomous driving. Finally, we advocate for the inclusion of
557   human oversight in the deployment of policies learned using the UNIQ framework to ensure ethical
558   and safe outcomes.

      two types of agents: Point (an easy agent) and Car (a more challenging agent), as well as two types

       B.2     DATASET GENERATION DETAILS
986
987    In this paper, we tackle the problem of Offline Reverse Imitation Learning by utilizing two datasets:
988
989
               • Unlabelled dataset DM IX : A large dataset comprising both desired and undesired demon-
990              strations, reflecting real-world data (e.g., chat conversations, driving behaviors, treatment
991              decisions, etc.).
992
993            • Undesired dataset DU N : A smaller, accurated dataset containing demonstrations that exhibit
994              behaviors we aim to avoid.
995
996    We simulate this scenario using the Safety-gym and Mujoco-velocity environments. First, we train
997    both unconstrained and constrained policies using PPO and SIM (Hoang et al., 2024a) (SIM is an
998    incremental method that achieves significantly higher returns while incurring lower costs compared
999    to other safe methods). We then collect the training datasets as follows:
1000
1001           • Unlabelled dataset DM IX : We roll out the constrained and unconstrained policies, mixing
1002             them at ratios of (1 : 1, 1 : 4, 1 : 16) to progressively increase task difficulty.
1003
               • Undesired dataset DU N : We roll out the unconstrained policy, gathering trajectories that
1004
                 violate the constraint.

       The main paper demonstrates that the policy returned by UNIQ achieves significantly lower costs
1370
       (indicating safety) but, in some cases, also lower rewards compared to other imitation learning

1373
1374
       In this section, we show that the conservativeness of UNIQ can be effectively controlled by introducing
       a parameter to the Weighted BC formulation. Specifically, we adjust the conservativeness of the
1375
       algorithm by adding a parameter α to the Weighted BC update:
1376
1377                                                  X
1378                                                              exp(α(Qw (s, a) − V Qw (s))) log πθ (a|s)
1379                                             (s,a)∼D Mix
1380
       When α = 1, the Weighted BC theoretically returns the exact policy derived from Q-learning, as
1381
       reported in the main paper. In contrast:
1382
1383                 • As α → 0, the Weighted BC returns a random policy.
1384
                     • As α → ∞, the resulting policy becomes deterministic, always selecting the best action
1385
                       with probability 1.
1386
1387   Thus, by varying α, we can deviate the outcome of the Weighted BC from the policy given by
1388   Q-learning, reducing the conservativeness of the learned policy.
1389
       To experimentally demonstrate this, we vary α and report the corresponding returns and costs on four
1390
       MuJoCo environments. The results are presented in Figure 8 and Table 7, showing how different
1391   values of α impact the trade-off between safety and performance.
1392
1393   Figure 8 demonstrates that UNIQ achieves its safest (and most conservative) performance when
1394
       α = 1. At this value, the policy prioritizes minimizing costs, making it the most risk-averse option.
       However, as α deviates from 1, both the cost and return increase. This indicates that the Weighted
1395
       BC formulation produces less conservative policies that are less safe but capable of achieving higher
1396
       rewards.
1397
1398   Table 7 provides a more detailed breakdown of the costs and returns for different values of α. The
1399   results show that UNIQ can effectively balance safety and performance: by adjusting α, it is possible
1400
       to achieve a safer policy (i.e., lower cost) while maintaining competitive returns (compared to other
       baselines). This adaptability highlights the flexibility of UNIQ.
1401
1402   When safety is critical, setting α = 1 ensures the most conservative policy, aligning with the objective
1403   of avoiding unsafe demonstrations. On the other hand, by varying α, one can tune the trade-off to
       achieve policies that are less safe but yield higher rewards, making UNIQ suitable for a range of

1447   C.7        D ISCUSSION ON DSRL
1448
1449   The DSRL dataset (Liu et al., 2024) is a public dataset specifically designed for safe reinforcement
1450   learning (RL), generated to encompass every possible pair of return-cost values. This comprehensive
1451   coverage helps agents learn to interpret and balance reward and cost signals effectively.
1452   The dataset is constructed using trajectories generated by a mix of safe and unsafe policies, making it
1453   particularly challenging for imitation learning methods, including standard approaches and our UNIQ
1454   framework, to achieve good performance. The primary challenge lies in the lack of access to reward
1455   and cost signals in imitation learning. When expert demonstrations are derived from a variety of
1456   policies, current imitation learning methods can only recover a mixture of these policies, rather than
1457   isolating the most desirable behaviors. Moreover, since imitation learning does not allow interaction
       with the environment, the absence of feedback further complicates the learning process. This is in


                                                                      27
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1458
       stark contrast to standard offline RL methods, where reward and cost signals are directly accessible,
1459   providing a significant advantage.
1460
1461   To illustrate this difficulty, we used Behavior Cloning (BC), a standard yet effective imitation learning
1462
       method when sufficient data is available, to learn from a set of expert trajectories in the DSRL dataset.
       However, BC failed to recover a good policy—the policy it returned deviates significantly from the
1463
       desirable regions in the return-cost space, as shown in Figure 10. This result highlights the inherent
1464
       challenges in applying imitation learning methods to DSRL.
1465
1466   We believe that the DSRL dataset is explicitly designed for offline constrained RL and is currently
1467   too challenging for existing imitation learning approaches. To our knowledge, no imitation learning
1468
       methods have been successfully tested on DSRL, and our observations confirm the significant
       difficulty of applying offline imitation learning in the absence of reward and cost information.
1469
       These findings reveal a critical limitation of current imitation learning algorithms, including UNIQ,
1470
