009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Molecular conformer generation is a critical task in computational chemistry and
012            drug discovery. Diverse generative deep learning methods have been proposed
013            and shown to outperform traditional cheminformatics tools. State-of-the-art mod-
014            els leverage neural transport, employing denoising diffusion or flow-matching to
015            generate or refine atomic point clouds from a prior distribution. Still, sampling
016            with existing models requires significant computational expense. In this work, we
017
               build upon flow-matching and propose two mechanisms for accelerating training
               and inference of 3D molecular conformer generation. For fast training, we intro-
018
               duce the SO(3)-Averaged Flow, which we show to converge faster and generate
019
               better conformer ensembles compared to conditional optimal transport and Kabsch
020            alignment-based optimal transport flow. For fast inference, we further show that
021            reflow methods and distillation of these models enable few-steps or even one-step
022            molecular conformer generation with high quality. Using these two techniques, we
023            demonstrate a model that can match the performance of strong transformer base-
024            lines with only a fraction of the number of parameters and generation steps. The
025            training techniques proposed in this work shows the path towards highly efficient
026            molecular conformer generation with flow-based models.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
      Molecular conformer generation is the task to predict the ensemble of 3D conformations of molecules
031   given the 2D molecular graphs (Hawkins, 2017). Generating high quality molecular conformers that
032   fit their natural 3D structures is a crucial task for computational chemistry because many physical
033   and chemical properties (Guimarães et al., 2012; Schwab, 2010; Shim & MacKerell Jr, 2011) are
034   determined by the conformers. In the domain of drug discovery, molecular conformer generation
035   is a prerequisite for both structure-based and ligand-based compound virtual screening applications
036   such as molecular docking (Trott & Olson, 2010) and shape similarity search (Rush et al., 2005).
037   For established computational chemistry molecular conformer generation tools, there is a trade-off
038   between generation speed and the quality/diversity of generated conformers (Axelrod & Gomez-
039
      Bombarelli, 2022). For example, enhanced molecular dynamics simulation (Grimme, 2019) can
      generate diverse conformer by sampling the conformation space rather exhaustively, but is slow
040
      due to multiple energy function evaluations. RDKit (Landrum, 2016) and some rule-based tools
041
      (Hawkins et al., 2010) are faster but may miss many low-energy conformer and the generation quality
042   can deteriorate when molecule size grows. Therefore, deep learning models are being sought as a
043   potential solution to overcome such trade-off and bring fast, diverse, and high-quality molecular
044   conformer generation.
045
      Many earlier works are based on generative models (Simm & Hernández-Lobato, 2019; Zhu et al.,
046
      2022; Luo et al., 2021; Shi et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2022) given the stochastic nature of the molecular
047
      conformer generation task. There is also regression model such as GeoMol (Ganea et al., 2021)
048   that operates on the substructures of the molecules. However, established cheminformatics tools
049   such as OMEGA (Hawkins et al., 2010) still has better generation quality with faster sampling
050   speed compared with early deep-learning based methods. Torsional diffusion (Jing et al., 2022) is
051   the first diffusion model that achieves better generation quality than cheminformatics model. By
052   restricting the degree-of-freedom on the torsion angles, torsional diffusion can generate diverse
053   conformers with lightweight model and less number of reverse diffusion steps. Molecular conformer
      field (MCF) (Wang et al., 2024) is a more recent work that does diffusion directly on the Cartesian

072   coordinates of the atoms. With highly scalable transformer architecture, MCF achieves the state-of-
073   the-art conformer generation quality at the cost of tens to hundreds of millions parameters in model
074   size. A more recent work, ET-Flow (Hassan et al., 2024), is also shown to have strong performance

076   noise and target distribution. With the maturing of diffusion and flow-matching models in the field of
077   molecular conformer generation, the major obstacle that hinders the wide adoption of those models
078   in real-world drug discovery industry is the sampling speed. Iterative ordinary differential equation
079   (ODE) or stochastic differential equation (SDE) solving with large transformer model to generate
080
      every conformer can still be computationally infeasible when the library to be virtually screened
      contains billions of compounds (Bellmann et al., 2022).
081
082   In this work, we propose a novel flow-matching training approach to improve the efficiency of deep
083   learning model training and sampling for molecular conformer generation. To improve training
084   efficiency, we design a new flow-matching objective called SO(3)-Averaged Flow (Fig. 1a). As
085   an objective, Averaged Flow avoids the need to rotationally align prior and data distribution by
086
      analytically computing the averaged probability path from the prior to all the rotations of the data
      sample. Model trained with Averaged Flow is experimentally shown to converge faster to better
087
      performance. To improve the sampling efficiency, we adopt the reflow and distillation technique (Liu
088
      et al., 2022) to straighten the flow trajectories (Fig. 1b). Straightened trajectories allow high quality
089   molecular conformer generation with few-step or even one-step ODE solving, thus significantly
090   relieving the computational cost.
091
092
      Our main contribution can be summarized as: (i) Proposed a novel SO(3)-Averaged Flow matching
      objective. Averaged Flow eliminates the need of rotational alignment between prior and data by
093
      training the model to learn the average probability path over all rotations of the data. Averaged
094
      Flow leads to faster convergence to better performance for molecular conformer generation, and can
095   be extended to other similar tasks. (ii) Introduced reflow with distillation to reduce the number of
096   ODE steps required for the model to generate high quality conformers. Such technique significantly
097   improves the sampling efficiency of flow-matching models in molecular conformer generation.

      2     BACKGROUND AND RELATED WORK
101
      2.1   G ENERATIVE MODELS FOR C ONFORMER GENERATION
102
103   The task of molecular conformer generation in its core is to sample from the intractable conformer
104   distribution conditioned on the 2D molecular graph. Therefore, generative deep learning model is
105   well-suited for such task and many methods have been proposed. Deep learning model are usually
106   trained on datasets containing molecular conformers generated by CREST (Pracht et al., 2020) using
107   computationally expensive semi-empirical quantum chemistry method (Bannwarth et al., 2019) under
      the hood. Earliest works in this field uses variational autoencoder to generate the intrinsic inter-atomic


                                                          2
108
      distance (Simm & Hernández-Lobato, 2019; Xu et al., 2021). Shi et al. (2021) proposed a score-
109   matching method that learns the gradient of intrinsic atom coordinates in molecular graph. Ganea
110   et al. (2021) started to tackle molecular conformer generation by designing a message passing neural
111   network to predict the local 3D structure and torsion angles. Xu et al. (2022) adopted diffusion model
112   and equivariant graph neural network to generate molecular conformers by iteratively denoising the
113   Euclidean atom coordinates from sampled noise. Torsional diffusion (Jing et al., 2022) reduced
114   the degree-of-freedom by refining the torsion angles of RDKit-generated (Landrum, 2016) initial
115   conformers with a diffusion process on the hypertorus. Such design allowed torsional diffusion
116   to significantly reduce sampling steps. One drawback of torsional diffusion is that it relies on an
117
      RDKit-generated conformer as the starting point of diffusion, which adds computational overhead to
      generation process. The generation quality of RDKit, especially for atom coordinates in rings, can
118
      also impact the sample quality of torsional diffusion. Another recent work called DiSCO (Lee et al.,
119
      2024a) has proposed to use a Schrödinger bridge-based method to optimize generated conformers.
120   DiSCO can refine molecular conformers generated by any method to lower energy state by aligning
121   the conformational distribution approximated by a prior model to the ground truth distribution. It
122   is shown to improve the conformer generation quality of many methods such as RDKit and even
123   Torsional Diffusion. Molecular conformer field (MCF) proposed by Wang et al. (2024) is a recent
124   work that leverages the scaling power of the transformer architecture (Jaegle et al., 2021) and diffusion
125   model. MCF achieves state-of-the-art performance in molecular conformer generation by training
126   models with tens to hundreds million of parameters to denoise the atoms’ Euclidean coordinates
127   using DDPM paradigm (Ho et al., 2020). Equivariant Transformer Flow (ET-Flow) is a concurrent
128
      work that trains a equivariant flow-matching model to generate conformers from prior distribution.
      By combining harmonic prior (Jing et al., 2023), flow-matching, and Kabsch alignment that reduces
129
      transport cost, ET-Flow is reported to outperform MCF on several metrics with less ODE steps.
130
131   Overall, the trade-off between conformer generation quality and speed is a prevailing issue. Specifi-
132   cally, semi-empirical quantum chemistry can sample very high quality conformers with high com-
133   putational cost. Diffusion or flow-matching models can generate high quality conformers but the
134
      iterative ODE/SDE solving process can be slow, making them less practical for large-scale virtual
      screening. Cheminformatics tools such as RDKit and OMEGA are very fast but generate conformers
135
      with underwhelming diversity.

      2.2   F LOW- MATCHING
138
139   Averaged Flow is based on Flow Matching (Lipman et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023a; Albergo &
140   Vanden-Eijnden, 2023), which models a probability density path pt (xt ) that gradually transforms
141   an analytically tractable noise distribution (t = 0) into a data distribution (t = 1), following a time
142   variable t ∈ [0, 1]. Formally, the path pt (xt ) corresponds to a flow ψt that pushes samples from p0
143   to pt via pt = [ψ]t ∗ p0 , where ∗ denotes the push-forward. In practice, the flow is modelled via
144   an ordinary differential equation (ODE) dxt = vtθ (xt )dt, defined through a learnable vector field
145   vtθ (xt ) with parameters θ. Initialized from noise x0 ∼ p0 (x0 ), this ODE simulates the flow and
146
      transforms noise into approximate data distribution samples. The probability density path pt (xt ) and
      the (intractable) ground-truth vector field ut (xt ) are related via the continuity equation dpt (x)/dt =
147
      −∇x ·(pt (x)ut (x)). To construct pt Lipman et al. (2023) introduce a conditional probability pt (x|x1 )
148
      and conditional vector field ut (x|x1 ) both related to their unconditional counterparts as follow:

      2.3   R ECTIFIED FLOW AND OTHER DISTILLATION
163
164   With the success of denoising diffusion probabilistic models (Ho et al., 2020), many attention has
165   been drawn to improve the sampling speed of diffusion models. DDIM (Song et al., 2020) shows that
166   the sampling steps can be significantly reduced by formulating the sampling process as ODE solving.
167   Knowledge distillation techniques (Meng et al., 2023; Salimans & Ho, 2022; Song et al., 2023; Song
168   & Dhariwal, 2023) are also proposed to reduce sampling steps and accelerate generation. Rectified
169   flow (Liu et al., 2022; Liu, 2022) is a method proposed to train the model to learn straight probability
170
      flow that bridges prior and data distribution. The reflow technique proposed in rectified flow can
      straighten the flow trajectory and reduce the transport cost, allowing very few-step generation with
171
      high quality. After reflow, the model can be further distilled to improve 1-step generation. The
172
      reflow and distillation technique has been proven effective in enabling few-step or even single-step
173   text-to-image (Esser et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023b) and point cloud (Wu et al., 2023) generation.

254   3.2     R EFLOW AND DISTILLATION
255
256   Flow-matching and diffusion-based molecular conformer generation model typically requires hun-
257   dreds or even thousands steps numerical solving of ODE or SDE during the sampling process. Such
258   iterative process adds computational overhead and hinders the adoption of those model in industrial-
259   level downstream applications, which desire fast generation. One effective technique to reduce the
260
      sampling steps without significantly sacrificing the generation quality is to straighten the trajectory.
      Inspired by the success of such technique in point-cloud generation (Wu et al., 2023) and text-image
261
      generation (Esser et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023b), we finetune our model vtθ trained with Averaged
262
      Flow using the reflow algorithm proposed in previous rectified flow works (Liu et al., 2022; Liu,
263   2022). Specifically, we first randomly sample atom coordinates X0′ from standard Gaussian and
264   generates the corresponding conformer X1′ using the Tsitouras’ 5/4 solver (Tsitouras, 2011). The
265   coupling (X0′ , X1′ ) is then used in the rectified flow objective to finetune the model:
266                                          h                             i
267                        LReflow (θ) = E ∥vtθ (Xt′ , t) − (X1′ − X0′ )∥2 , with t ∈ [0, 1]               (9)
268
269   Liu et al. (2022) proved that the coupling (X0′ , X1′ ) yields equal or lower transport cost than (X0 , X1 )
      where X0 is sampled from noise distribution and X1 from data distribution. Therefore, applying the

271   the trajectory.

      4     E XPERIMENTS
318   Following previous works, we train and evaluate our model on the GEOM-QM9 and GEOM-Drugs
319   dataset (Axelrod & Gomez-Bombarelli, 2022). We followed the splitting strategy proposed by Ganea
320   et al. (2021); Jing et al. (2022) and test our model on the same test set containing 1000 molecules for
321   both QM9 and Drugs dataset. Dataset and splitting details are included in Sec. A.1.3. The major model
322   evaluation metrics are the average minimum RMSD (AMR, the lower the better) and coverage (COV,
323   the higher the better). Both AMR and coverage are reported for precision (AMR-P and COV-P) and
      recall (AMR-R and COV-R). The definition of metrics are specified in Sec.A.2.1. Intuitively, coverage


                                                           6
324
      measures the percentage of ground truth conformers being generated (recall) or the percentage of
325   generated conformers being close enough to ground truth (precision), while AMR measures the
326   average RMSD between each ground truth and its closest generated conformer (recall) or vice versa
327   (precision). There are three types of baselines in this work, including (a) methods with fast inference
328   speed such as cheminformatics tools (RDKit, OMEGA) and regression model GeoMol(Ganea et al.,
329   2021), (b) lightweight diffusion model with reduced degree of freedom (Torsional Diffusion), and (c)
330   large transformer-based diffusion or flow model operating on Euclidean atomistic coordinates (MCF
331   and ET-Flow). Moreover, to fairly validate the effectiveness of the Averaged Flow objective, we
332   compare the performance of our NequIP-based architecture (Appendix A.1.2) trained with different
333
      objectives. Similarly, we compare the performance of the same model architecture before and after
      reflow+distillation to show the necessity of reflow for few-step generation.

494   5    C ONCLUSION
495
496   We have presented SO(3)-Averaged Flow as a new objective to accelerate the training of flow-
497   matching models for molecular conformer generation. Averaged Flow leads to faster convergence to
498
      better performance compared with conditional OT and Kabsch alignment. We have also experimented
      reflow and distillation to straighten the flow trajectory and enable few-step molecular conformer
499
      generation. Our model reaches the state-of-the-art performance on the coverage-recall metric of the
500
      GEOM-QM9 dataset. It is also matching the performance of transformer-based model which have
501   several times more parameters on the GEOM-Drugs dataset. By analyzing the effect of number of
502   ODE steps to the model generation quality, we find out that reflow and distillation are necessary when
503   very few steps (Nstep < 5) of conformer generation is desired. Finally, by comparing the sampling
504   time, we demonstrate that our model is approximately 21 to 50 times faster than the other state-of-the-
505   art models, while achieving second to the best generation quality and diversity. Overall, given that the
506   Averaged Flow and reflow training scheme can be applied to any models, our method bridges the gap
507   between multi-step flow-matching models and practical molecular conformer generation application
508   by pushing the boundary of quality-speed trade-off.

683   A.1.2    M ODEL ARCHITECTURE
684
685   The equivariant model used in this work is a modified variant (Fig. 5) of the NequIP model (Batzner
686   et al., 2022). The model takes 4 inputs including the atomic features Z, relative distance vector
687
      between atoms ⃗r, edge (bond) features e, and the flow-matching time-step t. The output model is
      a vector field corresponding to the probability flow at t. Compared to the original NequIP model,
688
      our variant has residue connection and equivariant layer normalization (Liao et al., 2023) after each
689
      interaction block, which we found to be highly effective in stabilizing the training of model with
690   more than 4 layers. Bond information in the 2D molecular graph is critical inductive bias for the
691   molecular conformer generation task. To add bond information into the model, we featurize the edges
692   in the molecular graph and concatenate the edge features e with the radial basis embedding of relative
693   distance vector ⃗r. The concatenated message is then fed into the rotationally invariant radial function
694   implemented as an multi-layer perceptron. To keep long-range information in the graph convolution
695   during intermediate time-step t, we remove the envelop function from the radial basis and keep only
696   the radial Bessel function.
697   For both the GEOM-Drugs and GEOM-QM9 dataset, we train a model with 6 interaction blocks. The
698   multiplicity is set to 96 and maximum order of irreps l is 2. The radial function MLP has 2 layers
699   and hidden dimension of 256. Molecular graph are fully-connected with non-bond as an specified
700   bond type. The relative distance vectors are scaled down by a soft cutoff distance of 10Å and 20Å for
701   QM9 and Drugs dataset, respectively. we used 12 Bessel radial basis functions in the model. The
      model is implemented using e3nn-jax (Geiger & Smidt, 2022; Geiger et al., 2022).

748
749   The model is trained with the Averaged Flow for 990 epochs on the GEOM-Drugs dataset and 1500
750   epochs on the GEOM-QM9 dataset using 2 NVIDIA A5880 GPUs. We used dynamic graph batching
751   to maixmize the utilization of GPU memory and reduce JAX compilation time. The effective average
752
      batch size is 208 and 416 for Drugs and QM9 dataset, respectively. We used Adam optimizer with
      learning rate of 1e−2, which decays to 5e−3 after 600 epochs and to 1e−3 after 850 epochs. We
753
      selected the top-30 conformers for model training.
754
755   To sample coupled (X0′ , X1′ ) for reflow and distillation, we generate 32 noise-sample pairs for each
      molecule in the Drugs and 64 for each molecule in the QM9 dataset. The reflow and distillation are

756
      done using 4 NVIDIA A100 GPUs and doubling the effective batch size of each dataset. During the
757
      reflow stage, the model is finetuned for 870 epochs on Drugs and 1530 epochs on QM9. We used

      Adam optimizer with learning rate of 5e−3, which decays to 2.5e−3 after 450 epochs for Drugs

774   A.2.1    E VALUATION M TRICS
775   We report the average minimum RMSD (AMR) between ground truth and generated structures,
776   and Coverage for Recall and Precision. Coverage is defined as the percentage of conformers with
777   a minimum error under a specified AMR threshold. Recall matches each ground truth structure
778   to its closest generated structure, and Precision measures the overall spatial accuracy of the each
779   generated structure. Following Ganea et al. (2021); Jing et al. (2022), we generate two times the
780   number of ground truth structures for each molecule. More formally, for K = 2L, let {Cl∗ }l∈[1,L]
781   and {Ck }k∈[1,K] respectively be the sets of ground truth and generated structures:
782
                                       1
783                   COV-Precision :=   {k ∈ [1..K] : minl∈[1..L] RMSD(Ck , Cl∗ ) < δ} ,
784
                                       K
                                        1 X                                                            (12)
785                   AMR-Precision :=          minl∈[1..L] RMSD(Ck , Cl∗ ),
786                                    K
                                              k∈[1..K]
787
788   where δ is the coverage threshold. δ is set to 0.75Å for the Drugs and 0.5Å for the QM9 dataset. The
789   recall metrics are obtained by swapping ground truth (K) and generated conformers (L) in the above
790   equations.
