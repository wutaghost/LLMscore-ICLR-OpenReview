009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Cooperative multi-agent multi-armed bandits (CMA2B) consider the collaborative
012            efforts of multiple agents in a shared multi-armed bandit game. We study latent
013            vulnerabilities exposed by this collaboration and consider adversarial attacks on a
014            few agents with the goal of influencing the decisions of the rest. More specif-
015            ically, we study adversarial attacks on CMA2B in both homogeneous settings,
016            where agents operate with the same arm set, and heterogeneous settings, where
017            agents may have distinct arm sets. In the homogeneous setting, we propose attack
018
               strategies that, by targeting just one agent, convince all agents to select a particular
               target arm T − o(T ) times while incurring o(T ) attack costs in T rounds. In the
019
               heterogeneous setting, we prove that a target arm attack requires linear attack costs
020
               and propose attack strategies that can force a maximum number of agents to suffer
021            linear regrets while incurring sublinear costs and only manipulating the observa-
022            tions of a few target agents. Numerical experiments validate the effectiveness of
023            our proposed attack strategies.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Cooperative multi-agent multi-armed bandits (CMA2B) have been widely studied in recent
029   years (Bistritz & Leshem, 2018; Boursier & Perchet, 2019; Yang et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023a). In
030   CMA2B, M ∈ N+ agents cooperatively play multi-armed bandits with K ∈ N+ arms in a sequential
031   manner. In each decision round, each agent picks one arm and observes a reward sample drawn from
032   a stochastic distribution associated with the pulled arm. Their cooperative objective is to maximize
033
      their total cumulative rewards in T ∈ N+ decision rounds, or minimize their total regret—the dif-
      ference between the total expected rewards of all agents constantly pulling the optimal arm and the
034
      actual expected rewards of the considered algorithm.
035
036   Leveraging cooperation between agents, CMA2B algorithms can achieve an improved total regret of
037   O(K log T ) (Wang et al., 2023a), compared with a total regret of O(M K log T ) if no cooperation is
038   used. However, a security caveat arises when some agents occasionally get unreliable observations
039
      that may have been tampered with by malicious attackers. This concern becomes more serious in
      large-scale multi-agent systems, where assuring consistent reliability of every agent’s observations
040
      becomes increasingly intricate. Given the collaborative nature of CMA2B, such adversarial attacks
041
      have the potential to not only influence the performance of their target agents, but also affect other
042   agents in the same learning system. For example, botnets can mimic user clicks on ads to mislead
043   learning algorithms in online advertising. When advertisers, or learning agents, exchange their
044   information such as the user’s clicks for cooperative user preference learning, it can be seen as
045   a CMA2B problem. The risks come from the malicious botnet, sitting between the users and the
046   agents. The objective of the bot is to undermine algorithm’s performance, however, due to the limit
047   of resources, the bot hope to achieve this objective with as few operations, such as removing the
048   user’s clicks or adding fake ones, as possible.
049   Adversarial attacks on single-agent bandits have been studied in (Jun et al., 2018; Liu & Shroff,
050   2019; Zuo, 2024). In single-agent bandits, a successful attack means convincing the agent to pull a
051   target arm a nearly linear number of times (i.e., T − o(T )) via manipulation of the agent’s reward
052   observations while only incurring a sublinear attack cost (i.e., o(T )). In contrast, in the multi-agent
053   settings, the definition of a successful attack may vary. It might involve misleading a single agent or
      all agents, and the manipulations could target one agent or span across all agents. In this paper, we

      Pursuing this objective, we discuss adversarial attacks in both homogeneous and heterogeneous
      CMA2B contexts. In the homogeneous settings, where each agent has access to all K arms, it is
066
      feasible to convince all agents to choose a specific target arm in most time slots (if the target is a
067
      suboptimal arm, agents suffer linear regret). However, the number of agents that must be attacked
068   and the overall cost of these attacks remain uncertain (addressed in Section 3). Conversely, in the
069   heterogeneous settings, where agents have different subsets of arms, the goal of directing all agents
070   to select a single target arm becomes unattainable, especially if some agents lack access to this
071   arm. As we elaborate in Section 4, even the task of convincing a subset of agents to select a target
072   arm could require linear attack costs. Consequently, we shift our attack objective towards inducing
073   the greatest number of agents to suffer linear regret. This objective leads to three new challenges.
074   First, determining the largest group of affected agents experiencing linear regret while incurring
075   only sublinear costs remains an unresolved issue. Second, it is unclear how to select a small number
076
      of target agents for attack. Third, for these chosen target agents, we need to design attack strategies
      that can effectively influence other agents.

079   In the homogeneous setting, we propose attack strategies that can convince all agents to select a des-
080   ignated target arm linear times by attacking only a single agent with sublinear attack costs, revealing
081   the inherent vulnerability of homogeneous CMA2B algorithms. In the heterogeneous settings, we
082

      that can compel a significant number of agents to experience linear regret by manipulating the ob-

      CMA2B in homogeneous settings can be broadly classified into two categories (Wang et al., 2023a):
175
      fully distributed algorithms and leader-follower algorithms. The distinction between them lies in
176   the presence or absence of a central agent (or server) that determines the actions of agents. In
177   fully distributed algorithms, all agents participate in exploration and exploitation. Conversely, in
178   leader-follower algorithms, the leader primarily manages exploration and plays a pivotal role. Due
179   to the space limit, we focus on attacks against fully distributed algorithms in this section. We also
180   design an attack strategy for the leader-follower algorithm DPE2 (Wang et al., 2020a) and provide a
181   detailed cost analysis in Appendix B.4. Our analysis shows that targeting only the leader is adequate
182   for misleading leader-follower algorithms.
183   Target Algorithms. There is an abundance of fully distributed algorithms in the literature; however,
184   for consistency with the heterogeneous setting (Section 4), we study CO-UCB (Yang et al., 2022), a
185   representative CMA2B algorithm that functions effectively in both homogeneous and heterogeneous
186   environments, as our attack target. With CO-UCB, in each round, each agent pulls the arm with
187   the highest UCB index and shares its reward observation immediately with others. We also extend
188   our attack methodologies to UCB-TCOM (Wang et al., 2023a), which incorporates efficient com-
189   munication mechanisms. Due to space constraints, we defer the detailed algorithm and analysis to
190
      Appendix B.3, where we address the issue of delayed feedback between agents. While most existing
      CMA2B algorithms are UCB-based, it is also possible to extend our findings to arbitrary no-regret
191
      CMA2B algorithms, based on the general attack strategy for single-agent bandits proposed by Liu &
192
      Shroff (2019). For further discussion, see Section 4.2.4.

      4     ATTACKS IN H ETEROGENEOUS S ETTINGS
227
228
      In this section, we study adversarial attacks on CMA2B in heterogeneous settings, where agents may
229
      have distinct local arm sets. We focus on the CO-UCB algorithm (Yang et al., 2022), a representative
230   CMA2B algorithm for heterogeneous settings. While there are other heterogeneous algorithms (Wang
231   et al., 2023b; Baek & Farias, 2021), all existing algorithms rely on UCB. Therefore, we choose CO-
232   UCB as our example for devising attack strategies and believe our methodology can be extended
233   to other UCB-based heterogeneous algorithms. We first discuss the viability of different attack
234   objectives. Following that, we propose attack strategies with the appropriate objective and offer
235   theoretical analyses of their associated costs.
236
237   4.1   ATTACK O BJECTIVES
238
239   While the majority of heterogeneous CMA2B algorithms are derivatives of their homogeneous coun-
240   terparts, the distinctiveness introduced by agent heterogeneity poses novel challenges in devising
241
      adversarial attacks. We first consider the original target arm attack as in the homogeneous settings,
      which aims to deceive all agents into selecting a target arm T − o(T ) times. Intriguingly, in het-
242
      erogeneous settings, achieving this objective might require linear attack costs. A simple example of
243
      two agents is shown in Figure 1a. We consider the target arm to be arm 3 in agent 1, and intuitively,
244   once arm 1 or 2 is pulled, the attacker needs to decrease their rewards. However, given this hetero-
245   geneous setup, agent 2 only has access to arms 1 and 2. Therefore, it is compelled to select them
246   repeatedly, and their reward samples are subsequently sent to agent 1. As a result, to deceive agent
247   1 into frequently selecting arm 3, linear attack costs on agent 2 become necessary. Proposition 1
248   formally shows the necessity of linear costs to realize the target arm attack.
249   Proposition 1. For any attack strategy that can mislead the agents running CO-UCB in Figure 1a
250   to pull the target arm T − o(T ) times, its attack cost is at least C(T ) ⩾ cT for some constant c > 0.
251
252   Thus, the target arm attack may not be an appropriate attack objective in heterogeneous environ-
253   ments. Shifting our focus, we consider an alternative attack objective: misleading all agents toward
254   linear regrets. While this objective seems less stringent, as it merely mandates agents not to choose
255   their local optimal arms, the intrinsic heterogeneity of available arms brings forth complexities. No-
256
      tably, there might be agents that, given the disparity in arm sets, cannot be simultaneously misguided
      towards linear regrets with only sublinear costs. Such agents are termed as “conflict” agents. An
257
      example of this scenario is depicted in Figure 1b. Our objective is to deceive agents 1 and 2, prevent-
258
      ing them from selecting their locally optimal arms, and thereby incurring linear regrets. Notably,
259   while arm 2 is suboptimal for agent 1, it is optimal for agent 2. After the attacks, agent 1 should pull
260   arm 2 linear times, and those rewards will be communicated to agent 2 to affect its arm selection.
261   Consequently, ensuring agent 2 chooses its suboptimal arm 3 almost linear times incurs linear attack
262   costs. In Proposition 2, we demonstrate that linear costs are inevitable when pursuing this objective.
263
      Proposition 2. For any attack strategy that can successfully mislead all agents running CO-UCB in
264   Figure 1b to suffer linear regrets, its attack cost is at least C(T ) ⩾ cT for some constant c > 0.
265
266   Although sublinear attack costs might not be sufficient in leading all agents to experience linear
267   regrets, they can still influence a subset of the agents. This realization prompts the final attack ob-
268   jective explored in this section: leveraging sublinear attack costs to misguide the maximum number
269   of agents, aiming to increase the overall count of agents enduring linear regrets. To realize this goal,
      it is necessary to identify the largest set of agents that do not have conflicts. The condition under

      4.2.4    D ISCUSSIONS
419
420   Discussion on General Attack Strategies. In this paper, we focus on representative UCB-
421   based algorithms, including CO-UCB (Yang et al., 2022), UCB-TCOM (Wang et al., 2023a), and
422   DPE2 (Wang et al., 2020a). However, our findings can be extended to more general CMA2B al-
423   gorithms, i.e., arbitrary no-regret bandit algorithms. In homogeneous settings, by substituting our
424   initial attack strategy on the single affected agent with the general attack strategy for single-agent
425   bandits from Section 4.2 of Liu & Shroff (2019), we can conduct an attack cost analysis similar
426   to that in Theorem 1 (see Appendix B.5 for more details). In heterogeneous settings, AAS and
427   TAS remain effective methods for identifying agents vulnerable to attacks and finding target agents.
428
      Nevertheless, a more detailed investigation is required to analyze the attack cost when applying the
      general attack strategy to these target agents, as our current analysis in Theorem 3 depends on spe-
429
      cific characteristics of UCB algorithms. We leave this as a promising direction for future research.
430
431   Discussion on Defense Strategies. In the context of single-agent stochastic bandits, Zuo (2024)
      claims that, if some bandit algorithm achieves regret REG(T ) in the absence of attacks, then there


                                                             8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      exists a strong attacker—who first observes the learner’s action and then alters their reward obser-
433   vation—with attack cost Θ(REG(T )) that can make the learner suffer linear regret (Fact 1 in (Zuo,
434   2024)). This implies that no algorithm can achieve sublinear regret when facing strong attackers with
435   adaptive attack cost. Nevertheless, it remains possible to defend against strong attackers if their at-
436   tack cost is limited, although designing and analyzing effective defense algorithms remains an open
437   problem. Concurrently, there exists a complementary line of research focuses on developing robust
438   single-agent bandit algorithms against corruption (Lykouris et al., 2018; Gupta et al., 2019), where
439   weak attackers can alter the reward realizations but cannot observe the learner’s current action.
440   Our work focuses on multi-agent stochastic bandits, uncovering latent vulnerabilities that arise
441   through collaboration. Our findings from the attacker’s perspective can also offer valuable insights
442   into designing defense algorithms against strong attackers with limited attack cost. For instance,
443   employing techniques such as TAS and AAS can help identify vulnerable agents that significantly
444   impact others. By enhancing the protection of these critical agents, we may reduce the number of
445   agents affected, thereby mitigating the impact of adversarial attacks.

      We introduce the Learning-Then-Attack (LTA) algorithm, detailed in Appendix A.6. We assume the
450

451

452   operates in two phases: an initial learning stage, where it discerns reward means and arm rankings,
453   followed by an attack phase akin to Algorithm 3. In the learning stage, the arm ranking is pivotal for
454   executing AAS and TAS, as they necessitate the knowledge of each agent’s local optimal arms to
455   optimize the affected agent group and minimize the target agent subset. To acquire this ranking, the
456   attacker needs to compel agents to pull each arm multiple times. This ensures a sufficient number of
457   pre-attack samples, allowing for a clear distinction between the LCBs and UCBs for every arm pair.
458   Given that agents choose arms based on UCB algorithms, the attacker needs to stimulate agents to
459   collect ample samples for suboptimal arms by increasing their UCB values through attacks. More
460   specifically, if the attacker wants to accumulate samples of arm k, it needs to attack the arm’s reward

530
531   This paper explores adversarial attacks on CMA2B, revealing significant vulnerabilities in both ho-
532   mogeneous and heterogeneous settings. Our proposed attack strategies demonstrate how minimal
533   manipulation of selected agents can degrade the performance of an entire cooperative learning sys-
534
      tem. It also opens up multiple future directions, including the development of attack strategies for
      competitive multi-agent bandits involving collisions. Further exploration could extend to dynamic
535
      and asynchronous systems, where agent populations and environments evolve over time. Finally,
536
      our insights into current CMA2B vulnerabilities highlight the need for designing robust algorithms
537   that can withstand adversarial manipulation. Future research should focus on creating defenses that
538   detect and mitigate attacks, ensuring the reliability of cooperative multi-agent systems in practice.
