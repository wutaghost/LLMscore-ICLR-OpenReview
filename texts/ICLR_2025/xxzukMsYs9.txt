009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Object manipulation in images aims to not only edit the object presentation but
012            also gift objects with motion. Previous methods encountered challenges in concur-
013            rently handling static editing and dynamic motion applications, while also strug-
014            gling to achieve realism in object appearance and scene lighting. In this work,
015            we introduce OMG3D, a novel framework that integrates the precise geometric
016            control with the generative power of diffusion models, thus achieving significant
017            enhancements in visual performance. Our framework converts 2D objects into 3D,
018
               enabling user-directed modifications and lifelike motions at the geometric level.
               To address texture realism, we propose CustomRefiner, a texture refinement mod-
019
               ule that pretrain a customized diffusion model to align the style and perspectives
020
               of coarse renderings with the original image. Additionally, we introduce Illumi-
021            Combiner, an lighting processing module that estimates and adjusts background
022            lighting to match human visual perception, resulting in more realistic illumina-
023            tion. Extensive experiments demonstrate the outstanding visual performance of
024            our approach in both static and dynamic scenarios. Remarkably, all these steps
025            can be done using one NVIDIA 3090. The code and project page will be released
026            upon acceptance of the paper.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Object manipulation seeks to modify or animate specific objects in an image to achieve enhanced
032   visual effects. It has been widely utilized across various fields, such as poster design, AR/VR,
033
      and the film industry. Some advanced tools like PhotoShop offer pixel-level editing capabilities,
      including object addition and removal, lighting adjustments, and animation creation. However, their
034
      complexity often poses a significant barrier for beginners. As a result, many generative methods
035
      have emerged to offer more accessible and user-friendly alternatives to traditional editing tools.
036
037   Through analysis of existing methods, we identify two key limitations in object manipulation: (i)
038   Difficulty in concurrently handling static editing and dynamic motion application. Current static
039
      editing methods, such as text based methods (Tumanyan et al., 2023; Hertz et al., 2022), effectively
      modify object appearances, and 3D reconstruction based methods (Chen et al., 2024b; Yenphraphai
040
      et al., 2024) offer precise control. But when applying these methods to animation, frame-by-frame
041
      editing fails to maintain consistency in both appearance and movement of objects, resulting in abrupt
042   shifts. (ii) Lack of realism in object appearance and scene lighting. During static editing and mo-
043   tion application, generative methods often cause distortions in the object’s presentations, yielding
044   unpredictable outcomes. And methods use 3D reconstruction (Liu et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024)
045   falls short in delivering an accurate texture representation relative to the original image. Addition-
046   ally, these methods typically overlook real lighting effects, lacking precise control over this aspect,
047   resulting in a lack of visual realism in generated images and videos.
048   Considering the aforementioned limitations, we propose corresponding solutions to comprehen-
049   sively achieve object manipultion. To tackle the inconsistency between static editing and dy-
050   namic motion application, we find that both tasks can be accomplished by binding skeletons to
051   3D geometric data and deforming them in 3D space. Thus, our framework, OMG3D, allows users
052   to interact with reconstructed models, thereby achieving predictable outcomes in object manipula-
053   tion. And by utilizing a graphics rendering pipeline, our framework ensures consistency in object
      appearance and smoothness in animation during the rendering process. For addressing the lack of

111   The rise of diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Peebles & Xie, 2023; Rombach et al., 2021; Podell

      To achieve more accurate control, ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023a) incorporates additional condi-

      tional inputs such as depth (Yang et al., 2024a), poses (Cao et al., 2017), and edges (Xie & Tu, 2015)

123   3D assets, enabling editing through control of these 3D assets. Recently, methods like OBJect3DIT
124   (Michel et al., 2024) have achieved 3D-aware editing using language instructions, but their capa-
125   bilities are limited due to training on synthetic data. Image sculpting (Yenphraphai et al., 2024), a
126   baseline of our paper, struggles to produce realistic lighting and shadow effects.
127   Image Animation. Animating static images has been studied for a long time. Previous methods
128   focus on simulating motion for natural dynamics (Holynski et al., 2021; Jhou & Cheng, 2015; Ma-
129   hapatra & Kulkarni, 2022), as well as human faces (Geng et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2022a) and bodies
130   (Weng et al., 2019; Siarohin et al., 2021). And video prediction methods (Franceschi et al., 2020;
131   Zhang et al., 2020) generate future frames from single images by extracting spatial-temporal pat-
132   terns from video data. Additionally, some techniques (Endo et al., 2019; Zhao & Zhang, 2022; Voleti
133   et al., 2022) integrate motion priors into their pipelines to achieve more consistent video outputs.
134   With the rise of Video Diffusion Models (Ho et al., 2022), numerous methods (Wang et al., 2024a;
135   Luo et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023) utilize images as conditions to generate videos. Previous meth-
136   ods (Shi et al., 2024; Mahapatra et al., 2023) utilize estimated optical flow in conjunction with
137   a pre-trained text-to-image diffusion model (Rombach et al., 2021) to achieve video generation.
138   Some concurrent works focus on controllable editing through motion conditions, including object
139   movement (Chen et al., 2024a), camera movement (Yang et al., 2024b), and methods for editing
140   (Bai et al., 2024). However, these approaches do not support region-specific image-to-video (I2V)
141
      transformations and rely on standard 1-D temporal attention for learning the complex mapping from
      images to videos. Due to the limitations of model capabilities, image-to-video techniques still have
142
      significant room for improvement in maintaining both object appearance and motion fluidity.
143
144   Image harmonization. Image harmonization is a crucial vision task that ensures objects are natu-
145   rally integrated into new backgrounds, and many studies have focused on addressing this challenge.
146   (Wang et al., 2022b) proposed a method that first estimates an HDR sky map and then employs
147
      Differentiable Object Insertion to blend virtual objects with the background, though their work is
      primarily focused on outdoor scenes. (Careaga et al., 2023) adjusted the foreground albedo to align
148
      with the background and estimated environmental lighting to refine the shading process. However,
149
      obtaining accurate albedo information for objects can be challenging, and their method struggles
150   to model the cast shadows that objects may produce in the new environment. (Enyo & Nishino,
151   2024) introduced a technique that utilizes 3D object information to generate high-quality Reflectance
152   Maps, enabling the recovery of detailed lighting information. However, for objects without specular
153   reflection, this model may be less effective in recovering high-frequency lighting details. Meth-
154   ods such as DiffusionLight (Phongthawee et al., 2024) and DiPIR (Liang et al., 2024) effectively
155   estimate environmental lighting from background images.
156   Our framework is designed to enable seamless combinations of arbitrary foregrounds and back-
157   grounds while maintaining consistency in shadows and lighting. DiPIR, which requires per-object
158   optimization for environmental lighting and is less suitable for our application, our approach empha-
159   sizes plug-and-play flexibility. Additionally, methods like DiffusionLight and DiPIR could serve as
160   alternative implementations for the lighting estimation module in our framework, further enhancing
161   its versatility and robustness.

      4.1   OVERVIEW OF OMG3D
217
218   Given a 2D image, our framework is designed to enable manipulation of the objects within a 3D
219   space, encompassing both presentation edits and motion applications, before eventually rendering
220   them back into a 2D image. This process unfolds in three key steps: 1. Converting the 2D object
221   into a 3D model and optimizing its texture, 2. Deforming the reconstructed model within 3D space,
222   3. Rendering the modified model with enhanced lighting to improve the visual output.
223   3D model reconstruction. Given an image of an object, we first isolate the object using methods
224   like SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023). We then use the object segmentation as a reference to reconstruct
225   the mesh through Image-to-3D methods (Liu et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024; Mildenhall et al., 2021;
226   HyperHuman, 2024; Zhang et al., 2023b; 2024; Xu et al., 2023; Long et al., 2023). Currently,
227   the reconstructed results often display a considerable gap in texture presentation compared to the
228   original image. To address this, we apply CustomRefiner to enhance the texture, significantly
229   improving the fidelity of the final output.
230   3D model deformation. After optimizing the 3D model, users can manually construct a skeleton
231   and interactively manipulate it by rotating the bones to achieve the desired pose or attach animations
232   to the skeleton, using 3D manipulation software such as Blender. Because the texture mapping of
233   a 3D model is exclusively linked to the vertices and independent of the pose, the appearance of the
234   model remains consistent throughout this process.
235   Rendering results. Our framework utilizes a graphics rendering pipeline, which inherently pro-
236   vides the advantage of utilizing light sources effectively. Consequently, we focus on estimating and
237   processing the lighting from the background image to derive an accurate spherical light thorough
238   IlluminCombiner. When we obtain the deformed model and processed light, we render the results
239   within the graphics rendering pipeline to achieve realistic and visually balanced effects.
240
241   4.2   C ROSS - VIEWPOINTS GENERATIVE TEXTURE REFINEMENT
242
243   To address the gap between rough models reconstructed from image-to-3D methods and the original
244
      image, we propose CustomRefiner, a texture refinement module that utilizes differentiable raster-
      ization (Laine et al., 2020) across multiple refined rendering viewpoints. Specifically, our module
245
      focuses on three key aspects: 1.Appearance and color preservation, 2.Geometry information injec-
246
      tion, and 3.UV-texture optimization.
247
248   Appearance and color preservation. Our method draws inspiration from customized models, re-
249   taining the original object’s appearance and color when generating new viewpoints. Initially, we
250
      train a customized diffusion model like DreamBooth (Ruiz et al., 2023) for each concept. And we
      convert various rough rendering viewpoints back to Gaussian noise using DDIM Inversion (Song
251
      et al., 2020). During the denoising stage, we leverage information preserved by the pretrained cus-
252
      tomized model to guide these viewpoints into visual alignment with the original image.
253
254   Geometry information refinement. Since we can easily obtain precise depth maps of rough models
255   through the graphics rendering pipeline, we incorporate Depth ControlNet (Mahapatra & Kulkarni,
256
      2022) into the DDIM Inversion stage and diffusion denoising stage to ensure consistency in shape
      and geometric information. We directly add the depth information to these two stages without
257
      any fine-tuning. Additionally, to further enhance the preservation of geometric details, we draw
258
      inspiration from PnP (Tumanyan et al., 2023). During the inversion stage, we save the feature
259   maps from the residual blocks and the self-attention maps from the transformer blocks. Then, in
260   the denoising stage, we replace these features with the saved maps, ensuring that more geometric
261   information is preserved during the refinement process.
262
      UV-texture optimization. Through DDIM Inversion (inverting coarse rendering viewpoints into
263
      gaussian noise), incorporating Depth ControlNet with feature injection, we achieve optimized results
264
      across various viewpoints. We achieve gradient backpropagation directly to the UV texture map
265   through differentiable rasterization, optimizing with the following pixel-wise MSE Loss:

      4.3   H YBRID LIGHTING PROCESSING
281   Achieving realistic lighting and shadow effects has long been a challenge that many methods seek
282   to address. Yet our framework enhances these effects by incorporating spherical light sources to
283   achieve superior results. To further enhance this capability, we propose IllumiCombiner, a lighting
284   processing module that delivers realistic and visually balanced spherical light. Specifically, the
285   process involves two steps: 1. estimating and processing the lighting from background images, and
286   2. creating a transparent plane for accurate shadow reception.
287   Light processing. We explore various lighting estimation methods (Zhan et al., 2021; Phongthawee
288   et al., 2024) to assess the background lighting. These methods export estimation as a spherical light
289   Le , which has two properties: color component ce and intensity component ie . However, we
290   find that images directly rendered with Le often exhibit reduced color, even with accurate lighting
291   direction. For color correction, we use SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023) to segment the image i, obtaining
292   a mask M . We then calculate the average color within M , resulting in object’s color ca .

313   model and applying precise manipulation over lighting and shadow effects, our approach enables us
314   to render more realistic and visually balanced results.

      5.1   E VALUATION
354
355   Quantitative Results. To demonstrate the effectiveness of our method, we compare it against
356   several baselines that are capable of achieving object manipulation. For the image editing task, we
357   select P2P (Hertz et al., 2022), PnP (Tumanyan et al., 2023), and Masactrl (Cao et al., 2023) for
358   comparison. While for the image animation task, we choose SVD (Blattmann et al., 2023), Pika
359   (pika, 2024), and DynamiCrafter (Xing et al., 2023) as video baselines. In our evaluations, we
360   found that GPT4o to some extent demonstrates the capabilities of editing methods, but it tends to
361   have higher tolerance when it comes to object appearance alignment.
362   As shown in Tab. 1, in the image editing task, our method achieves excellent results in both GPT-4o
363   and human evaluations. It performs the best in terms of aligning with the original appearance of the

462   5.2   A BLATION S TUDY
463
464   Impact of CustomRefiner. We design a series of ablation studies to evaluate the effect of using
465   CustomRefiner in OMG3D. As shown in Fig. 4, the use of CustomRefiner not only enhances the
466   object’s color but also increases image details, improving the realism of the object’s appearance.
467
      Impact of light estimation methods and IllumiCombiner. We compared two different light es-
468   timation methods, EMLight (Zhan et al., 2021) and DiffusionLight (Phongthawee et al., 2024).
469   From Fig. 4, we can see that the results rendered with EMLight exhibit overexposure and incorrect
470   shadow direction. In close-up comparisons, DiffusionLight produces more realistic shadows, while
471   EMLight’s shadows have harsh and inconsistent edges. By integrating color information from the
472   original image and adjusting the intensity and hue of the estimated light, IllumiCombiner ensures
473   that the lighting in the scene aligns more naturally with the background, leading to a more harmo-
474   nious and aesthetically pleasing final output.
475   Impact of shadow inclusion. As illustrated in Fig. 4, without shadows, the object appears flat and
476   lacks realism, making it seem disconnected from the surrounding environment. When shadows are
477   added, the object not only blends more naturally with the background but also gains a sense of depth
478   and dimensionality. The inclusion of accurate shadows helps anchor the object within the scene,
479   enhancing the overall visual coherence and realism.

499   6     C ONCULUTION
500
501   In this work, we presented OMG3D, a novel framework for object manipulation in images that not
502   only enhances object presentation but also brings objects to life with dynamic motion. OMG3D
503   seamlessly integrates precise geometric control with the powerful capabilities of diffusion models,
504
      offering significant improvements in visual quality. By converting 2D objects into 3D models, our
      framework allows user-directed manipulations while preserving object appearance with high fidelity.
505
      The introduction of CustomRefiner enables detailed texture refinement, aligning the rendering with
506
      the original image’s style and perspective. Meanwhile, IllumiCombiner provides advanced light-
507   ing adjustments, resulting in visually accurate and realistic illumination. Our experiments demon-
508   strate that OMG3D excels in both static and dynamic scenarios, significantly outperforming pre-
509   vious methods in terms of visual realism, motion accuracy, and adaptability to various conditions.
510   We show that our proposed framework outperforms state-of-the-art methods, enabling a wide range
511   of applications. Future work will focus on extending our framework to more complex scenes and
512   incorporating object dynamic interactions.

      A.1    L IMITATION AND FUTURE WORKS .
813
814   Despite the success of our method in object manipulation, there exists some limitations. For ex-
815   ample, the 3D-generated models show suboptimal mesh quality in complex cases, which could be
816   improved with advanced models or manual adjustments.
817
      About lighting: DiffusionLight sometimes produces inaccurate lighting, highlighting the need for
818   better light estimation methods. We have analyzed some existing lighting techniques in our Sec. 2.
819   However, in comparison to existing lighting methods, our IllumiCombiner provides a substantial
820   improvement over more recent methods, such as DiffusionLight (CVPR 2024), as demonstrated in
821   Fig. 4. We believe that our approach to lighting design strikes a balance between effectiveness and
822   efficiency, tailored specifically to the objectives of our framework. Due to the plug-and-play design
823   of our framework, we are also very excited about the possibility of new lighting methods emerging
824   in the future, which could further improve the overall performance of our framework.
825   In future work, we aim to expand our method further. Currently, the background is static, but
826   we plan to explore the use of video models to generate dynamic backgrounds or reconstruct 3D
827   scenes for more complex environments. Additionally, we intend to incorporate dynamic interactions
828   between objects, enabling the system to not only handle individual objects but also simulate complex
829   interactions between multiple objects.

      of our work lies in two aspects: First, achieving realistic lighting and shadow effects in both static
