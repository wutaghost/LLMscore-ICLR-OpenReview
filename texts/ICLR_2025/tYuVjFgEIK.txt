011                                            Abstract
012
013           In multivariate time series forecasting using the Transformer architecture,
014           capturing temporal dependencies and modeling inter-variable relationships
015           are crucial for improving performance. However, overemphasizing temporal
016           dependencies can destabilize the model, increasing its sensitivity to noise,
017
              overfitting, and weakening its ability to capture inter-variable relationships.
              We propose a new approach called the Temporal-Variable Decoupling Net-
018
              work (TVDN) to address this challenge. This method decouples the mod-
019
              eling of variable dependencies from temporal dependencies and further sep-
020           arates temporal dependencies into historical and predictive sequence de-
021           pendencies, allowing for a more effective capture of both. Specifically,
022           the simultaneous learning of time-related and variable-related patterns can
023           lead to harmful interference between the two. TVDN first extracts variable
024           dependencies from historical data through a permutation-invariant model
025           and then captures temporal dependencies using a permutation-equivariant
026           model. By decoupling variable and temporal dependencies and historical
027           and predictive sequence dependencies, this approach minimizes interference
028
              and allows for complementary extraction of both. Our method provides a
              concise and innovative approach to enhancing the utilization of temporal
029
              features. Experiments on multiple real-world datasets demonstrate that
030
              TVDN achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance. The code is avail-
031           able at the repository https://anonymous.4open.science/r/TVDN-366F

081

085   the model toward its true solution.

      In conclusion, based on the analysis above, we introduce a dual-phase deep learning network
      architecture. The initial phase, the Cross-Variable Encoder (CVE), aims to identify inter-
088
      variable dependencies, effectively extracting information from historical sequences. Once the
089
      CVE stabilizes, the second phase shifts to temporal dependency learning. In this phase, the
090   Cross-Temporal Encoder (CTE) combines the original input with the output from the CVE,
091   focusing on learning cross-temporal dependencies. This approach addresses the limitations
092   of temporal dependency learning inherent in the first phase’s cross-variable feature learning
093   and clarifies the temporal relationships within predictive sequences.
094

095
      the risk of overfitting and enhances the potential to discover better global solutions. Our
096   experimental results demonstrate that the proposed TVDN (Temporal-Variable Decoupling
097   Network) achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance on real-world forecasting bench-
098   marks, as illustrated in Figure 1. Our contributions can be summarized in three key aspects:

103          proving temporal feature utilization.
104

106          ical sequence temporal learning, and predicted sequence temporal learning, then

118   2   Related Work
119
120
121   Traditional time series forecasting methods such as ARIMA(Anderson, 1976), Holt-
122   Winters(Hyndman & Athanasopoulos, 2018), and Exponential Smoothing(Brown, 1959)
123   assume that temporal variations follow fixed patterns. However, real-world time series data
124   often contain complexities that these methods fail to capture, limiting their effectiveness in
125   practical applications(Box et al., 2015; Chatfield & Xing, 2019).
126   To address the shortcomings of classical models, deep learning approaches have been devel-
127   oped for temporal modeling, including TCN, RNN-based, and MLP-based methods. MLP-
128   based models(Challu et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2023) utilize MLPs along the temporal di-
129   mension to encode temporal dependencies into the fixed parameters of the MLP layers.
130   TCN-based methods capture temporal variations using convolutional kernels that slide along
131   the temporal dimension(Wu et al., 2022). RNN-based methods(Lai et al., 2018; Gu et al.,
132   2021) employ a recurrent structure to implicitly capture temporal variations through state
133   transitions over time.
134   The Transformer model, celebrated for its exceptional performance in diverse domains such
135   as natural language processing, speech recognition, and computer vision, has been adapted
136   for time series forecasting through various variants to enhance its self-attention mecha-
137   nism(Vaswani et al., 2017). These adaptations primarily focus on learning long-term depen-
138   dencies using cross-temporal attention mechanisms and optimizing computational eﬀiciency.
139   LogTrans(Li et al., 2019) introduces a convolutional self-attention layer with a LogSparse de-
140   sign, adept at capturing local information while reducing spatial complexity. Other models,
141   such as Informer(Zhou et al., 2022a) and Autoformer(Wu et al., 2021), innovate by replacing
142   the traditional self-attention mechanism, lowering computational complexity to O(L log L).
143   Pyraformer(Liu et al., 2021) integrates pyramid attention modules that connect across and
144   within scales, achieving linear complexity.
145   Further advancements include models like Autoformer, FEDformer(Zhou et al., 2022b), and
146   ETSformer(Woo et al., 2022), which incorporate TSFT with seasonal trend decomposition
147   and signal processing techniques, such as Fourier analysis, within their attention frameworks.
148   This enhances the interpretability of these models and eﬀiciently captures seasonal trends.
149
      To address stability in predictions, especially in non-stationary contexts, some Transformer
150   models incorporate stabilization modules and De-stationary into the standard Transformer
151   framework(Liu et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2021). This helps stabilize predictions while avoiding
152   the pitfalls of excessive stabilization, which can lead to a loss of important data variability.
153
      Recent developments in cross-variable Transformer models show significant promise. Mod-
154
      els like iTransformer(Liu et al., 2024) and Client(Gao et al., 2023b) enhance performance
155
      in long-term multivariate forecasting by using cross-variable Transformers instead of cross-
156   temporal ones. Additionally, Crossformer(Zhang & Yan, 2022) employs a two-stage at-
157   tention (TSA) layer to capture dependencies over time and across different dimensional
158   segments of the series. However, there is room for improvement in models like Crossformer
159   regarding their performance on various benchmark datasets.A recent work PatchTST (Nie
160   et al., 2022) studies using a vision transformer type model for long-term forecasting with
161   channel independent design. This work designs an encoder-decoder model utilizing a hier-
      archy attention mechanism to leverage cross-dimension dependencies.

186   3     Model Architecture
187
188   The architecture of TVDN is depicted in Figure 2. As previously discussed, we separate the
189   learning of variable dependency from that of temporal dependency. The process begins with
190   variable dependency learning (left), followed by temporal dependency learning(right), which
191   is further divided into two sub-modules: historical sequence dependency and predictive
192
      sequence dependency.
193
194   3.1    Cross-Variable Encoder (CVE)
195
      CVE is a permutation-invariant model used for modeling variable dependencies. CVE is
196
      based on the Cross-Variable Transformer (Liu et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2023b), which treats
197   the input data as a sequence of variables to capture complex dependencies among them.
198   The hallmark of CVE lies in its novel approach to token partitioning. Unlike traditional
199   methods, CVE segments tokens along the variable dimension, with each token representing
200   different temporal instances of the same variable. This is achieved by transposing the input
201   data. The process is illustrated as follows:
202

210   The operational sequence begins by transposing the input data Xenc to form V0 , where V
211   is a matrix containing D embedded tokens, each with a dimension of S. D is equal to the
212
      number of variables, S is the length of time series, and weight is a learnable parameter.
      Here, V0 ∈ RD×S represents the initial embedded form of the input. The superscript in                                                                               [tVnS]

      V(m+1) indicates the layer index in the progression of transformations.

215   Each subsequent layer V(m+1) is generated by applying a TransformerBlock to the out-
      put of the previous layer Vm . This process is repeated for m ∈ {0, 1, . . . , M − 1}. The

      TransformerBlock typically consists of self-attention mechanisms and a shared feed-forward
217   network (FFN), allowing the variable tokens within V to interact and be processed indepen-
218   dently at each layer. This iterative process enriches the data representation by capturing
219   complex dependencies and patterns.
220
      Finally, the Projection operation transforms the output of the last Transformer layer VM
221
      and the original input data Xenc into a common space, which is then added with a learnable
222
      weight weight to obtain the final output ZCVE , where ZCVE ∈ RO×D and O represents
223   the prediction length. This output is then used as input to the next phase of learning, the
224   Cross-Temporal Encoder (CTE). To address the issue of distribution shift, CVE employs a         [tVnS]
225   reversible instance normalization (RevIN) module (Kim et al., 2021). This module, charac-
226   terized by its symmetrical structure, can remove and restore the statistical information of
227   time series instances, thereby enhancing the model’s stability during the prediction process.
228
      CVE channels the extracted features into a projection layer to generate first-stage predic-
229   tions, deliberately omitting a Transformer decoder. This approach stems from the decoder’s
230   inherent assumption of future sequence invisibility, which overlooks the constraining influ-
231   ence of future sequences on historical data. Additionally, the Transformer module within
232   CVE operates predominantly as a feature extractor rather than a sequence generator, given
233   the absence of temporal interrelations among different variables.
234
235   3.2   Cross-Temporal Encoder (CTE)
236
237   The CTE plays a crucial role in modeling the temporal dependencies. CTE divides time
238   series dependence into two parts: historical sequences dependence and predictive sequences
239   dependence. It processes inputs that include the outputs of the original historical sequences
240
      combined with the results from the CVE. This combination of data allows the CTE to effec-
      tively capture the temporal dependencies of the history sequences and prediction sequences,
241
      overcoming the CVE stage’s limitations in recognizing dynamic temporal characteristics.
242
243   The output of the CTE is then combined with the output of the CVE through an additive
244   fusion process to optimize the residual between the CTE and the predictive sequence. The
245   CTE is simply expressed as:

261   Prediction Sequence Temporal Dependency (PSTD) The role of PSTD is to model
262   the time dependence of prediction sequences. The PSTD block consists of a convolutional
263   layer and employs a concatenation operation to ensure that no information is lost from the
264   input. To avoid performance degradation and the risk of overfitting due to an excess of
265   features, we employ point-wise convolutions to construct a Feature Down-Sample (FDS)
266
      module, which halves the input features.
267   Historical Sequence Temporal Dependency (HSTD) The role of HSTD is to model
268   the time dependence of historical sequences. The HSTD block consists of a convolutional
269   layer and employs a residual connection to ensure that important historical information is
      retained and to prevent performance degradation as the network deepens.

289   3.3       Training process
290
291   As shown in Figure3, first, during the variable dependence learning phase, the permutation-
292   invariant CVE completely disregards the temporal dependence of the sequence and only
293   extracts cross-features between variables, generating an initial prediction sequence. At the
294   same time, the CTE remains frozen at this stage. Next, HSTD extracts the temporal features
295
      of the historical sequences, while PSTD extracts the temporal features of the prediction
      sequences. The outputs of HSTD and PSTD are then fused to correct the initial prediction
296
      from the variable dependence learning phase (residual fitting). At the same time, the CVE
297
      and CTE model parameters are updated through backpropagation.

693   A.2    Baselines
694
695   iTransformer (Liu et al., 2024) introduces an innovative inversion of the traditional Trans-
696   former architecture for time series forecasting. Instead of embedding time steps, iTrans-
697   former treats each variable as an independent token, using self-attention to capture multi-

      D            Performance with increasing lookback length
919
920
      To investigate the impact of increasing lookback length on model performance, we conducted
921   comparative experiments across different input sequence lengths (L). As shown in Figure 10,
922   we evaluate TVDN against state-of-the-art baselines on the electricity dataset under both
923   short-term (T=96) and long-term (T=720) forecasting scenarios.
924
      Previous studies have observed that increasing lookback length does not necessarily improve
925
      forecasting performance in Transformer-based models, primarily due to distracted attention
926
      on growing input sequences (Zeng et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2023b). Our
927   experimental results reveal distinct patterns: while traditional Transformer-based models
928   show inconsistent performance with increased lookback lengths, TVDN demonstrates robust
929   and improving performance as L increases from 24 to 720.
930
      For T=96, TVDN’s MSE steadily decreases, effectively utilizing longer historical informa-
931
      tion. PatchTST and DLinear also show improvements with increasing lookback lengths, but
932
      their performances are worse than TVDN. In contrast, Transformer, FEDformer, and Auto-
933   former exhibit relatively unstable performance patterns, confirming the attention distraction
934   phenomenon noted in previous works(Liu et al., 2024).
935
936
      The advantage of TVDN becomes more pronounced in the long-term forecasting scenario
      (T=720). TVDN’s performance is consistently better than the other models, including
937
      PatchTST and DLinear. While Autoformer and Transformer show significant fluctuations,
938
      particularly in the L=48 to L=96 range, TVDN maintains stable performance and achieves
939   optimal results in the L=192-336 range. This demonstrates TVDN’s superior capability
940   in handling longer sequences decrease suffering from the attention distraction issues that
941   plague traditional Transformer architectures.

1114   perceive the data-driven learning of the Transformer model as two distinct parts. The first

1116   self-attention and feed-forward networks (FFNs). The second part is the decoder, which,
1117   in conjunction with the encoder’s output, models the associative relationships of the target
1118
       sequence.
1119   To investigate which part primarily contributes to the Transformer model’s benefits, we
1120   conducted an extreme experiment. This study tested the original Transformer model and
1121   a model using only the Transformer decoder on the Electricity and Traﬀic datasets. For
1122   the decoder-only model, we retained few historical sequences as start tokens for the Trans-
1123   former’s decoder, thereby minimizing the use of historical sequence information as much as
1124
       possible.

1126   we observed significant overfitting. As shown in the figure, despite setting a relatively small

1128   loss decreasing while the validation set loss increases. Moreover, the losses for both the
1129   validation and test sets stabilize quickly.

1246   Theoretical Motivation Previous studies have highlighted that Cross-temporal Trans-
1247   formers are prone to bad local minima and are harder to converge to their true solutions
1248   Ilbert et al. (2024). Modeling cross-temporal relationships first can provide an unstable
1249   optimization starting point for subsequent cross-variable learning. In contrast, starting
1250   with cross-variable modeling helps establish a stable inter-variable relationship structure
1251   Liu et al. (2024); Gao et al. (2023b), which in turn provides a better optimization starting
1252   point for cross-temporal learning. This order increases the likelihood of convergence to the
1253
       true solution and improves the overall performance of the model.
1254   Experimental Evidence To validate the importance of this modeling order, we conducted
1255   experiments where the order of learning was reversed. The results clearly demonstrate that
1256   the proposed sequence of learning cross-variable relationships first (CVE) followed by cross-
1257   temporal relationships (CTE) outperforms the reversed order. The results are summarized

1275   In multivariate time series analysis, the temporal relationships between variables manifest
1276
       as instantaneous and lagged effects. For example, in a biomedical time series, multiple
       physiological signals (e.g., heart rate and blood pressure) may be transiently correlated
1277
       simultaneously. In some cases, there may be delayed effects between some variables. For
1278
       example, the impact of temperature change on plant growth is usually gradual.
1279
1280   While our paper focuses on developing a general foundation model for various temporal data
1281   types, emphasizing the interaction between cross-variable and temporal dependencies, we
1282
       should have explicitly discussed these temporal relationship types.
1283   Cross-variable learning: Can capture interactions between variables at same or
1284   different timesteps but overlook the specific time ordering. In the cross-variable
1285   learning stage, the model can capture interactions between variables at different timesteps
                   j                                                              j
1286   (Vti and V(t+∆) ), where Vti represents the i-th variable at time t, and V(t+∆)  represents
1287   the j-th variable at time (t + ∆). The temporal offset ∆ allows the model to capture
1288   instantaneous effects (when ∆ = 0) and lagged effects (when ∆ ̸= 0). This formulation
1289   maintains temporal invariance, meaning the model can identify relationships regardless of
1290   the specific time ordering of the variables.
1291   Cross-temporal learning: Incremental learning instead of siloed learning. Our
1292   temporal learning component incrementally builds upon the cross-variable relationships
1293   identified in the first stage. Instead of treating these interactions in isolation, we integrate
1294   them to capture instantaneous and lagged effects better. This comprehensive approach en-
1295   sures that our model effectively captures complex temporal dynamics, including direct and
       delayed influences between variables.
