009                                           Abstract
010
011           Many machine learning algorithms solving various problems are available for
012           metric spaces. While there are plenty of distances for vector spaces, much
013           less exists for structured data (rooted heterogeneous trees) stored in popular
014           formats like JSON, XML, ProtoBuffer, MessagePack, etc. This paper
015           introduces the Hierarchically-structured Tree Distance (HTD) designed
016
              especially for these data. The HTD distance is modular with differentiable
              parameters weighting the importance of different sub-spaces. This allows
017
              the distance to be tailored to a given dataset and task, such as classification,
018
              clustering, and anomaly detection. The extensive experimental comparison
019           shows that distance-based algorithms with the proposed HTD distance
020           are competitive to state-of-the-art methods based on neural networks with
021           orders of magnitude more parameters. Furthermore, we show that HTD is
022           more suited to analyze heterogeneous Graph Neural Networks than Tree
023           Mover’s Distance.
024

      1   Introduction
027
      Most machine learning tasks can be approached by algorithms relying on the existence of
028
      distance. These tasks include classification (Fix & Hodges, 1951), anomaly detection (Breunig
029
      et al., 2000; Knorr et al., 2000), clustering (Rdusseeun & Kaufman, 1987; Sibson, 1973)),
030   dimensionality reduction for visualization (McInnes et al., 2018), indexing methods for
031   fast retrieval (Zezula et al., 2006), explanation (Chen et al., 2019; Guidotti, 2022), and
032   density estimation (Williams & Rasmussen, 2006). A suitable distance on a dataset of
033   interest therefore makes all this vast prior art readily available for solving downstream tasks.
034   Distance is also essential for studying theoretical properties of algorithms (Chuang & Jegelka,
035   2022).
036   While for Euclidean spaces distances are well known, it is much harder to define them on
037   objects with variable dimensional objects such as trees or graphs. A particularly important
038   but neglected type of objects are those stored in structured data formats such as JSON,
039   XML, or Protobuffer. These formats are popular among engineers since they allow them to
040   logically organize data with increasing levels of detail, which is natural for humans. Moreover,
041   the contemporary internet experience relies on exchanging messages stored in these data
042   formats.
043   Data stored in structured data formats, further called HS-Trees are rooted trees of fixed
044   depth, where a large number of nodes have different semantics and structure and where
045   some nodes can have a fixed number of edges (and child). These properties were exploited in
046   some supervised learning methods (Socher et al., 2011; Shuai et al., 2016; Tai et al., 2015;
047   Cheng et al., 2018; Woof & Chen, 2020) offering properties not available for general graphs,
048   such as theoretical guarantees due to an extension of approximation theorem (Pevny &
049   Kovarik, 2019), and low computational complexity, as single pass from leaves to root (Mandlík
050
      et al., 2022) is sufficient. Furthermore as shown in Chuang & Jegelka (2022) for graph
      neural networks (GNN) based on message passing process samples from HS-Trees when the
051
      computation graph is unrolled.
052
053   Despite the practical importance and ubiquity of HS-Trees, there is very little prior art
      about distance on HS-Trees. In Šopík & Strenáčik (2022) (further called TED) tree-edit

074   The performance of HTD distance is experimentally evaluated on i) supervised learning,
075   ii) anomaly detection, iii) analysis of heterogenous GNNs, (iv) clustering (presented in the
076   appendix due to lack of space), and (v) inside UMAP for visualization. The experimental
077
      results show that distance-based algorithms with the proposed distance are competitive (and
      frequently better) to state-of-the-art methods based on neural networks (Pevny & Kovarik,
078
      2019; Mandlík et al., 2022) while having a few orders of magnitude fewer parameters. We
079

080   graphs than Tree Mover’s Distance with the homogenization (Chuang & Jegelka, 2022).

      2   Background
090
      This section first defines schema, which is an important concept in the definition of HS-Trees,
091
      and then shows their relation to the computation graph of GNNs. The relation of HS-Trees
092
      to data stored in structured formats, like JSON, is left to the Appendix C.
093
094   The HTD distance is defined for samples with the same schema. Schema corresponds to "data
095   type" in programming languages, message type in protocol buffers, schema in JSON (Pezoa
096
      et al., 2016), and document type definition in XML files (Farrell & Lausen, 2007). Schema
      defines the set of possible values, their semantics, and the structure of the data (type of
097
      nodes and their branching). To prevent confusion, schemas are always denoted by blackboard
098
      letters. x ∈ S denotes that sample x is from the schema S, but one may also say that sample
099   x has schema S.
100
101   The definition requires the introduction of elementary data types, which are simple data
102
      types like numbers, tensors of fixed dimension, categorical variables, and strings. A second
      key part of the schema is multiset, denoted as J·K, which corresponds in structured formats to
103
      unordered arrays with possibly repeated elements. The third key component is the dictionary
104
      (hashmap), which introduces heterogeneity into the data. Formal definition follows.
105
106
      Definition 2.1 (Schema). The set of all schemas S, and the element of relation ‘∈’ is
      defined recursively as follows:
107

117   In Definition 2.1, Bags are used to represent multisets and sequences of arbitrary (including
118   empty) size. They are assumed to be permutation invariant; therefore, the position has to be
119   encoded through position encoding. Importantly, all items of the Bag have the same schema.
120   Models accepting Bags need to handle inputs of arbitrary lengths (or size) requiring some form
121   of aggregation which is either explicit through functions like mean, sum, and max (Muandet
122   et al., 2012; Zaheer et al., 2017; Pevný & Somol, 2017) or through recurrence (Hochreiter &
123   Schmidhuber, 1997). Dict represent Cartesian products of a fixed number of subspaces with
124
      a fixed schema. Neural networks processing Dict typically projects individual subspaces to a
      vector space and then concatenate the representations. The concatenation is impossible for
125
      Bags because they can have arbitrary sizes. Universal approximation theorem for HS-Trees
126
      has been proved in Pevny & Kovarik (2019).

      3     Metric on HS-Trees
200
201
      This section presents the HTD on the space of HS-Trees with the same schema. The con-
202
      struction is recursive and assumes the existence of distances on leaves, bags, and dictionaries.
203   These choices determine the final properties. Therefore, they are discussed first, and then
204   HTD is defined in Section 3.4.
205
206   3.1    Metric on Leaves
207
208   Leaves contain various elementary data types (real numbers, tensors of fixed dimensions,
209   categorical variables, and strings). It is assumed the distance on Leaves exists, and its
210   definition is outside of the scope of this work, but the most common ones are listed below.
211   Distances between tensors on Euclidean spaces are usually measured by distances induced by
212   Lp norms, of which L1 and L2 are the most popular. Categorical data are usually encoded
213   as one-hot vectors, and distances induced by Lp norms collapse to zero / one if two values
214   are equal/unequal. Strings offer two conceptually different approaches. The first, such as
215   the Levenshtein (Levenshtein, 1966) or Jaro-Winkler distance (Winkler, 1990), are defined
      directly on the space of all strings. A popular alternative is to measure the distance in the

300
301   The above distances defined on Dicts and Bags did not make any assumptions on the set of
302   child items except that there exists a distance. This generality is important for the recursive
303
      definition of distance on HS-Trees.
304   Definition 3.3 (HS-Tree distance (HTD)). Let H be an arbitrary fixed schema of HS-Trees
305   as defined in 2.1. Then the HTD distance dH on H is defined recursively:
306         1. Leaves: If H is a leaf, then distance dH is defined by a distance for the appropriate
307            data type (see Section 3.1).
308         2. Bags: If H = JIK is a Bag, then the distance dH is defined by a distance on (multi-
309            )sets (see Section 3.3) with distance on items dI being defined according to schema
310            I.
311
            3. Dicts: Let H = {(ki , Si )}mi=1 be Dict. Then the distance dH is as a distance on
               product of spaces (see Section 3.2), where distances dSi on sub-spaces are defined
312
               according to schemas {Si }mi=1 .
313
314   Theorem 2. Let H be an arbitrary fixed schema from HS-Trees. Then an HTD distance
315
      exists on H.
316
      The theorem is a consequence of the recursive definition. Formal proof is in Appendix D.

334   4   Related Work
335
336
      Tree-edit distance (Zhang & Shasha, 1989) (TED) quantifies structural dissimilarity between
337
      rooted trees by calculating the minimum edit operations required for transformation. TED’s
338
      applications range from computational biology to natural language processing (Sidorov
339   et al., 2015). TED has been extended to heterogeoenous trees (Bille, 2005) and to HS-
340   Trees in Šopík & Strenáčik (2022). Tree-edit distances are non-differentiable, and their
341   computational complexity is cubic (Demaine et al., 2009).
342
343
      A pseudo-distance for rooted homogenous trees (TMD) with fixed depth was proposed
      in Chuang & Jegelka (2022) to study properties of GNNs since the computational graph of
344
      GNNs equals to a tree (Xu et al., 2019; Errica & Niepert, 2023). The drawbacks of TMD are
345
      that it does not allow heterogeneous inner nodes and Leaves, it is not a proper distance, and
346   its computational complexity is cubic. Interestingly, TMD implicitly uses product metric (4)
347   with weights w = 1 and L1 distance to combine distance on features of the node with that of
348   the neighborhood. The HS-Trees formalism makes it explicit that TMD is a special case of
349   HTD for homogeneous graphs, using a different product metric and fixed weights.
350
      Tree Kernels (Culotta & Sorensen, 2004; Schölkopf et al., 2004) transforms the tree structures
351
      into strings, which are then compared by String Kernels (Lodhi et al., 2002) similar to the
352   Levenshtein distance. Kernels for sets viewed as samples from probability distributions have
353   been proposed (Gretton et al., 2005).
354
355
      Several methodologies emerged for supervised learning on rooted trees (Tai et al., 2015; Cheng
      et al., 2018; Socher et al., 2011; Mandlík et al., 2022; Woof & Chen, 2020), DAGs (Thost &
356
      Chen, 2021), and sets (Zaheer et al., 2017), but none of them is using distance. Recently,
357
      sum-product networks have been extended to HS-Trees (Papez et al., 2024), offering a
358   tractable probabilistic model.
359

365

      Modularity: The modularity of HTD is improved by selecting distances on Leafs and Bags.

454   accuracy can be achieved either by Haussdorff or Chamfer distance, which computational

456   contains categorical Leaves, which requires the recognition of multisets. Other datasets have
457   at least one leaf with real values so distances on sets are, therefore, sufficient.

459   that the latter was homogenized as needed for the TMD distance (Chuang & Jegelka, 2022).

461   3% and allows using the cheap Haussdorff with quadratic complexity. A similar experiment
462   reported below on GNNs led to the same results.

497   5.3    Analysis of Graph Neural Networks
498
499   Tree-Movers Distance (TMD) has been introduced in Chuang & Jegelka (2022) to study the
500   stability of homogeneous GNNs (Hamilton et
                                                al., 2018) by showing that their output correlates

                                                                                     1000,1000
501   with the TMD distance, estimated as corr {∥gnn(vi ) − gnn(vj )∥2 , d(vi , vj )}i,j=1       where
502   gnn(v) is embedding of vertex v provided by GNN and d(vi , vj ) is a distance between
503   computation trees of vertices vi and vj . The proposed HTD enables us to extend this
504   analysis to heterogenous GNNs since it adapts to their computational graph better than the
505   homogenization suggested in Chuang & Jegelka (2022).
506
      We measure the correlation on IMDB dataset (Fu et al., 2020), which is a heterogeneous
507   graph with three types of nodes (actors, directors, movies) and four types of edges. The goal
508   to predict the type of the actor based on its neighborhood is solved by heterogeneous (Zhang
509   et al., 2019) and homogeneous GNNs. The homogeneous variant of IMDB was created by
510   method from PyTorch geometric (Fey & Lenssen, 2019), which corresponds to the method
511   recommended in Chuang & Jegelka (2022).
512
      To demonstrate that the GNNs used for correlation analysis are well trained, Table 6b shows
513   an F1-score on the testing set. The table also shows scores of other prior art (Hong et al.,
514   2020; Jin et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2023; Lv et al., 2021) and for curiosity, an SVM classifier
515   with the proposed distance used in Section 5.1. The results show that heterogeneous GNNs
516   perform better than their homogeneous counterparts. Surprisingly, SVM with 6 parameters
517   for metric and 3600 of SVM multipliers performs frequently better than GNNs with orders
518   of magnitude more parameters.
519   The correlation coefficient between GNNs on heterogeneous and homogenized graphs and
520   HTD on heterogeneous and homogenized trees is shown in Table 6a. As expected, the
521   highest correlation occurs when the type of distance matches the type of computation
522   graph. Specifically, the heterogeneous/homogeneous tree distance correlates with heteroge-
523   neous/homogeneous GNNs, and the correlation decreases in the case of mismatch.

      6     Conclusion
526
527
      This paper introduced Hierarchically-Structured Tree Distance (HTD), measuring the distance
528   between samples emerging from popular data storage formats (e.g., JSON, XML, and
529   ProtoBuffer) and naturally representing message passes in heterogeneous GNNs. We have
530   demonstrated that this distance, paired with well-known distance and kernel-based algorithms,
531   can solve common machine learning tasks like classification, anomaly detection, visualization,
532   and clustering with performance frequently better than the state-of-the-art methods based
533   on neural networks with orders of magnitude more parameters. A good performance of HTD
534   is owed to its flexible differentiable parametrization, which allows it to be optimized for a
535   given problem by common metric-learning algorithms.
536   The HTD distance lays a foundation for future research of tree-structured data and the
537   development of new generative and self-supervised methods. We envision the use of HTD as
538   a reconstruction loss in variational and masked autoencoders, potentially leading to strong
539   pre-training methods for HS-Trees, which might be important for industry storing data in
      structured formats.

      A    Implementation details
757
758   This section provides details about methods used in our experiments for parameter learning.
759   Specifically, the parameters we learn are the weights from dWPM . We outline the specifics of
760   each method and how they were used in the study. Additionally, Tables 7 and 8 lists ranges
761   of hyperparameters. These tables serve as a reference for understanding the settings and
762   configurations used in our experiments. It’s important to note that all experiments were
763   repeated five times with different train/validation/test splits, where the validation splits
764   were used for selecting the best hyperparameters, including HTD’s parameters.
765
766   Random Sampling The most naive but powerful method to set weights is random
767
      sampling (RS). Weights are sampled from a predefined distribution, and the best model
      (kNN and SVM) is selected according to the accuracy on the validation split. RS is inexpensive
768
      to compute for a single realization of weights as it requires no gradients. However, to find
769
      optimal parameters, hundreds or thousands of combinations (depending on the dataset)
770   must be evaluated. For our study, we evaluated 500 random realizations of weights for each
771   dataset.
772
773   Contrastive Learning The second approach used contrastive learning, which uses labels
774   and needs the distance function to be differentiable. We use the usual triplet loss (Weinberger
775   & Saul, 2009), Ltr , as
776                      Ltr = max(d(xa , xp ) − d(xa , xn ) + 1, 0) + β · ∥θ − γ∥2 ,
777
      where xa , xp , xn are the anchor, positive, and negative samples, respectively, and theta
778
      represents the parameters of the metric d.
779
780   The process of sampling triplets xa , xp , xn for minibatches is often more important than
781   selecting appropriate values for β and γ. To address this, we explored three different
782
      strategies: Random, Batch Hard, and Alternating.
783        1. Random: This method selects an anchor randomly, and then, depending on its
784           label, positive and negative samples are again sampled randomly.
785
           2. Batch Hard: first samples a subset (batch) of samples and computes the pairwise
786           distance using the distance function d with the current θ. Then, it randomly samples
787           the anchor and creates the most difficult triplet by sampling the most distant
788           positive sample and the closest negative samples. This method encourages the
789           distance function d to distinguish the most challenging observations. However, it
790           may fail in the presence of multiple clusters and outliers.
791        3. Alternating: alternates between the Random and Batch Hard methods.
792
793   Kernel Learning with Gaussian Process Since HTD is a proper metric, it can be
794   readily used in a kernel function applicable within Support Vector Machines (SVMs) or
795   Gaussian processes. Optimization of kernel’s parameters for SVM is difficult to optimize,
796   but it is simpler in Gaussian Processes.
797   Gaussian Process acts as a probability distribution over training dataset x = {x1 , . . . , xn }
798
799                                 GP (x) = N m(x), K(x, x) ,
800
      where m(x) is mean function and K(x, x) is covariance (kernel) matrix
801                                                                   
802                                      k(x1 , x1 ) . . . k(x1 , xn )
803                          K(x, x) = 
                                            ..       ..       ..      
                                              .          .      .      
804                                           k(xn , x1 ) . . .   k(xn , xn ),
805
806
      with respect to kernel function k, which we define as k(x, y) = exp(−d(x, y)).
807   This means that when maximizing the likelihood of a Gaussian process, we can compute
808   gradients not only with respect to the covariance matrix K or the kernel function k but
809   also with respect to the parameters of the distance function d. This enables us to use this
      approach to learn the parameters of d.

915
916   This section uses the JSON format as a prototypical example of hierarchical formats. Data
917   in JSON format are stored by combining elementary data types: Strings, Numbers, Booleans,
      and Null with two composite data types: Objects and Arrays.

      The elementary data types corresponds to Leaf in HS-Trees, as they do not have children.
919   Distances for these data types exist and are discussed in Section 3.1.
920
921   Object is a key-value dictionary in which the key is restricted to String, and values can be
922
      any JSON data type. Keys must be unique and serve as a semantic data identifier in the
      corresponding value field. Object therefore corresponds to Dict in HS-Trees.
923
924   Array is a sequence of elements of any JSON data type with arbitrary length. In general,
925   items of the array do not have to be of the same data type (schema in the terminology of this
926   paper), but we impose this restriction as it is very common in the industry. This work also
927   assumes the data in arrays to be unordered, which can be restored using position encoding.
928
      With that, Arrays maps to Bag in HS-Trees.

934   D    Proof of Theorem 2
935
936   Theorem 2. Let H be an arbitrarily fixed schema of HS-Trees. Then, an HTD distance
937   exists in H.
938
939
      Proof. Due to the recursive construction of the set of all schemas, the proof is carried by
940
      induction.
941
942   Let H be a schema with depth 0, which means that all samples x ∈ H are trees with depth 0,
943   i.e. they are Leaves. Then H is an elementary data type, and the distance can be computed
944
      by an appropriate choice listed in Section 3.1.
945   Carrying the induction, we now assume to be able to define distance for all schemas of depth
946   l − 1, and we want to define distance on samples of schema H with depth l, where l > 0. Let
947   x, y ∈ H, then the top-node of x and y is either of type Bag or Dict.
948   Let’s first assume roots to be Bags and denote {xi }ni=1 and {yj }m j=1 items (childs) of x and
949   y respectively. By definition of HS-Trees and their schema 2.1, all items xi and yi have the
950   same schema I with a depth l − 1, and by induction assumption, there exists a distance
951   dI . The existence of dI is sufficient to define a distance between (multi)-sets as discussed in
952   Section 3.3.
953
      Alternatively, roots are Dict. Then x = {(ki , xi )}ni=1 and y = {(ki , yi )}ni=1 , where {ki }ni=1 are
954   unique keys and xi , yi are corresponding values with the schema Si of length at most l − 1.
955   By induction principle, there exist distances dSi on Si , which are the sufficient condition
956   to define distance between x, y using for example the weighted product metric discussed in
957   Section 3.2.

      E    Example of computing the complexity
961
      Let’s demonstrate the complexity of the distance between two samples in Fig. 2. For simplicity,
962
      we assume that Bags (labeled c) have the same length. The computation starts by computing
963
      distances between Leaf nodes f and g. Although L2 metric has linear complexity with respect
964   to its dimension, denoted by cf and cg , the Bag distances requires to compute all pairwise
965   distances and therefore the complexity with respect to their number, i.e. O(|f |2 (cf + cg )),
966   where |f | represents the number of Leaves with label f. The complexity of the dWPM for
967   e is the sum of complexities of leaves, which was already included. The complexity of
968   computing distance on Bags of node with label c is either cubic, which makes the complexity
969   O(|f |2 (cf + cg ) + |f |3 ), or quadratic which yields complexity O(|f |2 (cf + cg + 1)). To finish
970   the computation of distance, we need to compute the distance of Leaves b and d and add
971   it to the distance c. The final complexity is therefore O(|f |2 (cf + cg ) + |f |3 + cb + cd ) or
      O(|f |2 (cf + cg + 1) + cb + cd ).
