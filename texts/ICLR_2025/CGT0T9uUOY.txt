009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Lifting is an effective technique for producing a 3D scene segmentation by un-
012            projecting multi-view 2D instance segmentations into a common 3D space. Ex-
013            isting state-of-the-art lifting methods leverage contrastive learning to learn a fea-
014            ture field, but rely on a hyperparameter-sensitive and error-prone clustering post-
015            process for segmentation prediction, leading to inferior performance. In this paper,
016
               we propose a new unified object-aware lifting approach in a 3D Gaussian Splatting
               field, introducing a novel learnable object-level codebook to account for objects
017
               in the 3D scene for an explicit object-level understanding. To start, we augment
018
               each Gaussian point with an additional Gaussian-level feature learned using a con-
019            trastive loss. More importantly, enabled by our object-level codebook formulation,
020            we associate the encoded object-level features with Gaussian-level point features
021            for segmentation predictions. Further, we design two novel modules, the associ-
022            ation learning module and the noisy label filtering module, to achieve effective
023            and robust codebook learning. We conduct experiments on three benchmarks, i.e.,
024            LERF-Masked, Replica, and Messy Rooms datasets. Both qualitative and quanti-
025            tative results manifest that our new approach significantly outperforms the existing
026            methods in terms of segmentation quality and time efficiency.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Accurate 3D scene segmentation enhances scene understanding and facilitates scene editing, ben-
031   efiting many downstream applications in virtual reality, augmented reality, and robotics. However,
032   accurate 3D scene segmentation is challenging to obtain, due to limited 3D dataset size and labor-
033   intensive manual labeling in 3D. To bypass these challenges, recent studies (Zhi et al., 2021; Sid-
034   diqui et al., 2023; Bhalgat et al., 2023) suggest lifting 2D segmentations predicted by foundation
035   models (Kirillov et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2022) to the 3D scene modeled by a radiance field for
036   instance-level understanding. Yet, 2D instance segmentations predicted by models like SAM (Kir-
037   illov et al., 2023) lack consistency across different views, e.g., the same object may have different
038   IDs when viewed from different angles, leading to conflicting supervision. Besides, inferior seg-
039
      mentations, e.g., under- or over-segmentation, also make the lifting process challenging.
040   Various strategies have been proposed to address the above issues. An early work Panoptic Lift-
041   ing (Siddiqui et al., 2023) trains a NeRF to render instance predictions and matches the model’s
042   3D predictions with the initial 2D segmentation masks (see Fig. 1 (a)). However, its learned NeRF
043   representation lacks semantically meaningful instance features to effectively represent objects, thus
044   limiting its performance. Subsequently, (Ye et al., 2023; Lyu et al., 2024) propose object associa-
045
      tion techniques as a preprocessing to prepare view-consistent 2D segmentation maps with improved
      multi-view consistency (see Fig. 1 (b)). However, the preprocessing stage often struggles to pro-
046
      duce accurate results and the accumulated error can further degrade the performance. The recent
047
      state-of-the-art methods (Bhalgat et al., 2023; Ying et al., 2024) encode instance information in the
048   feature field using contrastive learning and apply a clustering as a postprocessing to produce the fi-
049   nal segmentations (see Fig. 1 (c)). Though significant improvements are achieved, without a global
050   object-level understanding across different views, their segmentation capability is still bounded.
051   Moreover, their performance is always constrained by the naive clustering postprocess, which is
052   hyperparameter-sensitive and also induces error accumulation. Given the above concerns, we come
053   up with this question: “Can we have a unified lifting framework by incorporating an explicit object-
      level understanding for accurate 3D scene segmentation, without pre- or post-processing?”

      In this work, we propose a new unified object-aware lifting pipeline for accurate 3D scene seg-
078
      mentation, facilitating the generation of coherent and view-consistent instance segmentation across
079   different views. We exploit the recent advancement of the radiance field, i.e., 3D Gaussian splatting
080   (3D-GS) (Kerbl et al., 2023), as the 3D scene representation due to its superior efficiency and ren-
081   dering quality. Basically, we augment each Gaussian point in 3D-GS with a Gaussian-level feature
082   and learn these features using contrastive learning defined in each individual view. In particular, we
083   introduce a novel object-level codebook to represent each object in the 3D scene. This codebook
084   is further associated with the rendered Gaussian-level features to predict segmentation results, en-
085   hancing object-level awareness during training. Moreover, we present effective learning strategies
086   to optimize the object-level codebook. First, we introduce a novel association learning module, in
087
      which we design an area-aware ID mapping algorithm to generate pseudo-labels of association with
      enhanced multi-view consistency. Additionally, we present two complementary loss functions, i.e.,
088
      sparsity and concentration parts, to achieve more reliable object-level understanding. Second, we
089
      design a novel noisy label filtering module to enhance the robustness of our method by estimating
090   an uncertainty map for the segmentation masks, leveraging the learned Gaussian-level features in
091   a self-supervised manner. During inference, we obtain novel-view instance segmentation results
092   without any pre- or post-processing, effectively avoiding error accumulation.
093
      To evaluate the effectiveness of our method, we conduct experiments on the widely-used LERF-
094
      Masked (Ye et al., 2023) dataset and the indoor scene dataset, Replica (Straub et al., 2019). Both
095
      quantitative and qualitative results demonstrate that our method outperforms all the existing lifting
096   methods by a notable margin. Furthermore, we conduct additional experiments on the challenging
097   Messy Rooms dataset (Bhalgat et al., 2023), where each scene contains up to 500 objects, demon-
098   strating the scalability of our method in handling large numbers of objects.
099
      Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
100
101
             • We propose a new unified object-aware lifting pipeline for accurate 3D scene segmentation
102
               by introducing an object-level codebook representation.
103
104          • We present a novel association learning module and a noisy label filtering module to facil-
105            itate effective learning of the object-level codebook.
106
107          • We set a new state-of-the-art performance on multiple datasets and demonstrate strong
               scalability in handling large numbers of objects.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
109
110   Radiance field: from implicit to explict. Radiance field emerges as a promising representa-
111   tion for reconstructing 3D scenes with various properties, e.g., geometries, colors, and seman-
112   tics, from only 2D inputs such as RGB images and segmentation masks. Neural Radiance Field
113   (NeRF) (Mildenhall et al., 2021) models the radiance field using a neural network composed of lay-
114
      ers of multilayer perceptrons. Since then, various works attempt to improve the efficiency of NeRF,
      e.g., by explicitly formulating the field using 3D structures such as voxels (Chen et al., 2022; Liu
115
      et al., 2020) and hash grids (Müller et al., 2022). Later on, 3D Gaussian Splatting (3D-GS) (Kerbl
116
      et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024b; Cheng et al., 2024; Huang et al.,
117   2024; Yu et al., 2024) is introduced to model the radiance field as a set of explicit Gaussian points.
118   This approach allows for a splatting-style rendering (Kopanas et al., 2021), which is highly efficient
119   and demonstrates great potential of real-time rendering. Given these advantages, we employ 3D-GS
120   as the backbone representation in our framework for creating consistent 3D segmentations.
121
      Segmentation: from 2D to 3D. Segmentation is a long-standing task in computer vision re-
122
      search. Recent progress witnesses advancements in 2D, thanks to the availability of large-scale
123   datasets. Notably, various foundation models, such as SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023) and its subsequent
124   works (Xiong et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023), show great performance in numerous 2D segmentation
125   tasks and demonstrate robust zero-shot segmentation capabilities.
126
      Beyond segmenting pixels in image level, 3D segmentation aims to partition 3D structures, such
127
      as point clouds and voxels (Zhou et al., 2021; Sirohi et al., 2021; Milioto et al., 2020; Gasperini
128
      et al., 2021), or to perform segmentation and 3D reconstruction simultaneously from input 2D im-
129   ages (Dahnert et al., 2021; Narita et al., 2019; Rosinol et al., 2020). However, due to tedious work
130   needed in collecting annotated 3D data, the scale of 3D datasets (e.g., 1,503 scenes in ScanNet (Dai
131   et al., 2017)) is usually at least one order of magnitude smaller than that of 2D datasets (e.g., 11M
132   diverse images and 1.1B high-quality segmentation masks in SA-1B (Kirillov et al., 2023)). Hence,
133   the trained models are applicable mostly to limited 3D object categories within the available dataset.
134   To effectively construct 3D segmentation, we propose lifting the segmentation results from 2D foun-
135   dation models by explicitly incorporating an object-level understanding of the 3D scene.
136
      Lifting 2D segmentation to 3D scene understanding in radiance field. Various works (Zhi et al.,
137
      2021; Qin et al., 2024; Bhalgat et al., 2023) propose leveraging radiance fields to lift independently-
138
      inferred 2D information into the 3D space for 3D scene segmentation and understanding. Some
139   works focus on semantic segmentation, aiming to infer semantic information in the 3D scene, such
140   as object properties and categories, where 2D segmentation predictions are obtained via differen-
141   tiable rendering. To accomplish this, most existing works tend to optimize a 3D radiance field,
142   which is supervised by semantic or feature maps derived from 2D foundation models. For example,
143   Semantic-NeRF (Zhi et al., 2021) optimizes an additional semantic field from 2D semantic maps for
144   novel-view semantic rendering. Besides, some studies (Zhang et al., 2024a; Qin et al., 2024; Kerr
145   et al., 2023) distill CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) or DINO (Oquab et al., 2023) features into a feature
146   radiance field to facilitate open-vocabulary semantic segmentation.
147   Unlike semantic segmentation, instance segmentation predicted by 2D foundation models, such as
148   SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023) and MaskFormer (Cheng et al., 2022), lack consistency across multiple
149   views. An early work, Panoptic Lifting (Siddiqui et al., 2023), formulates the radiance field as a dis-
150   tribution of instance IDs and employs the Hungarian algorithm for each 2D segmentation to obtain
151   pseudo labels as the supervision signal. To improve the performance, later works (Ye et al., 2023;
152   Lyu et al., 2024; Dou et al., 2024) attempt to pre-process the 2D instance segmentations (e.g., using
153
      video tracker (Cheng et al., 2023) or heuristic Gaussian matching (Lyu et al., 2024)) to simplify
      the task and obtain view-consistent labels for supervision. Recent state-of-the-art methods (Bhalgat
154
      et al., 2023; Ying et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2024; Choi et al., 2024; Dou et al., 2024) construct 3D
155
      consistent feature fields and supervise them using contrastive loss within each 2D segmentation.
156   This avoids the need to establish correspondences between different views. However, since radi-
157   ance fields contain only features, inferring the final segmentation requires an additional clustering
158   step, such as HDBSCAN (McInnes et al., 2017), which can be rather sensitive to the choice of the
159   hyperparameters. In this work, we propose a new unified object-aware lifting pipeline for accurate
160   3D scene segmentation, avoiding the need of pre- or post-processing. By formulating an object-
161   level codebook representation and designing dedicated modules for effective codebook learning, we
      obtain an object-level understanding of the scene to greatly enhance the segmentation quality.

233   explicit object-level understanding and require an additional clustering post-process to extract this
234   information for segmentation prediction (Bhalgat et al., 2023; Ying et al., 2024). Consequently,
235   these methods not only suffer from tedious hyperparameter tuning but also encounter issues such
236   as under- or over-segmentation due to the accumulated errors (see, e.g., Fig. 3). In contrast, we
237   propose to obtain an explicit object-level understanding of the 3D scene by directly learning from
238   the Gaussian-level features, rather than utilizing a post-processing.

      Implementation details. Our implementation is based on the official codebase of 3D-GS (Kerbl
368
      et al., 2023). We utilize the same photometric loss term in Kerbl et al. (2023) to optimize the as-
369
      sociated 3D Gaussian parameters. For the Gaussian-level features, we set the feature dimension to
370   16, following the baseline works such as OmniSeg3D-GS (Ying et al., 2024) and Gaussian Group-
371   ing (Ye et al., 2023). To optimize the Gaussian-level features, we apply the same contrastive loss
372   used in Ying et al. (2024). For the object-level codebook, we set the maximum object number L to
373   256 and use the proposed loss defined in Eq. 8 to optimize the object-level codebook from a random
374   initialization. Empirically, we set wclass = 1 × 10−3 and wconcen = 1 × 10−1 in our experiments. All
375   parameters are jointly optimized, with the number of training iterations set to 30,000 for all datasets.
376   More details are provided in the supplementary material.

      To demonstrate the scalability of our method across varying object quantities, we conduct addi-
420
      tional experiments on the widely-used Messy Rooms dataset (Bhalgat et al., 2023), which covers

422   protocol used in the previous work (Bhalgat et al., 2023) to calculate the metric that assesses the
423   consistency of instance IDs across multiple views (Siddiqui et al., 2023), denoting as PQscene in
424   Tab. 3. Specifically, we choose the segment with largest area in the generated instance segmenta-
425   tion across different views as the background, to generate the binary semantic segmentations for
426   PQscene metric calculations. This approach avoids the need to optimize an additional semantic fea-
427   ture in our method, as well as in all 3D-GS-based baselines. We compare our method with the
428   3D-GS-based baselines (i.e., OmniSeg3D-GS and Panoptic-Lifting-GS) and the NeRF-based base-
429
      lines (i.e., Panoptic Lifting (Siddiqui et al., 2023) and Contrastive Lift (Bhalgat et al., 2023)) for a
      comprehensive evaluation. As shown in Tab. 3, the quantitative results demonstrate that our method
430
      achieves improved performance compared to 3D-GS-based baselines, particularly in scenes with a
431
      large number of objects. Moreover, our method achieves results comparable to the current state-of-
      the-art NeRF-based method (Bhalgat et al., 2023), while requiring significantly less training time.
