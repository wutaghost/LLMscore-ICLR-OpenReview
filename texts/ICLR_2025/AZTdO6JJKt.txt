011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Deep Learning (DL) has advanced various fields by extracting complex patterns
014            from large datasets. However, the computational demands of DL models pose envi-
015
               ronmental and resource challenges. Deep Shift Neural Networks (DSNNs) improve
               the situation by leveraging shift operations to reduce computational complexity
016
               at inference. Compared to common DNNs, DSNNs are still less well understood
017
               and less well optimized. By leveraging AutoML techniques, we provide valuable
018            insights into the potential of DSNNs and how to design them in a better way. Since
019            we consider complementary objectives such as accuracy and energy consumption,
020            we combine state-of-the-art multi-fidelity (MF) HPO with multi-objective opti-
021            mization to find a set of Pareto optimal trade-offs on how to design DSNNs. Our
022            approach led to significantly better configurations of DSNNs regarding loss and
023            emissions compared to default DSNNs. This includes simultaneously increasing
024            performance by about 20% and reducing emissions by about 10%. Investigating
025            the behavior of quantized networks in terms of both emissions and accuracy, our ex-
026
               periments reveal surprising model-specific trade-offs, yielding the greatest energy
               savings. For example, in contrast to common expectations, selectively quantizing
027
               smaller portions of the network with low precision is optimal while retaining or
028
               improving performance. We corroborated these findings across multiple backbone
029            architectures, highlighting important nuances in quantization strategies and offering
030            an automated approach to balancing energy efficiency and model performance.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Deep Learning (DL) is a promising approach to extracting information from large datasets with
036   complex structures. This includes performing computations in IoT environments and on edge
037   devices (Li et al., 2018; Zhou et al., 2019), which can come with strict limitations on the energy
038   consumption. However, with the ever-increasing size and performance of such models due to the
039
      progress in science and industry, running these models is not free of computational costs (Sze et al.,
      2017) and minimizing this cost directly affects a model’s environmental impact (Schwartz et al.,
040
      2020). Even if resource consumption should not be considered crucial in view of environmental
041
      impact, efficiently designed neural networks free up resources that can be used for other tasks, e.g.,
042   edge computing or computations for advanced driver assistance systems (Howard et al., 2017). With
043   our approach, we contribute to DL by minimizing its environmental footprint and allow applications
044   in low-resource settings. Of particular interest to us are Deep Shift Neural Networks (DSNNs)
045   that offer great potential in reducing power consumption compared to traditional Deep Learning
046   models, e.g., by reducing the inference time by a factor of 4 (Elhoushi et al., 2021). Instead of
047   expensive floating point arithmetic, they leverage cheap shift operations — specifically, bit shifting —
048   as the computational unit, which boosts efficiency by replacing costly multiplication operations in
049   convolutional networks. Although DSNNs offer great promise, so far, the different design decisions,
050
      including training hyperparameters and shift architecture, have not been well-studied and there is
      little knowledge about their full potential. We suspect that the configuration of DSNNs has a huge
051
      impact on both performance and computational efficiency.
052
053   One of the key challenges with DSNNs is determining the appropriate level of precision for shift
      operations to minimize quantization errors without excessively increasing the computational load.

054
      To address this challenge, we propose to apply automated machine learning (AutoML) to DSNNs
055   to find their optimal configuration. This is achieved by hyperparameter optimization (HPO) (Bischl
056   et al., 2023) and a neural architecture search on a macro-level (Elsken et al., 2019). Integrating
057   multi-fidelity (MF) and multi-objective optimization (MO) techniques (Belakaria et al., 2020) facili-
058   tates an optimal exploration of the configuration space that trades off predictive performance and
059   energy consumption (Deb, 2014). To this end, we extended the SMAC3 approach (Lindauer et al.,
060   2022), a state-of-the-art HPO package (Eggensperger et al., 2021), such that its MO implementation
061   effectively balances the trade-off between achieving high predictive accuracy and minimizing energy
062   consumption. Employing tools like CodeCarbon (Lacoste et al., 2019; Lottick et al., 2019) during the
063
      training and evaluation phases provides insights into the energy consumption and carbon emissions
      associated with each model configuration. The MF aspect allows for the efficient use of computational
064
      resources by evaluating configurations at varying levels of approximation. Our work is in the spirit of
065
      Green AutoML (Tornede et al., 2023) by considering efficient AutoML for gaining insights into the
066   design of efficient DSNNs.
067
068   Contributions. Overall, we contribute to Green AutoML w.r.t. DSNNs by:
069
070         1. Specifying the first configuration space tailored to DSNNs which is efficiently optimized by
071            a combination of multi-objective and multi-fidelity AutoML approaches;
072         2. Empirically exploring how specific design choices in DSNNs lead to different trade-offs
073            between accuracy and energy efficiency, enabling stakeholders and researchers to leverage
074            these findings to develop energy-efficient applications that maintain high computational
075            accuracy; and
076         3. Identifying specific configurations of DSNNs that surpass the baseline results in both
077            dimensions of the performance-efficiency optimization problem.

079   2    R ELATED W ORK
080
081   Both multi-fidelity optimization (MF) (Bischl et al., 2023) and multi-objective optimization (MO)
082   (Morales-Hernández et al., 2022) for AutoML have gotten a lot of traction in recent years. The
083   combined integration of multi-fidelity multi-objective optimization (MFMO) has seen some ad-
084   vancements to enhance the efficiency of model training while minimizing environmental impact.
085   Belakaria et al. (2020) proposed an acquisition function based on output space entropy search for
086   multi-fidelity multi-objective Bayesian optimization (MFMO-BO-OSES). Their method addresses
087
      the exploration-exploitation dilemma by prioritizing the acquisition of data points that significantly
      reduce the entropy of the Pareto front. This approach enables more strategic sampling decisions and
088
      leverages lower-fidelity evaluations to approximate the Pareto front effectively, aligning with the
089
      sustainability goals of Green AutoML. Similarly, Schmucker et al. (2020) considered a combination
090   of multi-objective and multi-fidelity optimization but focused on fairness as the second objective.
091   With our MFMO approach, we contribute an algorithm tailored specifically for performing efficient
092   HPO tasks using BO, directly minimizing emissions in the process.
093
      A well-explored technique for reducing the computational complexity of neural networks themselves
094
      is network quantization. It involves lowering the precision of weights and activations, which decreases
095
      the model’s memory footprint and accelerates inference. Works by Courbariaux et al. (2015) and
096   Rastegari et al. (2016), for example, have demonstrated that techniques such as BinaryConnect
097   and XNOR-Net not only reduce computational requirements but also maintain near state-of-the-art
098   performance, underscoring the potential of quantization to balance performance with computational
099   efficiency. This has also been transferred into the field of LLMs, where 1-Bit transformer architectures
100   are used to address the challenges of increasing model size (Wang et al., 2023). Strongly related to
101   network quantization, Deep Shift Neural Networks (DSNNs), proposed by Elhoushi et al. (2021),
102   represent an advancement towards quantization combined with efficient operators. DSNNs employ
103   bitwise shift operations instead of traditional multiplications, thus further reducing the computa-
104
      tional overhead and power consumption. This innovation is particularly crucial for deploying Deep
      Learning models in power-sensitive or resource-constrained environments, further contributing to the
105
      environmental sustainability of AI technologies.
106
107   DSNNs offer a more balanced trade-off between computational efficiency and accuracy compared
      to binary methods like BinaryConnect and XNOR-Net by replacing multiplications with bitwise


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      shifts, which are more power-efficient yet maintain greater precision, reducing the accuracy loss
109   typically associated with binary quantization. This allows DSNNs to achieve better performance in
110   resource-constrained environments while still minimizing computational overhead (Elhoushi et al.,
111   2021).
112
      AdderNet (Chen et al., 2020) is another approach for reducing the amount of computationally expen-
113
      sive multiplications during network training and inference by using the ℓ1 -norm distance between
114
      input and filter vectors to compute activations. There are efforts in mixed-precision quantization,
115   where different bit-widths are assigned to different layers or channels of the network. It allows
116   for higher precision where necessary and lower precision where possible, optimizing the trade-off
117   between accuracy and efficiency (Motetti et al., 2024). Similarly, ternary quantization constrains the
118   weights to three discrete values: {−1, 0, +1}. To mitigate the accuracy loss from reduced precision,
119   approaches like trained ternary quantization (TTQ) introduce learned scaling factors, allowing net-
120   works to maintain performance comparable to their full-precision counterparts while significantly
121   reducing memory and power usage (Rokh et al., 2023). HPO combined with DSNNs further opti-
122   mizes both performance and resource efficiency by tailoring shift depths and quantization strategies,
123
      allowing for fine-tuned control over energy consumption and accuracy in constrained environments,
      making them ideal for mixed-precision and quantization-aware neural network applications.

      3.1   D EEP S HIFT N EURAL N ETWORKS
131
132   A Deep Shift Neural Network (DSNNs) is an approach to reduce the computational and energy
133   demands of Deep Learning (Elhoushi et al., 2021). They achieve a considerable reduction in latency
134   time by simplifying the network architecture such that they replace the traditional multiplication
135   operations in neural networks by bit-wise shift operations and sign flipping, making DSNNs suitable
136   for computing devices with limited resources. There are two methods for training DSNNs (Elhoushi
137   et al., 2021): DeepShift-Q (Quantization) and DeepShift-PS (Powers of two and Sign). DeepShift-Q
138
      involves training regular weights constrained to powers of 2 by quantizing weights to their nearest
      power of two during both forward and backward passes. In DeepShift-Q, the weights are quantized to
139
      powers of two by rounding the logarithm of the absolute weights to the nearest integer. This process
140
      simplifies the weight representation and ensures compatibility with bitwise shift operations. The sign
141   is then applied to preserve the original weight polarity. DeepShift-PS directly includes the values of
142   the shifts and sign flips as trainable hyperparameters, offering finer control over weight adaptation.
143   This approach removes the need for explicit rounding during training, potentially leading to improved
144   precision at the cost of additional parameter updates.
145
      The DeepShift-Q approach obtains the sign matrix S from the trained weight matrix W as
146
      S = sign(W ). The power matrix P is the base-2 logarithm of W ’s absolute values, i.e.,
147   P = log2 (|W |). After rounding P to the nearest power of two, Pr = round (P ), the quantized
148
      weights W̃q are calculated by applying the sign from S, shown as
149
150                                            W̃q = flip(2Pr , S) .                                     (3.1)
151
152   The DeepShift-PS approach optimizes neural network weights by directly adapting the shift (P̃ ) and
153   sign (S̃) values. The shift matrix P̃ is obtained by rounding the base-2 logarithm of the weight values,
154   P̃ = round (P ), and the sign flip S̃ is computed as S̃ = sign(round (S)). Weights are calculated as
155
156                                            W̃ps = flip(2P̃ , S̃) ,                                   (3.2)
157
158
      where the sign flip operation S̃ assigns values of −1, 0, or +1 based on s.
159   Directly training shift and sign values could allow for more precise control in optimizing the network’s
160   computational efficiency by reducing mathematical imprecision. On the other hand, training the
161   floating point weights and only rounding them during the forward and backward pass might increase
      the precision and reduce the error in training the weights.

      3.2   H YPERPARAMETER O PTIMIZATION
163
164   The increasing complexity of Deep Learning algorithms enhances the need for automated hyper-
165   parameter optimization (HPO) to increase model performance (Bischl et al., 2023). Consider a
166   dataset D = {(xi , yi )}N  i=1 ∈ D ⊂ X × Y, where X is the instance space and Y is the target
167   space, and a hyperparameter configuration space Λ = {λ1 , . . . , λL }, L ∈ N. In our work, M
168   denotes the space of possible DSNN models. The dataset D is split into disjunct training, validation,
169   and test sets: Dtrain , Dval , and Dtest respectively. An algorithm A : D × Λ → M trains a model
170
      M ∈ M, instantiated with a configuration of L hyperparameters sampled from Λ, on the training
      data Dtrain . The performance of the algorithm is assessed via an expensive-to-evaluate loss function
171
      L : Mλ × D → R, which involves both the training on Dtrain and the evaluation of the model on
172
      Dval . The direct optimization objective of HPO is to find the configuration λ∗ ∈ Λ with minimal
173   validation loss L, such that:
174
                                         λ∗ ∈ arg min L A(Dtrain , λ), Dval .

                                                                                                      (3.3)
175                                               λ∈Λ
176   Finally, the model’s final performance is assessed on Dtest .
177
178   3.3   BAYESIAN O PTIMIZATION
179
180   For a given dataset, Bayesian Optimization (BO) for HPO is a strategy for global optimization of
181   black-box loss functions L(λ) : Mλ × D →− R that are expensive to evaluate (Jones et al., 1998).
182
      BO uses a probabilistic surrogate model S to approximate the loss function, commonly given by a
183   Gaussian Process or a Random Forest (Rasmussen & Williams, 2006; Hutter et al., 2011; Shahriari
184   et al., 2016). An acquisition function α : Λ → − R guides the search for the next optimal evaluation
185   points by balancing the exploration-exploitation trade-off, based on the set of previously evaluated
186   configurations {(λ1 , L1 ), ..., (λm−1 , Lm−1 )} at time m. Common choices for acquisition functions
187   include expected improvement (EI) (Jones et al., 1998) since it calculates the expected improvement
188   in the objective function value and guides the search towards regions where improvements are most
189   likely.
190   Entropy-based methods like Entropy Search (ES) (Hennig & Schuler, 2012) and Predictive Entropy
191   Search (PES) (Hernández-Lobato et al., 2014) aim to reduce the entropy of the posterior distribution
192   of the maximizer, focusing on information-rich regions.
193
      The Knowledge Gradient (KG) (Frazier et al., 2009) offers a strategy for maximizing the expected
194
      improvement of the objective considering all potential outcomes, valuable in scenarios with noisy
195   measurements. BO is particularly well-suited for hyperparameter optimization in Deep Learning,
196   where evaluating the performance of a model configuration can be computationally expensive
197   because of the training of each configuration. BO is sample-efficient in evaluating L on only a few
198   configurations.
199
200   3.4   M ULTI -F IDELITY O PTIMIZATION
201
202   Since it is not feasible to fully train multiple configurations of DSNNs for comparison due to
203   computational efficiency, we employ a multi-fidelity (MF) approach (Li et al., 2017), which is a
204
      common strategy in AutoML to navigate the trade-off between performance and approximation
      error (Hutter et al., 2019). MF approaches train cheap-to-evaluate proxies of black-box functions
205
      following different heuristics, e.g., allocating a small number of epochs to many configurations in the
206
      beginning and training the best-performing ones on an increasing number of epochs. Formally, we
207   define a space of fidelities F and aim to minimize a function F ∈ F (Kandasamy et al., 2019):
208
                                                     min F (λ) .                                            (3.4)
209                                                  λ∈Λ
210   We approximate F ∈ F, using a series of lower-fidelity, i.e., less expensive approximations
211   {f (λ)1 , . . . , f (λ)j = F (λ)}, where j denotes the total number of fidelity levels. The target function
212   F ∈ F corresponds to the loss function L of HPO and BO. The allocated resources for evaluating a
213   model’s performance at various fidelities are referred to as a budget, e.g., training a DNN for only n
214   epochs instead of until convergence. MF typically assumes that the highest fidelities approximate the
215   black-box function best. The longer a model is trained, the more accurate its approximation of an
      underlying function gets.

      3.5   M ULTI -O BJECTIVE O PTIMIZATION
217
218   Multi-objective optimization (MO) addresses problems involving multiple, often competing, objec-
219   tives. This approach is used in scenarios where trade-offs between two or more conflicting objectives
220   must be navigated, such as, in the context of DSNNs, enhancing accuracy alongside reducing energy
221   consumption. MO aims to identify Pareto optimal solutions (Deb, 2014). New points are added
222   based on the current observation dataset Dobs = {(λ1 , L(λ1 )), . . . , (λn , L(λn ))} at time n + 1.
223   These points augment the surface formed by the non-dominated solution set Dn⋆ , which satisfies the
224
      condition for d objective variables and a loss function L = (L1 , . . . , Ld ), where Lk corresponds to
      the loss regarding objective k (Wada & Hino, 2019):
225
226                               ∀λ, (λ, L(λ)) ∈ Dn⋆ ⊂Dn , (λ′ , L(λ′ )) ∈ Dn                          (3.5)
227                                     ∃k ∈ {1, . . . , d} : Lk (λ) ≤ Lk (λ′ ).
228   W.l.o.g. we assume the minimization of all objectives. The observation dataset Dobs is iteratively
229   updated to search for solutions that approximate the Pareto front.

      5.2.2    H YPERPARAMETER I MPORTANCES
365
366   Another crucial aspect is the analysis of hyperparameter importance to learn their influence on
367   a model and lay the foundation of our DSNN design insights in the next subsection. We use
368   DeepCAVE (Sass et al., 2022) for analyzing the Pareto front of our MFMO analysis in Figure 15,
369   and computing the hyperparameter importance using fANOVA (Hutter et al., 2014). fANOVA fits
370   a random forest surrogate model to the hyperparameter optimization landscape and decomposes
371   the model’s variance into components corresponding to hyperparameters. This allows fANOVA to

373   extension to MO optimization, fANOVA can be applied to each objective’s performance surface
374
      separately. The hyperparameter importances are then computed for different weightings of the
      objectives.

      5.2.3   I NSIGHTS INTO THE D ESIGN OF DSNN S
450
451   Table 2 shows the specific configurations of the aggregated Pareto front of the ResNet20 architecture
452   on CIFAR10 dataset, see the appendix for similar tables for the other architectures and datasets.
453
      When looking at the specific configurations in Table 2, most solutions have a surprisingly small shift
454
      depth s ∈ {1, 3}, compared to 20 as the maximal value and the setting of the default. Hence, the
455
      Pareto optimal solutions are consistent with a very low shift depth. At the same time, the number of
456   activation fraction bits is often rather high. This leads to the assumption that the bulk of information
457   to be retained is in the fraction part of the activation value. A valid expectation since we used batch
458   normalization in the ResNet20, same as Elhoushi et al. (2021). In batch normalization, the layer inputs
459   are re-scaled and re-centered using the mean and variance of the corresponding dimension (Ioffe
460   & Szegedy, 2015). This usually leads to small weights, highlighting the importance of activation
461   fraction precision, which is higher in Pareto optimal configurations ranging from 8 to 32. This is still
462   likely to be a contributing factor to the emission reduction.
463   Intuitively, we assumed that the shift depth is proportionate to the savings in emissions. The
464   interaction between shift layers and other hyperparameters, such as the bit precision in weights
465   and activations, adds another layer of complexity. However, these hyperparameters interact in a
466   non-linear manner, influencing the model’s overall energy consumption and performance in ways that
467   are not immediately apparent. These results of the Pareto front analysis suggest that the relationship
468   between the configuration of shift layers — a hyperparameter anticipated to be directly proportional
469   to performance improvements and inversely proportional to loss — is not as straightforward as we
470
      initially hypothesized. We must recognize the intricate relationships among architectural decisions,
      hyperparameter configurations, and their consequent effects on model emissions and energy efficiency.
471
      Meaning, an increase in the number of shift layers does not uniformly lead to enhanced energy savings.
472
473   Another contributing factor to emission reduction is the precision of weight representation. Here,
474   however, there is no clear trend visible in the ResNet20 configurations examined before. This suggests
475   that this is a model-specific intricacy that needs to be tuned individually for each dataset.
476   These insights are corroborated when looking at the additional experiments in the appendix. Ad-
477   ditionally to the Resnet20 on CIFAR10, we computed the Pareto fronts of our MOMF analysis on
478   MobileNetV2 and GooGLeNet on CIFAR10. The corresponding results are shown in Figures 11 and
479   13. The overall Pareto optimal configurations from multiple seeds can be found in the appendix in
480   Tables 3 and 4. Again, the shift depths are generally very low, either one or three, with two exceptions
481   of seven and fourteen. The number of activation fraction bits is usually close to the upper bound of
482
      32 bits.
483   We have also computed Pareto fronts of our MFMO approach on the Caltech101 dataset, using
484   three architectures: ResNet20, MobileNetV2 and GoogLeNet. The results can be seen in Figures
485   14, 10 and 12. The configurations on the Pareto fronts are detailed in Tables 5, 6 and 7. Again, the
      large majority configurations have a low shift depth in the range of s ∈ {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}. Only two


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      GoogLeNet configurations have a shift depth of eight and 9. This is still relatively low, given that
487   GoogLeNet is a complex architecture with 22 layers in total.
488
489   The number of weight bits ranges from two to eight. As with the previously discussed results, this
490
      contributes to the reduction of emissions while not impacting the performance. Generally, the number
      of activation fraction and integer bits increases with lower shift depth and vice versa. This confirms
491
      our previous findings from the thoroughly discussed Resnet20 on CIFAR10.

494   6    C ONCLUSION
495
496   In this work, we presented our Green AutoML approach towards the sustainable optimization of
497   DSNNs through a multi-fidelity, multi-objective (MFMO) HPO framework. Our approach effectively
498   addressed the critical intersection between advancing the capabilities of Deep Learning and envi-
499   ronmental sustainability. By leveraging AutoML tools and integrating the environmental impact as
500   an objective, we adeptly navigated the trade-off between model performance and efficient resource
501
      utilization.
502   Our experimental results focused on a better understanding of DSNNs. We successfully optimized
503   DSNNs to achieve higher accuracy while minimizing energy consumption, surpassing the default
504   configuration settings in both aspects. Through systematic experimentation, we identified key
505   hyperparameters that significantly influence performance and emissions, such as shift depth and
506   number of weight bits. By optimizing these hyperparameters, our MFMO approach did not just
507
      improve one dimension of the problem – it concurrently enhanced both model loss and energy
      efficiency, showcasing a balanced improvement across essential performance metrics. We have
508
      thoroughly explored the configuration space for DSNNs, introduced a Green AutoML approach for
509
      efficiency-driven model development (Tornede et al., 2023), and provided valuable insights into the
510   design decisions impacting DSNN performance.
511
512   In this work, we conducted our experiments on the NVIDIA A100, a widely-used, state-of-the-art
513
      GPU standard in research and industry. Its computational capabilities and availability in many high-
      performance clusters make it a popular choice in the machine learning and deep learning communities.
514
      By using the A100, we ensure our findings are broadly applicable and relevant to real-world scenarios,
515
      aligning with hardware commonly utilized for training and deploying advanced neural networks.
516   While testing on other hardware, such as low-power chips or alternative GPUs, could offer additional
517   insights into hardware-specific performance trade-offs, this choice maximizes the potential impact
518   and accessibility of our research.
519
      While our use of CodeCarbon provides valuable insights into the emissions impact of training and
520
      inference for DSNNs, it is important to acknowledge the limitations and assumptions inherent in
521
      these measurements. CodeCarbon relies on real-time power draw metrics from tools like nvidia-smi
522   and estimates emissions based on the regional energy mix, which we manually specified for accuracy.
523   However, these estimates assume a steady power draw during computation and do not account for
524   fluctuations in hardware utilization or dynamic changes in the energy grid. Taking into account these
525   limitations, we consider CodeCarbon a reasonable approximation for measuring energy consumption.
526   This is also reflected in recent literature, where studies have been conducted comparing CodeCarbon
527   measurements to wattmeters that directly measure power consumption on machines (Bouza et al.,
528   2023).
529   Future work will focus on revisiting our MFMO implementation to find a more efficient way for
530   ParEGO and HyperBand to intertwine, such as by finding a more effective way to assign budgets and
531   weights of objectives. It is a part of our efforts to lighten the computational load when computing
532   the MFMO Pareto fronts. Further investigations will include exploring more DSNN-specific fidelity
533   types and multi-objective algorithms to achieve even greater reductions in model emissions. We
534   consider it especially interesting to use the number of weight bits as a fidelity type. By controlling
535   the precision of the weight quantization, training can be sped up in the earlier fidelity while regaining
536
      as much information as possible, to use this for full training of the most promising configurations at
      maximum precision.
537
538   Through these future initiatives, we aim to refine our methodology and extend the environmental
539   benefits of our optimized DSNNs, thereby contributing significantly to the sustainable advancement
      of AI technologies.

972    Algorithm 1 Multi-Fidelity Optimization with Parego for DSNNs
973
974
       Require: Configuration space C, objectives L = [Lloss , Lemissions ], budget range [Bmin , Bmax ], num-
           ber of trials N , DSNN architecture A.
975
       Ensure: Pareto optimal configurations PN .
976
        1: Initialize scenario S with C, L, (Bmin , Bmax ), and N .
977     2: Generate initial observation dataset Dinit by sampling k random configurations {λi }ki=1 ⊂ C.
978     3: Define intensifier H as Hyperband for budget allocation.
979     4: Initialize optimizer O using Parego and H.
980     5: for each trial t ∈ {1, . . . , N } do
981     6:     Select a configuration λt ∈ C using Parego.
982     7:     Allocate budget bt ∈ (Bmin , Bmax ) using H.
983     8:     Perform evaluation of λt with budget bt :
984          1. Convert DSNN A to a shift-based architecture A′ :
985
                                  A′ = convert to shift(A, λt [shift depth], λt [shift type])
986
987
                                  w′ = round(w, λt [rounding]),        ∀w ∈ A′ ,
988             where convert to shift replaces standard operations with shift operations, and round applies
989             deterministic or stochastic rounding.
990          2. Train A′ for bt epochs and compute the objective values:
991
                                                           1      X
992                                        Lloss (λt ) =                ℓ(fA′ (x), y),
993                                                      |Dtest |
                                                                (x,y)∈Dtest
994
                                       Lemissions (λt ) = measure emissions(A′ , bt ),
995
996             where Dtest is the test dataset, ℓ is the cross-entropy loss, and measure emissions computes
997             the energy consumption.
998          3. Update observation dataset:
999
                                      Dt ← Dt−1 ∪ {(λt , [Lloss (λt ), Lemissions (λt )])}.
1000
1001    9:     Update the Pareto front:
1002
                                 Pt = {(λ, L(λ)) ∈ Dt | ∄λ′ ∈ Dt : L(λ′ ) ≻ L(λ)},
1003
1004       where L(λ′ ) ≻ L(λ) denotes that λ′ dominates λ.
1005   10: end for
1006   11: return PN
