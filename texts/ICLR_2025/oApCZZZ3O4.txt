011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable proficiency in a
014            range of natural language processing tasks. Once deployed, LLMs encounter
015
               users with personalized factual knowledge, and such personalized knowledge is
               consistently reflected through users’ interactions with the LLMs. To enhance
016
               user experience, real-time model personalization is essential, allowing LLMs to
017
               adapt user-specific knowledge based on user feedback during human-LLM inter-
018            actions. Existing methods mostly require back-propagation to finetune the model
019            parameters, which incurs high computational and memory costs. In addition,
020            these methods suffer from low interpretability, which will cause unforeseen im-
021            pacts on model performance during long-term use, where the user’s personalized
022            knowledge is accumulated extensively. To address these challenges, we propose
023            Knowledge Graph Tuning (KGT), a novel approach that leverages knowledge
024            graphs (KGs) to personalize LLMs. KGT extracts personalized factual knowledge
025            triples from users’ queries and feedback and optimizes KGs without modifying
026
               the LLM parameters. Our method improves computational and memory efficiency
               by avoiding back-propagation and ensures interpretability by making the KG ad-
027
               justments comprehensible to humans. Experiments with state-of-the-art LLMs,
028
               including GPT-2, Llama2, and Llama3, show that KGT significantly improves
029            personalization performance while reducing latency and GPU memory costs. Ulti-
030            mately, KGT offers a promising solution of effective, efficient, and interpretable
031            real-time LLM personalization during user interactions with the LLMs.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Large language models (LLM) have shown increasing power on various NLP tasks (Devlin et al.,
037   2018; Raffel et al., 2020; Brown et al., 2020; Fedus et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2021; Zeng et al.,
038   2021). The development of LLMs typically encompasses several key phases (Hyperight, 2024):
039
      pre-training on vast corpora, alignment to ensure adherence to ethical guidelines, and domain-specific
      fine-tuning. While these steps are crucial, there is often an oversight in recognizing the need for
040
      further personalization during the deployment phase. Once deployed, LLMs encounter users with
041
      personalized factual knowledge (Petroni et al., 2019; Jiang et al., 2020; Roberts et al., 2020). These
042   individual knowledge bases are consistently reflected through users’ feedback on various queries and
043   the model’s responses during interactions, as shown in the interaction example in Figure 1. To enhance
044   user experience, real-time model personalization is essential, allowing LLMs to adapt and incorporate
045   user-specific knowledge based on persistent feedback throughout human-LLM interactions.
046
      Several technologies are applicable to personalize the LLM, including Parameter Efficient Finetuning
047
      (PEFT) (Ding et al., 2023), Knowledge Editing (KE) (Geva et al., 2020; Dai et al., 2022; Meng et al.,
048   2022), and in-context learning (Brown et al., 2020). Nevertheless, these methodologies are often
049   plagued by low efficiency, poor interpretability, or a combination of both drawbacks. PEFT and KE
050   require back-propagation to optimize the model parameters. Back-propagation incurs unacceptable
051   GPU memory and computational costs for the daily use of LLMs, especially for on-device applications
052   where the onboard resources are limited. The high computational overhead also makes it difficult to
053   realize real-time personalization. In addition to the low efficiency, these parameter-based methods
      also lack interpretability. Modifying the model parameters to satisfy the user’s personalized need for

094

095

097   human-LLM interactions. We summarize our contributions as follows:
098

100             the challenges of adopting existing technologies: low efficiency and poor interpretability.
101

103

104           • We conduct experiments on multiple datasets with SOTA pre-trained LLMs. Compared with
105             baselines, KGT achieves significantly better personalization performance while reducing
106             computational and memory costs. KGT demonstrates considerable scalability as the volume
107             of queries increases, a critical attribute for fulfilling the long-term needs of users who require

111   KG-enhanced LLM Large language models (LLMs) are acclaimed for their proficiency in as-
112   similating knowledge from expansive corpora and achieving groundbreaking results in numerous
113   natural language processing (NLP) tasks. Nonetheless, these models are frequently critiqued for
114   the hallucination issues (Lee et al., 2018; Bang et al., 2023) and their lack of interpretability. In
115   response to these shortcomings, the integration of knowledge graphs (KGs) with LLMs has been
116
      proposed (Chang et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2021; Yao et al., 2022; Song et al., 2021). KGs, by storing
      extensive knowledge in a structured and explicit format, enhance the knowledge capacity of LLMs.
117
      Some approaches (Zhang et al., 2019; Rosset et al., 2020) advocate for incorporating KGs during
118
      the pre-training phase of LLMs, enabling direct knowledge acquisition from these graphs. Oth-
119   ers (Chang et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2021) suggest the use of KGs during the inference phase to improve
120   access to domain-specific knowledge, thereby substantially enhancing the models’ performance. Fur-
121   thermore, to advance the interpretability of LLMs, researchers are utilizing KGs to elucidate both
122   the factual knowledge (Petroni et al., 2019) and the reasoning processes (Lin et al., 2019) of these
123   models. In this paper, we focus on the setting in which KG enhances the inference phase of an LLM.
124
125
126   LLM Personalization and Knowledge Editing Recently, Knowledge Editing (KE) approaches
127   have been proposed to personalize LLMs by localizing and modifying the factual knowledge within
128   transformers. Geva et al. (2020) suggests that the MLP layers within a transformer for masked
129   language models serve as memory units for entities and their associated information. Extending this
130
      idea, KN (Dai et al., 2022) developed a technique for updating facts in BERT by manipulating specific
      rows of the MLP matrix with the embedding of the target object. They pinpoint crucial neurons for
131
      storing knowledge through gradient-based methods. De Cao et al. (2021) implement a hyper-network
132
      that predicts necessary weight adjustments during test time to modify facts. Their experiments involve
133   BERT and BART (Lewis et al., 2019), particularly in models tailored for question-answering tasks.
134   Mitchell et al. (2022) introduce a hyper-network approach that adjusts gradient decomposition terms
135   to efficiently update knowledge, showcasing scalability across large models like T5 (Raffel et al.,
136   2020) and GPT-J (Wang & Komatsuzaki, 2022). The ROME method (Meng et al., 2022) employed
137   causal tracing to pinpoint knowledge-relevant layers and then edit its FFN module, achieving superior
138   outcomes. All these techniques employ gradient-based optimization methods, which are inefficient
139   and unsuitable for achieving real-time personalization under constrained computational resources.
140   Moreover, modifying model parameters lacks interpretability and may lead to unforeseen impacts on
141
      model performance. In contrast, our method solely necessitates model inference, which is significantly
      more efficient. By adjusting the knowledge graph (KG), our approach ensures that the personalization
142
      process is interpretable and the modifications in model performance are comprehensible. There are
143
      some recent works (Baek et al., 2024; Madaan et al., 2022) realizing personalization through building
144   a personalized knowledge base, which is similar to our work. However, these works employing
145   unstructured knowledge bases have trouble resolving conflicts in knowledge bases, while our work
146   can detect conflicting knowledge facts during the LLM-AI interaction and update the stale knowledge
147   in the knowledge base.

153   Knowledge Graph (KG) A knowledge graph G is a set of triples containing factual knowledge:
154   G = {(e, r, e′ )|e, e′ ∈ E, r ∈ R}, where E and R denote the set of entities and relations, respectively,
155   and the triple (e, r, e′ ) is referred to as a knowledge triple.
156
157
158   KG-enhanced LLM A KG-enhanced LLM fθ,G is parameterized by both the LLM parameters θ
159   and a KG G. Given a natural language query q, the KG-enhanced LLM generates an answer fθ,G (q)
160   based on both the model parameters and the retrieved knowledge triples from G. We follow previous
161   works (Sun et al., 2019; Jiang et al., 2022) and assume that the entities eq in the query and ea in the
      answer are labeled. In this paper, we focus on the setting where the LLM retrieves one-depth triples.

190   4.1        K NOWLEDGE G RAPH T UNING
191
      Recently, many techniques have been developed to efficiently finetune or personalize the pre-trained
192
      LLM parameters based on the user’s data (Ding et al., 2023; Geva et al., 2020; Brown et al., 2020).
193
      Instead of finetuning the model parameters, we modify the user’s KG to inject personalized knowledge
194   into the LLM.
195
196       Example A user wants to let the LLM-based assistant order some food for her vegetarian
197       dog. Instead of finetuning the LLM to remember the fact that the user’s dog is vegetar-
198       ian, the user can simply delete the knowledge triple (Dog, Enjoy, M eat) and add a triple
199       (Dog, Enjoy, V egetable) in his/her personal knowledge graph. To generate an appropriate
200       response, the LLM assistant will first retrieve the knowledge triple (Dog, Enjoy, V egetable)
201       based on the user’s query, then recommend vegetarian dog food for the user based on the query
202       and the retrieved personalized knowledge triple.
203

206   and 2. knowledge-enhanced reasoning. Following this insight, we formulate the KG-enhanced
207   LLM reasoning probability by marginalizing the knowledge triple distribution. Then, our KGT
208   is an optimization problem that aims to maximize the probability of reasoning the answer from a

303   4.3   O PTIMIZATION A LGORITHM
304
305   Unlike finetuning the model parameters with gradient descent, KGT is achieved by adding and
306   removing the knowledge triples to and from the KG. For a triple z and a KG G, we have two
307   operations: 1. G ⊕ z ≜ G ∪ {z}; 2. G ⊖ z ≜ G \ {z}. Because we focus on real-time model
308   personalization, we formulate the optimization in the online learning setting where the optimization
309
      algorithm only has access to the user’s current query qt at time t and feedback at . The trivial solution
      to this query is that we remove all the triples in G that are probably retrieved based on qt , which are
310
      the triples starting with eq . Then we add the triple z ∗ from H(qt , at , K) with the highest reasoning
311
      probability Pθ,G (at |qt , z ∗ ) into G. In this case, Pθ,G (z ∗ |qt ) = 1 and the loss is minimized over
312   (qt , at ). However, this greedy solution will potentially hurt the other queries due to removing too
313   much knowledge from the KG. Following the example in Section 4.1, the greedy solution will not
314   only remove the triple (Dog, Enjoy, M eat), but also remove other triples starting from the entity
315   Dog such as (Dog, Is, Animal). The removed triples might be general or personalized factual
316   knowledge that is essential to the other queries.
317
      We propose a heuristic optimization algorithm based on two principles: 1. The computational cost
318   should be low to achieve real-time personalization efficiency; 2. The KG should be less modified to
319   preserve more knowledge. Both principles require us to modify as few triples as possible to achieve
320   the optimization goal. The detailed algorithm is shown in Algorithm 1. In general, we evaluate the
321   reasoning probability Pθ,G (at |qt , z ′ ) for the triples z ′ in H(q, a, K) and triples in G that starting from
322   eqt . Then, we add the triples with the highest reasoning probability (i.e., the triples most encourage
323   the LLM to generate the correct answer) from H(q, a, K) to G and remove the triples with the lowest
      reasoning probability from G iteratively until the model generates the correct answer.

352   5     E XPERIMENTS
353
354   We evaluate KGT in terms of personalization performance and efficiency compared with the baselines.
355   The experiments are conducted on a server with eight A100 GPUs.
356
357   5.1    E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
358
359   Datasets We first evaluate KGT in the setting where the user provides answers that conflict with the
360   common factual knowledge that LLM learned from the pre-training dataset, which is an extremely
361   hard setting of personalization. We evaluate KGT on two datasets: CounterFact (Meng et al., 2022)
362   dataset and CounterFactExtension dataset we create based on PARALLEL dataset (Elazar et al., 2021)
363   utilizing GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2024). The details about our dataset can be found in Appendix B. Both
364
      datasets consist of query-answer pairs involving factual knowledge that conflicts with reality.
365   Even though the counterfactual datasets can mimic the extremely hard personalization settings, to
366   evaluate KGT in a more realistic setting, we build a real personalization interaction benchmark named
367   PeInt. We hired five real users to interact with the LLM (Llama3-8b) to discuss some topics based
368   on their preferences and experience and build a benchmark containing five users’ interactions. One
369   example of interaction can be found in Appendix B. We collect all the interactions and evaluate if our
370
      method and the baselines can adapt to these personalized knowledge bases in the interactions.
371   To evaluate the real-time model personalization in practice, we sequentially input query-answer pairs
372   into the model, ensuring that each pair is accessed only once during training. Once all the pairs in
373   the dataset have been processed by the model, we assess the personalization effectiveness across the
374   entire dataset.
375
376   Baselines and Configurations We compare KGT with fine-tunning (FT), ROME (Meng et al.,
377   2022), Knowledge editing (KE) (De Cao et al., 2021), Knowledge neurons (KN) (Dai et al., 2022),
      and MEND (Mitchell et al., 2022). We also compare with relevant work, K-LaMP (Baek et al., 2024),


                                                                        7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      that aims to personalize the responses of LLMs with the knowledge from the knowledge store. For all
379   baselines, we test several specifications of layers to edit and report the best results for each baseline.
380   For FT, we follow previous arts (Meng et al., 2022) to execute full fine-tuning on a single layer. For
381   KN, we specify a subset of all layers as candidates of knowledge neurons to reduce the search space
382   for large models. And baseline results where no knowledge is edited are referred to as ‘no edit.’
383   We conduct experiments on GPT2-xl (Radford et al., 2019), Llama2-7B (Touvron et al., 2023), and
384   Llama3-8B (AI, 2024). We equip each model with a KG, ConceptNet (Speer et al., 2017), to enhance
385   the inference. K in Equation (3) is set as five for experiments.
386
387   Metrics We utilize two metrics to evaluate the performance of personalization: (1) Efficacy
388   Score (Meng et al., 2022), measuring the success rate of personalization using the training query-
389
      answer pairs directly. If the model generates the user’s personalized answer with a higher probability
      than the answer before tuning, the model is considered successful for that pair. (2) Paraphrase
390
      Score (Meng et al., 2022), indicating the model’s ability to accurately recall personalized knowledge
391
      in paraphrased forms. This assesses its personalization capacity while mitigating the impact of
392   overfitting to specific contexts within the training dataset.

396   We evaluate the setting that the user only provides the answer a as the feedback, and the model will
397   extract the relations and construct H (q, a, K). The results on CounterFact dataset are shown in

399   scores. Specifically, using Llama3-8B, KGT improves efficacy by more than 39%, 41%, 55%, 45%,
400   43%, 35%, and 61% on efficacy compared with FT, ROME, KE, KN, MEND, K-LaMP, and no-edit,
401

402
      It is also observed that the results of KGT on Llama3 are better than Llama2. Our analysis is that
403
      Llama3 is more powerful in understanding and following instructions, which makes knowledge
404   enhancement from the KG more effective. Such an improvement suggests that KGT will achieve
405   better performance when the pre-trained LLMs are more and more powerful. Notably, K-LaMP
406   achieves high efficacy but a low paraphrase score on GPT-2. K-LaMP focuses on recording entities
407   rather than learning factual knowledge through interaction. As a result, it performs well when queries
408   are repeated verbatim but struggles when queries are rephrased. Besides, simple entity records fail to
409   contribute effectively to reasoning in more advanced models such as Llama2 and Llama3, leading to
410   both low efficacy and paraphrase scores.

461   5.3     E FFICIENCY C OMPARISON
462
463   In addition to the personalization performance, we also evaluate KGT’s efficiency in terms of latency
464   and GPU memory cost. The method must achieve low latency and low GPU memory cost to realize
465   real-time personalization in practice under the setting with limited computational resources. KGT
466
      can improve the time and GPU memory cost efficiency because it only requires inference, which is far
      more efficient than back-propagation. For latency, we test the average time that our method and the
467
      baselines require to complete the personalization for one query-answer pair. The latency results on
468
      CounterFact data are shown in Table 4. The results demonstrate that KGT achieves significant latency
469   reduction compared with most baselines. Notably, Llama3 requires less time on several baselines
470   than Llama2 and GPT2 because we stop training once the loss converges. The results on the GPU
471   memory cost can also be found in Table 4. The memory cost is reduced significantly because only
472   the inference is required for KGT. Specifically, KGT reduces 57%, 56%, 77%, 63%, 62% and 52%
473   GPU memory cost when adopting Llama3-8B compared with FT, KE, KN, MEND, and K-LaMP,
474   respectively.

      6   C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
541
542
      We propose an approach, KGT, that personalizes models by optimizing external knowledge graph
543   rather than model parameters. Our method and experimental results demonstrate that our approach
544   offers benefits in terms of performance and efficiency, as supported by empirical studies. By
545   addressing the critical challenges of efficiency and interpretability in model personalization, KGT
546   offers a promising direction for future research and application in enhancing user interactions with
547   LLMs, which has a positive societal impact. For this work, we only focus on the single-user scenario.
548   When there are multiple users, we can maintain just one base LLM and multiple personalized KGs
549   rather than multiple LLMs, which is more storage-efficient. When serving inferences for multiple
550   users, since there is only one base model, we can package their queries with the corresponding
551
      knowledge triples in one batch and conduct inferences in parallel. Thus, KGT can achieve high
      efficiency when serving multiple users. One limitation of this method (KGT) is that it depends on
552
      the LLM’s ability to follow instructions when calculating Pθ,G (a|q, z), Pθ,G (z|q), and collecting
553
      H(q, a, K). However, the existing state-of-the-art LLMs already meet KGT’s requirements for this
554   capability, and future LLMs will possess even stronger abilities to follow instructions.

           B     D ETAILS ON THE CounterFactExtension DATASET
727
728
           CounterFactExtension is a dataset that extends the CounterFact with 27737 additional samples.
729
           Specifically, CounterFactExtension is on the task of knowledge editing for Efficacy and Paraphrase
730        evaluation. Likewise, CounterFactExtension is crafted to identify the differences between minor
731        adjustments in the vocabulary used by the model and substantial, widespread shifts in the foundational
732        factual knowledge.
733
           CounterFactExtension is built on knowledge pairs processed by Dai et al. (Dai et al., 2022) based on
734
           PARAREL dataset (Elazar et al., 2021). Specifically, for each knowledge pair, we employ GPT-4 to
735
           extract the subject, relation, and the true target, t = (s, r, o∗ ), and generate a counterfactual target, oc .
736        Further, a generation prompt (generated by GPT-4) is also provided for the Paraphrase evaluation.
737        Finally, we manually check each sample and the generation to ensure the correctness.
