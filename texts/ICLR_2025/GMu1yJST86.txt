                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               Label distribution learning (LDL) is a novel learning paradigm that emulates label
010
               polysemy by assigning label distributions over the label space. However, recent
011            LDL work seems to exhibit a notable contradiction: 1) some existing LDL meth-
012            ods employ auxiliary tasks to enhance performance, which narrows their focus to
013            specific domains, thereby lacking generalization capability; 2) conversely, LDL
014            methods without auxiliary tasks rely on losses tailored solely to label distributions
015            of the primary task, lacking additional supervised information to guide the learning
016            process. In this paper, we propose S-LDL, a novel and minimalist solution that
017            partitions the label distribution of the primary task into subtask label distributions,
018            i.e., a form of pseudo-supervised information, to reconcile the above contradiction.
019
               S-LDL encompasses two key aspects: 1) an algorithm capable of generating sub-
               tasks without any extra knowledge, with subtasks deemed valid and reconstructable
020
               via our analysis; and 2) a plug-and-play framework seamlessly compatible with
021
               existing LDL methods, and even adaptable to derivative tasks of LDL. Experiments
022            demonstrate that S-LDL is effective and efficient. To the best of our knowledge,
023            this represents the first endeavor to address LDL via subtasks. The code will soon
024            be available on GitHub to facilitate reproducible research.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Multi-label learning (MLL) (Zhang and Zhou, 2013) handles label polysemy in a binary manner,
030   whereas label distribution learning (LDL) (Geng, 2016) offers a more nuanced perspective by
031
      answering: “How much does each label y describe the instance x?”. This is accomplished through
      the concept of a label distribution d, which is a form of probability simplex that assigns a real value
032
      (i.e., description degree dyx ) to each label of each instance. This form introduces a quantitative manner
033
      to address label polysemy and extends LDL’s practical applications to a wider range, e.g., counting
034   (or grading) (Geng et al., 2013; Wu et al., 2019), sentiment analysis (Chen et al., 2020; Le et al.,
035   2023), segmentation (Gao et al., 2017; Li et al., 2023b), etc. Concurrently, more and more derivative
036   tasks of LDL (González et al., 2021b; Lu and Jia, 2022; Wang, Jing and Geng, Xin, 2019; Xu and
037   Zhou, 2017; Xu et al., 2019) are emerging to offer assistance in various real-world dilemmas.
038
      However, LDL encounters a spectrum of challenges: 1) label    Pdistributions are bound by two con-
039
      straints, non-negativity (i.e., dyx ≥ 0) and sum-to-one (i.e., y∈Y dyx = 1), and are often formed
040
      from mixture distributions, posing significant hurdles for fitting, particularly when employing a
041
      maximum entropy model (Shen et al., 2017); 2) label distribution matrices are usually obtained
042   via crowdsourcing, which is time-consuming and labor-intensive, so one often copes with scarce
043   and low-quality datasets (Wang et al., 2023). These two key issues stand as formidable barriers to
044   performance improvement in LDL.
045
      With the widespread use of multi-task learning, some LDL work tries to compensate for performance
046
      from the perspective of auxiliary tasks, which are learned concurrently alongside the primary task,
047
      thereby refining its representations and ultimately boosting performance. Unfortunately, though these
048   methods can exploit additional supervised information, they 1) do not address the first key issue
049   mentioned above; and 2) require extra knowledge (e.g., facial characteristics (Chen et al., 2020),
050   pathology criteria (Wu et al., 2019), emotion wheel theory in psychology (Yang et al., 2017a), etc.)
051   or similar domain-specific data (Zhao et al., 2023b), limiting their generalization capability to those
052   corresponding specific domains. Conversely, LDL methods that do not take advantage of auxiliary
053   tasks, despite their efforts in loss function engineering and network structure design, they 1) do not
      address the second key issue mentioned above; and 2) focus solely on one aspect of label correlations

054
      (e.g., correlation of local instances (Jia et al., 2019), ranking relation (Jia et al., 2023), suboptimal
055   label (Wang, Jing and Geng, Xin, 2019), etc.), each with its own set of limitations.
056
057   The generalizability across various domains appears to conflict with the ability to exploit additional
058
      data, so benefiting from both simultaneously seems elusive. However, we can still see the light
      from some MLL methods, which partition the label space and apply operations on these subspaces
059
      (Tsoumakas et al., 2008; 2010). These methods construct subtasks without involving extra knowl-
060
      edge and exhibit applicability across various domains. Intuitively, in the context of LDL, reliable
061   supervised information can be generated from these subtasks, which can eventually be aggregated
062   and reconstructed to the information of the primary task via ensemble strategies. Although existing
063   label distribution ensemble practices demonstrate promising performance (González et al., 2021a;
064   Shen et al., 2017), they focus only on the supervised information of the primary task.
065
      In this paper, we introduce S-LDL, a novel and minimalist label distribution learning algorithm that
066
      constructs and exploits subtasks, to reconcile the contradiction between the generalizability across
067   various domains and the ability to exploit additional data. Serving as auxiliary tasks, subtasks 1)
068   provide different views of the primary task distribution, rendering the mixture of distributions more
069   traceable (i.e., the key issue one); 2) furnish additional supervised data to mitigate the scarcity and
070   ambiguity inherent in LDL datasets (i.e., the key issue two); 3) require no extra knowledge from
071   specific domains; and 4) emphasize various label correlations via partitioning of the label space.
072
      The main contributions of this paper are outlined below: 1) we propose S-LDL, which is considered
073   the first endeavor to address LDL via subtasks; 2) our analysis shows the validity and reconstructability
074   of these subtasks; 3) we present a plug-and-play framework seamlessly compatible with existing LDL
075   methods, and adaptable to derivative tasks of LDL; and 4) the code will be available on GitHub soon,
076   facilitating reproducible research endeavors.

      2    R ELATED WORK
080
081
      LDL Our work is mainly related to LDL. Initially employed to tackle age estimation (Geng et al.,
      2013), LDL has evolved into a novel machine learning paradigm (Geng, 2016), which is supported
082
      by theoretical underpinnings (Wang and Geng, 2019) and features various derivative tasks (González
083
      et al., 2021b; Lu and Jia, 2022; Wang, Jing and Geng, Xin, 2019; Xu and Zhou, 2017; Xu et al., 2019).
084   Most methods focus on improving performance via loss function engineering (Jia et al., 2019; 2023;
085   Ren et al., 2019; Wen et al., 2023) or efficient model structures (González et al., 2021a; Jin et al.,
086   2024; Shen et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2017b), while some work is dedicated to practical application
087   scenarios (Gao et al., 2017; Li et al., 2023a; Shirani et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2019). However, the
088   scarcity of label distribution datasets and the complexity of the label distribution itself make it difficult
089   to further improve performance, at which point one may think of leveraging auxiliary tasks.
090
091   LDL with auxiliary tasks While there are LDL methods that leverage auxiliary tasks to enhance
092   performance, they often rely on knowledge from disparate domains, extending beyond the scope of
093   the LDL task. For example, LDL-ALSG (Chen et al., 2020) designs auxiliary tasks dedicated to facial
094   emotion recognition, necessitating the use of external tools to extract facial points and action units
095
      from human faces. Wu et al. (2019) exploit the Hayashi criterion, a rule for counting and grading in
      acne lesions, which results in their method being only applicable in a small branch of the dermatology
096
      field. Yang et al. (2017a) employ a multi-task framework for image emotion classification, designing
097
      constraints inspired by Mikel’s wheel, a psychological emotion model, which also suffers from
098   similar limitations. As LDL methods of transfer learning, GLDL (Zhao et al., 2023b) utilizes data
099   from one or more source domains, which is not easy to obtain in practical applications. The need for
100   specific extra knowledge significantly narrows the application scenarios of these methods.
101
102   MLL with partitioning of the label space For reference, there exist MLL methods based on
103   partitioning of the label space, which can construct multi-label subtasks without involving additional
104   knowledge and can be widely used in various domains. The most classic related work is that of
105   HOMER (Tsoumakas et al., 2008) and RAkEL (Tsoumakas et al., 2010), the former forms a hierarchy
106   of label subspaces while the latter randomly selects label subspaces. Many subsequent papers have
107   been inspired by them (Prabhu et al., 2018; Read et al., 2013; Wang et al., 2021). Read et al. (2014)
      present a general framework of label subspaces and provide some theoretical justification for it. Since

124   LDL with ensemble strategy It is imperative to aggregate the output of subtasks. Fortunately,

316   even efficacious enough to reconstruct the label distribution of the primary task. Theorem 1 illustrates

      distributions, when the following conditions are satisfied: 1) G is connected; 2) G covers all labels in
      the label space, and 3) corresponding description degrees of all cut vertices of G are not zero.

      7   L IMITATIONS AND CONCLUSION
492
      Limitations First, S-LDL of the shallow regime is proposed out of intuition, and in Section 5, we
493
      have discussed its limitations, which are addressed via the designing of S-LDL of the deep regime.
494
      Second, when the label space is large, especially when labels are continuous and result in unimodal
495   label distributions (e.g., age estimation), our proposed cannot be rationally applied. Fortunately, one
496   possible workaround is to use a binning tricks for preprocessing, and then construct subtasks.
497
498   Conclusion We propose S-LDL, a subtask learning framework nested into LDL. S-LDL is generic:
499   it generates pseudo-supervised information via subtask construction without any extra knowledge;
500   S-LDL is minimalist: it can be attached to existing methods and handle derivative tasks; S-LDL
501   is efficient: it captures a wide variety of label correlations. The analysis shows the validity and
502   reconstructability of subtasks, and experiments show the superiority of our framework.
