009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The effectiveness of Visual Brain-Machine Interfaces (BMIs) is significantly
012            dependent on the accurate detection and interpretation of electroencephalogra-
013            phy (EEG) biomarkers, which frequently exhibit variability due to physiological
014            changes and environmental disturbances over time. Traditional EEG signal en-
015            hancement strategies largely concentrate on signal processing techniques such as
016
               feature extraction and filtering; however, these approaches often do not adequately
               address the inherent sources of variability that affect biomarker stability over time.
017
               To surmount these challenges, we have developed the Visual Evoked Potential
018
               Booster (VEP Booster), a novel closed-loop artificial intelligence framework de-
019            signed to produce reliable and stable EEG biomarkers under visual stimulation
020            protocols. Our system utilizes a Deep Convolutional Generative Adversarial Net-
021            work (DCGAN) to refine stimulus images based on real-time feedback from hu-
022            man EEG signals, thereby creating visual stimuli that are specifically tailored to
023            the characteristic preferences of neurons in the primary visual cortex. We evalu-
024            ated the efficacy of this system through the implementation of steady-state visual
025            evoked potential (SSVEP) protocols in nine human subjects. In our evaluations,
026            both the SSVEP biomarker amplitude and the single-trial SSVEP binary classi-
027
               fication experiments, encompassing intra- and inter-temporal analyses, exhibited
               statistically significant enhancements when employing the VEP Booster. These
028
               encouraging outcomes underscore the potential for broad applications in clinical
029
               and technological domains.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Visual Brain-Machine Interfaces (BMIs) have emerged as key technologies that bridge neural activ-
035   ity with external devices, enabling transformative applications in neurorehabilitation (Astrand et al.,
036   2014), diagnosis of brain disorders (Zhang et al., 2024), and human-computer interaction (Chen
037   et al., 2015; Zhu et al., 2022). The performance of visual BMIs critically depends on the detection
038
      and interpretation of reliable and stable electroencephalography (EEG) biomarkers. However, in
      real-life scenarios, EEG biomarkers often exhibit significant variability over time, which poses a
039
      major challenge to consistent BMI performance (Lotte et al., 2018).
040
041   The instability of EEG biomarkers is mainly due to several factors (Buzsáki et al., 2012), including
042   minute differences in electrode placement, amplitudes attenuations due to change in physiological
043   and psychological states of subjects, environmental noise and individual differences. These factors
044
      lead to significant variations in the EEG signals at different time points.
045   Despite significant advancements, most current EEG signal enhancement algorithms remain focused
046   on signal processing techniques such as filtering (Sanei & Chambers, 2013; Agarwal et al., 2017; He
047   et al., 2004) denoising (Luck, 2014; Makeig et al., 1996). Additionally, feature extraction methods
048   are applied to transform the enhanced signals into meaningful representations. While these meth-
049   ods can effectively reduce noise and emphasize certain features, they often overlook the inherent
050
      temporal dynamics of EEG biomarkers. This oversight means they cannot fundamentally address
      the issue of biomarker instability over time. As a result, models trained earlier data may experience
051
      performance degradation when applied at later times, a challenge that remains urgent to resolve.
052
053   In this work, we introduce the Visual Evoked Potential Booster (VEP Booster), a novel closed-
      loop artificial intelligence framework that fundamentally differs from existing methodologies. Our

105   Existing visual BMI EEG processing algorithms can generally be divided into three categories. The
106   first category relies on traditional signal processing techniques. Methods such as Canonical Correla-
107   tion Analysis (CCA) (Zhang et al., 2011) and Task-Discriminant Component Analysis (TDCA) (Liu
      et al., 2021) have achieved significant success in detecting steady-state visual evoked potentials

      (SSVEPs). In a recent study, the binocular Task-Related Component Analysis (bTRCA) algorithm
109   in visual BMIs achieved impressive information transmission rates (Sun et al., 2024).
110
111   The second category includes machine learning approaches utilizing deep neural networks (DNNs)
112
      to extract EEG biomarkers. DNNs are particularly effective at capturing spatial and temporal de-
      pendencies in EEG data (Pan et al., 2022b; Liu et al., 2023). For instance, Kwak et al. (2017)
113
      demonstrated that convolutional neural networks (CNNs) outperform traditional methods like CCA
114
      and its variants, achieving high classification accuracy in both static and ambulatory settings. Re-
115   cently, the application of large language models and transformer architectures to EEG data (Yi et al.,

117   els offer a robust framework for interpreting EEG signals by leveraging universal patterns across
118   various tasks.
119
      The third category Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs), utilize surrogate-gradient descent learning
120
      to reconstruct EEG biomarker sources and show potential for augmenting EEG signal data (Sin-
121   ganamalla & Lin, 2021). The results indicate improvements in tasks such as Steady-State Visual
122   Evoked Potentials (SSVEP) and motor imagery. Additionally, biologically-motivated Spiking Re-
123   current Neural Networks (SRNNs) employ the FORCE method to train network dynamics to align
124   with EEG dynamics (Ioannides et al., 2022).

      3.1   M ATHEMATICAL MODEL
129
130   The primary objective of the VEP booster is to generate images that elicit the strongest EEG
131   biomarker responses under a visual stimulus protocol. For a single loop, the system works as fol-
132   lows: 1) a generator generates visual stimulus images; 2) we record the human participant’s EEG
133   signals under each visual stimulus protocol with the generated images; 3) an EEG decoder scores
134   the generated images based on the EEG biomarker values; 4) a latent generator generates the latent
135   inputs (for the DCGAN) for the next iteration, which based on the latent vectors of images with
136   highest EEG biomarker values.
137   As illustrated in Figure 1, the system comprises of four interconnected sub-systems:

139
      sarial Network (GAN) to implement the generative model G : Z → X , where Z is the latent space,
140

141   to minimize the discrepancy between the generated visual stimuli and the dataset visual stimuli.
142

143   E → R, outputting a real-number score indicating the effectiveness of the stimulus. D is defined by

145   corresponding to the given EEG signals.

      3.2   S YSTEM IMPLEMENTATION
195
196
      To demonstrate the feasibility of our proposed framework, we conduct a case study using a SSVEP
197
      stimulation protocol. Our objective is to elicit the most robust EEG biomarker responses under
198   the SSVEP protocol. We collected EEG data using a dry wearable device (developed by BrainUp
199   research Lab), which has seven channels and can capture neural activity across a wide visual area.
200
201
      Our system, depicted in Figure 2, includes a pre-trained DCGAN with five layers of specified shapes.
      To customize the model for our purposes, we create a custom training dataset that featured visual
202
      stimuli with variations in brightness, stripe patterns, and checkerboards. The discriminator is con-
203
      structed as a convolutional neural network, specifically designed to process images with dimensions
204   64 × 64 × 3 (width × height × channels). It incorporates four strided convolutional layers, which
205   sequentially reduce the resolution of the input image. And the generator in the DCGAN framework
206   utilizes a latent vector of length 100, sampled from a standard normal distribution. This vector is
207   processed through a series of transposed convolutional layers to construct an image. More training
208   details are provided in Appendix A.1.
209
      The EEG decoder employs a rapid Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) approach, in conjunction with
210   Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) calculations at the target frequency, both as feature extraction tech-
211   niques. The FFT feature highlights the maximal response within V1 to the target frequency, effec-
212   tively identifying the most significant neuronal activation triggered by a specific visual stimulus.
213   Conversely, the SNR feature quantitatively assesses the contrast between the amplitude of the target
214   frequency and the energy of the background oscillations. This metric provides insights into the sig-
215   nal’s stability, offering an indication of the robustness and clarity of the neuronal response relative
      to background noise. Based on these two features, a score is calculated for each image using the

223   The latent vector generator algorithm is built based on Equation 1, which n equals two. One la-
224   tent vector originates from an image with the highest FFT value of the EEG signal, while another
225   latent vector is derived from an image with the highest SNR value of the EEG signal. These two
226
      latent vectors are combined to obtain the optimal latent vectors. The optimal latent vector is then
      used to generate eight individual offspring vectors. In each offspring, a portion of the latent vector
227
      is replaced with random numbers drawn from a a zero-centered Gaussian distribution to serve as
228
      mutation vectors. The proportion of the vector that is replaced varies among offspring, ranging from
229   p = 0% to 80%. Additionally, ten individual offspring vectors are generated using the interpolation
230   process between the best FFT and SNR latent vectors. Therefore, the total generated latent vector
231   is 20 for the next iteration. This introduces variability and potentially novel feature representations
232   into the generative process.

236   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
237
238   We conducted an experiment involving nine human participants (6 males and 3 females). At the
239   first iteration, a trained generator produced 50 images. Through an image pre-check process (details
240   are described in Appendix A.2), the five most diverse images were selected for further testing using
241   a SSVEP protocol at 4 Hz. The reason for selecting 4 Hz is that it is the frequency of the brain’s
242   background noise, which aids significantly in evaluating whether the testing system can effectively
243
      evoke the target frequency while suppressing the background noise. Each image was presented for
      a specified duration (125 milliseconds) per trial. Each trial lasted two seconds. After completing
244
      30 trials, participants were allowed a 30-second period to return to resting states. Then the latent
245
      generator used two best images (decided by an EEG decoder) to produce 20 new images for subse-
246   quent iterations in the experiment loop. The experimental picture is shown at Figure 3 (a), depicting
247   a typical experimental configuration, in which a participant outfitted with an dry electrode EEG
248   device (BrainUp research Lab developed with 7 recording channels) while engaged with a visual
249   stimulus presented on a computer screen. The electrode location is based on 10-20 system: O1,
250   O2,T5, P3, P4, T6 and Pz, which mainly cover V1 areas. The experimental details are described in
251   Appendix A.3.

517
518
      In this paper, we introduced the VEP Booster, a novel closed-loop AI framework designed to opti-
519
      mize reliable and stable EEG biomarkers. Utilizing a SSVEP protocol, our findings demonstrate that
520
      this system can robustly evoke individual biomarkers compared to natural states for all individuals.
521   In the context of single-trial Steady-State Visual Evoked Potential (SSVEP) binary classification ex-
522   periments, both intra- and inter-temporal analyses with the Visual Evoked Potential (VEP) Booster
523   yielded statistically significant enhancements.
524
      Also, our results have led us to identify several key factors that are critical for the effective operation
525
      of the system. First, the optimization of the DCGAN and EEG decoder must be synchronized to
526
      ensure that the decoder can detect subtle differences in generated images. Second, it is essential to
527   balance the GAN loss and the EEG decoder capabilities to prevent either aspect from dominating
528   the learning process, which could result in underfitting one side of the model.
529
530
      Regarding board applications, the VEP booster’s core capability is to generate customized visual
      stimuli based on real-time EEG feedback, which is fundamental not only for SSVEP but also for
531
      other types of visual protocols such as flash VEPs (fVEPs) and Event-related Potentials (ERPs),
532
      which enabling a broader application scope of visual stimulation protocols. This system’s perfor-
533   mance highlights its utility in providing tailored therapeutic interventions, promising a shift towards
534   more personalized and effective treatment strategies in neurological care.

637   A.1   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
638
639   The dataset was programmatically synthesized using a Python script and consists of images stan-
640   dardized to a resolution of 1024x1024 pixels against a black background. This dataset is categorized
641   into three distinct groups, with each group containing approximately 60 images. The first category
642   encapsulates a gradational luminance shift from black to white. The second category comprises
643   images with uniformly spaced black and white striped patterns. The third category features images
644
      with a progressively dense checkerboard grid.
645   The discriminator is constructed as a convolutional neural network, specifically designed to process
646   images with dimensions 64 × 64 × 3 (width × height × channels). It incorporates four strided
647   convolutional layers, which sequentially reduce the resolution of the input image. The layers are
      configured as follows:


                                                      12
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


648
             • Layer 1: Convolutional layer, accepting an input of 64 × 64 × 3, producing an output of
649            32 × 32 × 64.
650
651
             • Layer 2: Strided convolutional layer, outputting 16 × 16 × 128.
652          • Layer 3: Strided convolutional layer, further reducing to 8 × 8 × 256.
653
             • Layer 4: Strided convolutional layer, with a final output of 4 × 4 × 512.
654
655   Each convolutional layer, except the first, is followed by batch normalization and a LeakyReLU
656   activation function to improve training stability and introduce non-linearity. The output of the final
657   layer is passed through a sigmoid activation function, yielding a single scalar value. This scalar
658   represents the probability that the input image is classified as real.
659
      The generator in the Deep DCGAN framework utilizes a latent vector of length 100, sampled from
660   a standard normal distribution. This vector is processed through a series of transposed convolutional
661   layers to construct an image:
662
663          • Layer 1: Transposed convolutional layer, converts the latent vector into a 4 × 4 × 1024
664            feature map.
665
             • Layer 2: Transposed convolutional layer, upscales to 8 × 8 × 512.
666
667          • Layer 3: Transposed convolutional layer, further enlarges to 16 × 16 × 256.
668          • Layer 4: Transposed convolutional layer, increases resolution to 32 × 32 × 128.
669
             • Final Layer: Transposed convolutional layer, producing a full-resolution image of 64 ×
670
               64 × 3.
671
672   Batch normalization and ReLU activations are used in each layer, except in the final layer, where a
673   tanh activation function is used to normalize the image pixels between -1 and 1. This architecture
674   allows the generator to transform a simple distribution into complex data structures that mimic
675   the training images. All experiments were conducted on a computer running Linux Ubuntu 22.04
676   system, Python version 3.8 and pyTorch 1.10 . The hardware components are as follows: CPU: Intel
677   Xeon 6230 2.1GHz MEM: DDR4 1 TB DISK: 8 TB GPU: Quadro RTX 6000, 24 GB.

679   A.2   I MAGE P RE - CHECK P ROCESS
680
681   The image pre-check process extract a suite of five distinct features: standard deviation of pixel
682   values, edge count determined through the Sobel operator, energy of the high-frequency component
683
      from Haar wavelet decomposition, mean frequency from the Fourier transform, and skewness of the
      pixel intensity histogram. These features encapsulate various aspects of the texture of the image, the
684
      information about the edges, the frequency content, and the distribution characteristics. Based on
685
      these feature values, it computes the total pairwise Euclidean distances between the feature vectors
686   of the selected images. This metric serves as a quantitative measure of diversity, with the assumption
687   that a higher sum of distances indicates greater dissimilarity among images.
