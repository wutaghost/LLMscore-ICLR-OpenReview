009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The impressive performances of large language models (LLMs) and their im-
012            mense potential for commercialization have given rise to serious concerns over
013            the intellectual property (IP) of their training data. In particular, the synthetic texts
014            generated by LLMs may infringe the IP of the data being used to train the LLMs.
015            To this end, it is imperative to be able to perform source attribution by identifying
016            the data provider who contributed to the generation of a synthetic text by an LLM.
017            In this paper, we show that this problem can be tackled by watermarking, i.e.,
018            by enabling an LLM to generate synthetic texts with embedded watermarks that
019
               contain information about their source(s). We identify the key properties of such
               watermarking frameworks (e.g., source attribution accuracy, robustness against
020
               adversaries), and propose a source attribution framework that satisfies these key
021
               properties due to our algorithmic designs. Our framework enables an LLM to
022            learn an accurate mapping from the generated texts to data providers, which sets
023            the foundation for effective source attribution. Extensive empirical evaluations
024            show that our framework achieves effective source attribution.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Large language models (LLMs) (Ouyang et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023a) have recently demon-
030   strated remarkable performances and hence received a surging interest. These LLMs, trained using
031   massive text data, have displayed impressive text generation abilities. This has given rise to the
032   immense potential of adopting LLM-generated texts for commercial use. However, this potential
033
      commercialization has led to major concerns regarding the intellectual property (IP) of training data
      for LLMs because the texts generated by an LLM may infringe the IP of the data being used to train
034
      the LLM. These concerns have been reflected by the increasing regulations on data protection re-
035
      lated to AI models. For example, the Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity has stressed
036   the necessity of certifying the source of online content produced by generative models (Rosenthol,
037   2022). Therefore, it is of crucial importance for LLMs to be equipped with source attribution for
038   their generated synthetic texts.
039
      In source attribution, given some texts generated by an LLM, its aim is to find the source respon-
040
      sible for the generation of these texts. That is, if the data from a data provider has been used to
041
      train the LLM and contributed to the generation of a sentence by the LLM, then source attribution
042   identifies this data provider. Moreover, source attribution also improves the interpretability of LLM-
043   generated texts: for example, if the generated content from an LLM is attributed to a trustworthy
044   source (e.g., a peer-reviewed academic paper), then the user is likely to consider the content more
045   reliable. The ability to perform source attribution can endow the LLM with the capability of data
046   provenance, which presents a different problem where a data provider can verify whether its data
047   has been used to train the LLM. This problem can be solved with source attribution. Specifically, a
048   data provider can check the source of the generated texts from an LLM via source attribution, and
049   hence verify data provenance, as detailed in App. E.1.6.
050   While some recent works have addressed the problem of data provenance in LLMs (Kirchenbauer
051   et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023a), to the best of our knowledge, effective source attribution for LLMs
052   remains an open problem. In contrast to data provenance which presents a binary determination,
053   source attribution aims to identify the specific data source(s) influencing a particular output, which
      presents a more challenging task. Our work focuses on addressing source attribution rather than

069
      To perform source attribution for LLM-generated texts, a natural solution involves watermarking,
070
      i.e., by enabling the LLM to generate synthetic texts with embedded watermarks that contain in-
071   formation about their source(s). Consequently, source attribution can be performed by examining
072   the watermarks embedded in the generated texts. Our problem setting (Fig. 1) involves 3 parties:
073   data providers contributing text data that may be used for LLM training, an honest third-party LLM
074   platform operator producing an LLM with generated texts that embed watermarks (hence allowing
075   for source attribution), and users of the texts generated by this LLM. The users may request source
076   attribution for the LLM-generated synthetic texts to find out which data provider is responsible for
077   the generated texts. We consider scenarios where each data provider contributes ample balanced
078   data with unique characteristics, i.e., the data from different data providers exhibit dissimilarities.
079
      This encompasses a wide variety of real-world scenarios: For example, online articles written by
      different authors (i.e., data providers) usually feature their unique writing styles. On the other hand,
080
      we do not consider individual documents/sentences as data providers since they have insufficient
081
      data. Additionally, this work focuses on single-source scenarios, where the generated content can
082   be attributed to a single data provider.
083
084   An effective source attribution framework has to satisfy some key properties: The framework should
085
      (1) achieve accurate source attribution, (2) be robust against malicious attacks on the watermarks,
      (3) preserve the performance (i.e., text generation ability) of the LLM, (4) be scalable to a large
086
      number of data providers, (5) ensure that the generated watermarks are transferable to (i.e., persist
087
      after being used as training data for) other LLMs, and (6) be adaptable to fit different LLMs. Sec. 2
088   discusses these key properties in more detail. To this end, this paper introduces a WAtermarking
089   for Source Attribution (WASA) framework which, to our best knowledge, is the first framework
090   capable of enabling effective source attribution in text generated by large language models
091   Our WASA framework assigns a unique watermark (i.e., imperceptible to human eyes) to every data
092   provider, and enables an LLM (coined as WASA-LLM) to learn an accurate mapping from the texts
093   of different data providers to their corresponding watermarks (Sec. 3). So, if a data provider is
094   responsible for generating a sentence, then our WASA-LLM is able to include the unique watermark
095   of this data provider in this generated sentence, which naturally supports source attribution. Our
096
      contributions are summarized below:
097             • We propose to use watermarking for source attribution on LLM-generated synthetic texts
098               and identify the key properties of such source attribution frameworks.
099             • We introduce the WASA framework which satisfies these key properties and is hence capable
100               of producing LLMs whose generated texts allow for effective source attribution.
101             • We perform extensive empirical evaluations (Sec. 4) to verify that our WASA framework
102
                  satisfies these key properties and achieves effective source attribution.

104   2      K EY P ROPERTIES OF WATERMARKING FOR S OURCE ATTRIBUTION
105
106   Here, we first present a clear definition of source attribution. For a piece of LLM-generated synthetic
107   text s, if s correlates the most with the LLM’s training data provided by one data provider compared
      to other providers, we recognize that data provider as the source for s and denote as a one-hot label

108
      ys := {0, 0, ..., 1, ..., 0} where ys [i] = 1 if ys [i] is the source, otherwise ys [i] = 0; the dimension is
109   n, which is the total number of data providers and is fixed. The goal of source attribution is: given a
110   piece of LLM-generated text s, we want to find a mapping s → ys that attributes s to its source ys .
111
112
      To simplify the problem, we discuss the following scenarios: (1) While x may correlate with mul-
      tiple training data from provides, meaning that y may not necessarily be a one-hot vector, we only
113
      consider attribution to a single data source (that x correlates the most with), restricting the y to be
114
      one-hot vector in our case, and present case studies when attributing to more than one data source
115   in App. G.3; (2) There might be an edge case where the generated content x correlates the most
116   with pretraining data (from public training datasets) rather than data from data providers. We do not
117   consider this case in our paper and ensure that in our evaluations the generated contents are related
118   to the data from providers by carefully designing controlled experiments.
119
      In this paper, we would like to address the problem of source attribution with watermarking. Specif-
120
      ically, to use watermarking for source attribution, we first transform the data providers y to water-
121   marks wtm correspondingly: encoder(y) = wtm where encoder denotes the watermark encoder.
122   During LLM training, we aim to allow the LLM to learn a mapping g : s → wtm to generate
123   watermarks along with synthetic texts. Then during inference, we can perform the mapping s → ys
124   by ys = decoder(g(s)) where decoder(wtm) = y is the watermark decoder function, translating
125   the watermark to sources for the user. Importantly, since each generated content s must correlate
126   with some pieces of training data, there always exists a source ys which is the most correlated data
127   source with s. Hence, under all conditions (except the special case mentioned above), as long as a
128   user requests, s should be attributed to its source ys . In our WASA framework, since we assume that
129
      all data providers provide watermarked training data, we can perform source attribution under all
      conditions: Upon request, we can perform ys = decoder(g(s)) and map the generated watermark to
130
      the corresponding data provider ys .
131
132   Subsequently, we discuss the key properties for an effective watermarking source attribution frame-
133   work and how our WASA framework satisfies them.
134   Accuracy. Accurate source attribution should be enforced. Our WASA framework achieves this by
135   training the WASA-LLM to map texts from different data providers to their respective watermarks.
136   Specifically, we first train WASA-LLM using watermarked texts (Sec. 3.1) and separate the pre-
137   diction/generation spaces for the texts and watermarks to both reduce the complexity of watermark
138   prediction (Sec. 3.2) and explicitly enforce watermark generation (Sec. 3.3). Empirical results in
139   Sec. 4.1 demonstrate the effectiveness in source attribution.
140   Robustness. Generated text with watermarks should be robust against malicious attacks. Since
141   our trained WASA-LLM is able to learn an accurate mapping from the texts to the watermarks as
142   mentioned (a) it can be exploited to regenerate the watermarks even if generated texts are tampered
143   with and (b) it maintains generating the correct watermarks even if the input texts (prompts) are
144   perturbed, which are empirically verified in Sec. 4.2.
145   Scalability. The framework should cater to a large number of data providers. The design of the
146   watermark (Sec. 3.1) facilitates the generation of numerous unique watermarks and the scalability
147   can be empirically verified in Sec. 4.3.
148
      Performance Preservation. The introduction of watermarks should (a) not significantly degrade
149
      the text generation ability of the LLM (b) nor affect the readability of the LLM-generated synthetic
150
      texts too much. We empirically show in Sec. 4.4 that our WASA-LLM preserves (a), and the water-
151   marks are carefully designed to achieve (b) (see App. G.1).
152
153   Transferability. After the generated watermarked texts are used as training data for other LLMs,
154
      their generated texts should preserve the watermarks. We achieve this by ensuring that the water-
      marked training data of our WASA-LLM has the same structure as the generated watermarked data.
155
156   Adaptability. The framework should be easily adapted to fit different LLMs. Our WASA framework
157   only requires mild modifications to the LLMs and can hence adopt a wide variety of LLMs using
158   the transformer architecture, as shown in Sec. 4.1.
159   We have only listed above the most essential properties of such source attribution frameworks; there
160   may be additional considerations depending on specific applications. In Sec. 3, we will discuss in
161   more detail how our WASA framework satisfies these key properties due to our algorithmic designs.

170
171   Sec. 3.1 discusses watermark design and embedding process. Sec. 3.2 details the training of WASA-
172   LLM with watermarked texts and its alignment with key properties. Sec. 3.3 explains how our
173   trained WASA-LLM produces synthetic texts with watermarks for source attribution.

      3.1   E MBEDDING WATERMARKS INTO T EXTS
176   Firstly, the LLM platform operator embeds a unique watermark for each data provider’s texts.
177
178
      Design of Watermarks. We construct the watermarks using Unicode characters which are imper-
      ceptible to human eyes (yet can be decoded by machine learning models). Some of these invisible
179
      characters have also been adopted in other studies with language models (Boucher et al., 2022). Ev-
180
      ery watermark is made up of 10 characters, each of which is chosen among the following 6 Unicode
181   characters: U+200B, U+200C, U+200D, U+2062, U+2063, U+2064. We chose these characters
182   because they are found to be invisible on many commonly used platforms. So, these watermarks
183   preserve the semantic meaning of the original texts to human readers (Fig. 2). Also, our WASA frame-
184   work can easily adopt other choices of characters depending on the use cases. Moreover, these 10-
185   character watermarks allow us to construct numerous combinations and hence achieve scalability
186   to a large number of data providers. As shown in App. F.10, reducing the watermark length trades
187   off scalability for source attribution accuracy.
188   Embedding Watermarks into Sentences. To enable our WASA-LLM to learn the mapping from
189   the texts of different data providers to their watermarks, it is important to only embed watermarks
190   into the sentences that are representative of the unique characteristics of the data providers. To this
191   end, we calculate the term frequency-inverse document frequency (TF-IDF) scores of all sentences
192   from a data provider and select the sentences with the top 20% of the TF-IDF scores (i.e., most
193   representative sentences) for watermarking, which empirically yields the best trade-off of source
194   attribution accuracy vs. text generation performance among different tested proportions, as reported
195
      in App. F.8. For every selected sentence, we embed our 10-character watermark at a random position
      in the sentence, which allows the LLM to learn to map texts of different lengths to the watermarks
196
      and also makes it harder for an adversary to remove/modify the watermarks. As empirically verified
197
      in App. F.2, our method of selecting sentences for watermarking based on TF-IDF indeed leads to
198   more accurate source attribution than random selection.

275   3.3   G ENERATING T EXTS WITH E MBEDDED WATERMARKS USING WASA-LLM
276
277   After our WASA-LLM is trained (Sec. 3.2), it can generate synthetic texts which naturally include
278   both the word and watermark tokens due to their separate prediction/generation spaces. To further
279
      improve the alignment between our training and generation stages, we introduce a special token
      [W T M ] which is similar to other specialized tokens and in the vocabulary of V word tokens: When
280
      training our WASA-LLM using the watermarked texts, [W T M ] is added right before the watermark
281
      tokens during tokenization so that the presence of [W T M ] indicates that the subsequent m = 10
282   tokens are watermark tokens; when generating texts, if [W T M ] is encountered/generated, then it in-
283   dicates that our WASA-LLM should switch to generating watermark tokens. After watermark tokens
284   have been generated, our WASA-LLM resumes the word token generation. Fig. 9 (App. G.1) shows
285   the WASA-LLM-generated synthetic texts with embedded watermarks, which verifies that the water-
286   marks are imperceptible to human eyes. Subsequently, when a user requests source attribution for
287   some synthetic texts generated by our WASA-LLM, the LLM platform operator uses a designated
288   watermark decoder algorithm to extract the generated watermark from the texts and then attribute
289   these texts to the source (data provider) whose watermark matches the generated watermark (Fig. 1).
290
      The matching algorithm is elaborated in App. C.

      5     R ELATED W ORK
463
464   In this section, we will review related works on source attribution and data provenance; further
465   discussions on watermarking natural languages and models as well as text steganography are in
466   App. A. Recent studies by Song & Shmatikov (2019) verify dataset usage in language model training
467   through membership inference attacks. Liu et al. (2023a) have proposed to plant backdoor triggers in
468   training texts to check for data usage, but they can impair text generation performance. Importantly,
469   the above works have only focused on data provenance and cannot be easily adapted to perform
470   effective source attribution. Abdelnabi & Fritz (2021) have embedded messages post-generation via
471
      adversarial training, which means the messages can only be used for IP protection and cannot be
      used for source attribution during generation. Studies on data selection (Lin et al., 2024; Xia et al.,
472
      2024; Wettig et al., 2024) can potentially attribute data in supervised downstream tasks but cannot
473
      handle LLM generation in general settings when lacking test points with ground truth. Some works
474   in computer vision have tackled the problem of source attribution (Marra et al., 2018; Yu et al., 2019;
475   2021). However, to the best of our knowledge, effective source attribution for the texts generated by
476   language models remains an open problem and is the focus of our work here.

      6     C ONCLUSION
479
480   This paper describes our proposed WASA framework which allows for effective source attribution
481   as a solution to intellectual property infringement in the context of LLMs. By embedding unique
482   watermarks into LLM-generated texts, WASA not only enhances the reliability and interpretability of
483   LLM-generated content but also provides a crucial tool for data protection, allowing data providers
484   to verify the use of their contributions in LLM training processes. The extensive empirical evalua-
485   tions of the WASA framework affirm its effectiveness in achieving accurate source attribution while
      satisfying the key properties we have identified above. Since our WASA is the first effective source

      attribution framework for LLM-generated texts, it faces some limitations which may call for future
487   work. For example, though we have shown that our WASA is robust against various adversarial at-
488   tacks, it is unclear whether it is robust against more advanced/sophisticated attacks, which may be
489   achieved through adversarial training in future work.

499   E THICAL C ONSIDERATIONS
500
501   Similar to other research topics on LLMs, watermarking the synthetic texts generated by LLMs for
502
      source attribution requires a thoughtful and ethical approach due to its potential societal implica-
      tions. That is, it is important to take necessary measures to avoid causing harm to certain parties.
503
      Potential risks related to our watermarking framework include the following:
504
505          • Privacy Risks. Watermarking can potentially reveal sensitive information about data
506            providers, thus leading to privacy breaches or the possibility of re-identification if not han-
507            dled carefully. In our WASA framework, only the watermark can be seen in the generated
508
               data, which does not directly imply personal information about the data providers. Pri-
               vacy can be preserved given that the mapping from watermarks to data providers is kept
509
               confidential.
510
             • Chilling Effects. Watermarking may discourage some data providers from sharing their
511            datasets, especially if they fear potential misuse or unintended consequences of having
512            their data linked to specific research outcomes.
513          • Data Manipulation. While watermarks are meant to be unobtrusive and our WASA frame-
514            work has been shown to be robust against various adversarial attacks, there can be un-
515            foreseen real-world instances where malicious actors attempt to manipulate the watermark,
516            which may lead to negative consequences such as the dissemination of altered or mislead-
517            ing information.
518   To address these potential risks, it is essential to carefully consider the ethical implications of our
519   watermarking framework and implement measures to protect the privacy and interests of all involved
520   parties, particularly those who are more susceptible to harm. Researchers should conduct compre-
521   hensive risk assessments and engage in transparent communication with data providers to ensure the
522   responsible and ethical use of watermarked data. Additionally, incorporating diverse perspectives
523   and involving vulnerable communities in the decision-making process can help identify and mitigate
524   potential harm effectively.

      A    A DDITIONAL R ELATED W ORKS
811
812
813   In addition to the previous works discussed in Sec. 5 that are most closely related to ours, we will
814   give a review of additional related works on watermarking natural languages and text steganography,
815
      as well as recent works on watermarking language models.
816
817   Watermarking Natural Language/Text Stegano-graphy. In natural language processing, water-
818   marking and steganography are closely related in that they both desire stealthiness and robustness.
819   However, there are also important differences because watermarking emphasizes the importance
820   of ownership, whereas steganography focuses on the secret communication of messages. Language
821   watermarking is used to protect the integrity and authorship of digital texts (Kamaruddin et al., 2018;
822   Podilchuk & Delp, 2001). Early approaches of language watermarking are mostly rule-based and
823   make use of linguistic techniques such as synonym substitution (Topkara et al., 2006b) and sentence
824
      structure alteration (Topkara et al., 2006a) to embed watermarks while attempting to preserve the
      semantic meaning of the original texts. However, these approaches usually lead to deteriorated text
825
      quality and are not scalable. Some recent works have aimed to develop advanced text steganography
826
      methods using deep learning. The work of Yang et al. (2019) has utilized recurrent neural networks
827   to automatically generate steganographic texts, and the work of Ziegler et al. (2019) has proposed
828   to first convert the secret messages into bit strings and then map them to the cover text based on
829   arithmetic coding with the help of GPT2 (Radford et al., 2019).
830
831
832   Watermarking Language Models. Some recent works have proposed methods to add watermarks
833
      to language models in order to protect the IP of the models (Dai et al., 2022; Gu et al., 2023; He et al.,
      2022; Zhao et al., 2022). These methods allow the verification of model ownership and are hence
834
      able to protect the economic interests of model owners. Specifically, the work of He et al. (2022)
835
      has employed lexical replacement to watermark the language model output and used hypothesis
836   testing for post-hoc model ownership verification. The work of Gu et al. (2023) has adopted back-
837   door attacks to embed black-box watermarks into pre-trained language models, which is achieved
838   by using rare words as well as a combination of common words as backdoor triggers and verifying
839   the watermarks by calculating the extraction success rate. Apart from model protection, multiple
840   methods (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023; Kuditipudi et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024) have been proposed
841   to use watermarking to distinguish between human-generated and model-generated synthetic texts.
842   Kirchenbauer et al. (2023) softly constrain the word choices when the model generates synthetic
843   texts and use hypothesis testing to make the distinction. More recently, the work of Kuditipudi et al.
844
      (2023) has improved the above method by developing a distortion-free method, which ensures that
      the watermarks do not change the sampling distribution of the texts. The work of Lu et al. (2024)
845
      also refines the same method by ensuring the influence of a token during watermark detection to be
846
      proportional to its entropy. Finally, in terms of security in watermarking models, Liu et al. (2024b)
847   develop a compact watermarking model that embeds a semantic watermark within model outputs,
848   enhancing their robustness against adversarial text modifications. Meanwhile, Liu et al. (2024a)
849   employ two distinct neural networks to generate and detect watermarks, enabling public verification
850   of the watermark while maintaining the confidentiality of the secret key throughout the watermark
851   generation process. Additionally, He et al. (2024) introduce a Cross-lingual Watermark Removal
852   Attack (CWRA), which can effectively remove watermarks by interfering with the watermark gen-
853   eration process through translation into another language. Importantly, these methods cannot be
854   used to perform source attribution for the texts generated by language models, which we focus on in
855
      this work.
856

1335
1336   As discussed in Sec. 4.2, an adversary may additionally modify the content of the generated sentence
1337   while removing/modifying the generated watermarks. Here, we will consider insertion, deletion,
1338   synonym substitution, and syntactic transformation attacks. In insertion attacks on a generated
1339   watermarked sentence, either one word is randomly inserted into the sentence (i.e., localized inser-
1340   tion attacks), or various words are randomly interspersed throughout the sentence (i.e., dispersed
1341
       insertion attacks) (Kamaruddin et al., 2018). For dispersed insertion attacks, we vary the attack

1342

1343

1344   ated sentence with their synonyms while preserving the semantic meaning of the sentence. Again,
1345   we tested different attack strengths by varying the percentage of randomly deleted and substituted
1346   words. In addition, we also performed the syntactic transformation attack on the generated sen-
1347   tences whereby an adversary transforms the sentences (without altering their semantic meanings)
1348   via techniques such as modifying the prepositions, tenses, and other syntax components. Here, we
1349   adopt two strong variants of such attacks on our WASA-LLM obtained from Llama2: Firstly, we

1606   Here we consider the special case where the LLM-generated content is not attributable to any data
1607   provider. Note that in our main experiments, such a case does not exist since all data providers have
1608   watermarked their training data. Such unattributable content might be generated from public datasets
1609   used for pretraining the LLM, but we do not consider attributing sources to the public datasets in this
1610   paper as stated in Sec. 2; instead, we have focused on attributing to the data providers’ watermarked
1611
       private datasets. Moreover, it is hard to design prompts to enforce the model to generate content
       only from pretrain-knowledge, making it difficult to design corresponding experiments. Therefore,
1612
       here we choose the setting by training our framework with data from both 5 watermarked data
1613
       providers and 5 unwatermarked data providers to force our WASA-LLM to be able to generate content
1614   that is not attributable to the watermarked data providers. In this setting, our framework generates
1615   watermarks for 12% of the sentences generated from the 5 unwatermarked data providers while
1616   generating watermarks for 87.6% of the sentences generated from the 5 watermarked data providers.
1617   By analyzing the watermarks for sentences from unwatermarked data providers, we observe that
1618   100% of these watermarks are from the watermarked data providers. This suggests that if there
