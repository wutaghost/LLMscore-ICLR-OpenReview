009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            An emerging paradigm in vision-and-language navigation (VLN) is the use of
012            history-aware multi-modal transformer models. Given a language instruction, these
013            models take observation and history as input and predict the most appropriate
014            action for an agent. While employing these models has significantly improved
015            performance, the scale of these models can be a bottleneck in practical settings
016
               where computational resources are limited (e.g., in robots). In this work, we present
               a novel input-adaptive navigation method for efficient VLN. We first characterize
017
               the overthinking problem in VLN and show that none of the existing input-adaptive
018
               mechanisms successfully reduce overthinking without causing significant per-
019            formance degradation. Our method addresses this problem by developing three
020            adaptive algorithms deployed at different levels: (1) We develop an adaptive ap-
021            proach that improves spatial efficiency; we only process a subset of panoramic
022            views at each observation of an agent. (2) We also achieve model-level efficiency
023            by developing adaptive thresholding for the early-exit method we employ, based
024            on the importance of each view in navigation. (3) To achieve temporal efficiency,
025            we design a caching mechanism to avoid processing views that an agent has seen
026            before. In evaluations with six VLN benchmark tasks, we demonstrate over a 2ˆ
027
               reduction in computation across two off-the-shelf VLN agents.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Progress in vision-and-language navigation (VLN) has been enabled by larger models (Hao et al.,
032   2020; Chen et al., 2021; Moudgil et al., 2021; Guhur et al., 2021; Hong et al., 2021) trained on
033   increasingly large datasets (Wang et al., 2023). These models can process and interpret complex data,
034   enabling them to understand and act upon natural language instructions within visual environments.
035   Despite the success, there is a growing concern about their computational demands. The need for
036   substantial computational power poses a significant challenge for deployment in resource-constrained
037   settings, such as robots, where low-power consumption increasingly becomes critical.
038   A potential solution to addressing these computational demands is input-adaptive inference. The main
039   idea is to reduce overthinking (Kaya et al., 2019): as shallow networks are sufficient for the majority
040   of samples to make decisions, e.g., class predictions, input-adaptive methods (Huang et al., 2018; Liu
041   et al., 2020; Xin et al., 2020; Tang et al., 2023) stop forwarding preemptively during inference and
042   return intermediate outputs when the internal decisions of a model converge. During inference, they
043   demonstrate up to 50% computational savings while preserving model performance.
044   In this work, we study the overthinking problem in a new domain—vision-and-language navigation—
045   and address this issue by proposing a novel input-adaptive navigation method. Prior work has focused
046   on tasks where inputs are processed independently of one another, e.g., in classification. But they
047   do not account for sequential decision tasks like navigation, where a model makes several decisions
048   over time based on inputs with spatio-temporal dependencies. In consequence, existing methods are
049   designed to reduce overthinking within a model but do not consider the cognitive overload caused by
050
      the visual inputs the model needs to process in navigation. Although these models are not yet widely
      deployed in real-world navigation tasks, it is still crucial to evaluate whether our efficiency gains are
051
      robust to common visual corruptions.

      Vision-and-language navigation (VLN). Research in this area has been supported by the develop-
102   ment of high-quality simulators such as Matterport3D (Chang et al., 2017) which we heavily leverage
103   in our work. Agents developed towards this challenging problem have ranged from earlier recurrent
104   models (Anderson et al., 2018; Fried et al., 2018) to more recent transformer-based models (Hong
105   et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021; 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Kamath et al., 2023). While the recent agents
106   achieve superior visual-language alignment and improved performance, their increased complexity
107   leads to higher computational costs during inference. One reason for this can be attributed to the
      transformer’s quadratic complexity in the length of input tokens (Vaswani, 2017). In addition, they


                                                                  2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      operate in environments with panoramic observation and action space, often consisting of multiple
109   single-view images (Fried et al., 2018). Our work is the first providing a tunable trade-off between
110   computational demands and accuracy.
111
112
      Input-adaptive mechanisms for computational efficiency. Prior work introduces two distinct mech-
      anisms for input-adaptive inference: adaptive neural networks (AdNNs) and multi-exit architectures.
113
      AdNNs (Wang et al., 2018; Figurnov et al., 2017) are designed to dynamically skip certain blocks of
114
      the model to save the computations during inference. In contrast, multi-exit architectures (Teerapit-
115   tayanon et al., 2016; Huang et al., 2018; Kaya et al., 2019; Xin et al., 2020) introduce an additional
116   component to the model, such as classifiers attached to each internal layer (early-exits), allowing
117   the model to preemptively stop running forwards once stopping criteria are met. Both mechanisms
118   demonstrate computational savings while minimizing performance loss in classification tasks (e.g., a
119   50% reduction in computation at a utility loss of „10%). Our method employs multi-exit architec-
120   tures because AdNNs are constrained to ResNet-like architectures. Most multi-exit architectures are
121   developed for classification tasks and are not compatible with VLN, where an agent utilizes visual
122   and/or language representations generated from encoders. The closest work by Tang et al. (2023)
123
      developed an adaptation (MuE) to Transformer-based encoders. But despite our best effort, MuE
      does not provide any computational savings in VLN tasks (shown in Sec 4.1). A separate line of
124
      research explores methods for compressing models, such as quantization and pruning. These methods
125
      are orthogonal to our study and can be applied in conjunction with our method (see Appendix F).

129
130   Datasets. Following prior work, we evaluate our method on six different datasets: Room-to-
131   Room (R2R) (Anderson et al., 2018), R2R-Back (Chen et al., 2021), R2R-Last (Chen et al., 2021),
132
      REVERIE (Qi et al., 2020), CVDN (Thomason et al., 2020), and SOON (Zhu et al., 2021).
133   VLN Agents. We consider two off-the-shelf VLN agents: HAMT (Chen et al., 2021) and
134   DUET (Chen et al., 2022). HAMT has four components: (1) the Vision Transformer (ViT) (Dosovit-
135   skiy et al., 2021) that compute visual representations from panorama view captured at the current
136   navigation step, (2) BERT (Devlin et al., 2019) that returns language representations from human
137   instructions, (3) the Hierarchical ViT that progressively encode temporal dynamics across panoramas
138
      in the history into representations, (4) the Cross-modal Transformer that captures multi-modal rela-
      tionships between the three representations, and predicts the most appropriate action. DUET consists
139
      of five components but operates differently from HAMT. (1) the ViT extracts visual representations
140
      and object features (e.g., bounding boxes and labels) from panorama views, (2) and uses the panorama
141   encoder on them for topological mapping. For global action planning, (3) the text encoder generates
142   language representations of human language instructions. Next, (4) the Coarse-scale Cross-modal
143   Encoder determines the next action within a global action space, while (5) the Fine-scale Cross-modal
144   Encoder selects actions within a local action space. The final action is then made by fusing the both.
145
      Evaluation metrics. To evaluate how successful an agent’s navigation is, we employ four metrics
146
      from prior work (Chen et al., 2021; Krantz & Lee, 2022): (1) Trajectory length (TL): the path length
147   an agent navigated in meters. (2) Oracle Success rate (OSR): the ratio of trajectories where at least
148   one viewpoint along the agent’s path can see the target object within a 3 meter range. (3) Success
149   rate (SR): the ratio of trajectories where an agent’s final position is within 3 meters of the target.
150   (4) Success rate normalized by inverse path length (SPL): the SR normalized by the ratio between
151   the shortest path’s length and the predicted path’s length. We compute the total giga floating-point
152   operations per second (GFLOPs) an agent requires to navigate to measure the computational savings
153   our method offers. We also measure the wall-time (in seconds) it takes for an agent to navigate to its
154   final location. But, we prioritize GFLOPs over wall-time because the latter depends on the hardware
155
      accelerators and the software implementation. Please refer to Appendix for more details.

      4.2.2   U SING ADAPTIVE THRESHOLDS AS STOPPING CRITERIA
276
277   Now on top of our k-extension, we design an adaptive mechanism to early-exit the extended views and
278   further improve model-level computational efficiency. As we describe in the previous sections, we
279   focus on MuE, the only early-exit mechanism compatible with encoder models. All other early-exits
280   are developed for classification tasks (or decoder models for natural language processing).
281   Using the budgeted-batch inference. The current implementation of MuE processes each test sample
282   with its input-adaptive mechanism. However, this per-sample, anytime strategy is incompatible with
283   our scenario, where the agent processes a batch of 36 views in a single panorama at once with the ViT.
284   While each view in the batch should ideally exit at different layers, this per-sample approach forces
285   all the views to use the same exit layer. To address this issue, we employ the concept, budgeted-
286   batch inference, proposed by Huang et al. (2018). It allows each sample in a batch to use “uneven"
287   computations, meaning that processing can stop at different layers for each sample, all within a fixed
288
      computational budget. We assign a sufficiently large computational budget so that the mechanism
      can handle the worst-case complexity, where none of the samples in a batch utilize early stopping.
289
290   Our adaptive thresholding. In Sec 4.2.1, we find that navigable views are the most important, and
291   the importance of a view decreases as it gets farther from the navigable views in a panorama. We thus
292   design a mechanism to apply the early-exit threshold differently based on the importance of each
293   view. We propose a concept, rank: for views with a low rank, we use an aggressive early-exit with a
294
      smaller threshold, while for high-ranked views, we use a conservative (higher) threshold. Suppose

306   4.2.3   H ARNESSING TEMPORAL LOCALITY
307
308   Our final insight is that during navigation, an agent will encounter similar views multiple times,
309
      leading to temporal redundancy. For example, the views at navigation step i will not differ signifi-
      cantly from those at step i ` 1. The agent may also revisit the same surroundings due to misleading
310
      navigation or encounter similar-looking but less important surroundings, such as ceilings or walls.
311
312   To reduce this temporal redundancy, we employ locality-sensitive hashing (LSH). LSH works as a
313   caching mechanism: instead of processing similar views repetitively with the ViT, it stores views and
314   their visual representations encountered in previous navigation steps, and retrieves them at the current
315
      step. We use the hashing algorithm SimHash, which employs the random projection (Charikar, 2002;
      Andoni & Indyk, 2008). SimHash maps high-dimensional views (3ˆ224ˆ224 in our experiments)
316
      into low-dimensionality binary encodings. Given a view v and the randomly initialized hyperplanes
317
      hi , where i P t1, . . . , nu, the algorithm computes which side of the hyperplanes the view v is. If v is
318   on the top side of hi , SimHash assigns 1; otherwise, it is 0. Similar views are then encoded as the
319   same binary encoding of length n, e.g., 010. . . 1, and we use this as the key for storing pairs of views
320   and their visual encoding. We set n to 10. Like early-exiting, we refrain from hashing the navigable
321   views and fully process them. Combined with our k-extension, we significantly reduce the space
322   complexity increased by caching. We store only the subset of views processed by the ViT for each
323   trajectory. With this mechanism, we achieve an additional 2% computational savings without utility
      loss. Please refer to Appendix C for more details and an analysis of the added storage overhead.

      6    C ONCLUSION
529
      We propose an input-adaptive inference method to mitigate overthinking in vision-and-language navi-
530
      gation (VLN) and achieve computational efficiency. Unlike the overthinking problem in conventional
531
      domains, such as object recognition or natural language comprehension, addressing overthinking in
532   VLN presents three unique challenges: (1) How can we leverage spatial locality in views observed
533   by an agent at a navigation step? (2) How can we reduce temporal redundancy across the agent’s
534   navigation steps? (3) How can we use the mechanisms designed to address the two challenges to
535   adaptively set early-exit thresholds of an existing method? We present three novel techniques to
536   address them individually. In our evaluation, we demonstrate a 2ˆ reduction in computations while
537   preserving performance across six VLN benchmarks. Moreover, we assess the robustness of our
538   approach under various visual corruptions that may occur in practice, and identify challenges to
539   address for future work. We hope this work inspires future research on developing efficient (and
      robust) VLN algorithms and promote their widespread adoption in real-world settings.

      A    E XPERIMENT SETUP IN DETAIL
757
758   We describe the experimental setup used to evaluate our input-adaptive inference mechanism in detail.
759   We implemented our strategy on top of the codebases provided by the authors of the HAMT (Chen
760   et al., 2021) and DUET (Chen et al., 2022). During inference, instead of using cached image features,
761   we integrate a ViT-B/16 (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) model to process the images directly.
762
      Hardware and software. We run our experiments on a machine equiped with an Intel Xeon processor
763
      with 48 cores, 64GB of DRAM, and 8 NVIDIA A40 GPUs, with all inference tasks performed on a
764   single GPU using a batch size of 1. Following the original HAMT study, we use Python v3.8.5 and
765   PyTorch v1.7.1, along with CUDA v10.1. For GFLOPs calculations, we use the Python library thop.
766
767
      Datasets. We describe the benchmarking datasets we use in detail:
768    • R2R (Anderson et al., 2018) is based on Matterport3D (Chang et al., 2017), containing 10,567
769      panorama views taken from 90 photo-realistic houses. The dataset includes 7,189 shortest-path
770      trajectories, and each of them is associated with 3 natural language instructions. The training,
771      validation (seen), validation (unseen), and test (unseen) sets include 61, 56, 11, and 18 houses,
772      respectively. The validation (seen) set consists of houses in the training set, typically used to
773
         check the generalization status of a model during training, while the sets marked as ‘unseen’ are
         the houses not in the training set.
774
775    • R2R-Back (Chen et al., 2021) requires the agent to return to its starting point after reaching
776
         the destination. To complete the task, the agent must remember its navigation history. A return
         command is appended to each R2R instruction, and the reversed path is provided as guidance for
777
         the return trip.
778
779
       • R2R-Last (Chen et al., 2021) uses only the last sentence from the original R2R instructions to
         describe the destination.
780
781
       • REVERIE (Qi et al., 2020) provides high-level instructions, closer to those given by humans,
         replacing the step-by-step instructions of R2R. Instead of navigating to a target location, the agent
782
         is required to identify and localize the target object upon arrival, making the task more complex
783
         and realistic. The dataset includes 4,140 target objects, which are categorized into 489 distinct
784      groups.
785
       • CVDN (Thomason et al., 2020) requires the agent to navigate based on long, potentially unclear
786
         instructions. The agent interacts with a navigator through question and answer dialog to clarify
787      and complete the task. In total, it has 2,050 human-human navigation dialogues, consisting of
788      over 7,000 navigation trajectories accompanied by question-answer interactions, covering 83
789      matterport3D houses.
790    • SOON (Zhu et al., 2021) is similar to REVERIE but contains longer and more detailed instructions.
791      The average length of these instructions is 47 words, with path lengths varying from 2 to 21
792      steps. It requires the agent to navigate by understanding the relationship between objects in the
793      environment to accurately locate the target object.

      B    O PTIMAL HYPERPARAMETER CHOICE FOR ADAPTING M U E TO OUR WORK

      To best evaluate MuE on VLN tasks, we perform a hyperparameter sweep over the threshold used
798   for early-exiting. Figure 7 presents the performance (in SR) and GFLOPs across different early exit
799   thresholds applied to the MuE version of ViT used in the HAMT agent, tested on the R2R dataset.
800   The lowest threshold we report is 0.99, as lower thresholds caused a dramatic drop in performance
801   (more than 50%). As the threshold increases, the success rate of the MuE agent increases substantially
802   but at the cost of computational savings. Even for thresholds close to 1, meaning that the ViT is using
803   a majority of its layers for each input, we still see a large performance drop compared to the baseline
804   agent. As we discuss in Sec 4.2, this is likely because MuE statically applies early-exits, causing it to
805   under-process important components of the panorama such as navigable views.
806   Why does MuE underprocess important views? The intuition behind MuE (Tang et al., 2023) is that
807   the activations of Transformer-based vision models saturate, where their similarity between layers
808   peaks early on, and is maintained at future stages of computation, suggesting a lack of new/useful
809   information. MuE then exploits this property to skip the later layers without a significant loss in
      performance. So, for MuE to be successful, the similarity of activations must sufficiently saturate and

      C    O UR LSH ALGORITHM IN DETAIL
834
835   A core mechanism we introduce in Sec 4.2.3 is our SimHash algorithm, used to avoid reprocessing
836
      previously seen or near-identical images. Algorithm 2 covers our implementation in detail.
837   (line 1-9) Hashing RGB vectors. Given an image, we first hash the raw RGB vector into a short
838   binary encoding using random projection Charikar (2002); Andoni & Indyk (2008). The algorithm
839   calculates the dot product between the image vector and each hyperplane. If the dot product is
840   positive, it assigns a binary value of 1, otherwise it assigns 0. These binary values are sequentially
841   appended to form a complete binary hash key. The length of the hash key is determined by the
842
      number of hyperplanes used in the projection.
843   (line 10-14) Adding embeddings to the hash table. This function is used to insert processed images
844   and their corresponding embeddings into the hash table for future use.
845
      (line 15-32) Retrieving a similar embedding. This function takes an image we have not yet processed
846   and tries to find a suitable embedding candidate. We first obtain all embeddings with images similar
847   to the current image by hashing it into its binary encoding and accessing the corresponding bucket in
848   the hash table. We then loop through all images associated with the similar embeddings and find the
849   one yielding the highest similarity score (in our main experiments, the score is computed using cosine
850   similarity). If this score exceeds our threshold hyperparameter, we return the associated embedding;
851   otherwise, we return nothing.
852   Running the algorithm. We employ the above three functions to run SimHash on an arbitrary
853   panorama. For each extended navigable view (other views are omitted and explained in Algorithm 1),
854   we attempt to use a high-similarity embedding from the hash table. If it exists, we reuse this
855   embedding for the current view and continue to the next. If not, we need to process the view using the
856   ViT adapted for MuE, and then add the image and its embedding to the hash table. After processing
857   the entire panorama, we return the set of final embeddings to be used for agent navigation.
858   Storage overhead analysis. Here, we consider the storage overhead necessary to deploy our hashing
859   algorithm on VLN agents. Our LSH technique stores pairs of images and embeddings. In the
860   benchmarks we consider, these images are of size 3x224x224. The embedding size depends on the
861   model, which for HAMT and DUET is 197x768 (the number of ViT patches times the model’s hidden

999    E    P ER - MECHANISM ANALYSIS
1000
1001   In most experiments, we treat our proposed mechanisms as a single unit by applying all three
1002   simultaneously. While this is the most flexible and offers the best trade-off between performance
1003   and efficiency, analyzing each mechanism independently can provide valuable insights into its
1004
       effectiveness and robustness. Here, we present results on a per-mechanism basis.
1005   Effectiveness. In Sec 5.1, we apply our k-extension technique and then add adaptive thresholding
1006   early-exiting (denoted thresholds in Table 2) and locality-sensitive hashing (LSH) as we found those
1007   combinations of techniques offer the most computational savings. Here, we study all combinations of
1008   three efficiency mechanisms. To use early-exiting and LSH without k-extension, we treat every non-
1009   navigable view as one that can be early-exited or hashed. Navigable views are still fully processed.
1010
       We report results for the HAMT agent on the R2R benchmark in Table 10.
1011   The results show that between individual techniques, k-extension offers the best computational
1012   savings with a 49% reduction compared to the baseline agent. Early-exiting and LSH only reduce
1013   GFLOPs by „18% because early-exiting still requires processing every view, and LSH reuses only a
1014   minority of cached image embeddings. We find that LSH provides better performance than the other
1015   two individual mechanisms, with an SR only 0.05 lower than the baseline. This is likely because the
1016
       cached embeddings reused by LSH are near-identical, having a negligible impact on performance
       when interchanged. However, it is far less efficient than when combined with our other techniques.
1017
1018   The combination we do not present in Table 2, early-exiting and LSH (thresholds+LSH), provides
1019   slightly better performance than combinations using k-extension but at the cost of 39–66% more
1020   GFLOPs. Like the individual mechanisms, this suggests that retaining and partially processing/reusing
1021   the non-navigable views mitigates performance drop but is not nearly as efficient as k-extension.
1022
       Overall, we find that all combinations of our techniques fare well, offering different trade-offs between
       performance and efficiency.

       F    R ELATED WORK ON MODEL COMPRESSION
1060
1061
       Research has proposed an orthogonal approach to reduce the computational demands and memory
1062   footprint of deep-learning models: model compression. Quantization and pruning are the leading
1063   practice in model compression. Quantization (Jacob et al., 2018; Choi et al., 2018; Louizos et al.,
1064   2018; Bhalgat et al., 2020; Uhlich et al., 2019; Banner et al., 2019; Choukroun et al., 2019; Li et al.,
1065   2021; Nagel et al., 2020) transforms the memory representation of model parameters from 32-bit
1066   floating point numbers to a lower-bit integers (e.g., 4-bit integers), thereby making it more storage
1067   efficient and lowering memory usage. Pruning (Molchanov et al., 2016; Fan et al., 2019; Fang et al.,
1068   2023; Nova et al., 2023; Han et al., 2015b;a; Hoang & Liu, 2023) aims to create sparse models by
1069   removing parameters that are less important for maintaining performance, effectively reducing model
1070
       size and computation.
1071   While quantization and pruning have been demonstrated in simpler unimodal encoder settings for
1072   image and text, they are much more challenging in vision-language model(VLM) settings (Wang
1073   et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2024) and largely unexplored in VLN. (Wang et al., 2022) highlighted the
1074   challenges of pruning VLMs due to the unequal weighting of visual and linguistic modalities. They
1075   mitigated this by using a modal-adaptive approach, adjusting pruning ratios across different model
1076
       components based on downstream task sensitivity. Similarly, (Sun et al., 2024) demonstrated that
       naively applying post-training quantization to CLIP caused significant performance degradation,
1077
       which they addressed by introducing prompt tuning and alignment modules.
1078
1079   We expect similar challenges to be exhibited by VLN agents, if not exacerbated. VLN models, in
       addition to processing language and visual modalities, involve sequential decision-making dependent

1081   sources to require careful consideration while adapting model compression techniques. Future
1082   research on such techniques can be superposed along with our input-adaptive inference method to
1083   develop highly efficient models with an acceptable performance trade-off.
