009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Antibodies, crucial for immune defense, primarily rely on complementarity-
012            determining regions (CDRs) to bind and neutralize antigens, such as viruses. The
013            design of these CDRs determines the antibody’s affinity and specificity towards its
014            target. Generative models, particularly denoising diffusion probabilistic models
015            (DDPMs), have shown potential to advance the structure-based design of CDR
016            regions. However, only a limited dataset of bound antibody-antigen structures is
017            available, and generalization to out-of-distribution interfaces remains a challenge.
018
               Physics based force-fields, which approximate atomic interactions, offer a coarse
               but universal source of information to better mold designs to target interfaces.
019
               Integrating this foundational information into diffusion models is, therefore, highly
020
               desirable. Here, we propose a novel approach to enhance the sampling process
021            of diffusion models by integrating force field energy-based feedback. Our model,
022            D IFF F ORCE, employs forces to guide the diffusion sampling process, effectively
023            blending the two distributions. Through extensive experiments, we demonstrate
024            that our method guides the model to sample CDRs with lower energy, enhancing
025            both the structure and sequence of the generated antibodies.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Antibodies are key therapeutic proteins due to their ability to selectively bind to a variety of disease-
031   causing antigens, including viruses. Antibodies consist of two heavy and two light chains, forming a
032   Y-shaped structure. Critical to their ability to recognize diverse antigens are the six complementarity
033
      determining regions (CDRs) located at the tips of this structure. The diversity of antibodies is derived
      from the extensive combinatorial possibilities of these CDRs. A CDR of length L can theoretically
034
      have up to 20L different amino acid sequences, owing to the 20 types of amino acids that can be
035
      placed at each position. Therefore, a key step in developing therapeutic antibodies is designing
036   effective CDRs that specifically bind to target antigens (Kunik et al., 2012; Sela-Culang et al., 2013).
037
038   Traditional approaches to antibody design predominantly rely on animal immunization and computa-
039
      tional methods. Animal immunization is inherently limited to the production of naturally occurring
      antibodies and raises ethical concerns (Gray et al., 2020), despite its effectiveness in generating
040
      high-affinity antibodies. Traditional in silico methods, on the other hand, utilize complex biophysical
041
      energy functions (Warszawski et al., 2020; Adolf-Bryfogle et al., 2018) to predict how potential
042   antibodies might interact with their targets. However, they depend on expensive simulations, are
043   prone to convergence to local optima, and possess inherent limitations due to the complex nature of
044   interactions which cannot be efficiently represented by basic statistical functions (Graves et al., 2020).
045   This situation underscores the need for alternative approaches in antibody design.
046
      Recently, denoising diffusion probabilistic models (DDPMs) have emerged as a powerful technique
047
      for learning and sampling from complex, high-dimensional protein distributions (Watson et al.,
048   2023; Yim et al., 2023; Trippe et al., 2023). In particular, this advancement has shown potential
049   in the structure-based design of CDRs. Recent work (Luo et al., 2022; Martinkus et al., 2023) has
050   demonstrated the capabilities of diffusion models for modeling the CDRs of antibodies at the atomic
051   level, conditioned on the antigen and an antibody framework. However, the available dataset of bound
052   antibody-antigen structures is limited, and generalization to out-of-distribution interfaces remains a
053   challenge. While diffusion models provide accurate approximations within the known distribution,
      they struggle with out-of-distribution scenarios. This limitation poses a challenge for advancing CDR

071
      To address this challenge, we propose D IFF F ORCE, a force-guided DDPM sampling method inspired
      by traditional physics-based simulation techniques such as molecular dynamics (MD). Physics-based
072
      force fields, which approximate atomic interactions (as shown in Figure 1), provide a coarse but
073
      universal source of information to better align antibody designs with target interfaces. Integrating this
074   foundational data into diffusion models overcomes the limitations of distribution learning, as physics-
075   based models generalize well despite being poor approximators. By combining these approaches,
076   we enhance the ability to model out-of-distribution interfaces as we are guided by force field energy,
077   while the structural antibody-like details are left to be determined by the diffusion model. While
078   previous studies have used force field-based functions to refine antibody structures after diffusion
079   generation (Luo et al., 2022), or have trained separate networks to approximate the forces for guiding
080   an unconditional diffusion model (Wang et al., 2024), we are the first to construct a principled method
081   of force-guided DDPM sampling, effectively blending the two distributions. Given a protein complex
082
      consisting of an antigen and an antibody framework as input, we first initialize the CDR with arbitrary
      positions, sequence and orientations. Then, during the sampling stage, we iteratively update the atom
083
      positions guided by the gradients of force field energy, which are calculated for the denoised sample
084
      approximation. We highlight our main contributions as follows:
085
086          • We introduce the first force-guided diffusion model, which utilizes a differentiable force
087            field to guide the sampling process, effectively leveraging the weighted geometric mean
088            of the two distributions. Unlike existing methods, our model does not require to train a
089            separate network for energy approximation or condition the diffusion model on energy.
090          • We propose a method to approximate the denoised sample of antibody atom coordinates,
091            offering an elegant interpolative interpretation. This enables accurate energy computation,
092            ensuring the precise application of forces during diffusion sampling. We also present an
093            approach for approximating the denoised samples of amino acid types and orientations.
094
095   We evaluate our model on the CDR sequence-structure co-design task. We show that our proposed
096   method effectively guides the model to sample CDRs with lower energy, outperforming several
097   state-of-the-art models. We observe that our model generates more favorable structures earlier in the
098   sampling process, leading to an enhanced quality of produced antibody sequences.

101
102   Diffusion Models for Antibody Design Antibody design involves creating the sequence and
103   structure of antibodies that can bind to target antigens. This process differs from general protein
104   design, where sequences are derived from known structures (Dauparas et al., 2022; Ingraham
105   et al., 2019), or structures are predicted based on amino acid sequences (Jumper et al., 2021). In
106   antibody design, the sequences and structures of the CDRs are usually initially unknown. While
107   various generative models have been proposed to learn such data distribution, diffusion models
      (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021) have recently gained prominence for their


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      effectiveness in ensuring stable training and achieving good distribution coverage. Diffusion models
109   achieve state-of-the-art performance in antibody design by learning to generate new data through
110   denoising samples from a prior distribution. The DiGress model (Vignac et al., 2023) demonstrated
111   how to utilize a discrete diffusion process for molecules, while the work of DiffAb (Luo et al., 2022)
112   proposed the first diffusion model to perform joint design of sequence and structure of the antibody
113   CDR regions while conditioning on the antigen-antibody complex. AbDiffuser (Martinkus et al.,
114   2023) improved this further by incorporating strong priors and being more memory efficient with side
115   chain generation. However, these models still face challenges in accurately modeling the complex
116   interactions within antigen-antibody interfaces, particularly when dealing with out-of-distribution
117
      data.
118
119   Guided Generation Guiding generative models to produce specific outcomes is highly desirable for
120   a variety of applications (Ho et al., 2022; Nichol et al., 2023). To achieve this, two main methods have
121
      been proposed, a classifier guidance (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Song et al., 2021b) and a classifier-
      free guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2021). Recently, a concurrent work (Wang et al., 2024) introduced a
122
      force-guided diffusion model to produce protein conformations aligned with Boltzmann’s equilibrium
123
      distribution, based on the classifier guidance approach. However, this method requires training an
124   additional network to approximate the intermediate force vector to guide an unconditional model,
125   which can result in inaccurate estimates. In contrast, our method employs a differentiable force field
126   for guided sampling, eliminating the need for a separate energy approximation network and ensuring
127   more accurate energy calculations. Additionally, a loss guidance approach has been proposed (Song
128   et al., 2023), leveraging differentiable loss functions to guide the model without additional training
129   on noisy paired data. Similarly, our approach uses a differentiable force field to guide the sampling.

      3.1   D IFFUSION M ODEL
156
157   Our model builds upon the D IFF A B diffusion model (Luo et al., 2022). D IFF A B represents each
158   amino acid in an antibody by its type si ∈ {A . . . Y }, the coordinates of its Cα atom xi ∈ R3 , and
159   its orientation Oi ∈ SO(3). Assuming that the structures of the antigen, the antibody framework,
160   and five other CDRs are known, it designs one CDR loop at a time, denoted as R = {(sj , xj , Oj ) |
161   j = l + 1, . . . , l + m}, given the rest of the antibody-antigen complex C = {(si , xi , Oi ) | i ̸= j},
      which includes a set of five fixed CDRs.


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      The forward diffusion process from t = 0 to T , is Markovian and incrementally adds noise to three
163   different modalities using non-learnable distributions q: The Cα atom positions follow a Gaussian
164   distribution, q(xtj | x0j ); amino acid types follow a multinomial distribution, q(stj | s0j ); and the
165   orientations of amino acids follow an isotropic Gaussian distribution, q(Ojt | Oj0 ). The backward
166   diffusion process (from t = T to 0), refines each modality back towards the original data distribution.
167   The reverse process is guided by learnable models pθ , which approximate the posterior distributions
168   at each step using three distinct neural networks (further denoted as F, G, H, respectively) for the
169   three modalities. For more details on the D IFF A B model, see Section 3 of the original paper (Luo
170   et al., 2022), and for additional information on DDPMs, refer to Appendix A.

175
      Molecular dynamics (MD) simulations provide insights into the dynamic behavior of molecular
176   systems by numerically integrating Newton’s equations of motion (Chandler et al., 1987) for N
177   particles:
178                                         d2 xi             ∂
179                                     mi 2 = Fi = −            U (x1 , x2 , . . . , xN ),                 (1)
                                             dt              ∂xi
180
      where mi , xi , and Fi represent the mass, position, and force on each particle, respectively. The
181
      energy U (x1 , x2 , . . . , xN ) is a function of the coordinates of all N particles. By solving Newton’s
182   equation, MD simulations approximate the evolution of molecular systems over time.
183
184   An MD force field is a parametrised function used to evaluate the energy U (x1 , x2 , . . . , xN ) of a given
185
      configuration. For proteins, these forcefields are typically empirical, due to the large system sizes, and
      their functional forms and parameters are tuned to closely match experimental observations. Common
186
      terms include both bonded interactions, such as bond stretching, angle bending, and torsional angles,
187
      and non-bonded interactions, like van der Waals forces and electrostatic interactions.
188
189   In the context of antibody design, the force field takes a protein P (e.g., set of atom coordinates x)
190   and computes the energy U . By calculating the gradient ∇U , we can determine how U varies with
191
      changes in atomic positions. This gradient indicates how to adjust each atom’s position to minimize
      the total energy of the protein structure. Lower energy configurations often correspond to more
192
      thermodynamically stable antigen-antibody complexes, which are associated with higher affinity (Ji
193
      et al., 2023). Using the relationship between energy and force, −∇U (x) = F , we can simulate the
194   equations of motion to evolve this dynamical system according to the energy U .

321
322   Baselines We compare D IFF F ORCE against two baseline models, the diffusion model D IFF A B
323   (Luo et al., 2022) and the traditional energy-based method RA B D (Adolf-Bryfogle et al., 2018). Both
      baseline models are evaluated using default settings. For more details, see Appendix F.

      Dataset and Diffusion Model To evaluate our model, we use the SAbDab database (Dunbar et al.,
325
      2013) (with Chothia numbering scheme), filtering out complexes with resolutions worse than 4Å and
326   those targeting non-protein antigens. Following (Luo et al., 2022), we cluster antibodies based on
327   50% H3 sequence identity and select five clusters for the test set, comprising 19 complexes. We use
328   the codesign_single pre-trained model from D IFF A B, which generates one CDR region at a time.
329
330   Energy Energy of protein structures is evaluated using MadraX (Orlando et al., 2023), which
331   provides the Gibbs free energy (∆G) of the complex. Unlike other force fields, such as Rosetta
332   (Alford et al., 2017) or FoldX (Schymkowitz et al., 2005), Madrax is fully differentiable. MadraX
333   evaluates several categories of interaction energies, adapting 7 categories from FoldX (Schymkowitz
334   et al., 2005) into a differentiable format. The energy considers the full protein structure, whose
335   reconstruction is described in Appendix D. The energy is reported in kcal/mol.
336
337
      Metrics To validate our model’s performance, we use three key metrics; 1) Binding Energy Improve-
      ment (IMP) is calculated as the percentage of designed CDRs that show a reduction (improvement)
338
      in free binding energy (∆∆G) compared to the reference CDRs, indicating a stronger interaction
339
      with the target antigen. This evaluation uses the InterfaceAnalyzer from Rosetta (Alford et al., 2017).
340   2) Root Mean Square Deviation (RMSD) measures the average spatial discrepancy between the Cα
341   atoms of the generated and reference antibody structures, with a higher RMSD indicating greater
342   structural diversity. 3) Amino Acid Recovery Rate (AAR) is defined as the overlapping ratio of the
343   generated sequence to the ground truth, evaluating how accurately the generated CDR sequences
344   replicate the reference sequences (Adolf-Bryfogle et al., 2018).

346   4.2    R ESULTS
347
348   We evaluate the performance of D IFF F ORCE model on the sequence-structure co-design as introduced
349
      by (Luo et al., 2022), where the reference CDR is removed from the antibody-antigen complex.
      The diffusion model is therefore conditioned on antibody framework and antigen. For each antigen-
350
      antibody complex, we generate n = 25 samples for 3 heavy chain CDRs (HCDRs). We choose to
351
      focus on the heavy chain since it typically exhibits greater variability and influences on binding affinity
352   compared to the light chain (López-Requena et al., 2007). The samples are produced through 100
353   generative timesteps (T = 100), with each sample maintaining the same length as its corresponding
354   reference CDR in the test set. Finally, the generated structures, as well as reference original ones, are
355   relaxed using OpenMM (Eastman et al., 2017) and Rosetta (Alford et al., 2017).
356
      Table 1 shows that D IFF F ORCE model recovers all three HCDRs sequences with greater accuracy
357
      (higher AAR) than both D IFF A B and RA B D. Furthermore, D IFF F ORCE achieves improved binding
358   scores (higher IMP) for the H1 and H3 regions. The model exhibits RMSDs comparable to those
359   of D IFF A B. Overall, the most substantial improvement is observed in the H3 region, which can
360   be attributed to its significantly longer sequence and the smaller variability present in the H1 and

      5   C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
468
469
      Antibodies play a vital role in the immune system by identifying and neutralizing antigens, such as
470
      viruses. Inspired by the fact that integrating physics-based force fields with generative models can
471   improve out-of-distribution generalization for antibody design, we introduce D IFF F ORCE, a diffusion
472   model that incorporates force guidance into the sampling. Unlike existing methods, our model does
473   not require conditioning a diffusion model on energy or training a separate network to approximate
474   energy. We demonstrate that our model effectively guides the diffusion sampler to generate CDRs
475   of better energy, outperforming several state-of-the-art models. This results in improved structure
476   earlier in the sampling and enhances the sequences of the generated antibody CDRs.
477   While D IFF F ORCE demonstrates promising results, it focuses on CDR sequence-structure co-design,
478   with future potential in designing antibodies without bound framework structures. Moreover, the
479   generated samples will require wet-lab experiments to confirm efficacy. Despite these and other
480   limitations discussed in Appendix H, our work represents the first attempt to directly integrate a
481   differentiable force field within diffusion sampling, effectively blending two distributions together.

      Denoising diffusion probabilistic models (DDPMs), introduced by Ho et al. (2020), represent a
705   class of generative models that generate data by reversing a diffusion process. This process involves
706   gradually transforming a sample from a simple distribution, like Gaussian noise, into a complex
707   data distribution through learned reverse diffusion steps. The forward process incrementally adds
708   noise to the data over a series of steps, transforming an initial data distribution into a distribution that
709   is approximately Gaussian. This process is designed as a Markov chain, where each state xt only
710   depends on the immediate previous state xt−1 . The transition from xt−1 to xt is defined as:
711
712                                       √               √

713
714   In this equation, αt (where 0 < αt ≤ 1) is a predefined variance schedule decreasing over time,
715   which determines the proportion of the original data and noise at each step. The variable ϵ represents
716   isotropic Gaussian noise, introducing randomness into the process. The reverse process aims to
717   reconstruct the original data by sequentially removing the noise added during the forward process.
718   This is achieved by training a neural network to estimate the original data distribution at each previous
719
      timestep, effectively learning the reverse of the forward process. The transition from noisy data xt
      back to less noisy data xt−1 is modeled as:

725

726

727

789

      To calculate the energy, it is essential to reconstruct the full antibody-antigen complex C along with
813   the generated CDR region R. This process involves first reconstructing the complete 3D structure
814   of the atoms in the CDR, following the pipeline outlined in (Luo et al., 2022). The reconstruction
815   begins by determining the coordinates of the N, C, O, and side-chain Cβ atoms, which are positioned
816   relative to the Cα location and orientation of each amino acid (Engh & Huber, 2012). After these
817   core atoms are reconstructed, the remaining side-chain atoms are built using the side-chain packing
818   function in Rosetta (Alford et al., 2017). Once the CDR region is restored, the full antibody-antigen
819   complex C is reconstructed. With the complete structure (including the antibody with its 6 CDRs and
820   framework, as well as the antigen), the energy of the complex can be calculated. This process is then
821
      iteratively performed for λst × 100 timesteps, assuming diffusion occurs over t = 100 timesteps.
      The iteration begins when forces are first applied at λst and continues through the sampling process
822
      until the final timestep t = 0.

952   H    L IMITATIONS
953
954   Computational Cost The iterative use of the MadraX library (Orlando et al., 2023) for force
955   guidance during sampling is time-consuming due to the calculations involved. This process mimics
956   molecular dynamics (MD) simulations to continuously update atomic positions based on various
957   forces, including bond forces, electrostatic interactions, van der Waals forces, and solvent interactions.
958
      Each iteration estimates atomic movements, similar to gradient descent optimization steps. Thus, the
      computational demands should be considered when employing this approach.
959
960
      Reliability of Energy Function In our study, we utilized the Rosetta energy function (Alford et al.,
961
      2017) to evaluate the binding effectiveness of designed antibodies to their target antigens, a common
962
      metric in antibody design. Despite the integral role of Rosetta, along with tools such as FoldX
963   (Schymkowitz et al., 2005), in simulating protein interactions, their reliability remains a subject of
964   concern. These computational tools have been documented to exhibit inaccuracies when replicating
965   experimental results, often due to the oversimplified models of complex molecular interactions they
966   utilize (Ramírez & Caballero, 2016; 2018). This underscores the necessity for ongoing refinement of
967   these computational methods.
968
969   Evaluation Metrics In the field of antibody design, Amino Acid Recovery (AAR) and Root Mean
970   Square Deviation (RMSD) are commonly used as evaluation metrics. However, these metrics have
971   inherent limitations that may compromise the accurate assessment of an antibody’s functional efficacy.
      AAR may not always reliably reflect the functional performance of the generated antibody sequences,


                                                         18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       while RMSD primarily assesses the alignment of backbone atoms and overlooks the side chains,
973    which are crucial for the specificity and strength of antigen-antibody interactions. These limitations
974    underscore the need for the development of more comprehensive evaluation metrics.

978
979
       Integrating force guidance within the diffusion sampling process for antibody design can significantly
       accelerate therapeutic antibody discovery, with broad implications in fields like protein engineering.
980
       This advancement enables more precise modeling of atomic systems, enhancing predictions of protein
981
       stability, function, and interactions, crucial for designing enzymes and other biologically relevant
982    molecules. However, potential societal drawbacks exist, particularly in dual-use applications. While
983    aimed at therapeutic advancements, this approach could be misused to design harmful biological
984    agents, raising ethical concerns and underscoring the need for regulations to ensure responsible use
985    for societal benefit.
