011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013
               Open-world planning is crucial for embodied AI agents that must make decisions
               with incomplete task-relevant knowledge. In fact, the main challenges lie in rea-
014
               soning about objects and their affordances that are unknown to the agent. Large
015
               Language Models (LLMs), pre-trained on vast internet-scale data, have emerged
016            as potential solutions for open-world planning. However, LLMs have limitations
017            in long-horizon planning tasks and face problems related to interpretability, reli-
018            ability, and cost-efficiency. Symbolic planning methods, on the other hand, offer
019            structured and verifiable approaches to long-horizon tasks, but often struggle to
020            generate feasible plans in an open-world setting. In this work, we propose a novel
021            approach, called LLM-Regress, which combines the strengths of lifted symbolic
022            regression planning with LLM-based affordances. The lifted representation al-
023            lows us to generate plans capable of handling arbitrary unknown objects, while
024
               regression planning is the only planning paradigm that guarantees complete solu-
               tions using lifted representations. For such tasks, we leverage LLMs to supple-
025
               ment missing affordances knowledge for unknown objects. The regression nature
026
               of our approach enables the agent to focus on actions and objects relevant to the
027            goal, thus avoiding the need for costly LLM calls for every decision. We evaluate
028            our approach on the ALFWorld dataset and introduce a new ALFWorld-Afford
029            dataset with higher planning complexity and more affordances types. The empiri-
030            cal results demonstrate that our method outperforms existing approaches in terms
031            of success rates, planning duration, and number of LLM Tokens. Finally, we
032            show that our approach is resilient to domain shifts in affordances and generalizes
033            effectively to unseen tasks. This work underscores the importance of integrat-
034            ing symbolic reasoning with LLM knowledge for open-world decision-making in
035
               embodied AI.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
039
      One of the biggest hurdles facing embodied agents is how to plan effectively in open-world en-
040
      vironments with incomplete knowledge. From an object-centric perspective, making decisions in
041
      open-world environments requires reasoning about unobserved task-relevant objects and relation-
042   ships. For instance, when tasked with “cleaning the room”, an agent must infer the possible pres-
043   ence of certain objects (e.g., are there dirty plates to be cleaned?), their relationships with other
044   objects (e.g., is the plate on the dining table or the kitchen counter?), and determine appropriate
045   action affordances (e.g., should the plate be cleaned using a sink rather than a broom?). Given that
046   a common household may contain thousands of items, it is impractical to predefine all relational in-
047   formation and action affordances for each object. Therefore, the ability to plan and make decisions
048   with incomplete domain information is essential to developing a practical embodied agent that can
049   be deployed in real-world settings.

051   capabilities Yao et al. (2024); Ouyang et al. (2022). Many have proposed to leverage the LLMs’
052   reasoning capabilities for planning for AI agents (Yao et al., 2022; Shinn et al., 2024). LLMs do
053   not require structured inputs or explicit knowledge modeling, which technically makes them well-
      suited for open-world planning. However, growing evidence cast doubts on LLMs’ capabilities for

      2       M ETHODOLOGY
152
153
      Lifted regression enables plan generation for a given task involving unknown variables. The key
154
      idea is to use variables to represent unknown objects (lifting), allowing the reasoning process to
155
      proceed from a goal backward (regression). In contrast, classical planning approaches often use
156   forward progression by enumerating all actions involving observed objects and searching forward
157   until a goal is reached. The difference between lifted regression and grounded forward search is
158   illustrated in Fig. 1. The benefit of lifted regression is that it does not require all relevant objects to
159   be observed in order to generate a plan. This contrasts with grounded forward search, where no valid
160   plan can be deduced if no actions can directly lead to the goal. In this section, we will first formally
161   introduce lifted regression. Subsequently, we will present our own implementation for embodied
      agents and the integration of LLM-based affordances reasoning.

      2.1   F IRST O RDER P LANNING F ORMALISM
163
164   We use the STRIPS style of definition to define our lifted regression planning problem Fikes &
165   Nilsson (1971). We define a tuple Π =< L, A, G, I > where L is the function-free first-order
166   language, A is the set of action schema, G is the set of goal atoms, and I is the set of initial state
167   atoms. The first-order logic language L contains a set of variables V, a set of constants C, and a set
168   of predicate symbols P. An atom is denoted as p(t) where p ∈ P is a predicate symbol a vector
169   of terms t. The set of variables in t is denoted as vars(p). An atom p(t) is a grounded atom if the
170
      predicate does not contain any variable, vars(p) = ∅. The name of the terms is denoted name(t).
      A sub-goal s = {p1 , p2 , ..., pi } is a set of atoms that needs to be satisfied. Positive literals are a
171
      subset of s including all positive literals, s+ = {p ∈ s|p is a positive literal}. Similarly, negative
172
      literals s− contains all the negative literals in s, i.e., s− = {p ∈ s|p is a negative literal}. A literal
173   in s must belong in either s+ or s− , s = s+ ∪ s− . A = {A1 , ..., Am } is a finite set of action
174   schema. Each action schema contains three sets of atoms A = {pre(A), add(A), del(A)}. pre(A)
175   is the precondition set of atoms that must be true for the action schema A to be applicable. An
176   action is grounded when an action schema does not have variables in the three sets, vars(A) = ∅.
177   In this paper, we assume that the set of action schema A is given and accurate. The goal G =
178   {p1 , p2 , ..., pk } is given in the form of atoms for the embodied agent to satisfy.

180   2.2   L IFTED R EGRESSION A LGORITHM
181
182   In this section, we outline the lifted regression algorithm. The algorithm is adapted from Ghallab
183
      et al. (2004) with adjustment for open-world embodied planning. First, we define the necessary
      operators for lifted regression as follows:
184
185
             • Substitution: A SUBSTITUTION function θ(p) substitutes all the variables in an atom
186            with constants or other variables. It is formally defined as θ(p) : V → T such that θ(p) =
187            {vi 7→ ti |vi ∈ vars(p), 1 ≤ i ≤ k}.
188
             • Unification: UNIFY(p, q) operator checks if two sets of predicates with different variables
189
               are equivalent Russell & Norvig (2002). It return a substitution θ if it two predicates can
190
               be unified, UNIFY(p, q) = θ where θ(p) = θ(q). The substitution function θ is a most
191            general unifier (MGU) whose existence indicates two predicates are equivalent. Russell &
192            Norvig (2002) provides a detailed explanation of unification.
193
             • Standardization: We use STANDARDIZE(p) operator to replace all variables in p with
194
               variables v ′ ∈
                             / V such that ∀v ∈ vars(p), v ∈
                                                           / vars(STANDARDIZE(p)). Standardization
195            is introduced to avoid confusion between variable names for the same action schema in a
196            plan.
197
             • Relevency: RELEVANT(A, s) = (s ∩ (add(A) ∪ del(A)) ̸= ∅) ∧ (s+ ∩ del(A) = ∅) ∧
198
               (s− ∩ add(A) = ∅) determines if taking an action A leads to the state s. A is relevant if
199            the action’s effects set adds something to a sub-goal without contradiction.
200
             • Regression: The regression function is defined as γ −1 (s, A) = (s − add(A)) ∪ pre(A), it
201
               returns the previous state of s before taking the action A. We can iterate from the goal state
202
               n times to get sn = γ −1 (γ −1 (θn (G), θn (An )), θn−1 (An−1 ))...), where sn ∩ I ̸= ∅ unless
203            I = ∅.
204
205   The lifted regression algorithm takes in the first order language L, finite action set A, and the prob-
206   lem’s goal G. We assume that the planning domain is acyclic and the agent initially does not observe
207   anything thus I = ∅. The objective is to find a set S = {(G1 , π1 )....(Gn , πn )} which are ordered
208   pairs of sub-goals and their corresponding plans. We also assume that no actions have been taken at
209   the beginning of each task. For example, all the foods are not heated and all the TVs are thought to
210   be turned off. These assumptions are made to align with the implementation of ALFWorld and most
211   other embodied agent benchmarks. The lifted regression algorithm conducts an exhaustive back-
212
      ward search with each action schema A until it finds all sub-goals and plan pairs when subgoals can
      be no longer regressed. We keep track of visited sub-goals that can be unified to prune redundant
213
      branches. The regression algorithm is shown in Algorithm 1.

222   2.3   LLM S AS A FFORDANCES G ROUNDING F UNCTION
223
224   For a given lifted planning problem, we can obtain a regressed sub-goal Gn = {p1 , p2 ...} which is a
225   set of lifted predicates. When the embodied agent observes new objects, we need to check whether
226   there are objects and a grounding that can satisfy Gn . We assume the agent can ground either via
227
      observations or use LLM for grounding. Thus, we define the grounding function as a composition
      of two functions: θ = θo ◦ θa , where θo is based on the agent’s observation and θa is the grounding
228
      function from other knowledge (objects’ affordances in our case). In this paper, we assume the agent
229
      has complete knowledge of θo to ground any observations O = {o1 , o2 , ..., ok }, and the affordances
230   of the objects are unknown to the agent. Rather than have predefined affordances, we want to extract
231   them from LLM. This allows to define θa = LLM(PROMPTA , θo (Gn ), O). The function takes a
232   predefined PROMPT, a partially grounded sub-goal θo (Gn ), and a list of observed objects O as
233   inputs. Together they form the complete grounding needed for a regression plan.
234
      To continue our example based on Fig. 1, where we regressed action heat(x) with G =
235
      {holding(potato 1), isHot(potato 1)} to obtain subgoal G′ = {canHeat(r, x), holding(potato 1)}.

238   top 1}, we can query LLM ground predicate canHeat(r, potato 1). Assuming, that LLM returns the

      2.4   L IFTED R EGRESSION WITH LLM A FFORDANCES
265
266   Our proposed framework utilizes lifted regression to generate a set of plans and sub-goals. As shown
267   in Fig. 2, we use a knowledge base (KB) to store facts that we observe or assume to be true. In order
268   to distinguish predicates that are observable and the ones that need affordances reasoning, we de-
269   fine two sets of predicates: observation predicates Po = {po1 , po2 , . . . } and affordances predicates
      Pa = {pa1 , pa2 , . . . }. We assume that Po can be grounded via observation alone. For example,

      hold(eggs 1) is a case we assume the agent knows via feedback. Pa cannot be grounded by obser-
271   vation alone, but it is still required to check an action is feasible for some sub-goal. As previously
272   mentioned, we rely on LLM for affordances grounding using θa as outlined in the previous section.
273   If no subgoal can be satisfied, the agent randomly explores the environment. The separation between
274   observation and affordances predicates enables us to track exactly which affordances reasoning is

293
294   We assume a static environment with deterministic actions. In benchmarks like ALFWorld, no
295   actions are assumed to have occurred before the agent’s execution. As a result, we assume none of
296   the heating, cleaning, and cooling has been done on the goal object. The agent is provided with a set

      of action schemas, as is common in most embodied AI and robotics settings, which are detailed in

      Since ALFWorld goals are based on fixed templates, and existing research shows that LLMs can
299
      accurately convert these fixed template goals to PDDL goals Song et al. (2023). Therefore, we

305

310   3.2.1    ALFW ORLD AND ALFW ORLD -A FFORD
311
      ALFWorld. ALFWorld is a text-based virtual household environment with six distinct task
312   types: heating, cleaning, cooling, pick and place, picking two objects
313   and placing them, and examining an object under light. The environment is
314   partially observable where the agent need to explore to discover new items. We do not provide the
315   agent with a predefined set of objects available in the scene; instead, objects are discovered during
316   task execution. Initially, the agent is given access to potential locations within each room where
317   new objects can be found. The main affordances reasoning in this environment include determining
318   whether an object can be heated, cooled, cleaned, or turned on. Actions are deterministic, and the
319   agent receives feedback in the form of “nothing happens” when actions have no effect. The agent
320
      has a budget of 50 actions to complete the task, and the problem is considered as a failure case if the
      task cannot be complete within the step limit.
321

      4      R ESULTS AND D ISCUSSION
398
399   4.1     C OMPARISON OF P LANNING A PPROACHES FOR ALFW ORLD (RQ1)
400
401   As shown in Table 1, our lifted regression planning approach (LLM-Regress) outperforms other
402   baseline methods on both datasets in terms of success rate, token usage, and duration. On the
403   ALFWorld dataset, LLM-Regress achieves a success rate of 95%, significantly outperforming other
404
      baselines. The results we obtained for ReAct are also higher (70%) than the reported results in
      the original manuscript, likely due to the use of GPT-4o. Regarding token usage, unlike other
405
      LLM baselines that require prompting at each step of action in addition to the potentially large base
406
      prompt, our method only requires prompting for affordances information, resulting in significantly
407   fewer tokens used. This can lead to savings of millions of tokens, making LLM usage less costly
408   as the number of calls adds up. This is further reflected in the planning duration for LLM-based
409   methods, where our approach shows improved efficiency.
410
      Another observation is that ReAct-based prompting methods heavily rely on examples. When only
411
      provided with the action model description, ReAct’s performance drops drastically, indicating its
412
      inability to reason effectively without examples. Grounded planning methods are also prone to
413   LLM hallucinations, which can lead to syntax errors and infeasible affordances. This is expected
414   since fully specified LLM problem files contain around 4, 000 words, which increases the likelihood
415   of such issues. These results indicate that our lifted regression planning approach is more effective
416   and efficient than both LLM-based planners and grounded forward search methods using LLM-
417   generated affordances. By generating affordances on demand and focusing on relevant actions,
418   LLM-Regress reduces the computational overhead associated with exhaustive action enumeration
419   and extensive LLM queries.
420
421
      4.2     C OMPARISON OF P LANNING A PPROACHES FOR ALFW ORLD -A FFORD (RQ2)
422
423
      On the more complex ALFWorld-Afford dataset, which requires reasoning about a more diverse set
424
      of object affordances, LLM-Regress maintains a high success rate of 84%, while the performance
425
      of baseline methods decreases significantly. ReAct with Examples drops to a 57% success rate, and
426   the Grounded Planner achieves only 29%. We also observed that while LLM-generated affordances
427   might not always function correctly within the simulator, the commonsense reasoning behind them
428   is valid. For example, the LLM might suggest using a “Counter Top” to cool a potato, which
429   is plausible in the real world but not supported by the simulator’s environment. These insights
430   are easily obtainable with our method because we can trace LLM-generated affordances within
431   the knowledge base, allowing for human evaluation and verification. This is in contrast to LLM
      prompting methods, which become difficult to interpret and manage as problems scale in complexity.

450   4.3   S TRUCTURED K NOWLEDGE BASE FOR K NOWLEDGE R EUSE (RQ3)
451
452   Our approach leverages a structured knowledge base that allows for the easy transfer and reuse of
453   knowledge (e.g., grounded predicates) in new tasks. This transferability enables the agent to ap-
454
      ply previously acquired information across different contexts, including multiple trials of the same
      task, similar to the Reflextion setup Shinn et al. (2024). We evaluated knowledge transfer in both
455
      scenarios, and our agent showed significantly better performance as more affordances knowledge
456
      was transferred, achieving a 99% success rate on the ALFWorld dataset and 91% on the ALFWorld-
457   Afford dataset after 3 trials of the same task. Although other LLM-based methods also demonstrated
458   improved performance, it is difficult to determine the specific types of knowledge being reused, such
459   as affordances information versus room layout details. In contrast, our structured approach provides
460   clear tracking of knowledge reuse. We also evaluated the maintenance of a universal knowledge
461   base for affordances while the agent solved the entire ALFWorld and ALFWorld-Afford datasets.
462   This approach involved providing both positive and negative examples to guide LLMs in generating
463   affordances candidates. We do see performance improvements (+2% on ALFWorld and +9% on
464   ALFWorld-Afford) on both datasets when compared to treating each tasks with no knowledge trans-
465
      fer. Our results indicate the structured knowledge base do allow knowledge transfer in an efficient
      and tractable manner. Since we transfer affordances grounded in objects and types, we can easily
466
      determine whether new objects can use the same kind of affordances reasoning from the previous
467
      tasks.

      4.4   A BLATION
470
471   In this section, we present an ablation study to evaluate the impact of affordance knowledge and the
472   choice of LLM on the agent’s performance. The Perfect-Affordances method provides the agent with
473   ground truth affordances information, demonstrating the upper bound of agent performance. The
474   results also show that affordances generated by GPT-4o are better than those generated by GPT-3.5-
475   Turbo, indicating a better language model does improve affordances reasoning capabilities, although
476   the impact is still less significant compared to purely LLM-based methods that require the model
477   to perform long-horizon planning. In the No-Affordances scenario, all actions are made applicable
478
      to all objects, leading to a drastic drop in performance. This highlights the critical importance of
      affordance reasoning, as without this knowledge, the agent struggles to generate any reasonable
479
      plans and select appropriate actions to achieve the goal.

      While classical planning guarantees completeness and consistency in its solutions, it requires de-
501   tailed domain descriptions, which may be unrealistic in real-world settings. Classical planning tools
502   like PDDL (McDermott, 2000) have spurred the creation of a wide range of planning algorithms,
503   including the Fast-Forward planner (Hoffmann & Nebel, 2001) and the BFS(f) planner (Lipovetzky
504   et al., 2014). As a result, many recent works have attempted to combine classical planning by gener-
505   ating domain-specific models Arora & Kambhampati (2023); Guan et al. (2023); Xie et al. (2023);
506   Hazra et al. (2024). These works primarily focus on generating planning domains using LLMs that
507   can be solved by planners in the close-world setting. Our work also different from existing work that
508   focus on learning abstract model from past experiences Konidaris et al. (2018); James et al. (2022);
509
      Ugur & Piater (2015); Ahmetoglu et al. (2022); Asai & Fukunaga (2018); Chitnis et al. (2022);
      Silver et al. (2023); Shah et al. (2024), as we assume the actions (skills) are predefined, but the not
510
      actual objects and their relationships.
511
512   There are also recent works focusing on open-world reasoning Ding et al. (2023); Singh et al.
513   (2023a). These methods use LLMs to generate closed-world solutions that ”could be true”, based
514   on observation. However, these approaches rely on examples to generate possible closed-world
515
      problems to guide search, without guarantees of correctness and completeness. Other work also use
      retrival based methods to find previous epxeriences similar to the task at handWang et al. (2023b;
516
      2024). The reliance on examples also makes these methods difficult to generalize to new prob-
517
      lems. To our knowledge, we are the first to focus on a formal open-world solution that guarantees
518   completeness and makes LLM-generated knowledge traceable and verifiable.

      6   C ONCLUSION
522
523
      In this work, we proposed LLM-Regress a novel approach that combines the strengths of lifted sym-
      bolic regression planning with LLM-based affordances. The lifted representation lets us generate
524
      plans capable of handling arbitrary unknown objects, while regression planning is the only plan-
525
      ning paradigm that guarantees complete solutions using lifted representations. We also benefited
526   from LLMs to reason affordances of unknown objects. We also introduced the ALFWorld-Afford
527   benchmark which contains more complex goals and diverse object affordances compared to ALF-
528   World. Our experimental evaluation showed that LLM-Regress comparably outperformed known
529   state-of-the-art solutions against the ALFWorld and ALFWorld-Afford benchmarks.
530
      As for future work, we will enhance our solution for multi-modal settings where the agent can rely on
531
      VLMs or LMMs to segment any object and reason about their affordances, unlike ALWorld where
532   the text is the only modality and objects are given beforehand. Furthemore, we want to include
533   LLM commonsense knowledge to guide exploration in a more complex environment. We also want
534   to leverage LLMs to generate new actions for unseen objects on-demand, reducing the burden of
535   domain designers.
