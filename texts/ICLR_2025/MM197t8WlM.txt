                                                 A BSTRACT
008
009
               Flow Matching (FM) is a simulation-free method for learning a continuous and
010
               invertible flow to interpolate between two distributions, and in particular to gen-
011            erate data from noise in generative modeling. In this paper, we introduce Local
012            Flow Matching (LFM), which consecutively learns a sequence of FM sub-models
013            and each matches a diffusion process up to the time of the step size in the data-
014            to-noise direction. In each step, the two distributions to be interpolated by the
015            sub-model are closer to each other than data vs. noise, and this enables the use of
016            smaller models with faster training. The stepwise structure of LFM is natural to be
017            distilled and different distillation techniques can be adopted to speed up genera-
018            tion. Theoretically, we prove a generation guarantee of the proposed flow model
019
               in terms of the χ2 -divergence between the generated and true data distributions.
               In experiments, we demonstrate the improved training efficiency and competitive
020
               generative performance of LFM compared to FM on the unconditional genera-
021
               tion of tabular data and image datasets, and also on the conditional generation of
022            robotic manipulation policies.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Generative modeling has revolutionized the field of machine learning, enabling the creation of real-
028   istic synthetic data across various domains. Recently, diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al.,
029   2021) started to take over earlier models like Generative Adversarial Networks (GAN) (Goodfellow
030   et al., 2014), Variational Autoencoders (VAE) (Kingma & Welling, 2014) and Normalizing Flows
031   (Kobyzev et al., 2020), offering benefits in terms of stability, diversity, and scalability. Diffusion
032   models have found diverse applications in audio synthesis (Kong et al., 2021), text-to-image syn-
033
      thesis (Rombach et al., 2022), imitation learning for robotics (Chi et al., 2023), among others. A
      notable advantage of score-based diffusion models is their simulation-free training, meaning that the
034
      training objective is a “score matching” loss taking the form of an L2 loss averaged over data sam-
035
      ples. Under a Stochastic Differential Equation (SDE) formulation (Song et al., 2021), this allows
036   training of a continuous-time score function parametrized by a neural network from a large amount
037   of data in high dimensions.
038
039
      Compared to the SDE generation in diffusion models, the Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE)
      generation of a trained diffusion or flow model is deterministic and typically uses fewer time steps.
040
      Using an ODE formulation, Flow Matching (FM) models (Lipman et al., 2023; Albergo & Vanden-
041
      Eijnden, 2023; Liu, 2022) proposed a simulation-free training of flow models based on regressing
042   vector fields using an L2 “matching” loss and have shown state-of-the-art generation performance
043   on various tasks, including text-to-image generation (Esser et al., 2024), humanoid robot control
044   (Rouxel et al., 2024), audio generation (Guan et al., 2024), and application to discrete data in pro-
045   gramming code generation (Gat et al., 2024). The simulation-free training of flow models signif-
046   icantly alleviates the computational issue of earlier Continuous Normalizing Flow (CNF) models
047   using likelihood-based training Grathwohl et al. (2018). Going beyond the fast generation of ODE
048   flow models, recent works on model distillation (Salimans & Ho, 2022; Liu et al., 2023; Song et al.,
049   2023) have shown that the generation of large (diffusion and flow) generative models can be signif-
050
      icantly accelerated and in some cases computed in an extremely small number of steps.
051   In this work, we propose a simulation-free training technique of CNFs called “Local Flow Match-
052   ing” (LFM), which can be seen as a stepwise version of FM, and we call previous FMs the global
053   ones. The proposed LFM trains a sequence of small (sub-)flow models, which, after concatenation,
      transport invertibly from data to noise and back. In each sub-flow, any FM model can be plugged


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      in. In other words, the global FM tries to interpolate directly between noise and data distributions
055   which may differ a lot, while our local FM breaks down the job into smaller steps and interpolates
056   between a pair of distributions which are closer to each other (namely “local”) in each step, see Fig-
057   ure 1. Because the source and target distributions are not too far away, we expect to train a local FM
058   model with potentially smaller model and faster convergence in training. This would allow reduced
059   memory load and computational cost without sacrificing model quality. In addition to the benefits
060   when training from scratch, our framework is compatible with various distillation techniques, and
061   we empirically found that our model can have advantage over global FMs after distillation.
062   Specifically, in each step of LFM, we train a sub-flow model to interpolate between (pn−1 , p∗n ),
063   where p∗n evolves from pn−1 along the diffusion process for time up to the step size. The forward
064   process (data-to-noise) starts from p0 being the data distribution and ends at some pN which is close
065   to normal distribution. The reverse process (noise-to-data) uses the invertibility of each sub-flow
066   model to generate data from noise by ODE integration. The construction of using (the marginal
067   distribution of) a diffusion process as a “target” in each step allows us to theoretically prove the
068   generation guarantee of LFM by connecting to the diffusion theory.
069   The stepwise approach in our work is inspired by similar constructions in the literature, including
070   block-wise training of ResNet under the GAN framework Johnson & Zhang (2019) and flow-based
071   generative models implementing a discrete-time gradient descent in Wasserstein space following
072   the Jordan-Kinderleherer-Otto (JKO) scheme (Alvarez-Melis et al., 2022; Mokrov et al., 2021; Xu
073   et al., 2023b; Vidal et al., 2023). Unlike previous models, the proposed LFM is simulation-free,
074   which allows application to high-dimensional data like large images and robotics data as shown in
075
      our experiments.
076   In summary, the contributions of the work are:
077
078          • To generate data from the normal distribution, we propose to train a consecutive series of
079            FM sub-models which approximately follow the diffusion process in the data-to-noise di-
080            rection. Such breakdown of the global flow into small pieces makes the pair of distributions
081
               closer to each other in each step, and then the FM sub-model can be obtained with reduced
               model size and faster training convergence. Concatenation of all the sub-models provides
082
               an invertible flow that can go from noise to data in the reverse direction.
083
084          • Theoretically, we prove the generation guarantee, namely how the generated distribution
085            by LFM approximates the data distribution P , under χ2 divergence which implies Kull-
086            back–Leibler (KL) and Total Variation (TV) guarantees. We prove an O(ε1/2 )-χ2 guaran-
087
               tee, where ε is the L2 error of FM training objective, and the other technical assumptions
               are motivated by our stepwise FM to the OU process. Our theory applies when data density
088
               is regular and also covers cases when P merely has finite second moments (where we use
089
               a short-time initial diffusion to smooth P ), e.g., when P is compactly supported.
090
091
             • Our framework allows readily plugging in different designs of the FM in each local sub-
               flow model. In addition, the stepwise structure of LFM renders it natural to be distilled, and
092
               our approach is compatible with different distillation techniques. Empirically, the proposed
093
               LFM shows improved training efficiency and competitive generative performance against
094            existing FM methods on likelihood estimation, image generation, and robotic manipulation
095            tasks. The model gives strong performance when trained from scratch and in the distilled
096            setting.

104   1.1   R ELATED WORKS
105
106   Continuous normalizing flow (CNF). CNF uses a neural ODE model (Chen et al., 2018) opti-
107   mized by maximizing the model likelihood, which the ODE parametrization can compute, on ob-
      served data samples (Grathwohl et al., 2018). To facilitate training and inference, subsequent works


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      have proposed advanced techniques such as trajectory regularization (Finlay et al., 2020; Onken
109   et al., 2021) and block-wise training (Fan et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2023b). These techniques help
110   stabilize the training process and improve the model’s performance. Despite successful applications
111   in time-series analyses (de Bézenac et al., 2020), uncertainty estimation (Berry & Meger, 2023),
112   optimal transport (Xu et al., 2023a), and astrophysics (Langendorff et al., 2023), a main drawback
113   of CNF is its computational cost, since backpropagating the neural ODE in likelihood-based training
114   is expensive (non-simulation free) and not scalable to high dimensions.
115
      Simulation-free flow models. Flow Matching (FM) models (Lipman et al., 2023; Albergo &
116
      Vanden-Eijnden, 2023; Liu, 2022) are simulation-free and a leading class of generative models.
117   We review the technical details of FM in Section 3.1. FM methods are compatible with different
118   choices to interpolate two random end-points drawn from the source and target distributions, e.g.
119   straight lines (called “Optimal Transport (OT) path”) or motivated by the diffusion process (called
120   “diffusion path”) (Lipman et al., 2023). Later works also considered pre-computed OT interpolation
121   (Tong et al., 2023), and stochastic interpolation paths (Albergo et al., 2023). All previous works
122   train a global flow model to match between the two distributions, which could require a large model
123   that takes a longer time to train. In this work, we propose to train multiple smaller flow models. Our
124   approach is compatible with any existing FM method to train these so-called local flows, making it
125
      a flexible and extensible framework.
126   Accelerated generation and model distillation. Model compression and distillation have been
127   intensively developed to accelerate the generation of large generative models. Baranchuk et al.
128   (2021) proposed learning a compressed student normalizing flow model by minimizing the recon-
129   struction loss from a teacher model. For diffusion models, progressive distillation was developed
130   in (Salimans & Ho, 2022), and Consistency Models (Song et al., 2023) demonstrated high quality
131   sample generation by directly mapping noise to data. For FM models, (Liu et al., 2023) proposed
132   to distill the ODE trajectory into a single mapping parametrized by a network, which can reduce the
133
      number of function evaluations (NFE) to be one. The approach was later effectively applied to large
      text-to-image generation (Liu et al., 2024). More recent techniques to distill FM models include
134
      dynamic programming to optimize stepsize given a budget of NFE (Nguyen et al., 2024). In our
135
      work, each local flow model can be distilled into a single-step mapping following (Liu et al., 2023),
136   and the model can be further compressed if needed. Our framework is compatible with different
137   distillation techniques.
138
139   Theoretical guarantees of generative models. Guarantees of diffusion models, where the gener-
140   ation process utilizes an SDE (random) Lee et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2022; 2023a); Benton et al.
141
      (2024b) or ODE (deterministic) sampler Chen et al. (2023b; 2024); Li et al. (2024a;b); Huang et al.
      (2024), have been recently intensively developed. In comparison, there are fewer theoretical find-
142
      ings for ODE flow models both in training (forward process) and generation (reverse processes) like
143
      CNF or FM. For FM models, W2 -guarantee was proved in Benton et al. (2024a) and in (Gao et al.,
144   2024) with sample complexity analysis. The Wasserstein bound does not imply KL or TV bound,
145   which are more relevant for information-theoretical and statistical interpretation. For CNF trained by
146   maximizing likelihood, non-parametric statistical convergence rates were proved in (Marzouk et al.,
147   2024). KL guarantee of a step-wisely trained CNF model motivated by JKO scheme (Xu et al.,
148   2023b) was proved in (Cheng et al., 2024), yet the approach is likelihood-based and not simulation-
149   free. Recently, (Silveri et al., 2024) proved KL guarantee for an SDE interpolation version of the FM
150   model introduced in (Albergo et al., 2023). Our work analyzes a stepwise ODE FM model, which
151   is simulation-free, and the guarantee is in χ2 divergence which implies KL (and TV) guarantees.

509   6.4   ROBOTIC M ANIPULATION
510
      We consider robotic manipulation tasks from the Robomimic benchmark (Mandlekar et al., 2021),
511
      which consists of 5 tasks of controlling robot arms to perform various pick-and-place operations
512   (Figure 4). E.g., the robot may need to pick up a square object (Figure 4a) or move a soda can from
513   one bin to another (Figure 4b). Recently, generative models that output robotic actions conditioning
514   on the state observations (robot positions or camera image embedding) provide state-of-the-art per-
515   formance on completing these tasks (Chi et al., 2023). However, it is difficult to transfer pre-trained
516   diffusion or flow models (on natural images) to the conditional generation task here, because the
517   state observations are task-specific and contain nuanced details about the robots, objects, and envi-
518   ronment, which are not present in natural images but are necessary to be understood by the model
519   to determine the appropriate actions. Therefore, it is often needed to train a generative model from
520
      scratch to directly learn the relation from the task-specific state observations to the actions.
521   We train a (global) FM model and the proposed LFM from scratch, where the total number of param-
522   eters is kept the same for both methods. Additional experimental details are in Appendix C.2. As
523   shown in Table 4, LFM is competitive against FM in terms of the success rate, and the convergence
524   is faster in some cases (indicated by the higher success rate at early epochs). The performance on
525   the “Toolhang” task does not improve with longer training, as identified in (Chi et al., 2023).

527   7     D ISCUSSION
528   The theoretical analysis can be extended from several angles: First, we analyzed the χ2 guarantee
529   and induced KL and TV bounds from the former. One may obtain sharper bounds by analyzing
530   KL or TV directly and possibly under weaker assumptions. E.g., Lemma 2.19 in (Albergo et al.,
531   2023) can be used to derive a KL bound for the one-step FM under similar assumptions as our
532   Assumption 2(A2). Second, we currently assume that Tn−1 is exact in the reverse process (gener-
533   ation). The analysis can be extended to incorporate inversion error due to numerical computation
534   in practice, possibly by following the strategy in (Cheng et al., 2024). Meanwhile, the proposed
535   methodology has the potential to be further enhanced. In our current experiments, we learn the FM
536
      blocks independently parametrized as v̂(x, t; θn ). If we introduce weight sharing of θn across n, it
      can impose additional time continuity of the flow model. In addition, it would be useful to explore
537
      more distillation techniques under our framework and to extend the approach to more applications.

1046   C.2    ROBOTIC M ANIPULATION
1047
1048   In the context of generative modeling, this task of robotic manipulation can be understood as per-
1049   forming sequential conditional generation. Specifically, at each time step t ≥ 1, the goal is to model
1050   the conditional distribution At |Ot , where Ot ∈ RO denotes the states of the robots at time t and
1051
       At ∈ RA is the action that controls the robots. During inference, the robot is controlled as we itera-
       tively sample from At |Ot across time steps t. Past works leveraging diffusion models have reached
1052
       state-of-the-art performances on this task (Chi et al., 2023), where a neural network vθ (e.g., CNN-
1053
       based UNet (Janner et al., 2022)) is trained to approximate the distribution At |Ot via DDPM (Ho
1054   et al., 2020). More recently, flow-based methods have also demonstrated competitive performance
1055   with faster inference (Hu et al., 2024).
1056
1057
       We use the widely adopted success rate to examine the performance of a robot manipulator:
1058                                                    #success rollouts
                                       Success rate =                     ∈ [0, 1].                         (27)
1059                                                         #rollouts
1060   Specifically, starting from a given initial condition O1 of the robot, each rollout denotes a trajectory
1061   S = {O1 , A1 , O2 , . . . , AT , OT } where At |Ot is modeled by the generative. The rollout S is a
1062   success if at any t ∈ 1, . . . , T , the robotic state Ot meets the success criterion (e.g., successfully
1063   pick up the square as in the task “lift” in Figure 4a).
