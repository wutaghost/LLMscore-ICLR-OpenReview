009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            End-to-end learning directly maps sensory inputs to actions, creating highly inte-
012            grated and efficient policies for complex robotics tasks. However, such models
013            are tricky to efficiently train and often struggle to generalize beyond their training
014            scenarios, limiting adaptability to new environments, tasks, and concepts. In this
015            work, we investigate the minimal data requirements and architectural adaptations
016
               necessary to achieve robust closed-loop performance with vision-based control
               policies under unseen text instructions and visual distribution shifts. To this end,
017
               we design datasets with various levels of data representation richness, refine feature
018
               extraction protocols by leveraging multi-modal foundation model encoders, and
019            assess the suitability of different policy network heads. Our findings are synthe-
020            sized in Flex (Fly-lexically), a framework that uses pre-trained Vision Language
021            Models (VLMs) as frozen patch-wise feature extractors, generating spatially aware
022            embeddings that integrate semantic and visual information. These rich features
023            form the basis for training highly robust downstream policies capable of general-
024            izing across platforms, environments, and text-specified tasks. We demonstrate
025            the effectiveness of this approach on quadrotor fly-to-target tasks, where agents
026            trained via behavior cloning on a small simulated dataset successfully generalize
027
               to real-world scenes, handling diverse novel goals and command formulations.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   A significant dimension of human reasoning is mediated through the combination of vision and
032   language, facilitating our mobility in the physical world and our ability to follow directions. Such
033   flexibility and concept understanding are highly desirable in autonomous robots, enabling interactions
034   with humans and handling variants of complex real-world tasks from few representative examples.
035   This inspires an exploration of the conditions necessary to equip robots with a human-like intuition
036   and capacity to execute tasks across various contexts.
037   Despite advancements in end-to-end deep learning for autonomous navigation, these systems remain
038   largely black-box, lacking interpretability, adaptability, and the ability to generalize far beyond the
039   scope of training data. In contrast, VLMs have demonstrated robust open-world visual understanding
040   across tasks like classification, detection, and segmentation. These models are increasingly adopted
041   in robotics for open-vocabulary detection, object manipulation, and planning, but their reliance on
042   modular pipelines and global embeddings limits their utility for end-to-end robot learning.
043   To overcome these challenges, we embrace a minimalist design philosophy, leveraging pre-trained
044   vision-language encoders with lightweight adaptations and minimal training data. By extracting
045   fine-grained, text-fused features from patch-level embeddings, our approach bridges the gap between
046   global text and visual understanding and the spatial, context-aware reasoning required for robotics.
047   This streamlined methodology achieves strong generalization on out-of-distribution scenarios while
048   maintaining efficiency, offering a unified framework for vision-based robotic learning tasks.
049   Hence, we introduce Flex, a minimalist methodology that pioneers the integration of a data-efficient
050   approach with open-set capabilities into a robotic framework. We provide a foundational proof-of-
051   concept demonstrating the potential of leveraging VLM features for user-interactive, end-to-end
052   visual navigation agents, offering the flexibility to interpret open-set text instructions at both the
053   object and environment levels. By focusing on basic instructions, we address the core challenges of
      this novel integration without the added complexity of intricate language processing. This streamlined

068   End-to-end multi-modal imitation learning. The setup considered is that of an end-to-end control
069   system f that generates commands u ∈ Rn where n is the dimension of the output vector. The
070   system takes multi-modal input comprising of a RGB image I ∈ Rh×w×3 , with h, w representing
071   the frame height and width respectively, and a natural language text command T . f can be seen as
072   the composition of a feature extraction backbone ϕ and a policy head π, such that f = π ◦ ϕ, and

095
      Problem statement. We seek to establish the bare design criteria for training robust, text-instructed,
      end-to-end control agents. Specifically, our goal is to delineate the conditions for effective leveraging
096
      of off-the-shelf models to extract meaningful features suitable for compact downstream policy
097
      networks. Our agents should not only excel in learning tasks from very simplified datasets in
098   simulation but also demonstrate robust generalization capabilities to handle previously unseen
099   scenarios. We probe into the three pillars of the IL framework and attempt to answer the following
100   questions:
101
102         1. Dataset design: What is the minimal degree of data diversity required to obtain sufficiently
103
               rich feature representations?
104         2. Feature extractor: What are the suitable feature extractors for text and vision-based
105            robotics learning? How should they be employed to offer potent downstream generalization
106
               capability?
107         3. Policy network: What is the impact of the choice of policy network architecture on the
               performance and interpretability of the trained agents?

      3.1   T RAINING DATA
112
      A desirable property for an imitation learning system is to master a task from a handful of repre-
113
      sentative expert demonstrations without requiring extensive enumeration of use cases or intensive
114   randomization and augmentation techniques. Hence, relying on internet-scale trained VLMs for fea-
115   ture extraction largely mitigates the impediments on training dataset size, diversity and augmentation.
116   We investigate the extent to which this statement holds, and limit ourselves to the use of a single
117   simulated scene to generate four training datasets, evaluating the impact of diversity in the goal and
118   text instruction phrasing on generalization capabilities of trained agents:
119
      1. One object and one command, containing demonstrations reaching a single goal object (red sphere)
120   with a single command syntax (”Fly to the red ball”).
121
122   1M. One object and multiple commands, with the same goal object, but each run instructed with a
123
      lexical alternative of the instruction (as discussed in Appendix A.3).
124   2. Two objects and one command, with red and blue spheres as the example goals and single
125   command wording in either case (”Fly to red/blue ball”).
126
      2M. Two objects and multiple commands, containing both colored spheres and variations of the
127   syntax between demonstrations. (Training run sequence frames are provided in Figure 10)

130   3.2   PATCH - WISE T EXT- VISION S PATIAL F EATURES
131
132   Generic image-text features. Robust OoD generalization relies on universal rather than domain-
133
      specific features for policy learning. Foundation model encoders leverage internet-scale data to learn
      generic features from a wide spectrum of contexts. Moreover, incorporating textual instructions
134
      demands an extractor capable of seamlessly integrating text inputs with visual features. Thus, a
135
      natural choice is pre-trained VLM as our feature extractor cornerstone. More specifically, BLIP-2
136   (Li et al., 2023) is used throughout this work, as it is specifically designed to fuse multi-modal
137   information from large-scale textual and visual datasets, providing a cohesive representation.
138
139
      Spatial resolution for robotic tasks. Foundation models typically output a global feature vector
      representing the entire image. This coarse representation is unsuitable for robotics, where policy
140
      learning depends on fine-grained spatial information to effectively interpret and respond to the scene.
141
      Thus, we propose a method to extract spatial feature vectors for specific areas in an image. To obtain
142   the global descriptor for a frame, we collect such features for multiple areas/patches covering the
143   whole image. Specifically, given an input frame/image I ∈ Rh×w×3 , an input text command T , and
144   the patch-resolution h′ ≤ h, w′ ≤ w, we provide a method which utilizes a multi-modal foundation
                                                                                          ′   ′
145   model VLM : Rh×w×3 → Rd to derive a tensor of feature descriptors F ∈ Rh ×w ×d , that fuses
146   all the semantic information of I with the text input T and maintains its location in the scene. For
147   simplicity purposes, we equate h′ and w′ to the number of (non-overlapping) patches used to divide
148   the input image I when applying VLM on it (we will discuss how h′ and w′ can be adjusted to any
149   values ≤ h, w, respectively) and n = h′ w′ .
150   Notations. Let IMG-DESC be the image encoder of VLM consisting of L layers. For every layer
151   ℓ ∈ {1, · · · , L}, we use Qℓ , K ℓ ∈ Rn×dk , V ℓ ∈ Rn×d to denote the output query, key, and value
152   matrices of the ℓth attention layer, during the feedforward pass of applying IMG-DESC on I, i.e.,
153   during IMG-DESC(I). We now describe our mechanism for extracting patch-text fused features F(j)
154   for a single patch I(j) , where j ∈ {1, · · · , n}. Then, this can be applied sequentially or in parallel to
155   all patches.
156

      3.3     P OLICY NETWORK
227
228   We aim to identify the most effective policy network architecture for learning from generic extracted
229   features with limited simulated training data. Ideally, we want to preserve text-patch details while
230   aggregating information across layers, enabling decisions from nuanced, context-rich descriptors
231   tailored to the task. Vision Transformers (ViT) are of interest as they maintain spatial resolutions
232   across layers. We also consider simpler architectures such as Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN
233   or Conv), and Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLP) for learning the control policy. A detailed description
234
      of each model and its parameters is provided in Appendix A.5.

238   4.1     F LY- TO - ANY- TARGET TASK
239
240   Task description. The objective is to develop a vision-based quadrotor navigation agent capable of
241   reaching arbitrary user-specified goals present in its field of view (FOV) while ensuring generalization
242
      across visual scenes, in simulation as well as in the real world.
243   Evaluation protocol. A single test run consists of initializing a scene with a number of objects in the
244   drone’s FOV and providing text instructions about the goal to reach. The closed-loop inference is run
245   for a fixed number of steps, 80, slightly larger than the average training sequence length. The test is
246   successful if the agent can navigate towards the user-instructed object and center it in the middle of
247   the frame. Failures, on the other hand, can be identified when the drone loses the object (the target
248
      exits the FOV and/or another visual cue is centered in on), or fails to approach or center in on the goal.
      The evaluation of the closed-loop performance of our system is based on monitoring the success rates
249
      on repeated runs in various evaluation configurations.

478   6                          R ELATED W ORK
479
480   End-to-end robot learning. End-to-end deep learning has shown significant potential in autonomous
481   navigation tasks (Chib & Singh, 2023; Bojarski et al., 2016; Pomerleau, 1988). Advances in
482   safety (Xiao et al., 2023) and generalization (Chahine et al., 2023; Quach et al., 2024; Wang et al.,
483   2023b; Yin et al., 2023; Kaufmann et al., 2023) have improved performance, but these models remain
484   largely black-box, incapable of user interaction, and confined to the scope of training data. Moreover,
485   training robust, large-scale models is challenging due to the need for extensive, high-quality datasets,
      which are costly, time-consuming, and pose potential safety risks (Kendall et al., 2019).

                                                             9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Simulation-based training has emerged as a practical alternative, leveraging platforms such as
487   VISTA (Amini et al., 2022), Drake (Tedrake et al., 2019), PyBullet (Panerati et al., 2021), and
488   AirSim (Shah et al., 2018). However, simulated environments often fail to fully capture real-world
489   intricacies, leading to performance degradation and safety risks during deployment. Intermediate
490   visual abstractions (Müller et al., 2018; Toromanoff et al., 2020; Behl et al., 2020) address some of
491   these gaps, but such methods lack the multimodal reasoning required for truly generalizable systems.
492
      VLMs and Foundation Models in Robotics. Foundation models, particularly vision-language
493   models (VLMs), have revolutionized open-world visual understanding tasks, including classifi-
494   cation (Radford et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2022), detection (Li et al., 2022c; Zhong et al., 2022),
495   segmentation (Kirillov et al., 2023; Li et al., 2022a), and captioning (Li et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
496   2022). Within robotics, these models have been applied to open-vocabulary detection and manipula-
497   tion (Chen et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024), planning (Ahn et al., 2022), and action prediction (Brohan
498   et al., 2023). For navigation, approaches that decouple perception and control (Maalouf et al., 2023)
499   or generate waypoints explicit (Shah et al., 2023) have been proposed.
500   In dynamic, open-set environments, VLMs have facilitated applications like 3D mapping (Huang
501   et al., 2023; Ding et al., 2023), scene segmentation (Peng et al., 2023; Jatavallabhula et al., 2023), and
502   explainable, language-based representations (Kim et al., 2019; Omeiza et al., 2021; Kuo et al., 2022;
503   Tan et al., 2023; Zhong et al., 2023). However, despite their versatility across data modalities (Ramesh
504   et al., 2021; Crowson et al., 2022; Patashnik et al., 2021; Ramesh et al., 2022), these methods often rely
505   on modular pipelines and global embeddings, which limit their utility for text-instructed end-to-end
506   robotic learning.
507   Flex vs. Mainstream VLN Approaches. Recent advances such as RT-1 (Brohan et al., 2022),
508   RT-2 (Brohan et al., 2023), and Vint (Shah et al., 2023) represent significant progress in vision-based
509   navigation. RT-1 was trained on over 130,000 real-world demonstrations, while RT-2 incorporated
510   internet-scale pre-training with models up to 55 billion parameters. Similarly, VLN-BERT (Hong
511   et al., 2021) was trained on more than six million image-text-action triplets, and NavGPT (Zhou
512   et al., 2024) leverages GPT models for zero-shot action prediction. In stark contrast to our minimalist
513
      approach training small policy heads on relatively tiny amounts of data, these methods rely on
      extensive datasets and resource-intensive training pipelines.
514
515   Text-Patch Features for End-to-End Robotics. Patch-based feature extraction has been explored in
516   prior work, but existing methods face limitations. Some are not multimodal (Amir et al., 2021); others
517   fine-tune encoders for 2D-pixel alignment, losing critical concepts (Ding et al., 2022). Approaches
518   like SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023) rely on segmentation models that can miss important regions (Jataval-
519
      labhula et al., 2023; Maalouf et al., 2024), while others fail to fuse text queries with patch descriptors
      for semantic relations (Wang et al., 2023a).
520
521   Our approach bridges these gaps by extracting fine-grained, text-fused features from pre-trained
522   VLMs, enabling context-aware reasoning critical for end-to-end robotics tasks without relying on
523   hand-designed pipelines or intermediate representations (Li et al., 2022b; 2023; Radford et al., 2021).
524
525   7    C ONCLUSION
526
527   This work establishes the essential dataset and model requirements for robust generalization in
528   text-instructed end-to-end visual navigation agents using pre-trained VLM encoders as multi-modal
529   feature extractors. Our findings include the failure of training on a single data context (leads to
530   over-fitting), and the adequacy of two examples to train models that handle a wide spectrum of similar
531   use cases. We also advocate for simple text-space augmentations, which can improve performance in
532   more nuanced test settings. We shed light on the shortcomings of low-resolution patch-wise feature
533   extraction, with the fly-to-target task necessitating at least 8×8 patches. Finally, we ascertain the
534
      superiority of the ViT architecture as a policy head, in terms of task success and flight behavior, while
      uncovering aspects of its robust context invariant decision process via similarity-based clustering.
535
536   The synthesis of these findings is Flex, a new minimalist training framework capable of producing
537   user-interactive highly generalizing visual navigation agents. Our solution elegantly handles a suite
538   of in-simulation challenges and proves readily deployable in the real-world, robustly achieving direct
539   sim-to-real open dictionary out-of-distribution generalization.

      A.2   D ISCUSSION
783
      Limitations and Future Work The framework presented in this manuscript is limited to instantaneous
784   decisions. Indeed, the policy can only act with information from the current image and has no access
785   to a history of representations or actions. We are keen to incorporate our potent multi-modal feature
786   encoding scheme into sequential decision-making processes. This would enable Flex to go beyond
787   generalization between environments and objects, and handle instructions over actions, sequences
788   of steps, and behavior modes. An additional limitation of this work is its computational overhead,
789   which renders it impractical for real-time execution on small mobile robotic platforms. This pertains
790   to the wider effort in edge AI research to enable the deployment of foundation models directly on
791   edge devices. The robotics community will reap great benefits from these advances that will enable
792
      the widespread adoption of methods such as Flex.
793
794
      A.3   T RAINING
795
      Dataset description. A unique simulated dataset is used for training by all models discussed
796   throughout this paper. It consists of 300 goal approach flight sequences (22,844 frames in total, 76.15
797   frames per run on average), with a 90% training and 10% validation split. The same two objects, a
798   red and a blue sphere, are used in every sequence. All frames of a give sequence are labelled with the
799   same text, a natural language instruction sentence providing information on whether to fly to the red
800   or blue goal. We balance the number of runs headed for each of the two possible options.
801
      Trajectory design. Both spheres are initially positioned in the drone’s field of view such that all
802   trajectories generated carry recovery information (with the farthermost target at the border of the
803   image). They are positioned at the same altitude and relative position, equidistant from the drone.
804   We randomize the initial distance to the targets, thus ensuring the size of the objects in the image is
805   varying. This ensures we expose the network to trajectories that recenter and approach the goal target
806   from a wide spectrum of angles and distances. The control signals are obtained with the ground truth
807   knowledge available to us from the simulator, where PID controllers generate the vertical velocity
808   commands v z to reduce the altitude gap, the forward velocity commands v x to reduce the distance
809   gap, and the yaw rate ψ̇ to pilot the drone towards the instructed goal (centered in the middle of the
      frame).

                                                        15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      Label design. Each approach sequence is associated with a unique text instruction. We introduce
811   text domain augmentation by generating 25 synonyms to the verb ”fly to” and noun ”target”. The
812   verb phrases include: migrate towards, glide to, whiz to, steer towards, manoeuvre to, zoom to,
813   elevate towards, approach, propel to, make way to, orient towards, venture towards, soar to, advance
814   to, progress towards, journey to, hover to, proceed to, drift towards, rush to, shift to, head towards,
815   ascend towards, scene towards, travel to. The object terms include: signpost, point, terminus, stage,
816   station, location, interest, goal, setting, checkpoint, cue, objective, coordinate, emblem, locus, target,
817   marker, beacon, spot, destination, signal, symbol, sight, position, aim. We uniformly sample from
818   these terms to form instructions in the format: VERB PHRASE the [COLOR] OBJECT
819   Training run sequence frames are depicted in Figure 10.
820
      Training details. The loss used is the Mean Squared Error (MSE) between the network predicted
821
      commands and the values with which a dataset frame is labeled, with equal weights between all scalar
822
      outputs. We train the models for 20 epochs, using the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 1 · 10−4 .
823   Frames are uniformly sampled from the dataset to ensure shuffling. We take the checkpoint with
824   the best validation loss for each model. All training was performed on a single NVIDIA GeForce
825   RTX 3080 Ti GPU, with 12 GB memory and 10240 CUDA cores, with a single full training run
826   taking around 45 hours. The major compute bottleneck originates in repeated calls to the BLIP2
827   based multi-modal patch encoding, which can be alleviated by encoding and storing the entire dataset
828   features once instead of re-encoding at every epoch. Our setup is capable of handling 2.8 frames per
829   second on average during training.

832
833   Hardware. Our platform is a DJI M300 RTK quadcopter. The M300 interfaces with the DJI
834   Manifold 2 companion computer, enabling programmatic control of the drone. The DJI Onboard
835   SDK (Software Development Kit) and its associated ROS wrapper provide an interface for feeding
836   the drone’s low-level flight controller with desired high-level translation velocities and yaw rate
837
      commands. The flight controller onboard the DJI M300 is a black box system provided by the
      manufacturer which controls the four-rotor speeds to track the velocities specified by the companion
838
      TX2 computer. It is worth mentioning that the dynamics of the platform we use do not match those
839
      of the nano-quadrotors simulated. Input images gathered by the gimbal-stabilized camera, which
840   follows the drone’s yaw to always point forward, are available to the companion computer via the
841   SDK. The onboard computer runs an NVIDIA Jetson TX2, which has GPU capability. However,
842   Flex inference on a single image takes over 10 seconds. Thus, establish a connection over Wifi
843   between the onboard TX2 computer and a standalone Lenovo 16” ThinkPad P16 Gen 2 with Intel
844   Core i9-13980HX (13th Gen) CPU and NVIDIA RTX 5000 GPU with 16 GB GDDR6 VRAM.
845   The TX2 sends the latest image to the machine which runs inference and replies with the control
846   command to execute. We reach a runtime frequency of just over 1 Hz with our setup.
847   Real world scene. We conduct our flight tests on an outdoor lawn in an urban university campus.
848   In addition to having to bridge the sim-to-real transfer gap, agents are also exposed to a completely
849   new visual scene, with various buildings, reflective structures, and trees. Lighting conditions from
850   various starting positions now expose the agents to sunlight from various angles, making for a very
851   challenging sim-to-real generalization scenario.
852   Goal objects. We use cardboard cutouts that we position on tripods at a safe flight altitude as targets.
853   The list of props contains a red disk, blue disk, white disk, yellow square, yellow star, and human
854   figure printed cutout on top of which a wig is placed.

859   architecture consists of three Transformer blocks with a patch size of 1x1, a dimensionality of 128,

861   256. A fully-connected layer maps to a 4-dimensional output. The convolutional network (Conv)
862   architecture includes three 1x1 convolutional layers. The first layer has an input dimension of 64, and
863   all layers have a hidden dimension of 128. Each layer is followed by ReLU activation and dropout,
      and the output is flattened to (128 * 16 * 16) and a fully-connected layer maps to a 4-dimensional
