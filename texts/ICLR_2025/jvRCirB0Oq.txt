009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011              The diversity across outputs generated by LLMs shapes perception of their quality
012              and utility. Achieving high textual diversity in datasets is often a desired quality, but
013              there is no standard method to measure this aspect of model behaviour. In this work
014              we empirically investigate diversity scores on English texts and measure how much
015              overlapping information is captured in these metrics. We find that computationally
016              efficient compression algorithms capture information similar to what is measured
017              by slow-to-compute n-gram overlap homogeneity scores. Further, a combination
018
                 of measures—compression ratios, self-repetition of long n-grams and Self-BLEU
                 and BERTScore—are sufficient to report, as they have low mutual correlation
019
                 with each other. The applicability of scores extends beyond analysis of generative
020
                 models; for example, we highlight applications on instruction-tuning datasets and
021              human-produced texts. We release a diversity score package to facilitate research
022              and invite consistency going forward.1
023
024
025   1       I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Evaluation of LLM-generated texts is typically done with respect to accuracy or factuality, e.g., as
028   measured via entailment (Tang et al., 2023), or text quality aspects such as coherence and fluency
029   (e.g., estimated using LLMs as evaluators as in Liu et al. 2023). For summarization tasks where
030   reference summaries are available, the similarity of generated outputs to these is also often measured.
031   A complementary dimension of model performance is diversity, i.e., how much “boilerplate” content
032   is repeated across LLM outputs.
033   The diversity of generated outputs is intuitively important: A model prone to repeating a few sentence
034   constructions or boilerplate turns of phrase across its outputs will likely be deemed lower quality than
035   an LLM that offers more diverse generations, all else being equal. For example, when prompted to
036   summarize news articles, Llama-2 (Touvron et al., 2023b) tends to often generate text that begins
037   with “The article discusses that...”, “The article notes that...”. Table 1 illustrates example repetitions
038   from various models over a news summarization dataset.
039   This work primarily aims to standardize reporting of lexical diversity measures. We first analyze
040   existing diversity scores over English language outputs from several LLMs. This identifies a few
041   practical, (mostly) independent scores that characterize repetition. We highlight text length as an
042   important factor when assessing diversity, and examine diversity in downstream datasets such as
043   instruction tuning. We release an open-source Python package that can be used to explore and
044   evaluate the diversity of generated text datasets.1

095   The association between length and measures of diversity is well-established in corpus linguistics (for
096   a detailed discussion, see Brysbaert et al. 2016). The number of unique words in a corpus is a power
097   function of the total words seen, where the power is less than 1. The number of total words grows
098
      linearly, while the number of unique words is sublinear, so longer texts have more repetitions of
      unique words and n-grams than shorter texts (Covington & McFall, 2010; McCarthy & Jarvis, 2010).
099
100   LLM generations can reduce text diversity in a few ways. Guo et al. (2023) study the effect of
101   consecutive rounds of distillation, in which a language model produces the data on which a separate
102   language model is subsequently trained. They report dramatic reduction in diversity over 10 iterations.
103   However, this study did not report the length of text produced in consecutive distillation rounds.
104
      Given our findings, it would be prudent to check if output lengths remain comparable in length.
105   Padmakumar & He (2023a) show that when people write with the aid of an LLM (e.g., instructGPT)
106   they produce less diverse writing than when they do not. Here we ascertain that these results are
107   independent of length, indicating that there is a genuine reduction in diversity (rather than merely
      affecting lengths which in turn influence diversity measures).

      3   A S MORGASBORD OF T EXT D IVERSITY S CORES
109
110
      The variety of scores used to measure diversity across a corpus of texts derive from two core ideas:
111
      Computing average similarity between pairs of outputs produced by the same model for different
112
      inputs, and computing variants of token/type ratio. The former are adapted from common approaches
113   to text generation evaluation by comparing with references, using standard measures of pairwise
114   similarity; the latter track the diversity of vocabulary measured as the ratio of unique words to total
115   words produced, with the outputs from a model concatenated into a single text.
116
      We first describe each score, and then present insights regarding their mutual redundancy. We
117
      also consider their required run-times, which are lengthy for some metrics and may render them
118
      impractical for analysis of a large number of outputs. All scores are defined for a set of generated
119   texts D, each conditioned on its respective input.
120
121
      Self-BLEU The quality of text in machine translation, summarization, and image captioning is
122
      often reported in terms of overlap with a reference text. This idea can be adapted to measure diversity
123   across different outputs by using one generated text as a reference and measuring the similarity of
124   other outputs against this. Self-BLEU measures similarity between all text pairs in D using BLEU as
125   the similarity score (Zhu et al., 2018). BLEU can be replaced with an arbitrary similarity score, e.g.,
126   ROUGE-L or BERTScore. These variants are called homogenization scores and have recently been
127   used to compare the diversity of texts produced under several conditions (Padmakumar & He, 2023a).
128
129   Homogenization Score (ROUGE-L) All homogenization scores calculate an aggregate similarity
130   across pairs of examples (Equation 1). Here the similarity score of choice is ROUGE-L Lin & Och
131   2004. This quantifies overlap in terms of longest common sub-sequences between all pairs of text in
132   a corpus instead of the fixed n-gram size used in other ROUGE variants:

290   6    D IVERSITY OF M ODEL S UMMARIES
291
292   The confound of length complicates reporting. On both CNN/DM and XSUM (cf. Tables 2 and 3),
293   StableLM produces the longest summaries. All scores indicate that these are the least diverse, most
294   likely due to the length confound. In both sets of results, we look for models that produce shorter
295   summaries that are less diverse. These findings are notable and hold, despite length differences.
296
      Three types of differences are marked in the tables. Model summaries that are shorter but less diverse
297   than human summaries are marked in bold. Human texts here are written by journalists, so the
298   expectation is that they would be more diverse. More bold entries in a column indicate that the score
299   captures differences between human and machine diversity, a desirable trait. Underlined entries
300   denote models that are less diverse than other models that produce longer summaries. The more
301   underlined entries there are for a model, the more indicators there are that its output is less diverse.
302   Asterisks mark models that appear more diverse than a human text of shorter length.
303   The most interesting diversity scores are those that capture differences between human and automati-
304   cally produced text, without necessarily committing to an interpretation of which source is preferable.
305   Human evaluation in future work will address this question, which we discuss in 10. On the CNN/DM
306   dataset, Hom. (BERT) and MATTR are the two scores that detect no differences between human
307   and model texts. Compression ratio for part of speech sequences is the score that identifies the most
308   differences between human and model-generated text. Self-repetition stands out as the only score that
309   identifies model generated text as more diverse on the CNN/DM dataset. From this analysis, CR:POS
310   and self-repetition emerge as prime candidates of reportable scores, while Hom. BERT is less useful.

463   11    D ISCUSSION AND R ECOMMENDATIONS
464
465   Our in-depth analyses reveal that compression ratio is an excellent score to report, easy to compute
466   and strongly correlated with other scores used in past work. Compression ratio of part of speech
467   sequences capture differences between human and model-generated text. Self-repetition zeros in
468   only on repetition of longer n-grams across generations, and is only moderately correlated with
469   compression ratios. Finally Self-BLEU is only weakly correlated with the previous three, so is a
470   good complement score to report. In our analyses, we identified several drawbacks of BERTScore: it
471   does not show differences between human and model-generated text and barely varies when adjusted
472   for length. There is no good justification to report it.
473   Length of the analyzed text has to be reported alongside all these scores. When length differs, scores
474   are not meaningfully comparable. Truncating and downsampling text is one way to produce a set of
475   results that are intuitively comparable. Different random draws of the sample chosen to represent a
476   dataset will likely differ in diversity; the selection may lead to unwarranted conclusions. Truncating
477   texts prevents any possibility of discovering repetitive behavior towards the end of longer text. Future
478   research into a principled solution for this problem is urgently needed. Despite all this, we were able
479
      to glean meaningful insights about differences in diversity between human and model-produced text
      for summaries, essays and instructions.

807   A.5   I NSTRUCTION DATASETS , D ETAILS
808
809   Open Assistant is a collection of crowdsourced instructions (Köpf et al., 2024). The data was
      collected under detailed guidelines and includes questions that reflect real-life situations.


                                                                                         15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      Super-NaturalInstructions A corpus comprising crowdsourced instructions that transform 200
811   benchmarks and intermediate evaluation results into a set of instructions and demonstrations (Wang
812   et al., 2022).
813
814   Unnatural Instructions An (almost) automatically created dataset, using instructions from the
815   SuperNatural-Instructions dataset to automatically generate new instructions (Honovich et al., 2023).
816   To increase diversity, each instruction was also paraphrased. Honovich et al. (2023) compare the diver-
817   sity of instructions in Unnatural and Super-Natural Instructions with pairwise BERTScore similarities
818   (within each dataset), and find that the similarities are much higher in Super-NaturalInstructions.
819
820   Alpaca This dataset is created following the Self-instruct dataset (Wang et al., 2023b). GPT-3 was
821   prompted to create instructions and demonstrations based on a seed of 175 human-written instructions.
822   Crucially, the collection method includes a diversity filter, only including model-written instructions
823
      if their ROUGE-L similarity is less than 0.7 with an existing instruction. Length of instructions and
      demonstrations is also controlled for as a criterion for inclusion in the final instruction dataset.
824
825
      Dolly A set of human instructions and demonstrations, collected by Datrabricks employees
826
      (Conover et al., 2023). By design, they cover only eight classes of popular tasks: creative writ-
827   ing, closed and open QA, summarization, information extraction, classification and brainstorming.
828
829   Table 12 shows the number of instructions, the typical length of an instruction and average number of
830
      words per instruction set. All vary, making it even harder to control for length. Truncating makes less
      sense here, and down-sampling the number per instructions is counter-productive given our goal to
831
      understand the diversity of the entire dataset. We do make use of these instruments given the lack of
832
      alternatives, but note that more meaningful solutions are urgently needed.
