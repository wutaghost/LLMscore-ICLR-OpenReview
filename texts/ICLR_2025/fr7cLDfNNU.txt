011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Vision Transformers (ViTs) have become popular in computer vision tasks. Back-
014            door attacks, which trigger undesirable behaviours in models during inference,
015            threaten ViTs’ performance, particularly in security-sensitive tasks. Although back-
016
               door defences have been developed for Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs),
               they are less effective for ViTs, and defences tailored to ViTs are scarce. To address
017
               this, we present Interleaved Ensemble Unlearning (IEU), a method for finetuning
018
               clean ViTs on backdoored datasets. In stage 1, a shallow ViT is finetuned to have
019            high confidence on backdoored data and low confidence on clean data. In stage
020            2, the shallow ViT acts as a “gate” to block potentially poisoned data from the
021            defended ViT. This data is added to an unlearn set and asynchronously unlearnt via
022            gradient ascent. We demonstrate IEU’s effectiveness on three datasets against 11
023            state-of-the-art backdoor attacks and show its versatility by applying it to different
024            model architectures.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Vision Transformers (ViTs, Dosovitskiy et al. (2021)) have emerged as a powerful alternative to
      Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) for a wide range of computer vision tasks. ViTs have
030
      achieved state-of-the-art performance in various downstream tasks such as image classification,
031
      object detection, and semantic segmentation (Thisanke et al., 2023; Shehzadi et al., 2023). However,
032   the widespread deployment of ViTs have also raised concerns about their vulnerability to adversarial
033   threats, particularly backdoor attacks, which typically modify images and/or labels in the training
034   dataset to trigger attacker-controlled undesirable behaviour during inference (Gu et al., 2019; Sub-
035   ramanya et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2023). Backdoor attacks such as the BadNets attack in Gu et al.
036   (2019) can compromise model behaviour by embedding malicious triggers during training, leading to
037   security risks in real-world applications. As ViTs become increasingly popular in security-sensitive
038   domains such as autonomous driving and face recognition (Lai-Dang, 2024; Tran et al., 2022), it is
039   important to understand these vulnerabilities and develop robust backdoor defences for ViTs.
040   ViTs are often pretrained using self-supervised learning (SSL) on large datasets and then finetuned to
041   be deployed on specific tasks. Backdoor defences have been proposed to defend foundation models
042   pretrained on large datasets and can either prevent backdoor injection during the SSL process or
043   encourage removal of backdoors after pretraining (Tejankar et al., 2023; Bie et al., 2024); these
044   thwart backdoor attacks that occur during pretraining, such as a practical real-world attack on web-
045   scraped datasets in Carlini et al. (2024) and an SSL-specific imperceptible attack Zhang et al. (2024).
046
      The finetuning process for adapting ViTs to downstream tasks using supervised learning is equally
      vulnerable to backdoor attacks. The rationale behind developing ViT-specific defences for finetuning
047
      are two-fold: Mo et al. (2024) shows that there are few defences specifically designed for ViTs for
048
      image classification in existing literature (Doan et al. (2023) and Subramanya et al. (2024) being
049   notable examples of such defences); in addition, although existing defences designed for CNNs can
050   defend ViTs after modifying the defence implementations, they still lead to high ASR and/or low CA
051   when defending different flavours of ViTs (Tables 4 & 5 in Mo et al. (2024)).
052
      To fill the gap, this work propose a novel backdoor defence that uses an ensemble of two ViTs to
053
      perform interleaved unlearning on potentially poisoned data, demonstrating superior performance

069   et al. (2021b), we use a shallow ViT denoted by the “poisoned module” to defend the main ViT, which
070
      we call the “robust module”. Our design IEU has two stages as shown in Figure 1. In stage 1, the
      poisoned module (fp ), a shallow ViT, is tuned on the attacker-controlled finetuning data. Intuitively,
071
      shortcut learning (Geirhos et al., 2020) leads fp to learn shortcuts in the dataset, which are most
072
      prevalent in poisoned images. In addition, the simplicity of fp discourages it from learning clean
073   data that have fewer shortcuts. Therefore, the poisoned module is confident (where confidence is the
074   maximum class probability max[σ(ŷp )] predicted by fp ) when classifying poisoned data and not
075   confident otherwise. In stage 2, images pass through the tuned fp , which either queues data onto the
076   unlearn set or allows the defended main ViT (the robust module) to learn data normally based on
077   the confidence threshold cthresh . Whenever the unlearn set accumulates enough potentially poisoned
078   data, a batch is unlearnt by the robust module using a dynamic unlearning rate. Instead of using a
079   pre-determined unlearn set, our defence accumulates the unlearn set during stage 2. The benefits
080   are two-fold: compared to using ABL’s (Li et al., 2021b) method which isolates poisoned samples
081
      using the defended model, finetune-time unlearn set accumulation using fp ensures that the robust
082
      module learns as little poisoned data as possible; in addition, online accumulation of Dul is adaptive
      in the sense that the frequency of unlearning is high when more potentially poisoned images are
083
      encountered, quickly erasing the impact of a large number of poisoned data. In addition, we argue
084
      that core concepts developed in our method, namely applying interleaved unlearning, can defend
085   other model architectures in image classification. Here are our main contributions:
086
087   • We propose the universally applicable and novel interleaved unlearning framework as a backdoor
088
        defence. The defence, incorporated into IEU, alternates between learning benign data and unlearn
        backdoored data. We show that our IEU is successful without requiring high-precision isolation of
089
        poisoned data and performs especially well on ViTs.
090
      • We empirically demonstrate that our design out-performs existing state-of-the-art defences on
091     challenging datasets using 11 backdoor attacks by comparing to SOTA methods such as ABL and
092     I-BAU (Li et al., 2021b; Zeng et al., 2021b); Attack Success Rate (ASR) improved by 33.83% and
093     31.46% on average for TinyImageNet and CIFAR10, respectively, while maintaining high Clean
094     Accuracy (CA).
095   • We demonstrate IEU’s universality by successfully defending ViT variants and CNN architectures
096     (Table 8). Furthermore, we show that IEU successfully repels an adaptive attack (Table 18).
097   • We explore potential points of failure of unlearning-based defence mechanisms to defend against
098     weak attacks where “weakness” corresponds to lower ASR. We propose potential solutions to
099
        address these failures. In our opinion, weak attacks are as insidious as powerful attacks.

101   2    R ELATED WORK
102
103   Backdoor Attacks. Attackers aim (a) to induce a specific classification when the input is perturbed
104   by an attacker-specified transformation and (b) to maintain normal performance when images without
105   a backdoor trigger are classified (Gu et al., 2019). Often, the attacker achieves the two goals by
106   injecting poisoned images into the training set. Backdoor attacks for both SSL (Zhang et al., 2024;
107   Sun et al., 2024; Saha et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023a; Jia et al., 2022) and supervised learning (Chen
      et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2018b; Nguyen & Tran, 2021; Li et al., 2021a) have been proposed. There

108
      are three categories of backdoor attacks for supervised learning, which is the learning phase that this
109   work defends: dirty-label attacks which includes visible and invisible attacks (Tan & Shokri, 2020;
110   Lin et al., 2020; Doan et al., 2021), clean-label attacks which do not modify the label of backdoored
111   images (Gao et al., 2023b; Zeng et al., 2023; Turner et al., 2019), and clean-image attacks which only
112   modify data labels (Rong et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023). Authors have also developed ViT-specific
113   backdoor attacks. For example, Zheng et al. (2023) inserts a Trojan into a ViT checkpoint, while Lv
114   et al. (2021) modifies the finetuning procedure by using an attacker-specified loss function.
115   Backdoor Defences. Defenders aim to ensure that backdoor images do not trigger attacker-specified
116   model behaviour whilst maintaining high CA. A popular class of defences is model reconstruction
117   where defenders cleanse poisoned models of backdoors (Liu et al., 2017; 2018a). Works in this
118   category aim to remove backdoor neurons and include Neural Attention Distillation (NAD, Li et al.
119   (2021c)), Adversarial Neuron Pruning (ANP, Wu & Wang (2021)) Adversarial Weight Masking
120   (AWM, Chai & Chen (2022)), Shapley-estimation based few-shot defence (Guan et al., 2022), and
121   Reconstructive Neuron Pruning (RNP, Li et al. (2023b)). Another such cleansing defence, I-BAU
122   (Zeng et al., 2021b) connects the two optimisation problems in the minimax formulation of backdoor
123
      removal using an implicit hypergradient. Certified backdoor defences have also been developed
      (Weber et al., 2023). Another broad class of defences involves reconstructing the trigger in order to
124
      unlearn backdoor images. Notable examples include Neural Cleanse (Wang et al., 2019), DeepInspect
125
      (Chen et al., 2019) which checks for signs of backdooring without a reserved clean set, and BTI-DBF
126   (Xu et al., 2024) which decouples benign features for backdoor trigger inversion. Tuning clean
127   models on backdoored datasets (Borgnia et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022a;b; Zhang et al., 2023) is
128   also popular and is most related to our IEU. Additionally, methods such as Anti-Backdoor Learning
129   (ABL, Li et al. (2021b)) and ASD (Gao et al., 2023a) focus on isolating poisoned data.
130
      ViT-Specific Backdoor Defences. Few backdoor defences are specifically designed for defending
131
      ViTs during tuning (Mo et al., 2024) and existing defences that have CNNs in mind perform worse
132   on ViTs. Two notable defences are Doan et al. (2023) where backdoor images are identified using
133   patch-processing, and Subramanya et al. (2024) which is a test-time defence that uses GradRollout
134   (Gildenblat (2020), an interpretation method for ViTs) to block high-attention patches in images.
135
      Machine Unlearning (Xu et al., 2023a) focuses on removing data from models due to privacy
136
      reasons; additionally, unlearning is also useful for removing unwanted associations between certain
137
      undesirable features and labels, making it useful for backdoor defence as shown in Li et al. (2021b).

142   In this section, we first define our threat model and describe IEU in detail. We conclude this section
143   by briefly exploring the drawbacks of using a fixed-size unlearn set Dul .
144   Threat model. We focus on finetuning ViTs for image classification tasks and assume that the
145   pretrained model checkpoint initially given to the defender is not benign. We follow the threat model
146   of Li et al. (2021b). We assume that the finetuning procedure is controlled by the defender, which
147   means attacks that modify finetuning loss or the model’s gradient (Lv et al., 2021; Bagdasaryan &
148   Shmatikov, 2021) are out of scope. On the other hand, finetuning data is gathered from untrusted
149   sources and may contain backdoor data. The attacker knows the model architecture and the pretrained
150
      checkpoint’s parameter values, and may poison the finetuning dataset by modifying images and/or
      labels. The defender aims to tune a benign checkpoint for downstream tasks using Dtune and does not
151
      know the distribution/proportion of backdoor data in the attacker-supplied Dtune .
152
153   Notations. The finetuning set Dtune may contain an unknown proportion of backdoor samples; this
154   proportion (i.e., poisoning rate) is denoted by α. The defender unlearns data in the unlearn set Dul ,
155   whose size as a fraction of Dtune is defined as α̂ = |Dul | ÷ |Dtune |. The two sub-networks in the
156
      ensemble are the poisoned module and robust module, denoted by fp and fr , respectively. Data
      points (x, y) ∈ Dtune , which consist of unaugmented (xnoAug ) and augmented (xyesAug ) views of the
157
      original image (as in “data augmentation”), and potentially poisoned data points (xp̂ , yp̂ ) ∈ Dul
158
      are used to finetune and defend fr , respectively. For simplicity, we use x to denote images when
159
      data augmentation is not relevant. The logits produced by the two modules are referred to as
160   ŷp = fp (x ; θp ) and ŷr = fr (x ; θr ), where θp , θr are the potentially tunable parameters of fp and
161   fr , respectively. The two logits vectors ŷp and ŷr combine to form the logits vector ŷ based on mθp

170   Overview. Figure 1 summarises our method, which has two stages. During stage 1, the poisoned
171   module fp (· ; θp ) is pre-finetuned using finetuning data Dtune . During stage 2, fp is used to determine
172   whether incoming data is learned by the robust module fr (· ; θr ) or added to Dul for asynchronous
173
      unlearning based on the unlearn rate lrul in Equation 3.
174   Stage 1: Isolating backdoored data by pre-finetuning the poisoned module fp . This step applies
175   vanilla tuning (for hyperparameters see Table 11) using Dtune on fp (· ; θp ), where θp is initialised as
176   the first few layers of a pretrained checkpoint. Stage 1 solves the following optimisation problem:
177   minθp ExnoAug ∼Dtune [ℓ(fp (xnoAug ; θp ), y)], where ℓ(·, ·) is the cross entropy loss, y is the one-hot
178   ground truth vector, and fp is the poisoned module. As explained in Section 1, the intuition of
179
      overfitting fp on poisoned data is based on shortcut learning (Geirhos et al., 2020). These shortcuts
      are found in backdoored images, where the attacker-specified trigger acts as an easily identifiable
180
      artifact that causes fp to easily learn the connection between the trigger and attacker-specified label.
181
      Therefore, the tuned fp is likely to be confident when predicting images with the trigger. The poisoned
182   module fp is designed to be complex enough to learn shortcuts and shallow enough to avoid learning
183   much from benign data. The goal is for max(σ(fp (xnoAug ; θp ))) to be small for clean images and
184   large for backdoor images after stage 1. Note that the poisoned module is replaceable by other
185   methods (Doan et al., 2023; Li et al., 2021b; Gao et al., 2023a) that isolate backdoored data and fp is
186   not absolutely necessary for interleaved unlearning.
187
      Stage 2: Apply Interleaved Unlearning on the robust module fr . This stage optimises the objective
188   in Equation 2, minimising the loss on clean data and maximising the loss on poinsoned data; θr is
189   initialised as a pretrained checkpoint. During this stage, θp is frozen and only θr is tuned. The logits
190   ŷp = fp (xnoAug ; θp ) for the unaugmented view of each image is produced by fp in order to compute
191   maximum class probability max(σ(ŷp )), which is then compared to cthresh to determine whether the
192   data point should be learned by fr or added onto Dul . If max(σ(ŷp )) is above cthresh , the data point is
193   added to Dul . Otherwise, fr learns the augmented views as in regular finetuning. To prevent poisoned
194   data from being learned, we apply logit masking on the output logits of fr and fp (Equation 1)

521   6   C ONCLUSION
522
523   This work presents a novel and highly effective method for finetuning benign ViTs on backdoored
524   datasets called Interleaved Ensemble Unlearning (IEU). We use a small and shallow ViT (the poisoned
525   module) to distinguish between clean and backdoored images and show that alternating between
526
      learning clean data and unlearning poisoned data during defence is an effective way of preserving
      high clean accuracy whilst foiling the backdoor attack. We demonstrate that our defence is effective
527
      for complicated real-world datasets and discuss ways to make IEU more robust.
528
529   Impact. This paper’s impact goes beyond developing a backdoor defense that works particularly
530   well on ViTs. We believe that the Interleaved Unlearning framework, which extends ABL (Li et al.,
531   2021b) and Denoised PoE (Liu et al., 2024), can be used to tune benign models with a great variety
532
      of different model architectures. In addition, we encourage future work to consider and remedy the
      weaknesses we point out in Section 5 for unlearning-based backdoor defences.

       D    P OTENTIAL A DAPTIVE ATTACK

1059
       This section investigates how IEU performs when faced with a backdoor attack that is designed
       to bypass our IEU. This setting is more challenging for defenders, since the attacker can take
1060
       countermeasures that are specifically designed to evade IEU. Our defense mechanism uses the
1061
       poisoned module to filter out highly confident data which are then classified as backdoored data. A
1062   potential attack would be one that makes the backdoor trigger more hidden to induce the stage 1
1063   poisoned module to make incorrect classifications.
1064
1065
       A previous work (Mo et al., 2024) designed such an attack, named “Channel Activation Attack”
       (abbreviated CAT), whose aim is to produce adversarial perturbations to encourage the channel
1066
       activation patterns of benign and backdoored images to appear more similar. This serves as an
1067
       adaptive attack against our defence. We use a gray-box setting, where the attacker has full access
1068   to a version of the poisoned module that the defender has tuned using the same exact setting as the
1069   defender would use during normal application of IEU. With a tuned version of fp , the attacker adds
1070   adversarial perturbations onto existing backdoor attacks to evade detection. Using the adversarially
1071   perturbed backdoor data, we apply our IEU framework to defend the robust module. We apply the
1072   CAT attack on five different standard backdoor attacks. The results are shown in Table 18, which
1073   demonstrates that our method successfully defends the CAT attack.
1074   We use a very similar attack setup as attack’s original authors in Mo et al. (2024) (γ = 0.6 for the loss
1075   function in their Equation 3; 10 iterations and an ℓ2 -norm budget of ϵ = 16 ÷ 255 for the Projected
1076   Gradient Descent attack). However, we did not apply random masking to the perturbations. Please
1077   refer to Figure 5 to visualise the original backdoored image, the CAT-perturbed backdoor image, and
1078   the difference between the two.
