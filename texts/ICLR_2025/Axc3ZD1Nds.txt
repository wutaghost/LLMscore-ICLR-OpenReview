010                                                A BSTRACT
011
012            The widely used expected utility theory has been shown to be empirically incon-
013            sistent with human preferences in the psychology and behavioral economy liter-
014            atures. Cumulative Prospect Theory (CPT) has been developed to fill in this gap
015            and provide a better model for human-based decision-making supported by em-
016
               pirical evidence. It allows to express a wide range of attitudes and perceptions
               towards risk, gains and losses. A few years ago, CPT has been combined with
017
               Reinforcement Learning (RL) to formulate a CPT policy optimization problem
018
               where the goal of the agent is to search for a policy generating long-term returns
019            which are aligned with their preferences. In this work, we revisit this policy op-
020            timization problem and provide new insights on optimal policies and their nature
021            depending on the utility function under consideration. We further derive a novel
022            policy gradient theorem for the CPT policy optimization objective generalizing
023            the seminal corresponding result in standard RL. This result enables us to design
024            a model-free policy gradient algorithm to solve the CPT-RL problem. We illustrate
025            the performance of our algorithm in simple examples motivated by traffic control
026            and electricity management applications. We also demonstrate that our policy gra-
027
               dient algorithm scales better to larger state spaces compared to the existing zeroth
               order algorithm for solving the same problem.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In classical reinforcement learning (RL), rational agents make decisions to maximize their expected
033   cumulative rewards through interaction with their environment. This paradigm has largely been
034   prescribed by the expected utility theory model which has dominated decision making. Besides this
035   risk-neutral setting, risk-seeking and risk-averse behaviors can also be individually modelled within
036   the same expected utility maximization paradigm by considering the expectation of a modified utility
037   function as a policy optimization objective (see e.g. Prashanth et al. (2022) for a recent survey).
038   However, human decision makers might not act rationally due to psychological biases and personal
039   preferences, their decisions might not necessarily be dictated by expected utility theory. Consider
040   this simple example as a first illustration: A player must choose between (A) receiving a payoff of 80
041   and (B) participating in a lottery and receive either 0 or 200 with equal probability. The player’s pref-
042   erence depends on their attitude towards risk. While a risk-neutral agent will be satisfied with the
043   immediate and safe payoff of 80, another individual might want to try to obtain the much higher 200
044   payoff. In particular, different agents might perceive the same utility and the same random outcome
045
      differently. Furthermore, they can exhibit both risk-seeking and risk-averse behaviors depending on
      the context. Therefore, due to its failure to capture such settings as a descriptive model, the stan-
046
      dard expected utility theory has been called into question by the pioneering behavioral psychologist
047
      Daniel Kahneman together with his colleague Amos Tversky (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979). In
048   particular, Daniel Kahneman has been awarded the Nobel Prize in Economic Sciences in 2002 ”for
049   having integrated insights from psychological research into economic science, especially concern-
050   ing human judgment and decision-making under uncertainty”. In their seminal works combining
051   cognitive psychology and economics, they laid the foundations of the so-called prospect theory and
052   its cumulative version later on (Tversky & Kahneman, 1992) to explain several empirical obser-
053   vations invalidating the standard expected utility theory. Let us illustrate this in a simple example
      borrowed from Ramasubramanian et al. (2021) (example 2 in section IV therein) for the purpose of


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      our exposition. Consider a game where one can either earn $100 with probability (w.p.) 1 or earn
055   10000 w.p. 0.01 and nothing otherwise. A human might rather lean towards the first option which
056   gives a certain gain. In contrast, if the situation is flipped, i.e., a loss of 100 w.p. 1 versus a loss of
057   $10000 w.p. 0.01, then humans might rather choose the latter option. In both settings, the expected
058   gain or loss has the same value (100). The CPT paradigm allows to model the tendency of humans
059   to perceive gains and losses differently. Moreover, the humans tend to deflate high probabilities and
060   inflate low probabilities (Tversky & Kahneman, 1992; Barberis, 2013). For instance, as exposed in
061   L.A. et al. (2016), humans might rather choose a large reward, say 1 million dollars w.p. 10−6 over
062   a reward of 1 w.p. 1 and the opposite when rewards are replaced by losses.
063   Inspired by Kahneman and Tversky’s findings, CPT has been used in a number of applications in
064   the stateless setting such as energy retrofit decision for home renovations (Ebrahimigharehbaghi
065   et al., 2022) and smart home energy management Dorahaki et al. (2022), building evacuation (Gao
066   et al., 2023), shared parking services (Yan et al., 2020) and financial decision making (Ladrón de
067   Guevara Cortés et al., 2023; Luxenberg et al., 2024) to name a few. We refer the reader to appendix C
068   for an extended discussion regarding applications. Recently, a line of research initiated by L.A. et al.
069
      (2016) has combined CPT with RL to better account for the human behavior in decision making
      (Borkar & Chandak, 2021; Ramasubramanian et al., 2021; Danis et al., 2023; Ethayarajh et al.,
070
      2024). As highlighted in Borkar & Chandak (2021), this is particularly important in applications
071
      directly involving humans in the loop such as e-commerce, crowdsourcing and recommendation
072   to name a few. As empirically demonstrated and discussed in Tversky & Kahneman (1992), CPT
073   allows to capture two specific features of human decision making: Humans tend to (a) be risk-
074   seeking with potential losses and risk-averse with possible gains, this is modelled via using an S-
075   shaped non-linear transformation of the utility function; (b) overestimate the probability of rare
076   events and underestimate the probability of frequent events. CPT uses for this a weighting function
077   to distort the cumulative probability distribution function, inflating low probabilities and deflating
078   high probabilities. In this work, we focus on the policy optimization problem where the objective
079   is the CPT value of the cumulative sum of rewards, induced by a parametrized policy in a Markov
080
      Decision Process. Our main contributions are as follows:
081   About optimal policies in CPT-RL. We provide theoretical insights about the nature of an optimal
082   policy for CPT policy optimization. Unlike in standard MDPs, an optimal policy is stochastic and
083   non-Markovian in general. When we set the probability distortion function to identity, we show
084   that the policy search set can be significantly reduced to a much smaller policy class when solv-
085   ing (CPT-PO). In this same setting, we also characterize a family of utility functions (affine and
086
      exponential utility functions) for which the CPT value objective can be maximized with a Marko-
      vian policy. However, we prove that this characterization does not hold anymore when considering
087
      nontrivial probability distortion and (nonlinear) utilities together in (CPT-PO).
088
089   Policy gradient theorem and algorithm for CPT-RL. We establish a policy gradient theorem pro-
090   viding a closed form expectation expression for the gradient of our CPT-value objective w.r.t. the
091   policy parameter under suitable regularity conditions on the utility and probability distortion func-
092
      tions. This result generalizes the standard policy gradient theorem in RL. Building on this theorem,
      we design a policy gradient algorithm to solve the CPT policy optimization problem. The stochas-
093
      tic policy gradient we use involves a challenging integral term to be computed and we propose a
094
      tailored estimation procedure to approximate it.
095
096   Experiments. We perform simulations to illustrate our theoretical results on simple examples. In
097   particular, we test our PG algorithm in two CPT-RL applications: a traffic control application with
098
      finite discrete state action spaces and an electricity management task in a continuous state action
      setting. We also compare the performance of our PG algorithm to the previously proposed zeroth
099
      order algorithm to show the robustness and scalability of our algorithm to higher dimensional MDPs.

      initial state probability distribution, and γ ∈ (0, 1) is the discount factor. A randomized stationary

143   Cumulative Prospect Theory Value. Instead of the expected return, CPT prescribes to consider the
144   CPT value which will be defined in this paragraph. As previously mentioned, CPT relies on three
145   distinct elements which we further detail in the following:
146
      (a) A reference point. The human agent has a reference attainable reward value in comparison to
147
      which they evaluate their possible reward outcomes. Rewards larger than the reference are perceived
148   as gains whereas lower values are viewed as losses.
149
150   (b) A utility function U : R → R+ . The agent’s utility is a continuous and non-decreasing function
151
      which is not necessarily linear w.r.t. the total reward received by the agent. We consider the func-
      tion u+ : R → R+ describing the gains and defined for every x ∈ R by u+ (x) = U(x) if x ≥ x0
152
      and zero otherwise. Similarly, the function u− : R → R+ which encodes the losses is defined by
153
      u− (x) = −U(x) if x ≤ x0 and zero otherwise. Here, x0 denotes the reference point. Typically,
154   the utility function is concave (respectively convex) for positive (resp. negative) rewards w.r.t. the
155   reference point, i.e. u+ is concave on R+ and −u− is convex on R− . For concreteness, we will use
156   Kahneman & Tversky (1979)’s utility function as a running example: U(x) = (x − x0 )α if x ≥ x0
157   and U(x) = −λ(x−x0 )α if x < x0 , where λ = 2.25, α = 0.88 are recommended hyperparameters.
158   See Fig. 6 for an illustration with x0 = 0 .
159

      Example: Personalized Treatment for Pain Management. We illustrate our problem formulation
      with a concrete example in healthcare to give the reader more intuition about the different features
200
      of CPT-RL, its importance in applications when human perception and behavior matter and its dif-
201
      ferences compared to risk-sensitive RL. The goal is to help a physician manage a patient’s chronic
202   pain by suggesting a personalized treatment plan over time. The challenge here is to balance pain
203   relief and the risk of opioid dependency or other side effects that might be due to the treatment, i.e.
204   short-term relief and longer term risks. We propose to train a CPT-RL agent to help the physician.
205
206   1. Why sequential decision making? (a) The physician needs to adjust treatment at each time step
207      depending on the patient’s reported pain level as well as the observed side effects. Note here that
208      this is relevant to dynamic treatment regimes in general (such as for chronic diseases, see e.g. Yu
209
         et al. (2021) for a survey) in which considering delayed effects of treatments is also important
         (and RL does account for such effects). (b) Decisions clearly impact the patient’s immediate pain
210
         relief, dependency risks in the future and their overall health condition.
211
212   2. Why CPT-RL? Patients and clinicians make decisions influenced by psychological biases. We
213
         illustrate the importance of each one of the three features of CPT as introduced in our paper in
         section 2 (reference point, utility and probability distortion weight functions) via this example:
214
215       (a) Reference points: Patients assess and report pain levels according to their subjective (psy-
              chologically biased) baseline. Incorporating reference point dependence leads to a more


                                                          4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
              realistic model of human decision-making taking into account perceived gains and losses.
217           In our example, reducing pain from a level of 7 to 5 is not perceived the same way if the
218           reference point of the patient is 3 of it is 5. In contrast, risk-sensitive RL treats every pain
219           reduction as a uniform gain, regardless of the patient’s starting reference pain level.
220       (b) Utility transformation: Patients might often show a loss averse behavior, i.e., they might
221           perceive pain increase or withdrawal symptoms as worse than equivalent gains in pain re-
222           lief. Note here that loss aversion should not be confused with risk aversion. In short, loss
223           aversion can be defined as a cognitive bias in which the emotional impact of a loss is more
224           intense than the satisfaction derived from an equivalent gain. For instance, in our example,
225           a 2-point increase in pain might be seen as much worse than a 2-point reduction even if
226           the change is the same in absolute value. This loss aversion concept is a cornerstone of
227           Kahneman and Tversky’s theory. In contrast, risk aversion rather refers to the rational be-
228
              havior of undervaluing an uncertain outcome compared to its expected value. Risk sensitive
              approaches might be less adaptive to a patient’s subjective preferences if they deviate from
229
              objective risk assessments.
230
231
          (c) Probability weighting: Low probability events such as severe side effects (e.g., opioid over-
              dose or dependency) might be overweighted or underweighted based on the patient’s psy-
232
              chology.

234   Challenges. To conclude this section, we describe the challenges we face in solving CPT-PO. First,
235   the CPT value does not satisfy a Bellman equation due to the nonlinearity of the utility and weight
236   functions which breaks the additivity and linearity of the standard expected return. Second, CPT-PO
237   is a nonconvex problem involving several nonconvex functions: The utility itself is nonconvex in
238   general (recall the utility is convex w.r.t. gains and concave w.r.t. losses) and the probabilities are
239   also distorted by a nonconvex weight function. While the standard policy optimization problem is
240   already nonconvex in the policy, CPT-PO further introduces additional nonconvexity.

404   to maximize the CPT value of any real-valued random variable. However, we highlight that (a)
405   this cumulative reward return structure is natural and ubiquitous in RL and economics applications

531
      We investigated a CPT variant of the standard RL problem to model human decision making. We
      provided new insights on optimal policies in such problems to highlight their peculiarity compared to
532
      classical RL. Then, we designed a novel PG algorithm for CPT-PO. Finally, we showed the benefits
533
      of our algorithm in terms of scalability compared to prior work and we illustrated its performance
534   in applications including electricity management and traffic control. Our work opens the way to
535   interesting avenues for future work. Using CPT usually requires to know the utility and distortion
536   functions (or to posit models thereof) a priori. Can we learn such functions to align with the pref-
537   erences of the human decision maker involved? Looking forward, investigating incentive design
538   problems in which human agents are collectively modelled using CPT would be interesting. We
539   hope our work will stimulate further research in better capturing the behavior and preferences of
      human agents in real-world decision making applications beyond expected utility.

801   Risk-sensitive RL. There is a rich literature around risk sensitive control and RL that we do not hope
802   to give justice to here. We refer the reader to recent comprehensive surveys on the topic (Garcıa &
803   Fernández, 2015; Prashanth et al., 2022) and the references therein. Let us briefly mention that
804   there exist several approaches to risk sensitive RL. These include formulations such as constrained
805
      stochastic optimization to control the tolerance to perturbations and stochastic minmax optimization
      to model robustness with respect to worst case perturbations for instance. Another approach which
806
      is more relevant to our paper discussion consists in regularizing or modifying objective functions.
807
808      2
           By ‘non-Markovian’, we mean ‘non necessarily Markovian’ policies including Markovian ones. Elements
809   of ΠN M − ΠM,N S can be designated as ‘stricly non-Markovian’ policies. Likewise, by ‘non stationary’, we
      mean ‘non necessarily stationary’, and by ‘stochastic’ we mean ‘non necessarily deterministic’.

      Such modifications are based on considering different statistics of the return deviating from the
811   standard expectation such as the variance or the conditional value at risk (e.g. Tamar et al. (2012);
812   Chow & Ghavamzadeh (2014); Chow et al. (2018)) or even considering the entire distribution of
813   the returns like distributional RL (Bellemare et al., 2023). Another popular objective modification
814   consists in maximizing an exponential criterion (e.g. Borkar (2002); Noorani et al. (2022)) to obtain
815   robust policies w.r.t noise and perturbations of system parameters or variations in the environment.
816   Noorani et al. (2022) designed a model-free REINFORCE algorithm and an actor-critic variant of the
817   algorithm leveraging an (approximate) multiplicative Bellman equation induced by the exponential
818   objective criterion. Moharrami et al. (2024) recently proposed and analyzed similar PG algorithms
819
      for the same exponential objective. Vijayan & LA (2023) introduced a PG algorithm for solving
      risk-sensitive RL for a class of smooth risk measures including some distortion risk measures and
820
      a mean-variance risk measure. Their approach is based on simultaneous perturbation stochastic
821
      approximation (SPSA) (Bhatnagar et al., 2013) using zeroth-order information to estimate gradients.
822   Our CPT-PO problem covers several of the aforementioned objectives including smooth distortion
823   risk measures and exponential utility as particular cases (see appendix E for more details).
824
825
      Convex RL/RL with General Utilities. In the last few years, convex RL (a.k.a. RL with general
      utilities) (Hazan et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2020; Zahavy et al., 2021; Geist et al., 2022) has emerged
826
      as a framework to unify several problems of interest such as pure exploration, imitation learning or
827
      experiment design. More precisely, this line of research is concerned with maximizing a given func-
828   tional of the state(-action) occupancy measure w.r.t. a policy. To solve this problem, several policy
829   gradient algorithms have been proposed in the literature (Zhang et al., 2021; Barakat et al., 2023).
830   Mutti et al. (2022b;a; 2023a) challenged the initial problem formulation and proposed a finite trial
831   version of the problem which is closer to practical concerns as it consists in maximizing a functional
832   of the empirical state(-action) distribution rather than its true asymptotic counterpart. The particular
833   case of our CPT policy optimization problem without probability distortion (see (EUT-PO) below)
834   coincides with a particular case of the single trial convex RL problem (Mutti et al., 2023b) in which
835   the function of the empirical visitation measure is a linear functional of the reward function (see
836
      appendix E.4 for details). However, our general problem is not a particular case of convex RL which
      does not account for probability distortions. Furthermore, our utility function is in general non-
837
      convex in our setting (see example in Fig 6) and our policy gradient algorithm is model-free. More
838
      recently, De Santi et al. (2024) introduced a global RL problem formulation where rewards are glob-
839   ally defined over trajectories instead of locally over states and used submodular optimization tools
840   to solve the resulting non-additive policy optimization problem. While global RL allows to account
841   for trajectory-level global rewards, it does not take into consideration probability distortions. In
842   addition, their investigation is restricted to the setting where the transition model is known whereas
843   our PG algorithm is model-free.
844
      Cumulative Prospect Theoretic RL. Motivated by Prospect Theory and its sibling CPT (Kahne-
845   man & Tversky, 1979; Tversky & Kahneman, 1992; Barberis, 2013), L.A. et al. (2016) first pro-
846   posed to combine CPT with RL to obtain a better model for human decision making. Following
847   this first research effort, only few isolated works (Borkar & Chandak, 2021; Ramasubramanian
848   et al., 2021; Ethayarajh et al., 2024) considered a similar CPT-RL setting. In particular, Borkar &
849   Chandak (2021) proposed and analyzed a Q-learning algorithm for CPT policy optimization. Ra-
850   masubramanian et al. (2021) further developed value-based algorithms for CPT-RL by estimating
851   the CPT value of an action in a given state via dynamic programming. More precisely, they were
852   concerned with maximizing a sum of CPT value period costs which is amenable to dynamic pro-
853
      gramming. In contrast to their accumulated CPT-based cost (see their remark 1), our CPT policy
      optimization problem formulation is different: we maximize the CPT value of the return of a policy
854
      (see (CPT-PO)). In particular, this objective does not enjoy an additive structure and hence does
855
      not satisfy a Bellman equation. Moreover, their work relying on value-based methods is restricted
856   to finite discrete state action spaces. Our PG algorithm is also suitable for continuous state action
857   settings as we demonstrate in our experiments. More recently, Ethayarajh et al. (2024) incorporated
858   CPT (without probability distortion) into RL from human feedback for fine-tuning large language
859   models. CPT has also been recently exploited for multi-agent RL (Danis et al., 2023). Our work is
860   complementary to this line of research, especially to L.A. et al. (2016) and its extended version Jie
861   et al. (2018) which are the most closely related work to ours. While their algorithm design makes
862   use of simultaneous perturbation stochastic approximation (SPSA) (Spall, 1992) using only zeroth
863   order information, we rather propose a PG algorithm exploiting first-order information thanks to our

      special problem structure involving the CPT value of a cumulative sum of rewards. See section 4
865   for further details regarding this comparison.

      In this section, we compare the CPT-RL and trajectory-based reward RL (using a single reward for
      the entire trajectory, such as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) seen as preference
923
      learning paradigms. In particular, we also discuss the pros and cons of each one of them.
924
925   Regarding the structure of the final reward and the metric learning you mention, this is a fair point
926   and we agree that Our present work requires so far access to utility and weight functions whereas
927   trajectory-based reward RL learns the metric to be optimized using human preference data. How-
928
      ever, let us mention a few points:
929
      (a) These can be readily available in specific applications (for risk modeling or even chosen at will
930
          by the users themselves);
931
932   (b) CPT relies on a predefined model, this can be beneficial in applications such as portfolio opti-
933       mization or medical treatment where trade-offs have to be made and models might be readily
934
          available;
935   (c) Furthermore, we argue that having such a model allows it to be more explainable compared to
936       a model entirely relying on human feedback and fine tuning, let alone the discussion about the
937       cost of collecting human feedback. We also note that some of the most widely used algorithms
938       in RLHF (e.g. DPO) do rely on the fact that the reward follows a Bradley-Terry model for
939       instance (either for learning the reward or at least to derive the algorithm to bypass reward
940
          learning);
941   (d) Let us mention that one can also learn the utility and weight functions. We mentioned this
942       promising possibility in our conclusion although we did not pursue this direction in this work.
943       One can for instance represent the utility and weight functions by neural networks and train
944       models to learn them using available data with relevant losses, jointly with the policy optimiza-
945       tion task. One can also simply fit the predefined functions (say e.g. Tversky and Kahneman’s
946
          function) to the data by estimating the parameters of these functions (see η with our notations
          and exponents of the utility function in Table 1 for the CPT row). This last approach is already
947
          commonly used in practice, see e.g. Rieger et al. (2017).
948
949   CPT vs RLHF: General comparison. CPT has been particularly useful when modeling specific
950   biases in decision making under risk to account for biased probability perceptions. It allows to
951   explicitly model cognitive biases. In contrast, RLHF has been successful in training LLMs which
952   are aligned with human preferences where these are complex and potentially evolving and where
953   biases cannot be explicitly and reasonably modeled. RLHF has been rather focused on learning
954   implicit human preferences through interaction (e.g. using rankings and/or pairwise comparisons).
955   Overall, CPT can be useful for tasks where risk modeling is essential and critical whereas RLHF
956   can be useful for general preference alignment although RLHF can also be adapted to model risk if
957
      human preferences are observable and abundantly available at a reasonable cost. This might not be
      the case in healthcare applications for instance, where one can be satisfied with a tunable risk model.
958
      On the other hand, so far CPT does not have this ability to adapt to evolving preferences over time
959
      unlike RLHF which can do so via feedback.
960
961   CPT and RLHF: Pros and cons. To summarize the pros and cons of both approaches, we provide
962   the following elements. As for the pros, CPT directly models psychological human biases in deci-
963
      sion making via a structured framework which is particularly effective for risk preferences. RLHF
      can generalize to different scenarios with sufficient feedback and handle complex preferences via
964
      learning from diverse human interactions, it is particularly useful in settings where preferences are
965
      not explicitly defined such as for LLMs for aligning the systems with human preferences and val-
966   ues. As for the cons, CPT is a static framework since the utility and probability weight functions
967   are fixed, it is hence less adaptive to changing preferences. It uses a predefined model of human
968   behavior which is not directly using feedback. It also requires to estimate model parameters pre-
969   cisely, often for specific domains. As for RLHF on the other hand, the quality and the quantity of the
970   human feedback is essential and this dependence on the feedback clearly impacts performance. This
971   dependence can also cause undesirable bias amplification which is present in the human feedback.
      We also note that training such models is computationally expensive in large scale applications.


                                                        18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       CPT and RLHF are not mutually exclusive. While CPT and trajectory-based RL (say e.g. RLHF)
973    both offer frameworks for incorporating human preferences into decision making, we would like to
974    highlight that CPT and RLHF are not mutually exclusive. We can for instance use CPT to design an
975    initial reward structure reflecting human biases, then refine it with RLHF. We can also consider to
976    further relax the requirement of sum of rewards (which already has several applications on its own)
977    and think about incorporating CPT features to RLHF. Some recent efforts in the literature in this di-
978    rection that we mentioned in our paper include the work of Ethayarajh et al. (2024) which combines
979    prospect theory with RLHF (without probability weight distortion though, which limits its power).
980    Note that the ideas of utility transformation and probability weighting are not crucially dependent
981
       on the sum of rewards structure and can also be applied to trajectory-based rewards or trajectory
       frequencies for instance. We believe this direction deserves further research, one interesting point
982
       would be how to incorporate risk awareness from human behavior to such RLHF models using ideas
983
       from CPT.

1912   • State space: We consider a seven dimensional continuous state space. Features are constructed
1913     from the raw stock market data as previously explained. State transitions are described using the
1914     provided time series. See the publicly available code of the environment for more details.
1915   • Action space: We consider three classical types of positions the trader can take in a financial
1916     market: SHORT, OUT and LONG. These positions constitute the set of actions. These actions
1917     refer to whether the trader expects the price of an asset to rise or fall and how they are positioned
1918     to profit from that fluctuation. Extending this setting to a setting with a larger set of positions is
1919     straightforward as the environment implementation also supports more complex positions.
1920   • Rewards: The rewards we consider are given by the log values of the ratio of the portfolio valu-
1921     ations at times t and t − 1 . Borrowing interest rates and trading fees are also considered in the
1922     computation. The reward function can also be easily modified in the environment thanks to the im-
1923     plementation of the Gym Trading Environment which builds on the standard Gym environments.
1924   Remark 18. One can easily build their own environment by downloading their own dataframe for
1925   any historical stock market data and performing their desired preprocessing as for the features they
1926   would like to consider to build their states.

1944
       • Influence of the reference point: It can be seen that the reference point shifts the values of the
1945     achieved CPT returns: The smaller the reference point, the larger are the returns. This is because
1946     only values larger than the reference point are perceived as positive returns given the definition of
1947     the KT utility. This illustrates how the subjective perception of the agent of the returns is taken
1948     into account by the model.
1949   • Different return trajectories for different risk averse functions: Different values of β lead to dif-
1950     ferent trajectories overall which can translate to different levels of risk aversion. In particular, the
1951     curves do not match the identity utility case in the first episodes and show more or less risk taken
1952     towards optimizing the CPT returns.
1953   • Influence of the parameter α in KT’s utility (Fig. 21): Observe that the exponent α in the utility
1954     distorts the function and shifts the returns significantly. Lower values of α lead to higher returns
1955     in this setting where the returns (as per the ratio definition of the reward) are smaller than 1.
1956     This parameter α provides a degree of freedom to model the behavior of the agent as per their
1957     perception of the returns. Different values of α modify the curvature of the utility function (w.r.t.
1958     the reference point which is x0 = 0 here) which is concave for gains and convex for losses.
