009                                                      A BSTRACT
010
011                To reduce the computational and memory costs of Large Language Models
012                (LLMs), schemes that introduce dynamic training are increasingly emerging. Ex-
013                amples of dynamic models are: a) Mixture of Experts (MoEs) at which token
014                routing affects the compute balance, b) gradual pruning of the parameters of a
015                model, c) dynamically freezing layers, d) dynamic sparse attention schemes, e)
016                early exit of tokens as they pass through the model layers, and f) Mixture of
017
                   Depths (MoDs) schemes where tokens bypass blocks. One side effect that lim-
                   its the practical value of dynamic models is the introduction of workload im-
018
                   balance among workers, which in turn negatively affects the efficiency in dis-
019
                   tributed training. We propose a dynamic load balancing solution (DYN M O), with
020                a proof that it satisfies maximum reduction in imbalance, to adaptively maintain
021                equal compute workloads among different workers in pipeline parallelism. In
022                addition, DYN M O dynamically packs work into fewer workers, while sustaining
023                training throughput, to release the idle workers back to the job manager. DYN M O
024                supports both single nodes with multi-GPUs and systems with multi-GPU multi-
025                nodes. In comparison to static distributed training solutions (Megatron-LM and
026                DeepSpeed), DYN M O accelerates the end-to-end training of dynamic GPT models
027                by up to 1.23x (MoEs), 3.18x (parameter pruning), 2.23x (layer freezing), 4.02x
028
                   (sparse attention), 4.52x (early exit), and 1.17x (MoDs). DYN M O is available at
                   https://anonymous.4open.science/r/DynMo-4D04/.

      Sizes of neural networks used to train LLMs has exponentially grown since the introdution of trans-
034   formers Vaswani et al. (2017). This growth demands more memory and compute power. Yet, nei-
035   ther the memory capacity nor the compute capability of a single accelerator increases at the same
036   rate Sevilla et al. (2022). As a result, high-performance computing centers and cloud providers use a
037   mix of model and data parallelism for training large models Narayanan et al. (2021). One of the most
038   commonly used forms of model parallelism in LLMs is pipeline parallelism, in which consecutive
039   layers are grouped into stages, with each stage assigned to one accelerator (worker) Kahira et al.
040   (2021). Input mini-batches are split into micro batches (chunks) to improve accelerator utilization
041   by overlapping computation in a pipeline fashion Huang et al. (2019); Harlap et al. (2018); Fan et al.
042
      (2021); Li & Hoefler (2021); Qi et al. (2024).
043   In traditional LLMs training schemes, the workload for each pipeline stage is known in advance and
044   remains static throughout the training. To reduce computational resource requirements, new training
045   schemes that introduce dynamic training workloads are emerging. This includes: a) neural networks
046   where different input samples take different pathways through the model layers 1 , e.g. gated neural
047   networks Shazeer et al. (2017a), sparsely activated Mixture of Experts (MoEs) Zhou et al. (2022b),
048
      Switch Transformers Fedus et al. (2022), Mixture of Depths (MoDs) Raposo et al. (2024) etc., b)
      gradual pruning where the parameters of a model are pruned (i.e. sparsified) during training Gale
049
      et al. (2019), c) freeze training where some of the layers of the model are adapatively frozen during
050
      training Wang et al. (2022), d) different schemes to dynamically sparsify the attention matrix Liu
051   et al. (2022a); Pagliardini et al. (2023); Tay et al. (2020), and e) early exit strategies where tokens
052   skip remaining layers based on an exit decision Elbayad et al. (2020); Schuster et al. (2022); Liu et al.

087   that motivate the use of different forms of dynamic models to improve certain model attributes, such
088   as explainability and generalization. We refer the reader to the surveys Han et al. (2021); Tay et al.
089   (2022) on different forms of dynamic models.
090   One of the main downsides of using dynamic models is that they introduce load imbalance in
091   pipeline parallelism, effectively decreasing the throughput of LLM training Zhou et al. (2022a);
092   He et al. (2022). For example, Figure 1 shows the average idleness of GPUs for GPT language
093   models with different numbers of layers, for different types of dynamic models. Load imbalance
094   manifests itself as bubbles that appear in the pipeline due to a stalling accelerator waiting to receive
095   work from its late neighboring worker(s). Since a pipeline is only as fast as its slowest stage, load
096   balancing becomes crucial for efficient resource utilization.
097   Production distributed training solutions typically implement a static load balance at the beginning
098   of training and maintain the same load distribution throughout the training. For instance, Megatron-
099   LM Shoeybi et al. (2019) evenly distributes all transformer layers across the accelerators. Deep-
100   Speed Smith (2023) currently offers three partitioning methods for distributing model layers: Uni-
101   form, which balances the number of layers; param, which balances the number of parameters in each
102   stage; and regex, which balances layers whose names match a given regex pattern. However, this
103   approach operates on the assumption that the accelerators’ workloads remain roughly unchanged
104
      throughout training. As a result, it fails to address the pipeline stalls introduced by dynamic models,
      ultimately leading to a decrease in computational efficiency.
105
106   Considering the increasing importance of efficient MoEs/MoDs, sparse dynamic models, layer freez-
107   ing, and other dynamic training workloads, this work aims to mitigate the pipeline stalls introduced
      by dynamic models. We introduce DYN M O, an elastic load-balancing framework designed for

      dynamic models, to ensure balanced pipeline stages during training. DYN M O dynamically re-
109   distributes the workload among accelerators whenever an imbalance arises during training, con-
110   sequently enhancing computational efficiency and leading to cost savings. DYN M O incorporates
111   two different dynamic balancers, both proven to converge to the optimal workload balance among
112   workers. Our experiments demonstrate that DYN M O incurs negligible overhead and can scale effec-
113   tively in both: a) single-node multi-GPU environments and b) multi-node multi-GPU environments
114   typically used for training LLMs with hybrid parallelism.
115   DYN M O not only enhances performance through dynamic load balancing but also offers the ca-
116   pability to elastically adapt GPU resources. Specifically, as the total workload decreases during
117   training due to gradual pruning or early exit, the load balancer consolidates the work onto fewer
118   GPUs –subject to memory capacity constraints– while maintaining performance. GPUs that are no
119   longer needed for training can then potentially be released back to the job scheduler.
120
      DYN M O is the first work to study pipeline stalls caused by training dynamic models. Innovative
121   designs of dynamic models struggle to deliver practical impact, at large scale training, unless there is
122   a platform from which those models can be made efficient. DYN M O provides the essential platform
123   for achieving efficiency in these models. Additionally, considering the substantial costs required
124   for each training run of GPT-class models Li (2022); Heim (2022); Morgan (2022), improving the
125   efficiency of dynamic models can result in significant cost savings.
126
      Finally, we emphasize that DYN M O has no impact on model accuracy, as its role is solely to
127   redistribute workload, without interfering with the learning process or changing the learning
128   regime in any way, i.e., DYN M O functions as a complementary system software solution that op-
129   erates independently of the underlying strategies used for parameters pruning, early exist strategy,
130   layer freezing, experts routing etc. This makes DYN M O extendable and compatible with various
131   dynamic schemes. In principle, it can even be applied to models that undergo dynamic changes for
132   reasons other than the six example cases we list in this paper, such as manufacturing variability of
133   computing units Sinha et al. (2022). In short, our contributions are:
134
135   • We introduce DYN M O, which enables researchers to explore dynamic models and significantly
136     improves the end-to-end training efficiency of such models, making their practical application
137     more feasible. We invoke DYN M O to rebalance at regular intervals without prior knowledge of
138     dynamism, hence balancing the load in a fully automated and transparent manner. DYN M O is or-
139     thogonal to the underlying pipeline parallelism and dynamism scheme; it allows for compatibility
140
        with various dynamic compute and model reduction schemes.
141
      • We propose two load balancing algorithms proven to converge to optimal balancing in order to
142
        alleviate the negative effects of dynamic models on pipeline utilization. We further introduce a
143     scheme for reducing the number of GPUs used during training by re-packing work to fewer GPUs.
144
145   • We show the benefits of the framework with six different example cases of dynamic models, in
146     both single-node and multi-node settings. DYN M O achieves 1.23x (MoEs), 3.18x (parameter
147     pruning), 2.23x (layer freezing), 4.02x (sparse attention), 4.52x (early exit), and 1.17x (MoDs)
148     speedups over (static) Megatron-LM on multi-node hybrid data and pipeline parallelism with up
149     to 720 H100 GPUs. We demonstrate that the re-packing strategy can be effective in reducing the
150     number of GPUs by up to half while sustaining comparable performance.

155   2.1   B UBBLES IN P IPELINE PARALLELISM
156
157   There are two types of bubbles in pipeline parallelism: (i) inherent bubbles of the pipeline schedule
158   (e.g. bubbles in-between forward and backward passes in GPipe Huang et al. (2019)), and (ii)
159   bubbles introduced by the dynamic models during training (e.g. bubbles introduced by sparsification
160   during training). We aim to reduce the latter type of bubbles by carefully redistributing the layers
161   among stages to minimize the workload imbalance in the pipeline. Appendix B elaborates with
      analysis of bubbles in pipeline parallelism.

220   In practical scenarios, even for large batch sizes, this imbalance can be significant. For example,
221   empirical results from the Mixtral 8x7b model Jiang et al. (2024) demonstrate up to 40% difference
222   between the busiest and least busy experts. This is consistent with the imbalance observed in token
223   choice routing reported by several others (e.g., Zhou et al. (2022b); Raposo et al. (2024). This
224   discrepancy arises because the routing function R often includes a learned component, such as
225   an auxiliary loss attached to the MLPs or a linear assignment scheme, leading to suboptimal and
226   dynamic routing choices.

228   phase, particularly during the all to all collective operation, where tokens are returned to their orig-

231   systems.

519
520   In this work, we use six example cases of dynamic models for which current (static) distributed
521   training systems are not ready to handle efficiently. Even though we show the efficiency of our load
522   balancing system for dynamic LLMs with these example cases, they can be a basis for expanding to
523   other forms of dynamic models.
524   Algorithm 1 shows the overall flow of operations of DYN M O with model dynamism. The dynamism
525   function (line 10) depends on the target case. For instance, if the target case is parameter pruning,
526   the dynamism function would apply global pruning on different worker. The algorithm takes as in-
527   put a model, the number of training iterations, the rank of the accelerator, and several arguments for
528   managing the dynamism, balancing, and packing the model’s workloads. We start the training with
529   the original model and train it until a user-specified dynamism to apply is reached. The frequency
530   varies by the target case, in layer freezing it is as frequent as every 50 iterations, while in parameter

      tuning the pruning frequency is in the range of 3000-7000 iterations. The model or control flow

532
      dynamism f req iteration, where the model (or control flow inside the model) is modified until it
533
      meets the specified stopping criteria (lines 9-16). The first iteration after each dynamism operation

577   3.3   L OAD BALANCING
578
579
      DYN M O implements two load balancing algorithms, and can be extended to support other algo-
      rithms. The first is centralized parameter-based partitioning that balances partitions based on the
580
      number of parameters. The load balancing algorithm is built on top of DeepSpeed’s load balanc-
581
      ing utility functions for partitioning in model parallelism Smith (2023). The second algorithm is
582   an iterative decentralized diffusion-based algorithm that aims to minimize the variance between the
583   workload of each rank by attempting to move layers from overloaded workers to underloaded ones
584   in an iterative way. The workload cost can be described by either the layer execution times, or the
585   parameter counts as in the centralized partitioning method.
586
      The diffusion-based load balancing algorithm achieves ideal load balancing by iteratively minimiz-
587
      ing workload imbalances using a Lyapunov-inspired approach. The potential function ϕ, defined as
588   the sum of workload gaps between workers, serves as a measure of imbalance in the system. Each
589   iteration of the algorithm reduces ϕ by redistributing tasks from overloaded to underloaded workers,
590   prioritizing layer transfers that yield the largest reductions in imbalance while satisfying memory
591   constraints. The algorithm’s probabilistic analysis guarantees that ϕ decreases towards a conver-
592   gence threshold γ, with the rate of convergence bounded by O(N 2 log(SN /γ) log N ), where N
593   is the number of workers and S is the total pipeline size. This systematic reduction ensures that
      workload imbalances, quantified by the bubble ratio, are minimized, driving the system towards

594
      an optimally balanced state. The theoretical guarantees of convergence and imbalance reduction
595   underpin the algorithm’s robustness in dynamic environments.

613
614   Workload re-packing is the process of merging the total workload into a smaller number of work-
615   ers (GPUs) with the purpose of using the available resources more efficiently, i.e. unused GPUs
616   are released when the overall amount of work in training drops. This can be achieved with simple
617   algorithms (in small scale) such as first-fit, best-fit, and round-robin as well as complex optimiza-
618
      tion heuristics. Workload re-packing aims to increase the worker utilization and reduce the overall
      number of workers employed to continue the training process. For long training schedules that are
619
      common in LLM training, workload packing can result in substantial cost savings. It may also pro-
620
      vide improved performance due to reduction in the number of cross-worker communication calls,

666   3.5   OVERHEAD OF DYN M O AND F REQUENCY OF DYNAMISM
667
668   The overhead of DYN M O is negligible. For all the results we show in this paper, for all model
669   sizes and different dynamism example cases, the percentage of overhead is a few single digits at
670
      its highest; this includes the complete overhead of DYN M O: profiling data collection, rebalancing
      algorithm, and layers migration between GPUs. The evaluation section reports the load balancing
671
      overheads.
672
673   As a basic rule, we apply the rebalancing (via DYN M O) every time the model or the control flow
674   changes. Since the overhead of DYN M O is very low, we could apply it as frequently as needed,
675   based on the requirements of the applications. For example in gradual pruning the typical frequency
676
      of dynamism (i.e. model changes requiring load rebalancing) is in the order of 1,000s of iterations.
      On the other hand, for MoEs and MoDs the rebalancing in every iteration since the imbalance
677
      is unpredictable and dependent on point the routing decision is taken, i.e., in the forward pass at
678
      each FFN. For MoEs and MoDs we rebalance in the back propagation phase where we attach the
679   movement of layers to the pipeline parallelism scheme (i.e. we migrate the layers as we propagate
680   the gradients back down the pipeline from the last layer to the first layer).

      3.6   L IMITATIONS
684
      We focus on dynamism for LLMs and currently do not support other types of DNNs. Our method
685
      cannot automatically detect imbalance in a way that is transparent to the training process, i.e., we
686   apply DYN M O at a fixed frequency regardless of whether the model has changed or not. We also
687   allow the user to set the frequency. As future work, a background light weight tracker can be used
688   to identify the dynamism pattern. Another alternative would be training our method to forecast the
689   imbalance during training.

      4.3   R E - PACKING M ODELS TO F EWER GPU S
832
833   In the re-packing experiments, the training starts with 8 GPUs per node in pipeline parallelism. After
834   a dynamism step, DYN M O attempts to re-pack the total workload into fewer GPUs while satisfying
835   the memory capacity constraints. Figure 4 reports the throughput/number of GPUs for each model
836   size where the model is packed into 6, 4, and 2 GPUs. The 8 GPU setting for each model size serves
837   as a baseline where there is no re-packing. This measurement also corresponds to the performance
838   per dollar metric as the cost is directly proportional to the number of GPUs used in training.
839   We observe that in all model scales, re-packing can allow the training to be continued with fewer
840   GPUs which may result in significant cost savings. For example, in gradual pruning we reduce the
841   GPU count from 8 to an average of 5.8 GPUs while sustaining the training throughout.

844
845   DYN M O is a load-balancing system for dynamic models where the loads of the workers change
846
      during training. DYN M O provides better load balance than state-of-the-art static load balancing
      approaches, which results in better efficiency and faster end-to-end training time. Empirical results
847
      for LLMs with six example cases of dynamic models show that DYN M O significantly improves the
848
      training throughput over the counterparts. We foresee that dynamic models will be more prominent
849   in the future and dynamic load distribution will be of utmost importance.

       C.1       N EURAL N ETWORKS P RUNING
1191
1192   There are three main considerations that need to be taken into account when applying network
1193   pruning: criterion, structure, and schedule of the pruning.
1194
       Pruning Criterion: Every pruning scheme needs to define a criterion to choose which parameters to
1195   prune. A non-exhaustive list of pruning criteria used in the literature includes: weight magnitude Li
1196   et al. (2016); Renda et al. (2020), gradient magnitude Cun et al. (1990); Mozer & Smolensky (1989),
1197   Bayesian statistics-based criteria Dai et al. (2018); Molchanov et al. (2017), and reinforcement learn-
1198   ing based criteria Lin et al. (2017); He et al. (2018). These criteria can be applied either locally (i.e.
1199   considering each layer’s weights separately) or globally (i.e. considering weights in all layers).
1200
       Pruning Structure: Parameters in a model can be removed in a structured or unstructured way.
1201   Structured sparsity Kruschke & Movellan (1991) enforces a pattern to be applied while choosing
1202   the parameters to be pruned. This can range from removing filters in a convolution layer to remov-
1203   ing attention heads in a multi-headed attention layer. On the other hand, unstructured sparsity Han
1204   et al. (2015) is not under the constraint of a pattern (i.e parameters can be freely removed), hence,
1205   offers a finer granularity. Even though unstructured sparsity offers better flexibility, structured spar-
1206   sity is more prevalent since it is difficult to implement efficient kernels for sparse data structures
1207   in unstructured sparsity and deep learning frameworks have limited support for sparse computa-
1208   tions. However, it has been shown that the enforcement of a certain structure for the pruning of
1209
       parameters can result in significant degradation in model quality compared to unstructured sparsity
       Kalchbrenner et al. (2018); Elsen et al. (2020).
1210
1211   Pruning Schedule: After choosing the criterion and the structure of the pruning, one must decide
1212   when to prune and how often to prune. The most popular schedule in the literature consists of
1213   pruning after training is over, and then fine-tune the model to recover the loss introduced by the
1214   pruning Han et al. (2015). Another effective approach is to remove a certain percentage of weights
1215
       progressively during the training until the target sparsity is reached Zhu & Gupta (2017), which
       eliminates the fine-tuning process. There are also schedules that enforce a constant rate of sparsity
1216
       throughout the training Mocanu et al. (2018).
1217
1218   For a more comprehensive analysis of various sparsification procedures which are applied in deep
1219   learning, we refer the reader to Hoefler et al. (2021).

       C.3   L OAD BALANCING
1264
       DYN M O implements two load balancing algorithms, and can be extended to support other algo-
1265
       rithms. The first one is a centralized parameter-based partitioning method that balances partitions
1266   based on the number of parameters. We also implemented a version where the same algorithm is
1267   used for balancing partitions based on the layer execution times instead of the number of parame-
1268   ters. This algorithm with two variants is built on top of DeepSpeed’s load balancing utility functions
1269   for partitioning in model parallelism Smith (2023). The second algorithm is an iterative decentral-
1270   ized diffusion-based algorithm that aims to minimize the variance between the workload of each
1271   rank by attempting to move layers from overloaded ranks to underloaded ranks in an iterative way.
1272   The workload can either be the layer execution times or the parameter counts as in the DeepSpeed-
1273   based algorithms. The number of iterations to decide on the final load distribution is a user-defined
1274
       parameter.
1275   Algorithm 3 shows the pseudo-code for the diffusion-based load balancing algorithm. At the start
1276   of the balancing process, each worker evaluates their workload. A worker is deemed overloaded if
1277   their total workload exceeds the average workload across all workers and underloaded otherwise.
1278   Overloaded workers aim to offload some tasks to underloaded workers to achieve a more balanced
1279   distribution.
1280   The balancing process unfolds iteratively. In each iteration, overloaded workers identify a task that
1281   contributes the least to their workload (e.g., a computational layer with minimal execution time).
1282   This task is then considered for transfer to the underloaded worker with the lightest workload. For
1283   every potential transfer, the algorithm computes the new workload distribution and evaluates the
1284   variance. A transfer is accepted if it reduces the variance and satisfies the memory constraints of the
1285   receiving worker. Accepted transfers are tracked, ensuring that tasks are redistributed efficiently.
1286   By iteratively reducing workload variance through these localized decisions, the algorithm ensures a
1287   progressively balanced distribution. After the balancing phase, the transfer information is distributed
1288   to all workers, who update their local task assignments accordingly. This iterative and decentralized
1289   approach allows for effective load balancing in dynamic, distributed systems.

1440   C.4   R E - PACKING DYNAMIC M ODELS TO F EWER W ORKERS
1441
1442   Workload re-packing is the process of merging the total workload into a smaller number of worker
1443
       (GPUs) with the purpose of using the available resources more efficiently, i.e. unused resources
       can be released. This can be achieved with simple algorithms (in small scale) such as first-fit, best-
1444
       fit, and round-robin as well as complex optimization problems (for large scale) such as ant colony
1445
       optimization Dorigo et al. (2006) or genetic algorithms Dasgupta et al. (2013). Workload packing
1446   aims to increase GPU utilization and reduce the overall number of GPUs employed to continue the
1447   training process. For long training schedules that are common in LLM training, workload packing
1448   can result in substantial cost savings. It may also provide improved performance due to reduction in
1449   the number of cross-GPU communication calls, and smaller pipeline bubbles.
1450
       Algorithm 4 shows a first-fit algorithm that we used for workload consolidation. We iterate over all
1451
       the available GPUs (lines 2-3) and check if the combined memory usage of the two GPUs is less
1452   than the maximum memory capacity of a single GPU, and the number of active GPUs is greater than
1453   the target number of GPUs target num gpus for packing (lines 4-5). If that is the case, we transfer
1454   all layers of the source GPU to the destination GPU (lines 7-8). Then, it updates the memory
1455   usage and the number of layers on the destination GPU accordingly. This process continues until
1456   all the available GPUs have been checked and processed. The goal of this algorithm is to reduce
1457   the number of active GPUs to the target num gpus, while also ensuring that the total memory usage
       remains within device limits.

1460   The NCCL communicator cannot be resized, and there is no support for having more than one NCCL
1461   rank per GPU in the same communicator. Solutions around that include:
1462
1463
               • The solution implemented and used in DYN M O: While the NCCL communicator can not
                 be resized, NCCL supports splitting the communicator (using the ncclCommSplit()
1464
                 function) into multiple sub-partitions, or even creating a single communicator with fewer
1465
                 ranks Nvidia. The GPUs to which the model is re-packed can be split to their own sub-
1466             communicator, while the sub-communicator of the idle GPUs can be assigned to a different
1467             concurrent communicator of the new job to which they will be assigned (concurrent com-
1468             municator are allowed in NCCL Nvidia). Since the released GPUs would never be used in
1469             the sub-communicator of the repacked GPUs, there is no risk or need to mitigate deadlocks
1470             of multiple concurrent communicators.
1471           • Since large-scale full training runs for a long time (days and weeks) and MTBF (Mean
1472             Time Before Failure) in large scale training is typically in the order of hours, restarting
1473             from checkpoints becomes inevitable. On restarting the training from a checkpoint, the
1474             size of the communicator could be set to the new number of fewer GPUs.
1475           • Different NCCL communicators can be used in a hierarchical fashion (and it is often the
1476             case). To allow for repacking, one could use a hierarchy of communicators with the inten-
1477             tion to repack to one branch of hierarchy, and then assign a new job to the GPUs in the idle
1478             branch of the hierarchy.
1479
       Finally, we would like to emphasize that the primary focus of the paper is load balancing to make the
1480
       end-to-end training process faster. Re-packing (when possible) is an additional advantage to load
1481   balancing. We implement repacking in DYN M O at the load balancer level to enable the release of
1482   GPUs. We would like to point out that DYN M O’s task ends at releasing the GPUs: reclamation of
1483   the released GPUs and assigning them new jobs by the middleware or scheduler is outside the scope
1484   of this paper.

1559
       In fact DYN M O’s load balancing algorithm is desgined to, indirectly, handle variability/instability
1560   in the network. That is since we define our diffusion load balancing algorithm to consider the gaps
1561   between workers to include the total time until work arrives to node B from neighboring node A, i.e.
1562   we include the amount of time that A spent on work plus the time it takes to transfer the activations
1563   of the layers over the network. In intuitive terms, if worker B is stalling due to delay from neighbor
1564   A (in part due to a slow network connection between A and B), the load balancer would push more
1565   work to worker B until the amount of work in A plus the time it takes to transfer the activations over
       the network is roughly equal to the amount of work on B.

1722   Layer-wise balancing techniques work on layer granularity instead of operators. DeepSpeed Mi-
1723   crosoft (2023) offers three partitioning methods to balance the workload of stages: parameters,
1724   uniform, and regex. While the parameters method is trying to balance the number of parameters in
1725   each stage, the uniform aims to distribute the layers evenly. Regex only distributes the layers that
1726   match the given regex (e.g. transformer layers). Similar to the parameters method of DeepSpeed, He
1727   et al. He et al. (2021) balance the stages based on the number of parameters in each stage. Narayanan
       et al. Narayanan et al. (2021) assign each stage the same number of transformer layers to balance the

1731   out of the box.

1733   G.1.2    L OAD BALANCING VIA GRAPH PARTITIONING
1734
1735   Graph partitioning-based load balancing schemes find atomic operations in the model and consider
1736   them as nodes in a directed acyclic graph (DAG). Edges in the graph represent the dependencies be-
1737   tween operations. Tanaka et al. Tanaka et al. (2021) partition the DAG in three phases at which they
1738
       first find atomic operations, then group these operations into blocks according to their computation
       times, and finally, they combine blocks into final partitions by using a dynamic programming-based
1739
       algorithm. Qararyah et al. Qararyah et al. (2021) create disjoint clusters from the nodes of the graph
1740
       by finding critical paths and mapping these clusters to devices based on a mapping algorithm that
1741   takes both critical-communication minimization and temporal load balancing into account. Both
1742   studies perform profiling before the actual training and partition the graph once.

1744   G.1.3    L OAD BALANCING IN M IXTURE OF E XPERTS M ODELS
1745
1746   The mixture of experts (MoE) Jacobs et al. (1991) models contain many sub-networks (experts)
1747   where a router allocates inputs to top-k experts. At scale, experts are distributed across devices.
1748   Lepikhin et al. Lepikhin et al. (2020) defines an expert’s capacity to limit the maximum number of
1749
       tokens that can be processed by an expert to achieve workload balance. Fedus et al. Fedus et al.
       (2022) route each token to only one expert and use the same expert capacity for restrictions. Lewis
1750
       et al. Lewis et al. (2021a) employ an auction algorithm Bertsekas (1992) to solve the token-to-expert
1751
       assignment problem. This line of work is different from ours in the sense that their aim is to balance
1752   workload in the feed-forward network while our work aims to balance all layers of the transformer
1753   model.

       G.2     PACKING
1756
1757   In dynamic neural network models, packing the total workload into fewer number accelerators can
1758   provide significant cost-saving benefits. PipeTransformer He et al. (2021) offers an elastic pipelining
1759   system for freeze training where some of the layers of the model are frozen during the training.
1760   PipeTransformer packs the remaining active layers into fewer GPUs and creates pipeline replicas
1761   if possible. When PipeTransformer receives a notification for layer freezing, it attempts to divide
1762   the number of GPUs by 2 subject to the memory capacity constraints. On the other hand, our work
1763
       DYN M O can pack to an arbitrary number of GPUs specified by the user. Another difference between
       the packing mechanism of DYN M O and PipeTransformer is that PipeTransformer uses the parameter
1764
       size as a proxy to estimate the memory usage while DYN M O uses the actual memory usage from the
1765
       profiling step before load balancing. Finally, PipeTransformer is only capable of packing layers to
1766   fewer GPUs, and not load balancing. DYN M O, on top of being capable of re-packing when deemed
1767   beneficial, it can also redistribute the workload to achieve a better load balance.

       G.3     DYNAMIC P RUNING
1770
1771   Model pruning is a fast-paced research area. Since the optimization problem has many dimensions,
1772   there are many approaches to prune a model. We mainly focus on the schedule of the pruning rather
1773   than the decision of how to prune (e.g. magnitude pruning, variational dropout etc.) and what kind
1774   of structure (e.g. unstructured pruning, structured pruning) to be applied while pruning.
1775
       One of the commonly used sparsification technique is sparsification during training (i.e. gradual
1776   pruning) where the pruning starts before the model is trained until convergence. While some stud-
1777   ies Wortsman et al. (2019); Lin et al. (2020) use a binary mask to specify whether a parameter is
1778   pruned, which enables them to apply better weight regrowth or selection, others Gale et al. (2020)
1779   delete the pruned parameters to reduce the memory usage and number of operations. There are also
1780   many works on how fast to prune. For instance, Zhu and Gupta Zhu & Gupta (2017) prune the model
1781   rapidly in the first pruning steps when there are many abundant parameters in the model, and then
       reduce the pruning ratio as the number of parameters in the model are getting less and less. Dai et
