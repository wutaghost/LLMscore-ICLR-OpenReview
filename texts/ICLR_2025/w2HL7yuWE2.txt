                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
               Denoising diffusion models (DDMs) have shown significant potential in generating
012            oracle items that best match user preference with guidance from user historical
013            interaction sequences. However, the quality of guidance is often compromised by
014            the unpredictable missing data in the observed sequence, leading to suboptimal
015            item generation. To tackle this challenge, we propose a novel uncertainty-aware
016            guided diffusion model (DreamMiss) to alleviate the influence of missing data.
017            The core of DreamMiss is the utilization of a dual-side Thompson sampling (DTS)
018            strategy, which simulates the stochastical mechanism of missing data without
019            disrupting preference evolution. Specifically, we first define dual-side probability
020
               models to capture user preference evolution, taking into account both local item
               continuity and global sequence stability. We then strategically remove items based
021
               on these two models with DTS, creating uncertainty-aware guidance for DDMs
022
               to generate oracle items. This can achieve DDMs’ consistency regularization,
023            enabling them to resist missing data in real scenarios. Additionally, to accelerate
024            sampling in the reverse process, DreamMiss is implemented under the framework of
025            denoising diffusion implicit models (DDIM). Extensive experimental results show
026            that DreamMiss significantly outperforms baselines in sequential recommendation.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Sequential Recommendation (Kang & McAuley, 2018; Xie et al., 2022) is to predict the next item that
032   aligns with a user’s preferences based on his/her historical interaction sequence. Unlike conventional
033
      studies (Xie et al., 2022; Qiu et al., 2022; Hidasi et al., 2016) that learn to classify target items
      from sampled negatives, recent studies (Sachdeva et al., 2019; Xie et al., 2021; Rajput et al., 2023)
034
      shift towards generating oracle items (Yang et al., 2023) with generative models that best match
035
      user preference and grounding them in target items. A promising direction is employing denoising
036   diffusion models (DDMs) (Yang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Niu et al., 2024), which add noise to the
037   next items and iteratively denoise them toward oracle items, guided by interaction history conditions.
038
039
      However, we argue that DDMs’ ability to generate oracle items is largely constrained by the quality
      of guidance (i.e., the representation of interaction sequence). Typically, user interaction histories are
040
      only partially observed, with missing data occurring unpredictably (Zheng et al., 2022). Consider
041
      the case of item browsing as illustrated in Figure 1. The recommender system might only observe a
042   partial sequence, with items A, B, and C missing due to various factors, such as privacy concerns
043   (Feng et al., 2023) or technical limitations (Xu et al., 2020). Consequently, DDMs may be misled
044   by the unreliable guidance signal from the observed sequence and generate suboptimal oracle items.
045   Usually, data missing is uncertain in the observed sequence (Wang et al., 2023a; Fan et al., 2022b),
046   as it is hard to infer where the missing occurs and what content it might be due to the invisibility
047   of complete sequence. Thus, leading approaches that aim to tackle missing data issues, such as
048   imputation-based algorithms (which fill in the missing data) (Steck, 2013; Hikmawati et al., 2024)
049   and latent variable decomposition methods (which remove the latent variables causing missing data,
050
      such as exposure bias) (Saito et al., 2020; Li et al., 2023b), may introduce additional errors or distort
      user preference accidentally, as shown in Figure 1.
051
052   To enhance the robustness of DDMs, rather than inferring the occurred missing data, we aim to
053   simulate the uncertain data missing, creating uncertainty-aware guidance and achieving DDMs’
      consistency regularization (Zhang et al., 2020). To simulate data missing stochastically while

082     significant fluctuations, resulting in a stability score of 0.6.
083
084   High-continuity items in high-stability sequences are more likely to be removed by the dual-side
085   Thompson sampling strategy, which has little impact on preference evolution. This is evidenced
086   by the consistent green curves between the observed sequence and the one edited by our method,
087   as illustrated in Figure 1. Then, we treat such stochastically edited sequences as uncertainty-aware
088
      guidance for DDMs to generate the oracle items. Incorporating uncertainty into the guidance can
      achieve consistency regularization (Zhang et al., 2020) which endows DDMs with insensitivity
089
      to preference-preserving perturbations (i.e., simulated missing data) and can extrapolate to resist
090
      original missing data in real scenarios. We term this uncertainty-aware guided diffusion model as
091   DreamMiss, designed to mitigate the influence of missing data in sequential recommendation. To
092   further improve the efficiency, we utilize denoising diffusion implicit models (DDIM) to generate
093   oracles rather than denoising diffusion probabilistic models (DDPM), which can accelerate the
094   sampling in the reverse process. We validate the effectiveness of DreamMiss with experiments
095   on three datasets. Extensive experimental results demonstrate that DreamMiss can improve the
096   performance of sequential recommendation, outperforming multiple leading models.

141   2.2   T HOMPSON S AMPLING
142
143   Thompson sampling (TS) (Russo et al., 2018) has emerged as a prominent exploration strategy for
144   decision-making under uncertainty (McDonald et al., 2023; Wang & Zhou, 2020). To achieve a
145   balance between exploration and exploitation (Osband & Roy, 2015), TS utilizes a probability model
146   to sample greedily based on the values of execution results from the last round. Specifically, given the
147
      value v, the probability model of TS can be parameterized as F (v, p), where p is a random variable
      that ranges from 0 to 1. A higher value of v can result in a higher sampling probability p̂ from the
148
      probability model. Formally, at each round, we have the sampling probability:
149
150                                                    p̂ ∼ F (v, p).                                              (5)
151
      Then TS executes based on the sampling probability p̂, updating the value v and probability model
152
      F (v, p) based on the execution results.

      3.1     U NCERTAINTY- AWARE G UIDANCE WITH D UAL - SIDE T HOMPSON S AMPLING
191
      In real-world scenarios, missing data is inherently uncertain and unpredictable(Wang et al., 2023a;
192
      Fan et al., 2022b), so we simulate it stochastically rather than recovering it, creating uncertainty-aware
193   guidance and realizing DDMs’ consistency regularization. To preserve user preference evolution
194   during the simulation, we introduce a Dual-side Thompson Sampling (DTS) strategy, sampling and
195   removing items by exploiting user preference evolution with two probability models — one at the
196   local item level and the other at the global sequence level.
197

      3.3     T HEORETICAL A NALYSIS FOR S IMULATING M ISSING DATA OF D REAM M ISS
300
301   We denote the original missing data in sequence as δ, the simulated missing data as δ ′ , the unavailable
302   complete sequence as e1:N −1 ⊕δ, the stochastically edited sequence e′1:N −1 as e1:N −1 ⊖δ ′ . We justify
303   our method for addressing missing data issues through extrapolation and consistency regularization
304   (Zhang et al., 2020; Tack et al., 2022). .
305
      Extrapolation (Krueger et al., 2021): Let ĝ, ḡ, and g̃ denote the guidance encoded from observed
306
      sequence e1:N −1 , unavailable complete sequence e1:N −1 ⊕ δ and simulated sequence e1:N −1 ⊖ δ ′ ,
307   respectively. We can derive the following inequality for extrapolation:
308
309                     ∥fθ (eτNs , ḡ, τs ) − fθ (eτNs , ĝ, τs )∥ ≤ C∥fθ (eτNs , ĝ, τs ) − fθ (eτNs , g̃, τs )∥              (16)
310
      where C is the constant. A detailed derivation process is presented in the Appendix B.3. According
311
      to Equation 16, enhancing DDMs’ insensitivity to simulated missing data enables resilience against
312
      original missing data.
313
314   Consistency regularization (Zhang et al., 2020; Tack et al., 2022): According to Equation 10,
315   the sequence e′1:N −1 are edited stochastically across different epochs. Thus ĝ and g̃ can serve as
316   the perturbated pairs, which share the same target e0N . To ensure the insensitivity of DDMs to the
317   simulated missing data, we leverage consistency regularization to minimize the distance between
318
      fθ (eτNs , ĝ, τs ) and fθ (eτNs , g̃, τs ). By completing the square, we have:
319
        ||fθ (eτNs , ĝ, τs ) − fθ (eτNs , g̃, τs )||22 ≤ 2 ||fθ (eτNs , ĝ, τs ) − e0N ||22 + ||fθ (eτNs , g̃, τs ) − e0N ||22 . (17)

320
321   Minimizing the right-hand side, which stems from our reconstruction loss, serves as an upper
322   bound of the minimizer of the left-hand side, thus achieving consistency regularization. Such
323   consistency regularization endows DDMs with insensitivity to the simulated missing data, allowing
      the extrapolation to resist the original missing data.

      In this section, we conduct extensive experiments across three datasets to evaluate the effectiveness
327   of DreamMiss by answering the following questions. RQ1: How does DreamMiss perform in
328   the sequential recommendation compared with diverse baseline models? RQ2: How do different
329   probability models bring improvements to our method? RQ3: How robust is DreamMiss to varying
330   degrees of missing data in datasets? RQ4: How sensitive is DreamMiss to the strength of guidance
331   and the thresholds of removing? RQ5: Can DreamMiss generalize on different sequence lengths?

333   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
334
335   Datasets. We conduct experiments on three real-world datasets for sequential recommendation
336   following the settings of DreamRec (Yang et al., 2023): YooChoose (Ben-Shimon et al., 2015),
337   KuaiRec (Gao et al., 2022), and Zhihu (Hao et al., 2021). To mitigate cold-start issues, we implement
338
      a preprocessing step that excludes items with fewer than five interactions and sequences shorter than
      3 interactions. The detailed statistics of the datasets are provided in Appendix E.1. For each dataset,
339
      we sort all sequences chronologically and split the data into training, validation, and testing sets
340
      in an 8:1:1 ratio, ensuring that later interactions don’t leak into the training data (Ji et al., 2023).
341   Additionally, to validate the effectiveness of DreamMiss on larger or diverse datasets from different
342   domains, we conduct experiments on Steam, Amazon-beauty, and Amazon-toys in the appendix F.1.
343
344
      Baselines. We compare DreamMiss against multiple leading approaches:
345
      • Traditional sequential recommenders: GRU4Rec (Hidasi et al., 2016), Caser (Tang & Wang, 2018),
346     SASRec (Kang & McAuley, 2018), Bert4Rec (Sun et al., 2019), and CL4SRec (Xie et al., 2022),
347     which employ neural networks to capture user preferences.
348
      • Latent variable decomposition methods: S-IPS (Wang et al., 2022) and AdaRanker (Fan et al.,
349
        2022a), which are designed to address the issues of selection or distribution bias.
350
351   • Generative recommenders: DiffRec (Wang et al., 2023c), DiffRIS (Niu et al., 2024), and DreamRec
352
        (Yang et al., 2023), which generate target items directly with DDMs.
353   • Imputation-based algorithms: DiffuASR (Liu et al., 2023a), CaDiRec (Cui et al., 2024), PDRec
354     (Ma et al., 2024), STEAM (Lin et al., 2023), and SSDRec (Zhang et al., 2024). DiffASR, CaDiRec,
355     and PDRec generate supplement items to the observed sequences with DDMs to enhance traditional
356
        sequential recommenders. STEAM and SSDRec aim to correct the interaction sequence by “insert”
        or other operations.
357
358   Implementation Details. Following DreamRec (Yang et al., 2023), we set the sequence length
359   to 10, padding sequences with fewer than 10 interactions using a padding token. The parameter
360   tuning strategy is detailed in the appendix E.2. We adopt the widely used metrics in sequential
361   recommendation: hit ratio (HR@20) and normalized discounted cumulative gain (NDCG@20) (Kang
362   & McAuley, 2018) to evaluate the recommendation performance. We report the average performance
363   of five experimental runs, with their corresponding standard deviations.

      4.3    A BLATION S TUDY (RQ2)
407
408
409   The two probability models employed in DreamMiss are expected to capture the dynamic preference
410   evolution, preserving user preference when simulating data missing with dual-side Thompson sam-
411   pling. To evaluate their impact, we conduct ablation studies with eight variations of DreamMiss. The
412   experimental results are shown in Table 2, where “w/o L” and “w/o G” indicate variants where the
413   local or global probability model is replaced with random sampling, “w/o GL” denotes replacing both
414   models with random sampling, “Base” represents generating oracle items without simulating data
415   missing. To extend our method, we propose other metrics for probability models and compare their
416   performance. Specifically, “popularity” is measured by the frequency of each item in all interactions,
417
      which reflects popularity bias. “Diversity” within a sequence is calculated to indicate exposure bias.
      “Seq-len” (Sequence length) serves as a measure of user activity, while “Item-pos” (item position)
418
      reflects short-term and long-term preferences. All of these metrics can serve as probability measures
419
      for a specific type of missing data.
420
421   As can be seen, all variations incorporating data missing simulation (i.e., “w/o GL”, “w/o G”, “w/o
422   L”, “popularity”, “Diversity”, “Seq-len”, and “Item-pos”) outperform the “Base” model, highlighting
423
      the effectiveness of simulating data missing in enhancing the robustness of DDMs. Furthermore,
      both variations “w/o L” and “w/o G” exhibit superior performance compared to “w/o GL”, while
424
      DreamMiss outperforms all variations. This demonstrates that sampling based on probability models
425
      with item continuity and sequence stability metrics can effectively maintain user preferences, thereby
426   improving DDMs’ recommendation performance through the strategic simulation of missing data.
427   Additionally, DreamMiss (with item continuity and sequence stability metrics) achieves superior
428   performance to other variations (with “popularity”, “Diversity”, “Seq-len”, and “Item-pos” metrics),
429   demonstrating the reliability of continuity and stability metrics for probability models. The reliability
430   and robustness of the continuity and stability metrics lie in that they can create missing data regardless
431   of the specific factors (e.g., popularity bias or diversity bias), as long as such missing data does not
      lead to changes in user preferences.

517   The dynamics of user preferences may exhibit intricate patterns that transcend the capabilities of our
518   dual-side probability models. Future research could benefit from integrating a more sophisticated
519   understanding of user preference evolution into these models. Additionally, the issue of missing data,
520   which can stem from various factors, such as exposure bias or popularity bias, presents an opportunity
521   for targeted simulation to enhance the debiasing capabilities of DDMs.

      6     C ONCLUSION
525
526   In this paper, we propose DreamMiss, a novel approach that enhances the robustness of denoising
527
      diffusion models (DDMs) to uncertain missing data in sequential recommendation. By introducing
      a dual-side Thompson sampling strategy with local and global probability models, DreamMiss
528
      effectively simulates missing data while preserving user preference evolution, creating uncertainty-
529
      aware guidance for DDMs. We explain that incorporating such uncertainty-aware guidance for DDMs
530   can promote consistency regularization, thus resiling against the uncertain missing data. Extensive
531   experiments demonstrate that DreamMiss significantly outperforms multiple baselines, showcasing
532   its potential to improve recommendation performance in real-world scenarios.
533

535   E THICS S TATEMENT.
536
537   This work is designed for the generative sequential recommendation with DDMs, simulating missing
538   data with a dual-side Thompson sampling strategy to create uncertainty-aware guidance. It focuses
539   on mitigating the influence of missing data and enhancing DDMs’ robustness. We do not foresee any
      direct, immediate, or negative societal impacts stemming from the outcomes of our research.

      A     R ELATED W ORK
757
758
      In this section, we provide a review of missing data in recommendation and generative recommenders.
759
760   Missing Data in Recommendation can be classified into Missing At Random (MAR) without bias
761   and Missing Not at Random (MNAR) with bias (Zheng et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023b). In addressing
762   missing data in sequential recommendation systems, existing methods can be broadly categorized
763
      into two types: latent variable decomposition (Saito et al., 2020; Li et al., 2023b), which aims to
      debias the data, and imputation techniques that complete the sequence with missing data (Steck,
764
      2013; Hikmawati et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2019; Steck, 2010). For instance, Inverse Propensity Score
765
      (IPS) approaches (Schnabel et al., 2016; Li et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2022) assign weights based
766   on propensity scores to mitigate the bias. PDRec (Ma et al., 2024) leverages the DDMs to generate
767   supplement items to the observed sequences. Here we emphasize the uncertain nature (Wang et al.,
768   2023a; Fan et al., 2022b) of missingness (no matter MAR or MNAR) within the observed sequences
769   by simulating it stochastically.
770
      Generative Recommenders offer distinct advantages over discriminative recommenders, particularly
771
      in sequential recommendation tasks. Since GANs and VAEs are limited in the stability and quality
772   of generation (Wang et al., 2023c; Zhao et al., 2024), diffusion models (DDMs) have emerged as a
773   promising alternative, excelling at modeling complex data distributions and generating oracle items
774   (Yang et al., 2023; Niu et al., 2024). For example, DiffuRec (Li et al., 2024) and DreamRec (Yang
775   et al., 2023) generate the next items directly by corrupting them with noise and denoising based on
776   the historical sequence. Additionally, DiffuASR (Liu et al., 2023a) and CaDiRec (Cui et al., 2024)
777   address data sparsity by generating sequences or items with DDMs. In our work, we emphasize the
778   quality of DDMs’ guidance, tackling challenges posed by uncertain missing data and enhancing the
779   robustness through consistency regularization.
780
781   B     M OTIVATION AND E XPLANATION FOR O UR M ETHOD

      B.1   M OTIVATION FOR S IMULATING U NCERTAIN M ISSING DATA
784
785   We denote the original missing data as δ, the observed sequence as e1:N −1 , the simulated missing
786   data as δ ′ , the complete sequence e1:N −1 ⊕ δ, and the simulated sequence as e1:N −1 ⊖ δ ′ .
787
788   • Unavailability of complete sequence: Due to the lack of prior knowledge for the complete
789     sequence e1:N −1 ⊕ δ from an omniscient perspective, we cannot pinpoint where the missing data δ
790     occurs or what specific content it pertains to.
791   • Uncertainty of missing data: Due to the variability of user preferences, the missing data δ is
792     uncertain in the observed interaction sequence e1:N −1 . User interaction may miss anywhere
793     stochastically in the observed sequence.
794
795   Thus, compensating for missing data may introduce additional errors as shown in Figure 1 (a). The
796
      unavailability of the complete sequence e1:N −1 ⊕ δ and the uncertainty of missing data motivates us
      to simulate additional missing data δ ′ stochastically from the observed sequence e1:N −1 , treating
797
      e1:N −1 as the complete sequence for the simulated sequence e1:N −1 ⊖ δ ′ .
798
799
800   B.2   M OTIVATION FOR T HOMPSON S AMPLING
801
      To simulate missing data stochastically while maintaining user preference, we propose Dual-side
802
      Thompson Sampling. The motivation for DTS is as follows:
803
804   • Characteristics of TS: TS strategy can balance the exploration and exploitation based on the
805     known user preference in probability models. To capture user preference evolution, the probability
806     models are established based on item continuity and sequence stability metrics. The Dual-side
807     Thompson Sampling is more likely to remove items with high continuity in sequence with greater
808     stability, according to the probability models. Such removal has little impact on understanding user
809     preference evolution, as evidenced by the consistent green curves between the observed sequence
        and the one edited by DTS in Figure 1.

      C     R ATIONALE FOR DDPM AND DDIM
898
899   Here we explain the different rationale of DDPM (Ho et al., 2020) and DDIM (Song et al., 2021).
900   For DDPM, the forward process and reverse processes are both Markovian chains. The number of
901   iterative steps in the reverse process is equal to that in the forward process due to the reliance on the
902   Markov assumption. To ensure the generation quality, DDPM needs a substantial number of sampling
903   steps in the reverse process. Unlike DDPM, DDIM accelerates the reverse process by relaxing the
904   Markov assumption, achieving comparable generation quality with significantly fewer sampling steps.
905   Below we list the rationale of DDPM and DDIM in Table 3 and Table 4, respectively.

1176
1177   Computational complexity: DreamMiss employs a Transformer as the sequence encoder and a
1178   multi-layer perceptron (MLP) as the denoising model. The Transformer model consists of a Self-
1179   Attention Mechanism, a Feed-Forward Neural Network, and Layer Normalization. Let L be the
1180   length of the input sequence, d be the embedding dimension, and N be the number of layers in the
1181   model. The complexity for computing self-attention at each layer is O(L2 · d), and the complexity
1182   for the MLP network is O(L · d2 ). For N layers, the overall computational complexity becomes
1183   O(N · (L2 · d + L · d2 )) in the training phase. Let K be the number of reverse steps, the computational
1184
       complexity of DreamMiss can be O(K · N · (L2 · d + L · d2 )) in the inference phase.
1185   Complexity comparison: The computational complexity of DreamMiss for training each epoch
1186   is nearly similar to other diffusion-based recommenders and traditional recommenders that use the
1187   same sequence encoder. Furthermore, since we employ DDIM to accelerate sampling during the
       inference phase, the value of K for DreamMiss is significantly lower than that of DreamRec. As

       In large-scale recommendation systems, although the number of users and items increases, the
1211
       sequence encoder (Transformer) and denoising models (MLP) we employ do not require excessive
1212
       computational resources. Furthermore, Thompson sampling consumes almost no computational
1213   resources without involving model parameters. These factors enable DreamMiss to be effectively
1214   applied in large-scale systems. We detail some potential optimization strategies for larger datasets.
1215
1216   • Model Selection (Li et al., 2021): Choose lightweight architectures with lower computational
1217     complexity as the sequence encoder.
1218   • Accelerating inference for DDMs: Reduce the iterative steps in the inference phase by consistency
1219     models (Song et al., 2023), knowledge distillation (Meng et al., 2023), or Reflow (Liu et al., 2023b).
1220   • Starting Item (Lin et al., 2024): Instead of using pure noise as the starting item, use the mean of the
1221     sequence to reduce the iteration times during inference.

1224   G    D ISCUSSION ON THE M ISSING DATA I SSUE
1225
1226   The missing data issue refers to the absence of behaviors in interaction sequences, which can lead
1227   to incomplete observed sequences in sequential recommendation tasks. For example, the complete
1228   sequence is A → B → C → D → E → F . However, due to complex factors, we may only observe
1229   the partial interaction sequence A → C → E → F with B and D missing. Since the complete
1230
       sequence is unavailable, the data missing in the observed sequence is uncertain and unpredictable.
       Such missing data issues can be roughly divided into two types: missing at random and missing not
1231
       at random, as discussed in the related work A. Missing not at random often reflects bias, such as
1232
       popularity bias and exposure bias. Missing at random is often caused by factors such as technical
1233   issues or privacy concerns.
1234
1235   DTS can effectively address the missing data issues when the observed sequence accurately reflects
1236
       users’ complete preferences, regardless of the specific factors (such as popularity bias, exposure bias,
       or technique issues). According to the probability models that reflect user preference evolution, DTS
1237
       tends to remove items that have little impact on understanding users’ original preference evolution,
1238
       as shown in the consistent green curves in Figure B.2. By simulating uncertain missing data while
1239   maintaining the evolution of user preferences, DTS enables DDMs to generalize and effectively
1240   address the original missing data that does not disrupt users’ complete preferences.
