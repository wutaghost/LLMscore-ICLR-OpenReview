009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Solving time-dependent parametric partial differential equations (PDEs) is chal-
012            lenging, as models must adapt to variations in parameters such as coefficients,
013            forcing terms, and boundary conditions. Data-driven neural solvers either train on
014            data sampled from the PDE parameters distribution in the hope that the model gen-
015            eralizes to new instances or rely on gradient-based adaptation and meta-learning
016
               to implicitly encode the dynamics from observations. This often comes with in-
               creased inference complexity. Inspired by the in-context learning capabilities of
017
               large language models (LLMs), we introduce Zebra, a novel generative auto-
018
               regressive transformer designed to solve parametric PDEs without requiring gra-
019            dient adaptation at inference. By leveraging in-context information during both
020            pre-training and inference, Zebra dynamically adapts to new tasks by condition-
021            ing on input sequences that incorporate context trajectories or preceding states.
022            This approach enables Zebra to flexibly handle arbitrarily sized context inputs
023            and supports uncertainty quantification through the sampling of multiple solution
024            trajectories. We evaluate Zebra across a variety of challenging PDE scenarios,
025            demonstrating its adaptability, robustness, and superior performance compared to
026            existing approaches.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
      Training partial differential equation (PDE) solvers is a challenging task due to the variety of be-
030
      haviors that can arise in physical phenomena, and neural solvers have limited generalization capa-
031   bility(Chen et al., 2018; Raissi et al., 2019; Li et al., 2021). We tackle the parametric PDE problem
032   (Cohen & Devore, 2015), where a model is trained on trajectories defined by varying PDE param-
033   eters with the goal of generalizing across a wide range of parameters. The parameters may include
034   initial and boundary conditions, physical coefficients, and forcing terms. We focus on pure data-
035   driven approaches that do not leverage any prior knowledge on the underlying equations.
036
      A natural approach to this problem is to sample from the parameter distribution, i.e., to train using
037   different PDE instances or parameter values, along with multiple trajectories for each PDE instance.
038   This requires a training set representative of the distribution of the underlying dynamical system,
039   which is difficult to meet in practice given the complexity of physical phenomena. Other approaches
040   explicitly condition on specific PDE parameters, (Brandstetter et al., 2022b; Takamoto et al., 2023)
041   relying on the availability of such prior knowledge. This requires a physical model of the observed
042   system, making the incorporation of PDE parameters into neural solvers challenging beyond ba-
043   sic PDE coefficients. An alternative approach involves online adaptation to new PDE instances by
044   leveraging observations from novel environments. Here we consider that an environment is charater-
045
      ized by a set of parameters. This adaptation is often implemented through meta-learning, where the
      model is trained on a variety of simulations corresponding to different environments—i.e., varying
046
      PDE parameter values—so that it can quickly adapt to new, unseen PDE simulation instances using
047
      a few trajectory examples(Kirchmeyer et al., 2022; Yin et al., 2022). This method offers a high
048   flexibility but requires gradient updates for adaptation, adding computational overhead. Another
049   common setting involves leveraging historical data to condition the neural network, allowing it to
050   generalize to new PDE instances without retraining (Li et al., 2021; McCabe et al., 2023). Again the
051   generalization ability is limited to dynamics close to the ones used for training. Exploring another
052   direction and motivated by the successes encountered in natural language processing and vision,
053   some authors have begun investigating the development of foundation models for spatio-temporal
      dynamic physical processes (Subramanian et al., 2023; Herde et al., 2024; McCabe et al., 2023).

      This approach involves training a large model on a variety of physics-based numerical simulations
055   with the expectation that it will generalize to new situations or equations. While they consider mul-
056   tiple physics we focus on solving parametric PDEs, i.e. multiple variations of the same physical
057   phenomenon.
058
      We explore here a new direction inspired by the successes of in-context learning (ICL) and its ability
059
      to generalize to downstream tasks without retraining (Brown et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023). We
060
      propose a framework, denoted Zebra, relying on in-context pretraining (ICP), for solving paramet-
061   ric PDEs and learning to condition neural solvers to adapt fast to new situations or said otherwise
062   for solving for new parameter values. As for ICL in language the model is trained to generate ap-
063   propriate responses given context examples and a query. The context examples could be trajectories
064   from the same dynamics starting from different initial conditions, or simply a brief history of past
065   system states for the target trajectory. The query will consist for example of an initial state condi-
066   tion, that will serve as inference starting point for the forecast. This approach offers key advantages
067   compared to existing methods. It can leverage contexts of different types and sizes, it requires only
068   a few context examples to adapt to new dynamics and can handle as well 0-shot learning. It allows
069
      us to cover a large variety of situations.
070   On the technical side, Zebra introduces a novel generative autoregressive solver for parametric
071   PDEs. It employs an encode-generate-decode framework: first, a vector-quantized variational auto-
072   encoder (VQ-VAE) (Oord et al., 2017) is learnt to compress physical states into discrete tokens
073   and to decode it back to the original physical space. Next, a generative autoregressive transformer
074   is pre-trained using a next token objective. To leverage the in-context properties of the model,
075
      Zebra is directly pretrained on arbitrary-sized contexts such as extra trajectories or historical states
      of the target dynamics. At inference, Zebra can handle varying context sizes for conditioning
076
      and support uncertainty quantification, enabling generalization to unseen PDE parameters without
077
      gradient updates.
078
079   Our main contributions include:
080
081
             • We introduce a generative autoregressive transformer for modeling physical dynamics. It
               operates on compact discretized representations of physical state observations. This dis-
082
               cretization is performed through a VQ-VAE. The encoder tokenizes observations into se-
083
               quences of tokens, while the decoder reconstructs the original states. This framework repre-
084            sents the first successful application of generative modeling using quantized representations
085            of physical systems.
086
             • To harness the in-context learning strengths of autoregressive transformers, we develop a
087
               new pretraining strategy that conditions the model on historical states or example trajecto-
088
               ries with similar dynamics, allowing it to handle arbitrary-sized context token inputs.
089
090
             • We evaluate Zebra on a range of parametric PDEs on two distinct settings. In the first, the
               model infers dynamics from a context trajectory that shares similar behavior with the target
091
               but differs in initial conditions, representing a one-shot setting. Zebra’s performance is
092
               benchmarked against domain-adaptation baselines specifically trained for such tasks. In
093            the second scenario, only a limited number of historical frames of the target trajectory
094            are available, requiring the model to deduce the underlying dynamics solely from these
095            inputs. Zebra consistently demonstrates competitive performance across both evaluation
096            contexts.

      2.2   G ENERALIZATION FOR PARAMETRIC PDE
154
155
      Solving time-dependent parametric PDEs requires developing neural solvers capable of generalizing
156   to a whole distribution of PDE parameters. In practice, changes in the PDE parameters often lead
157   to distribution shifts in the trajectories which makes the problem challenging. Different directions
158   are currently being explored briefly reviewed below. We focus on pure data-driven approaches that
159   do not make use of any prior knowledge on the equations. We make the assumption that the models
160   are learned from numerical simulations so that it is possible to generate from multiple parameters.
161   This emulates real situations where for example, a physical phenomenon is observed in different
      contexts.


                                                                                 3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      0-shot learning with temporal conditioning A first direction consists in adapting the classical
163   ERM framework to parametric PDE solving by sampling multiple instances of a PDE, in the hope
164   that this will generalize to unseen conditions in a 0-shot setting. It is usually assumed that for both
165   learning and inference, a sequence of past states is provided as initial input to the model, leveraging
166   its potential to infer the dynamics characteristics in order to forecast future values. The neural solver
167   Gθ is then conditioned by a sequence of past states for a trajectory ut−m∆t:t := (ut−m∆t , . . . , ut )
168   where m ≥ 1. Depending on the architecture, this can be implemented by stacking the informa-
169   tion in the channel dimension (Li et al., 2021), or by creating an additional temporal dimension as
170   done in video prediction contexts (McCabe et al., 2023; Ho et al., 2022). This approach makes an
171
      implicit i.i.d. assumption on the training - test distributions which is often not met with dynamical
      phenomena. It offers a limited flexibility in cases where only limited historical context is accessible.
172
173
      Few-shot learning by fine tuning Another category of methods leverages fine tuning. As for the
174
      0-shot setting above, a model is pretrained on a distribution of the PDE parameters. At inference,
175
      for a new environment, fine tuning is performed on a sample of the environment trajectories. This
176   approach often relies on large fine tuning samples and involves updating all or a subset of parameters
177   (Subramanian et al., 2023; Herde et al., 2024).
178
179   Adaptive conditioning A more flexible approach relies on adaptation at inference time through
180   meta-learning. It posits that a set of environments e are available from which trajectories are sam-
181   pled, each environment e being defined by specific PDE parameter values (Zintgraf et al., 2019a;
182   Kirchmeyer et al., 2022). The model is trained from a sampling from the environments distribution
183   to adapt fast to a new environment. The usual formulation is to learn shared and specific environ-
184   ment parameters Gθ+∆θξ , where θ and ∆θξ are respectively the shared and specific parameters. At
185   inference, for a new environment, only a small number of parameters θξ is adapted from a small
186   sample of observations.

      3     Z EBRA F RAMEWORK
198
199
      We introduce Zebra, a novel framework designed to solve parametric PDEs through in-context
200
      learning and flexible conditioning. Zebra utilizes an autoregressive transformer to model partial
201   differential equations (PDEs) within a compact, discrete latent space. A spatial CNN encoder is em-
202   ployed to map physical spatial observations into these latent representations, while a CNN decoder
203   accurately reconstructs them. As illustrated in Figure 1, our pretraining pipeline consists of two
204   key stages: 1) Learning a finite vocabulary of physical phenomena, and 2) Training the transformer
205   using an in-context pretraining strategy, enabling the model to effectively condition on contextual in-
206   formation. At inference, Zebra allows both adaptive and temporal conditioning through in-context
207   learning (Table 1).
208

368   Setting We evaluate Zebra’s ability to perform in-context learning by leveraging example tra-
369   jectories that follow the same underlying dynamics as the target. Formally, in the n-shot adaptation

383   vironment. At the inference stage, CODA adapts to a new environment in a one-shot manner by
384   only tuning ce with several gradient steps. CAPE is not designed to perform adaptation via extra-
385   trajectories, but instead needs the correct parameter values as input to condition a neural solver. We
386   adapt it to our setting, by learning a context ce instead of using the real parameter values. During
387
      adaptation, we only tune this context ce via gradient updates. Additionally, we introduce a baseline
      based on a vision transformer (Peebles & Xie, 2023), integrating a [CLS] token that serves as a

      learned parameter for each environment. This token lets the model handle different dynamics, and

523   The quality of the generated trajectories is limited by the decoder’s ability to reconstruct details
524   from the quantized latent space. While the reconstructions are excellent for many applications, we
525   believe there is room for improvement. Future work could explore scaling the codebook size, as

530   regular domains. More flexible architectures, such as those proposed by Serrano et al. (2024), could

535   This study introduces Zebra, a novel generative model that adapts language model pretraining
536   techniques for solving parametric PDEs. We propose a pretraining strategy that enables Zebra
537   to develop in-context learning capabilities. Our experiments demonstrate that the pretrained model
538   performs competitively against specialized baselines across various scenarios. Additionally, as a
539   generative model, Zebra facilitates uncertainty quantification and can generate new trajectories,
      providing valuable flexibility in applications.

      A.1   L EARNING PARAMETRIC PDE S
705
706   The classical ML paradigm The classical ML paradigm for solving parametric PDEs consists
707   in sampling from the PDE parameter distribution trajectories to generalize to new PDE parameter
708   values. It is the classical ERM approach. The natural way for generalizing to new PDE parameters
709   is to explicitly embed them in the neural network (Brandstetter et al., 2022b). Takamoto et al. (2023)
710   proposed a channel-attention mechanism to guide neural solvers with the physical coefficients given
711   as input; it requires complete knowledge of the physical system and are not designed for other PDE
712   parameter values, e.g., boundary conditions. It is commonly assumed that prior knowledge are not
713
      available, but instead rely on past states of trajectories for inferring the dynamics. Neural solvers
      and operators learn parametric PDEs by stacking the past states as channel information as done in
714
      Li et al. (2021), or by creating additional temporal dimension as done in video prediction contexts
715
      (Ho et al., 2022; McCabe et al., 2023). Their performance drops when shifts occur in the data
716   distribution, which is often met with parametric PDEs, as small changes in the PDE parameters can
717   lead to various dynamics. To better generalize to new PDE parameter values, Subramanian et al.
718   (2023) instead leverages fine-tuning from pretrained models to generalize to new PDE parameters.
719   It however often necessitates a relatively large number of fine tuning samples to effectively adapt to
720   new PDE parameter values, by updating all or a subset of parameters (Herde et al., 2024; Hao et al.,
721   2024).
722
723   Adaptive conditioning To better adapt to new PDE parameters values at inference, several works
724   have explored learning on multiple environments. During training, a limited number of environ-
725   ments are available, each corresponding to a specific PDE instance. Yin et al. (2022) introduced
726
      LEADS, a multi-task framework for learning parametric PDEs, where a shared model from all envi-
      ronments and a model specific to each environment are learned jointly. At inference, for a new PDE
727
      instance, the shared model remain frozen and only a model specific to that environment is learned.
728
      Kirchmeyer et al. (2022) proposed to perform adaptive conditioning in the parameter space; the
729   framework adapts the weights of a model to each environment via a hyper-network conditioned by a
730   context vector ce specific to each environment. At inference, the model adapts to a new environment
731   by only tuning ce . Park et al. (2023) bridged the gap from the classical gradient-based meta-learning
732   approaches by addressing the limitations of second-order optimization of MAML and its variants
733   (Finn et al., 2017; Zintgraf et al., 2019b). Other works have also extended these frameworks to
734   quantify uncertainty of the predictions : Jiaqi et al. (2024) proposed a conditional neural process to
735   capture uncertainty in the context of multiple environments with sparse trajectories, while Nzoyem
736   et al. (2024) leveraged information from multiple environments to enable more robust predictions
737
      and uncertainty quantification.

739   A.2   G ENERATIVE MODELS
740
741
      Auto-regressive Transformers for Images and Videos Recent works have explored combining
      language modeling techniques with image and video generation, typically using a VQ-VAE (Oord
742
      et al., 2017) paired with a causal transformer (Esser et al., 2021) or a bidirectional transformer
743
      (Chang et al., 2022). VQGAN (Esser et al., 2021) has become the leading framework by incorpo-
744   rating perceptual and adversarial losses to improve the visual realism of decoder outputs from quan-
745   tized latent representations. However, while these methods succeed in generating visually plausible
746   images, they introduce a bias—driven by perceptual and adversarial losses—that leads the network
747   to prioritize perceptual similarity and realism, often causing reconstructions to deviate from the true
748   input. In contrast, Zebra focuses on maximizing reconstruction accuracy, and did not observe
749   benefits from using adversarial or perceptual losses during training.
750   In video generation, models like Magvit (Yu et al., 2023a) and Magvit2 (Yu et al., 2023b) adopt
751   similar strategies, using 3D CNN encoders to compress sequences of video frames into spatiotem-
752   poral latent representations by exploiting the structural similarities between successive frames in a
753   video. However, such temporal compression is unsuitable for modeling partial differential equations
754   (PDEs), where temporal dynamics can vary significantly between frames depending on the tempo-
755   ral resolution. With Zebra, we spatially compress observations using the encoder and learn the
      temporal dynamics with an auto-regressive transformer, avoiding temporal compression.

897            adapted to each environment using a context vector specific to each environment. For the
898

899
               dataset. At inference, we adapt to a new environment using 250 gradient steps.

             • CAPE: For CAPE (Takamoto et al., 2023), we adapted the method to an adaptation set-

903
               ronment. During inference, we only adapt ce with 250 gradient steps. For the architectures,
904            we use UNET and FNO as the backbones, and reported the best results among the two
905            architectures for all settings.
906
             • [CLS] ViT: For the ViT, we use a simple vision transformer architecture Dosovitskiy
907            et al. (2021), but adapt it to a meta-learning setting where the CLS token encodes the
908            specific variations of each environment. At inference, the CLS token is adapted to a new
909            environment with 100 gradient steps.

             • MPP: For MPP, we used the same model as the one used in the paper (McCabe et al., 2023).

1377    D.3              DATASET SCALING ANALYSIS
1378
1379    We investigate how the zero-shot error on the test set evolves as we vary the size of the training
1380    dataset. To this end, we train the auto-regressive transformer on datasets containing 10, 100, 1000,
1381    and 12,000 trajectories and evaluate Zebra’s generations on the test set, starting with two frames as
1382
        inputs. The training time is proportional to the dataset size: for example, the number of training
        steps for 1,000 trajectories is 10 times the number of steps for 100 trajectories. The results are
1383
        presented in Figure 15.
1384
1385    First, we observe that Zebra requires a substantial amount of data to generalize effectively to differ-
1386    ent parameter values, even within the training distribution. This aligns with findings in the literature
1387    that transformers, especially auto-regressive transformers, excel at scaling —performing well on
1388
        very large datasets and for larger model architectures. However, for smaller datasets, this approach
        may not be the most efficient. We believe that Zebra’s potential resides when applied to vast amounts
1389
        of data, making it an ideal candidate for scenarios involving large-scale training.
1390
1391    Second, for the Combined equation, we notice that performance plateaus between 100 and 1,000
1392    trajectories. This may be due to insufficient training or a lack of diverse examples, as the Combined
1393    equation is more challenging compared to the Advection equation, whose performance follows a
1394
        more log-linear trend. This suggests that additional data or targeted training strategies might be
        needed to achieve better generalization for more complex equations.
