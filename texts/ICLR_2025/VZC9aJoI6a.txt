009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) have transformed AI across diverse domains, with
012            prompting being central to their success in guiding model outputs. However, man-
013            ual prompt engineering is both labor-intensive and domain-specific, necessitating
014            the need for automated solutions. We introduce PromptWizard, a novel, fully
015            automated framework for discrete prompt optimization, utilizing a self-evolving,
016            self-adapting mechanism. Through a feedback-driven critique and synthesis pro-
017            cess, PromptWizard achieves an effective balance between exploration and ex-
018            ploitation, iteratively refining both prompt instructions and in-context examples to
019
               generate human-readable, task-specific prompts. This guided approach systemati-
               cally improves prompt quality, resulting in superior performance across 45 tasks.
020
               PromptWizard excels even with limited training data, smaller LLMs, and various
021
               LLM architectures. Additionally, our cost analysis reveals a substantial reduction in
022            API calls, token usage, and overall cost, demonstrating PromptWizard’s efficiency,
023            scalability, and advantages over existing prompt optimization strategies.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Large language models (LLMs) like GPT-4 (OpenAI et al., 2024) have achieved remarkable per-
029   formance across diverse tasks (Colombo et al., 2024; Nguyen et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024). At
030   the core of this success is prompting—the process of providing input instructions to guide models
031   toward desired outputs. Studies have shown that prompting significantly influences LLM perfor-
032   mance, making prompt engineering—the design and refinement of prompts—critical for maximizing
033
      accuracy (Wang et al., 2023c;b; Nori et al., 2023). However, crafting effective prompts remains a
      labor-intensive and domain-specific task, requiring human expertise and subjective judgment. As
034
      models evolve and tasks vary, the need to repeatedly design prompts raises an important question:
035
      Can prompt engineering be automated to streamline this process and enhance scalability?
036
037   Automatically generating optimal prompts is a key challenge in the era of LLMs (Pryzant et al.,
038   2023; Zhou et al., 2023). Some approaches, such as gradient-based methods, have been used to
039
      optimize prompts by leveraging token probabilities and model gradients (Deng et al., 2022; Zhang
      et al., 2022a). However, these methods are limited to white-box (open-source) models, as they require
040
      direct access to the model’s internal mechanics (Liu et al., 2023). The most powerful LLMs today,
041
      like GPT-4 and Gemini, are typically black-box (closed-source) and accessible only through APIs,
042   making such techniques impractical and are often resource-intensive.
043
044   This necessitates gradient-free prompt optimization strategies. Recent methods have focused on enu-
045
      merating diverse prompts or refining existing ones to optimize instructions for black-box LLMs (Zhou
      et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023; Fernando et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2024). These
046
      strategies can be broadly classified into two types: continuous and discrete prompt optimization.
047
      Continuous approaches, like InstructZero (Chen et al., 2023) and Instinct (Lin et al., 2024), convert
048   prompt optimization into a continuous problem by using soft prompts. These soft prompts are fed to
049   open-source LLMs to generate instructions, which are then evaluated by the target black-box LLM.
050   The feedback is used to train a Bayesian optimizer (BO) or neural network (NN) to predict better
051   instructions. However, these methods require additional training of NNs and their performance often
052   varies based on the open-source model and task complexity. For more complex tasks, learning the op-
053   timal prompt-performance mapping becomes challenging. On the other hand, discrete methods like
      PromptBreeder (Fernando et al., 2023) and EvoPrompt (Guo et al., 2024) generate multiple prompt

067   In this paper, we propose PromptWizard (PW), a discrete prompt optimization framework for
068   black-box LLMs. PromptWizard employs a self-evolving mechanism where the LLM generates,
069   critiques, and refines its own prompts and examples, continuously improving through iterative
070   feedback and synthesis. This self-adaptive approach ensures holistic optimization by evolving both
071   the instructions and in-context learning examples for better task performance. PromptWizard
072   operates in two phases: (i) Prompt generation (one-time), where it processes a high-level problem
073   description and training samples, using LLMs to mutate, score, critique, synthesize, reason, and
074   validate prompts and examples; (ii) Inference (test-time), where the final optimized prompt and
075
      examples are applied to test samples.
076   PW’s approach follows a structured strategy (See Figure 1): ➊ First, starting with a problem
077   description and initial prompt instruction, PW generates variations of the instruction by prompting
078   LLMs to mutate it. Based on performance, the best prompt is selected. Unlike uncontrolled evolutions
079   in prior methods (Fernando et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2024), PW incorporates a critique component
080   that provides feedback, thus guiding and refining the prompt over multiple iterations. ➋ Unlike other
081
      discrete approaches, PW also optimizes in-context examples. PW selects a diverse set of examples
      from the training data, identifying positive and negative examples based on their performance with
082
      the modified prompt. Negative examples help inform further prompt refinements. ➌ Examples and
083
      instructions are sequentially optimized, using the critique to generate synthetic examples that address
084   the current prompt’s weaknesses. These examples are integrated to further refine the prompt. ➍ PW
085   generates detailed reasoning chains via Chain-of-Thought (CoT), enriching the prompt’s capacity for
086   problem-solving. ➎ PW aligns prompts with human reasoning by integrating task intent and expert
087   personas, enhancing both model performance and interpretability.

      Our work distinguishes itself from previous approaches in several key aspects: 1. Guided Explo-
089
      ration: PromptWizard introduces a feedback-driven critique-and-synthesis mechanism, refining
090   prompts based on performance insights. This guided exploration systematically improves prompt
091   quality, overcoming the randomness and inefficiencies in methods like PromptBreeder (Fernando
092   et al., 2023), OPRO (Yang et al., 2024), and EvoPrompt (Guo et al., 2024)(Section 3.1). 2. Sequen-
093   tial Optimization of Instructions and Examples: PromptWizard dynamically and iteratively
094   optimizes both prompt instructions and in-context examples in tandem, outperforming methods that
095   optimize these components in isolation. This strategy allows deeper exploitation of task-specific
096   nuances, leading to superior prompt quality (Section 3.3). 3. Efficient Example Synthesis & Error
097   Analysis: PromptWizard enhances efficiency by utilizing a compact set of diverse examples (up
098
      to 25) and leveraging error-driven self-reflection to generate synthetic examples. Combined with
      Chain-of-Thought reasoning, this approach offers robust and scalable prompt refinement, setting it
099
      apart from existing methods (Section 3.4).

      toward automating prompt creation due to the limitations
125
      of handcrafted prompts (Moradi & Samwald, 2021; Madaan & Yazdanbakhsh, 2022; Wei et al., 2022).
126
      Recent work has introduced various techniques for automating prompt generation, broadly classified
127   into continuous and discrete (Yang et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024). Below, we examine these methods,
128   their limitations, and how PromptWizard (PW) advances the field.
129
130
      Continuous Prompt Optimization. Continuous methods, such as InstructZero (Chen et al., 2023)
      and Instinct (Lin et al., 2024), treat prompt optimization as a continuous learning problem using soft
131
      prompts—trainable vectors that fine-tune responses from open-source LLMs. These soft prompts are
132
      used to generate responses, with feedback guiding the optimization through models like Bayesian
133   optimizers or neural networks. While flexible, these methods face several key limitations: (i) They
134   require additional neural network training, leading to high computational costs, (ii) Their adaptability
135   to complex tasks that need nuanced prompts is limited, as soft prompts are not human-interpretable
136   and struggle to capture the depth of task-specific reasoning, (iii) For more intricate tasks, such as
137   arithmetic reasoning, mapping the relationship between prompt structure and performance becomes
138   challenging, often leading to suboptimal or inconsistent results. Thus, while continuous methods
139   improve prompt generation, their scalability and interpretability in complex tasks remain non-trivial.
140   Discrete Prompt Optimization. Discrete methods focus on exploration by generating multiple
141   prompt versions and selecting the best among candidates. These methods rely on strategies like Monte
142   Carlo searches or evolutionary processes. For example, APE (Zhou et al., 2023) iteratively proposes
143   and selects optimal prompts through a Monte Carlo search, while PromptBreeder (Fernando et al.,
144   2023) mutates prompts using different thinking styles, evolving prompts in a self-referential manner.
145   Other methods, such as OPRO (Yang et al., 2024) and EvoPrompt (Guo et al., 2024), rely on prompt
146   mutations, evolutionary algorithms and evaluations on fixed training samples. However, discrete
147
      methods have notable drawbacks: (i) They are often query-inefficient due to their reliance on local
      search techniques, which fail to balance exploration and exploitation effectively, (ii) These methods
148
      tend to explore the prompt space randomly or through mutations without a structured mechanism for
149
      feedback, resulting in suboptimal and unguided refinement of prompts. Recent methods optimize
150   both instructions and examples in prompting, emphasizing the importance of example selection
151   through random or diversity-based or adversarial techniques (Do et al., 2024; Wan et al., 2024). In
152   contrast, PW uses a LLM to analyze and synthesize examples, dynamically enhancing prompt quality
153   and outperforming traditional fixed-criteria strategies.
154
      Comparison and Motivation for PromptWizard. PromptWizard (PW) advances beyond these
155
      limitations by introducing a self-evolving and self-adaptive mechanism that better balances explo-
156   ration and exploitation. Unlike prior methods, PW utilizes a feedback-driven critique-and-synthesis
157   process, which iteratively refines both prompt instructions and in-context examples. This feedback
158   loop, guided by performance insights, leads to more systematic and efficient exploration compared to
159   random or mutation-based strategies like those employed by PromptBreeder and EvoPrompt. Key
160   advantages of PW include: (i) Deeper Exploitation of Task Nuances: By optimizing prompts and
161   examples together, PW can capture the nuanced requirements of complex tasks that continuous
      and discrete methods often miss, (ii) Human-Interpretable and Scalable: Unlike soft prompts, PW

182   3 P ROMPT W IZARD F RAMEWORK
183   We introduce PromptWizard (PW), a general-purpose framework designed to optimize prompts
184   through a self-evolving and self-adapting mechanism (see Figure 1). PW harnesses the capabilities of
185   LLMs to iteratively synthesize, critique, and refine both prompt instructions and in-context examples,
186   tailoring them to specific tasks across diverse domains. The five key steps are described next.
187   Problem Formulation. In our approach, we start with an initial prompt instruction P e.g., “Let’s
188   think step by step to arrive at the solution of this mathematical problem"), along with a problem
189   description and a set of training samples represented as (Q, A) = {(qi , ai )}N
                                                                                    i=1 , where qi and ai are
190   input-output pairs (questions and answers). The LLM model L generates outputs with probabilities
191   pl (ai | qi , P, af , qf ), where qf and af are the few-shot examples. The goal of PromptWizard
192   is to iteratively optimize both the prompt and the few-shot examples to maximize task accuracy A,
193   which represents the model’s performance on the target task. The refined prompt P̂ should improve
194   the model’s ability to generate accurate outputs.
195   3.1      I TERATIVE R EFINEMENT OF P ROMPT I NSTRUCTIONS
196
197   The first step of the PromptWizard framework focuses on refining prompt instructions through a
198   systematic, feedback-driven process. This ensures the prompt evolves in a targeted way, addressing
199   specific task needs while avoiding unnecessary changes (see Figure 3).
200   1. MutateComponent: PW starts with an initial problem description and generates prompt
201      variations using predefined cognitive heuristics or thinking styles. These heuristics guide the LLM
202      to create diverse perspectives on the problem, ensuring varied and rich prompt instructions. For
203      example, the thinking styles might encourage questions like "How can I simplify the problem?" or
204      "What alternative perspectives exist?" This targeted generation of mutations improves the diversity
205      of prompt instructions compared to random approaches. By using a single LLM call to generate
206      several mutated prompts, PW ensures computational efficiency. Figure 5 shows examples of
207
         mutated prompts for an initial problem description on the GSM8K.
      2. ScoringComponent: Next, PW employs a scoring mechanism to evaluate the performance of
208
         the generated mutated prompts. The scoring is based on how well each prompt performs against
209
         a mini-batch of 5 training examples with ground truth. The scoring mechanism can be either
210      using traditional metrics like F1 score or an LLM as an evaluator, PW supports both. This helps
211      systematically identify the most effective prompt while filtering out underperforming ones. The
212      use of multiple mini-batches ensures robustness in the evaluation. Examples of mutated prompts
213      with their scores are shown in Figure 3 and 5.
214   3. CritiqueComponent: Once the best-performing mutated prompt is selected, PW introduces
215      a unique feedback mechanism through its critique component. The critique reviews where the
         prompt succeeded and failed by analyzing cases where the LLM struggled, such as interpreting

      3.2      I DENTIFICATION OF D IVERSE E XAMPLES
248
249   Next, we focus on identifying a diverse set of candidate examples to enhance prompt effectiveness.
250   The choice of examples is critical, as diverse representations allow LLMs to better grasp various
251
      aspects of the information presented (Rubin et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022b; Liu et al., 2022; Chen
      et al., 2024). We begin by extracting candidate examples from the dataset and employ a scoring
252
      mechanism to assess the current prompt’s effectiveness against these examples, classifying them into
253
      positive and negative categories. Positive examples demonstrate where the prompt succeeds, while
254   negative examples highlight areas for improvement. We randomly select 25 examples and iterate
255   through them to find a targeted number of effective few-shot examples, typically taking five iterations.
256   If this process does not yield the desired count, we randomly select five examples from the initial 25.
257   This targeted approach maximizes efficiency by minimizing the need to evaluate the entire dataset,
258   ensuring that the chosen examples effectively contribute to refining the prompt. The use of both
259   positive and negative examples allows for comprehensive understanding and refinement of prompts.

277   3.4      S ELF - GENERATED R EASONING AND VALIDATION
278
279   With the optimized prompt and few-shot examples, we further enhance model performance by in-
280
      corporating chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning. Building on the hypothesis that reasoning chains
      improve problem-solving abilities of the model (Wei et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a; Ye et al.,
281
      2023). Specifically, we automatically generate a detailed reasoning chain for each selected few-shot
282
      examples. (i) ReasoningComponent: This takes the selected few-shot examples and generates a
283   detailed reasoning chain for each example to facilitate problem-solving. (ii) ValidateComponent:
284   The validation component uses an LLM to check the coherence and relevance of examples (ques-
285   tions,reasoning). This process effectively filters out incorrect examples and/or hallucinated reasoning.

287   3.5      I NTEGRATION OF TASK I NTENT AND E XPERT P ERSONA
288
      To enhance task performance, PW integrates task intent and an expert persona into prompts (Figure 7).
289
      (i) Task Intent: This ensures that the model stays aligned with task requirements, particularly
290
      in specialized domains. By incorporating specific hints or keywords (Sun et al., 2023), derived
291   from the problem description, PW guides the model to apply relevant approaches. We generate
292   these cues using SynthesizeComponent, informed by initial problem description. (ii) Expert
293   Persona: To maintain consistency and relevance in LLM interactions, we incorporate an expert
294   persona into prompts (Xu et al., 2023). To maintain consistency, PW introduces an expert persona,
295   preventing response variability. This persona is generated based on the problem description and
296   ensures consistent, domain-relevant outputs. All PW components utilize LLMs, with their prompt
297   templates provided in Appendix 16 and algorithmic details in Appendix 14.

302
      Tasks & Datasets. We assess the effectiveness of PromptWizard on the widely-used BIG-Bench
303
      Instruction Induction (BBII) dataset, a benchmark for prompt optimization in recent works such as
304
      Instinct (Lin et al., 2024), InstructZero (Chen et al., 2023), and APE (Zhou et al., 2023). The dataset

308   as well as domain-specific tasks from BigBench Hard (BBH) (Suzgun et al., 2022), which includes
309   23 challenging tasks. This brings the total to 45 tasks (19 BBII, 23 BBH, 3 math tasks), covering both
310   general and domain-specific problem settings. Additional details of all datasets are in Appendix 9.
311   Baselines. We compare our PromptWizard with five representative SOTA discrete and con-
312   tinuous methods: Instinct (Lin et al., 2024), InstructZero (Chen et al., 2023), PromptBreeder
313   (PB) (Fernando et al., 2023), EvoPrompt (Guo et al., 2024), and APE (Zhou et al., 2023).

      Implementation Details. We experiment with both ChatGPT (GPT3.5Turbo) and GPT-4 as the
315
      black-box LLMs for prompt optimization in PromptWizard. All the individual components such as
316
      mutate, score, critique, reason, synthesize and validate, rely on the same LLM either GPT3.5Turbo

361   (among different methods). PromptWizard consistently outperforms other methods across various

      thresholds, maintaining the highest p(τ ) values, indicating that it consistently performs near the best
      possible accuracy across all tasks. Additional analysis is available in Appendix 11.

393   5.2   C OST A NALYSIS AGAINST VARIOUS P ROMPTING BASELINES
394
      While high accuracy is crucial, the efficiency of generating prompts is equally important. We present
395
      a detailed cost analysis demonstrating that PW not only outperforms baselines in terms of task
396   accuracy but does so with minimal computational overhead. We conduct a comprehensive evaluation
397   by computing the total number of API calls, tokens processed, and the corresponding cost (Table 4).
398
399
      Instinct and InstructZero. Instinct and InstructZero use a mix of white-box and black-box models
      to continuously optimize soft prompts, with the number of API calls linked to the iterative process
400
      needed for convergence. According to their respective papers, the best performance is typically
401
      achieved after a maximum of 165 iterations. On average, across all tasks, we observed 1,730 API
402   calls to the black-box model per task, with approximately 67 input and output (IO) tokens per call
403   for the BBII dataset. Given the token billing structure of the GPT3.5Turbo API ($0.002 per
404   1,000 tokens), the total cost per task is estimated to be around $0.23. Detailed API call and token
405   breakdowns per task are provided in Appendix 13.2.
406
      PromptBreeder (PB). PromptBreeder (PB) uses a discrete optimization approach through self-
407
      referential improvement, evolving prompts over 20–30 generations with a population size of 20. This
408   results in significant API usage, with an estimated 18,600 API calls per task (30 generations × (20
409   mutations + 20×30 evaluations)) (Fernando et al., 2023). With an average of 80 input/output tokens
410   per call, the total cost per task for the BBII dataset is approximately $2.9, making PB one of the most
411   expensive methods among the baselines.
412
      EvoPrompt. EvoPrompt, a discrete optimization method, uses evolutionary algorithms to find
413
      optimal prompts. The number of API calls follows the formula: API calls = N (population size) × T
414   (iterations) × (1 + D (development size)). For BBII tasks, with a population size of 10, 10 iterations,
415   and a development set size of 50, this results in: API calls = 10×10×(1+50) = 5,000 API calls. With
416   an average of 80 input/output tokens per call, EvoPrompt incurs a total cost of $0.8 per task, which
417   is lower than PB but still considerable compared to other methods.
418
      PromptWizard (PW). PW employs a discrete optimization, similar to PB and EvoPrompt, but
419
      introduces key components- feedback-driven guided exploration, critique and synthesis process, and
420   sequential optimization of instruction and examples- that streamline prompt exploration and focus on
421   meaningful evolution. These innovations reduce unnecessary mutations, striking an effective balance
422   between exploration and exploitation. The API calls in PW are broken down into 48 for prompt
423   refinement, 5 for example selection, 12 for sequential optimizations, and 4 for reasoning, validation,
424   intent refinement, and expert identity (Algo. 1). This totals 69 API calls, substantially fewer than
425   PB’s 18,600 and EvoPrompt’s 5,000. The average input/output tokens per task is around 360, slightly
426   higher due to the addition of COT reasoning and expert identity during prompt optimization. Despite
427   this, PromptWizard costs just $0.05 per task with 5-60x reduction in overall tokens, significantly
428
      lower than other techniques. Note that, during inference, PW’s average input tokens are ∼200, which
      is comparable to other approaches. Appendix 13.2 shows the detailed task level computations.
429
430   PromptWizard’s efficiency is highlighted by being 5x cheaper than continuous methods like
431   Instinct and InstructZero, and 16-60x cheaper compared to discrete methods like EvoPrompt and
      PromptBreeder, while achieving superior performance.

      learning scenario by randomly selecting only 5 examples from each dataset as the training set (instead
      of 25). PW utilizes these examples for all evaluations, critique feedback, and the generation of
448
      diverse synthetic examples. This setup tests the framework’s ability to generalize and create robust,
449
      task-relevant prompts with minimal data.

452   when trained with only 5 examples (5 eg) compared to 25 examples (25 eg). Despite the drastic
453
      reduction in training data, PromptWizard demonstrates impressive resilience, exhibiting only a
      marginal 5% drop in accuracy on average. This resilience underscores the model’s adaptability,
454
      driven by two key mechanisms: (i) Synthetic Example Generation using critique-and-synthesize,
455
      which produces diverse, high-quality examples from limited inputs, reducing the impact of data
456   scarcity; and (ii) Reasoning Chain Guidance, where structured reasoning chains enhance the LLM’s
457   ability to generate accurate, contextually relevant responses.

459   6.2   P ROMPT W IZARD WITH S MALLER LLM S FOR P ROMPT O PTIMIZATION
460   In prior experiments, GPT3.5Turbo was used for both prompt generation and optimization. In this
461   section, we explore the feasibility of employing a smaller LLM, such as Llama-70B, for prompt
462   generation while reserving a more capable model like GPT-4 for inference. This approach reduce
463   computational costs during prompt optimization by leveraging the efficiency of smaller models
464   while still maximizing task accuracy with powerful model during inference. This strategy offers
465   two key advantages: (i) Computational Efficiency: Smaller LLMs like Llama-70B require fewer
466
      resources, making them ideal for generating prompts in resource-constrained environments. (ii) Task
      Performance: Despite using a smaller model for prompt generation, inference benefits from the larger
467
      GPT-4 model’s ability to interpret and execute the optimized prompt, ensuring minimal degradation.
468
469   Table 6 compares task accuracy across multiple datasets when Llama-70B is used for prompt
470   generation versus the default GPT-4. Impressively, the final prompts generated by PromptWizard
471   using Llama-70B show a negligible <1% drop in accuracy compared to those generated with
472
      GPT-4, highlighting PromptWizard ’s effectiveness even with smaller models. While we ex-
      perimented with smaller models like Llama-3-8B, they struggled to generate complex instructions,
473
      leading to significant performance degradation. Thus, mid-sized LLMs like Llama-70B are rec-
474
      ommended for prompt optimization, striking a balance between computational efficiency and task
475   performance. These findings demonstrate PromptWizard’s adaptability and its ability to maintain
476   high performance across different model sizes with minimal loss.

508   7    C ONCLUSIONS
509
510   This work introduces PromptWizard, a general-purpose framework for automating prompt and
511   example synthesis. By striking a balance between exploration and exploitation through a feedback-
512
      driven critique and synthesis process, PW systematically refines prompts and in-context examples
      to enhance task performance.Extensive evaluations across diverse datasets show it consistently out-
513
      performs state-of-the-art methods, demonstrating strong efficacy even with limited training data and
514
      smaller LLMs, with only a marginal drop in accuracy. Ablation studies highlight the importance
515   of each stage in refining prompts, generating diverse examples, and improving reasoning. Our
516   comprehensive cost analysis highlights significant reductions in API calls, token usage, and overall
517   expenses, showcasing PW’s cost-effectiveness—it is 5x cheaper than continuous optimization meth-
518   ods and 16-60x cheaper than discrete methods, all while delivering superior performance. This work
519   democratizes access to effective prompt engineering, enabling more efficient and accurate utilization
520   of LLMs across various domains and applications. Future work will focus on refining the validation
521   of synthetic examples and applying PW to real-world, resource-constrained environments.
522   Limitations: While we have conducted extensive experiments across a diverse set of tasks, careful
523
      validation is required for new tasks to ensure adaptability. Prompt response testing is essential before
      real-world deployment to verify effectiveness. Additionally, while PromptWizard automates prompt
524
      engineering, human expertise remains indispensable in guiding and refining the optimization process.
