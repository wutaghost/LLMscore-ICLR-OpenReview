008                                               A BSTRACT
009
010            As Large Language Models (LLMs) continue to expand in both variety and cost,
011            selecting the most appropriate model for each query is becoming increasingly
012
               crucial. Many existing works treat this as an offline problem, necessitating a data-
               gathering phase to compile a set of query-answer-reward triplets beforehand. They
013
               often struggle to determine the adequate number of triplets needed and are prone to
014
               overfitting if the data volume is insufficient. To address these limitations, we pro-
015            pose a new solution, the Multi-Armed Router (MAR), which applies multi-armed
016            bandit theory—a perspective previously unexplored in this domain. Unlike previ-
017            ous works that base decision-making solely on regression techniques using static
018            datasets (i.e., constructed triplets), our method treats this as an online multi-LLM
019            recommendation problem, which better mirrors real-world applications. More-
020            over, rather than the vanilla multi-armed bandit, our framework employs contex-
021            tual bandit algorithms to navigate the trade-offs between exploring new models
022            and exploiting proven models, while considering the dependency between the in-
023
               put query and the answer’s reward. Due to the lack of an off-the-shelf dataset
               in this area, we construct WildArena, a dataset of 4,029 real-world user queries.
024
               For each query, there are seven open-ended responses derived from seven leading
025
               LLMs, respectively, with an evaluation score for each answer by using the LLM-
026            as-a-Judge framework. We hope that the introduction of the new perspective and
027            the dataset will facilitate the research in per-query LLM routing. Code and data
028            will be made publicly available.

031   1   INTRODUCTION
032
033   The growing interest in Large Language Models (LLMs) has spawned a wide variety of models,
034   each differing in performance and inference costs. These costs drive the need for frameworks that
035   can assess both the performance quality and cost-effectiveness of these models effectively. The goal
036   is to pinpoint the most suitable LLM for specific queries. Defined as the multi-LLM recommenda-
037   tion problem, this process involves selecting the most appropriate LLM for a query based on both
038
      performance quality and cost efficiency. This need stems from the considerable variability in LLM
      performance and cost for different tasks. For simple queries that require straightforward responses,
039
      less expensive, smaller LLMs are often sufficient, as the more complex outputs from larger LLMs
040
      might not offer extra value in such cases.
041
042   The complexity of inference can vary significantly between queries that represent much the same
043   task. The query ’What is the asset value of Enron?’ was simple to answer until August 2001, and
044
      the query ’Which country is Hawaii part of?’ is simpler to answer than ’Which country is Antarctica
      part of?’. Static assignment of tasks to models, although simple, is unable to reflect the inevitable
045
      per-query variation in inference complexity.
046
047   The financial and environmental cost of inference on different LLMs varies by orders of magnitude.
048   The difference is particularly stark when considering mobile platforms, where on-device models
049   also have a significant latency advantage. As LLM inference inevitably becomes more common the
050
      associated costs will grow to the point where choosing the right model for a particular query will
      become essential.
051
052   One straightforward approach to this recommendation task involves collecting a large dataset of
053   queries and their corresponding response ratings from various target LLMs. This dataset then serves
      to train a model, either through an end-to-end structure (Lu et al., 2023) or by using simpler architec-


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      tures such as a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) (Hu et al., 2024) to establish these associations. Once
055   the training captures the essence of the routing function, the model is capable of making predictions
056   for new incoming queries based on the trained model’s insights. However, this process demands
057   an exhaustive exploration of all potential queries and all LLM candidates, which is typically pro-
058   hibitively costly. Moreover, due to the variability in real-world scenarios, the performance of these
059   trained models on specific queries can often be erratic.
060
      An alternative strategy is to improve response quality through augmentation. FrugalGPT (Chen
061   et al., 2023), employs a cascade of LLMs ranging from smaller, less expensive models to larger, more
062   costly ones, terminating the sequence when the response quality meets a satisfactory threshold. This
063   ensures that simpler inquiries are answered using less costly models, thereby conserving resources.
064   The method of (Li et al., 2024), in contrast, involves using a series of enhanced queries combined
065   with the majority voting to derive a superior response. These methods can reduce the costs associated
066   with inference and enhance response quality, but they often require multiple inference cycles, and
067   they bear the associated costs.
068   A practical recommendation framework must be cost-efficient and capable of performing its task
069   without access to the model’s internal parameters. This enables the use of proprietary models and
070   also requires the framework to dynamically adapt to a wide range of rapidly changing inputs. Tra-
071   ditional regression approaches often assume the presence of an ideal routing oracle and attempt to
072   replicate it through regression on training datasets. The variable and unpredictable nature of real-
073   world queries makes constructing a comprehensive static dataset difficult to achieve in practice.
074   Conventional static regression-based LLM recommenders struggle to identify the correct model in
075   the face of the inevitable topic drift of real world queries. Dynamic augmentation approaches ad-
076   dress this challenge but at the cost performing multiple inferences per query even when the same
077   query is submitted repeatedly. Such approaches are often described in terms of the trade off between
078   exploration and exploitation. The regression approach reflects a strategy of exhaustive static explo-
079   ration, which is an extremum of the explore-exploit trade-off. The augmentation method does not
080   use the results of previous queries to build a model to inform its decisions and thus represents an
081
      exploitation-focused strategy.
082   Our insight here is that the LLM recommendation problem mirrors the explore-exploit chal-
083   lenge (Thompson, 1933) that has been addressed repeatedly in developing other recommender sys-
084   tems. Exploration in this context corresponds to expending effort building a model that will predict
085   the best LLM, potentially by gathering data through evaluating queries. Exploitation corresponds
086   to performing inference on a recommended LLM to generate a response to a user query. The trade-
087
      off arises because building the perfect prediction model will waste effort, but a poor prediction
      model will recommend the wrong LLM. Multiple strategies exist that aim to perform only as much
088
      exploration as is required to support successful exploitation. By dynamically navigating between
089
      exploration and exploitation these methods avoid the pitfalls of both extremes(Schulz et al., 2018).
090
091   We propose the Multi-Armed Router (MAR), which employs a multi-armed bandit algorithm to
092   balance exploration and exploitation in per-query LLM selection. One of the practical advantages
093
      of this approach is that interacts on a query level without needing to access any other aspects of
      the selectable LLMs. Our primary contribution consists of adopting this methodology and proving
094
      its advantages. These include a significant reduction in costs, enhanced flexibility, and increased
095
      usability compared to earlier methods. Future research could potentially delve into improved tech-
096   niques for navigation between exploration and exploitation, along with the development of robust
097   neural networks to boost recommendations.
098
099
      We also examine the extensive diversity and substantial variability in subject matter that typify real-
      life settings. Previous investigations (Shnitzer et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2024) focused predominantly on
100
      evaluating specific datasets such as MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020), GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021),
101
      and MBPP (Austin et al., 2021), which encompass deliberately formulated questions aimed at partic-
102   ular domains, including undergraduate content, mathematics, and programming. Nevertheless, there
103   is a discernible lack of datasets comprising queries with open-ended responses derived from real-
104   world situations. To address this gap, we randomly selected 4029 queries from the chatbot-arena
105   (Zheng et al., 2024) and WildChat (Zhao et al., 2024), which collect data through actual user inter-
106   actions. We evaluated the responses using 7 LLMs from both open-source and proprietary sources
107   of varying sizes and utilized LLM-as-a-judge to score the responses. This newly established dataset

123   2.1   C ONTEXTUAL BANDITS
124
125
      Multi-armed bandits (MAB) are often employed to address the explore-vs-exploit dilemma that com-
      monly arises in various decision-making scenarios. Methods built on this framework vary from the
126
      classical ϵ-greedy technique (Langford & Zhang, 2007) to advanced strategies such as UCB explo-
127
      ration (Kaufmann et al., 2012). The MAB model has been successfully applied in numerous areas,
128   including recommendation systems and reinforcement learning. To overcome nonlinear problems
129   and incorporate supervised elements, recent research (Collier & Llorens, 2018; Zhou et al., 2020;
130   Zhang et al., 2020) has integrated neural networks within the MAB framework. This development
131   allows for the implementation of more intricate and nonlinear functions for estimating rewards, a
132   method that our study also adopts.
133
134   2.2   S UPERVISED LLM ROUTERS
135
136   In the field of LLM routing, researchers have explored different methods to improve efficiency and
137   performance. ZOOTER (Lu et al., 2023), for instance, combines multiple LLMs, each specializing
138   in a different area. The system uses a backpropagation of rewards to effectively train a routing
139   function, which predicts the best LLM to use during inference, without needing to involve all LLMs.
140   However, this method is limited to open-source models because it requires end-to-end training.
141
      Meanwhile, RouterBench (Hu et al., 2024) employs a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) to determine the
      indirect associations between queries and their respective response scores. This method is applicable
142
      to both open source and proprietary models, as it assesses the quality of responses using a structured
143
      scoring mechanism. Both ZOOTER and RouterBench require a thorough initial exploration of all
144   possible LLMs resulting a heavy data collection phase. This not only leads to high costs but also
145   restricts the adaptability of the systems, as they heavily depend on the initial exploration phase to
146   adjust their exploitation strategies. This could result in unpredictable performance, especially when
147   dealing with queries outside the initially trained domains. These challenges highlight the need for
148   more dynamic and cost-effective solutions in the deployment of LLM routers.
149
150   2.3   R ECOMMENDATION VIA QUERY AUGMENTATION
151
152   FrugalGPT (Chen et al., 2023) utilizes a cascaded model where responses are sequentially elicited
153   from a hierarchy of language models, starting with smaller, less expensive ones and escalating to
154   larger, more costly models. This progression is controlled by a trained scoring system that deter-
155   mines when the quality of the response is adequate to cease further inquiries. The success of this ap-
156   proach largely depends on the scoring mechanism’s accuracy, and inevitably requires extra inference
157   steps beyond the first appearance of the acceptable answer. As identified in LLM-as-a-Judge (Zheng
158
      et al., 2024), the evaluation capacity of these systems is often constrained by the performance limi-
      tations of the judging model, resulting in poor judgment capabilities for less sophisticated models.
159
160   (Li et al., 2024) introduces multiple prompts into a single query to generate diverse responses from
161   an LLM, subsequently employing majority voting to identify the most satisfactory response. This
      method offers flexibility and does not require prior information. It is therefore potentially extensible

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      to multiple LLMs and real-time adjustments. It incurs a higher operational cost per query, however,
163   and does not address the important case where the LLM is consistent in producing the wrong answer.

      2.4     LLM E VALUATION DATASETS
166
167   Various datasets exist for closed-ended evaluation, comprising queries with either a single or a
168   collection of ground truth responses. Notable among these are MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020),
169   GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021), and MBPP (Austin et al., 2021). The intrinsic requirement for defini-
170   tive answers in these datasets typically restricts their topics to areas such as knowledge, facts, math-
171   ematics, and programming. Such datasets fail to evaluate the capacities of different LLMs in per-
172   forming tasks like composing email replies, commenting, or giving suggestions, which are critical
173   to user interactions. In response, novel methodologies that utilize LLMs as evaluators have emerged
174
      (Zheng et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2023b; Kim et al., 2023). These approaches validate the alignment
      of LLM judgments with those of human evaluators and facilitate extensive automatic scoring for
175
      response quality in open-ended real-world situations. This was a critical development in enabling
176
      more realistic open-ended evaluation, and part of the motivation for WildArena. This dataset em-
177   bodies open-ended queries, tailored for a Multi-LLM recommendation system that aims to optimize
178   the quality of responses to open-ended questions.

276   3.3   DATASET
277
278   A crucial component is the dataset employed to assess such an algorithm. An apt dataset for the
279   Multi-LLM recommendation task encompasses a compilation of queries, various inferences from
280   distinct LLMs addressing these queries, and the pertinent scores for each response. For evaluation
281   purposes, continually invoking LLMs for inference and scoring is not feasible. Consequently, a
282   pre-compiled dataset containing the inference results of LLMs is essential to simulate actual usage
283
      scenarios. The dataset that most closely meets these criteria at the moment is RouterBench (Hu
      et al., 2024), which aggregates queries from various established LLM benchmarks and conducts
284
      inference using 11 LLM models.
285
286   Nevertheless, the bulk of these inquiries predominantly revolves around scholarly knowledge, log-
287   ical reasoning, mathematical challenges, and programming tasks. Such assignments usually come
288   with precise, correct answers and are carefully chosen for their uniqueness and scarcity, which no-
289
      tably differs from the type and frequency of questions encountered in everyday user interactions.
      Conversely, solutions to questions from the real world often fall into a broad set, lacking a singular
290
      optimal answer. Consequently, we adopt a strategy proposed in (Zheng et al., 2024) where we uti-
291
      lize an LLM in a judging capacity, evaluating all responses collectively to establish a graded ranking
292   of answers.
293
294   More specifically, we randomly selected 4, 029 queries from a pooled dataset combining chatbot-
295
      Anera (Zheng et al., 2024) and WildChat (Zhao et al., 2024), both of which are derived from
      real-world user interactions, and performed evaluations using seven LLMs recognized for leading
296
      performance in their respective scale among both open-source and proprietary variants.
297
298   The open-source models include: Qwen-32B (Bai et al., 2023) and Starling-7B (Zhu et al., 2023a).
299   The proprietary models comprise: GPT-3.5-0125, GPT-4-0125 (Achiam et al., 2023), Claude3-
300   Opus, Claude3-Sonnet, and Claude3-Haiku (Anthropic, 2024).
301
302
      As the judging model, we used Gemini 1.5 Pro (Reid et al., 2024) to eliminate any self-enhancing
      biases. We conducted five rounds of evaluation, averaging the scores to produce the final result. Fur-
303
      ther details on evaluating multiple queries concurrently are provided in the supplementary materials.

      4.3   C OMPARISON WITH CONVENTIONAL METHODS
412
413   We also present conceptual comparisons of our MAR methodology against former techniques, as
414   illustrated in Fig. 4. The depicted policy applies a ‘pure-performance’ cost metric. Moreover,
415   the regressive approach incorporates a data-gathering phase where each received response must be
416   evaluated using a scoring system to assess performance. Consequently, this method necessitates a
417   comprehensive exploratory phase prior to any exploitation activities. The study titled ’More agents’
418   (Li et al., 2024) employs an augmented query and voting mechanism. This method benefits from
419   not requiring post-process scoring, which often depends on LLMs for evaluation, leading to a more
420   gradual increase in cumulative regret compared to the regressive approach. Nonetheless, given that
421
      augmentation is required throughout the entire process, the cumulative regret eventually surpasses
      that of the regressive approach. In analyzing FrugalGPT under ideal conditions—where the scoring
422

453
454   The MAR approach provides several benefits for Multi-LLM recommendation tasks, including cost-
455   efficiency, quick convergence, adaptability, and practicality. Despite these advantages, the perfor-
456   mance achieved by MAR is not as substantial as desired, falling short of the theoretical optimum,
457   which would involve accurately routing all incoming queries.
458   We propose that this limited performance gain can be attributed to the inherent variability of the
459   queries. In Fig. 5, we visualized the queries by sorting them based on the distance of their em-
460   beddings to their nearest neighbors. The red dots on the left represent the most closely clustered
461   queries, while those on the right represent the most widely dispersed ones. Our analysis reveals that
462   queries with tightly clustered embeddings exhibit significantly lower cumulative regret compared to
463   those with more scattered embeddings. This observation implies that the available data may not pro-
464   vide adequate information for the router to make consistently accurate decisions. In such scenarios,
465   additional data would be required for the system to operate effectively.
466   Given these limitations, MAR’s cost-effective exploration-exploitation strategy offers a notable ad-
467   vantage over previous, more resource-intensive methods. This makes MAR a promising approach
468   for Multi-LLM recommendation tasks, particularly in situations where data availability is limited or
469   the cost of obtaining additional data is prohibitive.

489   that utilizes the multi-armed bandit approach to optimize performance and minimize costs without
490   accessing the internal elements of the LLMs. The multi-armed bandit approach is not only well
491   understood theoretically, it allows explicit control over the explore-exploit trade off. Additionally,
492   we present WildArena, a novel dataset comprising 4029 open-ended queries and their corresponding
493   responses from multiple models, which aims to support the development and evaluation of Multi-
