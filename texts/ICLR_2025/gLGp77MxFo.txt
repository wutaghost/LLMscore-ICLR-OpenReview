011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013           Integrating AI and human expertise can significantly enhance decision-making
014           across various scenarios. This paper introduces a novel approach that leverages
015           the Product of Experts (PoE) model to optimize decision-making by strategically
016           combining AI with human inputs. While human experts bring diverse perspectives,
017           their decisions may be constrained by biases or knowledge gaps. To address these
018           limitations, we propose an AI agent that provides probabilistic, rule-based insights,
019           complementing and filling human experts’ knowledge gaps. A key feature of
020           our approach is the strategic selection of human experts based on how well their
021
              knowledge complements or enhances the AI’s recommendations. By dynamically
              adapting the expert selection process, we ensure that decisions benefit from the
022
              most impactful and complementary inputs. Our PoE model calibrates inputs from
023
              both AI and human experts, leveraging their combined strengths to improve deci-
024           sion outcomes. Furthermore, operating in an online setting, our framework can
025           also continuously update the AI’s knowledge and refine expert selection criteria,
026           ensuring adaptability to evolving environments. Experiments in simulation envi-
027           ronments demonstrate that our model effectively integrates logic rule-informed AI
028           with human expertise, enhancing collaborative decision-making.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      In decision-making across various domains, human expertise is invaluable, but AI is increasingly
035
      being leveraged to augment and enhance these processes. Rather than replacing human specialists, as
036   noted by Duan et al. (2019), a more promising approach is to combine AI with human knowledge,
037   enhancing decision outcomes by utilizing the strengths of both. This collaboration between AI and
038   human expertise has already proven beneficial in fields such as human resources (Davenport et al.,
039   2010), marketing (Huang & Rust, 2022), and clinical radiology (Futoma et al., 2017; Bien et al.,
040   2018), enabling more informed, comprehensive, and reliable decisions.
041
      Consider the challenge of diagnosing and treating rare diseases in healthcare. Multidisciplinary
042   treatment (MDT) is crucial in these cases, as it combines expertise across various fields to address
043   the complexities of rare conditions. Despite this, individual biases and cognitive blind spots of
044   human experts can still lead to suboptimal decisions. An AI doctor could play a critical role here
045   by complementing human doctors’ knowledge, filling gaps, and offering new perspectives based on
046   its vast domain-specific data. This collaboration has the potential to improve diagnostic accuracy
047   and treatment outcomes. Similarly, in academic peer review, bias and inconsistency can impact
048   evaluations of research articles due to reviewers’ subjective preferences and varied expertise. By
049   integrating AI reviewers into this review process, we may bring objective, probabilistic insights to
050
      complement human judgment, improving fairness and efficiency in the review process.
051   Yet, key questions arise: How should AI be effectively integrated into expert teams? How can AI
052   and human decisions be combined to ensure optimal outcomes? And how can we ensure AI plays a
053   positive, complementary role without introducing new risks or biases? In this paper, we propose an
      AI-human collective decision-making framework to address these questions.

054
      Our framework features a logic-informed AI agent designed to be transparent and interpretable. By
055   grounding its decision-making process in rule-based logic, we aim to enhance human experts’ trust in
056   AI’s recommendations and foster more effective collaboration with human experts.
057
058
      At the core of our framework is the Product of Experts (PoE) model (Hinton, 2002; Cao & Fleet, 2014),
      which synthesizes AI and human inputs in a novel and elegant way. Unlike traditional ensemble
059
      methods such as bagging and boosting, PoE blends AI’s probabilistic rule-based insights with the
060
      often more intuitive, experience-based judgments of human experts. This integration harmonizes
061   diverse perspectives, ensuring that AI enhances decision-making without overriding human expertise.
062   Additionally, a confusion matrix is used to assess and estimate the reliability of human contributors,
063   enabling a more informed combination of AI and expert input.
064
      Crucially, our framework incorporates an active perception mechanism for selecting human experts
065
      from a diverse pool, such as medical specialists within a hospital or across different institutions. This
066
      ensures that only the most informative and complementary expertise is utilized for each case. In our
067   framework, the AI first generates initial recommendations, such as treatment options for a patient.
068   Based on these recommendations, the system identifies and selects human experts whose insights
069   complement and enhance the AI’s output. Leveraging information gain, the framework automatically
070   filters out less effective experts and adaptively chooses the most suitable ones for various scenarios
071   and patients, such as those with specific rare conditions or complex needs, grouping them according
072   to their strengths. The overall framework of the AI-human collaborative decision-making system
073   operates in an online setting. As more patient data becomes available, our algorithm can further
074   expand the AI’s cognitive regions and refine its understanding of each expert’s specialized areas,
075
      continuously improving decision-making, generalization, and predictive accuracy (Hoi et al., 2021).
      The architecture of our framework, illustrated in Fig. 1, highlights these innovations.
076
077   To summarize, our main contributions are as follows:
078
079   • We introduce a novel human-AI collaborative decision-making framework that integrates a logic-
080
        informed AI agent with the PoE model. This probabilistic model combines AI and human expertise,
        leveraging collective insights to enhance decision quality and reliability. The logic-informed AI
081
        agent ensures transparency and interpretability, grounding decisions in rule-based logic.
082
083   • Our framework features an active perception module that optimally selects human experts based on
084
        the estimation of their expertise. This module ensures that only the most relevant and impactful
        expertise is utilized, improving decision-making efficiency.
085
086   • We provide both theoretical analyses and empirical evidence to support the effectiveness of our
087     framework. We demonstrate how the PoE model with the logic-informed AI agent and the active
088
        perception module improves decision accuracy and robustness. Additionally, we illustrate how the
        framework’s adaptability to evolving data and environments is supported by empirical validation.

093   Ensembles and Opinion Pools Prior research has convincingly demonstrated the performance
094   advantages of leveraging multiple predictors over a single predictor. This principle is evident in
095   both model combinations (Kittler et al., 1998; Bagui, 2005; Sagi & Rokach, 2018) and human
096   opinion aggregations (Hong & Page, 2004; Lamberson & Page, 2012). Majority voting (Dietterich,
097
      2000) and naive Bayes aggregation (Xu et al., 1992) are prevalent methods for aggregating non-
      probabilistic classifiers. However, majority voting may fall short in accuracy enhancement with a
098
      limited number of predictors, and naive Bayes aggregation, while effective at the class level, does
099
      not fully exploit instance-level uncertainties presented by probabilistic labelers. In the realm of
100   human opinion ensembling, methods range from additive linear and log-linear opinion pools for
101   subjective distributions (Genest & Zidek, 1986), to techniques for weighting linear combinations of
102   continuous human predictions (Davis-Stober et al., 2015), and voting strategies for consolidating
103   label predictions from multiple human predictors (Lee & Lee, 2017). Our work differentiates itself
104   by focusing on the integration of label-based human decisions with probabilistic model predictions,
105   aiming to optimize the combination of these distinct sources of input.

154   In this section, we introduce our AI-human collaborative decision-making framework. We will use
155   the multidisciplinary treatment (MDT) setting commonly encountered in healthcare as an example.
156   In MDT, experts from diverse fields collaborate to diagnose and treat patients with complex medical
157   conditions. This scenario naturally requires a collective approach that brings together varied expertise
158   for optimal care.
159
160   Problem Setup At each decision point, the system is presented with a patient characterized by
161   features x ∈ X , and it must select an action a ∈ A (e.g., a treatment option). The final decision on the
      action a is made collectively by a human medical team with the support of an AI agent. The human

      with human opinions, effectively combining diverse perspectives. We provide a simple theoretical

182   3.1   D ESIGN A L OGIC -I NFORMED AI AGENT
183
184   In healthcare, domain knowledge, such as detailed disease pathophysiology, treatment strategies, and
185   clinical guidelines, is often represented as a compact set of logic rules. These rules can represent
186   critical medical insights, including diagnostic criteria, therapeutic interventions, and best practices
187
      for managing complex or rare conditions. We will explore how to incorporate this rich medical
      knowledge as prior information to construct a logic-informed probabilistic AI agent. This AI agent,
188
      enhanced with domain-specific expertise, aims to bridge the knowledge gaps of human doctors.
189
190   We develop our logic-informed AI agent using the classic Plackett-Luce model (Maystre & Gross-
191   glauser, 2015), which is commonly used to model the probability of selecting an option from a set,
192   based on the relative utility of each option. The core idea is to utilize the rule-based features to
193
      construct a utility function, which will then guide the AI agent in making probabilistic decisions.
194
      Logic Rules for Feature Construction The domain knowledge is encoded as Horn rules, defining
195
      the conditions under which particular actions should be taken. For example, some of the Horn rule
196
      examples are as follows:
197
198            Rule 1: If a patient shows specific symptoms, then a drug should be prescribed.
199            Rule 2: If a patient previously responded positively to treatment, continue with the same
200            treatment.
201
               Rule 3: If a patient is an adult with an underlying condition, consider surgery.
202

204   action a ∈ A, such as a ∈ {Drug, Surgery, . . . }, to take. Specifically, for each action a, we construct

257   Advantages of PoE in Collective Intelligence The PoE model offers an elegant approach to inte-
258   grating AI and human expert opinions by multiplying and renormalizing their probability distributions.
259   The PoE type of ensemble model provides several key appealing features:
260   1) Reinforcing good actions: In the region of features where all participants are highly accurate, i.e.,
261
      the probabilities pl (a | hl (x) = ul ) and pAI (a | x) are closer to one, PoE model (4) exponentially
      amplifies the probability of the correct action, ensuring near-optimal decisions in those areas.
262
      2) Robust to influence from weak experts: In regions of the feature space where not all experts are
263
      strong, the PoE model effectively balances their contributions. By preventing less confident experts
264   from disproportionately influencing the outcome, the model ensures that the overall decision remains
265   driven by the stronger experts. This stands in contrast to other ensemble models, where weak experts
266   may dilute the decision. For a more detailed discussion, refer to Appendix C.
267   3) Enabling a sequential expert selection with uncertainty quantification. Thanks to its probabilistic
268   nature, the PoE model enables us to identify and select the most informative and complementary
269   experts, optimizing the decision-making process by minimizing decision entropy, as we will establish
      in Section 3.3.

298   3.3   TARGETED E XPERT S ELECTION WITH ACTIVE P ERCEPTION
299
300   Our AI-human PoE model has a natural advantage in quantifying uncertainty, which provides
301   an additional benefit: targeted expert selection. For example, in a healthcare setting, this means
302   that rather than consulting a large panel of experts—an approach that can be both inefficient and
303   resource-intensive—we can strategically choose the experts whose insights are most pertinent to the
304   patient’s condition. This targeted approach not only reduces the time and cost associated with expert
305
      consultations but also enhances the accuracy and relevance of the final recommendations by focusing
      on the experts who provide the most valuable contributions.
306
307   We propose the following sequential expert selection scheme. The process begins with the AI agent
308   making an initial decision by generating a conditional probability distribution over actions using its
309   rule-based model (3). Based on this distribution, the system selects a human expert from the pool
310   who is expected to provide the greatest information gain or reduce the current entropy of the decision
311
      model, i.e., the PoE model. Once the expert is consulted, the system updates the PoE distribution
      to incorporate their opinion. This process is repeated iteratively, with the system selecting the next
312
      expert from the remaining pool until either the entropy of the decision model, which measures the
313
      ambiguity of the recommendation, falls below a predefined threshold, or a specified number of experts
314   have been consulted.
315

349   4    M ODEL L EARNING
350
351   In this section, we discuss how to update the AI agent model (as defined in Eqs. (1) and (3)) and
352   the human expert reliability model (as defined in Eq. (5)). By continuously refining these model
353   parameters as data accumulates, we ensure our AI-human collaborative framework aligns with
354   real-world needs, enhancing its accuracy and reliability in decision-making.
355   Learning AI-Agent Given the observed data (xi , ai , ri ), i = 1, . . . , n, where xi is the patient
356   feature, ai is the executed action, and ri is the observed reward, we will learn or refine the logic rule
357   set Γ and their associated weights {wa }a∈A by maximum likelihood estimation (MLE). However,
358   the original MLE problem involves continuous updates of rule weights alongside a discrete rule
359   set search, which is inherently a combinatorial problem. To tackle this complexity, we employ a
360   column generation method, starting with a smaller, manageable problem that reduces the search space
361   significantly. We iteratively identify new rules and update their weights, gradually expanding the
362   search space as we advance. For a detailed description of the AI agent model learning algorithm,
363
      please refer to Appendix A.
364   Learning Confusion Matrix of Human Experts We also address the update of the human expert
365   reliability model, specifically the confusion matrices {ψ l }l=1,...,L . In scenarios with limited data,
366   estimating the reliability of human agents (via confusion matrices) becomes challenging. To address
367   this, we use a Bayesian approach and employ the Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) estimator for each
368   parameter, assuming a Beta prior for the probability estimates. This allows for more robust estimates,
369   even with small datasets. The detailed derivation and methodology are provided in the Appendix. B.

      In this paper, we proposed a novel AI-human collaborative decision-making framework using a
533
      Product of Experts (PoE) model to dynamically integrate AI and human expertise. Our approach
534   strategically selects human experts based on their ability to complement the AI’s rule-based insights,

536   validation of our method, demonstrating its superiority over traditional ensemble techniques and
537   human-only approaches, particularly in complex, real-world-inspired scenarios. Additionally, we
538   present a feasible mechanism for improving the AI agent online, allowing continuous adaptation to
539   evolving environments. By ensuring transparency and interpretability through a logic-informed AI
      agent, our work pushes the boundaries of human-AI collaboration.

       A notable limitation of our proposed model lies in the rule-learning module of the branch-and-price-
1083   based column generation algorithm. While it effectively identifies rules that capture partial aspects of
1084   the ground truth, it occasionally fails to fully match clinical realities. Although stringent, these exact
1085   matches hold significant importance in clinical contexts where precision is paramount for diagnosis
1086   and treatment. In such scenarios, tailored strategies require more accurate rule-learning capabilities,
1087   highlighting the need for improvement in the robustness of our approach.
1088
       In real-world applications, the interaction between doctors and AI often occurs within a multi-
1089   agent system, where experts may take into account the perspectives of their peers and the AI’s
1090   recommendations before reaching a final decision. This introduces a more interconnected and complex
1091   decision-making environment than our current framework, which treats each expert independently.
1092   Furthermore, while our framework supports human intervention to modify or remove high-risk rules,
1093   this manual process can be time-consuming and requires substantial expertise. Future work could
1094   focus on developing more user-friendly interfaces and automated tools to assist human experts in this
1095   task, potentially increasing both efficiency and adoption.
1096   A promising direction for future research involves the introduction of hypernetworks to enable
1097   differentiable rule learning, which could improve the accuracy of rule discovery. However, purely
1098   data-driven approaches without expert knowledge may introduce noise and fail to capture patient-
1099   specific features. Therefore, combining knowledge-based and data-driven frameworks could enhance
1100   the robustness and accuracy of rule learning in clinical settings. Moreover, incorporating a human-in-
1101   the-loop algorithm would allow for the flexible integration of expert opinions in rule learning, further
1102   improving security and stability. While our column-generation algorithm produces relatively stable
1103
       rules, these enhancements could better align the model with real-world complexities.
