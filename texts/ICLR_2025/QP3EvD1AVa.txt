009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Commonsense reasoning is fundamentally based on multimodal knowledge.
012            However, large language models (LLMs), trained using textual data only, are
013            limited with their ability to incorporate essential visual information. In contrast,
014            Visual Language Models (VLMs), which excel at visually-oriented tasks, often
015            fail at non-visual tasks such as textual commonsense reasoning. This divergence
016            highlights a critical challenge - the integration of robust visual understanding with
017            foundational text-based reasoning. To this end, we introduce a method aimed at
018
               enhancing LLMs’ visual commonsense while maintaining textual modeling and
               commonsense reasoning performance. Specifically, our method generates multi-
019
               ple images based on the input text prompt and integrates these into the model’s
020
               decision-making process by mixing their prediction probabilities. To facilitate
021            multimodal grounded language modeling, we employ a late-fusion layer that com-
022            bines the projected visual features with the output of a pre-trained LLM condi-
023            tioned on text only. This late-fusion layer enables predictions based on compre-
024            hensive image-text knowledge as well as text only when required. We evaluate our
025            approach using several visual commonsense reasoning tasks together with tradi-
026            tional NLP tasks, including common sense reasoning and reading comprehension.
027            Our experimental results demonstrate significant superiority over existing base-
028            lines. When applied to recent state-of-the-art LLMs (e.g., Llama3), we observe
029
               improvements not only in visual commonsense but also in NLP benchmarks.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
      Large language models (LLMs) have shown significant success in advancing a variety of natu-
034
      ral language understanding and generation tasks (Devlin et al., 2019; Radford et al., 2019; Zhang
035
      et al., 2022b; Team et al., 2024; Touvron et al., 2023). As human knowledge is grounded in multi-
036   modal information, Vision Language Models (VLMs) have emerged, incorporating both images and
037   text (Alayrac et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023b;a; Li et al., 2023a; Dai et al., 2023; Cha et al., 2024), thus
038   enabling significant advances in multimodal tasks such as visual commonsense and visual question
039   answering (Zhang et al., 2022a; Xia et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b; Jin et al., 2024). However, while
040   VLMs excel at visually-oriented tasks, this success often comes at the expense of their performance
041   on non-visual tasks such as basic commonsense reasoning. This divergence highlights a critical
042   challenge - the integration of robust visual understanding with foundational text-based language
043   reasoning (Yun et al., 2021). We note that one cause for this divergence is the VLM’s over reliance
044
      on a single visual input, even when such input contains little relevant information.
045   To mitigate such discrepancy Visually-augmented Language Models (VaLMs) were proposed Wang
046   et al. (2023); Guo et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2022a); Cui et al. (2024); Tan & Bansal (2020).
047   VaLMs suggest augmenting text-based models with additional visual information. Recent studies
048   suggest VaLM like models improve visual commonsense performance in NLP benchmarks (Zhang
049   et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022a; Yang et al., 2022). Notice,
050
      unlike VLMs, VaLMs focus on utilizing relevant visual information to improve visual commonsense
      in language-oriented tasks, whereas VLMs are aimed at reason over visual inputs such as visual
051
      question answering, image captioning, etc.
052
053   In this study, we propose a novel VaLM like approach for improving visual commonsense reasoning
      in LLMs. The proposed approach comprised of two main components: (i) a novel architecture, that


                                                           1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      allows for the late fusion of text and images, and (ii) an inference-based procedure that integrates
055   multiple images generated by a pre-trained text-to-image model conditioned on the input text.
056
057   More specifically, in training, given an image and a corresponding caption, our method first encodes
058
      the image using a pre-trained multimodal encoder, mapping the input into a common representation
      space of text and images. Next, this encoded representation is passed through a projector, which
059
      maps this encoding to a sequence of pseudo-text token embeddings z1v , . . . , znv . Simultaneously,
060
      the input text is passed through a pre-trained LLM, producing text token embeddings z1x , . . . , zkx .
061   Finally, we combine z1v , . . . , znv and z1x , . . . , zkx through a late-stage attention-like mechanism, which
062   allows for text tokens to attend to the pseudo-text tokens generated from the visual input. Unlike
063   previous work, this integration is done once, just before the model’s prediction, and not as input
064   to the LLM. This late fusion enables the model to better focus on the input text to predict the next
065   token while also enabling it to use visual information if this is required to predict the next token.
066   We find that this formulation strikes the right balance, allowing success in both visual understanding
067   and text-based language reasoning.
068   The second component of our approach involves the integration of multiple visual inputs at infer-
069   ence. Unlike training, we do not have access to images corresponding to the input text at inference.
070   So, instead, we generate multiple images conditioned on the input text using a pre-trained text-to-
071   image model. More specifically, we consider different variations of the input text and pass it to a
072   pre-trained text-to-image generator to generate k image variations. Each generated image is fed into
073   our visually augmented LLM to generate k different predictions (probability vectors) and a predic-
074   tion when no input image is given, thus generating k + 1 predictions. Lastly, all probability vectors
075
      are weighted-averaged to produce the final output. By integrating different probability vectors, our
      prediction is based on several visualizations conditioned on the input text. Further, the aggregated
076
      probability vector will be highly influenced by confident predictions, being of low entropy. By pro-
077
      viding an option not to use an input image at all, we also enable the prediction to be made based on
078   the input text alone when this is required.
079
080   We evaluate the proposed approach on a set of object and visual common-sense tasks together with
081
      text-based commonsense reasoning. For object commonsense, we employ the zero-shot benchmark
      proposed by Wang et al. (2023), which focuses on questions related to colors, shapes, and sizes of
082
      different objects. For visual commonsense, we consider a more challenging benchmark, the Ima-
083
      geNetVC (Xia et al., 2023) dataset. ImageNetVC is composed of high-quality question-answer pairs
084   over diverse domains. For commonsense reasoning, we assess our method using standard bench-
085   marks, similarly to Dubey et al. (2024); Touvron et al. (2023); Team et al. (2023); Almazrouei et al.
086   (2023). We also consider the task of reading comprehension, where we adhere to the benchmark
087   framework suggested by Touvron et al. (2023). When considering object and visual commonsense
088   tasks, the proposed approach significantly outperforms the evaluated baselines across a variety of
089   architectures and model sizes. Interestingly, following the proposed approach also slightly improves
090   performance in text-based common-sense reasoning tasks. We conclude the experimental section
091   with an ablation study, analyzing the importance of each of the components composing our method.

      Large Language and Vision Models. LLMs have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in various
096   natural language processing tasks (Devlin et al., 2019; Radford et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2022b;
097   Team et al., 2024; Touvron et al., 2023). Their potential expands significantly when integrated
098   with visual modalities, giving rise to vision language models (VLMs) (Alayrac et al., 2022; Liu
099   et al., 2023b;a; Li et al., 2023a; Dai et al., 2023; Cha et al., 2024). By incorporating text and
100   image data during training, VLMs have enabled a new set of multimodal understanding capabilities,
101   allowing breakthroughs in tasks such as visual question answering (VQA), image captioning, and
102   visual commonsense reasoning (Zhang et al., 2022a; Xia et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b; Jin et al.,
103   2024). Despite their exceptional performance in visually oriented tasks, VLMs frequently exhibit a
104
      drop in performance in non-visual tasks that necessitate fundamental common-sense reasoning. In
      this work, we aim to improve performance in visual reasoning tasks while maintaining (or slightly
105
      improving) commonsense reasoning compared to language models.

      Multiple Generations Agreement. Several works encourage an agreement, or consistency, be-
155
      tween the predictions of a language model given perturbations of the input (Bachman et al., 2014;
156   Sajjadi et al., 2016; Xie et al., 2020; Zhai et al., 2019). In contrast, we model this agreement by
157   aggregating predictions given different visual inputs generated through a pre-trained text-to-image
158   model conditioned on the input text. Our work is also related to the ability to obtain the confidence
159   of LLMs, as derived by Portillo Wightman et al. (2023). They showed that one can estimate the
160   confidence of LLMs by aggregating their predictions under different prompts. Our motivation is
161   similar but uses the agreement of different visually generated inputs. In addition, while their work
      focuses on estimating confidence, we aim to improve visual commonsense reasoning.

      3     M ETHOD
163
164   The proposed approach, denoted as V LMIG, (stands for improving visual Language Models via
165   Multiple Image Generation), aims to leverage visual cues to improve object and visual common-
166   sense capabilities in LLMs while maintaining their performance in standard text benchmarks (i.e.,
167   commonsense reasoning and reading comprehension tasks). For that, V LMIG adopts a multi-modal
168   learning approach, where we incorporate visual cues within textual representation to perform next-
169   token prediction. During training, we utilize two types of input data: (i) a pair of images and their
170   corresponding text description, and (ii) a text and a synthetically generated image obtained from a
171   text-to-image model. During inference, given an input text prompt, we generate multiple images
172
      corresponding to different parts of the input text, feed them into the model, and aggregate their
      probability vectors based on their alignment with the input prompt. In the following subsections we:
173
      (i) outline the process of model optimization (Section 3.1); and (ii) introduce our visually driven
174
      inference method (Section 3.2).

297
298   We optimize V LMIG using a combination of natural and artificial text-image pairs, obtained by ap-
299   plying a pre-trained text-to-image model on texts from text-only datasets. We use the Visual Genome

      Regions dataset (Krishna et al., 2016), which consists of 5.4M images with region descriptions. We

301
      from the LVIS dataset (Gupta et al., 2019), and Wikitext-103-raw-v1 (Merity et al., 2016), a col-
302
      lection of over 100 million tokens extracted from verified Wikipedia articles. To simulate inference
303   with generated images, we randomly sample 2% of data from the Wikipedia textual dataset and use
304   it to generate the corresponding image.

306   4.2    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
307
308   In all experiments, we use CLIP-ViTB/32 (Radford et al., 2021) to compute the CLIP score for text-
309   image pairs and as the vision encoder. For text-to-image generation, we utilize SDXL-turbo (Sauer
310   et al., 2023). Model optimization was performed using four A100 GPUs following a dual training
311   pipeline. Initially, we trained the model for 40K iterations with a batch size of 256, employing the
312   AdamW optimizer at a learning rate of 5 × 10−4 and utilizing a constant learning rate scheduler.
313
      Subsequently, the model was fine-tuned for an additional 10K iterations with a batch size of 128 and
      a learning rate of 5 × 10−5 , again using a constant learning rate scheduler. Code and models will be
314
      made publicly available upon paper acceptance.

448   5.2   A BLATION S TUDIES
449
450
      We present three ablation studies: (i) analyzing the effect of using multi-modal representation, (ii)
      analyzing the effect of late vs. early fusion layers, and (iii) analyzing the effect of the number of
451
      generated images on model performance. More results and ablations can be found in Appendix B.2.
452
453   CLIP text embedding vs. image generation. One might argue that multi-modal representations,
454   which might serve as a bridge between image and text modalities, could be used instead to inject
455   visual information. For instance, one could extract a CLIP representation for each input prompt
456
      and obtain a visually driven text representation. Such representation could be later used under the
      same modeling setup instead of synthetic image generation. And so the natural question is, do
457
      synthetically generated images hold more information than multi-modal text representation?
458
459   To address this, we compare V LMIG, both of which are based on the Gemma-2B architecture,
460   against text representations obtained from a pre-trained CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) model, specifi-
461   cally the CLIP-ViTB/32 version. For a fair comparison, we adapt the proposed model architecture,
462
      datasets, and implementation details and only replace the visual representations with multi-modal
      textual representations. Initially, we tried to embed the full-text prompt using CLIP. However, this
463
      resulted in poor performance. Instead, as suggested by Guo et al. (2023), we extract noun entities
464
      from the text prompt using a part-of-speech tagger. Then, we embed this pre-processed text us-
465   ing CLIP text encoder. We report the visual commonsense, commonsense reasoning, and reading
466   comprehension results under the same settings described in Section 4.3. We also report the average
467   inference running time, of a token prediction, tested on the color test. Results are reported in Table 3.
468
      When considering visual commonsense, image generation significantly boosts performance com-
469
      pared to both the CLIP text embedding and the baseline (Gemma-2B), while CLIP text embedding
470
      itself improves over the baseline. On commonsense reasoning and reading comprehension, both
471   the image generation and CLIP text embedding methods either outperform or match the baseline.
472   In terms of inference time, using CLIP text embeddings helps close the gap between the generated
473   image method and the baseline. Overall, while the generated images method achieves the high-
474   est performance, CLIP text embeddings offer a balanced trade-off between accuracy and inference
475   run-time, providing a viable alternative for scenarios where computational resources or latency are
476   limited.
477   Late vs. early fusion of visual information. Visual information can be fused into LLMs in different
478   ways. We consider how fusing or injecting visual information in various stages affects downstream
479   task performance. We report the results of V LMIG when considering either early or late fusion. In
480   the case of early fusion, we apply our fusion layer to our visual pseudo tokens and textual tokens,
481   which are the output of a single encoding layer of the pretrained LLM. We then pass the resulting
482   output to the rest of the pretrained LLM. We additionally provide a comparison for an alternative
483   design choice, in which we omit the fusion layer and optimize the vision projector layer while
484   prepending such representation to the text input (Prepend Fusion), which is then fed into the LLM.
485
      Other training configurations, such as the loss function, remain unchanged. Results summarized in
      Table 4 suggest that late fusion provides the best results across all benchmarks.

      6   D ISCUSSION
520
521
522   Limitations. The main limitation of V LMIG is its inference time, which requires generating k
523   different images. Although recent diffusion models can generate high-quality images in a single
524   step, this still results in non-negligible latency during inference. In cases where inference time
525
      is a crucial constraint, one could replace generated images with multimodal representations under
      the same framework. This results in a worse performance with significantly better run-time (see
526
      Table 3). We also note that improving inference time is an orthogonal research direction to the one
527
      presented in this paper, which we intend pursue in future work.
528
529   Conclusion. We introduce V LMIG, a method designed to enhance the visual commonsense capa-
530   bilities of LLMs while maintaining their foundational text-based language reasoning capabilities. To
531
      enable this, V LMIG introduces two main novel components: (i) a novel training pipeline consisting
      of a late fusion layer applied over the output of a text-only LLM and a visually adapted pseuo-tokens,
532
      and (ii) the integration of multiple visual “experts” through the generation of multiple images from a
533
      text-to-image model and the aggregation of their “vote” (or vector probabilities), enabling the model
534   to leverage diverse visual perspectives. We perform an extensive evaluation showcasing the appli-
535   cability of our approach across a range of visual commonsense tasks demonstrating its significant
536   superiority over existing baselines. Notably, V LMIG not only excels in visual tasks, but also main-
537   tains, or even slightly improves, performance in text-focused commonsense reasoning and reading
538   comprehension. For future work, we aim to explore the potential of V LMIG in more complex visual
539   reasoning tasks, such as those involving intricate relationships between objects or requiring a deeper
      understanding of visual context.

      The broader impact of our method has both potential risks and benefits associated with using LLMs,
      visual encoders, and text-to-image generators. As our method uses these components, it inherits
792
      their associated issues. The following are points that should be considered:
793
794
            • Malicious input. This can be both at the text-to-image model, generating harmful content,
795           which the model can use, and the use of the LLM itself that gets input text as input.
796
797         • Hallucinations. Similar to LLMs, our model might generate outputs that are not grounded
798
              in facts. In our case, this can also happen in the text-to-image model, where the model
              generates factually incorrect visuals.
799
800         • Biases. Biases can be performed using the pre-trained LLM, the CLIP encoder, and the
801           text-to-image generator and transferred into our model. This may lead to biased output or
802           to unfair representations of diverse content.
803         • Engergy consumptions. While our model primarily uses pre-trained foundation models as
804           part of our model design and only adapts a lightweight vision projector fusion layer, train-
805           ing such pre-trained models requires significant energy consumption. Further, inference
806           time queries, which are performed many times, may be costly.
807
808   We note that significant research is devoted to mitigating each of these risk factors. In conjunction
809   with this research, our work enables significant advances in improving visual commonsense in LLMs
      that can benefit the community.
