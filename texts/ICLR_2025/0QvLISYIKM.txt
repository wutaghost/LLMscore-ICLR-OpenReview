011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Estimating the confidence of deep neural network predictions is crucial for en-
014            suring safe deployment in high-stakes applications. Softmax probabilities, though
015            commonly used, are often poorly calibrated, and existing calibration methods have
016            been shown to be harmful for failure prediction tasks. In this paper, we propose
017            to use information-theoretic measures to estimate the confidence of predictions
018
               from trained networks in a post-hoc manner, without needing to modify their ar-
               chitecture or training process. In particular, we compare three pointwise informa-
019
               tion (PI) measures: pointwise mutual information (PMI), pointwise V-information
020
               (PVI), and the recently proposed pointwise sliced mutual information (PSI). We
021            show in this paper that these PI measures naturally relate to confidence estima-
022            tion. We first study the invariance properties of these PI measures with respect to
023            a broad range of transformations. We then study the sensitivity of the PI measures
024            to geometric attributes such as margin and intrinsic dimensionality, as well as their
025            convergence rates. We finally conduct extensive experiments on benchmark com-
026            puter vision models and datasets and compare the effectiveness of these measures
027            as tools for confidence estimation. A notable finding is that PVI is better than
028            PMI and PSI for failure prediction and confidence calibration, outperforming all
029
               existing baselines for post-hoc confidence estimation. This is consistent with our
               theoretical findings, which suggest that PVI is the most well-balanced measure in
030
               terms of its invariance properties and sensitivity to geometric feature properties
031
               such as sample-wise margin.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   With the broader application of deep neural networks (DNNs), particularly in high-stakes areas like
038   healthcare and autonomous driving, the focus has shifted from merely achieving good accuracy to
039
      also ensuring trustworthiness for safe deployment (Kaur et al., 2023). One important aspect of a
      trustworthy model is uncertainty quantification (Abdar et al., 2021), which evaluates the model’s
040
      uncertainty or confidence in its predictions. It has been shown that softmax probabilities obtained
041
      from the neural networks tend to be overconfident (Guo et al., 2017). Many existing approaches
042   that address this issue involve modifying the network architecture (Corbière et al., 2019) or training
043   procedure (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016), which may not always be feasible in practice. Meanwhile,
044   popular confidence calibration methods have been shown to be useless or harmful for failure predic-
045   tion tasks (Zhu et al., 2022). This study addresses both failure prediction and confidence calibration
046   by analyzing various information-theoretic measures to estimate the confidence of predictions from
047   trained networks in a post-hoc manner, without altering their architecture or training process.
048   Mutual Information (MI) is the conventional information measure used to capture statistical depen-
049   dence between two random variables (Cover & Thomas, 2001). However, accurately estimating
050   MI in high-dimensional spaces, typically encountered in the context of DNNs, is challenging due
051   to an exponentially large sample complexity (Battiti, 1994). In recent years, there have been pro-
052   posals for alternative measures of informativeness that scale well with dimensions. The first is the
053   V-information (VI) which measures the amount of usable information under computational con-
      straints (Xu et al., 2020). The second is sliced mutual information (SMI) which is the average of the


                                                        1

054
      MI between one-dimensional projections of the random variables (Goldfeld & Greenewald, 2021).
055   Unlike MI, both VI and SMI can be estimated reliably from data, even in high dimensions.
056
057   To apply the three information-theoretic measures (MI, VI, and SMI) for confidence estimation, we
058
      use their pointwise variants: pointwise MI (PMI), pointwise VI (PVI), and pointwise SMI (PSI).
      Specifically, we use these pointwise information (PI) measures to quantify the degree of relevance
059
      between feature representation and predicted output of a model for each individual sample. We
060
      analyze their theoretical properties, including invariance, margin, intrinsic dimensionality, and con-
061   vergence rates, which we argue are relevant to predictive uncertainty. Empirically, we compare their
062   effectiveness in estimating confidence scores for predictions made by various computer vision ar-
063   chitectures on benchmark datasets. For a review of related work on confidence estimation and the
064   three PI measures, please refer to Appendix A.1.
065
      Motivation. We provide four factors that motivate the use of PI measures for confidence estimation:
066
067   1. Recent Applications of PI Measures: PI measures have recently found application in diverse
068      domains of DNNs, showcasing their versatility and effectiveness. For instance, the significant
069      work by Ethayarajh et al. (2022) showcases the applicability of PVI to the problem of dataset
070
         difficulty, which relates to predictive uncertainty as networks are naturally more uncertain about
         their predictions when the datasets are harder. While PI measures are more commonly applied
071
         in natural language, we focus on their potential in computer vision, an area still relatively under-
072
         explored from information-theoretic perspective. More closely aligned with our work is the study
073      by Wongso et al. (2023b) which proposed PSI for predictive uncertainty and explainability. We
074      extend their research by comparing the performance of PSI with PMI and PVI, providing deeper
075      theoretical insights, and conducting additional quality evaluations.
076   2. Theoretical Foundations: Despite the increasing applications of the PI measures, there has been
077      a notable lack of research devoted to exploring their theoretical properties. To the best of our
078      knowledge, only PMI has been theoretically studied (Fano & Hawkins, 1961b), albeit primarily
079      from a general standpoint. In this work, we derive and compare the theoretical properties of PMI,
080      PVI, and PSI, which we argue are relevant to predictive uncertainty. These properties include
081
         invariance, margin, intrinsic dimensionality, and convergence rates. We find that these measures
         exhibit desirable properties overall that can be relevant in the context of uncertainty estimation.
082
      3. Information Theoretic Connection: Another interpretation of the PI measures comes directly
083
         from the notion of information gain in information theory. Information gain, usually defined in
084      the aggregate sense, measures the degree of uncertainty reduction about a certain random variable
085      Y given another variable X, i.e., H(Y ) − H(Y |X). PMI, which is defined as log (p(ŷ|x)/p(ŷ)),
086      essentially measures a pointwise version of information gain, where x is the feature and ŷ is the
087      predicted output. We note that this measure is rooted in probability, and estimates priors and
088      posterior probability measures. This is unlike the typical neural network output, which, although
089      is supposed to model the conditional probabilities of each class p(ŷ|x), often turn out to be not a
090      good indicator of the true uncertainty. In contrast, pointwise information measures explicitly es-
091      timate the probability density ratio p(ŷ|x)/p(ŷ) based on the given data using benchmark density
092
         ratio estimators, and therefore represents an interesting alternative to the softmax operator. By
         computing the relative increase in p(ŷ|x) compared to its prior p(ŷ), PI measures are essentially
093
         estimating the relative increase in confidence for the predicted class, compared to its prior occur-
094
         rence probability. By doing so, can potentially reduce inherent bias in the conditional probability
095      p(ŷ|x), which can be caused due to underrepresentation of certain classes in the data.
096   4. Relationship to Probabilistic Causation: We find that PI measures can be also interpreted via
097      the lens of probabilistic causation. This perspective on causality, as outlined by Hitchcock (1997),
098      argues that X causes Y if P (Y |X) > P (Y ). The predictive uncertainty problem aims to quantify
099      the uncertainty that a feature x contains about ŷ as the network should naturally be more uncertain
100      in its predictions for samples where x has small influence on ŷ. We argue that this problem can
101      be mathematically formulated by measuring the quantity p(ŷ|x)/p(ŷ) which indicates the degree
102      to which a certain feature x influences the decision made for a single instance. This directly
103
         connects to PMI which is defined as pmi(x; ŷ) = log(p(ŷ|x)/p(ŷ)).
104   Contributions. The specific contributions of this paper are as follows:
105
      1. We compare the three PI measures (PMI, PSI, and PVI) across experiments on confidence score
106
         estimation We found that PVI outperforms PMI and PSI as well as benchmark post-hoc methods
107      for failure prediction and confidence calibration.

      2. We perform an in-depth study of theoretical properties of the three PI measures that are relevant
109      for predictive uncertainty problems. We analyze their invariance to a range of transformations
110      and show that PMI and PVI have more invariance properties, which we argue are desirable for
111      predictive uncertainty problems.
112   3. We derive theoretical results on the sensitivity of pointwise measures to sample-wise margin.
113      PMI fails to capture sample-wise margin for non-overlapping class-wise feature distributions,

      3.1    I NVARIANCE P ROPERTIES OF PI MEASURES
190
191   Here, we outline some invariance properties of PI measures. In what follows, we consider the case
192   where X ∈ Rdx are the features and Y ∈ {0, 1} are the labels, and (x, y) is a feature-label instance
193   sampled from PXY . Note that in what follows, other than Theorem 1, all other theoretical results
194   can be trivially extended to the multi-label setting.
195
      For convenience of notation, when (x, y) ∼ PXY , we denote pmi(x; y), psi(x; y) and pvi(x → y)
196   by pmiP (x, y), psiP (x, y) and pviP (x, y) respectively. For estimating pviP (x, y), we assume that
197   V refers to a fully connected neural network of arbitrary depth and fixed architecture, where each
198   layer contains both weights and biases. For any transformation T : Rd → Rd , we denote the
199   probability distribution P (T X, Y ) by T P . We then have the following results.
200   Proposition 1 (Invariance to shift, scale, and rotation). Let T x = αRx + p, where p ∈ Rdx
201   represents the extent to which the distribution is shifted, and α ∈ R is a scalar that represents how
202   much the distribution is scaled. Furthermore, R ∼ Rdx ×dx is a rotation matrix, such that we have
203   RRT = I and det(R) = 1, where I is the identity matrix and det represents the determinant
204   operator. Then, we have: pmiP (x, y) = pmiT P (αRx + p, y), psiP (x, y) = psiT P (αRx + p, y)
205   and pviP (x, y) = pviT P (αRx + p, y).
206
      Next, we have the following results for more general linear transformations and homeomorphic
207
      (continuous and invertible) transformations.
208
209   Proposition 2 (Invariance to general linear transformations). Let T x = M x, where M ∼
210
      Rdx ×dx is invertible. Then, pviP (x, y) = pviT P (M x, y) & pmiP (x, y) = pmiT P (M x, y)
211   Proposition 3 (Invariance to homeomorphic transformations). Let T x = f (x), where f :
212   Rdx → Rdx represents any homeomorphism. Then, pmiP (x, y) = pmiT P (f (x), y).
213   Remark 1 (Invariance and confidence estimation). We note that it is important to be invariant
214   to bijective transformations T in the context of confidence estimation, as otherwise the pointwise
215   measures will confound T in its resulting estimate. Contextualizing this using the terminology in
      (1) of (Mukhoti et al., 2023) we can write: H[Y |x, D] = H[Y |T x, T D], where H[Y |T x, T D]

238   distribution Q(X|Y = 1 − y).

311   for our subsequent experiments. These takeaways will also be referenced in our discussion of the
312   experimental results later.
313

      4.1   FAILURE P REDICTION
433
434   Goal: The goal of this experiment is to compare the effectiveness of different confidence estimates
435   for failure prediction. Failure prediction typically involves three tasks: misclassification detection,
436   selective prediction, and out-of-distribution detection (Jaeger et al., 2023). This work focuses on
437   the first two tasks. In misclassification detection, the objective is to identify incorrect predictions
438   made by trained networks. Ideally, confidence scores should be high for correct predictions and low
439   for incorrect ones. In selective prediction, the aim is to evaluate the improvement in classification
440   performance after excluding a certain percentage of low-confidence predictions.
441   Methodology: For misclassification detection, we evaluate the effectiveness of confidence estimates
442   in distinguishing between positive (incorrect predictions) and negative (correct predictions) samples.
443   We use a threshold-independent metric, AUROCf (Area Under the ROC Curve, with f denoting
444   failure), which is widely adopted in the literature (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2017; Jaeger et al., 2023).
445   Since AUROC is less informative when the positive and negative classes have significantly different
446   base rates, we also consider another metric called AUPR (Area Under the Precision-Recall Curve).
447
      Given that the base rate of the positive class greatly influences AUPR, we examine both scenarios:
      treating success classes as positive samples (AUPRf , success) and treating error classes as positive
448
      samples (AUPRf , error). For selective prediction, we examine the improvement in classification
449
      error rates by filtering out low-confidence samples. In this context, we define risk as the error
450   rate on the remaining samples, and coverage as the proportion of remaining samples relative to the
451   total samples. We employ a threshold-independent metric, AURC (Area Under the Risk-Coverage
452   Curve), as described in the literature (Jaeger et al., 2023). We compare our results against six
453   benchmark methods: maximum softmax probability (MSP) (Geifman & El-Yaniv, 2017), softmax
454   margin (SM) (Tagasovska & Lopez-Paz, 2019), max logit (ML) (Hendrycks et al., 2022), logits
455   margin (LM) (Streeter, 2018), negative entropy (NE) (Belghazi & Lopez-Paz, 2021) and negative
456   Gini index (NG) (Granese et al., 2021) ((83) - (88) in Appendix C.3.1). Additional details on the
457   metrics and methods can be found in Appendix C.3.1. We report the results in Table 2.
458   Results: We observe that PVI generally outperforms the other two PI measures, as well as other
459   benchmark post-hoc methods, across a range of metrics. After considering the margin of error, while
460   the performance improvement of PVI is less pronounced for AUROCf and AUPRf,success , it remains
461   notably significant for AUPRf,failure and AURC, which are the preferred metrics (Jaeger et al., 2023).
462   This indicates that PVI is the most suitable in terms of the proportion of prediction errors it detects.
463   Interestingly, we find that on F-MNIST, PVI has superior performance w..rt every evaluation metric,
464
      even after considering the standard deviation. This superior performance is likely due to PVI being
      the most well-rounded metric, particularly in terms of its invariance and margin-sensitivity (see
465
      Section 3.3). On the other hand, PMI and PSI performs relatively well on simpler models and
466
      datasets, such as MLP and MNIST, but struggles with more complex models and datasets. This
467   agrees with point T4 in Section 3.3, which finds that for datasets with greater degree of overlap and
468   more spread out distribution, PMI and PSI can have worse convergence behaviour.

503   5    R EFLECTIONS
504
505   We performed a comparative analysis of using three PI measures, namely PMI, PVI, and PSI, for
506   confidence estimation in DNNs. We study several theoretical properties which we believe can be
507   relevant to model uncertainty, including how well a measure behaves in response to data transfor-
508   mations (invariance properties), how well a measure tracks the geometric difficulty of classifying
509   a feature point (sample-wise margin), and how well a measure converges with data (convergence
510   rates). We performed a series of experiments on confidence estimation (failure prediction and con-
511   fidence calibration) to test and verify our theoretical hypothesis. Our findings demonstrate that PVI
512   outperforms both PMI, PSI, and benchmark post-hoc methods in failure prediction and confidence
513
      calibration tasks. This highlights PVI’s versatility, especially given that popular confidence calibra-
      tion methods have been shown to be ineffective or even detrimental for failure prediction tasks (Zhu
514
      et al., 2022). This is consistent with our theoretical findings which suggest that PVI is the most
515
      well-rounded among the three PI measures considered (as discussed in point T5 in Section 3.3).
516
517   One of our findings in this work has been that better sensitivity to margin doesn’t necessarily imply
518   better performance in the confidence prediction problem. We note that for the correlation to margin
519
      experiment, the focus is on whether the model assigns higher confidence to samples with a larger
      margin (and vice versa), regardless of whether the prediction is correct. On the other hand, for the
520
      misclassification detection, selective prediction, and calibration analysis, the focus is more on the
521
      correctness of predictions (directly linked to accuracy). The contrast lies in the interpretation of
522   confidence: margin experiments treat confidence as a measure of sensitivity to decision boundaries,
523   while the other tasks treat it as a measure of predictive reliability. Therefore, this may be a reason
524   why it is possible for PSI to perform better in the margin-based task and be more margin sensitive,
525   while PVI performs better in the accuracy-based tasks.
526
      For future work, one could consider using these PI measures to analyze model uncertainty in other
527
      modalities (e.g., image, audio, tabular, etc.). In addition, one could explore the potential of using
528   PI measures for other aspects of trustworthy machine learning such as explainability (as we briefly
529   show in Appendix D.3) and privacy. Furthermore, one could study other scaling/normalization
530   techniques to improve the performance of the PI measures. We hope that both our theoretical and
531   empirical findings will motivate more work in the direction of information theoretic approaches for
532   model uncertainty in the context of DNNs.
533
      Limitations. Our PI measures require training additional models to learn the probability distribu-
534   tion. Since estimators for PI measures are less common compared to their aggregate counterparts,
535   our work, which clearly demonstrates their applications in explainability and uncertainty quan-
536   tification, could motivate further research towards more accurate and efficient estimation of these
537   measures. In addtion, the PI measures are the optimal choice of explainability if we assume the
538   probability-raising based causal model for the problem. Exploring other causal models (such as
539   Judea Pearl’s structural causal models) from an information-theoretic perspective in the context of
      explainability is an interesting avenue for future work.

      A     R ELATED W ORK & I NFORMATION M EASURES D ETAILS
811
812
813   A.1   R ELATED W ORK
814
815
      Pointwise Mutual Information (PMI). PMI compares the probability of two outcomes occurring
      together to what the probability would be if they are independent. It is commonly in natural language
816
      processing to measure the association between words (Church & Hanks, 1990; Turney, 2001; Su
817
      et al., 2006; Padmakumar & He, 2021). In this setting, p(x) and p(x, y) can be obtained by counting
818   the occurrences and co-occurrences of words in the corpus. However, PMI can be sensitive to
819   the size of the corpus and may not perform well with very rare words or when the data is sparse.
820   Other variants of PMI have also been introduced, including positive PMI measure, which sets all
821   the negative values to zero (Dagan et al., 1993), and normalized PMI measure, which scales the
822   values to fall within the range [−1, 1] (Bouma, 2009). PMI has found applications in a wide range
823   of areas, including sentiment analysis (Ahanin & Ismail, 2022; Bandhakavi et al., 2017; Sintsova &
824   Pu, 2016), community detection (Luo et al., 2021), response generation (Nandwani et al., 2023; Ren
825   et al., 2023), truthful data acquisition (Zheng et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2020), and boundary detection
826
      (Isola et al., 2014). In this study, we use PMI to obtain both confidence scores and saliency maps
      for image classification tasks.
827
828   Pointwise Sliced Mutual Information (PSI). Wongso et al. (2023b) introduced PSI as a measure
829   for generating confidence scores and saliency maps for deep neural networks. For confidence scores,
830   they compute the PSI between features of the penultimate layer of a neural network and predicted
831   label for each sample and refer to this as the sample-wise PSI. For saliency maps, they compute
832
      the PSI between feature fiber of the last convolutional layer of a convolutional neural network and
      predicted label for each sample and refer to this as the fiber-wise PSI. In addition, they show that
833
      PSI, in contrast to PMI, exhibits sensitivity to sample-wise margin. Even though their findings
834
      demonstrate that PSI can produce sensible confidence scores and saliency maps the paper lacks a
835   profound perspective and the essential quality assessment of PSI as a metric for model uncertainty
836   and explainability. In this work, we provide a more comprehensive evaluation of PSI, comparing
837   it to the other two pointwise measures, namely PMI and PVI, to determine the relevance between
838   features and predicted labels. Additionally, we present a set of theoretical results that explore var-
839   ious properties of pointwise information measures, providing deeper insights into what they may
840   represent.
841   Pointwise V-Information (PVI). PVI was introduced to measure sample difficulty with respect to
842   a given distribution (Ethayarajh et al., 2022). In their research, they investigate natural language
843   inference tasks and observe that samples with high PVI are often predicted correctly, while those
844   with low PVI are more likely to be predicted incorrectly. It is important to note that in their paper,
845   they assess PVI in relation to the true label (also referred to as the gold label), making it a measure
846   of sample difficulty rather than a measure of the network’s confidence. They also show that PVI can
847   be used to identify which subsets of each class are more difficult than others. PVI has recently been
848   employed in a variety of NLP tasks (Lin et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2023; Prasad et al., 2023; Kulmizev
849
      & Nivre, 2023). In this study, we compute PVI to obtain both confidence scores and saliency maps
      for image classification tasks.
850
851   Predictive Confidence. The idea behind confidence estimation is closely connected to uncertainty
852   quantification. Simply put, when we are more confident in a prediction made by a model, it means
853   there is less uncertainty about that prediction. For a comprehensive survey/review on uncertainty
854   quantification in deep learning, we refer the readers to Gawlikowski et al. (2023); He & Jiang (2023);
855
      Mena et al. (2022); Abdar et al. (2021). There are two common lines of work for evaluating pre-
      dictive confidence: confidence ranking and confidence calibration. Works on confidence ranking
856
      focuses on ranking confidence scores such that the lower-ranked samples are more likely to misclas-
857
      sified while the higher-ranked samples are more likely to be correctly classified. Confidence ranking
858   is useful in applications such as misclassification detection (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2017; Jiang et al.,
859   2018; Corbière et al., 2019; Jaeger et al., 2023), out-of-distribution detection (Hendrycks & Gimpel,
860   2017; DeVries & Taylor, 2018; Liang et al., 2018) and selective classification (Geifman & El-Yaniv,
861   2017; Feng et al., 2023; Galil et al., 2023). On the other hand, research on confidence calibration
862   aims to provide confidence scores that accurately reflect the likelihood of a prediction being correct
863   (Guo et al., 2017; Nixon et al., 2019; Tao et al., 2024). This requires the confidence scores to be
      probabilities within the range of 0 to 1.

1330   of the three metrics, PMI has the most structure-preserving property, followed by PVI and then PSI.
1331   This makes sense as PMI is the most general and only depends on the distribution and doesn’t rely

1335   Remark 8. Note that PSI need not be invariant to both general linear transformations and home-
1336   omorphisms. To see why, just consider a simple case where T represents general linear transfor-
1337
       mations which scale each dimension of the input separately. Then, a sphere in the original domain
       of the distribution P gets transformed into an ellipse in the domain of the distribution T P . As PSI
1338
       uses a uniform distribution over all projections over the sphere, we cannot say with certainty that
1339
       the PSI in the new domain of T P will be preserved, because it will prefer some directions more over
1340   others. To see this, consider a specific case of T where one of the dimensions is scaled significantly
1341   more than the rest, thereby resulting in a ellipse that is very flat. In that case, most projections will
1342   contain more of that dimension, and we cannot say that PSI will be surely preserved.

1344   non-linear activations) will indeed not be invertible. However, our result is intended to apply to the
1345   case where the features at a certain layer turn out to be transformed versions of features at that
1346   same layer, from the same network, in another iteration of training. This is likely to happen because
1347   of random weight initializations, and the features at a certain layer T can very well be represented
1348   as W T ′ where T ′ represents the feature at the same layer after a different initialization and W is an
1349   invertible matrix. As fundamentally, all versions of T here carry the same information, the pointwise

1456   within it. As such, the radius R1 also does not directly impact the result. However, S2 should be
1457   ideally chosen such that it contains as much of the distribution P (X|Y = 1). To see this, we mainly
       look at how the choice of S2 impacts ϵ. Note that if ϵ is very large, such that pmax < pmin + ϵ, then

       the dependence on sample-wise margin reverses (less margin leads to more psi). To avoid this, we
1459   can always choose S2 such that ϵ is small. Let S2 be chosen such that p(x ∈ S2 |y = 1) = ρ.
1460   Furthermore, let us assume that there is another bigger sphere S3 such that S2 is contained in S3 ,
1461   such that p(x ∈ S3 |y = 1) = 1. Let the radius of S3 be R3 . Then, we can approximate ϵ as
1462   (1 − ρ)/(2(R3 − R2 )). This is because the projection of S2 will have a length of 2R2 and similarly
1463   for S3 . Thus, if we choose S2 such that ρ is made arbitrarily close to 1, we can make ϵ arbitrarily
1464   close to zero. However, do note that although this can be done when the distributions P (X|Y = 0)
1465   and P (X|Y = 1) have less overlap, for the case where P (X|Y = 0) and P (X|Y = 1) are
1466   highly overlapping, this may not be possible. As in most of our experiments x is taken from the
1467
       penultimate layer of neural networks which have separable features, the assumption will hold with
       high probability.

1469   Remark 14. (Regarding sensitivity to hard margins) When P (X|Y = 0) and P (X|Y = 1) are
1470   clearly separated, one should ideally have maximum confidence estimates everywhere. But the fact
1471
       that we do not know the ground truth distribution P (X, Y ) implies that even when the estimate
       of P , denoted by Q(X, Y ), from the training data, is perfectly separated, the separation of the
1472
       true unknown P (X, Y ) will be most likely smaller with potential overlap. This is because Q(X, Y )
1473
       clearly has a significant chance of overfitting the true distributions, as the objective of the classifier is
1474   always to separate the training feature distributions anyway. Due to this potential overestimation of
1475   the real margin, encoding additional geometric information about Q(X, Y ), such as the hard margin
1476   involved in the perfect separation, can inform about the probability of P (X|Y = 0) and P (X|Y =
1477   1) being perfectly separated as well. If Q(X, Y ) has a very small hard margin, then it is possible
1478   that P (X, Y ) ends up with overlapping class-wise feature distributions, and if it has a very large
1479   hard margin, then the opposite is likely. Lastly, correlation between the hard margin between the
1480   class-wise feature distributions and generalization has indeed been observed in literature (Grønlund
1481   et al., 2020), showcasing the significance of this issue.

       Results: We find that, interestingly, PSI outperforms all other measures. As shown in Figure 5,
       PMI and PVI tend to overestimate the salient region, often capturing a large part of the face for
2256

2257
       more localized and captures the relevant attribute more accurately in each case (as shown in Table
2258   13), even when compared to standard approaches such as Grad-CAM. For saliency maps, we reason
2259   that two properties are most relevant: margin sensitivity and convergence. Convergence is crucial
2260   because overestimation of pointwise measures can be particularly detrimental in this context, as
2261   many patches may not relate to any output class - unlike in confidence estimation experiments.
2262   Margin sensitivity is also important because it directly impacts the model’s ability to highlight the
2263   most relevant features for prediction. Considering both aspects, PSI is more likely to perform well,
2264   as observed in our empirical results.
