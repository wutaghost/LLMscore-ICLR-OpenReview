                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               Deep learning agents are advancing beyond laboratory settings into the open and
010
               realistic environments driven by developments in AI technologies. Since these
011            environments consist of unique sub-environments, empirical recognition of such
012            sub-environments that form the entire environment is essential. Through sub-
013            environment recognition, the agent can 1) retrieve relevant sub-environments for
014            a query, 2) track changes in its circumstances over time and space, and 3) iden-
015            tify similarities between different sub-environments while solving its tasks. To
016            this end, we propose the Object-Based Sub-Environment Recognition (OBSER)
017            framework, a novel Bayesian framework for measuring object-environment and
018            environment-environment relationships using a feature extractor trained with met-
019
               ric learning. We first design the (ϵ, δ) Statistically Separable (EDS) function to
               evaluate to show the robustness of trained representations both theoretically and
020
               empirically that the optimized feature extractor can guarantee the precision of the
021
               proposed measures. We validate the efficacy of the OBSER framework in open-
022            world and photorealistic environments. The result highlights the strong general-
023            ization capability and efficient inference of the proposed framework.

070   Sub-environment recognition is achieved by inference of three fundamental relationships: object-
071   object, object-environment, and environment-environment relationships. The object-object rela-

      Metric learning (Khosla et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2021; 2020a; Oquab et al., 2023) is a method that
      directly optimizes object-object relationships and is known for its robustness in dealing with unstruc-
074
      tured data in real-world problems (Cao et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2021; Choi et al., 2024). Therefore,
075
      we apply the metric learning model as a feature extractor for sub-environment recognition.
076
077   Then how can object-environment and environment-environment relationships be calculated using
078   a metric learning model trained only on object-object relationships? To address the issue, we re-
079

      distributions within sub-environments. We validate the proposed measures by introducing the (ϵ, δ)
080

081

085
      measures three fundamental relationships with given queries. To validate the OBSER framework,

086
      learning models in the artificially generated environment using ImageNet dataset (Deng et al., 2009).
087

      Environment recognition Environment recognition is essential for embodied agents to success-
111   fully perform tasks such as navigation (Feriol et al., 2020). Previous research has primarily focused
112   on understanding environments by analyzing the semantic distance between current and target ob-
113   servations (Yokoyama et al., 2023; Shah et al., 2023; Sridhar et al., 2024) or by generating scene and
114   environment graphs (Wang et al., 2023) based on trained models. In contrast, we propose a novel
115   approach that defines environmental relationships through the empirical distribution of occurring
116   objects, providing a new method for understanding environments.
117
118   Metric learning Metric learning (Sohn, 2016; Liu et al., 2017; Khosla et al., 2020) is a method
119   that optimizes the metric between objects on latent space to reflect the object-object relationships.
120
      Self-supervised learning (SSL) is introduced to replace an oracle data provider in metric learning,
      which selects the positive sample, to data augmentation. We use SupCon (Khosla et al., 2020) as
121
      metric learning model and MoCo variants (Chen et al., 2020b; 2021), SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020a),
122
      and DINO (Caron et al., 2021; Oquab et al., 2023) as SSL models to evaluate the proposed measures.
123
124
      Kernel method Kernel density estimation is a non-parametric method for estimating the measures
125   such as probabilistic density function (Zhang et al., 2018; Ghosh et al., 2006) and Kullback-Liebler
126   divergence (Ahuja, 2019; Ghimire et al., 2021). We utilize the object similarity as a kernel to ap-
127   proximate the distribution of sub-environments. We also validate the precision of the measures with
128   EDS function, which computes the kernel density accumulated with class-wise distribution.

      The object-object relationship is used to retrieve the most appropriate object that shares the same la-

      7    C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this paper, we propose a novel empirical method for sub-environment recognition. The fun-
530   damental relationships for sub-environment recognition, object-object, object-environment, and
531   environment-environment relationships can be measured with metric learning models via kernel
532   density estimation. The proposed measures successfully estimate the exact values in both open-
533   world and photorealistic environments, and their applicability is qualitatively demonstrated. Using
534   the OBSER framework, we claim that the agent can perform more composite inferences by chaining
535   inferences with the proposed measures.
536   Our main contribution to this work is extending the use of metric learning to measure environmental
537   relationships. Therefore, experiments have been conducted under the assumption that data acqui-
538   sition for object observation is feasible. We believe that by integrating the proposed method with
539   modules of embodied agents, such as object recognition (Kirillov et al., 2023) or navigation, the
      agent can perform robust and effective long-horizon inference at the level of sub-environments.

987    B.3     M INECRAFT DATASET
988
       In this paper, we construct a Minecraft dataset containing 26,000 images and the corresponding
989
       labels. We gather ego-centric observations of objects to train or fine-tune models. To build the
990    dataset, we first choose typical biomes that can represent all objects in the overworld in Minecraft.
991    The dataset is derived from two environments: an open-world environment and a miniature environ-
992    ment.
993
994    B.3.1    O PEN - WORLD MULTI - ANGLED DATA COLLECTION
995
996    For the open-world multi-angled data collection, we use the Minecraft’s default world generation
997
       settings to generate a world environment where the agent collects the dataset. In such an environ-
       ment, the agents can easily get stuck due to composite terrains and the arrangement of objects. This
998
       reduces the efficiency of data acquisition. Therefore, we manually locate 100 objects per biome in
999
       the world using a fixed seed, rather than relying on fully automatic methods such as the random
1000   walk algorithm. For each object, we gather 30 observations by rotating around the object’s posi-
1001   tion. Figure 11 shows examples of the observations collected. We split 10% of each observation to
1002   build the test set. Note that we do not split the dataset by object because object frequencies follow
1003   a long-tailed distribution, and object-based splitting could introduce further distortions in the data
1004   distribution. We choose two levels of hierarchical concepts to conduct experiments with different
1005   levels of abstraction. Table 4 shows the proportion of hierarchical concepts of the proposed dataset.

1008
       We also collected data from our miniature environment, which consists of 4 × 5 grids of 48 × 48
1009
       blocks with the same biome. The kind of 10 biomes in the dataset is the same as the ones in the
1010   open-world multi-angled data collection and in the table 4. To collect the data in the miniature
1011   environment, we used the same algorithm as used in open-world environment.

1014
1015   In the miniature scenario, the agent performs random exploration within the grid to collect visual
1016   observations programmatically. For each grid, we teleport the agent to its center. The agent wanders
1017
       randomly through the grid to collect visual observations and save them as images. The agent is
       prevented from going outside the grid by checking its distance from the grid’s center. If the agent
1018
       goes farther than 20 blocks from the center of the grid, we force the agent to look at the center of
1019
       the grid using the teleport command. To reduce the likelihood of the agent’s view capturing parts
1020   of neighboring grids with different biomes, we have the agent look slightly downward following
1021   a normal distribution, N (30, 52 ). In this setting, we also use Pareto distribution to determine the
1022   distance it moves forward before turning around by degrees randomly following N (30, 12 ).

       C.4   D IFFERENCE BETWEEN METRIC LEARNING AND SSL IN OBSER
1621
1622   We visualize the pair-wise environmental relationship with the Jensen-Shannon divergence. Figure
1623   19 shows the MDS visualization (Buja et al., 2008) of biomes in the Miniature environment with
1624   each model. The SSL models tend to keep the metrics of sub-environments farther apart, as they
1625   consider ambient biases beyond object distribution within each sub-environment. In contrast, the
1626   metric learning model better captures differences in object distribution but struggles to incorporate
1627   less task-relevant information.
1628   We have observed that both SSL and metric learning models demonstrate the capability of making
1629   sufficiently accurate inferences for each recognition task in OBSER. We have also observed distinct
1630   differences between these paradigms. SSL models tend to integrate environmental information that
1631   is not directly relevant to the task, while metric learning models focus on task-specific information
1632   for more accurate inference. We claim that SSL and metric learning models each offer unique
1633   advantages in terms of generalization and accuracy. Therefore, applying the appropriate model based
1634
       on the given situation of the problem is crucial for achieving effective sub-environment recognition
       in agents.
