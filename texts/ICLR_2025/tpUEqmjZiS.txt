011                                             A BSTRACT
012
013           The general-purpose robots need to continuously acquire new skills in life-
014           long spans without revisiting past experiences, known as Rehearsal-free Lifelong
015
              Learning, which remains significantly challenging. Recent advances learn a sep-
              arate adapter along pretrained policy for each new skill to address catastrophic
016
              forgetting problem, ignoring the shared knowledge between old skills and new
017
              ones. To tackle these issues, we propose Primitive-level Skill Prompt Learn-
018           ing (PSPL), to achieve lifelong robot manipulation via reusable and extensible
019           primitives. Within our two stage learning scheme, we first learn a set of prefix
020           skill prompts to extract shared knowledge through multi-skills pre-training stage,
021           where motion-aware skill prompts are learned to capture semantic and motion
022           shared primitives across different skills. Secondly, when acquiring new skills in
023           lifelong span, new prefix skill prompts are added and learned via cross-attention
024           between prefix prompts of old skills, boosting the new skills learning via shared
025           knowledge transfer. For evaluation, we construct a large-scale skill dataset and
026
              conduct extensive experiments in both simulation and real-world tasks, demon-
              strating PSPL’s superior performance over state-of-the-art methods. Code and
027
              dataset will be released upon acceptance.

075
      In this paper, we propose Primitive-level Skill Prompt Learning (PSPL) for lifelong robot manipu-
076
      lation, a novel two-stage framework that transfers the knowledge across skills via reusable and ex-
077
      tensible primitives. Our framework first learns a set of shared skill prompts to model shared knowl-
078   edge through primitive-level multi-skills pre-training. Specifically, we introduce motion-aware skill
079   prompt learning that adopt a text-flow query mechanism to capture semantic and motion shared
080   primitives across skills. For individual skill learning, skill-specific motion-aware skill prompt is
081   represented by weighted-sum of shared skill prompts and prepended into the keys and values of
082   multi-head self-attention layers of diffusion transformer-based policy. In this way, the primitive-
083   level shared knowledge learned and stored into the shared skill prompts. For new skill learning
084   lifelong span, we add new prefix skill prompts into previous learned shared skill prompts, and learn
085   them together with new skill demonstrations via cross-attention between old and new skill prompts.
086

087

088

      Lifelong Learning. Lifelong learning for decision-making aims to develop an agent that can con-
102   tinuously learn and adapt to new tasks from a stream of data while retaining previous knowledge
103   to avoid catastrophic forgetting Parisi et al. (2019); Lesort et al. (2020); Khetarpal et al. (2020).
104   Prior rehearsal-based approaches involve storing and replaying past experiences to maintain the
105   learned knowledge Rolnick et al. (2019); Shin et al. (2017); van de Ven et al. (2020). However, as
106   the number of tasks increases, the memory requirements grow significantly, limiting their scalabil-
107   ity for robot manipulation. Alternatively, another line of rehearsal-free work attempts to leverage
      regularization or dynamic architecture to achieve more efficient knowledge transfer compared to


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      rehearsal-based counterparts Kirkpatrick et al. (2017); Zenke et al. (2017); Li & Hoiem (2017).
109   These methods, primarily based on penalizing parameter changes and compartmentalizing model
110   components, often struggle with poor performance as indirect knowledge-retrain strategies, espe-
111   cially scaling to the complex vision-based manipulations domain Aljundi et al. (2018); Serra et al.
112   (2018). Most recently, inspired by the advancements of parameter-efficient fine-tuning in language
113   domains, TAIL Liu et al. (2023) with LoRA Hu et al. (2021) obtain state-of-the-art performance
114   with a few trainable parameters in lifelong learning scenarios. However, TAIL requires maintain-
115   ing specific parameters for each task and does not leverage learned knowledge to boost novel skill
116   acquirement, making it inefficient in the real world.
117   Skill-based Imitation Learning. Skill-based imitation learning focuses on leveraging temporally
118   abstract representations from sensory-motor data (termed skills) and learning a skill-conditional
119   policy to accelerate the imitation process for some long-horizon manipulation tasks. A series of
120   research segment expert demonstrations into sub-trajectories to learn these skill representations,
121   employing unsupervised strategy Shankar et al. (2020); Abi-Farraj et al. (2020); Sharma et al. (2022)
122   or relying on auxiliary supervised information Kipf et al. (2019); Lynch et al. (2020); Xu et al.
123
      (2018a). Additional work has shown promise for embedding fixed-length sub-trajectories without
      supervision through generative models such as variational auto-encoder Pertsch et al. (2020); Wang
124
      et al. (2021); Köhler et al. (2020) or diffusion model Janner et al. (2022); Chi et al. (2023); Xu et al.
125
      (2023). Furthermore, LISA Garg et al. (2022) incorporates skill learning with language instructions
126   by sampling multiple skills per trajectory and uniquely integrating language conditioning. In this
127   work, we adopt a similar setting due to its relative simplicity and scalability.

191
      As shown in Fig. 2, in the first stage of our method, we utilize a diffusion transformer policy with
192   our constructed skill dataset to perform multi-skill pre-training.
193
194   Specifically, we apply prefix-prompt learning to the diffusion transformer policy, instead of aug-
195
      menting the input tokens, prepending prompts to the keys and values of the MSA layers, with dis-
      tinct prompting parameters for each layer. We define our prompt parameter as p ∈ RLp ×D , where
196
      Lp represents the prompt length and D denotes the embedding dimension. In a typical MSA layer
197
      with input h ∈ RL×D , the query, key, and value are represented as hQ , hK , and hV respectively.
198
      The layer’s output is computed as follows:

207                                                                fP −T (p, h) = MSA(hQ , [pK ; hK ], [pV ; hV ])

      However, there is a key limitation of approaches that heavily rely on high-level representations such
210   as skill IDs or semantic information, which often face challenges in facilitating mutual improvement
211   between tasks that are not semantically similar, potentially overlooking the rich temporal and motion
212   information inherent in robotic actions. For example, while effective for knowledge transfer between
213   semantically similar tasks like ”grasp cube” and ”grasp mug”, these methods fall short in capturing
214   shared primitives across semantically distinct but motion-related tasks. This limitation can result
215   in sub-optimal knowledge transfer between seemingly unrelated tasks like ”grasp mug” and ”place
      banana”, which, despite their semantic differences, may share common underlying primitives.

      To address these limitations and capture semantic and motion shared primitives across differ-
217   ent skills, we propose Motion-Aware Prompting (MAP). MAP combines optical flow with task-
218   conditional semantic information, allowing us to capture and leverage common primitives across
219   seemingly disparate tasks. Specifically, motion-aware optical flow information provides a rich rep-
220   resentation of motion dynamics within the scene, capturing the essential kinematic properties of
221   primitive actions. This motion-centric approach allows us to identify and learn common move-
222   ment patterns across tasks, even when the high-level semantics differ. For instance, while ”grasp
223   cube” and ”place mug” may seem semantically unrelated, they both involve the primitive of arm
224   lowering. To capture these motion dynamics, we employ the Recurrent All-Pairs Field Transforms
225
      (RAFT) Teed & Deng (2020) model for optical flow estimation. In RAFT, the optical flow is com-
      puted iteratively:
226
                                               fk+1 = fk + ∆fk                                        (2)
227
228   where fk is the flow estimate at iteration k, and ∆fk is the flow update computed as:
229
                                        ∆fk , hk+1 = GRU(C(fk ), hk )                                  (3)
230
231   Here, C is a correlation volume, hk is a hidden state, and GRU is a gated recurrent unit. Optical
232   flow effectively captures these shared motion primitives, enabling more granular knowledge trans-
233   fer. Secondly, optical flow offers a degree of invariance to appearance changes, focusing instead on
234   the underlying motion structure. This property is particularly valuable in robotics, where the same
235
      primitive-level manipulation might be performed on objects with vastly different visual characteris-
      tics.
236
                                   I(x, y, t) = I(x + u∆t, y + v∆t, t + ∆t)                            (4)
237
238   where I is the image intensity, (u, v) is the optical flow vector, and ∆t is the time step. This
239   allows optical flow to capture motion information while being relatively insensitive to the specific
240   appearance of the scene. Concurrently, we embed conditional descriptions of tasks into a shared
241
      latent space using a pre-trained CLIP model. This allows us to leverage rich semantic understanding,
      providing a powerful representation of task semantics. By combining optical flow features with
242
      these task-conditional semantic embeddings, our Motion-Aware Prompting (MAP) achieves a dual
243
      purpose. We can represent this as:

      where T is the task description, F is the optical flow from RAFT, ECLIP (T ) is the CLIP-based
247
      semantic embedding function, Φ(F ) is a flow feature extraction function, and fprompt is a learned
248   function that combines semantic and motion information. The CLIP-based semantic embedding
249   ensures task-specificity, guiding the model towards relevant skills, while the flow feature enables
250   fine-grained decomposition of skills into primitives. This approach enables our model to learn and
251   transfer knowledge at the primitive level, thereby facilitating mutual improvement and lifelong ex-
252   pansion across diverse skills.

254   4.3   L IFELONG S KILL ACQUISITION
255
256   Parameter-efficient methods have shown remarkable success in mitigating catastrophic forgetting.
257   However, current state-of-the-art approaches exhibits limitations in expanding learning capacity
258   across tasks. They learn a single adapter for each new task, failing to leverage shared knowledge
259
      across different tasks. Therefore, we propose a novel lifelong skill acquisition method that during
      lifelong span, new prefix skill prompts are added and learned via cross-attention between prefix
260
      prompts of old skills, achieving helpful shared knowledge transfer from old skills to new ones.
261
262   Specifically, we introduce a new dimension to our learning capacity: a set of prompt compo-
263   nents. Our method combines these components through weighted summation, forming a decom-
264   posed prompt that is subsequently fed into the corresponding MSA layer. This enables us to expand
265
      our prompting capacity to arbitrary depths while maintaining a fixed prompt length. Notably, when
      prompting for new tasks in lifelong learning contexts, our method reuses previously acquired knowl-
266
      edge from past tasks, rather than initializing a new task prompt from scratch. Formally, we replace
267
      the learnable prompt parameter p with a weighted summation over the prompt components:
268

      Here, P ∈ RM ×D represents our set of prompt components, where M denotes the length of this set,
271   introducing an additional axis of capacity. The critical aspect of this formulation is determining the
272   appropriate weighting vector (α) for each task.
273
274
      To achieve dynamic prompt generation, we propose an innovative approach that computes the weight
      vector α based on the similarity between a primitive-based query θ(x) and a set of keys associated
275
      with the prompt components. This method allows for the production of primitive-based prompts
276
      without relying on the fixed task index. Specifically, the weighting vector is derived from the cosine
277   similarity between the query and a set of keys:
278

      where K ∈ RM ×D represents keys corresponding to the prompt components. This formulation en-
281   sures that each prompt component Pm contributes to the final prompt p in proportion to the similarity
282   between the query q(x) and its corresponding key Km .
283
284
      The challenge inherent in this prompt-query matching lies in its similarity to high-dimensional clus-
      tering, a notoriously difficult problem. To address this issue, the authors introduce an attention mech-
285
      anism to the key-query matching process. Each Pm is paired with both a key Km and an attention
286
      vector Am . This addition enables the query to focus on specific features within the high-dimensional
287   query q(x) output, potentially capturing more primitive-based features while disregarding less rele-
288   vant information. The implementation involves a straightforward feature-selection attention scheme.
289   An element-wise multiplication between the query vector and the attention vector produces an at-
290   tended query, which is then used for key-similarity matching. The refined approach to generating
291   the weighting vector is expressed as:

      Evaluation Metrics. Following Liu et al. (2023), we employ Forward Transfer Weight (FWT)
354
      and Backward Transfer Weight (BWT) to evaluate the performance of lifelong learning. FWT is
355
      computed by the maximum success rate our policy can achieve when adapting to a new task. We

471   Effect of Skill Prompt Count We conducted a comprehensive investigation into the optimal se-
472   lection of prompt count during the multi-skill learning. As various skills undergo joint optimization,
473   primitives are encoded and stored within prompts. For any specific task, only a subset of prompts
474   responds and matches to extract relevant prior knowledge, while unmatched prompts may introduce
475   noise. Consequently, as illustrated in Figure 7, an increase in the number of prompts does not neces-
476   sarily correlate with improved performance. Simultaneously, an insufficient number of prompts may
477   fail to encompass all primitives, underscoring the importance of achieving an appropriate balance in
478   prompt count.

480   Effect of Primitive Skill Prompt As illustrated in Figure 7, significant performance degradation
481   is observed when learning new skills under two conditions: (1) when prompt learning of primitives
482   is omitted during the pre-training phase, or (2) when pre-trained prompts are not utilized in the
483   acquisition of new skills. These findings substantiate the effectiveness of our proposed prompt
484   mechanism in extracting common knowledge from pre-trained skills. Moreover, they demonstrate
485   the mechanism’s capacity to repurpose this knowledge during the lifelong learning phase, thereby
      enhancing the performance of newly acquired skills.

      5.5   D ISCUSSION ON O UR M ETHOD V. S . L ORA AND M O E
501
      Recently, some studies have explored the effectiveness of LoRA Liu et al. (2023) and MOE Wang
502
      et al. (2024) in enhancing lifelong robot learning. However, as illustrated in Figure 7, our exper-
503   iments demonstrate that although MOE excels in terms of average success rate, its training speed
504   is slower due to the additional computational overhead introduced by its gating network and multi-
505   ple expert networks. MOE’s training time is approximately twice that of LoRA and our proposed
506   method. LoRA, on the other hand, emerges as the frontrunner in terms of training speed, while its
507   overall performance falls short of its competitors. Notably, our method achieves performance sur-
508   passing that of MOE while maintaining comparable training speed. This balance of efficiency and
509   efficacy enables our approach to effectively combine the strengths of LoRA and MOE, facilitating
510   faster skill knowledge acquisition while preserving high performance.

512   6     C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
513
514
      In this work, we present Primitive-level Skill Prompt Learning for lifelong robotic skill learning.
515
      Motion-aware skill prompts and text flow query mechanism are proposed to learn reusable and
516   extensible primitive-level knowledge across multiple skills and achieve superior results in multi-
517   task policy learning. Moreover, for new skill acquisition, new skill propmts are easily added and
518   learned for knowledge transfer between old and new skills, without redundant new parameters and
519   complex skill decomposition. Finally, we construct a large-scale primitive-level skill dataset and
520   demonstrate the superior perform of our method in multi-task policy learning and lifelong new skill
521   acquisition.
522
      Limitations: Our method requires the pre-processed primitive-level skill dataset for pre-training
523   stage, which is difficult to do for various human daily tasks. Moreover, motion-aware prompts relies
524   on optical flow estimators, which is unstable in lighting variation interactive environments. Future
525   work will focus on scaling our method to more daily tasks and extending the method to handle more
526   challenging lighting scenarios.
