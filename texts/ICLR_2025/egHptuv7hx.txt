011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Language models can intervened upon by steering their internal representations,
014            which alters the degree to which concepts such as emotional tone, style, truth-
015
               fulness, and safety are expressed in their generative outputs. This paper demon-
               strates that intervention efficacy, measured by linear steerability (the ability to
016
               adjust outputs via linear transformations of hidden states), emerges abruptly dur-
017
               ing pre-training, and furthermore, even closely-related concepts (e.g. anger and
018            sadness) can emerge at different stages of pre-training. To understand how the
019            steerability of internal representations changes during pre-training, we introduce
020            the “Intervention Detector” (ID), which applies unsupervised learning techniques
021            to hidden states under different stimuli, and generates concept representations that
022            can be used to steer the text generation of language models. The extracted concept
023            representations are used to compute an ID score, measuring their alignment with
024            the model’s hidden states. This ID score can be used to approximately predict the
025            time of emergence of effective intervention by steering different concepts, and the
026
               degree to which each concept is able to be intervened. By analyzing ID scores
               across a longitudinal series of models taken at different stages of pre-training, we
027
               demonstrate that, as pre-training progresses, concepts become increasingly easier
028
               to extract via linear methods, which correlates with the emergence of steerabil-
029            ity. For instance, in the CrystalCoder model, the linear steerability of the con-
030            cept “anger” emerges at 68% of pre-training, whereas the linear steerability of the
031            concept “sadness” emerges at 93% of the pre-training process. We use heatmap
032            visualizations and other metrics (eg., entropy, cosine similarity, tSNE) to study
033            these differences and validate the reliability and generalizability of the ID method
034            through model interventions using the extracted concept representations.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      Transformer-based language models have achieved considerable success, demonstrating substantial
      potential to enhance human productivity (Islam et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2021). To align these models’
040
      outputs more closely with desired outcomes (e.g. safe and truthful outputs, consistency in style and
041
      tone, and better reasoning abilities), a common approach is to fine-tune such models with carefully
042   curated datasets (Rai et al., 2024; Brown et al., 2020; Sébastien Bubeck, 2023; OpenAI et al., 2024).
043   However, this approach requires significant annotation effort and computational resources.
044
045
      Rather than resorting to expensive fine-tuning on large datasets, methods such as probing, which
      analyzes the information encoded in the hidden states of neural networks, have demonstrated that
046
      hidden states encode meaningful concept representations (Alain & Bengio, 2018; Hewitt & Man-
047
      ning, 2019; Tenney et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2019). Furthermore, by extracting those representations
048   from the hidden states, we can alter the representations to steer the models’s output towards desir-
049   able directions. Related methods, like steering vectors (Subramani et al., 2022; Turner et al., 2024)
050   and Contrast-Consistent Search (Burns et al., 2024), directly manipulate a language model’s hidden
051   states to control the model’s outputs. Among those methods, linear steering, a form of intervention
052   techniques which involves activation editing (Li et al., 2024; Hernandez et al., 2024), is adjustable,
053   minimally-invasive (Li et al., 2023), and has proven effective and cost-efficient at controlling model
      outputs (Cheng et al., 2024; Soatto et al., 2023; Bhargava et al., 2024). In particular, this intervention

083   To illustrate this point, we analyzed intervention effects throughout a longitudinal series of LLM360

085   Surprisingly, we found that the interventions were ineffective, or even counterproductive, during

091   To predict the emergence of linear steerability with respect to different concepts and understand the
092   causes of difference regard to steerability, we propose the Intervention Detector (ID) method, in-
093
      spired by Representation Engineering (Zou et al., 2023). This method (Figure 3) works by tracking
      concept representations associated with different interventions. By measuring changes in concept
094
      representations along the checkpoint steps, ID generates signals that suggest linear steerability will
095
      emerge soon with additional pre-training. Furthermore, ID can distinguish the times at which differ-
096   ent concepts first become steerable, and to what degree they will be steerable. ID can also serve as a
097   low-cost quality monitoring tool for applications requiring language models’ linear steerability, such
098   as language model agents, character-role-playing chatbots, or video game AI. To our knowledge, our
099   work is the first to conduct a longitudinal study (that is to say, across the pre-training life-cycle of
100   language models) on linear steerability and intervention effectiveness of language models.

141   Motivation: As scaling laws begin to show diminishing returns, enhancing model performance at
142   the post-training stage has garnered increasing attention. For instance, test-time computing (Snell
143   et al., 2024) has demonstrated that augmenting computational resources during inference can ef-
144   fectively improve the performance of large language models (LLMs). Similarly, optimizing the
145   representation to steer model outputs has also shown promise. Earlier studies on “linear steering”
146   methods (Li et al., 2023; Turner et al., 2024; Qian et al., 2024) have predominantly focused on fully
147   pre-trained and fine-tuned models, with limited emphasis on interventions during the pre-training
148   process. Understanding when a model exhibits steerability offers a more practical approach to as-
149
      sessing the pre-training. Since the emergence of steerability for concepts and the effectiveness of
      interventions vary, investigating these processes can make pre-training more efficient by identifying
150
      optimal stopping points, targeting specific concepts, and minimizing unnecessary computation.

153   2   R ELATED W ORK
154
155
      Past research on the mechanisms of fully pre-trained language models aimed to understand the repre-
156   sentation encoded by individual components in the models, including but not limited to neurons, hid-
157   den layers, and circuits (Madsen et al., 2022; Simonyan et al., 2014; Li et al., 2016; Ding & Koehn,
158   2021). Through probing methods, researchers have suggested that internal representations from
159   certain hidden layers may encode concepts we expect the model to learn (Alain & Bengio, 2018;
160   Hewitt & Manning, 2019; Tenney et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2019). Motivated by this understanding of
161   models’ inner working mechanisms, further studies take these concept features (extracted from the
      model) and reinforce them at inference time, thus controlling the model’s outputs (Li et al., 2023;


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Chen et al., 2024; Zou et al., 2023). Tan et al. (2024) using a metacognitive method to perform the
163   intervention, enabling the model to correct itself.
164
165   It has been demonstrated that sparse autoencoders could recover monosemantic features from a
166
      small one-layer transformer in Claude 3, indicating that in a well-trained model, concepts are well-
      separated and can be reduced to very low dimensionality (Templeton et al., 2024). Similar work,
167
      such as Representation Engineering (Zou et al., 2023), designs specific stimuli for a particular con-
168
      cept, and decomposes the model’s latent space by applying two opposing stimuli based on the
169   concept. The first principal component derived from this decomposition is considered the vector
170   representing the concept, a finding echoed by research at OpenAI (Gao et al., 2024).
171
172
      Currently, only a few teams have open-sourced checkpoints throughout the entire pre-training pro-
      cess, such as Bloom (Le Scao et al., 2023), Pythia (Biderman et al., 2023), MAP (Zhang et al.,
173
      2024) and LLM360 (Liu et al., 2023) (which we used the Crystal model from its family in this pa-
174
      per). As future work, we think that applying our proposed methods to other models’ pre-training
175   checkpoints would produce insights about how interventions are affected by different pre-training
176   datasets, though this is out of the scope of this paper.

487   common sense reasoning, and RACE is about extracting information from a passage. The Appendix
488   A.2 explains how we constructed stimuli based on ground truth data for specific reasoning datasets
489   and obtained ID scores that helped us investigate the emergence of linear steerability. To investigate
490   whether the improvement in ID scores was influenced by annealing effects or by using more training
491   data, we fine-tuned the model using two control groups with adjusted learning rates—one increased
492   tenfold and the other decreased tenfold. The results are shown in Figure 8. The second row of
493   the figure demonstrates a linear improvement in ID scores, which can be attributed to the increasing
494   separation of corresponding concepts in low-dimensional space during pre-training, with fine-tuning
495
      further enhancing this process.

497   scores across three pre-training stages. All results can be found in Figure 10 in Appendix A.3. This

      5   D ISCUSSION
512
513
      By utilizing the ID scores to evaluate changes in the concept extraction process across foundation
      model checkpoints, we identified three indicators that can approximately predict the emergence of
514
      model linear steerability: 1) a cessation in the decline of entropy following a sustained decrease,
515
      2) significant shifts in model representations during the pre-training stage, and 3) the maximum
516   divergence in ID scores across layers at a particular checkpoint during pre-training. A possible ex-
517   planation for these phenomena is that the pre-training process renders concepts linearly separable
518   in a low-dimensional space. In early checkpoints, the representations derived through linear de-
519   composition are insufficient to accurately reflect the true concept due to low signal-to-noise ratios
520   (SNR). However, as training progresses, the effectiveness of linear decomposition improves, leading
521   to better alignment between the extracted and true concept representations, and thus higher SNR,
522   resulting in more effective interventions. It is important to note that any open-source checkpoint, in
523   conjunction with a method for extracting concept representations through linear decomposition, can
524
      be evaluated for reliability using this metric.
525
526   6   C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this work, we first identify that model linear steerability—specifically, the effectiveness of inter-
529   ventions—differs from other capabilities and only emerges after a certain amount of pre-training.
530   Moreover, the linear steerability of different concepts vary significantly. We then provide a detailed
531   description of how to use the Inference Detector (ID) to observe changes in the extraction process
532   of internal concepts within a model and establish a connection between ID scores and the model’s
533
      linear steerability. By analyzing various metrics based on ID scores, we can approximate the point
      in training when linear steerability is likely to emerge. The emergence of linear steerability is an
534
      external manifestation of the model, internally driven by the increasing alignment of concept rep-
535
      resentations with human understanding. This alignment improves as the model’s internal features
536   become more easily extractable. As more layers of the model achieve higher ID scores, the represen-
537   tation of the concept in low-dimensional space becomes more pronounced. This analytical metric for
538   model linear steerability can also be applied to the checkpoints of other pre-trained models, serving
539   as a tool for analyzing the pre-training process and evaluating model performance.

813
      In supervised tasks, the construction of the stimulus set differs from that in unsupervised tasks. In
814

815

819

825
          the probability of this statement being false/wrong/incorrect is:

829   important to note that the representation extracted here may not directly correspond to ”truthfulness”
830   or ”correctness.” Instead, it represents the model’s attempt to give the correct answer when facing
831
      questions from the dataset. Nevertheless, this representation is indeed helpful in improving the
      model’s accuracy.
