011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Based on the alignment of image-text pairs, CLIP has demonstrated superior
014            performance across various tasks, even in a zero-shot setting. In person ReID,
015
               CLIP-based models achieve state-of-the-art results without explicit text descrip-
               tions for further fine-tuning. However, previous models are primarily initialized
016
               with weights from ImageNet or self-supervised methods, lacking cross-domain
017
               knowledge in both image and text areas. This paper introduces a novel approach
018            that aligns a pure image-domain pretrained student model with CLIP textual fea-
019            tures, distilling cross-domain knowledge from existing CLIP-ReID into the online
020            student model. To leverage CLIP’s textual features for each ID, we address the
021            challenge of mismatched feature dimensions between the teacher and student. A
022            trainable adapter is inserted on the student side to match dimensions and preserve
023            the prior knowledge within the pretrained student. For the student encoder yield-
024            ing lower or equal-dimensional features compared to the teacher, the adapter is
025            initialized as an identity matrix, while offline PCA is employed on the teacher
026
               side for dimensionality reduction. PCA eigenvectors are computed from all train-
               ing images and applied to existing text features for matching with the student. In
027
               cases where the student outputs exceed the teacher’s dimensions, the adapter is
028
               initialized using eigenvectors computed from the student side to retain knowledge
029            in the pretrained student model. After dimension alignment, text features for each
030            ID are compared with online image features, specifying cross-domain similari-
031            ties, which are further constrained to mimic the teacher through a KL-divergence
032            loss. Experiments with different pretraining encoder structures demonstrate the
033            effectiveness of this approach, which is also compatible with relation knowledge
034            distillation to enhance performance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      As a cross-domain model, CLIP Radford et al. (2021) can be directly utilized in many downstream
      tasks without any fine-tuning. E.g., it is able to classify images by inserting class keywords into a
040
      template like ”a photo of ...”, generating textual features that are compared with visual features from
041
      input image to make final predictions without prior training on labeled data. The zero-shot inference
042   capability of CLIP stems from its large number of training data consisting of image-text pairs, and
043   its training scheme which aligns visual and textual representations in a shared embedding space.
044   The cross domain alignment allows it to leverage knowledge from diverse data sources, making it
045   particularly effective for tasks where annotated data may be scarce or absent.
046
      In person re-identification (ReID), CLIP-based methods Li et al. (2023a); Lin et al. (2023); Zhai
047
      et al. (2024) that initialize with pretrained weights and fine-tune on downstream ReID data also
048   demonstrate competitive results. Notably, these approaches eliminate the need for explicit textual
049   descriptions by using trainable text prompt tokens to represent each ID, serving as constraints for
050   optimizing the image encoder. However, most of the previous ReID methods Zhou et al. (2019);
051   He et al. (2021); Luo et al. (2021); Chen et al. (2023), rely on the single domain pretraining model
052   derived only from supervised or self-supervised methods in the image domain. As a result, there
053   are no pretrained text encoders available to provide cross-domain descriptions. Such single-domain
      methods negatively impacts the model’s performance. To bridge the gap between single and cross

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      domain models, our method introduces a novel approach that aligns a image-domain pretrained
055   student model with CLIP’s textual features. This alignment provides a comprehensive solution to
056   the absence of pretrained text encoders, enabling the matching textual features of each ID for any
057   single domain image encoder. By effectively leveraging the strengths of both visual and textual
058   domains, we distill cross-domain knowledge from the teacher model, which is the CLIP-ReID Li
059   et al. (2023a), to enhance the student model’s performance.
060
      Particularly, to tackle the challenge of mismatched feature dimensions between the teacher and
061   student, a trainable adapter is inserted on the student side to ensure dimensional consistency while
062   preserving the prior knowledge in the pretrained student model. The adapter is a simple linear layer
063   with initialization schemes that vary based on the dimension comparison between the teacher and
064   student. For cases where the student outputs features of lower or equal dimensions compared to
065   the teacher, the adapter is initialized as an identity matrix. Meanwhile, we employ offline PCA on
066   the teacher model for dimensionality reduction, using PCA eigenvectors computed from the training
067   images to align existing text features with the student. When the student outputs exceed the teacher’s
068   dimensions, the adapter utilizes eigenvectors derived from the student model as the initial parameters
069
      to retain valuable knowledge. After achieving dimension alignment, we compare the text features
      of each ID with the online image features, specifying cross-domain similarities and constraining
070
      them through a KL-divergence loss to emulate the teacher model’s performance. To validate the
071
      effectiveness of our approach, we conduct experiments on various backbones, including TinyViT
072   Wu et al. (2022), OSNet Zhou et al. (2019), and Solider Chen et al. (2023), which are pretrained
073   using either supervised learning on ImageNet or self-supervised learning on LUPerson Fu et al.
074   (2021). In summary, the contributions of this paper are as follows.
075
076          • We propose to align the image-domain pretrained backbone with existing textual features
077            that describe each ID, creating a text-image cross-domain model. We address three scenar-
078
               ios in which the teacher’s feature dimension is larger than, equal to, or smaller than that of
               the student. To ensure dimensional alignment while preserving the knowledge within the
079
               student model, we utilize a parametric adapter with tailored initialization schemes for each
080
               scenario.
081
082
             • Our findings indicate that knowledge distillation using a pretrained CLIP-based ReID
               model as the teacher can significantly enhance the student’s performance. Notably, the
083
               cross-domain and relational knowledge distillation approaches are compatible in ReID
084
               tasks, effectively compensating for the triplet and ID loss during supervised fine-tuning.
085
             • We conduct extensive experiments across various person ReID datasets to demonstrate the
086
               effectiveness of our method. Specifically, we achieve state-of-the-art results on both the
087
               Market1501 and MSMT17 datasets using the Soldier pretrained backbone.

090   2   R ELATED W ORKS
091
092   Supervised Person ReID is a common representation learning task. CNN-based models, particu-
093
      larly those built on ResNet-50 Luo et al. (2019); Dai et al. (2019); Ye et al. (2022) pretrained on
      ImageNet, have been widely adopted across various ReID datasets. These models are typically op-
094
      timized using a combination of ID classification loss and metric learning, specifically the triplet loss
095
      Hermans et al. (2017), to reduce the distances between features of the same ID in the embedding
096   space, ensuring the model can generalize to unseen IDs during inference. However, CNNs often fo-
097   cus on small, irrelevant regions in the spatial feature maps, limiting the effectiveness of their feature
098   representations. To address this, researchers have introduced attention layers on top of CNNs Chen
099   et al. (2019); Wang et al. (2022a) to expand the receptive field. In addition to global representations,
100   local part features Sun et al. (2018); Wang et al. (2018); Li et al. (2021) and semantic parts Kalayeh
101   et al. (2018); Zhu et al. (2020) have also proven effective in learning more discriminative features.
102   Besides ResNet-50, other light-weight CNN backbones like Zhou et al. (2019); Gu et al. (2023) are
103   also proposed. They have advantages on training and inference speed.
104   Image transformers Dosovitskiy et al. (2021) have recently gained popularity in ReID tasks. Like
105   CNN-based models, they are also pretrained on ImageNet. Models such as TransReID He et al.
106   (2021), AAFormer Zhu et al. (2021), DCAL Zhu et al. (2022a), DC-Former Li et al. (2023b), and
107   PFD Wang et al. (2022b) have achieved better performance than CNN-based approaches, particu-
      larly on large and challenging ReID datasets like MSMT. Furthermore, self-supervised pretraining


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      methods built on larger datasets, such as LUPerson Fu et al. (2021), including TransReID-SSL Luo
109   et al. (2021), PASS Zhu et al. (2022b), and SOLIDER Chen et al. (2023), have further boosted
110   performance.
111
112
      All of the above methods utilize models pretrained in a single image domain. CLIP-ReID Li et al.
      (2023a) is the first work to leverage CLIP’s cross-domain pretraining and has achieved state-of-
113
      the-art results on MSMT, demonstrating the potential of cross-domain pretraining even for single-
114
      domain ReID tasks. Thus, we select CLIP-ReID as the teacher model, aligning its textual features
115   for each ID with a single-domain model, thereby incorporating cross-domain constraints during
116   optimization.
117
118
      Knowledge Distillation Hinton (2015) encourages the online student model to mimic the teacher
      model. Typically, the teacher has more parameters than the student and remains fixed during training.
119
      By applying a distillation loss that compares the outputs of the teacher and student models, the so-
120
      called ”dark knowledge” from the teacher can be transferred to the student. For classification tasks,
121   this distillation loss is often computed using KL divergence, which measures the difference between
122   the two predicted logit vectors produced by softmax functions. Distillation can also be performed in
123   other forms, such as feature distance Romero et al. (2014), pairwise similarity Tung & Mori (2019);
124   Park et al. (2019), or through contrastive learning Tian et al. (2019). The teacher model can be
125   updated online during training Tarvainen & Valpola (2017). Recently, knowledge distillation has
126   been widely adopted for training lightweight models He et al. (2022); Wu et al. (2022), and even for
127   ViT Touvron et al. (2021). It can also be performed more efficiently through fast distillation methods
128   Shen & Xing (2022). Moreover, online distillation has proven useful for cross-domain pretraining
129
      on image-text pairs Dong et al. (2023).

131   3     M ETHOD
132
133   We propose a novel knowledge distillation scheme for supervised learning in person ReID. Our key
134   idea is to leverage a CLIP-based model as the teacher, guiding the optimization of the student model
135   by incorporating a cross-domain constraint to mimic the teacher. The overview of the proposed
136   method is shown in Figure 1. Formally, we denote T as the teacher model, which consists of
137   an image encoder EI and text encoder ET structured as transformers. The image features fI =
138   EI (I) ∈ Rd output by EI represent each image and are used in the inference stage for pairwise
139
      distance calculations, where d is the feature dimension.

      3.2      A LIGNING T EXTUAL F EATURES FOR A NY P RETRAINED I MAGE E NCODER
198
      To fully utilize the cross domain description ability of CLIP-based ReID model, we consider to
199
      match textual features of all identities with any pretrained image encoder, therefore, converting the
200   single domain image encoder EI′ into the cross domain model including a pair of EI′ and ET . The
201   main challenge is to align the dimension between fI′ ∈ Rd′ in d′ dimensional space with fT in d
202   space. We consider following cases according to comparison results between d and d′ .
203
204
      3.2.1          D IMENSION REDUCTION AT THE TEACHER SIDE
205
206   When the dimensionality of the textual feature fT exceeds that of the image feature fI′ (i.e., d > d′ ),
207   it is necessary to reduce the dimensions of fT . For this purpose, we employ Principal Component
208   Analysis (PCA). Specifically, we collect all image features from the training data using EI . We
209   then compute the d′ eigenvectors corresponding to the largest eigenvalues in the feature space of
                                                                                       ′
210   fI , which collectively form a projection matrix denoted as Wd→d′ ∈ Rd ×d . Since CLIP-ReID
211   keeps the cross domain alignment characteristic of the original CLIP, this matrix is then applied to
212   all textual features fTi describing each identity, where i = 1, 2, . . . , C, resulting in the dimension-
                                   ′
213   reduced textual features fTi ∈ Rd′ . The PCA retains most of the relevant dimensions in the teacher
214   model while minimizing information loss. Note that the dimension reduction at the teacher side is
215   a fixed operation, where Wd→d′ is not learnable and remains static. Moreover, Wd→d′ is omitted
      when the student gives the larger dimension which d ≤ d′ .

      3.2.2    L EARNABLE ADAPTER AT THE STUDENT SIDE AND ITS INITIALIZATION
217
218   The online student model EI′ must be fine-tuned for person ReID while simultaneously aligning
219   with the teacher model. To leverage the pretrained weights of EI′ and utilize its prior knowledge, we
220   aim to make minimal modifications without introducing numerous randomly initialized parameters.
221   Therefore, we insert a simple linear learnable layer, parameterized by Wa , at the output of EI′ to
222
      align fI′ with the teacher model’s features. The dimensions of Wa depend on two cases: when d ≥ d′
      or when d < d′ . In the first case, where the student’s feature dimension is greater than or equal to
223                                     ′   ′

224
      the teacher’s (d ≥ d′ ), Wa ∈ Rd ×d maintains the feature dimension from EI′ . In the second case,
                                                                                                ′
225   where the student’s feature dimension is less than the teacher’s (d < d′ ), Wa ∈ Rd×d reduces the
226
      dimension to d.
                                                                                     ′   ′
227   Furthermore, it is important to initialize Wa appropriately. For Wa ∈ Rd ×d , we initialize it as
228   the identity matrix I. In other words, at the beginning of training, the features fI′ from the stu-
229   dent backbone EI′ are directly passed through the adapter unchanged, ensuring a warm start. In
                                    ′
230   the case where Wa ∈ Rd×d , we initialize it using PCA eigenvectors computed from all training
231   images processed by the pretrained EI′ . This initialization allows the adapter to capture the most
232   significant variance, preserving essential information and facilitating effective alignment with the
233   feature space of the teacher. In practice, we find that this straightforward linear adapter and its ini-
234
      tialization scheme not only facilitate teacher-student alignment but also ensure compatibility of the
      cross-domain knowledge distillation loss with other loss functions.

      3.3.2    R E ID LOSS
283
284   For supervised person ReID, the cross-entropy loss Lid and the triplet loss Ltri are two commonly
285   used loss functions for optimization. These two losses are also compatible with the knowledge
286   distillation losses Lkl and Lrkd . Specifically, Lrkd serves as a complement to Ltri , while Lkl

288   traditional ReID training and knowledge distillation techniques. In the experiment section, we verify

      4.1     I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
301
302   We utilize CLIP-ReID Li et al. (2023a) as the teacher model. It provides us image and textual
303   features from its image and text encoder EI and ET , respectively. Particularly, we choose the ViT-
304   B model without any camera ID as input for side information embedding. We only use the cross
305   domain (post) layer, which is the last layer of CLIP-ReID, and both the image and textual feature
306   fI , fT ∈ R512 . Features from middle and previous layer in the teacher model are not considered
307   during the distillation. We follow most of the training set in CLIP-ReID. Images are resized into the
308   resolution of 256 × 128. Each mini-batch consists of B = P × K images, where P = 16 represents
309
      the number of randomly selected identities, and K = 4 indicates the number of samples per identity.
      For data augmentation, random horizontal flipping, padding, cropping, and erasing are conducted
310
      on the student side.
311
312   We choose TinyViT (11M) Wu et al. (2022), OSNet Zhou et al. (2019), Solider-Tiny, -Small and
313   -Base models Chen et al. (2023) as the student for optimization. All these models are pretrained
314   within the single image domain. The first two are pretrained on ImageNet-22k and -1k in supervised
315
      way. The Solider series are pretrained by self-supervised learning. For TinyViT, image features fI′
      lie in lower dimensional space than the teacher, which means d′ = 448 < d. Hence, the learnable
316
      adapter is in size of 448 × 448 and initialized by the identity matrix. Offline PCA dimension reduc-
317
      tion is performed on textual features from the teacher model. For OSNet, image feature dimension
318   d′ = 512 = d, and the adapter is 512 × 512 and initialized in the same way. We omit PCA at
319   the student side. For Solider series models, all models have larger dimension than the teacher with
320   d′ > d. The tiny and small models have d′ = 768 and the adapter is of 768 × 512. While the base
321   model has d′ = 1024, and the adapter is 1024 × 512. They are initialized by PCA eigen vectors
322   computed from all training images. For TinyViT, it is trained for 90 epochs with Cosine annealing
323   for scheduling the learning rate. For optimization of OSNet and Solider series, it is trained for 120
      epochs. Extra training details are provided at the Appendix.

      4.3   A BLATION STUDIES AND VISUALIZATIONS
393
394
      Detailed ablation studies are carried out on MSMT17 datasets with TinyViT as the backbone, to
395
      show the effectiveness of our cross domain distillation scheme and usage of the inserted adapter.
396
      The results are listed in Table 4. Specifically, we first conduct series of experiments without bringing
397   in the learnable adapter. This is mainly for checking the utility of each loss term. It is obvious that
398   training ReID model in normal supervised manner by ID loss Lid and triplet loss Ltri only gives
399   an inferior result with mAP of 58.2 and R1 of 81.7. Incorporating any distillation loss, e.g., Lrkd ,
400   Lkl or Lckl , will give a boost on the metrics. Here Lkl defined in Equation 4 introduces the cross
401   domain similarity from CLIP-based teacher model. Lrkd proposed by Park et al. (2019) is computed
402   based on the pairwise relation of two image features. We also try knowledge distillation from the
403   parametric image classifier in the last layer of the teacher model, which is Lckl , and it is a pure
404   image domain distillation loss without considering the cross domain similarity like Lkl . Although
405
      any distillation loss gives the positive effect, Lrkl gives the best performance with mAP of 68.0.
      Moreover, they are not compatible with each other, and using any two of them leads to a worse
406
      metric. Then we insert a linear adapter at the student side to make Lkl to be compatible with Lrkd .
407
      In this case, these two knowledge distillation losses computed on different image features after and
408   before the learnable adapter, respectively. It gives the best mAP of 68.4. However, adding Lckl or
409   removing Lkl degrades the metric to 67.9 and 66.3, respectively. Moreover, we are curious about
410   the effect of Ltri and Lid , and find that removing any of them slightly decreases the metric to 68.2
411   and 67.7, respectively. We think these downstream task losses become important when optimizing
412   a better pretrained model like Solider series, and helps the student model beat the teacher. More
413   ablations on OSNet can be found in the Appendix, which also gives a similar results.
414   To further compare the teacher and student models and understand the effect of knowledge distil-
415   lation losses, TSNE visualization is performed in Figure 2. Particularly, we randomly select 10
416   identities and encode their images into the cross domain embedding layer after the adapter. Then
417   TSNE reduces these vectors into 2D space. We compare feature spread in the 2D space and find
418   that the student model highly mimic the behaivour of the teacher model, showing that knowledge
419   distillation losses indeed affect the training process for the student model.
420   We also perform Gradcam Selvaraju et al. (2017) visualizations and compare the results with CLIP-
421   ReID. Gradcam heat-maps aims to explain the model classification results. Usually, smaller model
422   tends to give a reasonable results. Figure 3 shows the results from CLIP-ReID and TinyViT-KD on
423   the top and bottom rows, respectively. Obviously, the bottom row tends to highlight on the body of
424   person, showing that TinyViT-KD have learned reasonable feature representations. Furthermore, we
425   also show person retrieval results in Figure 4.
426   Since knowledge distillation primarily aims to train models smaller than the teacher, we list and
427   compare the key parameters of different backbones in Table 3 to highlight the advantages of our
428   smaller models. From this comparison, we observe that TinyViT and OSNet are significantly smaller
429   than other models, with much lower feature dimensions. This is particularly beneficial for ReID
430   inference, as a large number of gallery images typically have pre-computed features, and online
431   inference mainly involves forwarding the query image and calculating a vast number of distances.
      Thus, a compact feature representation directly improves inference efficiency.

471   5   C ONCLUSION
472
473   This paper proposes a cross-domain knowledge distillation method for person ReID. We utilize an
474
      optimized CLIP-based ReID model as the teacher to provide cross-domain similarity and pairwise
      relational guidance to the online student model, which is pretrained solely in the image domain
475
      using either supervised or self-supervised methods. An adapter is incorporated on the student side
476
      to align its output with the cross-domain embedding of the CLIP-based model. Depending on the
477   dimensional relationship between the student and teacher models, we adopt different strategies for
478   initializing the adapter to better preserve the knowledge within the student model, and optionally
479   reduce textual feature dimension at the teacher side to compute the cross domain similarity for the
480   online model. Constrained by the relational and cross domain KD loss imposed before and after
481   the adapter, our method significantly enhances the performance of different backbones on multiple
482   ReID datasets.
