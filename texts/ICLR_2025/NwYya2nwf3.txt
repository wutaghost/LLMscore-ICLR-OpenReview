011                                                              A BSTRACT
012
013              Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) models have gained attention as a novel approach to
014              developing large language models (LLMs), praised for their ability to enhance
015              performance by utilizing multiple experts. However, while increasing the number
016              of experts in these models can yield performance gains, it also introduces sig-
017
                 nificant trade-offs, such as substantial memory overhead and increased inference
                 time, limiting their scalability and practical deployment. In this work, we conduct
018
                 a thorough analysis of expert utilization and identify inefficiency: many experts
019
                 are underutilized, leading to suboptimal resource allocation with limited improve-
020              ment. To address this issue, we propose ExpertZIP, a progressive framework for
021              MoE models that leverages a Huffman tree-based expert fusion technique. This
022              progressive approach systematically merges underutilized experts step by step,
023              ensuring their essential contributions are maintained while drastically reducing
024              memory usage and computational demands. Our approach yields a 17.23x reduc-
025              tion in model size and a 4.84x improvement in inference time, with only a 1.18%
026              decrease in average accuracy compared to the original 64-expert Switch Trans-
027              former model. Moreover, it demonstrates a 6.47% increase in accuracy relative to
028
                 models with an equivalent number of experts. These results demonstrate that our
                 optimized framework provides performance on par with larger models, offering
029
                 an efficient solution for resource-constrained and real-time applications.

073   ciency becomes crucial as models continue to scale. For example, deploying large MoE models in
074   real-time applications, such as on mobile devices, can be both expensive and impractical.

      Through our analysis of expert utilization within MoE models shown in Fig. 3, we observe that the
076
      usage of experts is often highly imbalanced. Many experts are underutilized, being rarely activated
077
      across different input samples. This imbalance is quantified by the selected frequency, represent-
078   ing the proportion of times an expert is chosen across the layers for a given dataset. The selected
079   frequency for each expert in a layer indicates how often it is activated, with the sum of selected
080   frequencies across all experts in a layer equal to 1. This imbalance suggests that the performance
081   gains from adding more experts may not scale proportionally, as certain experts are not fully lever-
082   aged. Importantly, we also observe that even the less frequently used experts still contribute valuable
083   information to the model’s overall performance. This finding suggests that removing underutilized
084   experts to reduce model complexity could result in losing important information, leading to a degra-
085   dation in model accuracy.
086   To address these inefficiencies, we propose a novel approach called ExpertZIP (see Fig. 1) that
087   enhances the efficiency of MoE models by merging underutilized experts rather than discarding
088   them. Our method employs a weight combination strategy, utilizing Huffman tree Huffman (1952)
089   to identify and combine the least significant experts. The Huffman tree, a well-known data structure
090   used for data compression and encoding, suits our approach as it allows progressive merging of
091   the least frequent experts and can preserve the most important one, perfectly aligning with our
092
      goal of balancing performance and efficiency. Additionally, since it is a tree structure, we can
      reduce the number of experts by stopping at a certain level. This approach ensures that the model
093
      retains the critical contributions of all experts while reducing redundancy and maintaining a compact
094
      architecture. Additionally, since we apply fusion to reduce the experts, we can preserve the original
095   model’s knowledge to achieve similar or surpassing performance compared to the original one.
096
097   This solution leads to a more efficient and deployable model architecture, making it suitable for
098
      real-world applications. By reducing the number of experts, we achieve significant savings in both
      memory usage and inference time. Our experiments demonstrate that the optimized model achieves
099
      performance comparable to or surpassing models with more experts. Specifically, in classification
100
      tasks, our method results in only a 1.18% drop in average accuracy, while in summarization tasks, the
101   ROUGE-1 score Lin (2004) decreases by just 3.09%. Despite these minor performance reductions,
102   the model achieves a 4.84x improvement in inference time and a 17.23x reduction in model size.
103   As for comparing with the same number of experts after fusion, our approach can increase at most
104   6.47% and 7.74% on the classification and summarization task. These results validate the effec-
105   tiveness of our approach, demonstrating its suitability for deployment in environments with limited
106   resources but still preserving its performance, such as edge devices and real-time applications.

130      2.1                     E XPERT M ERGING IN M IXTURE - OF -E XPERTS M ODELS
131
132      The Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) model has gained prominence in large language models (LLMs)
133      for its scalable capacity Rajbhandari et al. (2022); Li et al. (2023a); Jiang et al. (2024); Cai et al.
134      (2024). However, as complexity increases, efficient expert utilization and computational overhead
135      become significant challenges. Recent methods have aimed to enhance efficiency and scalability
136
         while maintaining or improving performance through expert merging.
137      Key approaches include MEO He et al. (2023), which enables top-K expert selection without signifi-
138      cantly increasing FLOPs. The Lory framework Zhong et al. (2024) optimizes auto-regressive models
139      by using similarity-based data batching. ZIPIT Stoica et al. (2023) introduces a “zip” operation for
140      merging disjoint tasks, aligning features across and within models for partial merging, effectively
141      addressing feature compatibility issues. MC-SMoE Li et al. (2023b) leverages routing policies in
142
         sparse MoE to merge experts based on activation frequency, followed by compression to reduce
         memory and FLOPs. REPAIR Jordan et al. (2022) mitigates variance collapse in model merging by
143
         rescaling activations, improving interpolation performance and reducing accuracy barriers.
144
145      Most existing methods focus on model architecture, limiting the use of underutilized experts and
146      failing to fully exploit their potential. Our approach addresses this by merging underutilized experts
147      and adjusting the number of experts per layer, leading to more efficient capacity utilization and
148
         greater adaptability across configurations.
149
150      2.2                     M ODEL E NSEMBLE T ECHNIQUES
151
152      Model ensemble techniques enhance robustness and performance in machine learning by leverag-
153      ing model diversity, improving generalization, and mitigating overfitting. Key approaches include
154      Bagging Breiman (1996); Khwaja et al. (2015); Błaszczyński & Stefanowski (2015), which reduces
155      variance by training on data subsets, Boosting Freund et al. (1996); Waltner et al. (2019), which
156      sequentially corrects errors, and Stacking Wolpert (1992); Low et al. (2019); Kang et al. (2020),
157      which combines model outputs using a meta-learner.
158      Recent advancements have applied ensemble methods to deep learning, such as Deep Ensembles
159      Lakshminarayanan et al. (2017); Buschjäger et al. (2020), which aggregate independently trained
160      neural networks for improved performance and uncertainty estimation, and Snapshot Ensembles
161      Huang et al. (2017); Zhang et al. (2020), which create ensembles within a single training run to
         minimize additional costs. Further innovations, like ME-TRPO Kurutach et al. (2018) for reinforce-

163   ensemble techniques in various domains.

172   3.1   M IXTURE OF E XPERTS
173
      Mixture-of-Experts models offer an advanced approach to improving the efficiency and scalability
174
      of Transformer architectures. These models replace feed-forward network (FFN) layers with MoE
175
      layers, where only a subset of expert FFNs is activated for each input. In an MoE layer, E experts
176   are defined as FFN(·; θ1 ), . . . , FFN(·; θE ), each mapping an input from Rd to Rd . For a given
177   input token x with hidden state hx ∈ Rd , the routing  P function R(hx ) selects the top k experts,
178   and the output ox ∈ Rd is computed as: ox =              i∈Top-k(R(hx )) ei · FFN(hx ; θi ), where ei =
179   Softmax(R(hx ))i represents the normalized routing score for the i-th expert.
180
181
      3.2   H UFFMAN T REE
182
183   Huffman tree Huffman (1952) is a widely-used data structure for lossless data compression designed
184   to merge the nodes based on frequencies. It starts by constructing a binary tree, where each leaf
185   represents a symbol, and the path from the root defines the binary code for that symbol. Given a
186   set of symbols with frequencies, the algorithm iteratively merges the two least frequent symbols,
187   assigns binary digits to each branch, and recalculates combined frequencies until only one node
188
      remains. In the context of our designed MoE models, each expert corresponds to a symbol, with
      activation frequency analogous to symbol frequency.

191   4     M ETHODOLOGY
192
193   We aim to enhance the efficiency of MoE models by strategically reducing the number of experts
194   while preserving or improving overall performance. Our approach is designed to facilitate the fusion
195   of any number of experts, ensuring the retention of essential knowledge throughout the process.
196   The methodology is structured into expert selection via the Huffman tree, expert fusion through a
197
      progressive weighted sum approach, and fine-tuning of the fused model. This process ensures that
      the contributions of each expert are preserved during fusion, allowing for flexible reduction without
198
      compromising the model’s integrity.
199
200
      4.1   E XPERT S ELECTION S TRATEGY U SING H UFFMAN T REE
201
202   The first phase of our approach involves selecting which experts to fuse based on their utilization
203   frequency within the MoE model. Our analysis has revealed that there is often a significant imbal-
204   ance in expert utilization, where specific experts are underutilized. This underutilization suggests
205   that these experts contribute less to the overall performance, making them prime candidates for fus-
206   ing. To systematically identify and select these underutilized experts, we employ the Huffman tree,
207   a well-known data structure for Huffman coding. Huffman tree is particularly suited for this task
208   because it optimally fuses elements with the lowest frequencies, which aligns with our objective of
209
      reducing the number of underutilized experts and supporting the fusion of any desired number of
      experts by halting at a specified stage.
210
211   For each MoE layer, we calculate the selected frequency of the experts based on the training data.
212   The distribution is denoted as F l = {f1l , f2l , . . . , fN
                                                                 l

213   the i-th expert at the l-th layer. The frequency fil is computed as the proportion of input tokens in
214   the dataset for which expert ei is among the top-k experts selected by the routing function R(hlx ),
215   where hlx is the hidden state of input token x at the l-th layer, and D represents the set of input
      tokens in the training data. The selected frequency fil is formally calculated as:

220   token, only the top-1 expert is selected. To control the reduction rate of experts, we define a variable
221   called speed, which dictates the rate at which experts are fused and reduced. For example, if the
222   original number of experts is 16 and the speed is set to 2, the experts are reduced to 8 before fine-

225   The Huffman tree process then fuses the two experts with the lowest frequencies iteratively, forming
226   a new expert whose frequency is the sum of the frequencies of the original two experts. This process
227   is repeated until the desired number of experts, Ne , is achieved. In practice, we use a min heap
228   to efficiently extract the node with the minimum frequency for each step. To illustrate the fusing

422     Expert Selection. We compare four methods based on performance to identify how more balanced
423     expert combinations lead to better results. The Max-Max strategy, which pairs experts with the high-
424     est selected frequencies, creates a more imbalanced expert selection. The random strategy, pairing
425     experts randomly, also fails to produce improvements as the lack of structure in expert selection leads
426     to unpredictability. In contrast, the Max-Min strategy, which pairs experts with the highest and low-
427     est frequencies, achieves a more balanced contribution, resulting in better performance than Max-
428     Max and random strategies. Our proposed ExpertZIP method, which ensures both flexibility and
429     a balanced distribution of expert contributions, achieves the best performance because it not only
430     promotes balance but also leverages the Huffman tree structure. This approach minimizes the im-
431     pact on high-frequency experts, focusing the merging process on lower-frequency experts, thereby
        preserving the performance of the most important experts (see Fig. 5).

519   7      C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
520
521   In this paper, we present ExpertZIP, a novel framework for optimizing Mixture-of-Experts models by
522   fusing underutilized experts through a Huffman tree-based expert fusion technique. Our approach
523   addresses the inefficiencies caused by imbalanced expert utilization, significantly reducing model
524   size and inference time while maintaining near-equivalent performance. Specifically, we demon-
525
      strated that our method, applied to the Switch Transformer model, achieves a 17.23x reduction in
      model size and a 4.84x improvement in inference time, with only a 1.18% drop in accuracy for clas-
526
      sification tasks and a 3.09% drop in ROUGE-1 score for summarization tasks. Furthermore, when
527
      compared with Switch Transformer models having the same number of experts after fusion, our
528   approach shows improvements of up to 6.47% on classification tasks and 7.74% on summarization
529   tasks. Additionally, we successfully test ExpertZIP on the Mixtral 8x7b model, designed to han-
530   dle multiple tasks simultaneously and enhance generalization. Our results confirm that ExpertZIP
531   remains effective even on this more generalized architecture, demonstrating its scalability and adapt-
532   ability. In our future planning, we aim to investigate strategies for dynamically adjusting the number
533   of experts at different layers. This could lead to even greater improvements in model efficiency and
534   performance, expanding the applicability of MoE models to a broader range of real-world scenarios.

      A    A LGORITHM
757
758
      In this section, we provide the detailed algorithms that form the core of our approach for compressing
759   Mixture of Experts (MoE) models. Algorithm 1 outlines the Huffman tree-based method for select-
760   ing and fusing underutilized experts, leveraging their utilization frequencies to identify candidates
761   for merging systematically. This process ensures that the most redundant experts are prioritized for
762   fusion, guided by the principles of Huffman coding.
763
      Algorithm 2 presents the weighted sum fusion process, where the parameters of the selected experts
764
      are aggregated based on their utilization frequencies. This technique ensures that critical knowledge
765   from the original experts is preserved, while maintaining model stability and performance. These
766   algorithms work in tandem to achieve significant compression in the number of experts, balancing
767   model efficiency and effectiveness.
768
769   Algorithm 1 Huffman Fusing
770
      Input: Selected frequency F = {f1 , f2 , · · · , fN }, number of required expert Ne
771
      Output: List of fusing indices Mf
772    1: heap ← heapifiy([f{1} , f{2} , · · · , f{N } ])                           ▷ Creating a data heap
773    2: Mf ← []
774    3: while size of heap > Ne do
775    4:     fx ← Extract minimum element from heap
776    5:     fy ← Extract minimum element from heap
777    6:     fx∪y ← fx + fy ; Insert fx∪y into heap           ▷ Fusing two experts and insert back to heap
778    7: end while
779    8: while size of heap > 0 do
780    9:     fK ← Extract minimum element from heap
781
      10:     Append K into Mf
      11: end while
782
      12: return Mf

             ′
