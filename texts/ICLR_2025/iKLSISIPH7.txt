009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In this study, we propose a new bandit framework of stochastic matching employ-
012            ing the Multinomial Logit (MNL) choice model with feature information. In this
013            framework, agents on one side are assigned to arms on the other side, and each
014            arm stochastically accepts an agent among the assigned pool of agents based on
015            its unknown preference, allowing a possible outside option of not accepting any.
016            The objective is to minimize regret by maximizing the probability of successful
017            matching. For this framework, we first
                                                   ‚ąö propose
                                                                an elimination-based algorithm
018            that achieves a regret bound of O K rKT over time horizon T , where K is the

019            number of arms and r is the rank of feature space. Furthermore, we propose an
020            approach to resolve the computation issue regarding combinatorial optimization
021
               in the algorithm. Lastly, we evaluate the performances of our algorithm through
               experiments comparing with the existing showing the superior performances of
022
               our algorithm.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      In recent times, the rising prevalence of matching markets, such as ride-hailing services, online job
028
      markets, and online labor markets, has increased the demand for strategies to enhance successful
029
      matching. In ride-hailing services, for instance, the goal is to pair riders with drivers to maximize
030   the probability of successful matched rides, thereby increasing revenue. In online job markets, the
031   goal is to maximize successful employment between applicants and employers.
032
033
      Due to the demand for matching problems, online (bipartite) matching problems have been widely
      studied (Karp et al., 1990; Mehta et al., 2007; 2013; Gamlath et al., 2019; Fuchs et al., 2005; Kessel-
034
      heim et al., 2013). In these problems, there are two sides for vertices where vertices are revealed
035
      one at a time. Then the focus is on optimizing to maximize matching. However, there exists a gap
036   between the models considered in online matching problems and their real-world applications. For
037   ease of presentation, in what follows, we refer to the vertices on one side as agents and those on the
038   other side as arms. Previous studies only focused on assigning a single agent to an arm, not allow-
039   ing the assignment of multiple agents to an arm. More crucially, based on these setups, the focus
040   has been solely on optimization to maximize matching rather than learning underlying models from
041   bandit feedback. In real-world applications such as ride-hailing services, however, preferences are
042   unknown in advance, and learning the latent preferences of drivers is necessary while maximizing
043   the probability of call acceptance.
044   Based on the demand for online learning in real-world applications, sequential matching under ban-
045   dit feedback, referred to as matching bandits, has recently gained attention (Liu et al., 2020; 2021;
046   Sankararaman et al., 2020; Basu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022; Kong & Li, 2023). The typical
047   problem setting studied in the matching bandits is as follows. In each round, each agent is assigned
048   to an arm, and then the arms accept an agent among the assigned pool of agents, which results in
049   the completed matching(s) of an agent and an arm. Then, stochastic reward feedback correspond-
050
      ing to each match is observed, where the true distributions of rewards are assumed to be unknown.
      The objective is to find stable matching based on the preferences as in the stable marriage problems
051
      McVitie & Wilson (1971) by learning reward distributions.
052
053   However, there is still a gap between models considered in the matching bandit literature and real-
      world applications. The previous studies presume that the behavior of arms is deterministic based on

072   on the deterministic behavior, the previous work focuses on finding stable matching. However, this

080
                How can we maximize the success of matching under this stochastic behavior?
081
082   In this work, we introduce a novel and practical online matching framework, termed stochastic
083   matching bandits, to describe the bandits for matching operating under stochastic matching con-
084   ditions characterized by unknown preferences. It is important to highlight that our framework is
085   fundamentally different from existing approaches, such as online matching problems and matching
086   bandits. We describe our proposed framework with an illustration in Figure 1 as follows. (a) Mul-
087   tiple agents and arms are involved with unknown utility values between them reflecting the arms‚Äô
088   preferences over agents. (b) In each time step, each agent is assigned to an arm. Note that multiple
089
      agents can be assigned to a single arm. These matching assignments are proposed to the arms, but
      the final matching has not yet been confirmed. (c) Arms accept a suggested matching from an agent
090
      among the assigned pool of agents based on their preferences or reject all of them, making stochastic
091
      decisions under unknown preferences.
092
093   For the choice model of arms over agents, we use the multinomial logit (MNL) function with fea-
094   tures. Significantly, our model incorporates outside options, allowing arms to opt out of accepting
095
      any agents from the assigned pool. This consideration of outside options reflects many real-world
      scenarios, such as drivers in ride-hailing services choosing not to accept calls from assigned riders
096
      based on their preferences, or employers in online job markets deciding not to hire any. We high-
097
      light that, in this problem, the success or failure of the match serves as the reward feedback for
098   agents. Then, our objective is to maximize the probability of successful matching in the systems
099   while learning unknown preferences in an online manner.

121   Online Matching Problem. In regards to matching optimization, the online matching problem
122   has been studied by Karp et al. (1990); Mehta et al. (2013; 2007); Gamlath et al. (2019); Fuchs
123   et al. (2005); Kesselheim et al. (2013), in which the objective is to maximize matching from ir-
124   reversible matching decisions under real-time arrivals. Online bipartite matching was first studied
125   by Karp et al. (1990), which provided a randomized algorithm for the online matching problem
126   in a bipartite graph. Subsequent research has expanded upon this foundation, with studies such as
127   Kesselheim et al. (2013) addressing weighted bipartite matching and Gamlath et al. (2019) explor-
128
      ing the generalized arrival model regarding vertices and edges. In contrast to the typical focus on
      optimization in online matching problems, our study, inspired by bandit research, concentrates on
129
      learning preference utilities related to preferences by allowing the assignment of multiple agents
130
      to an arm. Moreover, for the utility values, we explore a more generalized structure with features,
131   which has not been studied for the online matching problem. Importantly, our approach introduces
132   a fundamentally different aspect from the online matching problems by addressing the tradeoff be-
133   tween exploitation and exploration.
134
135   Matching Bandits. Here we examine the existing body of literature on matching bandits. The
136   exploration of regret minimization in matching markets was initially studied by Liu et al. (2020).
137   Their objective was to minimize regret by finding an optimal stable matching by learning agents‚Äô
138   side preferences through stochastic reward feedback. In their work, the Explore-then-Commit-based
139   algorithm, integrated with the Gale-Shapley platform (Gale & Shapley, 1962), was proposed. Sub-
140   sequent studies by Sankararaman et al. (2020); Liu et al. (2021); Basu et al. (2021) focused on de-
141   centralized agents within matching bandits. More recently, Zhang et al. (2022); Kong & Li (2023)
142   accomplished improved regret bounds. The previous work does not consider feature information or
143
      general structure, focusing solely on the basic MAB setting. We also note that all of the previous
      work only considered N ‚Č§ K.
144
145   Our research distinguishes itself from prior work on matching bandits in several aspects. Firstly,
146   prior work focuses on achieving a stable matching under the assumption that arm behavior is de-
147   terministic with known preferences. This implies that each arm accepts an agent deterministically
148   based on its known preference, given the assigned agents without outside options (that is, there is no
149
      option to not choose any agent). In contrast, we posit that arm behavior is stochastic with unknown
      preferences, highlighting the importance of learning arms‚Äô preferences. Furthermore, we allow arms
150
      to have outside options (not to accept any agent). Thus, successful matching by avoiding the out-
151
      side option is crucial. This naturally directs focus towards maximizing the likelihood of successful
152   matches while acquiring preference feedback rather than focusing on stability. This indicates that
153   our setting does not align with previous research but instead represents a distinct problem. Ad-
154   ditionally, we consider preference utility as a function of features. Lastly, we do not assume any
155   relationship between N and K, allowing for N ‚Č• K or N ‚Č§ K.
156
157   MNL-Bandits. As the first MNL bandit method, Agrawal et al. (2019) proposed an epoch-based
158   algorithm, followed by subsequent contributions from Agrawal et al. (2017b); Chen et al. (2023);
159   Oh & Iyengar (2019; 2021). In our study, we adopt the MNL model for arms‚Äô choice preferences in
160   matching bandits, which has not been studied before. Unlike selecting an assortment at each time
161   step, our novel framework for the stochastic matching market mandates choosing at most K distinct
      assortments to assign agents to each arm.
