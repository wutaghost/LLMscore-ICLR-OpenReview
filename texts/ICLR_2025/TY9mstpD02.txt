009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Understanding the world through models is a fundamental goal of scientific re-
012
               search. While large language model (LLM) based approaches show promise in
013
               automating scientific discovery, they often overlook the importance of criticizing
014            scientific models. Criticizing models deepens scientific understanding and drives
015            the development of more accurate models. Moreover, criticism can improve the
016            reliability of LLM-based scientist systems by acting as a safeguard against hallu-
017            cinations. Automating model criticism is difficult because it traditionally requires
018            a human expert to define how to compare a model with data and evaluate if the
019            discrepancies are significant–both rely heavily on understanding the modeling as-
020            sumptions and domain. Although LLM-based critic approaches are appealing,
021            they introduce new challenges: LLMs might hallucinate the critiques themselves.
022
               Motivated by this, we introduce CALM (Critic Automation with Language Mod-
               els). CALM uses LLMs to generate summary statistics that highlight discrepan-
023
               cies between model predictions and data, and applies hypothesis tests to evaluate
024
               their significance. We can view CALM as a verifier that validates models and
025            critiques by embedding them in a hypothesis testing framework. In experiments,
026            we evaluate CALM across key quantitative and qualitative dimensions. In set-
027            tings where we synthesize discrepancies between models and datasets, CALM
028            reliably generates correct critiques without hallucinating incorrect ones. We show
029            that both human and LLM judges consistently prefer CALM’s critiques over al-
030            ternative approaches in terms of transparency and actionability. Finally, we show
031            that CALM’s critiques enable an LLM scientist to improve upon human-designed
032            models on real-world datasets.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037
038   A longstanding goal of artificial intelligence research is to automate the discovery of scientific mod-
039
      els (Langley et al., 1987; Waltz & Buchanan, 2009). The rapid development of LLMs with remark-
      able reasoning capabilities and general knowledge has created exciting new opportunities within this
040
      domain. Recent work has shown that LLM based scientific agents can propose novel research ideas
041
      (Si et al., 2024), discover scientific models (Li et al., 2024), and implement experiments (Lu et al.,
042   2024; Huang et al., 2024). These results highlight the promise of using LLMs to automate many im-
043   portant aspects of scientific discovery. However, they overlook the crucial role that model criticism
044   plays in driving scientific progress. Understanding the limitations of existing models deepens our
045   understanding and often motivates new models. Furthermore, automated methods for criticism can
046   improve the reliability of LLM-based scientific discovery systems, as LLMs are prone to systematic
047   hallucinations (Lu et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024) that could undermine the broader goal of automating
048   scientific discovery.
049   Model criticism is hard to automate because it is inherently dependent on the model and problem
050   domain. In particular, it involves (1) determining which aspects to compare between the model and
051   data and (2) evaluating the significance of any differences. Each of these tasks typically requires
052   substantial human expertise (Gelman & Shalizi, 2012). While leveraging LLMs is an initially ap-
053   pealing approach to automation, it introduces new challenges: LLMs might also hallucinate the
      critiques themselves, undermining the effectiveness of automated model criticism.

054
      Motivated by these challenges, we introduce CALM (Critic Automation with Language Models),
055   which integrates LLMs within a principled model criticism framework. Specifically, given a pro-
056   posed scientific model and dataset metadata, CALM uses an LLM to generate summary statistics
057   that capture properties of the data that might violate the modeling assumptions. Importantly, these
058   summary statistics are tailored to the model and dataset. CALM implements these summary statis-
059   tics as Python functions, which can be easily executed and inspected by a human or LLM scientist.
060   This brings transparency to the critique process.
061   While these summary statistics can highlight potential discrepancies, we need a method to determine
062   whether these discrepancies are meaningful. To address this, we show how we can automatically
063   convert the summary statistics produced by CALM into hypothesis tests, for many commonly-used
064   scientific models. Specifically, if we can sample from the scientific model (Gelman et al., 2013;
065   Cranmer et al., 2019), we can form a null distribution for a summary statistic and compute an em-
066   pirical p-value. Thus, we can transform each summary statistic into a quantitative check, providing
067   a rigorous way to assess both the significance of the discrepancies and the validity of the model.
068   In doing so, we reduce the complex task of automatically validating proposed models and critiques
069
      to the well-understood problem of hypothesis testing. We can view these quantitative checks as
      (loosely) serving a role analogous to how formal verification systems validate proofs in LLM-based
070
      theorem proving systems like AlphaProof (DeepMind, 2024).
071
072   In experiments (Section 4), we evaluate CALM along key qualitative and quantitative properties cru-
073   cial for an automated critic system. In settings where we synthetically control discrepancies between
074   models and datasets, CALM consistently identifies true discrepancies and avoids hallucinating false
075
      ones. We also assess important qualitative aspects of CALM’s critiques (e.g., transparency), and find
      that both LLM and human judges prefer CALM’s critiques over alternatives. Finally, we demon-
076
      strate the practical impact of CALM’s critiques on the downstream task of guiding an LLM-based
077
      scientific model discovery system. On real-world datasets, CALM’s critiques enable an LLM-based
078   automated model discovery system (Li et al., 2024) to significantly improve upon initial human-
079   designed models.

      2   BACKGROUND
083
      In this section, we describe model criticism techniques, from different domains, that are commonly
084
      used to find discrepancies. Crucially, we can often formalize finding discrepancies as identifying
085
      suitable test statistics, using those statistics to compute discrepancies between model predictions
086   and data, and validating their significance using domain knowledge.
087
088   Regression analysis In regression analysis, we begin with a dataset D = {X , Y} of input features
089   X and targets Y; our goal is to predict Y from X . Given model predictions Y pred , we perform model
090   diagnostics that target the standard assumptions of linear regression (e.g., linearity, homoscedastic-
091   ity, uncorrelated errors). For example, to evaluate whether homoscedasticity holds, we can plot the
092   residuals against the input features. We can then either informally assess whether the pattern in the
093   residuals indicates a significant departure from homoscedasticity or perform statistical tests.
094
095   Computational models Computational models often make simplifying assumptions that can lead
096   to systematic errors, even after the parameters of these models are calibrated. This might be due to
097
      imperfect physical knowledge or systematic measurement errors; these systematic errors are often
      known as model inadequacies. Bayarri & Berger (2000) introduce a framework for understanding
098
      these inadequacies that involves defining domain-specific evaluation criteria or performing sensitiv-
099
      ity analyses and checking whether these accord with scientific intuition. Another very influential
100   approach is to cast this as a statistical modeling problem and directly build a statistical model of the
101   discrepancy (Kennedy & O’Hagan, 2001). Building on this work, Joseph & Yan (2015) show how
102   to study this discrepancy through an analysis of variance decomposition.
103
104   Bayesian statistical models In statistical modeling, we model the data as a probability distribu-
105   tion. More formally, a statistical model defines a joint probability distribution p(Y, θ|X , H) over
106   observed variables Y and latent variables θ; we use H to indicate a specific class of statistical mod-
107   els and the dataset D = {X , Y} can include both observations Y that we model as random variables
      and additional quantities X that we treat as fixed. By marginalizing out the latent variables, we

146   3     M ETHOD : CALM
147
148   In this section, we describe CALM, our system for finding systematic discrepancies between a sci-
149   entific model and dataset. We provide a brief overview here; for a schematic overview, see Figure 1.
150   CALM takes as input: dataset metadata, a symbolic representation of a model (e.g., program) and
151   model samples. Given these, CALM produces significant discrepancies. Each discrepancy is rep-
152   resented as a test statistic implemented as a Python function, an executable artifact that program-
153
      matically expresses the discrepancy, and a natural language criticism.

206   3.2   I NTERFACING WITH LLM SCIENCE AGENTS VIA NATURAL LANGUAGE CRITICISM
207
208   In many situations, we might want to integrate CALM within a broader scientific discovery system,
209   involving either human or LLM scientists. Therefore, CALM also produces natural language crit-
210   icism. This design choice is motivated by several considerations. By offering critiques in natural
211   language, which is flexible and generic, the system provides an additional medium for users to inter-
212
      pret results, which can be useful in fields where training in formal modeling is less common. Second,
      this design choice is natural given recent advances in LLM based agents for scientific discovery and
213
      modeling (Huang et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024).
214
215   We prompt an LLM to produce natural language criticism hk that summarizes the discrepancy
      implied by test statistic Tk and its p-value p˜k . Specifically, we ask the LLM to synthesize the test


                                                          4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      statistic in a way that’s informative to a colleague revising some initial model. For examples of the
217   natural language critiques produced, see Section A.4 and for the prompt see Figure 8.
218
219   We can easily integrate these three artifacts within an LLM based scientific discovery system.
220
      Specifically, we provide the system with (1) a Python implementation of the test statistic Tk ,
      (2) the natural language hk , and (3) the initial model; in our experiments these models will be prob-
221
      abilistic programs in pymc or stan (Carpenter et al., 2017; Abril-Pla et al., 2023). We use the
222
      LLM-based system for generating probabilistic programs introduced by Li et al. (2024).
223
224   In general, these hypothesis tests are cheap relative to the cost of model fitting. For example, poste-
225   rior inference is the dominating cost for Bayesian models and performing posterior predictive checks
226
      are cheap given posterior samples. Thus, CALM will generally introduce minimal overhead to the
      overall cost of an AI scientist system.

489   Experimental Setup We integrate CALM into the model discovery system introduced by Li et al.
490   (2024) by giving a revision-LLM three components: the initial model H implemented as a proba-
491   bilistic program, the test statistic Tk , and natural language criticism hk ; the criticism was produced
492   in the previous section by running CALM on the (fitted) initial model. We fit the models proposed
493   by the revision LLM using pymc (Abril-Pla et al., 2023); we repeat each proposal three times at
494   a temperature 0.0 since we noticed non-determinism. We allow the LLM to revise based on a fil-
495   tered set of significant test statistics. For details, see Section A.9. We report the best model across
496   proposals and test statistics.
497
498   Ablation We consider a data-blind LLM critic that receives only the statistical model, imple-
499
      mented as a probabilistic program; we run this at a temperature 0.0. This approach can be effective
      since modeling assumptions are enumerated in the initial probabilistic program.
500
501
      Quantitative Results In Tables 1 and 2, we evaluate CALM’s ability to produce critiques that
502
      improve upon an initial statistical model and outperform the data-blind method. To do this, we first
503   compute the expected log predictive density (ELPD LOO) score for both initial and revised models
504   (Vehtari et al., 2017). Next, we calculate the score difference between the initial and revised model
505   and determine the margin of victory by dividing the score difference by the standard errors (SE) of
506   the score difference. For a given dataset, a method is considered to “win” over another at a specific
507   SE margin if the score difference is larger than the margin. For example, if the score difference is
508   2 and the SE margin is 1, we count it as a significant win. Finally, we compute aggregated win
509   rates at various SE margins (1, 1.5, 2). The win rate is the percentage of datasets where one method
510   outperformed another at a given SE margin.
511   CALM’s critiques help a revision LLM significantly improve upon the initial model over 80% of
512   the time, which shows that CALM reliably produces actionable critiques. CALM’s successes are
513   often related to sliced statistics discussed in Section 4.3 (e.g., the revision-LLM introduces floor-
514   dependent variance terms). Furthermore, in Table 2, we show that CALM also outperforms the
515   data-blind critic. Next, we discuss CALM’s limitations.
516
517   Limitation 1: Suboptimal transformations of data CALM does not see the model predictions
518   or data. As a consequence, CALM sometimes does not provide good critiques on transforming
519
      data. This happens most prominently in the mesquite setting where the model predictions can be
      negative even though the data is non-negative.
520
521
      Limitation 2: Correct criticism but imperfect implementation In some cases, CALM identifies
522
      a legitimate discrepancy that the revision LLM incorrectly implements; the revision LLM correctly
523
      uses a Categorical likelihood but does not transpose the logits correctly.
524
525
526   5   C ONCLUSION
527
528   We introduced CALM, a framework for automated model criticism that leverages LLMs to identify
529   discrepancies between a model and dataset and then applies hypothesis tests to assess the signif-
530
      icance of discrepancies. CALM serves as a lightweight verifier, validating both scientific models
      and critiques within a hypothesis testing framework. Our experiments demonstrate that CALM reli-
531
      ably identifies true discrepancies without hallucinating false critiques. Futhermore, both human and
532
      LLM judges preferred CALM’s critiques over alternative approaches. CALM critiques enabled an
533   LLM-based system to substantially improve upon expert designed models. By automating model
534   criticism, CALM represents a step toward more reliable automatic scientific discovery systems.
535
536
      While our evaluation was limited to Bayesian models, which are commonly used in scientific do-
      mains, CALM’s design is versatile: the only requirements are the ability to simulate data from the
537
      model and a symbolic representation of the model. An exploration of other common classes of
538
      scientific models (Cranmer et al., 2019) is an exciting direction for future work.
