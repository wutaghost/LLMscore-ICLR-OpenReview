011                                             A BSTRACT
012
013           Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems have demonstrated considerable
014           effectiveness in querying private, short, unstructured data; however, they often
015
              encounter challenges in delivering accurate factual answers when working with
              larger corpora, frequently lacking context and failing to establish domain rela-
016
              tionships. In this paper, we introduce a novel collaborative multi agent Retrieval-
017
              Augmented Generation (CoMaKG-RAG) framework designed to enhance the ca-
018           pabilities of large language models (LLMs) in complex information retrieval sce-
019           narios involving multimodal data sources.Our framework comprises a pool of cus-
020           tomized collaborative agents, including a query generator agent, a domain model
021           generator agent, a domain model populator agent, a knowledge graph curator
022           agent, and a knowledge graph query agent, each tailored through a developed
023           customization model and historical domain questions. The query generator for-
024           mulates relevant queries related to text and image chunks within documents, while
025           the domain model generator constructs a structured domain model based on these
026
              queries. The domain model populator agent enriches the model by integrating ad-
              ditional text and image fragments, and the knowledge graph generator assembles
027
              a comprehensive unified knowledge graph using Neo4j.Each agent interacts with
028
              one another, evaluates outputs, and provides feedback to enhance the overall pro-
029           cess. Ultimately, user queries are transformed into cipher queries using the knowl-
030           edge graph query agent, processed by a unified knowledge graph engine, and con-
031           verted back into natural language responses. This approach enhances information
032           retrieval from multimodal sources by mitigating hallucinations, generic responses,
033           incomplete responses, and factual inaccuracies. We evaluated our method against
034           the publicly available technical report ”Operations Maintenance Best Practices”
035           and state-of-the-art knowledge graph generation and query software, Neo4j Graph
036           Builder. Our results demonstrate that our method identifies a substantially higher
037
              number of entities and uncovers unique, contextually significant relationships, sur-
              passing the performance of the graph builder in both the quantity and quality of
038
              extracted information. The proposed agentic graph RAG system was evaluated on
039
              both factual and descriptive queries and was able to provide accurate responses
040           for both text and image-based questions, whereas the Neo4j graph performed sub
041           optimally.

044   1   INTRODUCTION
045
046   The proliferation of digital documents, particularly in PDF format, has created significant chal-
047   lenges in information retrieval and knowledge management across various industries. While Large
048   Language Models (LLMs) have shown remarkable capabilities in natural language processing tasks,
049   their application to complex information extraction from unstructured documents remains an area of
050
      active research.
051   Traditional Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) approaches have made significant strides in
052   enhancing document querying by combining the strengths of LLMs with information retrieval tech-
053   niques. However, these methods often struggle with maintaining context over long documents,
      handling complex multi-hop queries, and providing transparent reasoning paths. Moreover, the flat


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      structure of typical RAG systems limits their ability to capture and utilize the hierarchical nature of
055   information present in many documents.
056
057   Knowledge Graph (KG) approaches offer a superior alternative for document querying and informa-
058
      tion retrieval. By representing information as interconnected entities and relationships, KGs can cap-
      ture the semantic structure of documents more effectively. This hierarchical representation enables
059
      more nuanced and context-aware querying, supports multi-hop reasoning, and provides clear prove-
060
      nance for extracted information. Furthermore, KGs allow for the integration of domain-specific
061   knowledge and ontologies, enhancing the overall quality and relevance of query responses.
062
063   However, the generation of high-quality knowledge graphs from unstructured text remains a signif-
064
      icant challenge. Existing approaches Sukumar et al. (2023); Schatz et al. (2023) for KG creation
      often produce graphs with superficial or less meaningful relationships between nodes, resulting in
065
      limited utility for complex querying and knowledge discovery. The main difficulties lie in accu-
066
      rately identifying relevant entities, establishing meaningful relationships, and capturing the hierar-
067   chical structure of information present in the source documents. To address these challenges, this
068   paper presents a novel multi-agent framework that integrates LLMs with RAG techniques to enhance
069   knowledge graph curation and document querying. Our approach aims to bridge the gap between
070   traditional RAG systems and KG-based information retrieval by introducing a collaborative multi-
071   agent system that not only automates and enhances the knowledge extraction and query process but
072   also generates more semantically rich and hierarchically connected knowledge graphs.
073   The proposed framework consists pool of five specialized agents working in concert: a query gen-
074   erator agent, a domain model generator agent, a domain model populator agent, a knowledge graph
075   generator agent,and a knowledge graph query agent
076
      The key innovation in our approach lies in the introduction of intermediate layers, particularly the
077
      question generation and domain model creation steps. These crucial components enable the gen-
078
      eration of more meaningful and relevant relationships between nodes, resulting in a hierarchically
079   connected graph rather than a collection of isolated nodes. This structure significantly enhances the
080   graph’s utility for complex querying and knowledge discovery.
081
082
      By integrating LLMs with RAG techniques and advanced knowledge graph technologies, our ap-
      proach offers a promising solution for enhancing information retrieval processes in data-intensive
083
      environments. This paper details the architecture of our multi-agent system, its implementation
084
      using Neo4j for knowledge graph storage, and presents experimental results demonstrating its effec-
085   tiveness in reducing manual reading time, improving query response accuracy, and generating more
086   semantically meaningful knowledge representations.
087
088
      The rest of this paper is organized as follows: Section (II) provides a background on LLMs, RAG,
      and knowledge graphs, as well as a review of existing approaches to KG generation from text.
089
      Section (III) details our multi-agent framework and methodology, particularly emphasizing the do-
090
      main model generation process. Section (IV) presents our experimental setup and results, including
091   comparisons with traditional KG generation methods. Section (V) discusses the implications of
092   our findings, potential applications, and the advantages of our hierarchical graph structure. Finally,
093   Section (VI) concludes the paper and outlines directions for future research.

098
099
100   2.1   L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS (LLM S )
101
102   Large Language Models are neural networks trained on vast amounts of text data to perform a wide
103   range of natural language processing tasks. These models, such as GPT (Generative Pre-trained
104   Transformer) series, BERT, and T5, have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in understanding and
105   generating human-like text Asmitha et al. (2024). LLMs have shown proficiency in tasks including
106   text summarization, question answering, and language translation Omrani et al. (2024). However,
107   their application to complex information retrieval and knowledge structuring tasks remains an active
      area of research.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

      2.2   K NOWLEDGE G RAPHS
109
110   Knowledge Graphs Knowledge Graphs (KGs) are structured representations of information that cap-
111   ture entities and their relationships in a graph format Osman & Barukub (2020). KGs have become
112   fundamental in various applications, including semantic search, question answering systems, and
113   recommender systems. They provide a means to organize and query complex, interconnected infor-
114   mation efficiently.
115
116   2.3   R ETRIEVAL -AUGMENTED G ENERATION (RAG)
117
118   Retrieval-augmented generation is a hybrid approach that combines the strengths of retrieval-based
119   and generation-based models . In RAG systems, a retrieval component first accesses relevant infor-
120
      mation from a knowledge base, which is then used to augment the input to a generative language
      model. This approach enhances the model’s ability to produce accurate and contextually relevant
121
      outputs by grounding its generations in retrieved factual information.
122
123
124
      2.4   E XISTING A PPROACHES FOR KG GENERATION
125
      Several approaches have been proposed for automatically generating knowledge graphs from un-
126
      structured text:
127
128         1. Rule-Based Methods: These approaches use predefined patterns and rules to extract entities
129            and relationships from text Khaing et al. (2019). While effective for specific domains, they
130            often lack flexibility and require significant manual effort to create and maintain rules.
131         2. Supervised Learning Methods: These techniques use machine learning models trained on
132            annotated datasets to identify entities and relations in text Zhao et al. (2024). They can
133            be more adaptable than rule-based methods but require large amounts of labelled training
134            data.
135
            3. Unsupervised and Semi-Supervised Methods: These approaches attempt to extract knowl-
136
               edge graph elements with minimal or no labeled data, often using techniques like clustering
137            or distant supervision Zhao et al. (2019). They can be more scalable but may suffer from
138            lower precision.
139
            4. Neural Network-Based Methods: Recent approaches leverage deep learning models, in-
140
               cluding LLMs, for KG construction Zhu et al. (2024). These methods have shown promise
141
               in capturing complex semantic relationships but often struggle with producing meaning-
142            fully structured and hierarchical knowledge representations.
143
144
      2.5   M ULTI -AGENT A PPROACHES IN AI AND NLP
145
146   Recent advancements in AI have shown the potential of multi-agent systems for tackling complex
147   tasks. These approaches distribute cognitive load across multiple customized agents, often leading
148   to more robust and effective solutions.
149
150         1. CoMM Framework: Chen et al. introduced the Collaborative Multi-Agent, Multi-
151            Reasoning-Path (CoMM) prompting framework Yang et al. (2024). This approach prompts
152            Large Language Models (LLMs) to play different roles in a problem-solving team, encour-
153
               aging collaborative problem-solving. CoMM applies different reasoning paths for different
               roles, effectively implementing few-shot prompting in multi-agent scenarios. This work
154
               demonstrated significant improvements in solving complex college-level science problems
155
156         2. AutoGen: Li et al. presented AutoGen, a framework for building multi-agent systems
157            with LLMs Wu et al. (2023). This work highlights how different agents can be assigned
158
               specific roles and collaborate to solve complex tasks, providing a foundation for multi-
               agent systems in various domains.
159
160         3. ChatDev: Qian et al. proposed ChatDev, a collaborative software development framework
161            using multiple LLM-based agents Qian et al. (2024). This system demonstrates how multi-
               agent approaches can be applied to complex creative tasks like software development.


                                                       3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
            4. Task-Oriented Dialogue Systems: Zhang et al. developed a multi-agent framework for task-
163            oriented dialogue systems Sun et al. (2025). Their work illustrates how multiple specialized
164            agents can work together to handle complex conversational tasks.
165
166   These multi-agent approaches offer valuable insights into designing collaborative AI systems. They
167   demonstrate the potential for breaking down complex tasks into subtasks handled by specialized
168   agents, a principle we adapt in our framework for knowledge graph generation. Our work builds
169
      upon these multi-agent concepts, particularly drawing inspiration from the CoMM framework, and
      applies them specifically to the challenge of generating semantically rich and hierarchically struc-
170
      tured knowledge graphs from unstructured documents. By leveraging the strengths of multiple spe-
171
      cialized agents, we aim to overcome the limitations of existing KG generation methods and produce
172   more meaningful and useful knowledge representations.
173
174   Key features of our collaborative multi-agent system include:
175
             • Adaptive learning capabilities, improving performance with exposure to diverse manual
176            content

             • Seamless integration between agents, with feedback loops for continuous improvement
178
179          • Customizable options for different domains, operational needs, and industry-specific re-

               quirements
181          • Strong focus on data quality, ensuring accuracy, completeness, and relevance of the gener-

      4.4   M ULTI -AGENT S YSTEM A RCHITECTURE
325
326   Our framework employs a team of specialized agents, each designed to perform specific tasks in the
327   knowledge graph generation process. The agents include:
328
329         1. Question Generator (ai agent01): This agent simulates a field engineer or operator, gen-
330            erating relevant questions about the domain. For our boiler maintenance example, key
331
               capabilities include:
332             (a) Formulating questions about maintenance procedures for various boiler types
333             (b) Generating queries related to troubleshooting common boiler issues
334             (c) Creating questions about inspection processes, including Non-Destructive Examina-
335                  tion (NDE) methods
336             (d) Asking about safety protocols and compliance guidelines
337            It’s important to note that this agent is highly customizable and can be adapted to generate
338            questions for any industry or domain, not just boiler maintenance.
339
            2. Comprehensive Domain Model Generator (ai agent02): This agent creates detailed and
340            hierarchical domain models from textual input. Key capabilities include
341
                (a) Detailed entity extraction
342
343
                (b) Multi-level relationship identification
344             (c) Hierarchical structure creation
345             (d) Attribute detailing
346             (e) Ontology integration and cross-domain connection identification
347            This agent is essential in our framework, creating a detailed domain model with multi-level
348            relationships and attributes that enable the construction of a precise and semantically rich
349            knowledge graph.
350         3. Domain Model Populator Agent (ai agent03): This agent populates the domain model with
351            content from the extracted manual text. Key capabilities include:
352             (a) Content extraction and mapping to the domain model
353
                (b) Relationship population
354
                (c) Contextual analysis and Implicit Information Inference
355
                (d) Consistency validation
356
357         4. Knowledge Graph Generator (ai agent04): This agent creates fully connected, hierarchical
358
               Knowledge Graphs in Neo4j from populated domain models. Key capabilities include:
359             (a) Converting domain models to graph structures
360             (b) Generating Cypher queries for graph creation
361             (c) Ensuring full connectivity and hierarchy
362             (d) Performing data cleaning and normalization
363             (e) Optimizing graph structure and implementing advanced graph algorithms
364
365   The agents collaborate leveraging natural language processing and structured data mapping capabil-
366   ities. They interact with integrated tools such as text analysis algorithms, graph modeling tools, and
367   data cleaning systems, as well as external APIs for industry standards and graph visualization.
368   This multi-agent approach allows for specialized knowledge to be applied at each stage of the knowl-
369
      edge graph generation process, from initial question generation to the creation of complex, hierarchi-
      cal relationships between concepts. The system is designed to be scalable, handling large volumes
370
      of text input and complex domain structures efficiently.
371
      Importantly, while our experiment focused on boiler maintenance, the multi-agent collaboration
372   system is designed to be highly flexible and can be leveraged for any industry or domain. By cus-
373   tomizing the Question Generator agent and adjusting the domain knowledge of other agents, this
374   system can be applied to diverse fields such as healthcare, finance, manufacturing, or any other area
375   where structured knowledge extraction from technical documents is required.
376   This architecture enables us to generate comprehensive, hierarchical knowledge graphs that accu-
377   rately represent complex domains, as described in source documents, while being adaptable to a
      wide range of industries and applications.

      In this paper, we introduced a collaborative novel multi-agent framework that integrates Large Lan-
534   guage Models (LLMs) with Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) CoMaKG-RAG techniques to
535   enhance knowledge graph curation and document querying from multi model sources. By leveraging
536   five specialized agents—question generation, domain model generation, domain model population,
537   knowledge graph creation, and query generation—our approach addresses the limitations of tradi-
538   tional RAG systems and flat knowledge graph structures. The introduction of intermediate layers
539   such as question generation and domain model creation enabled the generation of more seman-
      tically rich, hierarchically connected knowledge graphs, which significantly improved the quality

541   our multi-agent system can generate more meaningful relationships between entities and reduce
542   the time spent on manual document reading while improving the accuracy of query responses. By
543   incorporating domain-specific knowledge and creating a more interconnected and context-aware
544   knowledge graph, our approach provides a more powerful tool for knowledge management tasks in
545   data-intensive environments. This work highlights the potential of LLM-driven multi-agent systems
546   in transforming information retrieval processes and points towards further opportunities in refin-
547   ing knowledge graph generation, particularly in handling larger-scale documents and more intricate
548   queries. Future research may focus on expanding this framework to accommodate diverse domains
