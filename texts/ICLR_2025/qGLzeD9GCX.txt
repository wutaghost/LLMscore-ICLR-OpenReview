009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities, but
012            their training requires extensive data and computational resources, rendering them
013            valuable digital assets. Therefore, it is essential to watermark LLMs to protect
014            their copyright and trace unauthorized use or resale. Existing methods for wa-
015            termarking LLMs are mainly based on backdoors or knowledge injection, which
016            require burdensome training or degrade the generation quality. To address these
017            issues, we propose EditMark, a training-free and harmless watermarking method
018
               for LLMs based on model editing. We observe LLM has diversity and can gener-
               ate multiple logical and semantic correct answers to some open-ended questions.
019
               Therefore, we can use a watermark to generate a harmless mapping to control the
020
               LLM’s answer to an open-ended question. Inspired by this insight, EditMark in-
021            volves generating a harmless mapping based on the watermark, selecting a secret
022            key to generate watermarked inputs, and editing the outputs of LLM to align with
023            the harmless mapping. Extensive experiments show that EditMark can embed 8-
024            bit watermarks into LLMs within 2 minutes, with a watermark extraction success
025            rate close to 100%. External experiments further demonstrate that EditMark has
026            fidelity and is robust to model fine-tuning and editing attacks.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large Language Models (LLMs) Achiam et al. (2023); Touvron et al. (2023) have shown exceptional
032   capabilities across various tasks, e.g., text generation Yu et al. (2022), translation Xu et al., and
033
      dialogue systems Achiam et al. (2023). However, training these models demands vast amounts
      of high-quality data and significant computational resources, which makes LLMs valuable digital
034
      assets. Model owners can profit by selling or distributing their pre-trained LLMs, whereas malicious
035
      users may abuse or resell these base models without authorization. So, how can we protect the
036   copyright of open-source LLMs and trace who resells our LLMs?
037
038   Watermarking LLMs Liu et al. (2024) is a well-established technique for protecting model copy-
039
      rights. In this paper, we focus on watermarking open-source LLMs, where the attacker can access
      the internal parameters and weights of these LLMs. Recent methods for watermarking open-source
040
      LLMs primarily utilize backdoors or knowledge injection. As illustrated in Fig. 1, backdoor-based
041
      methods Xu et al. (2024); Li et al. inject a backdoor into the LLM as a watermark. When querying
042   the backdoor LLM with the input that includes a trigger, the backdoor LLM will generate a target
043   output. However, inserting backdoors into LLMs is not harmless and may bring potential threats Guo
044   et al. (2024) for LLM since the malicious users can exploit the backdoor to control the LLM to gen-
045   erate contextually malicious text Shao et al. (2024). The watermarking method based on knowledge
046   injection Li et al. (2024) embeds watermarks into knowledge and injects watermarked knowledge
047   into LLMs, which is harmless since the watermarked knowledge is logically correct. However, this
048   watermarking method is designed for training and leads to significant costs, particularly for large-
049   scale models. For instance, when selling the LLM to N users, the LLM needs to be trained N times
050
      to embed N different watermarks. Consequently, it is crucial to design a watermarking framework
      for open-source LLMs that is both training-free (efficient) and harmless.
051
052   To address these issues, we proposed a harmless and training-free watermarking framework based
053   on model editing to protect the copyright of LLMs. Notably, LLMs have multiple correct answers
      for the open questions, which we call the diversity of LLMs. For instance, both “0.67” and “0.667”

      2.1      L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODEL WATERMARKING
090
091   With the development of large language models, watermarking LLMs has been widely researched
092   to protect their copyright, categorized as generated text watermarking and LLM watermarking.
093
      Generated text watermarking methods Zhang et al. (2024); Kirchenbauer et al. (2023); Christ et al.
094
      (2024); Munyer et al. (2024) involve embedding watermarks within the text generated by the LLM to
095
      trace back their source. To achieve this, a feasible watermarking method Kirchenbauer et al. (2023)
096   involves defining green and red token sets and modifying the logits to make the LLM basis generate
097   green tokens. Based on this idea, recent works have focused on proposing to embed multi-bit wa-
098   termarks Wang et al. and improve the quality of the generated text Christ et al. (2024). However,
099   these watermarking methods require additional codes to modify the process of token sampling, and
100   experienced attackers can easily find these codes for the watermarking process and remove them to
101   remove the watermark.
102   Watermarking methods for LLMs focus on embedding watermarks directly into the models them-
103   selves, which aims to protect the copyright of LLMs. Backdoor-based watermarking methods Xu
104   et al. (2024); Li et al. (2023); Li et al. inject a backdoor into the LLM as the watermark. When
105   querying watermarked LLM with the input that includes a trigger, the watermarked LLM will gen-
106   erate a pre-defined output. The model owner can verify the existence of a backdoor to determine
107   whether the LLM is a watermarked LLM. However, backdoor-based watermarking is not harmless
      for the LLM Guo et al. (2024). To address this issue, the watermarking method Li et al. (2024)

108
      based on knowledge injection is proposed, which embeds the watermark into the knowledge and
109   injects the watermarked knowledge into LLM. In addition, the watermarked text is logically correct,
110   which makes it harmless for LLM. However, this method also requires training the LLMs to embed
111   watermarks, which is a huge cost for large-size LLMs.

      2.2   M ODEL E DITING
115
      Large language models have some time-sensitive knowledge, which requires updating to ensure it
116
      aligns with the facts. However, using fine-tuning techniques to update knowledge demands vast time
117   and resources. To address this issue, model editing techniques Wang et al. (2023); Mitchell et al.
118   (2021) have been widely studied for knowledge editing due to their efficiency.
119
120
      Model editing methods can be categorized as memory-augmented Mitchell et al. (2022); Zheng et al.
      (2023); Hartvigsen et al. (2024) and ‘Locate and Edit’ Dai et al. (2022); Meng et al. (2022); Meng
121
      et al.. Memory-augmented methods involve adding a new memory space or additional parameters
122
      that encapsulate the new knowledge while the original parameters of the model remain unaltered.
123   By storing new knowledge externally, these methods enable precise representation of the added
124   knowledge and offer scalability. Locate and Edit methods are more interpretable, and they treat the
125   MLP (multi-layer perceptron) layers as a form of key-value memory. These methods locate specific
126   neurons that store target knowledge and then edit the new knowledge based on backpropagation.
127   In this paper, we exploit the efficiency of model editing techniques to embed watermarks into large
128   language models.

131   3     T HREAT M ODEL AND P RELIMINARIES
132
133   3.1   T HREAT M ODEL
134
135   We assume two primary parties: the model owner and the attacker. Model owners possess large
136   language models (LLMs) which they commercialize. Before the sale, they embed a watermark into
137
      each LLM, associating it with the purchaser’s identity to monitor and trace unauthorized reselling or
      misuse. In addition, the model owner can extract the watermark under the black-box scenario where
138
      only the model responses are available.
139
140   The attacker aims to either resell the LLMs or deploy them for unauthorized API services. Their
141   knowledge regarding the watermark can be classified into four levels: 1. Unaware of the presence
142   of a watermark. 2. Aware of the presence of a watermark but unfamiliar with the watermarking
143
      technique. 3. Knowledgeable about the watermarking technique but unaware of which case we edit
      to embed watermark. In addition, the attacker will try to remove the watermark using strategies
144
      including model fine-tuning and model editing attacks.
145

197   4.1         I NSIGHTS OF E DIT M ARK
198
199   While model editing proves efficient, the question remains: how can we adeptly apply it to embed
200   watermarks?
201
      To answer this question, we should rethink the backdoor-based method. Assuming that harmlessness
202
      is not a primary concern, it becomes viable to use model editing techniques to implant a backdoor
203
      within the LLM, which means there is a mapping between trigger and target output. Moreover, this
204   backdoor can be directly linked to the model. The existence of the model watermark is then verified
205   by detecting the backdoor. Through these analyses, the primary challenge in the model editing
206   watermarking method emerges as how to create a harmless mapping for the watermarked text?
207
      Open-ended questions can yield multiple valid responses. For instance, when the LLM is queried
208
      with “The value of 32/64 is ?”, responses like “0.5” or “0.50” are both correct. The diversity in
209
      potential responses indicates we can manipulate the LLM’s output to embed a watermark. For
210   instance, to embed the watermark ‘0’, the LLM could be modified to produce the first response; for
211   ‘1’, it could generate the second. Naturally, the complexity of these choices can be increased, e.g., by
212   adjusting the precision of the response to embed multiple bits within a single query. Additionally,
213   various forms of answer diversity can be leveraged, like diversity in floating-point precision, the
214   sequence of responses, and the style of expression, as depicted in Tab. 1. Moving forward, we
215   will explore the watermarked mapping template focused on floating-point precision, introducing the
      EditMark framework as shown in Fig. 2, which includes both the embedding and extraction modules.

304   Model: For evaluation of the EditMark framework, we selected four common large language mod-

307   Metrics: EditMark employs three principal metrics: extract success rate (ESR), false positive rate
308   (FPR), and embedding time (ET). The ESR measures the proportion of successful watermark extrac-
309   tions out of total attempts, FPR assesses the rate at which false positives occur in non-watermarked
310   samples relative to all extractions, and ET quantifies the efficiency of the EditMark process. It is im-
311   portant to note that the architecture of GPT-2 does not inherently support LoRA fine-tuning, making
312   direct application challenging without large modifications. Consequently, some experimental results
313   involving GPT-2 and LoRA fine-tuning exhibit gap, indicated by ‘\’.

      instances across two rounds to embed the watermark. In addition to evaluating the embedding of
