011                                                    A BSTRACT
012
013             The acquisition of substantial volumes of 3D articulated object data is expen-
014             sive and time-consuming, and consequently the scarcity of 3D articulated object
015             data becomes an obstacle for deep learning methods to achieve remarkable per-
016
                formance in various articulated object understanding tasks. Meanwhile, pairing
                these object data with detailed annotations to enable training for various tasks is
017
                also difficult and labor-intensive to achieve. In order to expeditiously gather a sig-
018
                nificant number of 3D articulated objects with comprehensive and detailed annota-
019             tions for training, we propose Articulated Object Procedural Generation toolbox,
020             a.k.a. Arti-PG toolbox. Arti-PG toolbox consists of i) descriptions of articulated
021             objects by means of a generalized structure program along with their analytic cor-
022             respondence to the objects’ point cloud, ii) procedural rules about manipulations
023             on the structure program to synthesize large-scale and diverse new articulated ob-
024             jects, and iii) mathematical descriptions of knowledge (e.g. affordance, semantics,
025             etc.) to provide annotations to the synthesized object. Arti-PG has two appealing
026             properties for providing training data for articulated object understanding tasks: i)
027
                objects are created with unlimited variations in shape through program-oriented
                structure manipulation, ii) Arti-PG is widely applicable to diverse tasks by easily
028
                providing comprehensive and detailed annotations. Arti-PG now supports the pro-
029
                cedural generation of 26 categories of articulate objects and provides annotations
030             across a wide range of both vision and manipulation tasks, and we provide exhaus-
031             tive experiments which fully demonstrate its advantages. We will make Arti-PG
032             toolbox publicly available for the community to use. More details, analysis and
033             discussions are provided in technical appendices.

036   1    I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Articulated objects, comprised of rigid segments interconnected by joints that enable translation
039   and rotation movements, play an important role in daily life. Learning to understand articulated
040   objects is an essential topic in a wide range of research areas, including computer vision, robotics
041   and embodied AI. In the current data-driven era, the availability of a large amount of training data
042
      has become indispensable for the successful implementation of deep neural networks to understand
      articulated objects.
043
044   Common 3D articulated object data acquisition methods are either designing 3D CAD models by
045   artists (Chang et al., 2015; Xiang et al., 2020) or scanning real-world objects using scanners (Liu
046   et al., 2022)1 , both of which have huge demands on time and money. Furthermore, comprehensive
047   and detailed annotations are required for these object data to support training in various articulated
048
      object understanding tasks, which are also challenging to obtain. As a result, the issue of data
      scarcity is observed across different tasks supported by existing datasets (Mo et al., 2019; Liu et al.,
049
      2022), limiting the power of deep neural networks to comprehensively analyze and model articulated
050
      objects. Given that prior research has examined little on how to mitigate this issue, it remains a
051   pressing problem that requires attention.
052
         1
053        Here, we discuss about how the data are created from scratch, since it is usually unavailable to collect data
      from the Internet for novel categories in real-world applications.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      In this paper, we propose Articulated Object Procedural Generation toolbox (Arti-PG toolbox) as
055   a solution to this issue, which aids in expeditiously gathering a significant number of 3D articulated
056   objects with rich annotations. Arti-PG is developed based on the idea of procedural generation
057   (Togelius et al., 2014), referring to synthesizing data with generalized procedural rules.
058
      Inspired by research in visual cognition and brain science (Habel & Eschenbach, 2006; Ullman,
059
      2000; Palmeri & Gauthier, 2004; Biederman, 1987), we assume that a 3D object can be properly
060
      described as the combination of a macro spatial structure and micro geometric details. By first
061   describing an articulated object’s spatial structure as generalized programs and geometric details
062   as point-wise correspondence between the object’s point cloud and structure, novel 3D articulated
063   objects can be synthesized in two steps: i) create a variation of the structure via the application of
064   randomized mathematical rules to the programs, and ii) recover the geometric details according to
065   the point-wise correspondence. Subsequently, we are able to automatically assign annotations to
066   the synthesized objects using mathematical descriptions defined upon the structure programs. Such
067   annotated synthesized objects can then be used to enrich the training set for various tasks, facilitating
068   network training.
069   Therefore, we construct the Arti-PG toolbox with three components: i) structure programs of artic-
070   ulated objects along with their correspondence to the objects’ point cloud, ii) procedural rules for
071   structure program manipulation, and iii) mathematical descriptions of knowledge (e.g. affordance,
072   semantics, etc.) for annotations. Arti-PG now supports 26 categories of articulate objects that are
073   most commonly seen and provides different kinds of knowledge for a wide range of tasks. Users
074   can easily use the codes in the toolbox to synthesize large-scale and diverse articulated objects with
075
      rich annotations to train their models.
076   Our procedural approach has the following appealing properties. 1) Program-oriented Structure
077   Manipulation: Training set can be significantly enriched by synthesizing objects with unlimited
078   variations in shape through alterations of the structure program. Such alterations can be automati-
079   cally generated via randomized mathematical rules. 2) Analytic Label Alignment: Comprehensive
080   and detailed annotations of various types can be mathematically defined in the structure program,
081
      after which they can be analytically aligned with the synthesized object.
082   Benefiting from these properties, Arti-PG holds advantages in terms of the diversity of generated
083   objects, applicability to a wide range of tasks and effectiveness in solving data scarcity. Compared
084   to data augmentation methods which also increase the diversity of training data but cannot freely
085   assign labels to them and hence are limited to specific tasks, Arti-PG is applicable in different tasks
086   and therefore distinguishes itself from conventional data augmentation methods.
087   We have collected a total number of 3096 3D articulated objects across 26 categories with complex
088   shapes from influential and open-source datasets (Yi et al., 2016; Mo et al., 2019; Xiang et al., 2020)
089   to evaluate our approach. In the following sections, we will fully demonstrate the mechanism of
090   our approach and further showcase the superiority of Arti-PG through evaluations from both vision
091   and robotic aspects: part segmentation, part pose estimation, point cloud completion and object
092   manipulation.

097   2.1   A RTICULATED O BJECT DATASETS
098
099
      The enormous advancement of machine learning is accompanied by the vigorous development of
      large-scale datasets across various modalities. Although large datasets (Chang et al., 2015; Deitke
100
      et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2015) have appeared in research areas such as images and rigid shapes, it is
101
      much more costly and laborious to acquire articulated object data as well as annotations for various
102   articulated object understanding tasks (Liu et al., 2022; Xiang et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2019).
103   Therefore, there are not many large-scale articulated object datasets that have been proposed (Jiang
104   et al., 2022; Mao et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2022; Xiang et al., 2020). One of the
105   most commonly used dataset, PartNet-Mobility Xiang et al. (2020), offers 2,346 object models from
106   46 common indoor object categories, about only 50 objects per category on average. All the object
107   models are collected from 3D Warehouse, a 3D model library containing CAD models of real world
      brands promoting products designed by experts.

      2.2   A RTICULATED O BJECT U NDERSTANDING TASKS
109
110
      Articulated objects play an important role in human daily life and understanding these objects is
111
      crucial for machine intelligence to perceive and interact with them. To fully understand articulated
112
      objects, a series of vision and manipulation tasks have been studied.
113
114   Vision Tasks. Part segmentation, part pose estimation and point cloud completion are three impor-
115   tant vision tasks for articulated object understanding. Part segmentation (Qi et al., 2017a;b; Guo
116
      et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2021), which is one of the most fundamental tasks, assigns a semantic label
      to each point of the object. Part pose estimation (Geng et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023) involves query-
117
      ing the 7-dimensional transformation of detected parts on the object, including the scale, rotation
118
      and location of the parts. In these tasks, it is critical to have a good understanding of the spatial
119   structure of an object. On the other hand, point cloud completion aims to estimate the complete
120   shape of objects from partial observations (Yuan et al., 2018; Tchapmi et al., 2019; Wen et al., 2020;
121   Xiang et al., 2022), which pays more attention on the geometric details.
122
      Manipulation Tasks. Articulated object manipulation is a set of various tasks focusing on how
123
      an embodied agent properly interacts with articulated objects (Geng et al., 2023; Mo et al., 2021;
124
      Wang et al., 2022; Ning et al., 2024). For example, Where2Act (Mo et al., 2021) proposed to predict
125   per-pixel action likelihoods and proposals for manipulation. Where2Explore (Ning et al., 2024) pro-
126   posed a few-shot learning framework for articulated object manipulation that measures affordance
127   similarity across categories to migrate affordance knowledge to novel objects. GAPartNet (Geng
128   et al., 2023) released a dataset with semantic and affordance labels and proposed a manipulation
129   pipeline by leveraging the concept of actionable parts. The success rate of manipulation using these
130   proposals largely depends on the understanding of affordances on articulated objects.
131   In this paper, we will conduct exhaustive experiments on the four listed tasks to comprehensively
132   evaluate the quality of our synthetic training data in terms of spatial structure, geometric details and
133   annotations, and also demonstrate the wide applicability of our approach.
134

136   2.3   S CARCITY OF T RAINING DATA IN A RTICULATED O BJECT R ESEARCH
137
138
      In the era of deep learning, a sufficient amount of training data is crucial for neural networks to
139   achieve remarkable performance. However, in the field of articulated object research, the scarcity
140   of training data remains a major obstacle for various articulated object understanding tasks. The
141   challenge in object acquisition is one of the major reasons for data scarcity. When collecting 3D
142   articulated object data of novel categories, common practices would be to design CAD models or
143   scan real-world objects, both of which can be costly and time-consuming. Specifically, design-
144   ing one CAD model from scratch would generally require a specialized artist to spend more than
145   2 hours while the corresponding fees can exceed $100 (Liu et al., 2022). On the other hand, for
146   scanning objects, the high expenses associated with acquiring the scanner and numerous real-world
147
      objects, including high-value items like washing machines, also cannot be neglected. Meanwhile,
      the difficulties in data annotation further restrict the applicability of existing object data. Generally,
148
      manually annotating a 3D shape involves viewing it on a 2D screen, which would require the anno-
149
      tator to constantly change viewing angles to complete the annotation. Furthermore, some types of
150   annotations such as affordances for manipulation are extremely complicated to manually annotate
151   (Mo et al., 2021), resulting in few existing datasets available for affordance labels. Apart from the
152   above points, it is also challenging to comprehensively label an articulated object to support a wide
153   range of tasks, such as semantics, 6-dof pose, grasp pose, etc.
154
      Unfortunately, few researchers have focused their attention on directly addressing the data scarcity
155
      problem. Yet some previous studies on data augmentation (Chen et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020; Kim
156   et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2021) can be applied in this context to alleviate the impact of data scarcity,
157   leveraging their power to enhance the diversity of training data and prevent models from overfitting.
158   For example, PointMixup (Chen et al., 2020) proposed a technique of interpolation between existing
159   point clouds. PointWOLF (Kim et al., 2021) applied smoothly varying non-rigid deformations to
160   the point clouds for diverse and realistic augmentations. However, this line of works cannot provide
161   additional annotations for the augmented data unless they already exist in the original data, which
      restricts the augmented data to specific object modeling tasks.

185   3.1   OVERVIEW
186
187   The research in visual cognition and brain science (Humphreys et al., 1999; Habel & Eschenbach,
188   2006; Ullman, 2000; Palmeri & Gauthier, 2004; Biederman, 1987; Hummel & Biederman, 1992)
189   shows that the perceptual recognition of objects by human is conceptualized to be a process in which
190   the spatial properties of the object are segmented into an arrangement of simple geometric primitives
191   such as cuboids and spheres. Inspired by this point of view, we assume that an object in 3D space
192   can be properly represented with a macro spatial structure and its micro geometric details. Fig. 1
193   gives a brief illustration.
194   The macro spatial structure of an object includes aspects of the geometric primitives and the con-
195   nectivity relationships among them. By describing the primitives as i) specific shapes along with
196   corresponding geometric parameters and ii) their connectivity relationships as relative constraints
197   in DoF (degree of freedom), the structure of an object can be represented quantitatively. Then we
198   can further consider the micro geometric details as shape deformation on the geometric primitives
199   within the macro structure.
200   Intuitively, each primitive can be perceived as a class template which creates shape instances with
201   specific parameters, and the connectivity relationships can be defined as binary descriptors given two
202   shape instances. Based on this observation, we formulate the structure of an object as a program-like
203   representation in our implementation, where generalized geometric primitives and common connec-
204   tivity relationships are mathematically defined. To formulate the deformation for the geometric
205   details, we find the point-wise correspondence between the object’s point cloud and the points on
206
      each primitive’s surface and describe the deformation as the transformation of each pair of points,
      drawing inspiration from the idea in BPS (Prokudin et al., 2019).
207
208   After representing an object with its structure program and geometric details as aforementioned,
209   infinite new objects with unlimited variations in shape can be synthesized through i) alterations of
210   the program via generalized procedural rules and ii) recovering the geometric details according to
211   the point-wise correspondence. Given that the entire program is mathematically defined, we can
212
      easily describe different types of annotations on the program using mathematical descriptions and
      analytically align them to the synthesized objects. In this manner, numerous new objects with rich
213
      annotations can be effortlessly obtained.
214
215   In the following sections, we first introduce how to represent an object asset with a structure program
      and geometric details in Sec. 3.2 and Sec. 3.3, and then demonstrate the procedural generation rules

219   3.2    P ROGRAM D ESCRIPTION OF S PATIAL S TRUCTURE
220
221   In our approach, the spatial structure of an object, including parameterized geometric primitives and
222   connectivity relationships, is described in program form. Considering that each type of geometric
223   primitive represents a group of shapes that share the same properties, we design each geometric
224   primitive as a single class template, whose constructor depicts its general geometric properties. By
225   assigning corresponding parameters, the constructor will instantiate a specific shape of this primitive.
226
      The parameters include intrinsic ones describing the geometric attributes like height and radius of
      a cylinder, and extrinsic ones like positions and orientations of the whole shape. The connectivity
227
      relationship, as the other component in the structure program, is designed as a binary descriptor. It
228
      describes how two shape instances are physically connected, by imposing mathematical constraints
229   between them which reduce the total DoF. Fig. 1-c provides an example of a program description
230   for the spatial structure in Fig. 1-b.
231
232
      Class templates of elementary primitives, like cuboid and cylinder, are initially designed from
      scratch. Observing that common real-world objects within a category often exhibit a consistent hier-
233
      archy in structure (Ullman, 2000; Mo et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2011), we further introduce advanced
234
      primitive templates to capture the structural regularities of components in a high-level hierarchy of
235   an object category.
236
237   An advanced primitive template is constructed based on a set of elementary primitives with specific
238
      spatial layouts and their connectivity relationships. We additionally introduce discrete intrinsic pa-
      rameters in an advanced template to describe regular repetitions of certain elementary primitives.
239
      Given that there are naturally different types of structural regularities for high-level hierarchical
240
      components, we present multiple advanced primitive templates with various designs to cover the
241   diversity. After introducing advanced primitives in the structure program, the program can better
242   reflect the arrangement and relations between shape parts and be more concise, see Fig. 1-d.
243
244
      To efficiently and effectively obtain the structure program of a real object, we have elaborately de-
      signed a user-friendly structure program annotation system for guidance. Due to space limitations,
245
      we introduce the structure program annotation system in Appendix E and provide a video demon-
246
      stration in the supplementary material.

289   3.4      P ROGRAM -O RIENTED S TRUCTURE M ANIPULATION
290
      So far, we have discussed how to represent a given object with our structure program and geometric
291
      details. In this section, we delve into the process of manipulating the original structure of a given
292
      asset to create diverse new structures. We design generalized procedural rules which encompass dif-
293   ferent perspectives of the structure program’s alterations, including continuous parameters, discrete
294   parameters and advanced primitives. Fig. 2 illustrates examples of new structures after manipulation.
295
296
      Continuous Parameter Alteration (CPA). Apply random perturbations to the continuous param-
      eters of primitives in the structure program. Some of the continuous parameters are automatically
297
      adapted rather than being perturbed due to constraints imposed by connectivity relationships. Such
298
      constraints ensure the generated structure to be stable and valid, meaning that there are no primitive
299   collisions or floating elements. As shown in Fig. 2-I-(b1), the sizes of primitives and the angles
300   between them are perturbed in this process.
301
302
      Discrete Parameter Alteration (DPA). Apply random changes to the discrete parameters of ad-
      vanced primitives within a reasonable range. This will vary the total amount of elementary geomet-
303
      ric primitives in the structure program and thereby change the complexity of the whole structure.
304
      As shown in Fig. 2-I-b2, the number of arc sides on the USB body and legs of the globe base are
305   increased through DPA.
306
307   Advanced Primitive Alteration (APA). Randomly replace an advanced primitive with another that
308
      represents the same hierarchical component. This will significantly diversify the structure of syn-
      thesized objects. We let the replacement primitive inherit the overall dimensions of the replaced one
309
      so that it stays in proportion to other primitives in the structure. Additionally, APA will also make
310
      random alterations on the existence of non-essential high-level hierarchical components. As shown
311   in the example of Fig. 2-I-b3, the rotated cap and the rounded rectangle body in the original USB are
312   manipulated into a detached cap and a round tailed body. The bracket of the globe becomes more
313   complex and the legged base is altered to a ring base.
314
      We adopt the procedural rules in the order of APA, DPA, CPA with the aim of creating a wide
315
      variety of new structures. Considering that the randomness introduced in these procedural rules may
316
      lead to the occurrence of extreme parameters, the shape described by the structure program with
317   such extreme parameters will occasionally deviate from physical laws to some extent, e.g. collision
318   between two primitives. To this end, we design an exception handling module to verify the validity
319   of the structure program. This module will monitor the alternation process and automatically locate
320   and adjust the erroneous parameters. In Appendix H, we provide detailed examples of ‘globe base’
321   to better demonstrate structure manipulation with more details.

      3.5   R ECOVERY OF G EOMETRIC D ETAILS
325
326   Now we discuss how to recover the geometric details for a new structure by migrating the geometric
327   details from the original object. Intuitively, given that the geometric details are analytically bounded
328   to the geometric primitives in a structure as discussed in Sec. 3.3, the migration can be carried out
329   by finding the mapping between points from surfaces of the original and the new structures, i.e.
330   before and after the three kinds of alterations. 1) CPA: Since the surface points are analytically
331   bounded to the primitives, the mapping is automatically built according to the primitives’ parame-
332
      ters. 2) DPA: As the value of discrete parameter reduces, primitives are removed and the mapping
      can be ignored. Oppositely, primitives are added via replication and the mapping is automatically
333
      built among the repeated primitives. 3) APA: We assign correspondence between the elementary
334
      primitives in the original and altered advanced primitives based on their hierarchical consistency, to
335   simplify the mapping from the advanced primitive level to the elementary primitive level. If two cor-
336   responding elementary primitives belong to the same template, their mapping is built as discussed
337   in CPA. Otherwise, their mapping is built by map projection techniques (Snyder, 1987), Examples
338   are provided in Fig. 2-II.
339
      After finding the mapping, there are two issues that should be further dealt with. i) Only the points on
340
      visible surfaces are covered by geometric details in the original object. Noticing that some points on
341   the invisible surfaces of the original structure may become visible after structure modification, these
342   invisible points should also be covered by geometric details. Therefore, we complete the geometric
343   details separately for each elementary primitive, by duplicating the visible points to invisible areas
344   based on the properties of the primitive’s local geometric patterns such as translational and rotational
345   symmetry. ii) The geometric details in Eq. 2 are in the world coordinate system, which implies that
346   they cannot be directly used for migration as the normal direction of mapped points may be changed.
347   To this end, we transform each ∆xi to a new vector ∆x̂i relative to the point normal at xi .
348   Finally, we recover the geometric details for the new structure by i) assigning relative geometric
349   details (i.e. {∆x̂i }) to the points on the visible surface of the new structure according to the mapping,
350   and ii) transforming the relative geometric details back to the world coordinate system according to
351   the point normal.
352
353   3.6   A NALYTIC L ABEL A LIGNMENT
354
355   As described in previous texts, we are able to synthesize a new object according to the altered
356   structure program and geometric details, and each point of the new object is analytically bounded
357   to the geometric primitives in the structure program. Taking advantage of this property, we can
358
      analytically align knowledge labels to the object’s point cloud.
359   Specifically, we assign the labels onto the geometric primitives using functions defined on parame-
360   ters of the primitives. This allows for the automatic labeling of spatial structures when they change
361   with the variation of parameters. Fig. 3 shows examples of labeling on structures, including the cen-
362   ter of ring handles, the outer edge of doors and the rim on knobs, these labels provide affordances
363   for interaction. Then, through the point-wise correspondence of geometric details, the labels on the
364
      structures can be further automatically propagated to the point clouds of generated objects. Fol-
      lowing such approach, we are able to synthesize a wide array of labeled objects without additional
365
      human effort.

404   5            E XPERIMENTS
405
406   We thoroughly evaluate the effectiveness of our approach in synthesizing high-quality and richly-
407   annotated articulated objects for training deep neural networks in both vision and manipulation tasks.
408   The vision tasks include part segmentation, part pose estimation and point cloud completion. The
409   manipulation tasks focus on guiding an embodied agent to properly interact with articulated objects.
410
      From widely-used datasets (Yi et al., 2016; Mo et al., 2019; Xiang et al., 2020), we gather 3096 ar-
411   ticulated objects spanning over 26 categories with varying structures to support the evaluation across
412   the aforementioned tasks. We only use the objects in Arti-PG toolbox for procedural generation that
413   are in the training set for all these tasks.
414
      Representative approaches for each task (Zhao et al., 2021; Xiang et al., 2022; Mo et al., 2021; Ning
415
      et al., 2024; Geng et al., 2023) including state-of-the-art are adopted as baselines to evaluate the
416
      improvement achieved after being assisted by our synthesized data and annotations. The training is
417   conducted on randomly synthesized new objects and stops when the training loss converges. In the
418   following sections, we present the main results and analysis for each task. Please refer to Appendix F
419   for more details, results, comparisons and discussions of our experiments, and Appendix G for
420   visualizations of our synthesized objects.

      6     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we introduce Arti-PG toolbox, a procedural generation toolbox aids in synthesizing
      numerous and diverse 3D articulated objects associated with rich annotations, in order to deal with
532
      the data scarcity issue in various articulated object understanding tasks. The novelties of Arti-PG are
533
      threefold. First, we propose a program description for macro spatial structure and a point-wise cor-
534   respondence representation for micro geometric details to mathematically represent the object asset.
535   Second, we design generalized procedural rules to synthesize new objects by first creating a variation
536   of the structure via manipulating the structure program, and then recovering the geometric details
537   according to the point-wise correspondence. Third, we demonstrate how to automatically obtain a
538   wide array of labels for the synthesized objects with analytic label alignment. We comprehensively
539   evaluate the effectiveness of Arti-PG toolbox on four representative object understanding tasks from
      both vision and robotic aspects, and the experiments suggest the superiority of our approach.

711   A    A RCHITECTURE AND O PERATING P RINCIPLES OF S TRUCTURE P ROGRAM
712
713   In Sec. 3.2, we introduced an example of a washing machine to show our design of the structure
714   program and how to use it to describe the spatial structure of an articulated object. Here we pro-
715   vide another example to more clearly demonstrate the architecture and operating principles behind
716   the program description of the object structure (in this case, the structure of a sliding window with
717   two prismatic panels) step by step. This shows a better view of technical details such as the prim-
718   itive composition of an object, how connectivity relationships work between primitives, and how
719   advanced primitives are built upon elementary ones. For the rest of this section, we use two types of
720
      fonts, namely monospaced and italic, to indicate primitive instances and primitive class templates
      respectively.
721
722   OBJECT STRUCTURE: Let’s start from the top row in Fig. 4, where the structure of the object
723   is resolved into four components, frame and window 1-3, and all these windows are connected
724   to frame. Particularly, window 2 is in a fixed connection and window 1/window 3 are in a
725   prismatic connection. For fixed connection, we restrict the relative translations and rotations be-
726
      tween window 2 and frame to specific values. To implement the prismatic connection, we set the
      translation of window 1/window 3 along the x-axis free within the length of frame and restrict
727
      the other relative translations and rotations between window 1/window 3 and frame.
728
729   Frame: Frame is described with the primitive rectangular tube and its corresponding parameters.
730   Window 1-3: window 1-3 are instantiated from an advanced primitive of window, consisting
731   of a window base and optional handles. The window base is described with a concave cuboid
732   elementary primitive and the handle is a handle advanced primitive, and the two components are
733   connected with fixed connection. A discrete parameter is used to indicate the number of handles.
734   Through the window advanced primitive, we can use concave cuboid with different parameters and
735   handle 1-2 to describe window 1-3. Specifically, the number of handles is 0 for window 2
736   and 1 for the rest. This also shows that the same primitive templates implemented with different
737   parameters result in various structures.
738   Handle 1-2: handle 1-2 are instantiated from an advanced primitive of handle, whose
739   three components are all cuboid elementary primitives, and are instantiated into handle top,
740   handle middle and handle bottom in this case to construct both handle 1-2. The connec-
741   tion between handle top and handle middle is a fixed connection. Since handle middle
742   plays a role of a revolute joint, we connect handle bottom with handle middle by restricting
743   their relative translations and rotations with the exception of the rotational freedom along the joint’s
744
      axis of revolution. Together with these primitives and connectivity relationships between them, we
      get an advanced primitive template that describes a handle. By assigning specific parameters to the
745
      advanced primitive template, we are able to describe handle 1-2.

1702     I.1   S CALABILITY OF D ESIGNING P RIMITIVE T EMPLATES
1703
1704     Primitive templates are fundamental for Arti-PG, and we have already provided more than 200
1705     templates in the toolbox to cover 26 categories of commonly seen articulated objects. We also find
1706     that there may be users who want to customize their own templates to satisfy their needs, and here
         we show how the elaborate design of primitive templates can mitigate the costs to create new ones.


                                                        32
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1707
       As stated in Sec. 3.2, we propose a two-tier design of primitive templates. Elementary primitive
1708   templates, representing the basic and general geometric shapes, are first defined from scratch. Then
1709   advanced primitive templates can be defined upon elementary ones instead of from scratch, to rep-
1710   resent the diverse structures of articulated objects. Therefore, 1) with pre-defined elementary ones,
1711   scaling up the advanced ones is practically convenient at the program level, and 2) many advanced
1712   templates are reusable across object categories (e.g. a template of handle can be used in window,
1713   door, fridge, etc.), indicating that scaling up the number of object categories covered by Arti-PG is
1714   also convenient. To take a step further, as the scale of advanced primitives goes larger, the scaling
1715   of object categories can be easier.
1716   We will make the primitive templates that we have already created publicly available in the Arti-PG
1717   toolbox for researchers to use directly. If someone needs to define primitive templates for a new
1718   category, he/she can leverage the ones we provided, avoiding the burden of designing from scratch.
1719   We will also continue to extend our work to include more object categories and share the newly
1720   defined primitive templates with the community, making our work stronger.
1721
1722   I.2   A DVANTAGES OVER C OLLECTING AND A NNOTATING MORE R EAL O BJECTS
1723
1724
       To address the data scarcity issue of articulated objects, i.e. lack of both object data and annota-
       tions for various articulated object understanding tasks, there are currently two possible ways: (1)
1725
       collecting and annotating more real objects (abbreviated as CARO), and (2) procedurally generating
1726
       objects (our approach). For CARO, the obstacles are i) collecting real articulated objects and ii)
1727   providing different types of annotations for each object.
1728
1729   Regarding obstacle i), due to the complex structure of articulated objects, the object collection pro-
1730
       cess is difficult and time-consuming. For reference, the average time to collect a CAD articulated
       object is more than 120 minutes and the cost is more than $100 (Liu et al., 2022). The average time
1731
       to scan an articulated object is 20 minutes and an additional 15 minutes are needed to fix imperfect
1732
       meshes from the scan (Liu et al., 2022; Geng et al., 2023). As scanning requires purchasing objects,
1733   the cost can be high, especially for categories like electrical appliances and furniture (Liu et al.,
1734   2022). Further, both collection practices require experts, i.e. who are capable of designing CAD
1735   models, labeling the URDF or using a scanner (Liu et al., 2022).
1736
       As for obstacle ii), given the large number of articulated object understanding tasks as stated in
1737
       Sec. 2.2, many different types of annotations need to be annotated on these objects to enable training
1738
       for these tasks. For reference, the average time to annotate part semantics for a 3D object is about 8
1739   minutes (Mo et al., 2019), and to annotate part pose is about 10 minutes (Geng et al., 2023).
1740
1741   In summary, at least about an hour and tens of dollars are cost on average for only one object in
1742
       CARO. Therefore, CARO is expensive and time-consuming.
1743   In our approach, the design of primitive templates and structure program annotation requires human
1744   effort. The average time to design primitive templates to cover an object category is about 6 hours,
1745   which is a once-and-for-all effort. Additionally, the structure program annotation step takes about 6
1746   minutes per object. Further, as we will make these codes and data publicly available as a toolbox,
1747   such efforts are free for users in the community. This substantially demonstrates the efficiency and
1748
       scalability of Arti-PG, as well as its superiority compared to CARO.
1749
1750   I.3   L IMITATIONS
1751
       In this paper we propose a novel and effective procedural approach for synthesizing articulated
1752
       objects for network training. However, despite the great variations in the structure of the synthesized
1753   objects, there is still room for diversifying the geometric details. In addition, Arti-PG currently
1754   focuses on 3D visual features and is not coupled with rgb features like color and texture. We will
1755   take these points as our future work to better alleviate the data scarcity issue.
1756
1757   I.4   F UTURE W ORK
1758
1759   Our current approach is an exploration in the context of scarcity of 3D articulated objects. We believe
1760   that in the future, when 3D articulated objects are no longer scarce, abundant data will unleash
       greater potential for using Arti-PG toolbox to generate object spatial structures. A possible way is


                                                         33
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1761
       to first use a generative model to learn the distribution of parameters from the structure program
1762   annotation of abundant real articulated objects, and then use the distribution to infer parameters of
1763   the primitives to generate new instances. We will consider this as our future work. We will also
1764   continue working on extending Arti-PG toolbox to more object categories and tasks.
