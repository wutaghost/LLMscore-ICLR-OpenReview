009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We present a complete mechanistic description of the algorithm learned by a min-
012            imal non-linear sparse data autoencoder in the limit of large input dimension. The
013            model, originally presented in Elhage et al. (2022), compresses sparse data vectors
014            through a linear layer and decompresses using another linear layer followed by a
015            ReLU activation. We notice that when the data is permutation symmetric (no input
016            feature is privileged) large models reliably learn an algorithm that is sensitive to
017            individual weights only through their large-scale statistics. For these models, the
018
               loss function becomes analytically tractable. Using this understanding, we give
               the explicit scalings of the loss at high sparsity, and show that the model is near-
019
               optimal among recently proposed architectures. In particular, changing or adding
020
               to the activation function any elementwise or filtering operation can at best im-
021            prove the model’s performance by a constant factor. Finally, we forward-engineer
022            a model with the requisite symmetries and show that its loss precisely matches
023            that of the trained models. Unlike the trained model weights, the low random-
024            ness in the artificial weights results in miraculous fractal structures resembling a
025            Persian rug, to which the algorithm is oblivious. Our work contributes to neural
026            network interpretability by introducing techniques for understanding the structure
027            of autoencoders.

046   1   I NTRODUCTION
047
048   Large language model capabilities and applications have recently proliferated. As these systems
049   advance and are given more control over basic societal functions, it becomes imperative to ensure
050   their reliability with absolute certainty. Mechanistic interpretability aims to achieve this by obtaining
051   a concrete weight-level understanding of the algorithms learned and employed by these models. A
052   major impediment to this program has been the difficulty of interpreting intermediate activations.
053   This is due to the phenomena of superposition, in which a model takes advantage of sparsity in the
      input data to reuse the same neurons for multiple distinct features, obscuring their function. Finding


                                                         1
054
      a systematic method to undo superposition and extract the fundamental features encoded by the
055   network is a large and ongoing area of research Bricken et al. (2023); Cunningham et al. (2023);
056   Gao et al. (2024); Engels et al. (2024).
057
058
      Currently, the most popular method of dealing with superposition is dictionary learning with sparse
      autoencoders. In this method, the smaller space of neuron activations at a layer of interest is mapped
059
      to a larger feature space. The map is trained to encourage sparsity and often consists of an affine +
060
      ReLU network. This method has been applied to large language models revealing many strikingly
061   interpretable features (e.g. corresponding to specific bugs in code, the golden gate bridge, and
062   sycophancy), even allowing for a causal understanding of the model’s reasoning in certain scenarios
063   Marks et al. (2024).
064
      The sparse decoding ability of the affine + ReLU map was recently studied in the foundational
065
      work Elhage et al. (2022), which introduced and studied a toy model of superposition. The model
066
      consisted of a compressing linear layer modeling the superposition1 followed by a decompressing
067   affine + ReLU layer, trained together to auto-encode sparse data. They showed that the network
068   performs superposition by encoding individual feature vectors into nearly orthogonal vectors in the
069   smaller space. The affine layer alone is unable to decode sparse input vectors sufficiently well to
070   make use of superposition, but the addition of the ReLU makes it possible by screening out negative
071   interference.
072
      While Elhage et al. (2022) provides valuable empirical and theoretical insights into superposition, it
073   does not obtain a strong enough description of the model algorithm to quantitatively characterize the
074   algorithm’s performance. Given the extensive use of the affine + ReLU map for decoding sparse data
075   in practice, it is important to obtain a complete analytic understanding of the model behavior over
076   a large parameter regime. As we will see, this will inform the design of better sparse autoencoder
077   architectures.
078
      In this work we obtain such an understanding by considering a particularly tractable regime of the
079   Elhage et al. (2022) model: permutation symmetric data (no input feature is privileged in any way),
080   and the thermodynamic limit (a large number of input features), while maintaining the full range
081   of sparsity and compression ratio values. In this regime, the learned model weights are permuta-
082   tion symmetric on large scales, which sufficiently simplifies the form of the loss function to the
083   point where it is analytically tractable, leaving only a small number of free parameters. We then
084   forwards-engineer an artificial set of weights satisfying these symmetries and optimizing the re-
085   maining parameters, which achieves the same loss as a corresponding trained model, implying that
086   trained models also implement the optimal permutation symmetric algorithm. The artificial set of
087
      weights resembles a Persian rug fig. 1, whose structure is a relic of the minimal randomness used in
      the construction, illustrating that the algorithm relies entirely on large-scale statistics that are insen-
088
      sitive to this structure. Finally, we derive the exact power-law scaling of the loss in the high-sparsity
089
      regime.
090
091   We expect our work to impact the field of neural network interpretability in multiple ways. First,
092   our work provides a basic theoretical framework that we believe can be extended to other regimes
093
      of interest, such as structured correlations in input features, which may help predict scaling laws in
      the loss based on the data’s correlations. Second, our work rules out a large class of performance
094
      improvement proposals for sparse autoencoders. Finally, our work provides an explicit example of
095
      a learned algorithm that is insensitive to microscopic structure in weights, which may be useful for
096   knowing when not to analyze individual weights.
097
098   The paper is structured as follows. In section 2 we review the model and explain our training
099
      procedure. In section 3 we show empirically that large models display a “statistical permutation
      symmetry”. In section 4 we extract the algorithm by plugging the symmetry back into the loss,
100
      introduce the Persian Rug model which optimizes the remaining parameters, show that large trained
101
      models achieve the same loss, and derive the loss behavior in the high sparsity limit. In section 5 we
102   conclude and discuss related works.

111   We study the following non-linear autoencoder with parameters Win ∈ Rnd ×ns , Wout ∈
112   Rns ×nd , b ∈ Rns with nd ≤ ns ,

118   Here, Win is an encoding matrix which converts a sparse activation vector x to a dense vector, while
119   Wout perform the linear step of decoding. We also consider a simple model for the sparse data on
120
      which this autoencoder operates. We work with data that is permutation symmetric in the sense
      that (x1 , ..., xn ) is equal in distribution to (xπ(1) , xπ(2) , ..., xπ(n) ) for any permutation π. Each
121
      vector x is drawn i.i.d. during training, and each component is drawn i.i.d. with xi = ci ui , where
122
      ci ∼ Bernoulli(p) and ui ∼ Uniform[0, 1] are independent variables. This ensures that x is sparse
123   with typically only pns features turned on.
124
125   We train our toy models to minimize the expected L2 reconstruction loss,
126

130   It is known that for the linear model (eq. (1) without the ReLU), the optimal solution is closely
131   related to principle component analysis (see, for example, Plaut (2018) and p. 563 or Bishop &
132   Nasrabadi (2006)). In particular, the reconstruction loss decreases linearly in the hidden dimension
133   nd when all features are i.i.d. On the other hand, the model eq. (1) will have a much quicker
134   reduction in loss, as will be described in section 3.1.
135

136   continues as long as the average loss over the past 200 batches is lower than the average loss over
137   the 200 batches prior to that one. Our goal with training is to ensure that we have found an optimal
138   model in the large-data limit to analyze the structure of the model itself. See also appendix G for
139   more training details.

      3.3   O PTIMIZATION OF RESIDUAL PARAMETERS
271
272   The statistical permutation symmetry places constraints on the possible values of W and b. The
273   constraint on b is straightforward: it is proportional to the all ones vector, i.e. there is a number
274   b such that bi = b for all i. As will be explained in section 4.1, the relevant degrees of freedom
275   remaining in W are a number a equal to the diagonals (Wii = a for all i) and another number σ
276   characterizing the variance of νi (var νi = σ for all i). The precise values of the off diagonals can be
277   thought of as irrelevant “microscopic information”. Thus there are three relevant degrees of freedom
278
      remaining: b, a, and σ.
279   In section 4.2.2, we give a specific set of values for Win and Wout via the “Persian Rug” matrix,
280   which have the statistical permutation symmetry in the ns → ∞ limit while also optimizing σ. The
281   remaining parameters, a and b can be optimized numerically. In fig. 2 we compare the loss curve of
282   this artificial model with that of a trained model, and see that they are essentially the same.

284   4     E XTRACTING THE A LGORITHM
285
286   In this section, we give a precise explanation of the algorithm the model performs. We start with
287   a qualitative description of why the statistical permutation symmetry gives a good auto-encoding
288   algorithm when the remaining macroscopic degrees of freedom are optimized. We then find an
289   artificial set of symmetric weights with optimized macroscopic parameters. We show that the trained
290   models achieve the same performance as the artificial model, thus showing they are optimal even
291   restricting to statistically symmetric solutions. Finally, we derive an explicit form of the loss scaling
292
      and argue that ReLU performs near optimally among element-wise or “selection” decoders.

326   The impact of all the non-diagonal terms has been summed up in the “noise” ν. Though it is a

      4.2     O PTIMIZING THE MACROSCOPIC PARAMETERS
344
345   We have seen qualitatively that a statistically symmetric strategy exists in certain regimes of the
346   macroscopic parameters. Two of these parameters, a and b are unconstrained. Furthermore the loss
347   should be monotonically increasing with σ because a larger σ implies more noise which hinders
348   reconstruction. Thus we now prove lower bounds on σ and construct an artificial set of statistically
349   permutation symmetric weights which achieve this bound. Finally we will compare the reconstruc-
350   tion loss of this strategy with the learned one to justify that those ignored microscopic degrees of
351   freedom were indeed irrelevant.

452   Goldt (2023) show that shallow autoencoders learn the principal components of the data sequentially
453   and Nguyen (2021) shows a similar dynamical result via a mean-field analysis. It’s seen that such
454   autoencoders function even in a regime where the number of features and the size of the input
455   are proportional with numerical evidence for Gaussian universality (Shevchenko et al., 2023). This
456   universality is shown for shallow in auto-encoders following gradient dynamics (Kögler et al., 2024).

458   5.1.1    M ECHANISTIC I NTERPRETABILITY AND S PARSE AUTOENCODERS
459
      Mechanistic interpretability is a research agenda which aims to understand learned model algorithms
460
      through studying their weights (see Olah et al. (2020) for an introduction). Recent results relating
461
      to language models include Meng et al. (2023), which finds a correspondence between specific facts
462   and feature weights, along with Olsson et al. (2022), which shows that transformers learn in context
463   learning through the mechanism of “induction heads”.
464
465
      A key issue for the agenda of mechanistic interpretability is that the model stores features in su-
      perposition. Elhage et al. (2022) introduced the toy model of superposition we study in this paper.
466
      While that work focused on mapping empirically behaviors of the model in multiple regimes of in-
467
      terest such as correlated inputs, we focused on a regime with enough symmetry to solve the model
468   analytically given observed symmetries in trained models. Chen et al. (2023) study this model in the
469   context of singular learning theory. As part of their work, they characterize the loss using a different
470   high sparsity approximation than the one we present in this paper (they assume exactly one input
471   feature is on). Then they derive a subset of the critical points and their corresponding local learning
472   coefficients under the assumption nd = 2. Refinetti & Goldt (2023) study the learning dynamics of
473   the same model but without the sparsity assumption.
474   One way to extract interpretable features that are stored in superposition is through dictionary learn-
475   ing. While the concept of dictionary learning was introduced by (Mallat & Zhang, 1993), the prac-
476   tical use of sparse autoencoders to understand large language models has accelerated recently due
477   to mezzo-scale open weight models (Gao et al., 2024; Lieberum et al., 2024) and large-scale open-
478   output models Bricken et al. (2023). These features are highly interpretable (Cunningham et al.,
479   2023) and scale predictably. Interestingly, the scaling is quite similar for the various different ar-
480   chitectures they consider, differing primarily by a constant, which fits with the predictions in this
481   work.
482   Our study of Elhage et al. (2022)’s model of superposition lend some insight into the dictionary
483   learning problem. In particular, we have seen that the dominant source of error is not from deter-
484   mining which features are present, but rather the actual values of those features. Small modifications
485   to the activation functions, such as gating Rajamanoharan et al. (2024), k-sparse Makhzani & Frey
      (2013), or TRec non-linearity Taggart (2024); Konda et al. (2014), are insufficient to fix this problem


                                                         9
486
      as they do not solve the basic issue of noisy outputs. In this context our work implies that innovative
487   architectures, that are suitable both for gradient-based training and also for decoding sparse features,
488   must be developed.
489
490
      While our work focuses on Elhage et al. (2022) toy model of superposition, one can study the
      dictionary learning with sparse autoencoders problem directly under various models for the data and
491
      various algorithms (see, for example, Rangamani et al. (2017); Nguyen et al. (2019); Arora et al.

515   5.1.2   C OMPRESSED S ENSING , S TATISTICAL P HYSICS
516
      It is known that compressed sparse data can be exactly reconstructed by solving a convex problem
517
      (Candes & Tao, 2005; Candes et al., 2006; Donoho & Elad, 2003; Donoho, 2006) given knowledge
518
      of the compression matrix. Furthermore, using tools from statistical physics it is possible to show
519   that this holds for typical compressed sparse data (Ganguli & Sompolinsky, 2010). Learning the
520   compression matrix is also easy in certain circumstances(Sakata & Kabashima, 2013). For a more
521   general review on compressed sensing and it’s history consider the introduction by Davenport et al.
522   (2012). The reconstruction procedure typically used in compressed sensing is optimizing a (convex)
523   relaxation of finding the sparsest set of features which reproduces your data vector. This is signif-
524   icantly different to the setting of sparse autoencoders which try to obtain the sparse features using
525   only one linear + activation layer.
526   The discrepancy between the ability of convex optimization techniques to achieve zero loss while
527   a linear + ReLU model necessarily incurs an error suggests that a more complex model architec-
528   ture is needed for sparse autoencoders when it is desirable to calculate the feature magnitude to
529   high precision. This may occur, for example, if one wishes to insert a sparse autoencoder into a
530   model without corrupting its downstream outputs. An important line of work is algorithms based
531   on message-passing schemes brought to fame by Donoho et al. (2009), and extended to more gen-
532   eral encoding matrices by Rangan et al. (2019), a more general encoding scheme (Schniter et al.,
533
      2016), for ill conditioned matrices (Ma & Ping, 2017), and proved without statistical physics meth-
      ods by Takeuchi (2019). These works may hold the key to improving interpretability, particularly
534
      for downstream tasks such as circuit recovery.
