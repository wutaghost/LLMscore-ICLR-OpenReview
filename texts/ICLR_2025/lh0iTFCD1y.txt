009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Multimodal Deep Learning enhances decision-making by integrating diverse infor-
012            mation sources, such as texts, images, audio, and videos. To develop trustworthy
013            multimodal approaches, it is essential to understand how uncertainty impacts these
014            models. We propose LUMA, a unique benchmark dataset, featuring audio, image,
015            and textual data from 50 classes, for learning from uncertain and multimodal data.
016            It extends the well-known CIFAR 10/100 dataset with audio samples extracted from
017            three audio corpora, and text data generated using the Gemma-7B Large Language
018            Model (LLM). The LUMA dataset enables the controlled injection of varying
019            types and degrees of uncertainty to achieve and tailor specific experiments and
020
               benchmarking initiatives. LUMA is also available as a Python package including
               the functions for generating multiple variants of the dataset with controlling the
021
               diversity of the data, the amount of noise for each modality, and adding out-of-
022
               distribution samples. A baseline pre-trained model is also provided alongside three
023            uncertainty quantification methods: Monte-Carlo Dropout, Deep Ensemble, and
024            Reliable Conflictive Multi-View Learning. This comprehensive dataset and its
025            benchmarking tools are intended to promote and support the development, eval-
026            uation, and benchmarking of trustworthy and robust multimodal deep learning
027            approaches. We anticipate that the LUMA dataset will help the ICLR community
028            to design more trustworthy and robust machine learning approaches for safety
029            critical applications.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      In recent years, the use of Machine Learning and Deep Learning has surged across various fields, driv-
035
      ing advancements in data analysis and decision-making. In domains such as healthcare, autonomous
036   driving, and finance, information is distributed across multiple modalities including audio, video,
037   text, and images. To better understand the data and improve decision-making capabilities, it is crucial
038   for deep learning models to integrate diverse, multimodal sources of information. Multimodal Deep
039   Learning (MDL) addresses this need and improves the capabilities of uni-modal networks (Bayoudh
040   et al., 2022; Krones et al., 2024; Xiao et al., 2020; Lee & Yoo, 2020).
041
      Another important consideration for deploying deep learning models in safety critical fields is
042   trustworthiness. Traditional deep learning models are often overconfident in their predictions (Abdar
043   et al., 2021), which can lead to catastrophic results in areas such as healthcare or autonomous driving.
044   Although various techniques for uncertainty quantification have been proposed to measure the level
045   of uncertainty in data and model, this remains an open and challenging area. More research and
046   robust benchmarks are needed to advance the field of uncertainty quantification in deep learning
047   (Krishnan & Tickoo, 2020; Nado et al., 2021).
048   In probabilistic modeling, uncertainty is usually divided into aleatoric (data) and epistemic (model)
049   uncertainties (Kiureghian & Ditlevsen, 2009). Aleatoric uncertainty refers to the uncertainty in
050   the data due to inherent noise. It is impossible to reduce the amount of aleatoric uncertainty with
051   additional data (hence, it is also often called irreducible uncertainty). Epistemic uncertainty is the
052   uncertainty in model parameters, due to lack of data, hence, it can be reduced with additional data
053   samples. Epistemic uncertainty is also usually high for Out-of-distribution (OOD) data, and is
      commonly used for OOD detection.

054
      Multimodal uncertainty quantification (MUQ) is a relatively new research area that adapts uncertainty
055   quantification approaches to multimodal deep learning problems, aiming to enhance the trustworthi-
056   ness of these models (Jung et al., 2022). Due to the unsupervised nature of uncertainty quantification,
057   where the exact extent of uncertainty in the data and the model is unknown, analyzing and bench-
058   marking proposed UQ methods is challenging. Current multimodal datasets used for benchmarking
059   state-of-the-art models in multimodal uncertainty quantification (Han et al., 2023; Jung et al., 2022;
060   2023; Liu et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2024) lack the ability to inject a controlled amount and various
061   types of uncertainties for each modality. This limitation hinders the comprehensive benchmarking of
062   MUQ techniques, which is essential for developing trustworthy and robust multimodal deep learning
063
      approaches.
064   To address this challenge, we introduce LUMA (Learning from Uncertain and Multimodal dAta),
065   a multimodal dataset specifically designed for benchmarking multimodal learning algorithms on
066   uncertain data. The dataset includes 101,000 images, 135,096 audio recordings, and 62,875 text
067   passages, amounting to approximately 3 GB of data. Each modality is independently sourced,
068   reflecting real-world conditions where data is often collected under different conditions and times.
069
      For example, in medical contexts, diagnostic data from different modalities such as radiography,
      MRI, and ECG/EEG are gathered asynchronously, leading to modality-specific uncertainties. The
070
      modalities are carefully aligned, ensuring that each text passage is related to the object in the
071
      corresponding image, and each audio recording is the pronunciation of the object label in the image.
072   The provided Python toolkit allows the injection of aleatoric and epistemic uncertainties in a controlled
073   and parameterized way into each modality specifically.
074
075
      To summarize, our contributions are as follows:
076
              1. We propose LUMA1 , a benchmarking dataset for learning from uncertain and multimodal
077
                 data. It includes audio, image, and textual modalities across 50 distinct classes. We compiled
078              the images from the CIFAR 10/100 dataset (Krizhevsky, 2009), extracted, validated, and
079              associated the corresponding audio samples from three diverse audio corpora, and generated
080              the related text modality utilizing Gemma-7B Instruct (Mesnard et al., 2024) Large Language
081              Model (LLM). We also performed additional bias analysis of the dataset. Each generated
082              version of the dataset consists of 600 data records per class (500 for training, and 100 for
083              testing) belonging to 42 classes, and 3,859 OOD data points, belonging to the remaining 8
084              classes.
085           2. We offer a Python package 1 that generates dataset samples with varying levels of noise and
086              uncertainty. The uncertainty generator can effectively increase aleatoric uncertainty in the
087              data and epistemic uncertainty in the model.
088           3. Finally, we provide baseline models including three different uncertainly quantification
089              methods (Monte-Carlo Dropout (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016), Deep Ensemble (Lakshmi-
090              narayanan et al., 2017), Reliable Conflictive Multi-View Learning (Xu et al., 2024)), to
091              serve as a starting point for benchmarking.

      2       L IMITATIONS OF CURRENT DATASETS FOR MDL BENCHMARKING
095
      In practice, we often don’t know the extent of inherent uncertainties in the data or how accurately
096
      they represent the real-world data space. This often makes it hard to evaluate how well uncertainty
097
      quantification algorithms work. Moreover, deep learning algorithms may behave differently under
098   different amount of uncertainties (i.e., the robustness to noise may vary). Thus, it may be beneficial
099   to inject additional amount of noise in the data, and observe the change in uncertainty metrics and the
100   performance of the models. Since approaches to quantify different types of uncertainty vary, it is
101   beneficial to have options for injecting various types of uncertainties.
102
      Several datasets are used in multimodal uncertainty quantification settings. A notable line of work
103
      (Han et al., 2023; Jung et al., 2022; 2023) has employed datasets such as HandWritten2 , CUB3 ,

111   datasets to better evaluate uncertainty in deep learning models.
112
      Furthermore, the current approaches that introduce uncertainty in the data (Han et al., 2023; Jung

117   Additionally, how different modalities’ uncertainties interact significantly impacts the overall mul-
118
      timodal uncertainty. When both modalities encode redundant information, the total uncertainty
      might not decrease. Conversely, conflicting information can lead to increased uncertainty, while
119
      complementary information can reduce it. A deeper understanding of these phenomena is crucial.
120
      Fine-grained control over individual modalities’ uncertainties opens the way for more theoretical
121   research based on empirical observations.
122
123   To better understand and analyze uncertain multimodal data, as well as to debug and benchmark
124
      uncertainty quantification techniques in the multimodal learning context, we propose a dataset
      accompanied by an uncertainty generator package. This package includes various techniques for
125
      injecting uncertainty, such as controlling data diversity, adding different types of real-world noise,
126
      randomly switching labels to their closest class, and injecting out-of-distribution (OOD) data.

       Since most of the audio data is collected from forced alignments, it is possible to have misaligned
217    audio segments, which could introduce additional noise to the dataset. Moreover, since part of the
218    audio samples are from voluntary contributions, there can be very noisy samples, which are hard to
219    interpret, or audio samples with a strong accent, which again can be hard to interpret. To remove
220    such extreme cases in audio samples, we performed an automatic validation of the samples. Then, we
221    filtered out the false negatives with manual validation for the negative predictions.
222
      The automatic validation was achieved with the OpenAI’s Whisper Large V3 model (Radford et al.,
223   2023) for audio transcription, and transcribed the extracted audio samples. If the transcription
224   corresponded to the class label (or its plural form), then we considered the sample as valid. Otherwise,
225   the sample was sent for manual validation. Because of the huge output space of the Whisper Large
226   V3 model, the probability of false positives is quite low, so we did not perform a manual validation
227   for positive predictions. To summarize, we validated 130,069 out of 178,123 data samples with
228   automatic validation, and we performed a manual validation for the remaining samples.
229    For manual validation, we decided to check only the classes, which did not have more than 800
230    samples (to be able to sample 600 samples with different degrees of diversity, as described in Section
231    3.4). Hence, we filtered 8,372 samples, and scheduled them for manual labeling. We opted for Label
232    Studio (Tkachenko et al., 2020-2022) to build the labeling interface (see Appendix B.2.2 for the
233    annotation interface). The interface provided the audio sample, with the prompt "Is the audio saying
234    the word below? (An extra ’s’ or ’es’ in the pronunciation is okay.)" and answer options of "Yes" or
235    "No". We asked our colleagues (M.Sc. and PhD Students, and Professors) with advanced to fluent
236    English knowledge to annotate the samples.
237    In total, we collected 2 annotations per sample, from 17 annotators. We got 71.61% of annotation
238    agreement, and accepted 5,027 samples, where both annotators confirmed the validity of the sample.
239    Hence, we took the 42 classes that had more than 600 validated samples (automatically and manually)
240    as training/test data, and we took the remaining 8 classes as OOD data. In total, the auto-validated
241    and manually validated audio samples combined, LUMA has 135,096 audio samples. The final
242    distribution of audio data across classes can be seen in the Appendix B.2.2.

      Based on the collected samples from the 3 modalities (image, text, audio), we wanted to compile a
308   dataset with little uncertainties, and later, provide tool to inject uncertainties on demand. Our priority
309   was to propose several options for uncertainty control and parameter setting: data diversity, sample
310   noise, label noise, OOD injection.
311

312

313

314   which shall lead to greater epistemic uncertainty in areas where data is scarce (Figure 5). Hence,
315   controlling the diversity of the data allows us to study the behavior of epistemic uncertainties under
316   varying amounts of information.

      For image modality, we added different types of noise suggested and implemented by Hendrycks
379   & Dietterich (2018). 15 perturbations are included such as: adding Gaussian noise, shot noise,

      Label Noise: Another way to insert aleatoric uncertainty is to add label noise in the data (i.e.,
383
      randomly switch the labels of some samples). Since the uncertainty induced by this type of noise
384
      cannot be reduced with additional amount of data, this noise shall increase the aleatoric uncertainty
385   of the model. To insert this type of noise, we choose samples at random (according to a probability
386   defined by the user), then, based on their deep features (Wav2Vec (Baevski et al., 2020) for audio,
387   BERT (Devlin et al., 2019b) for text and VGG-11 (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2015) for images), we
388   find the average distance to the 5 nearest points from each class, and assign the label of the class that
389   has the closest mean distance.
390
      OOD Injection: Ideally, the models shall be uncertain on data points from unknown distribution (i.e.,
391   distribution they haven’t been trained on). In the literature, often the OOD samples are taken from
392   another dataset, which can simplify the problem, because such samples are far from the training data.
393   For this matter, we kept a separate set of samples from the same dataset, but belonging to classes that
394   are not present in the training data, as OOD samples.

      4     BASELINE MODELS WITH U NCERTAINTY Q UANTIFICATION
397
398
      4.1   BASELINE M ODELS
399
400   We develop baseline models with three different uncertainty quantification algorithms, to serve as a
401   starting point for other research and benchmarking initiatives. For the sake of simplicity, we choose
402   late or decision fusion approaches, where we have classification networks for each modality, and then
403   fuse their decision by simple averaging the output logits. These baselines were selected to instantiate
404   unimodal and multimodal architectures, which can be trained on the dataset and are not intended to
405   serve as a comprehensive benchmark, nor did we endeavor to achieve the best possible performance.
406   For the image modality, we used a simple convolutional neural network, as depicted in Figure 7.
407   For the audio modality, we extracted 128x128 mel-spectrograms from padded audio samples, and
408   used a convolutional network for classification, as depicted in Figure 1. For the text modality, we
409   extracted the BERT (Devlin et al., 2019a) embeddings for each token, and averaged them out, so
410   that we have one embedding per text passage. Then, we passed the embedding through a simple
411   feed-forward neural network (Figure 7) to get the predictions. As depicted in Figure 7, each model
412
      includes two output heads: one for the prediction and the other for aleatoric uncertainty, following
      the methodology outlined by Valdenegro-Toro & Mori (2022). Then, to combine the aforementioned
413
      unimodal networks into a multimodal architecture, we adopted the late fusion approach. In the Monte
414
      Carlo Dropout and Deep Ensemble methods, we obtained the multimodal prediction by averaging
415   the logits from the final layers of the classifiers. For the RCML, we modified the output of the last
416   layer in each network to produce evidence, as described in (Xu et al., 2024), and followed their
417   methodology for combining the evidence.
418
      The dropout probability is 0.3, with the deep ensemble comprising 10 networks. Networks were
419
      trained for up to 300 epochs, with early stopping after 10 epochs of no validation loss improvement.
420
