009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) has become a crucial approach in han-
012            dling the growing complexity of large models and vast datasets across multiple
013            fields such as Computer Vision or Natural Language Processing. Among the most
014            promising of these methods are Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) and its derivatives,
015            which fine-tune a pre-trained weight matrix W by introducing a low-rank update
016            matrix ∆W. While these approaches have demonstrated strong empirical perfor-
017            mance, they remain largely heuristic, with little theoretical grounding to explain
018
               their behavior or guide the design of ∆W for different objectives. This lack of
               theoretical insight limits our understanding of when these methods are most ef-
019
               fective and how they can be systematically improved. In this paper, we propose
020
               a theoretical framework for analyzing and designing LoRA-based methods, with
021            a focus on the formulation of ∆W. By establishing a deeper understanding of
022            the interplay between W and ∆W, we aim to enable more efficient and targeted
023            fine-tuning strategies, opening the door to novel variants that strike an optimal
024            balance between performance and efficiency. Our proposed method - Singular
025            Value Adaptation - uses insights from our theoretical framework to incorporate
026            inductive biases on the formulation of ∆W, leading to a PEFT method that is up
027            to 50× more parameter efficient that LoRA, while achieving comparable or better
028            performance across various vision and language tasks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031   Large pre-trained neural networks have become indispensable across a wide array of domains, in-
032   cluding natural language processing and computer vision, but fine-tuning them efficiently for spe-
033   cialized downstream tasks remains a significant challenge. The sheer scale of modern models makes
034   fine-tuning all parameters computationally expensive and often impractical. To address this, various
035   Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods have been developed, aiming to optimize model
036
      adaptation while minimizing computational overhead. PEFT techniques, such as Adapters, Prompts
      and Prefixes, Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA), and their numerous variants, have gained popularity
037
      due to their ability to fine-tune models by only modifying a small number of parameters, making the
038
      process more efficient. These methods have been successfully deployed across various applications,
039   demonstrating impressive performance gains while significantly reducing resource requirements.
040   LoRA and its derivatives have in particular become widely prevelant, owing to the fact that these
041   methods give comparable or improved performance over other PEFT methods, and do not incur any
042   additional computational costs over the base model during inference.
043
      Despite their success, there remains a lack of clarity regarding the underlying mechanisms that make
044
      LoRA and its derivatives effective. Most formulations of the low-rank update matrix are largely
045   heuristic, with no studies on how different formulations affect the final merged matrix at inference.
046   Our analysis on the original pretrained and and adapted weight matrices of a ViT-Base model finds
047   that they do not significantly differ in terms of their rank (see table 1). Our findings reveal that most
048   pre-trained matrices are in fact full-rank or near-full-rank, all. Instead, in this paper, we hypothesize
049   that the effectiveness of LoRA based techniques may be driven by changes in the ”effective rank”
050   (Roy & Vetterli, 2007) of the model’s weight matrices during fine-tuning. In table 1, we show
051   initial observations supporting this hypothesis, where increases in the effective rank correlate with
052   improved model performance, as evidenced by our sample results (Fig 1 (left)).
053   Building on this observation, we propose a novel method explicitly designed to max-
      imize the increase in effective rank under the constraint of few trainable parameters.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
118   LoRA and Variants. LoRA-based PEFT methods solve the computational overhead of fine-tuning
119   large models by modelling the change in parameters as a low-rank matrix. Various decomposi-
120   tions of the low-rank matrix have been proposed, leading to an entire family of LoRA derivatives
121
      (Kopiczko et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2024; Aghajanyan et al., 2021; Karimi Mahabadi
      et al., 2021; Edalati et al.; Liao et al., 2023; He et al., 2023). The original LoRA formulation (Hu
122
      et al., 2022) decomposed all weight matrices as a product of two rectangular learnable matrices
123
      of specified rank. Dynamic-rank LoRA derivatives (Zhang et al., 2023; 2024; Ding et al., 2023;
124   Valipour et al., 2023; Haobo et al., 2024) methods further refine this approach by using adaptive
125   ranks for different layers. More recent works propose the use of pseudo-random vectors or matrices
126   to achieve aggressive parameter compression. NOLA (Koohpayegani et al., 2024) learns the coeffi-
127   cient of linear combination of pseudo-random matrices, while FourierFT (Gao et al., 2024) samples
128   a random spectral basis and learns the sparse spectral coefficients , using an Inverse Discrete Fourier
129   Transform to get the weight update matrix. In similar spirit, VeRA (Kopiczko et al., 2024) samples
130   two random matrices, and scales them by learnable factors before multiplying them to obtain the
131   update matrix. These methods are largely heuristic, without any underlying common principle that
132
      guides the formulation of the weight update matrix.
133   Other PEFT Methods. Adapter Tuning methods (Pfeiffer et al., 2021; Houlsby et al., 2019b; Lei
134   et al., 2023; He et al.; Zhu et al., 2021) introduce task-specific parameters through small layers
135   (adapters) inserted within a pre-trained model. Prompt/Prefix Tuning methods (Li et al., 2023; Liu
136   et al.; Zhang et al.; Zhu & Tan; Wu et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2022; Lester & Constant; Liu et al.,
137   2023) prepend learnable vectors to the inputs of the model (prompts) or individual intermediate
138
      layers (prefixes). Some other methods include BitFit (Zaken et al., 2022) which only tunes the
      model biases and IA3 (Liu et al., 2022) which introduces additional parameters in the Self-Attention
139
      module. All these methods increase the complexity of the base model, leading to increased inference
140
      times and a requirement for additional space. LoRA derivatives do not suffer from this issue as the
141   newly learned parameters can be directly added to the parameters of the base model post training.

      3.2 T HEORETICAL FORMULATION
169   Since the effective rank increases monotonically with the entropy H, we shift our focus towards the
170   analysis of H. We first derive an expression for computing the change in entropy of the combined
171   matrix over the original matrix, i.e. ∆H = HW+∆W − HW . Using this expression, we proceed to
172
      show that for maximizing ∆H, the change in the ith singular value of W should be proportional to
      its own magnitude.
173
174   The next part of our analysis uses this observation to find the ideal singular values to learn given that
175   we wish to constrain the number of parameters (here, singular values) that are trained. We proceed
176   to show that if all singular values are not freely tunable, it is optimal to tune the lower singular values
177   of W using ∆W. Additionally, we further show that for two different ∆W matrices that have the
178
      same norm and the same number of tunable singular values, the one that has its singular vectors
      aligned with the singular values of W results in the maximum increase in the effective rank.
179
180   We then show that this formulation - training only the lower singular values of W by adding an
181   appropriately crafted ∆W - achieves an optimal solution for the Linear Regression Problem, and
182   the proceed to adapt this method for fine-tuning transformers.

297   3.3 M ETHOD
298   This section outlines the implementation of our method. We use insights from our theoretical ob-
299   servations to develop a PEFT method that is conceptually simple, aggressively parameter-efficient,
300   and highly performant. Our approach directly draws inspiration from our theoretical framework,
301   and is based on the following hypothesis - Modification of a small subset of singular values of the
302   pre-trained model weights is sufficient to achieve high performance, while simultaneously limiting
303
      the number of parameters to be trained. We formulate ∆W such that it selectively updates the
      lower singular values of a pre-trained weight matrix W when added to it. The pseudocode outlining
304
      the various components of SiVA is presented in Algorithm 1.
305
306   Formally, let W be the weight matrix to be adapted to a downstream task by adding the update
307   matrix ∆W. We compose ∆W as product of three matrices U, S, and VT , where U and V are
308   the left and right singular vectors of W respectively (Theorem 4). Note that this initialization of U
309
      and V is a one-time operation, performed only when attaching the SiVA module to the base layer.
      S is a diagonal matrix whose values are optimized using Gradient Descent.
310
311   To achieve parameter efficiency, we only train k diagonal elements of S, corresponding to the sin-
312   gular values of W that need to be modified. The remaining singular values are frozen, along with
313   U and VT . Only the lowest singular values are chosen for training (Theorem 3). Analogous to
314   the observation made by AdaLoRA (Zhang et al., 2023) about the suboptimal choice of having the
315
      same rank for each update matrix, maintaining a constant number of k per layer can also be subop-
      timal. Therefore, we determine k in each layer based on the variance captured by the corresponding
316
      eigenvalues. We pick the singular values corresponding to the eigenvalues that capture the bottom
317
      (1 − perc)% of the variance, where perc is a hyperparameter. The pseudocode for computing k for
318   a given value of perc is presented in the get values and vectors function in Algorithm 1.

              U, S, V = torch.svd(matrix)
348           # Calculate the total variance (sum of squared singular values)
              total_variance = (S**2).sum()
349
350              # Calculate the cumulative sum of squared singular values
                 cumulative_variance = torch.cumsum(S**2, -1)
351
                 # Find the number of singular values that capture perc% of the variance
352              num_frozen_singular_values = torch.searchsorted(
353                  cumulative_variance,
                     self.perc * total_variance
354              ) + 1
355              num_trainable_singular_values = S.shape[0] - num_singular_values
356
                 reduced_U = U[:, -num_trainable_singular_values:]
357              reduced_S = S[-num_trainable_singular_values:]
                 reduced_V = V[:, -num_trainable_singular_values:]
358              return reduced_U, reduced_S, reduced_V
359
          def forward(self, x):
360           result = self.base_layer(x)
              W = self.U @ self.S.diag() @ self.V.t()
361           result += F.linear(x, W)
362           return result

518   5   C ONCLUDING R EMARKS
519   In this work, we introduce Singular Value Adaptation (SiVA), a novel, simple and efficient PEFT
520   technique which is grounded in theoretical insights. SiVA increases the effective rank by selec-
521   tively training only a subset of singular values in the adapter weight matrices, ∆W. The method’s
522   efficiency stems from two core principles: (1) Sparsity, where instead of updating all singular val-
523   ues of the pre-trained weight matrix W, SiVA updates only the k-smallest singular values, with k
524   determined adaptively at each layer based on the cumulative sum of singular values and a hyperpa-
525
      rameter perc. This often leads to k being much smaller than the total number of singular values.
      (2) Alignment, where the singular vectors of the adapter matrix ∆W are aligned with those of W.
526
      This alignment reduces the need to train additional parameters, avoiding the necessity of training
527
      the singular vectors U∆W and V∆W as would be required with arbitrary updates. These principles
528   allow SiVA to dramatically reduce the number of trainable parameters while maintaining or exceed-
529   ing performance on large-scale datasets. Experimental results show SiVA achieves a 2× to 50×
530   reduction in trainable parameters compared to existing PEFT methods like LoRA and FourierFT,
531   while still achieving competitive performance. SiVA’s low parameter count makes it especially ad-
532   vantageous for applications requiring frequent model switching or the storage of many fine-tuned
533   models. Reuse of significant parts of the base model makes it ideal for tasks like domain adaptation,
534   continual learning, and multi-task learning, where maintaining a backbone of shared information
535   while adapting to specific downstream tasks is crucial.
536   As future work, SiVA’s parameter efficiency on Transformer architectures can be linked to the archi-
537   tecture’s inductive bias (Tarzanagh et al., 2023) and the cross-entropy loss. Additionally, exploring
538   the relationship between SiVA’s singular values and the intrinsic rank (Aghajanyan et al., 2021)
539   could help clarify how closely SiVA approaches the optimal number of parameters required for each
      task.
