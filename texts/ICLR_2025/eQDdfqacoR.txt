009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Complex distribution shifts are the main obstacle to achieving accurate long-term
012            time series forecasting. Several efforts have been conducted to capture the distri-
013            bution characteristics and propose adaptive normalization techniques to alleviate
014            the influence of distribution shifts. However, these methods neglect the intricate
015            distribution dynamics observed from various scales and the evolving functions
016            of distribution dynamics and normalized mapping relationships. To this end, we
017
               propose a novel model-agnostic Evolving Multi-Scale Normalization (EvoMSN)
               framework to tackle the distribution shift problem. Flexible normalization and
018
               denormalization are proposed based on the multi-scale statistics prediction mod-
019
               ule and adaptive ensembling. An evolving optimization strategy is designed to
020            update the forecasting model and statistics prediction module collaboratively to
021            track the shifting distributions. We evaluate the effectiveness of EvoMSN in
022            improving the performance of five mainstream forecasting methods on bench-
023            mark datasets and also show its superiority compared to existing advanced nor-
024            malization and online learning approaches. The code is publicly available at
025            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/EvoMSN-4E53/.

027   1 I NTRODUCTION
028
      Time series forecasting can provide valuable future information and thus plays an important role in
029
      many real-world applications (Lim & Zohren, 2021; Sezer et al., 2020; Qin et al., 2023; Karevan
030
      & Suykens, 2020). Abundant studies have been carried out for accurate time series forecasting,
031   ranging from traditional statistics-based methods (Contreras et al., 2003; Holt, 2004; Theodosiou,
032   2011; Qiu et al., 2017) to recent deep learning-based methods (Wen et al., 2022). However, accurate
033   forecasting of time series under distribution shifts is still a challenging and under-resolved problem.
034
      Recently, several pioneering studies have been
035
      proposed for time series forecasting under dis-
036
      tribution shifts by adaptive normalization ap-
037   proaches (Kim et al., 2021; Fan et al., 2023; Liu
038   et al., 2024b). These approaches first remove
039   the dynamic distribution information from the
040   original series, enabling the model to learn from
041   normalized data, and then predict the future
042   distribution and recover this information to the
043   model’s output by denormalization. However,
044   two limitations of these approaches can be iden-
045
      tified. Firstly, existing methods model the dy-
      namic of distribution P (Y ) merely from an in-
046
      stance level, neglecting the intricate dynamics
047
      spanning various scales. Real-world time se- Figure 1: The marginal distribution P (Y ) of a
048   ries present diverse variations at different tem- time series viewed from different scales will show
049   poral scales, such as the electricity load ex- diverse dynamics, while the conditional distribu-
050   hibits unique patterns spanning hourly, daily, tion P (Y |X) also evolves across time.
051   and weekly scales. The dynamic distribution
052   from a larger scale will show the general trend of the series, while the distribution on a smaller
053   scale presents the local variations. The visualization of distribution dynamics from a multi-scale
      perspective is shown in Fig. 5 in the appendix. To this end, the modeling of statistical distribution


                                                        1
054
      dynamics from a multi-scale perspective is needed but still lacking. Secondly, even though current
055   normalization methods can help alleviate the influence of non-stationarity, they are incapable of
056   handling changing input-output relationships caused by gradual distribution shifts, where the con-
057   ditional distribution P (Y |X) is not static. In this circumstance, an online approach is required for
058   model updates with continuous data to adapt to the changing distribution over time. The problem
059   can be illustrated by Fig. 1, where the distribution of a time series that is viewed from different
060   scales will show diverse dynamics, and the input-output mapping function will also evolve across
061   time.
062   To overcome the above limitations, we propose an Evolving Multi-Scale Normalization (EvoMSN)
063   framework for time series forecasting under complex distribution shifts. Specifically, we first pro-
064   pose to divide the input sequence into slices of different sizes according to their periodicity char-
065   acteristics, forming views of diverse temporal scales to be normalized by the slice statistics. The
066   backbone forecasting model can thus process the normalized series that are viewed from different
067   scales to generate multiple outputs. Then, we propose a multi-scale statistic prediction module to
068   capture the dynamics of statistics and predict the distributions of future slices. Consequently, we
069
      denormalize the outputs of the backbone forecasting model with the predicted distribution statistics
      and propose an adaptive ensemble method to aggregate the multi-scale outputs. With the proposed
070
      MSN approach, the dynamics of the distribution can be well modeled to address the non-stationarity
071
      issue. Finally, we tackle the challenge of gradual distribution shifts by proposing an evolving bi-
072   level optimization strategy to update the statistics prediction module and the backbone forecasting
073   model online.
074
075
      In summary, our contributions are as follows:
076         • We propose EvoMSN, a general online normalization framework that is model-agnostic
077
              to enhance arbitrary backbone forecasting models under distribution shifts by adaptively
              removing and recovering the dynamical distribution information.
078
            • We propose a multi-scale statistics prediction module to model the complex distribution
079
              dynamics and enable the estimation of future distributions. An adaptive ensembling strat-
080           egy is designed in the denormalization stage to aggregate the multiple forecasting outputs
081           and realize multi-scale modeling of time series.
082         • We propose an evolving bi-level optimization strategy, including offline two-stage pre-
083           training and online alternating learning to update the statistics prediction module and the
084           backbone forecasting model collaboratively.
085         • We conduct comprehensive experiments on widely used real-world benchmark datasets
086           with various advanced forecasting methods as the backbone. Experimental results demon-
087           strate the effectiveness of the proposed method in boosting the forecasting performance
088
              under distribution shifts and the superiority compared to other state-of-the-art normaliza-
              tion methods and online learning strategies.

092   2.1 T IME S ERIES F ORECASTING
093   Traditionally, time series forecasting is carried out by utilizing statistical methods, such as the auto-
094   regressive moving average model (Contreras et al., 2003), the exponential smoothing method (Holt,
095   2004), and decomposition-based methods (Theodosiou, 2011; Qiu et al., 2017). Recent decades
096   have witnessed the fast development of deep learning-based methods to achieve accurate time series
097   forecasting. The family of transformer models has shown great effectiveness in capturing both
098   temporal dependency and variable dependency by applying the self-attention mechanism (Wen et al.,
099
      2022), where successful examples include Informer (Zhou et al., 2021), Autoformer (Wu et al.,
      2021), FEDformer (Zhou et al., 2022), Pyraformer (Liu et al., 2021), and so on. However, some
100
      recent research questions the effectiveness of transformers in time series forecasting and shows
101
      a simple linear model can achieve comparable or even superior performance (Zeng et al., 2023; Li
102   et al., 2023; 2022). Such concerns are responded by the latest studies on transformers, which propose
103   constructing transformers with patching approaches and showcase that transformer architecture still
104   has its advantages in time series forecasting (Nie et al., 2022; Zhang & Yan, 2022; Chen et al., 2024).
105   In addition to transformers, convolutional neural networks also achieve state-of-the-art performance
106   in time series forecasting by extracting variation information along the temporal dimension (Bai
107   et al., 2018). Multi-scale isometric convolution network (Wang et al., 2022) is proposed to learn
      both the local features and global correlations from time series. TimesNet (Wu et al., 2022) and

      2.2 T IME S ERIES F ORECASTING UNDER D ISTRIBUTION S HIFTS
130
      Even though the above-mentioned methods are well-designed, they may still suffer from the complex
131
      distribution shifts problem. The inevitable temporal non-stationarity and the discrepancy between
132   the training and testing data caused by long-term continuous distribution shifts will largely influ-
133   ence the forecasting model’s performance and generalizability. Adaptive normalization approaches
134   have been investigated in recent research to account for the non-stationarity issue. The deep input
135   normalization method is proposed for time series forecasting with adaptive shifting, scaling, and
136   shifting (Passalis et al., 2019). Kim et al. suggest an instance-level normalization and design a
137   symmetric structure to remove and recover the distribution information according to the statistics of
138   the instance’s input window (Kim et al., 2021). Fan et al. summarize the distribution shifts problem
139   in time series forecasting into two categories, namely intra-space shift and inter-space shift (Fan
140
      et al., 2023). They propose Dish-TS with learnable distribution coefficients for normalization and
      denormalization. Liu et al. further point out that the distribution shift may also happen within the
141
      window, especially in long-term tasks, and propose a finer-grained slice-based normalization strat-
142
      egy (Liu et al., 2024b). Liu et al. investigate the non-stationarity issue, especially for transformer
143   models, and propose a de-stationary attention mechanism to alleviate the over-stationarization prob-
144   lem (Liu et al., 2022). Another class of studies mainly focuses on online learning to address the
145   evolving distribution problem (Anava et al., 2013; Liu et al., 2016; Aydore et al., 2019), where the
146   model will be updated with the continuous streaming data. Most recent advanced online learning
147   approaches enhance the model adaptability under distribution shifts by complementary continual
148   learning (Pham et al., 2022) and combining strategy (Zhang et al., 2024).
149   However, the above studies can only tackle one of the forms of distribution shifts but neglect sce-
150   narios where non-stationarity and evolving distribution shifts happen simultaneously, especially for
151   online long-term time series forecasting. Besides, most existing methods incomprehensively model
152   distribution dynamics, which only considers the statistics of the input window for normalization
153   and denormalization. Even Liu et al. (Liu et al., 2024b) predict the distribution and carry out nor-
154   malization from a finer-grained slice perspective, which is most relevant to our approach, it merely
155   considers a static scale to model the distribution dynamics and the effectiveness is subjected to the
156   predefined slice length. Unlike the existing methods, we propose a more comprehensive normaliza-
157
      tion framework to tackle the complex distribution shifts problem by modeling distribution dynamics
      in a multi-scale perspective.

296   4.1 E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
297   Datasets We evaluate our methods on five large-scale real-world time-series datasets: (1) Elec-
298   tricity transformer temperature (ETT) 1 records of oil temperature and electricity transform-
299   ers’ power load from July 2016 to July 2018. We choose the hourly ETTh1 data and 15-minute-
300   resolution Ettm1 data for our experiments. (2) Electricity 2 contains hourly electricity load data of
301   321 clients from July 2016 to July 2019. (3) Exchange 3 collects the panel data of daily exchange
302   rates from 8 countries from 1990 to 2016. (4) Traffic4 contains hourly traffic load recorded by 862
303   sensors from January 2015 to December 2016. (5) Weather5 contains meteorological time series
304   with 21 weather indicators collected every 10 minutes in 2020.
305   Backbone Models We evaluate the proposed model-agnostic EvoMSN framework with various
306   mainstream time series models as the backbone under both online and offline multivariate forecast-
307   ing settings. We selected backbone models including linear model DLinear (Zeng et al., 2023),
308   transformers Autoformer (Wu et al., 2021), FEDformer (Zhou et al., 2022), PatchTST (Nie et al.,
309   2022), and convolutional model TimesNet (Wu et al., 2022).
310   Implementation Settings We evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed method in tackling the
311   distribution shifts problem under two scenarios, including online and offline forecasting. For online
312   forecasting, we split the data into warm-up pretraining and online learning phases by the ratio of
313   20:75. We follow the optimization details in (Pham et al., 2022) by optimizing the l2 (MSE) loss
314   with the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017). According to the widely applied online
315
      setting, the forecasts will be made as each test data sample arrives, and the model will be updated
      by one epoch according to the online forecasting loss. For offline forecasting, we split the training
316
      and testing data with the ratio of 70:20 and follow the implementation details in (Liu et al., 2024b).

511   5   C ONCLUSION
512   In this paper, we propose an evolving multi-scale normalization framework for time series fore-
513   casting under complex distribution shifts, which enables a more comprehensive way to model the
514   distribution dynamics and facilitates the forecasting model to better track the evolving distributions.
515
      As a model-agnostic framework with arbitrary backbones, the proposed method effectively boosts
      mainstream forecasting techniques to achieve state-of-the-art performance on real-world time-series
516
      datasets by a significant margin. Extensive experiments have been conducted to examine the supe-
517
      riority of the proposed method in tackling distribution shift challenges compared to other advanced
518   normalization and online learning strategies. As our future direction, we will further investigate a
519   more general normalization framework that takes into account more detailed distribution informa-
520   tion beyond mean and variance.

794   To evaluate the computational efficiency of the proposed method, we have measured the computa-
795   tion time in seconds for each training epoch and testing batch, the time of each process in online
796   forecasting and offline forecasting is reported in Table 8 and Table 9, respectively. The proposed
797   method will cause extra computational complexity and running time from two perspectives. First,
798   the proposed method requires training multi-scale statistics prediction modules, where the complex-
799   ity is the number of considered periodicities times the complexity of an individual prediction module
800   for a single periodicity. The computational complexity of this part is independent of the complexity
801
      of the forecasting backbone model and we have designed this part to be lightweight with two-layer
      perception networks to avoid a large computation burden. Second, the backbone forecasting model
802
      will handle the time series from multi-scale perspectives, where the complexity is the number of con-
803
      sidered periodicities times the complexity of the backbone forecasting model. This part becomes the
804   main computation burden when the backbone model is much more complex than the statistics pre-
805   diction module. There are two possible approaches to accelerate the computation process, one is to
806   let the multi-scale analysis in parallel (our experiment is conducted in series); another approach is to
807   consider an attentive updation strategy in the online learning process, which is to update the model
808   only when server distribution shift is detected instead of updating for every sample. Overall, the
809   computational complexity of the proposed method is acceptable in many real-world applications,
      especially for low-frequency forecasting scenarios, such as day-ahead electricity load forecasting.

1019
       Even though the proposed method can greatly improve the overall forecasting accuracy, it may
1020   sometimes cause slight performance degradation. We conclude potential reasons for method failure
1021   as follows: 1) In the online forecasting setting, the multi-scale statistics prediction module is up-
1022   dated only once for each incoming new data, which may challenge the module in capturing some
1023   fast-changing statistics and will result in bad performance. Besides, the global dominant periodicity
1024   is determined according to the training data, where the relatively small training data split ratio in the
1025   online setting may cause an inappropriate periodicity extraction and thus affect the effectiveness of
       multi-scale slice-based analysis. 2) In the offline forecasting setting, the multi-scale statistics pre-


                                                                                                 19
1026
       diction module is only updated on the training data, where the distinct statistics evolving dynamics
1027   of training data and testing data may cause a worse performance in the evaluation stage. Despite
1028   the potential shortcomings identified above, it is essential to emphasize the overall strengths of the
1029   proposed method.
1030
       Moreover, we point out two directions to further improve the proposed method. First, we only model
1031
       the distribution dynamics by investigating slice statistics including mean and deviation, where more
1032
       comprehensive characteristics of distribution, such as minimum and maximum value, are important
1033   but neglected in the proposed framework. We will propose a more general approach to model data
1034   distribution dynamics in our future work. Second, the proposed EvoMSN may suffer from the
1035   stability-plasticity dilemma when the backbone model and statistics prediction module are updated
1036   online, where incremental learning techniques could be further investigated and integrated into the
1037   proposed framework to improve the online forecasting performance.
