011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Knowledge Distillation (KD) has demonstrated significant benefits for learning
014            compact models for object detection. Most current work focuses on general dis-
015
               tillation settings, where student models are relatively large and learnable, then
               compete with the distillation performance. However, due to the model scale and
016
               inference speed, these models are seldom deployed in real-world applications.
017
               In this paper, we dive into a challenging but more applicable setting: how to
018            distill rich teacher knowledge into tiny, faster models for object detection? We
019            first show that simply applying previous KD strategies under such settings cannot
020            achieve satisfying results, due to the extremely large model capacity gap between
021            the teacher-student pairs. To this end, we propose a simple prompt-based ob-
022            ject detection distillation framework, namely DualPromptKD, which aims to im-
023            prove knowledge transfer efficiency from both teacher and student perspectives.
024            Specifically, by distilling teacher representations into compact external prompts,
025            we enable the student model to fully leverage proficient teacher knowledge even
026
               at inference time. In terms of the limited learning ability of the student model,
               we introduce lightweight internal prompts tailored to bolster the feature imita-
027
               tion capability for the target model. Extensive experimental results on the COCO
028
               benchmarks validate the effectiveness and generalization of our approach, includ-
029            ing different image backbones and detector types. Notably, our DualPromptKD
030            surpasses the previous best distillation strategies by more than 2.0 mAP under
031            various experimental settings. The code will be available.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   The field of object detection has made remarkable advancements with the emergence of deep learn-
037   ing models (Cai & Vasconcelos, 2019; He et al., 2017; Tian et al., 2019; Li et al., 2020). However, the
038   practical deployment of large and computationally intensive models in real-world applications poses
039
      significant challenges in terms of model size, inference speed, and resource constraints. Knowledge
      Distillation (KD) (Hinton et al., 2015; Chen et al., 2017; Chang et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2022), trans-
040
      ferring knowledge from a well-performing teacher model to the target student model, has emerged
041
      as a promising technique to address these challenges. Current research primarily focuses on extract-
042   ing scenarios where teacher and student models have comparable sizes (Huang et al., 2022a; Cho
043   & Hariharan, 2019; Mirzadeh et al., 2020; Son et al., 2021; Cao et al., 2023). For example, DIST
044   (Huang et al., 2022a) replaces the traditional KL divergence with a correlation-based loss function to
045   better extract knowledge from a strong teacher model; MTPD (Cao et al., 2023) constructs a curricu-
046   lum of teacher models to progressively transfer knowledge from complex teacher models to student
047   model, effectively bridging the capacity gap and significantly enhancing the student’s performance
048   on object detection tasks. However, distilling knowledge into much smaller and faster models, which
049   receives more attention in practical scenarios, is seldom discussed in previous studies.
050   In this paper, we delve into the problem of distilling rich teacher knowledge into efficient small
051   models for object detection, considering the substantial model capacity gap. As shown in the Fig. 1,
052   we attempt several state-of-the-art KD algorithms (Yang et al., 2022a; Cao et al., 2022; Huang et al.,
053   2022a;b) to distill the GFL (Li et al., 2020) with GhostNet (Han et al., 2020), while they only
      achieve limited improvement. Among them, MasKD (Huang et al., 2022b) exhibits significantly

096   To address these issues, we propose DualPromptKD, an efficient knowledge distillation framework
097   specialized for tiny detectors. Alongside the conventional feature distillation for FPN features, Du-
098   alPromptKD incorporates two additional components: During the training process, we employ a set
099
      of external prompts to adaptively extract important representation characteristics from the teacher
      backbone using attention mechanisms, which are updated in a momentum-based manner. During
100
      the inference process, these extracted prompts are attached to the student backbone, enabling the
101
      student model to benefit from the comprehensive knowledge provided by the teacher. The attention
102   mechanism, acting as a soft association, helps mitigate the domain gap between the teacher and
103   student model representations. Additionally, we introduce lightweight internal prompts to guide
104   the LoRA (Hu et al., 2021; Aleem et al., 2024) as adapters using dynamically generated masks,
105   enhancing the feature extraction capability of the student model. The learnable prompts match stu-
106   dent features through the hard association of dot products and the generated soft mask can highlight
107   important areas while suppressing noisy areas. Internal prompts are only coupled with the student
      model, thereby preventing the drawback of blindly injecting knowledge from the teacher model.

119   2     R ELATED W ORKS
120
121   2.1   K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION FOR D ETECTION
122
123
      Knowledge Distillation (KD) (Romero et al., 2014; Huang & Wang, 2017; Liu et al., 2019; Wang
      et al., 2019; Zhang & Ma, 2020), is a kind of model compression and acceleration approach aiming
124
      at transferring knowledge from a teacher model to a student model. It was first proposed by Hinton
125
      (Hinton et al., 2015), using the output as soft labels to transfer the dark knowledge from a large
126   teacher network to a small student network for the classification task. Recently, some works have
127   successfully applied knowledge distillation to detectors (Cao et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2022a; Chang
128   et al., 2023; Lao et al., 2023). Chen et al. (2017) first calculated the distillation loss on the detector’s
129   neck and head. The key to distillation for object detection is where to distill, due to the extreme
130   imbalance between foreground and background. PKD (Cao et al., 2022) proposes imitating features
131   with Pearson Correlation Coefficient to focus on the relational information from the teacher and relax
132   constraints on the magnitude of the features. FGD (Yang et al., 2022a) proposes focal distillation
133   which forces the student to learn the teacher’s crucial parts and global distillation which compensates
134
      for missing global information. MGD (Yang et al., 2022b) first proposes masking out the feature
      maps in the knowledge distillation branch and using a generator to restore the teacher feature. And
135
      MasKD (Huang et al., 2022b) distills the valuable information in the features and ignores the noisy
136
      regions by learning to identify receptive regions that contribute to the task precision.
137
138
      2.2   P ROMPT L EARNING
139
140
      Prompt-based learning approaches have been extensively studied in NLP (Liu et al., 2023; Schick
141   & Schütze, 2020; Shin et al., 2020). The pioneer language model as GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020)
142   has shown its great few-shot or zero-shot potential across various tasks. The core of prompt-based
143   learning is to modify the input sample into a prompted version and embed the expected output
144   information as an unfilled slot inside the prompt. Prompting has also been applied to vision models
145   recently. CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) introduces the prompt-based learning approach into the image
146   recognition task by embedding the textual labels of the to-be-recognized objects into descriptive
147   texts, and the classification procedure can be transformed into a video-text matching problem. CoOp
148   (Zhou et al., 2022) utilizes learnable tokens as textual prompts and gains a promotion on few-shot
149
      image classification. There have also been initial approaches that attempt to prompt with images.
      CPT (Yao et al., 2024) converts visual grounding into a fill-in-the-blank problem by creating visual
150
      prompts with colored blocks and color-based textual prompts. Visual prompt tuning (Jia et al.,
151
      2022) proposes visual prompts specific to Vision Transformers (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020), using deep
152   prompt tuning (Li & Liang, 2021) by prepending a set of tunable parameters to each Transformer
153   encoder layer.

      3.2     P ROMPT D ISTILLATION
238
239   Although current feature-level distillations have achieved superior performance, traditional
240   paradigms are inherently limited. To illustrate, distilling features after the neck can facilitate the
241   propagation of supervised signals throughout the entire feature extraction module. However, the
242   gradient of KD signals is prone to disappear in shallow stages, making it difficult to optimize ef-
243   fectively. Furthermore, when the discrepancy in the number of parameters between the teacher and
244   student models is further amplified, constrained by the capacity of the model, relying solely on
245
      the student is insufficient to accurately predict the teacher’s output. To address these limitations,
      we propose the introduction of the Prompt Distillation technique, which utilizes additional inserted
246
      prompts as a storage medium for knowledge, effectively bridging the performance gap between the
247
      teacher and student models at minimal additional cost.

251   The teacher model exhibits superior feature extraction capabilities due to its intricate and precise
252   structure, which enables the effective enhancement of the information in the foreground region and
253   the suppression of noise in the background region. The utilisation of only the predicted features of
254   the teacher model as supervision is a suboptimal approach. It is anticipated that the general features
255   of the important regions extracted by the teacher model will be summarised and incorporated as part
256

      A.1   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
705
706   We train the student models with a batch size of 16 for 24 epochs (known as a 2× schedule). The
707   initial learning rate is set by 0.01 for one-stage detectors and 0.02 for two-stage detectors. We reduce
708   the learning rate by 0.1× at the 16th and 22nd epochs. We use SGD as the optimizer and set the
709   momentum and weight decay by 0.9 and 0.0001, respectively. All the experiments are conducted on
710   8 GPUs with mmdetection (Chen et al., 2019) and mmrazor (Contributors, 2021) on PyTorch.
711   In the Feature Distillation Module, our primary training strategy was inspired by PKD cao2022pkd.
712   For the hyperparameter loss weight, we set it to 100 for both GFL and RepPoint, and to 10 for both
713   RetinaNet and Faster R-CNN. In the External Prompt Distillation module, we employed prompts
714   of size 32×64 to convey knowledge, with a momentum coefficient set to 0.8. Within the Internal
715   Prompts Distillation, the dimensionality of the prompts must match the output feature dimensions
716   of each layer of the backbone. For GhostNet, these dimensions are 24, 40, 112, and 160; for Mo-
717
      bileNetV2, they are 24, 32, 96, and 1280; and for FasterNet, they are 40, 80, 160, and 320. The
      length was uniformly set to 8.
