                                                         A BSTRACT
037
038
039             Based on the success of large image diffusion models, multi-view diffusion mod-
040             els have demonstrated remarkable zero-shot capability in novel view synthesis
041             (NVS). However, the pioneering work Zero123 (Liu et al., 2023a) struggles to
042             maintain consistency across generated multiple views. While recent modifications
043             in model and training design have improved multi-view consistency, they often
044
                introduce new limitations, such as restricted fixed view generation or reliance on
                additional conditions. These constraints hinder the broader application of multi-
045
                view diffusion models in downstream tasks like 3D reconstruction. We identify
046
                the root cause of inconsistency as the excessive diversity inherent in generative
047             models utilized for the NVS task. To address this, we aim to utilize the stronger
048             supervise information to better alignment with ground truth images to constrain
049             the diversity, and propose Ctrl123, a closed-loop transcription-based multi-view
050             diffusion method that enforces alignment in the CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) patch
051             feature space. Extensive experiments demonstrate that Ctrl123 excels in arbitrary
052             novel view generation, significantly improving multi-view consistency compared
053             to existing methods.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
055
056
057   Recent advancements in novel view synthesis (NVS) have sparked considerable excitepment on 3D
058   generation (Poole et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023c; Liu et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024; Wu et al.,
059
      2024; Xu et al., 2024). The pioneering work of multi-view diffusion models, which achieve NVS,
      Zero123 (Liu et al., 2023a), utilizes 2D latent diffusion models to directly generate images of novel
060
      views. Although Zero123 (Liu et al., 2023a) demonstrates impressive zero-shot and open-world
061
      capabilities, it still encounters a multi-view inconsistency problem among the generated views, par-
062   ticularly on wild images, as shown in Figure 1(c). Subsequent studies (Shi et al., 2023a; Liu et al.,
063   2023b; Shi et al., 2023b; Long et al., 2023; Zheng & Vedaldi, 2024; Voleti et al., 2024; Yang et al.,
064   2024) attempt to address this inconsistency problem, but alter the original task settings and intro-
065   duce new limitations. Zero123++(Shi et al., 2023a) and SyncDreamer(Liu et al., 2023b) generate
066   images of multiple views simultaneously, but they are restricted to generating images of fixed views.
067   TOSS (Shi et al., 2023b) and Free3D (Zheng & Vedaldi, 2024) mitigate the inconsistency issue by
068   leveraging additional conditions (text descriptions and absolute camera extrinsic, respectively), but
069   the need of these additional conditions constrains the application scenarios of multi-view diffusion
070
      models. SV3D (Voleti et al., 2024) and Hi3D (Yang et al., 2024) seek to address the inconsistency
      problem using a video generation model, yet SV3D demonstrates poor performance when dealing
071
      with large relative elevation transformations, as illustrated in Figure 6, and Hi3D requires additional
072
      information about the camera’s absolute elevation and lacks the capability to generate novel views
073   with relative elevation transformations.
074
075   Although the modified task settings, like generating fixed-view multiple views, can enhance consis-
076
      tency, the original task setting of Zero123 (Liu et al., 2023a) enables multi-view diffusion models to
      generate arbitrary views, allowing them to be used in all downstream reconstruction models. Current
077
      reconstruction models (Liu et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024) use mul-
078
      tiple views generated by multi-view diffusion models as input, and different reconstruction models
079   employ different multi-view settings: while InstantMesh (Xu et al., 2024) inputs fixed multi-views
080   generated by Zero123++ (Shi et al., 2023a), Unique3D (Wu et al., 2024) employs four orthographic
081   views as input. Therefore, unlike most recent works (Shi et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2023b; Zheng &
082   Vedaldi, 2024; Voleti et al., 2024), our approach focuses on addressing the inconsistency problem
083   without altering the original task settings. We hypothesize that the inconsistency problem stems
084   from the excessive diversity of generated images. While this diversity is beneficial for text-to-image
085   generation where only one single image is desired, it undermines the multi-view consistency which
086   is crucial for NVS task. The excessive diversity among generated multiple views results in incon-
087
      sistency, leading to poor 3D reconstruction performance.
088   Learning to generate images identical to ground truth (GT) images is a simple yet effective objective
089   for controlling the excessive diversity. This objective does not encourage diversity, and we refer to
090   the learning process as fitting GT images. However, recent multi-view diffusion models typically
091   fine-tune pre-trained diffusion models with the original score matching (SM) training strategy, which
092   is designed to learn a distribution with inherent diversity, instead of fitting GT images. In fact, the
093
      traditional score matching (SM) training strategy has limited ability to fit GT images effectively.
      To support this claim, we train a new model, denoted as Zero123-small, with the same training
094
      strategy and model configuration as Zero123 (Liu et al., 2023a) on a small dataset containing only
095
      25 objects (see Section 4.2, Figure 1(b) and Table 1 for more details). Over-fitting on such a small
096   dataset should be an easy task, yet Zero123-small fails to do so, as shown in Figure 1(b). This
097   experiment highlights the poor alignment capability of the training strategy used in Zero123 (Liu
098   et al., 2023a), suggesting that its impressive NVS performance is primarily due to the large volume
099   of training data. Furthermore, simply increasing the amount of training data does not resolve the
100   poor alignment issue, as evidenced by the performance of Zero123-XL (Deitke et al., 2023a) in
101   Figure 1(a). To address the misalignment issue, an additional constraint is necessary: the final
102   generated images, after multiple denoising steps, should closely align with the GT images.
103   One straightforward approach to incorporate the additional constraint in latent diffusion models,
104   as utilized by contemporary multi-view diffusion models, is to introduce a loss function such as
105   Mean Square Error (MSE) in the VAE-encoded feature space between the generated images and the
106   GT images. However, our extensive experiments reveal that directly applying a loss in the VAE-
107   encoded feature space often leads to training collapse (see Section 4.5 for more details). Inspired
      by the recently proposed closed-loop transcription (CTRL) framework (Dai et al., 2022; Ma et al.,

121   periments, we demonstrate that Ctrl123 significantly improves consistency and leads to substantially
122   better NVS performance compared to current state-of-the-art methods. The main contributions of
123   this paper are:

144   Diffusion Models Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020) demonstrate remarkable capability in image
145   generation, especially text-to-image (Ramesh et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022; Nichol et al., 2021;
146   Rombach et al., 2022). Recent studies have explored distilling knowledge from pre-trained diffusion
147
      models for text-to-3D generation through optimizing a differentiable 3D representation with image
      priors (Poole et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023c;
148
      Huang et al., 2023). However, this line of work suffers from optimization efficiency, resulting in
149
      blurriness and the Janus problem in the generated 3D models. This arises due to the lack of 3D-
150   awareness in pre-trained text-to-image models.
151
152
153
      Multi-view Diffusion Models To achieve Novel view synthesis (NVS), Multi-view Diffusion
      Models are required to generate an object’s unobserved geometry and texture, which is a prereq-
154
      uisite capability for 3D generation. Recently, Zero123 (Liu et al., 2023a) proposes to perform
155
      zero-shot open-set NVS by fine-tuning a pre-trained text-to-image diffusion model on multi-view
156   renderings of diverse 3D data (Deitke et al., 2023b). However, Zero123 (Liu et al., 2023a) often
157   generates inconsistent multi-view images. Subsequent efforts (Liu et al., 2023b; Weng et al., 2023;
158   Voleti et al., 2024; Zheng & Vedaldi, 2024; Long et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024) aim to resolve
159   view inconsistency by facilitating information propagation across different views which naturally
160   require generating multiple novel views concurrently. While these models excel in generating more
161   consistent multi-view novel views, they alter the NVS task settings of Zero123 and face limitations
      in generalizing arbitrary camera poses.

      3     M ETHOD
183
184   Our goal is to learn a multi-view diffusion model that alleviates the multi-view inconsistency prob-
185
      lem and the excessive diversity by employing the idea of closed-loop transcription. To this end,
      we formulate the current state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods for single-image NVS as a broad open-
186
      loop (without looping generated images in the encoder) auto-encoder framework (Section 3.1 and
187
      Figure 2(a)). Then, we extend the framework, from open-loop to closed-loop (looping generated
188   images in the encoder), to optimize the latent diffusion model with the help of the closed-loop tran-
189   scription (CTRL) framework (Section 3.2 and Figure 2(b)). Finally, the choice of training strategy
190   is discussed in Section 3.2.

241   The Caveats of Current NVS Methods. While the showcased results in current works appear
242   impressive, we make a critical observation previously overlooked: when evaluated on the training
243   data, the generated images of novel views often lack alignment with the GT images, as exemplified in
244   Figure 1(a). This misalignment problem is rooted in the excessive diversity caused by the traditional
245   training strategy which separately trains the denoiser at different noise levels using solely score
246
      matching loss without any constraints on the generated images obtained through the whole denoising
      process. The above claim is demonstrated by the results of over-fitting experiments, as shown in
247
      Figure 1(b) and described in Section 4.2. The excessive diversity of the generated images is not
248
      beneficial for NVS and results in multi-view inconsistency problem. Therefore, in this work, we
249   explore ways to enforce alignment between the generated images and GT images to learn the suitable
250   diversity for the NVS task.

      We evaluate Ctrl123 on the task of NVS and show promising results of 3D reconstruction. 3D re-
318   construction is a challenging task that requires strong multi-view consistency. In Section 4.1, we
319   introduce the metrics, Angle Accuracy (AA) and Intersection over Union (IoU), used to addition-
320   ally measure rotation accuracy of the generated images. In Section 4.2, we provide results on a
321   small dataset which only obtains 25 objects to compare the alignment capability through a simple

365   The performance of multi-view diffusion models can be evaluated based on two key aspects: multi-
366
      view consistency and rotation accuracy, as illustrated in Figure 3. Current works (Shi et al., 2023a;
      Liu et al., 2023b; Shi et al., 2023b; Long et al., 2023; Zheng & Vedaldi, 2024; Voleti et al., 2024;
367
      Yang et al., 2024) assess model performance using traditional image generation metrics such as
368
      KID, FID, PSNR, and SSIM, which effectively measure multi-view consistency. However, these
369   traditional metrics fall short in evaluating rotation accuracy, particularly in the presence of slight
370   and reasonable artifacts. In our work, we introduce two new metrics—Angle Accuracy (AA) and
371   Intersection over Union (IoU)—to assess rotation accuracy at both fine-grained and coarse levels.
372   It is important to note that AA is not designed to disentangle the effects of artifacts but rather to
373   additionally focus on rotation accuracy. Therefore, high AA indicates strong multi-view consistency
374   and rotation accuracy. Furthermore, If the multi-view consistency is significantly poor, evaluating
375   rotation accuracy becomes meaningless.
376
377   Angle Accuracy (AA). AA is first introduced in (Zhou et al., 2019). In our work, we utilize
      MegaPose (Labbé et al., 2023), an excellent single-image pose estimator, to predict the camera pose


                                                                        7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      based on the generated images and the GT meshes. We then calculate the angular difference between
379   the predicted camera pose and the GT camera pose. We define accurate rotation as the situation that
380   the angular difference falls within a predefined threshold of x◦ , and calculate the percentage of
                                          ◦
381   accurate rotation, denoted as AAx . As shown in Appendix D, we set x to 5, 10, 15, and 20 degrees
382   and calculate the corresponding AA, all of which demonstrate the superior rotation accuracy of
383   Ctrl123. Additionally, as presented in Appendix D, we also provide the results of MegaPose (Labbé
384   et al., 2023) on generated images, which confirm that MegaPose can accurately predict camera poses
                                                                                                   ◦
385   even in the presence of slight artifacts. To account for reasonable artifacts, we select AA15 as the
386   final metric reported in the comparison tables (see more details in Appendix D about the credibility
387   of the selection of 15◦ as the threshold).
388
389   Intersection over Union (IoU). We also utilize the segmentation metric IoU (Garcia-Garcia et al.,
390   2017) to evaluate a more coarse aspect of rotation accuracy. DIS (Qin et al., 2022) is employed to
391   predict the masks of both the generated images and the GT images, which are then used to calculate
392   the IoU. The IoU is defined as the ratio of the overlap between the predicted masks and GT masks
393
      to their union. We define accurate rotation as the condition that the IoU falls within a predefined
      threshold of x, and calculate the percentage of accurate rotations, denoted as IoUx .

401   To evaluate the alignment capability with GT images, we collect a small dataset from Objaverse
402   (Deitke et al., 2023b), consisting of 25 objects, each with 12 randomly sampled views. Our goal was
403   to assess the alignment capability of Zero123’s (Liu et al., 2023a) training strategy and Ctrl123’s
404
      training strategy by evaluating their over-fitting performance on this small training dataset. For
      clarity, we refer to them in this experiment as Zero123-small and Ctrl123-small. Regarding the
405
      experiment details, we train Zero123-small from stable diffusion for 20000 steps with a batch size
406
      of 72 (equivalent to 400 epochs). Then, starting from the checkpoint of Zero123-small at 10000
407   iterations, we trained Ctrl123-small for 500 closed-loop iterations (with a batch size of 12) followed
408   by 4500 score-matching iterations (with a batch size of 72) per round.
409
410
      As shown in Figure 1(b), Zero123-small fails to achieve this simple task of over-fitting, whereas
      Ctrl123-small succeeds. In addition, we also increased the training steps and batch size of Zero123-
411
      small, but the results remain unchanged. As demonstrated in Table 1, Ctrl123-small effectively ad-
412
      dresses this issue, significantly outperforming Zero123-small both qualitatively and quantitatively
413   (more quantitative results can be found in Figure 8). The PSNR improves by 7.2 points, which is
414   nearly a 2× enhancement over the baseline 2 . On a fine-grained level, the Average Angle (AA)
415   increases from 22.62% to 57.78%. On a coarse level, the Intersection over Union (IoU) increases
416   from 30.93% to 73.44%. Therefore, the results of this experiment demonstrate the poor align-
417   ment performance of Zero123’s (Liu et al., 2023a) training strategy and confirm that the training
418   strategy of Ctrl123 successfully resolves this issue. For further supporting the better alignment
419   ability of Ctrl123, we also evaluate the alignment performance when training Ctrl123 on the large
420   dataset (Deitke et al., 2023b). As shown in Table 3, we provide the quantitative results on sampled
421
      views of randomly selected 100 objects in the large trainset (Deitke et al., 2023b), which also proves
      the better alignment capability of Ctrl123.

501   4.5   A BLATION S TUDY
502
503   All ablation studies are conducted through 1 round training with the same settings (batch size, learn-
504   ing rate, training steps and so on).
505
506   Mean-square error on different space. Applying MSE in the VAE-encoded feature space is a
507
      straightforward approach to address the misalignment issue in latent diffusion models. As shown in
      the ablation study presented in Appendix E, Figure 12 and Table 8, under the same training settings,
508
      we optimize our model only with MSE in pixel space and three different feature spaces: the VAE-
509
      encoded feature space, the CLIP class feature space, and the CLIP patch feature space. Our findings
510   reveal that directly applying MSE in the VAE-encoded feature space and pixel space often results
511   in training collapse and the looping strategy is necessary. Furthermore, due to the richer low-level
512   detail in CLIP patch features compared to CLIP class features, models trained with MSE in the CLIP
513   patch feature space demonstrate superior NVS performance. In addition, importantly, the MSE on
514   different space is only conducted on generated images instead of noised images in standard training
515   procedure, because MSE on noised images is the same as MSE on predicting noise.
516
517   Different Training Strategy. The “Simultaneous” training strategy, as mentioned in Section 3.2,
518   involves adding Equation 9 and the score-matching loss of Zero123 (Liu et al., 2023a) at the same
519   time. Because of large difference of loss values and fit race between them, it is time-consuming and
520
      energy-inefficient to grid search a sweet spot of hyper-parameters to make “Simultaneous” training
      strategy work. Even if we try different weights of the two loss and learning rates and report the best
521
      of them, the results in Appendix E and Table 7 show the “Alternative” strategy is better.
522
523
524
      Denoise Scheduler. The number of denoising steps t∞ used in the function g to generate X̂tg
525   is crucial for balancing the quality of X̂tg against memory consumption. To investigate this, we
526
      conducted experiments with different numbers of denoising steps (1, 10, 30, 50) to evaluate their
      impact on NVS performance and rotation accuracy (see more details in Appendix E and Table 6).
527
      Ultimately, we select 50 denoising steps during CL training.

530   5     C ONCLUSION
531
532   In this paper, we introduce Ctrl123, a closed-loop transcription-based multi-view diffusion method
533   that significantly alleviates the problem of multi-view inconsistency under the task of generating
534   arbitrary novel views. To quantitatively measure the improved NVS performance of Ctrl123, we in-
535
      troduce metrics AA and IoU. Through extensive experiments, we show that the closed-loop Ctrl123
      significantly improves consistency performance, and leads to excellent 3D reconstruction results
536
      compared to the current SOTA methods. Note that in (Ma et al., 2022; Dai et al., 2022) the closed-
537
      loop framework is proposed as a general framework for ensuring consistency. Hence we believe such
538   a closed-loop framework is a simple yet effective way to ensure consistency in content generation.
539   We will leave the application of Ctrl123 on the downstream tasks to future investigation.

      A     I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS OF C TRL 123
703
704
      We train Ctrl123 on Objaverse (Deitke et al., 2023b) dataset for 1 round of alternative training. We
705   initialize Ctrl123 with pre-trained weights of Zero123 (Liu et al., 2023a) and train on 8 A100 GPUs
706   with 80GB memory. For each alternative training round, we conduct 500 steps of closed-loop (CL)
707   training with a total batch size of 320 and a learning rate of 10−5 , followed by 5000 steps of score-
708   matching (SM) training with a total batch size of 1536 and a learning rate of 10−4 , each taking
709   around 2 days. We use gradient accumulation to increase the training batch size, 20 for CL training
710   and 1 for SM training respectively. During model training, we use the Adam optimizer (Kingma &
711   Ba, 2014) with β1 = 0.9 and β2 = 0.99. In all the experiments, we train our model with the 16-bit
712   floating point (fp16) format for efficiency. In addition, during the CL training, we use DDIM (Song
713
      et al., 2020) scheduler and set t∞ as 50.
714   Even if the training of Ctrl123 costs a large amount of memory and we need to set large accumula-
715   tion gradient step to enlarge batch size, the convergence rate of CL training is fast. Therefore, the
716   additional cost time of Ctrl123 is not a key limited issue.
