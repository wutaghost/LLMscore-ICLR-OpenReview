009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Detecting Out-of-Distribution (OOD) inputs is crucial for improving the reliabil-
012            ity of deep neural networks in the real-world deployment. In this paper, inspired
013            by the inherent distribution shift between ID and OOD data, we propose a novel
014            method that leverages optimal transport to measure the distribution discrepancy
015            between test inputs and ID prototypes. The resulting transport costs are used to
016
               quantify the individual contribution of each test input to the overall discrepancy,
               serving as a desirable measure for OOD detection. To address the issue that solely
017
               relying on the transport costs to ID prototypes is inadequate for identifying OOD
018
               inputs closer to ID data, we generate virtual outliers to approximate the OOD re-
019            gion via linear extrapolation. By combining the transport costs to ID prototypes
020            with the costs to virtual outliers, the detection of OOD data near ID data is em-
021            phasized, thereby enhancing the distinction between ID and OOD inputs. Experi-
022            ments demonstrate the superiority of our method over state-of-the-art methods.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Deep neural networks (DNNs) deployed in real-world scenarios often encounter out-of-distribution
      (OOD) inputs, such as inputs not belonging to one of the DNN‚Äôs known classes. Ideally, reliable
028
      DNNs should be aware of what they do not know. However, they typically make overconfident
029
      predictions on OOD data (Nalisnick et al., 2018). This notorious behavior undermines the cred-
030   ibility of DNNs and could pose risks to involved users, particularly in safety-critical applications
031   like autonomous driving (Filos et al., 2020) and biometric authentication (Wang & Deng, 2021).
032   This gives rise to the importance of OOD detection, which identifies whether an input is OOD and
033   enables conservative rejection or transferring decision-making to humans for better handling.
034
      The representations of ID data within the same class tend to be gathered together after the training
035
      process, as shown in Figure 1(a). In contrast, the representations of OOD data are relatively far away
036   from ID data, as they are not involved in the training process. In other words, the distributions of ID
037   and OOD representations in the latent space exhibit a distinct separation. Therefore, we can expect
038   that the distribution discrepancy between the representations of test inputs (i.e., a mixture of ID and
039   OOD data) and pure ID data is primarily caused by the presence of OOD data. Such a distribution
040   discrepancy motivates us to differentiate OOD data from test inputs by quantifying the individual
041   contribution of each test input to the overall distribution discrepancy.
042   In this way, a critical question arises: how to measure the distribution discrepancy between test
043   inputs and ID data, while quantifying the contribution of each test input? To this end, we utilize
044   optimal transport (OT), a principled approach with rich geometric awareness for measuring the dis-
045   crepancy between distributions. Concretely, OT aims to minimize the total transport cost between
046   two distributions to measure the distribution discrepancy based on a predefined cost function (typ-
047   ically the geometric distance between samples). The smaller the total cost is, the closer the two
048   distributions are. Since the total cost comprises the sum of transport costs between sample pairs, OT
049   facilitates a fine-grained assessment of individual sample contributions to the overall discrepancy,
050
      making it particularly well-suited for OOD detection. Furthermore, the transport cost captures the
      geometric differences between ID and OOD representations in the latent space, providing a geomet-
051
      rically meaningful interpretation.
052
053   Based on the above intuition, in this paper, we propose a novel OOD detection method called POT
      that utilizes the Prototype-based Optimal Transport. Concretely, we first construct ID prototypes

097   2.1   OOD D ETECTION
098
099   OOD detection has attracted growing research attention in recent years. Existing approaches can
100   generally be categorized into two major lines:
101
      (1) One line of work utilizes the outputs from pretrained models to design scoring functions for
102   differentiating OOD samples. These post-hoc methods can be further divided into three subcate-
103   gories. 1) The confidence-based methods (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2017; Sun et al., 2021; Song et al.,
104   2022; Hendrycks et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022b; Liu et al., 2023) adjusts model outputs to ob-
105   tain the desired confidence, including maximum softmax probability (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2017),
106   energy (Liu et al., 2020), and generalized entropy (Liu et al., 2023). 2) The density-based meth-
107   ods (Hendrycks et al., 2022; Sun & Li, 2022; Zhang et al., 2023c; Liu et al., 2024) identifies certain
      properties or patterns of ID data, such as neuron coverage (Liu et al., 2024), by learning the corre-


                                                             2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      sponding density functions, and the OOD samples that deviate from these properties or patterns tend
109   to reside in low-density regions. 3) The distance-based methods (Lee et al., 2018; Ren et al., 2021;
110   Sehwag et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2022) adopts distance metrics, e.g., Mahalanobis distance, between
111   test input and ID samples or centroids differentiate OOD samples, under the assumption that OOD
112   data lie relatively far away from ID data. Different from these works, we introduce a novel per-
113   spective for OOD detection on measuring distribution discrepancy. The most closely related work
114   is dual divergence estimation (DDE) (Garg et al., 2023), which estimates the dual KL-Divergence
115   between the test samples and ID samples. However, while DDE estimates divergence in a dual space
116   optimized by DNNs and relies heavily on the quality of transformed sample representations, POT
117
      enables direct measurement of distribution discrepancy in the latent space.
118   (2) Another line of work focuses on altering models with training-time regularization to amplify the
119   differences between OOD and ID samples (Hendrycks et al., 2019a; Chen et al., 2021; Ming et al.,
120   2022; Zhang et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2023). For example, by incorporating a
121   suitable loss, models are encouraged to produce predictions with uniform distributions (Hendrycks
122   et al., 2019a) or higher energy (Liu et al., 2020) for outlier data. Building on this, some approaches
123
      investigate refining or synthesizing outliers to improve the performance of models. For instance,
      Ming et al. (2022) utilizes a posterior sampling-based technique to select the most informative OOD
124
      samples from the large outlier set, while Wang et al. (2023) implicitly expands the auxiliary out-
125
      liers by perturbing model parameters. However, the computational overhead of retraining can be
126   prohibitive, especially when the parameter scale is large. Additionally, modifying models may also
127   have side effects of degrading model performance on the original task. In contrast, this paper focus
128   on post-hoc methods, which are easy to implement and generally applicable across different models.
129   Such properties are highly practical for adopting OOD detection methods in real-world applications.
130
131   2.2   O PTIMAL T RANSPORT
132
133   As a mathematical tool for comparing distributions, optimal transport (OT) has been successfully
134   employed in diverse machine learning tasks, including domain adaptation (Courty et al., 2016; Tur-
135   risi et al., 2022), generative adversarial training (Arjovsky et al., 2017), object detection (Ge et al.,
136   2021), and partial-label learning (Wang et al., 2022a). The most related one to our work is (Lu et al.,
137
      2023), which also applies OT for the OOD detection problem. By assuming both an unlabeled and a
      labeled training set, Lu et al. (2023) uses OT to guide the clustering of samples for label assignment
138
      to the unlabeled samples, thereby augmenting the training data and facilitating the model retraining
139
      for OOD detection. In contrast, our work operates in a post-hoc manner without the requirement of
140   extra training data or model retraining.

228   Data Augmentation via Linear Extrapolation. As described above, the transport cost can serve
229   as a measure for OOD detection. However, relying solely on the cost to ID prototypes is insufficient
230   for discerning OOD data with smaller distribution shifts from ID data. This is because such OOD
231   data are located closer to ID data in the latent space and tend to incur lower transport costs to the
232   ID prototypes. Meanwhile, the mass conservation constraint inherent in OT may exacerbate this
233   issue by enforcing transportation between OOD data and prototypes when ID inputs are sparse. To
234   address this issue, we propose to generate virtual outliers to approximate the OOD region using
235   representation linear representation extrapolation. By integrating the transport costs from test inputs
236
      to both virtual outliers and ID prototypes, we introduce a contrastive transport cost, which enhances

      What if training data is unavailable. Although existing post-hoc methods can be applied to pre-
      trained models without cumbersome retraining, many require access to at least a portion of training
424
      data (Liu et al., 2023). Considering some scenarios where training data is unavailable, such as
425
      commercial data involving privacy, some approaches further explore OOD detection without the
426   requirement for training data such as ASH and GEN. To make POT applicable to such scenarios,
427   we draw inspiration from the work (Tanwisuth et al., 2021) and construct the class prototypes with
428   the neural network weights W ‚àà Rd√óC of the classification layer f . Each column of the weight
429   matrix W corresponds to a d-dimensional class prototype. The underlying idea is that the process
430   of learning class prototypes with learnable parameters in the latent space, is closely similar to the
431   training process of the linear classification layer. In other words, obtaining prototypes in this way
      does not require any training data and additional parameters.

527   5    C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this paper, we tackle the problem of OOD detection from a new perspective of measuring distri-
530   bution discrepancy and quantifying the individual contribution of each test input. To this end, we
531   propose a novel method named POT, which utilizes the prototype-based OT to assess the discrep-
532   ancy between test inputs and ID prototypes and use the obtaining transport costs for OOD detection.
533   By generating virtual outliers to approximate the OOD region, we combine the transport costs to
534   ID prototypes with the costs to virtual outliers, resulting in a more effective contrastive transport
535   cost for identifying OOD inputs. Experimental results demonstrate that POT achieves better perfor-
536
      mance than 21 current methods. Moreover, we show that POT is pluggable with existing training
      methods for OOD detection and is applicable to the scenarios where the training data is unavailable,
537
      highlighting its generic nature and high practicality.
538
539   Limitations. Beyond extrapolation, other data augmentation techniques, such as Mixup (Zhang
      et al., 2018) and negative data augmentation (Sinha et al., 2021), are also employed in OOD detection


                                                               10
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


540
      methods (Zhang et al., 2023a; Yang et al., 2023). So how to generate outliers that best fits our
541   approach, along with the underlying theoretical analysis can be further explored. Additionally, given
542   the generic nature of our method, combining it with other OOD detection methods is one of the
543   potential directions for improvement.
