011                                                   A BSTRACT
012
013                Short-video platforms show an increasing impact on people’s daily lives nowadays,
014                with billions of active users spending plenty of time each day. The interactions
015                between users and online platforms give rise to many scientific problems across
016
                   computational social science and artificial intelligence. However, despite the rapid
                   development of short-video platforms, currently there are serious shortcomings
017
                   in existing relevant datasets on three aspects: inadequate user-video feedback,
018
                   limited user attributes and lack of video content. To address these problems, we
019                provide a large-scale dataset with rich user behavior, attributes and video content
020                from a real mobile short-video platform. This dataset covers 10,000 voluntary
021                users and 153,561 videos, and we conduct three-fold technical validations of the
022                dataset. First, we verify the richness of the behavior data including interaction
023                frequency and feedback distribution. Second, we validate the wide coverage of
024                user-side and video-side attribute data. Third, we confirm the representing ability
025                of the content features. We believe the dataset could support the broad research
026                community, including but not limited to user modeling, social science, human
027
                   behavior understanding, etc. Our dataset is available at this anonymous link:
                   http://101.6.70.16:8080/1 .

      1       I NTRODUCTION
031
032
      Short-video platforms such as Tiktok are redefining how users access information online. It is reported
033   by Demand Sage2 that Tiktok has attracted 1 billion active users who spend 1.5 hours each day on
034   the platform on average. With the widely deployed algorithms of personalized recommendation, the
035   platform can infer user preferences from users’ behavioral logs and users’ demographics (such as
036   country, occupation, etc.), which helps alleviate information overload. However, the intelligence
037   algorithm has also brought critical social concerns including echo chamber (Ge et al., 2020; Mosleh
038   et al., 2021), filter bubble (Nguyen et al., 2014; Zhou et al., 2010), user addiction (Montag et al.,
039   2018; Brand, 2022), etc. For example, it has been demonstrated that recommendation algorithms
040   might lean to recommend popular items (Abdollahpouri & Mansoury, 2020) and this algorithmic bias
041
      could be amplified over time when users interact with recommender systems. The bias, in turn, sets
      constraints to the preference learning of algorithms, leading to the feedback loop (Mansoury et al.,
042
      2020), which further causes these concerns.
043
044   However, despite the fast growth of short-video platforms, there is no publicly-available dataset that
045   well support the research of improving user modeling and alleviating the negative impact caused by
046   AI algorithms. The existing public datasets of short-video platforms are released by researchers and
047
      engineers of recommendation algorithms, such as two of the largest short-video platforms, Kuaishou
      and WeChat Channel. However, they only focus on user feedback while are quite limited in supporting
048
      the research areas beyond recommendation algorithms. In specific, MicroVideo-1.7M (Chen et al.,
049
      2018) records millions of interactions between users and videos with 10,986 users and 1,704,880 for
050   micro-video recommendation tasks. KuaiRec (Gao et al., 2022) is a fully observed micro-video dataset
051   with additional user and video attributes collected from Kuaishou platform. REASONER (Chen

      • Lack of video content. The video content is the most informative video-side data, which is

071
      • Shortage of user attributes. User attributes i.e., demographics are the basis of the research
072
        of understanding and further addressing the social-concerned issues in short-video platforms
073     such as user addiction, however, these data are not sufficient in most existing datasets such as
074     MicroVideo-1.7M (Chen et al., 2018), Tenrec (Yuan et al., 2022) and MicroLens (Ni et al., 2023).
075
076   In this paper, we provide a large-scale short-video dataset from 10,000 volunteers on one of the largest
077   short-video platforms. The data collection procedure strictly follows privacy and ethical regulations
078   (detailed in Section 2).
079   In terms of data scale, our dataset covers 1,019,568 interactions between 10,000 users and 153,561
080   videos, which is larger than existing released datasets, e.g., Kuairec (Gao et al., 2022) and REA-
081   SONER (Chen et al., 2023). Besides, compared with datasets used in real industrial scenarios, our
082   dataset has a similar scale to the datasets used to conduct offline experiments and analysis, such as the
083   dataset of WeChat Channel 3 (7,210,290 interactions of 20,000 users with 96,428 short videos) and
084   Tiktok 4 (1,047,358 interactions of 16,538 users with 366,017 short videos). Therefore, our dataset
085   can satisfy the requirements of both academic and industrial scenarios.
086   In terms of data coverage, our dataset has wider coverage than existing public datasets in three
087   aspects: user behavior, user/video attributes and video content. The user behavior data includes
088   user’s historical watching records (user ID, video ID, interaction timestamp), six kinds of explicit
089   feedback conveying obvious preference (including like, follow, forward, collect, comment and hate)
090   and implicit feedback indicated by watching time which indirectly reflects user satisfaction. As for
091   attribute data, we collect rich side information of users and videos in which most are overlooked by
092
      previous datasets. User-side attributes contain three categories: demographic attributes (e.g. age,
      gender), geographical attributes (e.g. city, community type) and some other attributes (e.g. phone
093
      price). Video-side attributes cover three-level content categories, author information (e.g. author ID,
094
      number of fans), title text and tag. By comparison, previous datasets (Chen et al., 2018; Li et al., 2019)
095   only cover a subset, e.g. age, gender and single-level categories. In terms of content data, our dataset
096   is the first to provide the raw video files which are always unavailable in existing datasets but essential
097   for describing and understanding video content. Moreover, we provide extracted visual features and
098   ASR results of videos for convenient use. We further conduct the analysis of the basic characteristics
099   of behavior and attribute data, verifying the comprehensiveness and distribution evenness of different
100   data fields. We also validate the quality of the content data through an embedding visualization
101   method and observe obvious clusters between different video categories. Finally, we discuss how our
102   dataset benefits the broad research community from three aspects: user modeling, social science, and
103
      human behavior understanding. We summarize our contributions as follows:
104
      • We collect a large-scale dataset from a real mobile short-video platform, covering rich user behavior,
105     attributes and video content, which are scarce in existing datasets.

138   2     DATA D ESCRIPTION
139
140   Our dataset possesses much richer information than existing datasets (Chen et al., 2018; Gao et al.,
141   2022; Li et al., 2019) on three aspects. First, the dataset contains large-scale interaction records
142   and multi-type user behavior helpful for user interest modeling and behavior analysis. Second, the
143   dataset contains various user attribute data which has never been covered by existing datasets. Third,
144   the video content is available in the dataset, which could support further information extraction and
145
      analysis. An overview of the dataset is shown in Figure 2. The structure and detailed data description
      are presented in the supplementary material.

      2.1   B EHAVIOR DATA
149
      We collected six-month historical user interactions of the 10,000 volunteers (the data of users under
150   20 years old has been removed from the actual dataset) under their consent, while in the paper we
151   focus on analyzing the first week’s data for a quick release. There are 153,561 involved videos and
152   1,019,568 interaction records. For each interaction record, we record the basic information including
153   anonymous user/video ID and timestamp of the interaction. More importantly, we have collected rich
154   user behavior which could convey diverse feedback signals of users. On the platform we use, users
155   can watch a variety of short-form videos, which are organized by recommending streaming where
156   each time the user can see only one video, which is known as the single-column user interface. In
157   this scenario, the video will automatically play, during which users can choose to stop viewing the
158
      current video by exiting this interface or switching to the next video. Overall there are mainly two
      types of feedback in this scenario: implicit feedback and explicit feedback.
159
160   Implicit feedback refers to the user’s watching behavior, more specifically, the watching time, which
161   is a vital signal to yield user satisfaction (Zheng et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2021). Traditional datasets
      usually focus on discrete user feedback, such as ratings (Koren, 2008; Koren et al., 2009) and click

184   users will watch the current video recurrently until they switch to the next video as Figure 1 shows,

189   also provides an "effective view" label by comparing watching time and a threshold (set as 3 seconds
190   in our dataset).
191   Explicit feedback, provided by users actively, usually conveys clear preference or disgust. For
192   example, if the user collects or forwards a video, we could infer that they are interested in the video
193   content with high confidence. Conversely, if a user conveys hate to the video, it could be assumed
194   reasonably that they show no interest in it. The behavior data records 6 kinds of explicit feedback
195   including like, comment, follow, forward, collect and hate.

197   2.2   ATTRIBUTE DATA
198
199   Compared with existing datasets, the dataset contains richer attribute information about the users and
200
      videos. Specifically, there are 9 video-side attributes and 6 user-side attributes in the dataset, shown
      in the supplementary material.
201
202   For the video side, we have fully exploited the key attributes of micro-videos including video category,
203   author information, duration, video text and video tag.
204
205
      • Video category. According to the regulation of the short-video platform, video authors are required
        to provide the title and tag for uploaded videos for better management and recommendation. We
206
        establish the hierarchical categories by integrating the semantics of the title and tag, which can
207
        cover most of the collected videos. As for the videos hard to categorize by the title and tag, we
208     train a classification model based on labeled data and take the prediction as the categories of these
209     videos.
210     The category of videos is divided into 3 levels hierarchically. For each video, the primary, secondary,
211     and tertiary category is represented by "Category I", "Category II" and "Category III", respectively.
212     For example, for a video recording a hockey game, "Category I" is "sports", "Category II" is
213     "ball game" and "Category III" is "hockey". Statistically there are 37 kinds of "Category I", 281
214     kinds of "Category II" and 382 kinds of "Category III". It is worth mentioning that the number
215     of Category II and Category III are similar since some Category II could be further divided into
        several Category III while some others could not. The hierarchical category organization possesses

216
        richer semantic information compared with existing datasets, which could support multi-level
217     video content understanding (Diba et al., 2020). In fact, both coarse-grained and fine-grained
218     categories are indispensable. For example, in video advertising, the coarse-grained category is
219     more appropriate to enlarge the audience group. While in tasks such as video retrieval, fine-grained
220     categories are necessary to ensure retrieval accuracy. Besides, the relation between different
221     categories is valuable, for example, if a user likes videos about delicious food, the platform could
222     reasonably recommend videos about cooking, improving the diversity of the recommendation list.
223   • Author information. The dataset totally covers 81,870 video authors in all. We collect the author
224     information including author ID and the number of fans, which is vital to the video-side modeling
225     and extends the social relation information on the platform. On a User-Generated Content (UGC)
226     platform like short-video platforms, author information (e.g. the number of fans) is useful for
227     social science research, such as studying social capital of short-video authors including social
228     networks and the recognition on the platform (Zhuge, 2018). This information is also indispensable
229
        in research of popularity bias (Tang et al., 2022) and celebrity effect(Luo et al., 2021).
230   • Duration. The dataset covers videos with diverse duration, varying from less than 30 seconds to
231     larger than 5 minutes and the majority are relatively short (less than one minute). In the research
232     of user addiction and engagement, it’s necessary to take the duration of watched videos into
233
        consideration. For example, some research (Yang et al., 2021) has studied watching behaviors on
        the short-video platform and found videos with less duration tend to be more addictive.
234
235   • Video text. The text of the video title is available in our dataset while often overlooked in
236     existing datasets. It summarizes the semantic content of videos and enhances video content
237
        understanding (Zolfaghari et al., 2018; Du et al., 2018). There are 140,341 unique titles in all
        videos.
238
239   • Video tag. Video tag is added manually by authors shown in each video for better video description
240     and understanding, such as "delicious food recommendation" and "travel tips". Note that video
241
        tags are collected from the platform while categories are processed and classified through our
        human effort. In practice, it has been utilized to summarize video content and support video
242
        understanding (Shamma et al., 2007; Mazeika et al., 2022). There are a total of 79,705 unique tags
243
        in all collected videos.
244
245   For the user side, the dataset records comprehensive user attributes including demographical, and
246   geographical characteristics, which are not adequate in existing datasets. This part of data is optionally
247   provided by volunteers and not involved with privacy issues since the user ID for each volunteer has
248   been hashed for privacy protection.
249
250   • Demographic attributes. The demographic attributes include gender and age of users, which are
251
        basic for the research of social-concerned issues in short-video platforms like user addiction (Mon-
        tag et al., 2018; Brand, 2022). For example, in the research on the fairness of recommender
252
        systems (Geyik et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2022c), gender and age are vital sensitive attributes for the
253
        selection of protected user groups. In the data analysis of the paper, we do not exclude the minor
254     users but their data has been removed in the actual dataset.
255
      • Geographical attributes. The geographical attributes here contain the city each user lives in, the
256
        city’s level (first-tier, second-tier, third-tier, fourth-tier, fifth-tier) and the community type (country,
257
        urban area and town). The geographical characteristics could complement the user-side profile and
258     support the analysis of regional user differences.
259
      • Other attributes. Our dataset records the price of user devices, which is seldom covered by
260
        previous datasets (Chen et al., 2018; Gao et al., 2022). Considering privacy protection, we only
261
        collect the phone’s model name with the permission of volunteers and retrieve the corresponding
262     phone price.

      3     T ECHNICAL VALIDATION
290
291
      In order to ensure the technical validity of the collected data, we have conducted validation from four
292   aspects. First, we establish a benchmark of recommendation algorithms using our dataset. Second,
293   we verify the richness and diversity of the behavior data including interaction frequency distribution
294   and feedback distribution. Third, we validate the coverage of different fields for both user-side and
295   video-side attribute data. Finally, we carry out the quality validation of the content data, especially the
296   preprocessed visual features of videos through the dimension reduction and visualization procedure.
297
298   3.1     B ENCHMARKING RECOMMENDATION ALGORITHMS
299
300   We provide experimental results of 8 recommendation algorithms in Table 1, to validate the practical
301   usage of our dataset on user modeling. The tested methods can be categorized into general and
302
      multimodal recommendation methods. The general recommendation method includes BPR (Rendle
      et al., 2009), LightGCN (He et al., 2020) and LayerGCN (Zhou et al., 2023b). The multimodal
303
      recommendation method includes VBPR (He & McAuley, 2016), MMGCN (Wei et al., 2019),
304
      GRCN (Wei et al., 2020), BM3 (Zhou et al., 2023c) and LGMRec (Guo et al., 2024). We divide
305   the train, validation and test set with the ratio 8:1:1, following the process pipeline introduced
306   in (Zhou et al., 2023a). From the result, BM3 achieves the best performance in the current compared
307   methods by effectively utilizing multimodal information, which is similar to the observations on other
308   multimodal recommendation benchmarks (Zhou et al., 2023a). This demonstrates the practical usage
309   of our dataset for benchmarking recommendation algorithms.

      We discuss the intended research directions that our dataset could support as follows:


                                                             9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      • User modeling and personalization. Nowadays making accurate user modeling and personalized
487     recommendations has become one of the main focuses of academia and industry (Wu et al., 2022a;b;
488     Volkovs et al., 2017). On the short-video platform, introducing rich multimodal information from
489     videos can help achieve better user modeling and personalization. However, these data are not
490     well supported by existing datasets due to the shortage of some necessary information such as
491     video content. By comparison, our dataset covers broader content information of users and videos
492     compared with existing datasets. For example, the available raw video files could support more
493     fine-grained multi-modal feature extraction and enhance content-based recommendation.
494   • Fairness of AI algorithms. It has long been blamed that artificial intelligence algorithms might
495     lead to and amplify inequity and unfairness (Kusner & Loftus, 2020; Espín-Noboa et al., 2022). One
496     important concern with short-video recommender systems is the potential for bias or discrimination
497     towards different users or video groups. For the user side, recommender systems might exhibit
498     unintentional discrimination across groups with different genders or ages. For the video side, if
499
        the recommender system favors certain categories of content or authors, it might lead to unequal
        exposure for different groups of videos. Our dataset covers necessary user-side (e.g. gender, age,
500
        living city) and video-side information (e.g. hierarchical categories and raw video content) for
501
        multi-aspect group division, which could support the research on the fairness issue.
502
503
      • Filter bubble on micro-video platforms. The filter bubble phenomenon has attracted increasing
        attention in recent years, which indicates algorithms used by online platforms could cause a decrease
504
        in information diversity (Zhou et al., 2010; Bozdag & Van Den Hoven, 2015). The recommendation
505
        algorithms tend to make recommendations based on users’ historical preferences, thus reinforcing
506     their existing interests and limiting exposure to diverse content, which harms user experience and
507     platform development. The filter bubble refers to the situation where recommendation algorithms
508     overly tailor content to a user’s preference, resulting in limited content exposure for the user on the
509     platform. To quantify content coverage, our dataset includes a hierarchical content category system.
510     Coverage at a category level c is defined as Nseen    (u,c)
                                                          Nall (c) , where Nseen (u, c) represents the number
511     of level-c categories a user u has interacted with over a period, and Nall (c) is the total number of
512     level-c categories available. A user’s filter bubble extent can then be determined by comparing
513     this metric against a threshold, such as the average across all users. Subsequent analyses could
514     investigate how user behaviors, such as viewing time and content preferences, influence the severity
515     of filter bubbles across different category levels. Additionally, tracking the temporal evolution of
516     filter bubbles at each level could provide insights into their dynamics and persistence over time.
517   • Polarization on online platforms. The polarization phenomenon on social networking platforms
518     might occur when users are exposed to limited content that reinforces their existing beliefs (Sikder
519     et al., 2020; Santos et al., 2021). One potential way to analyze the polarization phenomenon is to
520     identify clusters of users who are exposed to similar content and show similar attitudes towards
521     the same video. Our dataset covers various kinds of feedback representing their clear beliefs and
522     interests, which could support the identification and analysis of the polarization phenomenon on
523
        micro-video platforms.
524   • User addiction on micro-video platforms. User addiction on the Internet is a growing concern
525     nowadays (Montag et al., 2018; Brand, 2022). Analysis of user addiction relies on the tracking
526     of time spent on the platform and the consumed content. Our dataset covers large-scale records
527
        of users’ watching history on the micro-video platform, together with an accurate timestamp and
        content information, which can be used to conduct analysis on the user addiction problem.

532   In this paper, we introduce a large-scale dataset with rich user behavior, attributes and video content

      A.1    DATA C OLLECTION
759
760   In this section, we describe how we collect the datasets, addressing the shortcomings of the existing
761   publicly-available datasets. Given the limitation of existing datasets, we try to build a dataset with
762   behaviors, attributes, and content. Since the user-side data is highly sensitive to user privacy, we hire
763   volunteers to obtain their interaction with videos, along with attributes. It’s worth noting that our data
764   is collected from the consenting volunteers. Considering that the volunteer group is large enough
765   and covers a wide range of user attributes, the representativeness of the experimental group can be
766
      guaranteed. Specifically, we asked the volunteers to install a proxy agent on their mobile devices and
      sign up for a new account on one of the largest short-video platforms. The proxy agent can collect
767
      the users’ behaviors on the short-video app, and all volunteers fully acknowledge how the proxy
768
      agent works. Volunteers are asked to keep the agent installed for one week, and we are then able
769   to collect enough behavioral logs. During the data collection process, the volunteers are requested
770   to freely interact with the videos, following their own interests, without any prior restrictions. That
771   is, we either encourage or discourage volunteers from using the app. With the proxy agent, we
772   can collect (and only collect) the behaviors on the short-video App, including various user-video
773   feedback, such as watch, click, like, share, etc. The proxy also has access to the watched videos
774   cached in the memory of devices, making it possible to collect the video content. There are mainly
775   four components in the data collection process with the proxy agent:
776
      • Traffic interceptor. In order to collect the traffic between clients and the short-video platform, we
777
        use MITMproxy to transparently intercept all HTTP/HTTPS traffic between the mobile device and
778
        the platform. With the MITMproxy CA certificate installed on the mobile device, the proxy can
779     collect data including the cached videos, video attributes such as video titles, author information
780     and behavior information, e.g. user/video ID and request timestamps.
781
      • Video collection. The proxy can access the cached videos that have been watched by users and we
782
        export these files as video content data.
783
784
      • Screen-touching listening. The proxy agent can also automatically capture the screen-touching
        behavior backstage including click, slide and long press, etc. With the collected event, timestamp
785
        and position, we can infer the feedback provided by volunteers when watching videos such as like,
786
        collect and hate.
787
788
      • Privacy protection. Considering the need for privacy protection, we conducted hashing towards
        all user/video/author IDs and dropped all sensitive information during data collection. Besides, We
789
        conducted some modifications and summarization on the video title to make it hard to identify but
790
        reserved the keywords and original meaning.
791
792   The above four designs of the proxy agent are transparent and all get the agreement of volunteers.
793   Furthermore, the volunteers are required to fill an optional questionnaire for the basic demographics.
794   With the above operations, we collect the dataset with behaviors, video content, and user attributes.
795   The whole data collection procedure is illustrated in Figure 7. To ensure the quality of collected data,
796   we have the following actions:
797
      • Content filtering. We conducted a further check and removed the offensive videos from our dataset.
798
        These operations ensure that the offensive content has been excluded from our dataset.
799
800   • Self-reporting information check. To mitigate the self-reporting biases of volunteers, we made
801
        two-fold efforts. On the one hand, we introduced some special designs in the questionnaire used for
        collecting user-side information, which can help assess the consistency and validity of responses.
802
        For example, we have designed questions that ask respondents to convey their attributes in different
803
        ways. For the answers with conflicts, we have filtered out these volunteers in the dataset to reduce
804     self-reporting bias. On the other hand, we combined the self-reported data with other sources of
805     information (e.g., comparing the IP address of users recorded by the platform and self-reporting
806     cities) to cross-validate responses and identify potential discrepancies.
