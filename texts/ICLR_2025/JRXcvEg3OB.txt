011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Bayesian Flow Networks (BFNs) represent an emerging class of generative mod-
014            els that exhibit promising capabilities in modeling continuous, discretized, and
015            discrete data. In this paper, we develop Guided-BFNs to integrate BFNs with
016            conditional guidance and gradient guidance to facilitate the effective application
017            of such models in trajectory planning tasks. Based on our developments, we can
018            better comprehend BFNs by inspecting the generation dynamics of the planning
019
               trajectories. Through extensive parameter tuning and rigorous ablation experi-
               ments, we systematically delineate the functional roles of various parameters and
020
               elucidate the pivotal components within the structure of BFNs. Furthermore, we
021
               conduct a comparative analysis of the planning results between diffusion models
022            and BFNs, to discern their similarities and differences. Additionally, we under-
023            take efforts to augment the performance of BFNs, including developing a faster
024            and training-free sampling algorithm for sample generation. Our objectives en-
025            compass not only a comprehensive exploration of BFNs’ structural insights but
026            also the enhancement of their practical utility.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   Generative models (Shocher et al., 2023; Ho et al., 2020; Goodfellow et al., 2014; Kingma & Dhari-
033
      wal, 2018) have achieved remarkable progress in multimodal generation, with GPTs (OpenAI, 2023;
      Brown et al., 2020; Radford et al., 2019; 2018) and Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022; Podell
034
      et al., 2023) as the representative examples. The underlying technical foundations include Auto-
035
      Regressive Models (Vaswani et al., 2017; Beltagy et al., 2020; Kitaev et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021;
036   Parmar et al., 2018), Diffusion Models (Ho et al., 2020; Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015a; Nichol &
037   Dhariwal, 2021; Song et al., 2020a;b; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Ho & Salimans, 2022; Luo, 2022;
038   Ramesh et al., 2022), etc. However, issues exist that the generative modeling of both continuous and
039   discrete data have not been effectively uniﬁed.
040
      Bayesian Flow Networks (BFNs) (Graves et al., 2023) are an emerging type of deep generative
041
      model for addressing such an issue. They are conceived from the principles of data compression
042   theory (Lelewer & Hirschberg, 1987; Jain, 1981; Welch, 1984). In BFNs, the parameters of a set of
043   independent distributions are modiﬁed with Bayesian inference in the light of noisy data samples,
044   then passed as input to a neural network that outputs a second, interdependent distribution. Start-
045   ing from a simple prior and iteratively updating the two distributions yields a generative procedure
046   similar to the reverse process of diffusion models; however it is conceptually simpler in that no
047   forward process is required. The network inputs for discrete data lie on the probability simplex,
048   and are therefore natively differentiable. The loss function directly optimises data compression
049   and places no restrictions on the network architecture. In the experiments BFNs achieve competi-
050
      tive log-likelihoods for image modelling on dynamically binarized MNIST(LeCun et al., 1998) and
      CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009), and outperform all known discrete diffusion models on the
051
      text8 (Shannon, 1951; Cover & King, 1978; Zipf, 2013) character-level language modelling task.
052
053   However, the previous empirical studies on CIFAR-10 and text8 expose rare insights into the be-
      havior of BFNs and opportunities for further enhancement. We urgently need a good way to better

      inspect BFNs and to chase an in-depth understanding, which aids in bringing BFNs to the masses to
055   unleash their maximal potential. This paper takes the ﬁrst step toward bridging the gap.
056
057   We ﬁrst identify a reasonable way to visualize the internal behavior of BFNs—adapting them for
058
      policy modeling and evaluating in long-horizon decision-making scenarios in several reinforcement
      learning (RL) settings. Given that BFNs employ multi-step sampling in their generation process,
059
      akin to diffusion models (DMs), we have chosen to assess them within the context of policy plan-
060

062   the properties and nuances of the intermediate planning results. In contrast, generating images in-
063   volves alterations at the pixel level that are less traceable and exhibit reduced controllability. We
064   choose several RL settings, including the Maze-2D environment (Fu et al., 2020), the block stacking
065   tasks (Garrett et al., 2022), and the D4RL locomotion benchmark (Fu et al., 2020), then launch ex-
066   periments following the fundamental framework of Diffuser (Janner et al., 2022), a diffusion-based
067   method employed for trajectory planning problem. Under this framework, planning trajectories
068   and sampling from data become essentially equivalent. We will treat "planning" and "sampling" as

      interchangeable terms in the following paragraphs.
070   This paper represents the pioneering effort to apply BFNs in RL settings, contributing to the visual-

072

073   the datasets into the model in addressing RL settings using BFNs. In the sampling process, we devise
074   a novel classiﬁer-guided approach to implement gradient-guided and conditional-based sampling
075   with BFNs.
076
      Furthermore, through extensive parameter tuning and comprehensive ablation experiments, we have
077
      elucidated the critical components within BFNs’ structure and how various parameters function.
078
      Utilizing a straightforward and intuitive visualization approach, we visualize the input distribution,
079   output distribution, the sender distribution and the Bayesian update process (Graves et al., 2023),
080   which are the key components of BFNs’ sampling process. We also investigated the impact of vari-
081   ous factors on BFNs. These factors include the min variance on training loss, planning performance
082   and sample diversity, as well as the interplay between time steps and sample steps during sampling.
083
      Additionally, we conduct a comparative analysis of trajectories generated by both DMs and BFNs
084
      within identical RL planning settings. Employing the same network architecture and training pa-
085   rameters allows us to discern the similarities and differences between the two approaches, provid-
086   ing further insights into the functioning of BFNs. Experimental results across various RL settings
087   demonstrate that Guided-BFNs based on the brand new BFNs achieve competitive results compared
088   to extensively optimized DMs.
089
      In the ablation studies, we investigate the effects of the conditional scaling factor and gradient scal-
090
      ing factor on planning performance to demonstrate the effectiveness of the method of Guided-BFNs
091   applied in trajectory planning tasks. These insights signiﬁcantly contribute to our enhanced under-
092   standing of BFNs.
093
094
      Finally, we tried a interpolation method for training-free faster sampling and compare several in-
      terpolation ratios. Our ﬁndings on Guided-BFNs in the testbed of RL policy planning settings can
095
      be directly transferred to the original testbed focused on image and text data, and we leave that for
096
      future research. We hope our ﬁndings may shed light on the developing of more effective BFNs’
097   variants and optimization on BFNs’ architecture and acceleration on BFNs’ sampling process.

      In this section, we establish Guided-BFNs as a novel approach to tackle the planning problem in the
165   upcoming RL experiments. The primary framework, encompassing state-action pairs, temporal lo-
166   cality, trajectory representation, and model architecture, adheres to the structure of Diffuser (Janner
167   et al., 2022). This framework exhibits capabilities such as learning long-horizon planning, temporal
168   compositionality, generation of variable-length plans, and task compositionality.

343
344   In this section, we provide visualizations of the input distribution pI , the output distribution pO , and
345   the sender distribution pS (as discussed in section 2.2), presented in ﬁg. 1. Additional mathematical
346   details are available in the appendix. These visualizations aim to elucidate the workings of key
347
      components within BFNs.
348   The parameters of an input distribution (Gaussian, with both mean and variance, but only the mean
349   µ is considered) are inputted and processed by a pretrained neural network optimized using the
350   continuous time loss in eq. (3). This process yields the parameters of an output distribution (Delta
351   distribution). Noise is subsequently added to the output, resulting in a sender y, according to the
352   accuracy schedule outlined in section 2.2 and the appendix. Following this, the input and sender are
353
      utilized to update the initial input distribution through Bayesian inference. The updated input pa-
      rameters are then fed into the same neural network, producing the ﬁnal output, which is the planning
354
      trajectory in our setting.
355
356   Neural Network: The neural network addresses interrelated variables in data by leveraging its
357   capacity to amalgamate such variables and learn implicit representations. This is a fundamental
358   aspect observed in deep learning, where the output trajectories exhibit signiﬁcant deviations from
359
      the input ones, representing large transitions toward the correct trajectory.
360   Bayesian Update: The Bayesian update process deals with independent variables in accordance
361   with statistical theory. Trajectories undergo more moderate revisions towards the correct outcome
362   through this process. The combination of these components in BFNs harnesses the advantages of
363   both deep learning and Bayesian inference.

370   Min Variance σ1 The hyperparameter σ1 is of paramount importance in BFNs, inﬂuencing the
371   accuracy schedule, which determines the manner and rate of introducing noise to the original data
372   during both training and inference. Speciﬁcally, σ1 represents the standard deviation of the input
373   distribution at t = 1 in eq. (3). Although the original BFNs paper (Graves et al., 2023) provides
374
      an approach for deriving the accuracy schedule, the selection process for σ1 is not discussed or
      experimented within the context of BFNs.
375
376   In our observations, we note that the converged training loss tends to decrease as σ1 increases, as
377   shown in table 8 in the appendix. Simultaneously, the ﬂuctuation of loss during training shows an
      inverse relationship with σ1 ; it increases as σ1 decreases.

511   5   R ELATED W ORK
512   Deep generative modeling has signiﬁcantly advanced model-based reinforcement learning. Recent
513   studies explore dynamic models with neural ODEs (Du et al., 2020), vector quantized autoencoders
514   (Hafner et al., 2020), and Transformers (Chen et al., 2022). This reﬂects a shift in the research focus
515   and methods. Various works (Tamar et al., 2017; Farahmand et al., 2017; Rybkin et al., 2021) have
516
      investigated bridging the gap between model learning and planning. Notably, (Janner et al., 2022)
      introduced Diffuser, a diffusion (Ho et al., 2020; Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015a; Nichol & Dhari-
517
      wal, 2021)-based model that concurrently generates trajectory timesteps, conditioned with auxiliary
518
      functions. This paper pioneers in visualizing and comprehending Bayesian Flow Networks (BFNs)
519   (Graves et al., 2023). Their adaptability to continuous, discretized, and discrete data, with minimal
520   training adjustments, stands in contrast to discretized diffusion models (Austin et al., 2021), which
521   necessitate deﬁned transition matrices.

523   6 C ONCLUSION
524
      In summary, this paper introduces Guided-BFNs, an innovative extension of BFNs, showcasing
525
      their enhanced applicability in various RL scenarios through the integration of additional condi-
526
      tions and gradient guidance. Through systematic parameter tuning and rigorous experiments, we
527   uncover crucial components and elucidate the functional roles of parameters, providing valuable in-
528   sights for practitioners. A comparative analysis highlights difference between BFNs and DMs. Our
529   contribution includes a faster, training-free sampling algorithm, improving efﬁciency for real-time
530   applications. By presenting our ﬁndings, we aim to inspire further research in the realms of BFNs’
531   explanation and optimization, fostering advancements in these areas in the future.

      B.2   P SEUDOCODE FOR G UIDED -BFN S
919
920   In Guided-BFNs, conditional guidance is added in both training (algorithms 1 and 2) and sampling
921   (algorithms 1 and 3), while gradient guidance is added only in sampling process. Interpolation
922   acceleration sampling is also shown in algorithm 3.

      C.1   DATASETS
927
928   The Maze-2D dataset consists of three sub-datasets based on scale: Umaze, Medium, and Large,
929   which require traversing to a goal location where a reward of 1 is given. No reward shaping is
930   provided at any other location.
931
      The Kuka dataset focuses on block stacking tasks, where trajectories of a robotic arm are generated
932
      to manipulate blocks. This dataset includes three sub-datasets: Unconditional Stacking, for which
933
      the task is to build a block tower as tall as possible; Conditional Stacking, for which the task is to
934   construct a block tower with a speciﬁed order of blocks; Rearrangement, for which the task is to
935   match a set of reference blocks’ locations in a novel arrangement. We train Guided-BFNs on 10000
936   trajectories from demonstrations generated by PDDLStream Garrett et al. (2022)); rewards are equal
937   to one upon successful stack placements and zero otherwise. We use one trained Guided-BFNs for
938   all block-stacking tasks, only modifying the perturbation function h(τ ) between settings. In the
939   Unconditional Stacking task, we directly sample from the unperturbed output distribution pO (x |
940   θ; t) to emulate the PDDLStream controller. In the Conditional Stackingand Rearrangement tasks,
941   we compose two perturbation functions h(τ ) to bias the sampled trajectories: the ﬁrst maximizes
942
      the likelihood of the trajectory’s ﬁnal state matching the goal conﬁguration, and the second enforces
      a contact constraint between the end effector and a cube during stacking motions.
943
944   Final State Matching To enforce a ﬁnal state consisting of block A on top of block B, we trained a
945   perturbation function hmatch (τ ) as a per-timestep classiﬁer determining whether a a state s exhibits a
946   stack of block A on top of block B. We train the classiﬁer on the demonstration data as the diffusion
947   model.
948   Contact Constraint To guide the Kuka arm to stack block A on top of block B, we construct a
                                           ∑64
949   perturbation function hcontact (τ ) = i=0 −1 ∗ ∥τci − 1∥2 , where τci corresponds to the underlying
950   dimension in state τsi that speciﬁes the presence or absence of contact between the Kuka arm and
951   block A. We apply the contact constraint between the Kuka arm and block A for the ﬁrst 64 timesteps
952   in a trajectory, corresponding to initial contact with block A in a plan.
953   The Locomotion dataset, an ofﬂine RL dataset, comprises nine sub-datasets containing trajectories
954   related to various movement scenarios. We guide the trajectories generated by Guided-BFNs toward
955   high-reward regions using the sampling procedure and condition the trajectories on the current state
956   using the inpainting procedure. The reward function Jϕ is trained on the same trajectories as Guided-
957   BFNs.

959   C.2   M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
960
961   The neural network model architecture for Guided-BFNs is illustrated in ﬁg. 6. The Guided-BFNs
962   architecture comprises a U-Net structure with 6 repeated residual blocks. Each block consists of two
963   temporal convolutions, each followed by group norm Wu & He (2018), and a ﬁnal Mish nonlinearity
964
      Misra (2019). Timestep embeddings are generated by a single fully-connected layer and added to
      the activations of the ﬁrst temporal convolution within each block.
