009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Multiocular human reconstruction aims to create a high-quality 3D human repre-
012            sentation from sparse video data. Recently, 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) has
013            demonstrated impressive results in multiocular human reconstruction tasks, ex-
014            hibiting remarkable speed and accuracy. However, it encounters challenges in sce-
015            narios involving intricate clothing and dynamic postures. This problem may stem
016
               from pixel-level supervision during the 3DGS optimization process, which proba-
               bly lead to spurious associations between unrelated visual features (e.g., misinter-
017
               preting clothing wrinkles as dependent on body occlusions rather than recognizing
018
               that both are influenced by complex postures). To address this issue, we propose
019            the LAST framework for realistic 3D human reconstruction, which integrates a
020            pre-trained Image-to-Point (I2P) model to enhance the 3D Gaussian Splatting op-
021            timization pipeline. The core of the LAST is to disentangle meaningful latent
022            factors and realistic dependencies from the input video frames, which allows for
023            dynamic adjustments to the density and attributes of Gaussian points during the
024            optimization process. Experimental results demonstrate that our method signifi-
025            cantly improves accuracy and realism in 3D human reconstruction compared to
026            existing techniques, particularly in challenging scenarios involving complex pos-
027
               ture and intricate clothing details.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   3D human reconstruction is a critical task in computer vision with broad applications in virtual re-
032   ality and game development. Traditional 3D human reconstruction methods (Bradley et al., 2008;
033   Collet et al., 2015; Starck & Hilton, 2007; Guo et al., 2019) often rely on images from dense view-
034   points captured by numerous cameras. However, such methods face substantial limitations in terms
035   of hardware costs and the complexity of data collection. Recently, advanced methods (Weng et al.,
036   2022; Jiang et al., 2022b; Yu et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2021a) have demonstrated that high-quality hu-
037   man reconstruction can be achieved from sparse-view images using Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF)
038   representations. However, these methods typically require expensive time and computational costs
039
      in the training and rendering, making them difficult to apply in the real world.
040   Recent advances in 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS,Kerbl et al. (2023)), it is possible to achieve high
041   expressivity with significantly faster training and rendering speeds compared to NeRF-based meth-
042   ods. While recent works of 3DGS human reconstruction (Moreau et al., 2024b; Qian et al., 2024; Hu
043   et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2024a) perform well in most scenarios, they struggle with challenging poses
044   and delicate clothing, suffering from detail loss and distortion. This is because 3DGS encodes latent
045
      factors that influence 3D human reconstruction, such as lighting, clothing, and pose, into Gaussian
      points, which have dependency relationships between each other. For instance, variations in posture
046
      can affect clothing details including folds or stretches, it’s essential to account for these dependen-
047
      cies during the optimization process. However current methods directly use the difference between
048   rendered and real images to guide model optimization. Images contain highly coupled visual infor-
049   mation (Liu et al., 2022b), with their dependencies being messy and intertwined. When learning
050   dependencies based on statistical relationships, probably leads to misleading conclusions, which are
051   analogous to the spurious correlations in the causal theory (Eberhardt & Scheines, 2007). For exam-
052   ple, wrinkled clothing frequently occurs at the same time as body occlusion in the athletic position,
053   the optimization process might incorrectly assume that body occlusion depends on wrinkled cloth-
      ing, but such dependencies are not routinely valid in reality, as both scenarios depend on complex


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      posture. If spurious correlations are incorporated into the optimization process, latent factors will
055   interfere with each other during the optimization process. This interference results in a detrimental
056   effect where adjustments in one latent factor can inadvertently cause degradation in others, manifest-
057   ing as artifacts or loss of fine details in the final reconstruction. This issue is particularly pronounced
058   in challenging poses and delicate clothing, which exhibit more complex dependency relationships.
059
      To solve the above problems, we argue that two main goals should be achieved: (1) discover latent
060
      factors and their corresponding dependencies within the 3D model; and (2) control the 3DGS opti-
061   mization process to mitigate the mutual interference among different latent factors. For the first goal,
062   we propose a pre-training Image-to-Point (I2P) framework, which utilizes a Variational Autoencoder
063   (VAE, Kingma (2013)) to learn how to disentangle 2D images into semantic vectors representing la-
064   tent factors, and we design a latent structure that focuses on 3D reconstruction and incorporates
065   it into the I2P framework. This latent structure aims to learn a dependency matrix that represents
066   dependencies between latent factors. During the VAE training process, features of latent factors are
067   extracted from the encoder and propagated from the parent latent factor to the child latent factor, the
068   decoder then uses these propagated features to generate the point cloud, enabling a backpropagation
069
      process that simultaneously updates the parameters of the VAE and the dependency matrix. This
      training process generates latent representations with explicit semantics and dependency associa-
070
      tions, which serve as additional supervisory signals and guidance of the 3DGS optimization process
071
      to mitigate latent factor mutual interference.
072
073   For the second goal, traditional 3DGS optimization (Li et al., 2024a; Shao et al., 2024; Abdal et al.,
074   2024b) relies on image difference loss gradients to guide the densification and updating of Gaussian
075
      point attributes. However, this can inadvertently propagate potentially incorrect dependencies due to
      the image coupling mentioned above. To address this, we propose to incorporate the learned latent
076
      factors and dependency matrix from the I2P framework. To be specific, there are three core strate-
077
      gies: (1) decouple latent factors from images as new supervisory signals in the optimization process.
078   Compared to the pixel supervision, this allows for a clearer understanding of how each latent factor
079   influences the reconstruction; (2) utilize the decoder to directly transform the latent factors space
080   into the point cloud space, establishing a connection between the 3DGS and the latent factors. By
081   analyzing differences in latent factors, we can identify 3DGS critical regions that require updates.
082   (3) construct an updated sequential chain based on the topological order indicated by the depen-
083   dency matrix to minimize the risk of interference among latent factors. Combining these strategies
084   not only leverages the latent factors but also reduces the propagation of erroneous dependencies. We
085   evaluate our approach on standard benchmarks for 3D human reconstruction, comparing it against
086
      state-of-the-art methods. Our experiments show that our model achieves superior reconstruction
      quality, particularly in challenging scenarios involving complex poses and intricate clothing details.
087
088   Overall, this work makes the following contributions:
089
090         1. We introduce an approach to disentangle and learn the dependencies among latent factors
091
               from 2D visual information, which allows us to manage the complex interplay between
               factors such as posture, clothing, and lighting.
092
093         2. We introduce the dependencies between latent factors into the 3DGS optimization process
094            to mitigate mutual interference among latent factors. Empirically, this integration allows
095            for a more nuanced optimization strategy that maintains the fidelity of local detail while
096
               ensuring overall coherence in the reconstructed model.
097         3. We evaluate the proposed method on the ZJU-MoCap, MoCap, and DNA-Rendering
098            datasets. Across all datasets, our approach achieves state-of-the-art performance in render-
099            ing quality. Furthermore, detailed ablations are conducted to demonstrate the effectiveness
100            of the proposed components.

104   2.1   3D DIGITAL HUMAN RECONSTRUCTION
105
106   Previous studies (Alldieck et al., 2022; Choi et al., 2022; Halimi et al., 2022; Habermann et al.,
107   2021) often use point clouds or meshes as the output 3D representations. While they incorporate
      latent factors such as light fields and dynamic textures, fitting these priors to fine deformations and


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      texture details remains challenging. Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) (Zheng et al., 2023; Hong et al.,
109   2022; Xu et al., 2021; Jiang et al., 2022a; Hu et al., 2023)models the radiance (color and density) of
110   each point in the scene as a neural network, turning the learning of latent factor priors into a dense
111   regression task, which addresses some of these issues. However, NeRF models typically require
112   pre-training on clear human data and fine-tuning for new human performers, which is inefficient and
113   may take several hours of pre-training to obtain a 3D human representation.
114
      3D Gaussian Splatting (Hu et al., 2024a; Moreau et al., 2024a; Li et al., 2024b; Zheng et al., 2024;
115   Abdal et al., 2024a) offers a more efficient method to generate high-quality 3D human representa-
116   tions by mapping pixels from 2D images to Gaussian points in 3D space. It decouples latent factors
117   of 3D space into attributes of Gaussian points in the form of Gaussian distributions, enabling effi-
118   cient and accurate human reconstruction. In this work, we focus on dependency relationships among
119   latent factors that previous research has not considered.
120
121   2.2     C AUSALITY IN V ISION
122
123   Current research has made strides in integrating visual features with causal inference to learn vi-
124   sual representations (Wang et al., 2020; Lopez-Paz et al., 2017; Chalupka et al., 2014; Liu et al.,
125   2022c;a; Wang et al., 2021; Zareian et al., 2020), enhancing models’ understanding of objects and
126   their relationships within images, and improving performance on downstream tasks such as object
127
      detection, image classification, and visual question answering. Another aspect of research involves
      introducing causal relationships to address challenges in traditional visual tasks. For instance, Zhang
128
      et al. (2020) improved semantic segmentation quality by severing the causal link between contextual
129
      priors and images.Qi et al. (2020) enhanced the accuracy of dialogue systems by using questions as
130   intermediaries and cutting direct causal effects between dialogue history and answers. Yang et al.
131   (2021) improved image annotation accuracy by clarifying the causal relationships among image fea-
132   tures, potential confounding factors, and image labels. However, the existing methods lack research
133   in 3D visual information. Inspired by causal structure learning, we proposed latent structure learn-
134   ing to discover dependencies of latent factors and integrate them into the 3D Gaussian Splatting
135   optimization process for 3D human reconstruction.

      3.1     IMAGE-TO-POINT MODEL FOR IMAGE DISENTANGLEMENT
145
146   In the first stage, we need to reconstruct a coarse 3D human representation R of the from a 2D image
147   X. Specifically, we learn a feed-forward network that directly transforms the input image into the
148   point cloud representation, namely the Image-to-Point (I2P) model. When designing the network
149   structure of the I2P model, there are two important tasks for the network: (1) It should disentangle
150   latent factors representing 3D semantic information from observed 2D images. (2) It should learn
151   the correct dependencies from the chaotic dependencies of visual features. To this end, as shown
152
      in Figure 1, we design a VAE-based hybrid network consisting of Latent Factor Disentanglement
      module and Latent Structure Learning module.

196   In the implementation of our framework, we design the architecture consisting of an encoder and
197   a decoder. The encoder maps the input image X into the latent space Z, producing parameters for
198   a Gaussian distribution—namely, the mean and variance—thereby allowing the formulation of the

201   the 3D representation R from Z, outputting a probability distribution over the reconstructed result.

      During training, we train the encoder and decoder by minimizing the ELBO loss function and per-
      forming backpropagation to update the parameters θ and ϕ.

      3.1.2     L ATENT S TRUCTURE L EARNING
205
206   Once the latent factors are disentangled from 2D images, the second question is how to learn real
207   dependencies from chaotic dependencies of visual features that contain spurious correlations. For-
208   mally, spurious correlations can be denoted as a latent factor zk that affects both the other latent
209   factor zi and zj . There will be a conditional probability relationship between zi and zj , but there
210   is no relevance between them in real semantics, resulting in a distorted relationship between zi and
211   zj . Inspired by Structural Causal Model(SCM,Shimizu et al. (2006)), we incorporate intervention
212
      into the VAE training process to solve the above problem. The intervention operation aims to fix the
      value of the latent factor zk to cut off its influence on other factors, allowing us to handle relationship
213
      the between the zi and zj in isolation.
214
215   In the implementation, we incorporate Latent Structure Learning between the existing encoder and
      decoder of the VAE to simulate the intervention of latent factors. Latent Structure Learning is a

317
      to the training views in the captured dataset. This iterative process involves updating the attributes

322   explicitly introduce the dependence between latent factors into the optimization order of Gaussian
323   points. Specifically, the strategy is as follows: (1) We use the I2P model to generate a point cloud

      We introduced an effective method for Gaussian human reconstruction, which disentangles latent
      factors with semantic information from visual features and addresses spurious correlations problem
517
      of dependencies between latent factors. Our method also proposes an innovative progressive update
518

519   tion. This approach achieves a balance between local details and the overall result. Through exten-

521   in challenging scenarios involving complex poses and intricate clothing details. This innovative
522   method provides insights into solving long-standing reconstruction challenges. For instance, in sce-
523   narios involving multiple people interacting or object occlusions, dependency inference can help the
524   model better understand and handle dependency between objects. This improves the model’s per-
525   formance in reconstruction and reduces noise and inaccuracies during the process. In 4D dynamic
526   scenes, where objects and human poses change over time, counterfactual inference can simulate and

      better understand and reconstruct object poses and interactions at different time points.

712
713   A.1     PRELIMINARY
714
715   Human Reconstruction from Sparse Video: Given a set of sparse video frames V =
716   {v1 , v2 , . . . , vn }, where each frame vi contains a 2D image of the human body at different view-
717   points, the objective is to generate an accurate Y = {y1 , y2 , . . . , yn } that accurately represents the
718   shape and pose of the human body, where yi rendered result at viewpoint i. Our goal is to minimize
719
      the difference between the rendered result of yi and the ground truth vi .
720   Latent structure learning: In this work, latent structure learning is defined as the process of un-
721   covering the N latent factors that influence the effectiveness of 3D human reconstruction, denoted
722   as Z = {z1 , z2 , . . . , zn }, along with the dependencies ϵ between them, represented by A, from the
723   observational data X. Each latent factor z represents a specific characteristic of the human model
724   (e.g., lighting, clothing, posture). We use the bold letter z to denote the vectorial representation of
725
      z. Each element Ai,j ∈ {0, 1} in A represents whether the i-th latent factor zi has a direct effect
      on the j-th latent factor zj .
726
727   3D Gaussian Splatting: 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) is a 3D representation that approximates a
728   continuous volume distribution by discretizing it into Gaussian points, which encapsulate attributes
729   related to geometry (Gaussian scales) and appearance (opacities and colors). 3DGS begins with an
730   initial sparse set of points derived from Structure from Motion (SfM) and refines this representation
731
      by controlling the density and attributes of the Gaussian points. Specifically, 3DGS constructs a
      loss function based on the difference between the rendered image Xrender and the ground truth
732
      image Xgt , optimizing the geometric features and color properties of the Gaussian points through
733
      backpropagation. Additionally, 3DGS determines whether to clone or split a point by evaluating the
734   average loss gradient magnitude of the points in Normalized Device Coordinates (NDC), facilitating
735   the transition from an initial sparse set of Gaussians to a denser configuration that more accurately
736   represents the scene.
737

741
742   The number of latent factors determines the model’s ability to capture all relevant latent factors
743   associated with 3D reconstruction and their dependencies. In this experiment, we tested the impact
744   of different numbers of latent factors on the model performance using the ZJU-Mocap dataset. As
745
      shown in figure 5, the model performs best with 4 latent factors. When the number of latent factors
      is too low, the model may fail to capture all relevant latent factors in the 3D reconstruction process,
746
      and the dependency between these factors might be overlooked, leading to ineffective modeling of
747
      complex latent structures. Conversely, when the number of latent factors is too high, the model
748   may learn many redundant or meaningless latent factors and dependencies, causing the dependency
749   between factors to become vague and unclear.
