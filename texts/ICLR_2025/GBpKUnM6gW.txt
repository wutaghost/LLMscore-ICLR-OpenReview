011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            An unprecedented amount of existing functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging
014            (fMRI) data provides a new opportunity to understand the relationship between
015            functional fluctuation and human cognition/behavior using a data-driven approach.
016            To that end, tremendous efforts have been made in machine learning to predict
017            cognitive states from evolving volumetric images of blood-oxygen-level-dependent
018
               (BOLD) signals. Due to the complex nature of brain function, however, the evalua-
               tion on learning performance and discoveries are not often consistent across current
019
               state-of-the-arts (SOTA). By capitalizing on large-scale existing neuroimaging data
020
               (39,784 data samples from seven databases), we seek to establish a well-founded
021            empirical guideline for designing deep models for functional neuroimages by link-
022            ing the methodology underpinning with knowledge from the neuroscience domain.
023            Specifically, we put the spotlight on (1) What is the current SOTA performance
024            in cognitive task recognition and disease diagnosis using fMRI? (2) What are the
025            limitations of current deep models? and (3) What is the general guideline for
026            selecting the suitable machine learning backbone for new neuroimaging applica-
027            tions? We have conducted a comprehensive evaluation and statistical analysis,
028            in various settings, to answer the above outstanding questions. In addition, we
029
               explore a novel attention learning mechanism to provide meaningful spatial pattern
               of brain activation that is associated with various cognitive tasks and neurological
030
               conditions.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) is a popular non-invasive neuroimaging technology
036   extensively used to investigate brain activity (Smith et al., 2004; Van Dijk et al., 2004; Fox et al.,
037   2005) and diagnosis neurological disorders (Klöppel et al., 2008; Zhou et al., 2008; Khazaee et al.,
038   2015; Deshpande et al., 2013; Calhoun et al., 2004). Monitoring alterations in blood oxygen level-
039   dependent (BOLD) signal, an indicator of local blood flow and oxygenation changes in response
040   to neural activity, offers a window into the functional activity of the brain in-vivo (Biswal et al.,
041   2010). During fMRI scans, subjects typically engage in either task-related activities to measure neural
042   activity associated with specific cognitive tasks, or resting states to measure spontaneous fluctuations
043   supported by intrinsic neural activity.
044   Glance of deep learning on fMRI. As shown in Fig. 1 left, an fMRI scan consists of a set of
045   evolving volumetric brain mapping of BOLD signal over time (i.e., 3D + t). Instead of using 4D
046   image directly, it is a common practice to partition the whole brain into a set of parcellations (by
047   following a pre-defined atlas (Evans et al., 2001)) and analyze the mean-time course of BOLD signals.
048   In this regard, sequential models such as Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs)(Yan et al., 2019) and
049   Transformer architectures(Bedel et al., 2023; Thomas et al., 2022) have been adapted to map time
050
      series to the phenotypic traits such as cognitive status or diagnostic label.
051   Meanwhile, synchronized functional fluctuations between distinct brain regions, a.k.a. functional
052   connectivity (FC), has been discovered in many neuroscience studies (Biswal et al., 1995; Friston
053   et al., 1994; Sporns et al., 2005; Greicius et al., 2003). This discovery shifted research focus to
      investigating region-to-region interactions, giving rise to a new interdisciplinary field — network

      neuroscience, which conceptualizes the brain as a connectome: an interactive network map where
055   distinct brain regions synchronize their neural activities through myriad interconnecting nerve fibers
056   and functional co-activations (Bassett & Sporns, 2017; Park & Friston, 2013; Bassett & Bullmore,
057   2006). Since connectome is often encoded in a graph structure, graph neural networks (GNNs) come
058   to the stage for capturing putative graph representations for nodes and edges in the FC brain network
059   (Han et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2021). In contrast to conventional GNNs, current
060   deep models for FC predominantly focus on graph classification tasks, that is, assigning a label to a
061   graph using integrated feature representations of the brain network.
062   For simplicity, many deep models for FC assume that brain connectivities do not change while
063   scanning a resting-state fMRI. However, there is a growing consensus in the neuroimaging field that
064   spontaneous fluctuations and correlations of signals between two distinct brain regions change with
065   correspondence to cognitive states, even in a task-free environment (Allen et al., 2014; Bassett et al.,
066   2011; Calhoun et al., 2014; Hutchison et al., 2013). Thus, it is natural to perform graph inference in
067   the temporal domain by combining time series modeling and graph representation learning techniques.
068   For example, as FC is a symmetric and positive-definite (SPD) matrix on the Riemannian manifold
069
      (Dan et al., 2022b), a manifold-based deep model is trained to capture graph spatial features while
      the evolution of FC matrices is jointly modeled by an RNN.

      significant gap between the general

      scenarios in both neuroscience and
078   routine clinical practice, due to the fol-

082   simplification is responsible for lacking comprehensive and trustful benchmarks across various deep
083   models. Second, little attention has been paid to enhancing model interpretability despite the high
084   demand for an in-depth understanding of how anatomical structures support brain function and how
085   self-organized functional fluctuations give rise to cognition and behavior. Third, current works mainly
086
      focus on “one-size-fits-all” solution for all fMRI studies. Considering the complex nature of brain
      functions in different environments, we hypothesize that the best practice for real-world problems

088
      that end, it is critical to examine this hypothesis and further gain conclusive insights through existing

      2    R ELATED B ENCHMARK ON C OMPUTATIONAL F MRI A NALYSIS
121
122
      Several benchmark studies have been conducted on fMRI data. For instance, ElGazzar et al. (2022)
123
      evaluated the performance of various GNNs on the UK Biobank database, discussing different node
124   features and adjacency matrices for different GNNs, and conducted experiments on both static and
125   dynamic FCs. Similarly, Said et al. (2023) provided a more comprehensive discussion including
126   node feature types, the number of ROIs, and adjacency matrix sparsity, as well as identified optimal
127   settings for these parameters. They evaluated 12 models but on the HCP dataset only. Xu et al. (2024)
128   curated and released six disease-based datasets and assessed 13 various machine learning methods on
129   these datasets.
130   Despite these efforts, to the best of our knowledge, no benchmark work focuses on the general
131   principle of designing/tailoring suitable models for various fMRI studies. To address this, our
132   work includes a detailed analysis of the HCP dataset, which features both single-domain and multi-
133   domain cognitive tasks, as well as the ADNI, OASIS, and PPMI datasets for neurodegenerative
134   diseases, and the ABIDE dataset for neuropsychiatric disorders. Additionally, recent works (Bedel
135   et al., 2023; Thomas et al., 2022) have demonstrated the efficiency of sequential models, such as
136   Transformers, in fMRI analysis. Therefore, our study not only examines spatial models based on FC
137   but also extensively evaluates sequential models leveraging temporal information, offering a more
138
      comprehensive benchmark in the field.

      Remarks 1.1. One possible explanation, from the perspective of machine learning, for the enhanced
      performance of sequential models compared to spatial models could be attributed to a stronger
275
      correlation between the dynamic characteristics of BOLD signals and cognitive tasks, as opposed
276
      to the correlation with (static) wiring topology of functional connectivities in healthy brains. The
277   evidence for supporting this statement becomes particularly more apparent in HCP-WM experiment.
278   The biological basis for the differences in performance between sequential and spatial models lies
279   in the nature of task-evoked experiments. These experiments are designed to elicit specific brain
280   responses related to cognitive processes such as attention, memory, language, or emotion through
281   simple cognitive tasks or stimuli. In this context, some portion of the brain exhibits higher activity
282   levels, as evidenced by the fluctuation dynamics in BOLD signals, to support the targeted cognitive
283   tasks. Meanwhile, the human brain exhibits limited possibility to reconfigure the topology of whole-
284   brain functional connectivities as mounting evidence shows that network organization remains stable
285
      regardless of brain states switching from task to task (Buckner et al., 2009; 2013; Power et al., 2011).
286   Remarks 1.2. Sequential models are more effective in task fMRI classification, and there exist
287   significant variations of learning performance within spatial and sequential models. The evidence
288   supporting this remark is discussed method-to-method in Appendix A.4.1.

451   To generate an attention map that reflects the importance of specific regions-of-interest (ROIs), we
452   introduce a learnable global attention assigned to each ROI. This attention is applied by multiplying
453   it with the dimension of ROI of the initial input data, ensuring that the attention is directly associated
454
      with the input features. By applying attention prior to the data entering the models, we maintain
      a strict spatial correspondence between the attention map and the ROIs, preventing the possible
455
      misalignment caused by the non-linear operation in feature representation learning. The global
456
      attention vector is initialized with ones, and a sigmoid function is applied to constrain the attention
457   weights between 0 and 1, representing the attention score for each ROI.

459   Evaluation of brain attention maps on HCP task-specific data. For brain mapping, we selected

463   regions, with the corresponding weights are relatively high (indicated by colors). GCN also identifies

466   Among sequential models, the Transformer identifies more Motor-related brain regions, whereas the

469   Remarks 4.1. Figure 5 shows that while some models, such as GCN, can identify brain regions
470   corresponding to specific tasks, they do not entirely align with the brain activation patterns established
471   by neuroscience, and the selected brain regions appear relatively dispersed. We speculate that this is
472   likely attributable to the following reasons:
473
474         1. Our analysis of the global attention weights revealed that their values were quite similar,
475            leading to weak discrimination across ROIs. This may result in the failure to highlight the
476            correct brain regions, even though it performs well in classification. This issue could be
477            related to our initialization of global attention with all ones. When the input features exhibit
478
               inherent discriminability, the attention values tend to balance out, preserving the original
               discriminative relationships. To explore this further, we tried initializing attention weights
479
               randomly in the range [0, 1). While this approach promotes stability from initial randomness,
480
               the values remained fairly close, so the improvement in discrimination was limited.
481
482         2. Although the different models fit the data well and achieved high accuracy, the global
483            attention maps generated by various models vary. This raises the question of whether the
484            features highlighted by deep learning models align with those identified by neuroscience for
485            specific tasks. Because the models may have learned task-specific patterns that are effective
               for classification but lack biological relevance. Reasons for this issue include (1) substantial

489   To address the first challenge, we expect adding sparse constraint might be useful, however, it brings
490   extra cost of computational complexity. Regarding the second issue, possible solutions include
491   ensemble learning (to enhance the consensus) and dismantlement learning (to reduce the influence

493   Remarks 4.2. Although the prediction accuracy and the power of detect disease-specific brain
494   regions are promising, the findings are not yet converging. One possible reason is the lack of standard
495   attention module, integrated in the deep model, to estimate the importance of each brain region
496   on the fly. A few deep models (Bedel et al., 2023) have emphasized the model explainalibity in
497   the method design by reusing the existing attention components. However, it has not been fully
498   investigated whether the learning mechanism of attention coefficients consistently maintains region-
499   to-feature coherence (as explained in Remarks 4.1) throughout the entire deep neural network. To
500   that end, further investigation into biologically-inclined feature representation learning and attention

503   7   D ISCUSSIONS AND C ONCLUSIONS
504
505   Discussions. We have collected sufficient supporting evidence to answer the questions in the
506   beginning of Section 5. The quick answer to (H1) is ”NO”, based on the quantitative results shown
507   in Tables 2, Table 3 and Table 4. Converging evidence shows that an in-depth understanding of the
508   biological mechanism could significantly affect learning performance. Thus, the answer to (H2)
509   is ”YES” and the key is: integrating the neuroscience insight into model design which could not
510   only lead to high accuracy but also uncover novel biological mechanisms using machine learning
511
      techniques. Another useful guideline for future fMRI-based deep models is: Explainable deep model,
      which is of high demand to jointly recognize cognitive tasks (or forecast disease risk) and yield
512
      task-specific (or disease-relevant) brain mappings. A successful explainable deep model will be
513
      very attractive in the neuroscience field given the surging number of neuroimaging data in many
514   neuroimaging studies. Finally, the prediction accuracy for forecasting disease risks by current deep
515   models is very promising. Considering the wide availability of MRI scanner in the clinic, there is
516   substantial feasibility in implementing fMRI-based computer-assisted diagnosis systems for routine
517   clinical practice. However, caution is needed due to the complex nature of disease progression and
518   our incomplete comprehension of disease etiology.
519
      Conclusions. We have conducted a comprehensive benchmark for current SOTA deep models in
520   various fMRI studies. The strength of this work lies in a large scale of functional neuroimages with
521   professional data pre-processing. We provide a set of useful guidelines of developing suitable deep
522   models for new fMRI applications. Additionally, the pre-processed data is publicly available, fostering
523   collaborative research between the fields of machine learning and computational neuroscience.

816   Human Connectome Project - Young Adult (HCP-YA). The HCP dataset was collected using

818   adult participants, ensuring high spatial and temporal resolution. Tasks included Working Memory,
819   Gambling, Motor, Language, Social Cognition, Relational Processing, and Emotion Processing.
820
      Each task had two runs with alternating phase encoding directions. Key imaging parameters for the
      functional scans included a TR of 720 ms, TE of 33.1 ms, and 2.0 mm isotropic voxel size.
821
822   Human Connectome Project in Aing (HCP-A). The HCP-Aging dataset was collected using a
823   Siemens 3T Prisma MRI scanner with a 32-channel head coil, capturing high-quality imaging data
824   from older adult participants across a wide age range (36 to 100 years old). The dataset includes both
825   structural and functional MRI scans, allowing for the study of age-related changes in brain structure
826
      and function. Three PsychoPy tasks covered in the HCP-A dataset include VisMotor Task, CARIT
      (Conditioned Approach Response Inhibition) Task and Facename Task. Imaging parameters for the
827
      functional scans include a TR of 800 ms, TE of 37 ms, and 2.0 mm isotropic voxel size, ensuring
828
      high spatial and temporal resolution for brain connectivity analyses.
829
830   ADNI. The ADNI dataset primarily uses 3T field strength scanners. It includes high-resolution 3D
831   T1-weighted structural MRI and resting-state fMRI to capture resting brain activity. The protocols
832
      ensure standardized acquisition across sites, with quality control measures to maintain consistency at
      the Mayo Clinic. Pre-processing steps such as intensity normalization, gradient unwarping, and spatial
833
      normalization are applied to ensure reproducibility and reliability for longitudinal and cross-sectional
834
      Alzheimer’s disease research.
835
836   OASIS. The OASIS dataset uses Siemens 3T MRI scanners with 16-channel head coils to achieve
837   high-resolution imaging, typically with 1 mm isotropic voxel size for structural scans. The dataset
838
      includes T1-weighted, T2-weighted, and FLAIR structural MRI, along with BOLD functional MRI
      to assess brain activity during resting states. Comprehensive data pre-processing involves spatial and
839
      intensity normalization, alongside rigorous quality control to ensure high data quality. This dataset is
840
      important for longitudinal studies on aging and neurodegenerative diseases.
841
842   PPMI. The Parkinson’s Progression Markers Initiative (PPMI) dataset employs advanced MRI
843   scanners from GE, Philips, and Siemens, primarily using 3T field strength for high-resolution imaging.
844
      It includes high-resolution 3D T1-weighted structural MRI for anatomical mapping and resting-state
      fMRI to study functional connectivity. Data preprocessing involves intensity normalization, gradient
845
      unwarping, spatial normalization, and the use of Echo-Planar Imaging (EPI) for high temporal
846
      resolution. These protocols support robust longitudinal studies and cross-sectional comparisons in
847   Parkinson’s disease research.
848
849   ABIDE. The Autism Brain Imaging Data Exchange (ABIDE) has aggregated functional and structural
850
      brain imaging data to enhance our understanding of the neural bases of autism. All participants
      within ABIDE dataset were scanned on the same 3.0 Tesla Ingenia scanner with 15-channel head
851
      coil. ABIDE’s comprehensive data supports detailed studies on brain functional connectivity and
852
      structural differences in ASD.
