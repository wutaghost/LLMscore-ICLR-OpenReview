009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Increasing the size of a Transformer does not always lead to enhanced performance.
012            This phenomenon cannot be explained by the empirical scaling laws. Furthermore,
013            the model’s enhanced performance is closely associated with its memorization
014            of the training samples. We present a theoretical framework that sheds light on
015            the memorization during pre-training of transformer-based language models. We
016            model the behavior of Transformers with associative memories using Hopfield
017            networks, such that each transformer block effectively conducts an approximate
018
               nearest-neighbor search. Based on this, we use a distance-based energy function to
               approximate the one in the modern continuous Hopfield network, which provides
019
               an insightful explanation for the attention mechanism. Since the softmax function
020
               corresponds to the gradient of the LogSumExp function in the energy, using the
021            majorization-minimization technique, we construct a global energy function to
022            capture the layered architecture. We show a dependency between the model size
023            and the dataset for the model to attain optimal performance, and the achievable
024            cross-entropy loss is bounded below.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Transformer-based neural networks have exhibited powerful capabilities in accomplishing a myriad
030   of tasks such as text generation, editing, and question-answering. These models are rooted in
031   the Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) which employs the self-attention mechanisms
032   to capture the context in which words appear, resulting in superior ability to handle long-range
033
      dependencies and improved training efficiency. In many cases, models with more parameters result
      in better performance measured by perplexity (Kaplan et al., 2020), as well as in the accuracies of
034
      end tasks (Khandelwal et al., 2019; Rae et al., 2021; Chowdhery et al., 2023). As a result, larger and
035
      larger models are being developed in the industry. Recent models (Smith et al., 2022) can reach up to
036   530 billion parameters, trained on hundreds of billions of tokens with more than 10K GPUs.
037
038   Nevertheless, it is not always the case that bigger models result in better performance. For example,
039
      the 2B model MiniCPM (Hu et al., 2024c) exhibits comparable capabilities to larger language models,
      such as Llama2-7B (Touvron et al., 2023), Mistral-7B (Jiang et al., 2023), Gemma-7B (Banks &
040
      Warkentin, 2024), and Llama-13B (Touvron et al., 2023). Moreover, as computational resources for
041
      training larger models increase, the size of available high-quality data may not keep pace. It has
042   been documented that the generalization abilities of a range of models increase with the number of
043   parameters and decrease when the number of training samples increases (Belkin et al., 2019; Nakkiran
044   et al., 2021; d’Ascoli et al., 2020), indicating that generalization occurs beyond the memorization of
045   training samples in over-parameterized neural networks (Power et al., 2022). Therefore, it is crucial
046   to understand the convergence dynamics of training loss during memorization, both in relation to the
047   model size and the dataset at hand. There has been an increasing interest in the empirical scaling
048   laws under constraints on the training dataset size (Muennighoff et al., 2024). Extensive experiments
049   have led to the conclusion of the empirical scaling laws (Kaplan et al., 2020) in terms of the model
050
      performance measured by the test cross-entropy loss. Unfortunately, this scaling law does not explain
      why in many cases smaller models perform better.
051
052   In this paper, we focus on the theoretical aspects of the dependencies between the achievable
053   performance, indicated by the pre-training loss, for transformer-based models, and the model and
      data sizes during memorization. It has been observed that a family of large language models tends


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      to rely on knowledge memorized during training (Hsia et al., 2024), and the larger the models, the
055   more they tend to encode the training data and organize the memory according to the similarity of
056   textual context (Carlini et al., 2022; Tirumala et al., 2022). Therefore, we model the behavior of
057   the Transformer layers with associative memory, which associates an input with a stored pattern,
058   and inference aims to retrieve the related memories. A model for associative memory, known as the
059   Hopfield network, was originally developed to retrieve stored binary-valued patterns based on part
060   of the content (Amari, 1972; Hopfield, 1982). Recently, the Modern Continuous Hopfield Network
061   (MCHN) was proposed and has been shown to exhibit equivalence to the attention mechanism
062   (Ramsauer et al., 2020). However, the MCHN only explains an individual Transformer layer and
063
      relies heavily on regularization.
064   Transformer-based models consist of a stack of homogeneous layers. The attention and feed-forward
065   layers contribute to the majority of the parameters in large models and are also the key components
066   of the attention mechanism. Furthermore, the layered structure of the transformer networks induces
067   a sequential optimization, reminiscent of the majorization-minimization (MM) technique (Ortega
068   & Rheinboldt, 1970; Sun et al., 2016), which has been extensively utilized across domains such as
069
      signal processing and machine learning. Using the MM framework, we construct a global energy
      function tailored for the layered structure of the transformer network.
070
071   Our model provides a theoretical framework for analyzing the performance of transformer-based lan-
072   guage models as they memorize training samples. Large language models only manifest capabilities
073   for certain downstream tasks once the training loss reaches a specific threshold (Du et al., 2024). In
074   practice, the training of large language models is terminated when the loss curves plateau. On the one
075
      hand, the validation loss offers valuable insights for budgetary considerations; it has been observed
      that even after training on up to 2T tokens, some models have yet to exhibit signs of saturation
076
      (Touvron et al., 2023). On the other hand, implementing early stopping can potentially compromise
077
      the generalization capabilities of the models (Murty et al., 2023). In Appendix F, we include a series
078   of experiments utilizing GPT-2, vanilla Transformer, and OpenELM models on various data. The
079   experimental outcomes provide evidence to support our theoretical results. We believe this work
080   offers valuable theoretical perspectives on the pre-training of large language models.
081
082   Our Contribution: (1) We take a new perspective by studying Transformer behavior using associa-
083   tive memories with Hopfield networks. We reveal the underlying connection between the attention
084   mechanism and nearest-neighbor search. (2) We approximate the continuous Hopfield network
085   using a distance-based energy function, excluding additional regularization terms. By recogniz-
086   ing that the softmax function corresponds to the gradient of the LogSumExp function, we employ
087
      the majorization-minimization technique to construct a global energy function to accommodate
      the layered architecture of the Transformer. (3) Using our theoretical framework, we character-
088
      ize the dependencies between pre-training loss, model size, and dataset during memorization for
089
      transformer-based language models.

      Hopfield models. Classical Hopfield networks (Amari, 1972; Hopfield, 1982) were introduced as
119
      paradigmatic examples of associative memory. The network’s update dynamics define an energy
120
      function, whose fixed points correspond to the stored memories. An important indicator is the number
121   of patterns that the model can memorize, known as the network’s storage capacity. Modifications
122   to the energy function (Krotov & Hopfield, 2016; Demircigil et al., 2017) result in higher storage
123   capacities (see Table 1 in Appendix B). The original model operates on binary variables, and
124   continuous Hopfield Networks have been developed later (Hopfield, 1984). The modern continuous
125   Hopfield network (MCHN) (Ramsauer et al., 2020) connects the continuous formulation with the
126   attention mechanism by introducing a specific model with a softmax activation function. Given an
127   input (e.g., a prompt), the Hopfield layer retrieves a memory by converging to a local minimum of the
128   energy landscape, and the update rule has a nice correspondence to the query-key-value mechanism
129
      in attention. Krotov (2021) proposes a Hierarchical Associative Memory (HAM) model with a global
      energy function for layered networks, as opposed to energy functions for individual layers. Further
130
      discussions on the relationship between the energy function utilized in this paper and other energy
131
      functions found in the literature on Hopfield networks is detailed in Appendix A.

149   3.1   A SSOCIATIVE MEMORIES
150
151   We consider models trained with a causal language modeling objective. Given an input sequence of
152   tokens s≤t = (s1 , s2 , . . . , st ), the l-layer Transformer outputs a distribution over the next token st+1 .
153
      The transformer models are trained to maximize the log-probability of the correct token st+1 given
      s≤t . During pre-training, input texts are segmented into sequences of length Tmax , and the model
154
      develops the ability to generate desired content, such as predicting the correct continuations. Thus,
155
      the sequences can be viewed as patterns in the setting of associative memories, where stored patterns
156   (e.g., sequences) can be retrieved using partial contents of the patterns. Since each prediction may
157   have access to a varying number of preceding tokens, the sequences padded to a length of Tmax earlier
158   may have less amount of information for the associative memory retrieval. As demonstrated in (Svete
159   & Cotterell, 2024; Svete et al., 2024), a Transformer can be modeled as a representation-based n-gram
160   model with relative small n. Consequently, only a small number of padded sequences are affected
161   by this discrepancy due to non-uniform lengths, and it does not significantly influence the collective
      behavior as analyzed within the framework of statistical physics. We note that, for objectives that are


                                                             3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      not causal, such as masked token modeling and sequence-to-sequence modeling, analogous reasoning
163   can be applied within the latent space.
164
165   It has been observed that the models tend to memorize patterns from the training data (Carlini et al.,
166
      2021; Biderman et al., 2024). Empirical studies on large language models have shown that the larger
      the models are, the more they tend to memorize training data (Carlini et al., 2022; Tirumala et al.,
167
      2022). This memorization allows the models to learn important patterns, such as world knowledge
168
      (Hsia et al., 2024), individual words (Chang & Bergen, 2022), and linguistic structure (Chang &
169   Bergen, 2024). In light of these findings, we make the following assumption regarding memorization.
170   As passing the text data through an embedding layer reduces their correlation, we posit that the
171   Transformer blocks serve to store the resulting latent representations, which are extracted from the
172   sequences once they are embedded.
173   Assumption 1. During the pre-training process, the model memorizes the (latent) training samples
174   D as patterns {ρ1 , ρ2 , . . . , ρd }, where ρi ∈ Rn for i = 1, 2, . . . , d.
175
176   To be economical with notations, we use D to directly address the patterns D = {ρ1 , ρ2 , . . . , ρd }. By
177   memorizing the samples, we mean that the patterns are stored within the model and can be retrieved
178   when provided with an adequate prompt. Specifically, we follow the definitions in (Ramsauer et al.,
179   2020) for pattern storage and retrieval.
180   Definition 1. For every pattern ρi , denote by Bi := {x ∈ Rn : d(x, ci ) ≤ ri } an n-ball such that
181   ρi ∈ Bi . The pattern ρi is said to be stored if there exists a single fixed point ρi∗ ∈ Bi to which all
182   points x ∈ Bi converge, and Bi ∩ Bj = ∅ for i ̸= j. Such Bi is said to be associated to the pattern
183   ρi , and we denote Bi ∼ ρi . The pattern ρi is said to be retrieved if the converged point is ϵ-close to
184   the fixed point ρi∗ .
185   Remark 1. In the work of Saha et al. (2023), a collective attraction mechanism is introduced to
186   address the challenge of partial cluster assignments using associative memory. When the intersection
187   of clusters Bi and Bj is non-empty (Bi ∩ Bj ̸= ∅), analogous relaxations to those presented in
188   Equation (7) of (Saha et al., 2023) can be made.
189
      Without loss of generality, we assume ci = ρi . We also assume that the small set of held-out test
190
      samples exhibits the same patterns as those in the training set. In practice, the validation samples are
191   randomly selected from the same dataset as the training samples, preserving the distribution.
192
      Assumption 2. We posit that the latent representations derived from the validation set, after being
193
      processed through an embedding layer, are stored in a manner analogous to those extracted from
194
      the training set. Specifically, for every element in the set of latent patterns De corresponding to the
195
      validation data, there exists an Bi for some i ∈ [d]. Consequently, we assume that D    e ⊂ D.

366   The attention mechanism encompasses a softmax operation that generates a probability distribution
367   p ∈ ∆n . In practice, the final softmax output is subsequently input into a task-specific layer to
368   facilitate downstream tasks, such as predictions and classifications. The attention softmax influences
369   the model’s ability to understand and process the input data, which in turn affects the output proba-
370   bilities that are used to calculate the cross-entropy loss. In essence, the attention softmax indirectly
371   influences the cross-entropy loss by shaping the model’s predictions. Consequently, we evaluate
372
      the alignment between the final softmax output of the transformer blocks and the target distribution.
      We demonstrate that the cross-entropy loss can be articulated through the logarithm of the partition
373
      function of the model’s distribution. This formulation reveals how the allocation of attention weights
374
      is contingent upon the learned patterns, thereby establishing a relationship between the characteristics
375   of the training data and the model size, which is pivotal for attaining optimal performance.

      6   C ONCLUSION
494
495
      We model transformer-based networks with associative memory and study the cross-entropy loss
496
      with respect to model and data sizes. We employ a distance-based layer-wise energy function
497
      that corresponds to a nearest neighbor search across patterns memorized during training. We then
498   construct a global energy function for the layered structure of the transformer models using the
499   majorization-minimization technique. In practice, we have observed that the majority of transformer
500   models at the commercial level tend to achieve a cross-entropy loss of approximately 2.2. The optimal
501   balance between model and data sizes, however, is often determined by the collective expertise of
502   practitioners. Additionally, the performance of these models can be compromised by both early and
503   delayed stopping. We believe the current paper represents an important step towards understanding
504   the pre-training behaviors of large transformer models. Empirical evidence supporting our study can
505   be found in Appendix F. However, given the significant allocation of computational resources to other
506
      scaling law investigations, we acknowledge that our numerical experiments constitute a preliminary
      evaluation, constrained by computational restrictions. However, it is imperative to underscore the
507
      theoretical significance of these findings.
508
509
510   E THICS AND R EPRODUCIBILITY S TATEMENT
511
512
      Limitations. The theoretical results rely on the assumptions, including Assumption 1 and Assump-
513
      tion 3, made in the main content. We have shown that there exists a dependency between the best
514
      model size and the dataset used for training transformer-based models, both in theory and in practice.
515   The most notable limitation is that achieving optimal performance through memorization requires
516   high-quality data. Since most models are trained on data derived from the Internet, the resulting
517   patterns may not be well separated. Future work will be required to identify the relationship between
518   the dataset and the learned patterns. The experiments are conducted on GPT-2, OpenELM models,
519   and vanilla Transformers. We expect that these results generalize to other transformer models. Also,
520   we do not consider other modifications such as lateral connections, skip-layer connections, mixture
521   of experts, mixture of depths, routing, or other compressive modules in the current work. These will
522   be interesting future directions.
523
524   Broader impacts. This study elucidates the performance of transformer-based models, measured
525   by cross-entropy loss. Consequently, the findings presented herein have the potential to influence
526   strategic budget allocation and model lifecycle management. By offering insights into the balance
527   between model performance and resource efficiency, it provides insights into the theoretically optimal
528   cross-entropy loss, which can inform both budgetary planning and model termination strategies.
529
      We believe that this research delineates a constructive pathway for organizations to foster a more
      sustainable approach to optimize their machine learning initiatives.
530
531   Given that the patterns recognized are directly extracted from the dataset at hand, there exists
532   a potential negative societal consequence: the propensity for models to memorize and thereby
533   perpetuate biases against certain groups. This risk can be attenuated through the implementation of
534   rigorous class-balancing methodologies. Nonetheless, it is imperative that comprehensive fairness
535
      assessments be conducted prior to the deployment of any model.
536
537   Reproducibility. While the present study is predominantly theoretical in its nature, we have taken
538   multiple steps to ensure the reproducibility of our experiments. We refer the reader to Appendix F
539   and Appendix E for a complete description of the experiments. We have also attached necessary code
      in the supplementary material.

      A     D ISCUSSION

758
      Relationship to the Chinchilla Scaling Laws. Our findings are contingent upon the transformer
759   layers’ memorization of patterns, which are presumed to be well-separated points learned from the
760   data. In our experiments, we utilize a reduced dataset to emulate the conditions of pattern separation
761   and memorization. Moreover, we have trained the models on their respective datasets repeatedly,
762   ensuring that the training losses have stabilized and the test losses have begun to ascend, indicative
763   of a mild degree of over-parameterization. These conditions diverge from those of the Chinchilla
764   experiment. In practical scenarios, commercial LLMs, akin to the Chinchilla model, are not subjected
765   to such conditions. As we have noted in our paper, it has been observed that even after training on
766   up to 2T tokens, some models have yet to exhibit signs of saturation. In practice, we have observed
767
      that the majority of transformer models at the commercial level tend to achieve a cross-entropy
      loss of approximately 2.2. The optimal balance between model and data sizes, however, is often
768
      determined by the collective expertise of practitioners. Additionally, the performance of these models
769
      can be compromised by both early and delayed stopping. Therefore, our current experimental setup
770   represents an idealized condition that has not been encountered in commercial LLMs. Nevertheless,
771   considering the substantial computational resources allocated to other scaling law investigations, we
772   recognize that our numerical experiments represent a preliminary assessment that is contingent upon
773   computational limitations, with a thorough analysis reserved for subsequent research endeavors.
774
775   Relationship to Prior Work on Hopfield Networks Recently, there has been a growing interest
776   in physics-informed neural networks. The Energy Transformer (Hoover et al., 2024) designs a
777   energy attention mechanism and the corresponding energy function, resulting in a unique model
778   with a strong theoretical foundation that achieves SoTA results on graph anomaly detection and
779
      graph classification tasks. Wu et al. (2024) introduces a kernelized version of modern Hopfield
      networks, aiming to express energy in a feature space where patterns are well-separated, thus avoiding
780
      memory interference. Hu et al. (2024b) presents a theoretical framework for deriving and analyzing a
781
      family of modern Hopfield models, and Hu et al. (2024a) offers a compression method for Hopfield
782   models, showing superior post-quantization performance compared to vanilla Transformers. While
783   the existing literature, such as Hierarchical Associative Memory Krotov (2021), employs a system of
784   differential equations to design a global energy function that can encompass feedback connections,
785   our proposed model tackles the global energy specific to feedforward architectures. Predictive coding
786   networks (Tang et al., 2023; Li et al.) incorporate recurrent connections; however, their dynamics are
787   focused on minimizing the total squared prediction errors and has less connection to the attention
788   mechanism. By conceptualizing the information retrieval properties of the Transformer as a sequence
789   of associative memories, our model endeavors to establish relationships between model size and
790
      memorization (of training data) within the framework of statistical physics.

       E    T RANSFORMER DETAILS : USING GPT-2 AS AN EXAMPLE
989
990    The original GPT-2 model was trained on a 40GB large dataset called WebText that is made of data
991
       derived from outbound links from Reddit. The model is trained on the next sentence prediction (NSP)
       task in a self-supervised manner. A pre-trained tokenizer can be applied to convert the text into tokens
992
       using a fixed vocabulary. A max token length Tmax (e.g., Tmax = 1024) is set, so during training, if
993
       the number of tokens is greater than Tmax , the documents will be truncated. The model is trained
994    causally, which means that the prediction for the next token only depends on the inputs from earlier
995    tokens. The model was trained with a global batch size of 512, and the test perplexity still improves
996    if given more training time.
997
       GPT-2 uses a byte-level version of Byte Pair Encoding (BPE), and the vocabulary size is nvoc =
998
       50, 257. The hidden dimension for the medium size model is demb = 1024. So the input sequence
999
       is of Tmax demb dimension. These sequences are passed through the model. For the medium size
1000   model, these include 24 transformer encoder blocks with 1024 hidden units and 16 self-attention
1001   heads (i.e., l = 24, demb = 1024, nh = 16). The number of parameters used for word embedding is
1002   nvoc ·demb . The number of parameters in the multi-head attention layer is l·nh ·(3·demb ·demb /nh ) =
1003   3ld2emb = 75, 497, 472. The number of parameters in the dense weights and layer normalization is
1004   l(d2emb +2demb ) = ld2emb +2ldemb = 25, 214, 976, and the number of parameters in the feed-forward
1005   weight matrices and bias is l(2demb · dFF + demb + dFF ) = 6ld2emb + 4ldemb = 151, 093, 248, with
1006   dFF = 3072 = 3demb . As we can observe, the multi-head attention and feed-forward layers account
1007   for most of the parameters in the model, and N ≈ Ald2emb with some constant A ≈ 10 in this case.
1008   The loss used for the GPT-2 model is the log-probability of a dataset divided by the number of
1009   canonical units (e.g., a character, a byte, a word), which is equivalent to the cross-entropy loss. The
1010   cross-entropy loss is commonly used to measure the divergence between the predicted probabilities
1011   and the true labels. For the NSP task, the model is trained to predict the next token in a sequence
1012   based on the context of the previous tokens. So the cross-entropy is taken between the predicted
1013   probabilities Prθ (xi ) of the token xi and the labels’ probabilities PrD (xi ) for all tokens xi in the
1014
