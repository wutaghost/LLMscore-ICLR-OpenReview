009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Visual language is a system of communication that conveys information through
012            symbols, shapes, and spatial arrangements. Diagrams are a typical example of
013            a visual language depicting complex concepts and their relationships in the form
014            of an image. The symbolic nature of diagrams presents significant challenges for
015            building models capable of understanding them. Yet, recent studies seem to sug-
016
               gest that Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) can even tackle complex rea-
               soning tasks involving diagrams. In this paper, we investigate this phenomenon
017
               by developing a comprehensive test suite to evaluate the diagram comprehension
018
               capability of LVLMs. Our test suite uses a variety of questions focused on con-
019            cept entities and their relationships over a set of synthetic as well as real diagrams
020            across several domains to evaluate the recognition and reasoning abilities of mod-
021            els. Our evaluation of three LVLMs (GPT-4V, GPT-4o, and Gemini) shows that
022            while these models can accurately identify and reason about entities, their ability
023            to understand relationships is notably limited. Further testing reveals that the de-
024            cent performance on diagram understanding largely stems from leveraging their
025            background knowledge as shortcuts to identify and reason about the relational in-
026            formation. Thus, we conclude that LVLMs have a limited capability for genuine
027
               diagram understanding, and their impressive performance in diagram reasoning
               is an illusion emanating from other confounding factors, such as the background
028
               knowledge in the models.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Symbolic signals such as language serve as powerful tools in communication by abstracting and
034   interpreting information. Visual language is a form of communication that uses symbols, shapes, and
035   spatial arrangements to convey complex ideas (Greenspan & Shanker, 2009; Li, 2023). Diagrams,
036   which encapsulate symbolic information in the visual stream, are a prime example of visual language
037   (Zdebik, 2012; Anderson et al., 2011) that are extensively used in practice across various domains,
038   e.g., mathematics (Seo et al., 2015), science (Lu et al., 2022), education (Kembhavi et al., 2016;
039
      2017), and illustrations (Hiippala & Orekhova, 2018; Lu et al., 2021). Developing models capable of
      understanding symbolic information, e.g. in diagrams, is a critical milestone in advancing machine
040
      intelligence (Bauer & Johnson-Laird, 1993; de Rijke, 1999; Cromley et al., 2010). Even though
041
      recent Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs, OpenAI, 2023; Anil et al., 2023) have demonstrated
042   some success on diagram-based visual reasoning tasks (Lu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024; Chen
043   et al., 2024), it remains unclear whether the performance on these tasks truly reflects the models’
044   ability to comprehensively understand the symbolic information in diagrams.
045
      For this purpose, we design a comprehensive test suite that investigates the ability of LVLMs to un-
046
      derstand diagrams. As defined by Foucault (1977) and Deleuze (1986), diagrams are abstract tools
047
      that organize visual entities using relational information. Drawing inspiration from this, our test
048   suite focuses on evaluating diagram understanding by assessing how well models can understand
049   entities and relations in typical diagrams (§ 2.1). We evaluate diagram understanding by defining
050   two types of tasks pertaining to fast recognition of entities and relations and slow multi-step rea-
051   soning (Kahneman, 2011) over the relations (§ 2.2). While we cannot cover every diagram type for
052   practical reasons, we still cover diagrams across six domains. To ensure that our evaluation is both
053   controlled and generalizable, our test suite includes both clean synthetic diagrams and 1, 001 anno-
      tated real diagrams carefully selected from existing datasets Krishnamurthy et al. (2016); Kembhavi

      2.1    D IAGRAMS A S G RAPHS
123
      Diagrams work as an abstract tool to describe concepts and relationships (Foucault, 1977; Deleuze,
124   1986). In practice, we choose this representation as it is quite flexible and a broad set of diagrams can
125   be represented in a format as shown in Fig. 1. For example, logical diagrams such as water cycles
126   illustrate the process transitions (relations) among cycle stages (entities). Schematic diagrams such
127   as circuits demonstrate the connections (relations) among electronic components (entities). Previous
128   work (Song et al., 1995; Hiippala & Orekhova, 2018) also chose to annotate and model diagrams as
129   concepts and their connections.1
130   Following this representation, we define a diagram as a graph G = {V , E }. Here, V is the set of
131   entities (e.g., “Squid” in the example diagram in Fig. 1b). Each entity V ∈ V could be represented
132   in multiple ways, e.g., text and visuals in the diagram. Each relation E = (V , V ′ ) ∈ E connects
133   two entities. Relations are either explicitly represented by arrows or via implicit relationships (e.g.,
134   relative positioning of entities).
135
136   2.2    H OW D O W E E VALUATE T HE M ODEL’ S D IAGRAM U NDERSTANDING A BILITY ?
137
138   In designing our test suite, we draw inspiration from (Kahneman, 2011) who argues that the think-
139   ing process can be naturally divided into two modes: System 1, which handles automatic, quick,
140
      and intuitive thinking (e.g., pattern recognition and everyday decisions), and System 2, which is
      responsible for deliberate, slow, and logical thinking (e.g., logical reasoning and critical analysis).
141
      We evaluate both the recognition and reasoning abilities of models via question-answering (QA).
142
      We carefully design a set of questions posed in a multiple-choice QA format with each question
143   having one correct answer and three incorrect options and simply use the model’s accuracy in an-
144   swering the questions as an evaluation of the model’s ability on that skill. Overall, we denote the set
145   of questions as Q. We denote the set of questions related to understanding entities as Q(V ), while
146   those related to relations as Q(E ). Additionally, we use subscripts to distinguish between different
147   types of questions: questions on synthetic diagrams are denoted as QS , whereas questions for real
148   diagrams are denoted as QR . Our test suite questions are categorized as follows:
149
150   Recognition vs. Reasoning Questions. To measure the two key abilities of models in recognition
151   of entities and relations vs. reasoning, we design two types of questions: Name Recognition (NR)
152   and Number Counting (NC). Specifically, the NR questions measure the recognition ability of models
153
      by verifying the existence of specific entities or relations. In contrast, NC questions measure reason-
      ing ability by asking for the number of certain types of entities or relations. We formally denote
154
      these question sets as Q(·|NR) and Q(·|NC), respectively.
155
156
      Knowledge-Required vs. Knowledge-Free. Next, we test if LVLMs use any knowledge short-
157
      cuts (Ye & Kovashka, 2021; Tang et al., 2023) to answer our questions without true diagram under-
158
      standing. Diagrams often encode some background real-world knowledge, and the models may use
159   their background knowledge as a shortcut to answer the questions. To further tease out the models’
160

163   knowledge, allowing us to test the models’ capabilities in each scenario. If a question requires
164   the model to use background knowledge (e.g., semantic or commonsense knowledge), we call it
165   a knowledge-required (KR) question, which is denoted as Q(·|KR). On the other hand, questions

359   4     K NOWLEDGE A S S HORTCUTS : Q UANTITATIVE A NALYSIS
360
361   Given that rich knowledge has been encoded into LVLMs during various training stages, a possible
362   hypothesis emerges: these models may not truly understand diagrams but instead rely on their
363   ingrained knowledge as shortcuts to provide answers. In this section, we explore the impact of
364
      knowledge on the models’ ability to answer questions.
365
366   4.1   K NOWLEDGE G ROUNDING OF D IAGRAMS I MPROVES R ELATION R ECOGNITION
367
      To determine the effect of knowledge, we first compare the models’ QA performance on diagrams
368
      with and without embedded knowledge. Our focus is on the explicit relations in synthetic diagrams.
369   We construct relations that incorporate semantic knowledge to simulate practical conditions where
370   models might use this knowledge as shortcuts. If our hypothesis is correct, we would expect to see

510   5.2      LVLM S H ALLUCINATE R ELATIONS IN R EAL D IAGRAMS (C ASE S TUDY )
511
512   Next, we provide an intuitive and vivid case study to demonstrate that LVLMs hallucinate when
513   interpreting relations in diagrams. Using the example diagram from Fig. 1, we construct two special
514   cases for comparison: in the first case, we remove all relations from the diagram, and in the second
515   case, we replace the relations with random ones. The goal is to test the GPT-4o’s response when
516   there is either no relational information or when the relations presented conflict with background
517   knowledge. For evaluation, we pose an annotated question (QR (E |KR, NR)) and a complex reasoning
518
      question involving food chain counting and use the configuration in App. E.1.
519   In Fig. 3, when comparing the responses in the left subfigure (vanilla) with those in the middle
520   subfigure (w/o relation), we observe that even in the absence of explicit relational information, the
521   model still identifies the correct predator. Additionally, for the food chain counting question, the
522   model continues to provide the original answers. This indicates that the model has pre-existing
523   knowledge and it can use the knowledge as a shortcut for answering questions. Similarly, when
524
      comparing the responses in the left subfigure (vanilla) with those in the right subfigure (with random
      relations), we find that the model provides the same answers despite the introduction of random
525
      relations. The new correct predator could be “A)” or “C)”, and the new correct food chain count is
526
      4. This demonstrates that the model relies on learned knowledge rather than parsing the diagram
527   itself. With this additional evidence, we can reasonably conclude the following finding:
528
529   Finding: Current LVLMs can recognize and reason about entities in diagrams but struggle with
530
      understanding relations. However, they manage to answer diagram-related complex questions by
      identifying entities and leveraging relevant learned knowledge as a shortcut.

533   6      C ONCLUSION
534
535   We evaluate three LVLMs on diagram understanding using our test suite, including synthetic and
536   real diagrams. Our findings reveal that while these models can perfectly recognize and reason about
537   entities depicted in the diagrams, they struggle with recognizing the depicted relations. Furthermore,
538   we demonstrate that the models rely on knowledge shortcuts when answering complex diagram rea-
539   soning questions. These results suggest that the apparent successes of LVLMs in diagram reasoning
      tasks create a misleading impression of their true diagram understanding capabilities.

      B    L IMITATIONS
878
879
      Our work has two main limitations. First, we focus exclusively on diagrams that depict various
880
      entities and relationships. There may be other specialized types of diagrams that are not well-suited
881   to this representation. We encourage future research to explore and analyze model performance on
882   such diagrams. Second, while we demonstrate that LVLMs have limited diagram understanding
883   capabilities and that their strong performance is largely due to knowledge shortcuts, we do not offer
884   insights on how to address this issue. Future work could focus on developing strategies to enhance
885   LVLMs’ true diagram understanding abilities.

      C    R ELATED W ORK
888
889
      Evaluation of LVLMs. Various benchmarks have been introduced recently to evaluate the per-
890
      formance of multimodal models (e.g., LVLMs) focusing on different aspects like cognition and
891
      perception (Fu et al., 2023), knowledge (Yu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Yue et al., 2024), halluci-
892   nation (Li et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b) and reasoning with images (Yue et al.,
893   2024). These benchmarks include a wide range of images, some from natural image datasets (Li
894   et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b) and others newly gathered or generated specifically for these stud-
895   ies (Liu et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2023; Yue et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024). The benchmarks are used
896   either by employing a generative task of image captioning, where the model is prompted to describe
897   the image (Fu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a), or by visual question answering (VQA), where the
898   model is asked several questions related to the image (Yue et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,
899   2024). These benchmarks evaluate model performances qualitatively and quantitatively to report on
900
      their strengths and weaknesses.
901
902   Diagram Question Answering (DQA). Prior explorations on the DQA are diverse, focusing on
903
      various types of diagrams and questions. Some studies have addressed spatial reasoning challenges,
      by generating diagrams with basic shapes like in NLVR (Suhr et al., 2017) and ShapeWorld (Kuhnle
904
      & Copestake, 2017) or abstract representations of real-life scenarios (Antol et al., 2015; Zhang
905
      et al., 2016). Others have gathered text-book diagrams, such as FoodWebs (Krishnamurthy et al.,
906   2016), AI2D (Kembhavi et al., 2016) and TQA (Kembhavi et al., 2017), containing diagrams from
907   the grade-school science domain with more emphasis on relationships between diagram entities and
908   complex concepts like procedures, taxonomies, and labeling of components. Although these datasets
909   contain complex diagrams, most of the questions can be answered with commonsense knowledge,
910   without visual reasoning. In contrast, CLEVR (Johnson et al., 2017) and IconQA (Lu et al., 2021)
911   encompass simple diagrams but require a spatial and property-level understanding of the diagram.
912   With the development of recent LVLMs, a wave of explorations of (statistical) diagram understand-
913   ing coming out. Masry et al. (2022); Islam et al. (2024) find that recent vision-language models
914   cannot understand the basic elements in chart diagrams. Singh et al. (2024); Pan et al. (2024) reveal
915   that these large models still struggle with understanding basic entities and relations of the diagram,
916   which align with our findings on the synthetic diagrams. However, the rapid development of LVLMs
917   shows that these large models have achieved almost perfect performance on real-world image un-
      derstanding benchmarks (Li et al., 2021; Mathew et al., 2021), which align with our findings on real


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      diagrams. However, the discussion of the bizzared gap remains unclear. Our work lies in this place
919   by exploring the role of knowledge and propose a hypothesis with experimental verifications.
920
921
      Capabilities and Limitations of LVLMs. A common viewpoint on the success of LVLMs is
922
      optimistic. The great performance on various benchmarks is interpreted as the strong understanding
923   abilities of large models on diagrams, such as identification ability (Chen et al., 2024) and reasoning
924   ability (Lu et al., 2023). Besides diagrams, large language models are found powerful to solve
925   various complex question-answering tasks requiring strong reasoning ablities (Qin et al., 2023; Bang
926   et al., 2023; Fatemi et al., 2024). Large visual models are also shown to perform reasoning about
927   images pretty well (Gupta & Kembhavi, 2023; Chen et al., 2023).
928
      Another line of work focuses on the limitations of LVLMs. People find that large models act like
929   stochastic parrots which only mindlessly mimic humans (i.e., memorizing pertaining corpus without
930   any understanding) for both text reasoning and visual reasoning (Yang et al., 2024; Zecevic et al.,
931   2023; Hu et al., 2024; Mao et al., 2023). Meanwhile, a rising number of evaluations on LVLMs
932   exposing their limitations on more basic reasoning tasks such as counting (Fu et al., 2023) and per-
933   ception (Yue et al., 2024). This line of work indicates that LVLMs perform diagram understanding
934   via illusion, i.e., relying on the learned knowledge instead of real-time reasoning of the diagram.
