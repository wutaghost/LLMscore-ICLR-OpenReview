009                                                        A BSTRACT
010
011                   In this work, we explore the question: “How much prior art knowledge is needed
012                   to create art?” To investigate this, we propose a text-to-image generation model
013                   trained without access to art-related content. We then introduce a simple yet effec-
014                   tive method to learn an art adaptor using only a few examples of selected artistic
015                   styles. Our experiments show that art generated using our method is perceived by
016                   users as comparable to art produced by models trained on large, art-rich datasets.
017                   Finally, through data attribution techniques, we illustrate how examples from both
018
                      artistic and non-artistic datasets contributed to the creation of new artistic styles.

      1          I NTRODUCTION
022
023
      Is exposure to art truly necessary for creating it? Could someone who has never seen a painting,
      sculpture, or sketch still produce meaningful visual art? In a world saturated with cultural influences
024
      and artistic traditions, this question becomes challenging to answer. Movements like Outsider Art
025
      have already begun to explore the notion that artistic expression can emerge independently of for-
026   mal training or exposure to traditional art forms. Outsider Art showcases the work of self-taught
027   individuals who, largely disconnected from the art world, create without the influence of established
028   conventions. A more specific subset, Art Brut, focuses on the raw, unfiltered creativity of those
029   entirely outside the established art scene—psychiatric patients, hermits, and spiritualists—people
030   whose art emerges purely from internal drives, uninformed by external artistic influences.
031
      Inspired by these movements, we simulate an “artificial artist” with minimal exposure to art. In this
032   synthetic experiment, we wanted to train a text-to-image model primarily on natural images, with
033   no exposure to visual art. Then adapt the model using a few examples from a specific artistic style
034   to study how well the adapted model can mimic and generalize that style across different contexts.
035
      Powerful text-to-image generators have already proved their ability to produce art, some even win-
036
      ning prestigious competitions (Kuta, 2022). However, their ability is typically attributed to extensive
037
      training on large datasets rich with visual art. These models are often so familiar with specific artists’
038   styles that they can replicate them simply by including the artist’s name in a text prompt (Heikkilä,
039   2022). This ease of replication has raised ethical concerns, sparking lawsuits from artists who argue
040   that generative models are imitating their work without permission (Dickstein & Delman, 2023).
041
      In this work, we challenge this paradigm by asking: Can a model with minimal prior exposure to art,
042
      but trained on a selected style, compare to these powerful models? Can artistic ability be achieved
043
      with just a handful of images, in a controlled manner? To explore this, we develop an art-agnostic
044   model (Art-Free Diffusion) that deliberately excludes prior knowledge of visual art. We create
045   an ”art-free” dataset (Art-Free SAM) using a rigorous filtering method based on both captions and
046   image content to ensure that no artistic elements are included 1 . Then, using the LoRA technique, we
047   introduce the Art Adapter, a controlled method of injecting approved artistic knowledge, enabling
048   the Art-Free model to generate art after this carefully authorized input.
049

082   2        R ELATED WORK
083
084   Text-to-image models have garnered significant attention and popularity, particularly with the ad-
085   vent of open-sourced diffusion models (Song et al., 2020; Song & Ermon, 2019; Rombach et al.,
086   2022a). These models have dramatically improved the quality and fidelity of generated images to
087
      user-defined prompts, revolutionizing the field of generative AI. Notably, generated images have not
      only gained acclaim by winning art competitions (Kuta, 2022), but they have also sparked contro-
088
      versy, leading to lawsuits from artists against companies releasing these models (Guadamuz, 2023).
089
      The concerns largely revolve around the models’ ability to replicate specific artists’ styles (Casper
090   et al., 2023) and their tendency to memorize training data (Somepalli et al., 2023a;b).
091
092   In response to these challenges, the computer vision community has proposed several mitiga-
093
      tion techniques. Opt-out strategies, allow the removal of specific concepts from model weights
      (Gandikota et al., 2023a;b; Kumari et al., 2023a; Hong et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024; Park et al., 2024;
094
      Pham et al., 2024; Lyu et al., 2024; Heng & Soh, 2023; Zhang et al., 2024) though these methods
095
      often struggle with scalability when dealing with a large number of concepts. It has been also shown
096   that the erased concepts can be re-introduced to the model (Pham et al., 2023). Industry initiatives
097   are also focusing on allowing individuals to opt-out from the training datasets entirely (Spawning
098   AI Team, 2023). However, the effectiveness and implementation of these strategies vary, and they
099   do not fully address the concerns of overfitting and unauthorized style replication. Another line of
100   work involves watermarking training images (Zhao et al., 2023; Min et al., 2024; Cui et al., 2023)
101   to provide traceability and protect intellectual property. While effective to some extent, these ap-
102   proaches differ from our work, which aims to explore the boundaries of what can be introduced into
103   models post-training rather than focusing on in-training safeguards.
104   In a related vein, Gokaslan et al. (Gokaslan et al., 2024) trained a text-to-image model exclusively on
105   Creative Commons (CC) images. While this approach aims to address ethical concerns by limiting
106   the training data to more permissive licenses, it does not fully resolve the issues, as CC-licensed
107   images can still include artworks that pose similar ethical challenges. In contrast, our work addresses
      both the ethical concerns raised by artists and the technical challenge of adapting a model that has

                                                                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      been trained exclusively on natural images to learn and generate artistic styles. This exploration
109   offers a distinct perspective on whether such a model can effectively incorporate and reproduce
110   artistic concepts post-training, providing a new avenue for ethical model development.
111
112
      The most analogous work to ours comes from the field of natural language processing (NLP), where
      Min et al. (Min et al., 2023) trained a large language model exclusively on a specific subset of data,
113
      later introducing an external database for task-specific applications. This approach aligns with our
114
      methodology in the sense that both explore the introduction of new data post-training, though our
115   focus is on visual rather than textual data.
116
117   Finally, there are also several initiatives dedicated to examining the sources of training data, em-
118
      phasizing transparency and ethical considerations in the development of AI models (Longpre et al.,
      2023). Our research contributes to this ongoing discourse by proposing a novel approach to inte-
119
      grating new artistic concepts into models after they have been trained, thereby offering a potential
120
      solution to the ethical concerns raised by artists and other stakeholders.
121
122   Transferring visual features from one image to another has long been a central topic in computer
123   vision. Image analogies (Hertzmann et al., 2023), for instance, use a pair of example images to
124
      demonstrate a desired transformation, which can then be applied to a new image to achieve similar
      visual effects. Likewise, image quilting (Efros & Freeman, 2023) transfers textures by stitching
125
      together small, local patches from a source image, much like assembling a quilt, to synthesize seam-
126
      less textures on a new canvas. Deep learning methods like Neural Style Transfer (Gatys, 2015) take
127   this further by using convolutional neural networks to extract and recombine deep feature represen-
128   tations of content and style, allowing an image’s content to be re-rendered in the artistic style of a
129   reference image. Our method extends beyond traditional texture transfer and image stylization, as
130   we adapt an image generator to a new domain, enabling both the sampling of entirely new images
131   and the stylization of existing ones.

134   3   A RT- FREE TEXT- TO - IMAGE DIFFUSION MODEL
135
136   Art-agnostic dataset. To train an art-agnostic text-to-image model, we require a large text-image
137   dataset that is “art-free”. Most commonly used datasets contain numerous examples of art and
138   paintings as diverse visual content is desirable for image generators. We leverage the SAM-LLava-
139   Captions10M dataset (Chen et al., 2023), which is derived from the SA-1B dataset (Kirillov et al.,
140   2023) primarily intended for object segmentation in natural, open-world images. The text captions
141
      for SAM dataset are generated by a Large Vision-Language Model (Llava). Prior work has shown
      that automatically generated captions can also also be effective for training text-to-image models
142
      (Chen et al., 2023).
143
144   Although the dataset primarily focuses on natural images, we find that it still included instances of
145   visual art, such as stamps, paintings, and other artistic elements. While the dataset may not have been
146   intentionally curated to include artworks, visual art is often difficult to avoid in real-world images.
147
      For example, we find photographs of tapestries and baroque architecture featuring artistic details
      that are “clearly art”. Moreover, artistic expression frequently appears in unexpected places, from
148
      sculptural designs to logos and branding on everyday objects. Our goal is to distinguish between
149
      visual art and natural imagery, ensuring that everyday scenes and objects were represented while
150   minimizing intentional artistic expression. We illustrate in Fig 2 where we draw the line between
151   an art image and not an art image, specifically, we focus on removing graphic arts, and leave other
152   forms of art such as architecture.
153
      To ensure that our training set is free from incidental visual art, we develop a two-stage filtering
154
      method. In the first stage, we implement text-based filtering by searching for specific terms in im-
155
      age captions that indicate the presence of visual art. We exclude images whose captions contain
156   keywords such as painting, art, or drawings. In the second stage, we compute a cosine similarity
157   alignment score between each image and a set of art-related terms using the CLIP score (Radford
158   et al., 2021). By manually sampling and ordering images by score for each term, we identify a
159   threshold beyond which the images no longer contained visual art. We refer the reader to Ap-
160   pendix A for further details of the filtering process and the comprehensive keyword list of the art
161   terms. Our resulting Art-Free SAM dataset, constructed from SAM-LLava-Captions10M, retains
      9,119,455 images after removing 4.7% through text-based filtering and 16.7% through image-based

      filtering. We designate 9,140 images as a validation set, yielding a final training dataset of 9,110,315
163   image-text pairs.

191   Model architecture. Our Art-Free Diffusion model is built on a latent diffusion architecture (Rom-
192   bach et al., 2022b) and has three main modules: the VAE encoder, the UNET, and the Text Encoder.
193   To ensure that no module of our model has been exposed to art, we train both the VAE and UNET
194
      from scratch with our Art-Free SAM dataset. The pretrained diffusion models usually use CLIP
      as the text encoder (Radford et al., 2021; Patashnik et al., 2021), which is trained contrastively to
195
      learn associations between images and text. Previous works (Kim et al., 2022) show that a CLIP
196
      embedding can manipulate images even in unseen domains. To prevent any art-related knowledge
197   from leaking through the text embeddings, we instead use a language-only Text Encoder based on
198   BERT (Devlin et al., 2019). While the BERT may contain some conceptual knowledge of art, its
199   training process has no access to any visual representations or pixel data containing art, ensuring
200   that the model remains art-free.

219   quality images by modeling data distributions through successive denoising steps. Intuitively, the
220   forward process incrementally introduces noise to the data, transforming it into Gaussian noise over
221   time. At any given time step, the relationship between the image and the noise can be expressed as:

226   process, the model is trained to predict and eliminate the noise ϵ at each time step to reconstruct the
227   original image. The learning objective can be formulated as:

250   By combining the reconstruction loss and the preservation loss, our goal is to enhance the model’s
251
      ability to learn and apply artistic styles while maintaining its ability to generate natural images when

252

253
      time, we have the ability to fine-tune the balance between style and content in the generated images
254   by carefully selecting the denoising time step at which the art knowledge is injected. This technique
255   allows us to control how much of the artistic style influences the final output. By injecting the
256   style information earlier in the denoising process, the generated image tends to be more stylized,
257   as the model has more time to integrate the artistic features throughout the denoising trajectory.
258   Conversely, injecting the style information at a later stage in the denoising process allows the natural
259   image features to dominate, producing images that retain more natural images with subtle stylistic
260   influences.

350   Implementation Details For the Art Style Adaptation, we use our Art Adapter with prior preserva-
351
      tion weight w = 50, and the LoRA rank of 1, incorporating the low-rank Adapters into all attention,
      linear, and convolution layers (for more details see Appendix B,Appendix C). The learning rate was
352
      set to 2e-4 using the AdamW optimizer, and we trained for 1,000 steps with a batch size of 5 and the
353
      DDIM noise scheduler. In the experiments, we use ‘sks’ as the V* token, which serves as a random
354   new token for learning a new art style concept. We select 10 artists and their works from WikiArt
355   to create 10 style sets, each with a distinct style. We manually choose 10 to 40 paintings from
356   each artist with similar color composition, brushstroke techniques, and artistic content to ensure the
357   artistic knowledge dataset has a consistent and coherent style.
358
      To evaluate the art style similarity we use the CSD score (Somepalli et al., 2024), for each sample we
359
      compute the mean score between a given generated image and the Art-Adaptation training images.
360   To measure the content fidelity we use the cosine similarity between content features in the generated
361   and original images (ViTc ), further evaluating content consistency or the CLIP score to measure the
362   text and image alignment.
363
      For evaluation, we sample 500 images from the Landscapes HQ(LHQ) dataset (Skorokhodov et al.,
364
      2021), with automatically generated text caption. Our experimental setup spans across the 10 style
365

514   Introducing the Art Adapter to the Artist To explore the artistic community’s reaction to AI-
515   generated art, we conduct an interview with the renowned artist Alan Kenny. Upon obtaining Alan’s
516   permission, we train an Art Adapter on 11 artworks showing his distinctive style. We describe our
517
      work and present Kenny with the generated images imitating his style.
518   In the interview, the artist expresses a blend of astonishment and familiarity when observing the
519   AI-generated art, remarking, “I didn’t expect [this quality] if you were using a base model of blank
520   canvas... you probably achieved more than I would have expected for a base model with no infor-
521   mation.” He acknowledges that the AI has captured aspects of his distinct style to the extent that, “if
522   you were to post some of these images online, I would get people texting me, ‘I see your images.’
523
      They would spot it, and I spot it.” Despite noting that “compositionally, it is weak” and contrasting
      this with his own “well thought and meticulous” compositions, he recognizes that “there are some
524
      very positive things” in the AI’s work.
525
526   The artist describes the experience as “terrifying and a bit exciting at the same time,” specifically
527   pointing out how the AI imitates his signature “gradation of the landscape” and ”gradation of the
528   shapes.” Though he felt his style is largely captured, he admits, “there is kind of originality to them...
529
      I see me in them, yes, very strongly... but there is an originality to some of the images.”

531   6    D ISCUSSION
532
      In this paper, we introduce the Art-Free Diffusion model, which explores the ability to mimic an
533
      artistic style with minimal exposure to art. We propose a simple method for training an Art Adaptor
534   to achieve this goal and evaluate its performance in image stylization and art generation tasks using
535   both automatic metrics and a perceptual user study. Our experiments demonstrate that the model can
536   successfully imitate artistic styles. Additionally, we consulted a professional artist to gather expert
537   feedback on how well the artificial model replicates his artistic style, further validating our findings.
538   To support our thesis, we applied a data attribution method to understand how a model with limited
539   knowledge of artistic styles can still produce artistic images. The results provide intuitive insights
      into how the natural world can influence and inspire art.
