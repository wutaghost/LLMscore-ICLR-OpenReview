009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            The autoregressive approach to text generation in large language models (LLMs),
012            while widely used, is inherently suboptimal due to the lack of a built-in mechanism
013            to perform refinement and/or correction of the generated content. In this paper, we
014            consider optimality in terms of the joint probability over the generated response,
015            when jointly considering all tokens at the same time. We theoretically characterize
016
               the potential deviation of the autoregressively generated response from its globally
               optimal counterpart that is of the same length. Our analysis suggests that we need
017
               to be cautious when noticeable uncertainty arises during text generation, which
018
               may signal the sub-optimality of the generation history. To address the pitfall of
019            autoregressive text generation, we propose an approach that incorporates a sliding
020            reflection window and a pausing criterion, such that reflection and generation can
021            be carried out interchangeably as the generation proceeds. Our approach utilizes
022            a selective refinement mechanism to strike the balance between efficiency and op-
023            timality, and the experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of our method.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Large language models (LLMs) have become increasingly popular in both academia and industry due
029   to their remarkable performance across a wide range of applications (Chang et al., 2023; Qin et al.,
030   2024; Wang et al., 2024). Currently, the most commonly used sampling approach for transformer-
031   based model (Vaswani et al., 2017) is the maximization-based autoregressive decoding (Lin et al.,
032   2020), where the next token is selected according to the highest conditional probability. Leveraging
033
      flexible neural network architectures, these models have demonstrated significant empirical success
      in various tasks (Pan et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023).
034
035   Despite significant progress in recent language text generation, how to approach the optimal text that
036   one can possibly decode from a language model still remains an open question. Autoregressive sam-
037   pling is limited by its tendency to converge to local solution, which hinders the ability to handle certain
038   complex tasks (Holtzman et al., 2019). Previous works have demonstrated challenges it faces when
039
      generating outputs involve deep logical structures (Welleck et al., 2022) and processing long sequences
      (Wu et al., 2021). Other than the standard autoregressive methods, Holtzman et al. (2019) proposed
040
      Top-p sampling (also known as nucleus sampling), a stochastic method that adjusts the next-token set
041
      based on the shape of the probability distribution. Alternatively, different from top-p sampling that
042   selects tokens until their cumulative probability exceeds a certain threshold, top-k sampling limits
043   selection to a fixed number of tokens (Radford et al., 2019; Holtzman et al., 2018; Fan et al., 2018).
044   Another empirical technique involves modulating the probability distribution using the temperature
045   hyperparameter to control randomness in the output (Caccia et al., 2018; Fan et al., 2018; Ficler &
046   Goldberg, 2017; Ackley et al., 1985). Additionally, efficient inference with beam search has also been
047   explored in the recent literature (Xie et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024b).
048   Other than sampling approaches, previous literature has also explored improving generated contents
049   through self-correction and see if LLMs can refine responses based on their own outputs, leveraging
050   either external or internal feedback (Pan et al., 2023; Shinn et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2022; Madaan et al.,
051   2023; Bai et al., 2022; Ganguli et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023c; Kim et al., 2023; Tyen et al., 2023).
052   Numerous studies have demonstrated effective self-correction performance through the integration
053   of external feedback generated by the learned critic models (Paul et al., 2023; Akyürek et al., 2023;
      Welleck et al., 2022; Peng et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2023) or interacting with human or tools (Gou et al.,

142
      As we have seen in Section 2.1, one inherent shortcoming of the autoregressive way of text generation
      is the lack of built-in mechanism to correct or refine generated content, so that the overall response
143
      satisfies requirements or objectives that operate jointly over all involved tokens. In this subsection,
144
      we present a high-level summary of our approach as an attempt to address this issue.
145
146   Considering the fact that the text typically flows in a single dimension, i.e., from the start to the end,
147   there is a natural linear structure (of words, phrases, and sentences) in text generation. This linear
148
      structure differentiates text from other forms of objects that occupy multiple dimensional spaces, e.g.,
      images or videos. Therefore, taking advantage of this linear structure, we propose an approach to
149
      perform text generation and reflection at the same time as the output unfolds. There are two different
150
      modules: (1) a criterion that specifies whether we should pause the generation since we need to
151   correct/refine generated content, and (2) a mechanism that allows LLMs to always reflect on what was
152   generated and to decide whether a revision is necessary (according to the aforementioned criterion).
153
154
      As illustrated in Figure 2, given a pausing criterion, we can use fast and slow pointers on the
      generated content to form segments of a certain length, namely, the sliding reflection window, and
155
      perform potential refinements within this sliding window as the text generation proceeds.2 Notice that
156
      the reflection window mechanism does not require the completion of the entire LLM output before
157   the reflection can kick in. This is favorable for several reasons. To begin with, one do not have to wait
158   until the entire response to be finished before starting improving the content. Besides, if there are

333   Refinement Process When the pausing criterion is met in t-th step, the model will be looped back
334
      by d − 1 steps. The model have to regenerate previous d tokens in the reflection window. Since beam
      search can approximate the global optimum well, especially when the sequence length is fixed, we
335

336

525   6                  C ONCLUSION
526
527   In this paper, we theoretically characterize one inherent shortcoming, among others, of the autore-
528   gressive way of text generation in LLMs. In particular, we show that when the optimality is defined
529   in terms of the joint probability over all generated tokens, an oracle LLM can still potentially de-
530   viate from the globally optimal response of the same length. To mitigate the sub-optimality of the
531   autoregressive way of text generation, we propose an empirical approach guided by our theoretical
532
      characterization. We incorporate a sliding reflection window and a pausing criterion so that reflection
      and generation can be performed interchangeably. Our experimental results demonstrate that the
533
      reflection window strategy achieves significant improvement over greedy decoding in inference-
534
      intensive settings and maintains performance that is comparable, or even superior to, beam search
535   while being more efficient.
