009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce a new framework for analyzing classification datasets based on the
012            ratios of reconstruction errors between autoencoders trained on individual classes.
013            This analysis framework enables efficient characterization of datasets on the sam-
014            ple, class, and entire dataset levels. We define reconstruction error ratios (RERs)
015            that probe classification difficulty and allow its decomposition into (1) finite sam-
016            ple size and (2) Bayes error and decision-boundary complexity. Through system-
017            atic study across 19 popular visual datasets, we find that our RER-based dataset
018
               difficulty probe strongly correlates with error rate for state-of-the-art (SOTA) clas-
               sification models. By interpreting sample-level classification difficulty as a la-
019
               bel mistakenness score, we further find that RERs achieve SOTA performance on
020
               mislabel detection tasks on hard datasets under symmetric and asymmetric label
021            noise.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Data is the cornerstone of modern machine learning. As the data-centric AI movement has made
027   increasingly clear, both predictive and generative ML models rely on sufficiently large and diverse
028   high-quality datasets (Deng et al., 2009b; Radford et al., 2018; Kaplan et al., 2020). However,
029   it is well known that even popular visual datasets like CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky & Hinton, 2009),
030   Caltech-256 (Griffin et al., 2007), and ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009b) can have hundreds or thou-
031   sands of data quality issues, including up to 10% label errors (Northcutt et al., 2021). Consequently,
032
      curating a high-quality dataset requires not only data collection but also data cleaning, characteriza-
      tion, evaluation, and refinement.
033
034   Nevertheless, existing methods for data quality assessment are inherently limited. Methods that seek
035   to estimate the classification difficulty of a sample or dataset are either model-dependent (Ethayarajh
036   et al., 2021), computationally infeasible (Scheidegger et al., 2021), or break down when applied
037   to challenging datasets (Zhang et al., 2020). Likewise, mislabel detection methods either rely on
038
      training a strong classifier on the dataset (Pruthi et al., 2020; Pleiss et al., 2020), which becomes
      more time and compute-intensive for more complex datasets, or exhibit degraded performance on
039
      datasets with complex decision boundaries (Zhu et al., 2021; Northcutt et al., 2021).
040
041   To address these limitations, we propose a novel approach for characterizing the difficulty of clas-
042   sification datasets by decomposing complex multi-class classification problems into one manifold
043   learning problem for each class. Explicitly, we generate a feature vector for each sample from a
044
      foundation model like CLIP ViT-B/32 (Radford et al., 2021), train a shallow autoencoder on the
      feature vectors for each class. We call these autoencoders reconstructors, as they are used to capture
045
      how well a new sample is reconstructed by the shallow model. We then compute the reconstruction
046
      error for each sample with respect to each reconstructor, and use ratios of these reconstruction errors
047   to estimate the difficulty of individual samples, classes, subsets, and entire datasets.
048
049   This method, which we call Reconstruction Error Ratios (RERs), is theoretically motivated, intu-
050
      itive, and offers several key advantages:
051   Efficiency: Reconstructors can be trained in seconds, and training and inference can be parallelized
052   over CPU cores. Further acceleration can be achieved with minimal reduction in performance by
053   fitting the reconstructors on a fraction of the data — in many cases we observe SOTA performance
      when fitting on just 100 samples per class.


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Interpretability: RERs allow us to compare the relative difficulty of specific samples, entire classes,
055   data subsets, and entire datasets. They enable dataset-wide error rate estimation, and provide prin-
056   cipled label mistake probabilities for each sample.
057
058
      Generality: RERs provide a unified pipeline for processing datasets of different sizes and modalities,
      and work with features from any foundation model. They also extend readily to challenging datasets
059
      and datasets with arbitrarily many classes.
060
061   RERs perform remarkably well in both classification difficulty and mislabel detection tasks.
062   Through a comprehensive study across 19 visual datasets, we demonstrate strong correlations be-
063   tween RER-based difficulty measures and state-of-the-art classification error rates. By interpreting
064
      sample difficulty scores as mislabel likelihood scores and employing a simple threshold ansatz to
      classify samples as mistaken, we find that RERs outperform other feature-based mislabel detection
065
      techniques under various noise conditions.
066
067   Our primary contributions are as follows:
068
            1. A formal framework for applying Reconstruction Error Ratios for dataset analysis.
069
070         2. Empirical validation of RERs as a measure of the difficulty of classification.
071         3. A method for decomposing classification difficulty into distinct components representing
072            finite-size contributions and Bayes error and decision-boundary contributions.
073         4. Demonstration of RERs’ efficacy in mislabel detection tasks.
074
075   We believe that this work is a significant step forward in the direction of principled dataset analysis.

077   2     BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
078
079   Our work intersects with several areas of machine learning research, including dataset difficulty
080   assessment, autoencoder applications, and mislabel detection. In this section, we review relevant
081   literature in these domains and contextualize our contributions.
082
083   2.1   DATASET D IFFICULTY
084
085
      Understanding and quantifying the difficulty of classification tasks has long been a challenge in
      machine learning. Early work in the visual domain by (Ionescu et al., 2016) focused on human
086
      response times as a measure of image classification difficulty. While informative, this approach is
087
      not scalable and does not address dataset-level challenges.
088
089   (Ho & Basu, 2002) propose using geometric properties of datasets to assess difficulty, but focused
090   primarily on binary classification tasks in low-dimensional feature spaces. Through a UMAP graph-
091
      layout loss term, our method also utilizes geometric information to estimate dataset difficulty, and
      generalizes well to classification problems with many classes in high-dimensional feature spaces.
092
093   More recently, information-theoretic approaches like DIME (Zhang et al., 2020) and V-Usable In-
094   formation (Ethayarajh et al., 2021) have shown promise. However, the former gives only upper
095   bounds, ruling out strict ordering, and the latter is model-dependent, limiting its generalizability. Fi-
096   nally, (Scheidegger et al., 2021) explore using silhouette scores and FID scores for dataset difficulty
097
      assessment and introduce shallow classifiers called probe nets whose error correlate strongly with
      larger classification models. Our RERs are defined similarly to their silhouette score-based difficulty
098
      scores, offer faster computation than any of these methods, are more interpretable, and correlate as
099
      if not more strongly with error rate of state-of-the-art models.
100
101
      2.2   AUTOENCODERS AND T HEIR A PPLICATIONS
102
103   Autoencoders have a rich history in machine learning, dating back to the work of (Rumelhart et al.,
104   1986; Bourlard & Kamp, 1988; Hinton & Zemel, 1993). They have been used for dimensionality
105   reduction, feature learning, and generative modeling. Variants such as denoising autoencoders (Vin-
106   cent et al., 2008) and variational autoencoders (VAEs) (Kingma & Welling, 2022) have further ex-
107   panded their capabilities, and they are even used in the pretraining of diffusion models (Rombach
      et al., 2021).


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Autoencoders have also been used in the context of visual anomaly detection, where autoencoders
109   trained on normal data can identify anomalous samples by their high reconstruction errors. Our work
110   differs by using class-wise autoencoders to assess intra-class and inter-class similarities, focusing on
111   classification difficulty rather than anomaly detection. Furthermore, we perform autoencoding on
112   the features from a foundation model like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and DINOv2 (Oquab et al.,
113   2024), rather than on images themselves.
114
115   2.3   M ISLABEL D ETECTION
116
117   Mislabel detection seeks to identify erroneous labels in a dataset, with approaches falling into two
118   main categories: (1) feature-based approaches like SimiFeat (Zhu et al., 2021) and (2) training-based
119   approaches like (Pleiss et al., 2020) and TracIn (Pruthi et al., 2020), which are time-intensive and
120
      require access to the training dynamics.
121   Confident Learning (Northcutt et al., 2021) is a popular approach that uses any classifier trained on a
122   given dataset to estimate the joint distribution of noisy and true labels. A feature-oriented variant of
123   Confident Learning was recently found to achieve comparable performance when training a simple
124   logistic regression classifier on CLIP features (Srikanth et al., 2023b).
125   Like (Zhu et al., 2021) and (Srikanth et al., 2023b), our RER-based approach is feature-based,
126   but it differs from these methods by decomposing high-dimensional classification tasks into low-
127   dimensional class-specific manifold learning problems, offering an efficient alternative that achieves
128   better performance on hard datasets.

      While on average we expect ∆c (xc ) < ∆c (xc ), this will not always be the case. Our dataset may
      have epistemic uncertainty or ambiguously labeled samples, complex decision boundaries between
355
      classes, or even mislabeled samples. RERs provide a pathway to estimating these contributions to
356
      dataset difficulty as well.
357
358   In Appendix B.2 we show that χ increases as a function of the noise in the dataset. Empirically,
359   we verify this across all 19 datasets over a wide range of noise rates and types. Fig. 5 shows this
360
      dependence for symmetric, asymmetric, and confidence-based label noise.
361   We can make sense of these trends as follows: when we add a mistake via symmetric noise, we
362   convert an example that almost certainly would not have had χ(x) > 1 instead of an example that
363   almost certainly will have χ(x) > 1 so we add substantial error to the dataset. When we add
364   confidence-based noise, we are converting examples near class decision boundaries into mistakes.
365   On average, each confidence-based label mistake contributes less to the change in estimated noise.
366
      For asymmetric noise, transition matrix elements with nonzero entries are random, so at low noise
      rates we get the same behavior as symmetric noise. As we increase the amount of asymmetric noise,
367
      we significantly shift decision boundaries such that examples in asymmetrically connected classes
368
      become even more strongly tied together than confidence-based noise. As such, the contribution to
369   estimated noise from asymmetric label mistakes decreases with the amount of noise added.

474   We test RER-based mislabel detection on four types of label noise: symmetric, asymmetric,
475
      confidence-based, and human annotator-based, defined as follows:
476   Symmetric: With probability η, a label ci is swapped uniformly where a label cj , with i ̸= j.
477
      Asymmetric: With probability η, label ci is changed to ci+1 modulo the number of classes.
478
479   Confidence-Based: A classifier is trained on the clean labels and used to run inference on the sam-
480   ples. For a given sample with label ci , with probability η the label is changed to the highest likeli-
481   hood incorrect label predicted by the classifier for that sample.
482   Human Annotator-Based: A single human annotator assigns a label to each sample. This label
483   is mistaken when it is in disagreement with the ground truth label resulting from aggregation and
484   validation of human annotations. Mistakes from this set are randomly selected until η (which must
485   be less than or equal to the fraction of human annotator errors in the entire dataset) of the samples
      are assigned mistaken labels.

486
      We compare RERs to the two best prior feature-based approaches: SimiFeat (Zhu et al., 2021)
487   and a feature-based variant of Confident Learning (Srikanth et al., 2023a), as well as a zero-shot
488   baseline, which we detail in Appendix A.3.2. We restrict ourselves to realistic noise regimes 0 ≤
489   η ≤ 0.3, where at most 30% of labels are corrupted. We find that in this regime Confident Learning
490   outperforms SimiFeat, and human annotator noise behaves nearly identically to confidence-based
491   noise, so we omit these from plots for simplicity. Performance of RERs, Confident Learning, and
492   zero-shot mislabel detection are shown in Fig. 6, where RER-based mislabel detection is found to
493   consistently match or outperform all other feature-based methods under symmetry and asymmetric
494   label noise when η < 0.3.
495   Taking threshold selection out of the equation, we also compute the area under the ROC curve
496   (AUROC) for each dataset and noise setting, giving us a more complete picture of the strengths
497   and weaknesses of each method. Illustrative AUROC curves for specific datasets are included in
498   Fig. 13 in Appendix C.2. More generally, we find that RER-based mislabel detection consistently
499   achieves higher AUROC scores for symmetric and asymmetric noise on hard datasets, which we
500   define as datasets with SOTA classification accuracy < 0.95. Below we explain this by appealing to
501
      how Confident Learning and RERs work. We also note that AUROC scores obtained by RER-based
      mislabel detection are robust to the number of samples used to fit each reconstructor, stabilizing to
502
      near-optimal levels around 100 samples per class, as we demonstrate in Appendix C.2.
503
504   Easy vs Hard Datasets: Confident Learning trains a simple classifier and then assigns a label quality
505   score based on the confidence of that classifier. If a classification dataset is easy, then even a simple
506   classifier trained on rich features will be able to precisely learn class decision boundaries. RERs on
507
      the other hand train a separate reconstructor for each class. No reconstructor has explicit knowledge
      about other classes in the dataset. This makes the problem of mislabel detection more tractable by
508
      approximately decomposing it on a class-wise basis. For hard datasets, the tradeoff is well worth it,
509
      but for easy datasets the approximate decomposition may be substantial.
510
511   (A)symmetric vs Confidence-Based Noise: Reconstructors’ lack of explicit interclass awareness also
512   makes them especially susceptible to confidence-based noise, which perniciously persuade the re-
513
      constructions to learn class manifolds with slightly different shapes. Incorporating dataset-level
      awareness into the reconstructor training process is left for future work.
514
515   Probabilistic Interpretation: In addition to ranking samples according to their mistakenness and
516   assigning binary clean/dirty labels, we also show in Appendix D.1 that RER mistakenness scores
517   can be converted into mistakenness probabilities, reflecting consistent and accurate likelihoods that
518   a given sample has a mistaken label. Furthermore, in Appendix D.2 we demonstrate that these
519
      probabilities are meaningful by way of a new metric which we call the confidence-weighted F1 -
      score. Given these probabilities, one could make more informed decisions about how many samples
520
      to send for reannotation to ensure a predetermined level of data quality on a fixed budget.

523   6   C ONCLUSION
524
525   In this work, we introduced Reconstruction Error Ratios (RERs), a novel framework for analyzing
526   classification datasets using class-wise autoencoders which we call reconstructors. This approach
527   is fast, intuitive, interpretable, and model-agnostic, leveraging rich foundation model features and
528   shallow autoencoders to enhance data curation and enable cross-dataset comparison.
529   Through a comprehensive analysis of 19 visual classification datasets varying in size and number of
530   classes, we verify that RER-based dataset characteristics correlate strongly with SOTA classification
531   model performance. Furthermore, we find that RER-based dataset difficulty behaves predictably as
532   a function of the number of samples per class, providing useful information for dataset-reduction
533   tasks like pruning an dataset-enhancement tasks like collection or annotation of unlabeled data.
534   Subsequently, we demonstrate that RERs not only allow estimation of dataset-level noise rates, but
535   also enable competitive detection of label mistakes. Along the way, we highlight applications in
536
      pruning, data collection, reannotation, and model selection.
537   While our current work focused on visual classification datasets, the principles underlying RERs
538   are domain-independent. As such, the RER framework should be applicable to classification tasks
539   in text, audio, time-series data, or even activity recognition. Future work will also extend RERs to
      derive dataset difficulty estimates for object detection or segmentation tasks.

782
783   This section details UMAP and Autoencoder hyperparameters and training details.
784   Training and hyperparameters: Autoencoders with UMAP regularization loss are trained using the
785   ParametricUMAP class from the umap-learn library (Sainburg et al., 2021). The encoder and de-
786   coder are defined in keras and each have one hidden layer. Small l2 -regularization and dropout
787   are found to stabilize performance. ReLu activations are used for intermediate layers, and a sig-
788   moid activation function is used after the last layer in the decoder. The number of training epochs
789   is set to nepochs = 20, but early stopping consistently occurs before that, as the loss converges
790   quickly. Training is performed on CPU. Default hyperparameters used are detailed in Table 1. Sys-
791
      tematic ablations lead us to the conclusion that variations in the number of components, dropout,
      regularization, and hidden layer dimension are largely inconsequential, resulting in no downstream
792
      performance changes beyond random chance. Aside from spread and min dist (detailed below), the
793
      most significant hyperparameter choices are the number of neighbors for UMAP and the relative
794   weighting of the parametric reconstruction loss (relative to UMAP loss) in the autoencoder train-
795   ing. Hyperparameter sweeps for both are shown in Fig. 7, and in both cases, values obtained from
796   RER-based difficulty estimation are found to robustly stabilize for sufficiently large hyperparamater
797   values.
798
      Spread and min dist: The only hyperparameters on which reconstruction error is found to depend
799
      strongly are the spread and min dist, which together control how tightly points are packed into the
800   latent space. min dist is defined relative to spread, and we find that ratios close to one are near opti-
801   mal. Intuitively, we believe that regularizing autoencoders using large spread and minimum distance
802   between embedded points has a similar effect to KL-divergence in that it encourages exploration of
803   the latent space. We note that the spread and min dist values that are found to work best result in
804   negative values for UMAP’s a and b force hyperparameters. As a result, the Python library throws
805   warnings, but these do not hinder the resulting autoencoder’s ability to represent in-distribution data.
806   Using positive values of a and b results in a more well-behaved loss landscape but slightly dimin-
807   ished performance at mislabel detection. On the rare occasion that autoencoder training with spread
808
      = 25 and min dist = 24 threw an error, training was retried with spread = 24 and min dist = 23.

1064
1065
       All results in the body of the paper utilize features generated from either CLIP ViT-B/32 or CLIP
1066
       ViT-L/14 vision encoders. However, the reconstruction error ratio framework is not specific to
1067
       CLIP-style models. We demonstrate this explicitly by computing χ for all 18 non-ImageNet datasets
1068   from the main text using features from five models. We report the correlations between the dataset
1069   difficulty scores estimated with these five sets of features in Fig. 10. Note that we exclude ImageNet
1070   from this analysis, as the ResNet model we probe was pretrained on ImageNet, which could lead to
1071   unfair comparison. Given the generality of our findings, we also expect that RERs are intimately
1072   related to classification margins, among the varied signals that are captured by the RER framework.
1073   We plan to formalize this connection in future work.
1074   Beyond correlation on the dataset level, we find that various CLIP and DINOv2 backbones produce
1075   reconstruction error ratios that align well on the class and sample levels. Concretely, we analyze
1076   how consistent the rankings are across features by computing the Spearman Rank correlation and
1077   the normalized discounted cumulative gain (nDCG). Both metrics are computed on the sample level
1078   by taking the χ(x) for each sample, and are computed on the class level by taking the average χ
1079   value across all samples with a specific ground truth label, χc = Ex∈X c [χ(x)]. We choose the
       Spearman Rank correlation rather than the Kendall τ because the latter depends strongly on the


                                                                         20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       number of elements in the set to be ranked, leading to values that vary widely from dataset to dataset
1081   based on the number of samples and the number of classes. The results for CIFAR10 are shown
1082   in the first and second heatmaps in Fig. 11, demonstrating moderate-to-strong correlation between
1083   features.
1084
       We believe that these rank correlations alone undersell the effective alignment in RER ordering
1085
       between features, as in practice, the most important samples for mislabel detection are the highest-
1086
       scoring samples. To draw out this aspect, we look at the nDCG, which gives more weight to higher
1087   scoring samples (elements at the top of the ranking). Before computing the nDCG, we perform min-
1088   max normalization on the scores generated by each feature backbone. For both sample-wise (third
1089   heatmap) and class-wise ordering (fourth heatmap) in Fig. 11, we see very similar rankings across
1090   all CLIP and DINOv2 models. We also perform this same analysis on all 19 datasets in our study
1091   and present the results for CLIP ViT-B/32 ↔ DINOv2-B in Table 3, underlining the generality of
1092   this finding.
