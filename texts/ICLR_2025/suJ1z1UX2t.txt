                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Self-supervised learning has emerged as an effective pre-training strategy for
010
               representation learning using large-scale unlabeled data. However, models pre-
011            trained with self-supervised learning still require supervised fine-tuning to achieve
012            optimal task-specific performance. Due to the lack of label utilization, it is dif-
013            ficult to accurately distinguish between positive and hard negative samples. Su-
014            pervised contrastive learning methods address the limitation by leveraging labels,
015            but they focus on global representations, leading to limited feature diversity and
016            high cross-correlation between representation dimensions. To address these chal-
017            lenges, we propose Supervised Dimension Contrastive Learning, a novel approach
018            that combines supervision with dimension-wise contrastive learning. Inspired by
019
               redundancy reduction techniques like Barlow Twins, this approach reduces cross-
               correlation between embedding dimensions while enhancing class discriminabil-
020
               ity. The aggregate function combines the embedding dimensions to generate pre-
021
               dicted class variables, which are optimized to correlate with their corresponding
022            class labels. Orthogonal regularization is applied to ensure the full utilization of
023            all dimensions by enforcing full-rankness in the aggregate function. We evaluate
024            our method on both in-domain supervised classification tasks and out-of-domain
025            transfer learning tasks, demonstrating its superior performance compared to tra-
026            ditional supervised learning, supervised contrastive learning, and self-supervised
027            learning methods. Our results show that the proposed method effectively reduces
028            inter-dimensional correlation and enhances class discriminability, proving its gen-
029            eralizability across various downstream tasks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Recent advances in self-supervised learning (Chen et al., 2020; Zbontar et al., 2021; Caron et al.,
034
      2020; 2021) have demonstrated its effectiveness as a pre-training method using large-scale unlabeled
035
      data. This approach leverages data augmentation to generate semantically similar examples and then
036   aligns the representations of the two examples. Despite its success, models pre-trained through self-
037   supervised learning still require fine-tuning with labeled data to achieve sufficient performance on
038   specific tasks. Moreover, relying solely on data augmentation and sample discrimination limits the
039   ability to distinguish between positive and hard negative samples. effectively (Robinson et al., 2020;
040   Wu et al., 2020).
041
      With the growing availability of large labeled datasets, e.g. JFT-300M (Sun et al., 2017), supervised
042   representation learning has gained importance. By directly utilizing labels, supervised methods can
043   capture semantically meaningful relationships between data points without heavily depending on
044   data augmentation. Contrastive learning has shown promising results in supervised frameworks,
045   similar to its success in the field of self-supervised learning. Supervised contrastive learning meth-
046   ods (Khosla et al., 2020; Zha et al., 2024; Cui et al., 2021; 2023) encourage representations of
047   data points from the same class to cluster closely while pushing apart representations from differ-
048   ent classes in the representation space. It demonstrated superior performance in classification tasks
049   compared to both self-supervised learning and traditional cross-entropy-based approaches.
050   Despite these advantages, supervised contrastive learning methods primarily focus on global repre-
051   sentations and do not induce the learning of additional discriminative features, once different classes
052   are sufficiently separated. This results in a lack of feature diversity, as evidenced by the high cross-
053   correlation between dimensions of the learned representations in Figure 1. Consequently, supervised
      contrastive learning methods may underperform in tasks that require diverse feature representations

081   In this work, we propose a method called Supervised Dimension Contrastive Learning (SupDCL), a

083   SupDCL reduces cross-correlation between embedding dimensions while simultaneously enhanc-
084   ing class discriminability within each dimension. Specifically, we introduce a new concept called
085
      discriminativeness, which reflects how strongly each embedding dimension contributes to class sep-
      arability. Our method ensures that each dimension is trained to be discriminative, meaning it cor-
086
      relates with specific class variables, enhancing its ability to distinguish between different classes.
087
      We employ the aggregate function combining the embedding dimensions to generate predicted class
088   variables that are trained to correlate with their corresponding class labels. To ensure that the ag-
089   gregate function utilizes all dimensions without exclusion, we apply orthogonal regularization. It
090   enforces the function to be full-rank thereby leveraging the entire embedding space.
091

092
      task are defined by the same labels used during training, and out-domain transfer learning tasks,
093
      where the dataset and task differ from those encountered during pre-training. Our results show
094   that SupDCL outperforms self-supervised learning methods in in-domain classification tasks and
095   achieves results comparable to supervised contrastive methods. Moreover, in out-domain transfer
096   learning, SupDCL consistently outperforms all other methods, demonstrating its superior general-
097   ization capability across various downstream tasks. The main contributions of this work are sum-
098   marized as follows:
099
100   • We identify a limitation in existing supervised representation learning, specifically in supervised
101
        contrastive learning methods, which suffers from high cross-correlation between dimensions,
        leading to limited feature diversity and suboptimal generalization.
102
103   • We introduce SupDCL, a novel method that applies supervision to dimension contrastive learning,
104     reducing inter-dimensional correlation while enhancing discriminative ability.
105   • The proposed method outperforms self-supervised learning methods for in-domain classification
106     tasks and achieves comparable results to supervised contrastive methods. For out-domain transfer
107     learning, SupDCL surpasses all methods, demonstrating its ability to generalize effectively across
        diverse datasets.

      2.1   S ELF - SUPERVISED L EARNING
111
112   Recent advancements in self-supervised learning leverage the potential of unlabeled data, draw-
113   ing significant attention to various methodologies (Chen et al., 2020; He et al., 2020; Caron et al.,
114   2020; Grill et al., 2020; Chen & He, 2021; Caron et al., 2021; Zbontar et al., 2021; Bardes et al.,
115   2021). Self-supervised methods are typically categorized into three main directions: contrastive,
116   non-contrastive, and dimension contrastive learning, each addressing robust learning challenges.
117   Contrastive learning focuses on instance-level pairwise similarity by attracting positive pairs (aug-
118   mented versions of the same instance) and repelling negative pairs (different instances). Methods
119   like SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020) show the effectiveness of such learning but require large batches to
120   provide sufficient negatives. MoCo (He et al., 2020) addresses this issue with a memory bank and
121   momentum encoder for efficient negative sampling. These approaches excel at learning representa-
122   tions invariant to augmentations, enabling strong out-domain transfer performance.
123   Non-contrastive methods (Grill et al., 2020; Chen & He, 2021; Caron et al., 2021) avoid negatives,
124   addressing the collapse problem where representations converge to trivial solutions. BYOL (Grill
125   et al., 2020) and SimSiam (Chen & He, 2021) use asymmetric architectures with predictors and
126   stop-gradient mechanisms to prevent collapse. DINO (Caron et al., 2021) aligns student-teacher
127   networks via knowledge distillation, emphasizing consistency without contrastive objectives.
128
      Dimension contrastive learning methods (Zbontar et al., 2021; Bardes et al., 2021; Caron et al.,
129   2021) focus on relationships between embedding dimensions rather than instances. Barlow
130   Twins (Zbontar et al., 2021) minimizes correlation between dimensions to enforce independence,
131   while VICReg (Bardes et al., 2021) combines invariance, variance, and covariance terms to balance
132   dimension-level and global relationships. The duality between contrastive and dimension contrastive
133   learning (Garrido et al., 2022) highlights their complementary strengths.
134
135   2.2   S UPERVISED L EARNING
136
137   Supervised learning methods commonly rely on cross-entropy loss for classification tasks. However,
138   its limitations, including sensitivity to class imbalance and reliance on instance-specific predictions,
139   are well-documented (Elsayed et al., 2018; Cao et al., 2019; Zhang & Sabuncu, 2018). Studies
140   propose alternatives involving label distribution modifications (Müller et al., 2019; Szegedy et al.,
141
      2016) and advanced augmentations like MixUp (Zhang et al., 2017).
142   Contrastive-based supervised learning extends self-supervised methods by incorporating class in-
143   formation. SupCon (Khosla et al., 2020) generalizes contrastive learning by treating all same-class
144   samples as positive pairs, improving generalization compared to cross-entropy. PaCo (Cui et al.,
145   2021) and GPaCo (Cui et al., 2023) propose class-wise learnable centers, validating their effec-
146   tiveness under class-balanced and imbalanced settings. Despite these advancements, supervised
147
      representation learning approaches often fail to address feature redundancy and diversity in learned
      representations, limiting their out-domain performance.
148
149   Our work bridges this gap by proposing a novel framework that incorporates dimension decorre-
150   lation and class-specific discriminativeness to learn generalizable features. Unlike SupCon, which
151   emphasizes global relationships between instances within the same class, our method optimizes
152   dimension-level independence while explicitly maximizing class-related information, achieving
153
      strong transfer performance comparable to self-supervised methods.

339   4.2.1     R EDUNDANCY IN R EPRESENTATION
340
341   Measuring the number of decorrelated dimensions is crucial as it reflects how effectively a model
342   reduces redundancy across dimensions, ensuring that each dimension contributes unique infor-
343   mation rather than duplicating the information captured by other dimensions. For a representa-
344   tion Y = (R1 , . . . , RD ), we classify two dimensions Ri and Rj as redundant if their correla-
345   tion Cor[Ri , Rj ] exceeds a redundancy threshold τr . Dimensions are considered decorrelated if
346
      Cor[Ri , Rj ] < τr for all i ̸= j. The total number of decorrelated dimensions is calculated by
      grouping correlated dimensions based on a redundancy threshold τr . We construct a graph G(V, E),
347
      where V = {1, 2, . . . , D} represents the dimensions of Y , and E = {(i, j) : |Cor[Ri , Rj ]| ≥ τr }
348
      defines edges between dimensions i and j if their correlation exceeds τr . The total number of decor-
349   related groups corresponds to the number of connected components in G. This ensures that highly
350   correlated dimensions are grouped together, and the resulting count reflects the effective number of
351   independent, decorrelated groups.
352
      As shown in Table 2, with τr = 0.1, SupDCL-1024 achieves the highest number of decorrelated
353
      dimensions with 57, surpassing Barlow Twins with 48. At τr = 0.2, Barlow Twins achieves the
354
      highest count with 904, while SupDCL-1024 achieves 699, outperforming SupCon with 532 and
355   the supervised baseline with 39. This demonstrates that SupDCL effectively balances decorrelation
356   while retaining discriminative power, a key advantage over previous methods.

         5                D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
470
471
472
         We introduce Supervised Dimension Contrastive Learning (SupDCL), a novel method that applies
         supervision to dimension contrastive learning, reducing redundancy across embedding dimensions
473
         and improving the discriminativeness of the learned representations. By applying discriminative-
474
         ness loss, we ensure that each dimension contributes to class discrimination, while simultaneously
475      promoting augmentation invariance and dimensional decorrelation. Our approach demonstrates su-
476      perior performance across in-domain and out-domain tasks compared to existing self-supervised
477      and supervised methods. Additionally, our analysis confirms that the proposed method functions as
478      intended, further validating its effectiveness.
479
480      Limitations. Despite these successes, our approach relies on the use of fixed-form class labels
481      for supervision. This limitation suggests potential avenues for future work. Expanding SupDCL to
482      handle more diverse forms of supervision, such as missing or noisy labels, or even non-categorical
483      and multi-task labels, could further enhance its generalizability. Moreover, exploring the integration
484      of continuous or hierarchical labels, and even weak supervision, could broaden the applicability
485      of our method across different domains and tasks. Addressing these challenges will be critical in
         developing a more robust, flexible, and widely applicable representation learning framework.

771   2008), Caltech101 (Fei-Fei et al., 2004), Cars (Krause et al., 2013), Aircraft (Maji et al., 2013),
772   DTD (Cimpoi et al., 2014), SUN397 (Xiao et al., 2010). We follow the standard linear transfer
773   evaluation protocol (Sun et al., 2017), training linear classifiers on frozen features from 224×224 re-
774   sized images without data augmentation. We use L-BFGS to minimize ℓ2 -regularized cross-entropy
775   loss, selecting the regularization parameter from 45 logarithmically spaced values between 10−6
776   and 105 via the validation set. Then, the linear classifier is retrained on both training and validation
777   data with test accuracy.

      To validate our analysis of the redundancy and discriminativeness of representation dimensions, as
813   detailed in Table 2, we provide UMAP-based (McInnes et al., 2018) visualizations of the learned
814   representations to clarify the role of each dimension. These visualizations enable low-dimensional
815   projection while preserving the structure of high-dimensional data, offering insight into how the
816   learned representations encode class-relevant information and exhibit decorrelation.
817
      Dataset and Classes. We utilize a subset of ImageNet known as the ”16-class-ImageNet” (Geirhos
818
      et al., 2018), which maps ImageNet categories to 16 entry-level superclasses: airplane, bear, bicy-
819   cle, bird, boat, bottle, car, cat, chair, clock, dog, elephant, keyboard, knife, oven, and truck. This
820   mapping provides semantically diverse yet interpretable class groups for our analysis.
821
822
      Visualization Setup. Representations are extracted from the validation set of ImageNet-1K and
      mapped to two dimensions using UMAP with the following hyperparameters: the number of neigh-
823
      bors is set to 5, the minimum distance is 0.8, and the distance metric is Euclidean. For the visu-
824
      alizations, we use thresholds τd = 0.1 for discriminative dimensions, and τr = 0.2, τd = 0.1 for
825   decorrelated discriminative dimensions. The visualizations analyze four categories of dimensions:
826   (1) whole dimensions, (2) discriminative dimensions, (3) non-discriminative dimensions, and (4)
827   decorrelated discriminative dimensions.
828
      For whole dimensions, discriminative dimensions, and decorrelated discriminative dimensions, Fig-
829
      ures A1, A2, A3, and A4 demonstrate that the learned representations effectively separate the data
830
      based on class information. Among these, the decorrelated discriminative dimensions are sufficient
831   to represent the class-separating information, resulting in similar class separability compared to the
832   whole and discriminative dimensions while avoiding redundancy. In contrast, the non-discriminative
833   dimensions exhibit more diffuse distributions with unclear class boundaries, indicating that these di-
834   mensions fail to encode class-relevant information.
835
      We observe that for methods such as Barlow Twins and SupDCL, even the non-discriminative di-
836
      mensions exhibit a degree of class separability. This can be attributed to their explicit focus on
837   decorrelating dimensions, which allows a larger number of dimensions to encode non-redundant
838   features. This highlights the benefit of decorrelation in promoting feature diversity, even if all di-
839   mensions are not fully discriminative.
840
      Finally, we divide the decorrelated discriminative dimensions into four groups and visualize their
841
      class separation patterns. Each group exhibits distinct clustering behavior, indicating that these di-
842
      mensions learn complementary features. This analysis further supports our claim that the number of
843   decorrelated discriminative dimensions serves as an indirect indicator of the diversity in meaningful
844   features.
