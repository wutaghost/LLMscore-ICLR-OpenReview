009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Differentially Private Stochastic Gradient Descent (DPSGD) is widely utilized to
012            preserve training data privacy in deep learning, which first clips the gradients to a
013            predefined norm and then injects calibrated noise into the training procedure. Exist-
014            ing DPSGD works typically assume the gradients follow sub-Gaussian distributions
015            and design various gradient clipping mechanisms to optimize training performance.
016
               However, recent studies have shown that the gradients in deep learning exhibit a
               heavy-tail phenomenon, that is, the tails of the gradient may have infinite variance,
017
               which leads to excessive clipping loss with existing mechanisms. To address this
018
               problem, we propose a novel approach, Discriminative Clipping (DC)-DPSGD,
019            with two key designs. First, we introduce a subspace identification technique to
020            distinguish between body and tail gradients. Second, we present a discriminative
021            clipping mechanism that applies different clipping thresholds for body and tail
022            gradients separately to reduce the clipping loss. Under the non-convex condition
023            and heavy-tailed sub-Weibull  gradient noise assumption,       DC-DPSGD      reduces
                                                                      √                 √
024            the empirical risk from O logmax(0,θ−1) (T /δ) log2θ ( T ) to O log( T ) with
025            heavy-tailed index θ > 1/2, iterations T , and high probability 1 − δ. Extensive
026            experiments on five real-world datasets demonstrate that our approach outperforms
027            three baselines by up to 9.72% in terms of accuracy.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   DPSGD Abadi et al. (2016), as a mainstream paradigm of privacy-preserving deep learning, has
033
      wide applications in areas such as privacy-preserving recommender systems Liu et al. (2023), face
      recognition Tang et al. (2024), and medical diagnosis Meng et al. (2021); Ji et al. (2022). Essentially,
034
      in each iteration of model training, DPSGD clips per-sample gradient under the L2 norm constraint
035
      to obtain the maximum divergence between gradient distributions that differ by only one training
036   data and adds random noise within rigorous privacy bounds for unbiased gradient estimation.
037
038   Most of existing DPSGD works Bu et al. (2024); Xia et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023); Zhu &
039
      Blaschko (2023); Koloskova et al. (2023); Li et al. (2022); Fang et al. (2022); Yang et al. (2022)
      rely on the assumption that the gradient noise follows a sub-Gaussian distribution to devise effective
040
      clipping strategies. However, recent studies Zhang et al. (2020b); Simsekli et al. (2019; 2020);
041
      Camuto et al. (2021); Barsbey et al. (2021) have shown that SGD gradient noise in deep learning
042   often exhibit heavy-tailed distributions instead of light-tailed distributions (e.g., sub-Gaussian). This
043   occurs even when the dataset originates from a light-tailed distribution, the gradients still diverge to a
044   heavy-tailed distribution with infinite variance Gurbuzbalaban et al. (2021), which may slow down
045   the convergence rate and impair training performance Li & Liu (2022; 2023); Madden et al. (2020);
046   Gorbunov et al. (2020). To cope with this problem in SGD, Li & Liu (2023); Wang et al. (2021);
047   Gorbunov et al. (2020) suggest employing larger clipping thresholds to get rid of the oscillations
048   caused by heavy-tailed gradients on the training trajectory.
049   Nevertheless, the clipping operation in DPSGD is closely tied to the magnitude of DP noise added
050   to the gradients. Setting the clipping threshold too large can lead to a high-dimensional noise
051   catastrophe Zhou et al. (2021), which negatively impacts model performance and potentially disrupts
052   the convergence of DPSGD algorithms. Therefore, practitioners need to carefully strike a balance
053   between injected noise and clipping loss, as illustrated in Figure 1. The left sub-figure shows the
      trade-off under the light-tailed assumption. As the clipping threshold increases (i.e., when the red

070
      Previous clipping mechanisms for DPSGD Bu et al. (2024); Yang et al. (2022); Xia et al. (2023) have
      been proposed under the light-tailed assumption, but none of them can be adapted to our problem.
071
      Specifically, Bu et al. (2024); Yang et al. (2022); Xia et al. (2023) focus on small-norm gradients
072
      (i.e., those near the center of the distribution) and normalize them to be around 1. These approaches
073   reduce the maximum divergence, thereby requiring less noise to be injected. However, they do
074   not account for heavy-tailed gradients and thus cannot optimize the clipping loss. Another line of
075   work directly estimates the actual norm of the per-sample gradient and utilizes it as the clipping
076   threshold to reduce the clipping loss. For instance, Andrew et al. (2021) estimate the true gradient
077   trajectory by collecting the norms of historical gradients. However, this approach requires knowing
078   the upper bound of historical norms for adding noise, which is highly uneconomical under heavy-
079   tailed distributions, as the upper bound for moment generating function (MGF) Vladimirova et al.
080   (2020) can be immeasurable, making the scale of DP noise unbearable and the expectation bounds
081
      inapplicable. Moreover, due to the constraints of a finite privacy budget, practical private learning
      cannot perform indefinite training. Therefore, it is essential to obtain a high probability bound to
082
      ensure algorithm performance with the probabilistic nature of privacy noise on single runs.
083
084   In this paper, we present high probability bounds with faster convergence rates for DPSGD and
085   propose a novel approach, named Discriminative Clipping (DC)-DPSGD, to effectively balance the
086   trade-off between clipping loss and required DP noise under the heavy-tailed assumption. The key
087
      idea is to utilize different clipping thresholds for the body gradients and tail gradients respectively,
      retaining more information from tail gradients that can withstand more severe DP noise. We introduce
088
      two techniques to achieve this goal. First, we design a subspace identification technique to identify
089
      heavy-tailed gradients with high probability guarantees. We note that the body of heavy-tailed
090   distributions exhibits characteristics similar to those of light-tailed distributions, and the main
091   difference lies in the decay rate at the tails. Therefore, we extract orthogonal random vectors from
092   heavy-tailed distributions (e.g., sub-Weibull distribution) to construct a random projection subspace,
093   and compute the trace of the second moment matrix between gradients and this subspace to distinguish
094   heavy-tailed gradients. Second, we present a discriminative clipping mechanism, which applies a
095   large clipping threshold for the identified heavy-tailed gradients and a smaller one for the remaining
096   light-tailed gradients. We theoretically analyze the choice of the two clipping thresholds and the
097   convergence of DC-DPSGD with a tighter bound. Our contributions are summarized as follows.
098

        mechanism to optimize DPSGD under sub-Weibull gradient noise assumption. To our knowledge,
100     this is the first work to rigorously address heavy tails in DPSGD with high probability guarantees.

        of DPSGD, and improve it to faster rates by DC-DPSGD,         which shows that the empirical risk is

      Heavy-tailed noise and high probability bounds. Recently, from the perspective of escaping from
111
      stationary points and Langevin dynamics, the noise in neural networks is more inclined to anisotropic
112   and non-Gaussian properties Gurbuzbalaban et al. (2021); Simsekli et al. (2019); Gorbunov et al.
113   (2020); Zhang et al. (2020b), with specific heavy-tailed phenomena discovered and defined in gradient
114   descent in deep neural networks. Several works focused on heavy-tailed convex optimization in
115   privacy-preserving deep learning Lowy & Razaviyayn (2023); Wang et al. (2020); Kamath et al.
116   (2022). Building upon the work of Wang et al. (2020), Kamath et al. (2022) relax the assumption
117   of Lipschitz condition and sub-Exponential distribution to a more general α-th moment bounded
118   condition. However, no work has investigated the convergence characteristics of heavy-tailed DPSGD
119   in non-convex settings. Meanwhile, high probability bounds are more frequently discussed in
120
      optimization properties such as convex and non-convex learning with SGD, but rarely addressed
      in the context of private learning. Specifically, with bounded α-th moments assumption, Li & Liu
121
      (2023) provide a high probability theoretical analysis for variants like clipped SGD with momentum
122
      and adaptive step sizes. Nevertheless, these works on optimizing DPSGD rely on expectation bounds,
123   which are unsuitable for heavy-tailed assumptions.
124
125   Projection subspace in DPSGD. DPSGD has gained wide concerns for its detrimental impact on
126
      model accuracy. A series of works leverage projection techniques to improve performance. For
      instance, Zhou et al. (2021); Yu et al. (2021a;b) confine DPSGD training dynamics to more compact
127
      and condensed subspaces through projection. While ensuring the fidelity of training data compression,
128
      they decouple the irrelevant relationship between ambient features and DP noise, and reduce the
129   optimization error of DPSGD under stringent privacy constraints. However, existing works rely on
130   the assumption that public datasets are available for designing the techniques Golatkar et al. (2022);
131   Zhou et al. (2021); Yu et al. (2021a); Gu et al. (2023), which is rather strong, especially in sensitive
132   domains. In contrast, our approach does not rely on any public dataset.
133
      Gradient clipping. Gradient clipping is a widely adopted technique to ensure the sensitivity of
134
      gradients is bounded in both practical implementations and theoretical analysis for DPSGD Chen
135   et al. (2020); Zhang et al. (2020a; 2022); Andrew et al. (2021); Xiao et al. (2023); Wei et al. (2022);
136   Koloskova et al. (2023). Since the tuning parameters in the classical Abadi’s clipping function Abadi
137   et al. (2016) are complex, adaptive gradient clipping schemes have been proposed by Bu et al. (2024);
138   Yang et al. (2022). These schemes scale per-sample gradients based on their norms. In particular,
139   gradients with small norms are amplified infinitely. Building upon this, Xia et al. (2023) control the
140   amplification of gradients with small norms in a finite manner. However, no work has specifically
141   optimized gradient clipping under the heavy-tailed assumption of DPSGD. Due to the scale of noise
142   required to achieve differential privacy, trivial clipping methods and analysis are not applicable.

280   with a novel tail-aware clipping mechanism, as illustrated in Figure 2. The rationale is to divide
281   gradients following a heavy-tailed sub-Weibull distribution into two parts: light body and heavy

283   threshold is applied for light body and a larger one for heavy tail to mitigate the extra clipping loss.

371   Assuming that the gradients are classified into the correct heavy tail and light body regions, we then
372   apply two different clipping thresholds (denoted as c1 and c2 ) in our discriminative clipping method
373   for the tail and body gradients, respectively. This way, we can reduce tail gradients’ clipping losses
374
      and obtain faster DPSGD convergence, according to the analysis in Section 4.
375   Specifically, the tail probability P(|X| > x) = exp (−I(x)) ∀x > 0 of the sub-Weibull variables
376   X ∼ subW (θ, K) exhibits two different behaviors: (1) Light body: for small x values, the tail
377   rate capturing function I(x) decays like a sub-Gaussian tail. (2) Heavy tail: for x greater than the
      normal convergence region, i.e., x ≥ xmax is a large deviation region, its decay is slower than that

524
525   We now validate our empirical guidance for the clipping threshold in Theorem 5.3. The results in
526   Figure 4 indicate that the optimal ratio is approximately c1 ≈ 10c2 . We note that when c1 = 100c2 ,

528   DPSGD. From a theoretical perspective, given δ = 1e−5 , η/B = 0.04, and     √θ ≈ 2 (following Gur-

530   c2 = O(log1/2 (1/δ)), that is, c1 = log3/2 (1/δ)c2 , i.e., c1 ≈ 10c2 . In conclusion, the optimal
531   clipping threshold aligns with our empirical guidance.

534    In this paper, we propose a novel approach DC-DPSGD under the heavy-tailed assumption, which
535    effectively reduces extra clipping loss in the heavy-tailed region. We rigorously analyze the high
536    probability bound of the classic heavy-tailed DPSGD under non-convex conditions and obtain results
537    matching the expectation bounds. Furthermore, we sharpen the weighted average optimization
538    performance of DC-DPSGD. Extensive experiments on five real-world datasets demonstrate that DC-

       DPSGD outperforms three state-of-the-art clipping mechanisms.
