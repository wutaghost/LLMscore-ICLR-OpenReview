009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Understanding scene semantics plays an important role in solving the object navi-
012            gation task, where an embodied intelligent agent has to find an object in the scene
013            given its semantic category. This task can be divided into two stages: exploring
014            the scene and reaching the found target. In this work, we consider the latter stage
015            of reaching a given semantic goal. This stage is particularly sensitive to errors
016            in the semantic understanding of the scene. To address this challenge, we pro-
017            pose a multimodal and multitasking method called SegDT, which is based on the
018
               joint training of a segmentation model and a decision transformer model. Our
               method aggregates information from multiple multimodal frames to predict the
019
               next action and the current segmentation mask of the target object. To optimize
020
               our model, we first performed a pre-training phase using a set of collected trajec-
021            tories. In the second phase, online policy fine-tuning, we addressed the problems
022            of long-term credit assignment and poor sampling efficiency of transformer mod-
023            els. Using the PPO algorithm, we simultaneously trained an RNN-based policy
024            using ground-truth segmentation and transferred its knowledge to the proposed
025            transformer-based model, which trains the segmentation in itself through an ad-
026            ditional segmentation loss. We conducted extensive experiments in the Habitat
027            Sim environment and demonstrated the advantage of the proposed method over
028            the basic navigation approach as well as current state-of-the-art methods that do
029
               not consider the auxiliary task of improving the quality of the segmentation of the
               current frame during training.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Navigating an intelligent agent (e.g. a robot) to a target object in an unknown environment is still a
035   challenge for existing methods. This is confirmed by the results of modern benchmarks, for example
036   in the simulators Habitat (Savva et al., 2019), AI2Thor (Kolve et al., 2017), and others. There are
037   several reasons for this. First, the best existing neural network models that can operate in real time
038   still do not segment objects reliably enough, especially when they are far away or partially visible
039   (Miao et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2024). Second, the prediction of agent actions from visual data is also
040   performed with a large number of errors and has significant improvement potential for both modular
041   approaches (Chaplot et al., 2020) and end-to-end neural network models (Chen et al., 2023).
042   A separate problem is the related task of image sequence segmentation for intelligent agents. There
043   are several approaches based on direct fusion of image sequence features (Shang & Ryoo, 2023;
044   Su et al., 2023), auto-regressive prediction of segmentation masks based on previous masks and
045   images (Šarić et al., 2021; Graber et al., 2022), consideration of three-dimensional constraints when
046   segmenting objects of the sequence (Zhang et al., 2023b; WAN & FANG, 2023; Scarpellini et al.,
047   2023), including those based on Gaussian blending (Zhu et al., 2024; Lei et al., 2024). However,
048   in terms of the quality achieved, they still have significant limitations for use in the task of indoor
049   navigation of an intelligent agent.
050   The navigation task is a partially observable reinforcement learning (RL) problem where history is
051   fed into a sequence model (Sutton & Barto, 2018). While transformers are powerful tools in CV
052   and NLP tasks (Brown et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2024) and have long-term memory capability with
053   effective representation learning from context for specific tasks (Lu et al., 2024), they generally have
      poor sampling efficiency and do not improve long-term credit assignment compared to recurrent

080   a non-deterministic environment (Batra et al., 2020a).

100   2   R ELATED WORK
101
102   Recent methods for object goal navigation use scene semantic information for action prediction to
103   reduce overfitting and increase the navigation quality for unseen environments. The scene semantic
104   can be available in the form of a 2D semantic segmentation mask. For instance, authors of the THDA
105   method (Maksymets et al., 2021) introduce a policy network that uses depth and multichannel
106   semantic masks as input. SkillFusion approach (Staroverov et al., 2023) proposes a goal-reaching
107   policy that leverages an RGB observation and a binary segmentation mask of object goal. During
      inference time the success rate of such navigation approaches heavily relies on the quality of input


                                                                              2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      segmentation masks (Staroverov et al., 2023). Despite the active development of neural network
109   architectures, the state-of-the-art methods for semantic segmentation (e.g. Mask2Former (Cheng
110   et al., 2022), OneFormer (Jain et al., 2023), OpenSeeD (Zhang et al., 2023a), MQ-Former (Wang
111   et al., 2024)) still show imperfect segmentation quality, especially for indoor environments, where
112   objects can vary a lot within one semantic category.
113
      In addition, the state-of-the-art methods for semantic segmentation do not take into account the pe-
114
      culiarities of an embodied agent interacting with its environment during navigation. The agent has a
115   limited field of view, therefore instant observations may contain erroneous semantics when looking
116   at the object from certain view angles. During the navigation episode, the agent can update its se-
117   mantic understanding of the scene by observing the scene from more advantageous viewpoints. Such
118   refinement can occur explicitly by using the accumulated semantic map of the environment (Tao
119   et al., 2024; Morilla-Cabello et al., 2023). The explicit semantic maps of the environment can be
120   used as input to predict action policy (Ramakrishnan et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023b; Yu et al.,
121   2023). Other methods, such as (Chen et al., 2023), use implicit maps to model the history of obser-
122   vations. A major drawback of these methods is that as the navigable space expands, the size of the
123
      dense voxelized map can become infinitely large.
124   In contrast, we use a method that aggregates sequence information from previous semantic observa-
125   tions to refine semantic segmentation on the current frame and predict the next action. In this sense,
126   our method is related to methods that solve the task of video segmentation (Zhang et al., 2023c; Shin
127   et al., 2024). However, unlike such methods, our approach allows the agent to control its observa-
128   tions to navigate to the goal and improve the segmentation quality. At the same time, our method
129
      differs from existing embodied computer vision methods (Fan et al., 2023; Ding et al., 2023; Yang
      et al., 2019; Kotar & Mottaghi, 2022). These methods aim to improve the quality of visual per-
130
      ception, while our method increases both the quality of navigation and the quality of segmentation.
131
      The methods for embodied computer vision often operate in the next-best-view paradigm or use a
132   small sequence of frames to predict the next action. However, the agent needs a longer history of
133   observations to successfully solve the object goal navigation task. Unlike (Shang & Ryoo, 2023),
134   we consider a complex photo-realistic 3D environment of the HM3DSem v0.2 (Yadav et al., 2023b)
135   scenes.
136
      A special feature of our method is the joint training of a semantic segmentation model and a trans-
137
      former to predict the next actions. Previous works (Maksymets et al., 2021; Hong et al., 2023)
138   consider semantic loss as an additional task for model training. However, these methods use seman-
139   tic loss only to improve the action policy, and not to improve the quality of semantic segmentation
140   by aggregating information from a sequence of frames.

      In the literature (Batra et al., 2020b), the Semantic Object Navigation task is defined as follows.
      An agent is randomly initialized within an unfamiliar environment and needs to navigate toward an
147
      instance of a specified object category C ∈ {c1 , c2 , ..., cn } (e.g., a plant). The solution of this task
148
      usually consists of two stages. First, the agent explores the environment to find an instance of a
149   given semantic goal. Next, the agent reaches the found object. In this work, we consider the second
150   stage of reaching the semantic goal. Therefore, we initialized the agent at the random viewpoint of
151   the semantic goal at a maximum distance of seven meters (Fig. 1).
152

153
      fined as a tuple (S, A, P, R, ρ0 , γ) for underlying observation space S, action space A, transition
154
      distribution P , reward function R, initial state distribution ρ0 , and discount factor γ.
155

157   action space consisting of six types of actions: callstop to terminate the episode, forward by
158
      0.25 m, turnleft or turnright by angle 15◦ , lookup, lookdown by turning the agent
      head by angle 30◦ . This type of discrete action space is common for indoor simulators such as
159
      Habitat Yadav et al. (2023b) or AI2-Thor Kolve et al. (2017).
160

      the callstop action. We assess the agent’s performance via three common metrics for the Object

185   4        M ETHOD
186
187   Our method consists of training a decision transformer model with a multistage mask decoder. Pre-
188   diction at time t involves two stages. An observation at time t consists of an image It , a depth
189   map Dt , and a target category name c. First, multi-scale feature maps of It are generated using a
190   ResNet50 backbone and a pixel decoder. These feature maps, along with trainable query features,
191
      are then fed into the decision transformer. After processing, the trainable query features are decoded
      by a multi-stage mask decoder to generate segmentation masks for a fixed set of categories. From
192
      the set of masks, a binary mask for the target category is selected and its embedding is extracted.
193
      This embedding, combined with the depth map and the category name embedding, completes the
194   observation sequence embeddings. In the second step, the full sequence of observation embeddings
195   is fed into the decision transformer to predict the probability distribution and state value of the next
196   action. We then sample action at and add its embedding to the observation sequence to predict
197   actions at time t + 1. Figure 2 illustrates the model architecture.
198
199   4.1          S EGMENTATION MODULES
200
201   When choosing the architecture of the Segmenting Decision Transformer (SegDT) modules respon-
202   sible for segmentation, we take Mask2Former (Cheng et al., 2022) as a basis. Mask2Former is one
203   of the state methods for semantic segmentation. This method considers the segmentation problem
204   as a problem of predicting a set of binary masks and their classification. The segmentation model is
205   given an image of size (H, W, C) as input.
206   The main components of Mask2Former are a backbone, a pixel decoder, and a multistage decoder.
207   We use ResNet50 as the backbone. The output of the backbone is fed to the pixel decoder to generate
208   4 maps of high resolution per-pixel embeddings. The per-pixel embeddings have 1/4, 1/8, 1/16,
209   and 1/32 of the resolution of the input image. We use a 1/32 per-pixel embedding map as the image
210   embedding for the Transformer model input.
211   In the original single-frame Mask2Former model, binary segmentation masks and their classification
212   logits are decoded from N learnable query features using multiscale feature maps. In our work, we
213   use N learnable query features as input to the Transformer model to take into account the context
214   of previous observations. After passing through the transformer, the updated query features are
215   passed through the multistage decoder. Here, similar to the Mask2Former model, we use multi-
      scale feature maps to predict binary segmentation masks and their logits. From these binary masks,


                                                                                                                                 4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      a multi-channel semantic segmentation mask is formed for Ncl = 40. We then select the target
217   semantic mask and use the ResNet50 encoder to create a semantic feature of size (1, dsem ). This
218   feature describes the semantics of the current observation, similar to TDHA (Maksymets et al.,
219   2021).

221   4.1.1    O BSERVATIONS EMBEDDINGS
222
223   For each time point, we describe the current observation using 29 embeddings obtained from differ-
224   ent encoders and projected into the GPT hidden dimension dGP T = 768. For each of the T-frames,
225   we flatten the image pixel embeddings from Mask2Former into a sequence and project the image em-
226   beddings into dGP T using a linear layer. Thus, the image embedding for an image has a dimension
227
      of (H · W/32, dGP T ). The learnable queries are represented by a set of 50 embeddings with dimen-
      sion (1, dGP T ). We encode the semantics of each image using ResNet50 features obtained from the
228
      binary segmentation mask of the target object into a feature vector of dimension (1, dsem ). Thus,
229
      after projection, the embedding of semantic predictions for 1 image has a dimension of (1, dGP T ).
230   We encode depth for each of the observations using ResNet18, resulting in a feature vector of di-
231   mension (1, ddepth ). Using a linear layer, we project the depth features into the dGP T feature space.
232   Thus, the feature embedding of the depth 1 observation has dimension (1, dGP T ). To encode the
233   target category and the preformed action, we use a look-up table of learnable embeddings of dimen-
234   sions (Ncl , dGP T ) and (Nactions , dGP T ), respectively. We populate the GPT input sequence with T
235   observation embeddings. Thus, the dimension of the input sequence of observation embeddings is
236   (T · (H · W/32 + 4), dGP T ).
237
238   4.1.2    P REDICTIONS
239
      Since the goal of the semantic object navigation task is to reach an object of a certain target category,
240
      we expect that using the observation history can improve the segmentation quality for this target cat-
241
      egory. To decode semantic predictions, we use an idea from the original Mask2Former segmentation
242   model (Cheng et al., 2022). We take the output learnable query features from the SegDT and pass
243   them through the multistage decoder. To obtain the binary segmentation masks and their logits at
244   time t, we additionally use the multi-scale feature maps predicted by the pixel decoder at time t. We
245   use MLPs to decode the action distribution for the actor head and to estimate the state value for the
246   critic head.
247
      To predict the action at step t, we use the set of observations {o0 , ..., ot } and the previous actions
248   {a0 , ..., at−1 }. First, the sequence {o0 , a0 , ..., ot−1 , at−1 , ot } is passed to the SegDT input to predict
249
      the segmentation masks {Mipred }ti=0 . The mask corresponding to the target object category is used
250   as the semantic observation for the time t. Next, SegDT makes another prediction of the action at ,
251   taking into account the segmentation mask, the depth, and the target category at time t. In this case,
252   the last token of the output sequence of the transformer is used as input of the action decoder, i.e.
253   the last token of the observation ot .

257   4.2.1    J OINT L EARNING ON O FFLINE DATA
258
259   As a central aspect of our experiment, we initialize the ResNet50 backbone, the pixel decoder, and
260   the multi-stage decoder responsible for segmentation prediction with parameters of a pre-trained
261
      segmentation model. The primary goal during the initial phase of training is to establish an effective
      representation of the observations intended for navigation. To achieve this goal, we rely on an offline
262
      demonstration dataset composed of semantic goal-reaching instances between the start coordinates
263
      and the most proximal target. We collect the action probability distribution of a pre-trained RL
264   agent with RNN and ground truth segmentation as input. During these initial stages, both SegDT
265   (our multi-stage mask decoder) and our action decoder are trained simultaneously. To optimize mask
266   prediction, we use the sum of the pixel-by-pixel binary cross-entropy Lbce , the dice loss Ldice , and
267   the cross-entropy loss Lce for mask classification as our loss function. Behavior cloning (Lbce ) is
268   used to predict the action sequence. Additionally, we pretrain the Critic Value Decoder during this
269   training phase. We use the pre-collected critic values obtained by the RL agent and apply an MSE
      loss LM SE between them and the values predicted by SegDT, as articulated in equations 1 and 2.

      4.2.2    O NLINE F INETUNING
284   Limitations of Behavior Cloning arise primarily in two areas - an observable shift in distributions
285   when confronted with different states at training and test times, and a lack of flexibility to adapt
286   to evolving environments. In addition, when imitating suboptimal demonstrations, the results of
287   Behavior Cloning subsequently reflect these imperfections. To overcome these limitations, in the
288   second phase of policy training, we employed an online reinforcement learning approach that can
289   incrementally adapt to changes in the environment.
290   However, online reinforcement learning (RL) requires a significant number of samples to achieve
291   robust performance, which can be a significant limitation. In addition, the use of the transformer
292   model introduces significant computational cost, especially for long sequences, as causal transform-
293   ers require O(t2 ) time to compute the representation at time step t.
294
      To address this issue, we sampled trajectories using an RNN-based policy that can be efficiently
295   trained online with ground truth segmentation as input. Following the work of SkillFusion
296   (Staroverov et al., 2023), we implemented the RNN-based GoalReacher skill. The limitation of
297   this model is that it requires an external segmentation module during inference, which may output
298   noisy segmentation masks that differ from those seen during training.
299
      We then fine-tune the proposed SegDT policy with trajectories provided by the RNN-based policy.
300
      To generate actions, our model includes two segmentation-independent modules: actor and critic
301   heads, similar to the RNN-based policy. To fine-tune these on SegDT, we transferred knowledge
302   from the RNN-based policy using cross-entropy loss. In addition, we applied segmentation loss to
303   SegDT and used PPO loss for both models (Fig. 3).
304
      We demonstrate that integrating these insights into our pipeline significantly improves the perfor-
305
      mance of our navigation stack, achieving performance comparable to the advanced RNN-based pol-
306
      icy with ground truth segmentation as input, without the need for an external segmentation module.

363   rt (at , st ) = −∆geo dist − 0.01, where ∆geo dist is the change in geodesic distance to the goal by
364   performing action at in state st .

413
414   We compare the quality of our approach with state-of-the-art methods for object goal navigation.
415   The comparison results are shown in Table 1. We use weight models trained to solve the task of
416   navigation to a goal object. For each method, we use the action and observation spaces used in their
417
      training pipeline and limit trajectory length up to 64 steps if not stated otherwise.
418   The DD-PPO method(Wijmans et al., 2020) performs poorly with the goal reacher task since it
419   relies only on the object goal class and does not cope well with semantic understanding of the scene.
420   But in the task of navigating to a point, it shows results comparable to humans (Wijmans et al.,
421   2020). OnavRIM (Chen et al., 2023) and PIRLNav (Ramrakhya et al., 2023) are trained on human-
422   collected trajectories. These trajectories have great length and usually start with an exploration of the
423
      environment. Therefore, OnavRIM and PIRLNav (Ramrakhya et al., 2023) spend most of the time
      exploring the room and only then returning to the target, which in most cases exceeds the limit of 64
424
      steps. Additionaly, we present the navigation metrics for these methods with increasing the number
425
      of steps to 500 (the maximum episode length in the Habitat Challenge). As can be seen from Table 1,

      6     C ONCLUSION
475
476
      Our results show that joint training of a multimodal decision transformer for segmentation and nav-
477
      igation improves the performance of both tasks. Two-phase training of the Segmenting Decision
478
      Transformer (SegDT) using additional training using DD-PPO in the environment can further im-
479   prove the quality of navigation.
480
481   As a limitation of the proposed approach we can mention its computational complexity. The speed
482
      of inference slows down the fine-tuning of the action policy in the environment. Another limitation
      is the use of the pre-trained Mask2Former model to initialize the parameters of the segmentation
483
      modules of SegDT.
484
485   Another research direction is to create a method for selecting the most valuable frames for calculat-
      ing the segmentation loss during training of SegDT.
