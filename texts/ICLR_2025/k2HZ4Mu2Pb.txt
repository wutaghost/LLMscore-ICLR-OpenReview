009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011             Machine unlearning offers effective solutions for revoking the influence of specific
012             training data on pre-trained model parameters. While existing approaches address
013             unlearning for classification and generative models, they overlook an important
014             category of machine learning models: contrastive learning (CL) methods. This
015             paper addresses this gap by introducing the Machine Unlearning for Contrastive
016             Learning (MUC) framework and adapting existing methods. We identify limi-
017             tations in current approaches, noting that several methods perform inadequately
018
                as unlearners and that existing auditing tools insufficiently validate unlearning
                effects in contrastive learning. To address these issues, we propose Alignment
019
                Calibration (AC), a novel method that explicitly considers contrastive learning
020
                properties and optimizes towards new auditing metrics for easy verification of
021             unlearning. Through empirical comparisons with baseline methods on SimCLR,
022             MoCo, and CLIP, we demonstrate that AC: (1) achieves state-of-the-art perfor-
023             mance, approximating exact unlearning (retraining); (2) enables data owners to
024             clearly visualize unlearning effects through black-box auditing.

027   1       I NTRODUCTION
028
029   The success of modern machine learning models largely relies on training with a large corpus of data.
030   However, carefully annotated data are expensive and difficult to obtain, thus urging the utilization of
031   the vast amount of unlabeled data in the wild. The recent self-supervised learning methods, especially
032   contrastive learning methods (Chen et al., 2020; 2021; He et al., 2020), provide viable solutions to
033   learning general representations for various downstream tasks. For example, unimodal contrastive
034   learning models employ the InfoNCE loss to maximize the feature similarity between positive pairs
035
      (e.g., different data augmentations of the same image) while minimizing that between the negative
      ones (e.g., different images). This training scheme also applies to multi-modal training (e.g., CLIP
036
      (Radford et al., 2021)), and the learned encoders are widely applied in various tasks, e.g., GPT-based
037
      models (Achiam et al., 2023) and latent diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022).
038
039   To amass large-scale datasets for training contrastive learning models, practitioners often resort to
040   web crawling (e.g., using Common Crawl1 ). However, such data collection methods may disregard
041
      data owners’ privacy concerns, potentially retrieving their data without consent. Moreover, acquired
      training data may include copyrighted material or even inappropriate content, such as sexual abuse
042
      (e.g., in recent reports2 against content in LAION-5B (Schuhmann et al., 2022)). In these scenarios,
043
      data owners or authorities may rightfully request the removal of misused training data (i.e., unlearning
044   dataset) 3 , necessitating adjustments to the trained model parameters. While retraining the model from
045   scratch without the unlearning dataset is a straightforward solution, it incurs substantial computational
046   costs for large models and datasets.
047
      To eliminate the effect of the unlearning dataset on the model with minimum effort, machine

      unlearning methods (Cao & Yang, 2015; Bourtoule et al., 2021; Ginart et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2019;
049
      Neel et al., 2021; Ullah et al., 2021; Sekhari et al., 2021; Izzo et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2023; Zhang

      group removal and for generative models on sample or concept removal. However, the study of an
077
      efficient solution for contrastive learning models is under-explored. In this paper, we establish the
078
      foundation of Machine Unlearning for Contrastive Learning models (MUC). MUC adapts various
079   existing methods to contrastive learning and introduces the notion of data owners who request
080   unlearning and model owners who execute unlearning. Given candidate unlearning algorithms, the
081   model owners first perform white-box evaluation to select the best method and generate an optimal
082   unlearned model. The data owners then perform black-box auditing to validate the effect of the

085   Unfortunately, direct adaptations of existing unlearning approaches are unsatisfactory on both consid-
086   erations. Firstly, from the model owners’ perspective, such algorithms are suboptimal approximations
087   of exact unlearning (training from scratch) under different white-box evaluations and there lack
088   of a good candidate method. Secondly, from the data owner’s perspective, even given an optimal
089   unlearned model, it is difficult to discern the unlearning effect under existing black-box auditing tools,
090   rendering it hard to determine the success of unlearning.
091   Motivated by the above state of affairs, we introduce a novel unlearning method called Alignment
092   Calibration (AC) that is specifically tailored for contrastive learning. AC optimizes a novel loss
093   function that involves three terms: (1) a positive alignment calibration term that removes the footprint
094   of the unlearn set on the representation; (2) a negative alignment calibration term that leaves explicit
095   unlearning traces for auditing; (3) a performance preserving term that maintains uniformity.
096
      Finally, we empirically compare baseline methods with our Alignment Calibration algorithms on
097   unlearning models pre-trained on SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020) MoCo (He et al., 2020), and CLIP
098   (Radford et al., 2021). Under various unlearning settings (e.g., the fraction of the unlearning dataset)
099   and evaluation metrics, AC consistently outperforms the baseline methods, especially under unlearn
100   auditing, validating the benefits of our method. In summary, we make the following contributions:
101
102   • We propose the MUC framework that considers existing methods and various evaluation tools in
103     contrastive learning, including white-box evaluation and black-box auditing.
104
      • Motivated by insufficiencies of existing unlearning algorithms and auditing tools, we propose the
105
        novel Alignment Calibration method that satisfies both model owners and data owners.
106
107   • Our experiments initiate the evaluation of existing machine unlearning methods for contrastive
        learning and confirm the superiority of our new methods.

      2     BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      We first provide background and related work on contrastive learning and machine unlearning.
111
112   Contrastive Learning and Self-supervised Learning Contrastive learning learns general rep-
113   resentations by contrasting sample pairs (usually without labels), which analytically benefits the
114   downstream applications (Saunshi et al., 2019; Tosh et al., 2021). Popular contrastive learning meth-
115   ods such as Contrastive Predictive Coding (CPC) (van den Oord et al., 2018), SimCLR (Chen et al.,
116   2020), and MoCo (He et al., 2020) employ the InfoNCE loss to enforce the contrast between positive
117   and negative pairs. Other variants of the InfoNCE-based loss are also widely applied, e.g., f -MICL
118   (Lu et al., 2023), Alignment and Uniformity (Wang & Isola, 2020), and Pearson χ2 divergence (Tsai
119   et al., 2021). This contrastive training scheme is also applied to the context of multimodal learning,
120
      where images and texts are formed as pairs, e.g., in CLIP (Radford et al., 2021). There exist other
      self-supervised learning methods that also learn representations (Grill et al., 2020; Chen & He, 2021;
121
      He et al., 2022; Caron et al., 2021). In this paper, we mainly focus on developing unlearning recipes
122
      for contrastive learning methods, especially SimCLR, MoCo, and CLIP.
123
124   Specifically, contrastive learning usually applies the InfoNCE loss to learn a representation g. Given a
125   probability measure p, we define the density of positive pairs sampled from p as p+ , i.e., two samples
126
      with similar feature embeddings as the joint distribution; and the density of negative pairs as p× .
      Specifically, one minimizes the loss below as the objective:
127
128                 LInfoNCE = −        E    s(g(x), g(y)) + E log E exp (s(g(x), g(y)) ,                  (1)
                                   (x,y)∼p+                   x∼p     y∼p
129
130   where s is the cosine similarity after normalization with a temperature parameter, and g(x), g(y) are
131   the features extracted by a given encoder g, respectively. The above contrastive learning (pre-training)
132   scheme learns a general encoder g (image and text encoders for CLIP). Such a (fixed) g can be utilized
133   with an additional linear head or shallow models for downstream tasks. In this paper, we mainly
134   consider linear probing, where g is used for the classification of the same dataset with pretraining.
135
      Notably, we consider unlearning during the pretraining phase only.
136
137
      Machine Unlearning For Supervised Learning: Machine unlearning (MU) (Cao & Yang, 2015)
      requires an algorithm to revert to a state that specific data points are never trained on. While exact
138
      unlearning (Bourtoule et al., 2021) (e.g., retraining the model entirely on the retain dataset) provides
139
      a reliable solution, the additional computation requirement is also tremendous. In this paper, we
140   focus on approximate unlearning (Ginart et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2019; Neel et al., 2021; Ullah
141   et al., 2021; Sekhari et al., 2021; Izzo et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a; Fan et al.,
142   2024; Shen et al., 2024) to efficiently achieve the same goal.
143
      For Generative Models: MU methods are applied to diffusion models to avoid copyright infringement
144
      and inappropriate image generation (Gandikota et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023b; Heng & Soh, 2023;
145
      Kumari et al., 2023). For large language models, MU is applied as a model-editing (Yao et al., 2023)
146   tool to enable forgetting on certain training texts (Mitchell et al., 2022b;a; Jang et al., 2022; Eldan &
147   Russinovich, 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a; Hu et al., 2024; Jia et al., 2024; Maini et al., 2024; Liu et al.,
148   2024; Zhang et al., 2024b). In this paper, we focus on MU on self-supervised learning, specifically,
149   contrastive learning methods, which differs from the above two cases in both unlearning settings and
150   frameworks, which we specify in the following section.

185   3.2   A DAPTING EXISTING METHODS TO MUC
186
      We first adapt some existing unlearning methods designed for supervised unlearning to contrastive
187
      unlearning. Due to the lack of labels in contrastive learning pre-training, some approaches cannot
188
      be directly applied. For example, random labeling (Golatkar et al., 2020; Fan et al., 2024) relies on
189   flipping the labels of the unlearn data; boundary unlearning (Chen et al., 2023) expands or shrinks the
190   decision boundary, which does not exist in our context. In contrast, some other unlearning methods
191   can be tailored to contrastive learning. Specifically, we adapt the following methods:
192
193   • Retraining: (exact unlearning) trains on Dretain from scratch via minimizing Equation (1);
194   • Fine-tuning (Golatkar et al., 2020) updates the pre-trained model for several epochs on Dretain ;
195
      • Gradient Ascent (Golatkar et al., 2020; Neel et al., 2021; Thudi et al., 2022) reversely maximizes
196
        Equation (1) on the Dunlearn ;
197
198   • NegGrad (Kurmanji et al., 2023) jointly minimizes and maximizes Equation (1) on Dretain and
199
        Dunlearn respectively;
200   • ℓ1 -Sparsity (Jia et al., 2023) regularizes the ℓ1 -norm of model parameters based on fine-tuning.
201
202   The above methods manifest straightforward adaptations of unlearning from supervised learning
203
      to contrastive learning by changing the cross entropy loss to the InfoNCE loss in Equation (1). In
      Section 5, we show that approximate unlearning methods are suboptimal approximations of exact
204
      unlearning, namely that there still exists a performance gap compared with retraining. This motivates
205
      us to design new unlearning methods specifically for contrastive learning in Section 4.

242
243   After choosing an optimal unlearning algorithm, the model owner generates the unlearned model ĝ
244   as a response to the unlearning request made by data owners. However, it is impossible for the data
245
      owners to perform the same white-box evaluations.
246
247   For the data owners ² (Unlearning Auditing) : Recall that an individual data owner i
                                                                      i
248   performs black-box auditing due to the limited access to input Dunlearn and the output of the encoder
                                              i                    4              i
249   before/after unlearning. Specifically,    cannot train shadow models with Dunlearn   alone to perform
250   MIAs; and cannot obtain TA or RA to quantify performance. Additionally, there is a lack of the
251
      retrain baseline to compare with. To this end, the only auditing tool is the forgetting score FS on
      Dunlearn , which can be calculated with Equation (2). However, we argue that this auditing is neither
252
      sufficient nor reliable, and we use a simple empirical example to validate this claim:
253
254   Exact unlearning on MoCo (He et al., 2020): We perform exact unlearning (i.e., retraining) to forget
255   4500 training images of CIFAR-10 (randomly chosen) on MoCo (ResNet-18). We calculate the
256   forgetting score FS for every unlearn sample and calculate the mean µ and the standard deviation σ
257
      across the 4500 unlearning images. We obtain µ = 0.025, σ = 0.081.
258   Here we observe that the large standard deviation σ = 0.081 = 3.24 × µ makes the current auditing
                                                                                       i
259   largely unreliable. For individual data owners, if the unlearn subset size |Dunlearn     | is small, its
260   corresponding sample-wise FS is likely to be biased and the average could fluctuate around 0,
261   suggesting little forgetting. This could lead to the belief that “unlearning hasn’t been performed”
262   from the data owner’s side, thus rejecting the exact unlearned model, as well as any approximate
263
      unlearning algorithms, given that “matching exact unlearning” is the criteria for selection. This
      example reveals the insufficiency of using only FS as the unlearn auditing tool in contrastive learning.
264
265   In summary, under the current MUC framework, existing approximate unlearning algorithms and
266   auditing tools are insufficient. To address this, we design new unlearning algorithms for the model
267   owners and advanced auditing tools for data owners in contrastive learning in the next section, which
268   would benefit both parties in engaging the unlearning procedure.

      6                C ONCLUSION
530
      In this paper, we study the problem of machine unlearning for contrastive learning pre-training (MUC).
531
      We establish the foundations on this line of study by adapting existing unlearning methods and setting
532
      up baseline evaluation metrics, including white-box evaluation for model owners to choose an optimal
533   unlearning strategy, and black-box auditing for data owners to examine the effect of unlearning. After
534   identifying the suboptimality of existing unlearning methods and the insufficiency of current auditing
535   tools, we further propose our novel method called Alignment Calibration. Our approach introduces a
536   novel unlearning objective function to strategically optimize toward the unlearning goal and enable
537   straightforward visual auditing. Empirically, our method achieves state-of-the-art performance on
538   unlearning tasks for both unimodal and multimodal contrastive learning. Our paper initializes the
539   study of machine unlearning in self-supervised learning but only considers contrastive learning. We
      plan to extend our exploration of unlearning towards other SSL methods in the future.

      A.1    DATASETS
759
760
      CIFAR-10/100. Both datasets consist of 50K training images and 10K test images. All the images
761   are 32x32 colored. CIFAR-100 has 100 categories and CIFAR-10 has 10 categories. In unimodal
762   contrastive learning, the augmentations for training encoders include random resizing and cropping,
763   random grayscale, random color jitter, and horizontal flipping. We split the 50K training images into
764   a validation set of 5K images and a training set of 45K images. For example, when the unlearning
765   task is to forget 10% of training data, the unlearn dataset Dunlearn has 4.5K images and the retain
766   dataset Dretain has 4.05K images.
767
768   MS-COCO. COCO is a large-scale object detection, segmentation, and captioning dataset. Its
769   training set contains 118,287 images and 591,753 captions. Each image has several objects and
770   corresponds to at least 5 captions. Different from unimodal contrastive learning which uses strong
771   augmentations, CLIP employs only resizing, center cropping and horizontal flipping to make images
772   of 224x224 pixels.

774   A.2    C ONTRASTIVE L EARNING AND U NLEARNING M ETHODS
775
776   MoCo and SimCLR: For the pre-trained (clean) models, we train the encoder for 800 epochs using
777   an SGD optimizer with cosine-scheduled learning rate initialized at 0.06, momentum of 0.9, and
778   weight decay of 0.0005. For unlearning methods: Retrain applies the same training strategy as pre-
779   training; Fine-tuning and NegGrad updates the pre-trained encoder for 10 epochs with a learning rate
780
      searched in [0.003, 0.03]; Gradient Ascent updates the pre-trained encoder using reversed stochastic
      gradient descent for 5 epochs with a learning rate searched in [10−6 , 10−4 ; ℓ1 -Sparsity applies the
781
      learning rate as 0.006 and implements ℓ1 regularization with a coefficient searched in [10−6 , 10−3 ].
782
      For our Alignment Calibration method, we update the pre-trained encoder for 10 epochs and search
783   the learning rate in [0.003, 0.03] and the tunable parameter β in [0, 20] for different unlearning tasks.
784   If not otherwise stated, we adopt α = γ = 1. For simplicity, in our reported results on CIFAR-10/100,
785   we use a learning rate of 0.006 for 10% forgetting, and 0.02 for 50% forgetting. The linear probing
786   stage trains a linear classifier head for 100 epochs using an SGD optimizer with a cosine-scheduled
787   learning rate initialized at 1.0, and a momentum of 0.9. The batch size is set as 512 for both encoder
788   and linear head training.
789
790   CLIP: For the pre-trained (clean) CLIP, we train the model for 35 epochs on 2 NVIDIA RTX 4090
791   GPUs using an AdamW optimizer with a warm-up cosine-scheduled learning rate initialized at 5e-4
792   and momentum of 0.9. The total batch size is 256 (128 on each GPU). For unlearning algorithms:
793   Retrain again applies the same training strategy as pre-training; Fine-tuning update the pre-trained
794   model for 8 epochs with a fixed learning rate searched in [5e-5,5e-4]; NegGrad updates the pre-trained
795   model for 8 epochs with a fixed learning rate searched in [10−5 , 10−4 ]; Gradient Ascent updates the
796
      pre-trained model for 4 epochs with a fixed learning rate searched in [5e-6, 5e-4]; ℓ1 -Sparsity updates
      the pre-trained model for 8 epochs with a learning rate of 0.0005 and a regularization coefficient
797
      searched in [10−9 , 10−4 ]. For our Alignment Calibration method, we update the pre-trained model
798
      for 8 epochs with a fixed learning rate of 0.0002. We search α = γ in [0.5, 1] and β in [0, 1].
