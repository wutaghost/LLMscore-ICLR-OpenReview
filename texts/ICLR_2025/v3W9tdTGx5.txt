009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Federated learning (FL) involves multiple heterogeneous clients collaboratively
012            training a global model via iterative local updates and model fusion. The generaliza-
013            tion of FL’s global model has a large gap compared with centralized training, which
014            is its bottleneck for broader applications. In this paper, we study and improve FL’s
015            generalization through a fundamental “connectivity” perspective, which means how
016
               the local models are connected in the parameter region and fused into a generalized
               global model. The term “connectivity” is derived from linear mode connectivity
017
               (LMC), studying the interpolated loss landscape of two different solutions (e.g.,
018
               modes) of neural networks. Bridging the gap between LMC and FL, in this paper,
019            we leverage fixed anchor models to empirically and theoretically study the transitiv-
020            ity property of connectivity from two models (LMC) to a group of models (model
021            fusion in FL). Based on the findings, we propose FedGuCci(+), improving group
022            connectivity for better generalization. It is shown that our methods can boost the
023            generalization of FL under client heterogeneity across various tasks (4 CV datasets
024            and 6 NLP datasets) and model architectures (e.g., ViTs and PLMs).

      The notion of group connectivity is inspired by linear mode connectivity (LMC), which studies the
048   interpolated loss landscape of two SGD solutions (e.g., modes) (Draxler et al., 2018; Zhang et al.,
049   2021; Entezari et al., 2022). It is found that two trained models with different random seeds of batch
050   orders (depicted by SGD noise), even if have the same initialization, may cause a barrier along their
051   linear interpolation path (i.e., the LMC path), indicating the two SGD solutions are not in the same
052   loss landscape basin (Draxler et al., 2018; Garipov et al., 2018; Ainsworth et al., 2022). This observa-
053   tion is quite analogous to model drift in FL, where multiple local models are initialized the same, but
      due to SGD noise and bias (Li et al., 2020a; Karimireddy et al., 2020) caused by heterogeneous data

054
      and asynchronous training, local models drift from each other and have inferior generalization after
055   linear model fusion. This analogy inspires us to think about whether we can leverage the insights and
056   techniques from LMC to improve the generalization of FL through the lens of connectivity. Previous
057   works propose to learn neural network subspaces for increasing LMC between two models when
058   simultaneously training them (Wortsman et al., 2021; Garipov et al., 2018). They use the midpoints
059   of the improved LMC for ensembling. In this paper, we aim to leverage the idea of increasing LMC
060   to improve the connectivity among the local models in FL. However, there is a crucial gap between
061   LMC and FL. In Wortsman et al. (2021), they can retain and train two models simultaneously, while
062   in each round of FL, every local model is independently trained for several epochs. In addition, LMC
063
      only considers two models, while FL requires the connectivity of multiple models.
064   Therefore, we utilize a fixed anchor model to study the transitivity property of LMC and hypothesize
                                                               ∗
065   that: if LMC between model w1 and anchor model wanc        , as well as between model w2 and anchor
                ∗
066   model wanc , is independently enhanced, then the LMC between models w1 and w2 will also improve
067   (an illustration of the transitivity is in Figure 1). Through theoretical and empirical analyses, we
068   verify the transitivity of LMC and then extend it to the group connectivity of multiple models.
069   Based on the above findings, we propose Federated Learning with Improved Group Connectivity
070   (FedGuCci), which leverage the global models as the anchor models for improving group connec-
071   tivity of local models. Further, due to data heterogeneity in FL, clients’ local loss landscapes are
072   different and shifted. Thus, we propose a strengthened version, FedGuCci+, by incorporating some
073   heterogeneity-resistant modules for aligning local loss landscapes. Our contributions are listed below.
074
             • We study FL from the connectivity perspective, which is novel and fundamental to under-
075
               standing the generalization of FL’s global model.
076
077
             • We theoretically and empirically verify the transitivity of LMC and the group connectivity
               of multiple models.
078
079          • We propose FedGuCci and FedGuCci+. Extensive experiments show that our methods can
080            improve the generalization of FL across various settings.

117   2.2   P RELIMINARY OF L INEAR M ODE C ONNECTIVITY
118
119   Linear mode connectivity (LMC). LMC refers to the loss landscape where two models w1 and
120   w2 are linearly interpolated by w = αw1 + (1 − α)w2 , for α ∈ [0, 1]. Usually, there are three
121   forms of LMC regarding different w1 and w2 . (1) LMC between two SGD solutions with the same
122   initialization but different random seeds (batch orders) (Ainsworth et al., 2022); (2) LMC between
123
      two SGD solutions with different initializations (Entezari et al., 2022); (3) LMC from the initialization
      and the trained model (Vlaar & Frankle, 2022). LMC is depicted by the barriers in the landscape, the
124
      lower the barriers, the better the LMC. We present the definitions of loss and accuracy barriers below.
125
126   Definition 2.1 Loss and accuracy barriers. Let fw (·) be a function represented by a neural network
127   with parameter vector w that includes all parameters. L(w) is the given loss (e.g., train or test
128   error) of fw (·) and A(w) is its accuracy function. Given two independently trained networks w1
129   and w2 , let L(αw1 + (1 − α)w2 ) be the averaged loss of the linearly interpolated network and
130   A(αw1 + (1 − α)w2 ) be its accuracy, for α ∈ [0, 1]. The loss barrier Bloss (w1 , w2 ) and accuracy
131   barrier Bacc (w1 , w2 ) along the linear path between w1 and w2 are defined as:

137   Reducing the barriers in LMC. In Wortsman et al. (2021), the authors train two SGD solutions
138
      simultaneously while also learning a line of connected subspace between the two models. It also adds
      a regularization loss to make the two solutions orthogonal so that the midpoints of the LMC path
139
      can have diversity for ensembling. While in our paper, we also use similar techniques for improving
140
      LMC, but we do not require orthogonality. Also, instead of simultaneously training two models,
141   we individually train models, improve their LMC with a fixed anchor model, and verify the LMC’s
142   transitivity.

144   2.3   M OST R ELATED W ORKS
145
146   LMC and FL. In Hahn et al. (2022), the authors propose to train two models (one for personalization
147   and another for generalization) at clients and learn a connected subspace between the two models for
148   better personalization. Recently, a concurrent work (Zhou et al., 2023) empirically and theoretically
149   verifies that when clients’ data are more heterogeneous, the local loss landscapes will be more shifted,
150
      causing worse LMC. However, they haven’t proposed an effective algorithm in FL based on LMC
      insights, where our contributions lie. To the best of our knowledge, our paper may be the first paper
151
      to study and improve the generalization of FL from the connectivity perspective.
152
153   Comparison with FedProx. FedProx (Li et al., 2020a) adopts the current round’s global model as
154   a regularization term for tackling heterogeneity. Instead, we utilize the historical global models as
155   the anchor models and learn to improve the connectivity between the local model with these anchor
156
      models. Thus, our methods and FedProx have fundamental differences in leveraging the global
      models regarding motivation and implementations. Due to space limits, we include more related
157
      works in Appendix D, e.g., generalization of FL and LMC basics.

      In this paper, we study the transitivity of linear mode connectivity (LMC) and use this property to
      improve the generalization of federated learning (FL). We first empirically and theoretically verify
536
      the transitivity of LMC between two models by leveraging a fixed anchor model, and we extend
537
      it to group connectivity among multiple models. Then, we propose FedGuCci and FedGuCci+ in
538   FL. Extensive experiments demonstrate our proposed methods can improve the generalization of FL
539   under various settings.

      A.2   DATASETS
794
795   CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) consists of 60,000 32x32 color images, evenly distributed among
796   10 different classes, including airplanes, automobiles, birds, cats, etc., each represented by 6,000
797   images. The dataset is split into 50,000 training images and 10,000 test images. FashionMNIST (Xiao
798   et al., 2017) is designed as an advanced replacement for the MNIST dataset, suitable for benchmarking
799   machine learning models. It comprises 70,000 images divided into 60,000 training samples and
800   10,000 test samples. Each image is a 28x28 grayscale representation of fashion items from 10
801
      different classes, such as shirts, trousers, sneakers, etc. The CIFAR-100 dataset (Krizhevsky et al.,
      2009) is similar to CIFAR-10 but more challenging, containing 100 different classes grouped into 20
802
      superclasses. It includes 60,000 32x32 color images, with 600 images per class, divided into 50,000
803
      training images and 10,000 test images. This dataset is primarily used for developing and evaluating
804   more sophisticated image classification models. TinyImageNet TinyImageNet is a reduced-scale
805   version of the renowned ImageNet dataset, which comprises a total of 200 classes. The dataset is
806   structured into training, validation, and test sets, with 200,000 training images, 20,000 validation
807   images, and 20,000 test images. The GLUE benchmark is a compilation of 9 datasets for evaluating
808   natural language understanding systems. Tasks are framed as either single-sentence classification or
809   sentence-pair classification tasks. GLUE includes MNLI (inference, (Williams et al., 2017)), MRPC
      (paraphrase detection, (Socher et al., 2013)), MRPC (paraphrase detection, (Dolan & Brockett, 2005)),

      A.3    M ODELS
819
      SimpleCNN. The simple CNN for CIFAR-10 is a convolutional neural network model with ReLU
820
      activations, consisting of 3 convolutional layers followed by 2 fully connected layers. The first
821
      convolutional layer has a size of (3, 32, 3), followed by a max-pooling layer of size (2, 2). The second
822   and third convolutional layers have sizes of (32, 64, 3) and (64, 64, 3), respectively. The last two fully
823   connected layers have sizes of (6444, 64) and (64, num_classes), respectively.
824
825
      ResNets. We followed the model architectures used in (Li et al., 2018). The number in the model
      names indicates the number of layers in the models, whereas a larger number indicates a deeper
826
      network. We used ResNet18 and ResNet20 for CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, respectively. Notably, to
827
      mitigate abnormal effects introduced by batch normalization layers (Li et al., 2020b; Lin et al., 2020),
828   followed by (Adilova et al., 2023), we removed all batch normalization layers from the ResNets.
829
830   VGG. VGG (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2015) is a convolutional neural network (CNN) architecture
831
      that gained prominence in the field of computer vision. Among its variants, we used VGG11.
832   RoBERTa. RoBERTa is a natural language processing (NLP) model that builds upon the foundation
833   laid by BERT, which was introduced by (Liu et al., 2019) to address some limitations and improve the
834   performance of BERT on various NLP tasks. It comes in various sizes, and we used RoBERTa-base
835   considering to high computational costs.
836   ViT. ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) is a deep learning model for visual tasks that adopts the Trans-
837   former structure proposed in NLP. ViT divides a picture into several patches, treats the patch as a
838   word, and then uses a self-attention mechanism to capture the relationship between patches. When
839   ViT is pre-trained with a large amount of data, it will perform particularly well on downstream tasks.

      A.4    R ANDOMNESS
843
      In all experiments, we conducted each experiment three times with different random seeds and
844
      reported the averaged results along with standard deviations.
845
846   We ensured consistency by setting torch, numpy, and random functions with the same random seed,
847   thereby making the data partitions and other settings identical. To ensure all algorithms started with
848   the same initial model, we saved an initial model for each architecture and loaded it at the beginning
849
      of each experiment. Additionally, for experiments involving partial participation, the selection of
      participating clients in each round significantly influenced the model’s performance. To maintain
850
      fairness, we saved the sequences of participating clients in each round and loaded these sequences for
851
      all experiments. This procedure guaranteed that, given a random seed and participation ratio, every
852   algorithm had the same set of sampled clients in each round.

       Linear Mode Connectivity. Linear mode connectivity (LMC) refers to the phenomenon that there
       exists a loss (energy) barrier along the linear interpolation path of two networks, in the cases where i)
1196
       the two networks have the same initialization and are trained on the same dataset but with different
1197
       random seeds (data shuffles) or augmentations (Ainsworth et al., 2022); ii) the two networks are
1198   with different initializations but are trained on the same dataset (Entezari et al., 2022); iii) the two
1199   networks are the initial network and the final trained network (Vlaar & Frankle, 2022). In our paper,
1200   the transitivity of LMC can be applied to i), ii), and iii), and especially, the two trained models can
1201   have different initializations. Specifically, (Adilova et al., 2023) examines layer-wise LMC, and
1202   finds that there may be no barriers in the layer-wise manner. (Frankle et al., 2020) connects linear
1203   mode connectivity with the lottery ticket hypothesis and finds better connectivity can result in better
1204   pruning performances. (Vlaar & Frankle, 2022) studies the relationship between generalization
1205   and the initial-to-final linear mode connectivity. (Zhao et al., 2020) bridges mode connectivity and
1206
       adversarial robustness. Some works try to extend mode connectivity beyond “linear”, e.g., searching
       for a non-linear low-loss path (Draxler et al., 2018) or studying mode connectivity under spurious
1207
       attributes (Lubana et al., 2023).
1208
1209   Studying the barriers in LMC is an important direction of LMC. Previous works find that there may
1210   be no barriers between different modes, but the connected regions may be non-linear (Draxler et al.,
1211   2018; Garipov et al., 2018). In (Garipov et al., 2018), the authors propose to find paths along modes
1212
       by learning Polygonal chain and Bezier curve. Also, Nudged Elastic Band can also be used to find
       that connected paths (Draxler et al., 2018). In (Wortsman et al., 2021), the authors propose to learn
1213
       connected but diverse low-loss subspaces for efficient ensembling. Our work about the transitivity of
1214
       LMC is inspired by the previous works of learning connected paths. However, instead of learning
1215   diverse modes for ensembling, we aim to use the anchor model to improve the linear connectivity
1216   between two independent modes.

       Generalization of Federated Learning. Generalization and personalization are two important goals
       of federated learning systems (Chen & Chao, 2022; Li et al., 2023a;b; Yuan et al., 2022). Previous
1219
       works study and understand the property and nature of generalization in FL. In (Yuan et al., 2022),
1220
       the authors rethink the previous definition of generalization by considering the data distributions
1221   of non-participated clients as the participation gap and propose a new data split method based on
1222   the insight. In the paper of FedRoD (Chen & Chao, 2022), the authors claim that generalization
1223   and personalization are not conflicted; instead, improving generalization is the basis for better
1224   personalization.
1225
       Some works aim to improve generalization from both the server and client sides. For the clients,
1226
       sharpness-aware minimization methods are introduced at the local to find a flatter minimum of local
1227   solvers for better generalization (Caldarola et al., 2022; Qu et al., 2022). Global sharpness-aware
1228   minimization is also considered (Dai et al., 2023). In addition, previous literature seeks to tackle
1229   local heterogeneity to improve generalization, and methods like proximal terms (Li et al., 2020a),
1230   dynamic regularization (Acar et al., 2020), variance reduction (Karimireddy et al., 2020), logit
1231   calibration (Zhang et al., 2022), fixed classifier (Li et al., 2023b), and balanced loss (Chen & Chao,
1232   2022) are devised. For the server, weighted aggregation approaches to de-bias local updates (Wang
1233   et al., 2020) or heterogeneity (Ye et al., 2023) can improve generalization. Recently, global weight
1234   shrinking that sets smaller aggregation weights has been studied for unleashing the potential of weight
1235
       regularization in boosting the generalization of FL (Li et al., 2023a).
