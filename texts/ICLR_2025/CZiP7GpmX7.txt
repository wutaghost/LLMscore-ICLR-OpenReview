009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Time series forecasting is essential across various sectors, including finance,
012            transportation, and industry. In this paper, we propose FastTF, a powerful yet
013            lightweight model in Time-Frequency domain for long-term time series forecast-
014            ing. Our aim is to push the boundary of model lightweighting and facilitate the
015            deployment of lightweight model on resource-constrained devices. Leveraging the
016            global nature and information compressibility of the time series in frequency do-
017            main, we introduce patch-wise downsampling, Sparse Frequency Mixer (SFM),
018
               and patch predictor to capture the temporal variations of frequency components
               across different patches. Experimental results on five public datasets demonstrate
019
               that FastTF with very few parameters outperforms several state-of-the-art mod-
020
               els and demonstrates a strong generalization capability. Notably, on the ETTh1
021            dataset, FastTF with only 4 parameters achieves a performance that is close to
022            the DLinear and FITS in the horizon-96 forecasting. Furthermore, we deployed
023            our model on a FPGA development board (Zynq UltraScale+ RFSoC ZCU208
024            Evaluation Kit), where the corresponding resource usage statistics illustrate that
025            our model has a very low computational overhead and latency, making it easily
026            implemented on hardware devices.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   “Less is more” —— Ludwig Mies van der Rohe
032   Time series forecasting, a technique used to predict future data based on historical observations
033   and has found extensive applications in various fields such as finance, transportation, energy, and
034   meteorology. With the development of deep learning technologies, neural network-based models,
035   including MLP (Zeng et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Yi et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024),
036   CNN (Wu et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Luo & Wang, 2024), and Transformer (Zhou et al., 2021;
037   2022; Nie et al., 2022), have gradually supplanted traditional models like ARIMA (Shumway et al.,
038   2017) and have become mainstream. However, these models often face significant challenges: on
039
      one hand, their complexity makes them difficult to deploy on computationally constrained devices
      such as FPGAs or other embedded systems. On the other hand, as black-box models, they generally
040
      lack interpretability.
041
042   From the perspective of signal sequence characteristics, models only based on time-domain-based
043   are effective at capturing trend information but fail to leverage the global nature and information
044   compression capabilities of the frequency domain, leading to shortsightedness and high complexity.
045
      Conversely, models that only based on frequency domain are adept at capturing the inherent periodic
      characteristics within sequences but struggle to handle the sequential order of data, resulting in
046
      ambiguity in temporal information. To address these issues, we model the time series forecasting
047
      problem as one of intra-patch information fusion and inter-patch trend prediction. Specifically, the
048   proposed FastTF model segments the time series into patches in the time domain and employs a
049   downsampling strategy within patches, designed based on the Nyquist sampling theorem, to achieve
050   efficient weight sharing across subsequences. Additionally, a Sparse Frequency Mixer (SFM) is
051   introduced to address spectral leakage inherent in the discrete Fourier transform and to exploit the
052   naturally sparse correlations between frequency points for intra-patch information fusion. The patch
053   predictor is then used for inter-patch frequency trend prediction. This approach not only results in
      an exceptionally lightweight model that can be deployed on resource-constrained edge devices but


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      also provides a certain degree of interpretability. The contribution of this paper can be summarized
055   as follows:
056
057         • We propose FastTF, a powerful yet extremely lightweight model for long-term time series
058           forecasting.
059         • Based on the Nyquist sampling theorem, FastTF performs patch-wise downsampling and
060           sub-sequence rFFT on time series data after dividing it into patches, maximizing weight
061           sharing while minimizing information loss.
062         • Observing the sparse correlation between frequency points, we design SFM (Sparse Fre-
063           quency Mixer), a linear mapping layer with a block diagonal weight matrix to facilitate
064           information fusion between frequency points. The patch predictor then predicts the tempo-
065           ral variations of different frequency points and transform them back to the time domain.
066         • Experiments on five public datasets demonstrate that FastTF achieves superior performance
067           compared to most mainstream models with remarkably low parameter counts and training
068           overhead. Deployment results on an FPGA development board confirm that it can run with
069           minimal resource and time overhead.

073   Transformer Based Models Transformers (Vaswani, 2017) stand out in time series forecasting
074   due to their strong ability to capture long-range dependencies. For instance, Informer (Zhou et al.,
075   2021) and Autoformer (Wu et al., 2021) capture the temporal dependencies of time series, while
076   FEDformer (Zhou et al., 2022) models the frequency domain of time series. Recent study, like
077   PatchTST (Nie et al., 2022), showed the effectiveness of patch-based processing in time series
078   forecasting. However, these models are computationally expensive and suffer from a potential in-
079   formation loss due to the attention mechanism (Zeng et al., 2023).
080   MLPs and CNNs The latest research shows that simple linear models, like MLP and CNN, can
081   achieve competitive performance in time series forecasting. For example, DLinear (Zeng et al.,
082   2023), FITS (Xu et al., 2023) are two representative models that use single linear layers to capture
083   the time and frequency characteristics of time series. ModernTCN (Luo & Wang, 2024), a CNN-
084   based model that originated from modern convolution, also shows strong generalizability in time
085   series forecasting.

089   DFT & FFT & rFFT Given a discrete time sequence x(n) with n ranging from 0 to N − 1, the
090   Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) converts this time-domain sequence into its frequency-domain
091   representation. The DFT is defined as:
092
093                                               N

094                                      X(k) =          x(n)e−j2πkn/N                                 (1)
095                                               n=0

      where X(k) is the value of the sequence at frequency index k, N is the total number of samples
097
      in the sequence, and e−j2πkn/N is the complex exponential factor used to extract the frequency
098
      components of the sequence. FFT is a fast algorithm for computing the DFT which reduces the
099
      computational complexity from O(N 2 ) to O(N log2 N ), making it feasible to compute the DFT for
100   large sequences (Cooley & Tukey, 1965).
101
      Property 1. The DFT of a real-valued sequence x(n) is Hermitian symmetric, i.e., X(k) = X ∗ (N −
102
      k), where X ∗ denotes the complex conjugate of X.
103
104   Property 1 (with the proof given in the Appendix A.1) indicates that only around half of the fre-
105   quency points are unique, and the other half can be obtained by taking the complex conjugate of
106   the first half. Therefore, we use rFFT (Ziegler, 1972; Sorensen et al., 1987), which only computes
107   around half of the frequency points to further reduce computational overhead. Frequency domain
      analysis is widely applied to time series and has the following characteristics:

136   output, and bias, respectively (as shown in Figure 3). The complex linear layer has already been

161   The goal of this paper is to design a lightweight and efficient time series forecasting model. To
      achieve this, our approach is primarily based on three key ideas:


                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      1. Weight Sharing: We promote weight sharing by splitting the complete sequence into multiple
163      subsequences and applying the same operations to each subsequence or to the frequency points
164      in parallel.
165
      2. Weight Sparsification: The spectral leakage and the natural correlation (see Section 4.2) be-
166
         tween frequency points in time series indicate sparse correlations between these frequency points,
167
         leading us to apply group sparsification to the weight matrix in the frequency domain.
168
169   3. Patch-Scale Prediction: To reduce the number of parameters, while at the same time capturing
170      the temporal variations of frequency components, we predict the frequency points over an interval
171
         at the patch scale.
172
      The architecture of our model, FastTF, is shown in Figure 4. FastTF first accepts a single-channel
173
      sequence x ∈ RL and divides it into P patches, each of length L/P . Each patch is then down-
174
      sampled by a factor of M and each subsequence is transformed into the frequency domain using
175   rFFT, yielding XrFFT ∈ CP ×fc ×M , where fc is the cut off frequency. The properly designed SFM
176   (Sparse Frequency Mixer) is then applied to XrFFT to capture the sparse correlations between fre-
177   quency points in each subsequence, obtaining XSFM ∈ CP ×fc ×M . The patch predictor gets XSFM
                                                                                               HP
178   as input and predicts the future frequency components of each patch, yielding X̂ ∈ C L ×fc ×M .
179   Finally, the inverse rFFT (irFFT) and the reshape operation is applied to X̂ to obtain the predicted
180   time series x̂ ∈ RH . We now detail each component of FastTF in the following sections.

211   The SFM (Sparse Frequency Mixer) is designed to integrate information between frequency points
212   in the frequency domain. Specifically, the SFM is motivated by two key observations:
213
214   • Spectral Leakage: Proposition 1 (with the proof given in the Appendix A.3) and Figure 5a
215     illustrate that even for a single frequency signal, the sampled time sequence’s energy can spread
        to surrounding frequency points due to the mismatch of FFT frequency points.

      Proof. The phenomenon of spectral leakage occurs when a sinusoidal component within a signal
      does not align perfectly with the frequency bins of the Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT). The
697
      DFT calculates the frequency content of a discrete-time signal x[n] over a finite interval, producing
698
      frequency components at specific frequencies fk = kf N , where k ranges from 0 to N − 1 and fs is
                                                            s
699
      the sampling frequency.
700
701   When the frequency of a sinusoidal component f0 in the signal does not coincide with any of these
      discrete DFT frequency bins fk , the DFT cannot represent f0 as a single frequency component.


                                                           13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      Instead, the energy that should ideally be concentrated at f0 is distributed among several neighboring
703   frequency bins, a phenomenon known as spectral leakage.
704
705   To understand why this occurs, consider the DFT of a sinusoidal signal x[n] = A cos(2πf0 n + ϕ).
706
      If f0 aligns perfectly with one of the DFT bins fk , the DFT will represent this component as a peak
      at fk , with no energy in the other bins. However, if f0 does not align with any fk , we can express
707
      f0 as f0 = fk + ∆f , where ∆f is the mismatch between f0 and the nearest DFT bin frequency fk .
708
      The sinusoid can no longer be represented by a single complex exponential at fk , and instead, its
709   energy spreads across multiple bins.
710
711   Mathematically, this is due to the finite length of the signal. The DFT implicitly assumes that the
712
      signal is periodic with a period equal to the length of the signal N . When f0 does not match any
      fk , the assumption of periodicity introduces discontinuities at the boundaries of the signal, creating
713
      artifacts in the frequency domain. These artifacts manifest as energy in frequency bins that are not
714
      directly associated with f0 , leading to the appearance of spectral leakage.
715
716   To illustrate, consider the DFT of the signal x[n] = A cos(2π k0N+δ n), where k0 is an integer, and
717   δ is a small fractional frequency component such that f0 = (k0 +δ)f  N

718   not only at k0 but also in adjacent bins k0 ± 1, k0 ± 2, . . ., depending on the value of δ. The energy
719   spreads over several bins, reducing the sharpness of the spectral peak, which would otherwise be
720   concentrated if δ = 0.
721   In practice, spectral leakage can be mitigated by techniques such as windowing, where the signal
722   is multiplied by a window function that tapers the signal to zero at the boundaries, reducing the
723   discontinuities and therefore the leakage. However, even with windowing, some degree of leakage
724   is typically unavoidable when f0 does not exactly match a DFT bin.

728   A.4   E XPLANATION OF P ROPOSITION 2
729
730   The phenomenon of harmonics is a fundamental concept in signal processing, particularly when
731   dealing with signals that exhibit non-linearities or discontinuities. Consider a periodic signal x(n)
732   with a fundamental frequency f0 . The fundamental frequency is the inverse of the period T of the
733   signal, i.e., f0 = T1 . For a perfectly sinusoidal signal, the frequency spectrum would ideally consist
734   of a single peak at f0 . However, when x(n) contains non-linearities or sharp transitions, these
735   irregularities introduce additional frequency components into the spectrum, known as harmonics.
736
      These harmonics are integer multiples of the fundamental frequency, occurring at frequencies 2f0
      (second harmonic), 3f0 (third harmonic), and so on.
737
738   Non-linearities in a signal can arise due to various causes, such as amplitude clipping, rectification,
739   or the presence of sharp corners or edges. When a signal with such characteristics is analyzed
740   in the frequency domain, the Fourier transform reveals not only the fundamental frequency but
741   also its harmonics. For example, consider a signal x(n) = A cos(2πf0 n) that undergoes a non-
742
      linear transformation, such as squaring. The resulting signal y(n) = x2 (n) = A2 cos2 (2πf0 n)
      can be expressed using the trigonometric identity cos2 (θ) = 21 + 12 cos(2θ), leading to y(n) =
743
      A2     A2
744    2 + 2 cos(4πf0 n). The frequency spectrum of y(n) now includes a DC component (at f = 0)
745   and a component at 2f0 , the second harmonic.
746   Discontinuities or sharp transitions in a time series, modeled as abrupt changes in the signal’s ampli-
747   tude, also introduce a wide range of frequency components, particularly higher harmonics. A square
748   wave, for example, alternates between two levels, and its Fourier series expansion reveals that it
749   consists of the fundamental frequency and all odd harmonics (3rd, 5th, 7th, etc.). This behavior is
750   due to the sharp transitions between the high and low states of the square wave.
751   In practical applications, the presence of harmonics can have significant implications. In audio
752   signals, harmonics can add richness or distortion, depending on whether they are desired or not. In
753   communication systems, harmonics can cause interference, necessitating the use of filters to remove
754   them. In power systems, harmonics can lead to inefficiencies and potential damage to equipment.
755   Therefore, understanding the source of harmonics and how they manifest in a signal’s spectrum is
      crucial for effective signal processing and analysis.

789   B     T HE R EASON FOR NOT F ILTERING THE O RIGINAL T IME S ERIES
790
791   Low-pass filtering before downsampling is essential to prevent spectral aliasing, a phenomenon
792
      where higher frequency components of a signal are incorrectly interpreted as lower frequencies.
      This occurs because downsampling reduces the sampling rate, which can cause the signal’s fre-
793
      quency content to exceed the new Nyquist limit (half the new sampling rate). If the original sequence
794
      contains frequency components higher than this limit, they can fold back into the lower frequency
795   range, leading to distortion and loss of information. By applying a low-pass filter, these higher
796   frequency components can be effectively removed, ensuring that the downsampled sequence accu-
797   rately represents the original signal within the new bandwidth. This process preserves the integrity
798   of the signal and prevents aliasing artifacts, which could otherwise compromise the quality of the
799   downsampled data.
800
      However, in FastTF, we do not explicitly filter the original time series before downsampling. This
801   decision is mainly based on the following considerations:
802
803   • Additional Complexity: Incorporating a low-pass filter before downsampling would introduce
804     additional computational complexity to the model. Specifically, an extra rFFT and irFFT oper-
805     ation would be required to implement the filtering process, increasing the overall computational
806     load. Given that FastTF aims to be a lightweight and efficient model, minimizing unnecessary
807     complexity is crucial to maintain its simplicity and speed.
808   • Potential Information Loss: On the one hand, From a time domain perspective, the single-
809     sequence downsampling process itself acts as a low-pass filter, effectively removing high-
        frequency components that exceed the new Nyquist limit. This operation dicarded many point

990    F      T HE I NDIVIDUAL C ONFIGURATION
991
992    In practical applications, time series datasets are often multi-channel. Let C denote the number of
993    input channels, then a look-back window of data can be represented as X ∈ RC×L . For channel-
994    independent models like FITS and DLinear, there are generally two training strategies:
995
996    1. Train a separate single-channel model for each channel, with independent weights across chan-
997       nels, referred to as the Individual configuration.
998
999    2. Use a shared set of weights across all channels.
1000
1001
       For FastTF, we also employ the Individual configuration on the weather dataset, but with a key dif-
1002   ference: we train a SFM for each channel independently, while the patch predictor shares weights
1003   across channels. The motivation behind this strategy is to reduce the model’s parameter count and
1004   mitigate overfitting. Specifically, the SFM integrates frequency information over short time inter-
1005   vals, where different channels exhibit varying short-term behavior in the weather dataset. Con-
1006   versely, the patch predictor captures long-term trend changes across patches, which tend to be con-
1007   sistent across different channels.
