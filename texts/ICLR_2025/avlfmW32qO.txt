011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013              In the field of eXplainable AI (XAI) in language models, the progression from
014              local explanations of individual decisions to global explanations with high-level
015              concepts has laid the groundwork for mechanistic interpretability, which aims to
016              decode the exact operations. However, this paradigm has not been adequately
017              explored in image models, where existing methods have primarily focused on
018              class-specific interpretations. This paper introduces a novel approach to system-
019
                 atically trace the entire pathway from input through all intermediate layers to
                 the final output within the whole dataset. We utilize Pointwise Feature Vectors
020
                 (PFVs) and instance-specific Effective Receptive Fields (iERFs) to decompose
021
                 model embeddings into interpretable Concept Vectors. Then, we calculate the rel-
022              evance between concept vectors with our Generalized Integrated Gradients (GIG),
023              enabling a comprehensive, dataset-wide analysis of model behavior. We validate
024              our method of concept extraction and concept attribution in both qualitative and
025              quantitative evaluations. Our approach advances the understanding of semantic
026              significance within image models, offering a holistic view of their operational
027              mechanics. 1

030   1       I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In the field of eXplainable AI (XAI), efforts have historically transitioned from Local explanation
033   to Global explanation to Mechanistic Interpretability. While local explanation methods including
034
      Selvaraju et al. (2017); Montavon et al. (2017); Sundararajan et al. (2017); Han et al. (2024) have
      focused on explaining specific decisions for individual instances, global explanation methods seek
035
      to uncover overall patterns and behaviors applicable across the entire dataset (Wu et al., 2023; Xu-
036
      anyuan et al., 2023; Singh et al., 2024). One step further, mechanistic interpretability methods seek
037   to analyze the fundamental components of the models and provide a holistic explanation of opera-
038   tional mechanics across various layers.
039
040
      Recently, researchers in language models, (Geva et al., 2022; Bricken et al., 2023; Gurnee et al.,
      2024), have extensively studied mechanistic interpretability to reveal the precise mechanisms trans-
041
      forming inputs into outputs. They provide a dataset-wide explanations by utilizing the whole in-
042
      stances from the dataset, regardless of the classes. Interpretability in image models (Fel et al., 2023;
043   Ghorbani et al., 2019), however, have typically focused on class-wise explanations, which interpret
044   model decisions using only data from a specific class, thereby failing to capture shared concepts
045   across different classes. This distinction arises because images consist of pixels that do not inher-
046   ently represent concepts, unlike languages where each comprising word itself can be treated as a
047   concept. Additionally, as meaningful structures in images are localized and only occupy small re-
048   gions of the entire image, the embedding space in image datasets is far more sparse compared to that
049   in language datasets.
050   In this paper, we present a novel approach to mechanistic interpretation in image models by system-
051   atically decomposing and tracing the pathways from input to output across an entire dataset. Unlike
052   previous methods that often focus on individual classes or specific features, our approach provides
053

055   plain the model’s embedding within the whole dataset, throughout the whole layers (See Fig. 1). We
056   decompose the model’s embedding with the dataset-wide concept vectors, enabling the existence of
057   “Shared Concepts” unlike previous class-wise explanation methods (Fel et al., 2023), (Kowal et al.,
058   2024).
059
      Following the framework of Han et al. (2024), we use the Pointwise Feature Vector (PFV) for our
060
      analysis unit, which is defined as the channel-axis pre-activation vector of each layer, serving as
061   the fundamental unit encoding the network’s representations. We further utilize the instance-specific
062   Effective Receptive Fields (iERFs) of the PFVs to label their semantic meaning, enabling a direct
063   investigation of the PFV vector space. By leveraging iERF, we aim to identify clear and meaningful
064   linear bases within the PFV vector space, allowing us to decompose previously unknown PFVs into
065   interpretable principal components, which we refer to as Concept Vectors (CVs).
066
      To find out the bases and identify the CVs, we leverage several clustering methods including dic-
067   tionary learning, k-means, and Sparse AutoEncoder. Among the clustering methods, we employ
068   bisecting k-means clustering, since it is well-suited for the PFV vector space, which is highly sparse
069   and variably dense. For instance, background features often cluster densely, while critical features,
070   such as “the beak of a bird”, may occupy a broader, less dense area.
071
      To quantify the causal relationships between concept vectors in different layers, we introduce Gen-
072
      eralized Integrated Gradients (GIG), which effectively captures interlayer contributions. By com-
073   bining Concept Vectors with GIG, we can offer a comprehensive causal analysis of the ResNet50
074   model, from the lowest layers to the final class predictions. Fig. 1 shows examples of how our
075   method iteratively aggregates concepts in the previous layer to form a higher-layer abstract concept.
076
      In this study, we focus on the ResNet50 architecture. Yet, our approach is not limited to convo-
077
      lutional architectures and can be universally applicable across various modalities, including trans-
078
      former architectures, which we intend to explore in future work. Our framework facilitates a deeper
079   understanding of the semantic significance of features, thus advancing the mechanistic interpretabil-
080   ity image models.

083   2   R ELATED W ORKS
084
085   Local explanation Methods, often referred to as attribution methods, such as Sundararajan et al.
086   (2017); Montavon et al. (2017); Han et al. (2024), aim to explain model predictions for specific in-
087
      stances. These methods attribute the output to particular input features, such as pixels or neurons,
      offering instance-specific insights. However, they often suffer from reliability issues due to an am-
088
      biguity in interpreting what the generated explanation maps actually signify (Adebayo et al., 2018).
089
090   To address this, global explanation methods, known as concept attribution methods (Kim et al.,
091   2018; Fel et al., 2023), extend traditional attribution methods by associating model predictions with
092   high-level concepts. They aim to provide explanations at a broader, global scale by identifying and
093
      quantifying the importance of predefined or automatically extracted concepts. However, these meth-
      ods remain confined to a single layer of the model and are typically class-specific. This limitation
094
      highlights the need for methods that can analyze interactions between concepts across multiple lay-
095
      ers.
096
097   Lastly, mechanistic interpretability, widely recognized as interlayer concept attribution methods
098   (Achtibat et al., 2023; Kowal et al., 2024), take the field a step further by tracing the evolution
099
      and interaction of concepts across multiple layers of a model. Unlike local or global explanation
      methods, which are localized to specific instances or layers, these methods focus on systematically
100
      analyzing the model’s internal mechanisms throughout its entire architecture. For instance, CRP
101
      (Achtibat et al., 2023), an extension of LRP (Bach et al., 2015), provides detailed exploration of
102   how the concepts impact the model’s output at each layer, by introducing concept-conditional rel-
103   evance mapping. VCC (Kowal et al., 2024), an extension of TCAV (Kim et al., 2018), interprets
104   how individual concepts contribute to the model’s decisions across different layers. However, both
105   of them focus on a specific class, thereby limiting their capacities to offer a more comprehensive
106   and generalized view of the model’s behavior across the entire dataset. In contrast, our approach
107   broadens the interlayer analysis to include the entire dataset, enabling a more thorough examination
      of the model’s decision-making process. Our work is the first to provide dataset-wide mechanistic

203   3.1   A NALYSIS U NIT: PFV- I ERF DATASET
204
205   In our study on mechanistic interpretability in image models, we utilize several key components
206   essential for understanding and analyzing the network’s behavior from the work of Han et al. (2024).
207
      Specifically, we use a Pointwise Feature Vector (PFV) as the unit of analysis and its corresponding
      instance-specific Effective Receptive Field (iERF) as the visual label to effectively show and validate
208
      the knowledge encoded by the PFV.
209
210   Firstly, a PFV is a vector of neurons along the channel axis within a hidden layer that share an
                                                                                        l l   l
211   identical receptive field. Given the embedding of layer l denoted as Al ∈ RH W ×C , where C l is
212   the number of channels and H l W l represents the spatial dimensions of the feature map, the PFV
                                                                      l
213   at position p ∈ {1, · · · , H l W l } is represented as xlp ∈ RC . This vector encapsulates a localized
214   feature representation at a specific point within the input image, providing a clear characterization
215   of the features at that particular location. Unlike individual neurons, a PFV ensures monoseman-
      ticity, capturing a singular, coherent concept from the multi-channel features at a specific spatial


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      location. Therefore, we decompose a layer in a network using PFVs. More specifically, a PFV in the
217   preactivation space is linearly decomposed with the concept vectors.
218
219   Secondly, we use the iERF as the PFV’s label. Receptive Field (RF) denotes the region within the
220
      input image that influences the activation of a specific feature, defining the spatial extent over which
      the input pixels contribute to the feature’s activation. Luo et al. (2016) introduced the concept of the
221
      Effective Receptive Field (ERF), revealing that contrary to the theoretical RF, the actual impact of
222
      input pixels is concentrated around the center and follows a Gaussian distribution. Han et al. (2024)
223   further refined this concept to instance-specific Effective Receptive Field (iERF) to highlight the
224   differential impact of individual pixels, identifying those that are most influential in the computation
225   of the PFV. With iERF, we directly attribute a meaning (or a concept) to each hidden layer feature
226   vector (PFV in our case), in contrast to other existing methods, which infer feature vector meaning
227   through indirect techniques. Ghorbani et al. (2019) and Kowal et al. (2024) used global average
228   pooling after masking, and Fel et al. (2023) used bilinear interpolation on the masked feature maps
229   to create a squared region to provide an indirect explanation of feature vectors by transforming
230   segmented areas into representative vectors. Yet, with iERF, we explicitly assign meanings to the
231
      hidden layer feature vectors, treating them as representations of specific concepts so that we can offer
      a more straightforward interpretation of how particular features contribute to the model’s decisions.

235
236   3.2.1    C ONCEPT VECTOR GENERATION
237
      In our framework, a concept is defined as a linear basis in the high-dimensional PFV space that
238   represents semantically meaningful features across different images, transcending classes. To find
239   out the concept vector in each layer, we utilize ImageNet validation dataset, consisting of 50,000
240   images. Even though there are HW PFVs within a single layer, we take only one PFV and its corre-
241   sponding iERF, resulting in 50,000 PFV-iERF pairs per layer for the dataset. In an image, we sample
242   a PFV in a non-uniform sense to reflect its contribution to the output (logit), due to the foreground-
243   background imbalance problem in images; If we sample PFVs randomly from an image, then the
244   majority would capture the background, which would be irrelevant to the output class. For example,
245   the sky in an image could be present across various classes, leading to an overrepresentation of the
246
      class-irrelevant feature, sky, rather than critical features like a bird’s beak. This overrepresentation of
      irrelevant features within the PFVs could skew the identification of the principal axes of the PFVs.
247
      Thus, to address the foreground-background imbalance, we sample a single Pointwise Feature Vec-
248
      tor (PFV) from each image. This PFV is chosen probabilistically, with a preference for those that
249   contribute more significantly to the model’s output logits. This approach ensures that the selected
250   PFVs are highly relevant to the class predictions, thereby creating a more balanced representation of
251   important features across the dataset. Details of how the contribution was calculated are provided in
252   the Appendix. C.
253
      With this balanced PFV-iERF dataset, we employ a bisecting k-means clustering (Steinbach et al.,
254
      2000), which iteratively splits the data into two clusters until a predefined number of clusters is
255   reached. This approach effectively navigates the complex manifold of image data, where some re-
256   gions are sparse, containing rare or atypical features, while others are dense, filled with frequently
257   encountered features. After clustering, we assign the centroid of each cluster as a concept vector.
258   The detailed procedures are included in the Appendix. B.

420   4.2.1    VALIDATION OF C ONCEPT E XTRACTION
421
422   To validate our method, we assess its fidelity using the C-Deletion and C-Insertion metrics, as pro-
423   posed by Fel et al. (2024). These methods provide a robust framework for evaluating the alignment
424   between our explanation model and the original model’s behavior by systematically modifying con-
425   cept activations and observing the resulting impact on model predictions.
426   In C-Deletion and C-Insertions, concept vectors are removed or inserted in the order of their impor-
427   tance, and the Area Under the Curve (AUC) of the accuracy drop graph is measured. The importance
428   score of a concept is calculated with Eq. (7), as it is the most reliable CAT method (Fel et al., 2024).
429   For C-Deletion, a lower AUC indicates a more effective extraction method, as it signifies a greater
430   impact on model performance when key concepts are removed. Conversely, in C-Insertion, a higher
431   AUC is preferable, reflecting a more accurate prediction when important concepts are introduced.
      Finally, we measure the AUC difference to see the overall trends in every layer.

      To validate the effectiveness of our concept attribution method, Generalized Integrated Gradients
473
      (GIG), we adapted the concept insertion and deletion strategies typically used in evaluating Concept
474   ATtribution (CAT) methods. The original C-Insertion and C-Deletion metrics quantify the relation-
475   ship between the identified concept vectors and the target class. By systematically inserting or delet-
476   ing concept vectors according to their attribution scores and observing changes in the target class
477   score, we assessed the validity of the concept vectors of CAT methods.
478
      We extended this metric to validate the relationship between concept vectors in different layers. As
479
      derived in Sec. 3.3, the class label can be seen as the one-hot concept vector of the last layer after
480   the fully-connected layer. Therefore, we validated the efficacy of our inter-layer concept attribution
481   method, observing the changes in the direction of the target concept vector in the subsequent layer
482   by inserting or deleting concept vectors from a preceding layer. Specifically, we deleted or inserted
483   concept vectors from the source layer one by one and observed the changes in the output of the target
484   layer (with dimensions H b ×W b ×C). For instance, if we delete concept vectors related to the target
485   concept “dog nose” from the source layer in order of their GIG attribution, the output in the target
      layer corresponding to the “dog nose” direction should decrease accordingly. However, given that

534
535   In this paper, we firstly present a novel approach for extracting and attributing concepts within
536   image models, enhancing interpretability through a comprehensive layer-wise analysis. Unlike ex-
537   isting methods that often confine their explanations to specific classes, our approach provides a
538   comprehensive understanding by analyzing shared concepts throughout the dataset. The shift from
539   class-specific to dataset-wide explanations represents a significant advancement in the field of XAI
      in image models, allowing for a more holistic understanding of model behavior.

541   meaningful concept vectors, and further attribute these concepts across layers using the Generalized
542   Integrated Gradients (GIG) method. With our method, we can reveal how concepts evolve and influ-
543   ence decisions across different layers of the network. Through extensive qualitative and quantitative

      To enhance the clustering process, we adopt the Bisecting k-means clustering. This approach be-
735   gins by treating all the data as a single cluster. In each iteration, the largest cluster is selected for

738   then divided into two sub-clusters using the Spherical k-means algorithm. During this process, the
