011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Representation learning of Text-Attributed Graphs (TAGs) has garnered signifi-
014            cant attention due to its applications in various domains, including recommen-
015
               dation systems and social networks. Despite advancements in TAG learning
               methodologies, challenges remain in explainability due to the black-box nature
016
               of existing TAG representation learning models. This paper presents TAGEx-
017
               plainer, the first method designed to generate natural language explanations for
018            TAG learning. TAGExplainer employs a generative language model that maps
019            input-output pairs to explanations reflecting the model’s decision-making process.
020            To address the lack of annotated ground truth explanations in real-world scenar-
021            ios, we propose first generating pseudo-labels that capture the model’s decisions
022            from saliency-based explanations, then the pseudo-label generator is iteratively
023            trained based on three training objectives focusing on faithfulness and brevity via
024            Expert Iteration, to improve the quality of generated pseudo-labels. The high-
025            quality pseudo-labels are finally utilized to train an end-to-end explanation gen-
026
               erator model. Extensive experiments are conducted to demonstrate the effective-
               ness of TAGExplainer in producing faithful and concise natural language expla-
027
               nations. Our code is available at https://anonymous.4open.science/
028
               r/TAGExplainer-6FEB/.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Graph representation learning, which aims to understand graph-structured data, has garnered signif-
034   icant attention in research, particularly through methods like Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) (Kipf
035   & Welling, 2016; Hamilton et al., 2017). Recently, learning on Text-Attributed Graphs (TAGs), a
036   type of graph where nodes are associated with texts, has received increasing focus from the com-
037
      munity due to its universal discriminative power in various domains Zhou et al. (2019); Yang et al.
      (2021). For example, in e-commerce graphs of recommendation systems, products can be associated
038
      with textual descriptions; in social networks, each user can also be enriched by textual descriptions.
039
      There have been significant efforts made for TAG learning, including cascaded models which use
040   pre-trained language models to extract text embeddings from nodes and then feed to GNN as node
041   features (Zhou et al., 2019; Zhu et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2022), and nested models which use one
042   model to jointly process text and graph information (Yang et al., 2021; Chien et al., 2021; Jin et al.,
043   2023). The recent raising of Large Language Models (LLMs) also brings light to LLMs-augmented
044   TAG learning pipelines, such as (He et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024). However, similar to graph
045   learning, TAG learning also faces challenges in terms of explainability due to the inherent black-box
046   nature of models. Although LLMs can also generate rationales for explaining their predictions, they
047   do not necessarily reflect the inner decision process of the model (Agarwal et al., 2024; Parcalabescu
048
      & Frank, 2024). In this work, we dive into the problem of explainability in TAG learning, which is
      a rising yet not well-explored area.
049
050   For graph learning, explainability methods have received a large amount of attention (Ying et al.,
051   2019; Vu & Thai, 2020; Luo et al., 2020). Existing explanation models often provide feature
052   importance-based explanations, by giving node- and edge-level importance scores, but they lack the
053   ability to explain the semantic information in TAGs since the node- and edge-level importance scores
      cannot include any information in the text features. Some methods (Ying et al., 2019; Štrumbelj &


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Kononenko, 2014; Bach et al., 2015) can also explain the importance of each individual node fea-
055   ture, and thus can generate better TAG explanations by further giving the importance scores of each
056   token. However, TAG predictions are often made based on a subgraph with many nodes and their
057   associated texts, simply giving each token an importance score may bring too much redundant in-
058   formation without a well-integrated and narrated context, causing a low human understandability in
059   generated explanations, as illustrated in Fig. 1 (a). Therefore, a form of more human-understandable
060   explanation is necessary for TAG learning, which should be summative and concise, as one example
061   shown in Fig. 1 (b). The limitation and need for human understandability necessitates our research
062   on natural language explanations for TAGs.

088   In this work, we present TAGExplainer, the first method to generate natural language explanations
089   for TAG learning. Our goal is to have a generative language model that can be seen as a mapping
090   from input-output pairs to explanations, and the generated explanations should faithfully reflect the
091
      decision-making process and be friendly for humans to understand. This goal cannot be achieved by
      zero-shot querying an LLM since it has no access to the model’s internal decision-making process.
092
      Therefore, it is necessary to fine-tune the generative model with the labels of explanation. Since
093
      in real-world scenarios, it is impractical to have sufficient amount of annotated ground truth data
094   for explaining model behaviors, we prompt an LLM with saliency-based explanations to generate
095   natural language explanation pseudo-labels. To improve the quality of pseudo-labels, we propose
096   three training objectives related to faithfulness and brevity, and iteratively fine-tune the pseudo-label
097   generator model with these objectives with expert iteration. Finally, the generated pseudo-labels are
098   used to train an end-to-end explainer model, which serves as our end-to-end explanation generation
099   model.
100   Our contribution can be summarized as follows:
101
102          • We propose TAGExplainer, a novel framework to generate natural language explanations
103            for TAG learning models. TAGExplainer is a model-agnostic black-box model explanation
104            method that can generate post-hoc explanations based on the input and prediction.
105          • We propose to narrate saliency-based TAG explanations by our TAG explanation verbaliza-
106            tion and prompting. Our graph verbalization preserves the semantic information, structural
107            information as well as feature importance, and forms a more LLM-understandable format
               of input to be prompted the explanation pseudo-label generator LLM.

             • We propose to iteratively self-train the explanation pseudo-label generator LLM via TAG
109            Explanation Expert Iteration with three objectives for improving the graph explanation
110            quality, considering the faithfulness to important input, faithfulness to output, and brevity.
111
             • We conducted extensive experiments to validate the effectiveness of our proposed frame-
112
               work. Experiments show that TAGExplainer can self-improve for label generation and
113
               generate more faithful and brief explanations.

118   2.1   E XPLAINABILITY OF G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORKS
119
      GNNs have been widely adopted in fields such as social networks, molecular chemistry, and finan-
120
      cial systems, yet their interpretability remains a significant challenge. Existing GNN explanation
121
      methods are generally categorized into instance-level and model-level approaches. Model-level
122   methods seek to provide a broad understanding of GNN behavior independent of specific inputs.
123   For example, ProtGNN (Zhang et al., 2022) introduces prototype learning, allowing GNNs to ex-
124   plain predictions through comparisons with learned prototypes, while XGNN (Yuan et al., 2020)
125   uses reinforcement learning to generate synthetic graphs that reveal the structures driving GNN
126   decisions. Both methods offer global insights into model behavior, emphasizing interpretability
127   while maintaining performance. On the other hand, instance-level methods focus on explaining
128   individual predictions by identifying important features that influence the model’s decisions, pri-
129   marily through feature importance. These methods include gradients/features-based approaches like
130
      SA (Baldassarre & Azizpour, 2019) and Guided BP (Baldassarre & Azizpour, 2019), perturbation-
      based methods such as GNNExplainer (Ying et al., 2019) and PGExplainer (Luo et al., 2020), as
131
      well as decomposition techniques like LRP (Baldassarre & Azizpour, 2019; Schwarzenberg et al.,
132
      2019) and surrogate models such as GraphLime (Huang et al., 2022). While these methods pro-
133   vide valuable insights into model behavior, they rely on graphical or numerical outputs and do not
134   generate natural language explanations (NLE). Our proposed framework also focuses on instance-
135   level explanations, aiming to enhance the interpretability of individual predictions while addressing
136   current limitations in generating NLE. While significant progress has been made in improving the
137   explainability of GNNs, current instance-level methods are limited to providing feature importance-
138   based explanations, which is difficult for humans to understand when the input comes to TAGs. To
139   our best knowledge, no efforts have been made to generate natural language explanations of graph
140   models.
141
142   2.2   NATURAL L ANGUAGE E XPLANATION
143
      The traditional explanation methods for NLP, such as feature importance and saliency maps (Lei
144
      et al., 2016; Yu et al., 2019), often fall short in providing human-interpretable insights, motivating
145
      the development of more intuitive approaches like natural language explanations (Cambria et al.,
146   2023). These methods provide textual justifications for model predictions, aiming to bridge the
147   gap between model decisions and human understanding (Camburu et al., 2018; Rajani et al., 2019;
148   Narang et al., 2020). A notable example is self-explanation models, where models like those studied
149   by Wiegreffe et al. (2020) predict labels while simultaneously generating explanations in natural
150   language. Further advancements, such as STaR (Zelikman et al., 2022), incorporate ground truth
151   answers to refine explanations when model predictions are incorrect. With the advent of LLMs,
152   Chain-of-Thought prompting (Wei et al., 2022) and zero-shot reasoning (Kojima et al., 2022) have
153   enhanced their self-explanation abilities by generating coherent, step-by-step explanations. Addi-
154
      tionally, LLMs’ role as explainers for both their own predictions and other models’ outputs has
      significantly expanded (Kroeger et al., 2023; Gat et al., 2023; Martens et al., 2023). However, LLM-
155
      generated explanations are criticized as not able to reflect the internal model behaviours (Agarwal
156
      et al., 2024; Parcalabescu & Frank, 2024).

201
202   The overall framework of TAGExplainer is illustrated in Figure 2. The high-level idea is we first
203   train a Pseudo-Label Generator LLM to generate high-quality explanation labels, then the generated
204
      pseudo-labels are used to fine-tune an Explainer LLM. In the pseudo-labels generation phase, we
      first attain saliency-based TAG explanations with a saliency-based explainer, then verbalize it to
205
      the form of a Saliency Paragraph, and pass the Saliency Paragraph to the Pseudo-Label Generator
206
      LLM. The Pseudo-Label Generator LLM can be iteratively self-trained via Expert Iteration based on
207   our proposed three training objectives to improve the quality of generated explanations. Finally, the
208   generated high-quality pseudo-labels from the fine-tuned Pseudo-Label Generator LLM are used to
209   train the end-to-end Explainer LLM. The details of our proposed TAGExplainer framework will be
210   introduced in the following.
211
212   4.1         S ALIENCY- BASED TAG E XPLANATION G ENERATION AND V ERBALIZATION
213
214   Due to the lack of ground truth explanation labels, we propose to generate pseudo-labels from a
215   pre-trained LLM. Generally, we prompt this Pseudo-Label Generator LLM with saliency-based ex-
      planations and ask it to generate explanation candidates based on them. As illustrated in Fig. 2 (a),


                                                                                     4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      specifically, a saliency-based explainer is first adopted to get the Saliency Textual Graph explana-
217   tions, and then we transform the graph explanation into textual forms with two steps of BFS Tree
218   Construction with Importance Ranking and Pre-Order Traversal Organizing. Finally, we prompt
219   them to the pseudo-label generator LLM to get the initial explanation of pseudo-label candidates.
220   Such a procedure will be introduced in details as follows.
221
      Saliency-based explanation generation. As shown in Fig. 2 (a) Step ⃝   1 , the saliency-based expla-
222
      nations are generated by a feature importance-based post-hoc explainer. They are represented in the
223   form of the importance score of each node, edge, and token. An example is illustrated in Fig. 2 (a)
224   as Saliency Textual Graph, where the red color from light to dark denotes the importance of nodes
225   and tokens, and the boldness of the links between nodes denotes the edge importance. Note that here
226   the saliency-based explainer can be any explainer that can generate post-hoc explanations for a TAG
227   model, such as the widely used model-agnostic explanation methods including LRP, Input×Grad,
228   Saliency, etc., so TAGExplainer is a model-agnostic framework for explaining TAG learning.
229   Saliency textual graph verbalization. Since the generated Saliency Textual Graph is naturally
230   graph-structured data, it’s vital to transform it into a form that LLMs are easier to understand. There-
231   fore, we propose saliency textual graph verbalization, to transform the saliency-based graphs expla-
232   nation into a document-like Saliency Paragraph to let LLMs better understand it, without losing
233   any structural, semantic or feature importance information. Our verbalization methods include two
234   steps, 1) BFS Tree Construction with Importance Ranking and 2) Pre-Order Traversal Organizing,
235   as shown in steps ⃝2 and ⃝  3 in Fig. 2 (a).
236   1) BFS Tree Construction with Importance Ranking. In a TAG model prediction, the structure of
237   a node and its k-hop salient nodes can be represented as an ego graph, with the node itself as the
238   root. Using Breadth-First Search (BFS), this ego graph can be decomposed into a hierarchical tree,
239   as illustrated in step ⃝
                             2 in Figure 2 (a). Note that there can be a set of cross-edges that connect nodes
240   in the BFS tree, which is illustrated as the blue link in the above figure connecting nodes B and D.
241   During BFS, we rank the successors of the same node based on their node-level saliency scores,
242   where nodes with higher saliency scores appear earlier (in the left side of children nodes). This is to
243
      ensure important children nodes appear earlier in the following paragraph organizing procedure and
      be read earlier by the Pseudo-Label Generator LLM.
244
245   2) Pre-order Traversal Organizing. We adopt a Pre-Order Traversal-based procedure to organize the
246   tree structure into a document-like paragraph, as illustrated in step ⃝   3 in Figure 2 (a). To organize
247   them into a document-like saliency paragraph and keep the hierarchical structure of nodes, we adopt
248   the Pre-Order Traversal of our constructed tree structure (first visit the root, then the left subtree, then
249
      the right subtree). Each node’s text is represented as a section in the document, and the successors of
      a node are represented as subsections. The cross-edges are verbalized by adding references at source
250
      nodes to the sections containing their respective destination nodes if the destination node appears
251
      earlier in the traversal, as illustrated as the blue sentence in the Saliency Paragraph in Figure 2 (a).
252   This approach ensures that the document faithfully reflects the graph’s structure.
253
254   When organizing the Saliency Paragraph, we attach the saliency information both explicitly and
255
      implicitly to prompt the Pseudo-Label Generator LLM with the feature importance information.
      Firstly, as mentioned earlier, for nodes belonging to the same parent node, we re-rank them based
256
      on their node-level importance score from higher to lower. For tokens in nodes, we attach their
257
      importance score after the tokens to prompt with the importance information, in the form of
258   token(score), without perturbing the semantic order of tokens. For all cross-edges, we also
259   attach their importance score when mentioning them. An example of the Saliency Paragraph is also
260   given in Figure 2 (a).

268   improve the text explanation quality by an explanation expert iteration framework that iterates the
269   text explanation quality measuring, high-quality text explanation selecting, and text explana-

281   So naturally, we measure its faithfulness to R as the Pointwise Mutual Information (PMI) between

321   4.2.2   TAG E XPLANATION E XPERT I TERATION
322
323   To effectively optimize the objectives to improve the Pseudo-Label Generator LLM, we propose a
      TAG explanation iterative training method based on Expert Iteration (Dong et al., 2023; Gulcehre


                                                        6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      et al., 2023), as shown in Fig. 2 (b). Specifically, the training is composed of a loop of text expla-
325   nation quality measuring, high-quality text explanation selecting, and text explanation updating, as
326   introduced in details as follows:
327
328         (1) Text explanation quality measuring. Aligned with our training objectives, we calculate the
329             scores fS , fF and fB of the generated explanation pseudo-label E based on Eq. 2, Eq. 3
330             and Eq. 4.
331         (2) High-quality text explanation selecting. Among all generated explanations, a subset of
332             high-quality explanations is selected from all candidates with customizable criteria for ad-
333             justing the trade-off of fS , fF , and fB , such as weighted sum and top-k.
334
            (3) Text explanation updating. The selected high-quality explanations are used to fine-tune
335             the Pseudo-Label Generator LLM. Then the model generates a new batch of explanation
336             candidates, and it goes back to step (1).
337
338   Such three steps form a closed loop, allowing us to iteratively increase the performance of the
339   model. Finally, we got the Pseudo-Label Generator LLM fine-tuned to generate faithful and brief
340   explanations with input as the saliency paragraph.
341
342   4.3    E ND - TO -E ND E XPLAINER T RAINING VIA K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION
343
344   The above loop gives us a model expert in generating high-quality explanations based on saliency-
345   based explanations. However, our goal is to be an end-to-end explainer model that can generate
346
      natural language explanations based on the raw input and its prediction. Therefore, after fine-tuning,
      we distill the whole pipeline to the Explainer LLM. The distillation is conducted by generating a set
347
      of candidates with the pseudo-label generation pipeline, filtering the high-quality ones and using the
348
      filtered dataset to fine-tune the Explainer LLM.

353   5.1    E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
354
355   Datasets. We use three real-world TAG datasets, including two citation networks (Cora (Yang
356   et al., 2016) and DBLP (Tang et al., 2008)), and one E-commerce co-purchasing network (Book-
357
      History (Yan et al., 2023)), to evaluate the performance of our method. Cora is a widely used
      citation network where each node represents a research paper, and edges represent citation links be-
358
      tween papers, with each paper assigned to one of several topics. DBLP dataset is a comprehensive
359
      bibliography in computer science containing metadata on academic papers, authors, and collabora-
360   tions. We extracted the top 30 most frequently occurring categories from DBLP, along with their
361   corresponding papers. The Book-History dataset is an E-commerce co-purchasing network where
362   nodes represent books, and edges indicate books frequently purchased or browsed together, with
363   each book classified into different categories. More details of the datasets are in Appendix B.
364
      Compared Methods. To our best knowledge, no existing method can generate natural language
365
      explanations for graph learning. The most relevant method is SMV (Feldhus et al., 2022), which
366   is designed to verbalize saliency map explanations for text classification models. To evaluate the
367   effectiveness of TAGExplainer, we compare it with various most advanced LLMs to generate expla-
368   nations in a zero-shot manner given the input subgraph and the model prediction. We benchmarked
369   our method with the most advanced LLMs, including GPT-4o, GPT-3.5 turbo, LLaMA 3, and SMV
370   method based on GPT-4o (denoted as SMV in the results).
371
      Evaluation Metrics. For an overall evaluation of the quality, we evaluate the faithfulness and brevity
372   of explanations. Following previous research (Padmakumar & He, 2021; Li et al., 2020), Pointwise
373   Mutual Information (PMI) and Simulatability (Simul.) are used as indicators for faithfulness. PMI

518   5.4   A BLATION S TUDY
519
520   In the ablation study, we removed fS , fF , fB and Expert Iteration to test their corresponding ef-
521
      fectiveness, as shown in Table 2. The results highlight the effectiveness of different components
      of the proposed framework. Removing the saliency optimization objective (w/o fS ) leads to a de-
522
      crease in PMI scores, proving its importance for relevance. Excluding the fidelity objective (w/o
523
      fF ) results in lower simulatability, and removing the brevity optimization objective (w/o fB ) results
524   in decreases brevity, proving its role in keeping explanations concise. Removing the self-training
525   process (w/o Expert Iteration) leads to declines in all metrics, underscoring its importance for itera-
526   tive refinement of the proposed framework. Notably, when one objective is removed, the other two
527   often improve. For instance, removing the saliency objective (w/o fS ) decreases PMI scores, but
528   simulatability improves and brevity shortens. This is because the three objectives inherently involve
529   trade-offs—removing one allows the remaining two to be optimized within a larger space, free from
530   the constraints imposed by the removed objective, naturally leading to better performance in those
531   areas.

533   6     C ONCLUSION
534
535   In this paper, we present TAGExplainer, a model-agnostic post-hoc explainer to generate natural lan-
536   guage explanations for TAG learning models. TAGExplainer fine-tunes a generative language model
537   as an explanation generator with pseudo-labels derived from saliency-based explanations. Through
538   iterative self-training, we improve the quality of generated explanation pseudo-labels, ensuring the
539   explanation generator can be trained with high-quality data. Our extensive experiments demonstrate
      the effectiveness of TAGExplainer.
