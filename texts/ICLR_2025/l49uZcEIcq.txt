011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Although Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated their strong capa-
014            bilities in various tasks, recent work has revealed LLMs also exhibit undesirable
015            behaviors, such as hallucination and toxicity, limiting their reliability and broader
016            adoption. In this paper, we discover an understudied type of undesirable behavior
017            of LLMs, which we term Verbosity Compensation (VC) — similar to the hesita-
018            tion behavior of humans under uncertainty — where they respond with excessive
019
               words such as repeating questions, introducing ambiguity, or providing excessive
               enumeration. We present the first work that defines and analyzes Verbosity Com-
020
               pensation, explores its causes, and proposes a simple mitigating approach. Our
021
               experiments, conducted on five datasets of knowledge and reasoning-based QA
022            tasks with 14 newly developed LLMs, reveal three conclusions. 1) We reveal a
023            pervasive presence of VC across all models and all datasets. Notably, GPT-4 ex-
024            hibits a VC frequency of 50.40%. 2) We reveal the large performance gap between
025            verbose and concise responses, with a notable difference of 27.61% on the Qasper
026            dataset. We also demonstrate that this difference does not naturally diminish as
027            LLM capability increases. Both 1) and 2) highlight the urgent need to mitigate the
028            frequency of VC behavior and disentangle verbosity with veracity. We propose a
029            simple yet effective cascade algorithm that replaces the verbose responses with the
030
               other model-generated responses. The results show that our approach effectively
               alleviates the VC of the Mistral model from 63.81% to 16.16% on the Qasper
031
               dataset. 3) We also find that verbose responses exhibit higher uncertainty across
032
               all five datasets, suggesting a strong connection between verbosity and model
033            uncertainty. We will release our code and dataset upon acceptance.

037   1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039   Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across various tasks,
040   including reasoning (Hendrycks et al., 2021b; Cobbe et al., 2021), knowledge-based question an-
041   swering (Dasigi et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2018), and planning (Valmeekam et al., 2023; Zheng et al.,
042   2024). However, despite these impressive capabilities, numerous studies have highlighted that LLMs
043   struggle with undesirable behaviors, such as hallucination (Huang et al., 2023), toxicity (Wen et al.,
044
      2023), and ethical bias (Tao et al., 2023), which can pose significant risks to users.
045   Recently, few studies have studied lengthy responses in LLMs, under chain-of-thought (Chiang &
046   Lee, 2024; Nayab et al., 2024) and machine translation (Briakou et al., 2024) settings. However,
047   these studies only analyze the lengthiness of the responses. Singhal et al. (2023) found the correlation
048   between length and RLHF. However, the mechanism of decoding lengthy responses remains unknown.
049   In this paper, we discover an understudied undesirable behavior of LLMs. We term it Verbosity
050   Compensation (VC) behavior. We define VC as generating responses that can be compressed without
051   information loss when prompted to respond concisely. Instead of focusing merely on the lengthy
052   issue, VC emphasizes the detailed behavior of generating compressible tokens with a low density of
053   useful information. We also find VC is closely connected with the uncertainty of LLMs, demystifying
      the mechanism of the VC behavior, and improving the understanding of both VC and uncertainty.

071   Interestingly, VC is similar to the hesitation behavior of humans under uncertainty (Juola, 2008;
072   Brookshire & McNeil, 2014). Figure 1 shows a motivating example. In the first response, LLM
073   generates a concise answer that is correct with low uncertainty. In the second and third responses,
074   instead of generating an answer concisely, such as “16.5”, LLM repeats the question, and produces
075   ambiguity, leading to a VC response with low performance and high uncertainty. VC is harmful and
076
      undesired for both users and servers. For the users, VC will lead to confusion and inefficiency (Fowler,
      1927; Oppenheimer, 2006). When an LLM enumerates multiple answers, users are unclear which
077
      one is correct. Besides, VC also leads to bias among users of different length preferences if verbose
078
      answers attain higher/lower scores. For the servers, the verbosity leads to unnecessary higher costs
079   and higher latency by generating useless tokens.
080
081   To analyze the VC behavior systematically, we unify four long-context question-answering datasets
082
      and a reasoning-based language understanding dataset. Targeting observing indirect or low density
      of the information in the response rather than comparing the lengthiness of the result, we simply
083
      pick the samples with less than three tokens to easily judge VC behavior by counting the tokens in
084
      responses. We benchmark 14 newly proposed LLMs on proposed datasets. Although we find that
085   different models and datasets exhibit diverse distribution, we can categorize VC into five distinct
086   types, including repeating questions, enumerating, ambiguity, verbose details, and verbose format.
087   The result reveals a pervasive presence of verbosity compensation (VC) across all models and
088   all datasets. Notably, GPT-4 exhibits a VC frequency of 50.40%. Meanwhile, we found that verbose
089   responses exhibit significantly different recall from concise ones, with a notable drop of 27.61% on
090   the Qasper dataset, highlighting the urgent need to disentangle verbosity with veracity.
091   Next, we measure the uncertainty of model responses using perplexity and Laplacian scores for open
092   and closed-source models. We find that verbose responses exhibit higher uncertainty across all five
093   datasets, suggesting a strong connection between verbosity and model uncertainty. Finally, we
094   leverage the connection between performance and VC to develop a routing algorithm that obtains
095   significant improvements over the random selecting baseline and uncertainty-based routing. To
096   mitigate VC in LLMs, we propose a simple yet effective cascade algorithm that replaces verbose
097   responses with responses of larger LLMs. Experiments demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed
098   algorithm through tests on three model combinations: Gemma to Gemini, Mistral to GPT-4, and
099
      Llama to Claude. The results show that our approach effectively alleviates the VC of the Mistral
      model from 63.81% to 16.16% on the Qasper dataset. The insights above can inspire the development
100
      of practical applications and effective mitigation strategies. Future work can mitigate the uncertainty
101
      of the LLMs by alleviating VC behavior due to the proposed connections between them.
102
103   To summarize, our contribution is threefold:
104             • We define VC and propose a comprehensive benchmark to evaluate 14 LLMs, revealing
105               they suffer significantly from five types of VC.
106
107             • We conduct a rigorous analysis and connect VC to 1) model performance and 2) model
                  uncertainty, shedding light on future applications and mitigation.

112   2     R ELATED W ORK
113
114   Verbosity in LLM Responses Recently work has focused on the verbosity of LLM-generated con-
115
      tent and its implications. Concise thoughts (Nayab et al., 2024) use prompts to constraint the length
      of Chain-of-thought reasoning and generate more concise responses with better performance. Ivgi
116
      et al. (2024) investigate the fallback behavior of LLM-generated responses when facing uncertainty.
117
      They found that the more advanced an LLM is, its fallback behavior shifts from sequence repeti-
118   tions to degenerate text to hallucinations. Singhal et al. (2023) investigate the correlation between
119   generated length and reinforcement learning from human feedback(RLHF) techniques, discovering
120   that optimizing for response length is a significant factor behind RLHF. Saito et al. (2023) find that
121   LLMs sometimes prefer more verbose answers even if they have similar qualities. Zheng et al. (2023)
122   attack this by asking LLMs to evaluate longer and longer responses and observe if the performance
123   increases. By contrast, Huang et al. (2024) find that GPT-4 prefers short responses in faithfulness and
124   coverage when it comes to summarization. Unlike these works, we discover the connection between
125   performance and verbosity compensation behavior in both CoT and general QA settings and connect
126
      verbosity to uncertainty. Besides, we use the cascading model to mitigate verbosity while they use
      prompt engineering.
127
128
      Uncertainty Quantification of LLMs With the thriving of Large Language Models (LLMs),
129
      researchers have begun exploring uncertainty quantification in LLM responses (Geng et al., 2023).
130
      For white-box models, researcher have focused on unsupervised methods including entropy (Malinin
131   & Gales, 2020), similarity (Fomicheva et al., 2020; Lin et al., 2022), semantic (Kuhn et al., 2023;
132   Duan et al., 2023), and logits (Kadavath et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024), whereas for black models, the
133   uncertainty evaluation is based on multiple sampled output of the LLMs (Malinin & Gales, 2020; Lin
134   et al., 2023; Manakul et al., 2023) However, these works aim to improve the evaluation metrics for
135   LLM uncertainty while we focus on connecting uncertainty with verbosity compensation behavior.
136
137   Optimisation of LLM API Calls Recently, researchers have proposed to reduce the cost of
138   leveraging a pool of LLMs (Wang et al., 2024). Some of the works train a model to predict the
139   success rate of large or small LLMs (Ding et al., 2024) and route to the cheapest one that can
140   succeed (Šakota et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024). Different from routing algorithms that only pick one
141   LLM, FragulGPT (Chen et al., 2023) use a cascade algorithm to visit LLMs from weak to strong and
142
      use an LLM evaluator to judge if the response is good enough to use (Madaan et al., 2023). Ramı́rez
      et al. (2024) leverage the uncertainty of the prediction as the evaluator to evaluate both cascading
143
      and routing structures. Similarly, Gupta et al. (2024) inspect the bias of sequential uncertainty and
144
      propose to use token-level uncertainty as criteria. Our work, by contrast, aims at mitigating verbosity
145   compensation which has not explored before, and our evaluator is the verbosity of the response in the
146   cascade algorithm.

151
      In this section, we first introduce the definition and quantification of verbosity compensation, and
      then we propose the metrics for evaluating the correlation between verbosity compensation and
152
      performance, uncertainty, and alleviating it with LLM routing.

154
      3.1   V ERBOSITY C OMPENSATION OF LLM S
155
156   We first formalize the task as follows. A dataset D consists of multiple data samples where each
157   consists of a source text x, a query q, and a ground truth y. A large language model LLM (∗)
158   consumes the concatenation of x, q and an instruction I to produce the response r. We use |r| to
159   represent the tokens in r. For instruction I, we always ask LLM to generate as concisely as possible so
160   that the model is instructed not to generate verbose responses. We call these tasks Concise Question
161   Answering (CQA). Since the LLMs have maximum context window sizes Lc , we truncate the source
      to accommodate diverse context limits (details in A.3).

      We define a response r to exhibit verbosity if and only if it can be further compressed with fewer tokens
163   while keeping the same meaning. To detect VC, we define verbosity compensation detector V (x, y, r)
164   (abbreviated as the verbosity detector). Using this detector, VC behavior for an LLM is defined as a
165   triple (x, y, r) where V (x, y, r) = 1, describing that the VC occurs in the response   r. To quantify the

166   frequency of VC, we define it as the ratio of VC responses in each dataset (x,y)∈D V (x, y, r)/|D|.
167   It is worth noting that although V (x, y, r) contains ground truth answer y, y is usually used to provide
168   additional information and the verbosity detectors can work reference-free.

      3.4   A LLEVIATING V ERBOSITY C OMPENSATION WITH C ASCADE M ODEL S ELECTION
217
218   Although it is difficult to ask a single LLM to generate a concise but correct answer, the verbosity
219   compensation behavior can be mitigated by a ensemble of multiple models. To this end, we propose a
220   Cascade Model Selection algorithm (CaSel) to increase the chance of getting concise responses. The
221   algorithm is simple and straightforward (Algorithm 1). Given a list of LLMs from weak to strong, we
222   first ask the weak model to generate a response with r token. At any time if we detect V (x, y, r) = 1,
223   we stop the generation of the current sample and redo the generation by a stronger model and repeat
224
      the process. With the power of diverse LLM, the algorithm can finally obtain a response with less
      verbosity and better performance.

228
229   4.1   DATASET C ONSTRUCTION
230
231   The principles of constructing datasets are twofold. First, the quality of samples needs to be high. The
232   questions are picked from existing human-annotated datasets, with clear answers. We also filter out
233   Yes/No, True/False, or multi-choice questions to ensure the answer cannot be simply chosen from a
234   set of candidate answers. Second, the dataset should be challenging enough for LLMs with moderate
235
      performance levels. Otherwise, if the performance is close to 100 percent, the model is too certain
      about the answer and the phenomena is difficult to observe. Noting that most of the benchmark
236
      datasets LLMs already obtain performance higher than 90%, we craft two types of datasets to pose
237
      challenges to the model, including knowledge-based and reasoning-based question answering.
238
239
      Knowledge-based Question Answering. This category contains the QA datasets which aim at
240
      extracting knowledge from the given source text which is long (Bai et al., 2023) or in particular
241
      position (Liu et al., 2024). Firstly, we use long-context question-answering tasks whose difficulty
242   resides in picking out useful information across long context and gathering them to answer the
243   question. The distractor paragraphs will also incorporate the difficulty of recognizing the needed
244   information. Specifically, we collect the three long-form question-answering datasets as our evaluation
245   benchmark for long-context QA. These datasets display three levels of lengths, including short
246   (Qasper), medium (LongBench), and long (NarrativeQA). Qasper (Dasigi et al., 2021) is a question-
247   answering dataset over NLP papers. It also contains extractive, abstractive, yes/no, and unanswerable
248   questions. The average length of the source text is 4119.85 words. We also incorporate three datasets
249   from LongBench (Bai et al., 2023) to form a new dataset. We directly name it LongBench. It include
250
      HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018), MuSiQue (Trivedi et al., 2022), and 2WikiMultihopQA (Ho et al.,
      2020). The average length of the source text is 9522.36 words. NarrativeQA (Kočiský et al., 2018)
251
      is a QA dataset over entire books or movie transcripts. The answers can be abstract or extractive,
252
      yes/no, and unanswerable, and the average length is 70340.45 words.
253
254   LLMs are proven to show difficulties in understanding the information in the middle of the context (Liu
255   et al., 2024), known as lost-in-the-middle. We pick the most challenging split of the dataset in the
256
      original work, where the gold answer is in the middle of 30 documents for a QA pair in the Natural
      Question dataset. We call this NaturalQuestions 30 (NQ30). dataset. The average length of input of
257
      NQ30 is 3602.13. More details for dataset construction can be found in Appendix A.1.
258
259
260
      Reasoning-based Question Answering. We modify the multi-choice answering samples in
      MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021b;a) so that the options work as hints to the question. In this
261
      way, the model needs to generate the answer based on the hint rather than picking out the correct
262
      option, increasing the difficulty because of the flexibility of open-ended question answers.
263
264   For all these five datasets, to avoid the influence of gold length y and the easiness of measuring the
265   verbosity, we only pick the samples where the gold answer contains less than n words. We manually
266
      inspect the samples and find that when n = 4, the answers are concise without descriptive context,
      and any answer longer than gold length is verbose. Thus, we set n = 4 for all datasets.
267
268   For each dataset, if the number of samples is longer than 500, we randomly pick 500 samples from
269   them. Otherwise, we keep the entire dataset. Finally, there are 449 samples in LongBench, 410
      samples in NQ30, and 500 samples in Qasper, NarrativeQA, and MMLU.

      seen, for the uncertain response, the probability distribution is more flattened, and the tokens carrying

515   Using Cascade Model Selection for LLM Routing. Model routing aims to send the sample to

529   all settings and can surpass the performance of stronger models with less cost.

531   6      C ONCLUSION
532
533   In this paper, we systematically analyze the verbosity compensation behavior of LLM responses. We
534   first classify verbosity into five types and find all the models display high frequency on verbosity
535   responses. We further explore the reason behind and find uncertainty highly likely connected to
536   the phenomenon. We also propose a cascade model selection algorithm to mitigate it. Intensive
537   experiments show that LLMs suffer from verbosity compensation and the proposed simple approach
538   mitigates the verbosity compensation effectively.

760
761   Firstly, we use long-context question-answering tasks whose difficulty resides in picking out useful
762
      information across long context and gathering them to answer the question. The distractor paragraphs
      will also incorporate the difficulty of recognizing the needed information. Qasper (short) (Dasigi
763
      et al., 2021) is a question answering dataset over NLP papers. It also contains extractive, abstractive,
764
      yes/no, and unanswerable questions. The average length of the source text is 4119.85 words. We
765   also incorporate three datasets from LongBench (medium) (Bai et al., 2023) to form a new datasets.
766   HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018) is a Wikipedia-based multi-hop QA dataset. It requires reasoning
767   across multiple passages to find the answer. MuSiQue (Trivedi et al., 2022) is a multi-hop QA dataset.
768   It is much more difficult than HotpotQA as it contains more hops in one sample, unanswerable
769   questions, and harder distracting content. 2WikiMultihopQA (Ho et al., 2020) It consists of up to
770   5-hop questions that are synthesized through manually designed templates. The average length of the
771   source text is 9522.36 words. Different from the previous datasets, NarrativeQA (long) (Kočiský
772   et al., 2018) is a QA dataset over entire books or movie transcripts. The answers can be abstract
773
      or extractive, yes/no, and unanswerable, and the average length is 70340.45 words. Both Qasper
      and NarrativeQA datasets in our benchmark are extracted from SCROLLS dataset (Shaham et al.,
774
      2022). NaturalQuestions 30 (NQ30). LLMs are proven to show difficulties in understanding the
775
      information in the middle of the context (Liu et al., 2024), known as lost-in-the-middle. We pick the
776   most challenging split of the dataset in the original work, where the gold answer is in the middle of
777   30 documents for a QA pair in the Natural Question dataset. We call this NQ30 dataset. The average
778   length of input of NQ30 is 3602.13.
