009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Graph property prediction usually involves using a model to predict the label for
012
               the entire graph, which often has complex structures. Because input graphs have
013            different sizes, current methods generally use graph pooling to coarsen them into
014            a graph-level representation with a unified vector pattern. However, this coarsen-
015            ing process can lead to a significant loss of graph information. In this work, we
016            explore the graph representation by using a matrix pattern and introduce an al-
017            gorithm called Matrix-pattern-oriented Pooling (MatPool) that provides a unified
018            graph-level representation for different graphs. MatPool multiplies the transposed
019            feature matrix by the feature matrix itself and then conducts an isomorphic map-
020            ping to create a Matrix Representation (MR) that preserves the graph information
021
               and satisfies permutation invariance. Since the multiplication operation calculates
               the relationships between each feature, MR exhibits row-column correlations un-
022
               der the matrix pattern. To match this correlation, MatPool uses a novel and ef-
023
               ficient Matrix Neural Network (MNN) with two-sided weight matrices to match
024            the row-column correlation under the matrix pattern. We provide theoretical anal-
025            yses to reveal the properties of MatPool and explain why it can preserve graph
026            information and satisfy the permutation invariance. Extensive experiments on var-
027            ious graph property prediction benchmarks show the efficiency and effectiveness
028            of MatPool.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Graph-structured data Hu et al. (2020) are everywhere and play a key role in social networks
035   Fan et al. (2022), recommender systems Liu et al. (2023b), transportation Ye et al. (2020), and pro-
036   tein prediction Gao et al. (2023). With the development of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) Wu et al.
037   (2021; 2022b), GNNs are excellent at handling tasks that predict properties for individual nodes
038
      Gao et al. (2018); Wu et al. (2023). When working with individual nodes, the goal is to predict
      labels based on their connections and features within the graph.
039
040   In contrast, graph property prediction Xie et al. (2022) predicts labels for entire graphs, which can
041   vary significantly in size. Machine learning algorithms Bishop (2007); Goodfellow et al. (2016)
042   usually require data in a unified size, but the different sizes of graphs make it hard to use them
043   directly as inputs. Therefore, developing methods to provide a uniform graph-level representation
044
      is crucial for accurately predicting graph properties and improving the effectiveness of GNNs in
      various graph-level applications.
045
046   Similar to how pooling in Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) Rawat & Wang (2017) uses
047   down-sampling to gather feature information, graph pooling Liu et al. (2023a); Jin et al. (2020) gen-
048   erally applies down-sampling to coarsen node information into a unified graph-level representation
049   with a consistent vector size. This approach is especially useful for graphs that change in size, as it
050
      makes it easier to apply these representations to various machine-learning techniques.
051   Motivation: However, downsizing and coarsening in pooling methods often lead to significant loss
052   of graph information. To address this issue, we explore a new way of representing graphs using a
053   matrix pattern that retains graph information. Additionally, we design a specialized neural network
      to handle this matrix pattern, capturing the relationships between its rows and columns.

054
      Why matrix? The features of entire graphs are presented in the form of matrices. Although the
055   number of nodes in different graphs varies, resulting in different feature scales, all these graphs can
056   be transformed or mapped into a unified matrix space through isomorphic mappings. This means
057   that, despite the potential differences in their original features, through such transformations, they
058   can be compared, analyzed, or processed within a common space. Moreover, matrix pattern can
059   provide a more detailed and richer view of graphs than vector pattern.
060
      Consequently, we propose Matrix-pattern-oriented Pooling (MatPool) for graph property prediction.
061   MatPool has two key components: Matrix Representation (MR) to handle graphs of different sizes
062   and Matrix Neural Network (MNN) to explore the features of MR more deeply.
063
064
      Matrix Representation (MR): We design a message-passing way called Positive Eigenvalue Map-
      ping (PEM), enhancing the propagation influence of primary nodes and making the eigenvalues of
065
      the adjacency matrix positive. Next, we multiply the transposed feature matrix by the feature matrix
066
      itself and perform a isomorphic mapping to create a Matrix Representation (MR) for each graph.
067
068   Matrix Neural Network (MNN): The matrix-pattern-oriented MR for each graph exhibits strong
069   connections between rows and columns, which traditional CNNs or MLPs struggle to capture. To
070
      address this, we design a Matrix Neural Network (MNN) that uses two-sided weight matrices, al-
      lowing for more effective output calculations and naturally captures the row-column correlations.
071
072   As a result, we propose MatPool, a graph-level learning framework designed to predict properties
073   for graphs of varying sizes without losing graph information. The contributions of this study are
074   summarized as follows:
075
076
            • We create a Positive Eigenvalue Mapping (PEM), which enhances the propagation influ-
              ence of primary nodes, to aggregate the node features in the graph.
077
078         • We propose a Matrix Representation (MR) that isomorphically maps the varying graph
079           feature space into a unified graph-level space without lossing graph information.
080         • We design a novel Matrix Neural Network (MNN) that uses two-sided weight matrices to
081           efficiently capture row-column correlations and extract deeper features from the MR.
082
            • We provide theoretical analyses to reveal the properties of MatPool and explain why it
083
              can preserve graph information and satisfy permutation invariance. Extensive experiments
084           validate the efficiency and effectiveness of MatPool.
085
086   The rest of this paper is organized as follows: Section 2 briefly surveys related work on graph repre-
087   sentation learning and matrix learning. Section 3 describes the detailed implementation of MatPool.
088   In Section 4, experimental results on benchmark graph datasets demonstrate the effectiveness and
089   efficiency of MatPool. Finally, the conclusion is presented in Section 5.

      2   R ELATED WORK
092
093
      Graph Neural Networks (GNNs): GNNs are powerful machine learning algorithms for process-
094
      ing graph-structured data. They capture the dependency relationships between nodes during the
095
      message-passing process, enabling accurate and comprehensive analysis and prediction. As a novel
096   learning technology, GNNs continue to attract significant research interest and find applications in
097   various fields Gilmer et al. (2017); He & Zhao (2020); Saha et al. (2022). The Graph Convolutional
098   Network (GCN) Kipf & Welling (2017) is the most typical message-passing method for graph data,
099   aggregating node information from downstream layers.
100
      With in-depth research on graph-structured data, many algorithms have been proposed in recen-
101
      t years to address various problems such as heterogeneous graphs Wang et al. (2019); Ma et al.
102   (2022), over-smoothing Keriven (2022); Wei et al. (2023), and more Chen et al. (2022); Zhu et al.
103   (2023). For example, GraphSAGE Hamilton et al. (2017) utilizes sampling technology to solve
104   non-inductive and non-batch training issues. GIN Xu et al. (2019) improves the performance and
105   efficiency of graph neural networks based on the WL-test Shervashidze et al. (2011) that analyzes
106   the expressive ability of GNNs for different graph structures. DeepGCN Li et al. (2019; 2021b) and
107   DeeperGCN Li et al. (2021a) draw inspiration from the residual idea, modifying the propagating
      and aggregating framework to adapt to the training of deep models.

108
      Graph Pooling: The size of different graphs often varies, making it difficult for algorithms to handle
109   these size-varying graphs directly. Therefore, a unified graph-level representation is essential for
110   graph property prediction. Graph pooling methods Shen et al. (2018); Lee et al. (2018); Wang & Ji
111   (2023) effectively provide this unified representation for graphs of different scales. These methods
112   can be categorized into global pooling and hierarchical pooling.
113
      Global pooling methods Yuan & Ji (2020a); Bianchi et al. (2020b) consider the information of al-
114
      l nodes and pool the entire graph directly. For example, Set2Set Vinyals et al. (2016) finds the
115   importance of nodes to provide a unified graph-level representation for different graphs. Global-
116   Attention Li et al. (2016) uses an attention mechanism to aggregate entire graph information. Sort-
117   Pool Zhang et al. (2018) transforms the nodes by sorting and concatenating them. However, global
118   pooling may overly compress graph information during downsizing and coarsening.
119
      Hierarchical pooling methods Gao et al. (2022); Wu et al. (2022a) aggregate node features in a hi-
120
      erarchical structure. For example, DiffPool Ying et al. (2018) uses a differentiable pooling layer to
121   form a fixed number of clusters. TopK Gao & Ji (2019) scores nodes using a trainable projection vec-
122   tor and samples them based on their scores. Self-Attention Pooling Lee et al. (2019) improves TopK
123   by attention scores. Adaptive Structure Aware Pooling Ranjan et al. (2020) uses a self-attention
124   network to learn graph information by hierarchically capturing local subgraph information. These
125   hierarchical pooling methods align the size of graphs during the coarsening process.
126
      Matrix Learning: Matrix and vector features are two common data representations in machine
127   learning. Compared to vector features, matrix features Wang et al. (2008) provide a more detailed
128   and richer view, which is crucial for representing graph-structured data. Matrix features also nat-
129   urally express the interaction between features. Early methods like MatMHKS Chen et al. (2007)
130   were designed to handle matrix samples, such as images, without converting them to vectors, p-
131   reserving the spatial structure. Algorithms like MLMMPC Zhu et al. (2015) and BPDMatMHKS
132   Wang & Zhu (2018) integrate localization information to improve performance. More recent meth-
133   ods, such as EMatMHKS Zhu et al. (2020), greatly accelerate the training speed of MatMHKS and
134   demonstrate the generalization ability of matrix classifiers.
135   Although these matrix classifiers can be directly applied to matrix samples, they optimize the objec-
136   tive function using the Moore-Penrose inverse under the minimum square error. Consequently, they
137   fail to form a deep learning framework.
138
      Relations to Our Work: Key differences between our work and related research can be summarized
139   as follows: (i) We create a propagating and aggregating way called Positive Eigenvalue Mapping
140   (PEM), which differs from existing GNNs; (ii) We provide a graph-level Matrix Representation
141   (MR) and reveal its properties, which differ from existing vector-based models; (iii) We design a
142   novel Matrix Neural Network (MNN) using two-sided weight matrices to extract deeper features
143   from the MR.

      5                         C ONCLUSION
526
527
      Unlike existing graph pooling methods that provide graph-level representations based on vector pat-
528   terns and loss graph information, we explore the use of matrix patterns and propose a new method
529   named MatPool for representing and predicting graphs of different size. MatPool consists of three
530   main components: Positive Eigenvalue Mapping (PEM), Matrix Representation (MR), and Matrix
531   Neural Network (MNN). PEM reconstructs the adjacency matrix to have positive eigenvalues, en-
532   hancing the propagation ability of primary nodes. MR provides a unified matrix-pattern-oriented
533   representation with key properties such as permutation invariance and retention of graph informa-
534   tion. MNN is specifically designed to extract deeper features from the row-column correlated MR.
535   We have theoretically analyzed the properties of MatPool and conducted extensive experiments to
536
      validate its efficiency and effectiveness in graph properties prediction.
537   The main drawback of MatPool lies in the process of multiplying the feature matrix by its transpose.
538   Each feature in MR is obtained by summing the squares, which may result in significant numerical
539   variation. Therefore, a more reasonable normalization process and careful initialization of values in
      the MNN network are needed in the future.

       are n, e, and d respectively. The time complexity of PEM focuses on the propagating and aggregating
1022

1024
1025   The time complexity of MR focuses on the multiplication operation that costs O(nd2 ). Moreover,
       the dot product operation costs d2 . Therefore, the time complexity of MR is O(nd2 ).

       Finally, the time complexity of MNN focuses on the multiplication of two weight matrices. Suppose
1027   the output is ∈ Rd×d , both the left and right multiplications cost O(d3 ). Although the time com-
