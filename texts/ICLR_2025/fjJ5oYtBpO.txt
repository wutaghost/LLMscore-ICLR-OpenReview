009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011                E(3)-equivariant neural networks have achieved remarkable performance in
012                molecular modeling. However, the equivariance constraint limits the model’s ef-
013                fectiveness in learning tasks involving symmetry breaking, particularly those that
014                violate the celebrated Curie principle. Relaxing the equivariance constraint is es-
015                sential for addressing these challenges. In this paper, we explore the intricate sym-
016                metry relationships between an object and its spontaneously symmetry-broken
017                outcomes. We introduce a relaxed equivariance based on the molecule’s inher-
018
                   ent symmetries. Additionally, we develop SANN – a symmetry-adapted neural
                   network architecture that learns symmetry breaking through equivalence classes
019
                   of atoms. SANN decomposes the molecular point cloud into sets of symmetry-
020
                   equivalent atoms and performs message-passing both within and across these
021                classes. We demonstrate the advantages of our method over invariant and equiv-
022                ariant models through synthetic tasks and show that SANN effectively learns both
023                equivariance and symmetry breaking in various benchmark molecular modeling
024                tasks.

181   In this section, we begin by reviewing the concept of SSB. Then we formally characterize the in-
182   herent ambiguity associated with multiple equivalent outcomes of SSB; in particular, we employ
183   the recently proposed loss function by Xie & Smidt (2024) to measure this inherent ambiguity. We
184   further delve into the notion of relaxed equivariance through canonicalization introduced in (Kaba
185
      et al., 2023; Baker et al., 2024). This approach allows for a more flexible utilization of symmetries,
      especially in situations where strict equivariance may be overly restrictive. We explore how relaxed
186
      equivariance can enhance the learning of SB in terms of data efficiency.
187
188   3.1   S PONTANEOUS S YMMETRY B REAKING
189   While the Curie principle applies to many physical systems, there are real-world phenomena that
190   do not strictly adhere to it. SSB is a notable exception (Beekman et al., 2019; Castellani & Dar-
191   dashti, 2021), where the input exhibits a higher symmetry than the output. In SSB, the input can
192   spontaneously lose its symmetry and transition to one of several equivalent outputs, denoted by
193   SSB(x) := {g·y | g ∈ Sym (x)}, with lower symmetry. These outputs differ only by the symmetries
194   of the input x, meaning they share the same type of reduced symmetries, with their symmetry groups
195
      related by conjugation. To be more specific, for each possible output yi ∈ {g · y | g ∈ Sym (x)},
      we have
196
                      Sym (yi ) ⊊ Sym (x) , indicating that yi has lower symmetry than x.
197
198   Additionally, since two valid outputs yi , yj ∈ {g · y | g ∈ Sym (x)} differ only by a symmetry op-
                                                                                           −1
199   eration of x, say yi = gij · yj for some gij ∈ Sym (x), it follows that Sym (yj ) = gij Sym (yi ) gij ,
200   implying that these outputs shared the same reduced symmetry. An example of SSB is the transfor-
201   mation of a square into rectangles, as illustrated in Fig. 3. We can observe that the two rectangles
202
      differ only by the rotational symmetry of the square and share the same type of symmetries. More-
      over, these two rectangles are equivalent SSB outcomes of the square, as the two different stretching
203
      directions are indistinguishable from the perspective of the square itself.
204
205   3.2   S YMMETRY B REAKING M EASURE
206   Mathematically, SSB can be expressed as a set-valued function, mapping x to the set of equivalent
207   outputs SSB(x). However, it’s important to note that while an input may transition to different
208   outcomes at different times, it can only occupy one output state at a specific moment. Following
209   (Xie & Smidt, 2024), we define a single-valued function f : X → Y as learning the SSB of x ∈ X
210   if f (x) lies in the set of equivalent outputs SSB(x), i.e., f (x) ∈ SSB(x).
211   Consider a learnable function fθ . Suppose the training data includes only one possible outcome
212   of SSB for a given input x, says y ∈ SSB(x). If the function predicts a different valid output
213   fθ (x) = y ′ ∈ SSB(x), traditional loss functions like mean squared error (MSE) or cross-entropy
214   will report a non-zero value, even though both y and y ′ are valid. Moreover, if the training data
215   contains multiple equivalent outcomes y, y ′ ∈ SSB(x) for x, traditional loss functions might confuse
      the model, leading it to associate the incorrect output with the input. To address these challenges

      5    S YMMETRY-A DAPTED N EURAL N ETWORK

326   In this section, we present constitutions of our proposed symmetry-adapted neural network (SANN),
327   a new GNN architecture inspired by the concept of SALCs. SANN is designed to achieve relaxed
328   equivariance for learning spontaneous symmetry-broken features while compatible with the learning
329   of invariant and equivariant features. This flexibility allows SANN to serve as a powerful tool for
330   understanding molecular properties under varying symmetry constraints and SSB scenarios.
331   To effectively leverage molecular symmetries, SANN employs a new message-passing approach.
332   This approach involves passing messages within and across sets of symmetry-equivalent atoms,
333   respectively, followed by aggregation of information to capture the global features of molecules.
334   In this section, we will introduce the key components of our model: the canonicalization and the
335   construction of equivalence classes, the graph structures within and across equivalence classes, the
336   design of symmetry-adapted message-passing mechanisms, and the loss function for learning SSB.
337
338
      Setup. Let M = {(xi , ai )} be a molecular data. In particular, each atom ai might be associated
      with a vector of features provided by the dataset, denoted by ai .
339
340   Canonicalization and equivalence class construction. SANN achieves the relaxed equivariance
341   by leveraging canonicalization through a frame F (see Definition 3.5). Additionally, SANN relies
342   on the construction of quotient set M/ Sym (M) from each molecular structure M.
343   To simultaneously perform canonicalization and construct the quotient set, we adopt the asymmetric
344   unit normalization (ASUN) framework proposed by Baker et al. (2024). Notably, the quotient set
345   M/ Sym (M) and the asymmetric unit are closely related, with the latter being a minimal represen-
346   tative of the entire molecule selected from the former. Specifically, the asymmetric unit, ASU(M),
347   is defined as the smallest subset of M such that applying  S     the symmetry operations of Sym (M) to
348   ASU(M) will recover the entire molecule M, i.e. g∈Sym(M) g · ASU(M). It can be shown that

507   7     A DDITIONAL R ELATED W ORKS
508
509   We discuss some of the additional related works along two directions in the machine learning liter-
510   ature: relaxed equivariance and spontaneous symmetry breaking.
511
512
      Relaxed equivariance. Relaxed equivariance has been studied in various papers. For instance,
      Kaba et al. (2023) introduce relaxed equivariance to ease the learning of invariant feature embed-
513
      dings, which avoids using distinct homogeneous spaces in the framework of (Winter et al., 2022).
514
      Pertigkiozoglou et al. (2024) relax the hard equivariance constraint to ease the training of neural
515   networks. These existing relaxed equivariance approaches typically involve relaxing constraints on
516   specific layers of a neural network or learning canonicalization functions. This differs significantly
517   from our proposed work, which leverages the canonicalization approach introduced in Baker et al.
518   (2024) and avoids the need for additional learning processes. Moreover, these approaches are not ap-
519   plicable to learning symmetry-breaking. Besides relaxed equivariance, approximated equivariance
520   has also been studied in various works; see, e.g., (Huang et al., 2024; Samudre et al., 2024).
521   Symmetry breaking. SB has been explored in various ML contexts, including physical systems
522   (Wang et al., 2023b; Xie & Smidt, 2024; Lawrence et al., 2024) and the generation process of
523   diffusion models; see, e.g., (Raya & Ambrogioni, 2023; Ambrogioni, 2023; Li & Chen, 2024).
524   However, to the best of our knowledge, this paper is the first to investigate SB in ML-assisted
525   molecular modeling. By applying canonicalization to address the ambiguity of SSB and developing
526   a symmetry-adapted learning method, we effectively learn both symmetry-preserving and SB tasks
527   simultaneously.
528
529   8     C ONCLUSION
530
531   In this work, we have introduced SANN, a symmetry-adapted neural network for enhanced represen-
532   tation of molecular structures. SANN is capable of learning SSB via relaxed equivariance and our
533   newly proposed SB measure. Our work highlights the existing limitations of symmetry-preserving
534   neural networks using both synthetic tasks and real-world applications. Our work is limited by the
535   scale of existing real-world molecular datasets with SB molecular properties, and the lack of ac-
536   cessible existing SB benchmarks. Our theoretical foundation for SB models pave the way for the
537
      construction of a multitude of SB architectures. We leave it to future work to further explore optimal
      SB mechanisms.

803   The reliable development of our architecture requires significant improvement of existing methods.
804   We outline our adjustments in this section.
805
806   Equivalence class construction. The classical approach is to determine the point group of a
807   molecule via guess-and-check and then construct the classes of symmetry-equivalent atoms. The
808   minimal deterministic finite automata (DFA) (Hopcroft, 1971) constructed by the ASUN algorithm
809   of Baker et al. (2024), exactly consists of the classes of symmetry-equivalent atoms. While ASUN
      provides a lightweight technique for handling noisy data, our theoretical results are reliant on a


                                                                15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      robust construction of the equivalence classes. As a result, we enhance the tolerance mechanisms
811   of ASUN for handling angular, co-linear, and co-planar symmetries. Our enhancements denoted
812   ASUN+ achieve state-of-the-art results for canonicalizing QM9. Table 4 reports the mean earth
813   movers distance (EMD) between the initial canonical representation and 16 folds for all molecular
814   structures.

816
      Stabilizer construction. The point groups are nicely characterized in (Harris & Bertolucci, 1989),
817
      and an algorithm for point group detection is discussed. We follow this approach using the equiva-
818   lence class construction from above to construct the stabilizers for the point cloud. We denote linear
819   and planar structures as rank 1 and rank 2 respectively, following notation from (Baker et al., 2024).
820   In Figure 6 we outline the algorithm for stabilizer construction. The starting point in each rank is to
821   fix an axis of rotation. In rank 1 and rank 2 structures, this is already fixed by the nature of the data.
822   In rank 3 it requires a slightly more expensive process of checking subsequently larger equivalence
823   classes to determine if they have a mean which is not identically the origin. If they do then this point
824   and the origin form an axis of possible rotation. Planar reflections that lie along the axis of rotation
825   can be determined from the given axis of rotation.

939   C.4   I NSUFFICIENCIES IN E XISTING DATASETS
940
941   The aim of this call for expanded datasets is to encourage additional datasets analyzing order-
942   disorder and structural transitions Kivelson et al. (2024). In particular, we identify the cahllenges of
943   incorporating two types of datasets, trajectory and interaction datasets.
944   Trajectories. Trajectory datasets like MD17 contain snapshots of structures during relaxation with
945   the aim of predicting forces and energies from each snapshot. This equivariant task is marginal
946   compared to predicting the relaxed structure from the initial structure, which requires symmetry
947   breaking. Relaxed structure prediction is critical in drug design [4]. Initial trajectory datasets are
948   under development [6] and we leave this to future work.
949
      Trajectories. Trajectory datasets like MD17 contain snapshots of structures during relaxation with
950   the aim of predicting forces and energies from each snapshot. This equivariant task is marginal
951   compared to predicting the relaxed structure from the initial structure, which requires symmetry
952   breaking. Relaxed structure prediction is critical in drug design [4]. Initial trajectory datasets are
953   under development [6] and we leave this to future work.
954
      Interactions. Interaction datasets, the most prominent being the open catalyst project, contains
955
      interactions between adsorbates and catalysts. The adsorbates are small molecules that are ideally
956   suited to our architecture. However, the catalysts are crystalline structures with permutation group
957   symmetries that are not directly addressed by our approach. Therefore, we leave this to future work.

962   We assess the ability of PaiNN to learn without the tensor-valued predictive output. The two types of
963   models are denoted tensor-valued and vector-valued, respectively. The tensor-valued model utilizes
964   the gated equivariant output. The vector-valued model predicts a global invariant feature and then
965   regresses the value onto the original positions. We compare each type with +Noise and +MLP in
966
      Table 7. We observe that the tensor-valued PaiNN can learn the normal modes while the vector-
      valued PaiNN is not. This supports our conjecture that the normal modes, while ideal for symmetry
967
      breaking of vector-valued equivariant predictions, fail for higher-order representations.
