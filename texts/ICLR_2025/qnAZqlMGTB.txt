011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            The rapid development of Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) has
014            expanded their capabilities from image comprehension to video understanding.
015            However, most of these MLLMs focus primarily on offline video comprehension,
016            necessitating extensive processing of all video frames before any queries can be
017            made. This presents a significant gap compared to the human ability to watch,
018            listen, think, and respond to streaming inputs in real time, highlighting the lim-
019            itations of current MLLMs. In this paper, we introduce StreamingBench, the
020
               first comprehensive benchmark designed to evaluate the streaming video under-
               standing capabilities of MLLMs. StreamingBench assesses three core aspects of
021
               streaming video understanding: (1) real-time visual understanding, (2) omni-
022
               source understanding, and (3) contextual understanding. The benchmark con-
023            sists of 18 tasks, featuring 900 videos and 4,300 human-curated QA pairs. Each
024            video features five questions presented at different time points to simulate a con-
025            tinuous streaming scenario. We conduct experiments on StreamingBench with
026            13 open-source and proprietary MLLMs and find that even the most advanced
027            proprietary MLLMs like Gemini 1.5 Pro and GPT-4o perform significantly be-
028            low human-level streaming video understanding capabilities. We hope our work
029            can facilitate further advancements for MLLMs, empowering them to approach
030            human-level video comprehension and interaction in more realistic scenarios.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   The rapid evolution of Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) has significantly reshaped the
037   field of Artificial Intelligence (Yang et al., 2023; Reid et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024c;a). Current ad-
038   vanced MLLMs (Reid et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a; Yao et al., 2024) have already demonstrated
039
      exceptional performance in video understanding tasks, excelling on existing video benchmarks (Fu
      et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b; Zhou et al., 2024; Ataallah et al., 2024). Moreover, several pioneer-
040
      ing studies (Chen et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2024a; Wu et al., 2024) have focused on improving the
041
      ability of MLLMs to comprehend real-time online video streams, pushing the boundaries of their
042   applicability and efficiency in dynamic environments. In the industry, streaming video understand-
043   ing has also attracted significant attention, with OpenAI’s GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024) as a prominent
044   example that demonstrates human-like perception and understanding of streaming inputs.
045
      Despite the recognition of the importance of streaming video understanding for MLLMs, most ex-
046
      isting video understanding benchmarks (Fu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b; Zhou et al., 2024) are
047
      primarily designed for offline evaluation. In such setups, all video frames are pre-loaded into the
048   MLLMs before any queries are made, assuming the model has complete access to the entire video
049   content. In contrast, streaming video understanding tasks differ in three key aspects: (1) queries can
050   arise at any point during the video stream, rather than just at the end; (2) synchronized visual and
051   audio inputs must be considered as in real-world streaming scenarios; (3) the influence of context
052   must be taken into account, such as redundant information in long video streams and the history of
053   streaming interactions. These differences in design principles between offline and streaming tasks
      make it quite challenging to adapt offline benchmarks for streaming evaluation.

074   In offline video benchmarks, questions are designed based on the entire video being visible. In

077
078
079   To the best of our knowledge, the only current benchmark related to streaming video understanding
080   is VStream-QA (Zhang et al., 2024a). The main attribute of VStream-QA is that each question-
081
      answer pair is assigned a timestamp indicating its position in the video and is only related to the
      content preceding that point. However, VStream-QA includes only 32 videos from Ego4d (Grau-
082
      man et al., 2022) and MovieNet (Huang et al., 2020), with a limited variety of video types and a
083
      narrow range of scenarios. In addition, it only covers five types of tasks, focuses solely on the visual
084   modality, and the questions for each video are independent of each other. These limitations pre-
085   vent VStream-QA from fully assessing streaming video understanding abilities for MLLMs when
086   confronted with complex, multimodal streaming inputs in real-world scenarios.
087
      To address the limitations of existing video benchmarks, we introduce StreamingBench, the first
088
      comprehensive benchmark for assessing the streaming video understanding capabilities of MLLMs.
089
      StreamingBench consists of 900 videos and 4,300 questions, spanning eight diverse video categories
090   that reflect a wide range of real-world scenarios. Each video features five questions that are manually
091   curated to ensure a high level of relevance to the streaming video scenarios. These questions are
092   categorized into 18 tasks, and based on the characteristics of streaming video tasks, they can be
093   grouped into three main categories as illustrated in Figure 1:
094
095          • Real-Time Visual Understanding, which focuses on the ability of MLLMs to comprehend
096            visual content in real-time, recognizing and interpreting objects, actions, and changes as
097
               they happen within the video stream. For example, in Figure 1, the answer to the question
               “What words are currently shown?” may vary depending on the specific moment in time
098
               the question is asked, highlighting the dynamic nature of streaming video tasks.
099
             • Omni-Source Understanding, which refers to the ability integrate visual and audio infor-
100
               mation in real-time video streams. MLLMs must handle both sources simultaneously to
101            provide a comprehensive understanding of the scene and answer questions that depend on
102            their synchronization, such as “What is happening in the video when [sound] is made?”.
103          • Contextual Understanding, which evaluates the capability of MLLMs to comprehend the
104            broader context within a video stream, including detecting anomalies, filtering misleading
105            information, maintaining continuity across sequential interactions, and responding proac-
106            tively based on predefined conditions. For instance, as shown in the last query of Figure 1,
107            a follow-up question is asked based on the content of the previous query interaction, with
               a reference to“the event referred to in the previous query”.

124   We conduct experiments on StreamingBench with state-of-the-art MLLMs, including three propri-
125   etary models GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024), Gemini 1.5 Pro (Reid et al., 2024) and Claude 3.5 Son-
126   net (Anthropic, 2024), and 10 advanced open-source MLLMs like LLaVA-OneVision (Li et al.,
127   2024a), Qwen2-VL (Wang et al., 2024a) and MiniCPM-V 2.6 (Yao et al., 2024). Since these models
128   currently cannot accept streaming video input1 , we convert each streaming task into an offline one
129   for evaluation. For each question, the model processes the video segment from the start to the point
130
      when the question is asked, treating it as the complete input, and provides a response based on that
      segment. The results show that even the best-performing model, Gemini 1.5 Pro, achieves only an
131
      average accuracy of 67.36%, which is 24.30% lower than human performance. This indicates that
132
      there is a significant gap between MLLMs and human performance in understanding video streams.
133
134   To further investigate this gap, we conduct a series of analytical experiments, revealing that current
135   models perform poorly in terms of real-time processing. This may be attributed to the fact that
136
      most existing MLLMs are primarily trained on offline videos. Additionally, we find that these
      models generally lack the ability to understand and interact with streaming contexts. Specifically,
137
      redundant information in the context of streaming videos significantly affects model performance,
138
      and current models struggle with proactive output in streaming scenarios and fail to effectively
139   respond to continuous queries. We hope these findings will provide valuable insights for improving
140   future MLLMs and contribute to the development of the next generation of multimodal systems.

144
145   Video MLLMs. Recently, the development of advanced MLLMs has shifted from single image
146   understanding to video comprehension (Reid et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a; Yao et al., 2024; Lin
147
      et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2024a). These video MLLMs typically work by converting
      entire videos into visual tokens that can be processed by LLMs, through sampling and encoding
148
      video frames. However, these models are limited to offline video understanding rather than real-
149
      time, real-world streaming video comprehension. In contrast, GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024) explores the
150   potential for human-like perception and understanding of streaming inputs. There are also several
151   streaming video MLLMs in the academic field, including VideoLLM-online (Chen et al., 2024a),
152   Flash-VStream (Zhang et al., 2024a), and VideoLLM-MoD (Wu et al., 2024). With the growing
153   interest in research on streaming video MLLMs, there is an increasing urgency to comprehensively
154   evaluate their streaming video understanding capabilities.
155
      Video Understanding Benchmarks. The development of video understanding benchmarks has
156   progressed in tandem with advancements in MLLMs. Most current benchmarks are primarily fo-
157   cused on evaluating capabilities of either comprehensive video understanding (Li et al., 2024b; Fu
158   et al., 2024) or long-form video understanding (Wang et al., 2024b; Zhou et al., 2024). To our knowl-
159   edge, there is currently only one benchmark, VStream-QA (Zhang et al., 2024a), that is related to
160   streaming video understanding, where each question is assigned a timestamp to simulate a real-time

170   3.1     TAXONOMY
171
172   We identify three key distinctions between a streaming video understanding benchmark and tradi-
173   tional offline video benchmarks: (1) the inclusion of real-time queries that can appear at any point
174   during the video stream, rather than solely at the end; (2) the consideration of synchronized vi-
175
      sual and audio content, mirroring real-world video streams; and (3) the reflection of the complex
      and dynamic context of video streams, encompassing the evaluation of streaming interactions be-
176
      yond conventional isolated question answering. Based on these distinctions, we design three task
177
      categories: Real-Time Visual Understanding, Omni-Source Understanding and Contextual Un-
178   derstanding. Each category mainly addresses one of these distinctions and evaluates specific core
179   capabilities essential for streaming video comprehension.
180
181
      3.1.1    R EAL -T IME V ISUAL U NDERSTANDING
182
183   The tasks in this category aim to assess the ability of a model to perceive, comprehend, and reason
184   based on the visual content of video streams. Each question is accompanied by a timestamp that
185   indicates the time of the query and ensures that it only pertains to the visual content preceding that
186   specific moment. To emphasize the real-time nature of the questions, they include specific time
187   indicators such as “right now”, “just now”, or “currently”. As a result, the same question asked at
188   different times may yield different answers.
189   There are 10 tasks that belong to this category: (1) Object Perception (OP): Detect and identify
190   specific objects within the video. (2) Causal Reasoning (CR): Analyze cause-and-effect relation-
191   ships in events. (3) Clips Summarization (CS): Summarize main content in specific video clips.
192   (4) Attribute Perception (ATP): Identify and categorize object or individual attributes. (5) Event
193   Understanding (EU): Recognize and describe sequences of events. (6) Text-Rich Understanding
194   (TR): Interpret and explain text-rich content within the video. (7) Prospective Reasoning (PR):
195   Predict future events based on current video context. (8) Spatial Understanding (SU): Understand
196
      and describe spatial relationships and locations. (9) Action Perception (ACP): Identify and de-
      scribe specific actions in the video. (10) Counting (CT): Count occurrences of specific objects or
197
      actions. These tasks cover the main visual understanding tasks and effectively evaluate the ability
198
      of MLLMs to understand visual information in real-time in streaming scenarios. For deterministic
199   evaluations, all test examples are presented as multiple-choice questions with four distinct options
200   each. For examples of each task, please refer to Appendix D.
201
202
      3.1.2    O MNI -S OURCE U NDERSTANDING
203
204   These tasks evaluate the capability of a model to process visual and audio content in a video stream
205   simultaneously, especially focusing on the ability to integrate information from video and audio
206   content, or align them temporally. There are four tasks in this category:
207
      Emotion Recognition (ER): What is the mood of the person? The task involves identifying the
208
      current emotion of a particular person in the video and determining the cause of their emotional
209   change, based on the visual and auditory cues in the video stream.
210
211   Scene Understanding (SCU): Describe the scene that just occured. This task requires MLLMs to
212
      comprehend and describe dynamic scenes as they occur in a video stream, with a specific emphasis
      on accurately identifying both the visual elements and the audio that occurs simultaneously.
213
214   Source Discrimination (SD): Who just said “[quote]”? This task requires MLLMs to accurately
215   identify the speaker of specific lines of dialogue ([quote]) within a video stream, based on the visual
      and auditory cues presented just before or during the time the dialogue was spoken.


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      Multimodal Alignment (MA): Describe the scene when a person said “[quote]”. This task re-
217   quires MLLMs to accurately correlate spoken words ([quote]) with corresponding visual scenes in
218   a video. Based on the time intervals and context provided, MLLMs must describe the scene that
219   occurs when a specific line is spoken, ensuring that the visual and auditory elements are correctly
220   aligned.
221
      As with the questions in the previous task type, each question in omni-source understanding is set
222
      with a specific timestamp, and is a multiple-choice question with four options for the purpose of
223   deterministic evaluation. In addition, we make sure that all questions can not be answered without
224   understanding both visual and audio content. Please refer to Appendix D for data examples.
225
226
      3.1.3    C ONTEXTUAL U NDERSTANDING
227
228   These tasks focus on assessing the ability of MLLMs to provide accurate responses based on com-
229   plex context within a continuous video stream. Such context includes not only the redundant in-
230   formation presented throughout the video, but also the the streaming interactions such as prior
231   question-answer pairs or conditions for late proactive outputs. Overall, there are four contextual
232   understanding tasks. The first two involve filtering information from the redundant context:
233   Misleading Context Understanding (MCU): What are the cards on the table right now? In video
234   streams, misleading context can lead models to make false predictions. For instance, when playing
235   cards, different cards may have appeared on the table in previous video frames. To answer this
236   example question, the model must distinguishing the current state of the cards from that appeared
237   in earlier frames but are no longer present. This task challenges the model to maintain precision in
238   scenarios where similar but incorrect visual cues are prevalent, ensuring reliable understanding in
239   complex visual environments.
240   Anomaly Context Understanding (ACU): What unusual event just occurred? This task evaluate
241   the MLLMs’ ability to detect and accurately identify unusual or unexpected events within a video
242   stream. The model must differentiate between subtle variations in similar scenarios and correctly
243   identify the anomaly, ensuring precise understanding in dynamic and unpredictable environments.
244
      The form of these two tasks is the same as previous questions, i.e., multi-choice questions with
245   assigned timestamps. There are also two tasks related to streaming interactions:
246
247   Sequential Question Answering (SQA): What is the current outfit of the person mentioned in
248
      the first question? This task is characterized by a sequence of questions where each subsequent
      question is directly related to the entity or event mentioned in previous ones. The model must
249
      effectively utilize episodic memory to accurately link related information, ensuring coherent and
250
      contextually relevant responses throughout the task sequence.
251
252   Proactive Output (PO): When a goal is scored, output “GOAL”. Unlike typical input-output tasks
253   where the model responds directly to the input, this task requires the model to proactively determine
254
      when to generate output based on predefined conditions. This involves maintaining an internal state
      to track relevant information from incoming video frames, which is crucial for responsive AI systems
255
      in real-time streaming environments.
256
257   The question format of SQA is similar to other formats but includes an additional history of QA
258   sequences. In contrast, each question in the PO task includes an additional timestamp, indicating
259   the exact time when the output should occur. Data examples are in Appendix D.

265   competition, education, TV show, video games, documentary, animation & movie and unusual event.
266

      ensure that they possess attributes suited for different streaming video understanding tasks.
267

269   gories in StreamingBench. For real-time visual understanding tasks and the proactive output task,
      we first sample frames from the video at 1 fps and use GPT-4o to generate captions for every 20

304   process ensures that StreamingBench effectively challenges models to demonstrate their real-time

310   omni-source understanding category comprises 200 videos with 1,000 questions, and the contextual

360   define an interval spanning several seconds before and after the ground truth timestamp (the moment
361
      when the model is expected to output). During this interval, the model is queried every second with
      the question “Is it the right time to output?” This continues until the model responds with “Yes.”
362
      At that point, the model is prompted to provide the relevant keywords, and this moment is recorded
363
      as the actual output timestamp. A question in the PO task is considered accurately resolved only
364   if the difference between the actual output timestamp and the ground truth timestamp is less than
365   two seconds. The average accuracy across all queries is then computed and used as the performance
366   metric for the PO task. Please refer to Appendix A.2 for more evaluation protocals.

475
476   Do Redundant Information Affect Contextual Understanding? We observe that all models per-
477
      form unsatisfactorily in two contextual understanding tasks involving redundant information: mis-
      leading and anomaly context understanding tasks (MCU and ACU). To quantitatively assess the im-
478
      pact of highly redundant visual information on model performance, we sample 125 questions from
479
      these tasks and manually eliminate redundant information in them. For MCU, we extract frames that
480   contain clues for answering the question and discard other misleading frames. For ACU, we keep
481   only the video segments where the anomaly events occur as inputs. We conduct experiments using
482   four top-performing open-source MLLMs on StreamingBench. The results, as shown in Table 5,
483   indicate that MLLMs consistently achieve better performance when redundant visual information is
484   removed from the inputs. This finding underscores the insufficient robustness of current MLLMs
485   in handling redundant information. Future models should aim to improve their ability to accurately
      extract relevant information from inputs with high visual redundancy.

      Why Cannot MLLMs Handle the Proactive Output Task? We assume that the proactive output
504

506   on these two aspects, we further analyze why MLLMs struggle to handle the PO task effectively.
507   First, we relax the evaluation threshold for the time difference between the actual output time and the

509   suggests that MLLMs have a certain ability to respond to information, but lack precision in timing.
510   Next, we transform the PO task into a more traditional “passive” output task, where we directly
511   query the model for critical information at the ground truth timestamp and assess the correctness of

514
      shown in the right part of Figure 5, the model performance improves significantly. This indicates
      that the model struggles to adapt to the proactive output format, and further targeted improvements

      In this work, we introduce StreamingBench, the first comprehensive benchmark designed to as-
534   sess the streaming video understanding capabilities of MLLMs. StreamingBench consists of 900
535   videos and 4,300 QA pairs, covering 18 tasks across three main categories that evaluate key as-

      improved performance in real-world scenarios.

      We divide the streaming understanding scenarios into eight distinct categories to ensure a compre-
747
      hensive simulation of real-world, real-time streaming applications. The Life Record category in-
748   cludes videos that capture everyday activities such as travel vlogs, house tours, and reaction videos.
749   The Competition category features sports, including football, basketball. Video games category in-
750   cludes eSports and gaming videos. Education encompasses videos like lectures, tutorials, and other
751   instructional content. TV Show covers a range of media, including TV series, talk shows, and news
752   segments. Unusual Event focuses on unexpected scenarios such as car accidents, prank videos,
753   and magic shows. The Documentary category features content that includes science documentaries,
754   cultural explorations. Animation & Movie category includes comedies, kid’s shows and animated
755   films. This categorization ensures that the benchmark thoroughly simulates the diverse scenarios
      encountered in real-time streaming environments.

783   B.2   QA G ENERATION
784
785   To create questions that truly capture the streaming nature of video understandings, we selected
786   five distinct timestamps for each video to serve as query points. For tasks under the Real-Time
787   Visual Understanding category and the Proactive Output task, we adapted the traditional two-step
788   approach of generating questions based on captions. The pipeline for QA Generation is illustrated
789
      in Figure 6. Specifically, we employed GPT-4o to sample frames from the video at a rate of 1 frame
      per second (fps). We observed that for Single-Frame tasks, directly generating questions based on
790
      the sampled images, without an intermediate captioning phase, resulted in higher quality questions.
791
      Conversely, for Multi-Frame tasks, generating captions first and then formulating questions from
792   those captions yielded better results. Unlike other video benchmarks where queries are typically
793   presented at the end of the video, StreamingBench introduces queries at various points throughout
794   the video. To automatically generate appropriate query timestamps, we tagged each sampled frame
795   with its corresponding timestamp in the video. We found that this method helped us produce high-
796   quality questions with realistic query timings. Additionally, we tagged each question with the time
797   range during which the relevant clues appeared in the video, specifying the minimal video segment
798   necessary to answer the question accurately. This tagging approach also proved effective, ensur-
799   ing the generation of high-quality, contextually relevant questions. For tasks in the Omni-Source
800
      Understanding category and Contextual Understanding tasks (excluding Proactive Output), where
      questions require audio information, we employed meticulous manual annotation. Each video was
801
      carefully annotated to ensure the precision and relevance of the generated questions.
