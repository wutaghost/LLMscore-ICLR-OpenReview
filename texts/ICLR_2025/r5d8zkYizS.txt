011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Adversarial examples —imperceptibly modified data inputs designed to mislead
014            machine learning models— have raised concerns about the robustness of modern
015            neural architectures in safety-critical applications. In this paper, we propose a
016
               unified mathematical framework for understanding adversarial examples in neural
               networks, corroborating Szegedy et al. (2014)’s original conjecture that such ex-
017
               amples are exceedingly rare, despite their presence in the proximity of nearly ev-
018
               ery test case. By exploiting Mercer’s decomposition theorem, we characterise ad-
019            versarial examples as those producing near-zero Mercer’s eigenvalues in the em-
020            pirical kernel associated to a trained neural network. Consequently, the generation
021            of adversarial attacks, using any known technique, can be conceptualised as a pro-
022            gression towards the eigenvalue space’s zero point within the empirical kernel. We
023            rigorously prove this characterisation for trained neural networks that achieve in-
024            terpolation and under mild assumptions on the architecture, thus providing a math-
025            ematical explanation for the apparent contradiction of neural networks excelling
026            at generalisation while remaining vulnerable to adversarial attacks. We have em-
027
               pirically verified that adversarial examples generated for both fully-connected and
               convolutional architectures through the widely-known DeepFool algorithm and
028
               through the more recent Fast Adaptive Boundary (FAB) method consistently lead
029
               to a shift in the distribution of Mercer’s eigenvalues toward zero. These results are
030            in strong agreement with predictions of our theory.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Adversarial examples are specially crafted input data points designed to cause a model to output
036   an incorrect prediction. These examples are created by making small, imperceptible perturbations
037   to the input data (e.g., images, text or audio) which are typically indistinguishable to humans but
038   can have a significant impact on the model’s output (Szegedy et al., 2014; Goodfellow et al., 2015).
039   The existence of adversarial examples has raised questions about the robustness and reliability of
040   deep learning models when used in safety-critical applications (Ruan et al., 2021) since empirical
041   evidence shows that neural networks are particularly sensitive (Moosavi-Dezfooli et al., 2017).
042   Adversarial examples can occur in many domains, and reveal serious vulnerabilities. For example,
043   a self-driving car’s neural object detection system might misclassify a stop sign as a yield sign if an
044   adversarial sticker is placed on it (Akhtar et al., 2021). In NLP, attackers can make subtle changes to
045   text to deceive sentiment analysis or spam detection models (Zhang et al., 2020b) and, in cyberse-
046   curity, attackers can modify malware to evade detection by antivirus or intrusion detection systems
047   (Rosenberg et al., 2021). As a result, researchers and practitioners have developed techniques to de-
048   fend against adversarial attacks, such as adversarial training, input preprocessing, and robust model
049   architectures (Zhang et al., 2020a; Fowl et al., 2021; Carlini et al., 2019; Han et al., 2023).
050   The foundational paper introducing adversarial examples (Szegedy et al., 2014) characterised them
051   as “intriguing properties” of neural networks and raised a compelling question: how can neural
052   networks exhibit strong generalisation performance on test examples drawn from a data distribution,
053   while simultaneously being susceptible to adversarial examples? An interesting hypothesis, which
      we recapitulate next, was already outlined in the paper’s concluding remarks.

      “A possible explanation is that the set of adversarial negatives is of extremely low probability, and
055   thus is never (or rarely) observed in the test set, yet it is dense (much like the rational numbers), and
056   so it is found near virtually every test case.”

058
      Despite the substantial attention dedicated by researchers to explaining the prevalence of adversarial
      examples, as discussed in Section 6, and despite progress in identifying new attack variants and
059
      developing defensive approaches, robustness measures (Yu et al., 2019; Carlini et al., 2019; Wang
060
      et al., 2023), and theoretical guarantees based on such measures (Bhagoji et al., 2019; Shafahi et al.,
061   2019), the fundamental nature of adversarial examples continues to elude complete understanding
062   (Guo et al., 2018; Ilyas et al., 2019; Madry et al., 2018; Qin et al., 2019).

067   Our Contribution In this paper, we provide novel theoretical results that further substantiate the
068   original hypothesis by Szegedy et al. (2014). Our research relies on the formulation of neural net-
069   works as specific instances of parameter-dependent kernel machines, which has enabled researchers
070
      to leverage well-established results in the field of Kernel Theory (Györfi et al., 2002; Saitoh &
      Sawano, 2016) to investigate the generalisation properties of neural models (Berthier et al., 2020;
071
      Canatar et al., 2021; Simon et al., 2023). Specifically, a trained neural network can be equivalently
072
      viewed as realising a non-linear transformation of the input data, which is associated to an empirical
073   kernel, followed by a linear transformation characterised by the readout weights.
074
075   Mercer’s decomposition theorem (Mercer, 1909; Minh et al., 2006) is a fundamental result in Kernel
076
      Theory which plays a crucial role in various ML algorithms. Mercer’s theorem states that, for
      a kernel function applicable to pairs of data points sampled from a probability distribution with
077
      compact support, the kernel’s evaluation can be represented as an infinite sum of products. These
078
      products consist of the application of Mercer’s eigenfunctions mapping the input data points to real
079   values, weighted by Mercer’s eigenvalues—positive scalars determining the relative contribution
080   of each term in the decomposition. Such eigenvalues and eigenfunctions are specific to the kernel
081   function and input data distribution, but remain independent from the specific data points being
082   evaluated.
083
      We adopt the perspective that the introduction of an adversarial example can be regarded as a mod-
084
      ification of the empirical data distribution derived from the training set, expanded to incorporate
085   the adversarial example. By Mercer’s theorem, even when maintaining the same empirical kernel
086   associated to a trained model, this process results in a new set of Mercer eigenvalues and eigenfunc-
087   tions, since the data distribution used to compute the Mercer’s decomposition is changed. Our first
088   technical contribution is to characterise adversarial examples as those producing near-zero Mercer
089   eigenvalues in the updated decomposition of the neural network’s empirical kernel. This character-
090   isation then allows us to show that adversarial examples have measure zero in the limit where they
091   become infinitesimally close to any test example sampled from the original data distribution. Our
092   results provide a rigorous mathematical explanation for what appears to be a paradoxical empirical
093
      observation: while neural networks demonstrate strong generalisation to novel test examples, they
      are also susceptible to adversarial attacks. Indeed, it follows from our results that adversarial ex-
094
      amples are exceedingly unlikely to occur in the test set, thereby providing compelling support for
095
      the hypothesis by Szegedy et al. (2014). Our theory could also open the way to new approaches in
096   designing defense mechanisms and detection methods for adversarial attacks (Tramer, 2022).
097
098   We conducted experiments on a fully-connected neural network trained on a subset of MNIST, and
099
      on a pretrained convolutional neural network on a subset of CIFAR10, both achieving interpolation
      on the training set and perfect generalisation performance on a subset of the test sets. We then
100
      used the DeepFool algorithm (Moosavi-Dezfooli et al., 2016) (respectively, the FAB attack (Croce
101
      & Hein, 2020)) on the FCN (respectively, the CNN) to generate adversarial examples, and used the
102   numerical method by Baker (1977); Rasmussen & Williams (2006) to compute Mercer’s decompo-
103   sition on the empirical kernel associated with both the original empirical data distribution and our
104   updated empirical data distributions, which incorporate the adversarial examples. We have anal-
105   ysed the relevant distributions of eigenvalues, computed those with minimal value and estimated the
106   integral of relevant quantities near zero. Our numerical experiments align with our theory, demon-
107   strating that adversarial examples induce a shift in the distribution of Mercer eigenvalues towards
      zero, and corroborate that our theory is indeed architecture- and data-agnostic.

      The prediction discrepancy factors Madv , Mnat are introduced to adjust the standard definition of
163   adversarial example for classification to the regression setting, where there is no a-priori notion
164   of what it means for the adversarial example to change the prediction (in classification, changing
165   the prediction means predicting a different class). The prediction discrepancy factors Madv , Mnat
166   allows practitioners to quantify when a modification of the output is significant for the regression
167   task at hand, in particular they provide criteria for when an error is adversarial vs when an error
168   is natural. To simplify the presentation, we fix arbitrary such Madv , Mnat and speak from now
169   onwards of ϵ-adversarial examples. The first condition in the definition requires that the adversarial
170   example x′ is close in norm to some test example for which N generalises to small error; the second
171
      requirement ensures that the corresponding error w.r.t. x′ is large.

306   In this section, we exploit the result in Theorem 3.1 to show that adversarial examples have zero
307   measure with respect to the true data distribution p. As in the previous section, we assume that the
308   neural network is trained to zero error and that its generalisation error remains small for all examples

      this highly restricts the probability of sampling near-zero eigenvalues, and consequently also the
      probability of sampling adversarial examples.

356   To validate our theory, we conducted experiments on a subset of MNIST consisting exclusively of
357
      classes “0” and “1”, and a subset of CIFAR10 consisting exclusively of classes “plane” and “car”.

358

359

360   We deliberately opted for these reduced dataset sizes to accommodate computational constraints,
361   as our computations require diagonalising kernel matrices with dimensions of (P + 1) × (P + 1).
362   Furthermore, we used only two classes per experiment to conveniently turn classification tasks into
363   one-dimensional regression tasks. However, it is important to note that the size of the dataset is
364   inconsequential to our theoretical results, as they remain independent of dataset scale. All experi-
365   ments were conducted on a GPU-enabled platform within Google Colab for enhanced computational
366   efficiency.
367   Using Pytorch, we trained a ReLU FCN with one hidden layer of size N = 512 to zero error on our

415
416   Several explanations have been proposed for the pervasiveness of adversarial attacks in neural net-
417   works. The linearity hypothesis (Goodfellow et al., 2015) posits that, because of the (local) linear
418   nature of trained neural networks, small changes to each component of a high-dimensional vector
419   amount to a large change in the network’s output. The universality of ℓ2 -adversarial attacks on
420   ReLU networks with random weights (Daniely & Shacham, 2020) provides a strong argument sup-
421   porting this hypothesis, especially considering the locally linear characteristics of ReLU networks.
422   The linearity hypothesis has motivated a series of research endeavors analysing the topological char-
423
      acteristics of decision boundaries, with the aim to elucidate the nature of adversarial examples and
      develop techniques for enhancing adversarial robustness. Tanay & Griffin (2016) argues that the lin-
424
      earity hypothesis is not fully satisfactory, and rather attributes adversarial examples to the distance
425
      of the sampling subspace to the decision boundary. In other related works, such as Simon-Gabriel
426   et al. (2019), the susceptibility to adversarial attacks escalates with the increase in input dimension-
427   ality. Deficiencies in the topology become more pronounced in higher dimensions, a phenomenon
428   exacerbated by the curse of dimensionality. In some instances, particularly for synthetic data distri-
429   butions with sufficiently high dimensions, adversarial attacks become nearly unavoidable, as noted
430   in Shafahi et al. (2019). Moreover, data sparsity in relation to the input space heightens vulner-
431   ability to adversarial attacks, as discussed in Paknezhad et al. (2022); Weitzner & Giryes (2023).
      The dimensionality of the parameter space also plays a significant role in this context, with param-

      Another line of research identifies features as the key object driving the occurrence of adversarial
466
      examples. In Ilyas et al. (2019), it was shown that non-robust features (those resulting from spu-
467
      rious data correlations that are nevertheless highly predictive) are responsible for the presence of
468   adversarial examples. Similarly, Wang et al. (2017) established that the existence of an unnecessary
469   feature introduced to replicate the true underlying target function renders a system vulnerable to ad-
470   versarial attacks. This perspective is further supported by the observation that saliency methods tend
471   to emphasise class-discriminative features which can be exploited by attackers Gu & Tresp (2019).
472
      Our approach is positioned at the intersection of both these streams of research. By taking Mercer’s
473
      eigenvalues into account, our theory operates in the high-dimensional embedding space where the
474   predictions follow a linear pattern. Additionally, shifting towards zero eigenvalues can be under-
475   stood as exploiting directions of non-robust features. Indeed, our approach was first inspired by the
476   double-descent phenomenon in neural networks (Mei & Montanari, 2022), in which the spectrum
477   of the empirical kernel also shifts towards zero at the divergence in terms of generalisation error.
478   Within this body of literature, it is already established that stochastic cancellations give rise to the
479   emergence of spurious directions within the feature space (El Harzli et al., 2024), causing them to
480   overfit.
481   A final perspective, which bears loose connections with our approach, is based on information ge-
482   ometry (Zhao et al., 2019; Naddeo et al., 2022). This perspective centers on the use of the Fisher
483   Information Matrix which quantifies, for any pair of data points, the correlation between parameter
484   gradients of the log likelihood of the data. A technique involving the iterative elimination of the
485   dominant eigenvalue direction in the Fisher Information Matrix leads to the generation of adversar-
      ial examples. This process appears to yield configurations where variations in network parameters

      exhibit strong linear dependencies with respect to the data. These configurations may manifest
487   whenever certain features become perfectly correlated within the dataset and this, in turn, results in
488   the empirical kernel having Mercer eigenvalues that approach zero.

      7   L IMITATIONS A ND F UTURE W ORK
492
      Our results are currently directly applicable to one-dimensional regression tasks, but we anticipate
493
      that our core insights could be generalised to encompass classification and multidimensional regres-
494
      sion. Additionally, conducting comprehensive experiments on more extensive and diverse datasets
495   would be beneficial. Importantly, our findings remain unaffected by variations in problem dimen-
496   sions and data characteristics, offering a high degree of versatility.
497
498
      Our results are derived in the limit where adversarial examples become infinitesimally close to data
      points. Specifically, we have not characterised the rate at which the probability of encountering ad-
499
      versarial examples diminishes with the distance to an example from the true data distribution. This
500
      remains a subject for future research and requires the derivation of precise bounds for the generali-
501   sation error. Exploring specific criteria governing the data distribution and neural architecture that
502   lead to rapid convergence rates (as ϵ → 0) and result in low probabilities of generating adversarial
503   examples is a promising avenue. Achieving this understanding could lay the groundwork for design-
504   ing more robust architectures. Additionally, while we have proved that adversarial examples have
505   a measure zero with respect to the data distribution in the limit, we have not shown that examples
506   producing near-zero Mercer eigenvalues for the empirical kernel of a neural network always exist.
507   In conclusion, we anticipate that our findings will serve as a catalyst for research into the character-
508   istics and prevalence of adversarial examples. Furthermore, we hope to encourage the exploration
509   and development of novel defense mechanisms and detection methods against adversarial attacks.
