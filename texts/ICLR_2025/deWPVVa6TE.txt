009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011                Data and model heterogeneity are two core issues in Heterogeneous Federated
012                Learning (HtFL). In scenarios with heterogeneous model architectures, aggregat-
013                ing model parameters becomes infeasible, leading to the use of prototypes (i.e.,
014                class representative feature vectors) for aggregation and guidance. However, when
015                aligned with global prototypes, they still experience a mismatch between the ex-
016                tra guiding objective and the client’s original local objective. Thus, we propose a
017                Federated Learning-to-Guide (FedL2G1 ) method that adaptively learns to guide
018
                   local training in a federated manner and ensures the extra guidance is beneficial
                   to clients’ original tasks. With theoretical guarantees, FedL2G efficiently imple-
019
                   ments the learning-to-guide process using only first-order derivatives w.r.t. model
020
                   parameters and achieves a non-convex convergence rate of O(1/T ). We con-
021                duct extensive experiments on two data heterogeneity and six model heterogene-
022                ity settings using 14 heterogeneous model architectures (e.g., CNNs and ViTs) to
023                demonstrate FedL2G’s superior performance compared to six counterparts.

026   1       I NTRODUCTION
027
028   With the rapid development of AI techniques (Touvron et al., 2023; Achiam et al., 2023), public
029   data has been consumed gradually, raising the need to access local data inside devices or institu-
030   tions (Ye et al., 2024). However, directly using local data often raises privacy concerns (Nguyen
031   et al., 2021). Federated Learning (FL) is a promising privacy-preserving approach that enables col-
032   laborative model training across multiple clients (devices or institutions) in a distributed manner
033   without the need to move the actual data outside clients (Kairouz et al., 2019; Li et al., 2020). Nev-
034
      ertheless, data heterogeneity (Li et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023d;a) and model heterogeneity (Zhang
      et al., 2024b; Yi et al., 2023) remain two practical issues when deploying FL systems. Personalized
035
      FL (PFL) mainly focuses on the data heterogeneity issue (Zhang et al., 2023e), while Heteroge-
036
      neous FL (HtFL) considers both data and model heterogeneity simultaneously (Zhang et al., 2024a).
037   HtFL’s support for model heterogeneity enables a broader range of clients to participate in FL with
038   their customized models.
039
040
      In HtFL, sharing model parameters, a widely used technique in traditional FL and PFL is not ap-
      plicable (Zhang et al., 2024b). Instead, lightweight knowledge carriers, including small auxiliary
041
      models (Shen et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2022; Yi et al., 2024), tiny homogeneous modules (Liang et al.,
042
      2020; Yi et al., 2023), and prototypes (i.e., class representative feature vectors) (Jeong et al., 2018;
043   Tan et al., 2022b), can be shared among clients. Prototypes offer the most significant communication
044   efficiency due to their compact size.
045
046
      However, representative prototype-based methods FedDistill (Jeong et al., 2018) and FedProto (Tan
      et al., 2022b), still suffer from a mismatch between the prototype-guiding objective and the client’s
047
      original local objective. These methods typically introduce an extra guiding objective alongside the
048
      original local objective, aiming to guide local features to align with the global ensemble prototypes.
049   Due to the significant variation in width and depth among clients’ heterogeneous models, their fea-
050   ture extraction capabilities also differ considerably (Zhang et al., 2024a;b). On the other hand, the
051   data distribution also diverges across clients (McMahan et al., 2017; Li et al., 2022). Since the global
052   prototypes are derived from aggregating diverse local prototypes, they inherently cannot fully align

097   2.1   H ETEROGENEOUS F EDERATED L EARNING (H T FL)
098
099
      Presently, FL is one of the popular collaborative learning and privacy-preserving techniques (Zhang
      et al., 2023d; Li et al., 2020) and HtFL extends traditional FL by supporting model heterogene-
100
      ity (Ye et al., 2023). Prevailing HtFL methods primarily consider three types of model hetero-
101
      geneity: (1) group heterogeneity, (2) partial heterogeneity, and (3) full heterogeneity (Zhang et al.,
102   2024b). Among them, the HtFL methods considering group model heterogeneity extract different
103   but architecture-constraint sub-models from a global model for various groups of clients (Diao et al.,
104   2020; Horvath et al., 2021; Wen et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023). Thus, they cannot
105   support customized client models and are excluded from our consideration. Additionally, sharing
106   and revealing model architectures within each group of clients also raises privacy and intellectual
107   property concerns (Zhang et al., 2024a). As the server is mainly utilized for parameter aggregation
      in prior FL systems (Tan et al., 2022a; Kairouz et al., 2019), training a server module with a large


                                                            2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      number of epochs, like (Zhang et al., 2024b;a; Zhu et al., 2021), necessitates additional upgrades or
109   the purchase of a new heavy server, which is impractical. Thus, we focus on the server-lightweight
110   methods.
111
112
      Both partial and full model heterogeneity accommodate customized client model architectures, but
      partial heterogeneity still assumes that some small parts of all client models are homogeneous. For
113
      example, LG-FedAvg (Liang et al., 2020) and FedGH (Yi et al., 2023) stand out as two representative
114
      approaches. LG-FedAvg and FedGH partition each client model into a feature extractor part and a
115   classifier head part, operating under the assumption that all classifier heads are homogeneous. In LG-
116   FedAvg, the parameters of classifier heads are uploaded to the server for aggregation. In contrast,
117   FedGH uploads prototypes to the server and trains the lightweight global classifier head for a small
118   number of epochs. Both methods utilize the global head for knowledge transfer among clients but
119   overlook the inconsistency between the global head and local tasks.
120
      In the case of full model heterogeneity, mutual distillation (Zhang et al., 2018) and prototype guid-
121   ance (Tan et al., 2022b) emerge as two prevalent techniques. Using mutual distillation, FML (Shen
122   et al., 2020) and FedKD (Wu et al., 2022) facilitate client knowledge transfer through a globally
123   shared auxiliary model. However, sharing an entire model demands substantial communication
124   resources, even if the auxiliary model is typically small (Zhang et al., 2024b). Furthermore, aggre-
125   gating a global model in scenarios with data heterogeneity presents numerous challenges, such as
126   client-drift (Karimireddy et al., 2020), ultimately leading to a subpar global model (Li et al., 2022;
127   Zhang et al., 2023a;b;c). As representative prototype guidance methods, FedDistill (Jeong et al.,
128   2018) and FedProto (Tan et al., 2022b) gather prototypes on each client, aggregate them on the
129
      server to create the global prototypes, and guide client local training with these global prototypes.
      Specifically, FedDistill extracts lower-dimensional prototypes than FedProto. This difference stems
130
      from FedDistill applying prototype guidance in the logit space, whereas FedProto uses the interme-
131
      diate feature space. Sharing higher-dimensional prototypes can transfer more information among
132   clients but may also exacerbate the negative effects of objective mismatch.
133
134
135   2.2    S TUDENT-C ENTERED G UIDANCE
136
137   Our learning-to-guide philosophy draws inspiration from student-centered knowledge distillation
138   approaches (Yang et al., 2024). They are based on the insight that a teacher’s subject matter ex-
139   pertise alone may not match the student’s specific studying ability and style, resulting in negative
140   effects (Sengupta et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024). To address the mismatch between the teacher’s
141   knowledge and the needs of the student, updating the teacher model with concise feedback from
142   the student on a small quiz set represents a promising direction (Ma et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022;
143
      Sengupta et al., 2023).
144   However, these student-centered approaches are built upon a teacher-student framework, assuming
145   the presence of a well-trained large teacher model. They concentrate on a central training scheme
146   without factoring in distributed multiple students and privacy protection (Lee et al., 2022; Hu et al.,
147   2022), rendering them inapplicable in the context of HtFL. Additionally, modifying and extending
148   these student-centered approaches to HtFL requires significant communication and computational
149
      resources to update a shared large teacher model based on student feedback (Zhou et al., 2022; Lu
      et al., 2023). Nevertheless, the student-centered guidance concept inspires us to propose a learning-
150
      to-guide approach in HtFL. This involves substituting the large teacher model with compact guiding
151
      vectors and updating these guiding vectors based on clients’ feedback from their quiz sets, making
152   our FedL2G lightweight, efficient, and adaptable.

185   3.2   L EARNING TO G UIDE
186
187   Motivation. Initially, heterogeneous client models trained by ℓce can adapt to their local data with
188
      diverse feature extraction capabilities. However, directly adding ℓg without prioritizing ℓce can cause
      the model of each client to deviate from ℓce . On the other hand, since all feature vectors are extracted
189
      on heterogeneous client data, the aggregated global prototype, e.g., g y , is data-derived, which may
190
      deviate from the features regarding class y on each client. Both the model and data heterogeneity
191   result in the objective mismatch issue between ℓce and ℓg , which causes the negative effect to ℓce
192   when using ℓg , as shown in Fig. 1 and discussed further in Sec. 4.5. Therefore, we propose a
193   novel FedL2G method, which substitutes the data-derived prototypes with trainable guiding vectors
194   G = {v y }Cy=1 and ensures that G is learned to reduce ℓce when guided by ℓg . Formally, we replace
195   Eq. (3) with a new loss to train the client model:
196                     LDi (θi , G) := E(x,y)∼Di [ℓce (fi (x, θi ), y) + ℓg (fi (x, θi ), v y )],                (4)
197
      where the learning of guiding vectors G is the key step.

199   Learning guiding vectors.         Without relying on data-derived information, we randomly initial-
200   ize the global G on the server and update it based on the aggregated gradients from participating
201   clients in each communication iteration. Inspired by the technique of outer-inner loops in meta-
202   learning (Zhou et al., 2022), we derive the gradients of client-specific viy in the outer-loop, while
203
      focusing on reducing the original local loss, i.e., ℓce , in the inner-loop on each client. To implement

204
      the remaining training set as the study set Dis . Notice that we exclusively conduct model updates on
205

326   computation cost. Subsequently, we investigate the characteristics behind our FedL2G from an
327   experimental perspective.
328
      Data heterogeneity settings.      Following existing work (Zhang et al., 2023d; Lin et al., 2020;
329
      Zhang et al., 2023b; 2024a), we adopt two popular settings across four enduring datasets Ci-
330
      far10 (Krizhevsky & Geoffrey, 2009), Cifar100 (Krizhevsky & Geoffrey, 2009), Flowers102 (Nils-
331   back & Zisserman, 2008), and Tiny-ImageNet (Chrabaszcz et al., 2017). Concretely, we simulate
332   pathological data heterogeneity settings by allocating sub-datasets with 2/10/10/20 data classes from
333   Cifar10/Cifar100/Flowers102/Tiny-ImageNet to each client. In Dirichlet data heterogeneity settings,
334   we allocate the data of class y to each client using a client-specific ratio q y from a given dataset.
335   q y is sampled from a Dirichlet distribution with a control parameter β as described in (Lin et al.,
336   2020). By default, we set β = 0.1 for Cifar10 and Cifar100, and β = 0.01 for Flowers102 and
337   Tiny-ImageNet to enhance setting diversity. In both the pathological and Dirichlet settings, the data
338   quantity among clients varies to account for unbalanced scenarios.
339   Model heterogeneity settings.        To neatly denote model heterogeneity settings, we utilize the
340   notation HtFEX following the convention in (Zhang et al., 2024b) to represent a group of heteroge-
341   neous feature extractors, where X denotes the degree of model heterogeneity (positive correlation),
342   while the remaining classifier heads remain homogeneous. For example, HtFE8 denotes a group
343   of eight heterogeneous feature extractors from eight model architectures (4-layer CNN (McMahan
344   et al., 2017), GoogleNet (Szegedy et al., 2015), MobileNet v2 (Sandler et al., 2018), ResNet18,
345
      ResNet34, ResNet50, ResNet101, and ResNet152 (He et al., 2016)), respectively. In addition, we
      use the notation HtMX to denote a group of fully heterogeneous models. Within a specific group,
346
      for instance, HtFEX , we allocate the (i mod X)th model in this group to client i with reinitialized
347
      parameters. Given the popularity of all models within HtFE8 in the FL field, our primary focus is on
348   utilizing HtFE8 . Additionally, some baseline methods, such as LG-FedAvg and FedGH, assume the
349   classifier heads to be homogeneous, making HtMX inapplicable for them. Moreover, to meet the
350   prerequisite of identical feature dimensions (K) for FedGH, FedKD, and FedProto, we incorporate
351   an average pooling layer (Szegedy et al., 2015) before the classifier heads and set K = 512 for all
352   models.
353
      Other necessary settings.          Following common practice (McMahan et al., 2017), we execute a
354   complete local training epoch with a batch size of 10, i.e., ⌊ n10i ⌋ update steps, during each communi-
355   cation iteration. We conduct each experiment for up to 1000 iterations across three trials, employing
356   a client learning rate (ηc ) of 0.01, and present the best results with error bars. Moreover, we examine
357   full participation (ρ = 1), for 20 clients, while setting partial participation (ρ = 0.5) for scenarios
358   involving 50 and 100 clients. Please refer to the Appendix A for more details and results.

      4.4   C OMMUNICATION AND C OMPUTATION OVERHEAD
457
458   Communication cost. We consider both the upload and download bytes (across all participat-
459   ing clients) as part of the communication overhead in each iteration, using a float32 (= 4 bytes)
460   data type in PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019) to store each floating number. In Tab. 3, despite FML
461   and FedKD transmitting a relatively small global model, their communication costs remain signifi-
462   cantly high compared to other methods that share lightweight components. The SVD technique in
463   FedKD (Wu et al., 2022), does not significantly reduce the communication overhead. Given that we
464
      only upload the gradients of guiding vectors on the client, the communication cost of FedL2G-l
      and FedL2G-f is equivalent to that of FedDistill and FedProto, respectively. This cost falls within
465
      the lowest group among these methods.
466
467   Computation cost. To capture essential operations, we measure the averaged GPU execution time
468   of each client and the server on an idle GPU card in each iteration and show the time cost in Tab. 3.
469   As FedGH gathers prototypes after local training, it costs extra time for inferencing across the entire
470
      training set using the trained client model. In contrast, FedDistill and FedProto collect prototypical
      logits and features, respectively, concurrently with model training in each batch, thereby eliminating
471
      this additional cost. Besides, FedGH trains the global head on the server consuming relatively more
472
      power, even with one server epoch per iteration. Since we only average gradients on the server
473   and update G once without backpropagation, our FedL2G-l and FedL2G-f demonstrate similar
474   time-efficiency to FedDistill and FedProto, respectively. Due to the extra learning-to-guide process,
475   FedL2G costs more client time than FedDistill and FedProto. However, FedL2G-l still requires
476   less time than FML, FedKD, and FedGH, and the improved test accuracy justifies this cost.

533   5     C ONCLUSION
534
535   We observe the original local loss growth phenomenon on the client in prior prototype-based HtFL
536   methods when guided by global prototypes. Then we attribute this problem to the mismatch between
537   the guiding objective and the client’s original local objective. To address this issue, we propose a
538   FedL2G approach to reduce the client’s original objective when using guiding vectors by prior-
539   itizing the local objective during the learning of guiding vectors. The superiority of FedL2G is
      evidenced through theoretical analysis and extensive experiments.

704
      Datasets and environment. We use four datasets with their respective download links: Cifar102 ,
705
      Cifar1003 , Flowers1024 , and Tiny-ImageNet5 . We split all client data into a training set and a test set
706   for each client at a ratio of 75% and 25%, respectively, and we evaluate the averaged test accuracy
707   on clients’ test sets. All our experiments are conducted on a machine with 64 Intel(R) Xeon(R)
708   Platinum 8362 CPUs, 256G memory, eight NVIDIA 3090 GPUs, and Ubuntu 20.04.4 LTS. Most
709   of our experiments can be completed within 48 hours, while others, involving many clients and
710   extensive local training epochs, may take up to a week to finish.
711
      Hyperparameter settings.       For our baseline methods, we set their hyperparameters following
712   existing work (Zhang et al., 2024a;b). As for our FedL2G-l and FedL2G-f, we tune the server
713   learning rate ηs (the unique hyperparameter) by grid search on the Cifar100 dataset in the default
714   Dirichlet setting with HtFE8 and use an identical setting on all experimental tasks without further
715   tuning. Specifically, we search ηs in the range: {0.01, 0.05, 0.1, 0.5, 1, 10, 50, 100, 500}. We set
716   ηs = 0.1 for FedL2G-l and set ηs = 100 for FedL2G-f. The ηs hyperparameters of FedL2G-l
717   and FedL2G-f differ due to their discrepancy in the learnable knowledge capacity of the guiding
718   vectors. The dimension of the guiding vectors in FedL2G-f is larger than in FedL2G-l, necessi-
719   tating more server updates.
720   The small auxiliary model for FML and FedKD. As FML and FedKD utilize a global auxiliary
721   model for mutual distillation, this auxiliary model needs to be as compact as possible to minimize
722   communication overhead during model parameter transmission (Wu et al., 2022). Therefore, we opt
723   for the smallest model within each group of heterogeneous models to serve as the auxiliary model
724   in all scenarios.
