                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
012            Diffusion models have emerged as powerful generative models for image synthe-
013
               sis, yet the intricate relationship between input noise and generated images re-
               mains not fully understood. In this paper, we investigate the correlation between
014
               noise and images generated through deterministic DDIM sampling, uncovering
015
               fundamental elements that are present across different diffusion models. More
016            specifically, we demonstrate that a one-step approximation of the mapping learned
017            by these models closely relates to Zero-phase Component Analysis (ZCA) inverse
018            whitening transform, which maximizes the correlation between source and tar-
019            get distributions. We leverage this insight to develop a simple and yet effective
020            model-agnostic method for sampling correlated noises and showcase applications
021            for image variation generation and editing.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026
027
      The landscape of generative artificial intelligence has witnessed an unprecedented evolution, notably
      marked by the stellar advance of diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020).
028
      Diffusion models represent the state of the art in terms of their generative capabilities, being used
029
      for a myriad of generative tasks, ranging from text-to-image (Rombach et al., 2022) and text-to-
030   video (Ho et al., 2022; Villegas et al., 2022) models to sound guided (Alexanderson et al., 2022)
031   and text-based (Tevet et al., 2022) motion synthesis. Diffusion models operate on a straightforward
032   but powerful principle: reversing a diffusion process that progressively corrupts images into noise.
033   Due to its maximum entropy characteristic, Gaussian noise is commonly employed to train diffusion
034   models, since it allows the network to approximate image distributions in an unbiased way.
035
      In this paper, we investigate the relationship between noise samples and images produced by diffu-
036   sion models. We are motivated by the observation that diffusion models sampled by deterministic
037   DDIM (Song et al., 2020) inherently preserve and translate correlations present in noise samples
038   to images (Khrulkov et al., 2022). Figure 2 illustrates this observation: by progressively averaging
039   distinct noise samples and comparing with averages of their corresponding images, we demonstrate
040   that visual structures on these averages are spatially similar.
041
      To further understand this relationship, we propose a fixed point diffusion strategy that approximates
042   the mapping between a noise sample and a generated image in a single-step. Surprisingly, this single-
043   step mapping is a remarkable approximation of a ”de-whitening” operation, which is computed by
044   the inverse of the ZCA whitening transform (Bell & Sejnowski, 1997). Furthermore, we show that a
045   ZCA whitening matrix that is independently computed for the ImageNet dataset (Deng et al., 2009)
046   is remarkably close to the least-squares fitting between noise-image pairs of our single-step diffusion
047   approximation.
048   With the understanding that de-whitening is a fundamental element of diffusion models, we are able
049   to create an efficient optimization algorithm that generates initial noise samples that produce similar
050   images across completely different diffusion models (Figures 1 and 9). To the best of our knowledge,
051   our work is the first one to solve this inverse problem, resulting in model-agnostic noise samples that
052   generate images with a specific content. Furthermore, this relationship can be explored to improve
053   results of popular image editing approaches such as SDEdit (Meng et al., 2022) (Figure 10). In
      summary, our work makes the following key contributions:

      Image whitening or sphering (Bell & Sejnowski, 1997; Kessy et al., 2018) refers to the linear oper-
      ation that transforms a random vector x ∈ RD of mean µ and covariance matrix E[xxT ] = Σ to a
145
      new random vector
146
                                               z = W(x − µ)
147
148   such that z has zero mean and unit diagonal covariance E[zzT ] = I. The D × D matrix W is called
149   the whitening matrix. The objective is to remove correlations between features and to normalize
150   variances. It is a common preprocessing technique particularly useful in computer vision tasks,
151   dimensionality reduction, and feature extraction. In the context of images, the dimension of the
152
      vector is the number of pixels D = h × w × c. While it can be shown that the whitening matrix

346   5   N OISE O PTIMIZATION THROUGH S IMULATED ANNEALING
347
348
      We uncovered the existence of a strong correlation between noise samples and the images they
349   generate in diffusion models. Moreover, previous experiments demonstrated that an image z0 is
350   related to the corresponding solution of Equation (6) through a linear transform that is close to ZCA
351   whitening when t ≃ T . Properly blending the “noise” ϵ∗ (z0 , t) and the z0 image and passing it
352   through the diffusion model recovers the solution of Equation (6). This implies that the estimated
353   final image from step t matches exactly the input image in one step starting at t, i.e. z0|t = z0 , and
354   a single diffusion step is enough to generate the final image.
355   Because whitening is invertible, the reverse mapping is also well-defined: a one-step inference
356   starting from a random Gaussian noise sample effectively maps to the final estimated image, which
357   is a de-whitened version of the noise itself. This is an insightful trait, as it means we can obtain
358   an approximate estimated final image z0|T by simply de-whitening the input noise with a matrix
359   multiplication, without using any diffusion model.
360
      We leverage this discovery to create model-agnostic correlated noises. Given an input image x,
361   we propose to find a noise z that correlates with it by minimizing a loss subject to the noise being
362   in-distribution, i.e.

469
470   Whitening transforms in machine learning. Whitening transforms can be employed to normal-
471
      ize and decorrelate input data, and served as inspiration for batch normalization (Ioffe & Szegedy,
      2015). ZCA whitening can be particularly interesting when applied to activation functions, which
472
      can improve generalization, speed-up training and approximate better features Luo (2017); Huang
473
      et al. (2018). However, when indiscriminately applied to data without normalized gradient descent
474   methods (Grosse & Martens, 2016), data whitening can hinder generalization (Wadia et al.; Ah-
475   mad, 2024). These works demonstrate that whitening can provide effective insights into learning
476   dynamics, though its application in diffusion models remains underexplored.
477
      Noise analysis in diffusion models. Understanding how images progressively deteriorate with noise
478
      and how that affects diffusion models is paramount to improve accuracy and speed-up training (Lee
479
      et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024), facilitate temporal coherency (Ge et al., 2023;
480   Chang et al., 2023), and elucidate the analysis of noise parameters (Deasy et al., 2022; Jolicoeur-
481   Martineau et al., 2023). Different works (Xu et al., 2024; Mao et al., 2024) have studied the effect
482   of random seeds in generative models, underscoring the sensitivity of generative processes to noise
483   configurations. Moreover, taking into consideration noise correlations with itself (Ge et al., 2023;
484   Chang et al., 2023) and with samples on the data distribution (Mao et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024) can
485   be useful to improve the training and evaluation of diffusion models. Lastly, alternative corruption
      schemes (Bansal et al.; Daras et al., 2022; Bansal et al., 2023; Rissanen et al., 2023; Hoogeboom &

518   Inverse problems and noise optimization. Optimizing the generation process for image-space
519   objectives is a recent trend in diffusion literature. Inverse problems can be solved by guiding the
520
      diffusion process through a function that compares the image-space objective with the predicted
      Tweedie for a given timestep (Kadkhodaie & Simoncelli, 2021; Lugmayr et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2023;
521
      He et al., 2023; Song et al., 2023; Chung et al., 2024a;b). A more accurate approach compares the
522
      result of the diffusion model through a fixed computational graph that is fully differentiated through
523   noise optimization (Samuel et al., 2023; Wallace et al., 2023; Pan et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2023;
524   Ben-Hamu et al., 2024; Eyring et al., 2024; Samuel et al., 2024). While this approach can produce
525   more accurate results, its also computationally more expensive, since it requires the computation of
526   the gradients of the objective function relative to the noise. For a survey on inverse problems with
527   diffusion models, please refer to Daras et al. (2024).

      8                                           C ONCLUSIONS
532
533
      In this work, we focused on the correlation between noise and corresponding images in diffusion
534   models. To this extend, we proposed to study the optimization problem of finding a “noise” that
535   would minimize the fixed-time score matching objective for a given target image. This gave us new
536   insights on the distribution mapping learned by the diffusion model. Importantly, we empirically
537   demonstrated that diffusion models learn to perform ZCA de-whitening in the setting of single-step
538   inference from noise. Leveraging this interesting discovery, we proposed to replace a diffusion
539   evaluation by a simple matrix multiplication and use this to find model-agnostic noises, which can
      generate image variations and help image editing across diverse models.
