009                                            Abstract
010
011
                Using natural language as a vehicle-to-vehicle (V2V) communication protocol
012             offers the potential for autonomous vehicles to drive cooperatively not only
013             with each other but also with human drivers. Simple and effective messages
014             for sharing critical observations or negotiating plans to achieve coordination
015             could improve traffic safety and efficiency compared to methods without
016             communication. In this work, we propose a suite of traffic tasks in vehicle-
017             to-vehicle autonomous driving where vehicles in a traffic scenario need to
018             communicate in natural language to facilitate coordination in order to avoid
019             an imminent collision and/or support efficient traffic flow, which we model as
020
                a general-sum partially observable stochastic game. To this end, this paper
                introduces a novel method, LLM+Debrief, to learn a message generation
021
                and high-level command policy for autonomous vehicles through multi-agent
022
                discussion. To evaluate our method, we developed a gym-like simulation envi-
023             ronment that contains a range of accident-prone driving scenarios that could
024             be alleviated by communication. Our experimental results demonstrate
025             that our method is more effective at generating meaningful and human-
026             understandable natural language messages to facilitate cooperation and co-
027             ordination than untrained LLMs. Our anonymous code and demo videos are
028             available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/talking-vehicles.

031   1     Introduction
032
033   State-of-the-art autonomous driving policies are commonly designed from the perspective of
034   a single agent’s sensors. Therefore, to enhance safety, it is essential to account for multi-agent
035   interactions. Among the strategies employed is incorporating motion prediction or intention
036   inference of other traffic participants in decision-making. Some models independently forecast
037   the future movements of these traffic participants (Wu et al., 2023), whereas others account
038   for mutual interactions among autonomous vehicles and other traffic agents (Seff et al.,
039
      2023). However, the challenge lies in the substantial uncertainty in predicting other drivers’
      intentions, often resulting in excessively cautious driving strategies (Rhinehart et al., 2021).
040
041   Given its cooperative nature, the safe driving problem could be largely simplified by enabling
042   vehicles to communicate their intentions and observations with each other. The concepts of
043   vehicle-to-vehicle (V2V) and vehicle-to-infrastructure (V2I) communication have recently
044   emerged as a promising approach for multi-vehicle cooperation, garnering considerable
045
      research interest (Wang et al., 2020; Cui et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2022a;b). While the focus
      of V2V communication has predominantly been on cooperative perception rather than
      cooperative control, it often employs modalities (latent representations, LiDAR points,
046
047
      locations of objects, etc.,) that are not intuitively nor easily understood by humans, thus
048   requiring that all the participating vehicles be autonomous and share the same protocol.
049   Natural language, as a refined and highly adaptable form of human communication, offers
050   the potential for human drivers to also participate in this cooperation. If vehicles could
051   “speak” in human language, it would pave the way for developing technologies that facilitate
052   communication between autonomous vehicles and human drivers, enhancing cooperation and
053   understanding in mixed-autonomy traffic environments. Even in fully autonomous settings,
      a natural language interface could offer flexibility for agents to explain their decisions, talk

      As a concrete example of the power of using language in V2V communication, consider the
072
      following scenario, as illustrated in Figure 1. A truck has broken down and stopped on a
073
      two-way road divided by a yellow dashed line. The vehicles trailing the truck cannot see
074   whether the lane going in the opposite direction is free from oncoming cars, which could allow
075   them to use that lane to safely move around the truck to circumvent the growing traffic jam.
076   If the truck is capable of communication, it could relay a message such as, “The opposite
077   lane is clear, you may safely perform a lane change to pass me.” Alternatively, in busier times,
078   the queued vehicles could initiate a dialogue with oncoming traffic, proposing, “Can we take
079   turns using the lane to avoid major congestion?” Through simple but effective communication
080   about their intentions or observations, a mixture of human-driven and autonomous vehicles
081   could significantly enhance traffic flow and overall efficiency.
082   In this work, we first introduce the problem of talking vehicles. In this problem setting,
083   a scenario in autonomous driving is formulated as a multi-agent partially observable and
084   general-sum game wherein each traffic participant is pursuing a cooperative goal modulated
085   by individual preferences1 . When conflicts arise or unexpected events occur, vehicles have the
086   opportunity to generate and broadcast messages that contain observations of abnormal events
087   or negotiations to cooperatively reach their goals. The vehicles can then make decisions
088
      according to the received messages.
089   Enabling vehicles to “talk” to each other with intentions and conveying helpful information
090   in natural language presents significant challenges. There are studies like Dolphins (Ma
091   et al., 2023) and LINGO-1 (Wayve, 2023) that have trained Visual-Language-Action models
092   to both make driving decisions and articulate their reasoning to humans. However, training
093   such models requires extensive data. At the time of writing this paper, only a limited number
094
      of datasets exist that provide language commentary data for single-agent driving scenarios
      (Kim et al., 2018; 2019; Qian et al., 2023; Sima et al., 2023) or in-vehicle communication with
095
      human driver (Deruyttere et al., 2022). To the best of our knowledge, datasets featuring
096
      natural language data for inter-vehicle communication are not yet available.
097
098   On the other hand, multi-agent “self-play” learning in a high-fidelity simulator requires no
099   real-world data and allows for closed-loop learning and evaluation. With recent advances
100
      in large language models (LLMs), there has been a surge of interest in applying LLMs to
      multi-agent games (Bakhtin et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2023a; Light et al., 2023). However,
101
      the synergy between LLMs and multi-agent games remains unexplored in the context of
102
      autonomous driving. Existing efforts in autonomous driving mainly focus on leveraging
103   LLMs for decision-making in a single-agent setting (Mao et al., 2023a; Shao et al., 2023; Ma
104   et al., 2023).

      To enable autonomous agents to learn what to say to cooperate from interactions with each
109   other while still constrain the messages in the natural language space, we propose a novel
110   method, LLM+Debrief, as an initial attempt to use LLMs to help solve the cooperative
111   driving problem in a V2V communication framework. This method optimizes the in-context
112   knowledge of a language model as the message generation guideline through a turn-based
113   post-episode discussion(debriefing) and leverages LLMs to analyze the dialogue history among
114   neighboring vehicles to generate general driving commands and messages to send. Finally,
115   an atomic controller executes the control according to the updated instructions. To test our
116   method and provide a research test bed for the community, we build a simulation framework
117
      containing an array of interesting multi-agent driving scenarios that support communications
      in natural languages. Our experimental results show an improvement in driving safety and
118
      efficiency of our method compared to the methods without communication.
119
120   In summary, this paper makes the following contributions:
121
122
           1. We present the task of cooperative driving facilitated by vehicle-to-vehicle communi-
              cation using natural language.
123
124        2. We contribute a multi-agent simulation framework to realistically model vehicle-to-
125
              vehicle communication through natural language, featuring diverse scenarios that
              capture a range of traffic conditions and interactive challenges.
126
127        3. We propose a novel method that empowers autonomous vehicles to dynamically gen-
128           erate and integrate natural language messages, facilitating inter-vehicle cooperation
129
              and leveraging multi-agent debriefing for improved coordination after interactions.
130        4. We evaluate our methodology within this simulation framework, and find that our
131           method is able to generate meaningful messages and improve traffic safety as well as
132           efficiency compared to alternative approaches.

134   2   Related Work
135
136   Vehicle-to-vehicle Communication. Vehicle-to-Vehicle (V2V) or Vehicle-to-everything
137   (V2X) communication offers the potential to effectively facilitate multi-vehicle cooperation,
138   improving the safety and reliability of autonomous vehicles in urban driving scenarios.
139   Existing research predominantly concentrates on cooperative perception data sets (Yu et al.,
140   2022; Xu et al., 2022b; Li et al., 2022) and tasks like cooperative detection and prediction
141
      (Wang et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2022a), leveraging sensor data from cameras,
      LiDAR, and other resources. The message aggregation strategies include early fusion (Qiu
      et al., 2022), late fusion, and intermediate fusion (Wang et al., 2020). Considering the
142
143
      limited V2V bandwidth, efforts were made to reduce the message size (Hu et al., 2022). Aoki
144   et al. (2020) developed a reinforcement learning method for selecting what information to
145   be transmitted in cooperative perception. Although Cui et al. (2022) developed end-to-end
146   driving policies and derived the critical information from expert supervision, the cooperation
147   is still completed at the perception level. In contrast, our work focuses on the message
148   in the natural language space to enhance both cooperative perception and formulation
149   of cooperative driving strategies. Real-world communication often suffers from caveats
150   in the communication mechanisms, including packet loss, latencies (Lei et al., 2022), and
151   localization errors. Although some works consider adversarial attacks (Tu et al., 2021) in
152
      V2V communication, we assume all vehicles are cooperative in this work.
153
      Multi-Agent Social Interactions with LLMs. Large Language Models (LLMs) show
      promising communication and reasoning capabilities, suggesting their potential in multi-agent
154
155
      interaction scenarios. For instance, Generative Agents (Park et al., 2023) represents an
156   early attempt at employing LLM agents for free-form chatting, demonstrating the believable
157   behaviors of LLM agents in spreading information. However, this study did not evaluate the
158   LLMs’ capabilities in planning or solving multi-agent tasks. Cicero (Bakhtin et al., 2022),
159   on the other hand, finetunes a language model to imitate human behaviors from a dataset
160   to generate truthful messages in the game of Diplomacy, which is mixed-motive and requires
161   communication in natural language. They train reinforcement learning policies to analyze
      the dialogue select actions and generate deceptive messages through value filtering. Recent


                                                    3
162
      works on communication games Werewolf (Xu et al., 2023a) and Avalon (Light et al., 2023)
163   demonstrate that LLMs can achieve impressive results in multi-agent settings. This success
164   is particularly notable when LLMs are combined with reinforcement learning or fine-tuning.
165   Shi et al. (2023) find that LLMs can be applied to achieve Ad Hoc teamwork in the Avalon
166   game. Recently, a generative agent-based social simulator (Vezhnevets et al., 2023) has been
167   proposed to serve as a test bed for multi-agent LLM interactions as well.
168
169   LLMs for Autonomous Driving. LLMs have shown great potential in solving various
170   autonomous driving tasks. In particular, they are promising in tackling corner cases (Wen
171   et al., 2023b) due to their reasoning ability and the common-sense knowledge embedded,
172
      yielding a more generalizable autonomous driving stack. Recent studies have explored
      various approaches to tailor state-of-the-art LLMs for driving. Similar to other embodied
173
      tasks (Driess et al., 2023; Brohan et al., 2023), a foundational challenge lies in grounding
174
      LLMs in the real world—the LLMs need to perceive and understand the traffic scenarios. A
175   straightforward approach is to obtain the observations from oracle perception models (Mao
176   et al., 2023b) and convert them to textual descriptions (Mao et al., 2023a; Sha et al., 2023;
177   Jin et al., 2023; Cui et al., 2023). Some other studies tackled this challenge by introducing
178   Visual Language Models (VLMs), which are adapted to driving domains through in-context
179   instruction tuning (Ma et al., 2023) or fine-tuning (Wayve, 2023; Xu et al., 2023b; Ding et al.,
180   2023; Yang et al., 2023). To enhance LLMs’ reasoning ability, prior works have investigated
181   incorporating handcrafted guidance and examples in the prompts (Sha et al., 2023; Jin et al.,
182   2023; Cui et al., 2023), structuring the reasoning procedure (Mao et al., 2023b; Sima et al.,
183
      2023), and fine-tuning the models on driving datasets. Notably, fine-tuning LLMs and VLMs
      requires an extensive amount of driving data with language labels. While a limited number
184
      of such datasets are available (Kim et al., 2018; 2019; Malla et al., 2023), they were mostly
185
      created in the pre-LLM era and, thus, are not designed for LLM fine-tuning. While several
186   works have attempted to adapt existing language-driving datasets for LLM fine-tuning (Ding
187   et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023b; Ma et al., 2023), growing attention has been drawn to directly
188   augment large-scale multimodal driving datasets, such as nuScenes (Caesar et al., 2020),
189   Waymo (Sun et al., 2020), and ONCE (Mao et al., 2021)), with language labels (Qian et al.,
190   2023; Shao et al., 2023; Sima et al., 2023; Nie et al., 2023). Note that existing models
191   were predominantly evaluated in open-loop fashions, except for Shao et al. (2023); Sha et al.
192   (2023); Jin et al. (2023). The open-loop evaluation results may not effectively imply the
193   models’ closed-loop performance after deployment. In contrast, similar to Surrealdriver (Jin
194
      et al., 2023), we conduct closed-loop tests of the proposed multi-agent communication and
      control framework in CARLA (Dosovitskiy et al., 2017). More importantly, none of the
195
      existing works have explored LLMs in a multi-agent setting with V2V communication as we
196
      did. LanguageMPC (Sha et al., 2023) was demonstrated in a multi-agent scenario, yet its
197   controller is centralized.

      3   Problem Definition
201
      We frame the problem of Talking Vehicles as a general-sum partially observable stochastic
      game (POSG), focusing on optimizing the social welfare of a focal population (F) (Agapiou
202
203
      et al., 2022) — defined as the cumulative reward of all agents of interest — as the primary
204   objective. This problem is a general-sum game because it includes scenarios that are not
205   entirely cooperative; conflicts of interest may exist and some agents may have to sacrifice
206   their individual interests for the overall benefits. Each agent’s observation space is limited to
207   a partial view of the full state, and agents make decisions in a decentralized manner based
208   on ego partial observations and received messages from other agents. Within this problem,
209   the action space for each agent has two main components: 1. the generation of messages,
210   and 2. the control of the vehicle. In this work, the message generation space is constrained
211   within natural language.
212   A POSG can be described by the tuple hI, S, {Oi }, {Ai }, P, {Ri }, γi, where I = {1, 2, ..., N }
213   refers to the identities of actionable agents; S is the state space comprehensively describing
214   the environment; Oi is the observation space of agent i’s state; Ai is the action space of
215   agent i; P represents the state transition function S × A1 × A2 × ... × AN → S; Ri is the
      reward function of agent i; Finally, γ is the discount factor.

217   expected sum of all the agents’ returns in the focal population F ⊆ I:

      {Mj }j∈I to its action space Ai = hMi , Ci i, where Mi is the message generation space,

226   time step t is broadcast to all other connected agents within a specific communication radius
227   at the next time step t + 1.

229
      In summary, the essence of the talking vehicles problem is to enable each agent to derive
      effective control-communication strategies from its observations and the messages it receives.
      These strategies, coupled with appropriate vehicle control actions, aim to achieve coordinated
230
231
      driving behavior.
232
233   This problem presents the following technical challenges to the machine-learning community:
234
235
           1. How can learned agents understand the situation and generate meaningful messages
              to help others perceive the environment or potentially negotiate about motion plans;
236
237        2. How can learned agents comprehend the received messages and incorporate them
238           into high-level cooperative driving decisions.
239
240   In this work, we assume that the agents could communicate truthfully, meaning they
241
      accurately convey their real intentions and follow through on their stated decisions. However,
      since they act simultaneously, other agents will only be able to process the received message
      in the next decision step. Additionally, we assume that all agents aim to cooperate with
242
243
      the focal population and do not send deceptive messages or act aggressively to sabotage the
244   driving goals of others intentionally. Exploring scenarios where these assumptions are lifted
245   could be an interesting direction for future work.

      5   Method
292

      The core technical problem that we address is to let agents communicate purposely to facilitate
293
294
      cooperation and act correspondingly using human language as the medium. Training language
295   policies using gradient-based methods to perform particular communication tasks by self-play
296   is known to generate non-human-comprehensible artificial languages. To establish an initial
297   solution to the talking vehicles problem, we start with an agentic framework that prompts
298   large language models as a foundational prior for autonomous agents to engage in human-like
299   communication, regularizing the message within natural language space, allowing agents to
300   interpret messages and make informed driving decisions. A key challenge of using LLMs lies
301   in that they are not specifically trained for driving tasks. To overcome this limitation, we
302   introduce LLM+Debrief (Algorithm 1), a novel agentic framework built upon feedback
303
      loops that allow LLMs agents to iteratively refine their communication and motion policy
      through trial-and-error interactions with confederate agents. Additionaly, inspired by how
      humans reflect and debrief after a Hanabi game, we enable agents to discuss cooperative
304
305
      strategies after each interaction episode. As illustrated in Figure 3, our method consists of
306   three core components: In-episode communication, Chain-of-Thought Reasoning,
307   and Post-Episode Debriefing.
308
309   In-episode Communication. Each driving agent is equipped with a transceiver mod-
310   ule that enables real-time communication during episodes. Agents broadcast and receive
311   structured messages by subscribing to topic-specific communication channels. Each message
312   follows a predefined format containing key details such as content, timestamp, sender ID, and
313   sender location. This structured approach ensures that messages are contextually relevant
314   and easily interpretable within multi-agent communication. Received messages are stored
315   in a buffer, and recent message dialogs are incorporated into the agent’s observations for
316
      decision-making.
317
      Chain-of-Thought Reasoning. Ye et al. (2024) observed that current LLMs can make
318   irreversible mistakes when computing variables without sufficient context. To address this, we
319   prompt the LLM to first reason about the environment based on its task, observations, received
320   messages, cooperative strategy, and accumulated knowledge before making decisions. After
321   reasoning, the LLM generates actions in a structured JSON format with keys: {"command",
322   "message"}. These outputs are then translated into vehicle controls and communication
323   messages to publish. All observations, commands, messages, and reasoning are stored in a
      replay buffer for further learning and refinement.

340   the environment will provide the text observation of the surroundings and message dialogues. Along
341   with the previous learned knowledge, the policy first performs chain-of-thought reasoning about all

343
      on their corresponding policies. After each episode, agents receive feedback from the environment

345   along with the {observation, reasoning, message, commands, and others’ reactions}, are stored in a

      knowledge is then stored as a long-term knowledge for future decision-making.
348
349   Post-Episode Debriefing. When an episode concludes, the environment evaluates each
350   agent’s performance and provides rich semantic feedback, such as “Vehicle 109 collided
351   with Vehicle 110 in 2 seconds” or “Vehicle 109 stagnated too long to complete its task.”
352   Each learning data point in the replay buffer is retrospectively labeled with additional
353   information, including other agents’ responses, collision details, and stagnation details.
354   Before engaging in the post-episode discussion(debriefing), each learning agent replays and
355   reflects the past experience by sampling a batch of learning data from its own replay buffer.
356   The sampling process heuristically assigns higher probabilities to data that are pre-collision,
357   slowing down agents in stagnation, and involving intensive interactions; these samples will
358   serve as the context for analysis and strategy formulation.
359
      The debriefing is conducted in a turn-based manner over N rounds, centered around
360   improving cooperation in future interactions. In each discussion session, a discussion host
361   randomly decides the speaking order. The agent selected to speak first gets the opportunity
362   to propose a cooperative strategy. Other agents then take turns to express their opinions or
363   thoughts on the proposed strategy.
      After debriefing, agents gain a clearer understanding of one another and summarize the
364
365
      discussion to form cooperative strategies and individual knowledge as in-context guidelines
366   for future driving tasks. Chain-of-thought reasoning is applied during debriefing to reinforce
367   decision-making processes. This structured post-episode analysis resembles the Centralized
368   Training Decentralized Execution (CTDE) framework commonly used in multi-agent learning.

      5.1     Implementation Details
371
372   We employ Llama-3-8B-Instruct (Dubey et al., 2024) at a temperature of 0.2 for the agent
373   framework, deciding and collecting experiences every 0.5 seconds (10 simulation frames).
374   The received message dialog is maintained within 2 seconds of the message age during the
375   episode. The debriefing process happens after each episode for 30 episodes and spans 1
376   round of discussion, followed by a final round of individual reflection steps to summarize the
377   discussion. A batch size of 2 is used to sample transition data from the trajectory. Additional
      details including decision latencies can be found in Appendix A.

      6     Experiments
379
380
      In this section, we aim to verify the following hypotheses with empirical experiments:
381
382         1. LLM agents can perform better with communication than without; LLM agents can
383            effectively respond to natural language messages without training and cooperate
384            with each other through communication to improve their performance;
385         2. LLM agents can autonomously improve themselves in the collaboration tasks through
386            decentralized reflection without human intervention;
            3. Debriefing can further enhance LLM learning from interactions more effectively than
387
388
               decentralized reflection alone;
389
390
      Metrics. In each scenario, we define a focal population whose movements we are interested
391   in and can control to accomplish a specific task within a certain time limit. Evaluation
392   metrics are then established based on the performance of this focal population over 30
393   evaluation episodes. We utilize three key metrics: the average total reward (R) accrued by
394   the focal population, the average number of collisions per episode (CR) of the population,
395   and the average episodic success rate (SR) normalized by the size of the population. Success
396   is defined as reaching target locations within a designated time frame without collision. An
397   agent who successfully completes the task earns a reward of +1. Conversely, collision incurs
398   a penalty of −1 for each agent involved in the collision while remaining stagnant at any
399
      point until timeout results in a reward of 0 because, although not ideal, conservative policies
      are at least safe.
400
401
      Baselines. We established several baselines and scenarios to evaluate our hypothesis.
      These baselines include: (1) an untrained LLM, (2) an LLM trained with decentralized
402
403
      reflection that updates in-context knowledge (LLM+Reflection), (3) an LLM that corrects
404   past actions via decentralized reflection, storing these corrections in a vector-based, retrievable
405   memory and uses few-shot retrieval augmented generation (LLM+Reflection+RAG), and
406   (4) an LLM trained with debrief discussions as outlined in Section 5 (LLM+Debrief). The
407   retrieval augmented method without communication adapts DiLU (Wen et al., 2023a), a
408   non-communicating single-agent LLM-based approach that drives via reflection, to our
409   environment. The multi-agent communication extension of DiLU, AgentsCoDriver (Hu et al.,
410   2024), resembles the Reflection+RAG (Comm) method, but they do not actively optimize
411   the messages. For a fair comparison across DiLU, AgentsCoDriver, and other baselines, we
412
      do not initialize the knowledge with human data, nor is there human involvement during the
      learning process. Additionally, we include Coopernaut (Cui et al., 2022), a LiDAR-based
      cooperative driving method, as a reference for cooperative perception. Note that since
413
414
      Coopernaut relies on intermediate sensor data representations rather than natural language
415   communication, its results are not directly comparable to the other methods being compared.
416
417   Experiment Setup. For each baseline2 , we consider two settings labeled as “Silent” and
418   “Comm”, respectively. In the “Silent” setting, the method operates without communication,
419   where policies focus solely on controlling the vehicle without generating messages. In contrast,
420   the “Comm” setting allows the method to either generate messages alone or both messages
421   and driving commands. For each LLM-based learning method, we train the models for up
422   to 30 episodes per scenario, with early stopping if the scenario is solved, indicated by 10
423   consecutive successful episodes. After training, we evaluate each method over 30 episodes
424
      and report the average performance across these evaluations.

516   7   Conclusion and Future Work
517   In summary, we identify a novel application domain for large language models in multi-
518   agent learning systems, talking vehicles, where agents are required to send natural language
519   messages and understand natural language messages to incorporate them into driving plans.
520   Solutions to the talking vehicles problem have the potential to enable autonomous agents
521   to facilitate cooperative perception and negotiation with human drivers. As a first attempt
522   to solve the talking vehicles challenge, we propose a new method, LLM+Debrief, for
523   generating messages and comprehending received messages. Our experiments show the
524
      effectiveness of LLM+Debrief quantitatively and qualitatively.
525   Limitations and Future Work. While we provide initial evidence of LLM+Debrief’s
526   potential in the talking vehicles problem, this research opens up several exciting future research
527   areas for further exploration and development. First, the current LLM+Debrief framework
528   takes text descriptions as observations, which relies on an idealized perception system. The
529   TalkingVehiclesGym environment is able to provide multi-modal sensor observations. In
530   future work, we are interested in developing a multi-modal extension of LLM+Debrief,
531
      which allows end-to-end perception and reasoning over the rich context information embedded
      in multi-modal observations. Second, this paper reports on a successful proof-of-concept,
      we are interested in scaling the evaluation benchmark and solution to more diverse traffic
532
533
      scenarios and operation conditions resembling real-world V2V communication, e.g., subject to
534   time delays, adversarial attacks, and limited bandwidths. Last, our framework opens up the
535   exciting potential to create a cooperative driving system for mixed-autonomy traffic scenarios.
536   We are interested in realizing this potential, by studying the talking vehicles problem with
537   human-in-the-loop experiments and exploring framework design to enable efficient communi-
538   cation between autonomous vehicles and human drivers.We refer readers to Appendix E for
539   a full discussion of limitations and future work.

756
      A    Method
757
758
      The Algorithm 1 implements LLM+Debrief, a centralized multi-agent learning framework
759   that leverages communication and reflection using large language models (LLMs) to enhance
760   coordination between agents in a simulated environment.
761
762
      Messages exchanged between agents during the simulation are incorporated into their
      observations, allowing the agents to adapt their strategies continuously. After completing
      each episode, the environment provides feedback, which is used to label and process the
763
764
      experiences in the replay buffer for further learning. This feedback helps the agents refine
765   their knowledge and improve their decision-making in subsequent episodes.
766
767   The debriefing process plays a crucial role in this algorithm. After each episode, agents
768
      engage in multiple rounds of debriefing, where they propose or revise cooperative strategies
      based on their experiences and interactions. The order of debriefing is randomized to simulate
      natural dialogues, enhancing the realism of the communication. Once the debriefing rounds
769
770
      are complete, the agents reflect individually, summarizing the discussions and updating their
771   knowledge bases. This reflection step is critical for improving future performance, enabling
772   agents to learn from successes and failures.
773
774
      At the conclusion of the training process, the agents’ knowledge and policies are updated,
      with the final policies from the last self-play iteration being used for further evaluations.
      The entire process is designed to improve the agents’ ability to communicate effectively and
775
776
      make informed decisions in a multi-agent setting.
777
778   The agents use Llama-3-8B-Instruct to generate and interpret messages, with a temperature
779   setting of 0.2 to ensure more deterministic outputs. The environment updates every 0.5
780
      seconds (equivalent to 10 simulation frames), and the agents’ messages are considered relevant
      for up to 2 seconds, ensuring timely and efficient communication. The experiments were
      conducted on two Nvidia A40 40GB GPUs, which were used to manage both the LLM-based
781
782
      policies and the simulation environment. This setup allowed the agents to run their LLM-
783   based decision-making processes in parallel, enhancing the scalability of the system and
784   enabling more efficient training.
785
786
      Table 3 summarizes the average latencies and message sizes for each scenario under the
      communication setting, evaluated using Llama3-8B-Instruct on Nvidia A100 GPUs and
      Intel Gen 10 CPUs. The metrics include partial observable captioner latency (in seconds),

       E    Limitations and Future Work
1027
1028
       Although we demonstrate some initial success of LLM+Debrief in the talking vehicles prob-
1029   lem, this research opens up several areas that require further exploration and development.
1030
1031   Inference Time. The response time of large language models (LLMs) can be prohibitive,
1032   particularly when rapid decision-making is critical. Currently, it takes several seconds for
1033   LLMs to process and respond to prompts, which is too slow for real-time applications. Future
1034   efforts could explore model distillation techniques to create smaller, more efficient models
1035   that retain the capabilities of their larger counterparts but operate at a faster pace.
1036
1037   Human Evaluation. Designing an intuitive and user-friendly interface for human interac-
1038   tion with autonomous vehicles is essential. Although our framework opens up the potential
1039   to cooperate with human drivers, the complexity of effective communication interfaces is
1040
       substantial. Comprehensive human-centered evaluations using human-friendly interfaces are
       deferred to future studies.
1041
1042
       Communication Challenges. Real-world vehicle-to-vehicle communication faces numer-
1043
       ous challenges, including time delays that result in outdated information and the potential
1044   for adversarial attacks with deceptive messages. Future research should focus on develop-
1045   ing methods to mitigate these risks, ensuring timely and secure exchanges of information.
1046   Techniques such as real-time data verification and robust communication protocols will be
1047   critical for enhancing the reliability and safety of V2V systems.
1048
1049   Multi-modal Perception. Our experiments primarily rely on text-based inputs, assuming
1050   an idealized perception system, due to the strong performance of existing LLMs and the
1051   nascent state of multi-modal pre-trained models. The TalkingVehiclesGym allows for the
1052   integration of various sensors, presenting an opportunity to experiment with and refine
1053   multi-modal foundation models. Future work should leverage these capabilities to address
1054
       the complex dynamics of real-world environments, enhancing the applicability of LLMs
       across different sensory inputs.
1055
1056
       Scalability and Generalization While this paper reports on a successful proof-of-
1057
       concept, the scalability of LLM+Debrief to handle diverse traffic scenarios and different
1058   environmental conditions has not been extensively tested. Future research should investigate
1059   the generalization capabilities of the model across various settings and under different and
1060   denser traffic conditions to ensure its robustness and reliability in a wider range of real-world
1061   applications.
1062
1063   Knowledge Preservation and Batch Sampling The details of the learning framework
1064   LLM+Debriefcould be further improved. For example, selecting what data to learn from
1065   and how to sample the critical data and assign credits to each step, especially in the multi-step
1066   tasks. Another issue is that the LLMs could lose context during learning if we ask them to
1067   revise the existing knowledge, causing the knowledge “unclean” issue and instability in the
1068
       learning if we integrate the knowledge revision too much.
