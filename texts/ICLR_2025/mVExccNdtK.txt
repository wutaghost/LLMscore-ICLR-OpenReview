                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Modern graph neural networks (GNNs) can be sensitive to changes in the input
012
               graph structure and node features, potentially resulting in unpredictable behavior
013
               and degraded performance. In this work, we introduce a spectral framework known
014            as SAGMAN for examining the stability of GNNs. This framework assesses the
015            distance distortions that arise from the nonlinear mappings of GNNs between the
016            input and output manifolds: when two nearby nodes on the input manifold are
017            mapped (through a GNN model) to two distant ones on the output manifold, it
018            implies a large distance distortion and thus a poor GNN stability. We propose
019            a distance-preserving graph dimension reduction (GDR) approach that utilizes
020            spectral graph embedding and probabilistic graphical models (PGMs) to create low-
021            dimensional input/output graph-based manifolds for meaningful stability analysis.
022
               Our empirical evaluations show that SAGMAN effectively assesses the stability
               of each node when subjected to various edge or feature perturbations, offering
023
               a scalable approach for evaluating the stability of GNNs, extending to applica-
024
               tions within recommendation systems. Furthermore, we illustrate its utility in
025            downstream tasks, notably in enhancing GNN stability and facilitating adversarial
026            targeted attacks.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The advent of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) has sparked a significant shift in machine learning
032   (ML), particularly in the realm of graph-structured data (Keisler, 2022; Hu et al., 2020; Kipf &
033   Welling, 2016; Veličković et al., 2017; Zhou et al., 2020). By seamlessly integrating graph structure
034   and node features, GNNs yield low-dimensional embedding vectors that maximally preserve the
035   graph structural information (Grover & Leskovec, 2016). Such networks have been successfully
036   deployed in a broad spectrum of real-world applications, including but not limited to recommendation
037   systems (Fan et al., 2019), traffic flow prediction (Yu et al., 2017), chip placement (Mirhoseini
038   et al., 2021), and social network analysis (Ying et al., 2018). However, the enduring challenge in
039
      the deployment of GNNs pertains to their stability, especially when subjected to perturbations in
      the graph structure (Sun et al., 2020; Jin et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2019). Recent studies suggest that
040
      even minor alterations to the graph structure (encompassing the addition, removal, or rearrangement
041
      of edges) can have a pronounced impact on the performance of GNNs (Zügner et al., 2018; Xu
042   et al., 2019). This phenomenon is particularly prominent in tasks such as node classification (Yao
043   et al., 2019; Veličković et al., 2017; Bojchevski & Günnemann, 2019). The concept of stability here
044   transcends mere resistance to adversarial attacks, encompassing the network’s ability to maintain
045   consistent performance despite inevitable variations in the input data (graph structure and node
046   features).
047
      In the literature, while there are studies primarily focused on developing more stable GNN architec-
048   tures (Wu et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024; Song et al., 2022; Gravina et al., 2022), a few attempts to
049   analyze GNN stability comprehensively. Specifically, (Keriven et al., 2020) first studied the stability
050   of graph convolutional networks (GCN) on random graphs under small deformation. Later, (Gama
051   et al., 2020) and (Kenlay et al., 2021) explored the robustness of various graph filters, which are then
052   used to measure the stabilities of the corresponding (spectral-based) GNNs. However, these prior
053   methods are limited to either synthetic graphs or specific GNN models. Recent survey papers, such
      as (Dai et al., 2024), highlight the critical role of stability analysis in trustworthy GNNs.

054
      In this work, we present SAGMAN, a novel framework devised to quantify the stability of GNNs
055   through individual nodes. This is accomplished by assessing the resistance-distance distortions
056   incurred by the nonlinear map (GNN model) between low-dimensional input and output graph-based
057   manifolds. It is crucial to note that this study aims to offer significant insights for understanding
058   and enhancing the stability of GNNs. While most robustness techniques focus on enhancing overall
059   model robustness through architectural modifications or training strategies, they often lack fine-
060   grained, node-level stability assessments. In contrast, SAGMAN introduces a spectral framework that
061   quantifies stability at the individual node level, enabling targeted interventions such as precise attacks
062   or tailored stability enhancements. The key technical contributions of this work are outlined below:
063   • This study introduces a spectral framework (SAGMAN) for measuring the stability of GNN models
064   at the node level. This is achieved by effectively assessing the distance distortions caused by the
065   maps between the input and output smooth manifolds.
066
      • To construct the input smooth manifold for stability analysis in SAGMAN, we introduce a nonlinear
067   graph dimension reduction (GDR) framework to transform the original input graph (with node
068   features) into a low-dimensional graph-based manifold that can well preserve the original graph’s
069   spectral properties, such as effective resistance distances between nodes.
070
      • SAGMAN has been empirically evaluated and shown to be effective in assessing the stability of
071
      individual nodes across various GNN models in realistic graph datasets. Moreover, SAGMAN allows
072
      for more powerful adversarial targeting attacks and greatly improving the stability (robustness) of the
073   GNNs.
074
075   • SAGMAN has a near-linear time complexity and its data-centric nature allows it to operate across
076
      various GNN variants, independent of label information, network architecture, and learned parameters,
      demonstrating its wide applicability.

081   2.1   S TABILITY A NALYSIS OF ML M ODELS ON THE M ANIFOLDS
082
083   The stability of a machine learning (ML) model refers to its ability to produce consistent outputs
084
      despite small variations or noise in the input data (Szegedy et al., 2013). To assess this stability, we
      utilize the Distance Mapping Distortion (DMD) metric (Cheng et al., 2021). For two input data
085
      samples p and q, the DMD metric δ M (p, q) is defined as the ratio of their distance on the output
086
      manifold to the one on the input manifold:
087
088                                                     def dY (p, q)
                                              δ M (p, q) =                .                                 (1)
089                                                           dX (p, q)
090
091   By evaluating δ M (p, q) for each pair of data samples, we can assess the stability of the ML model.
092   Specifically, if two nearby data samples on the input manifold are mapped to distant points on the
093   output manifold, this indicates a large δ M (p, q) or equivalently a large local Lipschitz constant, and
094   thus poor stability of the model near these samples; On the other hand, a small δ M (p, q) implies that
095   the model is stable in that region.
096
097   2.2   P REVIOUS I NVESTIGATIONS ON THE S TABILITY OF GNN S
098
099
      The stability of a GNN refers to its output stability in the presence of edge/node perturbations (Sun
      et al., 2020). This includes maintaining the fidelity of predictions and outcomes when subjected to
100
      changes such as edge alterations or feature attacks. A desired GNN model is expected to exhibit
101
      good stability, wherein every predicted output or the graph embeddings do not change drastically in
102   response to the aforementioned minor perturbations (Jin et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2019). Several recent
103   studies have underlined the importance of analyzing the stability of GNNs. For instance, Sharma
104   et al. (2023) examined the task and model-agnostic vulnerabilities of GNNs, demonstrating that these
105   networks remain susceptible to adversarial perturbations regardless of the specific downstream tasks.
106   Furthermore, Huang et al. (2023) investigated robust graph representation learning via predictive
107   coding, proposing a method that enhances GNN stability by reconstructing input data to mitigate the
      effects of adversarial attacks.

      Moreover, while recent studies have investigated the stability issues of Graph Neural Networks
109   (GNNs) on synthetic graphs or specific models (Keriven et al., 2020; Kenlay et al., 2021), they have
110   not provided a unified framework for evaluating GNN stability. The latest state-of-the-art method
111   (Li et al., 2024) develops such a unified framework but does not consider feature perturbations. This
112   omission leaves a significant gap in understanding how GNNs respond to changes in node features,
113   which is crucial for applications where feature data may be noisy or subject to perturbations.

      Challenges in Applying DMD Metrics to GNN Stability Analysis. When adopting the DMD
118   metric for the stability analysis of GNN models, a natural approach is to use graph-based manifolds
119   (details in Appendix A.1) for calculating DMDs. However, directly using the input graph structure
120   as the input graph-based manifold may not produce satisfactory results as shown in our empirical
121   results presented in Table 2 and Appendix A.8. This inadequacy stems from the fact that the original
122   input graph data (including node features) may not reside near a low-dimensional manifold (Bruna
123   et al., 2013), while meaningful DMD-based stability analysis requires both the input and output data
124   samples to lie near low-dimensional manifolds (Cheng et al., 2021). Therefore, a naive application of
125   DMD metrics on the original graph structure is insufficient for assessing the stability of GNNs.

146   To extend the applicability of the DMD metric to GNN settings, we introduce SAGMAN, a spectral
147   framework for stability analysis of GNNs. A key component of SAGMAN is a novel distance-
148   preserving Graph Dimensionality Reduction (GDR) algorithm that transforms the original input
149
      graph data—including node features and graph topology, which may reside in high-dimensional
      space—into a low-dimensional graph-based manifold.
150
151   As illustrated in Figure 1, the SAGMAN framework comprises three main phases:
152
153           • Phase 1: Creation of the input graph embedding matrix based on both node features and
154             spectral properties of the graph. This embedding matrix is essential for the subsequent GDR
155
                step.
156           • Phase 2: Construction of low-dimensional input and output graph-based manifolds using a
157             Probabilistic Graphical Model (PGM) approach.
158           • Phase 3: Node stability evaluation using the DMD metric, leveraging a spectral graph
159             embedding scheme that utilizes generalized Laplacian eigenvalues and eigenvectors.
160
161   Detailed descriptions of each phase are provided in the following sections. The complete algorithmic
      flow of SAGMAN is shown in Section 3.5.

      3.2   P HASE 1: E MBEDDING M ATRIX C ONSTRUCTION FOR GDR
163
164   The calculation of the DMD metric inherently relies on pairwise distances between nodes. For
165   graph-based manifolds, two widely used metrics for evaluating pairwise node distances are: (1) the
166   shortest-path distance and (2) the effective resistance distance. As discussed in Appendix A.1, the
167   effective resistance distance is more closely related to the graph’s structural (spectral) properties,
168   providing a more meaningful measure of connectivity between nodes. Moreover, previous studies
169   have demonstrated a significant correlation between effective resistance distances and the stability of
170
      machine learning models (Cheng et al., 2021). Therefore, our focus is on performing dimensionality
      reduction on the input graph while preserving its original effective resistance distances.
171
172
      Graph Dimensionality Reduction via Laplacian Eigenmaps. To achieve dimensionality reduction
173
      of the input graph, we utilize the widely recognized nonlinear dimensionality reduction algorithm,
174   Laplacian Eigenmaps (Belkin & Niyogi, 2003). The Laplacian Eigenmaps algorithm begins by
175   constructing an undirected graph where each node represents a high-dimensional data sample, and
176   edges encode the similarities between data samples. It then computes the eigenvectors corresponding
177   to the smallest eigenvalues of the graph Laplacian matrix, which are used to map each node into a
178   low-dimensional space while preserving the local relationships between the data samples.
179
180   Spectral Embedding with Eigengaps. For a graph with N nodes, a straightforward approach to
181   apply Laplacian Eigenmaps is to compute an N × N spectral embedding matrix using the complete
182   set of graph Laplacian eigenvectors and eigenvalues (Ng et al., 2001), representing each node with
183
      an N -dimensional vector. However, computing the full set of eigenvalues and eigenvectors is
      computationally prohibitive for large graphs.
184
185   To address this issue, we leverage a theoretical result from spectral graph clustering (Peng et al., 2015),
186   which shows that the existence of a significant gap between consecutive eigenvalues—known as an
187   eigengap—implies that the graph can be well represented in a lower-dimensional space. Specifically,
188   the eigengap is defined as Υ(k) = λρ(k) k+1
                                                  , where ρ(k) denotes the k-way expansion constant, and
189   λk+1 is the (k + 1)-th smallest eigenvalue of the normalized Laplacian matrix. A significant eigengap
190   indicates the existence of a k-way partition where each cluster has low conductance, meaning the
191   graph is well-clustered. Based on this, we can approximate the spectral embedding using only the
192   first k eigenvalues and eigenvectors. We define the weighted spectral embedding matrix as follows:
193   Definition 3.1. For a connected graph G = (V, E, w) with its k smallest nonzero Laplacian eigen-
194   values denoted by 0 < λ1 ≤ λ2 ≤ . . . ≤ λk and correspondingh           eigenvectors
                                                                                   i        u1 , u2 , . . . , uk , the
195   weighted spectral embedding matrix is defined as Uk = √uλ1 , . . . , √uλk ∈ R|V |×k .
                                                                          1          k

197   For a graph with a significant eigengap Υ(k), this embedding matrix allows us to represent each node
198   with a k-dimensional vector such that the effective resistance distance between any pair of nodes can
199   be well approximated by deff (p, q) ≈ ∥Uk⊤ ep,q ∥22 , where ep ∈ R|V | is the standard basis vector with
200   a 1 at position p and zeros elsewhere, and ep,q = ep − eq .
201
202   Using Eigengaps for Graph Dimension Estimation. Determining the precise graph dimension
203   required to embed a graph into Euclidean space while preserving certain properties (e.g., unit edge
204   lengths) is an NP-hard problem (Erdös et al., 1965; Schaefer, 2012). However, the presence of
205
      a significant eigengap Υ(k) suggests that the graph can be well represented in a k-dimensional
      space (Peng et al., 2015), making k an approximate measure of the graph’s intrinsic dimensionality.
206
      While computing the exact value of Υ(k) may be challenging in practice, we can use the identified
207
      eigengap as an indicator of the suitability of SAGMAN for a given graph: graphs with significant
208   eigengaps are more suitable to our framework since they can be effectively represented in low-
209   dimensional spaces. Empirically, for datasets with c classes, we can approximate k as k ≈ 10c to
210   effectively capture significant eigengaps (Deng et al., 2022).

217   are not suitable for GNN stability analysis, as empirically demonstrated in Appendix A.6.

      Probabilistic Graphical Models (PGMs) for Graph-based Manifold Learning. PGMs, also
220   known as Markov Random Fields (MRFs), are powerful tools in machine learning and statistical
221   physics for representing complex systems with intricate dependency structures (Roy et al., 2009).
222   PGMs encode the conditional dependencies between random variables through an undirected graph
223   structure (see Appendix A.3 for more details). Recent studies have shown that the graph structure
224   learned through PGMs can have resistance distances that encode the Euclidean distances between
225   their corresponding data samples (Feng, 2021).
226   In our context, each column vector in the embedding matrix Uk (as defined in Definition 3.1)
227   corresponds to a data sample used for graph topology learning. By constructing the low-dimensional
228   graph-based manifold via PGMs, we can effectively preserve the resistance distances from the original
229   graph. Empirical evidence, detailed in Appendix A.6, supports that preserving these distances is
230   essential for distinguishing between stable and unstable nodes. Therefore, our methodology leverages
231   PGMs to maintain accurate effective resistance distances.
232   However, existing methods for learning PGMs may require numerous iterations to achieve conver-
233   gence (Feng, 2021), limiting their applicability to large graphs.
234

365   3.6   T IME C OMPLEXITY OF SAGMAN
366
367   The proposed SAGMAN framework is designed to be efficient and scalable for large graphs. Below,
368   we analyze the time complexity of its key components. We utilize fast multilevel eigensolvers to
369
      compute the first c Laplacian eigenvectors. These eigensolvers operate in nearly linear time, O(c|V |),
      without loss of accuracy (Zhao et al., 2021), where |V | denotes the number of nodes in the graph. To
370
      construct the initial graph for manifold learning, we employ the k-nearest neighbor algorithm, which
371
      has a nearly linear computational complexity of O(|V | log |V |) (Malkov & Yashunin, 2018). The
372   spectral sparsification step, performed via Low-Resistance-Diameter (LRD) decomposition, has a
373   time complexity of O(|V |dm), where d is the average degree of the graph, and m is the order of the
374   Krylov subspace used in the computation. By leveraging fast generalized eigensolvers (Koutis et al.,
375   2010; Cucuringu et al., 2016), we can compute all DMD values in O(|E|) time, where |E| denotes
376   the number of edges in the graph. Importantly, we ignore d (average degree) because most real-world
377   graphs are sparse (Miao et al., 2019), with average degrees much smaller than the number of nodes.
      In such cases, d can be treated as a constant or as growing slowly with |V |.

      Overall, the dominant terms in the time complexity are nearly linear with respect to the size of the
379   graph. This near-linear scalability allows SAGMAN to handle large graph datasets efficiently. Results
380   for runtime scalability are available in Appendix A.13

509   4.5   SAGMAN- GUIDED GNN S TABILITY E NHANCEMENT
510
511   A naive approach to improving the stability of a GNN model is to replace the entire input graph with
512   the low-dimensional graph-based manifold for GNN predictions. However, due to the significantly
513   increased densities in the graph-based manifold, the GNN prediction accuracy may be adversely
514   affected. To achieve a flexible trade-off between model stability and prediction accuracy, we select
515
      only the inter-cluster edges from the input graph-based manifold, as shown in Figure 2(d), and
      insert them into the original graph. Since the inter-cluster (bridge-like) edges are typically spectrally
516
      critical—with high sampling probabilities as defined in Theorem 3.2—adding them to the original
517
      graph significantly alters its structural (spectral) properties.
518
519   Whereas previous work (Deng et al., 2022) focuses on improving the robustness of GNNs against
520   poisoning attacks, our work centers on enhancing robustness against evasion attacks. In Table 4, we
521
      present the error rates for both the original and the enhanced graphs, where Nettack was employed to
      perturb SAGMAN-selected most unstable samples within each graph. Table 5 shows the error rates for
522
      the state-of-the-art GOOD-AT (Li et al., 2024) method and our proposed SAGMAN approach. Since
523
      SAGMAN is a versatile plug-in method that can be combined with other robustness techniques, we
524   also report results for the combined application of GOOD-AT and SAGMAN. Notably, reintegrating
525   selected edges into the original graph significantly reduces the error rate when subjected to adversarial
526   evasion attacks. To further demonstrate SAGMAN’s effectiveness under poisoning attacks and in
527   comparison with Lipschitz-based methods, we present additional results in Appendix A.13.

530   5     C ONCLUSIONS
531
532   In this work, we introduced SAGMAN, a novel framework for analyzing the stability of GNNs at
533   the individual node level by assessing the resistance-distance distortions between low-dimensional
534   input and output graph-based manifolds. A key component of SAGMAN is the proposed Graph
535   Dimensionality Reduction (GDR) approach for constructing resistance-preserving manifolds, which
536
      enables effective stability analysis.
537   Our experimental results demonstrate that SAGMAN effectively quantifies GNN stability, leading to
538   significantly enhanced targeted adversarial attacks and improved GNN robustness. The current SAG-
539   MAN framework is particularly effective for graphs that can be well represented in low-dimensional
      spaces and exhibit large eigengaps.

748
749
      Graph-based Manifolds. A manifold is a topological space that locally resembles Euclidean space
750   near each point (Lee, 2012). In our work, we utilize graph-based manifolds to represent complex data
751   structures. Specifically, we represent each manifold as an undirected (connected) graph G = (V, E),
752   where V is the set of vertices corresponding to data points, and E is the set of edges encoding
753   relationships (e.g., conditional dependencies) between these points (Tenenbaum et al., 2000). This
754   representation is particularly effective when the underlying data structure can be approximated by
755   a network of discrete points, as is common in spectral clustering and manifold learning (Belkin &
      Niyogi, 2003).


                                                      14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      Mappings Between Manifolds. Understanding the mappings between manifolds is essential for
757   transferring and comparing information across different data representations. In our context, these
758   mappings correspond to transformations between the input and output graph-based manifolds of
759   an ML model. Formally, a mapping φ : MX → MY from the input manifold MX to the output
760   manifold MY allows us to analyze how the model transforms data points and their relationships (Pan
761   & Yang, 2009). This is crucial for assessing the model’s stability with respect to small perturbations
762   in the input.
763
764   Distance Metrics on Manifolds. Measuring dissimilarities between points on manifolds requires
765   appropriate distance metrics. While geodesic distances, representing the shortest paths between
766   points on a manifold, are commonly used, they can be computationally intensive for large datasets
767   and may not capture global structural information due to their inherently local nature (Tenenbaum
768   et al., 2000).
769
770   Effective Resistance Distances on Graph-based Manifolds. To overcome these limitations, we
771   employ the effective resistance distance from electrical network theory. By modeling the undirected
772   graph as a resistive network, the effective resistance between two nodes captures both local relation-
773   ships and global structural properties of the graph. This metric effectively combines the advantages of
774   geodesic and global distances, providing a more comprehensive measure of similarity in graph-based
775
      manifolds (Tenenbaum et al., 2000). Additionally, it is computationally efficient for large-scale
      graphs (Klein & Zhu, 1998), making it practical for our analysis.
776
777   To illustrate the difference between geodesic distance and effective resistance distance, consider
778   the example in Figure 4. In all three graphs, nodes A and B have the same geodesic distance
779   (i.e., the shortest path length is identical). However, their effective resistance distances ΩAB vary
780   significantly due to differences in the global structure of each graph. This example demonstrates that
781
      while geodesic distance only accounts for the shortest path, effective resistance distance incorporates
      the overall connectivity and network topology, making it a more informative metric for assessing
782
      similarities in graph-based manifolds.

1078   model’s predicted class is denoted as ŷ = argmaxi (yi ). Now, let’s assume that the output vector
1079
       transforms to y′ = [y1′ , y2′ , ..., yk′ ], while preserving the ordinality of the elements, i.e., if yi > yj ,

       Relying solely on model accuracy can be deceptive, as it is contingent upon the preservation of
1081   the ordinality of the output vector elements, even when the vector itself undergoes significant
1082   transformations. This implies that the model’s accuracy remains ostensibly unaffected as long as the
1083   ranking of the elements within the output vector is conserved. However, this perspective neglects
1084   potential alterations in the model’s prediction confidence levels.
1085

1086

1087   a more granular insight into the impact of adversarial attacks on the model’s predictions.

1090

1091
