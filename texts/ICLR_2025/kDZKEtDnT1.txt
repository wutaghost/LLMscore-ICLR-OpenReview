009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011           The success of Large Language Models has inspired the development of Genomic
012           Foundation Models (GFMs) through similar pretraining techniques. However, the
013           relationship between pretraining performance and effectiveness in downstream
014           genomic tasks remains unclear. Additionally, the high computational cost of pre-
015           training raises questions about its cost-efficiency. To assess the usefulness of pre-
016           training in genomics, we evaluated seven different GFMs across various bench-
017
              marks, comparing them to their counterparts with randomly initialized weights.
              Surprisingly, we found that randomly initialized models can match or even sur-
018
              pass the performance of pretrained GFMs in finetuning and feature extraction
019
              tasks. We also discovered that pretrained GFMs fail to capture clinically relevant
020           genetic mutations, which are crucial for understanding genetic disorders and phe-
021           notypic traits. Our results indicate that most of the current pretrained GFMs lack
022           a “foundational” understanding of genomics and provide minimal utility, even for
023           basic tasks such as sequence classification. These findings collectively highlight
024           the need for critically rethinking the pretraining approaches for genomics. Our
025           code is available at github.com/nxifemwt/GFMs.
026
027
      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Recent advances in language modeling have led to the application of similar unsupervised pre-
030   training approaches in genomics. This facilitated the emergence of Genomic Foundation Models
031   (GFMs) (Consens et al., 2023) which learn representations from genomic sequences. This line of
032   work has attracted considerable attention due to the potential of GFMs to revolutionize our under-
033   standing of genomics (Benegas et al., 2024).
034
      GFMs typically use a two-step training approach akin to Large Language Models: unsupervised
035
      pretraining on a large dataset, followed by a supervised training. The pretraining phase usually
036
      involves either next token prediction (Brown et al., 2020) or masked language modeling (Devlin
037   et al., 2018). The promise of unsupervised pretraining is to extract knowledge from vast genomic
038   datasets (Consortium et al., 2015) and compress it into the model’s parameters, with the aim of
039   producing a generalist model applicable to a diverse set of tasks.
040
041   While some studies have explored scaling laws for GFMs (Nguyen et al., 2023; 2024), the relation-
042   ship between pretraining and downstream performance remains unclear, with no single GFM con-
043   sistently proving to be the best (Marin et al., 2023). Combined with large model sizes (Dalla-Torre
044   et al., 2023), long input sequences (Nguyen et al., 2023; 2024) and massive datasets, the pretrain-
045
      ing step demands substantial computational resources. The natural question arises: how effective is
      unsupervised pretraining in the genomics domain?
046
047
      To answer this, we conduct extensive experiments with seven recent GFMs across multiple bench-
048   marks, as shown on Figure 1, comparing the performance of pretrained models to their randomly
049   initialized counterparts. Our study reveals that randomly initialized models often perform competi-
050   tively with pretrained models or even surpass them, suggesting that current pretraining approaches
051   may not provide a significant advantage over random weight initialization. Specifically, we found
052   that on the Nucleotide Transformer Benchmark (Dalla-Torre et al., 2023), GUE (Zhou et al., 2024),
053   and Genomics Benchmark (Schiff et al., 2024) randomly initialized models trained from scratch in
      a supervised manner perform either better then or on par with finetuned pretrained GFMs.

088   In addition to finetuning, we examine feature extraction tasks to assess the quality of the represen-
089   tations learned during the pretraining. These tasks involve extracting features from the model with
090   frozen weights and applying a simple classifier to these embeddings. For randomly initialized mod-
091
      els, this means that the weights remain as originally randomly initialized, without any tuning at all.
      Intuitively, one would expect untrained models with randomly initialized weights to perform poorly
092
      compared to pretrained.
093
094   One such task is biotype classification, where the goal is to predict the functional type of a ge-
095   nomic sequence. Surprisingly, randomly initialized models demonstrate competitive performance
096   compared to pretrained in biotype classification. Moreover, simple modifications, such as chang-
097
      ing the tokenizer and increasing the embedding dimension, significantly boost the performance of
      randomly initialized models, enabling the completely untrained HyenaDNA to outperform all pre-
098
      trained GFMs on this benchmark.
099
100   Another important group of tasks uses mutation information as the primary predictive feature. These
101   scenarios require models to be highly sensitive to single nucleotide changes within long sequences.
102   We found that most pretrained GFMs fail in these tasks. For instance, even when up to half of the
103
      nucleotides in a DNA sequence are changed, some GFMs still produce embeddings with over 0.99
      cosine similarity to the original sequence. As a result, GFMs are currently unsuitable for applications
104
      that rely extensively on mutation data, including variant pathogenicity prediction, eQTL (Zhou &
105
      Troyanskaya, 2015), sQTL (Garrido-Martı́n et al., 2021), and phenotype prediction.
106
107   Overall, our results challenge current unsupervised pretraining methods used in genomics, suggest-
      ing that simply adapting NLP techniques is insufficient for developing true genomic understanding.

      Rather than continuing to invest substantial computational resources in existing pretraining methods,
109   we advocate for critically rethinking the fundamental building blocks of genomic foundation mod-
110   els. This includes developing biologically-informed tokenization strategies and establishing new
111   robust benchmarks that comprehensively test for the understanding of genomic mechanisms.

113   2     M ODELS
114
115   We selected six recently published GFMs for evaluation and also trained our own version of the
116
      Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023) model on 50 samples from the 1000 Genomes dataset (Consortium et al.,
      2015). The models in our analysis exhibit significant diversity in their architectures, pretraining
117
      objectives, tokenizers, model sizes, and pretraining datasets. Our model selection includes both en-
118
      coder and decoder architectures, transformer-based and state-space models, with model sizes rang-
119   ing from 450K to 500M parameters. Interestingly, our Mistral outperforms all other previous GFMs
120   on many tasks. We attribute the success of Mistral to an advanced architecture recipe which in-
121   cludes RoPE embeddings, big embedding dimension and character tokenizer. Model configurations
122   are summarized in Table 1, and model descriptions are provided in Section A.1 of the Appendix.
123
      We excluded the EVO model (Nguyen et al., 2024) from our analysis as it was trained on bacterial
124
      genomes and performed poorly in our preliminary tests on the Nucleotide Transformer Benchmark.
125
126   Random weight initialization of models throughout the paper follows the procedure from the
127   Transformers library (Wolf et al., 2020) for each particular model. This usually involves initial-
128   izing linear layers with values drawn from N (0, 0.02), and LayerNorm layers are initialized with
129
      γ = 1. Full random initialization details for each model are provided in Section A.2 in Appendix.

      3     E XPERIMENTS
132   3.1   F INETUNING
133
      To verify the usefulness of pretraining, we finetuned both pretrained and randomly initialized ver-
134
      sions of the models on Nucleotide Transformer Benchmark (Dalla-Torre et al., 2023), Genome
135   Understanding Evaluation (GUE) (Zhou et al., 2024), and Genomic Benchmarks (Grešová et al.,
136   2023) with exactly the same set of hyperparameters. This set of benchmarks together constitutes
137   52 genomic classification tasks. In total, we conducted nearly 10,000 finetuning experiments, this
138   considers: seven models, both pretrained and random, evaluated across different tasks, folds, and
139   learning rates. Full hyperparameter details for these experiments are provided in Section A.4 in the
140   Appendix. We display our results for these finetuning experiments in Figure 2.
141
      For each task, we first find the highest score among all randomly initialized models; for example,
142   if the scores obtained from randomly initialized models are 0.3, 0.4, and 0.5, we consider 0.5 as
143   the best random score. We then plot the difference between each pretrained model’s performance
144   and the best random score. Green bars show where pretrained models outperform the best random
145   model, while red bars show where they underperform. Ideally, if the pretraining is useful, we expect
146   to see a predominance of tall green bars.
147
      NT Benchmark results for histone and enhancer tasks are displayed in the top part of Figure 2. For
148
      the GUE Benchmark, we aggregate results by task categories. For example, in the Epigenetic Marks
149   category, we average the scores across all histone modification tasks. Similarly, we compute average
150   scores for other categories: Promoter Detection, TF Prediction Human and Mouse, Core Promoter
151   Detection, and Splice Site Detection (which contains a single task). These aggregated results are
152   presented in the middle part of Figure 2. For Genomic Benchmarks we display the performance for
153   six different tasks in the bottom part of Figure 2.
154

159   tasks on Genomic Benchmarks. In general, randomly initialized Caduceus significantly outperforms
160   larger pretrained models, including NT 500M, NTv2 50M, GENA-LM with 110M parameters, and

247
            Finding 1: Randomly initialized models can perform competitively with, and even sur-
248
            pass, pretrained models in finetuning tasks. Notably, this competitiveness is not a function
249         of model size - the randomly initialized Caduceus with only 8M parameters consistently
250         outperforms much larger pretrained models including NT 500M, GENA-LM (110M param-
251         eters), DNABERTv2 (117M parameters), and NTv2 50M. This pattern holds robustly across
252         three different genomic benchmarks and types of tasks.
253
254   Pretraining gains in genomics vs in other domains. The success of foundation models in com-
255   puter vision and NLP has been built on clear substantial gains from unsupervised pretraining. For
256
      example, CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) showed 10-30% improvements in robustness to distribution
      shift compared to standard ImageNet models, while GPT (Brown et al., 2020) few-shot performance
257
      outperformed SOTA finetuned model in question-answering tasks. However, our experiments show
258
      a different pattern in genomics, where the best randomly initialized models often outperform pre-
259   trained ones, and in case when pretrained models are better the difference with randomly initialized
260   is generally marginal, i.e. about 2-3%. together those gains do not justify large amounts of compute
261   needed for pretraining in genomics (Dalla-Torre et al., 2023).
262
263         Finding 2: While pretraining provides double-digit improvements in computer vision and
264         NLP, the gains in genomics are typically within 2-3% and often negative, challenging the
265         effectiveness of current genomic pretraining approaches.

         3.3 G ENOMIC VARIATION
338
339
         In this section, we shift our focus from functional element classification tasks to genomic variation
         tasks, which represents a fundamentally different problem in genomic analysis. Genomic variation
340
         tasks focus on the mutations in DNA sequences between individuals. These mutations, including
341
         single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs), insertions, and deletions, are crucial for understanding
342      human genetic diversity and its implications for personal health.
343
344      Unlike functional elements, which are largely consistent across individuals, genomic variations are
345
         unique to individuals or populations and can significantly affect phenotype and disease risk. These
         variant-based analyses use mutation information as the primary predictive feature, necessitating
346
         models to detect and interpret subtle sequence differences between individuals.
347
348      This group of tasks presents a critical challenge for GFMs: they must be highly sensitive to small
349      variations, as the genetic differences between populations often come down to single nucleotide
350      polymorphisms (SNPs) scattered throughout the sequence. This requirement tests the models’ abil-
351      ity to detect and interpret subtle genetic variation effects.
352      3.3.1 A NCESTRY P REDICTION
353
         Ancestry prediction is a multilabel classification task that involves predicting an individual’s ances-
354      try using only a short portion of their genome. In our experiments, we first constructed an ancestry
355      dataset using the 1000G data (Consortium et al., 2015). We used the HG38 and applied mutations
356      from every 1000G sample (Figure 1 C) to obtain consensus sequences for each individual. We fixed
357      the sequence length to 32K bases. These sequences differ by approximately 0.5% of positions and,
358      on average, contain 33 variants, including SNPs, insertions, and deletions. For each sequence, we
359      generated embeddings which served as features for XGBoost classification.
360      When generating the dataset, we selected eleven different regions of the genome, treating each as
361      a separate fold, and evaluated our models on each region independently, reporting average metrics
362      (Figure 5). A detailed description of the benchmark is available in Section A.6 in the Appendix.
363
         As shown in Figure 5, randomly initialized models can match the performance of pretrained models
364
         in ancestry prediction. Only the Mistral and GENA-LM pretrained models perform slightly better
365
         than their randomly initialized counterparts, with an F1 difference of 0.02. The best overall model is
366      Caduceus, achieving an F1 score of 0.71 for both its random and pretrained versions. Notably, even
367      the NT 500M model, specifically trained on data containing variants from 1000G, fails to outperform
368      its randomly initialized version.
369
370
         This can be attributed to the combination of two factors: the masked language modeling objective
         with a high masking probability (15%) and the k-mer tokenization strategy. The masking ratio far
371
         exceeds the natural mutation rate (0.5%), while the k-mer tokenizer, which processes sequences in
372
         chunks of 6 nucleotides, is poorly suited for capturing single nucleotide variations. Together, these
373      design choices make it difficult for the model to learn meaningful representations of genetic variants.
374

465
      Genomic Foundation Models. Encoder-only approaches have proven effective in sequence pre-
466   diction tasks, using k-mer tokenization (Ji et al., 2021; Dalla-Torre et al., 2023), Byte Pair Encod-
467   ing (Zhou et al., 2024; Sanabria et al., 2023), and learnable vector quantization codebooks (Li et al.,
468   2024) to enhance efficiency and manage longer sequences. Certain encoder architectures have been
469   enhanced with recurrent memory mechanisms (Fishman et al., 2023) to capture long-range depen-
470   dencies more effectively, while others utilize whole-genome alignments (Benegas et al., 2023) to
471   incorporate evolutionary context. More recent work has explored pan-genome graph representa-
472   tions (Zhang et al., 2024) to better capture genetic variation diversity.
473   Meanwhile, decoder-only architectures have shown potential by integrating structured state-space
474   models (Nguyen et al., 2023; Schiff et al., 2024), achieving competitive performance with mini-
475   mal parameters and supporting long context lengths. Hybrid architectures (Nguyen et al., 2024),
476   incorporating both attention and state-space blocks, have emerged, demonstrating great generative
477   capabilities spanning from molecular to genome scales. Our work introduces a GFM based on Mis-
478   tral architecture (Jiang et al., 2023) and performs performance analysis of the most recent GFMs.
479
      Genomic Foundation Models Analysis. It was shown that k-mer embeddings pretrained on ran-
480   dom DNA sequences can reach similar performance to those of trained on the real-world biological
481   data (Zhang et al., 2023). Another study found that character tokenization outperforms other meth-
482   ods in state-space models (Lindsey et al., 2024). Evaluation of GFMs across the BEND benchmark
483   reveals that they capture limited information on long-range features (Marin et al., 2023). It was also
484   shown that mean pooling improves performance of GFMs for genomic sequence classifications and
485   closes the performance gap between them (Feng et al., 2024). Pretrained DNA models were bench-
      marked (Tang et al., 2024) showing they do not offer great advantage over conventional machine

487   more models and also shows that randomly initialized models can be better as feature extractors.

489   5   D ISCUSSION
490
491   5.1 I NEFFECTIVENESS OF C URRENT GFM P RETRAINING M ETHODS
492   GFMs pretrained on vast amounts of data on many GPUs promise to revolutionize our understand-
493   ing of genomics. However, our study reveals a surprising reality: despite their name, current GFMs
494   lack substantial ”foundation” in genomic understanding. Across multiple benchmarks, we consis-
495   tently found that randomly initialized models perform competitively and sometimes outperform,
496   their pretrained counterparts. The competitive performance of randomly initialized models suggests
497
      that current pretraining methods for GFMs are ineffective.
498   This finding is particularly significant given the substantial computational resources and associated
499   costs required for model pretraining, often involving weeks of processing on many high-performance
500   GPUs (Dalla-Torre et al., 2023). Instead, the pretraining process seems to function merely as a
501   sophisticated, yet resource-intensive, weight initialization technique.
502
      We also assessed the performance of GFMs on one of the most critical set of tasks in genomics
503   which try to understand the impact of mutations in various applications. Many tasks in this do-
504   main require processing long sequences while maintaining sensitivity to single nucleotide changes.
505   Our experiments in Section 3.3 demonstrate insensitivity of current GFMs to these crucial genetic
506   variations. This limitation persists regardless of the tokenization and pooling methods used.
507
      It is important to clarify that we do not claim that current pretraining methods for GFMs are with-
508
      out any merit at all. They still may offer advantage in specific contexts, such as generative tasks
509   (Nguyen et al., 2024). However, our research shows that the current pretraining approaches do not
510   yet realize the potential of creating truly generalist models with broad applicability. The hallmark of
511   foundation models, as demonstrated by GPT and BERT in NLP, is their ability to generalize across
512   a wide spectrum of applications. Our results show that there is room for improvement in genomic
513   pretraining strategies to better capture this essence of versatility.
514
515
      5.2 A LTERNATIVE A PPROACH TO F OUNDATION M ODELS
516   Collectively our findings indicate that simply scaling up the pretraining further is unlikely to yield
517   significant benefits in genomic understanding. An alternative approach would be to focus on solv-
518   ing fundamental problems with specialized architectures. One such example is AlphaFold’s break-
519
      through in protein structure prediction (Jumper et al., 2021). Rather than aiming to be a foundational
      model with broad scope of application, AlphaFold targeted a specific yet fundamental challenge in
520
      structural biology. Its success can be attributed to the development of a protein-specific architec-
521
      ture, the availability of high-quality data (Berman et al., 2007), and the existence of the universally
522   respected CASP competition, which provided a clear metric for optimization (Moult et al., 2020).
523
524   Importantly, while AlphaFold focused on a specific problem, its success had far-reaching implica-
525   tions. It drew significant attention to the field and catalyzed numerous other impactful projects.
526
      For instance, AlphaMissense (Cheng et al., 2023), a state-of-the-art method for predicting protein
      variant pathogenicity, was built upon AlphaFold. This illustrates how solving a core problem can
527
      naturally lead to a model becoming foundational through its wide-ranging applications.
528
529
      6   C ONCLUSION
530
531   We conducted a comprehensive evaluation of Genomic Foundation Models by comparing pretrained
532   models with their randomly initialized counterparts across multiple benchmarks. Our experiments
533   reveal that randomly initialized models can perform competitively with, and sometimes surpass,
534   their pretrained versions in both finetuning and feature extraction tasks.
535   Additionally, our analysis of genomic variation tasks demonstrated that current GFMs lack sensi-
536   tivity to mutations, limiting their utility in many clinical applications. These findings challenge the
537   effectiveness of current pretraining approaches in genomics and suggest that the substantial compu-
538   tational resources invested in pretraining may not yield proportional benefits. We hope our analysis
539   will encourage the development of more effective approaches for creating genomic models that truly
      capture the complexities of biological sequences.

704   A.1 M ODELS
705   • HyenaDNA (Nguyen et al., 2023): Decoder-only state-space model with 450K parameters. Uses
706     character tokenizer and was pretrained on the Human Reference Genome with a 1024 base pair
707     sequence length.
708   • Caduceus (Schiff et al., 2024): Decoder-only model with 8M parameters. Trained on sequences of
709     131k base pairs on HRG. Combines a bidirectionally equivariant decoder with character tokenizer.
710
711
      • Mistral (our version): Decoder-only transformer model with 500M parameters. Uses character
        tokenization and was trained on the 1000 Genomes dataset (Consortium et al., 2015). Details for
712
        pretraining are provided in Section A.3 in Appendix.
713
714   • Nucleotide Transformer (Dalla-Torre et al., 2023): Encoder-only model presented in two ver-
715     sions: a 500M parameter model trained on the 1000 Genomes Project data and its v2 with 50M
716     parameter model trained on multispecies data. Both use k-mer tokenization.
717   • GENA-LM (Fishman et al., 2023): Encoder-only model with 110M parameters. Employs BPE
718     tokenizer and was pretrained on the HRG with 1000G augmentations.
719
      • DNABERTv2 (Zhou et al., 2024): Encoder-only model with 117M parameters. Uses BPE tok-
720
        enization and was trained on multispecies data.

722   A.2 R ANDOM W EIGHT I NITIALIZATION
723   We initialized the model weights following a procedure using standard Hugging Face Transformers
724   library initialization methods:
725
      • For linear layers: Weights were initialized from a normal distribution N (0, 0.02), biases were
726
        initialized to zero.
727
728   • For LayerNorm: The scaling factor (gamma) was initialized to 1. The bias term (beta) was initial-
729     ized to 0.
730   • For Embedding Layers: Embeddings were initialized from the same normal distribution
731     N (0, 0.02).
732
        For Caduceus and HyenaDNA we performed prenorm residual rescaling, which is the default
733
        weight initialization procedure for these models. Biases for linear layers were initialized as zeros.

      We use the following datasets for finetuning:

        transcription factor prediction, splice site detection, etc. with sequence lengths ranging from 70
766
        to 1000 base pairs.
767
768   More details about the benchmarks can be found the corresponding original papers.
769   We finetune random and pretrained initializations of the chosen model using the configuration pro-
770   vided in Table 6. We found that final result was quite sensitive to learning rate, so we conducted a
771   learning rate sweep over six different values and reported the highest result. Each task was run on 3
772   different folds, and the results were averaged. We used a validation holdout set for model selection
773   and reported test scores for the epoch that corresponded to the highest score on validation set.
774
      In our preliminary experiments, we found that max pooling performed better than cls / last pooling

      for randomly initialized models while maintaining performance for pretrained, so we used max
