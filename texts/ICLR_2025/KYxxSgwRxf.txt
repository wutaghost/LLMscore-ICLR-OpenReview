009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Zero-Shot Video Anomaly Detection (ZS-VAD) is an urgent task in scenarios
012            where the target video domain lacks training data due to various concerns, e.g.,
013            data privacy. The skeleton-based approach is a promising way to achieve ZS-
014            VAD as it eliminates domain disparities in both background and human appear-
015            ance. However, existing methods only learn low-level skeleton representation and
016            rely on the domain-specific normality boundary, which cannot generalize well to
017            new scenes with different normal and abnormal behavior patterns. In this paper,
018
               we propose a novel skeleton-based zero-shot video anomaly detection framework,
               which captures both scene-generic typical anomalies and scene-adaptive unique
019
               anomalies. Firstly, we introduce a language-guided typicality modeling module
020
               that projects skeleton snippets into action semantic space and learns generalizable
021            typical distributions of normal and abnormal behavior. Secondly, we propose a
022            test-time context uniqueness analysis module to finely analyze the spatio-temporal
023            differences between skeleton snippets and then derive scene-adaptive boundaries.
024            Without using any training samples from the target domain, our method achieves
025            state-of-the-art results on four large-scale VAD datasets: ShanghaiTech, UBnor-
026            mal, NWPU, and UCF-Crime. The Code will be publicly available.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Video Anomaly Detection (VAD) aims to temporally locate abnormal events, which has wide appli-
032   cations in the context of video surveillance and public safety (Liu et al., 2018; Sultani et al., 2018).
033
      Current mainstream paradigms include one-class (Wang et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2021; Hirschorn &
      Avidan, 2023) and weakly supervised methods (Sultani et al., 2018; Cho et al., 2023; Shi et al.,
034
      2023), which require samples from the target video domain for training. However, in surveillance
035
      scenarios involving privacy or newly installed monitoring devices, training samples from the tar-
036   get domain are often not available. Therefore, designing a Zero-Shot Video Anomaly Detection
037   (ZS-VAD) method is necessary, as shown in Fig. 1 (a).
038
039
      Despite the success of zero-shot anomaly detection in image domain (Zhou et al., 2024; Cao et al.,
      2024), only a few works (Aich et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2024) have ventured into the more complex
040
      video domain with underwhelming performance. Although recently (Zanella et al., 2024) proposes
041
      to leverage large visual language models to tackle the ZS-VAD task, it relies on multi-stage reason-
042   ing and the coordination of multiple large models, posing challenges for widespread deployment.
043   Compared with them, we find skeleton-based method (Hirschorn & Avidan, 2023; Flaborea et al.,
044   2023) is a promising way to achieve ZS-VAD as they are computation-friendly and can exclude the
045   domain gap in human appearance and background. Existing methods use skeleton prediction, recon-
046   struction, or coordinate-based normalizing flow to learn the normal skeleton distribution. However,
047   they can only learn low-level spatio-temporal representation and rely on domain-specific normality
048   boundaries, which cannot generalize well to new scenes with different normal and abnormal pat-
049   terns. Within this line of work, ZS-VAD seems to become an ill-posed problem, because the new
050
      scene’s decision boundary is unattainable, as illustrated in Fig. 1 (b). This limitation leads to a
      question: “Can we design a model that can generalize to various target scenes with generalizable
051
      representation learning and prior injection?”
052

078   Based on these complementary priors, we propose a novel skeleton-based zero-shot video anomaly
079
      detection framework, which captures both typical anomalies guided by language prior and unique
      anomalies in spatio-temporal contexts. Firstly, we introduce a language-guided typicality model-
080
      ing module that projects skeleton snippets into a semantic space and learns typical distributions
081
      of normal and abnormal behavior. To obtain the semantic representations of skeleton snippets, we
082   align skeleton snippets with CLIP’s language embeddings using external skeleton action recognition
083   datasets. Furthermore, by leveraging human experiential typicality labels obtained from language
084   model, we train a feature normalization flow to learn domain-general boundaries between normal
085   and abnormal behavior. Secondly, we propose a context uniqueness analysis module at test time
086   to derive scene-adaptive boundaries. To gain a fine-grained understanding of the scene context, we
087   construct two types of spatio-temporal context graphs: a cross-person graph and a self-inspection
088   graph. The uniqueness scores are derived within these graphs, indicating whether the current skele-
089   ton is unique in the surroundings or if there is a sudden change in the motion state. Without using
090
      any training samples from the target domain, we achieved state-of-the-art results on four large-scale
      VAD datasets: ShanghaiTech (Liu et al., 2018), UBnormal (Acsintoae et al., 2022), NWPU (Cao
091
      et al., 2023), UCF-Crime (Sultani et al., 2018). Our contributions are as follows:

104   2     R ELATED W ORK
105
106   Video anomaly detection. Most previous video anomaly detection studies can be grouped into
107   frame-based (Liu et al., 2018; 2021; Sultani et al., 2018), object-centric (Wang et al., 2022; Sun &
      Gong, 2023; Micorek et al., 2024), and skeleton-based methods (Markovitz et al., 2020; Morais


                                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      et al., 2019; Flaborea et al., 2023; Hirschorn & Avidan, 2023). In this work, we focus on the
109   skeleton-based methods, which detect anomalies in human activity based on preprocessed skele-
110   ton/pose snippets. Morais et al. (Morais et al., 2019) propose an anomaly detection method that uses
111   RNN to learn the representation of pose snippets, with prediction errors serving as anomaly scores.
112   GEPC (Markovitz et al., 2020) utilizes autoencoders to learn pose graph embeddings, generates soft
113   assignments through clustering, and uses Dirichlet process mixture to determine anomaly scores. To
114   model normal diversity, MoCoDAD (Flaborea et al., 2023) leverages diffusion probabilistic mod-
115   els to generate multimodal future human poses. STG-NF (Hirschorn & Avidan, 2023) proposes a
116   simple yet effective method by establishing normalized flow from normal pose snippets to obtain
117
      normal boundaries. However, these methods rely on training with normal data from the target do-
      main, while overlooking the semantic understanding of human behavior, which makes it difficult to
118
      ensure performance when the data is unavailable.
119
120   Zero-shot anomaly detection. Thanks to the development of vision-language models, zero-shot
121   anomaly detection has received a lot of attention (Jeong et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Gu et al., 2024;
122   Aota et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024), especially in the field
123
      of image anomaly detection (Miyai et al., 2024). The pioneering work is WinCLIP (Jeong et al.,
      2023), which utilizes CLIP (Radford et al., 2021)’s image-text matching capability to distinguish
124
      between unseen normal and abnormal anomalies. Building on that, AnomalyCLIP (Zhou et al.,
125
      2024) proposes to learn object-agnostic text prompts that capture generic normal and abnormal
126   patterns in an image. AdaCLIP (Cao et al., 2024) introduces two types of learnable prompts to
127   enhance CLIP’s generalization ability for anomaly detection data. Despite the success in the image
128   domain, only a few works (Aich et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2024) have ventured into zero-shot video
129   anomaly detection with underwhelming performance. Although recently (Zanella et al., 2024)
130   proposes to leverage large visual language models for zero-shot video anomaly detection, it requires
131   multi-stage reasoning and the collaboration of multiple large models, making it less user-friendly.
132   We aim to develop a lightweight, user-friendly, and easily deployable zero-shot anomaly detector
133   starting from skeleton data. Our work shares some similarities with a recent study (Sato et al.,
134
      2023). However, we emphasize that our approach differs significantly from (Sato et al., 2023) in
      the following ways: 1) Different tasks: (Sato et al., 2023) addresses abnormal action recognition,
135
      involving no more than two individuals in a short video, without the need for temporally localizing
136
      abnormal events. 2) Novel perspective: We combine the action typicality and uniqueness priors to
137   address zero-shot anomaly detection challenges in video surveillance scenarios.

142   3.1   OVERVIEW
143
      The objective of ZS-VAD is to train one model that can generalize to diverse target domains. For-
144
      mally, let V train be a training set from source video domain and {W1test , W2test , ..., WN
                                                                                                 test
                                                                                                      } be mul-
145
      tiple test sets from target video domain. The test videos are annotated at the frame level with labels
146   li ∈ {0, 1}, and the VAD model is required to predict each frame’s anomaly score. In this work,
147   we focus on the skeleton-based paradigm, as it is computation-friendly and can benefits ZS-VAD by
148   reducing the domain gap in both background and appearance.
149
      Fig. 2 overviews our proposed approach. Our model tackles the ZS-VAD problem from the perspec-
150
      tive of action typicality and uniqueness learning. First, the Language-Guided Typicality Modeling
151
      module projects skeleton snippets into a semantic space and effectively learns the typical distribu-
152   tion of normal and abnormal behavior based on experiential knowledge from the language model.
153   Second, the Test-Time Uniqueness Analysis module finely analyzes the spatio-temporal differences
154   between skeleton snippets and derives scene-adaptive boundaries. During inference on unseen VAD
155   datasets, our model integrates typicality scores and uniqueness scores of human behavior to provide
156   a holistic understanding of anomalies.

187   Skeleton-text alignment. For achieving a generalizable semantic understanding of human behav-
188   ior, we first propose to align the skeleton snippets with the corresponding semantic labels. For
189   such skeleton-text pairs, we utilize external action recognition datasets (e.g., Kinect (Carreira &
190   Zisserman, 2017) as the training set instead of specific VAD datasets (e.g., ShanghaiTech). The raw
191   skeleton data of an action video is typically formally represented as Xi ∈ RC×J×L×M , where C
192   is the coordinate number, J is the joint number, L is the sequence length, and M is the pre-defined
193   maximum number of persons, respectively. In addition, each video is annotated with a text label gi
194
      representing the action class, which can be also transformed into a one-hot class vector yi .
195   Compared to action recognition tasks that only predict video-level categories, the VAD task is more
196   fine-grained, focusing on predicting frame-level anomaly scores. Therefore, we decompose the
197   original sequences into multiple short skeleton snippets Ai ∈ RC×J×T using a sliding window, and
198   discard snippets that are predominantly composed of zeros. For the snippets from the same action
199   video, they share the same labels and undergo a normalization operation to make different snippets
200
      independent. Inspired by the recent multimodel alignment works (Wang et al., 2021; Xiang et al.,
      2023), we then perform a skeleton-text alignment pretraining procedure to learn the discriminative
201
      representation. The procedure is built with a skeleton encoder E s and a text encoder E t , for gen-
202
      erating skeleton features Fs and text features Ft , respectively. Additionally, the skeleton encoder
203   also predicts a probability vector ŷi using a fully-connected layer. The training loss consists of a
204   KL divergence loss and a cross-entropy classification loss, which are presented as follows:

359   We conduct a comprehensive comparison of the performance of ZS-VAD, comparing the frame-
360   based/object-based (Liu et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022), skeleton-based (Flaborea et al., 2023;

364   shown in Tab. 1, their generalization capabilities on new scene datasets are relatively poor due to the

372   full-shot setting, where target domain data is used for training. Table 2 shows that our zero-shot
373   approach can achieve comparable or even superior results to SOTA full-shot performance.
374   Comparison with LVLM-based methods. While LAVAD (Zanella et al., 2024) recently proposes
375   leveraging large visual language models for ZS-VAD, it relies on multi-stage reasoning and the
376   coordination of multiple large models with over 13 billion (B) parameters, posing challenges for
377   widespread deployment. In contrast, we develop a lightweight zero-shot anomaly detector with a
      mere 5.0 million (M) parameters, just one in two thousand of LAVAD’s parameters.

      4.3      A BLATION STUDY
404
405
      Ablation of typicality module. We conduct ablation experiments on the typicality modeling module
      with the following settings: (a) removing the aligning stage, training with raw skeleton data and
406
      the typicality labels; (b) removing the collection phase, training with VAD source data (SHT); (c)
407
      removing the normalizing flow and calculating typicality scores using k-nearest neighbors distance
408   techniques. As shown in Table 3, the model shows poor performance without the aligning stage, as
409   it fails to learn generalizable and discriminative semantic representations. Moreover, performance
410   deteriorates without the selection of typicality knowledge, as the model can only learn a limited
411   normality boundary from VAD source data. Furthermore, without the normalizing flow, the model
412   also loses flexibility in modeling the distribution of typical behaviors.
413
      Comparison with prompt-based methods. Since prompt-based techniques have been popular in
414   other zero-shot tasks (Sato et al., 2023; Jeong et al., 2023), we conduct experiments to compare our
415   typicality module with theirs. To this end, we design typical normal prompts (NP), typical abnormal
416   prompts (AP), and the ensemble (NAP), then using the skeleton-prompt similarity as the anomaly
417   score. More details can be found in the appendix. As shown in Table 3 (d) and Fig. 3 (a), the
418   results are suboptimal. Unlike various forms of text seen in CLIP image-text alignment, the current
419   skeleton-text alignment scheme has only encountered text of action class names, thus the alignment
420   capability for prompt text is relatively weak. Our method, on the other hand, leverages LLM to
421   collect typicality labels, avoiding directly using the skeleton-prompt similarity as anomaly scores.
422   Ablation of uniqueness module. We ablate the uniqueness scores and the holistic scores in this
423   part. As demonstrated in Table 4, when only using the cross-person distance, the model can identify
424   contextual anomalies with acceptable performance. When combined with the self-inspection score,
425   the model can spot changes in motion states, aiding in detecting a wider range of anomalies. The
426   reason for the suboptimal performance of uniqueness score on UBnormal is that UBnormal is a syn-
427   thetic dataset where some videos contain only one person with relatively short movement durations,
428
      which does not align well with real surveillance video scenarios. By integrating both the typicality
      and uniqueness modules, our approach can achieve optimal performance.
429
430   Hyper-parameter sensitivity. We ablate the hyper-parameters in the typicality knowledge selec-
431   tion step. As shown in Fig. 3 (b)-(c), the optimal value of β n is 90%, and a smaller β a can enhance
      performance by filtering out noisy data and normal snippets within the anomalous sequences.
