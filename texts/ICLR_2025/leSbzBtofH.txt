011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            We introduce AutoAdvExBench, a benchmark to evaluate if large language mod-
014            els (LLMs) can autonomously exploit defenses to adversarial examples. We be-
015
               lieve our benchmark will be valuable to several distinct audiences. First, it mea-
               sures if models can match the abilities of expert adversarial machine learning re-
016
               searchers. Second, it serves as a challenging evaluation for reasoning capabili-
017
               ties that can measure LLMs’ ability to understand and interact with sophisticated
018            codebases. And third, since many adversarial examples defenses have been bro-
019            ken in the past, this benchmark allows for evaluating the ability of LLMs to re-
020            produce prior research results automatically. We then benchmark the ability of
021            current LLMs to solve this benchmark, and find most are unable to succeed. Our
022            strongest agent, with a human-guided prompt, is only able to successfully generate
023            adversarial examples on 6 of the 51 defenses in our benchmark. This benchmark
024            is publicly accessible at redacted for review.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Language models are traditionally evaluated on knowledge-based tasks like MMLU (Hendrycks
029
      et al., 2020) and reasoning tasks like GPQA (Rein et al., 2023). However, state-of-the-art models
030   have outgrown the usefulness of many of these benchmarks, as they now exhibit capabilities beyond
031   text understanding that require novel benchmarks (Jimenez et al., 2023). For example, language
032   models can now be used as agents that interact with an environment, plan their actions, test their
033   own outputs and refine their responses independently (Yang et al., 2024; Yao et al., 2022).
034
      These advanced capabilities drive the need for evaluating capabilities beyond simple reasoning tasks,
035
      and towards potential applications of these models, such as their ability to solve security-critical
036   tasks independently (e.g. penetration testing (Happe & Cito, 2023)). Towards this end, we introduce
037   AutoAdvExBench, a challenging but tractable benchmark for both AI security and AI agents. Au-
038   toAdvExBench evaluates the ability of large language models to autonomously generate exploits on
039   adversarial example defenses. Specifically, our benchmark consists of 51 defense implementations
040   from 37 papers published in the past decade, making it the largest collection of defenses ever studied
041   in one analysis. When solving this benchmark, we provide LLM agents with the paper detailing the
042   defense method and its corresponding implementation. The benchmark evaluates LLMs’ ability to
043   construct adversarial examples that bypass these defenses.
044   We believe AutoAdvExBench has broad interest beyond just measuring the security capabilities of
045   LLMs. For instance, it is a valuable benchmark for software engineering progress, as it evaluates
046   LLMs’ ability to reason over large, unstructured codebases. It also measures progress in research
047   automation and reproducibility, as most of these defenses have been exploited by researchers in the
048   past. Finally, it serves as a proxy to measure the growing concern of potential attacks mounted be-
049   tween competing LLM agents—whether intentional or not (Anwar et al., 2024). Since constructing
050
      adversarial examples for image classifiers is significantly simpler than jailbreaking language models,
      this task provides a lower bound for LLMs’ ability to exploit other AI systems.
051
052   Finally, we evaluate the efficacy of current state-of-the-art LLMs at solving our benchmark, and find
053   that AutoAdvExBench is (at present) challenging. In the best configuration, a human-guided agentic
      LLM only generates adversarial examples for 11% of the defenses.

      2     BACKGROUND
083
084
      2.1     L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODEL E VALUATIONS
085
086   Benchmarking language models is a challenging task for many reasons. Unlike classical machine
087   learning tasks that measure the accuracy of some classifier on a specific test set, language models
088   are meant to be “general purpose”. This means that there is often a difference between the training
089   objective (reduce loss when predicting the next token), and testing objective (“be helpful”).
090
      As a result, LLMs are often benchmarked on generic tasks that serve as a proxy for overall model
091   capabilities. Yet, the rapid advancement of LLM capabilities makes it difficult to design bench-
092   marks that stand the test-of-time. Early language understanding evaluations such as GLUE (Wang,
093   2018) and SuperGLUE (Wang et al., 2019), were effectively solved within a year of their intro-
094   duction (Raffel et al., 2020; Chowdhery et al., 2022). Similarly, MMLU (a collection of multiple-
095   choice questions (Hendrycks et al., 2020)) has seen performance increased from 43% (marginally
096   above random guessing) to 90% (surpassing human performance) in just three years (OpenAI). Even
097   datasets specifically designed to address these challenges and evaluate more advanced knowledge,
098   such as GPQA (Rein et al., 2023), have progressed remarkably quickly. In November 2023, GPT-
099
      4 achieved a (then) state-of-the-art accuracy of 39% on GPQA. Less than a year later, OpenAI’s
      o1-preview model reached 77% accuracy, outperforming human domain experts (OpenAI).
100
101   To make matters worse, since LLMs are trained on a large fraction of the public Internet, it is chal-
102   lenging to distinguish performance gains due to improved capabilities from unintentional leakage of
103   benchmarks into a model’s training set (Deng et al., 2023a; Golchin & Surdeanu, 2023).
104
105   Agentic benchmarks. For all of these reasons, recent benchmarks have shifted focus from evalu-
106   ating models on specific (often multiple-choice) questions to measuring their ability to solve open-
107   ended tasks like software engineering. For example, SWE-Bench (Jimenez et al., 2023) measures
      a model’s ability to independently update a codebase to solve GitHub issues; CORE-Bench (Siegel

      et al., 2024) measures the ability of a model to reproduce research code; AgentBench (Liu et al.,
109   2023) benchmarks how agentic LLMs perform in a suite of environments that range from an OS
110   to a digital card game. WebArena (Zhou et al., 2023) evaluates models’ interactions with realistic
111   websites to complete tasks; and AgentDojo (Debenedetti et al., 2024) benchmarks whether models
112   can solve complex tasks in realistic adversarial environments (e.g. handling an e-mail client).
113
114   Security benchmarks. Although there are several recent benchmarks for open-ended security
115   tasks (Deng et al., 2023b; Shao et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Fang et al., 2024; Bhatt et al.,
116   2024), these rely on simplified environments that have well-defined solutions, like capture-the-flag
117   challenges. These benchmarks simplify some of the common difficulties that LLMs will face when
118   interacting with real-world environments (e.g. poorly documented and written codebases) or when
119   reproducing research (e.g. relating details in academic papers to specific implementations).

121   2.2   A DVERSARIAL E XAMPLES D EFENSES
122
123   Our benchmark will focus on so-called adversarial examples. For an image classifier f , an adversar-
124   ial example is an image x belonging to a class y to which we added a carefully crafted perturbation
125
      δ (usually of `p norm bounded by some threshold  ) so that the classifier f misclassifies the image
      with a class ŷ 6= y. That is, f (x + δ) = ŷ.
126
127   A defense to adversarial examples is a classifier fˆ that is designed to correctly classify any image
128   x + δ. Most defenses follow one of three common approaches: 1) they are explicitly trained to
129   classify adversarial examples correctly (Madry et al., 2017; Papernot et al., 2015), 2) they employ
130   a separate classifier to detect whether an image is an adversarial example and reject it (Sitawarin &
131   Wagner, 2019; Xu et al., 2017), or 3) they apply some form of “purification” to the input image that
132   aims at removing the perturbation δ at inference time (Li & Li, 2017; Guo et al., 2017).
133

      3     AUTOA DV E X B ENCH
135
136
      Overview. AutoAdvExBench evaluates the ability of LLMs to automatically implement adversar-
137
      ial attack algorithms that break defenses designed to be robust to adversarial examples. The LLM
138   is provided a description of the defense (e.g., the paper that introduces it), an implementation of the
139   defense (e.g., from the original author’s code release, or a re-implementation), and must generate a
140   program that outputs adversarial examples that evade the defense.
141
142
      3.1   M OTIVATION
143
144   Before describing our benchmark in detail, we begin with a motivation for why we believe this
145   benchmark is worth constructing and analyzing.
146
147   Difficulty. Benchmarks should be appropriately difficult to warrant further study. We believe au-
148   tonomously breaking adversarial example defenses is of an appropriate difficulty level for current
149   models. This is because analyzing the robustness of adversarial example defenses is challenging
150   even for expert researchers. For example, over thirty peer-reviewed and published adversarial ex-
151   ample defenses have been shown to be ineffective under subsequent analysis (Carlini & Wagner,
152   2017a; Tramer et al., 2020; Croce et al., 2022; Carlini, 2020; 2023).
153   And yet breaking adversarial example defenses is typically viewed as much easier than breaking
154   “traditional” security systems, and within reach of many machine learning researchers. To illustrate,
155   the academic community typically does not see a break of any one individual defense as a “research
156   contribution”; instead, published attack research tends to identify new failure modes that break many
157   (e.g., eight Athalye et al. (2018), nine Croce et al. (2022), ten Carlini & Wagner (2017a), or thirteen
158   Tramer et al. (2020)) defenses at the same time. And so we believe that breaking adversarial example
159   defenses is a hard, but not intractably hard, challenge for language models today.
160
161   Security relevance. Our primary motivation for constructing this benchmark is to evaluate to what
      extent it may be possible to automate security tasks with LLMs. AutoAdvExBench measures LLMs’

201             advanced AI systems.
202
203   Smooth measure of capability advancements. A key advantage of our benchmark is its ability
204   to provide a more fine-grained measurement of success compared to many other security capability
205
      benchmarks. Most current benchmarks often rely on binary success or failure metrics, such as the
      number of vulnerabilities found or the number of challenges solved. In contrast, AutoAdvExBench
206
      offers a continuous measurement of the attack success rate for adversarial examples on each defense,
207
      ranging from 0% to 100%. This allows us to discern subtle differences in model capabilities, as the
208   benchmark can capture intermediate solutions and incremental improvements.

      3.2    D ESIGN M ETHODOLOGY
211
212   We aim to build the largest collection of adversarial examples defenses studied in a single research
213   paper. Towards that end, we begin by crawling (almost) all 612,495 papers uploaded to arXiv in
214   the past ten years, and training a simple Naive Bayes model to detect papers related to the topic of
215   adversarial machine learning. We filter this set of papers down by a factor of 60× to a collection of
      just over 10,000 papers potentially related to adversarial examples. From here, we reduce this list


                                                                4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      to a set of 1,652 papers (potentially) related to defending against adversarial examples, by few-shot
217   prompting GPT-4o. Here we aim to be conservative, and tolerate a (relatively high) false positive
218   rate, to ensure that we do not miss many defenses.
219
220
      We then extract the text of each of these papers, and filter out any papers that do not link to GitHub
      (or other popular code hosting repositories). We then manually filter these papers down to a set of
221
      211 papers that are certainly (a) defenses to adversarial examples with code available, and (b) are
222
      diverse from each other.
223
224   Choosing diverse defenses is an important step that requires some manual analysis. There are dozens
225   of variants of adversarial training (Madry et al., 2017) that differ only in particular details that are in-
226
      teresting from a training perspective, but which make no difference from an evaluation perspective.
      Therefore, it is highly likely that an attack on any one of these schemes would constitute an attack on
227
      any of the others—and so we aim to introduce only one (or a few) defenses of this type. However,
228
      in several cases, we have also included the same defense multiple times if there is a significantly
229   different version of that defense (e.g., implemented in a different framework or using very different
230   techniques).
231
232
      Finally, we then try to actually run each of these defense implementations. The vast majority do
      not reproduce after a few hours of manual effort.1 Most reproduction failures are due to the use of
233
      outdated libraries (e.g., TensorFlow version 0.11), missing documentation for how to train a new
234
      model, missing documentation on how to install dependencies, etc. Nevertheless, we are able to
235   identify a set of 37 papers that we could reproduce.
236
237   These papers correspond to 51 unique defense implementations. This number is larger than the
238
      number of papers primarily because many papers are implemented both by the original authors and
      also by other third-party researchers—in which case we include both—or because a single defense
239
      paper may propose multiple (different) defenses.
240
241   It is important to note that while our collection of defenses creates a diverse benchmark, the success
242   of an attack against any particular defense should not be interpreted as a definitive break of that
243   defense. Due to the practical constraints of our large-scale implementation, we may have chosen
244
      sub-optimal hyperparameters or implemented simplified versions of some defenses. Thus, while
      our results provide valuable insights for benchmarking purposes, they should not be considered as
245
      conclusive evidence against the efficacy of any specific defense method in its optimal form.

      3.3   L IMITATIONS
249
      Our dataset has several limitations that may make it an imperfect proxy for measuring LLM capa-
250   bilities. We feel it is important to be upfront with these limitations, so that the success (or failure) of
251   LLMs at solving our benchmark will not be generalized beyond what can be reasonably inferred.
252
253   Several of these defenses have published breaks. One potential limitation of AutoAdvExBench
254   is the risk of benchmark contamination. Since some of the defenses included in our dataset have
255   been previously broken in published papers, it is possible that a language model—which has been
256   pre-trained on a large fraction of the internet—has already seen the attack paper, or corresponding
257   attack code if it exists. In principle this could artificially inflate the success of a language model
258   agent on our dataset.
259   However, we do not believe this is a major concern at the moment for two reasons. First, the attack
260   success rate of even our best agent is very low, suggesting that even if benchmark contamination did
261   occur, it was not enough for the models to perform well on this task. Second, we found that even if
262   we explicitly place the previously-written attack paper in the language model’s context, the success
263   rate does not significantly improve. This indicates that the models are currently not sophisticated
264   enough to fully leverage such information, even when it is directly available.
265   Finally, while this dataset in particular may (in the future) become even more contaminated as others
266   break the defenses here, so too are new defenses being rapidly developed. This should, in principle,
267   allow us to create updated versions of our dataset that contains new defenses as they are published.
268

      Gradient-free optimization can break many defenses. It is often possible to break an adversarial
271   example defense through gradient-free optimization alone (Croce et al., 2020). This means for some
272   defenses it is not necessary to implement white-box attacks at all, which is the entire purpose of the
273   benchmark here. Nevertheless, white-box attacks often out-perform black-box attacks, and so in the
274   limit we believe this will not be a significant concern.
275
276   Research code is not representative of production code. There are two key reasons for this.
277   First, since research code is not designed to be used in a production environment, research code is
278   often significantly more “messy” (e.g., without a consistent style of structure) and less well docu-
279   mented. Therefore LLMs may find it more challenging to process this kind of code than they would
280
      with better-structured, well-commented production code. On the other hand, research code tends to
      be much smaller in scale. Unlike production code, which can span hundreds of thousands of lines,
281
      research projects are usually more concise, making it easier for models to work with.
282
283   Put differently, research code comes from a slightly different data distribution than the types of
284   code typically studied for security attacks. This makes it neither strictly harder nor easier to work
285   with. The smaller size of research code generally makes it easier, but its lack of structure and
286
      documentation can present added challenges.
287
288
      Adversarial examples attacks are not representative of common security exploits. Related to
      the prior consideration, another potential limitation of this dataset is that the distribution of attacks
289
      used in adversarial example evaluations is very different from the standard distribution of attacks
290
      commonly found on the internet (and in the wild). For example, there are likely thousands of
291   tutorials and examples online about web security exploits or memory corruption exploits. As a
292   result, models might be (much) better at performing these types of attacks, even if they struggle with
293   generating adversarial examples due to a lack of comparable educational resources online. However,
294   we do not see this as a significant consideration for two key reasons.
295
      First, when exploits are common and relatively easy to implement, it is unlikely that adversaries
296
      would need to use advanced language models for their development. For example, Metasploit
297   (Kennedy et al., 2011) already contains pre-built exploits for many common vulnerabilities out-of-
298   the-box. In such cases, leveraging a LLM adds little value since these tasks are already automated.
299
300
      And second, adversarial example evaluations test the ability of the model to generalize to new forms
      of attack, which allows us to assess the model’s “intelligence” and ability to “reason” about unfa-
301
      miliar problems, rather than simply its ability to recall prior attacks that have been well-documented
302
      on the Internet.

307   Unlike question answering benchmarks, where it is obvious2 how to evaluate utility on the bench-
308
      mark, there are many more degrees of freedom in evaluating accuracy for attacks on adversarial

309

310
311

317   turbations of the original images under a given perturbation bound. We choose an `∞ perturbation
318   bound of 8/255 for CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, and 0.3 for MNIST—standard values from the liter-
319   ature (Carlini et al., 2019). The model is allowed to perform any action it wants on these inputs to

      5   B ENCHMARKING C URRENT LLM S
369
370   The purpose of this paper is not to construct an agent that solves this benchmark. We believe
371   achieving this is a research result in and of itself, and is beyond what is possible with current LLMs.
372   Nevertheless, in order to establish a baseline for how well current LLMs are able to solve this
373   task, we perform a preliminary evaluation with some simple and common evaluation strategies. We
374
      believe it should be possible to improve these results by using more advanced agentic systems.
375   We evaluate LLMs in two ways: first, we evaluate their ability to “zero-shot” generate solutions
376   without tool use by providing the model the code as input and ask for an attack implementation; and
377   second, we evaluate their ability to generate solutions in a simple “agentic” framework, where we
      allow the model to iteratively fix bugs in its prior solutions.

      5.1   E ND - TO - END E VALUATION
392
      We begin by benchmarking current state-of-the-art models in a “zero-shot” approach, and evaluate
393   whether or not they are able to construct correct attacks in a single forward pass. We place in context
394   the source code for the defense, and prompt the model to write an adversarial attack that will break
395   the defense. We then run this code, and evaluate its success rate.
396
      Unsurprisingly, we find that current defenses fail completely at this task, and never successfully
397
      generate an adversarial attack. We therefore consider two alternate approaches which have, in the
398
      past, been found to be effective at increasing the success rate of code-generation agent systems.
399
400
401
      Pass@K. Instead of running the LLM a single time, one obvious method to improve performance
      is to run the model multiple times and report “success” if any of the attack attempts succeed. De-
402
      spite being a remarkably simple approach, in the past this approach has been a surprisingly simple
403
      technique to significantly increase the success rate (Li et al., 2022).
404
405   The challenge in many domains is, after generating K candidate solutions, how to pick the best one.
406   In coding tasks, for example, this is often done by picking the program that passes the most test cases.
407
      However, here we do not need any heuristic: because security is a worst-case property, we can run
      the attack as many times as we would like, evaluate the robustness of the defense under all attacks,
408
      and pick the most successful. Unfortunately, even by doing this and attempting 8 solutions at once,
409
      we observe a 0% attack success rate: the model never succeeds at generating even a single attack
410   function that runs without crashing and matches our input/output specification. (Implementing the
411   attacks in this way is also rather (rather) expensive, and increases the cost of an evaluation from 4
412   USD to 32 USD for no gain.)
413
414   Iterative debugging. Instead of simply generating 8 solutions and hoping that one of these will be
415   effective, we can approach the problem more intelligently, and allow the LLM to see what happens
416   when its code is executed, and provide a fix of any issues. We find that this debug loop gives, for
417   the first time, the model the ability to write a successful adversarial attack. While it is only effective
418   in two cases (for models that were designed as undefended baselines), even this limited progress
419   hints at the possibility that future models may be able to solve this benchmark with stronger attacks.
420   The cost of implementing this loop is somewhat expensive (costing 50 USD) in the case of GPT-4o
421   which does not support prompt caching, but with Claude 3.5-Sonnet’s prompt caching ability, this
422
      attack costs just 10 USD.

424   5.2   L ETS THINK STEP BY STEP
425
426   Given that an entirely end-to-end attack fails for almost all defenses, we now attempt to gain some
427   insight where the model gets stuck. To do this, we break down the task of constructing adversarial
428
      examples into four sub-tasks, and ask the agent to solve each task in sequence.
429   For each sub-task, we provide the agent with a clear objective and ask it to generate the code that
430   would accomplish this task. We then run the generated code and return the output to the agent,
431   allowing it to refine its implementation in the event of errors. As above, the agent is allowed up to
      ten iterations to correct any errors in the code.

445
446
447   Our process consists of four steps that directly mirror the process a human would take to break many
448   adversarial example defenses (Carlini et al., 2019).
449
450
            1. The first task is to implement a forward pass of the model. This means the agent must be
               able to receive an input image as a tensor, and output a probability distribution over the
451
               output classes. This step ensures that the agent can execute the code correctly as intended.
452
453         2. The second task asks the agent to convert this forward pass to a differentiable forward pass.
454            While in some cases this requires no additional work (if the defended model is already
455            differentiable), this is often the most challenging step of an adversarial attack. Gradient
456
               masking (Papernot et al., 2017) and obfuscation (Athalye et al., 2018) are the most com-
               mon reasons why papers make incorrect adversarial robustness claims. Many defenses,
457
               e.g., pre-process the image before classification, post-process the output, detect and reject
458
               adversarial examples, or modify the network architecture. Each of these requires care in
459            order to ensure that gradient-based attacks function correctly.
460
461         3. The third step is to use this differentiable function to perform a single iteration of the Fast
462
               Gradient Sign Method (FGSM) (Goodfellow et al., 2014)—a very simple attack that just
               takes a single step in the direction of the gradient. The goal of this step is to verify that the
463
               gradient direction is actually a useful attack direction.
464
465         4. The final step is to extend the single-step FGSM into a multi-step, iterative attack (Madry
466            et al., 2017; Carlini & Wagner, 2017b). It is the final output of this step that we return as
467            the resulting adversarial examples to evaluate robust accuracy.
468
469
      Sub-task results. Splitting out each of these tasks explicitly increases the attack success rate to six
      unique defenses successfully attacked, at a cost of 22 USD. Below we investigate how far agents
470
      progress in the four attack steps
471
472
            • Forward pass. Over half (58%) of defenses can be converted into a tensor-in-tensor-out
473
              format. We find that there are two reasons our agent often fails to make defenses implement
474           correct forward passes. The most common reason is that many defenses implement com-
475           plicated (pure-Python) modifications of the input and it is often challenging to convert this
476           into tensor-to-tensor operations. The second most common failure is due to the model’s
477           inability to load the model parameters from the correct file on disk; while we provide the
478           model with a correct forward pass, often there are dozens of configurations available and
479           the model is unable to select the right one.
480         • Gradients. A quarter (25.9%) of defenses can be made differentiable. When the model
481           successfully implements a forward pass but fails to construct a differentiable function, in
482           almost all cases this is due to the defense applying some Python pre-processing code that
483           is not easily made differentiable. While this might be expected to have been a failure in
484           making the function tensor-in-tensor-out, we find that often times the model “succeeds” at
485           the first step by accepting a tensor as input, converting it back to a Python object, operating
              on the Python object, and then converting back to a tensor output.

496   6   C ONCLUSION
497
498   Current language models do not have the capability of autonomously breaking most adversarial ex-
499   ample defenses. While they can succeed for the simplest possible defense approaches when imple-
500
      mented in the simplest possible way, current models fail to generate successful attacks on complex
      defenses, even when given a human-written 4-step process that walks the model through how to
501
      break most defenses.
502
503   In almost all cases, current models fail at even very early steps necessary to break defenses. Specif-
504   ically, aggregated across all models and attack approaches, models were only able to implement a
505   differentiable forward pass in 23% of cases—a necessary prerequisite before any “attacking” can
506
      even begin.
507   But this is exactly why we believe this benchmark is interesting. As mentioned earlier, existing
508   benchmarks largely side-step the fact that real-world code is difficult to understand, challenging
509   to modify, and often is only designed for one specific purpose (which is not amenable to security
510   evaluation). Turning this original code artifact into something that can be reasonably studied requires
511   significant effort, and current models fail at solving this step of the attack.
512   We hope that it will be some time before automated methods are able to effectively solve this task,
513   but the rate of progress in LLMs has been surprisingly rapid; and so we believe constructing chal-
514   lenging benchmarks such as this one is important. We do not believe an agent that could solve
515   this task is likely to cause any immediate harm (because humans can already break many of these
516   defenses, and these attacks have not caused any harm yet).
517   In the future it may be interesting to extend this style of evaluation to domains beyond image ad-
518   versarial examples. One promising direction could be to study defenses to jailbreak attacks. But
519   at present, compared to the decade of research and hundreds of papers on defending against im-
520   age adversarial examples, there are relatively few papers that focus on defending against jailbreak
521   attacks.
522   Overall, we believe it is valuable to benchmark potentially dangerous capabilities in ways that
523   closely mirror what actual attackers would have to implement. Such end-to-end evaluations that
524   directly measure the ability of models to cause damage (instead of through some proxy metric) can
525   help serve as a potential warning sign that models possess dangerous capabilities.

777   Identifying gradient masking. One of the simplest defenses in our benchmark is a reproduction
778   of defensive distillation (Papernot et al., 2015), which appears to be robust due to gradient masking
779   While the model is unable to break this defense end-to-end, when we break down the attack into
780   four steps, the model succeeds.
781   The difficulty of this break occurs at the second step, when it must handle the fact that the gradients
782   after the softmax operator are identically zero. Initially, the model identifies this flaw, and makes a
783   correction:
784
785     It removes the softmax layer from the output to return logits instead of probabilities, which is
786     useful for computing gradients.
787
788   However it does so incorrectly, and returns logits[:-1], which is a modification of the logit
789   array, and not a modification to the number of layers. When this code is executed and the model is
790   given the response to analyze, the model corrects itself.
791
792     The return logits[:-1] line in the forward method is incorrectly slicing the output, removing all
793     elements instead of just the last layer.
794
795   It then attempts to remove the softmax layer a different way, but fails:
796
797     The DifferentiableModel is now correctly instantiated and producing output. The output of
798     self.convnet(xs) is a tensor with shape [1, 10], which is the expected shape for logits in a 10-
799     class classification problem. The output tensor has a gradfn=<SoftmaxBackward0>, indicating
800
        that a softmax operation is being applied at the end of the network. The gradient test is still
        failing because the gradients are zero.
801
802
803   On its third attempt, the model again fails to correct the issue, this time because it incorrectly slices
804   the a torch.ModuleList, but it is able to correct this issue:
805
806     I think the most likely reason is the first one: self.convnet.layers is a ModuleList, which doesn’t
807
        have a forward method. To fix this, we need to modify our forward method to properly use the
        layers in the ModuleList.

      Failing to implement a differentiable forward pass. In one case, we observed the model fail to
811   implement a differentiable forward pass correctly after 7 rounds of debugging. Eventually the agent
812   “gave up”, and responded with the following
813
814     This implementation does the following:
815     - We add assertions to check the input tensor’s type, shape, and value range.
816     - We create a dummy variable with ‘requires grad=True‘ to enable gradient computation.
817     - We perform the forward pass through the ‘TensorModel‘.
818     - We add the dummy variable (multiplied by 0) to the output to make it differentiable.
819
820     This implementation allows gradients to flow through the model, even though the underlying
821
        ‘TensorModel‘ is not inherently differentiable. The dummy variable trick enables PyTorch’s
        autograd to compute gradients with respect to the input.
822

825   ally resorts to “cheating” its way through the test cases. (Of course, after cheating at one step, the

      next step becomes functionally impossible.)
