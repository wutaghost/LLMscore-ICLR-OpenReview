008                                              A BSTRACT
009
010             We tackle the problem of solving shape puzzles, that is, reassembling randomly-
011             partitioned and scattered pieces of 2D or 3D shapes into an original shape. This
012
                task is challenging since it only relies on geometric features without rich visual
                information. Specifically, we are supposed that target shapes and their randomly-
013
                partitioned pieces are pattern-free and irregular. Existing methods tend to rely
014
                on specific constraints regarding piece shapes and neglect the consideration of
015             invariance and equivariance. We propose learning a robust puzzle solver through
016             a generative diffusion process in which the roto-translational equivariance holds.
017             Experiments on 2D and 3D puzzle benchmarks including the Breaking Bad dataset
018             demonstrate that our method successfully assembles given geometric pieces into
019             a target shape. We also provide in-depth ablation studies showing the effects of
020             our equivariant design and the components in our proposed framework.
021

023   1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025   The task of shape puzzles aims to assemble given parts into a complete and assembled shape. In
026   particular, we solve pattern-free and irregular shape puzzles by assuming the unavailability of visual
027   and semantic information, unlike semantic shape assembly approaches (Li et al., 2020a;b; Lee et al.,
028
      2021; Wang et al., 2022). More specifically, the problem solved in this work more focuses on the
      geometric information of each shape rather than semantic information.
029
030   As discussed in the previous work (Hosseini et al., 2023) on a 2D geometric puzzle dataset, there
031   are a few inherent characteristics of simple puzzles, making shape puzzles a highly suitable task
032   for exploring the potential of geometric features. Firstly, the puzzle dataset is built with a minimal
033   number of points defining each shape, simplifying a solution space. Secondly, each puzzle can
034
      possess explicit geometric constraints such as edge matching constraint. Lastly, it is easy to control
      the puzzle’s constraints, one can adjust the level of generality within the task.
035
036   Recognizing the potential of solving the 2D puzzle dataset, our work first proposes the creation
037   of a generic 3D puzzle dataset, and then showcases a novel puzzle solver based on a diffusion
038   model-based conditional generation framework for 2D and 3D puzzles including the Breaking Bad
039   dataset (Sellán et al., 2022). Inspired by the existing studies on complex representations and SE(3)
040
      equivariant network such as the work (Deng et al., 2021), our approach demonstrates performance
      improvements by integrating simple yet effective representations that satisfy permutation equivari-
041
      ance, SE(2)/SE(3) invariance, and potentially SE(2)/SE(3) equivariance. This design is theoretically
042
      and empirically validated with significant performance gains in both 2D and 3D puzzle tasks.
043
044   In summary, our contributions are as follows.
045
046
          • We design a dataset generating framework which allows direct adjustment of piece number and
            shape complexity, and propose a new 3D puzzle dataset.
047
048       • We propose a novel diffusion-based framework which solves puzzles on 2D and 3D domains.
049         To the best of our knowledge, this is the first approach to solving a 3D assembly task via the
050         coniditional equivariant diffusion model.
051       • We demonstrate theoretical results and performance improvements with ablation studies prov-
052         ing the effectiveness of each component included in our framework.
053
      We will make our implementation public upon publication.

      Shape and Puzzle Assembly Problems. Shape assembly is an active research area in computer
060
      vision and graphics. It is to construct complete shapes from given parts. Previous research often
061   utilizes public datasets such as PartNet (Mo et al., 2019) and IKEA-Manual (Wang et al., 2022),
062   which provide detailed semantic part information. Thus, they can be categorized into semantic shape
063   assembly. The recent studies (Li et al., 2020a;b; Lee et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022) have addressed
064   assembly from single images or focused on specific applications such as furniture assembly.
065
      While many prior approaches heavily rely on semantic part information, the work (Cho et al., 2010;
066
      Chen et al., 2022) has tackled an assembly problem by prioritizing shape geometries over seman-
067   tic details. Also, the Breaking Bad dataset (Sellán et al., 2022) has introduced challenges in as-
068   sembling non-semantic fragments into complete shapes, highlighting the complexity of fractured
069   shape reassembly. On the other hand, there is a line of research on shape assembly with LEGO
070   bricks (Thompson et al., 2020; Kim et al., 2020; Chung et al., 2021), where each brick is seman-
071   tically meaningless. Moreover, the work (Lee et al., 2022) tackles the problem of 2D sequential
072   puzzles with fixed target shapes using the Transformer-based network.
073
074   SE(2)/SE(3) Invariance and Equivariance. The special Euclidean groups in two and three di-
075   mensions (denoted as the SE(2) and SE(3) groups, respectively) represent a set of all possible com-
076   binations of rotation and translation in 2D and 3D spaces. For the sake of brevity, we only describe
077   the details of the SE(3) group in this section; the SE(3) group can readily be reduced to the SE(2)
078
      group. Each instance of the SE(3) group is represented by a tuple of a rotation matrix R ∈ R3×3 and
      a translation vector t ∈ R3 , i.e., (R, t). Specifically, R indicates an orientation change in 3D space
079
      where this matrix is orthogonal; R⊤ R = I, and has a determinant of 1, ensuring that it represents
080
      pure rotation without reflection. Moreover, t indicates a position shift in 3D space where it specifies
081   how much an object moves along x, y, and z directions. Formerly, a function f to apply rotation
082   and translation to an input variable x is given by the following: f (x; (R, t)) = Rx + t ∈ R3 .
083
084
      With the definition of the SE(3) group, SE(3) invariance and equivariance are significant for defining
      robustness against arbitrary SE(3) transformations, supposing that a function f invariant or equivari-
085
      ant against a transformation T if it satisfies f (T (x)) = f (x) or f (T (x)) = T (f (x)), respectively.
086
      These properties are particularly desirable in specific tasks where the tasks should not depend on
087   transformations such as scaling, rotation, or translation of an input data. Without loss of generality,
088   we can define the SE(3) invariant function f : f (Rx + t) = f (x) ∀(R, t) ∈ SE(3). This invariance
089   property implies that the outputs of f are all identical when applying any SE(3) transformation,
090   so that it ensures that a model is invariant to object’s positioning and orientation. Furthermore,
091   the SE(3) equivariance is readily defined, based on the equivariance and SE(3) group. These are
092   well-known desirable properties in robotics and 3D vision (Chen et al., 2021; Thomas et al., 2018).

100
      The task of 2D and 3D shape puzzles involves solving the reconstruction of irregular 2D and 3D
101
      objects from disassembled, irregular, and pattern-free pieces, as shown in Figure 1. The goal of this
102   task is to accurately identify the correct spatial orientation and proper placement of all geometric
103   pieces. In particular, irregular target objects and geometric pieces complicate the assembly task as
104   it requires the precise understanding of spatial relationships between pieces and demands complex
105   reasoning on the occupation of 2D and 3D spaces. Moreover, the characteristics on pattern-free
106   objects make the problems more difficult since we cannot resort to the visual information captured by
107   modern feature extraction techniques. To address such tasks, in this paper we employ the equivariant
      diffusion model with overcomplete representations; see Section 4 for details.

339   5    T HEORETICAL A NALYSIS ON O UR M ETHOD
340
341   We leverage equivariance to enhance the model’s training efficiency and increase its robustness to
342   transformations applied to the puzzle. Specifically, the proposed model enforces an SE(3)-invariant
343   density function for the assembled puzzle, allowing for a more efficient assembly process. In this
344   section, we provide the theoretical analysis to support this approach.
345   In order to analyze our model from the perspective of whole assembly processes, we suppose that
346   we are interested in the invariance of a set of all pieces, each of which is defined on the SE(3)
347   manifold. In particular, Cbi is defined by multiplying the arrangement parameters to Ci for i ∈ [n], as
348   mentioned in Section 3.2. The translation part of the arrangement parameters is naturally defined on
349   the SE(3) manifold, but their rotation part may not be on the SE(3) manifold due to our definition
350   on orientation. This issue can be readily resolved by the normalization of the rotation part of the
351   arrangement parameters.
352   Proposition 1. Suppose that Ci is a representation without shape canonicalization. There always
353   exists some arrangement parameter for transforming Ci to the canonicalized Ci .
354   Proposition 2. Let Cb be an assembled puzzle of pieces C with arrangement parameters. By us-
355
      ing the arrangement parameters sampled from the standard Gaussian distributions, Cbi is a linear
356   transformation of the canonicalized Ci and the sampled arrangement parameter for i ∈ [n]. There-
357
      fore, the consideration of the sampled arrangement parameter for calculating Cbi is equivalent to the
358
      consideration of Cbi .
359
360   Proposition 3. We have SE(3)-invariant density pθ (Cb(0) ) if the denoising model ϵθ (Z (t) , t) is SE(3)-
361   invariant, i.e., ϵθ (Z (t) , t) = ϵθ (Tg Z (t) , t), where Tg is some roto-translational transformations of a
362   group element g ∈ SE(3).
363   Proposition 4. The proposed SE(3)-invariant feature embedding H(Z (t) , t) ensures that the de-
364   noising model ϵθ (Z (t) , t) satisfies SE(3)-invariant, provided that its constituent modules preserve
365   permutation equivariance.
366   Proposition 5. Our proposed Global MSA, Piece Masked MSA, and Corner Connection Masked
367   MSA always satisfy permutation equivariance.
368
369   Considering Propositions 1 to 5 together, the proposed model generates an SE(3)-invariant density
370   function for the entire puzzle. All proofs are provided in the appendices.

      Unlike the previous work focuses on 2D geometric shape puzzle (Lee et al., 2022; Hosseini et al.,
774   2023), this research explores the shape puzzles in a 3D domain. To achieve this, we propose a novel
775   method to generate a dataset of 3D puzzles. An initial step requires creating random 3D convex
776   meshes. Their shape complexity can be adjusted by the number of points for 3D meshes; this
777   analysis will be shown in Section 6. This step is accomplished through a process of random point
778   sampling, which is combined with the application of the 3D convex hull algorithm (Khosravani
779   et al., 2013). As the subsequent step, these randomly-generated 3D meshes are recursively cut 3
780   times, yielding 8 irregular pieces. As a result, a set of 8 geometric pieces, derived from a random 3D
781   mesh, forms a single puzzle problem. Leveraging this procedure, we generate a dataset of 30,000
782
      puzzle sets.
783   The existing work (Lee et al., 2022; Hosseini et al., 2023) on 2D puzzle tasks has utilized datasets
784   with substantial geometric constraints, which are prone to relying on particular models. For in-
785   stance, the cross-cut dataset mainly solved in PuzzleFusion (Hosseini et al., 2023) comes with 180◦
786   matching and edge matching constraints, which serve as hints to assembly models, making the
787   problems much easier. On the contrary, our 3D recursive-cut puzzle dataset eliminates the edge
788
      matching constraints that are uncommon in general real-world puzzle tasks. This design choice
      lets the conventional puzzle assembly methods which relies heavily on these constraints (Lee et al.,
789
      2022; Gallagher, 2012) fail to solve the puzzles, and introduces a unique challenge such that more
790
      delicate understanding of spatial and geometric relationships between puzzle pieces is required.

      I    DATASETS AND BASELINES
817
818
      In shape puzzles, the following geometric constraints are commonly included.
819
820       • 180◦ matching constraint: This stipulates that the sum of the two angles adjacent to any corner
821         must be equal to 180 degrees, ensuring that the pieces fit together to form straight lines or
822         correct angles.
823       • Edge matching constraint: This requires that two adjacent edges not only have equal lengths
824         but also align perfectly with one another, ensuring the continuity of the puzzle’s surface.
825
          • Fixed target shape: Across a dataset, a ground-truth assembled target shape does not change.
826
            While the shape itself is consistent, variations may occur in scale or the target shape may appear
827
            rotated in different instances.
828
829   Among the datasets used in 2D shape puzzle tasks, one of relatively general 2D shape puzzles is
830   the cross-cut shape puzzle dataset proposed in the work (Hosseini et al., 2023). These puzzles are
831   created by performing n cuts on a randomly-shaped 2D polygon to produce 2n pieces, resulting in a
832   dataset of randomly-partitioned puzzle pieces. While the cross-cut dataset holds the 180◦ matching
833   and edge matching constraints, our new generalized recursive-cut dataset on 2D space only holds
834   the edge matching constraint, following the similar procedure shown in Section G.
835   Incorporating the 3D shape puzzle dataset introduced in Section G, we design to apply the step-wise
836   enhancements discussed in Section 4. These enhancements grounded in the principles of equivari-
837   ance and invariance demonstrate intuitive and substantial performance improvements in both 2D and
838   3D puzzles.
839
840   J    E VALUATION M ETRICS
841
842   In the 2D shape puzzle tasks, metrics such as overlap score and point connection precision and
843   recall are employed. The overlap score measures the extent to which a reconstructed shape overlaps
844   with a ground-truth shape, providing the indication of both placement accuracy and shape fidelity.
845   The point connection evaluates the model’s ability to correctly identify and reconstruct connections
846   between points that are supposed to be connected in the ground-truth.
847
      In the 3D assembly tasks, traditional metrics such as the root mean square errors of rotation (denoted
848   as RMSE-R) and translation (denoted as RMSE-T) are widely used. In addition to this metric, in this
849   research, we propose the use of point connectivity (denoted as PC) as another metric for assessing
850   3D puzzle performance. This metric evaluates how well the points of interest are connected in the
851   final prediction. In addition to discrete thresholding classification of point connection, we use the
852   continuous values of point distances (denoted as PC-C) for this metric, enabling the more detailed
853   evaluation of assembly accuracy.

920
      The model is specifically designed to tackle puzzle datasets, and as such, this method cannot be
921   directly applied to point clouds. Some adjustments around the options will be required to ensure
922   compatibility and effectiveness in a different context.
923
924
      Training time is significantly influenced by the length of the query, which equates to the overall
      number of corners in the puzzle dataset. Due to the overcomplete representation, the query length is

      determined by both the number of pieces and the number of corners per piece. This factor can lead
926
      to exceedingly prolonged training times, especially in scenarios where the piece number exceeds 10
927   or the data complexity is greater than 30.
