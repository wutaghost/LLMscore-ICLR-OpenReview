009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Anomaly detection (AD) is crucial for visual inspections, and includes two main
012
               types: structural and logical anomalies. Despite growing interest in AD, most
013
               methods focus on structural anomalies, while few works address logical anomaly
014            detection (LAD), which requires a global understanding of the context. Lead-
015            ing LAD methods often advocate segmentation algorithms to parse logical rela-
016            tions within images, necessitating extensive training images or elaborate labels,
017            but they undergo significant performance degradation in low-data scenarios at a
018            risk of over-fitting. This study explores a practical yet challenging scenario where
019            only few-shot normal images are available. To the end, we introduce CLIP-LAD,
020            a novel, training-free method for few-shot LAD. We propose a coarse-to-fine seg-
021            mentation process, involving foreground extraction and fine-grained alignment,
022
               to progressively harness the CLIP’s generalization abilities for LAD. Specifically,
               we first aggregate visual features into different regions with clear boundaries, ben-
023
               efited from the strong visual coherence in vision transformer (ViT), and leverage
024
               coarse prompts to help identify the foreground. Within the foreground, we further
025            conduct per-pixel fine-grained classification with fine prompts to parse different
026            parts of an object. The anomaly scoring is derived from the class histograms in
027            the precise segmentation masks. For comprehensive evaluation, we build up a
028            few-shot LAD benchmark based on the MvTec-LOCO dataset and include a se-
029            ries of comparison methods. Experiments on this benchmark demonstrates our
030            superiority in low-data regime.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Anomaly detection is a fundamental yet challenging problem, which entails identifying anoma-
037   lous patterns that deviate significantly from the training distribution. Specifically, in visual inspec-
038   tion, defects can be broadly classified into structural and logical anomalies (Bergmann et al., 2022).
039
      Structural anomalies refer to deviations in the visual structure, texture, or shape of an object from
      its expected norm, e.g., cracks or scratches. In contrast, logical anomalies emphasize violations of
040
      logical constraints or expectations, such as missing, surplus objects, or misplacement.
041
042   While AD has recently garnered significant research interest, the majority of these meth-
043   ods (Bergmann et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021; Li et al., 2024) are biased towards identifying
044   structural anomalies, with limited focus on logical anomaly detection (LAD), which necessitates
045
      an understanding the global context beyond patch-level visual analysis. Current LAD approaches
      can be classified into two categories: feature-based and segmentation-based. Feature-based meth-
046
      ods implicitly capture the intricate logical dependencies within images through either knowledge
047
      distillation (Batzner et al., 2024) or image reconstruction (Yang et al., 2023). Here, the discrepancy
048   between the original input and its reconstructed counterpart serves as an anomaly indicator. Con-
049   versely, segmentation-based methods explicitly infer the relationships among objects or their parts
050   through segmentation (Kim et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023), delivering enhanced performance due to
051   the granular part-level analysis. Unfortunately, all these LAD methods involve a training process
052   and tend to be data-intensive (Li et al., 2024; Batzner et al., 2024) or require elaborate per-pixel
053   annotations (Kim et al., 2024). This dependency on the data scale and labels poses a significant risk
      of over-fitting, particularly in low-data regime.

074   In this study, we address the challenging problem of few-shot LAD to meet the requirements of
075   real-world applications, where access to abundant training images is impractical and per-pixel la-
076   beling is resource-intensive. Building on the success of segmentation-based methods, we model ob-
077
      ject/part relationships through segmentation. Motivated by the remarkable results achieved by Con-
      trastive Language-Image Pre-training (CLIP) in open-vocabulary semantic segmentation (OVSS) us-
078
      ing image-text pairs, we embrace CLIP to segment regions of interest within the images. However,
079
      directly applying CLIP inevitably leads to noisy predictions, especially around the object bound-
080   aries, due to the imperfect vision-language alignment at patch level, even with the advanced OVSS
081   method (Hajimiri et al., 2024).
082
083
      To overcome this issue, we introduce CLIP-LAD, a novel, training-free method to refine the segmen-
      tation process for few-shot LAD. We propose a coarse-to-fine segmentation pipeline that consists of
084
      foreground extraction and fine-grained alignment, ensuring more accurate object/part segmentation
085
      within a few-shot framework. Benefiting from the strong visual coherence in shallow ViT stages,
086   we first cluster the features extracted from these stages into distinct regions. Each of these regions
087   serves as a class-agnostic mask proposal, which is utilized to aggregate patch tokens from the fi-
088   nal ViT stage, representing the embedding of the corresponding region. This embedding is then
089   matched with pre-set coarse prompts to distinguish the foreground from the background. Within
090   the identified foreground region, we further perform fine-grained visual-language matching using
091   fined prompts at patch level, ultimately yielding the segmentation masks. Thanks to the ease of
092   region-level discrimination in the first stage, the foreground with clear boundaries can be effectively
093   identified and proceeded in the second stage of fine-grained prediction, eliminating false positives
094
      within the background. Regarding the anomaly scoring, we adopt histogram statistic of segmenta-
      tion for logical anomalies and multi-level feature comparison for structural anomalies, respectively.
095
      We use scale-invariant Mahalanobis distance to fuse the two types of scoring functions. To com-
096
      prehensively evaluate our method, we also establish a few-shot LAD benchmark based on MvTec
097   LOCO (Bergmann et al., 2022), the first large-scale dataset featuring LA. A variety of learning-based
098   and training-free LAD methods are included in the benchmark. Notably, owing to its training-free
099   characteristic, our method naturally extends to multiple categories via a unified model, in contrast
100   to the previous state-of-the-art PSAD (Kim et al., 2024; Batzner et al., 2024) that requires training a
101   specialized model for each category. The contributions are three-fold:
102
103          • We build up a simple yet effective training-free baseline that only leverages CLIP to parse
104            the logical relations within the image through segmentation.
105
106          • We introduce a two-stage segmentation pipeline, which utilizes visual coherence and cross-
107            modal alignment with a coarse-to-fine prompting strategy, to harness the CLIP potentials
               for accurate segmentation.

114   Logical Anomaly Detection (LAD). Industrial anomaly detection primarily focuses on two types
115   of anomalies: structural and logical. In contrast to structural anomalies such as bents and scratches,
116   which locally exhibit conspicuous visual inconsistency against normal patterns, logical anomalies
117   violate the logical constraints e.g., the quantity, spatial layout or composition of objects. The ma-
118
      jority of AD methods (Bergmann et al., 2020; Salehi et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2022; Jeong et al.,
      2023; Li et al., 2024) and widely-used benchmarks (Bergmann et al., 2019; Zou et al., 2022) are bi-
119
      ased towards structural anomalies, inapplicable for detecting logical ones, which requires alternative
120
      designs for capturing the global dependencies within the image.
121
122   With the release of MvTec LOCO dataset (Bergmann et al., 2022), few efforts are devoted to ad-
123   dressing the issue, and can generally be categorized into two streams. The first is the embedding
124
      based methods (Bergmann et al., 2022; Batzner et al., 2024) , which train a model to match with
      the outputs of the other pre-trained one. Specifically, Efficient-AD (Batzner et al., 2024) equips the
125
      student-teacher learning framework with an autoencoder to learn the logical constraints of normal
126
      images. GCAD (Bergmann et al., 2022) inherits the framework but improves the anomaly scoring
127   by using reconstruction errors and feature distance to address picturable and unpicturable anoma-
128   lies, respectively. Some other works attempt synthesize pseudo logical anomalies by either utilizing
129   a diffusion model (Dai et al., 2024) or edge manipulation (Zhao, 2024). Alternative methods rea-
130   son about logical constraints in images through segmentation. ComAD (Liu et al., 2023) performs
131   K-means on the pre-trained DINO features (Caron et al., 2021) to segment images into multiple
132   components, based on which a series of meticulously designed techniques are developed to model
133   metrological features. The follow-up work PSAD (Kim et al., 2024) improves the segmentation
134   precision and granularity by introducing elaborately annotated masks to train a model to segment
135
      object parts. While effective, it heavily depends on the well-trained segmentation model subject to
      massive training images with a few elaborately annotated ones.
136
137   More importantly, all the existing methods work within the full-shot setting and require an additional
138   training process, while degenerate significantly in few-shot setting at a risk of over-fitting. Differ-
139   ently, this work especially focuses on the more challenging few-shot setting, in which only a few
140   normal images without any annotations are available at training, and presents a simple yet effective
141
      training-free framework that only utilizes the powerful CLIP to detect logical anomalies.
142   Few-shot Anomaly detection (FSAD). FSAD aims at detecting anomalies with only access to a
143   limited number of normal images. RegAD (Huang et al., 2022) sets up a new paradigm that trains
144   a single generalized model for new categories via feature registration. WinCLIP (Jeong et al.,
145   2023) embraces the pre-trained CLIP to identify anomalies through matching the well-designed
146   text prompts with window-based visual features in the shared vision-language space. The recent
147
      PromptAD (Li et al., 2024) improves the results by introducing learnable prompts conditioned on
      the few-shot normal images. Nevertheless, all these methods work on detecting structural anomalies
148
      and fail to acquire the logical dependencies within the image, leaving much room for improvement
149
      in LAD.
150
151   Open-vocabulary Semantic Segmentation (OVSS). Different from conventional segmentation
152   methods that are confined to a infinite visual concepts, OVSS endeavors to segment semantic el-
153
      ements of arbitrary categories, with CLIP being the essential impetus for the growth. However,
      directly applying it to dense prediction tasks is sub-optimal. SCLIP (Wang et al., 2023) attributes
154
      the inferiority to spatial misalignment of patch representations caused by vanilla self-attention mod-
155
      ules. To address this, SCLIP introduces a novel self-attention mechanism that facilitates covariant
156   visual features. More recent methods have further enhanced spatial consistency by encouraging
157   each patch attend to its neighbours (Hajimiri et al., 2024; Shao et al., 2024), thereby enhancing the
158   localization capabilities. Despite the promising achievements of these advanced OVSS methods,
159   their direct application to LAD still yields sub-optimal results, due to the domain gap between the
160   general-purpose pre-training data and the images in industrial scenarios. To unleash the potentials
161   of CLIP for LAD, we introduce a series of modifications to fully utilize both visual coherence and
      cross-modal alignment capabilities at inference, ensuring more accurate object/part segmentation.

165   3.1   C OARSE - TO - FINE S EGMENTATION
166
167   The main concept for LAD is to analyze the relationships among objects/parts through the precise
168   segmentation. Though CLIP demonstrates strong generalized abilities in zero-shot classification and
169   segmentation, directly adapting it to industrial images yields false positive predictions. We attribute
170
      the imprecise segmentation to two key factors: 1) individual foreground patch token at the last ViT
      stage probably carries ambiguous semantics infiltrated by background; 2) vision-language alignment
171
      at patch level is imperfect due to the CLIP pre-training on matching global image embeddings with
172
      captions. In this paper, we argue that with proper modifications in CLIP inference, it can precisely
173   identify objects of interest. Inspired by chain-of-thought (CoT) in natural language process (Wei
174   et al., 2022), which involves providing a series of intermediate reasoning steps to guide the model
175   in solving complex problems, we devise a coarse-to-fine segmentation pipeline to derive a precise
176   fine-grained segmentation mask. This approach operates in a top-to-bottom fashion, considering
177   both visual spatial size and textual prompt granularity.
178
      Foreground Extraction. While several training-free OVSS methods achieve impressive segmenta-
179   tion results on popular benchmarks such as ADE-20K (Zhou et al., 2019), which primarily feature
180   natural images with rich semantic content, their direct application to industrial images often leads
181   to noisy predictions due to the significant domain gap. Specifically, these incorrect predictions fre-
182   quently arise around object boundaries or background regions (Fig. 3). This issue is exaggerated
183   when matching visual patch tokens with finer text prompts. The observation inspires us to first ex-
184   tract the foreground region and then perform the fine-grained segmentation within the foreground.
185   Benefiting from the strong visual coherence in shallow ViT stages, we propose to aggregate patches
186   based on their visual similarities. Specifically, given k normal images I ∈ Rk×H×W ×3 , we first

209

215   fine segmentation strategy, the resulting segmentation mask features clear boundaries with precise
      fine-grained object part masks.

245   Inference. On the LAD, we calculate the class histogram hi based on the segmentation mask for
246   each of the k normal images and maintain a memory bank Mhist = {hi }ki=1 . The number of bins
247   in the histograms corresponds to the number of classes of interest, as indicated by the text prompts,
248
      excluding the background class. This histogram memory is assumed to reveal the compositional
      relationships among objects within the images. Moreover, we reuse the cluster centroids µi derived
249
      from the k-shot normal images to partition a query image Itest , which helps make logical anoma-
250
      lies more recognizable through low-level feature matching. We then apply the same coarse-to-fine
251   vision-language alignment used for processing the normal images to obtain the histogram htest .
252   While high-level compositional relations are crucial for LAD, investigating low-level appearance
253   differences is essential for effective structural anomaly detection. To the end, we adopt per-patch
254   comparison in feature space to spot the visual defects. Specifically, we first extract multi-level ViT
255   features Fi for all k images and save patch features fj ∈ RC at all positions to create a patch
256                                          k× H × W
      memory Mpatch = {fj }j=1s s . The anomaly map is obtained by comparing the query patch
257   fpatch with the those in Mpatch . Multi-level feature comparison is enabled for robust detection
258   following (Salehi et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021).
259

      Anomaly Scoring. Based on the constructed memories Mhist and Mpatch , we inspect the dif-
      ference between a query image and its nearest samples in the Mhist and Mpatch to measure the
261
      anomaly scores. On the logical anomalies, we adopt the histogram difference ratio as the anomaly
262
      scoring, different from directly calculating their the L2 distance (Kim et al., 2024) in consideration
263   of the unbalanced class distribution.

296   Score Normalization. We adopt segmentation analysis and patch comparison to cater for detecting
297   logical and structural anomalies, respectively. However, the two strategies generate anomaly scores
298   slog and sstr with different scales. Thus, it is essential to reasonably normalize the two types of
299   scoring before aggregation. Following (Batzner et al., 2024), we use the validation set, which con-
300   tains anomaly-free images and has no overlaps with the training set, to estimate the scales of the two
301   scoring types. With the assumption that each type of anomaly scoring follows a Gaussian distribu-
302   tion, we derive the mean µ and covariance matrix Σ for the set of anomaly scores (slog , sstr ) across
303   the validation images. For an input tuple x, we define the Mahalanobis distance as the resulting

310
311   Benchmark Set-up. To the best of our knowledge, MvTec LOCO (Bergmann et al., 2022) is the
312   only large-scale dataset featuring logical anomalies. It contains 3,644 images across five categories
313   from industrial inspection scenarios. The training and validation sets consist solely of anomaly-free
314   images, while the test set contains both anomaly-free images and anomalous images. Typical logical
315
      anomalies include missing or surplus objects, and misplacement. To comprehensively evaluate our
      method based on the dataset, we first establish a few-show FAD benchmark, considering 1/2/4-shot
316
      normal images only for training. However, since the categories juice bottle and splicing connec-
317
      tors in the dataset have multiple sub-types, randomly sampling k images constituting the training
318   set could cause label ambiguity. For instance, juice bottle includes three types of liquid-orange,
319   cherry and banana juice-that are all considered normal. The sampled k-shot training images may
320   not necessarily include all these sub-types. To eliminate the ambiguity in determining normality
321   or abnormality, we split these two categories into three sub-types each, resulting in a total of nine
322   categories. The area under the ROC curve (AUROC) is used as evaluation metrics. For each k-shot
323   setting, we randomly select k normal images across five random seeds. The models are evaluated
      on both SA and LA detection together, i.e., micro-average. We also provide separate results in


                                                                                6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      Fig. 2. Following (Kim et al., 2024), we consider image-level AUROC, as logical anomalies are
325   context-based and typically pertain to the entire image.
326
327   Comparison Methods. Our few-shot LAD benchmark considers a variety of methods: training-
328
      free and learning-based. Training-free methods include CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) PatchCore (Roth
      et al., 2022), WinCLIP (Jeong et al., 2023), ComAD (Liu et al., 2023), SINBAD (Cohen et al., 2023).
329
      The learning-based ones are PromptAD (Li et al., 2024), RD4AD (Deng & Li, 2022), STFPM (Wang
330
      et al., 2021) and EfficientAD (Batzner et al., 2024). Notably, since all these comparison methods
331   are developed for full-data scenarios, we adapt them to our few-shot setting by using their official
332   implementations to train the models with only the given k normal images.
333
334
      Implementation Details. We use OpenCLIP’s implementation1 with DataComp-1B (Gadre et al.,
      2024) and CLIP ViT-L/14 for all experiments. Images are resized to 448 × 448. Following (Chen
335
      et al., 2023), we evenly divide the visual encoder into four stages and apply K-means clustering on
336
      embeddings from the first two stages. We adopt NACLIP (Hajimiri et al., 2024), a top-tier training-
337   free method for OVSS, and up-scale feature maps by ×2. Histogram statistics are calculated on
338   the 64 × 64 segmentation masks. We find that the widely-used mask refinement techniques, such
339   as DenseCRF (Krähenbühl & Koltun, 2011) and PAMR (Araslanov & Roth, 2020) yield similar
340   results. We use the same prompt templates as (Radford et al., 2021) and ensemble text embeddings.
341   For the complex juice bottle, we perform region-level fine-grained classification in Fig. 5 to avoid
342   noisy patch-level results.
343   Experimental Results. Tab. 1 shows the overall comparison with both learning-based and training-
344   free methods in 1/2/4-shot settings. While learning-based methods achieve descend results in some
345   categories, such as juice bottle which is consistently positioned in the center of the image, they all
346   fail in more challenging categories like pushpins and screw bag, where objects are placed randomly.
347   This failure is due to the risk of over-fitting on the limited number of normal images.Training-free
348   methods attempt to leverage the generalized capabilities of pre-trained foundation models; however,
349   they typically perform feature matching at either patch-level (Roth et al., 2022) or image-level (Co-
350   hen et al., 2023), which fails to capture object relationships adequately, leading to inferior detection.
351
      In contrast, our method, with tailored designs that fully harness the potential of CLIP, significantly
      outperforms these alternatives.
352
353   In Fig. 3, we visualize the segmentation results of NACLIP (Hajimiri et al., 2024), as well as the
354   coarse and the fine stages of our method. Obviously, NACLIP with fine-prompts tends to make
355   incorrect prediction scattered across the image,particularly around object boundaries and in back-
356   ground regions, whereas our method provides more precise segmentation masks. This improvement
357
      can be attributed to our coarse-to-fine cross-modal alignment design. The coarse stage involves un-
      supervised clustering on shallow embeddings, which results in clearer class boundaries but lacks
358
      discriminative labels. The second stage focuses on per-pixel prediction using fine text prompts. By
359
      combining the results from these two stages, we achieve precise segmentation results that are well-
360   suited for inferring composition relationships for LAD. Additionally, with more training images
361   available, we observe an increase in average performance. To further understand the performance
362   gain, we separately calculate the metrics for logical and structural anomalies, which in Tab. 2 con-
363   firms our method’s superiority for LAD.

469   Effect of Number of Clustering Centers. The success of our methods lies in its segmentation
470
      precision. To achieve precise zero-shot image segmentation, we propose a coarse-to-fine cross-
      modal alignment. In the coarse stage of foreground extraction, we perform unsupervised clustering
471
      on the visual features to aggregate similar patches. The segmentation quality depends largely on the
472
      number of clustering centers used. Fig. 4 displays the effects of different cluster numbers across five
473   categories. Over-clustering leads to a large number of fragmented regions, each inevitably mixed
474   with background noise, making it difficult to accurately identify the foreground regions of interest.
475   On the other hand, under-clustering directly results in unclear object boundaries. Thus, it is essential
476   to select an appropriate cluster number for better aggregate the foreground regions.

479   5   C ONCLUSION
480
481   In this work, we consider the challenging few-shot logical anomaly detection and present a simple
482   yet effective training-free method CLIP-LAD. We leverage the off-the-shelf vision-language model
483   to comprehensively understand the logical constraints through segmentation. Specifically, we devise
484   a cross-modal coarse-to-fine segmenting strategy to take full advantage of visual coherence and
485   cross-modal alignment capabilities in CLIP, facilitating precise segmentation. Experimental results
      on the few-shot LAD benchmark demonstrate the superiority of our method.
