                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
               This paper introduces MMTryon, a multi-modal multi-reference VIrtual Try-ON
012
               (VITON) framework, which can generate high-quality compositional try-on results
013
               by taking a text instruction and multiple garment images as inputs. Our MMTryon
014            addresses three problems overlooked in prior literature: 1) Support of multiple
015            try-on items. Existing methods are commonly designed for single-item try-on
016            tasks (e.g., upper/lower garments, dresses). 2) Specification of dressing style.
017            Existing methods are unable to customize dressing styles based on instructions
018            (e.g., zipped/unzipped, tuck-in/tuck-out, etc.) 3) Segmentation Dependency. They
019            further heavily rely on category-specific segmentation models to identify the re-
020            placement regions, with segmentation errors directly leading to significant artifacts
021            in the try-on results. To address the first two issues, our MMTryon introduces
022
               a novel multi-modality and multi-reference attention mechanism to combine the
               garment information from reference images and dressing-style information from
023
               text instructions. Besides, to remove the segmentation dependency, MMTryon
024
               uses a parsing-free garment encoder and leverages a novel scalable data generation
025            pipeline to convert existing VITON datasets to a form that allows MMTryon to be
026            trained without requiring any explicit segmentation. Extensive experiments on high-
027            resolution benchmarks and in-the-wild test sets demonstrate MMTryon’s superiority
028            over existing SOTA methods both qualitatively and quantitatively. MMTryon’s im-
029            pressive performance on multi-item and style-controllable virtual try-on scenarios
030            and its ability to try on any outfit in a large variety of scenarios from any source
031            image, opens up a new avenue for future investigation in the fashion community.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   VIrtual Try-ON (VITON) technology aims to generate photo-realistic images featuring individuals
037   adorned in specific garments according to input images of both the garments and the person. Due to
038   its promising potential to revolutionize the online shopping experience, VITON has garnered con-
039
      siderable attention both in the academic and industry communities. Despite the substantial progress
      achieved in academic benchmarks (Choi et al., 2021; Davide et al., 2022), previous methods (Xie
040
      et al., 2021b; Ge et al., 2021; He et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2023; Morelli et al., 2023;
041
      Gou et al., 2023) still fall short of meeting the requirements of real-world applications, in particular
042   for: 1) Multiple-garment Compositional Try-On: Users should be able to freely choose and
043   combine a diverse range of tops, bottoms, and accessories from in-the-wild images, rather than being
044   confined to the single-garment try-on scenario. 2) Flexible Try-On Styles: Users should have the
045   flexibility to select their preferred try-on style, allowing them to specify how a garment is worn, such
046   as deciding whether a top should be tucked into the trousers or draped over them.
047
      Due to their focus on single garments, these methods encounter challenges when multiple garments
048   and accessories need to be changed simultaneously. This limitation arises because collecting training
049   data for single-garment try-on is relatively easy, whereas gathering data for multi-garment try-on
050   is significantly more difficult. Additionally, these methods struggle to model the subtle differences
051   in how each piece of clothing is styled during a compositional try-on, such as the overlapping
052   relationship between tops and bottoms. Furthermore, given a flat in-store image of a coat with a
053   zipper, existing methods are limited to replicating the garment in its original state onto the target,
      without the ability to adjust for open or closed styles.

069   Additionally, existing methods lack the capability to precisely identify try-on areas, often resorting to
070   trained segmentation models (Gong et al., 2019; Li et al., 2020) for explicit try-on area localization.
071   This reliance on segmentation models makes these approaches highly dependent on the segmentation
072   model’s precision, which can be unsatisfactory in complex scenarios, introducing additional noise
073   during training. Additionally, conditioning the generation on the segmented garment results in a loss
074   of information of how the garment is worn, leading to highly unrealistic try-on outcomes, particularly
075
      for uniquely designed garments. Moreover, the dependence on segmentation models severely restricts
      the flexibility of the try-on process, as these models are typically confined to a predefined set of
076
      garment classes.
077
078   To address these challenges, we propose MMTryon, a more flexible try-on model capable of achieving
079   multi-modal compositional dressing without the need for pre-segmenting the try-on areas. In our
080   approach, a general try-on scenario is decoupled into text and image information, where the text
081
      information encompasses high-level garment descriptions and try-on style instructions, while the
      image information contains fine-grained texture details. MMTryon is based on a conditional diffusion
082
      model that, to ensure efficient incorporation of both the image and text information in challenging
083
      scenarios, leverages our novel multi-reference image attention and multi-modal instruction attention
084   modules. Specifically, to exploit the image information, we pre-trained a robust garment encoder
085   on a large dataset to project image features into the multi-reference image attention module. This
086   module then can utilize text as queries to extract the necessary features with the aim of eliminating
087   our dependency on segmentation. For the text information, we develop a sophisticated multi-modal
088   prompt and employ a pre-trained text encoder to project the text information into the specifically
089   designed Multi-Modal Instruction Attention module. Finally, we train our model on an synthetically
090   enhanced dataset, facilitated by our proposed data pipeline, using the pre-trained encoders and Stable
091   Diffusion-initialized weights. Despite the difficulty of obtaining ideal multi-garment-to-single-model
092
      pairs for compositional try-on, our pre-trained garment encoder ensures excellent results even with
      limited datasets. Additionally, by overcoming the segmentation accuracy limitations in existing
093
      VITON models, our model can more effectively handle the nuances of different clothing items and
094
      styles (see Fig. 1).
095
096   Overall, our contributions can be summarized as follows: First, we propose MMTryon which, to
097   our knowledge, is the first model to support the multi-modal compositional try-on task. MMTryon
098
      enables the combination of one or multiple garments for try-on, while also allowing the manipulation
      of try-on effects through textual commands. We further introduce a scalable data generation pipeline
099
      and a specially designed garment encoder. This eliminates the need for any prior segmentation
100
      networks during both the training and inference phases to get garments, relying solely on text, images
101   of individual garment items, or model images to identify the areas of interest. Finally, extensive exper-
102   iments conducted on public try-on benchmarks and in-the-wild test sets are performed, demonstrating
103   that our approach surpasses existing state-of-the-art methods such as Outfit Anyone(Sun et al., 2024).

105   2     R ELATED WORK
106
107   GAN-Based Virtual Try-on. Traditional virtual try-on methods (Wang et al., 2018; Dong et al.,
      2019; Yang et al., 2020; Ge et al., 2021; He et al., 2022; Choi et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2021b; Zhao

      et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2021a; Lee et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022; Davide et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2022;
109   Dong et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2023) usually adopt a two-stage pipeline with
110   Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The first stage employs an explicit warping module to
111   deform in-shop clothing to the target shape, while the second stage uses a GAN-based generator to
112   fuse the deformed clothing onto the target person. For theses methods, the synthetic quality largely
113   depends on the quality of the deformation in the first stage, prompting current methods to emphasize
114   enhancing the non-rigid deformation capabilities of the warping module. Meanwhile, some GAN-
115   based approaches (Cui et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2022) have explored the compositional try-on task,
116   utilizing a cyclical generation mode to achieve sequential try-on. However, when the outcome of
117
      compositional dressing is entirely determined by the try-on order, the lack of understanding of the
      semantic information of the garments often leads to unrealistic results. Furthermore, most GAN-based
118
      solutions mentioned above directly utilize UNet-based generators for try-on synthesis, with little
119
      exploration into how to enhance the generator’s capabilities. This results in poor resolution and
120   visual quality of the try-on outcomes, making it difficult to integrate them with diverse linguistic
121   instructions.
122
123
      Diffusion-Based Virtual Try-on. Compared to GAN-based models, diffusion models have made
      significant strides in high-fidelity conditional image generation (Rombach et al., 2022; Saharia et al.,
124
      2022; Ramesh et al., 2022). Image-based virtual try-on is essentially a specialized form of the image
125
      editing/repair task conditioned on a given garment image. Therefore, a straightforward adaptation is
126   to extend text-to-image diffusion models to accept images as conditions. While methods such as Yang
127   et al. (2023); Huang et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2023) have demonstrated their capabilities in virtual
128   try-on, they fall short in preserving the texture details of the try-on results due to inadequate extraction
129   of fine-grained features by the image encoder. Recent methods (Morelli et al., 2023; Gou et al.,
130   2023) have further aimed to integrate traditional GAN-based approaches with diffusion models. They
131   employ explicit warping modules to create deformed garments and use diffusion models to blend these
132   with reference person images. Beyond simply incorporating explicit warping modules, the newly
133   introduced TryonDiffusion (Zhu et al., 2023), conversely, implements an implicit warping mechanism
134
      for clothing deformation. This addresses the issues of texture misalignment mentioned earlier and
      achieves promising try-on synthesis. However, TryonDiffusion relies on precise parsing to achieve
135
      various conditions, making it unable to handle more complex try-on scenarios. Additionally, none
136
      of the existing approaches have effectively leveraged the capabilities of current large multi-modal
137   models and have discarded text as an input, lacking the ability of free-form instruction following.
138   The try-on effect in these approaches is solely inferred from the garment image.
139
140
      Recent methods have also explored compositional try-on tasks. For example, MMVTON Zhu et al.
      (2024) utilizes multiple garments as input, but it only supports label-level text input and cannot
141
      control free-text attributes such as the background. AnyFit Li et al. (2024) uses multiple flat-lay
142
      garment images as input but lacks additional control over wearing styles. Wear Any Way Chen et al.
143   (2024) incorporates point-based input to achieve style control, while ImageDressing Shen et al. (2024)
144   employs lightweight training methods for embedding injection, enabling flexible results. While these
145   methods have made progress in exploring related tasks, they often rely heavily on prior parsing
146   networks, which may lead to unintended outcomes due to the dependency on precise parsing.
147
      Our approach instead, through the specialized design of a parsing free garment encoder and the
148
      multi-modal multi-reference attention mechanism, achieves a more versatile try-on experience. This
149   not only addresses the limitations seen in both GAN and diffusion model-based methods but also
150   utilizes the strengths of large multi-modal models, allowing for more detailed and flexible adaptation
151   to various clothing styles and try-on scenarios.

182   3.1   OVERVIEW
183
184   Given a target person image I, multiple reference images containing try-on items {Gi } and a text
185   instruction T describing how the items from those reference images are dressed, MMTryon aims
186
      to seamlessly follow T to synthesize photo-realistic try-on results I ′ . We model this task as a
      conditional diffusion model, which takes I, {Gi }, and T as conditions and gradually estimates a
187
      denoised version of I ′ from pure noise. Our diffusion model is built upon the public available Stable
188
      Diffusion text-to-image model (Rombach et al., 2022).
189
190   Stable Diffusion is a text conditioned latent diffusion model (Rombach et al., 2022). For an
191   VAE (Kingma & Welling, 2013) encoded image latent feature z0 , the forward diffusion process is
192
      performed by adding noise according to a predefined noise scheduler αt (Ho et al., 2020):

194
      To reverse the diffusion process, a noise estimator ϵθ (·) parameterized by an UNet is learned to
195
      predict the forward added noise ϵ with the objective function,
196

198   where c is the text condition associated with image latent z, and D is the training set.
199   In MMTryon, our condition is not merely a text condition but a multimodal condition that includes
200   texture information as well as a multimodal prompt that integrates global image information and
201   instructions. Modulating this condition into the diffusion model is a challenging problem. Prior
202   works (Yang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023) have relied on transforming pixel features into text-
203   aligned features using a CLIP encoder and injecting them into the cross-attention layers. However,
204   this approach often results in a loss of attention to detail. In this work, we instead draw inspiration
205   from Hertz et al. (2022); Ge et al. (2023); Cao et al. (2023), who show that the cross-attention layer
206
      learns correspondences between the image features and the text embedding tokens, generating an
      overall image semantic structure, while the self-attention layers are more related to detailed texture
207
      generation. We thus focus on enhancing the cross-attention layers conditioned on the original text,
208
      integrating it with our multi-modal instruction to improve generation of the final try-on results.
209   Additionally we train a garment encoder to align pixel features with the stable diffusion features,
210   enhancing the self-attention layers and seamlessly transfer detailed image features from the garment
211   images to the desired human images.

      3.3.3    M ULTI -R EFERENCE T EXTURE ATTENTION
329   The Multi-Modal Instruction Attention cannot fully preserve the details of the garment due to the
330   use of the CLIP ViT image encoder. To guarantee texture consistency, we therefore first prompt the
331   garment encoder with the textual label. The textual label Pi is derived from the instruction prompt T
332   and used to extract features for the particular subject from image Gi . After obtaining the features, in
333   each self-attention layer of the diffusion UNet, we extract a set of garment features corresponding to
334   that layer, denoted as {FG1 , FG2 , . . . , FGn }. We denote the original feature map from the diffusion
335   UNet as Ftarget . To perform the attention operation, the query Q is derived from Ftarget , while
336   the K, V pairs are obtained from the concatenated features of {Ftarget , FG1 , FG2 , . . . , FGn }. This
337
      multi-reference warping attention allows us to transfer the features of different reference images
      onto the target image, thus effectively preserving the texture features of each object without mutual
338
      interference. The process is illustrated in Fig. 2.

347   additional large-scale proprietary e-commerce dataset. Experiments are conducted under a resolution
348   of 1024 × 768. Specifically, VITON-HD comprises 13,679 image pairs of front-view upper-body
349   women and upper-body in-store garment images, which are further divided into 11,647 training
350   pairs and 2,032 testing pairs. DressCode includes 48,392 training pairs and 5,400 testing pairs of
351   front-view full-body person and in-store garment images, consisting of three subsets with different
352
      category pairs (i.e., upper, lower, dresses). Our proprietary e-commerce dataset contains 203,239
      training pairs and 5,219 testing pairs of front-view full-body person and in-store garment images, and
353
      450,931 training pairs and 9,198 testing pairs of multiple front-view images of the same full body.

530   5     C ONCLUSION
531
532   In this work, we introduce MMTryon, a novel and powerful try-on model capable of freely generating
533   high-fidelity VITON results with realistic try-on effects based on text and multiple garments. By
534   using a pretrained encoder, MMTryon not only avoids the need for segmentation but also achieves
535   compositional try-on even with limited data. To support multi-modal and multi-reference dressing
536   modes, MMTryon introduces multi-modal instruction attention and multi-reference attention modules.
537   Experiments conducted on high-resolution VITON benchmarks and in-the-wild test sets demonstrate
538   MMTryon’s superior efficacy compared to existing methods.

      A.2     H UMAN E VALUATION D ETAILS
838
839   For the human evaluation, we designed questionnaires for the single garment try-on, multi-modal
840   garment try-on, and compositional garment try-on. 100 volunteers were invited to complete a
841   questionnaire comprised of 50 assignments for each task. Specifically, for the single garment try-on,
842   given a person image and a garment image, volunteers were asked to select the most realistic and
843   accurate try-on result out of seven options generated by our MMTryon and baseline methods (i.e.,
844   PF-AFN (Ge et al., 2021), FS-VTON (He et al., 2022), SDAFN (Bai et al., 2022), GP-VTON (Xie
845   et al., 2023), DCI-VTON (Gou et al., 2023), ,Stable-VTON (Morelli et al., 2023)),anydoor (Chen
846
      et al., 2023) and Ootdiffusion (Xu et al., 2024). The order of the generated results in each assignment
      was randomly shuffled. For the compositional garment try-on, given a person image and a set of
847
      garment images, volunteers were asked to select the most realistic and accurate try-on result out of
848
      four options generated by our MMTryon and baseline methods (i.e., Paint by example Yang et al.
849   (2023), Midjourney, DALLE3 Betker et al. (2023)). For the multi-modal garment try-on, given a
850   person image, a set of garment images, and a text instruction, volunteers were asked to select the
851   most realistic and instructionally relevant try-on result out of four options generated by our MMTryon
852   and baseline methods (i.e., Paint by example Yang et al. (2023), Midjourney, DALLE3 Betker et al.
853   (2023)).

      A.8   I NFERENCE SETTING
951
952   In this section, we provide a detailed explanation of the inference process for our method. In the
953   single-image setting, we use a single garment image, a protection region map, and the corresponding
954   caption as inputs. For the multi-image setting, we use multiple garment images, such as tops, pants,
955   shoes, and bags, along with a protection region map and the corresponding caption. When a reference
956   image for a specific region is not provided, our model generates a suitable garment randomly for that
957   region. This capability ensures that the try-on process remains flexible and produces coherent results
958
      even in the absence of complete input references.
959   Finally, the inference time and GPU RAM requirements are as follows:
