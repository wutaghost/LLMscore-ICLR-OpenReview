011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Drawing inspiration from the hierarchical processing of the human auditory
014            system, which transforms sound from low-level acoustic features to high-level
015            semantic understanding, we introduce a novel coarse-to-fine audio reconstruc-
016            tion method. Leveraging non-invasive functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging
017            (fMRI) data, our approach mimics the inverse pathway of auditory processing.
018            Initially, we utilize CLAP to decode fMRI data coarsely into a low-dimensional
019
               semantic space, followed by a fine-grained decoding into the high-dimensional
               AudioMAE latent space guided by semantic features. These fine-grained neu-
020
               ral features serve as conditions for audio reconstruction through a Latent Dif-
021
               fusion Model (LDM). Validation on three public fMRI datasets—Brain2Sound,
022            Brain2Music, and Brain2Speech—underscores the superiority of our coarse-to-
023            fine decoding method over stand-alone fine-grained approaches, showcasing state-
024            of-the-art performance in metrics like FD, FAD, and KL. Moreover, by employing
025            semantic prompts during decoding, we enhance the quality of reconstructed au-
026            dio when semantic features are suboptimal. The demonstrated versatility of our
027            model across diverse stimuli highlights its potential as a universal brain-to-audio
028            framework. This research contributes to the comprehension of the human auditory
029            system, pushing boundaries in neural decoding and audio reconstruction method-
030
               ologies.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Hearing is one of the most important senses for humans, responsible for receiving external auditory
036   stimuli and transmitting the information to the brain for processing and understanding. Researchers
037   aim to explore the auditory perception mechanisms of the human brain from the fields of both
038   neuroscience and computer science (Kell et al., 2018; Millet et al., 2022; Tuckute et al., 2023;
039   Giordano et al., 2023; Caucheteux et al., 2023). A key goal is to decode neural information from
040   the human brain and reconstruct the original stimuli. This can be applied to auditory attention
041   decoding (Van Eyndhoven et al., 2016; O’Sullivan et al., 2017), with practical uses in enhancing
042   hearing aids and assisting communication in noisy environments. Additionally, by generalizing from
043   perception tasks to imagination tasks (Horikawa & Kamitani, 2017; Tang et al., 2023), it serves as a
044
      foundation for future research on reconstructing imagined audio.
045   The common brain-to-audio reconstruction tasks can be categorized into brain-to-sound task (San-
046   toro et al., 2017; Park et al., 2023) for reconstructing all natural sounds in the environment, brain-
047   to-music task (Bellier et al., 2023; Daly, 2023; Denk et al., 2023) for the music, and brain-to-speech
048   task (Pasley et al., 2012; Yang et al., 2015; Hassan et al., 2018; Shigemi et al., 2023; Kim et al.,
049   2023; Chen et al., 2024) for the human voice, based on the different stimulus audios.
050   Some researchers first attempted to map brain signals to the spectrograms or mel-spectrograms of
051   the stimulus audios using linear regression (Pasley et al., 2012; Yang et al., 2015; Hassan et al., 2018;
052   Bellier et al., 2023). Others introduce non-linear units and use simple networks such as MLP (Yang
053   et al., 2015; Hassan et al., 2018; Bellier et al., 2023), BiLSTM (Shigemi et al., 2023; Daly, 2023),
      Transformer (Shigemi et al., 2023), etc. This approach can restore the overall temporal and fre-

073   As research progresses, researchers have found that Deep Neural Network (DNN) features are closer
074   to neural responses in the human brain compared to artificial acoustic representations like spectro-
075   grams (Kell et al., 2018; Li et al., 2023; Tuckute et al., 2023; Giordano et al., 2023). Therefore,
076   researchers (Park et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024) first decode neural signals into
077   DNN features as an intermediate representation and then use generative models to reconstruct the
078   spectrogram. The intermediate representation is typically chosen from the intermediate layers of
079   DNN (Iashin & Rahtu, 2021; Baevski et al., 2020), serving as fine-grained features that contain both
080   semantic and acoustic information of sound. However, decoding the fine-grained features directly is
081
      often challenging due to the high dimensionality, yielding limited outcomes in reconstruction.
082   There are also works that decode neural signals coarsely into the low-dimensional semantic space.
083   For example, Denk et al. (2023) decodes fMRI data into 128-dimensional MuLan (Huang et al.,
084   2022b) embeddings, which are aligned with simple music descriptions in natural language, and then
085   generates music using MusicLM (Agostinelli et al., 2023). Hence, Denk et al. (2023) primarily
086   focuses on decoding the semantic features within the music, while the acoustic details are largely
087
      inferred from the MusicLM’s priors, resulting in limited reconstruction similarity. In addition, this
      model struggles to reconstruct audio beyond music and exhibits poor generalization capabilities.
088
089   To enhance the fine-grained decoding, we refocus our attention on neuroscience. As shown in Fig-
090   ure 1(a), research has indicated that in the cochlea and subcortical structures of the human ear,
091   sound is decomposed into frequency-specific temporal patterns similar to spectrograms (Pickles,
092   1988; Shamma & Micheyl, 2010; Schnupp, 2011; Moore, 2012). Further into the cerebral cor-
093
      tex, the human auditory system has two information processing pathways from low-level to high-
      level (Rauschecker & Tian, 2000; Kaas & Hackett, 2000; Scott & Johnsrude, 2003; Hickok & Poep-
094
      pel, 2007; Rauschecker & Scott, 2009). In recent years, an increasing amount of research has found
095
      that this cortical processing hierarchy aligns with the functional hierarchy of auditory DNN (Güçlü
096   et al., 2016; Kell et al., 2018; Vaidya et al., 2022; Millet et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023; Tuckute et al.,
097   2023; Giordano et al., 2023). The primary auditory cortex is more sensitive to shallow or intermedi-
098   ate DNN features, which represent low-level acoustic features, while the nonprimary auditory cortex
099   is more sensitive to deep DNN features, which represent high-level semantic features.
100
      Inspired by the acoustic-to-semantic stream, we model each physiological structure of the auditory
101
      processing pathway and propose an opposite coarse-to-fine audio reconstruction method, as shown
102   in Figure 1(b). We use non-invasive fMRI as the neural signal. To ensure generalizability across di-
103   verse audio stimuli, we choose pretrained models designed for broad applications: CLAP (Wu et al.,
104   2023), a contrastive audio-language model that maps audio to a semantic space; AudioMAE (Huang
105   et al., 2022a), a self-supervised audio model for learning rich acoustic representations; and a La-
106   tent Diffusion Model (LDM)(Rombach et al., 2022), which enables high-quality generation. First,
107   we conduct a coarse-to-fine brain decoding process, decoding fMRI data into the low-dimensional
      CLAP space for coarse-grained semantic features and further into the high-dimensional AudioMAE

      latent space for fine-grained acoustic features. Next, the fine-grained features guide mel-spectrogram
109   reconstruction via LDM, with the final waveform restored by a Vocoder(Kong et al., 2020a).
110
111   We validate our approach on three publicly available fMRI datasets with different types
112
      of audio stimuli: Brain2Sound (Park et al., 2023), Brain2Music (Nakai et al., 2022), and
      Brain2Speech (LeBel et al., 2023) datasets. Our model achieves state-of-the-art levels in metrics
113
      such as FD, FAD, and KL. Experimental results show that our coarse-to-fine framework signifi-
114
      cantly enhances the decoding of fine-grained audio embeddings and performs well across various
115   datasets, showcasing the potential as a general framework.
116
117   Analyzing the coarse-to-fine decoding process, we find that poor semantic decoding can weaken
118
      the semantic information in fine-grained embeddings. Given the complexity of sound signals and
      the low resolution of neural data (Park et al., 2023), brain-to-audio decoding, especially semantic
119
      decoding, is highly challenging. To address this, we provide simple semantic prompts (e.g., music
120
      genres or speaker genders) during decoding. Experiments show that prompts enhance the semantic
121   quality of reconstructed audio when decoded features are insufficient.
122
123   Our contributions are as follows: (1) We propose a coarse-to-fine neural decoding model and recon-
124
      struct high-quality waveforms with both semantic and detailed information. We also confirm that
      coarse-to-fine decoding is superior to solely fine-grained decoding. (2) Our model achieves good
125
      results on datasets with three different kinds of stimuli, demonstrating its strong transferability. It
126
      can serve as a universal brain-to-audio framework. (3) We attempt to provide semantic prompts and
127   prove that they can enhance the reconstruction quality when semantic decoding is challenging. The
128   code is anonymously released at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/C2F-LDM.

133   Let y ∈ RL represent an audio stimulus and x ∈ RV represent the corresponding fMRI signal,
134   where L is the length of the audio samples and V is the number of voxels in x. The brain-to-
135   audio reconstruction process can be formulated as R : x 7→ y. Our approach is to first decode an
136   intermediate representation c from x, and then generate y using a generative model G conditioned
137   on c. To obtain the condition c, we follow a coarse-to-fine process. First, we perform a coarse-
138
      grained decoding by a Semantic Decoder DSem : x 7→ s to extract the semantic embedding s from
      fMRI. Then, we use a semantically-guided Acoustic Decoder DAco : (s, x) 7→ c to jointly decode
139
      the condition c with both semantics and acoustic details. After decoding, we use an LDM as the
140
      generative model G : c 7→ y to reconstruct the stimulus audio conditioned on c. We will introduce
141   the coarse-grained decoding process of DSem in Section 2.1.1, discuss the design of DAco and the
142   fine-grained decoding process in Section 2.1.2, and describe the training of G in Section 2.2.

      2.1       C OARSE - TO - FINE BRAIN DECODING

146   2.1.1       C OARSE - GRAINED SEMANTIC DECODING
147
148   We use the CLAP feature as the coarse-grained semantic embedding of audio. CLAP, or contrastive
149   language-audio pretraining (Wu et al., 2023), is a pretrained multi-modal model that aligns represen-
150   tations of audio with natural language descriptions. Pretrained on LAION-Audio-630K dataset (Wu
151   et al., 2023) containing audios of human speech and song, natural sounds, and audio effects music,
152   CLAP features are semantically aligned with various categories of audios, providing rich semantic
153
      information.
154   We model the Semantic Decoder DSem : x 7→ s as a ridge regression model. As shown in Figure
155   2, we firstly use the final-layer feature of CLAP’s Audio Encoder as the ground truth semantic
156   feature of the stimulus audio y, denoted as sgt ∈ R512 . Then, we perform the L2-regularized linear
157   regression from x to sgt using PyFastL2LiR1 toolkit, which provides fast ridge regression and voxel
158   selection functionalities. For each dimension of sgt , we only select 500 voxels for regression based
159   on the correlation coefficient. Thus, we obtain a sparse mapping matrix W ∈ RV ×512 and a bias
160   b ∈ R512 . The semantic embedding s of fMRI can be inferred by s = xW + b and s ∈ R512 .

180   2.1.2     F INE - GRAINED ACOUSTIC DECODING
181
182   We use the AudioMAE latent feature as the fine-grained acoustic embedding of audio. AudioMAE,
183   or audio mask autoencoder (Huang et al., 2022a), is a self-supervised pretrained model, which con-
184   sists of an encoder E A and a decoder DA and focuses on the reconstruction of the masked patches.
185   The reason we choose the AudioMAE latent embedding as the acoustic feature instead of other DNN
186   features is threefold: (1) AudioMAE is trained on a generative task, which retains more low-level
187   acoustic details compared to the discriminative models like VGGish-ish (Iashin & Rahtu, 2021)
188   used in Park et al. (2023). (2) Compared to the normal autoencoder used in Chen et al. (2024), Au-
189   dioMAE performs a masked patch prediction task, which models the whole patches of the spectro-
190   gram. The empirical evidence (Liu et al., 2023b) shows that this makes the AudioMAE feature space
191   more inclined to cluster audio of the same category together compared to VAE, indicating that Au-
192
      dioMAE better preserves high-level semantic information. (3) Pretrained on AudioSet (Gemmeke
      et al., 2017) which consists of natural sounds, human and animal sounds, and music, AudioMAE
193
      can work well in the general audio domain. In comparison, the MuLan (Huang et al., 2022b) used
194
      in Denk et al. (2023) and Wav2Vec 2.0 (Baevski et al., 2020) used in Kim et al. (2023) can solely
195   be utilized for music or speech. Considering all the points mentioned above, AudioMAE features
196   are highly suitable for fine-grained features in our method, containing rich semantic and acoustic
197   details.
198
      As shown in Figure 2, we first transform the stimulus waveform y into 128 Kaldi (Povey et al.,
199
      2011)-compatible Mel-frequency bands with a 25ms Hanning window that shifts every 10 ms fol-
200
      lowing AudioMAE (Huang et al., 2022a), obtaining the mel-spectrogram m. Then we divide m
201   into 16×16 patches mp ∈ RNpatch ×256 and encode the patches into cgt = E A (mp ) ∈ RNpatch ×768
202   with no mask, where Npatch represents the number of patches. cgt is then decoded into the recon-
203   structed patches mpupp = DA (cgt ) and unpatchified into the mel-spectrogram mupp . We consider
204   cgt as the ground truth acoustic feature of the stimulus audio y and mupp as an upper bound for the
205   reconstructed mel-spectrogram.
206
      We model the Acoustic Decoder DAco : (s, x) 7→ c as a Transformer-based model, which captures
207
      the dependencies between s and x, and decodes fMRI into the latent space of AudioMAE through
208   a Seq2Seq generation. First, we project s and x into the 768-dimensional representation space of
209   the Transformer. For s, we use a linear layer to project it to a semantic token s′ . For x, we select
210   768 voxels with the highest responses based on the mapping matrix W , forming the fMRI token x′ .
211   Then we concat the tokens and encode them with a Transformer Encoder E T , obtaining the neural
212   embedding n = E T ([s′ , x′ ]). We create a learnable embedding q as the query to a Transformer
213   Decoder DT along with n as key and value, obtaining the decoded acoustic feature c = DT (q, n).
214

280   2.3   C ONDITIONAL RECONSTRUCTION
281
282   In practical applications, brain-to-audio reconstruction is not always unconditional. Firstly, com-
283   pared to images, audios exhibit strong temporal correlations. If a subject listens to a long segment of
284   stimulus audio but only a portion needs to be reconstructed using brain signals, considering that other
285
      audio segments and the target segments may be semantically similar, they can serve as conditions
      to provide additional semantic information. Secondly, we may know in advance the coarse-grained
286
      category (e.g., human speech or animal sound) of the audio to be reconstructed. Given the challenge
287
      of semantic decoding from fMRI (see Section 3.3 for details), we can use the coarse-grained cate-
288   gory as the semantic prior to guide the reconstruction process. Thus, we attempt to provide semantic
289   prompts in the form of audio or text to our model for conditional reconstruction, to assess whether
290   it can enhance the quality of the reconstructed audio.
291
      The conditional reconstruction process is straightforward. We utilize CLAP’s Text Encoder and
292
      Audio Encoder to extract the semantic embedding sprompt of the text prompt and audio prompt.
293
      Then we replace s with sprompt as input to DAco : (sprompt , x) 7→ c, to obtain the fine-grained
294
      acoustic embedding c. Finally, we use G : c 7→ y to reconstruct the stimulus audio conditioned on c.

300
      Datasets. We use three publicly available fMRI datasets to validate our method’s performance
301
      across different kinds of stimuli: Brain2Sound (Park et al., 2023), Brain2Music (Nakai et al., 2022),
302
      and Brain2Speech (LeBel et al., 2023) datasets. Brain2Sound Dataset comprises fMRI signals from
303   five subjects listening to 4-second natural sounds, including human speech, animal, musical instru-
304   ment, and environmental sounds. The dataset consists of 14,400 training samples and 150 test sam-
305   ples. Brain2Music Dataset comprises fMRI signals from five subjects listening to 1.5-second music
306   clips, consisting of 4,800 training samples and 600 test samples. Brain2Speech Dataset comprises
307   fMRI signals from seven subjects listening to 2-second voice segments, consisting of 9,137 training
308   samples and 595 test samples. For detailed information about the datasets and the preprocessing
309   methods, please refer to section A.1.
310
311   Metrics. We use PCC (Pearson Correlation Coefficient) and PSNR (Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio)
312   to measure the similarity between the mel-spectrograms of reconstructed audio and stimulus audio,
313
      evaluating the low-level fidelity quality. In addition, we use FD, FAD, KL, and CLAP score, which
      are commonly employed in audio generation tasks, to evaluate the high-level perceptual quality of
314
      the reconstructed audio. FD (Fréchet Distance) calculates the distance in features between gener-
315
      ated samples and target samples, extracted from an audio classifier PANNs (Kong et al., 2020b).
316   KL (Kullback–Leibler divergence) calculates the KL divergence of classification logits based on
317   PANNs. FAD (Fréchet Audio Distance) is similar to FD, but it uses VGGish (Kilgour et al., 2018).
318   CLAP score calculates the cosine similarity of CLAP (Wu et al., 2023) embeddings. In our experi-
319   ments, each subject is trained and tested individually, and the metrics are averaged across subjects.
320

410   3.3     S EMANTIC ANALYSIS OF ACOUSTIC FEATURES
411
      An intuitive idea is that the introduction of coarse-grained semantic decoding enhances the semantic
412
      information in the acoustic features, thereby improving the fine-grained decoding. Is this really the
413
      case? We will discuss this issue in this section.
414
415   It is generally believed that a representation space with strong semantic information typically clus-
416   ters samples of the same category while separating those of different categories. Therefore, we
417
      conduct a classification experiment using two datasets with clear category labels, the Brain2Music
      Dataset with the labels of music genres (10 classes) and the Brain2Speech Dataset with the labels
418
      of speakers’ genders (2 classes). Gender is chosen as the semantic label for human speech primarily
419
      because it is the most intuitive and easily annotated attribute within speech semantics. Addition-
420   ally, we reference the LAION-Audio-630K dataset (Wu et al., 2023) used by CLAP, where gender
421   information serves as a key element in the text captions for human speech. We perform 5-fold
422   cross-validation on the test set, using SVM to classify acoustic features obtained through coarse-to-
423   fine decoding or directly fine-grained decoding. The average classification accuracy measures the
424   semantic information in the acoustic features, with identical experimental conditions ensuring an
425   unbiased comparison.
426   As shown in Figure 5, the chance levels are 0.1 and 0.5 for the two datasets, while the upper
427   bounds are the classification accuracy on the ground truth acoustic features, which are 0.33 and
428   0.95. After introducing the coarse-grained decoding, the classification accuracy for each subject
429   in the Brain2Music Dataset either increases or remains essentially unchanged. However, in the
430   Brain2Speech Dataset, the classification accuracy for some subjects decreases. It suggests that the
431   semantic information in the acoustic features is diminishing, while the coarse-grained decoding pri-
      marily enhances the low-level acoustic information.

461   hanced if the semantic features of guidance are poor. In such cases, the coarse-grained decoding
462
      mainly enhances the low-level acoustic information.

466
      acoustic features during guided decoding. Therefore, in the conditional reconstruction task men-
467
      tioned in Section 2.3, it is preferable to use prompt-based semantic features as coarse-grained fea-
468   tures instead of decoded ones. We define two types of prompts: (1) Text prompts, which specify
469   the stimulus audio category. For the Brain2Music Dataset, these include 10 music genres (e.g., pop
470   music, rock music), while for the Brain2Speech Dataset, they indicate the speaker’s gender (man
471   speaking or woman speaking). (2) Audio prompts, where the last 10 stimulus audio clips from two
472   test set stories in the Brain2Speech Dataset are used as prompts, with results averaged.
473

474   in Section 2.1.2 under three conditions: Pgt = 0.0, 0.25 and 0.5. The results are shown in Table
475   3. We observe that incorporating ground truth semantic features during training improves brain-
476   to-audio reconstruction, but higher Pgt values do not always yield better results. For instance, in
477   the Brain2Music Dataset, Pgt = 0.5 performs worse than Pgt = 0.25. Since fMRI data is used
478   exclusively during testing, excessively high Pgt creates a mismatch between training and testing,
479   reducing reconstruction accuracy. Balancing decoding from fMRI and ground truth, we select Pgt =
480   0.25 as the optimal value based on metrics from both datasets.
481   For the Brain2Music Dataset, the high-level metrics decrease after incorporating the text prompts.

485   in the Brain2Music Dataset as mentioned in Section 3.3, the loss of the individual specificity across

506   4      L IMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK
507
508
      Temporal resolution. Given the advantages of high spatial resolution and high signal-to-noise
509   ratio in non-invasive neural signals, fMRI has been commonly employed in the field of neural en-
510   coding and decoding. Research (Santoro et al., 2017) has confirmed that the reconstruction from the
511   BOLD response (TR=2.6s) can exhibit a temporal specificity of about 200 ms, which is adequate
512   for capturing certain semantics and details of the audio. Since then, numerous works (Santoro et al.,
513   2017; Park et al., 2023; Denk et al., 2023) and datasets (Nastase et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021; Nakai
514   et al., 2022; Park et al., 2023; LeBel et al., 2023) have emerged to support research on fMRI-to-audio
515   tasks. However, the limited temporal resolution of fMRI consistently hampers the temporal decod-
516   ing of audio. The aim of this article is to enhance the reconstruction performance further from a
517
      neuroscience perspective. To make a breakthrough in temporal decoding, it is imperative to leverage
      other neural signals with high temporal resolution, such as EEG and MEG.
518
519
520
      Model and voxel selection. The main purpose of this article is to illustrate the superiority of hi-
      erarchical decoding over direct decoding. Therefore, we build a generic brain-to-audio framework,
521
      selecting the most suitable models, CLAP and AudioMAE, without comparing them to other repre-
522
      sentation models. In the future, we will switch to different models within our framework to attempt
523   further improvement of reconstruction results. Furthermore, we utilize all the voxels of the auditory
524   cortex (AC) in our work. However, there are gradients in the voxels of different brain regions within
525   the AC (Kell et al., 2018; Tuckute et al., 2023; Giordano et al., 2023). In the future, we plan to
526   consider the gradients of voxels to further enhance the hierarchy of information processing.
527

      5      C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we propose a novel coarse-to-fine audio reconstruction method inspired by the hier-
      archical processing of the human auditory system. Our method begins by decoding fMRI into the
532
      CLAP space to extract coarse-grained semantic features. Subsequently, leveraging these semantic
533
      features, we decode fMRI into the AudioMAE latent space to capture fine-grained acoustic features.
534   Next, we use the acoustic features as conditions to reconstruct the stimulus audio using a Latent
535   Diffusion Model. By validating on three diverse fMRI datasets, our method has shown superior
536   performance in brain-to-audio reconstruction compared to previous fine-grained methods. We have
537   illustrated its state-of-the-art capabilities, achieving remarkable results in FD, FAD, KL, and CLAP
538   score. The integration of semantic prompts during decoding further enhances the semantics of re-
539   constructed audio, particularly when dealing with suboptimal semantic decoding. We will discuss
      the broader impacts of our paper in Section A.7.

781   Brain2Sound Dataset As proposed by Park et al. (2023), this dataset3 records the fMRI signals
782   of five subjects (one female) while they are listening to natural sounds, including human speech,
783   animal, musical instrument, and environmental sounds. fMRI data are acquired using a 3.0-Tesla
784   Siemens MAGNETOM Verio scanner at the Kyoto University Institute for the Future of Human
785   Society. Functional images that cover the entire brain are obtained with TR = 2,000 ms, TE = 44.8
786   ms, flip angle = 70 deg, FOV = 192 × 192 mm, voxel size=2 × 2 × 2 mm, number of slices = 76 and
787   multi-band factor = 4. We utilize fMRI data preprocessed by Park et al. (2023), primarily involving
788   motion correction, slice time correction, co-registration, BOLD time-series resampling, etc.
789   The stimuli consist of 1,250 8-s natural sound segments, with 1,200 for the training set and 50 for the
790   test set, selected from the VGGSound dataset (Chen et al., 2020). All the sounds are extracted from
791   the videos uploaded to YouTube. To increase the sample number, we preprocess the audio segments
792   in the same way as Park et al. (2023): 4-s sliding windows are utilized with a 2-s stride to extract 3
793   4-s segments. All audio clips are resampled to 16kHz. During the collection of fMRI signals, each
794   stimulus is repeated four times, resulting in 14,400 samples4 for the training set (1,200 stimuli × 4
795
      repetitions × 3 samples = 14,400 samples). For the test set, we average the multiple fMRI samples,
      resulting in 150 samples (50 stimuli × 3 samples = 150 samples).

834   A.2    E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
835
836   In the stage of coarse-grained decoding, for the Brain2Sound and Brain2Speech datasets, we only
837   utilize voxels from the auditory cortex (AC) area, whereas for the Brain2Music Dataset, we use
838   voxels from the entire brain. The specific brain regions and voxels can be found in Table 4.
839   In the stage of fine-grained decoding, we utilize a 4-layer Transformer Encoder and Decoder in DAco
840   and use the default configuration of AudioMAE (Huang et al., 2022a), initialized with the pretrained
841   weights.8 The AudioMAE Encoder is a vanilla 12-layer ViT-B, while the AudioMAE Decoder is
842   a 16-layer Transformer with shifted local attention. Since AudioMAE requires 10-second audios
843   (128×1024 mel-spectrograms) as inputs, we duplicate the stimulus waveforms to 10 seconds. After
844   encoding with the AudioMAE Encoder E A , we select the embeddings of the first Npatch patches as
845
      cgt , corresponding to the length of the stimulus audio. We set Npatch = 208 for the Brain2Sound
      Dataset, Npatch = 80 for the Brain2Music Dataset, and Npatch = 112 for the Brain2Speech Dataset.
846
847   In the stage of brain-to-audio reconstruction, we follow the formulation in AudioLDM2 (Liu
848   et al., 2023b) to implement the LDM G and utilize two checkpoints9 as the initialization weights:
849   audioldm2-full for the Brain2Sound and Brain2Music datasets, and audioldm2-speech-gigaspeech
850   for the Brain2Speech dataset.
851   We use the AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) optimizer to train DAco and DA with a learning
852   rate of 1e-6, and train G with a learning rate of 1e-4. We train on the Brain2Sound, Brain2Music, and
853   Brain2Speech datasets with a batch size of 8 for 30, 40, and 30 epoches. All training is completed
854   on a single NVIDIA A100 80GB GPU.

876   A.4     C OMPARISON WITH C2F-Decoder
877
878
      When performing fine-grained decoding, although we use the AudioMAE Decoder to reconstruct
      the mel-spectrogram, it is not suitable to serve as the generative model for our method. There
879
      are two main reasons for this: (1) The mel bins and window parameters of the mel-spectrograms
880
      in AudioMAE do not align with those of commonly used pretrained Vocoders. This mismatch
881   prevents the generated mel-spectrograms from being directly converted into audio. Moreover, the
882   cost of training a compatible Vocoder from scratch is prohibitively high. (2) The primary task
883   of the AudioMAE Decoder is to predict masked patches, with a focus on low-level details of the
884   spectrogram. This limitation leads to insufficient reconstruction quality in terms of semantic content.
885   In contrast, the mel-spectrograms generated by LDM can be directly restored to audio using the
886   pretrained HiFiGAN (Kong et al., 2020a) vocoder, and the generated audio has richer semantic and
887   acoustic details.
888   To investigate the reconstruction performance of C2F-Decoder, we need to transform the mel-
889   spectrograms generated by the AudioMAE Decoder, denoted as mA , into mel-spectrograms that
890   the Vocoder can accept, denoted as mV . We assume that mA and mV have a linear relationship, so
891   we use an L2-regularized linear regression model trained on mA and mV of the stimulus audio in
892   the training set. The results in the test set are as follows: PCC = 0.938 in the Brain2Sound Dataset,
893   PCC = 0.967 in the Brain2Music and Brain2Speech datasets. Based on the results, we believe that
894   this transformation is almost lossless. As shown in Figure 7, Table 1 and Table 2, C2F-Decoder is
895
      similar to the direct decoding methods in that they both focus on modeling the overall spectrograms
      but lack details and semantic information compared to C2F-LDM. It demonstrates the superiority of
896
      LDM over employing the AudioMAE Decoder directly.

       A.7   B ROADER I MPACTS
973
974    This research contributes to enhancing our understanding of brain function and cognitive processes,
975    playing a crucial role in further exploring the mechanisms of the human auditory system and pro-
976    moting the development of related technologies. In contrast to audio tasks like audio generation,
977    speech synthesis, and sound editing, our neural decoding task focuses on reconstructing the audio
978    heard by the subjects. The primary application scenario is a brain-computer interface system de-
979    signed to aid individuals with voice disorders. This task does not pose potential ethical challenges
980
       such as forging or tampering with someone’s voice. In addition, the experiments are conducted in a
       controlled laboratory setting with the participants’ consent and cooperation. It is not applicable in
981
       real-world scenarios, thereby posing a minimal risk of privacy leakage.
