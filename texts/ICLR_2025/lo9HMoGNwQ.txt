011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            This work introduces the Sequential Multiple Instance Learning (SMIL) framework,
014            addressing the challenge of interpreting sequential, variable-length sequences of
015            medical images with a single diagnostic label. Diverging from traditional MIL
016
               approaches that treat image sequences as unordered sets, SMIL systematically
               integrates the sequential nature of clinical imaging. We develop a bidirectional
017
               Transformer architecture, BiSMIL, that optimizes for both early and final prediction
018
               accuracies through a novel training procedure to balance diagnostic accuracy with
019            operational efficiency. We evaluate BiSMIL on three medical image datasets to
020            demonstrate that it simultaneously achieves state-of-the-art final accuracy and
021            superior performance in early prediction accuracy, requiring 30-50% fewer images
022            for a similar level of performance compared to existing models. Additionally, we
023            introduce SMILU, an interpretable uncertainty metric that outperforms traditional
024            metrics in identifying challenging instances.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Medical imaging is a fundamental component of modern medical diagnosis. With the surge in
030   availability of imaging, there has been widespread interest in leveraging computer vision techniques
031   to aid interpretation of medical images.
032   A common challenge in medical imaging is that each image study often contains multiple number
033   of image instances, with only one associated diagnostic label. The sequence length can also vary
034   significantly across patients, making conventional deep learning models, largely tailored for fixed
035   input sizes, inappropriate.
036   To solve this problem, there has been a growing literature to develop Multiple Instance Learning
037   (MIL) methods that can tackle this setting. There has been significant work in developing MIL
038   methods for whole slide images (Courtiol et al., 2018; Campanella et al., 2019; Shao et al., 2021b;
039   Li et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023), where the set of images are
040   often treated as an order-independent set denoted as a "bag". In many clinical imaging settings,
041   however, clinicians are creating the images sequentially to discover features of interest. They often
042   have control over how many sequential images should be created (e.g. CT scan levels) or when to
043   stop the sequential imaging process (e.g. ultrasound). This sequential nature is currently largely
044
      ignored in applications of MIL to clinical imaging (Ostrowski et al., 2023; Fuhrman et al., 2023).
045   In this work, we present a Sequential MIL (SMIL) framework that aims to systematically incorporate
046   the sequential nature of clinical imaging into MIL. In particular, the sequential nature of clinical
047   imaging generates a unique tradeoff between accuracy and efficiency: as the clinician creates more
048   images, the resulting diagnostic accuracy is likely to increase, but it comes at the expense of efficiency
049   and patient radiation exposure. Therefore, in the SMIL framework, it is critical to develop methods
050
      that can achieve accurate predictions in an early subsequence. However, existing medical imaging
      datasets most often do not have labels for subsequences, and the bag-level label might not be correct
051
      for the subsequence, making training difficult.
052
053   To tackle the SMIL framework, we formulate a new bidirectional Transformer architecture, BiSMIL,
      that exploits the sequential nature of clinical images. We further develop a novel training procedure


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      for the BiSMIL model that encourages the model to give an accurate early prediction while ensuring
055   it has a high final accuracy.
056
057   We evaluate the BiSMIL model on three independent medical image datasets, including a new dataset
058
      on ultrasounds for pediatric urology, where the sonographer has full control over the number of
      images he/she wishes to create to classify urinary tract dilation. We demonstrate that the BiSMIL
059
      model is able to consistently outperform existing approaches in both final prediction accuracy and
060
      early prediction accuracy. Importantly, the BiSMIL model can achieve high early prediction accuracy
061   with 40%-60% fewer instances compared to existing models.
062
063   To further the applicability of the model, we also develop an interpretable, sequence-aware uncertainty
064
      metric SMILU that allows clinicians to understand the certainty of the SMIL prediction. SMILU
      depends not only on its final prediction, but also the incremental predictions over the sequence of
065
      images. Our experiments demonstrate that the SMILU metric is able to better capture difficult-to-
066
      classify, uncertain instances better than common metrics that are based solely on the final output.
067
068   In summary, our contributions are three-fold:
069
             • We introduce the SMIL framework that systematically incorporates the sequential structure
070
               of clinical imaging into MIL. The SMIL framework exhibits unique challenges for MIL
071
               methods to provide early, accurate predictions without access to subsequence labels.
072
             • We propose a bidirectional transformer architecture, BiSMIL, to tackle the SMIL framework,
073
               and formulate a novel training procedure to reliably encourage accurate early predictions
074            while still ensuring high accuracy for final predictions.
075
             • We provide an interpretable, sequence-aware uncertainty metric SMILU that allows clin-
076            icians to understand the certainty of the SMIL prediction. We show that the uncertainty
077            metric outperforms common metrics in recognizing uncertain, difficult-to-classify instances
078            in the SMIL framework.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
082
      Multiple Instance Learning (MIL). Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) is a weakly supervised
083
      learning framework, wherein instances are grouped into bags with labels designated at the bag level
084
      (Dietterich et al., 1997; Ramon and De Raedt, 2000; Andrews et al., 2002; Settles et al., 2007; Li and
085   Vasconcelos, 2015; Ilse et al., 2018). There has been a particularly high level of interest in utilizing
086   the MIL framework for histopathology slides which possesses high resolutions up to 105 × 105 . To
087   address the issue of training neural networks on such images, each slide is commonly divided into
088   hundreds or thousands of tiles, and the MIL framework has been widely developed and utilized for
089   this application (Courtiol et al., 2018; Campanella et al., 2019; Shao et al., 2021a;b; Li et al., 2021;
090   Lu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023). However, this means that traditionally MIL
091   assumes an absence of sequential interactions between instances. The few works that do capture
092   relationships between instances within a bag (Zhou et al., 2009; Tu et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2023) do
093
      not systematically consider the sequential nature of clinical imaging.
094   Interpretability for MIL. There has been significant work in enhancing the interpretability of
095   MIL methods. Most work has focused on identifying particular instances in a bag that contribute
096   significantly to the final prediction (Pirovano et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2019a; Javed et al., 2022; Ilse
097   et al., 2018; Molnar, 2020; Early et al., 2022). Our focus diverges from these works as we aim to
098   provide a bag-level metric that signals the certainty of the MIL model in predicting a particular bag.

127   Xi is associated with a binary label yi ∈ {0, 1}, where yi = 0 if all instances are negative and yi = 1

131   In the Sequential MIL (SMIL) Framework, instances within each bag i are generated sequentially,
132   implying an associated time tij for each instance xij , with tij < tik for all j < k and i ∈ [n].
133   Therefore, we denote Xi as a sequence rather than a bag to emphasize this temporal dependance.
134   The aim is to provide a model f that, upon the generation of the j-th image, offers an incremental
135   prediction pij = f (Xji ) ∈ [0, 1], reflecting the likelihood that the current subsequence Xji =
136   {xi1 , . . . , xij } warrants a positive diagnosis. An accurate incremental prediction pij can facilitate
137
      clinicians to make informed decisions on whether to change or terminate the imaging sequence. This
      can improve clinical efficiency and reduce radiation exposure.
138
139   Thus, in the SMIL framework, accurate, early incremental predictions are crucial for a successful
140   model. To further illustrate this concept, in Figure 2, we showcase the incremental prediction results
141   for a positive sample (yi = 1) from one of the medical imaging datasets. Notably, we observe that the
142   prediction values indicate the 2nd, 5th, and 6th image appears to have significantly contributed to a
143
      positive prediction. We note that these corresponded with a high attention value to these images, and
      importantly corresponded with evaluation from a clinician, who stated that only these three images
144
      showed any signs of abnormality. Given the three strong incremental predictions, the clinician can
145
      arguably stop the imaging sequence after the 6th instance to improve operational efficiency.
146
147   However, Figure 2 also highlights a fundamental challenge within the SMIL framework: Ideally, each
148   subsequence Xji would be matched with a specific label to generate accurate incremental prediction
149   values, yet the reality of medical imaging is such that records typically conclude with a singular, final
150   diagnosis for the entire collection of images. For a dataset of even modest size, say n ≈ 1000, the
151
      task of securing expert labels for every subsequence across all sequences becomes daunting, as the
      number of instances per sequence, mi usually ranges between 10 and 100. It is also insufficient to
152
      directly utilize the sequence-level label as a stand-in for the labels of individual subsequences, as
153
      any given subsequence may lack instances that are indicative of positive findings. In the example
154   of Figure 2, it would be incorrect to train the first subsequence Xi1 on the positive label yi with the
155   same weight as training the last subsequence Xi6 , as doing so would result in unrealistic incremental
156   predictions. Thus, in Section 3.2, we propose an innovative modeling and training approach designed
157   to navigate this challenge, enabling the generation of meaningful predictions for subsequences.

205   3.3   S UBSEQUENCE -AWARE T RAINING P ROCEDURE
206
      The goal of the SMIL Framework is to produce incremental predictions that achieve both high final
207
      accuracy and high early accuracy, while being faithful to the (unobserved) subsequence labels.
208
209   To satisfy all these objectives, we design a novel training procedure for the BiSMIL model. For
210   each dataset, we first determine a minimum subsequence percentage γ ≥ 50% so that the minimum
211   subsequence length for training each sequence is ⌊γmi ⌋. We can utilize cross-validation to select the
212
      optimal γ for each dataset, but our experiments suggest γ ∈ [50%, 70%] generally produce the best
      results. We include a sensitivity analysis of the γ values on our datasets in Appendix A.3.1 to reflect
213
      this fact.
214
215   Then for each l ∈ {⌊γmi ⌋, · · · , mi }, we take an l-length subsequence for both the front and

281   In many real-world scenarios, beyond accurate predictions, there is a significant need to understand
282   how certain a model is in making the prediction. This is particularly critical in the sequential clinical
283   imaging setting where the certainty in the current prediction can help the clinician determine whether
284   to continue, modify or terminate an imaging sequence. To further improve the applicability of the
285
      SMIL Framework, we introduce SMILU, a sequence-aware, interpretable uncertainty metric that
      combines two uncertainty representations to provide clinicians with a useful tool to determine the
286
      certainty of a MIL model.
287

373   scans forming a sequence. The average number of scans across each patient is 11.7. We collapse the
374
      different grades of UTD to a binary label of {0, 1} that indicates if UTD is present in the sequence of
      ultrasound scans. In the overall dataset, the prevalence of UTD is 48.3%.

377   (RSNA) challenge. We randomly selected a subset from the entire RSNA Dataset, comprising

      Precision, Recall, and F1 Score of all models across the three medical imaging datasets. We observe
      that across all metrics and all datasets, the BiSMIL model outperforms all leading benchmarks, often
402
      with statistical significance. These results reflect the importance of leveraging sequential information
403
      in clinical imaging datasets. In Appendix A.3.2, we demonstrate that the position embedding module
404   is an important driver of the BiSMIL’s performance, providing further evidence of the importance
405   of the image ordering. Furthermore, we observe that the BiSMIL model achieves moderate, but
406   statistically significant gains compared to the SiSMIL model, which suggests that bidirectionality
407   provides extra information that can improve the effectiveness of the model.

410
411   To further understand the performance of our BiSMIL model, we compare the accuracy of the BiSMIL
412   model against the three comparison models when only a subsequence of instances are revealed. We
413   only include the UTD and RSNA datasets for this experiment as the COVID CT scan dataset is
414   insufficiently large to draw conclusions. We observe in Figure 5.2 that in general, as more instances
415
      are added, the performance of all models increase. However, we observe that the BiSMIL model
      achieves high prediction accuracy significantly earlier than comparing methods: for the UTD dataset,
416
      with just 50% of the instances the BiSMIL model achieves an accuracy that is comparable to ADMIL
417
      with 100% of the instances and SA-DMIL with 70% of the instances. Alternatively, this means that
418   BiSMIL can achieve the same accuracy with 30 − 50% fewer instances compared to benchmarks. The
419   results are generally similar with the RSNA dataset. These results, together with Table 1, demonstrate
420   that our novel training procedure and bidirectional architecture can simultaneously achieve high final
421   accuracy while providing exceptional early accuracy.

      6    L IMITATIONS
460
461   Despite the promising results achieved by the BiSMIL model and the SMILU uncertainty metric
462
      across various datasets, this study includes multiple limitations.
463   First, we focus only on binary classification, while many medical imaging tasks admit natural
464   multi-class classification or regression formulations. It remains to be seen if a similar approach can
465   also perform in these contexts. Second, the proposed SMILU metric provides a novel approach to
466   quantify uncertainty in sequence predictions but the validation of this metric is primarily empirical.
467   A theoretically-grounded metric or formulation could further improve the usability of the metric for
468
      the clinical decision process. Finally, although our experiments encompass a range of conditions, the
      generalizability of our model to other tasks, such as MRI and X-ray, are untested. External validation
469
      of datasets from different institutions would also enhance the robustness of the performance.
470

472   7    C ONCLUSION
473
474   In conclusion, our research introduces the Sequential MIL (SMIL) framework that systematically
475   incorporates the sequential nature of clinical imaging into the MIL framework. The SMIL framework
476   presents new tradeoffs and challenges for MIL methods, as it is important in the SMIL framework
477   to provide accurate, early incremental predictions. We propose a bidirectional Transformer model,
478   BiSMIL, along with a novel training procedure that aims to balance the importance of an accurate
479   final prediction and an accurate early prediction. Experiments on multiple medical image datasets
480   demonstrate that the BiSMIL model is able to outperform current benchmarks on final prediction
481   accuracy while significantly improving the accuracy of incremental predictions. We further propose
482
      an interpretable, sequence-aware uncertainty metric, SMILU, that is able to better capture difficult-to-
      predict instances compared to metrics that rely solely on the final output. This again demonstrates the
483
      importance of incorporating the sequential nature of the setting.
484
485   Although this work has largely focused on clinical imaging settings, there are other important settings
      that share this sequential multi-instance learning structure. Common examples include time-series

      event prediction and online video analysis. We hope this work can encourage further method
487   development within this setting.
