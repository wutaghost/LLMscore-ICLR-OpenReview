009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper concerns the evaluation of reward models used in language modeling.
012            A reward model is a function that takes a prompt and a response and assigns a
013            score indicating how “good” that response is for the prompt. A key challenge
014            is that reward models are usually imperfect proxies for actual preferences. For
015            example, we may worry that a model trained to reward helpfulness learns to
016            instead prefer longer responses. In this paper, we develop an evaluation method,
017            RATE (Rewrite-based Attribute Treatment Estimators), that allows us to measure
018
               the causal effect of a given attribute of a response (e.g., length) on the reward
               assigned to that response. The core idea is to use large language models to rewrite
019
               responses to produce imperfect counterfactuals, and to adjust for rewriting error by
020
               rewriting twice. We show that the RATE estimator is consistent under reasonable
021            assumptions. We demonstrate the effectiveness of RATE on synthetic and real-
022            world data, showing that it can accurately estimate the effect of a given attribute on
023            the reward model.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027   In the context of large language models (LLMs), reward models are functions that take a prompt
028   and a response as inputs and return a real number indicating how good the response is for the
029   prompt. Such models are useful in a variety of settings, including alignment of large language
030   models, ranking output samples (e.g., to use in a best-of-n sampling procedure), or evaluation of
031   LLM performance. Ideally, reward models would directly and perfectly measure whatever aspect of
032   the output is important—e.g., we might have such a reward for mathematical problem solving based
033
      on whether the generated response is correct. However, commonly, reward models are learned from
      training data that imperfectly measures somewhat nebulous attributes. For example, a common task is
034
      to train a reward model based on human preferences for which of two responses is more helpful. This
035
      results in a challenge where, even with a reward model in hand, we are not certain what it is actually
036   rewarding. For example, we might worry that a model trained to reward helpfulness learns to instead
037   simply prefer longer responses (Park et al., 2024c; Shen et al., 2023; Singhal et al., 2024).
038
039
      Accordingly, we would like a way to measure how sensitive a reward model is to a given attribute of
      a response. A straightforward approach would be to collect a dataset of prompt/response pairs, label
040
      each response as having or not having the attribute of interest, and then compare the average reward
041
      assigned to responses with and without the attribute. However, this approach has the limitation that it
042   does not account for ‘spurious’ correlations that may exist in the data. For example, it may be that
043   longer responses are more likely to be helpful (even though simply making a response longer does
044   not make it more helpful). Then, if we applied the straightforward approach to this data to assess
045   whether a given model is rewarding helpfulness, we would conclude that it is even if the model only
046   rewards length and is indifferent to helpfulness. If we then used this reward model as a proxy for
047   helpfulness in a downstream alignment task, then the actual effect of alignment would be to make
048   responses longer, without (necessarily) affecting helpfulness.
049   Instead, we are actually interested in knowing how the reward would change if we were to change
050   some attribute in the response, such as length, while holding all else fixed. This is the causal effect of
051   the attribute on the reward. There is a growing literature on estimating the causal effects of attributes
052   of text (Feder et al., 2022). Generally, these provide methods for estimating the causal effect using
053   observational data, where we have only the naturally occurring variation in the data to work with.
      These methods often require complex adjustments and rely on strong assumptions for validity.

074
075         1. We develop a practical method of estimating the causal effect of an attribute of a response

      Suppose we have a dataset of prompt-completion pairs {(xi , y ij )}, where the xi are prompts and the
085
      y ij are completions (also referred to as ‘responses’). We have a reward model R(xi , y ij ) that assigns
086
      a scalar reward to each prompt-completion pair. We are interested in understanding how the reward
087   model is sensitive to some attribute W in the completions, where wij = hW (y ij ) is a binary attribute
088   value which the measurable function hW ‘reads’ from the completion. For example, W might be
089   helpfulness, in which case wij = 1 if y ij is helpful and wij = 0 otherwise.
090
      We focus on binary attributes for simplicity—many attributes of interest (such as length) can often be
091
      naturally binarized (see Section 6).

096
      are higher than the rewards for the unhelpful responses. Mathematically, we define this average

      Treatment Effects To isolate the effect of a given attribute on the reward model, we must take
125   a causal perspective. Concretely, we can formalize the responsiveness of a reward model to some
126   attribute W as the average treatment effect (ATE) of W on the reward:
127
128                                      ATE = E[R(X, Y (1)) − R(X, Y (0))]
129   where X is a random variable for the prompt, and Y (1) and Y (0) are potential outcomes for responses.
130   This quantity is the expected change in reward if we were to change the attribute W from 0 to 1,
131   while keeping all other aspects of the response fixed. The random pair of responses (Y (0), Y (1)) are
132   identical in all aspects except for the attribute W —e.g., if W is helpfulness then each counterfactual
133   response should have the same writing level, sentiment, topic, etc. In general, we only actually
134   observe one of the counterfactual responses in our dataset (Figure 1, left).
135   Choice of Estimand Beyond the ATE, we will also consider the average treatment effect on the
136   treated (ATT) and the average treatment effect on the untreated (ATU). These are defined as:
137
138
                                    ATT = E [R(X, Y (1)) − R(X, Y (0))|W = 1]
139                                 ATU = E [R(X, Y (1)) − R(X, Y (0))|W = 0]
140
141   Intuitively, if W = 1 is a helpful response, the ATT measures the change in reward when we take
142   a helpful response and make it less helpful, and the ATU measures the change in reward when we
143
      take an unhelpful response and make it more helpful. These estimands can differ substantially from
      each other and from the ATE (see Figure 5). There is no reason to expect these quantities to align in
144
      general, so some thought should be given to which is most relevant to the question at hand. Indeed,
145
      even human preferences are often asymmetric (Kahneman & Tversky, 2013), so we might expect
146   reward model preferences to be as well.

148   3    RATE: R EWRITE - BASED ATTRIBUTE T REATMENT E STIMATORS
149
150   Whatever our choice of estimand, we need a method to estimate it. Here, we develop a method,
151
      RATE, that uses rewrites to estimate the causal effect of an attribute on a reward model. The core idea
      is to create pairs of responses where the only difference is in the attribute of interest. For example,
152
      we might modify a response to change its sentiment from positive to negative, while keeping all other
153
      aspects of the response the same (see Table 1). The goal is for these modified responses to directly
154   approximate the unobserved counterfactual responses.
155
156   Rewrites With LLMs In practice, we implement rewrites using a large language model (LLM). We
157   begin with a labeled dataset containing ground truth binary variables for attributes such as complexity,
158
      sentiment, or helpfulness. We then instruct the LLM to rewrite the responses to the opposite state of
      the binary variable. For example, a typical instruction might be: “Rewrite this response to express
159
      negative sentiment and change nothing else.”
160
161   We denote the rewrite operation that takes a response y ij and a desired attribute value w′ and returns
      a modified response with W = w′ as Re(y ij , w′ ).

      Rewrite Instructions There is substantial freedom in the precise instructions we give to an LLM
184
      to generate rewrites. For instance, when rewriting for ‘helpfulness’, we might instruct the LLM to
185   “Rewrite this response to be more helpful”, or instruct it to “Rewrite this response to be more helpful,
186   providing additional relevant information or clarification.” In this example, the second instruction
187   makes the meaning of “helpful” more precise. Generally, changing the instruction changes the nature
188   of the rewrites generated, and thus changes the attribute that is being modified.
189
      This is inevitable. Ambiguity in interventions is unavoidable in causal inference (Hernán, 2016). In
190
      our context, this is obvious: there is subjectivity in what helpfulness, complexity, or sentiment actually
191   mean. An advantage of the rewrite approach is that it allows us to use natural language to specify,
192   as clearly as possible, what property we are actually trying to modify. We can understand whether
193   our instructions are having the intended effect by qualitatively examining the rewritten outputs and
194   checking that they vary the attribute of interest while leaving the rest of the response unchanged.
195   In practice, finding effective rewrite instructions requires an iterative cycle of generating rewrites,
196   examining the responses, and adjusting the rewrite prompt to be more clear and specific.
197   Imperfect Rewrites If the rewrites produced perfect counterfactuals, it would then be straight-
198   forward to estimate the causal effect of the attributes. Namely, we could compare the rewards of
199   the original responses to the rewards of the rewrites. However, the rewrites are often imperfect,
200   modifying off-target attributes. These off-target modifications may affect the reward, causing the
201   simple comparison to be misleading. For example, in Table 3, the rewrite changes not only the length
202   of the response, but also removes some HTML tags. Changing the off-target attributes can affect the
203   reward, leading to a biased estimate of causal effects.
204   Mathematically, each rewrite (to W = w) introduces some error εij

272   We now turn to establishing that, under reasonable assumptions, RATE is in fact a sound estimator of
273   the causal effect of an attribute on a reward model.
274
      Latent Variable Model To analyze the rewrite operation, we introduce a latent variable model that
275
      allows us to partition the attributes of a response into the target and off-target attributes:
276
277                                                    Y = Y (W, Z, ξ)
278
279
      where:
280
               • Y is the observed response
281
282            • W is the target attribute we aim to manipulate (e.g., sentiment, complexity)
283            • Z represents off-target attributes that are invariant to rewrites (e.g., topic, language)
284
               • ξ represents off-target attributes that may be affected by rewrites (e.g., specific word choice,
285
                 grammatical structure)
286
287   Within this model, our rewrite operator Re(Y, w′ ) has the following action:
288
289                                          Re(Y (w, Z, ξ), w′ ) = Y (w′ , Z, ξ ′ )
290
      where ξ ′ may differ from the original ξ due to the imperfect nature of the rewrite process. w is the
291
      original realization of the target attribute, and w′ is the rewritten value. That is, if the target attribute
292   is sentiment and the original response is positive sentiment, then w = 1 and w′ = 0.
293
294   Intuitively, we expect some off-target attributes Z to remain unchanged during rewrites. For example,
295
      if we ask a large language model to change the sentiment of an English text, we don’t expect it to
      suddenly produce Korean. However, other off-target attributes ξ may change: for instance, grammar
296
      and punctuation might be corrected.

407   is very accurate, longer responses should not increase the likelihood that a response is classified
408   as positive. We then introduce a correlation between response length and positive sentiment (see
409   Table 6), and show that the naive estimator shows a large effect size. The RATE estimator shows an
410   effect size close to zero for length on positive sentiment score, aligning with the ground truth.
411   In Figure 3 (right), we evaluate ArmoRM (Wang et al., 2024a) in a similar manner on the HelpSteer
412   dataset. Here, we do not have access to a ground truth, but we do know that if RATE is correctly
413   capturing the ATE, it should be robust to distributional shift. We can see that the RATE estimate is
414   stable as spurious correlation is introduced into the dataset.
415
416   Rewrites of Rewrites vs. Single Rewrites Is it better to use rewrites of rewrites, or is a single
417   rewrite sufficient?
418
      RATE uses rewrites of rewrites to estimate the causal effect of an attribute on a reward model, ad-
419
      dressing concerns that the rewrite process may distort off-target concepts. Figure 4 shows how reward
420   distributions differ between original responses and rewrites of rewrites, highlighting these distortions.
421   Note that these distortions are not always favorable; while rewrites often correct formatting and make
422   text more ‘GPT-like,’ increasing rewards as in Table 3, they can also produce odd completions. For
423   instance, GPT-4o changed "always the same size" to "annoyingly the same size" when rewriting
424   negative sentiment (see Table 5).
425
      How significant are these distortions? Figure 5 illustrates that the ‘double rewrite’ method produces
426   substantially different estimates compared to the ‘single rewrite’ method. In this case, we intervene
427   on the "Length" attribute in the ELI5 dataset, corresponding to the distortions shown in Figure 4
428   (right). Although the reward score distributions between original responses and rewrites-of-rewrites
429   are only slightly misaligned, the difference in their means is large enough that the single rewrite
430   method reports drastically different estimates for ATE, ATT, and ATU compared to the double rewrite
431   method. This is not unique to the (Length, ELI5) pair; we observe similar disrepancies across multiple
      attributes and datasets (see Appendix B).

462   Implementation Details For all experiments, we use OpenAI BatchAPI to generate rewrites of
463   text, instructing the LLM to modify the target attribute without changing any other aspects of the
464   response.
465   Crafting instructions to generate appropriate rewrites requires examining rewritten examples and
466   adjusting the instructions accordingly to account for unexpected behavior. This process is iterative
467   and requires a human-in-the-loop to ensure that the rewrites are appropriate for the task. In particular,
468   safety-tuned LLMs are reluctant to rewrite text to be more unhelpful, and so the instructions must be
469   carefully examined to ensure that the LLM is willing to generate the desired rewrites.
470
      One surprising behavior we encountered is that, when the example to rewrite was phrased as a
471   question, the LLM would often answer the question rather than rewriting it. Based on this, we
472   included specific instructions not to answer questions but, rather, to rewrite them for the HH-RLHF
473   dataset.
474
      Using the ‘gpt-4o-2024-08-06’ model through OpenAI’s BatchAPI in September 2024, we incurred
475
      $1.25 per 1M input tokens and $5.00 per 1M output tokens. For instance, generating rewrites and
476
      rewrites-of-rewrites for 25,000 IMDB samples cost approximately $60.

478   6    D ISCUSSION
479
480   Dynamic Benchmarking Static benchmarks offer limited insight for model deployment compared
481
      to dynamic benchmarking, which is less vulnerable to memorization and can be easily tailored to
      specific task constraints (Saxon et al., 2024). While the evaluations in this work augment static datasets
482
      for the sake of demonstrating its validity, RATE can be easily adapted to dynamic benchmarking by
483
      rewriting responses in real-time.
484
485   Rewriting the Prompt Wang et al. (2024b) showed that rewriting prompts outperforms rewriting
      completions when generating synthetic preference data. Though applied to generic preferences (rather


                                                            9
486
      than specific attributes), this suggests that rewriting the prompt may be a useful extension of our
487   method. That is, we could rewrite the prompt to change the attribute of interest, and then generate
488   a completion as usual (the same for rewrites of rewrites). Further research in this direction would
489   need to adapt the latent variable model and consequent RATE estimator, but it could be a promising
490   direction for future work.
491
492   Beyond Binary Concepts This paper focuses on binary attributes, in line with binary treatments in
493   causal inference. Although this may seem limiting, continuous attributes like length can be binarized
494   using thresholds (e.g., above or below a character count), and categorical attributes can be simplified
495
      with binary contrasts. This approach works well for many applications, but future work could explore
      explicit handling of continuous and categorical attributes.
496
497
498   7    R ELATED W ORK
499
      Challenges in Reward Modeling Our work is particularly motivated by the challenges identified
500
      in reward modeling. Lambert et al. (2024) introduced RewardBench, a dataset for comparing reward
501
      models, providing a non-causal approach that contrasts with our causal inference framework. Casper
502   et al. (2023) highlighted issues such as misgeneralization and reward hacking in reward models,
503   which our work addresses by quantifying how reward models incentivize specific attributes. Gleave
504   et al. (2021) offered a global metric for comparing reward models, while our approach provides a
505   more fine-grained analysis focused on specific attributes.
506
507   Causal Inference in NLP The theoretical foundation for our work draws from recent developments
508   in understanding large language models and causal inference. Park et al. (2024b) conceptualized
509   attributes in next-token prediction using counterfactual pairs, which we extend to multi-token evalua-
510
      tion of reward models. While Pryzant et al. (2021) and Veitch et al. (2020) addressed challenges like
      confounding in causal inference with text data, our work circumvents causal identification through
511
      our calibrated rewrite-based approach.
512
513   Counterfactuals in Language Models The use of counterfactuals in language models has been
514   explored in various contexts. Feder et al. (2021) introduced CausaLM, which employs counterfactual
515   language models for causal explanations. Since this predates general-purpose LLMs capable of
516   producing counterfactual rewrites, the focus is on how to create rule-based rewrites. Similarly,
517   Butcher (2024) ask an LLM to generate pairs by adding guidance to the prompt (“respond in a kind
518   way”) but without directly rewriting the completions; hence there is no assurance that the pairs
519   share the same off-targets. Wu et al. (2021) developed Polyjuice, a system for generating diverse
520   counterfactuals to evaluate and improve models, but the focus is on training a separate model to
521
      generate counterfactuals. Fryer et al. (2022) use various metrics to assess the quality of rewrites
      on four dimensions: fluency/consistency, presence of a particular attribute, similarity of label, and
522
      similarity of meaning. Our work extends assessments of rewrite quality (through rewrites of rewrites)
523
      to correct for bias in the evaluation of reward models, allowing us to account for the quality of
524   rewrites on all dimensions simultaneously.
525
526
527
      8    C ONCLUSION
528   We rely on reward models to align LLMs to human values, but reward models are black boxes and it
529   is unclear what aspects of the text they are actually rewarding. In this work, we formalized whether
530   a reward model responds to a given attribute (e.g. “helpfulness”, “complexity”, “sensitivity”, etc.)
531   through the language of causality. Specifically, we estimated the average treatment effect of an
532   attribute by counterfactually rewriting natural language responses to differ only on the target attribute.
533   Although this rewrite process introduces bias, we account for it using rewrites of rewrites, which, in
534   expectation, cancel out off-target changes. We call this procedure “RATE”: Rewrite-based Attribute
535   Treatment Estimator.
536   Experimentally, we showed that RATE is robust to distributional shift, reports very different effect
537   sizes for a variety of real-world reward models, and that rewrites-of-rewrites are substantially different
538   from single-rewrite estimators. Our method computes causal effects of individual attributes on reward
539   models without enumerating all off-target attributes and introduces a procedure to find out what
      attributes reward models are really rewarding.
