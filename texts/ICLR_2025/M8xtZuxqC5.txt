009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) involves training policy
012            models (PMs) and reward models (RMs) to align language models with human
013            preferences. Instead of focusing solely on PMs and RMs independently, we pro-
014            pose to examine their interactions during fine-tuning, introducing the concept of
015            seamlessness. Our study starts with observing the saturation phenomenon, where
016            continual improvements in RM and PM do not translate into RLHF progress. Our
017            analysis shows that RMs fail to assign proper scores to PM responses, resulting
018            in a 35% mismatch rate with human preferences, highlighting a significant dis-
019
               crepancy between PM and RM. To measure seamlessness between PM and RM
               without human effort, we propose an automatic metric, SEAM. SEAM quantifies
020
               the discrepancies between PM and RM judgments induced by data samples. We
021
               validate the effectiveness of SEAM in data selection and model augmentation.
022            Our experiments demonstrate that (1) using SEAM-filtered data for RL training
023            improves RLHF performance by 4.5%, and (2) SEAM-guided model augmentation
024            results in a 4% performance improvement over standard augmentation methods.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) has emerged as a popular technique to optimize
030
      and align a language model with human preferences (Stiennon et al., 2020; Nakano et al., 2021;
031   Menick et al., 2022; Glaese et al., 2022; Ouyang et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023; Achiam et al.,
032   2023; Bai et al., 2023; Rafailov et al., 2024). RLHF provides a natural solution for optimizing
033   non-differentiable, scalar objectives for language models and has been the centerpiece of recent
034   state-of-the-art large language models (LLMs) (Lu et al., 2022; Hejna III & Sadigh, 2023; Go et al.,
035   2023; Korbak et al., 2023; Achiam et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023). In RLHF, a reward model (RM)
036   generates scalar rewards for a policy model (PM) generated outputs as supervision signals during
037   reinforcement learning. Since policy gradient methods (Schulman et al., 2017) optimize based on such
038   signal, the PM and RM inevitably dictate the behavior of the resultant RLHF model. As such, the
039
      properties of RMs (or PMs) and their impact on RLHF models have become points of interest for the
      community (Gao et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2023).
040
      Unlike prior work that examines the individual capabilities of each model, in this work, we introduce
041
      and explore the concept of seamlessness between the PM and RM, focusing on their interactions.
042
043   Our study begins with the observation of a saturation phenomenon in the RLHF process (§3): beyond
044   a certain threshold, improvements in the quality of the RM and PM do not translate into increased
045
      RLHF performance (Figure 1). To understand this phenomenon, we explore whether the RM can
      assign appropriate scalar rewards to responses r generated by the PM prompted by instruction I. This
046
      inquiry addresses the seamlessness between the RM and PM. Although the RM performs well on
047
      standard preference benchmarks, it struggles to evaluate PM-generated responses effectively. This is
048   demonstrated by a 35% mismatch rate between reward scores and human preferences, indicating a
049   significant, persistent discrepancy between the RM and PM as reflected in the reinforcement learning
050   (RL) training data. This discrepancy does not diminish even as the PM and RM are individually
051   optimized according to their respective evaluation paradigms, thus disrupting their seamlessness.
052   Remarkably, when we remove instructions from the RL dataset that contribute to this discrepancy and
053   re-conduct RLHF, we observe an improvement in RLHF performance. This outcome suggests that
      enhancing the seamlessness between PM and RM benefits the overall RLHF process.

      9 L IMITATIONS
519   One limitation of our framework is that it is restricted to offline RLHF experiments rather than being
520   tested in an online RLHF scenario. In online RLHF, the RM and PM are updated continuously based
521   on real-time feedback from user interactions, offering a more dynamic and realistic setting. Despite
522   this, we propose that SEAM can still be effectively utilized by segmenting the online RLHF into a
523
      series of offline RLHF cycles. At the beginning of each cycle, the same analyses for data selection
      and model augmentation could be applied. This adaptation would allow us to extend the benefits of
524
      SEAM to more practical, real-world applications. Another limitation of our framework is inherent in
525
      the SEAM metric, which assesses the seamlessness of data only comparatively rather than absolutely.
526   Consequently, while we can selectively filter portions of data (e.g., the lowest 20%), we cannot
527   establish a definitive threshold to categorize data as good or bad outright. However, to understand
528   the impact of different filtering rates more thoroughly, we have conducted an analysis detailed in
529   Appendix E, where we see 20% is a practical choice.

532   10      C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this paper, we explored the concept of seamlessness between policy and reward models within
535   Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), uncovering significant discrepancies
536   between the models as reflected in the data. We introduced SEAM, an automated method to
537   quantify this seamlessness, demonstrating its practical benefits for improving RLHF outcomes. Our
538   findings emphasize the critical interplay between policy and reward models, contributing to a deeper
539   understanding of RLHF dynamics. We hope our insights will guide future research toward developing
      more effective and nuanced RLHF strategies.

      A     P RELIMINARIES : A THREE - STAGE PARADIGM FOR RLHF
757
758   A RLHF practice includes three stages: policy modeling, reward modeling, and RL training, which
759   involve three benchmarks: an SFT dataset Dp , a preference benchmark Dr , and an RL dataset Drl .
760
761   Policy model. Following the setup (Ouyang et al., 2022), we obtain the policy model (PM) by
762   supervised fine-tuning (SFT) the base version of LLM. Given an SFT dataset Dp , each instance in
763   the dataset consists of an instruction and its golden response. Then, we train the LLM on Dp with
764   language modeling loss to obtain the PM: π SFT .
765
766   Reward model. Following the conventional setup (Ouyang et al., 2022), we are given a dataset
767   of human preferences Dr . Each instance in this dataset (Ii , ri+ , ri− ) is comprised of an instruction
768   prompt Ii , a pair of responses ri+ , ri− where ri+ is preferred over ri− by humans. On this labeled data,
769   RM Rθ is trained to assign a higher scalar reward to human-preferred ri+ over non-preferred ri− in
770   the context of Ii . This can be achieved by minimizing the ranking loss L, where σ is the sigmoid
771   function and Ii ◦ ri+ is the concatenation of Ii and ri+ .
                       L(θ) = −E(Ii ,r+ ,r− )∼Dh log σ Rθ (Ii ◦ ri+ − Rθ Ii ◦ ri− ) .

772                                                                                                         (6)
                                        i    i
773
774   Reinforcement Learning. The last stage of RLHF is reinforcement learning. Specifically, a per-
775   token KL penalty from the SFT model at each token is used to mitigate over-optimization of the
776   reward model, and the value function is initialized from the RM. We maximize the following combined
777   objective function J (ϕ) in RL training based on PPO algorithm (Schulman et al., 2017; Ouyang et al.,
778
      2022), RL training dataset Drl and pre-training dataset Dpre :
                      J (ϕ) = E(I,r)∼DπRL Rθ (I ◦ r) − β log πϕRL (r | I)/π SFT (r | I)

779
                                          ϕ
780
781   where πϕRL is the learned RL policy parameterized by ϕ initialized from a pretrained supervised
782   trained model π SFT . The first term encourages the policy πϕRL to generate responses that have higher
783   reward scores. The second term represents a per-token KL reward controlled by coefficient β between
784   πϕRL and π SFT to mitigate over-optimization toward the reward.

891
892   We employed several adversarial attack strategies to challenge the integrity of the reward model (RM).
893   Specifically, for each instruction along with its corresponding better response r+ and worse response
894   r− , these adversarial attacks introduce a perturbation α to r− . The goal is for r− + α to receive a
895   higher reward score than r+ , thereby compromising the RM. The attacks we utilized include GA
896   (Wang et al., 2019), Bert-Attack (Li et al., 2020), PWWS (Ren et al., 2019), KATG (Shen et al., 2022),
897
      and TextFooler (Jin et al., 2020). However, a common limitation of these methods is that they tend to
      produce sentences with extremely low likelihood according to the policy model. Below, we present
898
      some examples illustrating the discrepancies between the original responses and those generated by

      the adversarial attacks.
