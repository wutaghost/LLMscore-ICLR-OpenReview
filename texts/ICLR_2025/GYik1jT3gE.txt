009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Initializing with pre-trained models when learning on downstream tasks is now
012            standard practice in machine learning. Several recent works explore the benefits of
013            pre-trained initialization in a federated learning (FL) setting, where the downstream
014            training is performed at the edge clients with heterogeneous data distribution. These
015
               works show that starting from a pre-trained model can substantially reduce the
               adverse impact of data heterogeneity on the test performance of a model trained
016
               in a federated setting, with no changes to the standard FedAvg training algorithm.
017
               In this work, we provide a deeper theoretical understanding of this phenomenon.
018            To do so, we study the class of two-layer convolutional neural networks (CNNs)
019            and provide bounds on the training error convergence and test error of such a
020            network trained with FedAvg. We introduce the notion of aligned and misaligned
021            filters at initialization and show that the data heterogeneity only affects learning
022            on misaligned filters. Starting with a pre-trained model typically results in fewer
023            misaligned filters at initialization, thus producing a lower test error even when the
024            model is trained in a federated setting with data heterogeneity. Experiments in
025            synthetic settings and practical FL training on CNNs verify our theoretical findings.
026
027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Federated Learning (FL) (McMahan et al., 2017) has emerged as the de-facto paradigm for training a
030   Machine Learning (ML) model over data distributed across multiple clients with privacy protection
031   due to its no data-sharing philosophy. Ever since its inception, it has been observed that heterogeneity
032   in client data can severely slow down FL training and lead to a model that has poorer generalization
033   performance than a model trained on Independent and Identically Distributed (IID) data (Kairouz
034   et al., 2021; Li et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2021a). This has led works to propose several algorithmic
035   modifications to the popular Federated Averaging (FedAvg) algorithm such as variance-reduction
036
      (Acar et al., 2021; Karimireddy et al., 2020), contrastive learning (Li et al., 2021; Tan et al., 2022)
      and sophisticated model-aggregation techniques (Lin et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2020), among others
037
      to combat the challenge of data heterogeneity.
038
039   A recent line of work (Chen et al., 2022; Nguyen et al., 2022) has sought to understand the benefits
040   of starting from pre-trained models instead of randomly initializing the global model when doing FL.
041   This idea has been popularized by results in the centralized setting (Devlin et al., 2019; Radford et al.,
042
      2019; He et al., 2019; Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), which show that starting from a pre-trained model
      can lead to state-of-the-art accuracy and faster convergence on downstream tasks. Pre-training is
043
      usually done on internet-scale public data (Schuhmann et al., 2022; Thomee et al., 2016; Raffel et al.,
044
      2020; Gao et al., 2020) in order for the model to learn fundamental data representations (Sun et al.,
045   2017; Mahajan et al., 2018; Radford et al., 2019), that can be easily applied for downstream tasks.
046   Thus, while it would not be unexpected to see some gains of using pre-trained models even in FL,
047   what is surprising is the sheer scale of improvement. In many cases Nguyen et al. (2022); Chen et al.
048   (2022) show that just starting from a pre-trained model can significantly reduce the gap between the
049   performance of a model trained in a federated setting with non-IID versus IID data partitioning with
050   no algorithmic modifications. Figure 1 shows our own replication of this phenomenon, where starting
051   from a pre-trained model can lead to almost 14% improvement in accuracy for FL with non-IID data
052   (i.e., high data heterogeneity) compared to 4% for FL with IID data and 2% in the centralized setting.
053
      This observation leads us to ask the question:

      Our contributions. In this work we provide a deeper theoretical understanding of the importance of
071
      initialization for FedAvg by studying two-layer ReLU Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) for
072   binary classification. This class of neural networks lends itself to tractable analysis while providing
073   valuable insights that extend to training deeper CNNs as shown by several recent works (Cao et al.,
074   2022; Du et al., 2018; Kou et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2021; Jelassi & Li, 2022; Bao et al., 2024; Oh &
075   Yun, 2024). Our data generation model, also studied in Cao et al. (2022); Kou et al. (2023), allows us
076   to utilize a signal-noise decomposition result (see Proposition 1) to perform a fine-grained analysis
077   of the CNN filter weight updates than can be done with general non-convex optimization. Some
078   highlights of our results are as follows:
079
      1. We introduce the notion of aligned and misaligned filters at initialization (Definition 1) and show
080
         that data heterogeneity affects signal learning only on misaligned filters while noise memorization
081      is unaffected by data heterogeneity (see Section 4). A pre-trained model is expected to have fewer
082      misaligned filters, which can explain the reduced effect of non-IID data.
083
      2. We provide a test error upper bound for FedAvg that depends on the number of misaligned filters
084
         at initialization and data heterogeneity. The effect of data heterogeneity on misaligned filters
085      is exacerbated as clients perform more local steps, which explains why FL benefits more from
086      pre-trained initialization than centralized training. To our knowledge, this is the first result where
087      the test error for FedAvg explicitly depends on initialization conditions (Theorem 2).
088
      3. We prove the training error convergence of FedAvg by adopting a two-stage analysis: a first stage
089      where the local loss derivatives are lower bounded by a constant and second stage where the model
090      is in the neighborhood of a global minimizer with nearly convex loss landscape. Our analysis
091      shows a provable benefit of using local steps in the first stage to reduce communication cost.
092   4. We experimentally verify our upper bound on the test error in a synthetic data setting (see Section 3
093      as well as conduct experiments on practical FL tasks which show that our insights extend to deeper
094      CNNs (see Section 5).
095
096   Related Work. The two-layer CNN model that we study in this work was originally introduced
097   in Zou et al. (2021) for the purpose of analyzing the generalization error of the Adam optimizer in
098   the centralized setting. Later Cao et al. (2022) study the same model to analyze the phenomenon of
099
      benign overfitting in two-layer CNN, i.e., give precise conditions under which the CNN can perfectly
      fit the data while also achieving small population loss. Oh & Yun (2024) use this model to prove the
100
      benefit of patch-level data augmentation techniques such as Cutout and CutMix. Kou et al. (2023)
101
      relaxes the the polynomial ReLU activation in Cao et al. (2022) to the standard ReLU activation and
102   also introduces label-flipping noise when analyzing benign overfitting in the centralized setting. We
103   do not consider label-flipping in our work for simplicity; however this can be easily incorporated as
104   future work. To the best of our knowledge, we are only aware of two other works (Huang et al., 2023;
105   Bao et al., 2024) that analyze the two-layer CNN in a FL setting. The focus in Huang et al. (2023) is
106   on showing the benefit of collaboration in FL by considering signal heterogeneity across the data
107   in clients while Bao et al. (2024) considers signal heterogeneity to show the benefit of local steps.
      Both Huang et al. (2023) and Bao et al. (2024) do not consider any label heterogeneity and there is

191   3.1   F ILTER A LIGNMENT AT I NITIALIZATION
192
      Given datapoint (x, y), for the CNN to correctly predict the label y and minimize the loss
193
      ℓ(yf (W, x)), from equation 2-equation 3, we want yf (W, x) = Fy (Wy , x)−F−y (W−y , x)) ≫ 0.
194
      At an individual filter r ∈ [m], this can happen either with ⟨wy,r , yµ⟩ ≫ 0 or ⟨wy,r , ξ⟩ ≫ 0. How-
195   ever, we want the model to focus on the signal yµ in x while making the prediction. Therefore, for
196   filter (j, r) we want ⟨wj,r , yµ⟩ ≫ 0 if j = y and ⟨wj,r , yµ⟩ ≪ 0 if j = −y. Depending on the
197   initialization of our CNN, we have the following definition of aligned and misaligned filters.
198
      Definition 1. The (j, r)-th filter (with j ∈ {±1}, r ∈ [m]) is said to be aligned (with signal) at
199                       (0)
      initialization if ⟨wj,r , jµ⟩ ≥ 0 and misaligned otherwise.
200
201
      We shall see in Section 4 that the alignment of a filter at initialization plays a crucial role in how well
202   it learns the signal and also the overall generalization performance of the CNN in Theorem 2.

      In this work we provide a deeper theoretical explanation for why pre-training can drastically reduce
      the adverse effects of non-IID data in FL by studying the class of two layer CNN models under a
532
      signal-noise data model. Our analysis shows that the reduction in test accuracy seen in non-IID FL
533
      compared to IID FL is only caused by filters that are misaligned at initialization. When starting from a
534   pre-trained model we expect most of the filters to be already aligned with the signal thereby reducing
535   the effect of heterogeneity and leading to a higher ratio of signal learning to noise memorization.
536   This is corroborated by experiments on synthetic setup as well as more practical FL training tasks.
537   Our work also opens up several avenues for future work. These including extending the analysis
538   to deeper and more practical neural networks and also incorporating multi-class classification with
539   more than two labels. Another interesting direction is to see how pre-training affects other federated
      algorithms such as those that explicitly incorporate heterogeneity reducing mechanisms.

865
866
      Use of Pre-Trained Models in Federated Learning. Tan et al. (2022) explore the benefit of
867
      using pre-trained models in FL by proposing to use multiple fixed pre-trained backbones as the
868
      encoder model at each client and using contrastive learning to extract useful shared representations.
869   Zhuang et al. (2023) discuss the opportunities and challenges of using large foundation models for FL
870   including the high communication and computation cost. One solution to this as proposed by Legate
871   et al. (2024) is that instead of full fine-tuning as done in Chen et al. (2022); Nguyen et al. (2022), we
872   can just fine-tune the last layer. Specifically Legate et al. (2024) proposes a two-stage approach to
873   federated fine-tuning by first fine-tuning the head and then doing a full-finetuning. This approach
874   is inspired by results in the centralized setting Kumar et al. (2022) which show that in some case
875   fine-tuning can distort the pre-trained features. Fanì et al. (2023) also study the problem of fine-tuning
876   just the last layer in a federated setting by replacing the softmax classifier with a ridge-regression
877
      classifier which enables them to compute a closed form expression for the last layer weights.
878   There has also been some recent work on exploring the benefit of pre-training for federated natural
879   language processing tasks including the use of Large Language Models (LLMs). Wang et al. (2023)
880   discuss how to leverage the power of pre-trained LLMs for private on-device fine-tuning of language
881   models. Specifically, Wang et al. (2023) proposes a distribution matching approach to select public
882   data that is closest to private data and then use this selected public data to train the on-device language
883
      model. Zhang et al. (2023) propose to first pre-train on synthetic data to construct the initialization
      point followed by federated fine-tuning. Hou et al. (2024) propose that clients send DP information
884
      to the server which then uses this information to generate synthetic data and fine-tune centrally on
885
      this synthetic data. Liu & Miller (2020) discuss the challenges of pre-training and fine-tuning BERT
886   in federated manner using clinical notes from multiple silos without data transfer. Tian et al. (2022)
887   propose to pre-train a BERT model in a federated manner in a more general setting and show that their
888   pre-trained model can retain accuracy on the GLUE (Wang et al., 2018) dataset without sacrificing
889   client privacy. Gupta et al. (2022) propose a defense using pre-trained models to prevent an attacker
890   from recovering multiple sentences from gradients in the federated training of the language modeling
891   task.
892
893   Use of Pre-trained Models for Private Optimization. We note that an orthogonal line of work
894   has explored the benefits of starting from a pre-trained model when doing differentially private
895   optimization Dwork et al. (2006) and seen similar striking improvement in accuracy De et al. (2022);
896   Li et al. (2022b), as we see in the heterogeneous FL setting. Ganesh et al. (2023) study this
897   phenomenon for a stylized mean estimation problem and show that public pre-training can help the
898   model start from a good loss basin which is otherwise hard to achieve with private noisy optimization.
899   Li et al. (2022a) study differentially private convex optimization and show that starting from a
900
      pre-trained model can leads to dimension independent convergence guarantees. Specifically Li et al.
      (2022a) define the notion of restricted Lipschitz continuity and show that when gradients are low rank
901
      most of the restricted Lispchitz coefficients will be zero.
902
903
904   Generalization performance in Federated Learning. Several existing works have studied the
905
      generalization performance of FL in different settings Cheng et al. (2021); Gholami & Seferoglu
      (2024); Huang et al. (2023); Yuan et al. (2021). Some of the initial works either provide results
906
      independent of the algorithm being used Mohri et al. (2019); Hu et al. (2022); Sun & Wei (2022), or
907
      only study convex losses Chen et al. (2021); Fallah et al. (2021). Barnes et al. (2022); Sefidgaran
908   et al. (2022) derive information-theoretic bounds, but these bounds require specific forms of loss
909   functions and cannot capture effects of heterogeneity. Huang et al. (2021) study the generalization
910   of FedAvg on wide two-layer ReLU networks with homogeneous data. Collins et al. (2022) studies
911   FedAvg under multi-task linear representation learning setting. In Sun et al. (2024), the authors have
912   demonstrated the impact of data heterogeneity on the generalization performance of some popular FL
913   algorithms.

       The assumptions are primarily used to ensure that the model is sufficiently overparameterized, i.e.,
1081   training loss can be made arbitrarily small, and that we do not begin optimization from a point where
1082   the gradient is already zero or unbounded. We provide a more intuitive reasoning behind each of the
1083   assumptions below:
1084
1085          • Bounded number of communication rounds: This is needed to ensure that the magnitude of
1086            filter weights remains bounded throughout training since they grow logarithmically with the
1087            number of updates (see Theorem 3). We note that this is quite a mild condition since the
1088
                max rounds can have polynomial dependence on 1/ϵ where ϵ is our desired training error.
1089          • Dimension d is sufficiently large: This is needed to ensure that the model is sufficiently
1090            overparameterized and the training loss can be made arbitrarily small. Recall that our input
1091
                x consists of a signal component µ ∈ Rd that is common across all datapoints and noise
                component ξ ∈ Rd that is independently drawn from N (0, σp2 · I). Having a sufficiently
1092
1093
                large d ensures that the correlation between any two noise vectors, i.e. ⟨ξ, ξ ′ ⟩/∥ξ∥2 is not
                too large. Otherwise if the correlation between two noise vectors is large and negative, then
1094
                minimizing the loss on one data point could end up increasing the loss on another training
1095
                point which complicates convergence and prevents loss from becoming arbitrarily small.
1096
1097

1099
                training set size also ensures that there are a sufficient number of datapoints with negative
1100            and positive labels (see Lemma 8).
