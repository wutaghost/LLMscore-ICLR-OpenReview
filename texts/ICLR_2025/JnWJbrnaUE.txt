                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               Large language models (LLMs) inevitably exhibit hallucinations since the accu-
010
               racy of generated texts cannot be secured solely by the parametric knowledge
011            they encapsulate. Although retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) is a practicable
012            complement to LLMs, it relies heavily on the relevance of retrieved documents,
013            raising concerns about how the model behaves if retrieval goes wrong. To this end,
014            we propose the Corrective Retrieval Augmented Generation (CRAG) to improve
015            the robustness of generation. Specifically, a lightweight retrieval evaluator is
016            designed to assess the overall quality of retrieved documents for a query, returning
017            a confidence degree based on which different knowledge retrieval actions can be
018            triggered. Since retrieval from static and limited corpora can only return sub-
019
               optimal documents, large-scale web searches are utilized as an extension for
               augmenting the retrieval results. Besides, a decompose-then-recompose algorithm
020
               is designed for retrieved documents to selectively focus on key information and
021
               filter out irrelevant information in them. CRAG is plug-and-play and can be
022            seamlessly coupled with various RAG-based approaches. Experiments on four
023            datasets covering short- and long-form generation tasks show that CRAG can
024            significantly improve the performance of RAG-based approaches.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Large language models (LLMs) have attracted increasing attention and exhibited impressive abilities
030   to understand instructions and generate fluent language texts (Brown et al., 2020; Ouyang et al.,
031   2022; Touvron et al., 2023a). Nevertheless, LLMs inevitably manifest hallucinations (Ji et al., 2023)
032   due to their struggle with factual errors (Mallen et al., 2023; Min et al., 2023) and inability to secure
033
      the accuracy of generated texts solely by the parametric knowledge they encapsulate (Zhang et al.,
      2023b; Muhlgay et al., 2023).
034
035   Prior research has introduced the retrieval techniques to incorporate the knowledge relevant to input
036   and augment generation, as exemplified by retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) (Lewis et al.,
037   2020). In this framework, the input to models is augmented by prepending relevant documents
038   that are retrieved from an external knowledge corpus (Guu et al., 2020). While RAG serves as a
039
      practicable complement to LLMs, its effectiveness is contingent upon the relevance and accuracy
      of the retrieved documents (Li et al., 2022; Tan et al., 2022). The heavy reliance of generation on
040
      the retrieved knowledge raises significant concerns about the model’s behavior and performance in
041
      scenarios where retrieval may fail or return inaccurate results (Shi et al., 2023). As Figure 1 shows
042   that a low-quality retriever is prone to introducing a substantial amount of irrelevant information,
043   impeding the models from acquiring accurate knowledge and potentially misleading them, resulting
044   in issues such as hallucinations (Zhang et al., 2023b). However, most conventional RAG approaches
045   indiscriminately incorporate the retrieved documents, regardless of whether these documents are
046   relevant or not (Rony et al., 2022). Furthermore, current methods mostly treat complete documents
047   as reference knowledge both during retrieval and utilization. But a considerable portion of the text
048   within these retrieved documents is often non-essential for generation, which should not have been
049   equally referred to and involved in RAG.
050   On account of the above issues, this paper particularly studies the scenarios where the retriever
051   returns inaccurate results. A method named Corrective Retrieval-Augmented Generation (CRAG)
052   is proposed to self-correct the results of retriever and improve the utilization of documents for
053   augmenting generation. A lightweight retrieval evaluator is designed to assess the overall quality
      of retrieved documents for a query. This serves as a crucial component in RAG, contributing to


                                                         1

054
      informative generation by reviewing and evaluating the relevance and reliability of the retrieved
055   documents. A confidence degree is quantified based on which different knowledge retrieval actions
056   of {Correct, Incorrect, Ambiguous} can be triggered. For the latter two actions, large-
057   scale web searches (Piktus et al., 2021; Komeili et al., 2022) are integrated as a strategic extension,
058   since retrieval from static and limited corpora can only return sub-optimal documents in terms
059   of scope and diversity. This augmentation is implemented to broaden the spectrum of retrieved
060   information, harnessing the expansive and dynamic nature of the web to complement and enrich the
061   initially obtained documents. Furthermore, to eliminate redundant contexts contained in retrieved
062   documents that are unhelpful for RAG, a decompose-then-recompose algorithm is meticulously
063
      crafted throughout the retrieval and utilization process. This algorithm ensures the refinement of
      retrieved information, optimizing the extraction of key insights and minimizing the inclusion of
064
      non-essential elements, thereby enhancing the utilization of retrieved data.

093   2   R ELATED W ORK
094
095   Hallucinations of LLMs Although LLMs have exhibited impressive abilities to understand
                                                                                       Retrieved
096   instructions and generate fluent language texts (Bang et al., 2023; Qin et al., 2023; Zhong et al.,
                                                                                      Documents
097   2023), one of the most severe issues that LLMs have still been struggling with is hallucinations. As
098   many studies found (?Shuster et al., 2021), either outdated information or incorrect knowledge that is
099   activated would seriously result in hallucinations. Large-scale unregulated training data collection,
100
      low proportion of high-quality sampling data, imperfection of data allocation in the input space,
      and many other realistic factors could impact the LLMs and exacerbate the problems. Thus, it is
101
      obvious that the lack of accurate and specific knowledge can lead to misleading or even inaccurate
102
      generation, which will severely hurt the experience of users in most practical applications.
103
104   Retrieval-Augmented Generation RAG (Lewis et al., 2020; Guu et al., 2020) is regarded as a
105   useful method to address the issues above, which enhances the input questions of generative LMs
106   with retrieved documents. It usually provides an extra knowledge source from a specific corpus,
107   i.e., Wikipedia, which greatly improves the performance of LMs in a variety of tasks, especially
      in the knowledge-intensive ones. The proposed methods generally leverage information retrieval


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      to supply documents containing relevant knowledge for generative LLMs. Earlier studies adopt
109   either sparse or dense retrievers at the front end of a pre-trained language model that specializes in
110   response generation. Despite this, the methods above usually ignore a question, what if the retrieval
111   goes wrong? Since the purpose of introducing a retrieval is to secure that generative LMs can obtain
112   relevant and accurate knowledge. If retrieved documents are irrelevant, the retrieval system can even
113   exacerbate the factual error that LMs make.
114
115
      Advanced RAG Many advanced approaches have been developed from the original RAG in recent
      years (Zhang et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). Considering that
116
      retrieval is sometimes unnecessary for some queries, conversely, responses without retrieval are even
117
      more accurate in many situations. Self-RAG (Asai et al., 2024) is proposed to selectively retrieve
118   knowledge and introduce a critic model to decide whether to retrieve. Yoran et al. (2024) designed an
119   NLI model to identify the irrelevant context and improve robustness. SAIL (Luo et al., 2023) is tuned
120   on instructions to insert retrieved documents before instructions. While Toolformer (Schick et al.,
121   2023) is pre-trained for calling APIs such as Wikipedia. In addition, in some long-text generation
122   tasks, external knowledge is needed more than once, and when to retrieve should be concerned. Jiang
123   et al. (2023) actively anticipate future content and decide when and what to retrieve in long-form
124   generation.
125   Compared with recent studies (Schick et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023; Asai et al., 2024) that are the
126   most relevant to our work, a main difference should be highlighted. These approaches target on
127   exploiting retrieval as a useful tool to augment generation or whether retrieval is necessary, while
128   this study particularly studies the scenarios where the retriever returns inaccurate results. To the best
129   of our knowledge, this paper makes the first attempt to explore and design corrective strategies for
130   RAG to improve its robustness of generation.

      3     TASK F ORMULATION
133
134
      Following previous work (Lewis et al., 2020; Asai et al., 2024), given input X and an accessible
135
      corpus containing a large amount of knowledge documents C = {d1 , ..., dN }, the system is expected
136
      to generate the output Y. The entire framework is usually divided into a retriever R and a generator
137   G. The retriever R aims to retrieve the top-K documents D = {dr1 , ..., drk } that are relevant to the
138   input X from the corpus C. Based on the input X and the retrieved results D, the generator G is
139   responsible for generating the output Y. This framework can be formulated as:
140
141                                      P (Y|X ) = P (D|X )P (Y, D|X ).                                   (1)
142
      It shows that the retriever and generator are seamlessly coupled, exhibiting low risk tolerance. Any
143
      unsuccessful retrieval can result in an unsatisfactory response, regardless of the impressive abilities
144   of the generator. This is exactly the focus of this paper to improve the robustness of generation.

149   4.1   OVERVIEW OF M ODEL I NFERENCE
150
151   Figure 2 and Algorithm 1 present an overview of CRAG at inference, which designs corrective
152   strategies to improve the robustness of generation. Given an input query and the retrieved documents
153
      from any retriever, a lightweight retrieval evaluator is constructed to estimate the relevance score of
      retrieved documents to the input query (Section 4.2). The relevance score is quantified into a total of
154
      three confidence degrees and then triggered the corresponding actions: {Correct, Incorrect,
155
      Ambiguous} (Section 4.3). If the action Correct is triggered, the retrieved documents will
156   be refined into more precise knowledge strips. This refinement operation involves knowledge
157   decomposition, filter, and recomposition (Section 4.4). If the action Incorrect is triggered,
158   the retrieved documents will be discarded. Instead, web searches are resorted to and regarded
159   as complementary knowledge sources for corrections (Section 4.5). Eventually, when it cannot
160   confidently make a correct or incorrect judgment, a soft and balanced action Ambiguous which
161   combines both of them is triggered. After optimizing the retrieval results, an arbitrary generative
      model can be adopted.

192   4.2      R ETRIEVAL E VALUATOR
193
194   It is natural to wonder whether the retrieved documents are accurate or not before using them,
195   which is significant since irrelevant or misleading messages can be identified in this way. The
196   accuracy of the retrieval evaluator undeniably plays a pivotal role in shaping the overall system
197   performance, as it influences the outcomes of subsequent processes. Our objective is to correct the
198
      retrieved documents if they are irrelevant. Specifically, T5-large (Raffel et al., 2020) is adopted for
      initializing the retrieval evaluator and fine-tuned. Its parameter size is much smaller than the most
199
      current LLMs (Touvron et al., 2023a;b; Chowdhery et al., 2023; Anil et al., 2023; Brown et al.,
200
      2020; Ouyang et al., 2022; OpenAI, 2023). To ensure all experimental results were comparable
201   with Self-RAG (Asai et al., 2024), the same retrieval results through Contriever (Izacard et al.,
202   2022) provided by Self-RAG were also adopted in our experiments. The relevance signals for fine-
203   tuning the evaluator can be collected from the existing datasets. For example, PopQA (Mallen
204   et al., 2023) provides the golden subject wiki title from wikipedia for each question. We can use
205   that to track a not 100% relevant but rather high-quality passage. We utilized that as the relevance
206   signals for fine-tuning the retrieval evaluator.1 On the other hand, the negative samples for fine-
207   tuning were all randomly sampled from the retrieval results, which are rather similar to the input
208   query but not relevant. More details about this fine-tuning step can be referred to in Appendix B.3.
209
      For every question, there are generally 10 documents retrieved. The question is concatenated with
      each single document as the input, and the evaluator predicts the relevance score for each question-
210
      document pair individually. We also tried to prompt ChatGPT to identify the retrieval relevance for
211
      comparison, but it underperforms as elaborated in Section 5.5. Based on these calculated relevance
212   scores, a final judgment is made as to whether the retrieval is correct or not associated with the
213   action trigger. In our proposed framework, the retrieval quality is evaluated at a relatively low cost
214   without the need to have access to large and expensive LLMs. Compared with the critic model of

241     4.3   ACTION T RIGGER
242
243     To correct the irrelevant documents and refine the target documents as needed, actions should be
244     executed discriminately. Based on the aforementioned confidence score for each retrieved document,
245     three types of actions are designed and triggered accordingly where the upper and lower thresholds
246     are set. If the confidence score is higher than the upper threshold, the retrieved document is identified
247
        as Correct, while identified as Incorrect if below the lower threshold. Otherwise, a more
        soft and intermediate action, i.e., Ambiguous is executed. Each retrieved document is conducted
248
        individually and integrated eventually.
249
250     Correct Here, a retrieval is assumed Correct when the confidence score of at least one retrieved
251     document is higher than the upper threshold. If so, it means that there are relevant documents in
252     the retrieved results, and the knowledge from the retrieval results is supposed to be more reliable
253     and accurate. However, even if a relevant document can be found, there is inevitably some
254     noisy knowledge strips in this document. To extract the most critical knowledge strips within
255     this document, a knowledge refinement method is further designed which will be elaborated in
256
        Section 4.4.
257
        Incorrect Besides, a retrieval is assumed Incorrect when the confidence scores of all retrieved
258     documents are below the lower threshold. This indicates that all retrieved documents are considered
259     irrelevant, which are unhelpful for generation. Once the knowledge from the retrieval results is
260     judged to be inaccurate, it is unwise to still get stuck in it, which is likely to result in fabricated facts.
261     Therefore, we need to seek new sources of knowledge for correction. Here, web search is introduced
262     to search from the Internet as elaborated in Section 4.5. This corrective action helps overcome the
263     embarrassing challenge where no reliable knowledge can be referred to.
264
        Ambiguous Except for the above two situations, the remaining will be assigned to an intermediate
265
        action of Ambiguous. This generally occurs when the accuracy of the retrieval is hard to
266
        distinguish and the evaluator gives an intermediate score. Since the retrieval evaluator is not
267     confident in its judgment, both types of processed knowledge in Correct and Incorrect
268     are combined to complement each other. Implementing such a moderating and soft strategy can
269     significantly contribute to strengthening the robustness and resilience of the system, fostering a
        more adaptable framework for optimal performance.

      Discussion Preliminary experiments of employing only the Correct and Incorrect actions
271   show that the efficacy of CRAG was easily affected by the accuracy of the retrieval evaluator.
272   The reason might be the distinct knowledge switch for all input cases, regardless of the level of
273   confidence in their judgment. The design of the Ambiguous action significantly helps to mitigate
274   the dependence on the accuracy of the retrieval evaluator.
275
276   4.4   K NOWLEDGE R EFINEMENT
277
278   Given a retrieved relevant document, a decompose-then-recompose knowledge refinement method
279   is designed to further extract the most critical knowledge strips in it. To obtain fine-grained retrieval
280   results, we segmented the retrieved results into internal strips. If a retrieved result is as short as one
281
      or two sentences, it is regarded as an individual strip, otherwise, retrieval documents are required to
      be split into smaller units which generally consist of a few sentences according to the total length.
282
      The scale is assumed to include an independent piece of information, and the filtering is based
283
      on the segments. Then, the retrieval evaluator fine-tuned in Section 4.2 is employed to calculate
284   the relevance score of each knowledge strip. Based on these scores, irrelevant knowledge strips
285   are filtered out, while relevant ones are recomposed via concatenation in order, namely internal
286   knowledge.
287
288   4.5   W EB S EARCH
289
290   It would be more intelligent if a system itself could determine that its existing knowledge corpus
291   could not solve the problem well and turn to additional external knowledge for help. On the
292   contrary, even if a system knows that the existing knowledge cannot solve the problem, but still
293
      sticks to the limited knowledge corpus, it will only give a fabricated fact in the end, which is called
      hallucination. Therefore, it is extremely important to seek complementary external knowledge
294
      if the retrieved results are all assumed irrelevant, and we consider a system that knows what it
295
      doesn’t know and what it cannot answer to be more intelligent than one that clings to limited
296   knowledge and is incapable of seeking external knowledge. Since retrieval from static and limited
297   corpora can only return sub-optimal documents in terms of scope and diversity, large-scale web
298   searches (Piktus et al., 2021; Komeili et al., 2022) are integrated as a strategic extension of RAG.
299   Specifically, the inputs are rewritten into queries composed of keywords by ChatGPT to mimic the
300   daily usage of search engine. The prompt for rewriting is shown in Appendix A. In CRAG, a
301   public and accessible commercial web search API is adopted to generate a series of URL links for
302   every query. Considering that knowledge from large-scale web searches could introduce biases or
303   unreliable information, authoritative and regulated web pages like Wikipedia are preferred, which
304
      can significantly help mitigate these issues. Moreover, we utilize the URL links to navigate web
      pages, transcribe their content, and employ the same knowledge refinement method as Section 4.4
305
      to derive the relevant web knowledge, namely external knowledge.

359
360
361   We primarily compared CRAG with both approaches with and without retrieval, where the latter
362
      can be further split into standard RAG and latest advanced RAG, including:
363   Baselines without retrieval. We evaluated some public LLMs, LLaMA2-7B,13B (Touvron et al.,
364   2023b), instruction-tuned models, Alpaca-7B,13B (Dubois et al., 2023), and CoVE65B (Dhuliawala
365   et al., 2024) which introduces iterative engineering to improve the factuality of LLM generations.
366   Propriety LLMs such as LLaMA2-chat13B and ChatGPT are also included.
367   Standard RAG. We evaluated the standard RAG (Lewis et al., 2020) where an LM generates
368   output given the query prepended with the top retrieved documents using the same retriever as in
369   our system. Here we adopted several public instruction-tuned LLMs, including LLaMA2-7B, 13B
370   (Touvron et al., 2023b), Alpaca-7B,13B (Dubois et al., 2023), as well as LLaMA2-7B instruction-
371   tuned in Self-RAG (Asai et al., 2024).
372   Advanced RAG. (1) SAIL (Luo et al., 2023) that instruction-tuned an LM on the Alpaca instruction-
373   tuning data with top retrieved documents inserted before instructions. (2) Self-RAG (Asai et al.,
374   2024) that tuned the LLaMA2 on the instruction-tuning data comtaining several sets of reflection
375   tokens which were labeled by GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023). (3) Following Asai et al. (2024), we also
376   cited the results of retrieval-augmented baselines trained with private data: Ret-ChatGPT and Ret-

383   can conclude the following findings:
384
      First, the proposed method can significantly improve the performance of RAG and Self-RAG.
385
      Specifically, as shown in table 1, CRAG outperformed RAG by margins of 19.0% accuracy on
386   PopQA, 14.9% FactScore on Biography, 36.6% accuracy on PubHealth, and 8.1% accuracy on
387   Arc-Challenge when based on SelfRAG-LLaMA2-7b, as well as by margins of 9.6% accuracy on
388   PopQA, 2.8% FactScore on Biography, and 2.0% on Arc-Challenge when based on LLaMA2-hf-7b.
389   Compared with the current state-of-the-art Self-RAG, Self-CRAG outperformed it by margins of
390   20.0% accuracy on PopQA, 36.9% FactScore on Biography, and 4.0% accuracy on Arc-Challenge
391   when based on LLaMA2-hf-7b, as well as by margins of 6.9% accuracy on PopQA, 5.0% FactScore
392   on Biography, and 2.4% accuracy on PubHealth, when based on SelfRAG-LLaMA2-7b. These results
393   demonstrated the adaptability of CRAG which is plug-and-play and can be implemented into RAG-
394
      based approaches.
395   Second, the proposed method demonstrated great generalizability across a variety of generation
396   tasks. In particular, these benchmarks reported in Table 1 respectively represent different practical
397   scenarios including short-form entity generation (PopQA), long-form generation (Biography), and
398   closed-set tasks (PubHealth, Arc-Challenge). These results verified the consistent effectiveness
399   of CRAG. Its versatility across a spectrum of tasks underscores its robust capabilities and
400
      generalizability across diverse scenarios.
401   Third, the proposed method exhibited greater flexibility in replacing the underlying LLM generator.
402   It can be seen that CRAG still showed competitive performance when the underlying LLMs was
403   changed from SelfRAG-LLaMA2-7b to LLaMA2-hf-7b, while the performance of Self-RAG dropped
404   significantly, even underperforming the standard RAG on several benchmarks. The reason for these
405   results is that Self-RAG needs to be instruction-tuned using human or LLM annotated data to learn
406
      to output special critic tokens as needed, while this ability is not learned in common LLMs. CRAG
      does not have any requirements for this ability. As you can imagine, when more advanced LLMs are
407
      available in the future, they can be coupled with CRAG easily, while additional instruction tuning
408
      is still necessary for Self-RAG.

528   6     C ONCLUSION & L IMITATION
529
530   This paper studies the problem where RAG-based approaches are challenged if retrieval goes
531
      wrong, thereby exposing inaccurate and misleading knowledge to generative LMs. Corrective
      Retrieval Augmented Generation is proposed to improve the robustness of generation. Essentially,
532
      a lightweight retrieval evaluator is to estimate and trigger three knowledge retrieval actions
533
      discriminately. With the further leverage of web search and optimized knowledge utilization,
534   CRAG has significantly improved the ability of automatic self-correction and efficient utilization
535   of retrieved documents. Experiments extensively demonstrate its adaptability to RAG-based
536   approaches as well as generalizability across short- and long-form generation tasks. While we
537   primarily proposed to improve the RAG framework from a corrective perspective and CRAG
538   can be seamlessly coupled with various RAG-based approaches, fine-tuning an external retrieval
539   evaluator is inevitable. How to eliminate this external evaluator and equip LLMs with better retrieval
      evaluation capabilities will be our future work.

      B     E XPERIMENTS
865
866
      B.1   TASKS , DATASETS AND M ETRICS
867
868   CRAG was evaluated on four datasets, which are in public domain and licensed for research
869   purposes, including:
870
      PopQA (Mallen et al., 2023) is a short-form generation task. Generally, only one entity of factual
871
      knowledge is expected to be answered for each single question. In our experiments, we exactly
872   followed the setting in Self-RAG (Asai et al., 2024) which evaluated methods on a long-tail subset
873   consisting of 1,399 rare entity queries whose monthly Wikipedia page views are less than 100.
874   Accuracy was adopted as the evaluation metric.
875
      Biography (Min et al., 2023) is a long-form generation task that is tasked to generate a detailed
876
      biography about a certain entity. Following previous work, FactScore (Min et al., 2023) was adopted
877
      to evaluate the generated biographies.
878
879   PubHealth (Zhang et al., 2023a) is a task in health care domain consisting of true-or-false questions.
880   Claims are represented about health with factual information, and the model is tasked to verify the
881
      authenticity and give the judgment. Accuracy was adopted as the evaluation metric.
882   Arc-Challenge (Bhakthavatsalam et al., 2021) is a multiple-choice question task about some daily
883   commonsense science phenomena. Given a scientific event that occurs in daily life, the model is
884   required to select the correct description among 3 or 4 optional choices. Accuracy was adopted as
885   the evaluation metric as well.
886
887   B.2   E XPERIMENTS COMPUTE R ESOURCES
888
889
      We used NVIDIA A800 80GB GPU for experiments. For LLaMA-2 (7B) generation, it occupies
      over 40GB memory during inference. For T5-large (0.77B) fine-tuning, it takes much less compared
890
      with LLaMA-2.

      B.3   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
893
894   Retrieval Evaluator: We fine-tuned the retrieval evaluator based on the lightweight T5-large (Raffel
895   et al., 2020) pre-trained model. The dataset we used is the version provided by Self-RAG (Asai et al.,
896   2024). Specifically, the original PopQA dataset consists of 14k samples, 1,399 of which were used
897   for testing following Self-RAG (Asai et al., 2024), and the remaining were used for fine-tuning
898   to avoid information leakage. Besides, the fine-tuned evaluator was transferred and also utilized
899   on the Bio, Pub and ARC datasets during inference. The label of positive samples was 1, while
900   that of negative ones was -1. At inference, the evaluator scored the relevance from -1 to 1 for each
901   document. The two confidence thresholds for triggering one of the three actions were set empirically.
902
      Specifically, they were set as (0.59, -0.99) in PopQA, (0.5, -0.91) in PubQA and Arc-Challenge, as
      well as (0.95, -0.91) in Biography.
903
904   Internal Knowledge: To obtain fine-grained retrieval results, we segmented the retrieved results
905   into internal strips. If a retrieved result is as short as one or two sentences, it is regarded as an
906   individual strip, otherwise, retrieval documents are required to be split into smaller units which
907   generally consist of a few sentences according to the total length. The scale is assumed to include
908
      an independent piece of information, and the filtering is based on the segments. We directly adopted
      the evaluator again for knowledge strips filtering, and the top-k is set to 5, filter threshold as -0.5.
909
910   External Knowledge: Google Search API was adopted to search for the relevant URLs, top-k is set
911   to 5, and pages from Wikipedia will be added preferentially. The searched web pages are generally
912   in the form of HTML files, where content is split with special tokens like <p> and </p>. Thus an
913   extra segmentation like the knowledge refinement is not required, related knowledge paragraphs can
914
      be directly selected with the evaluator similar to internal knowledge. In this way, the accuracy of the
      search outcomes can be ensured without compromising the quality and relevance of the information
915
      used for generation.
916
917   Generator: As CRAG is a plug-and-play method, all generation models that can be utilized in
      RAG fit our approach as well. To be consistent with baselines for comparison, we adopted LLaMA2


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      (Touvron et al., 2023b) for the generation. We first introduced the LLaMA2-hf-7b from huggingface
919   to generate responses. Since Self-RAG (Asai et al., 2024) fine-tuned LLaMA2 and reached a new
920   state-of-the-art performance on several tasks, we further utilized the launched model, SelfRAG-
921   LLaMA2-7b, as a new generator to be consistent with their work and study the specific improvement
922   of our method.
923
      Self-CRAG: To demonstrate that our plug-and-play approach can be utilized in other concurrent
924
      studies, we specifically designed to insert our CRAG into the Self-RAG (Asai et al., 2024)
925   framework and named it Self-CRAG. Self-RAG is an advanced RAG approach that introduces a
926   critic model to decide whether to retrieve and which retrieved document to be referred for generation.
927   It meets our demand for deciding which action to be triggered, thus we replaced the retrieved
928   items in Self-RAG with our processed internal knowledge for Correct, external knowledge for
929   Incorrect, and combined knowledge for Ambiguous.
