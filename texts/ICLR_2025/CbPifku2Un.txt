009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Learning a safe policy from offline data without interacting with the environ-
012            ment is crucial for deploying reinforcement learning (RL) policies. Recent ap-
013            proaches leverage transformers to address tasks under various goals, demonstrat-
014            ing a strong generalizability for broad applications. However, these methods ei-
015            ther completely overlook safety concerns during policy deployment or simplify
016
               safe RL as a dual-objective problem, disregarding the differing priorities between
               costs and rewards, as well as the additional challenge of multi-task identifica-
017
               tion caused by cost sparsity. To address these issues, we propose Safe Multi-task
018
               Pretraining with Constraint Prioritized Decision Transformer (SMACOT), which
019            utilizes the Decision Transformer (DT) to accommodate varying safety threshold
020            objectives during policy deployment while ensuring scalability. It introduces a
021            Constraint Prioritized Return-To-Go (CPRTG) token to emphasize cost priorities
022            in the Transformer’s inference process, effectively balancing reward maximization
023            with safety constraints. Additionally, a Constraint Prioritized Prompt Encoder is
024            designed to leverage the sparsity of cost information for task identification. Exten-
025            sive experiments on the public OSRL dataset demonstrate that SMACOT achieves
026            exceptional safety performance in both single-task and multi-task scenarios, satis-
027
               fying different safety constraints in over 2x as many environments compared with
               strong baselines, showcasing its superior safety capability.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Deep reinforcement learning (RL), a machine learning method that optimizes decision-making by
033   maximizing cumulative rewards, has gained significant attention (Wang et al., 2022) and demon-
034   strated remarkable potential in applications like autonomous driving (Zhao et al., 2024), industrial
035   robotics (Haarnoja et al., 2024), healthcare (Yu et al., 2021), and the value alignment or reasoning of
036   large language models (Ouyang et al., 2022; Shinn et al., 2023). However, real-world applications
037   often require policies to adhere to additional safety constraints, such as speed and lane restrictions
038   in autonomous driving to prevent accidents (Krasowski et al., 2020; Wang, 2022), as well as fuel
039
      limitations to ensure the vehicle remains operational (Lin et al., 2023). Furthermore, the trial-and-
      error nature of online RL becomes impractical when addressing safety concerns (Xu et al., 2022b).
040
      Consequently, offline safe RL (Le et al., 2019), which learns safe policies using pre-collected offline
041
      data without interacting with the real environment, has emerged as a major research focus.
042
043   Previous works in offline safe RL typically model the problem as a Constrained Markov Decision
044   Process (CMDP) (Altman, 2021) with fixed elements, and ensure policy safety by solving con-
045
      strained optimization problems (Wachi et al., 2024). However, such modeling restricts the policy to
      handling only one safety threshold under the specific task, reducing flexibility in real-world applica-
046
      tions where multiple tasks and varying safety thresholds are often required. For instance, different
047
      roadways may have distinct speed limits, and vehicles may need to adjust behavior based on fuel
048   levels to maintain safety performance. Therefore, multi-task safe RL, aiming to solve the mentioned
049   problems, should be considered a significant topic for RL deployment. Leveraging the strong ex-
050   pressive power and scalability of Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017; Lin et al., 2022), several works
051   have attempted to apply them to multi-task decision-making. For instance, MGDT (Lee et al., 2022)
052   simply models all visual tasks in a consistent sequential format, leveraging the Decision Trans-
053   former (DT) (Chen et al., 2021a) framework for multi-task learning. Prompt-DT (Xu et al., 2022c)
      introduces additional expert trajectory segments as prompts, enhancing multi-task identification in


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      non-visual tasks. These approaches showcase the powerful ability of Transformer-based sequential
055   decision models to quickly adapt their behavior based on different token inputs to achieve various
056   goals. Consequently, recent works have also explored applying Transformers to offline safe RL for
057   decision-making under diverse safety thresholds. These methods either employ Cost-To-Go (CTG)
058   tokens (Zhang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023) or logic tokens (Guo et al., 2024) to integrate safety con-
059   straints into the Transformer inputs, allowing the policy to adjust its conservatism based on different
060   safety-related tokens, thus facilitating varied decision-making across numerous safety thresholds.
061   Despite their progress in multi-task or multi-safety threshold decision-making, these methods have
062   not effectively utilized Transformers for multi-task safe RL. Firstly, they overlook the differing pri-
063   orities between satisfying safety constraints and maximizing rewards by treating safety and rewards
064   equally in model inputs. As a result, during deployment, if safety constraints conflict with reward
065   maximization, the policy may prioritize rewards, potentially ignoring safety limits and leading to un-
066   safe decisions. Additionally, these methods fail to address the extra challenges of task identification
067   posed by multi-task safe scenarios, where differences between tasks mostly come from cost varia-
068   tions. Due to the sparsity of cost signals, short trajectory segments may lack sufficient distinguishing
069
      information, leading to failures in task identification and subsequently poorer safety performance.
      In conclusion, applying Transformers to multi-task safe RL still presents two key challenges that
070
      need to be addressed:
071
072          • How to model the higher priority of cost compared to reward within the Transformer?
073
             • How to design prompts to extract information from sparse costs for task identification?
074
075   To address the aforementioned challenges, we propose a novel algorithm Safe Multi-task Pretrain-
076   ing with Constraint Prioritized Decision Transformer (SMACOT) built upon the DT framework.
077   Firstly, to prioritize costs over rewards, SMACOT introduces a novel Constraint Prioritized Return-
078   To-Go (CPRTG) token by explicitly modeling RTG conditioned on CTG. Next, to efficiently extract
079   task-related information from sparse costs, SMACOT introduces a Constraint Prioritized Prompt
080   Encoder, which segments samples in the trajectory into safe and unsafe patches based on cost in-
081   formation, and then encodes them separately. This enables effective task identification based on the
082   varying safe (or unsafe) transition distributions for different tasks. SMACOT effectively resolves
083
      the conflict between reward maximization and safety constraints satisfaction, while efficiently over-
      coming the challenge of task identification caused by sparse characteristics of costs. Extensive ex-
084
      periments on the open-source OSRL (Liu et al., 2024b) dataset demonstrate that SMACOT achieves
085
      exceptional safety performance across various safety thresholds in both single-task and multi-task
086   scenarios. Compared to the previous SOTA sequence modeling method, it meets safety constraints
087   in more than 2x tasks, and is currently the only algorithm to surpass the Oracle baseline BC-Safe.

090   2   R ELATED W ORK
091
092   Safe RL and Offline Safe RL Safe RL ensures the safe deployment of policies by requiring
093
      them to meet additional safety constraints besides maximizing rewards, which is often modeled as
      constrained optimization problems (Garcıa & Fernández, 2015; Wachi et al., 2024), and Lagrangian
094
      multiplier methods are used as foundational techniques to solve it (Wachi et al., 2024). Lagrangian
095
      multiplier-based algorithms typically learn a cost value function and a parameterized Lagrangian
096   multiplier, adjusting the multiplier based on the policy’s cumulative cost to enhance safety (Chow
097   et al., 2018; Stooke et al., 2020; Tessler et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2021b). However, these algorithms
098   fail to consider the unsafe interactions with real-world environments during training, making them
099   impractical. To make safe RL conform to reality, some works recently explore to learn safe policies
100   using only pre-collected data, avoiding unsafe exploration, and hasten the offline safe RL. These
101   approaches evaluate the safety performance of policies in a conservative manner, treating out-of-
102   distribution (OOD) samples as unsafe to reduce visits to these regions (Le et al., 2019; Xu et al.,
103   2022a; Zheng et al., 2024; Yao et al., 2024), thus mitigating the negative impacts of extrapolation
104
      errors (Fujimoto et al., 2019).
105   Policy Learning as Sequence Modeling Due to the impressive performance of Transformers in
106   complex sequential tasks such as large language models (Zhao et al., 2023) and time series analy-
107   sis (Nie et al., 2023), many works aim to leverage their expressive power in offline RL, which can
      also be modeled a sequential task. For instance, DT (Chen et al., 2021a) uses a GPT-like Transformer


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      architecture (Achiam et al., 2023) with historical sequences and RTG tokens to infer optimal actions,
109   breaking traditional RL paradigms and circumventing extrapolation errors directly. Variants like
110   ODT (Zheng et al., 2022) and QDT (Yamagata et al., 2023) enhance DT’s performance with online
111   fine-tuning and Q-Learning (Watkins & Dayan, 1992), respectively. MGDT (Lee et al., 2022) and
112   Prompt-DT (Xu et al., 2022c) extend DT to multi-task scenarios by using visual inputs or adding ad-
113   ditional expert trajectory segments as prompts. For the safe problem, SaFormer (Zhang et al., 2023)
114   and CDT (Liu et al., 2023) introduce CTG tokens to apply DT first in safe RL. SDT (Guo et al.,
115   2024) utilizes signal temporal logic tokens to incorporate more information about safety constraints
116   into DT. However, they all fail to account for the differing priorities between RTG and safety-related
117
      tokens, resulting in an incomplete resolution of the conflict between reward maximization and safety
      constraints, which motivates our work. More related work will be discussed in App. B.

122   3.1   S AFE RL AND M ULTI - TASK S AFE RL
123
      Safe RL can be modeled as a Constrained Markov Decision Process (CMDP), which is defined as a
124
      tuple ⟨S, A, r, c, P, γ, b⟩, where S and A represent the state space and the action space, respectively,
125
      r : S × A → [−Rmax , Rmax ] and c : S × A → {0, 1} denote the reward and cost functions, respec-
126   tively. P : S × A × S → [0, 1] is the transition probability function, γ ∈ (0, 1) is the discount factor,
127   and b represents the safety threshold. A policy π : S → ∆(A) maps states to action    P∞ distributions.
128   Under a given policy π, the reward return can be expressed as R(π) = Eτ ∼Pπ [ t=0 γ t r(st , at )],
129   where τ = (s0 , a0 , s1 , a1 , . . . ) denotes a trajectory and τ ∼ Pπ indicates that the distribution of
130   trajectories induced byP∞ policy π and the environment dynamics P . Similarly, the cost return is given
131   by C(π) = Eτ ∼Pπ [ t=0 γ t c(st , at )]. Thus, the objective of solving a CMDP is to learn a policy
132   that maximizes reward return while adhering to safety constraints, which can be represented as:
133                                           max R(π), s.t. C(π) ≤ b.                                      (1)
                                                π
134
135   In multi-task safe RL, the policy π needs to be trained across multiple tasks to develop the capability
136   to handle them simultaneously. Each task is defined as a unique CMDP, and differences between
137   tasks may arise from changes of any element in (S, A, P, r, c, b). Specifically, different tasks with
138   the same (S, A, P ) are referred to as same-domain, otherwise the cross-domain tasks. In this paper,
139
      both scenarios are considered. We refer to the “domain” in this context as the “environment”, which
      is uniquely determined by a group of (S, A, P ), and “tasks” in an environment only differ in (r, c, b).
140
      For simplicity, we assume that the environment ID is known during both training and deployment.
141
142   During training, the policy is provided with a set of environments {Ei }N                    M
                                                                                i=1 and tasks {Tj }j=1 , where
143   each task belongs to a specific environment, i.e. an injection that maps task ID to environment ID
144   is known. During deployment, the policy is required to make decisions for a task T which belongs
145
      to environment Ei . If T ∈ {Tj }M  j=1 , then the policy can only utilize one expert trajectory for task
      identification. Otherwise, it is provided with L expert trajectories of T to achieve efficient transfer.

      4.1    CPRTG T OKEN G ENERATION
193
194   In safe RL, the policy optimization objective is represented by Eqn. 1. This constrained optimization
195   problem implicitly prioritizes constraint satisfaction over reward maximization, as solutions that fail
196   to meet constraints cannot be considered valid (Heath, 2018). In DT, these objectives are typically
197   expressed through the RTG token R̂t and the CTG token Ĉt . To ensure safety performance, CTG
198   should be given higher priority when the policy falls short of meeting both reward maximization and
199   safety constraint objectives.
200   Modeling RTG Conditioned on CTG To achieve the prioritization of CTG in SMACOT, a
201   straightforward approach is to model RTG as conditioned on CTG, i.e., learning the model p(R̂t |Ĉt )
202   from the offline dataset. Since the relationship between RTG and CTG is primarily derived from
203   offline trajectories, where multiple RTG values might correspond to the same CTG, we further con-
204   strain the generation process by incorporating state information at each time step, and model p as a
205   non-deterministic normal distribution N approximated by a neural network qϕ . Formally, given the
                                                                       |D|
206   offline dataset D = {(st , at , s′t , rt , ct , R̂t , Ĉt , t)k }k=1 , we have:
207

      In this section, we present our experimental analysis conducted on 26 tasks from the OSRL (Liu
341
      et al., 2024b) dataset. The experiments aim to answer the following questions: (1) How does the tra-
342   ditional DT behave when reward maximization conflicts with safety satisfaction, and can SMACOT
343   address this issue (Sec. 5.2)? (2) Can SMACOT outperform other baselines in safety performance
344   across single-task and multi-task settings, and do its components contribute to this (Sec. 5.3)? (3)
345   Will pre-training SMACOT helps improve learning efficiency in new tasks (Sec. 5.4)?
346
      For a thorough evaluation, all results are averaged across twenty evaluation episodes, three random
347
      seeds, and four safety thresholds. For the baselines that do not use the DT framework, we train sep-
348   arate models for each safety threshold and report the average performance across these thresholds.
349   For page limits, additional experimental information and results will be provided in App. G.

352   5.1   BASELINES AND TASKS
353
354   To provide a more comprehensive evaluation of SMACOT’s performance, we conducted experi-
355   ments with the following algorithms:
356
357          • Ours: (1) SMACOT (ST) is the single-task version of SMACOT with unified hyperpa-
358
               rameters. (2) SMACOT (MT) is the multi-task version of SMACOT with unified hyperpa-
               rameters. (3) SMACOT (Oracle) is the single-task version of SMACOT with task-specific
359
               βend . It is not compared directly with the other baselines due to hyperparameter differences.
360
361          • Single-task: (4) CPQ (Xu et al., 2022a) applies CQL’s (Kumar et al., 2020) conservative
362            estimation to the cost critic and achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) in traditional CMDP based
363            offline safe RL algorithms. (5) CDT (Liu et al., 2023) adds CTG token to DT and reduces
364            the conflict between CTG and RTG via data augmentation. It achieves SOTA in sequence
365            modeling based offline safe RL algorithms. (6) BC-Safe modifies the dataset for behavior
366            cloning only on safe trajectories, which is considered Oracle and also will not be compared
367            to the other algorithms due to different learning data.
368
             • Multi-task: (7) MTCDT extends CDT to multi-task settings with separate input or output
369
               heads for each environment without prompts. (8) Prompt-CDT (Xu et al., 2022c) builds
370            on MTCDT with additional expert trajectory segments as prompts.
371
372   We first selected 21 tasks from the OSRL dataset, all part of the Safety-Gymnasium (Ji et al., 2023)
373   benchmark, including 16 navigation tasks and 5 velocity tasks. The navigation tasks use 2 types of
374   robots (Point and Car) across 4 scenarios: Button, Circle, Goal, and Push, with 2 tasks per scenario.
375   The original 5 velocity tasks involve robots Ant, HalfCheetah, Hopper, Swimmer, and Walker2d,
376   with one task for each robot. To enhance the dataset, we added one additional velocity task per
377   robot with different velocity thresholds, bringing the total to 26 tasks across 13 environments. More
      details about baselines and tasks are provided in App. E.

      5.2   C ASE S TUDY: W HEN RTG CONFLICTS WITH CTG
379
380   To explore the behavior of previous DT algorithms when RTG and CTG objectives conflict, we
381   conducted experiments using the CDT algorithm on the PointButton1 task, just as shown in Fig. 2(a).
382   We tested four different target rewards (initial RTG inputs) [10, 20, 30, 40] and four different target
383   costs [10, 20, 40, 80] to compare policy behavior. The results, shown in Fig. 2(b) and Fig. 2(c),
384   reveal that as the target reward increases, the achieved reward also rises, while the cost remains
385   mostly unchanged regardless of the target cost. However, as the target reward increases, the cost also
386   noticeably increases, indicating that the policy may prioritize RTG over CTG when they conflict.
387
      This reveals why previous DT methods fail to solve the conflict between rewards and costs.
388   Then, we aim to demonstrate that the CPRTG in SMACOT effectively resolves this issue. We
389   conducted experiments on the same task using SMACOT with four different target costs, as shown
390   in Fig. 2(d). The results show that the average CPRTG R̃ increases with the target cost but remains
391   within a conservative range, while the real cost increases with the target cost but stays within safe
392   limits, proving the effectiveness of SMACOT in resolving RTG-CTG conflicts.
393
394   5.3   C OMPETITIVE R ESULTS AND A BLATIONS
395
396   To validate the generality of SMACOT’s outstanding safety performance, we conduct extensive ex-
397   periments in the OSRL dataset. The experimental results of SMACOT and various baselines across
398   26 tasks are shown in Tab. 1. In the single-task setting, CPQ, which is based on CMDP, shows the
399
      poorest safety performance due to significant extrapolation errors and unstable training, failing to
      achieve high reward returns. In contrast, CDT, based on sequence modeling, performs better, with
400
      higher rewards and improved safety over CPQ, though still lacking satisfactory safety performance.
401
      SMACOT demonstrates a substantial improvement in safety, nearly doubling the average safety per-
402   formance compared to CDT, without significantly sacrificing rewards, highlighting its effectiveness
403   in balancing reward maximization with safety constraints.
404
405
      Next, comparing the results in the multi-task setting, both MTCDT and Prompt-CDT show unsatis-
      factory safety performance. Part of this is due to CDT’s inherent limitations in safety, while another
406
      factor is task identification failure. This issue is particularly evident in the Velocity tasks, where
407
      both MTCDT and Prompt-CDT exceed safety thresholds by tens of multiples in some tasks. This
408   indicates that relying solely on the first K time steps or short expert trajectory prompts fails to cap-
409   ture necessary cost-related information. In contrast, SMACOT demonstrates safety performance on
410   par with the single-task setting, showcasing the effectiveness of the Constraint Prioritized Prompt
411   Encoder in generating prompts for task identification.
412
      Finally, comparing the results in the Oracle setting, BC-Safe effectively ensures policy safety by
413
      filtering offline data, meeting safety constraints in most environments. However, it cannot adapt to
414   different safety thresholds with a single policy and requires retraining and data filtering for each new
415   threshold. SMACOT, on the other hand, adapts to varying safety thresholds using a single policy
416   model through different CTG token inputs and fine-tunes conservatism via the βend hyperparameter.
417   As a result, SMACOT achieves safety in more environments and typically yields higher rewards
418   than BC-Safe. This demonstrates that SMACOT effectively leverages both safe and unsafe training
419   data, resulting in superior decision-making capability. The accompanying Fig. 3(a) shows the total
420   number of tasks where each algorithm meets safety constraints, revealing that SMACOT consistently
421   outperforms other baselines across all settings, achieving safe performance in more than twice as
422
      many tasks as previous approaches. Experiments about SMACOT under different safety thresholds
      and the visualization of prompt encodings are provided in App. G.
423
424   Next, we conducted ablation studies on the 26 tasks in the multi-task setting to investigate the impact
425   of different modules on SMACOT’s performance. The baselines used in the ablation studies include:
426   (1) W/o CP means SMACOT without CPRTG. (2) Det CP uses a deterministic qϕ rather than a
427   normal distribution. (3) W/o CD does not use the CTG-based β decay. (4) W/o PE does not use
428
      the Constraint Prioritized Prompt Encoder for prompt generation. (5) Simp PE uses a simple MLP
      prompt encoder without separating safe and unsafe patches. (6) Small DT utilizes a DT backbone
429
      with fewer parameters.
430

      In this work, we present a novel algorithm, SMACOT, designed to tackle the challenge of learning
      safe policies using the Transformer in both single-task and multi-task scenarios. SMACOT assigns
532
      higher priority to safety constraints through the use of CPRTG token, effectively addressing con-
533
      flicts between RTG and CTG. Additionally, the design of Constraint Prioritized Prompt Encoder
534   leverages the sparse and binary nature of cost for efficient information extraction and task identifi-
535   cation. Extensive experiments on the OSRL dataset demonstrate SMACOT’s strong performance in
536   both safety and task identification, as well as its effective task transfer capabilities. Further studies
537   on optimizing the Constraint Prioritized Prompt Encoder’s learning, such as introducing contrastive
538   loss, could be a promising way to improve task identification performance. In addition, achieving
539   zero-cost exploration in safe RL using large language models (Wang et al., 2024) and deploying safe
      RL in embodied robots (Liu et al., 2024a) are also highly promising future research directions.

875   which is difficult to alter. As for ϵC , we rely on the generalization ability of the Transformer for the
876   CTG to adapt to different safety thresholds, and thus, we do not wish to modify the initial settings
877
      or update method of the CTG. Therefore, a natural approach to improving the lower bound of policy
      performance is to reduce the value of ϵR . In this context, the CPRTG generator in SMACOT can be
878
      viewed as a neural network approximation of p2 (R̂t+1 |Ĉt+1 , s′ , s, R̂t , Ĉt , a), which helps to lower
879
      ϵR during deployment.
880
881   Future research could also explore addressing OOD CTG values, for instance, by mapping large
882   initial CTGs (those exceeding the maximum in the offline dataset) to the dataset’s maximum, thus
883   ensuring safety while further mitigating OOD-related extrapolation errors.

885   B    M ORE D ETAILS A BOUT R ELATED W ORK
886
887   Safe RL Safe RL is a kind of machine learning approach aimed at learning policies that maximize
888   cumulative rewards while adhering to additional predefined safety constraints (Gu et al., 2022).
889   Safe RL algorithms are broadly divided into two categories: safe exploration and safe optimiza-
890   tion (Garcıa & Fernández, 2015). Safe exploration algorithms do not focus on directly optimizing
891   the policy. Instead, they aim to modify the policy’s behavior through additional mechanisms to pre-
892
      vent violations of safety constraints. A typical example of these algorithms is shielding-based meth-
      ods (Alshiekh et al., 2018; Cheng et al., 2019; Xiao et al., 2023), which construct or learn logical
893
      structures known as ”shields” or ”barriers” that ensure the actions taken in a given state comply with
894
      the safety constraints. However, the decoupling from policy learning of safe exploration methods
895   results in lower learning efficiency, leading to a growing focus on safe optimization algorithms. Safe
896   optimization algorithms typically model the problem as a CMDP, with Lagrangian multiplier-based
897   algorithms being the mainstream solution, as discussed in the main paper. Other than Lagrangian
898   multiplier-based algorithms, trust region methods are among the most prevalent approaches in safe
899   optimization. They attempt to keep policies within a safe trust region during updates via low-order
900   Taylor expansions (Achiam et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2020) or variational inference (Liu et al., 2022).
901   Due to their robust learning process, trust region methods are also further applied to multi-agent
902   scenarios (Gu et al., 2023). However, their on-policy nature results in lower data efficiency. In re-
903
      sponse, recent works have increasingly focused on off-policy safe optimization. CAL (Wu et al.,
      2024) improves the optimization of Lagrange multipliers using the augmented Lagrangian method
904
      and enhances the conservatism of the cost function learned off-policy via the use of upper confi-
905
      dence bound. Meanwhile, SafeDreamer (Huang et al., 2024) increases data efficiency by learning
906   an environment model and using model rollouts for data augmentation. Recently, more attention
907   has been directed toward safety-conditioned RL. CCPO (Yao et al., 2023) effectively adapts to dif-
908   ferent safety thresholds in an online algorithm by incorporating the safety threshold into the input
909   of CVPO. On the other hand, SDT (Guo et al., 2024) attempts to integrate safety prior knowledge
910   expressed through temporal logic into the input of DT, further enhancing the policy’s safety perfor-
911   mance while adapting to various temporal logic safety constraints. This provides a fresh perspective
912   for the practical application of safety RL. With the growing body of research on safe RL, these algo-
913   rithms have found increasing applications in various fields. Notable examples include ensuring the
914
      safety of vehicles in autonomous driving (Kiran et al., 2021) and safeguarding robots in industrial
      settings (Brunke et al., 2022). In cutting-edge research, safe RL has also been applied to safe value
915
      alignment in large language models (Dai et al., 2024).
916
917   Offline RL Offline RL trains policies using pre-collected datasets, avoiding real-world trial and
      error, which is critical for deploying RL in practical settings. Its primary challenge is addressing


                                                          17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      the extrapolation errors (Prudencio et al., 2023). Methods like BCQ (Fujimoto et al., 2019) and
919   CQL (Kumar et al., 2020) tackle this by constraining actions to those seen in the offline data or by
920   penalizing unseen actions. Others, such as MOReL (Kidambi et al., 2020) and MOPO (Yu et al.,
921   2020), learn the environment models from the offline data and utilize these models with uncertainty
922   estimates to avoid OOD regions with low model accuracy. However, these methods only focus on
923   reducing extrapolation errors, without addressing the challenge of generalizing in OOD areas. To
924   tackle this limitation, MOREC (Luo et al., 2024) employs adversarial learning in the model learning
925   process, improving model generalization abilities.
926   Meta RL Meta RL is similar to multi-task RL, with both involving multi-task training. However,
927   Meta RL does not receive additional expert trajectories as prompts during testing. Instead, it must
928   collect data in the unknown environment to generate prompts. Additionally, it focuses on training
929   across large-scale similar tasks for generalization to new ones (Zhu et al., 2023). PEARL (Rakelly
930   et al., 2019) uses a probabilistic encoder to facilitate task identification and employs Thompson
931   sampling for data collection in new environments. Other works, like FOCAL (Li et al., 2021)
932   and CORRO (Yuan & Lu, 2022), focus on designing contrastive loss functions for the encoder,
933
      improving task encoding robustness. COSTA (Guan et al., 2024) first considers safety in meta RL,
      designing a cost-based contrastive loss and a safety-aware data collection framework, improving
934
      policy safety in both task identification and deployment.

       In this section, we further examine the zero-shot generation ability of SMACOT to new tasks with
1206
       different constraints and different safety thresholds.
1207
1208   First, we conducted experiments to evaluate the performance of three different multi-task algo-
1209   rithms, including SMACOT, when directly deployed on new tasks with different constraints without
1210
       fine-tuning. The results are shown in Tab. 5. It can be observed that none of the methods achieve
       satisfactory safety performance when facing new constraints without fine-tuning. We believe this
1211
       phenomenon is expected, as in our pre-training tasks, only two tasks are similar to the target gen-
1212
       eralization task, making it difficult to acquire the necessary knowledge for generalization from just
1213   these two tasks. In contrast, traditional meta RL, which emphasizes generalization, may require pre-
1214   training on dozens of similar tasks to achieve even limited generalization capability (Rakelly et al.,
1215   2019; Li et al., 2021; Yuan & Lu, 2022). Additionally, we believe that, in safety-critical settings,
1216   fine-tuning is a more appropriate approach when encountering new constraints, as it better ensures
1217   safety performance.
1218
       Next, we tested the generalization capability of SMACOT and CDT under ten safety thresholds in
1219   four tasks. The results are shown in Fig. 6. It is shown that SMACOT is able to effectively adapt
1220   under any safety threshold, ensuring the safety performance of the policy. At the same time, both
1221   its cost and reward exhibit a clear increasing trend as the safety threshold rises. Although CDT
1222   shows some advantages in terms of reward, it clearly lags behind in terms of safety. In three of
1223   the environments, it fails to demonstrate adaptability to different safety thresholds, resulting in poor
1224   safety performance when the safety threshold is low. These experimental results validate the strong
1225   generalization capability of SMACOT across different safety thresholds.
1226   Based on the results from Fig. 2, Fig. 3(c), and Fig. 6, we can perform a more comprehensive
1227   analysis of the trade-off between safety and performance. First, from the changes in cost and reward
1228   under different objectives for the CDT algorithm, as shown in Fig. 2, we observe that the trends in
1229   cost and reward are generally consistent. As the reward increases, the cost also rises, leading to a
1230   decrease in safety. We can attribute these identical changes in reward and cost to the same factor,
1231   namely, the conservatism of the policy. Therefore, the core objective in safe RL is to find an optimal
1232   level of conservatism for the policy. In traditional safe RL algorithms, once a policy is learned,
1233
       its conservatism is fixed. However, Fig. 3(c) and Fig. 6 demonstrate two ways in which SMACOT
       can effectively adjust the conservatism of the policy without altering the parameters of the policy’s
1234
       neural network. Fig. 3(c) shows that by adjusting the βend hyperparameter, it is possible to change the
1235
       policy’s conservatism effectively while keeping the safety threshold fixed. A higher value of βend
1236   makes the policy more aggressive, leaning towards higher rewards while slightly compromising
1237   safety. Fig. 6 shows that as the safety threshold (i.e., the initial CTG input) increases, SMACOT
1238   also becomes more aggressive and achieves higher rewards. Thus, when the algorithm is applied

       G.6               C OMPARISON WITH FISOR
1380
       To further demonstrate the benefits of using CPRTG in SMACOT for improving policy safety per-
1381
       formance, we additionally compared it with the latest SOTA offline safe RL method, FISOR (Zheng
1382   et al., 2024). FISOR uses a diffusion model (Yang et al., 2023) to identify the feasible region and
1383   solve the hard constraint problem. When the policy is in an unsafe region, FISOR guides the policy
1384   towards a safe region, and when the policy is in the safe region, it seeks the behavior that maximizes
1385   reward while keeping the policy within the safe region. We conducted the comparison in two differ-
1386   ent settings: Oracle and Single-Task. In the Single-Task setting, FISOR was trained using the hyper-
1387   parameters proposed in the original paper for all tasks. In the Oracle setting, we adjusted FISOR’s
1388   reverse expectile parameter τ for each task, [0.8, 0.8, 0.7, 0.7, 0.8, 0.8, 0.9, 0.8] for the 8 test tasks
1389   specifically. According to the ablation results in FISOR, τ is positively correlated with the conserva-
1390
       tiveness of the policy. The results are shown in Table 7. First, in the Single-Task setting, SMACOT
       demonstrates significantly better safety performance than FISOR, highlighting the effectiveness of
1391
       CPRTG in resolving the conflict between reward and safety. In the Oracle setting, while both meth-
1392
       ods are able to meet safety constraints for all tasks, SMACOT achieves significantly better reward
1393   performance, which underscores the higher flexibility of CPRTG in adjusting the conservativeness
1394   of the policy. Another clear advantage of SMACOT in the Oracle setting is that the hyperparameter
1395   βend is a test-phase-only parameter. This means that adjusting this parameter does not require retrain-
1396   ing the policy, making it extremely convenient to fine-tune. In contrast, FISOR’s hyperparameter
1397   τ is a training-phase parameter, so adjusting it requires retraining the policy. Overall, these results
1398   clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of CPRTG in handling the reward-safety trade-off, which is a
1399   core challenge in safe RL.

       G.8             D ISCUSSION AND A BLATION ON INVERSE DYNAMICS MODEL g
1438
1439   In Sec. 4.2, we introduced an additional inverse dynamics model g to compute the inverse dynam-
1440   ics error for training the environment-specific state encoders. The primary motivation for using the
1441   inverse dynamics model is to address tasks with identical state and action spaces but different dy-
1442   namics transitions. While such tasks have not appeared in our main experiments, they are still quite
1443   common (Nagabandi et al., 2019; Eysenbach et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2024). In these cases, the
1444   inverse dynamics error based on the inverse dynamics model can effectively produce different state
1445
       representations during the environment-specific state encoder learning phase, thereby reducing the
       learning difficulty for the Constraint Prioritized Prompt Encoder. Moreover, as described in Sec. D,
1446
       when the environment ID is unknown, the inverse dynamics error based on the inverse dynamics
1447
       model becomes the core method for distinguishing these tasks, making it an essential component.
1448   We also conducted an additional ablation study to ensure that the use of the inverse dynamics model
1449   does not negatively impact the policy performance, with results shown in Fig. 9(c), which aligns
1450   with our expectations.
