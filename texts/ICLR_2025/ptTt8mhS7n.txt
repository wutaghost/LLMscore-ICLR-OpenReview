011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            In-context learning (ICL) is an effective approach to help large language mod-
014            els (LLMs) adapt to various tasks by providing demonstrations of the target task.
015            Considering the high cost of labeling demonstrations, many methods propose syn-
016
               thesizing demonstrations from scratch using LLMs. However, the quality of the
               demonstrations synthesized from scratch is limited by the capabilities and knowl-
017
               edge of LLMs. To address this, inspired by transfer learning, we propose In-
018
               Context Transfer Learning (ICTL), which synthesizes target task demonstrations
019            by transferring labeled demonstrations from similar source tasks. ICTL consists
020            of two steps: source sampling and target transfer. First, we define an optimization
021            objective, which minimizes transfer error to sample source demonstrations similar
022            to the target task. Then, we employ LLMs to transfer the sampled source demon-
023            strations to the target task, matching the definition and format of the target task.
024            Experiments on Super-NI show that ICTL outperforms synthesis from scratch by
025            2.0% on average, demonstrating the effectiveness of our method.
026

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   In-context learning (ICL) is an effective approach for large language models (LLMs) to adapt to
031   various tasks based on the brilliant generalize ability of LLMs (Xun et al., 2017; Song et al., 2023b;
032   Luo et al., 2024a). During the inference with ICL, input not only includes user questions but also
033
      several demonstrations to guide LLMs in generating answers correctly. Considering the high cost
      of demonstration labeling, many methods utilize LLMs to synthesize demonstrations from scratch
034
      without human involvement (Kim et al., 2022; Jin & Lu, 2024). For instance, Self-ICL (Chen et al.,
035
      2023b) employs LLMs to synthesize demonstration based on the task definition, while Su et al.
036   (2024) improves the synthesis through iterations, where each iteration uses the previous results.
037
038   However, the synthesis using LLMs from scratch is constrained by the capabilities and knowledge
039
      of LLMs, limiting the quality of the synthesized demonstrations (Yu et al., 2023). For example, a
      model trained pre-2023 can not use knowledge after 2023, while a model not trained on coding tasks
040
      cannot understand code well (Rozière et al., 2024; Luo et al., 2024b). To solve this issue, thereby
041
      improving ICL performance while reducing human involvement, motivated by transfer learning (Pan
042   & Yang, 2010; Iman et al., 2023), we propose to synthesize demonstrations for the target task by
043   transferring the labeled demonstrations of similar tasks. We use the idea of transfer learning since
044   the previous works show that given similar source tasks, the performance of the target task can be
045   enhanced according to the source task learning (Sun et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2024b). For example,
046   as shown in Figure 1, the model can combine the context and the answer in the input of the sampled
047   source demonstration, which is then used as the demonstration of the target task.
048   Based on the above discussion, we present In-Context Transfer Learning (ICTL), which obtains
049   the demonstrations of the target task by transferring the demonstrations of the source tasks. ICTL
050   consists of two steps: sample the demonstrations similar to the target task, and transfer the sampled
051   demonstrations to the target task, as shown in Figure 1. First, we present an optimization objective
052   to measure the transfer error, where we minimize the transfer error to sample the demonstrations
053   highly similar to the target task. Then, we transfer the sampled demonstrations to the target task
      with LLMs, taking the sampled results and the target task definition as the input.

081
082   • We argue that answering from scratch is constrained by the capabilities and knowledge of LLMs

084
        tasks;

086     tween the sampled results and the target task;
087   • Experiments on Super-NI show that, compared with the synthesis from scratch, ICTL delivers a
088     2.0% performance improvement on Super-NI, proving the effectiveness of ICTL.

093   2.1      D EMONSTRATION S YNTHESIS
094
      Demonstrations are of great importance in ICL, which can effectively help LLMs adapt various tar-
095
      get tasks (Dong et al., 2024). Considering the high cost of human labeling, many methods present to
096   synthesize demonstrations using LLMs from scratch, lowering the human involvement (Kim et al.,
097   2022; Chang & Fosler-Lussier, 2023; Jin & Lu, 2024). Some methods focus on ensuring the cor-
098   rectness of the synthesized demonstrations, meeting the task definitions by filtering out low-quality
099   synthesized results (Chen et al., 2023b; Su et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024). Another type of method
100   aims to increase the diversity of the synthesized demonstrations, creating ones dissimilar to synthe-
101   sized results (Zhang et al., 2023; Shum et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024a).
102   However, the demonstrations synthesized by the current methods are constrained by the knowledge
103   and capabilities of LLMs themselves, limiting their performance on the tasks unseen in their pre-
104   training (Yu et al., 2023). Although human-labeled demonstrations for new task scenarios can help
105   LLMs generalize to these new tasks, labeling demonstrations for any new task or domain is costly
106   (Wang et al., 2013). To address these issues, we present ICTL, which synthesizes demonstrations
107   for new target scenarios by transferring labeled source demonstrations similar to the target task,
      addressing the limitation of the knowledge and capabilities of LLMs.

      2.2      D EEP T RANSFER L EARNING
129
      Transfer learning is a widely researched direction aimed at helping models acquire the ability to
130
      solve target tasks based on their existing capabilities from the source tasks (Pan & Yang, 2010;
131   Zhuang et al., 2020). With the impressive performance demonstrated by deep learning methods,
132   deep transfer learning has become an important approach within the field of transfer learning (Iman
133   et al., 2023). Some methods focus on transferring and freezing model parameters to retain and learn
134   features of different tasks (Scialom et al., 2022; Song et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023; Rostami et al.,
135   2023; Du et al., 2024). Other transfer learning methods enhance the performance from the data
136   perspective, studying how to adjust the training sequence of tasks, mix source task data with target
137   task data, or modify the source task format to improve transfer learning performance (Xu et al.,
138   2023; Wang et al., 2024b; Madine, 2024).
139   However, current transfer learning methods rely on the labeled data of the target task and the model
140   training, leading to the high cost of the adaption considering the high cost of labeling and LLM
141   training. Therefore, in this paper, we present to employ transfer learning to enhance ICL by syn-
142   thesizing demonstrations using the labeled source demonstrations, lowering the human involvement
143   and training cost, meanwhile helping LLMs adapt to various target tasks.

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
146
147
      In this section, we present ICTL, which synthesizes the demonstrations of the target task by trans-
148
      ferring the labeled source demonstrations. The illustration of ICTL is shown in Figure 2, which
149
      consists of two steps: source sampling (§3.1) and target transfer (§3.2). Following the previous
150   methods (Wang et al., 2024a; Yang et al., 2024), we synthesize demonstrations for each target task
151   offline, where we do not synthesize for each target question since we want to ensure high efficiency
152   of the inference. The prompts we used can be seen in Appendix B. The computational efficiency
153   analysis of ICTL is shown in Appendix E.
154
155
      3.1      S OURCE S AMPLING
156
157   The source sampling step is designed to sample demonstrations that are highly similar to the target
158   task from the labeled source demonstrations. In this paper, we define the similarity as: If we want
159   to sample N source demonstrations, the N source task demonstrations can minimize the target task
160   error after transferring. We first present an optimization objective to guide the source demonstration
161   sampling by minimizing the transfer error. Then, we discuss how to sample the source demonstra-
      tions similar to the target task using our objective specifically.

167   sample scale for each task, and φ is a negligible function. The previous work (Redko et al., 2017)
168

172

173

174   source demonstrations can not be modified. So we aim to minimize the target error by minimizing

201   3.1.2    S AMPLING WITH E QUATION 3
202
203   Based on the above discussion, we then discuss how to sample source demonstrations specifically.
204   First, we embed the definitions and demonstrations of all source tasks, as well as the definition of
205   the target task, into vectors using an embedding model. Following previous work (Wang et al.,
206
      2024b), we then filter the source tasks to select those most similar to the target task, reducing the
      overhead of subsequent calculations while ensuring performance. The filtering is done by ranking
207
      the Wasserstein distance between the embedding vectors of the source and target task definitions.
208
      From the filtered source tasks, we sample a fixed number of demonstrations using Equation 3. We
209   employ a randomized algorithm for the sampling, with details provided in the Appendix C.
210
211   3.2     TARGET T RANSFER
212
213   The target transfer step focuses on transferring the sampled demonstrations to the target task while
214   ensuring that the transferred demonstrations are consistent with both the target task and the sampled
215   demonstrations, transcending the limitations of the inherent capabilities and knowledge of LLMs.
      The target transfer step consists of: Transfer, Verify, and Sample.


                                                                  4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      Transfer is to transfer the sampled demonstrations to match the target task definition and format.
217   We employ LLMs for the transfer, where the input includes the definitions of both the source and
218   target tasks, the source demonstration to be transferred, and a human-labeled example of the target
219   task to specify the input and output formats.
220
221   Verify is designed to check whether the transferred demonstration is consistent with the definition
222   of the target task, improving the quality of the transferred demonstrations. We employ LLMs to
223   verify the transferred results. The target task definition, one example, and the transferred demon-
224   stration are provided as input to check whether the transferred demonstration consistent with the
225   task definition, with the correct input and output formats. Any demonstration verified by the LLM
226   as inconsistent is discarded to ensure the quality of the transferred results.
227
228
      Sample is to sample the verified target demonstrations with Equation 2, ensuring that the sam-
      pled demonstration is consistent with the target task while staying similar to the sampled source
229
      demonstrations, thereby transcending the limitations of the capabilities and knowledge of LLMs.
230
      The sampling algorithm used for the transferred demonstration sampling is the same as the source
231   sampling, with the optimization objective defined by Equation 2. The sampled demonstrations are
232   considered as the final output of our transfer method.

      4.1     E XPERIMENT S ETUP

238   4.1.1    DATASET
239
240   We use the Super-NaturalInstructions dataset (Super-NI) (Wang et al., 2022) to validate our method,
241   which contains over 1, 600 tasks, allowing for a comprehensive evaluation of the model cross-task
242   generalization ability. Following previous work (Wang et al., 2024b), we conduct experiments on all
243
      English tasks in Super-NI, including 756 tasks in the training set and 116 tasks in the test set. Based
      on prior research (Wang et al., 2024b), we categorize all tasks in the test set into six categories to
244
      better analyze the performance of our method across different tasks, as shown in Appendix D.
245
246
      4.1.2    M ETRIC
247
248   Following the Super-NI setup, we use Rouge-L (RougeL) and Exact Match (EM) as the evaluation
249   metrics. RougeL measures the overlap between the predicted output and the reference answer, while
250   EM assesses whether the predicted output exactly matches the reference. Following Wang et al.
251   (2022), we mainly use RougeL as the evaluation metric, since EM is not suitable for tasks that can
252   be answered in multiple ways (e.g., summarization, title generation).
253
254
      4.1.3    M ODEL
255   We use BGE-EN-ICL (Chen et al., 2023a) to embed task definition and demonstrations for the
256   sampling, which is the state-of-the-art (SOTA) embedding model during our experiments. For
257   the transfer and inference, we use Llama3.1-8b-Instruct (Llama3.1-8b) (Dubey et al., 2024) and
258   GPT-4o (OpenAI et al., 2024) as the experimental models. Llama3.1-8b is one of the current
259   best-performing open-source LLMs. GPT-4o is one of the most powerful LLMs at present, which
260   achieves SOTA performance on multiple mainstream benchmarks. We mainly use Llama3.1-8b as
261   the model of our analysis experiments due to the high cost of GPT-4o.
262

266   • Zero: No demonstrations are provided during inference, using a zero-shot setting;
267   • Direct: Directly use the sampled source demonstrations without transferring;
268
      • Single: Only use the single human-labeled example as the demonstration;
269
      • Synthesis: Synthesize demonstrations from scratch based on the one example provided.

      4.2     M AIN E XPERIMENT
302
303   As shown in Table 1, ICTL outperforms all baselines without transfer across different metrics and
304   models on most categories, showing the effectiveness of our method. Additionally, the results in the
305   table also reveal the following insights:
306
307   Baseline Compared to all baselines without transfer, our method achieves better performance,
308   demonstrating the effectiveness of transferring. Notably, ICTL brings 2.0% improvement on aver-
309   age compared to the Synthesis setting. This shows that the demonstrations synthesized by LLMs
310   from scratch are constrained by the capabilities and knowledge of LLMs themselves. In contrast,
311   ICTL overcomes this constraint by providing the labeled demonstrations of other similar tasks, low-
312
      ering the capability and knowledge requirement. Additionally, the Direct setting directly using the
      sampled results as demonstrations leads to worse performance compared to the Zero setting. This
313
      indicates that transfer is necessary when using demonstrations from other tasks to enhance perfor-
314
      mance, even if the sampled source demonstrations are highly similar to the target task.
315
316
      Task ICTL improves performance across most task categories, proving its effectiveness. Specif-
317
      ically, the performance improvement is more significant for tasks with a higher rate in all test data,
318   as there are sufficient similar source demonstrations for transfer, where the rates of different tasks
319   are shown in Appendix D. However, our method slightly underperforms compared to other settings
320   in the Dialogue and Extraction tasks. This is because these two tasks comprise only about 5% of the
321   total data, leading to lower-quality transfer results due to a lack of similar source demonstrations.
322   These findings suggest that it is important to use source demonstrations that are highly similar to the
323   target task, as discussed in detail in §4.4.3. To better observe the relationship between the source
      and target tasks of various categories, we static the transfer status of ICTL in Appendix F.3.

      4.4.1       S OURCE D EMONSTRATION S CALE
419
420   The scale of source demonstrations available for different practical applications varies, so we ana-
421   lyze the impact of different scales of source demonstrations on the performance of our method, as
422   shown in Figure 3a. From the figure, we can see that: (i) When the scale of the source demonstration
423   sampling is smaller than 128, the overall experimental results exhibit an upward trend, demonstrat-
424   ing that increasing the amount of source demonstrations can effectively enhance the performance of
425   our method; (ii) When the sampling scale exceeds 128, there is a slight decrease in performance,
426   indicating that further addition of new source demonstrations does not continue to improve per-
427   formance, as the number of demonstrations similar to the target task is limited. Therefore, when
428
      obtaining demonstrations of source tasks, it is necessary to obtain as many demonstrations as possi-
      ble to ensure that there are enough different abilities or knowledge for the target task.
429
430   Notably, compared to not using transfer learning, even transferring using one source demonstration
431   can also effectively improve the performance of the target task. This is because: (i) Even using
      one single source demonstration, we can also synthesize a large amount demonstrations of the target


                                                                   8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      task, resulting in a high-quality demonstration pool and thus better performance than without transfer
433   learning; (ii) Previous research (Kim et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024a) and the Synthesis setting of
434   Table 1 show that even without source demonstrations, LLMs can still synthesize demonstrations
435   based on the inherent knowledge of themselves, thereby enhancing inference performance.
436
437   4.4.2   S OURCE TASK S CALE
438
439   The scale of source tasks that can be obtained varies in practical applications, so we analyze the
440   impact of different task scales on the performance of ICTL. The experimental results are shown in
441   Figure 3b, from which we can see that: (i) When the scale of source tasks is less than 16, the overall
442
      performance exhibits an upward trend, while when the scale exceeds 16, the performance starts
      to decline sharply, showing that blindly increasing the scale of the source task cannot bring about
443
      continuous improvement and the importance of ensuring the similarity between the source and the
444
      target tasks; (ii) Compared to the source demonstration scale, the performance degradation is more
445   pronounced with the increase in source task scale, since the scale of source tasks similar to the
446   target task is limited, whereas simply increasing the scale of tasks, rather than the demonstrations,
447   introduces more irrelevant information, leading to a more significant decrease in the quality of the
448   transferred demonstrations and the inference performance.
449
450   4.4.3   TASK S IMILARITY R ANK
451
452   Considering there could be many new tasks emerging in future research and applications, to ex-
453   plore the adaptability of ICTL to new tasks, we conduct experiments to examine the impact of the
454
      similarity between the source and target tasks on performance. We rank the Wasserstein distance
      of the embedding vectors of the source and target task definition in descending order, selecting the
455
      1st, 10th, 100th, and last-ranked (756th in the Super-NI train set) source tasks to be transferred. The
456
      experimental results are shown in Figure 3c, from which we can observe the following: (i) When the
457   similarity ranking of the source tasks is within the top 10, the performance of our method does not
458   fluctuate significantly, since there exists multiple source tasks similar to those in the Super-NI test
459   set, resulting in transferred demonstrations of comparable quality; (ii) After the similarity ranking
460   exceeds 10, the performance of our method begins to decline sharply, indicating that demonstra-
461   tions of tasks with large gaps can not help the target task, showing the importance of ensuring the
462   similarity between the source tasks and target tasks.
463
464   4.4.4   TARGET T RANSFER S CALE
465
466
      Due to the computational resource limitation in practical applications, the scale of the transferred
      demonstrations could be limited. Therefore, we evaluate the performance of ICTL under different
467
      scales of transferred demonstrations, as shown in Figure 3d. From the figure, we can observe the
468
      following: (i) In cases where only one single demonstration is transferred, the model performance
469   decreases compared to without transfer, since the quality of the single transferred demonstration is
470   lower than the provided example labeled by humans, leading to a performance decline; (ii) Even only
471   obtains 10 demonstrations by transferring, our method achieves better performance than no transfer,
472   whereas the scale of transferred demonstrations increases, the performance improves accordingly,
473   demonstrating the necessity of sufficient transferring; (iii) However, after the transferred demon-
474   strations reach a certain scale, the model performance plateaus, since the information contained in
475   the sampled source demonstrations is fully represented with 512 transferred demonstrations, and
476   further increasing the scale does not yield new high-quality demonstrations, while the performance
477
      is reduced since mixing more low-quality demonstrations.
478
479   5   C ONCLUSION
480
481   In this paper, motivated by transfer learning, we propose ICTL, which synthesizes the demonstra-
482   tions of the target task by transferring the similar labeled demonstrations, addressing the constraint
483   that synthesizing from scratch with LLMs is limited by the capabilities and knowledge of LLMs.
484   We first present an optimization objective for sampling source demonstrations, aiming to minimize
485   transfer errors by ensuring that sampled demonstrations are highly similar to the target task. Sub-
      sequently, we transfer the sampled demonstrations to the target task using LLMs without human


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      involvement, taking the sampled results and the target task definition as the input. Experiments on
487   Super-NI demonstrate that our method achieves an average improvement of 2.0% over demonstra-
488   tions synthesized without transfer, validating its effectiveness. Additionally, analysis confirms that
489   our method ensures a high similarity between sampled source demonstrations and the target task,
490   proving the effectiveness of our proposed optimization objective.

957   Simulated annealing is a probabilistic global optimization algorithm that initially accepts suboptimal
958   solutions at high temperatures to avoid local optima. As the temperature gradually decreases, the
959   algorithm converges. The initial solution is generated through random sampling, where samples
960   from the given demonstrations are randomly selected as the starting candidate solution. We use
961   Equation 3 and Equation 2 as the score function to evaluate the quality of random sampling from the
962
      given demonstrations, where we calculate the Wasserstein distance following Rostami et al. (2023).
963   During each iteration, the algorithm perturbs the current candidate solution to generate a new one.
964   If the algorithm fails to find a better solution after several attempts, the perturbations are triggered
965   to escape local optima. Whether the perturbed candidate is accepted depends on the difference in
966   scores between the new and current solutions. Even if the new candidate is worse, there is a certain
967   probability it is accepted. This probability decreases as the temperature drops, promoting sufficient
968
      search space exploration.

970   perature decays after each iteration until it reaches the minimum value of 10−4 , at which point the
971   algorithm stops. Additionally, we set a threshold: if no better solution is found after 100 iterations,
