011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            The recent surge in data availability across many fields, such as medicine, so-
014            cial science, and marketing, has brought to the forefront the problem of estimat-
015            ing Individual Treatment Effect (ITE) from observational data to effectively tailor
016
               treatment to personalized characteristics. ITE estimation is known to be a chal-
               lenging task because we can only observe the outcome with or without treatment,
017
               but never both. Moreover, observational datasets exhibit selection bias induced by
018
               the treatment assignment policy. In this paper, we present a new approach con-
019            sisting of two novel aspects. First, we depart from conventional approaches that
020            minimize the covariate shift. Instead, we incorporate it as a crucial element in ITE
021            estimation, recognizing that it stems from highly predictive features that exhibit
022            significant imbalance in observational data. Second, unlike existing methods, our
023            approach utilizes hypothesis functions to directly estimate outcomes under covari-
024            ate shift, enhancing reliability across observed and unobserved outcomes. To sup-
025            port this approach theoretically, we derive a new upper bound of the expected ITE
026            loss and show that it explicitly depends on the discrepancy between the hypothesis
027
               functions, which are absent from the objectives of existing methods. Based on this
               new approach, we present LITE: Laplacian Individual Treatment Effect, a novel
028
               method that leverages Laplacian-regularized representation and incorporates both
029
               the covariate shift and the hypothesis functions for ITE estimation, effectively
030            bridging observed and unobserved outcomes. We demonstrate LITE on illustra-
031            tive simulations and two leading benchmarks, where we show superior results
032            compared to state-of-the-art methods.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Individual Treatment Effect (ITE) has come to the forefront of precision medicine (Prosperi et al.,
038   2020; Glass et al., 2013), targeted marketing (Lemmens & Gupta, 2020), personalized education
039   (Beemer et al., 2018),and various other fields (Wang et al., 2016) that require individual-level pre-
040   dictions. ITE specifically aims to quantify the unique outcome of an action (also referred to as
041   a treatment or intervention) for each individual based on their specific characteristics, which is a
042   departure from traditional methods that focus on average treatment effect (Abadie & Imbens, 2016).
043   The focus on individual outcomes is critical, especially since individuals are unlikely to conform to
044   average behavioral patterns (Schork, 2015). Customizing treatments based on unique characteristics
045   is therefore essential for achieving both effective and efficient interventions. In this approach, an
046   individual is characterized by their features, with typically two possible outcomes considered: the
047   outcome with the applied treatment and the outcome in the absence of the treatment. The objec-
048   tive is to estimate the difference between these outcomes (i.e., the treatment effect) based on the
049   individual’s features, enabling to customize treatment policies for that individual.
050   Accurate estimation of ITE is a challenging task, mainly because only one potential outcome for
051   each individual is observable based on the applied action (i.e., the factual outcome). Inferring the
052   unobserved outcome (i.e., the counterfactual outcome) from the outcomes observed in other indi-
053   viduals often leads to poor estimates due to selection bias (Vokinger et al., 2021) (also referred to
      as confounding bias). This bias stems from the assignment policy to treatment or control groups,


                                                        1
054
      typical in studies where randomized controlled trials (RCTs) are unavailable due to budget consid-
055   erations , difficulty in recruiting patients, or ethical constraints. For example, suppose a medication
056   is primarily given to patients with severe symptoms. Using the observed data from the control group
057   —those not receiving the medication— to predict the counterfactual outcome of the treated group,
058   i.e., their predicted outcome of lack of treatment, would result in overly optimistic estimates. Con-
059   versely, using the treated group to predict the outcome of treatment in the control group would likely
060   result in pessimistic estimates. Furthermore, in observational data, we often lack direct insight into
061   the mechanisms (i.e., the confounding variables) to infer the treatment policy.
062   Traditional methods approximate ITE by identifying nearest neighbors using matching techniques
063   to estimate counterfactuals (Ho et al., 2007; Gu & Rosenbaum, 1993; Dehejia & Wahba, 2002;
064   Schwab et al., 2018). Tree-based methods (Chipman et al., 2010; Green & Kern, 2012; Lu et al.,
065   2018; Athey & Imbens, 2016; Wager & Athey, 2018) view forests as an adaptive neighborhood
066   metric and estimate treatment effects at the leaf nodes (Wager & Athey, 2018). Other approaches
067   use Gaussian processes for ITE estimation (Alaa & Van Der Schaar, 2017; Alaa & Schaar, 2018).
068   Representation learning has become central in ITE estimation by harnessing the power of latent
069
      representations (Bengio et al., 2013). To tackle selection bias, these methods (Johansson et al.,
      2016; Shalit et al., 2017; Yao et al., 2018; Yoon et al., 2018; Guo et al., 2023; Du et al., 2021;
070
      Johansson et al., 2022) often aim to minimize covariate shift by balancing covariate representations
071
      across groups using distance metric regularization (Johansson et al., 2016; Shalit et al., 2017; Yao
072   et al., 2018; Guo et al., 2023) or adversarial methods (Yoon et al., 2018; Du et al., 2021), ensuring
073   that counterfactual predictions are guided by the most reliable aspects of the data (Johansson et al.,
074   2016). However, we assert that unlike classical domain adaptation, where covariate shift is treated
075   as an artifact to be mitigated (e.g., blurred vs. clear images), in ITE estimation, the covariate shift
076   is inherent and directly impacts the causal treatment effect. Directly minimizing covariate shift can
077   inadvertently reduce predictive components that are essential for understanding the causal treatment
078   effect (Yoon et al., 2018; Yao et al., 2018; Du et al., 2021), and thus produce biased ITE estimate even
079   in the limit of infinite data (Johansson et al., 2018). This is because the selection policy is typically
080
      applied based on highly predictive features that often show significant imbalance, as doctors, for
      example, usually assign treatments according to these features.
081
082   This paper introduces a new approach that revisits the role of covariates and hypothesis functions in
083   ITE estimation. We present a new upper bound of the ITE estimation error that shifts focus from
084   the covariate shift to the hypothesis function discrepancies. Following this result, unlike traditional
085   methods that minimize the covariate shift, our method, termed LITE (Laplacian Individual Treat-
086
      ment Effect), goes beyond this by integrating both covariate and hypothesis considerations into the
      ITE estimation process. To this end, we construct a graph within the latent learned representation
087
      space, capturing the covariate shift and serving as our model’s foundational structure. Utilizing
088
      this graph, we compute the graph Laplacian, which in turn, is used for regularizing the hypothesis
089   functions to allow estimate ITE directly under the shift. Specifically, we use the graph Laplacian
090   quadratic form to facilitate the smoothness of the hypothesis functions with the geometry of the
091   learned representation across predicted factual and counterfactual outcomes. We demonstrate LITE
092   on a simulation and two leading benchmarks. We show that LITE outperforms both established and
093   recent methods by a large margin, achieving state of the art results.
094
      Our main contributions are as follows:
095
096          • Theoretical foundation: We present a new upper bound of the ITE estimation error that
097            redirects the focus from the covariate shift to the discrepancies between hypothesis func-
098            tions.
099          • LITE: We present a new method for ITE estimation that unlike existing methods considers
100            both the covariate shift and the hypothesis function discrepancies.
101
             • Geometry-aware representation: We utilize Laplacian regularization not only to align
102
               the learned representation with the hypothesis function outcomes, but also to dynamically
103            learn the graph structure itself through the optimization process.
104
105
             • State of the art results: LITE demonstrates state of the art results on leading benchmarks.
106          • Broader impact: Our method is easily extended to handle multiple treatment scenar-
107            ios, thus enhancing practicality in many fields, and specifically, in healthcare applications,
               where multiple treatments are often available.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110   Laplacian regularization has been widely used in various domains (Pang & Cheung, 2017; Liu et al.,
111   2018; Ziko et al., 2020), and more particularly in semi-supervised learning, as demonstrated by
112   Belkin et al. (2006); Cabannes et al. (2021); Calder et al. (2023). However, to the best of our
113   knowledge, its application within the latent space for ITE estimation is novel. Traditional Laplacian
114   regularization is often applied in the input space, where geometry can be obscured by non-relevant
115   features. Our approach contrasts with this by promoting geometry-aware structures within the la-
116   tent space, focusing on predictive features relevant to the task. Furthermore, by incorporating this
117   Laplacian regularization in the learning objective, the latent representation and the resulting graph
118
      are continuously refined through optimization, systematically aligning the learned representation
      geometry with the intrinsic geometry of the underlying data structure.
119
120   While covariate balancing helps reduce the impact of selection bias, it might inadvertently reduce
121   intrinsic differences that are important for accurate ITE estimation (Yoon et al., 2018; Johansson
122   et al., 2018; Yao et al., 2018; Du et al., 2021). There exist representation learning methods for ITE
123   estimation that aim to mitigate the adverse nature of covariate balancing. For instance, Yao et al.
124
      (2018) proposed local similarity-preserved representations to prevent the potential loss of local sim-
      ilarity information during distribution balancing. Alternatively, Johansson et al. (2018) combined
125
      re-weighting methods to alleviate predictive information loss. Du et al. (2021) employed mutual in-
126
      formation regularization to retain information that is highly predictive of the outcome. Despite such
127   mitigation strategies, these methods still apply direct minimization while regularized, which can
128   reduce the crucial intrinsic differences necessary for accurate ITE estimation. Moreover, these ap-
129   proaches often neglect the critical role of hypothesis functions in addressing selection bias, focusing
130   instead solely on covariate balancing at the expense of the predictive capacity of these functions.

      3.1   P ROBLEM FORMULATION
135
136   We adopt the framework of potential outcomes Pearl (2009), as originally formulated in Rubin
137   (1974); Rosenbaum & Rubin (1983), to analyze the ITE. We follow the notations from Shalit et al.
138   (2017). The ITE problem aims to learn the potential effect of a treatment t based on individual
139   features x (also referred to as covariates). Within this framework, let X be the input space, T be the
140   action (treatment) space, and Y be the outcome space. An individual is characterized by features
141   x ∈ X . In this paper, to simplify the presentation, we assume a binary treatment setting where each
142
      individual is assigned an action t ∈ T = {0, 1}, where 0 denotes the absence of treatment and 1
      indicates the presence of treatment. The probability of assigning treatment, given a set of covariates,
143
      is defined as the propensity score π(x) = p(t = 1|x) (Rosenbaum & Rubin, 1983), and reflects the
144
      treatment assignment policy.
145
146   For each individual, two potential outcomes exist: Y0 without treatment and Y1 with treatment.
147   However, in this setting, for each individual, only one of these potential outcomes, either Y0 or Y1 ,
148
      is observed via y, depending on the applied treatment action:

164   Minimizing PEHE enhances the accuracy of ITE estimates, which is crucial for developing targeted
165   interventions that are optimally tailored to individual characteristics. Our objective is to estimate
166   the function τ (x) in a manner consistent with causal inference principles (Sauerbrei et al., 2014;

168   pendent samples of features, action, and outcome, represented by the tuple (xi , ti , yi ).
169   Two assumptions are commonly considered in ITE estimation.
170
      Assumption 1 (Positivity). There exists a positive probability of receiving any treatment action,
171   conditional on the individual features. Formally: ∀t ∈ {0, 1}, ∀x ∈ X , 0 < P (t|x) < 1.
172
173   This assumption ensures an overlap between the control and treatment groups. Violation of this pos-
174   itivity assumption implies that for some x, we lack any observation of one of the potential outcomes,
175   making the counterfactual outcome estimation even more challenging.
176   Assumption 2 (Ignorability). The treatment assignment is conditionally independent of the potential
177   outcomes, given the observed features. Formally: {Y0 , Y1 } ⊥⊥ t|x.
178
179
      This condition is crucial for the identifiability of the ITE. The ignorability assumption (often referred
      to as “unconfoundedness”) ensures that there are no hidden confounders affecting both treatment
180
      assignment and potential outcomes.

      3.2   T HE SELECTION BIAS
183
184   Our objective is to estimate the ITE τ (x) in Eq. equation 1 by learning two separate functions:
185   m1 (x) = E[Y1 |x] and m0 (x) = E[Y0 |x]. To this end, access to the joint distribution function
186   p(x, t, Y0 , Y1 ), defined on the input-action-potential outcome space, is essential. However, we have
187   a sample with the factual outcome: (x1 , t1 , y1 ), ..., (xn , tn , yn ), where yi ∼ p(Y1 |xi ) if ti = 1, and
188   yi ∼ p(Y0 |xi ) if ti = 0. This provides just a partial view of the joint distribution.
189   Definition 3 (ITE estimate). For an individual x, let f : X × T → Y be a function that maps
190   individual and treatment pairs to outcomes. The ITE estimate of the hypothesis f is defined by:
191                                           τ̂ (x) = f (x, 1) − f (x, 0),                                     (3)
192
193   where f (x, 1) and f (x, 0) are the estimates of m1 (x) and m0 (x), respectively.
194
      One may suggest learning two separate functions: f (x, 1) from individuals who received the treat-
195   ment and f (x, 0) from those who did not. However, these functions are susceptible to the selection
196   bias in the observational dataset (Vokinger et al., 2021). More specifically, f (x, 1) and f (x, 0) stem
197   from different distributions, i.e., the treated and control distributions p(x|t = 1) and p(x|t = 0),
198   respectively, and these distributions are shifted relative to the covariate marginal p(x).

240   4   P ROPOSED APPROACH
241
242   To address the challenges associated with estimating ITE, we employ a representation-learning
243   framework (Bengio et al., 2013), which is widely used in the ITE literature (Johansson et al., 2016;
244   Shalit et al., 2017; Guo et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2018; Shi et al., 2019). This framework utilizes
245
      a representation function Φ : X → R that maps observed features x into a latent space R via
      Φ(x). In this latent space, we use a hypothesis function h with two possible second arguments:
246
      h(Φ(x), t = 0) and h(Φ(x), t = 1), for predicting the potential outcomes Y0 and Y1 , respectively.
247
      Our general hypothesis function is then expressed as f (x, t) = h(Φ(x), t).
248
249   To effectively capture the complex relationships between observed features x and potential out-
250   comes, we parameterize Φ(x) and h(Φ, t) using deep neural networks. Specifically, h(Φ, t) is
251
      realized by two separate fully connected networks (Caron et al., 2022), one for each treatment
      t ∈ {0, 1}.In addition, Φ is realized using fully connected layers. This parameterization choice lever-
252
      ages the advanced capabilities of neural networks to capture intricate patterns in the data. Moreover,
253
      employing a shared representation Φ(x) across treatment assignments leverages the commonalities
254   among treated and control groups, enhancing generalization and efficiency. While we use fully
255   connected layers to realize Φ(x), h(Φ, t = 0), and h(Φ, t = 1), our approach is flexible and can
256   accommodate alternative network architectures to potentially enhance model performance.
257

      Broadly, the adjacencies in A (based on ∥Φ(xi ) − Φ(xj )∥) carry information on the covariate shift,
379   while |ht [i] − ht [j]| induces the hypothesis function smoothness. The quadratic form minimization
380   promotes predictions that live in the span of smooth eigenvectors of the Laplacian graph. Our pro-
381   posed approach leverages available factual labels, ensuring that model predictions are grounded in
382   observations, while counterfactuals are inferred from them under this smoothness requirement.
383
      We now clarify the connection between Theorem 3 and the LITE method, which employs Laplacian
384
      regularization, highlighting how the regularization enforces smoothness across samples and reduces
385   the difference between prediction losses for both potential outcomes. Theorem 3 establishes an
386   upper bound on the ITE error based on differences in expected loss predictions for both potential
387   outcomes. The graph Laplacian regularization enforces smoothness across samples by minimizing
388   the quadratic form of both factual and counterfactual samples in the latent space. We argue that
389   smoothness across outcomes of different samples leads to reducing the difference between pre-
390   diction losses. In particular, in regions without selection bias, both potential outcome predictions
391   would ideally have similar levels of predicted losses, and thus, the difference would be small. How-
392   ever, due to the selection bias, and particularly in regions with severe selection bias, one potential
393
      outcome prediction function is based on factual samples, while the other potential outcome pre-
      diction function is insufficiently represented by unobserved counterfactual outcomes. This leads to
394
      higher predicted losses and larger differences between the predicted losses. The Laplacian addresses
395
      the unobserved regions by enforcing smoothness across outcomes of different samples, allowing
396   the model to infer counterfactual from factual samples and, thus, effectively reducing the difference
397   between prediction losses.

      rem is general and can accommodate other alternatives incorporating function handling rather than
400
      covariate minimization. Following this approach, we show that LITE achieves significant empirical

      improvements over existing methods.

832
833   Network architecture. Our representation network Φ(x) and the hypothesis networks h(Φ, t = 0)
834   and h(Φ, t = 1) are realized using fully connected layers with ELU activation functions (Clevert
835   et al., 2015). While our framework accommodates more complex architectures, the current imple-
836   mentation achieves robust ITE estimation, demonstrating its effectiveness even when compared to
837   other methods (Yoon et al., 2018; Du et al., 2021; Louizos et al., 2017) that employ more sophis-
838
      ticated architectures. Following Shalit et al. (2017), we normalize the representation layer. In our
      case, this methodology prevents the optimization from favoring solutions that minimize the Lapla-
839
      cian term by trivially setting Φ(x) = 0.
840
841
      Dataset splitting and optimization. The benchmark datasets are divided into 63/27/10 splits for
842
      training, validation, and testing, in alignment with Johansson et al. (2016); Shalit et al. (2017). The
843   same test realizations are used to maintain consistency with previous studies. Optimization employs
844   the Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2014), using the default parameters: β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.999, and
845   ϵ = 10−8 . We implement an exponential decay schedule for the learning rate, decreasing it by 0.95
846   every 50 iterations. The training process includes early stopping (Prechelt, 2002) based on the LITE
847   objective, as defined in 12, evaluated on the validation set, with a maximum of 10,000 iterations and
848   a patience of 2,000 iterations for early stopping.
849
850   Hyperparameter Selection. We follow the commonly used practice in the literature for hyperpa-
851   rameter selection (Johansson et al., 2016) based on the PEHE metric on the validation set, while the
852   hyperparameters are fixed across multiple realizations. Although not applicable to real-world data,
853
      this approach validates the robustness of the selected parameters and prevents overfitting.

976    D    LITE SCALABILITY AND COMPUTATION TIME
977
978    LITE is designed to handle large datasets efficiently and is implemented to allow for fast computa-
979
       tion.
980    While some methods struggle with scalability on high-dimensional data, LITE mini-batching train-
981    ing enhances scalability, allowing us to handle large datasets efficiently.
982
       The computation involves two key steps. First, the computation of predicted potential outcomes
983    involves calculating both factual and counterfactual samples by simply forwarding them through the
984    network. Second, the computation of the Laplacian operator requires calculating pairwise distances
985    between samples within the representation layer for kernel construction. This step encompasses
986    the primary computational burden. The kernel construction in the representation space is common
987    also to covariate shift minimization methods like CFRNET (Shalit et al., 2017) employing MMD or
988    Wasserstein distance for shift minimzation. The Wasserstein distance, which generally offers better
989    performance over the MMD, requires additional computational steps involving Sinkhorn-Knopp
990    (Cuturi, 2013) iterations to compute the Wasserstein distance.
991    For the early stopping phase of optimization, we employ the entire validation set to ensure a com-
992    prehensive geometric inference. This validation set for the News dataset includes 1,350 samples in
993    a single batch, each with a latent space dimension of 150. We optimize pairwise distances using
994    vectorized operations, significantly reducing processing time to just 0.002 seconds on a GPU.
995    To further illustrate the efficiency of LITE compared to CFRNET Wasserstein distance (using the
996    POT package), we created a Colab notebook, which is available here(Please note: the reported time
997    from the first run may be inaccurate due to server GPU initialization. It is recommended to run it
998    twice).
999
       The results show that LITE takes 0.0012 seconds on a GPU, while the CFRNET Wasserstein Dis-
1000
       tance takes 0.017 seconds, making LITE 14 times faster – an order of magnitude difference. On a
1001   T4 GPU, LITE remains highly efficient, taking less than 2 seconds for 1,000 optimization iterations,
1002   with the potential for even better performance on advanced GPUs.
