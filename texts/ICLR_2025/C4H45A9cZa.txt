009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper introduces the Hierarchical Multiscale Diffuser (HM-Diffuser), a novel
012            approach for efficient long-horizon planning. Building on recent advances in
013            diffusion-based planning, our method addresses the challenge of planning over
014            horizons significantly longer than those available in the training data. We de-
015            compose the problem into two key subproblems. The first phase, Progressive
016
               Trajectory Extension (PTE), involves stitching short trajectories together to cre-
               ate datasets with progressively longer trajectories. In the second phase, we train
017
               the HM-Diffuser on these extended datasets, preserving computational efficiency
018
               while enhancing long-horizon planning capabilities. The hierarchical structure of
019            the HM-Diffuser allows for subgoal generation at multiple temporal resolutions,
020            enabling a top-down planning approach that aligns high-level, long-term goals
021            with low-level, short-term actions. Experimental results demonstrate that the com-
022            bined PTE and HM-Diffuser approach effectively generates long-horizon plans,
023            extending far beyond the originally provided trajectories.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
      The ability to envision a long future to plan optimal decisions is a fundamental ability of intelligent
028
      agents operating in highly complex and dynamic environments (Hamrick et al., 2020; Mattar &
029
      Lengyel, 2022). This capability allows agents to avoid suboptimal, short-sighted decisions by
030   exploring future states that align with long-term goals, even when rewards are sparse (Silver et al.,
031   2016; Hafner et al., 2019; Hansen et al., 2022). However, learning an effective world model (Ha
032   & Schmidhuber, 2018) necessary for long-horizon planning is challenging due to the difficulty in
033   modeling intricate and high-dimensional dynamics.
034
      Traditional approaches to planning rely on learning the forward dynamics model that predicts the
035
      next state from the current state and action. Long-horizon planning is then achieved by iteratively
036   applying one-step predictions in an autoregressive manner. A major limitation of this approach is the
037   compounding of errors (Lambert et al., 2022), where minor inaccuracies accumulate over time. This
038   leads to deviations from the intended trajectory and degraded performance as the planning horizon
039   extends (Bachmann & Nagarajan, 2024). One way to mitigate this is by introducing a multiscale
040   hierarchy (Sutton et al., 1999; Chung et al., 2017; Kim et al., 2019), where high-level planners
041   perform planning on jumpy or temporally abstract states to reduce the frequency of planning steps.
042   The Diffuser approach (Janner et al., 2022; Ajay et al., 2022) extends Diffusion Models (Sohl-
043   Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020) to planning tasks and has recently emerged as a promising
044   paradigm in planning. Diffuser addresses the limitations of traditional autoregressive planning by
045   removing the forward dynamics model. Instead, it generates an entire sequence simultaneously and
046   holistically, similar to how image diffusion models generate all pixels. This approach eliminates error
047   compounding and thus leads to accurate planning, particularly for long-horizon scenarios.
048   While Diffuser is highly effective for long-horizon planning, it faces notable limitations. A primary
049   issue is that its planning horizon is restricted by the trajectory lengths present in the training data,
050   making it challenging to model trajectories longer than those encountered during training. However,
051   in many applications, the ability to plan beyond the sequence length directly experienced is essential.
052   In contrast, planning with forward models can extend the horizon to previously unseen lengths by
053   simply rolling out longer sequences, although this introduces compounding of errors over time. One
      possible solution is to collect longer training trajectories, but this significantly reduces practicality. For

054
      example, for a robot to plan at a week- or month-long horizon based on visual experiences, it would
055   require collecting videos of that length and training a Diffuser on those extended sequences—an
056   approach that is highly impractical with the current Diffuser framework. Furthermore, even if such
057   long trajectories were collected, it is well-established that planning performance degrades on these
058   extended sequences (Chen et al., 2024b). Moreover, they would cover only a small fraction of the
059   possible long-horizon planning space.
060
      In this paper, we pose the following question: How can we plan over horizons significantly longer
061   than those available in the training data without suffering from compounding errors? For example,
062   can a robot create a week- or month-long plan using training data that contains only hour-long
063   experiences? This is the challenge we tackle in this paper, a problem we refer to as extendable long-
064   horizon planning. To address this, we introduce the Hierarchical Multiscale Diffuser (HM-Diffuser)
065   framework. Our method tackles extendable long-horizon planning by dividing the problem into two
066   subproblems: (1) extending the short original trajectories into longer ones through a process we
067   call Progressive Trajectory Extension (PTE), and (2) efficiently training a diffusion planner on these
068   extended trajectories by incorporating a hierarchical multiscale structure into the Diffuser framework.
069   PTE is a novel augmentation method that iteratively generates longer trajectories by stitching together
070   previously extended trajectories over multiple rounds of extension. HM-Diffuser then trains on these
071   extended trajectories, breaking down planning tasks across multiple temporal scales, enabling efficient
072   training and execution even for very long horizons. To overcome the complexity of maintaining
073   multiple separate diffuser models, we further introduce Adaptive Plan Pondering and Recursive
074   HM-Diffuser, which uses a single diffuser to recursively handle different plan scales. Our results
075
      demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach in various long-horizon planning tasks, showcasing its
      potential to significantly advance efficient long-horizon decision-making.
076
077   The main contributions of this paper are as follows: (i) We introduce the problem of extendable
078   long-horizon planning in Diffuser, where the task is to plan for trajectories longer than those seen
079   during training. (ii) We propose the Hierarchical Multiscale Diffusion framework, which includes
080   (ii-a) a novel augmentation method called Progressive Trajectory Extension (PTE) and (ii-b) a new
081
      planning diffuser, such as the Recursive Hierarchical Diffuser. (iii) We introduce new benchmarks,
      including the Extendable-Large & XXLarge Mazes, Extendable-Gym-MuJoCo, and Extendable-
082
      Kitchen, as previous benchmarks for Diffusers in the context of extendable long-horizon planning
083
      were not available in the community.

      in the initial dataset. Our approach consists of two phases. First, we generate longer trajectories from

      our hierarchical multiscale planner on these extended trajectories to improve long-horizon planning.

      To address this issue, we observe that the Hierarchical Diffuser (HD) approach (Chen et al., 2024b) is
      well-suited to our setting. Therefore, our first strategy is to apply this approach to our extended dataset
233
      generated by the PTE process. Specifically, our planner consists of a hierarchy of L level-planners,
234
      pθℓ (τ ) for ℓ = 1, . . . , L. The ℓ-th planner is defined by its jump length jℓ and jump count kℓ . That is,
235   planner pθℓ is trained on trajectories of length Hℓ = jℓ × kℓ , randomly selected from D. However,
236   instead of densely utilizing all the states in the trajectory, it only considers every jℓ -th state over kℓ
237   iterations. This sparse approximation of a trajectory allows the planner to have low output dimensions
238   for efficient computation while still mataining an effective receptive horizon of Hℓ . We refer to
239   these intermediate states as subgoals, g1ℓ , . . . , gkℓ ℓ . The jump length at the lowest level j1 is set to
240   1 to produce a short dense plan. The key idea of hiearchical planning is to use the first subgoal of
241   level ℓ + 1 to the last goal of the lower level ℓ for ℓ = L − 1, . . . , 1. That is, given the current state
242   s0 , we have the following plan: s0 , g1ℓ , g2ℓ , . . . , gkℓ ℓ −1 , g1ℓ+1 ∼ pθℓ (τ |g0ℓ = s0 , gkℓ ℓ = g1ℓ+1 ). We can
243
      make this condition satisfied by setting Hℓ = jℓ+1 . That is, one jump segment of the above layer is
      decomposed into kℓ subgoals in the lower layer.

245   Adaptive Plan Pondering. While effective in leveraging the hierarchical multiscale structure in
246   planning, the above approach comes with a couple of limitations. The first is the fact that the planning
247   always starts from the highest level L and goes down level-by-level to obtain the action to execute
248   finally. It becomes an issue if the final goal is placed much nearer than the highest plan horizon HL ,
249
      because it would generate a long detour trajectory to move to the nearby state. To resolve this, we
      introduce Adaptive Plan Pondering (APP) by training a pondering depth predictor ℓ̄ = fϕL (s0 , sg ).
250
      This is straightforward because we know the associated level of each trajectory in D during training.
251
      At test time, it becomes possible to start the planning directly from a lower level, when necessary,
252   while skipping higher levels. This prevents planning inaccurate detouring and saves computation.

254   Recursive HM-Diffuser. The second inefficiency in the hierarchical multiscale diffuser described
255
      above is the need to maintain multiple diffuser models pθℓ , each with separate parameters θ1 , . . . , θL .
      An interesting question, therefore, arises: can we use a single diffusion model to cover all levels of the
256
      hierarchy? While this may not necessarily improve performance compared to the non-shared version,
257
      which has a larger number of parameters, it would significantly reduce the complexity of managing
258   multiple models. Therefore, it becomes a desirable approach, as long as comparable performance can
259   be maintained. To address this, we extend the model to recursive hierarchical multiscale planning,
260   allowing for a single diffusion model to handle the entire hierarchical structure.
261
      We first replace the level-Diffusers, pθ1 , . . . , pθL , by a single level-conditioned diffusion model
262
      pθ (τ |ℓ). Since this model must support planning across all levels, we set the output dimen-
263
      sion of the diffuser to d¯ = max dℓ , where dℓ is the output dimension of the ℓ-th diffuser (i.e.,
264   dℓ = (kℓ + 1) × dim(st )). If the required output dimension is smaller than d,      ¯ we mask the extra
265   dimensions. During training, we randomly sample ℓ ∼ uniform(1, . . . , L) and train the parameter-
266   shared diffuser. For planning, we predict the starting level using an adaptive plan pondering mecha-
267   nism and initiate planning from that level. After obtaining a sequence of subgoals, the first subgoal
268   is fed back into the diffuser by setting it as the final goal while decreasing the level indicator by
269   one: pθ (τ |ℓ, g0ℓ = s0 , gkℓ ℓ = g1ℓ+1 ). Repeating this process implements a form of recursive planning,
      where the plan is refined through cyclic iterations of a single diffuser.

      Hierarchical Planning. Hierarchical frameworks are widely used in reinforcement learning (RL) to
273   tackle long-horizon tasks with sparse rewards. Two main approaches exist: sequential and parallel
274   planning. Sequential methods use temporal generative models, or world models (Ha & Schmidhuber,
275   2018; Hafner et al., 2019), to forecast future states based on past data (Li et al., 2022; Hafner et al.,
276   2022; Hu et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023a). Parallel planning, driven by diffusion probabilistic models
277   (Janner et al., 2022; Ajay et al., 2022), predicts all future states at once, reducing compounding errors.
278   This has combined with hierarchical structures, creating efficient planners that train subgoal setters
279   and achievers (Li et al., 2023; Kaiser et al., 2019; Dong et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024a).
280   Diffusion-based Planners in Offline RL. Diffusion models are powerful generative models that
281   frame data generation as an iterative denoising process (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020). They
282   were first introduced in reinforcement learning as planners by Janner et al. (2022), utilizing their
283   sequence modeling capabilities. Subsequent work (Ajay et al., 2022; Liang et al., 2023; Rigter et al.,
284   2023) has shown promising results in offline-RL tasks. Diffusion models have also been explored
285   as policy networks to model highly multi-modal behavior policies (Wang et al., 2023; Kang et al.,
286   2024). Recent advancements have extended these models to hierarchical architectures (Wenhao Li,
287
      2023; Chen et al., 2024b; Dong et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024a), proving effective for long-horizon
      planning. Our method builds on this by not only using diffusion models for extremely long planning
288
      horizons but also exploring the stitching of very short trajectories with diffusion models.
289
290   Data Augmentation in RL has been a crucial strategy for improving generalization in offline RL.
291   Previous work has used dynamic models to stitch nearby states from trajectories (Char et al., 2021),
292   generate new transitions (Hepburn & Montana, 2022), or create entire trajectories from sampled
293
      initial states (Zhou et al., 2023; Lyu et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023). More recently,
      diffusion models have been applied for augmentation (Zhu et al., 2023b). Lu et al. (2023) used
294
      diffusion models to capture the joint distribution of transition tuples, while He et al. (2024) extended
295
      this to multi-task settings. Li et al. (2024) used diffusion to connect trajectories through inpainting.

      5.1   A NALYSIS ON THE P ROGRESSIVE T RAJECTORY E XTENSION
307
308   To address our first question, we conduct illustrative experiments in the Maze2D environment. We
309   tested the effectiveness of our proposed Progressive Trajectory Extension (PTE) process for long-
310   horizon stitching in larger mazes. Specifically, we used the Large Maze from D4RL and designed a
311   new XXLarge Maze (Figure A.6), which we refer to as the Extendable Maze2D benchmark.
312   Datasets. Since the existing benchmarks do not suit our problem setting, which assumes the target
313   task cannot be solved using only the short base training data, we created base short trajectories for our
314   maze benchmark. We began by dividing the maze into subregions of roughly equal size and defining
315   start and goal locations for each subregion. For data collection, we randomly selected a start-goal
316   pair within the same region and used a PD controller to collect data, navigating from start state to
317   the goal state. Following D4RL (Fu et al., 2020), we collected 1 million transitions for each Maze
318   setting, as depicted in Figure A.6.

509   6    C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
510
511   In this work, we introduce the hierarchical multiscale diffuser framework for extendable long-horizon
512   planning via Diffusion. Starting from a set of short trajectories that are insufficient for solving the
513   target task, our method first extends these trajectories using Progressive Trajectory Extension (PTE).
514   We then train a Hierarchical Multiscale Diffuser planner on this augmented dataset. In experiments,
515   we demontrate promising results on the long-horizon Maze2D task, as well as the dense-reward
516   Gym-MuJoCo and high-dimension manipulation Kitchen tasks.
517   Despite this success, our method has several areas for improvement. First, using a generative model
518   as a stitcher limits the quality of stitched trajectories to the offline dataset used for training. Similarly,
519   as an offline method, the effectiveness of our planner depends on the quality of the stitched dataset.
520   Extending the approach to online fine-tuning is an important future direction. Second, the recursive
521   version of HMD slightly underperforms compared to the non-shared HMD, likely due to differences
522   in the number of parameters. Finding ways to enhance the shared version would be a valuable avenue
523
      for exploration. Third, our plan pondering currently predicts discrete plan levels; allowing it to regress
      continuous levels could improve model flexibility. Fourth, while outstretching is beneficial, it does
524
      not completely eliminate noisy trajectories. Finally, extending the model to handle high-dimensional
525
      visual observations would be an intriguing direction for future work.

      In this section, we describe the architecture and the hyperparameters used for our experiments.

             • We build our code on the Decision Diffuser Ajay et al. (2022). We use a similar architecture

               for the temporal U-Net.

711
             • We represent the level embeddings with a 2-layered MLP with a one-hot level encoding input.

712
               training, we sample different levels and the level determines the resolution of the sampled

               trajectories.

715
             • Following Diffstitch Li et al. (2024), we use MOPO Yu et al. (2020) for the inverse dynamic

719          • We represent the level classifier fϕL (l|s1 , s2 ) with a 3-layered MLP with 256 hidden units
