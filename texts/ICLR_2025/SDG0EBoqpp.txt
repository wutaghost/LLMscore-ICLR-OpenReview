009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Foundational models hold significant potential for advancing brain function re-
012            search, particularly with recent technological advancements enabling the capture
013            of spatiotemporal dynamics of brain signals. However, existing methods are pri-
014            marily limited to characterizing observed brain signals and cannot infer continu-
015            ous future signals—an essential component for understanding the brain’s causal
016
               structure and its role in various cognitive states. Current research leaves a substan-
               tial gap in forecasting whole-brain signal sequences. To address this, we propose a
017
               self-supervised model that embeds momentary whole-brain fMRI signals into vec-
018
               tor representations and predicts continuous future signals. Our model is trained
019            on a large-scale fMRI dataset, encompassing both resting-state and naturalistic
020            stimuli conditions. Experimental results demonstrate that the model performs ef-
021            fectively in zero-shot forecasting of future whole-brain signals on unseen data and
022            excels in downstream tasks such as task-based functional state decoding. To the
023            best of our knowledge, this is the first approach to forecast and model whole-brain
024            signals at such a large scale. The experimental results validate the feasibility of
025            our method, offering new directions for theoretical research on brain signal time
026            series and potential applications in diagnosing and treating brain disorders.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) has revolutionized our ability to non-invasively
031   study brain function by providing rich spatiotemporal data of whole-brain neural activity (Van Essen
032   et al., 2013a). However, analyzing and modeling the complex dynamics of brain signals remains a
033   significant challenge (Bzdok et al., 2020). Recent advances in machine learning, particularly the de-
034   velopment of deep learning methods, offer promising new approaches for capturing intricate patterns
035   and relationships within brain functional data (Thomas et al., 2022).
036   Deep learning models, which learn general-purpose representations through training on large-scale
037   datasets, have achieved remarkable success across various domains of artificial intelligence (LeCun
038   et al., 2015). In neuroscience, these models hold great potential for advancing our understanding
039   of brain function by learning robust and generalizable representations of neural activity patterns
040   (Schirrmeister et al., 2017). Recent studies have introduced techniques that employ deep learning to
041   capture the spatiotemporal dynamics of brain signals (Kong et al., 2022). However, existing methods
042   (Ortega Caro et al., 2023; Thomas et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2024) are limited to describing observed
043   brain signals and cannot infer continuous future signals—a capability crucial for understanding the
044
      brain’s causal structure and its roles in different cognitive states.
045   This limitation highlights the need for more sophisticated approaches, especially in the domain of
046   brain signal sequence prediction (Shine & Breakspear, 2023). Accurately predicting future signals
047   from past signals can provide valuable insights into the temporal evolution of neural processes,
048   cognitive dynamics, and causal relationships between brain regions. Such predictive capabilities
049   can significantly enhance our ability to study complex cognitive phenomena, diagnose neurological
050
      disorders, and develop brain-computer interfaces.
051   In this paper, we present a novel self-supervised deep learning model designed for whole brain
052   functional signal forecasting, called BrainSF. Our model embeds instantaneous whole brain signals
053   into vector representations and predicts continuous future signals. BrainSF is trained on large-scale
      fMRI datasets encompassing resting-state and naturalistic stimulus conditions (Van Essen et al.,


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2013a; Nastase et al., 2021b), enabling it to learn generalizable representations of brain dynamics
055   across diverse cognitive states. We focus on resting-state and naturalistic stimulus data for two pri-
056   mary reasons: first, these data types are relatively accessible, allowing for the collection of large
057   samples to support large-scale model training (Turner & Calhoun, 2023); second, compared to tra-
058   ditional task-based fMRI, brain activity under resting-state and naturalistic conditions encompasses
059   a broader and richer set of functional networks, providing the model with a more comprehensive
060   representation of brain dynamics (Simony et al., 2016). This approach enables our model to cap-
061   ture more generalized patterns of brain function, thereby demonstrating superior performance across
062   various downstream tasks.
063   Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
064
065          • We propose a novel foundational model architecture specifically tailored for brain func-
066            tional signal prediction, capable of handling input and output sequences of varying lengths,
067
               and trained on large-scale, diverse fMRI datasets.
068          • We demonstrate the model’s strong zero-shot signal prediction performance on unseen data,
069            validating the generalization capability of the learned representations and the model’s ef-
070
               fectiveness in downstream tasks such as task-state decoding.
071          • We conduct an in-depth analysis of the representations learned by the model, revealing
072            how it captures key features of brain signal dynamics across continuous time dimensions.
073
               This opens new avenues for theoretical studies of brain signal time series and potential
               applications in diagnosing and treating brain disorders.
074
075   To the best of our knowledge, this is the first approach capable of predicting and modeling brain
076   signals at such a scale. Our experimental results validate the feasibility of this method, offering
077   promising directions for theoretical research on brain signal time series and potential applications
078   in diagnosing and treating brain disorders. In the subsequent sections, we detail our methodology,
079   experimental setup, and results, and discuss the broader implications of this work for the fields of
080   neuroscience and artificial intelligence. We will make the code and model publicly available upon
081
      acceptance.

085   2.1   TIME SERIES FORECASTING
086
087
      Recent progress in deep learning has greatly advanced time series forecasting. Although recurrent
      neural networks (RNNs) such as long short-term memory (LSTM) networks (Hochreiter & Schmid-
088
      huber, 1997) and gated recurrent units (GRUs) (Cho et al., 2014) are effective at capturing temporal
089
      patterns, they face limitations with handling very long sequences. To address this, researchers have
090   turned to alternative models like temporal convolutional networks (TCNs) (Bai et al., 2018) and
091   Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017; Zhou et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2021). Moreover, strategies such
092   as attention mechanisms (Qin et al., 2017), multi-task learning (Rodrigues & Pereira, 2018), and
093   transfer learning (Laptev et al., 2018) have improved forecasting accuracy, helping to tackle issues
094   like missing data (Che et al., 2018), anomaly detection (Hundman et al., 2018), and uncertainty
095   quantification (Zhu & Laptev, 2017). Graph neural networks (GNNs) have also shown potential, as
096   they can model both temporal and spatial dependencies (Bai et al., 2022; Shang et al., 2023).
097
098   2.2   BRAIN FUNCTIONAL SIGNAL REPRESENTATION
099
      Recent advancements in deep learning have significantly improved brain functional signal repre-
100
      sentation. Techniques such as convolutional autoencoders (CAEs) (Li et al., 2018) effectively learn
101
      compact and informative representations of fMRI data by capturing spatial patterns and temporal
102   dynamics. To model the intrinsic structure of brain networks, graph convolutional networks (GCNs)
103   and graph autoencoders (Arslan et al., 2018) preserve topological structures, with spatio-temporal
104   GCNs (Gadgil et al., 2021) incorporating temporal dynamics for time-varying signals. Attention
105   mechanisms further enhance representational power: attention-based LSTMs (Mahmud et al., 2020)
106   focus on relevant spatial and temporal features, and graph attention networks (GATs) (Jiang et al.,
107   2022) assign different weights to neighboring nodes for more expressive and interpretable represen-
      tations.

      2.3   FOUNDATION MODELS FOR F MRI DATA MODELING
109
110
      Recently, several foundational models for fMRI data modeling have emerged, leading fMRI research
111
      into a new paradigm. BrainLM (Ortega Caro et al., 2023) uses the MAE technique to segment brain
112   signals by time intervals, embed them, and then reconstruct the signals to capture spatiotemporal
113   representations. BrainMAE (Yang et al., 2024) follows a similar approach, representing each brain
114   region individually over a time period and then combining the information from all brain regions
115   for signal reconstruction. Dahan et al. (Dahan et al., 2024) proposed a surface-based MAE tech-
116   nique that focuses on reconstructing representations from short time intervals of signals. Armin et
117   al. (Thomas et al., 2022) introduced a BERT and GPT-based approach for brain signal represen-
118   tation, leveraging prior knowledge of brain networks. These models utilize the MAE technique to
119   reconstruct brain signals for capturing spatiotemporal representations, but they do not represent the
120
      whole-brain signals from the perspective of temporal signal prediction.
121
122
      2.4   BRAIN FUNCTIONAL SIGNAL FORECASTING
123
124
125
      Recent advancements in deep learning have significantly enhanced brain functional signal forecast-
      ing. Techniques such as convolutional neural networks (CNNs) (Xu et al., 2018), multi-scale CNNs
126
      (Yue et al., 2020), and hierarchical LSTM models (Liu et al., 2021) have been successfully ap-
127
      plied to predict future fMRI volumes, effectively capturing spatial patterns, temporal dynamics, and
128   hierarchical structures of brain signals. Graph neural networks (GNNs) have emerged as a promis-
129   ing approach for modeling the complex topological structure of brain networks; spatio-temporal
130   GNNs (Wang et al., 2022) and dynamic GNNs (Zhong et al., 2023) incorporate both spatial and
131   temporal dependencies, as well as the time-varying nature of brain connectivity, leading to more
132   accurate predictions. Additionally, integrating prior knowledge and multi-modal information has
133   further advanced the field: physiologically informed CNNs (Zhu et al., 2019) and anatomically in-
134   formed GNNs (Chen et al., 2020) leverage domain expertise for more interpretable and biologically
135   plausible outcomes, while multi-modal fusion frameworks (Sun et al., 2021) combine fMRI, EEG,
136
      and MEG signals to exploit complementary information from different imaging modalities. These
      developments not only enhance the predictive capabilities of deep learning models for brain signals
137
      but also contribute to a deeper understanding of neural mechanisms, thereby advancing the field of
138
      neuroscience and opening new avenues for clinical applications.

144   3.1   FMRI DATA AND WHOLE BRAIN SIGNALS EMBEDDING
145
146   Functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) signals are collected across multiple brain regions
147   in a series of 3D volumes, at distinct time intervals known as repetition times (TRs). Each TR rep-
148   resents a snapshot of brain function at a specific moment, typically ranging from 1 to 3 seconds in
149   length, depending on the scanning protocol used. The dynamic interactions between brain regions
150   form what we refer to as brain networks—functional units responsible for various cognitive pro-
151   cesses. Unlike prior methods (Thomas et al., 2022), which directly utilized brain networks derived
152   using techniques like seed-based correlation or independent component analysis (ICA) (Maglanoc
153
      et al., 2020), our model utilizes raw whole-brain signals, thereby avoiding the risks associated with
      prior knowledge, and allows for a more flexible and accurate representation of the brain’s natural
154
      connectivity structure. In our model, we use a parcellation of 1000 brain regions (Yan et al., 2023) to
155
      capture the fine-grained structure of brain activity which strikes a balance between the resolution of
156   whole-brain data and the computational efficiency required for large-scale modeling (Glasser et al.,
157   2016). To this end, first, the BrainSF takes as input a parcellated BOLD sequence X ∈ RT ×N ,
158   where T represents the length of time points and N denotes the brain regions (was set to 1000 in
159   the subsequent experiments). Then, as shown in Figure 1, the brain signals at each time point are
160   embedded into EmbSi ∈ R1×e (e was set to 1024. In the figure, it is composed of colored blocks),
161   which can be seen as a brain network learner. The brain signal representations at all time points are
      represented as EmbS ∈ RT ×e .

199   3.2   E NCODER AND DECODER
200
201   A positional encoding is performed on every time point t to obtain the position embedding EmbTi ∈
202   R1×e . Here, we use a learnable encoding strategy to fully capture the temporal relationships between
203   the preceding and subsequent signals. The positional embedding and whole brain signal embedding
204   for each time point are then summed to form the input data embedding Embi . This Embi is passed
205   to a channel weighting module, which calculates the weight of each representation dimension based
206   on a learnable self-attention block. As mentioned earlier, learning the temporal dynamics and in-
207   teractions of brain region signals is essential for representing brain functional signals. Here, we
208   further compress and extract the previously Embi by channel weighting to support the subsequent
209
      time series learning. Then, the obtained weights were directly multiplied by the previously obtained
      Embi to produce the final embedding EmbWi for the signal of Ti .
210

212   obtaining embeddings for all input time points, we prepend a learnable class (cls) token to the
213   sequence to capture latent features of the brain signals across all time points. These n+1 embeddings

237

240   model performance and computational resources. The current numbers are what our computational
241   resources can handle, and we have found that as the model size increases, its performance also
242   improves. We have demonstrated this in our ablation experiments.

      3.3   I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
263
264   During the pretraining phase, we randomly select 30-110 TRs of fMRI signals from the original
265   sample each time, using the first 70% as the input signal and predict the subsequent 30%. We train
266   all models using the ADAM optimizer with the following parameters: β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.999, and
267   ϵ = 1 × 10−7 . The training process consists of 15,000 epochs and involves a total of 109,978,984
268   trainable parameters. The training batch size is set to 256, and the learning rate is 5 × 10−5 , with a
269   warm-up ratio of 0.001. The training phase was conducted on a single NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4090
      GPU, utilizing 256 CPU threads and 90 GB of RAM, and took approximately 9 hours to complete.

      3.4     VALIDATION OF THE PRETRAINED B RAIN SF
271
272   Upon completing model training, we conducted a series of validation tasks to assess the performance
273   of BrainSF. First, we evaluated the model’s forecasting ability by predicting whole-brain signals on
274   the testing dataset, using both the pretrained and fine-tuned versions of BrainSF. These results were
275   then benchmarked against other existing methods applied to the same dataset to ensure a compre-
276   hensive comparison. Subsequently, we assessed the model’s generalization capability in a zero-shot
277   learning setting, testing its performance on out-of-domain data. In addition, we investigated how
278
      the model performed when predicting signals of varying temporal lengths, providing insight into
      its robustness over different time scales. Finally, the latent embeddings generated by the model
279
      were utilized for mental state decoding on unseen HCP-task data, further demonstrating the model’s
280
      versatility in neuroimaging tasks.

      4.3     INTERPRETATION AND DOWNSTREAM TASK
433
434   To explore the reliability of the model’s representation of fMRI data and its performance on down-
435   stream tasks, we visualized the latent embeddings of various types of data and then performed de-
436   coding on a specific fMRI task-based mental state. The main goal of this work is to forecast brain
437   function signals, but we also explored the representations learned by the model. We only trained the
438   BrainSF on natural stimulation and resting state data, without any task state data. We hope to verify
439   that the BrainSF learns a general and rich spatiotemporal representation from natural stimulation
440
      and resting state data that can also be used for task state fMRI data.
441
      4.3.1    L ATENT EMBEDDING OF TIME - SERIES SIGNALS
442
443   As illustrated in the left panel of Figure 3, we selected one sample from each of the three test
444   datasets—HCP-resting, HCP-movie, and Narratives—each consisting of 60 time points. These
445   samples were input into the pre-trained BrainSF model to extract the final encoder output, which
446   serves as the latent embedding. Each time point’s embedding is 1024-dimensional, resulting in a
447   60x1024 matrix for each sample. To visualize the relationships among the time points, we applied
448   UMAP for dimensionality reduction, projecting the embeddings of all 180 time points into a two-
449   dimensional space. The visualization clearly shows that data from the three distinct brain states
450
      cluster into separate regions, with embeddings from the same brain state grouped closely together.
      To further evaluate the model’s performance on out-of-domain data, we applied the same embedding
451
      procedure to HCP-task data. We randomly selected task data from 20 subjects across 7 major task
452
      categories, with each sample representing signals recorded during the execution of a specific task,
453   and the number of time points varied across tasks. As shown in the right panel of Figure 3, time
454   point embeddings within each task form well-defined clusters, often aligning along a linear struc-
455   ture, and the embeddings from different task types are clearly separated. This indicates the model’s
456   strong capability to effectively capture and represent temporal sequences from diverse and unseen
457   data sources.

523   5     C ONCLUSION
524
525
      In this work, we propose a foundational model for forecasting whole brain functional signals with
526
      multi-scale input and output trained on large-scale fMRI data. The model can fully exploit the
527
      temporal information of brain signals by separately learning representations of the fMRI signals at
528   each time point, then representing the input tokens in the encoder module and merging the fore-
529   casted tokens in the decoder module. The signal forecasting results on the test data and external data
530   demonstrate the model’s strong representation performance and generalization capability. Surpris-
531   ingly, even without training on any task-based fMRI data, the model is still able to produce strong
532   temporal representations for such data, particularly demonstrating notable performance in mental
533   state decoding. It can be found that naturalistic stimuli and resting-state fMRI data may contain
534   sufficient representations of brain functional networks information, which can be easily transferred
535   to various task-state data.
536   Our model has the potential for further exploration and significant breakthroughs in brain cognition
537   research and the study of brain diseases through its refined representation of brain state information.
538   Currently, the decoding stage only includes positional information, but in the future, various prior
539   knowledge can be integrated into the decoding stage as needed to enhance the model’s interpretabil-
      ity and causal representation capabilities.

735   A.2   FMRI, BRAIN NETWORK AND PARCELLATION
736
737   Functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) is a non-invasive neuroimaging technique that mea-
738   sures brain activity by detecting changes in blood oxygenation levels, which correlate with neural
739
      activity over time. fMRI data is collected in a series of time points, known as ”time repetitions”
      (TRs), where each TR represents a snapshot of the brain’s activity at a specific moment . This
740
      temporal resolution enables researchers to track dynamic changes in brain activity across different
741
      regions. A key application of fMRI is the study of brain functional networks, which are groups of
742   brain regions that show synchronized activity patterns over time. These networks, including well-
743   known ones like the default mode network and the frontoparietal network, are critical for various
744   cognitive and behavioral functions. Analyzing fMRI signals at different TRs provides insights into
745   the temporal interactions between these networks, helping researchers map the brain’s functional
746   organization and connectivity .
747
      Resting state fMRI data from 1489 subjects were registered using surface-based alignment. A gradi-
748   ent weighted markov random field approach was employed to identify cortical parcels ranging from
749   100 to 1000 parcels, the 1000 parcels was adopted in this work, more details are available in (Yan
750   et al., 2023).
751
752   A.3   L IMITATIONS
753
754   Despite the significant achievements of this work, several limitations remain. First, while the model
755   performs well on the HCP tasks, its ability to generalize to a broader range of cognitive tasks and
      states requires further validation. Additionally, the current temporal resolution (0.72-1.5 seconds)


                                                         14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      may not be adequate for capturing fast, millisecond-level neural dynamics. Regarding spatial res-
757   olution, although the division into 1000 regions balances spatial specificity and computational effi-
758   ciency, it may miss finer spatial patterns. Another limitation is the model’s interpretability, which
759   still needs improvement. Further research is required to map the learned representations to known
760   functional brain networks and cognitive processes.
761
      Moreover, the training data primarily consist of young, healthy adults from the HCP dataset, so the
762
      model’s performance in clinical populations, across different age groups, or in more diverse demo-
763   graphic samples remains uncertain. While BrainSF can predict future brain states, it does not provide
764   insights into the causal relationships between different brain regions over time. The model’s sub-
765   stantial computational demands may also limit its accessibility and applicability in certain research
766   or clinical settings. Furthermore, due to computational constraints, we did not perform an exhaustive
767   search of model architectures and hyperparameters, which could have impacted the model’s optimal
768   performance.
769   These limitations do not diminish the promising potential of this approach for modeling whole-brain
770   functional dynamics. Future work will focus on addressing these challenges to further enhance the
771   foundational model’s utility in both neuroscience research and clinical applications.
