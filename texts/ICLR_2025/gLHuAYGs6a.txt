009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Multi-view clustering, which aims to partition data samples into disjoint clusters
012            by leveraging information from multiple views, has been shown to be highly ef-
013            fective when incorporating structure information, as widely acknowledged in re-
014            cent works. This paper presents a novel structural multi-view clustering network
015            via heterogeneous random walks, guided by a unified sample-level structure to
016            enhance clustering performance. We first construct a multi-view heterogeneous
017            graph consisting of sample nodes and view nodes, capturing correlations between
018
               views while preserving their specific structures. Then, a multi-step random walk
               strategy on the heterogeneous graph is introduced to explore high-order sample
019
               structures across various views, ensuring that each view structure is taken into
020
               account. Based on this, a lightweight network is designed to facilitate structure
021            learning both within-view and cross-view, guided by the unified structure derived
022            from heterogeneous random walks, ultimately achieving representations that are
023            conducive to clustering. Extensive experiments on five real-world datasets demon-
024            strate the superiority of the proposed method.

027   1   INTRODUCTION
028
029   Multi-view clustering (MVC), which exploits the multi-view information to partition data samples
030   into disjoint clusters, has garnered significant attention in recent years. For example, DEMVC (Xu
031   et al., 2021) explores deep embedded multi-view clustering with collaborative training, learning
032   individual view representations and refining them collaboratively to enhance clustering performance.
033
      SCMRL (Zhou et al., 2023) seeks to uncover underlying multi-view semantic consensus information
      and leverage it to guide the learning of integrated feature representations.
034
035   Despite the success of these methods, they primarily focus on data representation learning and often
036   neglect the structure information of the data. However, the importance of accounting for struc-
037   tures among data samples has been extensively recognized, as they reveal hidden similarities and
038   associations among samples that extend beyond representation spaces, particularly in the context
039
      of multi-view data. Such structure is valuable for representation learning, further facilitating the
      discovery of latent patterns within the data. Recently, some methods have attempted to utilize struc-
040
      ture information to explore the intrinsic relationships among data samples. GDMVC (Bai et al.,
041
      2024b) combines both feature and structural information using a graph convolutional autoencoder
042   to more effectively capture the underlying data relationships. DIVIDE (Lu et al., 2024) introduces
043   high-order random walks within each individual view to learn latent structure information, which is
044   then used to guide multi-view representation learning and promote subsequent clustering.
045
      Existing methods learn representations by incorporating view-specific structure information, but
046
      most of them still have a gap in discovering a unified sample-level data partition. Notably, in multi-
047
      view scenarios, data samples are described by different views, meaning that such data is charac-
048   terized by multiple view representations rather than explicit sample representations. That is to say,
049   in multi-view clustering tasks, we can only utilize the view structure to assist in obtaining sample
050   partitions, instead of using the sample structure. Existing methods such as (Bai et al., 2024b; Lu
051   et al., 2024) have made considerable attempts to utilize view structures, but they share common lim-
052   itations: obvious differences between the structures of multiple views make it difficult to achieve a
053   consistent sample structure across views, potentially leading to the collapse of the representations;
      additionally, each view exhibits its own specific structure, with no evidence to support one another,

055   structure, to say nothing of guaranteeing the optimality of the sample structure. Therefore, the struc-

057   one hand, it should retain view-specific structure information to reflect the characteristics of the data
058   from different views, even if these structures may differ; on the other hand, it should extend to the

074   In this paper, we propose a novel structural multi-view clustering network via heterogeneous ran-
075   dom walks, guided by a unified sample-level structure through hybrid structure learning, termed
076   SMvCNet. To obtain the structure information required for multi-view clustering, we construct a
077
      multi-view heterogeneous graph (shown in Figure 1), which contains two types of nodes: sample
      nodes and view nodes. In detail, sample nodes are connected through their affiliation with view
078
      nodes, while view nodes retain the view structure information. By constructing such a graph, we
079
      can better capture the correlations between views while preserving their distinct structures, thus
080   providing a foundation for multi-view learning. Then, we design a multi-step random walk strat-
081   egy on the constructed heterogeneous graph. By leveraging the designed random walks, we uncover
082   shared high-order sample structures across different views while taking view structures into account;
083   during these walks, the contributions of the view structures are automatically integrated, allowing
084   us to select the optimal sample structure. Finally, a lightweight network is introduced that jointly
085   performs hybrid structure learning, guided by the unified sample-level structure derived from het-
086   erogeneous random walks. This learning process incorporates both within-view and cross-view
087   structure to produce clustering-friendly representations and can be trained end-to-end for clustering
088
      tasks. Extensive experiments conducted on five real-world datasets demonstrate the superiority of
      the proposed network over existing multi-view clustering models.

091   2    RELATED WORK
092
093   Multi-view clustering(Bickel & Scheffer, 2004; Fang et al., 2023) aims to achieve enhanced cluster-
094   ing performance by integrating information from different views. With the growing diversity of data
095   forms, multi-view clustering has emerged as one of the most popular tasks in data mining. Based
096   on the design of multi-view learning objectives, this paper classifies existing multi-view clustering
097   methods into two categories: traditional representation-based multi-view clustering and structural
098   multi-view clustering methods that take into account relationships among data samples. With the
099
      rise of deep learning, representation-based deep multi-view clustering has seen substantial advance-
      ments. DEMVC (Xu et al., 2021) is a deep embedded multi-view clustering model with collabo-
100
      rative training, where individual view representations are learned and then refined collaboratively
101
      to enhance clustering performance. Yan et al. (2023) presented a multi-view clustering framework
102   that aggregates global and cross-view features to learn a consensus representation for enhancing
103   clustering. MFLVC (Xu et al., 2022) is designed through multi-level feature learning for contrastive
104   multi-view clustering to improve clustering performance. CVCL (Chen et al., 2023) learns invari-
105   ant representations by contrasting cluster assignments across views. Despite these advancements,
106   challenges such as incomplete multi-view data persist. To tackle these, recent studies have incorpo-
107   rated contrastive learning and self-supervised objectives to learn more discriminative representations
      (Yang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023; Chao et al., 2024). The core of clustering tasks lies in the similar-

108
      ity among samples. As a result, many researchers argue that incorporating the structural information
109   of samples in multi-view representation learning can improve clustering performance(Wang et al.,
110   2023; Yang et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2024a). Liu et al. (2022) used neural networks to estimate sta-
111   tionary diffusion states, leveraging graph neural networks for multi-view learning. Gu et al. (2024)
112   introduced a multi-view clustering method (ONESELF), which integrates robust latent representa-
113   tion extraction with target structured graph construction into a cohesive optimization framework.
114   DIVIDE(Lu et al., 2024) is a robust contrastive multi-view clustering method that uses high-order
115   random walks to consider sample relationships. SURER (Wang et al., 2024a) refines view-specific
116   attribute graphs and integrates them into a unified heterogeneous graph, which is used by a graph
117
      neural network to learn a consensus representation for clustering. In addition, there are also some
      approaches that introduce structural information through subspace learning. For example, Si et al.
118
      (2022) proposed to enhance multi-view clustering by enforcing diversity among views and apply-
119
      ing a structure constraint to the subspace self-representation. And Wang et al. (2024b) developed a
120   framework that jointly learns a latent subspace and structured graph for multi-view clustering.

150   3.1   M ULTI - VIEW R ANDOM WALKS ON H ETEROGENEOUS G RAPH
151
152   To explore the unified sample structure across multiple views, we proposed employing multi-view
153
      multi-step random walks to progressively identify the high-order neighbors of each data sample.
      With the high-order neighbors across all views and the heterogeneous affinity, our method could
154
      model the consistency and complementariness of multi-view data. For ease of presentation, we
155
      briefly introduce the preliminary on traditional random walk. Specifically, a random walk on a
156   graph is a process that starts at some nodes and moves to other nodes according to the edge weights
157   at each step (Lovász, 1993). In other words, it can be represented by as a transition matrix M. And
158   each element Mij indicates the probability of moving from the i-th node to the j-th node in one
159   step. In this way, the probability after multi-step random walks could be denoted by:

176   Heterogeneous Graph construction To integrate structure information from different views with-
177   out disrupting the view-specific structures, we construct a heterogeneous graph containing two types
178
      of nodes, sample node and view node. Specifically, the sample node has no explicit representation,
      it can only be linked to the view node; and the view node is represented by the hidden embedding,
179
      which can be linked to both the view node and the sample node. For each sample node, it is linked
180
      to V view nodes describing the sample with same probability, and there are no links between sample
181   nodes. For each view node, it is linked to other view nodes according its corresponding transition
182   probability; and each view node must be linked to its sample node. According to this, we can explore
183   structural relationships among multi-view sample nodes through the weights between view nodes.

532   5   C ONCLUSIONS
533
534   This paper proposes a structural multi-view clustering network via heterogeneous random walks.
535   By constructing a heterogeneous graph and employing a multi-step random walk, we uncover high-
536   order sample structures across various views while preserving view-specific structures. The network
537   is guided by the unified structure derived from heterogeneous random walks, enhancing represen-
538   tation and improving clustering performance. Extensive experiments demonstrate the superiority
539   of SMvCNet over existing models. In future work, we will further explore graph simplification
      strategies to improve efficiency and effectiveness of designed heterogeneous random walks.
