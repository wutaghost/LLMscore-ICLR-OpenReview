009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Contemporary evaluation techniques are inadequate for agentic systems. These
012            approaches either focus exclusively on final outcomes—ignoring the step-by-step
013            nature of the thinking done by agentic systems—or require excessive manual
014            labour. To address this, we introduce the Agent-as-a-Judge framework, wherein
015            agentic systems are used to evaluate agentic systems. This is a natural exten-
016
               sion of the LLM-as-a-Judge framework, incorporating agentic features that en-
               able intermediate feedback for the entire task-solving processes for more precise
017
               evaluations. We apply the Agent-as-a-Judge framework to the task of code gen-
018
               eration. To overcome issues with existing benchmarks and provide a proof-of-
019            concept testbed for Agent-as-a-Judge, we present DevAI, a new benchmark of
020            55 realistic AI code generation tasks. DevAI includes rich manual annotations,
021            like a total of 365 hierarchical solution requirements, which make it particularly
022            suitable for an agentic evaluator. We benchmark three of the top code-generating
023            agentic systems using Agent-as-a-Judge and find that our framework dramatically
024            outperforms LLM-as-a-Judge and is as reliable as our human evaluation baseline.
025            Altogether, we believe that this work represents a concrete step towards enabling
026            vastly more sophisticated agentic systems. To help that, our dataset and the full
027
               implementation of Agent-as-a-Judge will be publically available at [REDACTED]

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Recent years have seen multimodal agentic systems move from occasionally being able to solve
032   small toy problems to being regularly deployed for challenging real-world problems (the dream of
033
      most AI research). Yet, the current evaluation methods and the available benchmarks for agentic
      systems are struggling to keep up with these rapid advances, dramatically slowing true progress.
034
035   We believe that the current issue with evaluating agentic systems stems from the lack of feedback
036   during the intermediate task-solving stages for these nontraditional systems. Agentic systems think
037   more like a human, often act step-by-step (Wooldridge, 1999) and often hosting very human-like
038   natural language discussions internally to solve problems (Zhuge et al., 2023). And thus agentic
039
      systems should be evaluated like a human, with rich evaluative feedback which looks at the full
      thought and action trajectory; evaluating an agentic system in the traditional way is like evaluating
040
      a student using multiple-choice testing—a comparatively unreliable estimator (Park, 2010). For
041
      example, while SWE-Bench (Jimenez et al.) is widespread, its evaluation method, which relies
042   solely on the final resolve rate for long-term automated repair tasks, does not effectively pinpoint
043   what is happening within agentic systems that affects the resolve rate. On the other hand, performing
044   a better evaluation with a human is prohibitively expensive. We instead propose that agentic systems
045   should be used to evaluate agentic systems. Inspired by LLM-as-a-Judge (Zheng et al., 2024; Fu
046   et al., 2024; Chen et al.), which uses LLMs to evaluate LLMs, we call this framework Agent-as-a-
047   Judge, of which it is a key extension to the world of agentic systems (see Figure 1). It not only retains
048   the cost-effectiveness of LLM-as-a-Judge but is also equipped with agentic features, allowing it to
049   provide rich intermediate feedback throughout the entire process, as it acts as an agentic system. We
050
      apply the Agent-as-a-Judge systems to the problem of evaluating code generating systems—one of
      the areas where agentic systems have looked the most promising recently.
051
052   In code generation, the development of benchmarks has also lagged behind the rapid advancement
053   of agentic systems. HumanEval (Chen et al., 2021), for example, focuses exclusively on algorithmic
      problems, while MBPP (Austin et al., 2021) deals with simple programming tasks. Although they

      2.1       M OTIVATION
140
141   Background The code generation domain is an area where agentic systems have seen significant
142   industrial deployment during the past 2 years (e.g., see Devin1 and Cursor2 ). However, in code
143   generation, there isn’t yet a benchmark that accurately reflects realistic user queries for developing
144   complete AI systems. We believe this is because of the difficulty to evaluate such complex, real-
145   world tasks. For example, while many companies advertise their systems based on the performance
146   of it on benchmarks like SWE-Bench (Jimenez et al.) (for automated repair) or HumanEval (Chen
147   et al., 2021) (for algorithmic tasks), these benchmarks cover only a small bit of an actual develop-
148   ment process. A benchmark that can evaluate the entire development process—ideally in a way that
149
      can help understand the degree to which current AI methods can reduce human labour—is missing.
150   Topic We chose automated AI development as our main topic. While AI and ML tasks are often
151   more complex, they follow clear, standard procedures. For example, data processing typically comes
152   first in an AI pipeline, and performance reporting goes at the end. We believe this topological nature
153   can help better monitor the development process and provide useful signals to the agentic systems.
154   Goals An ideal benchmark should address critical issues in automated development by focusing
155   on three key factors. First, it should reflect practical software scenarios, where tasks are often too
156   complex for a single LLM, requiring human or agentic systems. Second, it should emphasize the
157   development process, not just final outcomes (e.g., pass@1 rates offer limited feedback and fail
158   to highlight intermediate problems). Lastly, the evaluation should be computationally cost-effective
159   and efficient, avoiding long training times or excessive manual oversight.

197   2.2       T HE D EVAI DATASET
198
199   Motivated by the ideas outlined above, we propose the DevAI dataset. DevAI consists of a carefully
200   curated set of 55 tasks, each defined by (1) a plain text query that describes an AI development task;
201   (2) a set of plain text requirements (for a total of 365 requirements), each with a set of dependencies
202   connecting them to other requirements; and (3) a set of preferences (for a total of 125 preferences)
203   which represent softer requirements. DevAI is structured so that an agentic system starts by re-
204   ceiving a user query to begin development. The system is then evaluated on how well it meets the
205   requirements, with preferences serving as optional, softer criteria. An example of one of the DevAI
206
      tasks can be seen in Figure 3 and the full suite is available at [REDACTED]
207   The tasks in DevAI are relatively small-scale but cover commonly used key development techniques.
208   As shown in Figure 2, our tasks are tagged and cover a variety of key areas in AI: supervised
209   learning, reinforcement learning, computer vision, natural language processing, generative models,
210   and others. Each of the tasks is a real-world problem that could be given to a research engineer,
211   while simultaneously being relatively inexpensive computationally to run so as to reduce the cost
212
      of evaluating a method on this benchmark. Details of the sample collection and human labeling
      process for DevAI are provided in Appendix E.
213

215   process and are arranged as a directed acyclic graph (similar to the work by He et al. (2021)), with
      requirements such as visualizing results depending on correct data loading and modeling. This

289
      analysis, we have three expert human evaluators (referred to here by their anonymous names: 231a,
290   38bb, and cn90) review the outputs of AI developer baselines to assess whether each requirement
291   was satisfied. We have two rounds of human evaluations. To capture the bias inherent in typical
292   human evaluation (this is desirable to capture here as it represents a likely scenario in deployment),
293   in the first round, our evaluators first discussed the basic standards but were given minimal instruc-
294   tions. The templates the evaluators were given for the evaluation and their self-reported post-hoc
295   descriptions of how they resolved ambiguities are reported in Figure 12 in Appendix H.
296   After the initial round of human evaluations (which totaled an estimated total of 58 human hours), we
297   asked our evaluators to discuss and reach a consensus on their assessments (which took an estimated
298   total of 28.5 additional human hours). This consensus, achieved after long sessions of debate, was
299   used as the final human evaluation result for each method.
300

301
      two best-performing methods (GPT-Pilot and OpenHands) could satisfy about 29% of the require-
302   ments (or around 44% if prerequisites are ignored) but only on one task could they meet all the
303   requirements. This highlights that DevAI offers a considerable but appropriate level of challenge
304   for current and future methods. Moreover, the fulfillment of intermediate requirements aligns with
305   our expectations (see Section 2) that DevAI provides richer feedback by uncovering how agentic
306   systems falter during the process instead of just focusing on a single performance metric at the end.

343   Error Analysis As previously noted, the evaluators engaged in a round of debating after their
344   initial evaluations until they reached a consensus on each requirement in each task (with the results
345   of this consensus evaluation shown in Table 2).
346   In our Human-as-a-Judge pipeline, evaluators could be convinced by evidence from others and ac-
347   knowledge their judgment errors, adjusting their answers accordingly. This can be used to approxi-
348   mate individual errors. If the consensus evaluation more accurately predicts any extant ground truth,
349   we would expect the majority vote from the individual evaluations to more closely approximate this
350   than any single evaluation, due to the fundamental properties of ensemble classifiers (see Hastie
351   et al. (2009)).
352   While the consensus evaluation may not represent the absolute ground truth (we acknowledge that
353   some quantity of error likely would still exist even after this procedure), we expect the consensus
354   evaluation to more accurately approximate the extant ground truth (Clemen, 1989). If this holds, the
355   majority vote should align more closely with the consensus than with any individual evaluation. As
356   shown in Figure 5, this is indeed the case.
357   As seen in the results, although significant errors occur among all evaluators, the majority vote
358   effectively corrects most of these errors. Notably, cn9o made the most errors (for example, 23.77%
359   in evaluating GPT-Pilot). After applying the majority vote from all three evaluators, the overall error
360   rate dropped to 6.01%, demonstrating the inherent benefits of majority voting.
361
362
      Conclusion Human judgment errors are inevitable. To reduce them, we suggest two methods.
363
      First, like in this work, introduce a debate round after each judgment, where individuals present
364   evidence and either persuade others or adjust their own opinions after discussion. This is particularly
365   important when there are only a few evaluators, as majority voting with a small group can still
366   lead to errors (around 5% compared to consensus evaluation, as shown in Figure 5). The second
367   approach involves assembling a larger panel of experts (more is better when their accuracy exceeds
368   50% (Grofman et al., 1983)), with over 5 people recommended by Hastie & Kameda (2005); Larrick
369   & Soll (2006), and relying on a majority vote. However, due to the high cost of engaging more
370   experts and the fact that this is not always feasible in practice, we argue for the former.

504   5     R ELATED W ORK
505
506   Agentic systems is a highly active research area, so we only detail the most closely related works
507   here. We provide a treatment of the marginally less relevant related works in Appendix D.
508   AI Developers AI in software development is growing fast (Liu et al., 2024). AI-driven developers
509   have been applied to directly imitate software companies (Hong et al., 2024b; Qian et al., 2024a),
510   debug code (Yang et al., 2024a), run data science methods (Guo et al.; Hong et al., 2024a; Li et al.,
511   2024; Qiao et al., 2023), and even write academic papers (Lu et al., 2024a).
512   Benchmarks for AI developments Benchmarks like MLAgentBench (Huang et al., 2024), ML-
513   Bench (Liu et al., 2023), and SUPER (Bogin et al., 2024) all focus on benchmarking agentic sys-
514   tems using AI tasks. However, DevAI distinguishes itself from all of these by focusing on realistic
515   user queries that target a complete development cycle. It further includes a more comprehensive
516   evaluation with multiple hierarchical requirements and preferences for each task. Comparatively,
517   MLAgentBench (Huang et al., 2024), for example, focuses on final performance for a limited set of
518   well-known tasks, which risks overfitting and fails to assess a system’s generalization or adaptability.
519   AI Judges Several works have looked at using AI systems as judges. The work by Chan et al.;
520   Zhao et al. (2024), for example, extends LLM-as-a-Judge to have multiple LLMs in their evaluation
521   process for conversational tasks. Unlike Agent-as-a-Judge, they employ a trivial agentic system and
522   apply it only to evaluate LLMs under traditional evaluation setups. In contrast, (Lu et al., 2024b) uses
523   a single LLM-based evaluator but, unlike LLM-as-a-Judge, applies this to multimodal tasks rather
524   than just for evaluating LLMs. Less relevant are frameworks like those by Chen et al. (2024a); Arora
525   et al. (2024); Mündler et al. (2024), where intermediate signals are used during coding development.
526
527   6     D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
528
529   Discussion A key power of the Agent-as-a-Judge that we have not exploited here is the feedback it
530   provides being directly used by the agentic system being evaluated. Perhaps the greatest strength of
531   the Agent-as-a-Judge framework is that an agentic system could use it to fix issues in their solutions
532   to complex multistage problems on the fly—something older delayed feedback methods did not
533   permit. In addition, a cycle of mutual improvement between Agent-as-a-Judge and the evaluated
534   agents, where both evolve together through iterative feedback, presents a promising perspective.
535   Conclusion In this work, we introduced the Agent-as-a-Judge method to use agentic systems to
536   evaluate agentic systems. We simultaneously released DevAI: a new benchmark that evaluates
537   the code-generating ability of agentic systems on complete AI development tasks when used with
538   Agent-as-a-Judge. We went on to show that Agent-as-a-Judge outperforms existing methods on
539   this task and that it performs similarly to an ensemble of expert human evaluators. Altogether, we
      believe that the above opens the door for scaling up agentic far more than before.

975        Paper: Agent-as-a-Judge: Evaluating Agents with Agents
976
977                                                Key Logic
978
           ■   Step 1: Concept Proposal
979            Description: We propose the Agent-as-a-Judge concept, an extension of the LLM-
980            as-a-Judge framework, aimed at evaluating agentic systems using other agentic sys-
981            tems.
982
           ■   Step 2: Dataset Creation
983            Description: To address the lack of suitable datasets for evaluating agentic systems
984            in automated AI development, we introduce DevAI, a new dataset consisting of 55
985            realistic AI code generation tasks. This also serves as a testbed for the Agent-as-a-
986            Judge proof-of-concept.
987        ■   Step 3: Baseline Evaluation of Developer Agents (Experiment Level 1)
988            Description: In the first level of experiments, we select three popular open-source
989            developer agents: MetaGPT, GPT-Pilot, and OpenDevin. These agents are evalu-
990            ated on the DevAI tasks to establish performance baselines.
991        ■   Step 4: Conducting Human-as-a-Judge Evaluation
992            Description: We conduct a Human-as-a-Judge experiment, where three human ex-
993            perts assess the performance of the developer agents on the same DevAI tasks.
994
           ■ Step 5: Human-as-a-Judge Analysis (Experiment Level 2)
995          Description: In the second level of experiments, we statistically analyze the results
996          of Human-as-a-Judge evaluations, focusing on the costs of human labor and potential
997          biases, highlighting the challenges of relying on human evaluation for complex tasks.
998
           ■ Step 6: Agent-as-a-Judge Implementation
999          Description: We design and implement the Agent-as-a-Judge proof-of-concept to
1000         evaluate code generation on the DevAI dataset. This system incorporates modules
1001         such as graph, search, read, and ask, providing multi-dimensional evaluation metrics.
1002
           ■   Step 7: Comparing Judgment Systems (Experiment Level 3)
1003           Description: In the third level of experiments, we compare three judgment systems:
1004           Agent-as-a-Judge, LLM-as-a-Judge, and Human-as-a-Judge, all applied to the
1005           same DevAI tasks. Our results show that Agent-as-a-Judge performs comparably to
1006           human evaluators and surpasses LLM-as-a-Judge in more complex reasoning and
1007           evaluation tasks.
1008
1009
                                               Future Directions
1010       ■   Direction 1: Enhancing Agent-as-a-Judge
1011           Description: Future work should focus on improving the performance of Agent-as-a-
1012           Judge, especially in more complex and diverse environments, to handle increasingly
1013
               sophisticated decision-making tasks.
1014       ■   Direction 2: Intermediate Feedback for Self-Improvement
1015           Description: A promising extension of this work is enabling Agent-as-a-Judge to
1016           provide intermediate feedback, helping developer agents iteratively improve and self-
1017
               optimize their decision-making processes.

       LLM-based Autonomous Agents Recent developments in LLM-based agents have expanded
       their capabilities beyond simple task execution to more autonomous problem-solving and decision-
1142

1143

1144   MetaGPT (Hong et al., 2024b), AutoGen (Wu et al., 2023) and CAMEL (Li et al., 2023) focus on
1145   role-based multi-agent communication, improving collaboration among agents. However, the chal-
1146   lenge of maintaining coherence in agents’ dialogue and preventing hallucination remains promi-
1147   nent (Du et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2023). Most recently, using graphs to build agents has gained
1148   prominence. Earlier work like GPTSwarm (Zhuge et al.) and LangGraph (LangChain-AI, 2024)
1149   proposed using nodes to represent operations and edges to represent the connections between them.
1150   In GPTSwarm, multiple agents represented as subgraphs in a graph are connected by optimizable
1151   edges, and reinforcement learning is employed to optimize the edges. Following this approach, sev-
1152
       eral agent frameworks have incorporated graphs into their designs (Hong et al., 2024a; Zhou et al.,
       2024; Qian et al., 2024b). Additionally, various optimization methods have been developed to en-
1153

1154
       plications, many studies focus on understanding and interacting with GUIs (Wang et al., 2024a; Chen
1155   et al., 2024b; Yang et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024; Tan et al.). For code generation agents (Jin et al.,
1156   2024), current research mainly emphasizes automated repair (Yang et al., 2024a; Phan et al., 2024;
1157   Tao et al., 2024), computational modular design (Khattab et al., 2024; Cheng et al.), and automated
1158   development (Tufano et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2023). Among these, open-sourced frameworks like
1159   OpenHands (Wang et al., 2024b) have gained popularity due to their strong user experience. More-
1160   over, scientific discovery (Jansen et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024a) and ML agents (Yang et al., 2024b)
1161   are also receiving increased attention.

       E.1   M ANUALLY DRAFT USER QUERIES
1245
1246   Given the execution cost of the developer agents, we collect small-scale AI tasks to ensure the prac-
1247   tical applicability of our benchmark. Since these tasks are small-scale and well studied, which are
1248   easy to overfit in terms of task performance metrics, unlike previous benchmarks (e.g., (Huang et al.,
1249   2024)), we do not evaluate task performance as the development performance measure. Instead, we
1250   prioritize the step-by-step task-solving ability, which is essential for real-world development. Our
1251   quires are specifically designed to require the development agents to understand user intentions,
1252
       solve the task in multiple steps, and adapt to unexpected step outcomes. This approach also makes
       our benchmark user-friendly, transparent, and better reflects real-world deployment situations. To
1253
       enable effective evaluation, our queries present a specific development file structure for the devel-
1254
       oper agents to follow. To ensure that the developer agents save the files to be evaluated in the
1255   workspace, we develop constraint prompts added to the query to form an extended query. The con-
1256   straint prompts guide the developer agents to save and execute the source codes, which are in line
1257   with the real-world development standard. See Appendix I for our constraint prompts.
1258
1259   E.2   S ET J UDGING C RITERIA
1260
1261   To make the evaluation of the developer agent precise, we assign to each task query a list of require-
1262   ments as task milestones. The requirements are chosen so that satisfying all the requirements is a
1263   necessary condition to consider the task to be solved successively. Since our tasks are AI-centric, our
1264
       queries target essential elements of AI development, including data processing, AI method, presen-
       tation of evaluation metrics, visualization, and human-computer interaction, covering the key areas
1265
       that matter most in real-world scenarios. On the other hand, breaking down tasks into individual
1266
       requirements also reflects the multi-step nature of code development. Importantly, to avoid ambigu-
1267   ity, we set the requirements to be explicit, binary, and straightforward to evaluate. To include other
1268   human predispositions, we include a list of preferences per task that covers subjective, ambiguous,
1269   or non-explicitly stated characteristics.
1270
1271   E.3   B UILDING D EPENDENCY A MONG R EQUIREMENTS
1272
1273   To enhance the realism of our benchmark, we analyzed the dependencies among requirements.
1274   Over the past decades, methodologies such as the KDD Process (Fayyad et al., 1996) and CRISP-
1275   DM (Wirth & Hipp, 2000) have guided ML/AI development, establishing foundational frameworks
1276   that have been further refined with the rise of AutoML (He et al., 2021).
1277   Inspired by these methodologies, we identified a seven-step process for automated AI development
1278   tasks. This process includes critical stages such as data preprocessing, feature engineering, model
1279   selection, and hyperparameter tuning, along with essential post-development activities like metrics
1280   recording, report generation, and the development of interactive user applications. This structured
1281   approach allows us to evaluate the agent’s ability to manage task dependencies and effectively navi-
1282   gate complex, real-world scenarios.
1283
1284   E.4   R EFINE THE DATASET
1285
       Manual refinements were necessary to ensure the accuracy and clarity of DevAI. We perform two
1286
       rounds of comprehensive review and edits on DevAI, each round being done by a different partic-
1287
       ipant. During these manual refinements, we focus on the logical consistency of our queries and
1288   requirements, the correctness and ambiguity of the language, and the applicability of the content to
1289   the task domain. We identified a moderate number of errors in our dataset during the review.
1290
1291   E.5   A NALYSE THE DATASET
1292
1293   We categorized each requirement based on its focus, whether it was related to the data set, the
1294   machine learning method, visualization, metrics, HCI, or data processing. While this approach
1295   provides a useful framework, it is important to recognize that these categories might overlap or miss
       certain nuances. Similarly, preferences were classified by how strongly they appeared in the query

1299   and preferences this way, we aim to enhance the evaluation process, though flexibility and context
1300   awareness remain crucial for truly robust assessments.

       F    U SER EXPERIENCES OF CODE - GENERATION AGENTIC SYSTEMS
1459
1460
       OpenHands (Wang et al., 2024b) offers the most refined user experience, leveraging its highly inter-
1461   active frontend to enable seamless user interaction and task execution. This interface allows users to
1462   engage directly with the system, resulting in a smoother and more intuitive workflow, which drives
1463   operational efficiency.
1464
       In contrast, MetaGPT (Hong et al., 2024b) excels in task decomposition through its use of Directed
1465
       Acyclic Graphs (DAGs), a well-structured and scalable approach aligned with industry best practices
1466
       in system modularization. This enhances its appeal for users focused on task clarity and modular
1467   breakdowns. However, in practical deployments, MetaGPT tends to be less aggressive in file man-
1468   agement and preservation, potentially due to its core positioning as a data analysis tool, which does
1469   not prioritize persistent state management. Similarly, OpenDevin demonstrates a notable overconfi-
1470   dence in its code generation, frequently skipping the critical step of post-generation code execution,
1471   requiring users to intervene manually.
1472
       GPT-Pilot (Pythagora.io, 2023), praised for its detailed task delegation via over 20 specialized
1473   agents, suffers from reduced interactivity due to an overly granular division of responsibilities, re-
1474   sulting in a more fragmented user experience. These qualitative insights, although not fully captured
1475   by quantitative metrics, were evident through the DevAI dataset, providing key areas for improve-
1476   ment in user engagement and operational fluidity in future releases of these frameworks.

       H    H UMAN E VALUATION P ROCEDURE
1621
1622
       We recruited three AI experts from the authors to perform human evaluation on the output of agentic
1623   code generation systems. There we present the evaluation details.
1624
1625   First round For the first round of evaluations, our three evaluators reported spending 16.5, 19.5,
1626   and 22.0 hours, respectively. To capture the bias that a human evaluator will have, the instruc-
1627   tions given to our experts were minimal, with them only receiving a scorecard to complete for each
1628   agentic system and each task. Results that all evaluators agree on are considered trustworthy. The
1629   assumption here is that it is unlikely that all three evaluators make a mistake or have an effective
1630   bias in the same judgment. The self-reported post-hoc evaluation criteria are shown in Figure 12.
1631
1632   Second round In the second round, the evaluators present and discuss their reasons for disagree-
1633   ing with judges. In doing so, human errors are likely corrected by their peers. Discussion among
1634
       evaluators also helps reduce human bias by examining each other’s thought processes thoroughly.
       Furthermore, the consensed results are considered trustworthy given the assumption that it is un-
1635
       likely that all three evaluators are convinced by the same mistake or the same cognitive bias. The
1636
       three evaluators took 9.5 hours together for this second round of evaluation.

       components ask, graph, read, and locate resulted in significant performance gains. However,
2122   when the search component was introduced, there was a noticeable decline in performance.
2123
2124   We hypothesize that the performance drop from search is due to its role in retrieving relevant code
2125
       snippets (top-3) using BM25. The retrieval accuracy of BM25 (Robertson et al., 2009) might not
       have been high enough, potentially introducing noise. Moreover, as noted in Table 1, the DevAI
2126
       tasks in our experiments did not generate a large amount of code. In fact, even when all code was
2127
       fed into an LLM, the total content typically stayed within the maximum context length. Therefore,
2128   in simpler workspaces, search was less critical. However, we believe this component will become
2129   more important as the complexity of the workspace increases, making it more valuable in larger and
2130   more complex environments.
2131
       We also observed that the introduction of the planning mechanism did not bring a noticeable
2132
       improvement in performance. This may be related to the nature of the Judge - it needs clean factual
2133
       information. When planning is unstable, the evidence collected from different actions can be-
2134   come inconsistent, leading to a decline in performance. Finally, we experimented with a memory
2135   mechanism. Initially, we hypothesized that since DevAI tasks often involve interconnected require-
2136   ments, memory could help track whether requirements were met. However, in practice, we saw
2137   no improvement. We suspect that the interconnected nature of the requirements may have caused
2138   biases: specifically, once a prior requirement was fulfilled, it might have overly influenced positive
2139   judgments on subsequent requirements, even if they were not fully met.
