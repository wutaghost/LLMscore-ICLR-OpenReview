009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph Transformers (GTs) have emerged as a promising approach for graph rep-
012            resentation learning. Despite their successes, the quadratic complexity of GTs
013            limits scalability on large graphs due to their pair-wise computations. To funda-
014            mentally reduce the computational burden of GTs, we introduce Primphormer,
015            a primal-dual framework that interprets the self-attention mechanism on graphs
016            as a dual representation and then models the corresponding primal representation
017            with linear complexity. Theoretical evaluations demonstrate that Primphormer
018
               serves as a universal approximator for functions on both sequences and graphs,
               showcasing its strong expressive power. Extensive experiments on various graph
019
               benchmarks demonstrate that Primphormer achieves competitive empirical results
020
               while maintaining a more user-friendly memory and computational costs.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Graph representation learning has been successfully applied in various fields, including social net-
026   work analysis (Li et al., 2023), traffic prediction (Dong et al., 2023), and drug discovery (Liu et al.,
027   2023), among others. Much of the research in graph representation learning has focused on Mes-
028   sage Passing Neural Networks (MPNNs) which rely on local message-passing mechanisms. Al-
029   though MPNNs have emerged as a powerful approach to short-range tasks that require information
030   exchange among nodes in local neighborhoods, MPNNs face inherent limitations such as over-
031   smoothing (Nguyen et al., 2023), over-squashing (Giraldo et al., 2023), and limited expressivity (Xu
032
      et al., 2019; Morris et al., 2019) in long-range tasks (Dwivedi et al., 2022b).
033   To overcome the limitations, Graph Transformers (GTs) which allow each node to globally attend
034   to all other nodes is proposed to enable the learning of long-range dependencies within the graph
035   (Rampasek et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022). While GT is a promising approach, it has a notable
036   drawback in the quadratic complexity, i.e., pair-wise computations in self-attention mechanisms,
037   preventing their practical use.
038   The key to reducing the quadratic complexity is to use computationally efficient attention mecha-
039   nisms. Linear attentions like Performer (Choromanski et al., 2021) and BigBird (Zaheer et al., 2020)
040   have been integrated into GTs. However, they need to introduce additional computational overhead,
041   which becomes the dominating source of computation for medium-sized graphs (Rampasek et al.,
042   2022). An alternative approach is sparse attention. Shirzad et al. (2023) introduced Exphormer, a
043   sparse attention mechanism that exchanges information only across edges. The efficiency of Ex-
044
      phormer benefits from the sparsity of graphs. However, its computational complexity increases to
      quadratic with the number of nodes as graphs become denser, thereby limiting its scalability.
045
046   To fundamentally enhance the scalability of GTs, it is crucial to avoid pair-wise computations,
047   prompting us to consider the primal-dual relationship in kernel machines. Examples of models
048   leveraging this relationship include the support vector machine (Cortes & Vapnik, 1995), the least
049   squares support vector machine (Suykens & Vandewalle, 1999), and the kernel principal component
050
      analysis (Mika et al., 1999). The primal-dual relationship represents pair-wise symmetric similarity
      in duality as an inner product of feature mappings in the primal space. By solving optimization
051
      problems in the primal space with these feature mappings, quadratic complexity can be avoided.
052
053   When constructing the primal representation of the self-attention mechanism, we encounter an es-
      sential problem that attention scores are inherently asymmetric, violating the Mercer’s condition


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      (Mercer, 1909), which causes the classical primal-dual discussion to fail. Recent research on primal-
055   dual relationships has sought to explore methods for accommodating asymmetry in kernel machines
056   (Suykens, 2016; He et al., 2023a). In Chen et al. (2023), the self-attention on sequences was in-
057   terpreted through kernel singular value decomposition. This approach collects data information
058   through uniformly sampling the sequence under an inductive bias assumption that sequences are
059   ordered. However, this assumption does not hold for graphs, as the graph structure is determined by
060   the edges, and the arrangement or ordering of nodes is not explicitly specified, making it unsuitable
061   for graph-based learning tasks.
062   Our contributions. We propose a novel primal representation for graph Transformers, named Prim-
063   phormer. This method supports asymmetry in self-attention on graphs by introducing an asymmetric
064   kernel trick. It avoids costly pair-wise computations and storage overhead without introducing addi-
065   tional heavy computational burden. The primal-dual analysis reveals that Primphormer can leverage
066   graph information to adjust the basis of outputs, thereby potentially enhancing the model’s capacity.
067   Since Primphormer is a new architecture for GTs, we are also interested in its expressive power. To
068   explore this, we prove that Primphormer serves as a universal approximator for arbitrary continu-
069
      ous functions on a compact domain. Through extensive experiments on various graph benchmarks,
      we show that Primphormer achieves competitive empirical results while maintaining a more user-
070
      friendly memory and computational costs.

214   Chen et al. (2023) introduced an alternative form of primal-dual relationship for sequence data. Its
215   data-dependent projection is uniformly sampled from sequences under an inductive bias assumption
      that sequences are ordered, which is natural to sequences but not graphs. Sequences are inherently

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      ordered, and thus such sub-sequences contain semantic information from the original sequence. In
217   contrast, for graph data, their structure is dictated by the edges, and the arrangement or ordering
218   of nodes is not explicitly specified, rendering this method unsuitable for graph data. Moreover, its
219   data-dependent projection is integrated into the kernel trick as a data-adaptive weight, incapable of
220   altering the space where potential outputs may lie. In contrast, our data-adaptive basis aggregates
221   graph information in the form of virtual nodes and directly influences the basis of outputs, as shown
222   in equation (2.8), potentially enhancing the model’s capacity.
223   Model architecture. The Transformer layer consists of two core components: the self-attention
224   module and the feed-forward module which is applied token-wise (Vaswani et al., 2017). In this
225   paper, we consider GPS, a powerful GT architecture that merges the MPNN and Transformer layers
226   (Rampasek et al., 2022). We replace the self-attention module in the Transformer layer with our pri-
227   mal representation and name our method Primphormer. Illustrations of Primphormer’s architecture
228   are shown in Figure 1, with detailed algorithms presented in Appendix D.
229   Complexity analysis. The primal representation is a more user-friendly approach in terms of
230   both time and memory costs. The dual representation requires O(N 2 s) time complexity and
231   O(N 2 + N s) memory complexity. In contrast, the primal representation only requires O(N ps)
232   time complexity and O(2Ns s + 2N p) memory complexity with Ns ≪ N making an efficient
233   self-attention mechanism feasible. The final output is obtained by concatenating two projection
234   scores o(x) := [e(x); r(x)]. To align with the user-dependent dimension do , a compatibility ma-
235   trix Wc ∈ Rdo ×2s can be further applied to the output score.

      3.1   Z ERO - VALUED OBJECTIVE
243
244   In the implementation of Primphormer, our goal is to reach the KKT point. Theorem 1 establishes
245   that when the KKT conditions are met, the dual representation of Primphormer aligns with the
246   standard self-attention formulation. However, solving the linear system (2.6) in the dual space in-
247   troduces a cubic computational complexity. To efficiently approach the KKT points, we introduce
248   the following theorem,
249   Theorem 2 (Zero-valued objective with stationary solutions). The solutions of He , Hr , Σ in the
250   dual space (2.6) lead to a zero-valued objective J in the primal space (2.5).
251
252   The essence of Theorem 2 lies in the necessity for the primal objective value to be zero under
253   the KKT conditions, suggesting an alternative optimization approach instead of solving the dual
254   problem. Therefore, we implement Primphormer by jointly minimizing an additional loss towards
255   zero as follows,

412   5     R ELATED W ORK
413
414   Graph Transformers. Transformers have demonstrated success in natural language processing
415   (Vaswani et al., 2017) and computer vision tasks (Liu et al., 2021). Recently, researchers have
416   explored the application of Transformers in graph representation learning to address issues such as
417
      over-smoothing (Nguyen et al., 2023) and over-squashing (Giraldo et al., 2023) observed in MPNNs.
      Graph Transformers operate on a fully connected graph where nodes are pairwise connected, en-
418
      coding the original graph structure into positional encodings. Spectral Attention Networks (SAN)
419
      (Kreuzer et al., 2021) introduce conditional attention for both real and virtual edges and implement
420   Laplacian positional encoding for nodes. Graphormer (Ying et al., 2021) and GraphiT (Mialon et al.,
421   2021) incorporate relative positional encodings based on pairwise graph distances and diffusion ker-
422   nels, respectively. GPS proposes a framework that combines MPNNs with attention mechanisms
423   (Rampasek et al., 2022).
424
      The quadratic complexity in traditional GTs has motivated the development of computationally ef-
425
      ficient attention mechanisms. Nodeformer (Wu et al., 2022) utilizes the kernelized Gumbel softmax
426   operator to facilitate information propagation between all pairs of nodes efficiently. Difformer (Wu
427   et al., 2023) introduces a diffusion-based Transformer model with linear complexity, although their
428   attention mechanisms are limited to nodes in randomly sampled mini-batches. Another strategy is
429   the sparse Transformer, which enhances computational efficiency by restricting node interactions.
430   Exphormer (Shirzad et al., 2023) limits interactions across real and expander edges, achieving linear
431   complexity to the number of nodes and edges. However, the efficiency of Exphormer diminishes as
      graphs become denser. A survey on efficient Transformers is given by Fournier et al. (2023).

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      Primal-dual relationship. The quadratic complexity also arises in kernel machines in duality and
433   can be circumvented by transferring a dual problem to its primal form. Models such as the support
434   vector machine (Cortes & Vapnik, 1995), least squares support vector machine (Suykens & Vande-
435   walle, 1999), and kernel principal component analysis (Mika et al., 1999) exhibit this characteristic.
436   The associated pair-wise kernels are symmetric and positive-definite, whereas attention scores are
437   inherently asymmetric, violating the Mercer condition (Mercer, 1909). Recent research has explored
438   a new primal-dual perspective to accommodate such asymmetry in kernel machines. To incorporate
439   asymmetric kernel functions, Lin et al. (2022) propose an asymmetric kernel trick from a pair of
440   RKBSs. He et al. (2023b) convert an asymmetric kernel to a complex-valued Hermitian function
441
      by the magnetic transform. Suykens (2016) introduces a novel variational principle to dissect the
      primal-dual relationship concerning the singular value decomposition of an asymmetric kernel ma-
442
      trix, a concept further extended to classification tasks by He et al. (2023a). This variational principle
443
      is also leveraged by Chen et al. (2023) to interpret attention mechanisms in sequences. However, due
444   to the distinctions between sequences and graphs, this model is unsuitable for graph-based learning.

447   6   C ONCLUSION
448
449
      In this paper, we propose Primphormer, a new framework for graph Transformers. Primphormer
      models the self-attention mechanism on graphs in the primal space, avoiding pair-wise computa-
450
      tions, which enables an efficient variant of graph Transformers. Our primal-dual analysis shows
451
      that Primphormer can be implemented by introducing an additional primal objective loss. Due to
452   its efficiency in both runtime and memory storage, Primphormer has the potential to support larger
453   and deeper neural networks and enable larger batch sizes, enhancing model capacity and general-
454   ization ability. Primphormer also benefits from the universal approximation property for functions
455   on both sequences and graphs, potentially possessing strong generalization capabilities to unseen
456   data or tasks. Experimental results on various graph benchmarks demonstrate the effectiveness and
457   efficiency of the proposed Primphormer.
458   An interesting avenue for future work is exploring how edge features can be incorporated into Prim-
459   phormer’s structure. Edge features can be added to attention scores in an entry-wise manner as
460   data-adaptive kernels (Liu et al., 2020). Exploring the primal representation of these kernels allows
461   us to incorporate edge information into attention mechanisms, potentially resulting in a stronger
462   GT. Additionally, fine-tuning schemes like LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) are promising for large models.
463   Studying LoRA from a primal-dual perspective may lead to more efficient fine-tuning methods. Al-
464   though experimentally evaluating the universal approximation property poses challenges, it is crucial
465   and valuable for our theoretical foundations. We can begin by manually designing graph-to-graph
466
      functions and then study validation errors concerning hidden dimensions and sample size. For tasks
      that focus on short-range interactions, the data-dependent projection could be further adjusted to
467
      better aggregate local information.

      A    DATA D ESCRIPTIONS
652
653
      Here, we introduce the datasets in the experiments. A summary of the dataset statistics is shown in
      Tab. A1.
654
655   CIFAR10 and MNIST. CIFAR10 and MNIST are the graph equivalents of the image classification
656   datasets of the same name. A graph is created by constructing the 8-nearest neighbor graph of the
657   SLIC superpixels of the image. These are both 10-class graph classification problems (Dwivedi
658   et al., 2023).
659   PascalVOC-SP and COCO-SP. These are similar graph versions of image datasets, but they are
660   larger images and the task is to perform node classification, i.e., semantic segmentation of super-
661   pixels. These graphs are larger, and the tasks are more complex than CIFAR10 and MNIST (Dwivedi
662   et al., 2022a).
663   CLUSTER and PATTERN. PATTERN and CLUSTER are node classification problems. Both
664   are synthetic datasets that are sampled from a Stochastic Block Model (SBM), is a popular way to
665   model communities. In PATTERN, the prediction task is to identify if a node belongs to one of the
666   100 possible predetermined sub-graph patterns. In CLUSTER, the goal is to classify nodes into six
667   different clusters with the same distribution (Dwivedi et al., 2023).
668
      MalNet-Tiny. Malnet-Tiny is a smaller dataset generated from a larger dataset for identifying mal-
669   ware based on function call graphs from Android APKs. The tiny dataset contains 5000 graphs,
670   each with up to 5000 nodes. The task is to predict the graph as being benign or from one of four

1029   Algorithm 1 PyTorch-like Pseudo-Code for Primphormer Module.
1030
       import torch
1031   import torch.nn as nn
1032   import torch.nn.functional as F
       from torch_geometric.nn import global_mean_pool
1033   from torch_geometric.utils import to_dense_batch
1034
       class Primphormer(nn.Module):
1035      def __init__(self, in_dim, out_dim, n_heads, Ns, low_rank):
1036         super().__init__()
             self.d_keys = out_dim // n_heads # key dimension.
1037         self.q_proj = nn.Linear(in_dim, out_dim) # query
1038         self.k_proj = nn.Linear(in_dim, out_dim) # key
             self.vn_proj = nn.Linear(in_dim, out_dim) # virtual node
1039         self.n_heads = n_heads
1040
              self.We = nn.Parameter(nn.init.orthogonal_(torch.Tensor(Ns, n_heads, self.d_keys)))
1041          self.Wr = nn.Parameter(nn.init.orthogonal_(torch.Tensor(Ns, n_heads, self.d_keys)))
1042          self.Lambda = nn.Parameter(nn.init.uniform_(torch.Tensor(n_heads, low_rank)))
              self.concate_weight = nn.Linear(2*low_rank, self.d_keys)
1043
1044       def feature_map(self, Q, K):
              Q = F.normalized(Q, p=2, dim=-1)
1045          K = F.normalized(K, p=2, dim=-1)
1046          return Q, K

1047       def propagate_vn(self, batch, h):
1048          h = self.vn_proj(h)
              h_vn = global_mean_pool(h, batch.batch).unsqueeze(1) # aggregate by the virtual node.
1049          fx = h_vn + batch.fx # update f_X by the virtual node.
1050          return fx

1051       def forward(self, batch):
1052          x = batch.x
              x_dense, mask = to_dense_batch(x, batch.batch)
1053          B, M = mask.shape # batch, maximal #nodes
1054          fx = self.propagate_vn(batch, x)
              Q = self.q_proj(x_dense).view(B, M, self.n_heads, -1)
1055          K = self.k_proj(x_dense).view(B, M, self.n_heads, -1)
1056          Q, K = self.feature_map(Q, K)

1057          # compute data-dependent projections
1058          We_X = torch.einsum(’bdv,vhe->bdhe’, fx.transpose(2, 1), self.We)
              Wr_X = torch.einsum(’bdv,vhe->bdhe’, fx.transpose(2, 1), self.Wr)
1059
1060          # compute projection scores
              escore = torch.einsum(’bmhd,bhde->bmhe’, Q, We_X.permute(0, 2, 3, 1))[mask]
1061          rscore = torch.einsum(’bmhd,bhde->bmhe’, K, Wr_X.permute(0, 2, 3, 1))[mask]
1062
              score = torch.cat((escore, rscore), dim=-1)
1063          out = self.concate_weight(score).contiguous()
1064          out = out.view(-1, self.n_heads * self.d_keys) # final output
              batch.fx = fx #update for the next layer
1065
1066          loss_escore = (torch.einsum(’nhd,hd->nhd’, escore,
                   self.Lambda).norm(dim=-1,p=2)**2).mean() / 2
1067          loss_rscore = (torch.einsum(’nhd,hd->nhd’, rscore,
1068               self.Lambda).norm(dim=-1,p=2)**2).mean() / 2
              loss_trace = torch.einsum(’dhe,ehk->dhk’, self.We.permute(2, 1, 0),
1069               self.Wr).mean(dim=1).trace()
1070          loss_svd = (loss_escore + loss_rscore - loss_trace) ** 2

1071          return out, loss_svd
