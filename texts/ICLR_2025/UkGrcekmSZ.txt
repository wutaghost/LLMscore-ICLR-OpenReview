011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The nightmare of cancer as a leading cause of premature deaths worldwide is be-
014
               coming real and turns out to be one of the major problems of humanity nowadays.
               Cancer diagnostics at the early stage is Critical to cancer recovery and survival. In
015
               this context, renal diseases, including kidney cysts, stones, and tumors, pose sig-
016
               nificant global health challenges, affecting approximately 12% of the population
017            and contributing to chronic kidney disease (CKD). Notably, renal cancer ranks
018            as the tenth most prevalent cancer type, accounting for 2.7% of all cancer cases.
019            This work presents a deep learning (DL) framework utilizing transfer learning
020            (TL) for the early detection of renal diseases and categorizing the conditions into
021            four binary classifications: Cyst vs Normal, Cyst vs Stone, Cyst vs Tumor, and
022            Stone vs Tumor, allowing for a more specific understanding of each stage. By
023            analyzing CT scans and microscopic histopathology images, the framework em-
024            ploys convolutional neural networks (CNNs) with pre-trained models to facilitate
025
               automatic and precise classification of renal conditions. Specifically, two CNN
               models ResNet-50 and EfficientNetV2 are implemented, providing a comprehen-
026
               sive analysis of each stage of the DL architecture. Comparative evaluations of
027
               training outcomes across various datasets revealed that EfficientNetV2 performed
028            marginally better than ResNet-50, achieving an impressive testing accuracy of up
029            to 100% for all cases. These results underscore the effectiveness of the DL-based
030            system and highlight its potential for widespread clinical application in renal dis-
031            ease diagnosis.
032            Keywords- CNN, kidney, image classification, deep learning, transfer learning

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Renal organs are vital, bean-shaped located below the rib cage that filters blood, removes waste, and
038   balances fluids and electrolytes, with each kidney containing about a million nephrons Raghavendra
039
      & Vidya (2013). Additionally, they control blood pressure, stimulate red blood cell production
      through hormone secretion, and maintain overall homeostasis, making their proper function essential
040
      for health. Renal cancer originates in the kidneys when malignant cells form in the tubules, often
041
      requiring treatments like immunotherapy Navani & Heng (2023). Early detection and advanced
042   medical interventions can significantly improve outcomes for affected individuals Jacobson (2013).
043   Kidney cancer is a growing public health concern, in the year 2022, it ranked as the 14th most
044   common cancer and the 16th leading cause of cancer-related death globally Can (2022) to the 5th
045   by 2040 Foreman et al. (2018). Kidney diseases commonly consist of cysts, stones, and renal cell
046   carcinoma (RCC) Hsieh et al. (2017), while nephrolithiasis affects approximately 12% of the global
047   population Alelign & Petros (2018). Despite control efforts, the prevalence of these conditions
048   continues to increase, highlighting the need for enhanced medical interventions and public health
049   strategies Hsieh et al. (2017). Computed tomography (CT) scans are particularly effective for kidney
050
      examinations, offering three-dimensional, cross-sectional images ideal for identifying abnormalities
      like cysts, stones, and tumors Sagel et al. (1977).
051
052   It is challenging for doctors to clinically determine invasive cancer by identifying the captured im-
053   ages, and it needs to administer safe and expensive treatments for the patients Wang et al. (2020).
      Therefore, tracking the growth of every stage is essential to develop customized medications based

054
      on a patient’s disease profile Hsieh et al. (2017). There is a significant global shortage of nephrol-
055   ogists and radiologists, particularly in Asia, where there is only about one nephrologist for every
056   million people, in contrast to Europe, which has approximately 25.3 nephrologists per million Sozio
057   et al. (2021). Given the widespread impact of kidney diseases and the scarcity of specialists, devel-
058   oping DL-based models to assist in detecting kidney abnormalities has become essential Bi et al.
059   (2022). Recent advancements in DL-based models for vision tasks offer promising solutions to
060   support doctors and alleviate patient suffering.

065   for detection across the four stages of renal disease. Finally, Section 4 presents concluding remarks
066   and outlines potential pathways for future works.

      CNNs are a powerful DL algorithm commonly used for classifying grid-like data, such as images Si-
074   monyan & Zisserman (2015); Szegedy et al. (2014); He et al. (2015a); Tan & Le (2020). Specifically,
075   renal US images are enhanced using median and Gaussian filtering techniques and morphological
076   operations Verma et al. (2017). Relevant features from images are extracted using various unsu-
077   pervised techniques and classified using supervised algorithms. In Aksakalli et al. (2021), authors
078   demonstrated a range of traditional supervised machine learning algorithms, including decision tree
079   (DT), random forests (RF), K-nearest neighbors (KNN), and multilayer perceptron (MLP), as well as
080   CNN. They achieved the best F1 score of 0.853 with those methods. In Sudharson & Kokil (2020),
081   they employed pre-trained CNN models like ResNet-101, MobileNet-v2, and ShuffleNet to extract
082
      features from kidney US images, got an accuracy of 95.58% using support vector machine (SVM).
      In Fu et al. (2021) residual dual-attention (RDA) module utilized for segmenting kidney cysts from
083
      the CT images. In Zheng et al. (2019), combined features extracted through TL approaches, which
084
      were subsequently utilized to distinguish between affected and non-affected ultrasound images by
085   SVM classifier. In Parakh et al. (2019), two consecutive CNN models were employed: first CNN
086   identified the urinary region, and second CNN detected the existence of stones, both achieved an ac-
087   curacy of 95%. In Yildirim et al. (2021) introduced an automated method for the detection of kidney
088   stones using coronal CT images and DL techniques, achieving an accuracy of 96.82%. In Blau et al.
089   (2018), researchers developed a system to detect kidney cysts in the images of abdominal CT scans
090   by utilizing a fully connected CNN. They reported an 84.3% true-positive rate (TPR) for their ap-
091   proach. In recent studies on kidney disease detection from CT images, the EANet, ResNet50, and a
092   customized CNN model achieved accuracies of 83.65%, 87.92%, and 98.66%, respectively Hossain
093
      et al. (2023). In summary, initiatives employing ML and DL approaches to classify kidney-related
      radiological findings have demonstrated encouraging outcomes, primarily concentrating on CT and
094
      US images.
095
096   The rapid advancement of CNNs has led to the development and utilization of various DL architec-
097   tures Khan et al. (2020), including EfficientNet Tan & Le (2020) a highly efficient CNN by Google
098   AI that balances depth, width, and resolution for superior performance and reduced computational
099
      cost in image classification tasks. After that, it was updated utilizing sophisticated DL methods like
      the fused mobile inverted bottleneck (Fused-MBConv) operation to produce even better performance
100
      and was named EfficientNetV2 Tan & Le (2021). While ResNet-50 CNN model has been granted
101
      as a promising CNN model for image classification task He et al. (2015b). This study utilized the
102   EfficientNetV2 architecture and contrasted its performance with ResNet-50 for the classification of
103   four different stages of kidney cancer image dataset “CT KIDNEY DATASET” Kid (2022). This
104   study subdivided into four binary classifications: Cyst vs Normal, Cyst vs Stone, Cyst vs Tumor,
105   and Stone vs Tumor, aiming to distinguish between specific kidney conditions and streamline the
106   diagnostic process. Each of them trained to differentiate between them, allowing for more focused
107   and precise detection of kidney abnormalities. Those CNN models demonstrate high accuracy across
      all conditions, highlighting their potential for kidney cancer diagnosis and prediction.

      3.2   I NITIALIZATION OF WEIGHTS
183
      Weight initialization is a critical factor influencing the efficiency and effectiveness of NN train-
184   ing Vedanshu & Tripathi (2018). When weights are initialized to zero, a symmetry problem arises
185   during gradient descent, causing neurons within a layer to learn identical features and hindering
186   effective learning. To address this, specialized initialization methods like Xavier Glorot & Bengio
187   (2010) and He He et al. (2015c) initialization are utilized, each tailored to different activation func-
188   tions. Xavier initialization, also known as Glorot initialization Glorot & Bengio (2010), is designed
189   for layers using activation functions such as tanh and sigmoid. This method initializes weights using
190   a uniform distribution:
191
                                                             1      1
                                            WXav = U −     √     , √                                       (1)
192                                                           m      m
193   Where the number of input neurons is represented by m. Xavier initialization helps maintain the
194   variance of activations and gradients throughout the network layers Ramachandran et al. (2017).
195   This promotes smoother convergence during gradient descent, preventing the gradients from van-
196   ishing or exploding, which can be crucial for deep networks. For activation functions like ReLU
197   or SiLU Agarap (2019), Xavier initialization can lead to vanishing gradients, particularly for deep
198
      networks. He initialization, also known as Kaiming initialization, addresses this by using a Gaussian
      distribution for weight initialization:
199
200
                                                           r !
                                                               2
201                                           WHe = N 0,                                                (2)
                                                               m
202
203
      where N (0, σ 2 ) denotes a normal distribution with mean 0 and σ 2 He et al. (2015c). This ap-
204   proach scales the weights to ensure that the variance of the activations remains consistent, which
205   is particularly important for ReLU and similar activation functions that can otherwise suffer from
206   sparse gradients. Modern NN architectures such as ResNet-50 and EfficientNetV2 predominantly
207   use ReLU or SiLU activation functions, as a result, creators of those models used He initialization
208   in these layers He et al. (2015b). Consequently, these models typically employ He initialization
209   to ensure effective training. ResNet-50, leverages residual connections that help mitigate the van-
210   ishing gradient problem, and He initialization further complements this by maintaining appropriate
211   gradient scales. EfficientNetV2, emphasizing optimization accuracy and computational efficiency,
212
      similarly benefits from He initialization to achieve robust performance.
213   TL Zhuang et al. (2020) is a powerful technique that significantly enhances training efficiency, es-
214   pecially when data is limited. It involves taking a pre-trained model, often trained on a large dataset
215   like ImageNet, and fine-tuning it for a different but related task. By initializing the model with
      pre-trained weights, such as those from ImageNet Abadi et al. (2016b), TL allows the model to


                                                         4

216
      leverage previously learned features and patterns. This provides a strong starting point and leads to
217   more effective training with less data Abadi et al. (2016b). For example, when fine-tuning ResNet-
218   50 or EfficientNetV2 for a new task, initializing with pre-trained ImageNet weights enables faster
219   convergence and often results in better performance than training from scratch.

327   Now for the second model, EfficientNetV2 represents cutting-edge supervised tasks, developed by
328
      Google Brain in 2021 Tan & Le (2021). This model leverages the method, known as neural ar-
      chitecture search (NAS) Tan et al. (2019), which systematically explores various ML architectures
329
      to identify the most effective design by sampling different configurations and evaluating their per-
330
      formance. EfficientNetV2 builds upon its predecessor, EfficientNetV1, by optimizing speed during
331   training and incorporating a valuable operation, Fused-MBConv at the earlier layers. Unlike the
332   original EfficientNetV1, which employs depthwise convolutions, the Fused-MBConv layer utilizes
333   standard 3 × 3 convolutions, as illustrated in Figure 4 (a). Additionally, EfficientNetV2 incorporated
334   strategies from several prior studies to enhance training efficiency keeping the number of parameters
335   at manageable levels. One such technique is progressively adaptive regularization by learning, pro-
336   viding a regularized framework that aligns with the image resolution during training Sandler et al.
337   (2019). This approach gradually adjusts the regularization and image size, starting with lower val-
338   ues in the early epochs and increasing them in later stages. Although progressive learning was not
339
      implemented here, the TL methods reduced the number of parameters and allowed for a significant
      reduction in training time approximately less than the ResNet-50 model. The principal components
340
      of the EfficientNetV2 architecture include MBConv and Fused-MBConv layers, which are reused
341
      multiple times throughout the model. As depicted in Figure 4 (a), the architecture is divided into
342   segments for feature extraction and binary classification similar to ResNet-50. The feature extractor
343   begins with a stem that includes a standard convolution layer, followed by six distinct blocks that
344   consist of several repetitions of Fused-MBConv and MBConv layers. After collecting feature tensors
345   of size 7 × 7 × 1280, global average pooling is applied Lin et al. (2014), resulting in a single value
346   per channel. Then passing through three layers (two ReLU activation and one sigmoid activation)
347   output is obtained.
348   The training and validation process results, shown in Figure 4 (b) demonstrate the model accuracy
349   and loss over each category across the epochs. The Cyst vs Normal dataset is shown at the top,
350   followed by Cyst vs Stone and Cyst vs Tumor in the middle, and Stone vs Tumor at the bottom.
351   Similar to ResNet-50 the most effective epoch is identified throughout the training process, by im-
352   plementing a TensorFlow checkpoint Abadi et al. (2016b). As a result, the best weights are retained
353   corresponding to the best validation accuracy. While the training time of EfficientNetV2 is much
354   faster than the ResNet-50. Overall, the EfficientNetV2 model demonstrates comparable stability in
355   accuracy and loss to the ResNet-50 model. Training with EfficientNetV2 was approximately faster
356
      than ResNet-50, thanks to optimizations introduced through neural architecture search (NAS).

358   3.4   M ODEL PERFORMANCE
359
360   The performance of the ResNet-50 model across four binary classifications demonstrates its high
361   reliability in medical image classification [see Figure 5]. The model achieves perfect or near-perfect
362   accuracy in all four conditions, with minimal misclassifications, highlighting its robust capability in
363   distinguishing between the conditions. The Cyst vs Tumor task shows a slight decrease in perfor-
364   mance compared to the others, reflecting the inherent challenge in differentiating these two classes.
365   Precision remains consistently at 100% for all tasks, indicating the absence of false positives, while
366   recall is also perfect except for a minor reduction in the Cyst vs Tumor task. These results estab-
367   lish ResNet-50 as a highly effective tool for binary classification in medical imaging, capable of
368
      delivering reliable predictions with minimal errors across diverse categories.
369   In contrast, the EfficientNetV2 model demonstrates strong performance across the four binary clas-
370   sification tasks [see Figure 6]. It achieves perfect accuracy for all tasks and near-perfect accuracy in
371   the Cyst vs Stone. Precision remains consistently high across all of the classifications, with only a
372   slight drop in the Cyst vs Stone. Similarly, recall is perfect across all classifications. These results
373   underscore the model’s reliability and effectiveness in medical image classification, with minimal
374
      errors, making it well-suited for clinical applications.
375   Both models exhibit similarly high performance across all four binary classification tasks, but Ef-
376   ficientNetV2 shows a slight advantage in the Cyst vs Tumor, where it achieves flawless classifica-
377   tion, unlike ResNet-50, which had minor misclassifications. However, EfficientNetV2 experienced a
      small dip in precision during the Cyst vs Stone task, where ResNet-50 maintained perfect precision.

      4   C ONCLUSION
467
      The DL frameworks presented in this study effectively address the critical need for early detection of
468
      renal diseases. By leveraging TL and advanced CNNs system achieved remarkable testing accuracy
469
      of up to 100% across multiple classifications. These findings not only demonstrate the potential for
470   precise and automatic classification of renal conditions but also highlight the framework’s applica-
471   bility in clinical settings. By improving early diagnosis, this approach could lead to better patient
472   management, reduced rates of chronic kidney disease, and ultimately improved survival rates in
473   renal cancer. However, future work is needed to inflate the robustness and generalizability of the
474   model. This includes expanding the dataset to encompass a wider variety of demographic and clini-
475   cal variables, integrating multimodal imaging techniques, and conducting prospective clinical trials
476   to validate performance in real-world scenarios. Additionally, further research could focus on the
477   interpretability of the model’s predictions to support clinicians in decision-making processes.
