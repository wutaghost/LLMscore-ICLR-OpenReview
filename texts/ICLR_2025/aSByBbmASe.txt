009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            3D geometric shape completion hinges on representation learning and a deep
012            understanding of geometric data. Without profound insights into the three-
013            dimensional nature of the data, this task remains unattainable. Our work addresses
014            this challenge of 3D shape completion given partial observations by proposing
015            a transformer operating on the latent space representing Signed Distance Fields
016
               (SDFs). Instead of a monolithic volume, the SDF of an object is partitioned into
               smaller high-resolution patches leading to a sequence of latent codes. The ap-
017
               proach relies on a smooth latent space encoding learned via a variational autoen-
018
               coder (VAE), trained on millions of 3D patches. We employ an efficient masked
019            autoencoder transformer to complete partial sequences into comprehensive shapes
020            in latent space. Our approach is extensively evaluated on partial observations from
021            ShapeNet and the ABC dataset where only fractions of the objects are given. The
022            proposed POC-SLT architecture compares favorably with several baseline state-
023            of-the-art methods, demonstrating a significant improvement in 3D shape comple-
024            tion, both qualitatively and quantitatively.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      The 3D measurements from scanners or sensors can be used to assemble virtual objects or entire
029
      worlds. This can be used not only for visualizations but also for reasoning about the world, e.g., in
030   self-driving cars. In general, with optical sensors, it is only possible to measure 3D surfaces that are
031   visible to the sensor from within its range of motion (if any). Even after combining all observations
032   taken from different angles, gaps remain in the observed surface. One can usually not observe an
033   object from all directions, e.g., from below the surface it is standing on. Similarly, one might only
034   be able to take pictures from the front of an object but may not be able to peek behind it.
035
      In this work, we present a new method for partial object completion using a transformer in the style
036   of Masked Auto Encoders (MAE) (He et al., 2022) operating in latent SDF space. For a unknown
037   or masked regions, it is supposed to fill in a plausible shape that connects well with the given
038   geometry. The method consumes and produces shapes represented as Signed Distance Fields (SDF)
039   on volumetric patches. Similar to Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022), the incomplete shapes
040   are first encoded into a smooth, compressed latent space representation using our dedicated Patch
041   Variational Autoencoder (P-VAE). For high-resolution models, the entire shape is represented as a
042   sequence of latent codes generated by the P-VAE on volumetric patches. Completion is performed
043   in latent space and the completed shape is decoded back to an SDF using the decoder of the P-VAE
044
      on each patch. Note that our SDF-Latent-Transformer is a Masked Autoencoder. It therefore solves
      the task directly in a single inference step without a transformer decoder.
045
046   Shape completion requires a strong prior for real-world shapes in order to estimate the most likely
047   shapes for incomplete inputs. We trained our P-VAE and SDF-Latent-Transformer on ShapeNet-
048   CoreV1 (Chang et al., 2015) with all 55 categories. We further fine-tuned the transformer on a
049   subset of the ABC dataset (Koch et al., 2019) with 100K meshes.
050   By thoroughly evaluating our methods on shape completion tasks, we demonstrate superior quality
051   compared to state of the art. Our key contributions to the field are: i) A comprehensive Patch
052   Variational Autoencoder (P-VAE) to compress SDF shapes into sequences of codes in a smooth
053   latent space. ii) A SDF-Latent-Transformer trained as Masked Autoencoder completes input shapes
      within milliseconds in a single inference step. iii) Upon acceptance, we will release code and model

      We review related point-based, occupancy-based, and SDF-based 3D completion methods and meth-

      ods inspiring our deep-learning architecture.

069
070   Point-based Shape Completion. 3D Shape completion tackles the task of reconstructing 3D ob-
071   jects from partial observations in the form of 2D images, sometimes with depth information, or 3D
072   point clouds. Areas occluded during capture need to be filled in to form a complete object.
073   PointNet and its variations (Qi et al., 2017a;b) have pioneered the direct processing of 3D coor-
074   dinates with neural networks, catalyzing research in numerous downstream tasks, including point
075   cloud completion, as for example PCN (Yuan et al., 2018). PCN uses PointNet in an encoder-
076   decoder framework and integrates a FoldingNet (Yang et al., 2018) to transpose 2D points onto a 3D
077   surface by simulating the deformation of a 2D plane. Following PCN, a plethora of other methods
078   have emerged (Tchapmi et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2020; Xie et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2019), aiming
079
      to enhance point cloud completion with higher resolution and increased robustness.
080   PointTR (Yu et al., 2021) was one of the first approaches which used transformers (Vaswani et al.,
081   2017) for point cloud completion. They derive the point proxies from a fixed count of furthest
082   point sampled representatives, which are turned into a feature vector using a DGCNN (Wang et al.,
083   2019). Completion is done by a geometry-aware transformer, which is queried for missing point
084   proxies. The missing points are extracted from the predicted proxies using a FoldingNet (Yang et al.,
085
      2018). The completed points are finally merged with the input point cloud. AdaPointTR (Yu et al.,
      2023) addresses discontinuity issues and is more robust to noisy input. SeedFormer (Zhou et al.,
086
      2022) completes a sparse set of patch seeds from an incomplete input and upsamples the seeds to a
087
      complete point cloud. The seeds are sparse 3D positions enriched by a transformer with semantic
088   information about the local neighborhood. SnowFlakeNet (Xiang et al., 2021) solves this decoding
089   task with a SkipTransformer-based architecture, which repeatedly splits and refines low-resolution
090   points. LAKe-Net (Tang et al., 2022) first localizes aligned keypoints to generate a surface skeleton
091   mesh which aids in producing a complete point cloud. AnchorFormer (Chen et al., 2023) extracts
092   global features and predicts key ”anchor” points which are combined with a subset of input points
093   and then upsampled into a dense mesh. VRPCN (Pan et al., 2021) models the shape from the partial
094   input as probability distributions, which are sampled and then refined by a hierarchical encoder-
095   decoder network. This is related to our approach as we also use a Variational Auto Encoder (VAE)
096
      to compress the shape in latent space. LION (Zeng et al., 2022) introduced a flexible latent diffusion
      model for point clouds also using a VAE to map point cloud features into latent space. The features
097
      and diffusion model are constructed from Point-Voxel CNNs.
098
099
100   Occupancy-based Shape Completion. Occupancy Networks (Mescheder et al., 2019) introduced
101   a functional, implicit representation of 3D shapes via their occupancy. These methods can be queried
102   for arbitrary 3D locations and will return whether the queried position is inside or outside of the
103   represented object. Meshes can quickly be recovered from this representation using the MISE al-
104
      gorithm (Mescheder et al., 2019). Peng et al. (2020) extend this to a convolutional method and
      represent entire scenes by storing and interpolating latent representations in 2D/3D grids. Chibane
105
      et al. (2020) combine local and global information using multi-scale feature grids in IF-Nets.
106
107   These methods focus on identifying and representing 3D objects seen in images and adding detail
      to uniformly low-resolution point cloud and voxel input.

108
      ShapeFormer (Yan et al., 2022) compactly encodes shapes into a sparse grid of vector-quantized
109   deep implicit functions (VQDIF). Partial inputs are completed by an autoregessive transformer op-
110   erating on a sequence of location and patch index pairs. 3DILG (Zhang et al., 2022) uses an irregu-
111   lar grid of latent codes for shape representation and completes partial inputs with an autoregressive
112   transformer. 3DShape2VecSet (Zhang et al., 2023) derives multiple global vectors for shape repre-
113   sentation using cross-attention between input points and sampled query points. The (variationally)
114   auto-encoded shape is then completed using latent-space diffusion. The output occupancy is queried
115   by cross-attention between a dense point cloud and the global vectors.
116   The input to these methods is typically a point cloud generated from a single camera view. Occluded
117   parts need to be inferred from the partial point cloud. In many cases, the input still covers large parts
118   of the object, exposing global information about the shape, while our method completes objects
119   from a small subset of complete patches.

123   SDF-based Shape Completion. SDFs represent 3D shapes implicitly as continuous 3D scalar
124   functions which describe the distance of any 3D point to the closest surface. The surface is given by
125   the zero-level-set while in- or outside areas are identified by the sign. This ensures that objects rep-
126   resented by SDFs are always watertight. In contrast, meshes are often non-manifold since they are
127   commonly created to ”look right” on screen, rather than to represent the volume of solid geometry.
128   While SDFs could be modeled in any functional basis, we use regular 3D grids.
129   There are only few approaches related to shape completion on SDFs in the literature. For SDF
130   representation and class-related shape generation, DeepSDF (Park et al., 2019) proposes to use an
131   AutoDecoder (Tan & Mavrovouniotis, 1995) to jointly optimize a compressed latent representation
132   of an SDF and the decoder to extract the full SDF from the latent representations. Due to the
133   fully connected layer, the approach has limited resolution. Our approach instead splits the SDF
134   into patches, which are encoded with a rigorously trained VAE (Kingma & Welling, 2013). Those
135
      patches can be assembled to very highly detailed shapes. We experimented with using a similar
      AutoDecoder optimization scheme to refine latent codes for our frozen decoder but found that the
136
      optimized latent codes complicate subsequent shape completion tasks (Appendix A.1).
137
138   To represent entire scenes, LIG (Jiang et al., 2020) autoencodes TSDF patches and operates on
139   oriented points during test time. They divide the scene into multi-scale patch-grids and use trilinear
140   interpolation to produce seamless results. Similar to their work, we divide objects into patches and
141
      also found medium patch-sizes to be most effective: Patches need to be small enough to generalize
      across objects but large enough to represent interesting geometric features.
142
143   AutoSDF and SDFusion (Mittal et al., 2022; Cheng et al., 2023) focus on multi-modal shape gener-
144   ation and completion in latent space, e.g. from an input image, point clouds, or depth maps. Both
145   approaches use Vector Quantized Variational Auto Encoders (VQ-VAEs) (van den Oord et al., 2017;
146   Razavi et al., 2019) to patchwise compress SDF shapes to a compact latent space. SDFusion then
147
      uses a latent diffusion model, while AutoSDF employs an autoregressive transformer to generate
      or complete shapes sequentially. AutoSDF (Mittal et al., 2022) is in many regards similar to our
148
      approach. The main differences are: i) the choice of a VAE over a VQ-VAE to avoid quantization
149
      errors. ii) Larger patches with 323 grid points over the 83 grid points used by AutoSDF. iii) Our
150   transformer is trained as Masked Auto Encoder (MAE) (He et al., 2022) while the AutoSDF Trans-
151   former is trained autoregressively. This drastically changes how the model can be used as our ap-
152   proach does not require a sequential generation. iv) Our approach is trained on SDF grids with a
153   resolution of 1283 and trained on the full ShapeNetCoreV1 (Chang et al., 2015) and refined on the
154   ABC datasets (Koch et al., 2019) instead of the SDF resolution of 643 on a 13-category subset of
155   SapeNetCoreV1 for AutoSDF.
156   PatchComplete (Rao et al., 2022) and DiffComplete (Chu et al., 2023) both complete 323 Trun-
157   cated SDF input using multi-scale features. While PatchComplete uses an attention mechanism,
158   DiffComplete uses diffusion and also allows combination of multiple inputs. Both methods learn
159   strong priors that generalize well to new inputs, albeit at comparably low resolution. While our
160   method works on a subset of clean SDF patches to extend partial geometry, these methods work on
161   the 3D-EPN (Dai et al., 2017) dataset, containing noisy, low-resolution partial SDFs generated from
      a single camera view.

      Variational Autoencoders (VAE) (Kingma & Welling, 2013) are networks that learn latent rep-
163   resentations of input data. Unlike standard autoencoders, they learn a probability distribution over
164   the latent space. This generates a smooth latent space which allows manipulation and computations
165   in latent space. Inspired by Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022), we train a VAE to encode the
166   323 SDF patches into a smooth latent space. We call this network Patch Variational Autoencoder
167   (P-VAE). A full 1283 SDF grid is therefore represented by only 64 latent tokens. This makes any
168   further processing very efficient and scalable.
169
170   Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) are a network architecture for sequence-to-sequence tasks.
171   It uses attention as the core mechanism to identify pairwise relationships between elements in a se-
172
      quence. They have revolutionized Natural Language Processing and are the foundation of all current
      Large Language Models. Transformers can however process any sequence of data. In Dosovitskiy
173
      et al. (2020), images are split into a sequence of patches, which is then processed by transformers.
174
      The approach in He et al. (2022) masks out some of those patches and train to fill in the masked-
175   out patches. Similarly, we split the SDF grid into patches and encode them into a sequence of
176   latent codes. We mask out parts of the latent codes that contain unknown geometry and use our
177   SDF-Latent-Transformer to complete the missing patches.

198   Our POC-SLT shape completion pipeline consists of four main steps which are depicted in Figure 1:
199
      First, the input 1283 SDF grid is split into 4×4×4 patches of size 323 1 . Each of those patches is
      encoded into a latent vector using the P-VAE 2 . The latent codes z that form the input shape are then
200
      assembled in a sequence, which is masked and processed by the SDF-Latent-Transformer (SLT) 3 .
201
      Finally, the resulting sequence is decoded patch-by-patch with the decoder from the P-VAE into a
202   completed SDF 4 . We will now elaborate on these four steps.

204
      3.1   P-VAE
205
206   The task for the P-VAE is to encode 323 -SDF grid patches (pi )i=0,...,n into latent code vectors
207   (zi )i=0,...,n . For details about the architecture and implementation of our P-VAE, refer to Ap-
208   pendix D. The P-VAE consists of an encoder EVAE and a decoder DVAE , which are both based
209   on 3D-convolutional layers. A patch pi is encoded by the encoder into mean µi and variance σi2
210   of a Gaussian distribution. During training, this distribution is sampled to obtain the latent code
211   zi ∼ N (µi , σi2 ), which is then decoded by the Decoder DVAE into an SDF patch p̃i = DVAE (zi ).
212
      We use the mean absolute error between pi and p̃i as reconstruction loss. To regularize the latent
      space of the VAE, we follow the KL-regularized VAE implementation from Stable Diffusion (Rom-
213
      bach et al., 2022). During inference, we directly use the means zi = µi without additional sampling.
214
215   The P-VAE is trained on SDF patches extracted randomly from parts of the meshes of ShapeNet-
      CoreV1 (Chang et al., 2015). Each patch has a fixed SDF resolution of 323 . We however strongly

246   3.2            SDF-L ATENT-T RANSFORMER
247
248   The actual shape completion happens completely in latent space. Our approach is visualized on the
249   right in Figure 2. The incomplete input shape is provided as a sequence of latent space vectors.
250
      We employ a standard transformer encoder as a Masked Auto Encoder to fill in missing latent vec-
      tors. The missing input parts are marked by a special trainable mask token (blue in the figure). The
251
      positions of all vectors, including masked ones, are provided to the transformer as 3D positional
252
      encoding (Sitzmann et al., 2020). Linear layers before and after concatenating the positional em-
253   bedding have empirically shown to improve convergence. Finally, the transformer completes the
254   sequence.
255
256
      Outside SDF Patches. For many, especially anisotropic shapes, some of the encoded SDF-Patches
257
      will not contain any geometry either because they are completely inside or outside an object. Note
258
      however, that those patches still contain valid distance values towards surfaces that lie outside those
259   patches. We therefore do not differentiate between patches that explicitly contain surfaces and those
260   that lie outside.
261
262
      Masking during Training. The training strategy of the SDF-Latent-Transformer is visualized
263
      in Figure 2. For creating the mask for a given training example, we randomly choose from one
264   of the following masking strategies: 1. Random Masking, 2. Octant, 3. Half, 4. Slice. With
265   Random Masking, each patch is masked out with a 40% probability. With Octant, all but one of the
266   octants of the patch-grid are masked out. With Half, one half of the patch-grid is masked out. With
267   Slice, everything but one axis-aligned slice through the patch-grid is masked out. The four different
268   masking schemes are visualized in the same order from top to bottom on the right side of Figure 2.
269   We use Random Masking with a probability of 35% and all the other strategies with a probability of
      21.7% each.

294   of the P-VAE which encodes SDF patches into latent space and the SDF-Latent-Transformer which
295   fills in missing patches in latent space. To demonstrate the effectiveness of our method, we measure
296   its performance on various completion tasks and compare it to related work below. Additional tasks
297
      are demonstrated in Appendix A. All metrics used in the experiments are defined in Appendix B.
      Data preparation for turning meshes into SDFs is described in Appendix C.

334   4.1   C OMPLETION
335
336   The SDF-Latent-Transformer is the core component of our completion pipeline. It receives an in-
337   complete sequence of SDF patches in latent space and completes it to a full sequence of latent codes
338   in a single forward step. The latent codes of the completed sequence are independently decoded into
339   SDF patches with the P-VAE decoder. The patches are trivially assembled into a high-resolution
340
      shape volume from which we extract surface meshes using Marching Cubes (Lorensen & Cline,
      1987). We trained two slightly different variants of the SDF-Latent-Transformer: SLT: trained on
341
      ShapeNetCoreV1 with latent codes z from P-VAE as ground truth and SLT ABC: SLT fine-tuned on
342
      a subset of the ABC dataset. The quality of our shape completion approach is evaluated on unseen
343   objects from ShapeNetCoreV1 (Chang et al., 2015) and ABC (Koch et al., 2019).
344
345   Qualitative Results (ShapeNet). Examples for the previously described bottom half (Half) com-
346   pletion task on ShapeNetCoreV1 (Chang et al., 2015) are shown in Figure 3. Here, we compare
347   our SLT against AutoSDF (Mittal et al., 2022) and AnchorFormer (Chen et al., 2023) as recent
348   state-of-the-art methods.
349
      Our POC-SLT pipeline generates highly plausible shapes. Both AutoSDF (Mittal et al., 2022) and
350   AnchorFormer (Chen et al., 2023) struggle to fill in the missing parts. Compared to AutoSDF, our
351   method works at a significantly higher resolution and can handle all ShapeNet classes. The higher
352   resolution is for example important for the fine structures on the bench table in column four or
353   the faucet in column two. AutoSDF (Mittal et al., 2022) completely fails to reconstruct the car in
354   column one or the armchair in column three, while our SLT on the same input produces correct
355   results. AnchorFormer (Chen et al., 2023) often produces sparse and simplistic completions for
356   inputs with unknown top halves which is especially visible for the faucet in column two. However,
357   the sparseness and therefore lack of detail are also visible in the car in the first column, the armchair
358
      in the second, and the cupboard in the last column.

507   5    L IMITATIONS
508
509   While the presented results are of high quality, some downstream tasks for the POC-SLT pipeline
510   might need additional work. At the moment, it is trained assuming a fixed bounding box. This is
511
      a valid assumption for most objects but might be too limiting for open scenes. In principle, the
      transformer should be able to cope with sequences of basically arbitrary length. However, it still
512
      needs to be investigated if the current model can deal with higher-resolution spatial encodings to
513
      make use of additional tokens.
514
515   The completion is performed for masked patches. Implicitly, the approach always assumes either
516   completely given SDF patches or completely unknown patches. In completion applications for
517
      images, depth maps, or partial 3D scans, one might additionally need to indicate that the patch
      information itself might be incomplete, e.g. a missing occluded surface in the same cell.
518
519   We do not consider the single-view 3D reconstruction task. Previous work (Tatarchenko et al., 2019)
520   has shown that predicting object-centered results heavily relies on identifying (and augmenting)
521   similar objects in the training set and can even be outperformed by object retrieval. Lacking a single
522   global object representation, our method is not well-suited for such a classification task. Instead,
523
      one could first estimate a canonical pose, monocular depth, and then compute 3D SDF patches from
      the then recovered partial geometry in order to meaningfully perform geometric 3D reconstruction
524
      with our method. Evaluation of such an approach would highly depend on the models employed for
525
      pose estimation and depth estimation in addition to our model, preventing meaningful comparisons.
526
527
528   6    C ONCLUSION
529
530   With POC-SLT, we proposed an accurate and efficient new method for SDF shape completion in
531
      latent space. POC-SLT processes SDFs in patches of fixed size. Two main components are used
      to refine and fill in missing patches in a shape. Firstly, an extensively trained Patch Variational
532
      Autoencoder (P-VAE) for accurately compressing the patches into a sequence of latent codes and
533
      back. Secondly, an SDF-Latent-Transformer (SLT) which completes and refines the latent sequence
534   of an incomplete shape in a single inference step. We demonstrate that our approach produces highly
535   accurate and plausible 3D shape completions, outperforming prior works. POC-SLT is trained on
536   ShapeNetCoreV1 (Chang et al., 2015), is class agnostic, and can easily be adapted to new datasets,
537   which we demonstrated with the ABC (Koch et al., 2019) dataset. We show that the P-VAE works
538   across different datasets, even without additional training, and hope that it will be a helpful tool
539   for future research. Extending the approach to deal with partial information within patches and
      developing applications like real-time scan completions are left open for future work.
