      1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038

      The linear bandit (LB) problem is a framework in decision theory and machine learning designed to
      address real-world scenarios with large, and potentially uncountable, decision sets (Abbasi-Yadkori
040
      et al., 2011; Russo & Van Roy, 2014; Soare et al., 2014). In this setting, the expected reward for an
041
      action (or “arm”) is modeled as the inner product between a feature vector and an unknown parame-
042   ter. To maximize cumulative reward over a sequence of trials, an agent must balance two competing
043   objectives: exploration, where actions are chosen to estimate this unknown parameter, and exploita-
044   tion, where the agent uses the estimation to select actions that yield high rewards. Striking the right
045   balance between exploration and exploitation is key to optimizing rewards over time.
046
      Many real-world applications impose strict limitations that require instantaneous hard constraints
047
      to be satisfied at every time step Shi et al. (2023). For instance, in resource allocation, resource
048   constraints must be met in real-time to avoid stockouts or logistical failures. Similarly, in AI-driven
049   medical treatments, decisions must consistently prioritize safety (Xiong et al., 2024; Vamvoudakis
050   et al., 2021; Thomas et al., 2019). This work aims to address the problem of LB under instantaneous
051   hard constraints, specifically in non-convex and discrete feature spaces. Earlier studies, such as
052   Amani et al. (2019); Moradipari et al. (2021); Pacchiano et al. (2024), have shown that near-optimal
053   performance can be achieved in linear bandits with instantaneous hard constraints in convex or star-
      convex feature spaces respectively. In these approaches, the agent initially constructs a conservative


                                                        1

054
      estimated safe set and begins interacting with the environment by sampling from this set. It then
055   gradually expands the estimated safe set toward the true safe set as it gathers more experience.
056
057   One might ask why we are interested in problems with non-convex and discrete feature spaces. The
058
      reason is that many real-world applications inherently involve structures that convex and star-convex
      assumptions fail to capture. Applications such as financial management, recommendation systems,
059
      and medical treatment selection often involve action sets that are neither convex nor star-convex,
060
      but rather composed of discrete or separated subsets (see Section 5.2). Additionally, non-convexity
061   frequently arises in modern machine learning problems due to the use of function approximators
062   with non-linear feature functions, such as Deep Neural Networks (DNNs), Radial Basis Functions
063   (RBFs), and Fourier basis features (Sutton & Barto, 2018; Zhu et al., 2023; Mnih et al., 2016;
064   Kalashnikov et al., 2018). In DNNs, for example, non-convex activation functions like ReLU, Sig-
065   moid, and Tanh contribute to the overall non-convexity of the feature space.
066
      A question arises: can the same conservative strategy used in convex and star-convex settings, as
067   discussed in Amani et al. (2021); Pacchiano et al. (2024), be directly applied to our case? The answer
068   is no, as applying this approach in non-convex spaces is not straightforward. In fact, conservative
069   strategies in such settings may introduce a bias toward suboptimal directions, leading to linear regret,
070   as shown in Fig. 1a. (See Section 6 for a complete description of the simulation.) We refer to this
071   issue as non-convexity bias.

091   To understand non-convexity bias, consider the following example: a LB problem with an action
092   set A = {a1 , a2 , a3 , a4 } where a3 is the optimal action as illustrated in Fig. 2a. Assume that the
093   agent initially knows the actions in the rectangle R are safe, specifically {a1 , a2 }, are safe but cannot
094   verify the safety of {a3 , a4 }.
095
      The core idea of the conservative strategy in LB, as proposed by Amani et al. (2021); Pacchiano
096   et al. (2024) is that the agent can gather noisy information about the cost of a3 by playing action
097   a1 , as both lie along the same direction, i.e., the x-axis. Similarly, the agent can estimate the cost of
098   a4 by playing action a2 , as both are aligned along the y-axis. Thus, a UCB-based bonus is used to
099   ensure the agent explores both the x- and y-directions by playing a1 and a2 enough times, eventually
100   expanding the safe set to include the optimal action a3 .
101
      In Amani et al. (2021); Pacchiano et al. (2024), the bonus term for a1 is designed based on the
102   distance between a3 and the current safe set’s boundary along the x-axis. In the context of our
103   problem, the bonus term in Amani et al. (2021); Pacchiano et al. (2024) is calculated based on the
104   distance between a3 and the rectangle R, i.e., d1 , mistakenly assuming a1 lies on R. However, as
105   depicted in Fig. 2a, a1 is far from R, and the distance between a1 and a3 , i.e., d2 , is significantly
106   larger than d1 . As a result, the bonus for a1 is not large enough to incentivize the agent to play
107   a1 , potentially biasing it toward playing a2 instead. Consequently, the agent may not explore the
      x-direction sufficiently to estimate the cost of a3 and verify its safety, leading to linear regret.

109   or star-convex settings, as these structures always ensure that d1 = d2 since all the points connecting
110   a1 and a3 are in the action set. For a more detailed discussion on this bias, refer to Section 5.2.

      linear bandit problems with instantaneous hard constraints in non-star-convex and discrete spaces.
113

114
      of a known, fixed feature mapping ϕ(a), where ϕ : A → Rd (Amani et al., 2019; Pacchiano et al.,
115   2024; Moradipari et al., 2021). We summarize our main contributions below:

254   Real-World Implications of Assumption 3. Consider an investment problem where a venture
255
      capitalist (VC) needs to decide how to allocate its funds. Suppose the VC can invest at different
      levels in a company and must determine how to hedge the associated risks. If the VC makes a small
256
      investment, it risks losing only a small amount of money if the company goes bankrupt. However,
257
      if the company does very well, the VC only owns a small portion, so the reward is also limited. The
258   reverse is true if the VC makes a large investment. The VC could hedge its bets by initially making
259   several small investments in different startups to gather information on how these investments per-
260   form. Once the VC identifies a promising startup, it can then take on more risk by making a larger
261   investment in that company. In this context, the smaller, safer investments represent the ϵ-condition
262   in Assumption 3, meaning they are small and close to the origin (a safe point). On the other hand,
263   the larger, higher-risk investments in the profitable startup corresponds to the ι-condition, which is
264   further from the origin, closer to the safety threshold, but with the potential for higher returns as its
265   closer to the optimal decision(See Fig. 2b). For more example please see Appendix C

519   7       C ONCLUSION
520
521   In this paper, we developed an algorithm to address the challenges of non-convex spaces in linear
522   bandits with instantaneous hard constraints. Unlike previous works that relied on convexity and
523   star-convexity, we demonstrated that local assumptions around the starting safe point and the op-
524   timal point are sufficient for near-optimal performance. We provided an upper bound that nearly
525   matches the regret bounds under star-convexity and convexity assumptions, and a lower bound that
526   highlights the necessity of parameters related to local assumptions in the upper bound. Additionally,
527   our method also captures discrete cases with finite action spaces. An interesting area for future work
528
      is the adaptation of our approach to gradient-based methods. This adjustment is crucial, as the max-
      imization step in non-convex scenarios often becomes intractable in non-convex continuous cases.
529
      Therefore, analyzing the impact of the non-convex optimization step on convergence is an essential
530
      next step.
