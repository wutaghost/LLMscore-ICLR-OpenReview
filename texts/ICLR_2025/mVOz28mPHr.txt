                                                 A BSTRACT

 1            Volume parameterizations abound in recent literature, from the classic voxel grid
 2            to the implicit neural representation and everything in between. While implicit
 3            representations have shown impressive capacity and better memory efficiency
 4            compared to voxel grids, to date they require training via nonconvex optimization.
 5            This nonconvex training process can be slow to converge and sensitive to initial-
 6            ization and hyperparameter choices. We introduce a family of models, GA-Planes,
 7            that is the first class of implicit neural volume representations that can be trained
 8            by convex optimization. GA-Planes models include any combination of features
 9            stored in tensor basis elements, followed by a neural feature decoder. They gen-
10            eralize many existing representations and can be adapted for convex, semiconvex,
11            or nonconvex training as needed for different inverse problems. In the 2D setting,
12            we prove that GA-Planes is equivalent to a low-rank plus low-resolution matrix
13            factorization; we show that this approximation outperforms the classic low-rank
14            plus sparse decomposition for fitting a natural image. In 3D, we demonstrate
15            GA-Planes’ competitive performance in terms of expressiveness, model size, and
16            optimizability across three volume fitting tasks: radiance field reconstruction, 3D
17            segmentation, and video segmentation.


18   1   I NTRODUCTION

19   Volumes are everywhere—from the world we live in to the videos we watch to the organs and
20   tissues inside our bodies. In recent years tremendous progress has been made in modeling these
21   volumes using measurements and computation (Tewari et al., 2022), to make them accessible for
22   downstream tasks in applications including manufacturing (Intwala & Magikar, 2016; Šlapak et al.,
23   2024), robotic navigation (Ming et al., 2024; Wijayathunga et al., 2023), entertainment and culture
24   (Liu et al., 2024; Croce et al., 2023), and medicine (Udupa & Herman, 1999; Masero et al., 2002;
25   Richter et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024). All methods that seek to model a volume face a three-way
26   tradeoff between model size, which determines hardware memory requirements, expressiveness,
27   which determines how faithfully the model can represent the underlying volume, and optimizability,
28   which captures how quickly and reliably the model can learn the volume from measurements. Cer-
29   tain applications place stricter requirements on model size (e.g. for deployment on mobile or edge
30   devices), expressiveness (e.g. resolution required for medical diagnosis or safe robotic navigation),
31   or optimizability (e.g. for interactive applications), but all stand to benefit from improvements to
32   this three-way pareto frontier.
33   Many existing strategies have been successfully applied at different points along this pareto frontier;
34   some representative examples from computer vision are summarized in Appendix A.1. Our goal is
35   to maintain or surpass the existing pareto frontier of model size and expressiveness while improving
36   optimization stability through convex optimization.
37   Our approach introduces convex and semiconvex reformulations of the volume modeling optimiza-
38   tion process that apply to a broad class of volume models we call Geometric Algebra Planes, or
39   GA-Planes for short. We adopt the term semiconvex for Burer-Monteiro (BM) factorizations of a
40   convex objective, as introduced in Sahiner et al. (2024), within the context of convex neural net-
41   works. BM factorized problems have the property that every local minimum is globally optimal
42   (Sahiner et al., 2024).

43   GA-Planes is a mixture-of-primitives model that generalizes several existing volume models includ-
44   ing voxels and tensor factorizations. Most importantly, most models in this family can be formulated
45   for optimization by a convex program, as long as the objective function (to fit measurements of the
46   volume) is convex. At the same time, any GA-Planes model can also be formulated for nonconvex
47   optimization towards any objective, matching the range of applicability enjoyed by common mod-
48   els. While only our convex and semiconvex models come with guarantees of convergence to global
49   optimality, all the models we introduce extend the pareto frontier of model size, expressiveness, and
50   optimizability on diverse tasks.
51   Concretely, we make the following contributions:
52          • We introduce GA-Planes, a mixture-of-primitives volume parameterization inspired by ge-
53            ometric algebra basis elements. GA-Planes combines any subset of line, plane, and volume
54            features at different resolutions, with an MLP decoder. This GA-Planes family of parame-
55            terizations generalizes many existing volume and radiance field models.
56          • We derive convex and semiconvex reformulations of the GA-Planes training process for
57            certain tasks and a large subset of the GA-Planes model family, to ensure our model opti-
58            mizes globally regardless of initialization.
59          • We analyze GA-Planes in the 2D setting and show equivalence to a low-rank plus low-
60            resolution matrix approximation whose expressiveness can be directly controlled by design
61            choices. We demonstrate that this matrix decomposition is expressive for natural images,
62            outperforming the classic low-rank plus sparse approximation.
63          • We demonstrate convex, semiconvex, and nonconvex GA-Planes’ high performance in
64            terms of memory, expressiveness, and optimizability across three volume-fitting tasks: 3D
65            radiance field reconstruction, 3D segmentation, and video segmentation.

66   2   R ELATED W ORK
67   Volume parameterization. Many volume parameterizations have been proposed and enjoy
68   widespread use across diverse applications. Here we give an overview of representative methods
69   used in computer vision, focusing on methods that parameterize an entire volume (rather than e.g. a
70   surface). These parameterizations achieve different tradeoffs between memory usage, representation
71   quality, and ease of optimization; richer descriptions are provided in Appendix A.1.
72   Coordinate MLPs like NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2020) and Scene Representation Networks (Sitz-
73   mann et al., 2019b) are representative of Implicit Neural Representations (INRs), which excel at
74   reducing model size (with decent expressiveness) but suffer from slow optimization. At the oppo-
75   site end of the spectrum, explicit voxel grid representations like Plenoxels (Sara Fridovich-Keil and
76   Alex Yu et al., 2022) and Direct Voxel Grid Optimization (Sun et al., 2022) can optimize quickly but
77   require large model size to achieve good expressiveness (resolution). Many other methods (Chen
78   et al., 2022; Fridovich-Keil et al., 2023; Müller et al., 2022; Kerbl et al., 2023; Reiser et al., 2023;
79   Lombardi et al., 2021) find their niche somewhere in between, achieving tractable model size, good
80   expressiveness, and reasonably fast optimization time in exchange for some increased sensitivity (to
81   initialization, randomness, and prior knowledge) in the optimization process. GA-Planes matches or
82   exceeds the performance of strong baselines (Chen et al., 2022; Fridovich-Keil et al., 2023; Barron
83   et al., 2021) in terms of model size and expressiveness, while introducing the option to train by
84   convex or semiconvex optimization with guaranteed convergence to global optimality.

85   Radiance field modeling. Most of the works described above are designed for the task of model-
86   ing a radiance field, in which the training measurements consist of color photographs from known
87   camera poses. The goal is then to faithfully model the optical density and view-dependent color of
88   light inside a volume so that unseen views can by rendered accurately. This task is also referred to as
89   novel view synthesis (Mildenhall et al., 2020; Sitzmann et al., 2019b). Although we do demonstrate
90   superior performance of GA-Planes in this setting, we note that the volumetric rendering formula
91   used in radiance field modeling (Max, 1995; Kajiya, 1986; Mildenhall et al., 2020) yields a noncon-
92   vex photometric loss function, regardless of model parameterization.

93   3D segmentation. We test our convex and semiconvex GA-Planes parameterizations on fully con-
94   vex objectives, namely volume (xyz) segmentation with either indirect 2D tomographic supervision

103   Convex neural networks. Recent work has exposed an equivalence between training a shallow
104   (Pilanci & Ergen, 2020) or deep (Ergen & Pilanci, 2024) neural network and solving a convex pro-
105   gram whose structure is defined by the architecture and parameter dimensions of the corresponding
106   neural network. The key idea behind this convexification procedure is to enumerate (or randomly
107   sample from) the possible activation paths through the neural network, and then treat these paths as
108   a fixed dictionary whose coefficients may be optimized according to a convex program.

109   Geometric (Clifford) algebra. Geometric algebra (GA) is a powerful framework for modeling
110   geometric primitives and interactions between them (Dorst et al., 2009). The fundamental entity in
111   GA is the multivector, which is a sum of vectors, bivectors, trivectors, etc. In 3D GA, an example is
112   the multivector e1 e2 + e1 e2 e3 , representing the sum of a bivector (a plane) and a trivector (a vol-
113   ume). The geometric product in GA allows us to derive a volume element by multiplying a plane and
114   a line, e.g. (e1 e2 )e3 = e1 e2 e3 . We use the shorthand e123 = e1 e2 e3 , and similarly for other multi-
115   vector components throughout. Inspired by this framework, we define the GA-Planes model family
116   to include any volume parameterization that combines any subset (including the complete subset
117   and the empty subset) of the linear geometric primitives {e1 , e2 , e3 }, planar geometric primitives
118   {e12 , e13 , e23 }, and/or volumetric primitive {e123 } with a (potentially convexified) MLP feature
119   decoder. We leverage geometric algebra to combine these primitives into a trivector (volume). To
120   our knowledge, this work is the first to use geometric algebra in neural volume models.

188   Notation. As usual, we use ◦ to denote elementwise multiplication and ⊙ to denote concatenation.
189   We use 1a×b to denote the all-ones matrix of size a × b and 1[·] to denote the indicator function,
190   which evaluates to 1 when its argument is positive and 0 otherwise. Our theorem statements consider
191   equivalence to a matrix completion problem with target matrix M ∈ Rm×n and low-rank compo-
192   nents U ∈ Rm×k , V ∈ Rn×k to be optimized. We include theoretical results for 2D GA-Planes

196   deferred to Appendix A.2.

197   Assumptions. Our theorem statements assume that the line feature grids have the same spatial
198   resolution as the target matrix, and thus do not specify the type of interpolation. However, the results
199   hold even if the dimensions do not match and nearest neighbor interpolation is used; the empirical
200   performance is similar or even slightly improved in practice by using (bi)linear interpolation of
201   features (see Appendix A.3 and A.2 for a discussion of other interpolation methods). The theorems
202   assume that the optimization objective is to minimize the Frobenius norm of the error matrix; this

204   objective function is the one we use for our convex experiments (video and volume segmentation

277   4.2   I NTERPRETATION : L OW R ANK + L OW R ESOLUTION

278   Combining multiple parameterization strategies with complementary representation capacities is a
279   time-honored strategy in signal processing. A classic example is the combination of sparse and
280   low-rank models used to represent matrices in the compressive sensing literature (Chandrasekaran
281   et al., 2009). As shown in Theorem 2, we can view the GA-Planes family as following a similar
282   strategy with a combination of low-rank and low-resolution approximations. This low-rank plus
283   low-resolution parameterization is generally easier to train, because sparse models must either store
284   large numbers of empty values (high memory) or store the locations of nonzero entries and suffer
285   from poorly-conditioned spatial gradients (difficult optimization). Indeed there are volume param-
286   eterizations that utilize sparsity, such as point clouds, surface meshes, surfels, and Gaussian splats,
287   but these tend to be more challenging to optimize (e.g. requiring high memory (Sara Fridovich-Keil
288   and Alex Yu et al., 2022) or heuristic updates and good initialization (Kerbl et al., 2023)).
289   We illustrate this low-rank plus low-resolution interpretation in Figure 2 with a simple experiment,
290   in which we approximate a grayscale image (the astronaut image from SciPy) using either a sum
291   of low-rank and low-resolution components (similar to the structure of GA-Planes) or the classic
292   sum of low-rank and sparse components. In this experiment we compute the optimal low-rank
293   components of each model type using the SVD, which corresponds to multiplication of features.

347   6            D ISCUSSION
348   In this work we introduce GA-Planes, a family of volume parameterizations that generalizes many
349   existing representations (see Appendix A.1). We specifically focus on two members of the GA-
350   Planes family (with concatenation versus multiplication of features), and offer both theoretical in-
351   terpretation and empirical evaluation of these models. Our nonconvex GA-Planes model shows
352   pareto-optimal performance in terms of model size and quality on fitting a 3D radiance field, while
353   our convex and semiconvex GA-Planes formulations are effective on several 3D linear inverse prob-
354   lems. In 2D, we show connections between GA-Planes and a low-rank plus low-resolution matrix
355   completion model, and derive how this model’s rank capacity is affected by various design decisions.
356   Limitations. Here we focus on 3D (or smaller) representations, rather than higher dimensions
357   (e.g. dynamic volumes), and we demonstrate GA-Planes for reconstruction rather than generation
358   tasks. Both of these extensions are promising avenues for extending GA-Planes. In our experiments,
359   we use the same first-order optimization algorithm for all models. However, our convex GA-Planes
360   formulation is compatible with any convex solver (e.g. cvxpy), and we expect its performance may
361   improve by leveraging these efficient convex optimization algorithms. We demonstrate preliminary
362   benefits of convexity and semiconvexity in terms of training stability in Appendix A.5.

572   represent different possible activation patterns of the hidden neurons as {Di }Pi=1 := {Diag(1[Xu ≥

575   the number of regions in the partitioned input space. Enumerating all such patterns would yield an
576   exact equivalence with the global minimizer of the nonconvex ReLU MLP in eq. (12), but may be
577   complicated or intractable due to memory limitations. Subsampling P̃ patterns results in a convex
578   program with tractable size, whose solution is one of the stationary points of the original non-convex
579   problem (Pilanci & Ergen, 2020). We apply this idea to create convex and semiconvex GA-Planes
580   models by convexifying the feature decoder MLP according to this procedure.
