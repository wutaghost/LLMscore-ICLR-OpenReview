009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The key issue of zero-shot image recognition (ZIR) is how to infer the relation-
012            ship between visual space and semantic space from seen classes, and then effec-
013            tively transfer the relationship to unseen classes. Recently, most methods have
014            focused on how to use images and class semantic vectors or class names to learn
015            the relationship between visual space and semantic space. The relationship es-
016
               tablished by these two methods is class-level and coarse-grained. The differences
               between images of the same class are ignored, which leads to insufficiently tight
017
               relationships and affects the accurate recognition of unseen classes.To tackle such
018
               problem, we propose Common Feature learning for Zero-shot Image Recognition
019            (CF-ZIR) method to learn fine-grained visual semantic relationships at the image-
020            level. Based on the inter class association information provided by class semantic
021            vectors, guide the extraction of common visual features between classes to obtain
022            image semantic vectors. Experiments on three widely used benchmark datasets
023            show the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      In recent years, the development of general artificial intelligence has been rapid, and as a key link,
028
      zero-shot learning has received widespread attention. The key problem of zero-shot learning is how
029
      to infer potential knowledge between visual space and semantic space from seen categories, and then
030   effectively transfer knowledge to unseen categories, finding corresponding semantic categories for
031   the visual features of unseen class images, and achieving accurate class prediction of unseen class
032   images.
033
      Among the existing two types of ZSL methods, the generative model based method Wu et al. (2020);
034
      Chen et al. (2021a) learns the mapping from semantic space to visual space to generate visual fea-
035
      tures of unseen categories, thereby transforming the ZSL task into a traditional image classification
036   task. This article believes that this method does not fundamentally solve the zero-shot problem and
037   requires a large amount of computational resources, which is inconsistent with the original intention
038   of the zero-shot problem. Another ZSL method, based on embedding methods Radford et al. (2021);
039   Chen et al. (2022b); Shen et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2022), typically learns a common representation
040   space between visual space and semantic space, where visual features and semantic vectors are pro-
041   jected onto the common representation space, enabling knowledge transfer from seen categories to
042   unseen categories. However, most of these embedding based methods rely on image visual features
043   and class semantic vectors to establish visual semantic connections, ignoring the fine-grained inter
044
      class association information provided by attributes.
045   It is worth noting that most methods focus on how to use images and class semantic vectors or class
046   names to learn the relationship between visual space and semantic space, and the relationships estab-
047   lished by these two methods are class level and coarse-grained. The differences between images of
048   the same class are ignored, which leads to insufficient closeness and affects the accurate recognition
049   of unseen classes.
050   Considering the above issues, this paper proposes a Common Feature learning for Zero-shot Image
051   Recognition (CF-ZIR) method, which guides the extraction of common visual features between
052   categories through attributes, and simulates expert scoring to obtain the degree to which an image
053   contains a certain attribute, thus forming an image semantic vector. Specifically, by constructing a
      visual attribute cross domain dictionary, guidance is provided for the extraction of visual common


                                                        1

054
      features by attributes. At the same time, the semantic vectors of images obtained based on common
055   visual features are constrained to be similar to the semantic vectors of their respective categories,
056   ensuring the effectiveness of common visual features. Finally, a fine-grained visual semantic cross
057   domain dictionary is constructed based on image visual features and image semantic vectors to better
058   capture the fine-grained associations between class independent visual and semantic information,
059   thus achieving high-precision zero-shot image classification tasks.
060
      Our contributions in this paper are summarized as follows:
061
062         • We propose the Common Feature learning for Zero-shot Image Recognition (CF-ZIR)
063           method, which breaks new ground by discerning fine-grained visual-semantic relationships
064           at the image level. This method leverages inter-class association information from class se-
065           mantic vectors to guide the extraction of common visual features, leading to more nuanced
066           image semantic vectors.
067         • CF-ZIR introduces a dual-layer embedding method, two layers of embeddings were estab-
068           lished between visual-attribute and visual-semantic, respectively.
069
            • A large number of experiments have been conducted to demonstrate that the CF-ZIR pro-
070
              posed in this chapter has achieved significant performance improvements on three bench-
071
              mark datasets.
072

075   benchmark datasets. The conclusion is given in Section 5.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
079
080   Zero-shot learning (ZSL) emerged from the challenge introduced by Larochelle et al. Larochelle
081
      et al. (2008), which questioned how to recognize images with limited labeled or unlabeled data.
      Lampert et al. Lampert et al. (2009) further propelled interest in ZSL within the image recogni-
082
      tion community by introducing the Animals with Attributes (AwA) dataset, built on the concept of
083
      utilizing unlabeled data.
084
085   ZSL diverges from conventional image recognition by enabling the identification of new class im-
086   ages not encountered during model training, thus offering potential for numerous practical appli-
087
      cations.Existing ZSL techniques can be broadly categorized into generative and embedding-based
      approaches.
088
089   Generative methods typically address ZSL by generating samples of unseen classes to train clas-
090   sifiers. Various methods leveraging Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) Goodfellow et al.
091   (2014) and other generative models have been proposed Xian et al. (2018); Ji et al. (2019); Han
092   et al. (2021); Wu et al. (2020); Zhao et al. (2022); Chen et al. (2021a); Radford et al. (2021). Xian et
093
      al. Xian et al. (2018) presented a conditional GAN-based Mirza & Osindero (2014) approach where
      the discriminator was trained with class attribute classification loss, and the generator employed
094
      class attributes to produce visual features. Ji et al. Ji et al. (2019) suggested a dictionary-based
095
      method to generate pseudo-images for unseen classes, learning a dictionary for each seen class and
096   generating pseudo-images for unseen classes by combining seen class dictionaries based on attribute
097   distances. Recent generative ZSL methods, such as SDGN Wu et al. (2020) and FREE Chen et al.
098   (2021a), focus on enhancing the discriminative power of generated visual features using constraints
099   like feature refinement and self-supervised learning. However, generative methods tend to be more
100   complex and computationally intensive than embedding-based approaches.
101
      Embedding-based methods Jiang et al. (2018); Chen et al. (2022b); Shen et al. (2022); Yang et al.
102   (2022); Wang et al. (2022) generally map visual features and semantic attributes into an embedding
103   space and use distance metrics to find the closest class attributes to unseen class images. Jiang et al.
104   Jiang et al. (2018) introduced CDL, an embedding-based method, which creates structured embed-
105   dings and aligns visual and semantic spaces by training a coupled dictionary with visual prototypes
106   and class attributes. Chen et al. Chen et al. (2022b) proposed a mutual semantic distillation network
107   that builds visual-semantic embeddings from regional visual features and attribute features. Shen et
      al. Shen et al. (2022) introduced a spherical ZSL method that measures similarities in a spherical


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      embedding space. Wang et al. Wang et al. (2022) incorporated local image information through a
109   fully pixel-to-attribute embedding approach.
110
111   However, existing embedding-based methods concentrate on leveraging images and class semantic
112
      vectors or class names to establish the correlation between the visual and semantic domains, inculd-
      ing CLIP Radford et al. (2021) et.al cross-modal large model. These approaches typically construct
113
      relationships at a class-level, which are broad and do not account for fine-grained details. As a result,
114
      they overlook the variations among images within the same class. This oversight can lead to a lack
115   of closeness in the learned relationships, thereby adversely impacting the precision of recognizing
116   classes that were not seen during the training phase.
117
118
      In this paper, we introduce the Common Feature learning for Zero-shot Image Recognition method,
      which delves into the fine-grained visual-semantic relationships at the image level. Utilizing inter-
119
      class association cues from class semantic vectors, CF-ZIR enhances the extraction of common
120
      visual features across classes, resulting in more discriminative image semantic vectors.

164   green solid line represents the model construction process in the visual semantic embedding stage,
165   and the gray dashed line represents the class prediction process for unseen class images.
166
      In the visual attribute embedding stage, seen class images are first subjected to a visual feature ex-
167
      traction network (e.g. Res Net101) to extract visual features. The dictionary trained based on the
168
      visual features of seen class images can serve as common visual features between categories. By
169   designing a visual attribute cross domain dictionary, the relationship between visual space and at-
170   tribute space is established, which constrains the expression of common visual features and attribute
171   features in the embedding space to be the same. In other words, common visual features are the
172   corresponding expressions of attribute features in the visual space. Therefore, the sparsity coeffi-
173   cient obtained from the reconstruction of the common visual feature dictionary atom for a seen class
174   image can describe the degree to which each attribute is included in the seen class image, that is, to
175   obtain the semantic vector of the seen class image. By constraining the semantic vector of the seen
176   class image to be similar to the semantic vector of the image’s class, the accuracy of the common
177
      visual feature dictionary is ensured.
178   In the visual semantic embedding stage, the relationship between visual space and semantic space is
179   established by constraining the seen class visual features and the seen class image semantic vectors
180   to have the same expression in the embedding space. This relationship is class independent, so it
181   can be generalized from the seen class to the unseen class, ensuring the model’s recognition ability
182   on the unseen class.
183   On the basis of the above two stages of model construction, there are three ways to predict the
184   categories of unseen class images, namely: recognition in visual space, recognition in embedding
185   space, and recognition in semantic space. Fig. 1 shows the process of semantic space recognition.
186   unseen class images obtain visual features through feature extraction networks, generate semantic
187   vectors of unseen class images through a visual semantic cross domain dictionary, and then find
188   the closest class to the unseen class image by calculating the distance between the image semantic
189
      vector and each unseen class semantic vector.

417   4.0% and 6.0% on AwA1 and AwA2 datasets, respectively.

      quality, which is conducive to the establishing of visual semantic alignment.

      This paper proposed a common feature learning method for zero-shot image recognition (CF-ZIR),
      innovatively segmented into visual attribute and visual semantic embedding stages. By extract-
459
      ing common visual features and achieving cross-domain alignment between visual and semantic
460
      spaces, CF-ZIR adeptly captures the subtleties essential for recognizing classes not encountered
461   during training. The method’s efficacy is underscored by its exceptional performance on three major
462   benchmark datasets, with ablation studies confirming the pivotal role of the discriminative term loss
463   and cross-domain alignment in bolstering recognition accuracy.
