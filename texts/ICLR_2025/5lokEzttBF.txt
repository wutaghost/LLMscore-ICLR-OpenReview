011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent studies have shown that maintaining a consistent response style by human
014            experts and enhancing data quality in training sets can significantly improve the
015            performance of fine-tuned Large Language Models (LLMs) while reducing the
016
               number of training examples needed. However, the precise definition of style and
               the relationship between style, data quality, and LLM performance remains unclear.
017
               This research identifies two key stylistic elements in responses: linguistic form and
018
               semantic surprisal. We find that, among training data of comparable quality, higher
019            consistency in these response elements leads to better LLM performance. Inspired
020            by this, we introduce Style Consistency-Aware Response Ranking (SCAR), which
021            automatically prioritizes instruction-response pairs in the training set based on their
022            response stylistic consistency. By selecting the most style-consistent examples,
023            sometimes as few as 0.7% of the full dataset, the fine-tuned LLMs can match or
024            even surpass the performance of models trained on the entire dataset in coding
025            and open-ended question-answering benchmarks. Code and data are available
026            at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/SCAR-0233/.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Instruction-following Large Language Models (LLMs), such as GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 (Achiam et al.,
032   2023), have transformed natural language processing by demonstrating remarkable generalization
033
      across a wide range of tasks (Brown et al., 2020; Chung et al., 2022; Ouyang et al., 2022). These
      models are typically trained through several stages: an initial phase of unsupervised pre-training on a
034
      vast corpus of text, followed by post-training stages, which include supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on a
035
      smaller dataset of instruction-response pairs and reinforcement learning (Bai et al., 2022).
036
037   Recent studies, such as AlpaGasus (Chen et al., 2024) and LIMA (Zhou et al., 2024), demonstrate that
038   carefully curated, smaller datasets can outperform larger ones in improving LLM SFT performance.
039
      AlpaGasus finds that smaller datasets with higher quality scores, rated by GPT-4 for helpfulness or
      correctness, outperform significantly larger ones when used to fine-tune high-capacity LLMs. The
040
      Superficial Alignment Hypothesis, proposed in LIMA, suggests that pre-trained language models
041
      already possess the necessary knowledge, and the primary goal of fine-tuning is to guide the model
042   toward adopting specific response styles, thus not requiring large amounts of data. LIMA achieves
043   notable performance with only 1,000 high-quality instruction-response pairs, optimized for consistent
044   style by human experts. However, this hypothesis raises three open questions: (i) What key elements
045   constitute response styles that impact LLM SFT? (ii) How do data quality (i.e., helpfulness, cor-
046   rectness) relate to style consistency in influencing efficient SFT? (iii) Can we develop an automatic
047   method that measures stylistic elements to curate smaller, stylistically consistent datasets for more
048   efficient SFT at a lower cost, without relying on human experts?
049   Text style is shaped by consistent choices across various linguistic elements (Kang & Hovy, 2021;
050   Karlgren, 2004), such as lexical, syntactic, and semantic features (DiMarco & Hirst, 1993). Our
051   empirical studies have identified two key stylistic factors within responses that significantly affect
052   LLM SFT performance: Linguistic Form and Semantic Surprisal. Linguistic Form mainly
053   involves lexical and syntactic choices that shape how a response is presented without altering its
      meaning. This includes tone (formal or informal), word choice, sentence structure, formatting (bullet

      points or headings), and transition words. Semantic Surprisal, in our definition, refers to the choices
055   of solutions, ideas, or approaches in a response that affects how predictably or unexpectedly it
056   addresses the instruction, focusing primarily on its semantic relationship to the instruction. We
057   find that when comparing instruction-response pairs with similar levels of data helpfulness and
058   correctness, greater consistency in both linguistic form and the semantic surprisal leads to notably
059   improved LLM performance on downstream tasks.
060
      Achieving style consistency is challenging, even for human experts. We discover that data with
061   LLM-generated responses exhibiting consistent styles and can significantly outperform human-
062   crowdsourced data in improving LLM performance. Inspired by this, we introduce Style Consistency-
063   Aware Response Ranking (SCAR), a novel ranking-based model that prioritizes instruction-response
064   pairs by ensuring their stylistic consistency while maintaining data quality. SCAR is trained on LLM-
065   synthesized and human-crowdsourced datasets to reward responses with higher style consistency
066   regarding linguistic form and surprisal. Enhanced with representation learning, SCAR can better
067   distinguish between these two elements and prioritize aspects that improve LLM performance.
068   Experiments show that by selecting the most style-consistent examples, sometimes as little as 0.7%
069
      of the original dataset, fine-tuned LLMs can match or surpass the performance of models trained on
      full datasets like Octocoder-15.5b (Muennighoff et al., 2023) and OLMO-7b-SFT (Groeneveld et al.,
070
      2024) on coding (HumanEval; Chen et al. 2021) and open-ended question answering (AlpacaEval;
071
      Dubois et al. 2023) benchmarks. SCAR outperforms leading data selection baselines for efficient
072   SFT, enhancing LLM performance while reducing computational costs.
073
074   In summary, our contributions are three-fold:
075          • We introduce and define key response style elements that influence LLM SFT performance.
076            Our empirical analysis shows that, for training datasets with similar correctness and helpful-
077            ness, greater consistency in linguistic form and semantic surprisal significantly enhances
078            LLM performance across various benchmarks.
079          • We present SCAR, a ranking method that learns distinct representations for linguistic form
080            and semantic surprisal, selecting style-consistent and high-quality examples for efficient
081            LLM SFT.
082          • Our extensive experiments demonstrate that SCAR outperforms data selection baselines,
083            enabling LLMs trained on a small fraction (e.g., 25%, 0.7%) of the original data selected by
084            SCAR to match or exceed the performance of models trained on the full dataset for coding
085            and open-ended tasks, significantly reducing computational costs.

089
090   In this section, we study two research questions: i) What key elements in response style can influence
091
      LLM SFT? and ii) How do style consistency and data quality impact LLM performance?
092
093   RQ1: W HAT FACTORS C ONSTITUTE R ESPONSE S TYLE
094
095   Through empirical analysis of stylistic differences between synthetically generated and human-written
096   instruction-tuning data, we identified two key sets of stylistic features in responses that significantly
097   influence LLM alignment performance.
098   Linguistic Form refers to the structure of language, including how words and sentences are organized
099   and interact (Fabb, 2001; Chomsky, 1957; Jurafsky, 2000). In our context, it denotes elements that
100   shape the presentation of a response, mostly independent of semantics, such as tone (formal or
101   informal), transitional word choice, sentence structure, formatting (bullet points or heading lines),
102   variable naming conventions, etc. For example, GPT-3.5 responses tend to be formal, structured
103   with bullet points, and frequently use transitional phrases. In contrast, human responses, authored by
104
      diverse individuals, typically exhibit greater variation in their use of these linguistic elements.
105   Semantic Surprisal refers to how predictable or surprising a response is in relation to the given
106   instruction, largely influenced by its semantic content, such as the choice of solutions, ideas, or
107   approaches. For example, when presented with questions about sorting algorithms, GPT-3.5 typically
      follows a consistent style, often suggesting less surprising solutions like merge sort. In contrast,

132   RQ2: I NFLUENCE OF S TYLE C ONSISTENCY AND DATA Q UALITY ON LLM P ERFORMANCE
133
134
      We collect both human-written and synthetic data in coding and general open-ended domains, and
      conduct stylometric and quality analyses on this data. Following this, we fine-tune base LLMs to
135
      explore the effects of style consistency and data quality on their performance.
136
137   We control style variations to create three dataset types—human-written, referenced, and direct—to
138   explore how linguistic form and response surprisal impact LLM performance. In the coding domain,
139   we collect 10,000 human-written instruction-response pairs from StackExchange1 , an online platform
140
      that includes 11 million pairs of coding questions and answers. We use the LIMA dataset, including
      1,000 human-generated examples, for the general domain. Additionally, we generate two synthetic
141
      response types with controlled styles: “referenced” and “direct.” “Referenced” responses are crafted
142
      by an instruction-tuned chat-LLM that rewrites human responses to retain their semantic meaning,
143   similar to the method in Yang et al. (2024). This approach largely preserves the surprisal level of
144   the human-written responses while altering their linguistic form. In contrast, chat-LLM creates
145   “direct” responses from scratch with a temperature of 0 after reading the instructions, potentially
146   producing different semantics and significantly varying their levels of predictability compared to
147   human responses. Table 1 illustrates these response types.
148
      We also isolate the effects of data quality on LLM performance by using three chat-LLMs with
149   different capabilities to generate synthetic “referenced” and “direct” datasets. The models employed
150   are GPT-3.5- TURBO, L LAMA -2-70 B - CHAT- HF, and L LAMA -2-13 B - CHAT- HF (Touvron et al.,
151   2023), with GPT-3.5- TURBO being the most advanced, followed by L LAMA -2-70 B - CHAT- HF
152   and L LAMA -2-13 B - CHAT- HF, according to the arena-leaderboard (Zheng et al., 2024). We find
153   hallucinations that occur during the LLM rewriting and generation of “referenced” and “direct”
154   responses can significantly affect the quality of the resulting synthetic data.

158   (MTLD) (McCarthy, 2005) for functional words, Flesch score (Kincaid et al., 1975), average sentence
159   length, and punctuation frequency. Higher TTR and MTLD values indicate greater lexical diversity,
160   while a higher Flesch score suggests improved readability. We identify functional words (yp )

246

                data significantly influence the performance of LLM SFT.
249           • The LLM-generated responses demonstrate higher style consistency than human responses,
250
                with “direct” responses showing the greatest consistency in linguistic form and semantic
                surprisal.

252
              • Both improved data quality and style consistency in a dataset enhance LLM SFT, and
                among datasets of similar quality, those with higher style consistency yield better LLM

                performance.

257
258   Inspired by the findings, we develop a Style Consistency-Aware Ranker to capture response differ-
259   ences in linguistic form and surprisal-determining features. This ranker selects training examples
260   with consistent response styles to enhance LLM SFT.

286   Our independence tests reveal that semantic content is more instruction-dependent than linguistic
287   form, motivating separate pathways for vp and vc . Prior work measures text surprisal via text–context
288   representation similarity (Michaelov et al., 2023; Karampiperis et al., 2014). Inspired by this, we
289   derive vc by concatenating the initial token representations of the instruction and response (Vx0 and
290   Vy0 ) and processing them through an MLP inspired by Relation Networks (Sung et al., 2018) to
291
      capture their semantic relationship. In contrast, vp is obtained by applying max pooling over the
      response sequence Vy to capture surface-level features. Sequence representations V are generated
292
      using an encoder such as RO BERTA - BASE (Liu et al., 2019). This separation also helps disentangle
293
      the two types of features, enabling better representation learning for distinct style elements.
294

296

297   similarities: the linguistic form of “referenced” responses is more similar to “direct” responses than

299   responses than to “direct” ones, as shown in Figure 1. We introduce a regularization term using triplet

312   style-consistent subset for fine-tuning pre-trained LLMs. This filtered dataset is expected to improve
313   the performance of fine-tuned LLMs on target tasks more than using the entire original dataset.

401
402   Effectiveness of SCAR-Selected Data. As in Figure 2, SCAR(ID) can enhance SFT performance
403   while lowering computational costs. LLMs fine-tuned on only 25% and 10% of SCAR(ID)-selected
404   data achieve comparable or superior performance to models trained on full datasets in coding and
405
      general domains, respectively.
406   SCAR(ID)-selected data consistently outperforms other baselines in fine-tuning LLMs, while some
407   baselines show unstable performance. S UPERFILTERING performs poorly in the coding domain. We
408   observe that it may assign high IFD scores to erroneous examples in crowdsourced coding data of
409   varying quality. P ERPLEXITY and A LPAG ASUS-selected data result in similar LLM performance
410   trends. However, their performance is inferior to SCAR(ID), which we attribute to their lack of
411
      style consistency. Traditional active learning methods like R ANDOM and D IVERSITY sampling
      are less effective for LLM fine-tuning. This is likely due to LLMs requiring less data diversity for
412
      effective fine-tuning, as evidenced by smaller datasets outperforming larger ones, and because our
413
      style-inconsistent target scenario inherently includes diversity. Surprisingly, HFR underperforms in
414   most scenarios, suggesting that training the ranker on inconsistent human preferences from diverse
415   authors may hinder its ability to select the most beneficial training data for LLMs.

      5               R ELATED W ORK
510
511   Instruction-Tuning Data Selection. Instruction-tuning trains LLMs to follow complex instructions
512   in various contexts (Wei et al., 2021; Sanh et al., 2021). Data are sourced from human-curated
513   examples (Wang et al., 2022b; Zhou et al., 2024) and LLM outputs (Xu et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
514   2022a). Studies (Zhou et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; 2023a; Lu et al., 2023; Liu
515   et al.) show that smaller, high-quality datasets can outperform significantly larger ones in boosting
516   LLM performance. LIMA uses expert human curation for stylistic consistency (Zhou et al., 2024),
517   while AlpaGasus (Chen et al., 2024) utilizes LLMs to assess data quality. Li et al. (2024; 2023a)
518
      apply Instruction Following Difficulty scores to identify effective training examples. Lu et al. (2023)
      enhances data diversity by tagging instructional elements while Bukharin & Zhao (2023) does so by
519
      measuring instruction embedding similarities.
520
521   Automatic Authorship Detection. Our method relates to authorship detection studies. Traditional
522   authorship detection used lexical features like TTR, MTLD, and Flesch readability scores (Tripto
523   et al., 2023; Zheng & Jin, 2023). Recent focus has shifted to distinguishing human and machine-
524
      generated texts using advanced neural networks to analyze styles at the corpus (Mitchell et al., 2023;
      Su et al., 2023) or the sentence levels (Zeng et al., 2024; 2023; Wang et al., 2023a; Zeng et al.).
525
      Recent studies (Xu & Sheng, 2024; Su et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a; Mitchell et al., 2023; Wu
526
      et al., 2023a) have demonstrated that perplexity can effectively differentiate between human and
527   machine-generated text styles. These findings further validate our choice of using perplexity for
528   stylometric analysis.

      6               C ONCLUSION
533
534   Our empirical study demonstrates that, among training datasets with comparable helpfulness and
535   correctness, those with higher consistency in linguistic form and semantic surprisal significantly
536   enhance the performance of fine-tuned LLMs. Building on this insight, we propose SCAR, a ranking
537   method designed to measure and select stylistically consistent training data for LLM fine-tuning. Our
538   experiments show that LLMs fine-tuned on small subsets of the original dataset—using as little as
539   0.7% of the data selected by SCAR—can outperform models trained on the full datasets. Moreover,
      SCAR consistently outperforms other data selection baselines in LLM fine-tuning.

962

964

               lexical diversity.
965
966         2. Measure of Textual Lexical Diversity (MTLD): MTLD is less sensitive to text length
967
               compared to TTR. It computes the average length of sequential word strings that maintain a
               given TTR value, where higher MTLD scores suggest greater lexical diversity.
968
969         3. Average Sentence Length (Avg. Sent. Len.): Calculates the average number of words per
970
               sentence, providing insights into the syntactic complexity of the text.
971         4. Punctuation Frequency (Punct. Freq.): Computes the frequency of punctuation marks
               within each response, reflecting the density of punctuation usage.

1071   A.8   I DENTIFICATION OF S EMANTIC AND N ON -S EMANTIC W ORDS
1072
1073   To distinguish between semantic content (yc ) and non-semantic (linguistic form-related) words (yp )
1074   in the responses, we adopt a heuristic approach based on part-of-speech (POS) tagging. Specifically,
1075   content words—nouns, verbs, adjectives, and adverbs—are classified as semantic, while other POS
1076
       tags (e.g., pronouns, conjunctions, prepositions, and determiners) are categorized as non-semantic.
1077   For code-related responses, we also treat code blocks as semantic content, given their integral role in
1078   conveying the main content of the response. Code blocks are identified using regular expressions that
1079   capture common code delimiters, such as triple backticks (```), tildes (~~~), and inline code marked
       by single backticks (`).

1084   our approach effectively captures the impact of semantic and non-semantic content on stylistic
