009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We consider the problem of novel view synthesis from unposed images in a sin-
012            gle feed-forward. Our framework capitalizes on fast speed, scalability, and high-
013            quality 3D reconstruction and view synthesis capabilities of 3DGS, where we
014            further extend it to offer a practical solution that relaxes common assumptions
015            such as dense image views, accurate camera poses, and substantial image over-
016            laps. We achieve this through identifying and addressing unique challenges aris-
017            ing from the use of pixel-aligned 3DGS: misaligned 3D Gaussians across dif-
018
               ferent views induce noisy or sparse gradients that destabilize training and hin-
               der convergence, especially when above assumptions are not met. To mitigate
019
               this, we employ pre-trained monocular depth estimation and visual correspon-
020
               dence models to achieve coarse alignments of 3D Gaussians. We then introduce
021            lightweight, learnable modules to refine depth and pose estimates from the coarse
022            alignments, improving the quality of 3D reconstruction and novel view synthesis.
023            Furthermore, the refined estimates are leveraged to estimate geometry confidence
024            scores, which assess the reliability of 3D Gaussian centers and condition the pre-
025            diction of Gaussian parameters accordingly. Extensive evaluations on large-scale
026            real-world datasets demonstrate that PF3plat sets a new state-of-the-art across all
027            benchmarks, supported by comprehensive ablation studies validating our design
028            choices.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      In recent years, 3D reconstruction and novel view synthesis have garnered significant attention, par-
      ticularly with the emergence of Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) (Mildenhall et al., 2021) and 3D
034
      Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) (Kerbl et al., 2023). These advancements have enabled the high-quality
035
      3D reconstruction and novel view synthesis. However, many existing methods rely on stringent as-
036   sumptions, such as dense image views (Yu et al., 2024; Barron et al., 2021; 2022), accurate camera
037   poses (Kim et al., 2022; Kwak et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Charatan et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024),
038   and substantial image overlaps (Yu et al., 2021; Johari et al., 2022), which limit their practical ap-
039   plicability. In real-world scenarios, casually captured images contain sparse and distant viewpoints,
040   and lack precise camera poses, making it impractical to assume densely captured views with ac-
041   curate camera poses. Ideally, a practical novel view synthesis solution should operate quickly and
042   effectively with as few as two images, even under significant viewpoint changes.
043   To address some of these limitations, recent efforts (Yu et al., 2021; Johari et al., 2022; Chen et al.,
044   2021; Yang et al., 2023) have introduced generalized view synthesis frameworks capable of perform-
045   ing single feed-forward novel view synthesis from sparse images with minimal overlaps (Du et al.,
046   2023; Xu et al., 2023a). Among these methods, particularly those utilizing 3DGS (Charatan et al.,
047   2023; Chen et al., 2024), have demonstrated remarkable rendering speed and efficiency, alongside
048   impressive reconstruction and view synthesis quality, highlighting the potential of 3D Gaussian-
049   based representations. However, they still depend on accurate camera poses, which are challenging
050
      to acquire in sparse settings, thereby restricting their practical use.
051   More recently, pose-free generalized view synthesis frameworks (Chen & Lee, 2023; Fan et al.,
052   2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Smith et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2024) have been introduced to decouple
053   3D reconstruction and novel view synthesis from camera poses. Given a set of unposed images,
      these frameworks aim to jointly learn radiance fields and 3D geometry without relying on addi-

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      tional data, such as ground-truth camera pose. The learned radiance fields and geometry can then
055   be inferred through trained neural networks, enabling single feed-forward inference. While these
056   pioneering efforts enhance practicality, their performance remains unsatisfactory and their slow
057   rendering speeds (Chen & Lee, 2023; Fan et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Smith et al., 2023) re-
058   main unresolved. In an attempt to boost performance and robustness under extreme scenarios when
059   wide-baseline images are given, Hong et al. (2024) developed a unified framework that jointly esti-
060   mates camera poses, correspondences, and radiance fields using additional data, such as ground truth
061   poses, for supervision. However, this approach still inherits the inherent limitations of NeRF, includ-
062   ing intensive memory consumption and slow rendering speeds, making it impractical for real-world
063
      applications.
064   In this work, we propose PF3plat (Pose-Free Feed-Forward 3D Gaussian Splatting), a novel frame-
065   work for fast and photorealistic novel view synthesis from unposed images in a single feed-forward
066   pass. Our approach leverages the efficiency and high-quality reconstruction capabilities of pixel-
067   aligned 3DGS (Charatan et al., 2023; Szymanowicz et al., 2024), while relaxing common assump-
068   tions such as dense image views, accurate camera poses, scene-specific optimization and substan-
069
      tial image overlaps. However, a primary challenge in using pixel-aligned 3DGS is its dependency
      on precise depth and camera pose estimates for accurate localization of 3D Gaussian centers. In-
070
      accuracies in these estimates can cause misalignments, leading to noisy or sparse gradients that
071
      destabilize training and hinder convergence, especially when the above assumptions are relaxed or
072   scene-specific optimizations to rectify errors cannot be applied during multi-scene training.
073
074   To mitigate these issues, we find that leveraging pre-trained monocular depth estimation (Piccinelli
075
      et al., 2024) and visual correspondence (Lindenberger et al., 2023) models to achieve a coarse align-
      ment of 3D Gaussians is highly effective, thereby promoting a stable learning process. Subse-
076
      quently, we introduce learnable modules designed to refine the depth and pose estimates from the
077
      coarse alignment to enhance the quality of 3D reconstruction and view synthesis. These modules
078   are geometry-aware and lightweight, since we leverage features from the depth network and avoid
079   direct fine-tuning. These refined depth and pose estimates are then used to implement geometry-
080   aware confidence scores to assess the reliability of 3D Gaussian centers, conditioning the prediction
081   of Gaussian parameters such as opacity, covariance, and color.
082
      Our extensive evaluations on large-scale real-world indoor and outdoor datasets (Liu et al., 2021;
083
      Zhou et al., 2018; Ling et al., 2024) demonstrate that PF3plat sets a new state-of-the-art across
084   all benchmarks. Comprehensive ablation studies validate our design choices, confirming that our
085   framework provides a fast and high-performance solution for pose-free generalizable novel view
086   synthesis. We summarize our contributions below:
087
088          • We address the challenging task of pose-free, feed-forward novel view synthesis using
089            3DGS, relaxing many common assumptions of existing methods to improve practicality.
090          • To address the unique challenges arising from the misalignment of 3D Gaussians that desta-
091            bilizes the learning process, we devise an approach to provide coarse alignment. We then
092            introduce lightweight refinement modules and geometry-aware scoring functions, which
093            not only enhance the reconstruction and view synthesis quality, but also prevent catas-
094            trophic forgetting issues typically associated with direct fine-tuning.
095          • Our framework presents an effective approach to enable fast and high-performance 3D
096            reconstruction and novel view synthesis from sparse and unposed images. We have shown
097            that our method sets a new state-of-the-art across all benchmarks.

182   3.2.1      C OARSE A LIGNMENT OF 3D G AUSSIANS
183
      Inspired by recent advancements (Charatan et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024) that highlight the ad-
184
      vantages of pixel-aligned 3D Gaussians, such as speed, efficiency, and high-quality reconstruction
185   and view synthesis, we extend these benefits to more challenging scenarios, specifically in the con-
186   text of pose-free feed-forward view synthesis. To this end, we adopt pixel-aligned 3D Gaussians as
187   our scene representation. However, this representation also poses certain challenges. Unlike previ-
188   ous methods for generalized novel view synthesis that utilize implicit representations (Chen & Lee,
189   2023; Smith et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2024) and benefit from the interpolation capabilities of neural
190   networks, our approach is challenged by the explicit nature of this representation. Specifically, our
191   method directly localizes 3D Gaussian centers using depth and camera pose estimates (Charatan
192   et al., 2023), making it highly sensitive to inaccuracies in these estimates, which cannot be easily
193
      compensated.
194   Such misalignments can cause severe performance degradation and disrupt the learning process by
195   producing sparse and noisy gradients. This issue is particularly exacerbated when wide-baseline
196   images are given as input or the absence of ground-truth pose prevents alignments of 3D Gaussians.
197   Without effectively addressing these challenges, we find the problem becomes nearly intractable.
198   These issues can be mitigated by employing iterative scene-specific optimization steps or by as-
199
      suming ground-truth camera poses to guide 3D Gaussians toward object surfaces. However, these
      solutions are incompatible with our goal of achieving a single feed-forward process from unposed
200
      images. Therefore, overcoming these limitations requires a novel strategy that can handle depth and
201
      pose ambiguities while maintaining efficiency in a feed-forward manner.
202
203   To mitigate the challenges mentioned above, we find it necessary to provide coarse alignment of 3D
204   Gaussians, where quantitative results can be found in Tab. 4. To this end, we employ off-the-shelf
205
      models (Piccinelli et al., 2024; Lindenberger et al., 2023) to estimate initial depths Di and camera
      poses Pi for our images Ii . Specifically, given depth maps Di and sets of correspondences Mij
206
      and their confidence values Cij acquired from each pairwise combinations of images, e.g., (Ii , Ij ),
207
      where i, j refer to image indices, we use a robust solver (Fischler & Bolles, 1981; Li et al., 2012)
208   to estimate the relative poses Pij between image pair. Integrating these components, we provide
209   the necessary coarse alignment to promote stabilizing the learning process and serve as a strong
210   foundation for further enhancements.

235
236   In this module, we further refine camera poses to enhance reconstruction and view synthesis quality.
237   Initially, we replace the estimated relative poses Pij with newly computed camera poses P̂ij derived

240   translation offsets. To streamline this process, we first utilize a fully differentiable transformation
241   synchronization operation that takes P̂ij and Cij as inputs. Using power iterations (El Banani et al.,
242   2023), this operation efficiently recovers the absolute poses P̂i prior to the refinement module.

257   3.2.4   3D G AUSSIAN PARAMTER P REDICTIONS
258
      Cost Volume Construction and Aggregation. Using the refined pose and the monocular depth
259
      estimates, P̂i and D̂i , we assess the quality of the predictions to obtain confidence scores, to assist
260
      predicting 3D Gaussian parameters. To achieve this, we construct both a conventional multi-view
261
      stereo cost volume and a guidance cost volume derived from Di .
262
263   Specifically, given the current pose estimates P̂i , we build a multi-view stereo cost volume Cimulti ∈
264   Rh×w×K following the plane-sweeping approach (Yao et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2021). For each of
265   the K depth candidates within specified near and far ranges along the epipolar lines, we compute
266   matching scores using cosine similarity (Cho et al., 2022; 2021; Hong et al., 2024). Subsequently, to
267   guide the depth localization along the epipolar lines, we construct a guidance cost volume Ciguide (Li
268   et al., 2023), where each spatial location is represented by a one-hot vector indicating the depth
269   candidate closest to the monocular depth estimate. The constructed Cimulti and Ciguide undergo cross-
      attention to update the multi-view cost volume Cˆimulti .

288   3.3   L OSS F UNCTION
289
290
      Reconstruction Loss. By making both depth and camera pose refinement modules learnable and
      differentiable, our network leverages supervision signals from the pixel-aligned 3D Gaussians and
291
      the novel view synthesis task (Zhou et al., 2017) to refine these estimates during training. Specifi-
292
      cally, we combine photometric loss, defined as the L2 loss between the rendered and target images,
293   as well as SSIM (Wang et al., 2004) loss LSSIM and LPIPS (Zhang et al., 2018) loss LLPIPS to form
294   our reconstruction loss Limg .
295
296   2D-3D Consistency Loss. We identify that provided good coarse alignments, RGB loss is suffi-
297   cient, but with larger baselines, the training process starts to destabilize. Moreover, one remaining
298
      issue with learning solely from the photometric loss is that the gradients are mainly derived from
      pixel intensity differences, which suffer in textureless regions. To remedy these, we enforce that cor-
299
      responding points in the set of images {I}N i=1 lie on the same object surface, drawing from principles
300
      of multi-view geometry Hartley & Zisserman (2003).
301
302   Formally, using the estimated depths D̂, the camera poses P̂, and the correspondence sets M, we
303   can define a geometric consistency loss that penalizes deviations from the multi-view geometric
304   constraints. For each correspondence (p, q) ∈ Mij between images Ii and Ij , we compute the 3D
305   point from the pixel p and its estimated depth D̂i (p) using the camera intrinsics. We then transform
306   this to the coordinate frame of Ij using the relative pose P̂ij and project it back P onto the image
307   plane to obtain the predicted correspondence p̃. This is defined as L2D−3D = (p,q)∈M φ(p̃, q),
308   where φ(·) denotes huber loss. By integrating this loss into the learning pipeline, we improve the
309   robustness of the model in regions with low texture or significant viewpoint changes, ensuring that
310   the estimated pose and depth are consistent with the underlying 3D structure of the scene.
311
      3D-3D Consistency Loss. While the multi-view consistent surface loss directly connects each pair
312   of corresponding Gaussians and their centers, guiding the model towards the object’s surface, we
313   find that relying solely on this loss can lead to suboptimal convergence, especially in regions with
314   sparse correspondences. To further stabilize and enhance the learning process, we introduce an addi-
315   tional regularization term that minimizes the discrepancies among the centers of the corresponding
316   Gaussians.
317
      Intuitively, this differs from L2D−3D in that, unlike the previous function, which considers the
318   alignment of Gaussian centers from only one side when dealing with pairwise correspondences,
319   the regularization term symmetrically enforces consistency from both sides. Specifically, while
320   the multi-view consistent surface loss projects the Gaussian center from one view to another us-
321   ing the estimated depth and camera pose, e.g., from source to target, the regularization term
322   jointly minimizes the distances between all corresponding Gaussian centers across multiple views.
323   By considering both directions in pairwise correspondences, this approach promotes a more co-
      herent and robust estimation of the object’s surface, reducing the influence of outliers and im-

351   In this work, we assume intrinsic parameters are given, as they are generally available from modern

355
      For training on the RealEstate10K and ACID datasets, we gradually increase the number of frames
356   between I1 and I2 as training progresses, initially setting the frame distance to 45 and gradually
357   increasing it to 75. For the DL3DV dataset, we start with a frame distance of 5 and increase it to 10.
358   The target view is randomly sampled within this range. We train under the assumption of N = 2 and
359   render Iˆt . Our code is implemented using PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2017). Additional implementation

412   Evaluation Protocol For the evaluation on RealEstate-10K and ACID, we follow the protocol out-
413   lined by Hong et al. (2024), where evaluation is conducted using unposed triplet images (I1 , I2 , It ).
414   The test set is divided into three groups, small, middle, and large, based on the extent of overlap
415   between I1 and I2 . This allows the model’s performance to be assessed under varying levels of
416
      difficulty, reflecting different real-world scenarios. For the relatively new DL3DV dataset, we in-
      troduce a new evaluation protocol. For each scene, we select two context images, I1 and I2 , by
417
      skipping frames at intervals of 5 and 10, creating two groups per scene, each representing small and
418
      large overlap cases. We then randomly select three target images from the sequence between the
419   context images, similar to the above protocol. For evaluation metrics, we use standard image quality
420   measures, PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS, and MSE, for novel view synthesis. For camera pose estimation,
421   we compute the geodesic rotation error and angular difference in translation, as commonly done in
422   classical methods (Nistér, 2004; Melekhov et al., 2017). These errors are measured by comparing
                                         GT
423   the ground truth relative pose P12    and our estimated pose P̂12 . Our statistical analysis reports both
424   the average and median errors, with the median providing robustness against outliers.

532
      In this paper, we have introduced learning-based framework that tackles pose-free novel view syn-
533
      thesis with 3DGS, enabling efficient, fast and photorealistic view synthesis from unposed images.
534   The proposed framework, PFSplat, is built on the base model comprising of foundation models to
535   overcome inherent limitations of 3DGS. While the devised base model already surpasses the existing
536   methods, we have also devised modules to address the limitations of the base model, enhancing the
537   overall performance. This method is capable of training and inference solely from unposed images,
538   even in scenarios where only a handful of images with minimal overlaps are given. We have shown
539   that our approach surpasses all existing methods on real-world large-scale datasets, establishing new
      state-of-the-art performance.

      A.1       T RAINING D ETAILS
867
868   We train MVSplat (Chen et al., 2024) and CoPoNeRF (Hong et al., 2024) using our data loaders,
869   similarly increasing the distance between context views during training, as explained in Sec 4.1.
870   Specifically, we train MVSplat for 200,000 iterations using a batch size of 8 on a single A6000
871   GPU. All the hyperparameters are set to the authors’ default setting. For CoPoNeRF, we train it
872   for 50,000 iterations using 8 A6000 GPUs with effective batch size of 64, following the authors’
873
      original implementations and hyperparameters. Finally, for InstantSplat (Fan et al., 2024), we train
      and evaluate on a single A6000 GPU with a batch size of 1 by following the official code1 , and the

      hyperparameters were set according to the default settings provided by the authors.

881   A.3       L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
882
883   As our model currently lacks a mechanism to handle dynamic scenes, it is unable to accurately
884   capture scene dynamics or perform view extrapolation. Additionally, our model’s performance is
885   contingent on the quality of the coarse alignments, which rely on the accuracy of the depth and
886   correspondence models. In cases where either of these models fails, our approach may not function
887   optimally. However, because our refinement modules are lightweight, simple, and model-agnostic,
888
      incorporating more advanced methods for coarse alignment could further enhance performance.
889   For future work, we plan to train our model on diverse large-scale datasets. Since our approach relies
890   exclusively on supervision signals from RGB images, it is straightforward to scale up the training
891   data. We also aim to extend our method to handle 4D objects, ultimately enabling the modeling of
892   4D scenes, which would be beneficial for applications such as egocentric vision and robotics.
