009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            Model pruning is a performance optimization technique for large language and vision
012            models. However, existing pruning methods often lead to significant performance degra-
013            dation or require extensive retraining and fine-tuning. This technique aims to identify
014            and remove neurons, connections unlikely leading to the contribution during the machine
015            generation phase. Our goal is to obtain a much smaller and faster foundational model
016            that can quickly generate content almost as good as those of the unpruned models. We
017            propose MAMA Pruning (short for Movement and Magnitude Analysis), an improved
018            pruning method that effectively reduces model size and network computational complex-
019
               ity while maintaining performance comparable to the original unpruned model even at
               extreme pruned levels. The improved method is based on weights, bias, activations and
020
               proposed novel pruning indicators. Empirical results show that our method outperforms
021
               and be comparable to state-of-the-art methods across various pruning levels. All our code,
022            models, dataset, and demo are publicly available.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Large language and vision models(Brown et al., 2020a; OpenAI, 2023) face significant computational chal-
028   lenges due to massive model sizes and the high query loads that these systems need to support. These
029   models, along with related large-scale production systems, are responsible for processing and integrating
030   vast amounts of data—including web pages, videos, and multimodal content—into underlying network ar-
031   chitectures such as Transformers and Diffusion models. One crucial cost factor is the query processing per
032   user, which must scale with both data size and query load. As a result, large foundational models devote
033
      substantial hardware and energy resources to this kind of generation task. There has been extensive research
      on improving query processing performance, including work on various caching techniques, retrieval infor-
034
      mation systems, and high-performance knowledge representation. To address these challenges, a significant
035
      number of optimization techniques, commonly referred to as model pruning, have emerged to enhance the
036   efficiency and effectiveness of AI-Generated Content (AIGC) generation processes. In this paper, we pro-
037   pose an improved model pruning algorithm based on novel indicators derived from an in-depth analysis of
038   weights, biases, and activations. Our method significantly enhances the performance and efficiency of large
039   language and vision models. We demonstrate through extensive experiments that our approach outperforms
040   existing state-of-the-art pruning techniques across various metrics.
041
      In this paper, we focus on model pruning, an optimization technique aimed at enhancing neural network ef-
042   ficiency. Our approach involves analyzing learning representations, network architectures, and performance
043   metrics to identify neurons and connections that significantly contribute to the model’s output in response
044   to user input. By systematically removing neurons unlikely to improve the network’s performance, we
045   reduce the model’s size and complexity. The resulting pruned neural network maintains nearly the same
046   output quality as the original unpruned model while requiring substantially less CPU power, memory, and

050   To motivate the problem, consider a state-of-the-art language model with around 175 billion parameters,
051   deployed across various services and processing an estimated 100 million user queries daily. Interestingly,
052

056   This imbalance raises an important question: Can we reduce the computational load by pruning these inac-
057   tive weights without sacrificing the model’s overall performance? Pruning 70-80% of the model’s parameters
058
      could theoretically reduce the active parameter count significantly, lowering the operational costs of running
      the service. However, aggressive pruning might lead to unacceptable losses in quality, causing user-facing
059
      services to suffer noticeable degradation in response accuracy or fluency. The challenge, therefore, lies in
060
      developing a pruning method that effectively reduces the parameter count while maintaining high accuracy
061   and minimizing computational overhead. Given the scale of such large models, even a 1-2% drop in accu-
062   racy could result in millions of queries per day yielding suboptimal results. This example underscores the
063   urgent need for improved pruning methods that can balance sparsity and efficiency without compromising
064   the quality of large-scale models.

066

071   sured by standard retrieval evaluation metrics. In our approach, we consider pruning as a prediction problem
072   within a feature-rich environment, aiming to determine which neurons, weights, or layers to retain.

120   2.1.1    N EURAL N ETWORK A RCHITECTURES
121
122   Neural network architectures have undergone significant evolution over the past decades, moving from sim-
123   ple multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) to highly sophisticated models such as convolutional neural networks
124   (CNNs), recurrent neural networks (RNNs), and transformers. These architectures are designed to handle
125   different types of input data and tasks. This enables deep learning models to tackle complex challenges
126
      across various domains, including natural language processing (NLP), computer vision, and time-series pre-
      diction.
127
128   CNNs were pivotal in advancing the field of computer vision by introducing specialized layers that focus on
129   spatial hierarchies in data. AlexNet(Krizhevsky et al., 2012), for instance, brought CNNs to prominence by
130   demonstrating their superiority in tasks like image classification. These models use convolutional layers to
131   capture local features from the input, pooling layers for dimensionality reduction, and fully connected layers
132
      to make final predictions. More advanced versions like ResNet(He et al., 2016) introduced the concept
      of residual learning, enabling the training of ultra-deep networks by allowing information to bypass layers
133
      through skip connections.
134
135   In contrast, RNNs and their variants, such as long short-term memory (LSTM) networks (Hochreiter &
136   Schmidhuber, 1997), have excelled in sequential data processing. These models capture temporal depen-
137   dencies, making them suitable for tasks like speech recognition and machine translation. However, their
138
      limitations in handling long-range dependencies led to the rise of attention mechanisms and transformers.
139   Transformers(Vaswani et al., 2017) revolutionized the field by discarding the need for recurrence, relying
140   instead on self-attention mechanisms to capture relationships between tokens, regardless of their distance in

142
      from Transformers)(Devlin et al., 2019) and GPT (Generative Pre-trained Transformer) (Radford et al.,
143   2018), which set new benchmarks across a range of NLP tasks.
144
145   As neural networks continue to grow in size and complexity, designing architectures that balance perfor-

      2.1.2   H UMAN I NPUT & M ACHINE G ENERATION
151
152   The interaction between human input and machine generation is a rapidly evolving area with significant
153   implications for artificial intelligence (AI) systems designed to augment or replace human decision-making.
154   Human input—whether in the form of annotations, feedback, or direct manipulation of model outputs—plays
155   a critical role in training and refining AI models. This interaction is key in supervised learning, where labeled
156   datasets curated by humans guide the learning processes of models (LeCun et al., 2015).
157   In domains like content generation, machine learning models utilize human inputs to produce creative out-
158   puts such as text, images, or music. Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)(Goodfellow et al., 2014) have
159   demonstrated remarkable success in generating high-quality images by leveraging an adversarial relation-
160   ship between a generator and a discriminator. The generator creates new images, while the discriminator
161   evaluates their authenticity compared to real images, driving iterative improvements in generation quality.
162
      Similarly, transformer-based models like GPT-3(Brown et al., 2020b) have showcased the power of machine
163   generation in natural language tasks. GPT-3 can generate human-like text ranging from simple responses
164   to complex narratives or technical content. The flexibility of transformer architectures allows them to be
165   fine-tuned for diverse applications, from automated customer support to creative writing.
166
      However, machine generation presents challenges, particularly in controlling and guiding model outputs to
167
      align with human intentions. While human input can define high-level goals or constraints, fine-tuning mod-
168
      els often requires extensive iterations and feedback loops. In creative fields, for instance, human designers
169   may provide input to a generative model, but the output might still require refinement to meet aesthetic
170   or functional criteria. Techniques like Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback (RLHF)(Christiano
171   et al., 2017; Ouyang et al., 2022) address this issue by using reward mechanisms to guide models toward
172   more desirable outputs based on human evaluations.
173
      The relationship between human input and machine generation continues to evolve, driving innovations in
174
      AI systems that not only automate tasks but also enhance creative and decision-making processes. Under-
175   standing this interplay is essential for developing efficient model pruning techniques that preserve the quality
176   of machine-generated content while optimizing computational resources.

      2.1.3   M ODEL Q UANTIZATION & C OMPRESSION
179
180   Model quantization and compression are essential techniques aimed at reducing the computational and mem-
181   ory footprints of deep learning models, making them more suitable for deployment in resource-constrained
182   environments such as mobile devices and embedded systems. These techniques allow models to maintain
183   high accuracy while being smaller, faster, and more energy-efficient, which is critical in real-time applica-
184   tions and edge computing.
185   Quantization refers to the process of reducing the precision of the weights and activations in a neural network
186   (Krishnamoorthi, 2018). Instead of using 32-bit floating-point numbers, which is the default precision in
187   many models, quantized models use lower precision formats, such as 16-bit or 8-bit integers. Quantization-

189
      to lower precision during learning (Jacob et al., 2018). Techniques like Post-Training Quantization (PTQ)
190   enable quantization after training, making it easier to deploy pre-trained models with minimal loss in accu-
191   racy (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2015).
192
193
      Model compression involves various techniques that aim to reduce the overall size of a neural network. This
      can be achieved through weight pruning, where less important weights are removed (Han et al., 2016), or
194
      through knowledge distillation, where a smaller ”student” model is trained to mimic the behavior of a larger
195
      ”teacher” model (Hinton et al., 2015). Model compression techniques often complement quantization, as

197
198   Recent advances in model quantization and compression have introduced more sophisticated strategies that

200
      more aggressively. Compression-aware training techniques further optimize the model architecture, ensuring
201
      that compressed models remain robust during inference (He et al., 2018).
202
203   These techniques are particularly important in real-world scenarios where latency, memory usage, and en-
204   ergy efficiency are crucial. From autonomous driving systems to personal digital assistants, quantization and
205
      compression enable AI to function effectively in environments where computational resources are limited.

207   2.1.4   M ODEL P RUNING & I NDICATORS D ISCOVERY
208
209   Model pruning is a widely used technique aimed at reducing the complexity of deep neural networks by
210   removing unnecessary parameters, resulting in more efficient models in terms of both size and inference
211   speed (Han et al., 2015a). Pruning can target weights, neurons, channels, or even entire layers within the
212   network. A crucial aspect of the pruning process is the discovery of reliable indicators that determine which
213   parts of the network can be safely removed without significantly affecting model performance.
214   Traditional pruning techniques, such as magnitude-based pruning, operate under the assumption that smaller-
215   magnitude weights contribute less to the overall model output and can be pruned with minimal loss of accu-
216   racy (Han et al., 2015a). While this method is straightforward and effective in some cases, it overlooks the
217   complex interdependencies between weights and the hierarchical structure of neural networks, potentially
218   leading to suboptimal pruning choices.
219   In contrast, structured pruning techniques operate at a higher level by removing entire channels, filters, or
220   layers (Li et al., 2017). This approach offers more systematic reductions in model size and is particularly
221   advantageous for hardware implementations, as it leads to predictable reductions in computational load.
222   However, identifying reliable indicators for structured pruning—such as the importance or sensitivity of
223   specific channels or layers—remains a challenge.
224   Layer-wise pruning approaches introduce the concept of adaptive sparsity, where different layers are pruned
225   at varying rates based on their sensitivity to performance degradation (Zhu & Gupta, 2018). Techniques like
226   variational dropout (Molchanov et al., 2017a) and dynamic sparse training enhance pruning by dynamically
227   adjusting sparsity during training. These methods enable the model to discover optimal pruning strategies
228   that balance performance and efficiency.
229
      Recent advancements in pruning have focused on identifying novel indicators for pruning decisions.
230   Gradient-based methods, such as SNIP (Wang et al., 2024), evaluate the importance of weights based on
231   the magnitude of their gradients during backpropagation. Additionally, the Lottery Ticket Hypothesis (Fran-
232   kle & Carbin, 2019) suggests that sparse sub-networks exist within dense models that can be trained from
233   scratch to achieve comparable performance. This has opened new avenues for efficient pruning by empha-
234   sizing the selection of these ”winning tickets.”

243   2.2.1    T HE M AGNITUDE P RUNING A LGORITHM
244
245
      The magnitude pruning algorithm (Han et al., 2015a) is one of the simplest and most widely used methods
      for reducing the size of neural networks. It operates by pruning weights based on their absolute magnitude:
246
      weights with smaller absolute values are considered less critical to the model’s performance and are pruned,
247
      while larger weights are retained. The typical approach involves setting a global threshold—determined by
248   the desired sparsity ratio—below which weights are set to zero. Magnitude pruning is unstructured, meaning
249   it can prune individual weights from any part of the model, leading to irregular sparsity patterns.
250
251
      Advantages: Magnitude pruning is computationally inexpensive and fast. Evaluating weights based on their
      magnitude requires minimal computational overhead and can be applied to any layer of the network without
252
      complex calculations or additional data. This method is also versatile, as it can be used across different types
253
      of neural network architectures.
254
255   Limitations: Despite its simplicity, magnitude pruning has inherent drawbacks in terms of accuracy degra-
256   dation and the resulting sparsity patterns. Since the algorithm considers only the magnitude of individual
257
      weights, it may inadvertently remove important connections, potentially leading to significant loss in model
      accuracy. Additionally, the unstructured nature of the resulting sparsity leads to irregular memory access
258
      patterns, making it challenging to achieve computational speed-ups on standard hardware due to poor cache
259
      utilization and inefficient parallelization.
260
261   To address these limitations, researchers have explored more structured versions of the algorithm that target
262   specific parts of the network—such as entire filters, channels, or blocks—rather than individual weights (Li
263
      et al., 2017). Structured pruning results in more regular sparsity patterns that are better suited for hardware
      acceleration. Moreover, combining magnitude pruning with retraining or fine-tuning steps can help mitigate
264
      accuracy loss by allowing the model to adjust to the pruned architecture (Han et al., 2015a).
265
266
      2.2.2    T HE S PARSE GPT P RUNING A LGORITHM
267
268   The SparseGPT pruning algorithm (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023a) is an advanced method specifically designed
269   to handle large language models like GPT. It employs a gradient-based approach, utilizing gradient infor-
270   mation during pruning to identify and remove less important connections in the model. By calculating the
271   gradients of the loss function with respect to network weights, SparseGPT assesses the significance of each
272   weight, allowing for more informed pruning decisions.
273   SparseGPT adopts an iterative pruning strategy, gradually increasing sparsity while minimizing accuracy
274   loss at each step. In each iteration, a subset of weights with the least impact on the model’s performance is
275   pruned based on gradient evaluations. This progressive approach enables the algorithm to carefully balance
276   the trade-off between model size and accuracy.
277   An essential feature of SparseGPT is its encouragement of block-sparse structures, where entire blocks or
278   groups of weights are pruned together. Block sparsity results in regular sparsity patterns that are more
279   hardware-friendly and can be efficiently leveraged by modern high-performance computing architectures.
280   This makes SparseGPT particularly suitable for hardware acceleration and allows it to take advantage of
281   libraries optimized for such sparsity patterns.

      Limitations: Despite its advantages, SparseGPT has limitations related to computational expense and com-

289

293

299   2.2.3   T HE WANDA P RUNING A LGORITHM
300
301
      The WANDA (Weights and Activations) pruning algorithm(Sun et al., 2024a) introduces an importance-
      aware approach to pruning by considering both weight magnitudes and activation statistics. By integrating
302
      activation information, WANDA makes more informed pruning decisions based on the relative importance
303
      of weights to the network’s output.
304
305   The algorithm begins with a calibration phase, where the model processes a small calibration dataset to
306   collect activation data. This data is used to normalize the weight magnitudes within each row of the weight
307
      matrices, effectively weighting the importance of each connection based on both its inherent strength and
      its activation impact. This normalization allows WANDA to identify and retain weights that contribute most
308
      significantly to the model’s performance.
309
310   WANDA can be applied in both unstructured and structured pruning settings. In unstructured pruning,
311   individual weights are pruned, leading to irregular sparsity patterns. In structured pruning, entire filters,
312   channels, or neurons are pruned, resulting in regular sparsity patterns that are more conducive to hardware
313
      acceleration. This flexibility allows WANDA to be utilized across various models and hardware configura-
      tions.
314
315   By considering activation statistics alongside weight magnitudes, WANDA achieves better accuracy-sparsity
316   trade-offs compared to traditional magnitude-based pruning. Empirical results have demonstrated that
317   WANDA maintains higher model accuracy at comparable levels of sparsity(Sun et al., 2024a).
318   Potential Optimizations: To further enhance WANDA’s efficiency, the calibration phase could be streamlined
319   by employing smaller datasets or more efficient methods for collecting activation statistics. Additionally,
320   combining WANDA with other pruning techniques, such as gradient-based methods, may yield even better
321   accuracy and sparsity outcomes. For example, integrating gradient information could help in identifying
322   weights that are critical for minimizing the loss function.

      2.2.5   C OMPARISON TO O UR W ORK
333
334   MAMA (Movement And Magnitude Analysis) pruning is fundamentally different from existing pruning
335   methods in its approach to identifying and preserving important neural connections. Unlike magnitude-
336   based pruning, which simply removes weights below a certain threshold, or methods like SparseGPT that
337   use gradient information, MAMA employs a novel three-step process that considers both the magnitude and
338   the dynamic behavior of weights during training. We will describe our proposed methods in detail in the
339
      following sections.

354   4   O UR P ROPOSED M ETHOD : MAMA P RUNING
355
356   MAMA Pruning is grounded in a systematic approach that identifies and preserves dynamically significant
357   weights by redistributing less important weights to more critical connections within the network. This
358   ensures the maintenance of overall information flow and network adaptability, even under high pruning
359   ratios. The methodology encompasses three core steps:
360   1. Identify the pruned weights. The first step involves identifying weights eligible for pruning based
361   on both their magnitude and dynamic behavior during training. This dual analysis ensures that weights
362   contributing minimally to the network’s performance are targeted for pruning.
363
      2. Redistribute weights to related neurons. Following the identification of prunable weights, MAMA
364
      Pruning undertakes a redistribution phase, wherein the values of unimportant weights are ”moved” to more
365
      significant connections within the same layer. This strategic redistribution ensures the preservation of the
366   network’s information flow and compensates for the pruned weights.
367
368   3. Execute the pruning. The final step involves pruning the identified weights to achieve the desired sparsity
369
      level within the model. This step actualizes the reduction in model parameters, preparing the network for
      deployment or further optimization.
