                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce the Scene Language, a visual scene representation that concisely and
012            precisely describes the structure, semantics, and identity of visual scenes. The
013            Scene Language represents a scene with three key components: a program that
014            specifies the hierarchical and relational structure of entities in the scene, words
015            in natural language that summarize the semantic class of each entity, and em-
016            beddings that capture the visual identity of each entity. This representation can
017            be inferred from pre-trained language models via a training-free inference tech-
018            nique, given text or image inputs. The resulting scene can be rendered into im-
019
               ages using traditional, neural, or hybrid graphics renderers. Together, this forms
               a robust, fully automated system for high-quality 3D and 4D scene generation.
020
               Compared with existing representations like scene graphs, our proposed Scene
021
               Language generates complex scenes with higher fidelity, while explicitly mod-
022            eling the scene structures to enable precise control and editing. Project page:
023            https://sclg-page.github.io/.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027   How do you describe a scene? Imagine that you just traveled to Easter Island and would like to
028   explain to Alice the wondrous scene of Ahu Akivi: “There are seven moai in a row, facing the same
029   direction.” “What is a moai?” Alice asked. “A moai is a stone human figure without legs, but each
030   of them also looks slightly different.” At this point, you realize it seems difficult to precisely explain
031   the scene using natural language alone.
032   In fact, this example highlights a complete scene representation requires at least three types of com-
033   plementary information: (1) structural knowledge, which is about the joint distribution of multi-
034   ple instances, like “seven moai in a row, facing the same direction,” most naturally described as
035   programs; (2) category-level semantics, which may be shared across instances, often described in
036   words, such as “moai”; (3) instance-level intrinsics, tied to the identity of each specific object or
037   part, such as its geometry, color, and texture, which is hard to describe but easy to recognize.
038   Modern AI techniques provide natural grounding for each of the three modalities, while also falling
039   short of capturing all: in-context learning of pre-trained language models (LMs) enables the in-
040   ference of domain-specific programs (Brown et al., 2020); LMs capture rich semantic information
041   based on words in natural language; embeddings obtained via techniques like textual inversion (Gal
042   et al., 2023) or low-rank adaptation (Hu et al., 2021) best capture object identity. However, none of
043   these existing representations alone is sufficient for scene generation and editing.
044   We introduce the Scene Language, a representation that integrates the three modalities—programs,
045   words, and embeddings—to precisely and concisely describe the structure, semantics, and identity
046   of visual scenes. In the Scene Language, a program specifies a computation process that defines
047   the organization of a collection of entities in the scene, including extrinsics like poses and structural
048   regularity like repetitions. Each entity is associated with a word referring to its semantic group, as
049   well as an embedding describing its instance-specific attributes.
050   In addition to the representation itself, we propose a training-free inference module using a pre-
051   trained LM as a backbone to infer the Scene Language from texts and images. When provided with
052   a domain-specific language (DSL) for scenes, LMs decompose the task of complex scene genera-
053   tion into simpler tasks of scene component generation by predicting their corresponding modular
      functions. We also discuss possible neural, traditional, and hybrid graphics engines that render the

      2     R ELATED W ORK
087
088   Visual scene representations are arguably the most fundamental problem in computer vision; thus,
089   for sure, we may not enumerate all related work. As our Scene Language comprises programs,
090   words, and embeddings, we will organize our discussion accordingly into three categories: scene
091   representations that use program-based representations (Section 2.1), semantic graph-based repre-
092
      sentations (Section 2.2), and a pre-trained generative model’s latent space (Section 2.3).
093   2.1    R EPRESENTING S CENES AS P ROGRAMS
094
      Programs can specify not only the relations among scene components mentioned in Section 2.2, but
095
      also structural patterns such as hierarchy and repetitions, making them suitable as explicit descrip-
096   tions of scene structures. Prior works have proposed to use programs in the form of sequences of
097   execution commands as object-centric representations, followed by neural executors that render the
098   programs into 3D shapes (Tian et al., 2019; Sharma et al., 2018; Deng et al., 2022). In comparison,
099   ShapeAssembly (Jones et al., 2020) introduces higher-level functions with semantically meaningful
100   function names, e.g., “chair” and “back”, to its program representation. Both ShapeAssembly and
101   ours adopt the design principle of function abstraction, which results in clearly stated hierarchy re-
102   lation among components and better program editability. However, ShapeAssembly uses cuboids
103   as the shape representation and does not model appearance, while ours allows for more precise
104
      geometry and appearance modeling using expressive neural embeddings.
105   All the representations mentioned above require 3D datasets for training. More recently, with the
106   advance of language models (LMs), several methods (Zhou et al., 2024b; Hu et al., 2024; Yamada
107   et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a; Tam et al., 2024) have proposed to use zero-shot
      LM inference for generating programs that will be rendered into scenes. These methods operate on

124   2.2      R EPRESENTING S CENES WITH S EMANTIC G RAPHS
125   Prior semantic scene representations often adopt a graph to encode semantic scene components, such
126   as objects and parts. In particular, Yuille & Kersten (2006); Huang et al. (2018) propose to employ
127   a parse graph of context-free grammar, using terminal nodes to correspond to objects and their
128   attributes, to represent a scene. Both works employ an analysis-by-synthesis approach to infer the
129   representation from images that heavily rely on domain-specific priors. Alternative representations
130   include scene graph (Johnson et al., 2015; 2018; Gao et al., 2024), where each node in a graph
131   corresponds to an object and an edge corresponds to a pairwise relation, and StructureNet (Mo
132   et al., 2019), which focuses on an object-centric setting and uses nodes for object parts. While
133
      these representations preserve the high-level semantics of scenes or objects, they leave out low-level
      precision; thus, geometric, textural, or relational details that cannot be fully specified by language
134
      or hand-crafted rules are often ignored. We address this issue via the inclusion of embeddings.
135
136   2.3      R EPRESENTING S CENES WITH G ENERATIVE M ODEL L ATENTS
137   The latent space of visual generative models can serve as a representation space for visual scenes.
138   Such latent space can effectively capture the exact visual content of scenes, including geometry and
139   appearance details, and can be either directly inferred, e.g., in variational inference (Kingma, 2013)
140   and model inversion (Zhu et al., 2016). More recently, text-to-image diffusion models have shown
141   remarkable results in image synthesis. This class of models offers several candidate representation
142   spaces including the space of textual embeddings (Gal et al., 2023), low-rank network weights (Hu
143   et al., 2021), full model weights (Ruiz et al., 2023), or noise vectors in the diffusion process (Song
144
      et al., 2021; Mokady et al., 2023; Ho et al., 2020). However, such representations typically do
      not offer interpretable semantics or explicitly encode hierarchical scene structures. We incorporate
145
      textual embeddings into our structural representation in this work, leveraging its high expressivity
146
      to preserve visual details.

148   3       T HE S CENE L ANGUAGE
149
      We aim to design a visual scene representation that encodes the structure, semantics, and visual
150
      content of scenes. Towards this goal, we propose the Scene Language, which represents a scene
151
      with three components: a program that encodes scene structure by specifying the existence and
152   relations of scene components, which we will refer to as entities; words in natural language that
153   denote the semantic group of each entity in the scene; and neural embeddings that pertain the low-
154   level visual details and identities of the entities by permitting an expressive input parameter space.
155   In the following, we will first give a formal definition of the representation (Section 3.1), and then
156   introduce a domain-specific language (DSL) (Section 3.2) as its realization.

192   Here, P := {fw }w∈W is a program consisting of a set of entity functions fw , where each entity
193   function fw defines a class of entities in the scene, such as “board” and “pawn” illustrated in Fig. 2
194   and is uniquely identified by such a word, e.g., w = “board” in natural language, which succinctly
195   summarizes its semantic meaning. W denotes the collection of words corresponding to all the entity
196   functions in the program. Each entity function fw maps a neural embedding z to a specific entity h
197   in the scene, where z specifies the attributes and identity of the output entity, like a specific color of
198   a “pawn”. Hence, the complete Scene Language Φ(s) of a particular scene s also contains a list of
199   neural embeddings Z := [z1 , z2 , · · · , zJ ] encoding J specific entities [h1 , h2 , · · · , hJ ] in the scene.
200   Crucially, the program P captures scene structures in three aspects. First, each entity function fw in
201   P transforms and composes multiple sub-entities (e.g., 64 squares) into a new, more complex entity
202   (e.g., board), naturally reflecting the hierarchical, part-whole relations in the scene, as the arrows
203   in Fig. 2 highlight. Second, multiple entities hj in the scene may belong to the same semantic
204   class w (e.g., “square”), and can thus be represented by reusing the same entity function fw with
205   distinct embeddings zj . Finally, each entity function also captures the precise spatial layout of the
206
      sub-entities by specifying their relative poses during the composition, such as 64 squares forming
      an 8 × 8 grid.

263

299   4     R ENDERING
300
301
      Applying the proposed scene representation to image generation tasks requires rendering a Scene
      Language Φ(s) into images. To do so, first, the program interpreter evaluates Φ(s) to obtain a
302
      data object of type Entity. Afterward, a graphics renderer maps the Entity data object to its
303
      rendering parameter space and renders it into a final image.
304
305
      Renderer Specifications. We define the specification of a graphics renderer, a module in the pro-
306
      posed representation, as follows. A graphics renderer is determined by (1) primitive parameter
307
      space Θ and (2) a rendering operation R : P (Θ × T ) → I, where T is the space of 3D affine
308   transformations representing poses, P denotes all possible subsets, and I is the space of rendered
309   images. We assume access to a reparameterization function greparam that maps from Tuple[Word,
310   Embedding] to Θ, which consequently determines a mapping from program outputs of type
311   Entity to the admissible input domain of rendering operation R.
312
313   Renderer Instantiations. An example renderer instantiation is with Score Distillation Sampling
314   (SDS) (Poole et al., 2022) guidance, where Θ is a differentiable 3D representation and greparam :
315   ZCLIP → Θ corresponds to the SDS-guided optimization process of finding a solution that aligns
316   with the input of greparam . To compute z given a word, e.g., “pawn” for an entity of white pawn from
317   Fig. 2, and an embedding, e.g., <z68>, we use a manually specified language template c, or “a
318   pawn, <z68>, 3D model” in this example, to embed them into embedding z = gCLIP (c) ∈ ZCLIP ;
319   gCLIP is the pre-trained CLIP text encoder.
320   For the underlying 3D representation, we use 3D Gaussian Splatting (Kerbl et al., 2023) where im-
321   ages are rendered by splatting a set of 3D Gaussians onto the image plane; other differentiable
322   3D representations such as neural fields will also be suitable. We base our implementation on
323   GALA3D (Zhou et al., 2024c), and use MVDream (Shi et al., 2023) and a depth-conditioned Con-
      trolNet (Zhang et al., 2023b) for guidance.

      5       I NFERENCE VIA P RE - TRAINED L ANGUAGE M ODELS
342
343   We introduce a training-free method to infer a Scene Language from text or image descriptions
344   of scenes using pre-trained language models (LMs). LMs have shown remarkable capability in
345   code generation with common programming languages such as Python. In our implementation, we
346
      prompt LMs to generate a Python program, which is further executed with a program interpreter and
      rendered into an image using a graphics renderer.
347
348   In particular, we include the following in the LM prompt: 1) the input condition, which is a scene
349   description in texts or an image; 2) a Python script of helper functions converted from the macros
350   from the DSL; and 3) an example program using the helper functions. We use Claude 3.5 Son-
351   net (Anthropic, 2024) for all experiments for our method and LM-dependent baselines. Full lan-
352
      guage prompts for all experiments are listed in Appendix E.
353   Recall from Section 3.1 that functions in program P are evaluated on embeddings from Z. The
354   function arguments in the LM-generated programs, which are numeric values or string tokens, are
355   converted to embeddings from ZCLIP (Section 3.2) using language templates and the CLIP text
356   encoder gCLIP . For example, in the LM-generated program, function calls for white pieces in Fig. 2
357   have input attribute {‘‘color’’:(.9,.9,.9)}, and we prompt LM to describe the color value
358
      as a word, and feed the word into gCLIP to compute <z68>. For image-conditioned tasks, for each

530   7     C ONCLUSION
531   We have introduced a visual scene representation,
532   termed the Scene Language, which encodes three key aspects of visual scenes: (1) scene structure,
533   such as hierarchy and repetition, specified via programs; (2) semantics of individual scene compo-
534   nents succinctly summarized via words in natural language; and (3) identities of each component
535   precisely captured via neural embeddings. We formalize the representation as a programming lan-
536   guage defined using a DSL. We show that the proposed representation can be efficiently inferred
537   from both text and image inputs using pre-trained language models. Once the program is executed,
538
      the resulting scene can be rendered into images using a variety of graphics renderers. Compared
      with existing methods, our Scene Language produces 3D and 4D scenes with significantly higher
539
      fidelity, preserves complex scene structures, and enables easy and precise editing.

823
