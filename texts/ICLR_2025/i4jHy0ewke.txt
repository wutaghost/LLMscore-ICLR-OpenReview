011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            General anesthesia transitions the brain from a conscious to an unconscious state,
014            but how does sensory processing differ between these conditions? To address this
015            question, we trained neural network encoding models to predict the responses of
016            auditory cortical neurons to natural sounds in both awake and anesthetized ferrets.
017
               Utilizing mechanistic interpretability methods, such as feature visualization, lin-
               earization and sparse autoencoders, we analyzed these networks tuning and con-
018
               nectivity to uncover key differences in sensory processing. We found that anesthe-
019
               sia decouples neural connectivity, shifting neurons from polysemantic (respond-
020            ing to multiple inputs) to monosemantic (responding to a single input), resulting
021            in a lower-dimensional population code. These findings illuminate how anesthe-
022            sia alters neural connectivity and encoding, offering new insights into the neural
023            mechanisms underlying sensory processing.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   General anesthetics have the remarkable ability to shift a conscious mind into an unconscious state,
029   marking a significant milestone in modern medicine by alleviating patient suffering during major
030   surgery (Voss et al., 2019). Much of our understanding of visual and auditory processing in the
031   brain has been derived from studies on anesthetized animals, based on the assumption that sensory
032   processing is largely unaffected by anesthesia (Gaese & Ostwald, 2001; Sellers et al., 2015). How-
033   ever, this assumption is now known to be inaccurate, as anesthesia has been shown to alter sensory
034
      processes (Fontanini & Katz, 2008). In the auditory cortex, anesthesia modulates the variability
      of auditory-evoked responses (Kisley & Gerstein, 1999), reduces the number of active neurons, in-
035
      creases response onset latency (Cheung et al., 2001; Noda & Takahashi, 2015), and inhibits sustained
036
      firing to preferred sounds (Wang et al., 2005). Despite these findings, it remains largely unclear how
037   the spectrotemporal tuning properties of auditory cortical neurons are affected by anesthesia.
038
039   In this work, we used neural networks to predict the responses of neurons in the primary auditory
040
      cortex (A1) of awake and anesthetized ferrets to natural sounds. Using advances in machine learn-
      ing interpretability techniques, we analyzed differences in tuning and connectivity between these
041
      networks to reveal key distinctions in auditory cortical processing between awake and anesthetized
042
      states. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
043
044         1. We found that adding a gated output to various encoding models of the auditory cortex
045            improves their performance and obtains state-of-the-art response prediction.
046
            2. We demonstrated how the highly nonlinear spectrotemporal tuning of awake A1 neurons
047
               can be visualized using feature visualization (Olah et al., 2017).
048
049         3. We identified awake A1 neurons as polysemantic (tuned to different spectrotemporal in-
050
               puts) and anesthetized A1 neurons as monosemantic (tuned to fixed spectrotemporal inputs)
               using an ANN-linerization technique (Keshishian et al., 2020).
051
052         4. Using principal component analysis (PCA) and sparse autoencoders (SAEs) (Olshausen
053            & Field, 1997), we showed that the population code of awake A1 neurons has a higher
               dimensionality compared to that of anesthetized neurons.

054
               5. Finally, we found that the neural network trained on anesthetized responses exhibits sparser
055               connectivity to the output units, suggesting that anesthesia decouples cortical connectivity.

060   A1 neural datasets We utilized two publicly available datasets of single-unit responses to natural
061   sounds recorded in the primary auditory cortex (A1) (and anterior auditory field) of awake and
062   anesthetized ferrets. The awake dataset contains responses from N = 185 neurons to 299 4-second
063   sound clips (Lopez Espejo et al., 2019), while the anesthetized dataset consists of responses from
064   N = 73 neurons to 20 5-second sound clips (Harper et al., 2016). Anesthesia was induced using
065   a combination of ketamine and medetomidine. We excluded four sound clips from training in both
066
      datasets and reserved them for testing model performance. The anesthetized dataset has 20 repeats
      for each sound in the training and test set, and the awake dataset has no repeats for the training set
067
      and 10 repeats for the test set. Neuronal responses were binned at 4ms intervals and we trained the
068
      encoding models to predict the trial-averaged responses to the sounds. Both datasets include natural
069   sounds of human speech, animal vocalizations, and environmental sounds, and exhibit a similar
070   average power spectrum, with power declining from the higher to the lower frequencies (Appendix
071   Fig. 1), as has similarly been reported in prior work (Machens et al., 2004). Both datasets exhibit
072   similar firing rate distributions, with the firing rates being slightly more elevated for the awake
073   (14.28Hz) than the anesthetized (12.54Hz) responses (Appendix Fig. 2).
074
075   Cochleagrams Encoding models of the auditory cortex typically use spectrograms (or cochlea-
076   grams) - frequency-decomposed representations approximating cochlear transformations - rather
077   than raw sound waveforms (Linden et al., 2003; Gill et al., 2006; David et al., 2009; Harper et al.,
078   2016). We constructed each sound clip’s cochleagram by applying a short-time Fourier transform
079   with a 4ms temporal resolution; calculating the power over C = 64 logarithmically spaced fre-
080   quency channels (500-22000Hz) via weighted summation of overlapping triangular windows; and
081   finally taking the logarithm of the power in each time-frequency bin. All cochleagrams were nor-
082
      malized using the mean and standard deviation computed from the corresponding training datasets.
083

190   3.1   I MPROVING RESPONSE PREDICTION IN AWAKE AND ANESTHETIZED AUDITORY CORTEX
191         VIA GATING
192
193   We fitted prior auditory encoding models of increasing complexity to the awake and anesthetized
194   auditory recordings, including the LN (Atencio et al., 2008), NRF (Harper et al., 2016) and TC
195
      (Pennington & David, 2023) models. Additionally, we extended each of these models with a gating
      mechanism that modulates the predicted responses by multiplicatively adjusting the output. This ex-
196
      tension was motivated by two factors: 1. in neuroscience, auditory cortex responses are known to be
197
      temporally modulated (i.e. changing in response amplitude to the same input stimulus depending on
198   recent stimulus history) (Willmore & King, 2023), and 2. in machine learning, gating mechanisms
199   have been shown to improve neural network performance on sequential tasks (Cho, 2014; Dauphin
200   et al., 2017; Shazeer, 2020).
201
      We qualitatively observed that the TC model with the gating mechanism provided the best match to
202
      both awake and anesthetized neural responses, particularly in capturing peak firing rates compared to
203
      the other models (Fig. 1a). To quantify prediction performance, we used the normalized correlation
204   coefficient (CCnorm), where CCnorm = 0 represents chance-level prediction and CCnorm = 1
205   indicates a perfect fit. Including the gating mechanism improved the performance of all models, with
206   the gated-TC model achieving the highest performance on both the awake (CCnorm = 0.64) and
207   anesthetized (CCnorm = 0.75) datasets (Fig. 1b). We found the inclusion of the gating mechanism
208   in the TC model to reduce the peak activity MSE over all neurons on both the awake (3.6%) and
209   anesthetized (4.4%) datasets (Fig. 1c). Thus, the reduction in prediction error during the peak
210   activity periods appears to be the drive in performance of the gating mechanism. However, as this
211   improvement was largely similar between the two datasets, suggests that anaesthesia does not disrupt
212
      the biological gating mechanisms within cortical auditory neurons.
213   All models predicted responses in the anesthetized state more accurately than in the awake state
214   (Fig. 1b). The gated-TC model showed a larger relative performance increase over the gated-LN
215   model in the awake condition (31%) compared to the anesthetized condition (13%). As the gated-
      TC model contains more non-linear operations (i.e., convolutions and stacking of layers) compared

251   3.2                  V ISUALIZING NON - LINEAR SPECTROTEMPORAL TUNING OF AWAKE AUDITORY
252                        CORTICAL NEURONS

254   Spectrotemporal tuning of auditory neurons is typically assessed using the spectrotemporal receptive
255
      field (STRF) (the weights of the LN model) (Atencio et al., 2008; Rabinowitz et al., 2011). In our
      analysis, the STRFs of the anesthetized neurons exhibited distinct tuning patterns, such as excitation
256
      near the present followed by lagging inhibition into the past (DeCharms et al., 1998). In contrast,
257
      many of the STRFs of the awake neurons generally lacked clear tuning. To further investigate the
258   tuning properties of the awake neurons, we used feature visualization (FV) (Olah et al., 2017). This
259   is an interpretability technique often used to study the tuning properties of units in convolutional
260   neural networks trained on images, where neural network inputs are iteratively optimized via gra-
261   dient descent to maximally activate particular unit responses. After optimization, these learnt input
262   weights give an estimate of a units tuning preference. As commonly done (Olah et al., 2017), we ap-
263   plied an L2 smoothing regularization constraint to the input weights during training, where different
264   levels of regularization can drastically influence the structure of the learnt input weights (Appendix
265   Fig. 3).
266   We observed that the FV-STRFs of the anesthetized neurons were more strongly correlated with
267   their corresponding LN-STRFs (CC = 0.34), compared to the awake neurons (CC = 0.21), across
268   a range of smoothing regularizations (Fig. 2a). The FV-STRFs of the anesthetized neurons closely
269   resembled the LN-STRFs. In contrast, we found that the LN-STRFs of the awake neurons that did
      not exhibit clear tuning properties reveal a more distinctive spectrotemporal structure in their FV-

299
300   FV-STRFs provide a clear interpretation of the stimuli that maximally drive an auditory neuron’s
301   response at a fixed point in time, but they do not capture how tuning evolves over time. Dynamic-
302   STRFs - a neural network linearization technique - have been used to study the dynamic tuning
303
      properties of primary and non-primary auditory cortical neurons from electrocorticography record-
      ings in neurosurgical patients with epilepsy (Keshishian et al., 2020). We used this technique to
304
      examine the differences in dynamic tuning between electrophysiologically recorded responses in
305
      awake and anesthetized A1 neurons, using the predicted responses of the gated-TC model.
306
307   We found the DSTRFs of the awake neurons to exhibit dynamic spectrotemporal tuning in time (Fig.
308   3a). When visualized in a two-dimensional space using UMAP, the dimensionality-reduced repre-
309
      sentations of the DSTRFs formed distinct clusters, each corresponding to different spectrotemporal
      tunings. Applying PCA to each DSTRF showcased the variance in each DSTRF to be dispersed
310
      across multiple principal components (PCs). In contrast, the DSTRFs of the anesthetized neurons
311
      showed a fixed spectrotemporal structure over time. The UMAP visualization did not reveal distinct
312   clusters, and the majority of the DSTRF variance was concentrated in the first PC dimension (Fig.
313   3b).
314
315
      To quantify these observations across the neuron population, we measured each neuron’s DSTRF
      tuning variability (the variability in spectrotemporal tuning) and activity (the fraction of time the
316
      dynamic-STRF is ”on”) (see Methods). We found that awake neurons exhibited significantly greater
317
      tuning variability (Fig. 3c) and activity (Fig. 3d) compared to the anesthetized neurons. To address
318   the potential confound of the awake dataset containing more training stimuli than the anesthetized
319   dataset, we retrained the gated-TC model on a uniformly sampled subset of awake stimuli, matched
320   in number to the anesthetized training stimuli. We found the DSTRFs to still exhibit dynamic tuning
321   (Appendix Fig. 4) and the differences in tuning variability and activity to persist (Appendix Fig.
322   5), although the tuning variability was slightly reduced. Further subsampling the number of stimuli
323   and neurons to be matching also did not change the significance in the tuning variability and activity
      (Appendix Fig. 6). Lastly, comparing the tuning measures on the same datasets sounds with an

      3.4                         T HE CONSCIOUS AUDITORY CORTEX EXHIBITS A HIGHER - DIMENSIONAL POPULATION
362
                                  CODE THAN THE UNCONSCIOUS AUDITORY CORTEX
363
364
      Having identified different coding properties at the individual neuron level, we sought to understand
365
      how these characteristics influence the population code - the collective response of neurons in pri-
366   mary auditory cortex. We examined this question by analyzing the dimensionality of the population
367   code, where lower dimensionality indicates more redundancy and less capacity for encoding, while
368   higher dimensionality suggests greater diversity and complexity for neuronal encoding of diverse
369   sounds.
370
      We first applied PCA to the trial-averaged population responses from the held-out test datasets
371
      (Fig.4a) and found the awake population code to require more principal components to capture
372   the same amount of variance compared to the anesthetized neural population (Fig.4b). We made a
373   similar observation when applying PCA to the trial-averaged population responses from the train-
374   ing datasets (Appendix Fig. 8). This suggests that the awake neural population exhibits a higher
375   dimensionality. To corroborate this observation, we examined the population codes using sparse
376   autoencoders (SAEs) (Fig.4c), which serve as a normative model of brain function (Olshausen &
377   Field, 1997) and are increasingly being used to interpret the hidden-activity of large language mod-
      els (Cunningham et al., 2023).

404   SAEs are autoencoders with an overcomplete basis (i.e. more hidden units than input/output units)

409
      decoder vector) is thought to represent a different variable/feature within the networks hidden activ-
      ity (Cunningham et al., 2023). Although technically, we slightly abuse the conventional definition
410
      of the dimensionality of a space here, as the hidden states of the SAE may be non-orthogonal. We
411
      found the awake neural population to require a larger number of decoder vectors to achieve the same
412   reconstruction accuracy as the anesthetized population, demonstrating the awake neurons to exhibit
413   a higher-capacity coding regime than the anesthetized neurons (Fig.4d). To facilitate a more direct
414   comparison between the awake and anesthetized datasets, we randomly subsampled neurons from
415   the awake dataset to match the number of neurons in the anesthetized dataset.

      3.5            A NESTHESIA DECOUPLES CORTICAL CONNECTIVITY
418
419
      The question remains how anesthesia transforms the high-dimensional population code of poly-
420   semantic neurons in the auditory cortex into a lower-dimensional code of monosemantic neurons.
421   Anesthesia is known to suppress cortical connectivity (Voss et al., 2019). To investigate if disrup-
422   tions in connectivity underpin our observed differences in sensory processing and population coding,
423   we analyzed the number of incoming connections to the output units in the gated-TC model trained
424   on the awake and anesthetized datasets.
425
      We quantified the incoming connections to each output unit by counting the number of absolute
426   weight values exceeding a threshold of σ standard deviations above their mean. We observed the
427   model trained on the awake dataset to have more incoming connections per output unit compared to
428   the model trained on the anesthetized dataset (Fig. 5a), finding a statistically significant difference
429   in the number of connections across various threshold values (Fig. 5b). In contrast, the magnitude
430   of the weights was largely similar between the models across the various threshold values, apart
431   from the highest threshold values (Fig. 5c). These findings suggest that our observed differences in
      sensory processing and population coding between the conscious and unconscious auditory cortex

      4      D ISCUSSION
458
459   We explored how general anesthesia affects sensory processing in the primary auditory cortex (A1)
460
      using auditory encoding models trained to predict neuronal responses to natural sounds in awake and
      anesthetized ferrets. Utilizing mechanistic interpretability techniques, we discovered that anesthesia
461
      induces a shift in neuronal tuning from polysemantic - where neurons respond to multiple spec-
462
      trotemporal features - to monosemantic, responding to a single spectrotemporal input. Furthermore,
463   we found this transition to reduce the dimensionality of the population code due to a decoupling of
464   connectivity in the models.
465
466
      Gated encoding models of auditory cortex The gating mechanism in neural networks modulates
467
      the activations of units and has been shown to enhance the capacity of recurrent (Hochreiter, 1997;
468
      Cho, 2014), convolutional (Dauphin et al., 2017) and Transformer network architectures (Shazeer,
469   2020; Hua et al., 2022). Similarly, we found that extending standard encoding models of the auditory
470   cortex with a gated-output to improve response predictions to natural sounds, by multiplicatively
471   scaling the model output. This mechanism likely captures the time-varying sensitivity of sensory
472   neurons to input stimuli (Weber et al., 2019; Willmore & King, 2023), where neurons within the
473   auditory pathway have been shown to adjust their responses to changes in mean sound level (Dean
474   et al., 2005), sound contrast (Rabinowitz et al., 2011); and more complex features like timbre (Pi-
475   azza et al., 2018) and spatial-statistics (Gleiss et al., 2019). Such adaptive mechanisms are believed
476   to expand the sensory encoding range to accommodate a broader spectrum of stimulus values (Bar-
477
      low et al., 1961; Laughlin, 1981). We found the gating mechanism to reduce response prediction
      error during periods of peak activity, which likely explains its performance boost. Thus, the gating
478
      mechanism likely ”amplifies” the sensitivity of the auditory encoding models to particular stimuli
479
      - in contrast to traditional neural network models like the LSTM in which the gating mechanism
480   rather ”forgets” particular sensory input. Although the gating mechanism improves response predic-
481   tion, it is still far from perfectly capturing the response properties of cortical A1 neurons to natural
482   sounds. The missing gap in performance may be attributed to missing non-linear mechanisms in
483   the model, not enough training data or due to internal biological states which cannot be recorded.
484   Lastly, it would be interesting to explore other output mechanisms in future work such as dynamic
485   thresholding, of only outputting a predicted response when a certain criterion is met. This may boost
      performance by filtering out less relevant signals that the gating mechanism may not capture.

486
      Anesthesia induced notable changes in auditory cortical processing How anesthesia affects
487   sensory processing is still not completely understood. We found that in awake ferrets, neurons in the
488   primary auditory cortex exhibit polysemantic spectrotemporal tuning, responding to multiple spec-
489   trotemporal features. Under anesthesia, neurons shift to monosemantic tuning, preferring a fixed
490   spectrotemporal input. This shift aligns with previous observations: distinguishing complex sound
491   classes becomes difficult under anesthesia (Plourde et al., 2006), auditory processing in associated
492   cortical areas is disrupted (Krom et al., 2020), and A1 neurons become less selective to specific
493   frequency ranges (Gaese & Ostwald, 2001). Our findings reconcile these observations, as reduced
494   dynamic tuning under anesthesia could explain decreased selectivity and processing disruptions.
495
      We also observed that the population code in A1 exhibits reduced dimensionality under anesthe-
      sia, indicating decreased cortical encoding capacity. This corroborates Noda & Takahashi (2015),
496
      who reported increased noise correlations in the auditory cortex under anesthesia, leading to more
497
      redundant neural activity.
498
499
      Disruptions to cortical connectivity: top-down or bottom-up? We found that our neural net-
500
      work model trained on the awake dataset had more incoming connections per output unit compared
501
      to the model trained on the anesthetized dataset. This suggests that the changes in spectrotempo-
502   ral tuning and population coding of A1 neurons under anesthesia may result from a decoupling of
503   cortical connectivity. However, it remains unclear whether these decoupled connections correspond
504   to disruptions in corticocortical (top-down) or thalamocortical (bottom-up) pathways, as both have
505   been shown to be suppressed during general anesthesia (Voss et al., 2019). Given that our model
506   uses a strictly feedforward architecture, it primarily captures bottom-up processes. Therefore, our
507   results support the notion that the changes in A1 neuron tuning and population coding during anes-
508   thesia result, at least in part, from disruptions in thalamocortical connectivity. This interpretation is
509   consistent with the findings of Schumacher et al. (2011), who reported that the excitability of audi-
510
      tory midbrain neurons is reduced during anesthesia, which would result in less afferent input to the
      cortical neurons. Nevertheless, it is also possible that disruptions in top-down cortical inputs also
511
      contribute to the observed changes under anesthesia. Future studies incorporating recurrent archi-
512
      tectures could help elucidate the relative contributions of top-down and bottom-up pathways to the
513   alterations in auditory processing during anesthesia.
514
515
      Experimental limitations Although we used neural recordings from the same sensory region (A1)
516
      and species (ferret), the awake and anesthetized recordings were obtained from different labs us-
517   ing different animals and in response to different sets of natural sounds. This introduces potential
518   confounds related to individual variability and stimulus differences that could affect the results.
519   However, both datasets include similar natural sounds of human speech, animal vocalizations, and
520   environmental sounds, which we found to exhibit similar average power spectra between the datasets
521   (Appendix Fig. 1). Additionally, both datasets exhibit similar tuning properties, where we found the
522   averaged population STRF of the neurons in the awake and anesthetized datasets to exhibit a tempo-
523   rally asymmetric power profile with excitation near the present followed by lagging inhibition into
524   the past (Appendix Fig. 9), as has similarly been reported in prior work (DeCharms et al., 1998).
525   Although the datasets were recorded using different hardware and spike-sorting software, they have
526   previously been used in prior work for side-by-side comparison, where Rahman et al. (2020) used
527   these datasets to contrast the performance of various cochleagram models in predicting auditory
528   cortical responses under awake and anesthetized conditions. Although the anesthetized recordings
529   were done using silicon probe electrodes and the awake recordings were done using tungsten mi-
530   croelectrodes, these different systems have previously been shown to provide comparable action
531
      potential recordings (Saha et al., 2010). Furthermore, both datasets were gathered from reputable
      labs, which have likely calibrated their recording systems to be as precise as possible. Thus, it
532
      seems unlikely that the differences between the different labs’ experimental setups would explain
533
      the auditory tuning differences we report. Where possible, we attempted to mitigate the potential
534   confounding issues in the dynamic spectrotemporal tuning analysis (section 3.3) by qualitatively
535   comparing DSTRFs (Appendix Fig. 4), by matching the number of training stimuli between the
536   datasets (Appendix Fig. 5); by matching the number of training stimuli and neurons between the
537   datasets (Appendix Fig. 6); and by comparing the tuning measures on the same datasets with an un-
538   matched and matched number of training samples (Appendix Fig. 7). We also matched the number
539   of neurons in the population-coding dimensionality analysis (section 3.4). Future follow-up work
      should ideally obtain recordings in the same animals to the same sounds.
