009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have seen significant advances in recent years,
012
               yet their application to multigraphs, where parallel edges exist between the same
013
               pair of nodes, remains under-explored. Standard GNNs, designed for simple
014            graphs, compute node representations by combining all connected edges at once,
015            without distinguishing between edges from different neighbors. There are some
016            GNN architectures proposed specifically for multigraph tasks, yet these architec-
017            tures perform only node-level aggregation in their message-passing layers, which
018            limits their expressive power. Furthermore, these approaches either lack permu-
019            tation equivariance when a strict total edge ordering is absent, or fail to preserve
020            the topological structure of the multigraph. To address all these shortcomings, we
021            propose MEGA-GNN, a unified framework for message passing on multigraphs
022
               that can effectively perform diverse graph learning tasks. Our approach introduces
               a two-stage aggregation process in the message passing layers: first, parallel edges
023
               are aggregated, followed by a node-level aggregation that operates on aggregated
024
               messages from distinct neighbors. We show that MEGA-GNN supports permu-
025            tation equivariance and invariance properties. We also show that MEGA-GNN
026            is universal given a strict total order on the edges. Experiments on synthetic and
027            real-world financial transaction datasets demonstrate that MEGA-GNN either sig-
028            nificantly outperforms or is on par with the accuracy of state-of-the-art solutions.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) (Xu et al. (2019); Gilmer et al. (2017); Veličković et al. (2018);
035   Corso et al. (2020); Hamilton et al. (2017)) have become Swiss Army knives for learning on graph-
036   structured data. However, their widespread adoption has primarily focused on simple graphs, where
037   only a single edge can connect a given pair of nodes. This simplification overlooks a crucial as-
038   pect of many real-world scenarios, where multigraphs, graphs that feature parallel edges between
039
      the same pair of nodes, are common. For instance, financial transaction networks, communication
      networks and transportation systems are often modeled as multigraphs, allowing multiple different
040
      interactions between the same two nodes. Existing GNN architectures are inherently limited in han-
041
      dling such multigraph structures since they aggregate messages only at the node level, combining
042   information from all incoming edges at once without distinguishing between parallel edges and their
043   sources. Although such an aggregation mechanism is sufficient to construct provably powerful GNN
044   models on simple graphs (Maron et al. (2019); Xu et al. (2019)), the expressivity of such models are
045   theoretically and empirically limited on directed multigraphs as shown by Egressy et al. (2024).
046
      In the literature, only two GNN-based solutions, namely Multi-GNN (Egressy et al. (2024)) and
047
      ADAMM (Sotiropoulos et al. (2023)), have been proposed for multigraphs, yet both exhibit critical
048   limitations as summarized in Table 1. Multi-GNN, while addressing expressivity concerns, fails
049   to preserve permutation equivariance. ADAMM, on the other hand, fails to effectively support
050   diverse graph learning tasks, thus limiting its practical applicability. To address these limitations,
051   we propose Multi-EdGe Aggregation GNNs, henceforth referred to as MEGA-GNN. MEGA-GNN
052   is a unified message passing framework specifically designed for multigraphs. It employs a two-
053   stage message aggregation process: first, parallel edges between the same two nodes are aggregated,
      and then, the aggregated messages from distinct neighbors are further combined at the node level.

132   3     M ULTIGRAPH M ESSAGE PASSING
133
134   This section introduces a novel message-passing scheme for multigraphs which combines node-level
135   aggregation with multi-edge aggregation and supports a general set of graph learning tasks such as
136   node, edge, and graph classification. Additionally, the resulting models are permutation invariant or
137   equivariant depending on the chosen multi-edge aggregation function and the downstream task.
138
139   3.1   M OTIVATION FOR M ULTI -E DGE AGGREGATIONS IN M ULTIGRAPH GNN S
140
141   We argue that a two-stage aggregation approach is more powerful than a single-stage aggregation in
142   a multigraph setting, wherein aggregation of the parallel edges between the same source and destina-
143   tion nodes is performed before a node-level aggregation. We first give a motivating example, which
144   shows that two-stage aggregation functions are more expressive than their single-stage counterparts.
145   Consider the example graph depicted in Figure 3, with four edges e1 , e2 , e3 and e4 . Suppose that we
146   have an aggregation function that operates on multisets and can simultaneously compute both the
147   sum and the maximum of the elements included in the set. To demonstrate that two-stage aggrega-
148   tion approach is more powerful than the single-stage one, we will show that two-stage approach can
149   perform any computation that the single-stage approach can, but not vice versa.
150   A single-stage scheme, can compute only (SUM{{e1 , e2 , e3 , e4 }} and MAX{{e1 , e2 , e3 , e4 }}). The
151   two-stage approach can also compute these results by aggregating the edges in pairs first:
152   (SUM{{SUM{{e1 , e2 }}, SUM{{e3 , e4 }}}}, MAX{{MAX{{e1 , e2 }}, MAX{{e3 , e4 }}}}). However, the
153   two-stage aggregation allows for more nuanced computations that the single-stage approach cannot
154   perform. For example, the two-stage approach can compute SUM{{MAX{{e1 , e2 }}, MAX{{e3 , e4 }}}}
155   and MAX{{SUM{{e1 , e2 }}, SUM{{e3 , e4 }}}}, which the single-stage approach cannot compute.
156   This distinction is significant; the two-stage aggregation enables us to capture the statistics of edges
157   originating from different neighbors. Consider a financial application, in which a receiver may
158   receive the highest total payment from one sender (Sender 1), while the highest single payment may
159   originate from another sender (Sender 2). Using the two-stage scheme, we can detect the maximum
160   of the sums of payments from each sender, allowing us to distinguish between their contributions.
161   In contrast, the single-stage scheme can only compute the maximum among all payments without
      differentiating between identical and distinct senders, which can obscure critical insights.

196
197   Definition 3.1. (Strict Total Order) A multiset of edges E in a graph is said to have a strict total
198   order under a binary relation < if the following conditions hold for every a, b and c ∈ E: (1) a ̸= b,
199
      either a < b or b < a, (2) not a < a, (3) if a < b and b < c, then a < c (see Munkres (2000), p. 22).

201   ariant continuous functions defined on graphs (Keriven & Peyré (2019)). According to Loukas
202   (2020), universality is achieved if the following conditions are satisfied: there are enough layers,
203
      the layers are sufficiently expressive and wide, and the nodes can uniquely distinguish each other.
204   Definition 3.3. (Permutation Equivariance) A function ψ is said to be permutation equivariant
205   with respect to node and edge permutations if, for any permutation ρ acting on the nodes and edges
206   of a graph G = (V, E), the following holds:
207                                    ψ(ρ ◦ G(V, E)) = ρ ◦ ψ(G(V, E))

      3.5    P ROPERTIES
299
300   In this section, we establish several key theoretical properties of MEGA-GNN and Multi-GNN,
301   analysing and comparing their expressiveness and permutation invariance under different conditions.
302   In particular, we show that MEGA-GNN is always permutation equivariant, a property not shared
303   by Multi-GNN. Next, we prove that MEGA-GNN is universal given a strict total order on the edges.
304   Theorem 3.1. (Permutation Equivariance). Given permutation-invariant EdgeAgg and AGG
305   functions, the framework defined by Equations 4, 5, and 6 is permutation-equivariant.
306
307   The proof of Theorem 3.1 is provided in Appendix A.2. For neural networks operating on graph-
308   structured data, permutation equivariance is a crucial property, ensuring that the model’s output
309   remains consistent regardless of the ordering of nodes and edges. Our model is explicitly designed
310
      to maintain permutation equivariance at both the node and edge levels. Permutation invariance for
      graph-level outputs can be achieved by using a permutation-invariant graph readout function.
311
312   Lemma 3.1. The message-passing framework defined by Equations 4, 5, and 6 can compute unique
313   node IDs in connected multigraphs given a strict total ordering of the edges.
314
      The proof of Lemma 3.1 (provided in A.3) shows that, when a strict total ordering among the edges
315   (e.g., based on timestamps or other contextual edge features) is available, the model is able to assign
316   unique identifiers to the nodes in a connected multigraph.
317
      Theorem 3.2. Universality. Given sufficient layers with sufficient expressiveness and width as well
318
      as a strict total ordering of the edges, the MEGA-GNN is universal.
319
320   The definition of universality is given in Definition 3.2. Lemma 3.1 establishes that the MEGA-
321   GNN can compute unique node IDs in connected multigraphs when a contextually-driven strict total
322   ordering of edges is provided, which makes nodes to uniquely distinguish each other. Assuming
323   MEGA-GNN also has sufficient layers, depth and expressiveness, it meets all the conditions pre-
      sented in Loukas (2020) and is, therefore, universal in the sense defined by Loukas (2020).


                                                             6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      We emphasize that the ability to assign unique node IDs does not violate the permutation equivari-
325   ance of MEGA-GNN. In practice, the model does not explicitly assign IDs or rely on node/edge
326   indices or their order. Instead, edge features such as timestamps provide a natural strict total order-
327   ing. As shown theoretically, if such an ordering exists, MEGA-GNN can assign unique node IDs,
328   ensuring that permutation equivariance and universality can coexist without conflict.
329
      All the above claims remain valid when the bi-directional message passing mechanism of Section
330
      3.4 is used. Extending Theorem 3.1 to the bi-directional case is straightforward, and the proof of
331   Lemma 3.1 in Appendix A.3 already incorporates bi-directional message passing.
332
333   To sum up, the theoretical analyses presented in this section lead to the following insights:
334
335          • Both MEGA-GNN and Multi-GNN are permutation equivariant and universal when there
336            is a strict total ordering of the edges.
337
338          • When such an ordering is not available, 1) MEGA-GNN is permutation equivariant but
339            non-universal, and 2) Multi-GNN is universal but not permutation equivariant.

      study, we use the Ethereum transaction dataset (Chen et al. (2021)), where accounts are treated as

472   5     R ELATED W ORK
473
474   The expressive power of GNNs is crucial in evaluating their capabilities, and is mainly discussed
475   in the context of simple graphs, which can have only a single edge between two graph nodes. Xu
476   et al. (2019) demonstrated that the expressive power of standard message-passing GNNs on simple
477   graphs is fundamentally limited by the 1-WL (Weisfeiler-Lehman) test for distinguishing isomor-
478   phic graphs. To address this limitation, they introduced the Graph Isomorphism Network (GIN),
479   which is provably the most expressive among standard message-passing GNNs. Recent research on
480   enhancing GNN expressivity has pursued several directions. Higher-order GNNs were proposed by
481   Maron et al. (2019); Morris et al. (2019), Bouritsas et al. (2023); Barceló et al. (2021), leverage pre-
482
      computed local structural features around target nodes, while Bevilacqua et al. (2022; 2024); Frasca
      et al. (2022) focus on increasing expressivity through subgraph-based aggregation techniques.
483
484   The expressiveness of GNNs is closely linked to their universality, as more expressive models can
485   approximate a broader class of functions. Sato et al. (2021); Loukas (2020) demonstrated that incor-
      porating unique node identifiers can make GNNs universal, though this comes at the cost of losing


                                                                                                9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      permutation invariance. However, Abboud et al. (2021) showed that partially randomized node fea-
487   tures can achieve universality while preserving permutation invariance. In a related line of work, set
488   functions, such as those proposed in Zaheer et al. (2017), have been shown to be universal under
489   certain input constraints, and Fuchs* & Veličković* (2023) made the connection between univer-
490   sal set and graph functions. While not theoretically more expressive than GIN, Corso et al. (2020)
491   demonstrated that combining arbitrary aggregation functions leads to better empirical performance.
492   In Kortvelesy et al. (2023), a learnable aggregation functions were proposed that improve sample
493   efficiency compared to both PNA and DeepSet-based aggregations on multiset neighborhoods.
494   There has been relatively little work on GNNs for multigraphs. Two notable prior works are
495   Sotiropoulos et al. (2023) and Egressy et al. (2024). The former (ADAMM) transforms a multigraph
496   into a simple graph using a DeepSet-based approach Zaheer et al. (2017), while the latter (Multi-
497   GNN) uses simple adaptations on top of baseline GNN methods to achieve universality. However,
498   these methods still simplify the graph structure or lose permutation equivariance.
499   Multi-level message aggregation schemes have been proposed also in other settings, but these meth-
500   ods are not directly applicable to multigraphs. For instance, Hypergraph GNNs (Feng et al. (2019);
501   Huang & Yang (2021)) first aggregate node features within each hyperedge to compute latent fea-
502   tures of the hyperedges, which are then used to aggregate node features. In contrast, our multigraph
503   GNNs directly handle multi-edges, representing multiple connections between the same pair of
504   nodes. Furthermore, our work differs from Relational GNNs (Schlichtkrull et al. (2018); Vashishth
505   et al. (2020)), which are designed for heterogeneous graphs with different edge types. MEGA-GNNs
506   do not require explicit edge type definitions and operate directly on edge features.
507
508   6   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
509
510   We contribute the first message-passing framework explicitly designed for multigraphs, enabling
511   a two-stage aggregation scheme in every message-passing layer. Unlike the method of Egressy
512   et al. (2024), our framework guarantees permutation equivariance without strict total ordering of the
513   edges. Our framework also achieves universality when we make the same assumptions made by
514   Egressy et al. (2024). Furthermore, unlike the method of Sotiropoulos et al. (2023), we do not trans-
515   form a multigraph into a simple graph before the message passing layers. Thus, our message passing
516   framework supports a more general set of multigraph learning tasks including the classification of
517   multigraph edges.
518   Our experiments on financial transaction datasets show that MEGA-GNN significantly outperforms
519   or matches the accuracy of state-of-the-art multigraph GNN models. Notably, when detecting illicit
520   transactions on the synthetic AML datasets of Altman et al. (2023), we achieve improvements of
521   up to 13.31% in minority-class F1 scores over the method of Egressy et al. (2024), demonstrating
522   the effectiveness of MEGA-GNN. When detecting phishing accounts on the Ethereum transaction
523   dataset of (Chen et al. (2021)), we achieve improvements of around 20% in minority-class F1 scores
524
      over the method of Sotiropoulos et al. (2023), while slightly improving the results of Egressy et al.
      (2024).
525
526   Limitations and Future Work. Our work does not consider dynamically evolving multigraphs
527   with several different snapshots. Future work could investigate strategies to support such setups.
528   Other interesting research directions include improving the scalability and GPU performance of our
529   methods, and building federated multigraph learning solutions with differential privacy guarantees.

      A.3   P ROOF OF L EMMA 3.1
791
792   Proof: Given a graph G(V, E) with n nodes and m edges, assume that there is a strict total ordering
793   among the graph edges represented by an edge labeling function L : E → N ∩ [1, m], which assigns
794   unique labels to the edges. We will prove that MEGA-GNN can compute unique node IDs under
795   these assumptions.
796
      Egressy et al. (2024) showed that a GNN can mimic a Breadth-First Search (BFS) algorithm to com-
797
      pute unique node IDs given pre-computed port IDs for the edges. We follow the same BFS-based
798   approach and derive unique node ids without relying on pre-computed port IDs. Instead of the pre-
799   computed port ids, we use the unique edge labels provided by L(e) to guide the node ID assignment
800   process. As in Egressy et al. (2024), we use the Universal Approximation Theorem Hornik et al.
801   (1989) for MLPs, to avoid explicit construction of the MEGA-GNN layers. We also assume that
802   the MEGA-GNN aggregates the multi-edges by computing their minimum, which is followed by a
803   node-level aggregation, where an MLP is applied element-wise to the incoming messages, followed
804   by another minimum computation.
805   Following the approach of Egressy et al. (2024), the node ID assignment algorithm starts from a root
806   node (also called the ego node) and assigns IDs to all the other nodes connected to it via message
807   passing . We are not going to reiterate the setup of the entire proof and focus on the differences.
808   Namely, instead of pre-computing port-IDs, we use an edge labeling function, which assumes a strict
809   total ordering among the edges based on the original edge features. In addition, the multi-edges are
      aggregated by selecting the edge with the minimum label.

      D     S CALABILITY AND M EMORY C ONSIDERATIONS
929
930   In the upcoming analysis, for simplicity, we omit the detailed examination of the computational
931
      complexity and memory overhead associated with the Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) layers. In-
      stead, we focus on the unique components of our method, specifically the two-stage aggregation
932
      mechanism, which constitutes the primary contribution of our approach.
933
934
935   D.1   S CALABILITY
936
937   Real-world graph data, particularly large and densely connected multigraphs, can pose significant
938   challenges in terms of computational cost and memory usage. To assess the scalability of our ap-
939   proach, we analyze the asymptotic computational complexity of the two-stage aggregation process
940
      in comparison to only node-level aggregation methods, comparing aggregation functions commonly
      used in the PNA framework.
941
942   Let V denote the set of nodes, E the multiset of edges in the sampled batch, and E supp ⊆ E denote
943   the support set of E, which consists of unique (i, j) pairs that have a multiplicity of at least one in
944   the multi-set E. Let denote the dimensionality of edge embeddings by d. The computational cost
945   of performing a single-stage aggregation (for functions such as sum, standard deviation, max, and
946
      min) is O(|E|d).
947   In our two-stage aggregation process, the complexity can be broken down as follows:
948
949             • Parallel Edge Aggregation: The cost of aggregating parallel edges is O(|E|d) for all
950               considered functions (sum, std, min, max).
951
                • Distinct Neighbor Aggregation: The cost of aggregating over distinct neighbors is
952
                  O(|E supp |d), where |E supp | is the number of unique edges.
953
954
      Thus, the total computational cost of our two-stage aggregation is O(|E|d + |E supp |d), which sim-
955
      plifies to O(|E|d) since |E supp | ≤ |E|. This shows that the asymptotic complexity of our method
956   is comparable to that of the single-stage aggregation methods, ensuring scalability to large multi-
957   graphs.
958
959
960   D.2   M EMORY OVERHEAD
961
962
      In terms of memory, we assume that the multigraph is attributed, with feature vectors associated
      with each edge. The memory required to store the edge embeddings is O(|E|d) in the standard case.
963
964   For the two-stage aggregation, additional memory is needed to store a tensor of size |E| that indexes
965   parallel edges in the multiset. This tensor is computed during preprocessing and reused across
966   batches, thus avoiding redundant calculations. Furthermore, a tensor of size |E supp | × d is created
967   dynamically during the forward pass to store the features of the artificial nodes corresponding to
968
      distinct neighbors.
969   As a result, the overall memory overhead per batch is O(|E|d + |E| + |E supp |d) = O(|E|d), which
970   scales linearly with the number of sampled edges. Importantly, this does not asymptotically alter the
971   overall memory complexity, meaning that the model remains efficient even as the size of the input
      graph increases.
