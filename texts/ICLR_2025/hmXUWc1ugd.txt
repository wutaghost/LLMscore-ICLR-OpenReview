009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep Learning has made remarkable strides, yet models trained under conventional
012            Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) approaches encounter challenges regarding
013            their generalization capabilities. In particular, a lack of robustness to spurious
014            correlations. In response, Group Robustness Methods (GRMs) have been developed
015            to combat them. These methods partition training datasets into distinct groups
016            based on spurious features and class labels and adjust their weighting in the loss
017            function. These methods show remarkable performance in dealing with spurious
018
               correlations. The underlying mechanisms for their success, however, are not so
               well understood. Our work contributes by shedding light on the learning dynamics
019
               of GRMs, through an empirical and theoretical analysis of them that reveals the
020
               differences in feature learning and the type of classifiers they learn versus ERM.
021            Surprisingly, both GRMs and ERM models retain spurious information in their
022            representations, even when it is irrelevant to the task at hand. We find evidence
023            that suggests that the key to GRMs’ success is two-fold: distributing prediction
024            across multiple features in representation space to avoid relying on few but spurious
025            attributes and incentivizing the classifier to become orthogonal to spurious features.
026            We verify our findings by proposing an upgrade to the Subsampling baseline
027            method called Group Distributionally Robust Feature Reweighting (GDRFR) that
028            is easy to compute and only requires a fraction of group labels during a finetuning
029
               phase and retrieve most of GRMs performance gains over ERM.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
      In the past decade (Bengio et al., 2014), Deep Learning has witnessed remarkable progress. However,
034
      models trained using the conventional Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) (Vapnik, 1991) approach
035
      encounter various challenges. Specifically, by prioritizing the reduction of training error, these
036   models often learn features optimized for this objective, sacrificing their generalization capabilities
037   in underrepresented groups. An exemplar of this phenomenon is the case of a model trained to
038   distinguish between cows and camels. When confronted with test images where the cows are on a
039   beach, and camels in grass, the model fails utterly. This is because the model has learned to use the
040   simpler background features, instead of the shape of the animal to make its classification. This issue
041   presents itself in the literature under different names such as spurious correlations (Arjovsky et al.,
042   2020), simplicity bias (Shah et al., 2020), or shortcut learning (Geirhos et al., 2020). It is not related
043   only to the image domain but to other modalities as well (Williams et al., 2018; Pavlopoulos et al.,
044
      2020).
045   To tackle this problem, some approaches have been proposed for enhancing model robustness. Among
046   these, Group Robustness Methods (GRMs) have demonstrated state of the art performance. Typically,
047   these methods involve partitioning the training dataset into distinct groups, often delineated by the
048   presence of spurious features and class labels and optimize to maximize the Worst Group performance
049   Hu et al. (2018). Group Distributionally Robust Optimization (GDRO) (Sagawa* et al., 2020) is
050
      one of the most effective among them, with many subsequent works following in its steps to try and
      overcome its drawbacks (Seo et al., 2022; Sohoni et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021; Idrissi et al., 2022;
051
      Kirichenko et al., 2023).
052
053   Yet, despite these recent advances, there remains a limited understanding of the inner workings behind
      current state-of-the-art models. In this context, our work contributes by shedding light on the learning

054
      dynamics of GRMs. We study what makes them so successful through an empirical and theoretical
055   lens. Our study is conducted in a controlled and limited setting, that is of vision datasets, but we
056   believe that our findings provide valuable insights that can motivate further research on how current
057   machine learning models handle relevant problems, such as learning in the presence of spurious
058   correlations. We believe that more research focused on understanding these mechanisms is crucial
059   for the advancement of our field.
060
      Our study begins by understanding what is learned in representations trained through GRMs. Our
061   first hypothesis is that spurious features are ignored from their representations. Without spurious
062   information in the representation, the model would necessarily be robust to spuriousness. Surprisingly,
063   we find that both GRMs and ERM still learn spurious features, even when they are not relevant for
064   the task at hand. We then turn to the role of the final linear classifier of a model. We show both
065   theoretically and empirically, that GRMs produce classifiers that will tend to be more orthogonal to
066   directions in representation space aligned with spurious attributes than classifiers produced by ERM.
067   However, we find this effect on its own is not enough to explain GRMs’ success.
068   Finally, we test the following hypothesis: GRMs work by disentangling spurious attributes from
069   invariant ones in representation space, allowing the classifier to ignore said spurious attributes by
070   becoming orthogonal to them. Surprisingly, we find the opposite is true: ERM produces more
071   disentangled features, however, GRMs tend to rely on multiple features for prediction, making it
072   more robust to rely on spurious ones, while ERM relies on less.
073   We also study Subsampling (Idrissi et al., 2022), a method that achieves good results without any
074   feature learning whatsoever. This method has allowed for the development of more practical GRMs,
075   in particular, Deep Feature Reweighting (Kirichenko et al., 2023). We find evidence that this method
076   is mostly supplementary to the feature-learning effects of GRMs. These findings allow us to propose a
077   new competitive and simple baseline: Group Distributionally Robust Feature Reweighting: a method
078   that only requires group labels during a single finetuning pass, with reduced data requirements as well.
079   It retrieves most of GDRO’s performance gains over ERM (at least 92% of them) and sometimes
080   even surpasses it.
081   Thus, our contributions can be summarized as follows:
082
083       • We show that ERM and GRM models tend to learn spurious information in their representations
084         in spite of them not being useful for the current task or being trained with specific methods to
085         mitigate performance based on such features.
086
087       • Group Robustness Methods such as Group DRO or Reweighting work by aligning the classifier
088         so it ignores spurious information in the learned representation. We show empirical evidence
089
            for this and show mathematical results that suggest why this should be so for the spurious
            correlation setting. Our theoretical results hold for an arbitrary non-linear SGD trained model.
090
091
          • We find evidence that suggests the difference between ERM and GRMs features lies in that
092         GRMs distribute predictive features across many dimensions while ERM tends to produce
093         representations that rely on less features for prediction. Surprisingly, Disentanglement is higher
094         for ERM than GRMs.
095
096       • We find evidence that suggests that Subsampling partially retrieves GRMs feature learning
097         capabilities, or put in other words, is supplementary to the feature learning effects of GRMs.
098         GRMs seem to learn specific features that ERM does not capture.
099
100
          • Our findings derive in an improved baseline that retrieves most of GDRO’s performance gains
            while only requiring a fraction of group labels for finetuning which requires only a single
101
            finetuning pass.

      2.4   ROBUSTNESS M ETHODS
155
      Group Robustness Methods (GRMs) are based around the idea of reweighting examples from the
156   training set. The most successful methods like Group DRO and Reweighting assume the training
157   distribution to be a mixture of G groups. In practice, what they do is to create G = |A| × |Y| groups.
158   Where |A| is the number of possible values an input spurious attribute might have, while |Y| is the
159   number of classes in the classification problem. To define these groups requires previous knowledge
160   of spurious correlations in the data. Plenty of methods attempt to forego this requirement for group
161   labelling in the training phase by finding proxies for it through clustering (Seo et al., 2022) or other
      means Liu et al. (2021).

355   4.3   E MPIRICAL E FFECTS OF F INETUNING F INAL C LASSIFIER L AYER ON GRM S
356   A necessary path forward is to test how important this classifier effect is. If most of the task of
357   achieving robustness is done by the final linear classifier, then it could allow us to forego the need for
358   feature learning using group labels. To test this, we train models on ERM then train the classification
359   heads using ERM, RW and GDRO for all datasets. Results are shown in Table 1. For MNIST-
360   CIFAR and CelebA, we can retrieve most of the gains from using GDRO, sometimes even surpass
361   them, simply by retraining the classifier using GDRO based on ERM features. This effect, however,
362
      dissipates when testing on Waterbirds, a more complex dataset where using ERM features with
      GDRO on the classifier yields similar results to ERM. If the spurious information is retained for all
363
      methods, but the classifier alone cannot ignore it, then it can mean at least one of two things: that
364
      spurious features are more entangled with the invariant features when training with ERM and/or that
365   even if disentangled, ERM has no incentive to use the invariant features. Supporting evidence for the
366   latter assumption is given by previous experiments as ERM-FT performs equally or worse than ERM
367   when finetuned using ERM. Therefore, to understand our failure in Waterbirds, we come back again
368   to studying representations, in particular, how different methods affect disentanglement.

414   This is what has been traditionally reported when analyzing representations through training of linear
415   probes. Theoretically, a good representation would have high disentanglement, high completeness and

420
      difference: Completeness is consistently lower across all datasets for GRMs than for ERM. This
421   implies that representations derived from GRMs are more robust because they allow predictability of
422   attributes to rely on more dimensions, which in the case of spurious correlations is highly relevant,
423   as one (spurious) dimension is too predictive. See Table 4 in the Appendix for expanded results,
424   including CelebA. Thus, what is expected of a good representation in the general case, may not
425   necessarily be desirable in the spurious correlation scenario, where having multiple dimensions that
426   are predictive of a latent attribute (i.e. low completeness) aids in making the model more robust to
427   focusing on potentially spurious dimensions for prediction.

429   4.5   S KIPPING GRM S FOR R EPRESENTATION L EARNING
430
431   Interestingly, as we discussed in Section 2.4.4, there exists a baseline method which has been applied
      in recent work (Kirichenko et al., 2023) with great success, which finetunes a classifier using SUBG,

432
      along with feature normalization, L1 loss and an ensemble method akin to model soups (Wortsman
433   et al., 2022). This suggests a path forward to dispense with group labels during training of the full
434   model. Therefore, it would seem to pose an alternative to the representation-learning effects of GRMs.
435   As our aim is to understand better how these methods work, we use a simplified version of this
436   method that only uses SUBG to finetune ERM features. We dub this method SUBG-FT.
437
      Table 1 shows that SUBG-FT is a massive improvement over ERM on all datasets, but underperforms
438
      against GDRO, except in Waterbirds. We believe this is because of GDRO’s effect on the classifier
439   which is better at ignoring spurious attributes. Therefore, finetuning a classifier on features derived
440   from ERM combining SUBG-FT and GDRO should yield most of GDRO’s performance, which
441   is our proposed new baseline method: Group Distributionally Robust Feature Reweighting. This
442   is listed as "GDRFR" in Table 2. We observe that indeed a performance of 88.99% is achieved on
443   Waterbirds (c=0.9) just finetuning the classifier, 2% over performance from full training based on
444   GDRO. This is relevant, as we used around 5% of the training data with group labels which are both
445   necessary for SUBG-FT and GDRO to finetune the classifier, allowing for practical uses on already
446   deployed systems. For MNIST-CIFAR and CelebA, GDRFR retrieves most of the performance of
447
      GDRO, (94,38% and 97,79% respectively for the hardest setting). In Waterbirds and CelebA, we
      see that GDRFR significantly outperforms SUBG-FT obtaining around 10% and 6% more gains
448
      respectively. Please see Table 5 in the Appendix for detailed information. An important practical
449
      observation, is that for these methods to perform well, the finetuning data should be different than the
450   training data. Finetuning on the same training data does not seem to work well.
451
452   Finally, we perform ablations on the size of the finetuning data. Results are shown in Table 3 in
453
      the Appendix. GDRFR which already uses little data (around 5-10% of the original training data
      size), works reasonably well with even 50% of that data, suggesting that it may work well in practical
454
      applications.

457   4.5.1    S UBSAMPLING IS SUPPLEMENTARY TO GDRO’ S FEATURE LEARNING EFFECTS
458
      Perhaps using SUBG-FT is just a complementary technique to GDRO. To test this, we train a classifier
459   using GDRFR (GDRO) on GDRO features. Table 2 shows that this (GDRO + GDRFR(GDRO)) does
460   not always work better than GDRO. In fact, when it does, the standard deviations are too high to assert
461   this with statistical significance. This suggests that whatever gains are made by using Subsampling in
462   the finetuning stage are achievable from better representations. That is to say, Subsampling seems to
463   be supplementary to GDRO’s feature learning effects.

467
468   5.1     M ETHODS FOR ROBUST L EARNING
469
      Invariant Risk Minimization (Arjovsky et al., 2020) modifies the loss function to make the model
470
      invariant to different environments. Group Reweighting schemes manipulate the importance of
471
      samples during training: Reweighting (Shimodaira, 2000) reweights group to eliminate the impact of
472   group size on the loss, Group Distributionally Robust Optimization (GDRO) (Sagawa* et al., 2020)
473   reweights group losses based on their magnitude, while other methods (Seo et al., 2022; Sohoni
474   et al., 2020) try to apply GDRO by finding proxies for the group labels needed. Other methods are
475   based on multiple training passes: some finetune an ERM trained model on non correlated data
476   (Kirichenko et al., 2023; Qiu et al., 2023; Ghaznavi et al., 2023) or use that same model to find
477   samples to upweight to train a new model from scratch (Liu et al., 2021) or finetune a classifier.
478   Others have worked on creating non-linear classifiers that are orthogonal to a set of attributes (Xu
479   et al., 2022), this requires having a notion of both the train and test distribution of those attributes.
480
      Other methods have used contrastive losses (Zhang et al., 2022) to create representations that are
      invariant to spurious attributes. Finally, some have used Self Supervised pretrained models to estimate
481
      a logit adjustment term on the loss (Tsirigotis et al., 2023).
482

      features are more predictive than simpler spurious features. Other work (Kirichenko et al., 2023)

523   6   C ONCLUSIONS
524
      In this work, we present a study on the mechanisms by which Group Robustness Methods are
525
      successful. We arrive at a few conclusions: they learn representations that, while surprisingly
526
      retaining spurious information, seem to distribute predictive features along many dimensions to
527   achieve robustness to using just few spurious features; second, their loss function induces a behaviour
528   in the classifier to learn to ignore spurious attributes more than ERM; this effect is compounded by
529   the representation learning aspect of these methods. This is shown both empirically and theoretically.
530   We also do a comparison against a Subsampling baseline which seems to achieve good results without
531   any representation learning whatsoever. We find evidence that the effects of Subsampling seem to be
532   supplementary to those representation learning effects of GRMs. Finally, through all of these findings
533   we propose a simple new baseline that retrieves most of GDRO’s performance gains that requires
534   group labels only during a finetuning phase and using less than 5% of the original training data.

        To understand the failure of just finetuning the classifier on Waterbirds, we finetune classifiers on
759
        ERM features using GDRO, but progressively unfreeze layers closer to the classifier. In this way,
760     we progressively adjust the quality of the representation by way of GDRO’s feature learning effects.
761     Results for these experiments are shown in Table 2 under "GDRO-FT (FRZX/RSTX)", where X
762     is a reference to which convolutional block onwards are being unfrozen. As we can see, by just
763     unfreezing the final convolutional layer before the classifier we retrieve most of GDRO’s performance.
764     This suggests that most of GDRO’s feature learning effects are taking place at the topmost layer.
765     Better results come from restarting these layers instead of unfreezing them, with each restarted

      C    L IMITATIONS AND S OCIETAL I MPACT
831
832
      Our analysis hinges on the following assumptions: groups within the dataset are usually unbalanced
      with the most represented groups benefiting from the spurious correlation. However, this is a standard
833
      setting in the robustness literature. Our theoretical analysis depends on a specific definition of a
834
      spurious vector, with which some may disagree.
835
836   Our proposed method requires extra data with annotations of both the class and spurious label for the
837   finetuning stage. It does not require much of it, 5% for the full method, but Table 3 shows decent
838
      results with even less. All competing baselines require at least this much extra data.
839   We believe this work may have a positive societal impact as it is trying to mitigate a problem of
840   spurious correlations that usually happens on underrepresented subsets of the data, which may
841   represent in practice underrepresented parts of society. Moreover, our work seeks to understand
842   the mechanisms by which GRMs work which could aid in developing better methods that work to
843   achieve robustness and fairness.
