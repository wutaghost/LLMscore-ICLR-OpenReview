011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Gesture recognition based on surface electromyography (sEMG) has been gaining
014            importance in many 3D Interactive Scenes. However, sEMG is easily influenced
015            by various forms of noise in real-world environments, leading to challenges in
016            providing long-term stable interactions through sEMG. Existing methods often
017
               struggle to enhance model noise resilience through various predefined data aug-
               mentation techniques. In this work, we revisit the problem from a short-term en-
018
               hancement perspective to improve precision and robustness against various com-
019
               mon noisy scenarios with learnable denoise using sEMG intrinsic pattern infor-
020            mation and sliding-window attention. We propose a Short Term Enhancement
021            Module(STEM), which can be easily integrated with various models. STEM of-
022            fers several benefits: 1) Noise-resistant, enhanced robustness against noise without
023            manual data augmentation; 2) Adaptability, adaptable to various models; and 3)
024            Inference efficiency, achieving short-term enhancement through minimal weight-
025            sharing in an efficient attention mechanism. In particular, we incorporate STEM
026            into a transformer, creating the Short-Term Enhanced Transformer (STET). Com-
027            pared with best-competing approaches, the impact of noise on STET is reduced
028
               by more than 20%. We report promising results on classification and regression
               tasks and demonstrate that STEM generalizes across different gesture recognition
029
               tasks. The code is available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/
030
               short_term_semg.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036
      Surface Electromyographic (sEMG) is a non-invasive technique for monitoring muscle neurons fir-
      ing, which is an effective way to capture human motion intention and has shown great application
037
      potential in the field of human-computer interaction (HCI) Xiong et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2021b;
038
      2020). A schematic diagram of the EMG-based HCI System is shown in Figure 1. Compared to
039   traditional HCI channels, sEMG has the advantages of being generated prior to actual motion (50-
040   150 ms), containing rich motion intention information, and being easy to collect Sun et al. (2020).
041   Therefore, there has been increasing interest in exploring EMG-based motion track Liu et al. (2021a)
042   and pathological analysis.
043
      By treating sEMG as time series, deep sequential models Bi et al. (2019); Tsinganos et al. (2019);
044
      Becker et al. (2018); Li et al. (2021); Du et al. (2017) have been applied to sEMG modeling. For
045   example, Zhang et al. employs a multi-task encoder-decoder framework improve the robustness
046   of sEMG-based Sign Language Translation (SLT) Zhang et al. (2022). Rahimian et al. employs
047   Vision Transformer (ViT)-based architecture (TEMGNet) to improve the accuracy of sEMG-based
048   myocontrol of prosthetics Rahimian et al. (2021). Although these methods demonstrate enhanced
049   performance compared to traditional approaches, they process sEMG signals as generic time-series
050   data without specifically tailoring their design to address the unique characteristics of sEMG, such as
051   their high variability and sensitivity to external noise and interference. This oversight leads to issues
052   such as difficulty in handling low signal-to-noise ratios due to changes in skin surface conditions and
053
      signal interference, and failing to capture subtle but important motion information in sEMG signals.
      As a result, the robustness and accuracy of existing models remain significantly challenged.

069
      To this end, in this paper, we present a lightweight but powerful Module called Short-Term En-
      hanced Module (STEM) which utilizes sliding window attention with weight sharing to capture
070
      short-term features. Building on STEM, we further propose the Short-Term Enhanced Transformer
071

072   combines STEM with long-term features, further improving accuracy for downstream predictions.
073   Furthermore, to enhance model robustness with minimal annotation, we propose a self-supervised
074   paradigm based on sEMG Signal Masking to leverage the inherent variability in sEMG signals.
075
      Finally, we conducted extensive experiments on the largest public sEMG datasets. The experi-
076
      mental results show that STET surpasses existing methods by a significant margin in both gesture
077
      classification and joint angle regression tasks for single-finger, multi-finger, wrist, and rest gestures.
078   Meanwhile, STET achieves strong robustness even when trained on pure data and tested on noisy
079   data. Compared with best-competing approaches, the impact of noise on STET is reduced by more
080   than 20%. Moreover, through visualizations, we show that the long-term and short-term features
081   are complementary in sEMG-based gesture recognition tasks, and the fusion of the two features can
082   make the classification boundary more obvious. This clearly demonstrates that short-term infor-
083   mation is critical for sEMG-based gesture recognition and will provide a new design paradigm for
084   future sEMG model design. In particular, we have deployed STET as an important functional com-
085   ponent in our HCI system, which can offer a more intuitive and effective experience. Our real-world
086
      deployment is shown in the appendix 5.
087   To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to highlight the short-term features in sEMG-based
088   gesture recognition. Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
089   1) From the perspective of enhancing short-term features, we propose STEM, a learnable and adapt-
090      able, noise-resistant module. The integration of STEM into various neural networks has resulted
091      in a marked improvement in their performance;
092   2) we introduce sEMG Signal Masking to self-supervised sEMG Intrinsic Pattern Capture Module
093      to leverage the inherent variability in sEMG;
094   3) we conduct experiments on the largest wrist sEMG dataset, showing that our proposed method
095
         outperforms existing approaches in terms of accuracy and robustness. Short-term enhancement
         can be extended to other models like Informer.

097   2    RELATED WORK
098
099
      The EMG-based Intention Prediction of Human Motion can be broadly divided into model-based
      and data-driven methods. Model-based methods typically combine disciplines such as kinesiology,
100
      biomechanics, and human dynamics to explicitly model the relationship between EMG and outputs
101
      (such as joint angles and forces). The model often includes specific parameters, such as joint posi-
102   tions and bone-on-bone friction, that need to be repeatedly experimented with and adjusted until the
103   desired performance is achieved. In terms of parameter selection and determination, model-based
104   methods can be further divided into kinematic models Borbély & Szolgay (2017), dynamic models
105   Koike & Kawato (1995); Koirala et al. (2015); Liu et al. (2015), and muscle-bone models Wang &
106   Buchanan (2002); Zhao et al. (2020); Yao et al. (2018). Clancy et al. used a nonlinear dynamics
107   model to identify the relationship between constant posture electromyography and torque at the el-
      bow joint Clancy et al. (2012). Hashemi et al. used the Parallel Cascade Identification method to


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      establish a mapping between forearm muscles and wrist forces Hashemi et al. (2012). However,
109   model-based methods have a large number of parameters that are difficult to measure directly. Cur-
110   rently, only simple motion estimation with a limited number of joints and degrees of freedom is
111   possible. In contrast to model-based approaches, data-driven methods do not require the measure-
112   ment of various parameters. Recently, some researchers have begun to use temporal deep learning
113   models to extract motion information from sEMG Lin et al. (2022); Zhang et al. (2022); Guo et al.
114   (2021). Lin et al. proposed a BERT-based structure to predict hand movement from the Root Mean
115   Square (RMS) feature of the sEMG signal Lin et al. (2022). Rahimian et al. proposed a novel
116   Vision Transformer (ViT)-based neural network architecture to classify and recognize upper-limb
117
      hand gestures from sEMG for use in myocontrol of prostheses Rahimian et al. (2021). However,
      these methods have neglected the modeling of short-term dependencies and have not considered the
118
      inherent variability in sEMG signals.

      3.1 DATASET
122
123
      We conduct the experiments on the GRABMyo Dataset Pradhan et al. (2022) and the Ninapro DB2
      Atzori et al. (2014), which are the largest and most widely used sEMG datasets and have great
124
      potential for developing new generation human-machine interaction based on sEMG.
125
126   Data processing. The subjects performed 17 gestures of hand and wrist (including a rest period
127   sEMG) according to the prompts on the computer screen. Each gesture was repeated 7 times, each
128   lasting 5 seconds. In order to improve the convergence speed of the model, we use two methods
129
      (Max-Min normalization, µ-law normalization) to normalize the data Rahimian et al. (2020); Rec-
      ommendation (1988). After normalization, we use a time-sliding window to split samples. We
130
      set the window size as 200ms, and the overlap of adjacent windows is 10ms. µ-law normalization
131
      can logarithmically amplify the outputs of sensors with small magnitudes, which results in better
132   performance than linear normalization.
133
134   Definition 1 (sEMG Signal Sequence) An sEMG signal sequence is defined as a temporal sig-
135   nal sequence sampled by multiple sensors from a human wrist, which can be formulated as
136   X = [x1 , x2 , .., xt ], where t is the time window. xi = [xi,1 , xi,2 , ...xi,c ] represents the signal
137   vector of c sensors, where xi,j is the signal value of the j-th sensor in the i-th time step.

139   4   T ECHNOLOGY D ETAIL
140   4.1 M ODEL OVERVIEW
141
      Figure 2 illustrates the overview of our proposed framework for gesture recognition, which con-
142
      tains three components: (1) The sEMG Intrinsic Pattern Capture module encodes the sEMG signal
143   sequence into the hidden sEMG representations. A pre-training model with a segment masking strat-
144   egy and MSE reconstructing loss is proposed to learn inherent variability from the sEMG signals
145   into the model’s parameters. (2) The Long-term and Short-term Enhanced module uses two decou-
146   pling heads to extract the long-term and short-term context information separately, which improves
147   the sEMG representations in preserving both the global sEMG structure and multiple local signal
148   changes of the sEMG. (3) The Asymmetric Optimization strategy addresses the problems of sample
149   biases and imbalance in gesture recognition via an asymmetric classification loss, which can make
150   the model focus on hard and positive samples to improve the recognition.

219   4.3 L ONG - TERM AND S HORT- TERM D ECODING
220   Then, based on the pre-trained sEMG Intrinsic Pattern Capture, we develop two decoder heads to
221   further extract the long-term and short-term dependency on the signal sequences, respectively. In-
222   tuitively, both the long-term and short-term information on signal sequences are significant in the
223   gesture recognition problem. Long-term information refers to the global context of an sEMG se-
224   quence, which provides the overall structure of a signal to help the interpretation of the gesture.
225   Short-term information refers to the movement signal in a short time interval of the whole sequence,
226
      which can provide specific local characteristics for accurate recognition when the overall structures
      of sEMGs are ambiguous. For example, distinguishing between Index Finger Extension (IFE) and
227
      Middle Extension (ME) movements requires a closer examination of the local signal changes in
228
      sEMG, whereas differentiating gestures with large variations, such as hand gestures and wrist ges-
229   tures, necessitates a focus on the global sEMG information.

274   4.4    A SYMMETRIC O PTIMIZATION
275   As the common use in multi-label classification, we reduce the gesture recognition problem into
276   a series of binary classification tasks. However, we consider two tricky problems that exist in the
277   above model optimization. (1) As the sampled signals are usually unstable over time, the samples
278   of the sEMG signal sequence may be critically biased. Some samples with strong signals are easily
279   predicted, while many samples with fuzzy signals are hard to predict. (2) We set 17 classes for the
280   sEMG signals, and each class contains a comparable number of samples. Thus, each class contains,
281   on average, many more negative samples than positive ones. This imbalance may cause the model
282   to eliminate the gradients from the positive samples in the optimization process, resulting in poor
283
      accuracy. Realizing this, we introduce the Asymmetric loss Ridnik et al. (2021) for the gesture clas-
      sification task. Asymmetric loss is a variant of Focal loss. (1) It uses focusing parameters to reduce
284
      the contribution of easily predicted samples and make the model optimization focus on hard sam-
285
      ples; (2) It further introduces asymmetric focusing parameters and asymmetric probability shifting
286   to down-weight the contribution from massive easy negatives and emphasizes the contribution of
287   positive samples. Thus, we define the loss function as follows:

      5.1.1                 E VALUATION M ETRICS
336
337   Following the prior worksGuo et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2020); Chen et al. (2021); Rahimian et al.
338   (2021), we choose the below metrics to evaluate the model’s performance. Pearson Correlation
339
      Coefficient (CC) is a widely used measure of the linear relationship between two variables. It
      ranges from -1 to 1, where a larger CC value indicates greater similarity between the predicted and
340
      estimated joint angles curve, indicating improved estimation. Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) is
341
      a common metric for evaluating the deviation between predicted and observed values. As the range
342   of fluctuations in the curves of different joint angles can vary significantly, it is difficult to evaluate
343   the performance of models using RMSE alone fairly. Normalization of RMSE addresses this issue,
344   resulting in the Normalized RMSE (NRMSE). Average curvature (κ) of all points for each joint is
345   used to measure the smoothness of an estimated curve. A smaller κ indicates a smoother curve.
346
347   5.2               C OMPARISON WITH BASELINES
348   We compare the accuracy (ACC) and Standard deviation (STD) between our proposed STET and
349   previous sEMG-based gesture recognition methods. Specifically, we train the model on the GRAB-
350   Myo dataset Pradhan et al. (2022) (the detail of data processing shown in 3.1 ) and separately report
351   the classification results on the categories of Single-finger gestures, Multi-finger gestures, Wrist
352   gestures, Rest, and the overall results. The ratio of training set to test set for each gesture is 5 to 2.
353   From the experimental results, we can observe that STET consistently performs best on four cat-
354   egories of gestures and overall data. In particular, STET and Zerveas et al. (2021) both use
355   Transformer-based encoders. In the decoder part, STET introduces both the short-term and long-
356   term decoder rather than the fully connected layers used in Zerveas et al. (2021). As a result, the
357   overall accuracy of STET is improved from 80.43% to 90.76% compared to Zerveas et al. (2021).
358   This is because the proposed long-term and short-term decoupling module can extract both the
359
      global and fine-grained dependency on the signal dependency and thus can learn better sEMG rep-
      resentations.
360
361   Among the transformer-based methods, Informer and STET performed best, with accuracy rates of
362   87.71% and 90.76%, respectively. Informer relies heavily on max pooling layers to aggregate fea-
363   tures, leading to the relative weakness in extracting some short-term features. STET enhances accu-
364   racy and stability by strengthening the short-term feature extraction. The improvement is remarkable
365
      on the Rest gestures, where the accuracy improves from 83.56% to 95.33%. Furthermore, after in-
      corporating our designed short-term encoder into Informer, its accuracy rate increased from 87.71%
366
      to 89.14%, and the classification accuracy for Rest gestures improved from 83.56% to 90.33%.
367
368   Note that the Rest category of gesture, as the resting state of devices such as interactive bracelets,
369   is the most frequent gesture that appears in the signals. Thus, the stability of its prediction plays a
370   significant role in the problem. STET achieves the highest and most stable results on the prediction
371
      of the Rest category compared with all baselines, indicating the robustness of STET.

478
      Current sEMG-based gesture recognition models usually fail to handle various noisy and distinguish
479
      similar gestures, especially in non-laboratory settings. In this paper, we found using short-term infor-
480   mation and self-supervised EIPC mitigates this issue. Therefore, we proposed STEM for capturing
481   local signal changes and enhancing noise resistance. The STEM is easily deployable and serves as
482   a plug-in that can potentially be applied to most time series deep learning models. According to
483   our experimental results, our method significantly improved performance for both classification and
484   regression tasks in sEMG, and the model’s ability to resist signal loss, Gaussian additive noise, and
485   Gaussian multiplicative noise was clearly improved. This will further drive the practical application
      of sEMG in VR, AR, and other human-computer interaction scenarios.

622
623   A.1.1    T HE DETAILS OF S EMG S IGNAL M ASKING
624
625   Detail of Rationale Behind Our Mask Methods:
626   Sensor-wise Mask: Each electrode has distinct conditions (e.g., moisture level, coating thickness),
627   resulting in varying signal quality. In real-world scenarios, many types of noise are sensor-specific
628   (e.g., signal loss from certain electrodes or poor electrode contact).
629
      Contiguous Masked Segments: The primary frequency range of sEMG signals is 20–500 Hz, while
630   dataset sampling rates are typically around 2000 Hz. This results in similar values for adjacent
631   sampling points. Contiguous masking prevents the model from relying on adjacent sampling points
632   to predict the masked values.
633
      The Length of Masked Segments Follows a Geometric Distribution: The effective frequency range
634
      of sEMG signals varies within 20–500 Hz. Frequency changes manifest as variations in the number
635
      of effective sampling points in the time domain. Using dynamic-length masked segments better cap-
636   tures these variations. Geometric distribution aligns with the characteristics of electrode distribution
637   and the frequency dynamics of sEMG signals
638
639
      The choice of a sensor-wise masking strategy aligns well with the practical scenarios of sEMG
      noise, where signal disturbances often occur at the level of individual sensors. For instance, signal

      B    T HE DIFFERENCE FROM LST-EMG-NET
865
866
      Our proposed approach exhibits a marked deviation from the LST-EMG-Net as detailed in Zhang
867   et al. (2023). In the LST-EMG-Net, the raw sEMG data is pre-segmented into long and short dura-
868   tions prior to the network ingestion. This pre-segmentation could potentially hinder the encoder’s
869   capability to grasp the intrinsic patterns of sEMG. In contrast, our method divides the data into
870   short-term and long-term segments during the decoding process, which we believe is a more effec-
871   tive strategy. Our results support this, with our method achieving an accuracy of 90.8% compared
872   to 85.3% achieved by the LST-EMG-Net model.
