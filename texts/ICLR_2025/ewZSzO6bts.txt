                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
               As neural networks continue to grow in size but datasets might not, it is vital to
012            understand how much performance improvement can be expected: is it more im-
013            portant to scale network size or data volume? Thus, neural network scaling laws,
014            which characterize how test error varies with network size and data volume, have
015            become increasingly important. However, existing scaling laws are often appli-
016            cable only in limited regimes and often do not incorporate or predict well-known
017            phenomena such as double descent. Here, we present a novel theoretical charac-
018            terization of how three factors — model size, training time, and data volume —
019            interact to determine the performance of deep neural networks. We first establish
020
               a theoretical and empirical equivalence between scaling the size of a neural net-
               work and increasing its training time proportionally. Scale-time equivalence chal-
021
               lenges the current practice, wherein large models are trained for small durations,
022
               and suggests that smaller models trained over extended periods could match their
023            efficacy. It also leads to a novel method for predicting the performance of large-
024            scale networks from small-scale networks trained for extended epochs, and vice
025            versa. We next combine scale-time equivalence with a linear model analysis of
026            double descent to obtain a unified theoretical scaling law, which we confirm with
027            experiments across vision benchmarks and network architectures. These laws ex-
028            plain several previously unexplained phenomena: reduced data requirements for
029            generalization in larger models, heightened sensitivity to label noise in overparam-
030            eterized models, and instances where increasing model scale does not necessarily
031
               enhance performance. Our findings hold significant implications for the practical
               deployment of neural networks, offering a more accessible and efficient path to
032
               training and fine-tuning large models.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Progress in artificial intelligence (AI) has relied heavily on the dramatic growth in the size of models
038   and datasets. An active area of research focuses on understanding how test error decreases with
039   increases in model and data size. This work has led to the development of scaling laws which posit
040   that test error decreases as a power law with both. However, several theoretical aspects remain
041   unclear. One significant gap is understanding how test error and the existing scaling laws change as
042   the training time is varied (Kaplan et al., 2020; Bahri et al., 2021; Rosenfeld et al., 2020; Sharma &
043   Kaplan, 2022).
044   The practical relevance of this question is clear: under a fixed compute budget, what is the optimal
045   balance between scaling the model size and dataset volume, and what is the right amount of train-
046   ing for a given data volume? This is particularly relevant in the context of large language models
047   (LLMs), which are often trained for a single epoch, raising questions about the potential efficacy of
048   training smaller models for longer (more epochs).
049   Furthermore, current scaling laws do not account for other well-known phenomena in learning,
050   such as double descent (Belkin et al., 2019), in which model performance exhibits non-monotonic
051   changes with respect to training data volume, model size, and training time. In particular, double
052   descent theory predicts that test error should increase rapidly at the interpolation threshold, the point
053   at which the model interpolates the training set (Nakkiran et al., 2021; Advani & Saxe, 2017). Like
      scaling laws, current theories of double descent leave several empirical phenomena unexplained:

      past explanations of double descent require it to occur, but empirically double descent is often not
055   observed; it is unclear whether the interpolation threshold should grow or shrink with model size;
056   prior theory does not explain why models in the infinite-parameter limit sometimes perform worse
057   than their finite-parameter counterparts.
058
      We seek the simplest possible unified framework in which to understand learning with respect to
059
      model size, data volume, and training time. In doing so, we aim to capture the essential scaling
060
      properties of learning in deep neural networks. Our results unify double descent with scaling laws
061   and help to understand when models are sufficiently large for effective performance, how double
062   descent is affected by varying training time and model size, and the variability and shape of loss
063   curves across different problems.
064
      The contributions of this paper are multi-fold:
065
066
             • We theoretically and empirically demonstrate that scaling the size of a neural network is
067            functionally equivalent to increasing its training time by a proportional factor.
068
069          • Leveraging this insight, we 1) predict the performance of large-scale networks using small-
070            scale networks trained for many epochs and 2) predict the performance of networks trained
071
               for many epochs using the performance of large networks trained for one epoch.
072          • Using scale-time equivalence, we propose a unified scaling law for deep neural networks
073            that provides a new explanation for parameter-wise double descent: double descent occurs
074            when small models, which effectively train slower than larger models, acquire noisy data
075            features.
076          • Through experiments conducted on standard vision benchmarks across multiple network
077            architectures, we validate that our model explains several previously unexplained phenom-
078            ena, including 1) the reduced data requirement for generalization in larger models, 2) the
079            large impact of label noise on overparameterized models, 3) why error of overparameter-
080            ized models often increases with scale.

      2     R ELATED WORK
084
085   2.1   S CALING LAWS
086
087
      Neural network scaling laws describe how generalization error scales with data and model size. A
      number of works have observed power-law scaling with respect to data and model size (Kaplan et al.,
088
      2020; Rosenfeld et al., 2020; Clark et al., 2022), which has been explained theoretically (Bahri et al.,
089
      2021; Sharma & Kaplan, 2022; Hutter, 2021; Paquette et al., 2024).
090
091   However, other work demonstrates that model scale may not be sufficient to predict model perfor-
092   mance (Tay et al., 2022), and casts doubt on power laws as the best model of error rate scaling (Alab-
093
      dulmohsin et al., 2022; Bansal et al., 2022; Mahmood et al., 2022). Moreover, scaling with respect to
      training time, holding data volume fixed, remains poorly understood. These observations highlight
094
      the need for a more general framework that can predict model performance under many settings.
095
096
097   2.2   D OUBLE DESCENT
098
099
      Double descent is an empirically observed phenomenon in which generalization error of machine
      learning models with respect to training data volume, model size, and training time exhibits an
100
      initial decrease, followed by a brief, sharp increase followed by a final decrease (Belkin et al., 2019;
101
      Nakkiran et al., 2021). Double descent with respect to model and data size has been theoretically
102   understood as occurring due to a high degree of overfitting at the interpolation threshold, the point
103   at which the model size is just sufficient to interpolate the training data (Adlam & Pennington, 2020;
104   D’Ascoli et al., 2020; Belkin et al., 2019; Advani & Saxe, 2017). Typically, this work uses tools
105   from random matrix theory to explain double descent for random feature models, in which linear
106   regression maps a random, fixed feature pool to the desired output (Simon et al., 2024; Atanasov
107   et al., 2024; Adlam et al., 2022; Bordelon et al., 2024; Maloney et al., 2022; Mei & Montanari, 2019;
      Ali et al., 2019; Lin et al., 2024).


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      However, these models generally do not explain double descent behavior in terms of training time.
109   Originally, Nakkiran et al. (2019; 2021) hypothesized that training models for longer results in an
110   effective increase in model size, thus allowing time-wise double descent to be explained in the same
111   way as scale-wise double descent. Recent works have provided an alternate explanation: time-wise
112   double descent occurs due to data features being learned at varying scales (Pezeshki et al., 2022;
113   Heckel & Yilmaz, 2021; Stephenson & Lee, 2021). Error rises as models overfit to quickly-learned
114   noisy features, but then falls as models more slowly learn signal features. In this work, we unify this
115   explanation of time-wise double descent with the traditional account of double descent.

117   3     S CALE - TIME EQUIVALENCE IN NEURAL NETWORKS
118
119   In this section, we demonstrate that model size and training time may be traded off with each other.
120   This result is consistent with and generalizes prior results demonstrating that models learn functions
121   of increasing complexity over time (Nakkiran et al., 2019). We establish the result theoretically
122   in a simplified model and validate it empirically in neural networks across several datasets and
123   architectures.

417   5.3   L ESS DATA REQUIRED FOR GENERALIZATION WITH MODEL SCALE
418
      The interpolation threshold can be defined as the point when the number of data points equals the
419
      effective complexity of a model (Nakkiran et al., 2019). Thus, for any model, the location of the
420   interpolation threshold with respect to data volume is the model’s effective capacity: higher capacity
421   models have a rightward-shifted interpolation threshold with respect to data volume. Conventional
422   double descent theory argues that this point corresponds to a sharp decrease in the test set error as
423   the amount of data grows. Starting from zero data, we would therefore expect that larger models
424   (which have a larger effective capacity), would experience a sharp decrease in test set error at higher
425   data volumes: larger models require more data to generalize. By contrast, under our explanation, as
426   model size grows, the amount of data needed to generalize decreases: since model size corresponds
427   to training time, generalization occurs more easily with larger models (equivalently, more training
428
      time).

468
      theory typically assumes complete training convergence, which does not explain phenomena under
469   the practically relevant setting of a fixed training budget. In summary, the increased ease of general-
470   ization with model scale does not neatly fit into standard theories of double descent, but readily fits
471   in our explanation.
472
473
      5.4   P ERSISTENT VS LOCAL EFFECTS OF NOISE ON ERROR CURVES
474
475   Another key distinction is how the two explanations behave under varying levels of noise. Under
476   the conventional explanation, as the noise (|Ni |) increases, the error increases mostly locally around
477   p ≈ n, near the interpolation threshold. This is because in Equation 9, the noise coefficients Ni
478   multiply terms that are largest near the interpolation threshold and decrease as either data volume or
479   model scale grow larger than the other (Schaeffer et al., 2023). In contrast, under our hypothesis, as
480   the noise increases, the error monotonically grows with both model scale and time (see Equation 10).
481   In other words, when the noise level changes, our hypothesis predicts a global performance change
482
      with respect to model scale while the conventional explanation predicts a primarily local change.
483   We conduct experiments on benchmark datasets and architectures to validate this. We add label
484   noise to the training set and evaluate performance as a function of epochs, model scale and data size.
485   Appendix C includes experimental details. Noise leads to an increase in error in all cases, Figure 6
      (See Appendix D for additional plots), with a persistent or growing error with training time (epochs)

486
      and model scale. However, it leads to only localized increases in error in terms of the dataset size:
487   in the case of MNIST and SVHN, for instance, there is nearly no change in error at the highest data
488   volume.
489
490
      5.5   S HAPE OF LOSS CURVE : UPTURN WITH MODEL SIZE
491
492   A final key distinction between the two explanations is their prediction of the shape of the loss curve.
493   Under the conventional explanation of double descent, past the interpolation threshold, increasing
494   model size always improves performance. However, under our hypothesis, increasing model size
495   may in certain cases worsen performance, if signal features are learned before noisy features. In
496   Figure 6, we find that CIFAR-10 and SVHN indeed exhibit a U-shaped error curve with respect to
497
      model scale such that larger models do worse. This departure from a conventional double descent
      curve is consistent with our model.
498
499
500   6     D ISCUSSION
501
502   In this work, we demonstrate that model scale and training time can be traded off with each other.
503   This enables us to re-frame parameter-wise double descent as occurring due to the same mechanism
504   as epoch-wise double descent, which occurs due to the early acquisition of noise features during
505   training. This framing of parameter-wise double descent has a number of surprising implications
506   unexplained by standard explanations of double descent: 1) generalization requires less data with a
507
      larger model, 2) label noise significantly increases test error even for highly overparameterized mod-
      els, 3) increasing model scale for overparameterized models need not always improve performance.
508
509   How can we reconcile our findings with conventional theory and experimental findings on double de-
510   scent and scaling laws? Note that in past work, the presence of double descent with respect to model
511   scale is often dependent on the choice of dataset, model, and whether label noise is added (Nakki-
512   ran et al., 2021). While past explanations of parameter-wise double descent necessitate that it must
513
      occur, our explanation is more flexible: double descent need not occur if noisy features are acquired
      later in training than signal features. Indeed, our theory explains why local increases in test error in
514
      terms of model scale are often relatively modest in contrast to the sharp spike predicted by conven-
515
      tional theory (Belkin et al., 2019). Thus, our explanation is more consistent with the variability of
516   double descent observed in the literature.
517
518   Regarding scaling laws, our predicted scaling law in Equation 10 is more flexible than the power law
519
      scalings predicted in prior literature; indeed, with the proper settings of signal and noise parameters
      Si and Ni , we may recover power-law scalings of error with respect to time, model scale and data
520
      volume. However, our approach retains the flexibility to explain error scalings in settings where a
521
      power law does not explain empirical error trends, as we see in our experimental results.
522
523   Given the parametric flexibility of our scaling law, how can we use it to predict performance as model
524   scale or training time increases? We demonstrate that by scale-time equivalence, performance under
525
      varying training times can be used to predict performance under varying model scales and vice versa.
      Our approach eliminates the need for strong parametric assumptions in the form of scaling law to
526
      make extrapolation predictions. This is particularly useful in cases where error increases with scale
527
      for overparameterized models since our approach can be used to predict an optimal model size.
528   On the other hand, we require empirically evaluating the performance of models (under either a
529   smaller scale or lower training time). We believe our approach can be valuable to practitioners who
530   have the flexibility to run some limited empirical small-scale experiments before full-scale training.
531   Finally, our results suggest that smaller models trained for a longer time may behave as well as larger
532   models, which we empirically observe on vision benchmarks. This is particularly important in the
533   age of LLMs, where very large models are trained for a small number of epochs (often just one).
534   We also highlight some important limitations of our work. Our experiments are all conducted on
535   standard vision benchmarks; we believe testing our theory on the language domain is a critical future
536   direction. Another limitation is that we do not yet have a fundamental understanding of what sets
537   the appropriate model scale of a neural network. Experimentally, we found that the cube root of
538   the number of model parameters is appropriate, but without a strong theoretical basis; this deserves
539   further study. Overall, we believe our contributions not only shed light on neural scaling laws, but
      also present exciting directions for future work.

868
      We used three benchmark datasets: CIFAR-10, MNIST, and SVHN. Each dataset was subject to
869   preprocessing involving standard normalization. For CIFAR-10 and SVHN, the normalization was
870   performed using means and standard deviations of (0.5, 0.5, 0.5). For MNIST, the normalization
871   used a mean and standard deviation of 0.5.
872
      Two types of neural network architectures were evaluated:
873
874
             • Convolutional Neural Network (CNN): Our CNN architecture consisted of six ReLU acti-
875
               vated convolutional layers, with kernel sizes (3, 3, 3, 3, 3, 2 for CIFAR-10/SVHN or 1 for
876            MNIST), strides (2, 1, 2, 1, 1, 1), and number of filters (5s, 10s, 20s, 40s, 80s) where s is a
877            width parameter. This was followed by a fully connected layer.
878
879          • Multilayer Perceptron (MLP): The MLP architecture comprised six fully connected layers
880
               with hidden layer width 10s where s is a scale parameter.
881
      The width parameter was set to values of 1, 2, 5, 10, 20, 50, and 100. When we refer to ”model
882
      scale”, we quantify this as the cube root of the number of network parameters rather than the value
883   of the width parameter. We tested two learning rates (0.001 and 0.01) in combination with two
884   optimizers (Adam and SGD respectively). Label noise was introduced at levels of 0.0 (no noise)
885   and 0.2 (20% noise) to evaluate the robustness of the models. The number of training samples
886   was varied among 100, 200, 500, 1000, 2000, 5000, 10000, 20000, and 50000. The samples were
887   randomly selected from their respective base training sets. A constant batch size of 32 and 100
888   training epochs were used across all experiments. Mean Squared Error (MSE) was used as the loss
889   function. Model performance was assessed using Mean Squared Error (MSE) on both training and
890   test sets. For reproducibility, we set a manual seed for the PyTorch random number generator. Five
891
      different seeds (101, 102, 103, 104, 105) were used to assess the variance in results due to random
      initialization.
