                                                  A BSTRACT
013
014
               Off-policy reinforcement learning (RL) requires extensive real interaction with
015            environment to gain experience for policy learning, presenting a challenge of low
016            sample efficiency, especially in the condition of sparse rewards. To address this,
017            we propose a Sample-Imagined Generator (SIG), which automatically trains a
018            sample generator during environment interaction and could adaptively generate
019            valuable imagined samples for policy learning. Through SIG, the policy greatly
020            reduced the interaction with the environment during training and achieved compa-
021            rable or even higher performance with those trained only through real interactions.
022            SIG could be combined with any off-policy RL algorithm. Experiment in 5 con-
023
               tinuous control tasks demonstrate that by substituting imagined samples for real
               ones to supplement the experience pool, SIG accomplishes tasks with significantly
024
               less interaction with the environment, notably improving sample efficiency across
025
               10 off-policy reinforcement learning algorithms.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Reinforcement learning (RL) has achieved great success in decision-making tasks (Xu et al.,
031   2018; Loftus et al., 2020; Holcomb et al., 2018). Compared to on-policy RL (Singh et al., 2000;
032   Andrychowicz et al., 2020; Hausknecht et al., 2016), off-policy RL (Fujimoto et al., 2019; Thomas
033
      & Brunskill, 2016; Peng et al., 2019) learns the policy through the historical samples, which is more
      robust when adapting to the environment and becomes a promising way in task learning (Yu et al.,
034
      2020). However, extensive interaction with the environment also brings the challenge of low sample
035
      efficiency (Buckman et al., 2018). Meanwhile, providing the agent with appropriate dense rewards
036   (Eschmann, 2021; Devidze et al., 2021) is another challenging issue. It is difficult to find an accurate
037   reward function that guides the agent to reach the goal step by step. In the vast majority of current
038   RL research, agents are provided with sparse rewards (Wang et al., 2020; He & Lv, 2023) that are
039   easy to set, namely rewards are given only upon completion of the task. The limited information
040   further leads greater interaction for the agent to successfully complete the task.
041
      This issue can be mitigated by improving the algorithm’s target updating or value estimation method
042   to encourage exploration, thereby improving sample efficiency. Prior works have considered several
043   modifications, such as introducing an entropy maximization goal during training through an en-
044   tropy adjustment mechanism (Haarnoja et al., 2018), and adding random noise to the updated target
045   value (Fujimoto et al., 2018) to incentivize broader exploration by the agent. The value estimation
046   method can be modified in combination with Monte Carlo estimation (Wilcox et al., 2022a) to fur-
047   ther improve sample efficiency. In other works, expert data can be provided to the agent, including
048   demonstration data (Nair et al., 2018), and pre-collected large-scale datasets for offline reinforce-
049   ment learning (Kumar et al., 2020). Although these works exhibit remarkable performance, they
050
      necessitate the introduction of demonstrations or meticulously designed hyperparameters, which
      impedes their application to diverse tasks. Additionally, they cannot actively reduce actual interac-
051
      tions with the environment and still require extensive interaction steps to complete learning.
052
053   In this paper, we propose a simple and efficient sample generation method called Sample-Imagined
      Generator (SIG), which learns from experienced real samples and continuously generates close-


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      to-real imagined samples for policy learning, greatly improving learning efficiency while actively
055   reducing actual environmental interactions. SIG can be combined with any off-policy RL method as
056   an additional module and easy to implement. The main contributions of this paper are summarized
057   as follows:
058
      • We propose a Self-validating Sample Generator module which introduces an action-validation
059
      mechanism to make sample generation in a closed loop. This module could verify the rationality of
060
      the imagined states and progressively enhance the state-action mapping relationship, supplying the
061   high-quality imagined samples.
062
063   • We propose a Self-adaptive Imagination Inference module which can adaptively adjust the length
064
      of the imagined sample trajectory and the quantity incorporated into policy learning, thereby pro-
      viding the agent with the most appropriate imagined samples at each updating step. This module
065
      also actively determines the switch time when the agent can start to reduce interaction, guaranteeing
066
      the steady policy convergence during training under reduced environmental interactions.
067
068   • We combined SIG with 10 off-policy RL algorithms, chosen from classic methods and recent sig-
069   nificant improved methods. To further prove the effectiveness of our method, we selected 5 contin-
070
      uous control tasks with sparse rewards, which makes them more challenging. The empirical results
      of 50 scenarios show that our method achieved comparable or even better performance compared
071
      to the baselines with as little interaction as possible, and greatly improved the sample efficiency of
072
      off-policy RL.

075   2   R ELATED W ORK
076
077
      The issue of low sample efficiency in off-policy reinforcement learning under sparse rewards can be
078
      mitigated by introducing expert data, improving Q-value estimation or policy optimization, which
079   are respectively external and internal improvement methods.
080
081   Introducing Expert Data: Since the reward signal is sparse, the agent requires more experience
082
      gained from exploration to update its policy. One approach to improve sample efficiency is to pro-
      vide the agent with additional expert experience. A typical type of expert data is demonstration,
083
      which led to the development of reinforcement learning from demonstrations (RLfD) (Brys et al.,
084
      2015; Gao et al., 2018; Alakuijala et al., 2021). OEFD (Nair et al., 2018) is based on the Deep
085   Deterministic Policy Gradient (DDPG) (Lillicrap et al., 2015) and the Hindsight Experience Replay
086   (HER) (Andrychowicz et al., 2017), demonstrating significant improvements in simulated robotics
087   tasks with limited demonstration data. FLAIR (Chen et al., 2023) combines demonstration data with
088   inverse RL and employs an adaptive mechanism to learn from demonstrations. It can quickly adapt
089   to new demonstrations and make personalized adjustments. RoboCLIP (Sontakke et al., 2024) lever-
090   ages pre-trained video and language models (Bagad et al., 2023) to generate rewards from human
091   demonstration videos, significantly improving zero-shot performance on robot operation tasks. In
092   other works, large-scale expert datasets can also be used to directly perform offline RL (Levine et al.,
093
      2020; Prudencio et al., 2023). CQL (Kumar et al., 2020) addresses the problem of value function
      overestimation by learning a conservative Q-function and combines expert data for offline learning,
094
      which significantly outperforms existing methods.
095
096   Improving Policy Optimization or Q-value Estimation: Some work focuses on the algorithm’s
097   intrinsics, encouraging agents to explore or stabilize the learning process by improving policy opti-
098   mization (Hu et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024), thereby improving sample efficiency. SAC (Haarnoja
099
      et al., 2018) maximizes the entropy of the policy, encouraging agents to search for high-reward poli-
      cies, thereby conducting comprehensive exploration in environments with sparse rewards or high
100
      uncertainty. TRPO (Schulman, 2015) defines a trust region to ensure that each policy update does
101
      not deviate too far from the original policy, thereby reducing instability in policy learning. POMO
102   (Kwon et al., 2020) exploits symmetry in combinatorial optimization problems and finds all optimal
103   solutions through diversified policy optimization. Other work stabilizes algorithm learning by im-
104   proving Q-value estimation (Yang et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2024), better utilizing the current sample
105   smooth policy updating and enhancing sample efficiency. TD3 (Fujimoto et al., 2018) introduces
106   noise disturbance in the Q-value calculation to enhance the robustness of learning. GQE (Schulman
107   et al., 2015) proposes a novel estimation method which significantly reduces the variance of policy
      gradient estimates. This method exhibits robustness and efficiency when training value functions,


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      mitigating issues of overfitting. Based on part of demonstrations, MCAC (Wilcox et al., 2022a)
109   proposes a new Q-value estimation method combined with Monte Carlo estimation, enabling sparse
110   rewards to propagate for a long time during the learning process and improving the sample efficiency
111   of off-policy RL.
112
      The above works have performed well for off-policy RL under sparse rewards. However, these
113
      methods do not consider interaction issue with the environment, and require numerous interactions
114
      to complete related tasks. In this paper, we design an independent enhancement module from a more
115   intuitive perspective of sample provision, greatly reducing actual interaction with the environment
116   and further improving sample efficiency.

126   optimal actions in any st ∈ S to maximize returns.

310   4.3    T HE IMPLEMENTATION OF SIG
311
312   SIG can be combined with any off-policy RL algorithm to improve learning efficiency while reduc-
313   ing environmental interaction. In the early stages, off-policy RL interacts with the environment to
314
      accumulate environmental experience, storing it in RB. SIG continuously trains on these samples to
      improve SSG’s prediction accuracy. SIG utilizes SII to self-adaptively adjust the imagined trajectory
315
      length Hsig , the sampling ratio Rsig , and the switch time Tsig .
316
317   At each time step SIG randomly selects a sample s1 from RB and uses this as a starting point for in-
318   ference, generating imagined trajectory τsig = {(s1 , a1 , r̂1 , ŝ2 ), ..., (ŝHsig , aHsig , r̂Hsig , ŝHsig +1 )}
319   stored in RB ima to enhance sample diversity in experience pool. The off-policy RL samples from
320
      RB and RB ima at ratio Rsig for accelerating learning.
321   When the condition of Tsig is satisfied, the frequency f of the agent interacting with the environ-
322   ment reduces from fenv to fless . At each subsequent time step, more accurate imagined samples
323   progressively replace the real samples, promoting efficient learning and reducing environmental de-
      pendence. This results in notable sample efficiency.

510   6   C ONCLUSIONS
511
512   We propose SIG (Sample-Imagined Generator) which is a sample efficiency enhancer for off-policy
513   RL with sparse rewards. SIG consists two modules including SSG (Self-validating Sample Gen-
514
      erator) and SII (Self-adaptive Imagination Inference). SSG generates the high-quality imagined
      sampled with an action-validation closed loop mechanism. SII adaptively adjusts the length and
515
      sample-ratio of imagined trajectories and selects the optimal switch time of reducing interaction
516
      according to the accuracy of SSG. The synergy of the two modules could continuously supply the
517   valuable imagined samples for policy training and greatly reduce the real interactions. The empirical
518   results verified the satisfied performance of SIG with 10 off-policy RL algorithms across 50 scenar-
519   ios and showed the promise of applying RL in real applications. In the future, we would continue
520   to focus on the research of high sample efficiency algorithms for RL and further develop SIG to be
521   more stable and applicable in real-world.
