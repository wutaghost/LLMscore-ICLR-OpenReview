008                                                A BSTRACT
009
010            Uncertainty quantification is crucial to account for the imperfect predictions of
011            machine learning algorithms for high-impact applications. Conformal predic-
012            tion (CP) is a powerful framework for uncertainty quantification that generates
013            calibrated prediction intervals with valid coverage. In this work, we study how
014            CP intervals are affected by bias – the systematic deviation of a prediction from
015            ground truth values – a phenomenon prevalent in many real-world applications.
016            We investigate the influence of bias on interval lengths of two different types of
017            adjustments – symmetric adjustments, the conventional method where both sides
018
               of the interval are adjusted equally, and asymmetric adjustments, a more flexible
               method where the interval can be adjusted unequally in positive or negative direc-
019
               tions. We present theoretical and empirical analyses characterizing how symmet-
020
               ric and asymmetric adjustments impact the “tightness” of CP intervals for regres-
021            sion tasks. Specifically for absolute residual and quantile-based non-conformity
022            scores, we prove: 1) the upper bound of symmetrically adjusted interval lengths
023            increases by 2|b| where b is a globally applied scalar value representing bias, 2)
024            asymmetrically adjusted interval lengths are not affected by bias, and 3) condi-
025            tions when asymmetrically adjusted interval lengths are guaranteed to be smaller
026            than symmetric ones. Our analyses suggest that even if predictions exhibit sig-
027            nificant drift from ground truth values, asymmetrically adjusted intervals are still
028            able to maintain the same tightness and validity of intervals as if the drift had never
029
               happened, while symmetric ones significantly inflate the lengths. We demonstrate
               our theoretical results with two real-world prediction tasks: sparse-view computed
030
               tomography (CT) reconstruction and time-series weather forecasting. Our work
031
               paves the way for more bias-robust machine learning systems.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   With the growing application of deep learning algorithms to high-impact applications such as health-
037   care, finance, and climate science, it is equally crucial to develop methods that can robustly quantify
038   their uncertainties. This is particularly important since deep learning algorithms are known to yield
039   confident yet incorrect prediction values (Guo et al., 2017; Wang, 2023; Niculescu-Mizil & Caruana,
040   2005). Given a prediction by a learning algorithm on a fresh test example, uncertainty quantification
041   methods typically aim to return a prediction set with some guarantee that the true value lies within
042   that set. In particular, a prediction set for a regression problem consists of an interval with lower and
043   upper bounds (Lei et al., 2018; Romano et al., 2019).
044   Conformal Prediction (CP) is a powerful family of uncertainty quantification methods that is
045   distribution-agnostic, i.e., makes no assumptions about the underlying data distribution, and gen-
046   erates prediction sets with guarantees of containing ground truth values with some probability (An-
047   gelopoulos & Bates, 2021; Fontana et al., 2023; Shafer & Vovk, 2008; Papadopoulos et al., 2002).
048   For example, split CP is based on collecting a separate (from training) calibration dataset containing
049   both ground truth and predicted values from the algorithm of interest. Then, given the algorithm’s
050
      prediction on a test example, split CP computes a non-conformity score quantifying how “unusual”
      new predictions will be with respect to the calibration dataset, and adjusts the prediction via the
051
      empirical quantile of the calibration scores to generate a prediction set.
052
053   In practice, to minimize prediction uncertainty, we aim to obtain the tightest possible intervals that
      maintain valid coverage. Interval tightness (or, inversely, its length) depends on various factors:

      training distributions (Nandy et al., 2022), sensor drift (Jing et al., 2013; Ying et al., 2007; Piazzo

095
096
      In this paper, we argue that the effects of bias on CP interval lengths can be mitigated by com-
      puting intervals with asymmetric adjustments (Linusson et al., 2014; Romano et al., 2019) instead
097
      of conventional symmetric adjustments. Asymmetric adjustments allow lower and upper endpoints
098
      to be adjusted independently to account for directional bias, maintaining the guarantee that the re-
099   sulting interval contains the ground truth with high probability (Fig. 1-right). While asymmetric
100   adjustments have been theorized to yield longer interval lengths as a consequence of stronger guar-
101   antees (Romano et al., 2019), our work observes that with bias, that may not be the case. We expand
102   the theoretical understanding of CP interval lengths for absolute residual (L1 ) and quantile adjusted
103   (i.e., Conformalized Quantile Regression or CQR (Romano et al., 2019)) non-conformity scores
104   by analyzing their behavior for symmetrically and asymmetrically adjusted interval lengths under
105   prediction bias. Specifically, we prove the following:
106
            1. The upper bound of symmetrically adjusted interval lengths increases by 2|b| (Thm. 2),
107
            2. Asymmetrically adjusted interval lengths are not affected by bias (Thm. 3), and

            3. Conditions when asymmetrically adjusted interval lengths are guaranteed to be smaller than
109            those of symmetric adjustments (Cor. 3.1).
110
111   Our theoretical results are significant for several reasons. First, existing theory shows that while
112   asymmetric adjustments give stronger coverage guarantees, they also result in slightly longer inter-
113   vals (Romano et al., 2019). However, contrary to this theory, it has also been empirically observed
114   that asymmetric adjustments yield tighter intervals than symmetric ones (Linusson et al., 2014;
115   Wang et al., 2023; Cheung et al., 2024), but the underlying reasons were unclear. Our work provides
116   a theoretical answer to this phenomenon. Second, our theoretical results suggest that asymmetric
117   adjustments are preferable in practice to symmetric ones when systematic biases are expected, e.g.,
118   with sensor drift. However, if symmetric adjustments are desired (e.g., when error distributions are
119
      assumed to be symmetric), we also offer a method to achieve tighter symmetric intervals during
      calibration.
120
121   We validate that our theoretical analyses align with synthetic and real prediction tasks. Our synthetic
122   experiments validate our theoretical analyses in the ideal setting when n is large for no skew and
123   skewed distributions. Our real tasks validate our theoretical analyses in two settings: when n is
124   extremely low and time series. Our real tasks deal with bias arising in different contexts: (1) when
125
      a medical imaging reconstruction algorithm systematically under- or over-estimates volumes of an
      anatomical region (computed tomography (CT) reconstruction), and (2) temporal “drift” of values
126
      over time (weather forecasting). The contributions of this study give machine learning practitioners
127
      fundamental and practical insight into using CP under the common scenario of biased predictions.

479   5   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
480
481   We will never collect perfect data in practice, or build perfect predictive models that are robust over
482   time. Therefore, it is integral to account for these imperfections when designing practical systems.
483   In this work, we argue that the effects of bias on CP prediction interval lengths can be mitigated
484   by computing asymmetric adjustments as opposed to the conventional symmetric adjustments. We
485   prove the following for L1 and CQR non-conformity scores. In Thm. 2 we showed that the upper
      bound of the prediction interval lengths with symmetric adjustments increases by 2|b|. In Thm. 3, we


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      showed that prediction interval lengths with asymmetric adjustments are not affected by bias. In Cor.
487   3.1, we showed the conditions when prediction interval lengths with asymmetric adjustments are
488   guaranteed to be smaller than those of symmetric intervals. We proposed an algorithm to empirically
489   determine the bias and showed empirical evidence using synthetic and real-life data. Our results have
490   have important implications on accounting for algorithmic bias, while also suggesting further areas
491   of investigation:
492
      Stability of bias estimation for low n. Our work suggests that estimating bias based on sym-
493   metrically adjusted intervals is a straightforward, practical, and computationally efficient way to
494   account for systematic errors in predictions. However, it is crucial to consider the impact of sample
495   size on the reliability of these estimates when the calibration set is small (Tversky & Kahneman,
496   1971). Small calibration sets can lead to noisy estimates of bias (Springate, 2012). The challenges
497   associated with limited data include under or overestimating the true bias, greater bias estimation
498   variability, and being more susceptible to skewed, outliers, and random fluctuations. We recommend
499   considering the uncertainty in bias estimates when applying corrections and increasing sample size
500   to improve the reliability of bias estimates where possible.
501   More complex scores. Our analysis reveals that while simple non-conformity scores, as presented
502   in Eq. 2 and 4, are tractable for theoretical guarantees, more complex non-conformity scores such as
503   locally adaptive scores Papadopoulos et al. (2008; 2011); Lei et al. (2018) and CQR variants Sesia &
504   Candès (2020) present challenges. These challenges arise due to modeling bias as a globally applied
505   additive constant. This simplification, although useful for theoretical and empirical analysis, may
506   overlook that biases could exhibit more intricate patterns, possibly varying across the input space or
507
      depending on specific features. For example, in time series data, we use a uniform weighting scheme
      that effectively reduces to split CP over each time window. Our results suggest that incorporating
508
      these techniques in more complex settings could reveal interesting behaviors and could help design
509
      more bias-robust scores.
510
511   Covariate shift and Bias. Our work suggests correcting for bias using a globally applied constant
512   to the predictions can significantly reduce the interval lengths. However, our approach does not ac-
513
      count for situations where the predicted distribution changes between calibration and test datasets.
      Prior work on CP under covariate shift (Tibshirani et al., 2019) weighted predictions by a proba-
514
      bility proportional to their likelihood ratio. However, when calibration predictions are “far away”
515
      from the expected test predictions, the likelihood ratio may be very small or zero. Thus, it is impos-
516   sible to perform a covariate shift without significant overlap between the calibration and expected
517   test predictions when the expected bias is large. Our work suggests exploring both bias correction
518   and covariate shift together could lead to tighter and more reliable prediction intervals for these
519   situations.
