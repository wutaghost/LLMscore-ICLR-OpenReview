      1     I NTRODUCTION
031
032
      Embedding spaces are widely recognized for encapsulating rich information learned by deep learning
      models. In language models, for instance, the distance between embedding vectors often reflects
033
      the semantic similarities of corresponding textual elements (Devlin et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2020).
034
      However, despite their widespread use, the understanding of embedding spaces remains limited
035   (Ethayarajh, 2019; Clark et al., 2019; Simhi & Markovitch, 2023). Two primary challenges make
036   understanding embedding spaces difficult:
037
038       (1) Abstract Nature: Embedding spaces reflect complex, high-dimensional relationships between
039
              data points, making them inherently abstract. To interpret these spaces, we need a clear
              connection between the embedded data and their underlying semantics. However, a universally
040
              accepted definition of semantics within these spaces remains elusive.
041
          (2) Computational Complexity: Understanding embedding spaces requires substantial data,
042           and as these spaces grow richer and more complex, the need for samples increases, straining
043           computational efficiency. Managing this expanding data demands advanced computational
044           resources and optimized algorithms to ensure practical and timely analysis.
045
      Extensive research on embedding spaces spans various perspectives, with two lines of research most
046
      relevant to our work: geometry-based and interpretability-based methods. The first line of research
047
      focuses on the geometric properties of the embedding space, describing vector distributions and their
048   desired characteristics (Mu & Viswanath, 2018; Liu et al., 2019; Demeter et al., 2020; Ethayarajh,
049   2019). However, these approaches primarily enhance representation quality by manipulating the ge-
050   ometry of the space, often overlooking the interpretability and semantic coherence of the embeddings.
051   The second line of research concentrates on making the embedding space more interpretable using
052   techniques like rotation, probing (Park et al., 2017; Dufter & Schütze, 2019; Clark et al., 2019; Dalvi
053   et al., 2019), or transforming data into more interpretable dimensions (Simhi & Markovitch, 2023).
      Despite providing insights into individual dimensions, they often rely on significant assumptions


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      about the interpretability of original dimensions or require constructing new spaces, which can be
055   computationally intensive and may not preserve the original semantic relationships.
056
057   In this paper, we investigate the semantic structure of embedding spaces through textual elements.
058
      To address their abstract nature and the challenges of interpretation, we introduce key concepts that
      link embedding spaces to the underlying semantics. We define each direction as a unique semantic
059
      set, serving as a foundation for understanding the structure. Recognizing the context-dependent
060
      nature of semantics, we introduce the concept of a Semantic Field for more nuanced interpretation.
061   Identifying Semantic Fields within embedding spaces is framed as an optimization problem. We
062   approximate each Semantic Field as a subspace, referred to as the Semantic Field Subspace, and
063   solve the optimization using Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) (Halko et al., 2009; Trefethen &
064   Bau, 2022).
065
      Building on this concept, we propose SAFARI, a novel algorithm for determining Semantic Field
066
      Subspaces through hierarchical clustering. By introducing the concept of Semantic Shift, SAFARI
067   accurately identifies the start and end points of Semantic Field Subspaces within a clustering den-
068   drogram, unveiling the hierarchical structure of Semantic Fields. To overcome the computational
069   challenges of analyzing large datasets, we extend Weyl’s Theorem (Weyl, 1912) to approximate
070   Semantic Shift without relying on full SVD, significantly improving computational efficiency.
071
      We evaluate SAFARI on five real-world datasets to validate its ability to uncover hierarchical
072
      semantic structures. The results confirm that SAFARI efficiently identifies Semantic Field Subspaces,
073   revealing natural and interpretable hierarchies. Moreover, our approximate method for Semantic
074   Shift computation delivers a 15∼30× speedup with errors less than 0.01, making it highly practical
075   for large-scale applications. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
076
          (1) We introduce the concept of Semantic Field Subspaces, a novel mapping that bridges embedding
077
              spaces with their underlying semantics. It enhances the interpretability of high-dimensional
078
              embeddings, facilitating deeper insights into the semantics encoded within vector spaces.
079       (2) We present SAFARI, a creative algorithm that leverages Semantic Shift to determine Semantic
080           Field Subspaces. By employing hierarchical clustering, SAFARI effectively uncovers the
081           hierarchical semantic structures. We also develop an efficient approximate method for Semantic
082           Shift computation, significantly improving computational efficiency.
083       (3) We systematically evaluate the efficacy of SAFARI through extensive experiments, demon-
084           strating that our algorithm successfully and efficiently identifies Semantic Filed Subspaces,
085           revealing their hierarchical structures.

087   2     R ELATED W ORK
088
089   Geometry-based Approaches. This research focuses on the geometric properties of embedding
090   spaces, aiming to describe the vector distributions and their desired characteristics (Mu & Viswanath,
091   2018; Liu et al., 2019; Demeter et al., 2020; Ethayarajh, 2019). For instance, Mu & Viswanath (2018)
092   improved word representations by removing the top principal components, while Liu et al. (2019)
093
      suppressed transformed dimensions with large variances. Demeter et al. (2020) highlighted how the
      softmax function weakens geometric structures, introducing bias. A key finding by Ethayarajh (2019)
094
      showed that most vectors reside within a narrow cone in the embedding space. Unlike geometry-based
095
      research, we do not aim to prove or find the ideal distribution or other geometric properties in the
096   embedding space to improve the model. Instead, SAFARI focuses on revealing and understanding
097   structures within a given embedding space, regardless of its geometric properties. This allows us to
098   maintain the original semantic relationships while uncovering meaningful patterns.
099
      Interpretability-based Approaches. This research targets making embedding spaces more inter-
100
      pretable, often through rotation and probing methods (Park et al., 2017; Dufter & Schütze, 2019;
101
      Clark et al., 2019; Dalvi et al., 2019). Park et al. (2017) employed rotation algorithms to improve
102   word vector interpretability, while Dufter & Schütze (2019) applied rotation to enhance word space
103   comprehension. Clark et al. (2019) analyzed attention mechanisms in pre-trained models, particularly
104   BERT (Devlin et al., 2019), to gain insights into how the model processes information. Dalvi et al.
105   (2019) examined individual vector dimensions in NLP models to uncover the roles of these dimen-
106   sions. Recently, Simhi & Markovitch (2023) transformed latent spaces into a new one with more
107   conceptualized and interpretable dimensions. Although SAFARI also seeks to interpret embedding
      spaces, it differs by not assuming that the original dimensions are inherently interpretable or by

108
      transforming them into new spaces. Instead, SAFARI identify comprehensible structures by linking
109   embedding vectors to their underlying semantic space, preserving the original embeddings and
110   providing a robust framework for semantic interpretation.

205
206   Motivation. SAFARI is designed to identify Semantic Field Subspaces from a set E of embedding
207   vectors. While Definition 5 provides a theoretical framework for constructing these subspaces, the
208   primary challenge lies in selecting appropriate subsets C ⊆ E, especially in a large, diverse text corpus.
209   SAFARI addresses this by leveraging the natural clustering property of embedding vectors, where
210   clusters represent specific topics or semantic themes, enabling efficient identification of meaningful
211   subspaces in complex, high-dimensional embedding spaces.
212   Algorithm Description. SAFARI utilizes hierarchical clustering to iteratively identify potential
213   Semantic Field Subspaces, as it offers flexibility by not requiring a predefined number of clusters,
214   allowing for dynamic exploration of the data. Additionally, hierarchical clustering produces a
215   dendrogram, which helps interpret relationships between clusters and their semantic structures. The
      pseudo-code for SAFARI is shown in Algorithm 1.

289   Approximate Semantic Shift Computation. Exact Semantic Shift computation is computationally
290
      intensive due to the need for a full SVD on two dense matrices, Ax and Anew . For a matrix of size

291
      2022), which becomes prohibitive when repeated at each iteration. To address this, we develop an
292
      efficient approximation algorithm (pseudo-code in Appendix C). Given the larger cluster Cx and the
293   smaller cluster Cy , we first construct matrices Ax and Ay . We then compute the spectral norm of Ay
294   and the maximum singular value σmax from Ax , using them to approximate the Semantic Shift of
295   Cx and Cnew :

347   be approximated by the largest singular value. This completes the proof of Theorem 3.
348
349   By utilizing this approximation for Semantic Shifts, we can bypass the need for full SVD in each
350   iteration. Instead, we only need to compute ∥Ay ∥2 and σmax (Ax ), significantly reducing the time
351
      complexity in Algorithm 1 (Meyer, 2023; Horn & Johnson, 2012).

531   6        C ONCLUSIONS
532
      In this paper, we tackled the challenge of understanding the abstract and intricate structure of
533
      embedding spaces. We introduced SAFARI, a novel algorithm for identifying Semantic Field
534   Subspaces through hierarchical clustering and the concept of Semantic Shift, with an efficient
535   approximation method that avoids SVD, reducing the computational cost. Extensive experiments
536   on five real-world datasets demonstrated SAFARI’s capability to uncover interpretable, hierarchical
537   semantic structures while achieving substantial computational savings. SAFARI has shown to be
538   effective for tasks like classification and explanation. This work not only bridges the gap between
539   embedding spaces and their underlying semantics but also offers new insights into the structure and
      utility of embedding spaces, enhancing both interpretability and efficiency in their analysis.

744   progression is not always intuitive. The distinction between “specific” and “abstract” depends more
745   on the data and model than on human reasoning, often leading to groupings that diverge from what
746   we would expect based on natural language understanding.

      Fig. E3 depicts more comparisons between Reliable and other categories of fake news. Before
      analyzing the results, we want to highlight that although this dataset is labeled as “fake news”,
838
      the categories presented here are vague or not necessarily fake. These categories are not clearly
839
      distinct from each other; for instance, a news piece labeled as Fake could also fall under Bias or
840   Unreliable simultaneously, making it unreasonable to strictly assign it to one category over another.
841   Consequently, the analysis below may be based on an inherently uncertain foundation.
842
843        • Bias: The node types for Bias are similar to those for Reliable, particularly in areas like
844
             Location and Entertainment (see Fig. 3(a)). This is consistent with the dataset’s description
             of biased content, which often involves propaganda or opinions presented as facts, rather than
845
             outright falsehoods. One notable difference is that Bias lacks prominence in node types like
846
             Science, Religion, and Sports, which appear more in Reliable news.
847        • Conspiracy: The node types for Conspiracy differ markedly from Reliable, with a notable
848          emphasis on Location and Entertainment (see Fig. 3(b)). This aligns with the nature of conspir-
849          acy theories, which often center around speculative narratives involving covert operations or
850          unverified claims. In contrast, Reliable news exhibits a more balanced spread across categories
851          like Science and Sports, highlighting the factual and grounded nature of its content. The disparity
852          between these node type patterns underscores the speculative and unverified themes prevalent in
853          conspiracy content.
854        • Fake: The Fake category shows a scattered pattern in node types, with no strong emphasis
855
             on any particular theme (see Fig. 3(c)). This reflects the diversity and often random nature
             of fake news, which may cover a wide range of topics with little factual basis. Compared to
856
             Reliable news, which focuses on well-defined topics such as Science, Religion, and Sports, Fake
857
             news lacks consistency, mirroring the dataset’s characterization of this category as containing
858          misleading or fabricated stories.
859        • Political: As expected, the node type patterns for Political news are closely aligned with
860          Reliable, with significant overlap in categories such as Science, Location, Entertainment, and
861          Sports (see Fig. 3(d)). This similarity is consistent with the dataset’s description of Political
862          news as verifiable but presented with a specific viewpoint. The comparison with Reliable
863          indicates that Political content, while biased toward certain ideologies or perspectives, does not
             stray far from reliable reporting in terms of the types of entities discussed.
