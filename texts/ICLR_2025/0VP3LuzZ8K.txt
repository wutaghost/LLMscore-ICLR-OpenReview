009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We study the generalization of iterative noisy gradient schemes on smooth non-
012            convex losses. Formally, we establish time-independent information theoretic
013            generalization bounds for Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD) that
014            do not diverge as the iteration count increases. Our bounds are obtained through a
015            stability argument: we analyze the difference between two SGLD sequences ran
016            in parallel on two datasets sampled from the same distribution. Our result only
017            requires an isoperimetric inequality to hold, which is merely a restriction on the
018
               tails of the loss. We relax the assumptions of prior work to establish that the iter-
               ates stay within a bounded KL divergence from each other. Under an additional
019
               dissipativity assumption, we show that the stronger Renyi divergence also stays
020
               bounded by establishing a uniform log-Sobolev constant of the iterates. Without
021            dissipativity, we sidestep the need for local log-Sobolev inequalities and instead
022            exploit the regularizing properties of Gaussian convolution. These techniques al-
023            low us to show that strong convexity is not necessary for finite stability bounds
024            and thus for finite generalization and differential privacy bounds.

029   Learning algorithms whose outputs are not highly sensitive to the specifics of their training data are
030   likely to generalize well. This is the intuitive idea that undergirds the framework of information-
031   theoretic generalization. The seminal contributions of Russo & Zou (2016) and Xu & Raginsky
032
      (2017) establish that the expected generalization gap of an algorithm A can be controlled by the
      amount of information the algorithm extracts from its training dataset D of size n. Formally, they
033
      show under reasonable assumptions that

036                           |generalization gap(A, D)| ≤ O
                                                                          n
037
038   where the input-output mutual information I(A(D), D) measures the dependence of the algo-
039   rithm’s output on the observed samples D. Unlike uniform-convergence-based bounds, information-
040   theoretic bounds depend on the algorithm and the data distribution. This makes them well
041   suited to assess the performance of models whose complexity renders vacuous all classic uniform-
042   convergence bounds. More importantly, they align with the practical observations that generaliza-
043   tion is data distribution dependent, as observed in experiments contrasting random versus real labels
044
      Zhang et al. (2021).
045   For them to be useful however a major difficulty remains in controlling this input-output mutual
046   information for specific algorithms. Of particular interest for machine learning are algorithms ob-

058
      The step-wise analysis leads to vacuous generalization bounds as iterations increase even if the
      recursion in equation 1 converges to a limit. Even more curious is the fact that this limit approxi-
059
      mates the Gibbs distribution e−Fn , where Fn is the optimized training loss, which has been shown
060
      to achieve a finite information-theoretic bound Xu & Raginsky (2017); Pensia et al. (2018). We
061   are faced with the strange situation where the iterates have an exploding bound but converge close
062   to a distribution that has a finite one. Our aim in this work amend this strange gap to answer the
063   following question:
064
065       Do noisy iterative schemes in non-convex settings admit generalization bounds that go to zero as
066                  n → ∞ without becoming vacuous as the number of iterations increases?
067
068
      Our main motivation for tackling this question is to understand whether early-stopping is necessary
      for generalization. If generalization gaps indeed diverge as iterations increase, then long training
069
      runs with non-vanishing step sizes are proscribed, by theory. If the divergence is merely the result of
070
      a loose analysis, then we should be able to establish better bounds that are more faithful to practice,
071   where long training runs are common Power et al. (2022); Nakkiran et al. (2019).
072
073   We thus want to establish properties of noisy iterative schemes in their most realistic setting, i.e.,
074
      when they are run for thousands of iterations with non-vanishing stepsizes. The algorithm we
      study as a representative of such schemes is noisy SGD, or Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics
075
      (SGLD)Welling & Teh (2011). To study its generalization, we show that characterizing its stability
076
      is sufficient. Informally speaking, if SGLD outputs weights that are close (in a well-defined sense)
077   when ran on two, different, independently-sampled datasets then it must not be overfitting. The
078   difference between the outputs of SGLD measured using the KL and a stronger Rényi divergence
079   relates to the expected generalization of the algorithm. Rényi stability goes further and also relates
080   to differential privacy, a notion closely related to generalization. Through these techniques, we will
081   show that noisy iterative schemes can have finite generalization and privacy bounds in unbounded
082   non-convex settings, even when run for a large number of iterations. We make the following precise
083   contributions.
084   Contributions:
085
086            • Under a structural assumption on the optimized loss, namely dissipativity, we show that
087              uniform-in-time bounds can be established for both generalization and (ϵ, δ)-differential
088              privacy of noisy SGD. Our bound only involves stability-related quantities and does not
089              rely on ergodicity. We thus improve over the prior work in this setting namely Farghly &
090              Rebeschini (2021); Futami & Fujisawa (2024); Zhu et al. (2024) who either rely on ergod-
091
                 icity (thus involving non-stability related constants) or obtain bounds that do not decay to
                 zero as n → ∞.
092
093            • To achieve our result, we resolve in passing an open question of Vempala & Wibisono
094              (2019) on the isoperimetric properties of the biased limit of discrete Langevin iterates. We
095
                 show that under dissipativity, all the iterates verify a uniform log-Sobolev inequality, a
                 result which, to date, was only shown under strong convexity.
096
097            • As dissipativity is a crude assumption used control the log-Sobolev constant that often
098              introduces constants exponential in dimension, we establish a secondary result that removes
099              the dissipativity assumption but exploits ergodicity. Our bound in this case is polynomial
100
                 in dimension and in the Gibbs’ distribution’s log-Sobolev constant. Unlike the analysis of
                 Futami & Fujisawa (2024) who use dissipativity and rely on an involved extension of the
101
                 parametrix method to unbounded drifts Bally & Kohatsu-Higa (2015), our analysis relaxes
102
                 dissipativity and only exploits the regularizing properties of Gaussian convolution.

      2.4   D IFFERENTIAL PRIVACY
166
167   A closely related notion to generalization is differential privacy. For ϵ, δ > 0, (ϵ, δ)-Differential pri-
168   vacy Dwork (2008) is a standardized formalization of the notion of privacy. A useful interpretation
169   of it is given by Wasserman & Zhou (2010) who show that an (ϵ, δ)-differential privacy guarantee on
170   an algorithm PX|D outputting weights X given a dataset D, equates to a guarantee that no statistical
171   test (or null-hypothesis test) on the output can reliably determine if a specific data point was part of
172   the training set D. The false-positive and false-negative rates of any such test will be controlled by
173
      ϵ and δ. Algorithms with small ϵ and δ are those for which no powerful test exists. Remarkably,
      as shown below, stability in terms of the stronger Rényi divergence implies that an algorithm is
174
      (ϵ, δ)-differential private.
175
176   Lemma 3 (From Rényi stability to (ϵ, δ)-DP (Thm.21 Balle et al. (2020))). Let PXk |D be a random-
177   ized algorithm outputting weights given a dataset D. Let q > 1, ϵ > 0. If Dq PXk |D ||PXk |D′ ≤ ϵ
178   for D, D′ adjacent datasets, then PXk |D is (ϵ + log 1/δ−log(q)
                                                             q−1      + log q−1
                                                                             q , δ)-differentially private for
179   any δ > 0.

181   The study of the privacy properties of noisy iterative schemes appears in Minami et al. (2016). A
182   comprehensive treatment from a perspective of privacy amplification by iteration in convex setting is
183   provided in Feldman et al. (2018) using Rényi differential privacy Mironov (2017). Fundamentally,
184
      the technical problem of showing stability is identical for generalization and privacy, which is why
      we mention differential privacy here. The analysis of Ganesh & Talwar (2020); Chourasia et al.

193   Gaussian (or uniform on the unit sphere) like concentration properties on the distribution Gozlan

195   Definition 4 (LSI). A distribution b is said to verify the log-Sobolev inequality (LSI) with constant
196   cb if for any a ≪ b,
197                                             cb
                                  DKL (a||b) ≤ Ea ∥∇ log a − ∇ log b∥2 .

198                                              2

      Instead of assuming strong convexity of the optimized loss Fn , we assume in the following that the
200   Gibbs distribution with density proportional to e−Fn satisfies the LSI. A precise discussion on which
201   conditions of Fn yield the LSI is provided in section 5. Moreover, our main technical tool will rely

209   3     R ELATED W ORK
210
211   Time-independent information-theoretic generalization Several authors have considered the
212   question of time dependence of generalization bounds of noisy iterative schemes. The work of Mou
213   et al. (2018) was the first to notice that each step includes a decay factor that can compensate for
214   step-wise increases derived in previous analyses. Unfortunately, only a degrading decay factor is
215   established, making vanishing step-sizes mandatory. Li et al. (2019) build on their result to show
      a time-independent bound for non-convex losses obtained as bounded perturbations of a strongly


                                                          4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      convex loss. Unfortunately, their bounds do not go to 0 as n → ∞. In bounded settings, where a
217   projection step follows each noisy gradient update, the work of Wang et al. (2023) and Chien et al.
218   (2024) establish that a uniform decay factor can be established. They show that if each iteration of
219   SGLD is followed by a projection on a convex set C, then a constant decay factor that depends on
220   exp (diam(C)/η) can be established.
221
      Fewer papers tackle the unbounded setting. Using coupling techniques, Farghly & Rebeschini
222
      (2021) establish a time-uniform bound but incur inelegant step size dependences and do not ob-
223   tain a bound going to 0 as n → ∞ for fixed stepsizes. The recent work Zhu et al. (2024) considers
224   the same dissipative setting and exploits Markov chain perturbation results but their Wassertein anal-
225   ysis requires that Lipschitz losses be used to measure the generalization gap. The work most closely
226   related to ours is Futami & Fujisawa (2024). For dissipative losses, their result involves dimension-
227   dependent quantities unrelated to stability. We show that the dissipative setting is friendly enough
228   to not require such constants, and we improve their analysis to remove the dissipativity assumption
229   under ergodicity. We include a table for ease of comparison in Appendix A.

369   5.2   D ISSIPATIVE FUNCTIONS
370
371   The standard way of relaxing strong convexity is by adding perturbations. A classic result estab-
372   lishes that bounded perturbations of strongly convex functions still satisfy the LSI, albeit with an

      turbations can preserve the LSI. For instance, if V is strongly convex and H a Lipschtiz function,

375
      Lipschitz losses with weight decay analyzed by Farghly & Rebeschini (2021).

      bounds as long as the SGLD iterates converge towards a target distribution that verifies the LSI. It is
464   possible to make milder requirements on f at the cost of introducing quantities unrelated to stability:

      7     C ONCLUSION
526
527
      Our Rényi and KL stability bounds directly imply generalization and differential privacy guarantees
528   for disspativite objectives, extending results only available for strongly convex settings. Noisy it-
529   erative algorithms can be run ad infinitum with non-vanishing step sizes without early-stopping in
530   non-convex settings. This is in accordance with the practical observations that long training runs
531   do not always harm generalization and in fact can sometimes improve it Olmin & Lindsten (2024).
532   We also relax the dissipativity assumption to show a generalization bound that holds solely under an
533   isoperimetric assumption of Fn .
534   The main limitation of our work is the dimension dependence of our bounds, which is affected by
535   the choice of β. For the algorithm to be useful at minimization, a choice β = O(d) Raginsky
536   et al. (2017) is necessary. However, the dependence of cπ on β is in general poor. Under additional
537   assumption on Fn , this dependence can be made linear Li & Erdogdu (2023), but remains unavoid-
538   able for information-theoretic bounds Livni (2024). The extension of our analysis to non-isotropic,
539   non-Gaussian, or state-dependent noise will inch closer to capturing SGD and further confirm that
      early-stopping is not a requirement in non-convex settings.
