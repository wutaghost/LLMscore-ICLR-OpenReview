009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Weakly supervised learning (WSL) is a popular machine learning paradigm in
012            recent years that aims to learn a classifier with incomplete, imprecise, or inac-
013            curate supervision. Existing WSL approaches have mainly focused on design-
014            ing different loss functions or training strategies and then training models from
015            scratch. In this paper, we first empirically show that a zero-shot baseline based
016            on the Contrastive Language-Image Pre-Training (CLIP) model with class de-
017            scriptions empowered by GPT-4o can outperform previous state-of-the-art meth-
018
               ods trained from scratch on various WSL problems. Therefore, this motivates
               us to fine-tune pre-trained models to further improve the performance. How-
019
               ever, our additional experiments show that naive use of existing WSL losses de-
020
               grades performance due to severe overfitting exacerbation and feature degener-
021            ation problems. To address these problems, we propose a novel weakly super-
022            vised fine-tuning approach using dual classification heads that are trained syn-
023            ergistically by alternately distilling reliable supervision and performing efficient
024            model fine-tuning. Theoretically, we prove the consistency and convergence rate
025            of the proposed risk estimator. Empirically, extensive experiments on bench-
026            mark datasets of different WSL problems validate the effectiveness of the pro-
027            posed approach against state-of-the-art competitors. The code is provided at
028            https://github.com/ICLR2025-6897/WSFT_code.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      The success of deep learning depends on a large amount of training data with high-quality labels.
      However, such a condition can be an obstacle in many real-world scenarios where labeled training
034
      data is scarce. Weakly supervised learning (WSL) aims to train a classifier with only weak supervi-
035
      sion that can achieve comparable performance to fully supervised learning approaches (Zhou, 2018;
036   Sugiyama et al., 2022). In recent years, WSL has made great progress, and a variety of WSL prob-
037   lems have been studied, such as positive-unlabeled (PU) learning (Elkan & Noto, 2008; Kiryo et al.,
038   2017; Bekker & Davis, 2020), unlabeled-unlabeled (UU) learning (Lu et al., 2019; 2020; Xie et al.,
039   2024), and similarity-based classification (Bao et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2023a).
040
      In the current era of foundation models, a common strategy to solve machine learning problems is to
041
      adopt a pre-trained model and then fine-tune it based on specific downstream training data. Existing
042   WSL approaches have paid most attention to the design of loss functions, using either unbiased risk
043   estimators (UREs) with solid theoretical guarantees (Niu et al., 2016; Kiryo et al., 2017; Wang et al.,
044   2024b) or effective regularization techniques to improve classification performance (Berthelot et al.,
045   2019; Wang et al., 2022a; Li et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023b). A common benchmark solution is to
046   train a deep neural network from scratch to validate the effectiveness of the proposed loss function.
047   However, the classification performance may degenerate significantly when using more complex
048   datasets. This motivates us to consider a natural and practical research question: Can we fine-tune
049   pre-trained models with weakly supervised data to achieve better performance?
050   In this paper, we attempt to investigate how pre-trained models can be appropriately exploited
051   for WSL problems. First, we find that by using GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024) to summarize and en-
052   rich the class description, and then directly applying a zero-shot Contrastive Language-Image Pre-
053   Training (CLIP) model, we can achieve better classification performance than some state-of-the-
      art (SOTA) WSL algorithms trained from scratch (see Section 3.1). Such an observation indicates


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      that the performance of WSL approaches can be improved greatly by exploiting pre-trained models
055   appropriately. To further improve performance on more complex datasets, it may often be beneficial
056   to fine-tune pre-trained models with downstream weakly supervised training data. However, we find
057   that naively fine-tuning pre-trained models with off-the-shelf unbiased or corrected risk estimators
058   can often lead to inferior performance (see Section 3.2). We identify several causes of the shortcom-
059   ings of existing risk estimators, including overfitting exacerbation and feature degeneration, which
060   severely limit the effectiveness of model fine-tuning.
061   To this end, we propose a weakly supervised fine-tuning (WSFT) approach to effectively fine-tune
062   pre-trained models for WSL problems (see Section 3.3). To overcome the shortcomings of existing
063   risk estimators during the fine-tuning process, we directly perform empirical risk minimization by
064   selecting training data with reliable labels. More specifically, we perform sample selection by dis-
065   tilling reliable supervision from the classifier obtained as a minimizer of the corrected risk estimator.
066   Then, we alternately perform efficient model fine-tuning. The supervision distillation process and
067   model fine-tuning are synergistically encapsulated in a unified framework and are mutually benefi-
068   cial to improve the downstream classification performance. Our contributions can be summarized
069
      as follows:
070   • We find that pre-trained models can significantly outperform SOTA WSL approaches on bench-
071     mark datasets, suggesting a way to improve benchmark evaluation of WSL.
072   • We propose a WSFT approach to effectively fine-tune pre-trained models for various WSL prob-
073     lems. WSFT encapsulates reliable supervision distillation and efficient model fine-tuning seam-
074
        lessly supported by theoretical guarantees.
      • Extensive experiments on various WSL problems and benchmark datasets validate the effective-
075
        ness of our proposed method against SOTA WSL approaches (see Section 4).

083   2.1   P ROBLEM S ETTING
084
085   From the perspective of the data generation process, WSL can be roughly categorized into two
086   main formulations (Sugiyama et al., 2022), i.e., mutually contaminated distributions (MCD) (Scott
087   et al., 2013; Katz-Samuels et al., 2019) and class-conditional noise (CCN) (Natarajan et al., 2013;
088
      2018; Van Rooyen & Williamson, 2018). In particular, CCN can be shown to be a special case of
      MCD (Menon et al., 2015). Without loss of generality, we mainly discuss UU learning (Lu et al.,
089
      2019), the most general formulation of MCD in this paper. It has been proved that a wide variety
090
      of WSL problems are special cases of UU learning (Chiang & Sugiyama, 2023), including PU
091   learning, pairwise-comparison (Pcomp) (Feng et al., 2021) learning, and similarity-unlabeled (SU)
092   learning (Bao et al., 2018). Notably, our proposal can handle any kind of weak supervision generated
093   based on MCD or CCN, and can be extended to multi-class setting.

141   In this section, we first introduce a zero-shot baseline based on the CLIP model, which can outper-

145   3.1   A Z ERO -S HOT BASELINE

148

149

229   3.3   A W EAKLY S UPERVISED F INE -T UNING A PPROACH
230
231   To overcome the deficiency of UREs and CREs, a natural remedy is to perform ordinary empirical
232   risk minimization directly on labeled data, since it does not involve mismatched loss terms. In
233
      this way, each class can learn discriminative feature representations, which will contribute to better
      performance. However, since true labels may not be accessible in WSL, it is necessary to investigate
234
      how to select reliable labeled data. In this paper, we rely on high-confidence predictions made by
235
      off-the-shelf classifiers, which have been shown to be a type of reliable supervision information
236   widely adopted in the literature (Chen et al., 2020b; Zhang et al., 2021a; Kim et al., 2022).
237

269   Discussion. Different from self-training in the SSL and domain adaptation literature (Lee et al.,
      2013; Zou et al., 2019; Wei et al., 2021), our approach adopts two classification heads to separate


                                                             5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      the generation of pseudo-labels from the training of the classifier. The advantage is that this can
271   effectively mitigate the confirmation bias that widely exists when training the classifier and gener-
272   ating pseudo-labels with a single classifier (Arazo et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2022). Moreover, unlike
273   some SSL approaches that try to mitigate the confirmation bias by constructing teacher models with
274   different strategies (Laine & Aila, 2017; Tarvainen & Valpola, 2017; Xie et al., 2020), the classifier
275   used to generate pseudo-labels is not explicitly exposed to pseudo-labels.
276
      Extension. Although this paper discusses binary classification, without loss of generality, our ap-
277   proach can be easily extended to multi-class classification problems. In particular, for weakly super-
278
      vised multi-class classification (Cour et al., 2011; Lu et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024b), Rp1 pθ, ω1 q
279   can be instantiated accordingly with the CREs for specific WSL problems. Also, the supervision
280   distillation procedure should be adapted to the multi-class classification setting.

478   In this section, we discuss related work on WSL, pre-trained models, applications of pre-trained
479   models to WSL, and knowledge distillation.
480   Weakly supervised learning. In WSL, we want to train a model that can perform comparably
481   to supervised learning under incomplete, inexact, or inaccurate supervision (Zhou, 2018; Sugiyama
482   et al., 2022). WSL in machine learning is fundamentally different from weakly supervised seg-
483   mentation (WSS) in computer vision (Ahn & Kwak, 2018; Lin et al., 2023), where WSS refers to
484   segmenting images with image-level labels. In recent years, many WSL problems have been stud-
485   ied (Ratner et al., 2016; Lu et al., 2019; Bao et al., 2018; Feng et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021c;
      Li et al., 2023). The most common strategy for WSL is to rewrite the classification risk and then

498   derive an URE. It has typically been shown that the URE is a consistent risk estimator with respect
499
      to the expected classification risk and enjoys optimal convergence rates (Sugiyama et al., 2022). Al-
      though UREs are elegant and theoretically sound, a common drawback is that they often encounter
500
      overfitting problems. Another group of WSL methods aims to improve classification performance
501
      by designing various regularization techniques (Yu et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2024), such as contrastive
502   learning (Li et al., 2021a; Wang et al., 2022a; Acharya et al., 2022). However, such complicated
503   training strategies may not be computationally efficient when fine-tuning large pre-trained models.
504
505
      Pre-trained models. Pre-trained models have achieved great success in many machine learning
      applications (Brown et al., 2020; Caron et al., 2021; Oquab et al., 2024; Jia et al., 2021; Bommasani
506
      et al., 2021). For example, the CLIP model (Radford et al., 2021) uses a contrastive loss to align
507
      image and text features within a common manifold, ensuring that semantically similar images and
508   text are positioned close together. As a result, CLIP achieves high zero-shot classification accuracy,
509   often comparable to or even better than supervised models (Radford et al., 2021). To further improve
510   the performance of pre-trained models, various fine-tuning methods have been proposed (Jia et al.,
511   2022; Zhou et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2022). While pre-trained models have achieved significant success
512   in the standard supervised learning paradigm, their impact on WSL remains underexplored.
513
      Weakly supervised learning with pre-trained models. Hendrycks et al. (2019) found that using
514   pre-trained models can improve the model robustness to noisy labels. Yu et al. (2021) investigated
515   how to fine-tune pre-trained language models for text classification tasks with only noisy and unla-
516   beled data. However, their scope is limited to the classification problem of noisy and unlabeled text,
517   and their training strategies are less efficient. Some recent works in SSL or noisy-label learning have
518   tried to use or fine-tune pre-trained CLIP models and achieved better empirical performance (Wang
519   et al., 2022b; Gan & Wei, 2024; Wang et al., 2024a; Ahn et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2024). However,
520   their approaches can only be applied within a single problem setting and cannot be extended to solve
521   other WSL problems.
522   Knowledge distillation. The goal of knowledge distillation (KD) (Hinton et al., 2015) is to train
523   a student model by distilling knowledge from a teacher model (Zhang et al., 2018; Zhao et al.,
524   2022a). Recently, KD has been applied to many areas, such as vision-language models to maintain
525   the generalization ability of models (Yao et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024), SSL to leverage the teacher’s
526   knowledge (Sohn et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2024). In particular, the supervision distillation process in
527   the proposed WSFT approach can be considered as a kind of knowledge distillation. However, the
528
      teacher model in WSFT is another classification head of the same size that is efficient and effective
      in mitigating confirmation bias.
529
530
531   6     C ONCLUSION
532
      In this paper, we investigated the impact of pre-trained models on WSL. Our empirical results
533
      showed that the use of pre-trained models, such as CLIP, can significantly improve the classification
534   performance for WSL even without fine-tuning. To further improve the classification performance
535   of pre-trained models, we proposed a novel WSFT approach that uses dual classification heads to
536   alternately perform efficient model fine-tuning and supervision distillation. Extensive experiments
537   on different WSL settings demonstrated the superiority of WSFT over SOTA WSL methods. A
538   limitation of our approach is that it is specifically designed for image data. In the future, it is also
539   promising to investigate how LLMs, another large category of pre-trained foundation models, affect
      and improve WSL.
