009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Airport video semantic segmentation is fundamental to airport surveillance appli-
012            cations, yet there currently lacks a specialized benchmark and algorithms for this
013            task. In this paper, we introduce the first large-scale Airport Video Semantic Seg-
014            mentation dataset (AVSS) for airport surveillance. AVSS comprises 18 common
015            semantic categories at airports, and 250 videos, totaling over 140,000 frames with
016            accurate manual annotations. AVSS covers a wide range of challenges for airport
017            video surveillance, such as extreme multi-scale, intra-class diversity, inter-class
018            similarity, etc. We analyze statistical information and evaluate 21 state-of-the-
019            art (SOTA) semantic segmentation algorithms on AVSS. The significant perfor-
020
               mance degradation indicates that current models are far from practical applica-
               tion. Furthermore, we discuss how to develop video semantic segmentation al-
021
               gorithms for airport surveillance and the generalizability of AVSS to other tasks
022
               and datasets. AVSS serves as a research resource for airport semantic segmen-
023            tation and a robustness evaluation tool for segmentation algorithms in practical
024            applications. AVSS is available at www.agvs-caac.com/avss/avss.html.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030      As global air passenger and cargo traffic rapidly increases, airports become more crowded, leading
031   to frequent flight delays and occasional collisions. Thus, building intelligent airports is crucial
032   to improving the efficiency and safety of airports. Intelligent airport applications, such as video
033
      docking and conflict warning systems, demand high-precision scene analysis to ensure safety and
      operational efficiency. These tasks rely on semantic segmentation to produce pixel-level masks,
034
      which are essential for accurately identifying and distinguishing different objects, such as cars,
035
      runways, and airplanes, within complex environments. Pixel-level segmentation provides precise
036   boundaries, allowing for the detection of even slight overlaps or proximity between objects, which
037   is crucial for preventing accidents (as shown in Figure 1). Bounding boxes alone cannot achieve this
038   level of detail, as they only offer rough object localization and may miss critical spatial relationships
039   and fine boundaries that could impact safety. So we observe the performance of existing semantic
040   segmentation algorithms on airports.
041
         Our experiments show that semantic segmentation algorithms performing well on public datasets
042   suffer a significant performance drop in airport scenarios, with mean Intersection over Union (mIoU)
043   decreasing by up to 78%. This decline is due to the unique semantic categories and structure of
044   airports. For example, airplanes are challenging to segment due to their low-compact profile, un-
045   like common categories such as buildings and vehicles. Additionally, airport imaging can reach
046   kilometers, introducing extreme multi-scale challenges. These factors render public dataset-trained
047   algorithms unsuitable for airport scenes, prompting us to develop a video semantic segmentation
048   dataset specifically for airports and design specialized algorithms for this context.
049      This paper introduces a novel benchmark for semantic segmentation in airport video surveillance,
050   called Airport Video Semantic Segmentation (AVSS). AVSS comprises 18 major semantic cate-
051   gories in airport ground (as shown in Figure 2), over 250 videos with more than 140,000 frames,
052   addressing challenges like extreme multi-scale, intra-class diversity, inter-class similarity, etc. We
053   manually annotated all frames to generate precise pixel-level ground truth. We evaluated 21 state-of-
      the-art segmentation models on AVSS, fine-tuning them for optimal performance. However, results

      2.1    S EMANTIC S EGMENTATION DATASETS
109
110     The semantic segmentation datasets are divided into image semantic segmentation datasets and
111   video semantic segmentation datasets.
112
113
         Image semantic segmentation datasets focus on pixel-level classification of static images, in-
      cluding ADE20K Zhou et al. (2017), Pascal VOC Everingham et al. (2010), and COCO Lin et al.
114
      (2014). ADE20K is for scene understanding, containing nearly 30,000 annotated images for seman-
115
      tic segmentation. Pascal VOC includes 20 semantic classes and one background class, comprising
116   11,000 images for recognition tasks. MS COCO is a large-scale dataset for object detection and seg-
117   mentation, the images are from real-world scenarios, which adds diversity and realism. There are
118   80 object classes and one background class in COCO. Video semantic segmentation datasets in-
119   corporate the temporal dimension, including Cityscapes Cordts et al. (2016), and VSPW Miao et al.
120   (2021). Cityscapes is the most widely used video semantic segmentation dataset of urban scenes,
121   providing a wide variety of city street scene images with 5,000 high-quality pixel-level annotations.
122   VSPW is the first large-scale multi-scene video semantic segmentation dataset. It is larger, more
123   densely annotated, and covers more diverse scenes than other video segmentation datasets. It in-
124
      cludes 3,536 videos and 251,632 semantic segmentation frames, including 124 semantic categories.
      However, current dataset types mainly focus on general scenes or urban streets, lacking datasets
125
      specifically for traffic scenes, especially airports.
126
127
128   2.2    S EMANTIC S EGMENTATION A LGORITHMS
129
130      Current mainstream image semantic segmentation algorithms are designed for general scenes, but
131   they perform poorly in specific scenarios, necessitating the development of semantic segmentation
132
      algorithms tailored to these specific contexts.
133      FCN Long et al. (2015) is the first CNN-based image semantic segmentation model, replacing
134   fully connected layers with fully convolutional layers. PSPNet Zhao et al. (2017) introduced a
135   pyramid pooling module to obtain multi-scale contextual information. DeepLab V3 Chen et al.
136   (2017) further incorporated atrous spatial pyramid pooling and separable convolution to improve
137   computational efficiency and segmentation accuracy. DeepLab V3+ Chen et al. (2018) optimized
138
      the decoder module for finer edge processing. ICNet Zhao et al. (2018) introduced a cascade branch
      network, gradually refining feature maps through cascading multiple branches, balancing segmenta-
139
      tion speed and accuracy. HRNet Wang et al. (2020) proposed high-resolution sub-networks, present-
140
      ing new solutions to resolution-limited and multi-scale problems. BiSeNetV1 Yu et al. (2018) and
141   BiSeNetV2 Yu et al. (2021) utilize a dual-stream network to simultaneously capture spatial and se-
142   mantic information, enabling real-time semantic segmentation. MobileNetV3 Howard et al. (2019)
143   is a lightweight convolutional neural network designed for efficient performance on mobile and edge
144   devices to enhance both speed and accuracy. Additionally, there are image semantic segmentation
145   algorithms based on Transformers with global receptive field and better feature interaction, such as
146   SETR Zheng et al. (2021), Swin Transformer Liu et al. (2021), Segmentor, and Segformer Xie et al.
147   (2021). For video semantic segmentation, CFFM Sun et al. (2022a) and CFFM++ Sun et al. (2024)
148   combine the dynamic and static contexts of the video to improve segmentation performance. And
149
      MRCFA Sun et al. (2022b) enables better temporal information aggregation by mining cross-frame
      affinities. Tube-Link Li et al. (2023) is a unified, near-online framework for video segmentation
150
      that enhances spatial-temporal tube tracking through attention and temporal contrastive learning.
151
      DVIS++ Zhang et al. (2023) proposes a decoupled framework for universal video segmentation, sim-
152   plifying spatio-temporal modeling through separate segmentation, tracking, and refinement stages.

         Dataset Design: The fundamental principle of dataset design is to cover as many semantic cate-
      gories and variations as possible on the airport ground. Based on long-term observation and analysis,
181
      we identified 18 major semantic categories. These categories are divided into moving objects on the
182
      airport ground (airplanes, vehicles, and people), the airport ground itself (runways, liaison roads,
183   aprons, lawns, landmarks, containers, and signs), inside the airport (tower, airport terminal, board-
184   ing bridge, pole, and security post), and external factors (sky, buildings, background).
185
186
         Data Collection: Since airports are semi-militarized areas, we obtained data collection permis-
      sions through cooperation with the Civil Aviation Administration of China. The data collection sites
187
      are large international airports in Southwest China. To ensure data diversity, we recorded over 5,000
188
      short videos within a three-month collection period, with an average duration of 15 seconds and
189   a frame rate of 30 FPS. The data collection equipment included four fixed cameras and one PTZ
190   camera, with resolutions varying in 1280×960, 1440×1080, and 1920×1080. During data collec-
191   tion, we increased data diversity by varying collection times and adjusting camera angles and focal
192   lengths. We then strictly selected data based on diversity principles, picking 250 videos totaling
193   about 140,000 frames to form the AVSS.
194
         Data Annotation: The data annotation process is shown in Figure 3. First, open the annota-
195   tion software AnyLabeling NGUYEN et al. (Figure 3 (a)). The left side shows annotation options,
196   including commonly used polygon annotation tools. The center one displays the image to be anno-
197   tated. The right side shows possible semantic categories and the current annotation directory. Next,
198   annotate objects such as airplanes (Figure 3 (b)). The image is zoomed to an appropriate size. We
199   use the polygon tool, clicking points along the airplane’s contour accurately (Figure 3 (c) (d)). Then,
200   annotate the next object, such as the car (Figure 3 (e)). After annotating all objects, the visualization
201   of the annotation results is shown in Figure 3 (f).
202      To improve annotation efficiency and maintain continuity between frames, the annotations from
203   a single frame can be reused for subsequent frames. In this approach, the labels from the previous
204   frame are applied directly to the next frame, with minor adjustments made for any moving objects.
205   This method ensures temporal consistency across frames while significantly enhancing the speed of
206   annotation.

446   4.4      F UTURE RESEARCH D ISCUSSION
447
448      We discuss the causes of each challenge by combining statistical information with segmentation
449   visualization results and introduce airport scene-specific prior knowledge to address challenges, such
450   as Multi-scale, Moving Semantic Objects and Winged Targets. Specifically, we propose a semantic
451   segmentation algorithm based on airplane 3D models.
452      Extreme Multi-scale: Small moving targets and large stationary ones present different chal-
453   lenges. Distant people disappear from the feature map due to their small size, while closer indi-
454   viduals have shape distortions. In Figure 8 (row 5), distant airplanes are segmented roughly, with
455   incomplete wings and tails due to their small pixel count. In contrast, large objects like the sky and

462
463     The various special challenges present in airports make general semantic segmentation algorithms
464
      unsuitable for airport scenes. Therefore, we propose three feasible solutions as follows.
465      Categorical Symbiosis: Multi-scale segmentation is difficult because the scale variation in the
466   scene is unknown and the processing of different scales is incompatible. Observations have shown
467   that the scale of a target can be predicted based on the background structure. For example, the ”sky”
468   is usually located at the far end of the airport scene. While the ”apron” is usually located at the near
469   end. When an airplane appears in the sky, it is generally a small target. When an airplane appears
470
      on the apron, it is generally a large-scale target. Therefore, a priori information on airport structures
      can be used to solve multi-scale problems.
471
472      Motion Priori: Several semantic objects in airports have special motion patterns. For example,
473   airplanes have only two directional patterns, straight ahead and turning. Since airplanes are large
474   mass objects, they must follow fixed routes such as runways and liaison roads, or else they will lead
475   to serious safety problems. This inspires us to model the motion and then use it to guide semantic
476
      segmentation.
477      Shape Priori: It has been observed that for a wingspan target such as airplanes, strong shape
478   constraints can be imposed by simple manual interaction. For example, the approximate shape of
479   an airplane can be obtained by drawing two cross-crossing lines connecting the head and tail of the
480   airplane and the two flanks. The semantic segmentation algorithm designed based on cross-shape
481   constraints can effectively improve the segmentation accuracy of the wingspan target.
482       Concretely, as the most important semantic category in airports, we aim for high segmentation
483   accuracy for airplanes. Therefore, we introduce the prior information of 3D airplane models. We
484   first project the 3D models from multiple angles to create a library of 2D projections. During the
485   training of the semantic segmentation network, we also train an object detection head, which iden-
      tifies airplane regions in the image. Then model selects the most similar 2D projection from the

526
527      In this work, we introduced AVSS, a novel benchmark specifically designed for airport video
528   surveillance. With 250 video sequences and over 140,000 pixel-level annotated images, AVSS ad-
529   dresses key challenges in airport scenes by incorporating prior domain knowledge and proposing
530   a semantic segmentation algorithm based on 3D airplane models. Extensive analyses of category
531   distribution, video coherence, and video attributes demonstrated the benchmark’s diversity and ro-
532   bustness. By fine-tuning and evaluating 21 state-of-the-art image/video segmentation models, we
533   highlighted the complexity of AVSS. Additionally, comparative studies across different datasets val-
534
      idated the generalizability of AVSS. Overall, AVSS serves as a valuable resource for advancing
      research in semantic segmentation of airports.
