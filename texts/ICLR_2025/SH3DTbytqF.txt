009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            World models play a crucial role in decision-making within embodied environ-
012            ments, enabling cost-free explorations that would otherwise be expensive in the
013            real world. However, to support faithful imagination in out-of-distribution (OOD)
014            regions, world models must possess significant generalizability, which poses sub-
015            stantial challenges for previous scalable approaches. This paper addresses two
016            primary sources of the world model generalization error: the policy distribution
017            shift caused by the divergence between test and data-collection policies, and the
018
               compounding error arising from long-horizon autoregressive rollout. To tackle
               these issues, we introduce the policy-conditioning and the retracing-rollout tech-
019
               niques, respectively. Incorporating these two techniques, we present Whale, a
020
               scalable spatial-temporal transformer-based world model with enhanced generaliz-
021            ability. We first demonstrate the effectiveness of the two techniques, showcasing
022            their consistent superiority over previous baselines in both trajectory generation
023            quality and value estimation accuracy. Furthermore, we propose Whale-X, a 414M
024            parameter world model trained on 970K trajectories from Open X-Embodiment
025            datasets. We show that Whale-X exhibits promising scalability and strong general-
026            izability in real-world manipulation scenarios using minimal demonstrations.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Human beings have the capability to envision an imagined world in their minds, predicting how
032   different actions might lead to different outcomes (Maus et al., 2013; Nortmann et al., 2015). Inspired
033
      by this aspect of human intelligence, world models (Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018) are designed to
      abstract real-world dynamics and provide such "what if" prediction. As a result, embodied agents
034
      can interact with world models instead of real-world environments to generate simulation data,
035
      which can be used for various downstream tasks, including counterfactual prediction (Chen et al.,
036   2023), off-value estimation (Fu et al., 2021), and offline reinforcement learning (Levine et al., 2020).
037   However, the requirement for accurate out-of-distribution (OOD) predictions for reliable model
038   imagination poses significant challenges to the generalizability of world models, which has not been
039   well addressed by previous approaches (Schubert et al., 2023).
040
      In this work, we investigate the sources of the generalization error in world models, identifying two
041
      primary factors: 1) policy distribution shift (Janner et al., 2019), stemming from the divergence
042   between the test policy and data-collection policies, and 2) error compounding (Xu et al., 2020),
043   resulting from long-horizon autoregressive rollouts. The interplay of these two factors intensifies the
044   challenge of generalization in world models.
045
      To mitigate the generalization error caused by policy distribution shift, we introduce the policy
046
      conditioning, building upon the concept of policy-conditioned model learning (Chen et al., 2024a),
047
      aims to embed the policy information into the dynamics model learning, allowing the model to
048   adapt to different policies actively to mitigate the extrapolation error caused by distribution shift.
049   Furthermore, we propose a simple yet effective technique called retracing rollout, to reduce the
050   long-horizon compounding error during test time. This approach fixes the first frame of the moving
051   contexts to be the initial real observation and relabels the corresponding action by retracing the effects
052   of the original actions at the history timesteps. As a plug-and-play solution, retracing rollout can be
053   efficiently applied to end-effector pose control in various embodiment tasks without necessitating any
      changes to the training process.

      Incorporating these two techniques, we present Whale, a scalable embodied world model based on
055   the spatial-temporal transformer (Ma et al., 2024; Bruce et al., 2024), designed to enable faithful long-
056   horizon imagination for real-world visual control tasks. To substantiate the effectiveness of Whale,
057   we conduct extensive experiments on both simulated Meta-World (Yu et al., 2019) benchmark and a
058   physical robot platform, encompassing a variety of pixel-based manipulation tasks. Experimental
059   results on the simulated tasks show that Whale outperforms existing world model learning methods
060   in both video fidelity and value estimation accuracy. Moreover, we also validate the effectiveness
061   of policy-conditioning and retracing-rollout techniques in reducing the generalization error. As a
062   further step, we introduce Whale-X, a 414M parameter world model trained on 970k real-world
063
      demonstrations from Open X-Embodiment datasets (Collaboration et al., 2023). Whale-X serves as
      a foundational embodied world model for evaluating real-world behaviors. With fine-tuning on a
064
      few demonstrations in completely unseen environments and robots, Whale-X demonstrates strong
065
      OOD generalizability across visual, motion, and task perspectives. Furthermore, by scaling up the
066   pre-training dataset or model parameters, Whale-X shows impressive scalability during both the
067   pre-training and fine-tuning phases.

085
086   A typical formulation of sequential decision tasks is the Markov decision process (MDP) (Puterman,
087   1990) specified by the tuple M = (S, A, r, T ∗ , γ, H, ρ0 ), where S is the state space, A is the action
088   space, r(s, a) is the reward function, T ∗ (s′ |s, a) is the real transition probability, γ ∈ (0, 1] is the
089   discount factor, H is the decision horizon, and ρ0 (s) is the initial state distribution. In this work, we
090   simply consider the case where γ = 1 and H < ∞. In reinforcement learning (Sutton & Barto, 2018),
091   the objective is to learn a policy that maximizes the expected return in the MDP, which involves
092   estimating the value of different policies. Specifically, the value of policy π is defined as:

      2.2   W ORLD M ODELS FOR V ISUAL C ONTROL
109
110   Real-world control tasks often involve high-dimensional visual observations and a partially observable
111   nature. These visual control environments can be further described by a partially observable Markov
112   decision process (POMDP) (Åström, 1965) specified by tuple (S, O, ϕ, A, r, T ∗ , γ, H, ρ0 ), where
113   the agent receives visual observation ot = ϕ(st ) at each step, only containing incomplete information
114   of st , and executes an action based on history observations at ∼ π(·|o1:t ). The environment
115   then transitions into the next state st+1 according to T ∗ (·|st , at ) and provides the agent the next
116   observation ot+1 = ϕ(st+1 ) and a reward signal r(st , at ). The agent must predict future outcomes
117
      and make decisions based on historical observations due to incomplete information, making the
      learning of general environment models in visual domains a significant challenge.
118
119   World models (Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018) are proposed as a general framework of learning visual
120   dynamics. A vision module learns an abstract, compressed representation of high-dimensional
121   image observations zt = Eθ (ot ), a memory model tries to predict the future representations based
122   on the history Pθ (zt+1 |z1:t , a1:t ), compressing what happens over time, and a decoder recovers
123
      the observation and reward predictions from the predicted representation ôt+1 , r̂t+1 = Dθ (zt+1 ).
      The combination of vision and memory modules enables efficient autoregressive future predictions,
124
      allowing agents to plan or learn policies through model imaginations for visual control. Advanced
125
      approaches (Hafner et al., 2020; 2023; Babaeizadeh et al., 2021; Gupta et al., 2023; Wu et al.,
126   2024) largely retain this architecture, but replace the encoder, decoder, and latent dynamics with
127   different model architectures (e.g. transformer with video tokenizer and detokenizer). However, these
128   works have not emphasized the generalizability of world models, which is crucial for sequential
129   decision-making but has not been well addressed by previous approaches (Schubert et al., 2023).

189   3.1    OVERALL M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
190
191   In Figure 1, we illustrate the architecture of the Whale. Specifically, Whale comprises three main
192   components: policy embedding model, video tokenizer, and dynamics model. Inspired by previous
193   works (Bruce et al., 2024), these modules utilize a spatial-temporal transformer (ST-transformer)
194   architecture. Within this framework, each token is designed to attend only to other tokens in the
195   current frame and those at corresponding positions in prior frames. Additionally, Whale is capable of
196   generating all tokens for the next frame in parallel at one time. These designs significantly simplify
197   the computational demands from a quadratic to a linear dependency relative to sequence length,
198
      reducing both the memory usage and computational costs of the model training while increasing
      model inference speed.

242   World models typically consist of an observation encoder that encodes the raw observation into a
243   compact representation and a dynamics model that predicts future transitions within this representation

330   4.1    S IMULATION E VALUATION
331
332   Experiment Setups We conduct our simulated task experiments on the Meta-World (Yu et al.,
333   2019) benchmark, which offers a diverse set of vision-based manipulation tasks. In this experiment,
334   we construct a training dataset with 60k trajectories collected from 20 tasks. The model learning
335   algorithms are required to use all the data for training from scratch. During evaluation, given an
336   initial observation and a sequence of actions, the world model should reconstruct the corresponding
337   video trajectories. More detailed information about data collection can be found in Appendix D.1.
338   Baselines We compare Whale against several world model learning baselines, including
339   (1) FitVid (Babaeizadeh et al., 2021), a variational-based world model that can fit large diverse video
340   datasets. (2) MCVD (Voleti et al., 2022), a diffusion-based world model that can perform video
341   generation conditioning on different subsets of video frames and actions. (3) DreamerV3 (Hafner
342   et al., 2023), a recurrent world model that outperforms specialized methods across diverse control
343   tasks. (4) iVideoGPT (Wu et al., 2024), a scalable transformer-based world model that achieved
344
      state-of-the-art results in video generation and embodied control tasks. Complete descriptions and
      implementation details are provided in Appendix B.2.
345
346   Evaluation Metrics The evaluation scenarios are divided into two categories: seen policies and
347   unseen policies. Specifically, seen policies involve tasks and action sequences that both appear in the
348   training set, unseen policies refer to tasks from the training set with action sequences generated by
349   unseen policies. Moreover, we assess the performance of world models from two perspectives: 1)
350
      Video fidelity. Measures the quality of video trajectory generation, in terms of Fréchet Video Distance
      (FVD) (Unterthiner et al., 2018), Peak Signal-to-noise Ratio (PSNR) (Huynh-Thu & Ghanbari, 2008),
351
      Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity (LPIPS) (Zhang et al., 2018), and Structural Similarity
352
      Index Measure (SSIM) (Wang et al., 2004). 2) Value estimation accuracy. Verifies whether the model
353   can correctly estimate the value of a given action sequence, in terms of Value Gap.
354

      Pre-training. We present Whale-X, a 414M parameter world model pre-trained on 970K real-world
401
      robot demonstrations from Open X-Embodiment datasets. We use the entire dataset to pre-train
402   both the policy embedding model and the video tokenizer, selectively using a subset of the data
403   to pre-train the dynamics model. Whale-X serves as a foundational embodied world model for
404   evaluating real-world behaviors, capable of generating realistic and controllable video trajectories
405   that align with the given actions, as shown in Figure 3. Additional details on the pre-training process
406   and the generated results can be found in Appendix D.2 and Appendix F.3, respectively.
407
      Experiment Setups. To evaluate the out-of-distribution generalizablitiy of Whale-X in the physical
408   world, we conduct comprehensive real-world experiments on ARX5 robotic platform. The evaluation
409   tasks differ significantly from the pre-training data, in terms of the robotic platform, camera angles,
410   and background visual information, posing considerable challenges for world models.
411
      We carefully collect a limited dataset for fine-tuning, consisting of 60 trajectories for each of the
412
      four tasks: open bin, push plate, throw ball, and move bottle. Following this, we designed several
413
      challenging unseen tasks for testing, with a focus on evaluating the model from the perspectives of
414   visual generalization, motion generalization, and task generalization perspectives. Further details on
415   the data collection process can be found in Appendix E.
416
417
      Evaluation Metrics. Given an initial frame and a sequence of subsequent actions, world models
      should autoregressively generate future video trajectories. For a visual world model to be effective
418
      in decision-making, it needs to focus more on reasoning about the consequences of actions than on
419
      reconstructing irrelevant visual information like backgrounds. Thus we introduce the consistency
420   rate to assess whether the differences in reconstructed object positions, interactive object states,
421   and robot arm positions fall within an acceptable range compared to the ground truth. We use the
422   multimodal large model GPT-4o (Achiam et al., 2023) for this evaluation through multiple rounds
423   of Q&A. Details of the prompts and the evaluation process can be found in the Appendix H, with
424   results presented in Figure 4. In addition, we employ several video fidelity metrics, similar to those
425   in Section 4.1, to assess the quality of video generation by the world models.

482
483   In this section, we aim to investigate the scaling behavior of Whale-X. Specifically, We freeze the
484   video tokenizer and policy embedding model, adjusting only the model size and pre-training data
485   size of dynamics models, considering the impact of model size and data size for the pre-training and
      fine-tuning phases.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Pre-training Scaling Experiments. With a freezed video tokenizer and policy embedding model,
487   we train four dynamics models ranging from 39M to 456M parameters during the pre-training phase,
488   with results shown in the first two plots of Figure 5. These results demonstrate that Whale-X exhibits
489   strong scalability, as increasing either the pre-training data or the number of parameters reduces the
490   training loss. Notably, the training loss of Whale-X follows a log-linear relationship with FLOPs,
491   which can guide the design of larger models and appropriate data ratios for future experiments.
492
      Fine-tuning Scaling Experiments. Apart from the scalability in the pre-training stage, it is also
493   worth verifying whether a larger model can exhibit better performance during the fine-tuning phase.
494   To this end, we fine-tune a series of dynamics models and show the test mean-squared-error losses in
495   the leftmost plot in Figure 5. The results indicate that after fine-tuning, the larger model demonstrates
496   lower loss on test data, highlighting promising scalability of Whale-X for real-world tasks.

      5    R ELATED W ORKS
499
500
      Learning accurate dynamics models has been a long-standing challenge in sequential decision-making.
501
      Many works focused primarily on learning transition models in lower-dimensional proprioceptive
502   state spaces from the perspective of model architecture (Chua et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2021; Janner
503   et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2024b) or learning objective (Xu et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2023; Luo et al.,
504   2024a; Lin et al., 2024). The environment model learning provides benefits for downstream tasks,
505   especially model-based reinforcement learning (Janner et al., 2019; Yu et al., 2020; 2021; Sun et al.,
506   2023). Recent research interest has shifted towards learning environment models for high-dimensional
507   image-based tasks (Hafner et al., 2020; Babaeizadeh et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2024), commonly
508   referred to as world models (Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018).
509   Some recent model-based RL algorithms leverage latent imagination for more efficient and accurate
510   rollouts (Hafner et al., 2020; 2021; 2023; Hansen et al., 2022; Schrittwieser et al., 2020), but they
511   become more complex by tightly coupling model and policy learning. Advanced methods leverage
512   modern action-conditioned video prediction models (Oh et al., 2015; Kaiser et al., 2020) to model
513   the visual dynamics and pre-train from large-scale video experience data (Mendonca et al., 2023b;
514   Wu et al., 2023). Various models have been adopted in these methods, including RNNs (Villegas
515   et al., 2019; Hafner et al., 2020; Babaeizadeh et al., 2021), diffusion models (Voleti et al., 2022),
516
      and transformers (Gupta et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024). These interactive models generate videos
      under the control of the executed actions, with the goal of capturing real visual dynamics for
517
      various decision strategies. However, these works have not emphasized the generalizability of world
518
      models, which is crucial for sequential decision-making but has not been well addressed by previous
519   approaches (Schubert et al., 2023). In contrast, our work focuses on the world model generalizability
520   from a perspective of evaluation accuracy and utilizing model adaptation to policies and retracing
521   rollout to mitigate the generalization error in scalable world models.
522

      6    D ISCUSSIONS AND L IMITATIONS
525
      We introduce Whale, a scalable and generalizable embodied world model that incorporates the policy-
526
      conditioning mechanism and retracing-rollout technique to enhance out-of-distribution generalization,
527
      and pre-train a 414M-parameter Whale-X on large-scale real-world robot data to assist physical robot
528   manipulation. As a powerful world model with strong generalizability and promising scalability,
529   Whale enables high-fidelity imagination and accurate value estimation, even in novel scenarios,
530   thereby facilitating downstream control tasks.
531
      Limitations and future work. Although Whale-X marks significant progress, there remains
532
      substantial room for further improvement in future work. One limitation is the lack of diversity in
533
      real-world robotic data, typically collected by a narrow range of policies (e.g. near-optimal policies).
534   This poses significant challenges to the generalization of world models. Additionally, we found that
535   the quality of reward models with visual input plays a crucial role in accurate value estimation, which
536   remains an unsolved challenge for future research. Lastly, we have to mention that although Whale’s
537   generalization capability has significantly improved compared with previous methods, it remains
538   limited for zero-shot transfer in the face of the diversity and complexity of unseen real-world tasks.
539   Integrating existing prior knowledge into the data-driven world model learning process could enable
      broader generalization, presenting a valuable avenue for long-term research.

      A     A NALYSIS OF POLICY CONDITIONING
865
866
      In this section, we provide some theoretical explanations about why policy-conditioning mechanism
867   helps mitigate the generalization error caused by the policy divergence. The analysis is mainly
868   adapted from Chen et al. (2024a).
869
870
      First, we introduce an assumption on the smoothness of a well-trained dynamics model:
871
      Assumption A.1 For the learned dynamics model T , the point-wise total-variation model error
872
      DTV [T ∗ (·|τh ), T (·|τh )] is L-Lipschitz with respect to the trajectory inputs, i.e.,
873
874                   DTV [T ∗ (·|τh1 ), T (·|τh1 )] − DTV [T ∗ (·|τh2 ), T (·|τh2 )] ≤ L · D(τh1 , τh2 ),
875
876   where D(·, ·) is some kind of distance defined on the trajectory space.
877
878   Assumption A.1 measures the local extrapolation ability of a world model. Based on this assumption,
879
      the value gaps of common dynamics model T without a policy-conditioning mechanism can be
      controlled:
880
881
      Proposition A.2 Under Assumption A.1, for any policy π, the value gap of common dynamics model
882   T without policy conditioning has an upper bound:
883                                            p
884                   VTπ − VTπ∗ ≤ 2Rmax H 2      2 lKL (T ; Π) + L · W1 (dπ , dΠ )   ,
885
                                               |      {z      }    |      {z     }
                                                          Train Error       Policy Divergence Error
886
887   where W1 (dπ , dΠ ) is the Wasserstein-1 distance between the π-induced trajectory distribution dπ (τ )
888   and the behavior trajectory distribution dΠ (τ ) = Eµ∼Π [dµ (τ )].
889
890   Proposition A.2 shows that the generalization of common dynamics model T solely relies on its point-
891
      level smoothness over the trajectory inputs, resulting in an inevitable extrapolation error of the policy
      distribution. In contrast, a policy-conditioned dynamics model T (·), which yields adapted dynamics
892
      model T (π) for some policy π, takes a further step to reduce the policy distribution extrapolation
893
      error:
894
895   Proposition A.3 Under Assumption A.1, for any policy π, the value gap of policy-conditioned
896   dynamics model T (·) has an upper bound:
897                                        p
898             VTπ(π) − VTπ∗ ≤ 2Rmax H 2      2 lKL (T ; Π) + L · W1 (dπ , dΠ ) − C(π, Π) ,
                                            |      {z      } |               {z          }
899                                                    Train Error       Reduced Policy Divergence Error
900
901   where the adaptation gain C(π, Π) := Eµ∼Π Eτ ∼dπ DTV [T ∗ , T (µ)](τ ) − Eτ ∼dπ DTV [T ∗ , T (π)](τ )
902
      summarizes the policy adaptation effect.
903
      Proposition A.3 explains the benefit brought by policy-conditioning: a positive adaptation gain
904   C(π, Π), which quantifies the advantage of the policy adaptation effect. The key insight is that when
905   testing on an unseen policy π within some effective region, the model T (π), customized for π, should
906   exhibit a smaller model error under the target trajectory distribution dπ compared to models T (µ)
907   trained on behavior policies µ ∈ Π, which mitigates the generalization error caused by the policy
908   extrapolation. Although it is challenging to rigorously analyze the adaptation gain C(π, Π) due to the
909   complexity of neural networks and the optimization process, qualitative discussions and empirical
910   evidence, as shown in Chen et al. (2024a), justify the underlying rationale.
