011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Liberal political philosophy advocates for the policy of equal treatment as blindness,
014            which seeks to achieve fairness by treating individuals without considering their pro-
015            tected characteristics directly. However, this policy has faced longstanding criticism
016
               for perpetuating existing inequalities. In machine learning, this policy can be trans-
               lated into the concept of fairness as unawareness, and be measured using disparate
017
               treatment metrics such as Demographic Parity (a.k.a. Statistical Parity). Our analy-
018
               sis reveals that Demographic Parity does not faithfully measure whether individuals
019            are being treated independently of the protected attribute by the model. We intro-
020            duce the Explanation Disparity metric to measure fairness under equal treatment as
021            blindness policy. Our metric evaluates the fairness of predictive models by analyz-
022            ing the extent to which the protected attribute can be inferred from the distribution of
023            explanation values, specifically using Shapley values. The proposed metric tests for
024            statistical independence of the explanation distributions over populations with dif-
025            ferent protected characteristics. We show the theoretical properties of "Explanation
026            Disparity" and devise an equal treatment inspector based on the AUC of a Classifier
027
               Two-Sample Test. We experiment with synthetic and natural data to demonstrate
               and compare the notion with related ones. We release explanationspace, an
028
               open-source Python package with methods and tutorials.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Liberal-oriented politics, put forward by scholars such as Friedman et al. (1990) and Nozick (1974),
034
      often advocate for a policy of equal treatment as blindness, where fairness is aimed to be achieved by
035   making decisions without directly considering protected attributes. This principle can be translated
036   to machine learning as fairness by unawareness, where protected attributes are excluded from the
037   training data (Simons et al., 2021; Cornacchia et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2019a; Fabris et al., 2023).
038   However, this policy of ignoring the protected attribute has been historically widely criticized, by
039   gender and ethnic studies (MacKinnon, 1989; Crenshaw, 1997; Minow, 1990), for example:
040
041             A notion of equality that demands disregarding a “difference” calls for assimilation
042            to an unstated norm. To strip away difference, then, is often to remove or ignore a
043            feature distinguishing an individual from a presumed norm [. . . ] but leaving that
044
               norm in place as the measure for equal treatment.
               Martha Minow, 1990. Making all the difference. 59-60
045
046
      These critiques suggest that simply ignoring the protected attribute does not achieve equality; instead,
047
      it risks perpetuating the very inequalities it seeks to eliminate. By analyzing the college admission use
048   case in Appendix A, we collect a set of requirements towards measuring the criticism of this policy
049   and argue that existing fairness metrics do not manage to account for inequalities in this policy. For
050   example, individual fairness (Dwork et al., 2012) cannot identify group discrimination. The measure
051   of equal opportunity (Hardt et al., 2016) focuses on discrepancies in error rates rather than differences
052   in treatment and necessitates labelled data. Similarly, causal fairness (Plecko and Bareinboim,
053   2024) demands background knowledge. Unfortunately, both labelled data and sufficient background
      knowledge are often unavailable in many practical scenarios. Demographic Parity (DP), also called


                                                          1
054
      Statistical Parity, which compares the distributions of predicted outcomes of a model f for different
055   social groups has been proposed as a potential metric to measure this effect by researchers(Simons
056   et al., 2021; Heidari et al., 2019; Wachter et al., 2020). However, we show that Demographic Parity
057   may indicate fairness, even when groups are being treated differently by the model.
058
      We propose the fairness metric of Explanation Disparity for supervised machine learning models,
059
      to measure the longstanding criticism of the policy of equal treatment as blindness. The notion of
060
      Explanation Disparity considers the contribution of non-protected features to the machine learning
061   model output as explained by Shapley values (Lundberg and Lee, 2017). If two social groups are
062   treated the same, the distributions of feature contributions, which we call explanation distributions,
063   will not be distinguishable. Thus, Explanation Disparity tests the independence of Shapley values
064   with the protected feature, via a classifier two-sample test that infers the protected attribute. We
065   introduce a decision tool, the “Equal Treatment Inspector”, that implements this idea. When detecting
066   unequal treatment, it explains the features involved in such inequality, supporting understanding the
067   roots of unequal treatment in the machine learning model.
068   In summary, our contributions are:
069
070         1. The definition of Explanation Disparity metric, that measures the independence of the
071            protected attribute based on explanation distributions.
072         2. The definition of an “Equal Treatment Inspector” workflow, based on a classifier two-sample
073            test, for recognizing and explaining un-equal treatment.
074         3. The study of the formal relationships between Demographic Parity and Explanation
075            Disparity.
076         4. Extensive experiments, both on synthetic and natural datasets, to demonstrate our method
077            and compare it with related work.
078         5. An open-source Python package explanationspace implementing the “Equal
079            Treatment Inspector”, which is scikit-learn compatible, and includes documentation
080            and tutorials.

104   2.1   BASIC N OTATION AND D EFINITIONS OF FAIRNESS
105
106   A supervised learning model is a function fθ : X → Y induced from a set of observations, called the
107   training set, where X = {X1 , . . . , Xp } are p predictive features, Y is the target feature, and θ are
      the models’ parameters. The domain of the target feature is dom(Y ) = {0, 1} (binary classification)


                                                         2
108
      or dom(Y ) = R (regression). For binary classification, we assume a probabilistic classifier, and we
109   denote by fθ (x) the estimate of the probability P (Y = 1|X = x) over the (unknown) distribution
110   of X × Y . For regression, fθ (x) estimates E[Y |X = x]. A dataset of observations from the
111   distribution of X × Y is denoted by D = {(x1 , y1 ), . . . , (xn , yn )} ∼ X × Y . We call the projection
112   of D on X, written DX = {x1 , . . . , xn } ∼ X, the empirical input distribution. The dataset
113   fθ (DX ) = {fθ (x) | x ∈ DX } is called the empirical prediction distribution.
114
      We assume a feature Z representing social groups, called the protected feature, and assume it to
115   be binary valued in the theoretical analysis – thus modeling membership to a protected social group.
116   Z can either be included in the predictive features X or not. If not, we assume that it is still available
117   for a test dataset.
118
      We write A ⊥ B to denote statistical independence between the two sets of random variables A
119
      and B, or equivalently, between two multivariate probability distributions. We define two common
120
      fairness notions and corresponding fairness metrics that quantify a model’s degree of discrimination
121   or unfairness (Mehrabi et al., 2022).
122
123
      Definition 2.1. (Demographic Parity (DP)). A model fθ achieves Demographic Parity if fθ (X) ⊥ Z.
124
      Thus, Demographic Parity holds if ∀z. P (fθ (X)|Z = z) = P (fθ (X)). For binary Z’s, we can derive
125   an unfairness metric as d(P (fθ (X)|Z = 1), P (fθ (X)), where d(·) is a distance between probability
126   distributions.
127
      Definition 2.2. (Equal Opportunity (EO)) A model fθ achieves equal opportunity if
128
      ∀z. P (fθ (X)|Y = 1, Z = z) = P (fθ (X) = 1|Y = 1).
129
130   Unfairness can be measured for binary Z’s as d(P (fθ (X)|Y = 1, Z = 1), P (fθ (X) = 1|Y = 1)).

      2.2   P HILOSOPHICAL F OUNDATIONS AND C OMPUTABLE FAIRNESS M ETRICS
133
134   Political and moral philosophers from the egalitarian school of thought often consider equal oppor-
135   tunity to be the key promoter of fairness and social justice, providing qualified individuals with equal
136   chances to succeed regardless of their background or circumstances Rawls (1958; 1991); Dworkin
137   (1981a;b); Arneson (1989); Cohen (1989). In fair-ML, Hardt et al. (2016) proposed translating equal
138   opportunity into the inter-group difference of the true positive rates. Heidari et al. (2019) provided a
139   moral framework to ground such a metric of Equal Opportunity. Gabriel (2022) explored the rela-
140   tionship between AI and principles of Rawlsian distributive justice. Kuppler et al. (2021); Baumann
141
      et al. (2022) discuss the mismatches of egalitarian distributive justice with fairness metrics. From a
      machine learning perspective, the technical drawback is that metrics for equal opportunity require
142
      label annotations for true positive outcomes, which are not always available after the deployment
143
      of a model. Acquiring labelled data post-deployment poses a considerable challenge and is often
144   impractical, or even impossible, exhibiting well-known biases, such as confounding, selection, and
145   missingness (Feng et al., 2023; Ruggieri et al., 2023).
146
147
      The liberalism school of thought argues that individuals should be treated equally independently of
      outcomes (Friedman et al., 1990; Nozick, 1974). Equal treatment has also been defined as “equal
148
      treatment-as-blindness" or neutrality Sunstein (1992); Miller and Howell (1959). A policy that can be
149
      translated as “fairness through unawareness” (Grgic-Hlaca et al., 2018; Cornacchia et al., 2023; Chen
150   et al., 2019a; Fabris et al., 2023), which considers an algorithm fair if the protected attribute(s) Z is
151   not explicitly used in decision-making. Any mapping fθ : X → Y that excludes Z from X satisfies
152   this policy. The criticism relies that even without learning from or using the protected feature, a
153   model can discriminate against the protected groups through correlated features in X as a proxy of
154   the protected one (Pedreschi et al., 2008).
155
      From a technical perspective, a potential metric to measure the impact of equal treatment as blindness
156   policy can be Demographic Parity or Statistical Parity (used synonymously) (Dwork et al., 2012). As
157   we will analyze in Section 4.1, Demographic Parity implies that demographic groups experience the
158   same distribution of model outcomes. However, we might still observe Demographic Parity while
159   individuals from different groups are treated differently. One instance is when the contribution of
160   some features that correlates with the protected attributes cancel out (Ruggieri et al., 2023). In a
161   hypothetical hiring scenario, this might represent a situation in which men are hired for a reason (e.g.,
      they have university degrees), while women are hired for a different reason (e.g. they have industrial


                                                          3
162
      working experience). In this situation, Demographic Parity might still be satisfied, but individuals of
163   different groups are treated differently, thus not accurately measuring the criticism of equal treatment
164   as blindenss political philosophy policy. Our metric of Explanation Disparity remedies this drawback.
165
166
      Demographic Parity implies that two demographic groups experience the same distribution of model
      outcomes, even if the first has much better prospects for achieving the predicted outcome. Thus, a
167
      model f that achieves Demographic Parity may have to prefer individuals from one group over those
168
      from another, violating the requirement for equal treatment of all individuals. In Appendix A.3, we
169   illustrate the use-case of college admissions, where equal treatment is desirable, but neither equal
170   opportunity nor equal outcomes achieve it.

      2.3   R ELATED W ORK
173
174   We briefly review related works below. See Appendix C for an in-depth comparison.
175
176   Related Fairness Metrics In recent years, fairness metrics have proliferated. Yet, a significant
177   gap exists in their alignment with distributive justice principles and political philosophy (Baumann
178   et al., 2022; Hertweck et al., 2024; Fazelpour and Lipton, 2020; Fazelpour et al., 2022). To bridge
179   this gap in the criticism of liberal-oriented politics, our focus is on establishing a set of criteria that
180   align with these principles. (R1) Group Discrimination, decisions based on protected characteristics
181
      that individuals did not choose at birth. (R2) Unlabelled Data metrics should prioritize assessing
      disparities in treatment rather than fixating on error disparities. (R3) No Background Knowledge not
182
      necessitating understanding of causal or structural aspects. (R4) Mitigating Proxy Discrimination
183
      detecting that certain features may indirectly contribute to biased decisions. (R5) Explanation
184   Capability offer both theoretical underpinnings and empirical validation of the sources driving
185   discrimination. We add a base requirement, (R0) Predictive Performance, which requires the model
186   fθ to have predictive performance, as a random classifier achieves several fairness metrics. In
187   Table 1 and Appendix A.3, we present a comparison of various fairness metrics against these
188   criteria. Furthermore, in the detailed use case presented in Appendix A, we extract and emphasize
189   philosophical requirements to underscore the alignment with liberal political philosophy.
190
191   Testing for Demographic Parity (DP) Most related works measure DP using statistics such as
192   Mann–Whitney, Kolmogorov-Smirnov or Wasserstein distance, and related statistical tests (Raji et al.,
193   2020; Kearns et al., 2018; Cho et al., 2020). Other research lines have aimed at measuring DP when
194
      the protected attribute is a continuous variable (Jiang et al., 2022). We also measure and test for DP
      through the AUC of a classifier two-sample test. Detailed comparisons are reported in Appendix G.5.
195
196
197
      Classifier Two-Sample Test (C2ST) We formalize a classifier two-sample test (C2ST) based on the
      AUC to measure the independence of sets of random variables. Lopez-Paz and Oquab (2017) explored
198
      C2ST using accuracy metrics. Chakravarti et al. (2023) used AUC for C2ST without providing a
199
      formal proof of correctness. Moreover, we use a Brunner-Munzel test statistics, which exhibits a
200   better power than the previous two approaches – more in Appendix G.1.
201
202   Explainability for Fair Supervised Learning Lundberg (2020), and the related work by Chang
203   et al. (2023), apply Shapley values to DP. Their approach can be rephrased as decomposing fθ (X) ⊥
204   Z by examining S(fθ , X)i ⊥ Z individually for every Shapley value component S(fθ , X)i , with
205   i ∈ [1, p]. First, they intend to tackle DP, not the Explanation Disparity notion we will introduce.
206   Moreover, even when considering DP, their approach suffers from statistical pitfalls as we show in
207   Appendix A.3.2. Other recent lines of work assume knowledge about causal relationships between
208   random variables, such as Grabowicz et al. (2022). Our work does not rely on knowledge of causal
209   graphs but exploits the Shapley values’ theoretical properties to obtain fairness model auditing
210
      guarantees.

      To establish a criterion for equal treatment, we rely on the notion of explanation distributions.


                                                          4
216
      Definition 3.1. (Explanation Distribution) An explanation function S : F × X → Rp maps a model
217   fθ ∈ F and an input instance x ∈ X into a vector of reals S(fθ , x) ∈ Rp . An (empirical) explanation
218   distribution is then defined on an input distribution DX as S(fθ , DX ) = {S(fθ , x) | x ∈ DX } ⊆ Rp .
219
220   We use Shapley values as an explanation function (cf. Appendix B). In Appendix H, we discuss the
221   usage of LIME. Next, we introduce our new fairness criterion, Explanation Disparity, which asks for
222   the independence of a model’s explanations from the protected feature.
223   Definition 3.2. (Explanation Disparity (ED)) A model fθ achieves ED if S(fθ , X) ⊥ Z.
224
      Such a definition aims to characterize the philosophical notion of equal treatment by encoding the
225
      “treatment” performed by the model through the attribution of the importance of its input features. As
226
      we will see later in Section 4, Explanation Disparity is a stronger metric than Demographic Parity
227   since it not only requires that the distributions of the predictions are similar but that the processes of
228   how predictions are made (i.e., the explanations) are also similar.

315   4.2.1    S TATISTICAL I NDEPENDENCE T EST VIA C LASSIFIER AUC T EST
316
      In this subsection, we formalize a statistical test of independence based on the AUC of a binary
317   classifier. The test of W ⊥ Z is stated in general form for multivariate random variables W and a
318   binary random variable Z with dom(Z) = {0, 1}. In the next subsection, we will instantiate it to the
319   case W = S(fθ , X).
320
      Let D = {(wi , zi )}ni=1 be a dataset of realizations of the random sample (W × Z)n ∼ F n where F
321   is unknown. The independence W ⊥ Z can be tested via a two-sample test. In fact, we have W ⊥ Z
322   iff P (W|Z) = P (W) iff P (W|Z = 1) = P (W|Z = 0). We test whether the positive and negative
323   instances in D are drawn from the same distribution by a classifier two-sample test, which does not
      rely on permutation of data nor it assumes equal proportion of positive and negatives as in (Lopez-Paz


                                                          6
324
      and Oquab, 2017, Sections 2 and 3). We rely on a probabilistic classifier f : W → [0, 1], for which
325   f (w) estimates P (Z = 1|W = w), and on its AUC:

328                          E(W,Z),(W′ ,Z ′ )∼F [I((Z − Z ′ ) · (f (W) − f (W′ )) > 0)]

355
      We instantiate the previous AUC-based method for testing independence to the case of testing for
      Explanation Disparity via the Equal Treatment Inspector.
356
357   Theorem 4.3. Let gψ : S(fθ , X) → [0, 1] be an “Equal Treatment Inspector” for the model fθ ,
358   and α a significance level. We can test the null hypothesis H0 : S(fθ , X) ⊥ Z at 100 · (1 − α)%
359
      confidence level using a test statistics of AU C(gψ ) = 1/2.
360
361   Proof. Under H0 , by Lemma 2 with W = S(fθ , X) and f = gψ , we have AU C(gψ ) = 1/2.
362
363   Results of the test can report the p-value of the adopted test for AU C(gψ ) = 1/2, or the confidence
364   interval for AU C(gψ ), as returned by the Brunner–Munzel test or by the methods (DeLong et al.,
365
      1988; Cortes and Mohri, 2004; Gonçalves et al., 2014). AU C(gψ ), or its confidence intervals, can be
      used to quantify the Explanation Disparity unfairness.

      4.2.3   E XPLANATION C APABILITIES OF E QUAL T REATMENT I NSPECTOR
368
369   The following example showcases one of our main contributions: detecting the sources of Explanation
370   Disparity through interpretable by-design inspectors. Here, we assume that the model is linear. In the
371   Appendix G.4, we will experiment with non-linear models.
372   Example 4.3. Let X be three independent features such that E[X1 ] = E[X2 ] = E[X3 ] = 0, and
373   X1 , X2 ⊥ Z, and X3 ̸⊥ Z. Given a random sample of i.i.d. observations from X × Y , a linear
374   model fβ (x) = β0 + β1 · x1 + β2 · x2 + β3 · x3 can be built by OLS (Ordinary Least Square)
375   estimation, possibly with β1 , β2 , β3 ̸= 0. It turns out (see Lemma B.1) that S(fβ , x)i = βi · xi .
376   Consider now a linear ET Inspector gψ (s) = ψ0 + ψ1 · x1 + ψ2 · s2 + ψ3 · s3 , which can be written
377   in terms of the x’s as: gψ (x) = ψ0 + ψ1 · β1 · x1 + ψ2 · β2 · x2 + ψ3 · β3 · x3 . By OLS estimation
      properties, we have ψ1 ≈ cov (β1 · X1 , Z)/var (β1 · X1 ) = cov (X1 , Z)/(β1 · var (X1 )) = 0 and


                                                          7
378
      analogously ψ2 ≈ 0. Finally, ψ3 ≈ cov (X3 , Z)/(β3 · var (X3 )) ̸= 0. In summary, the coefficients
379   of gψ provide information about which feature contributes (and how much it contributes) to the
380   dependence between the explanation S(fβ , X) and the protected feature Z. Notice that fβ (X) ̸⊥ Z
381   as well, but we cannot explain which features contribute to such a dependence by looking at fβ (X),
382   since βi ≈ cov (Xi , Y )/var (Xi ) can be non-zero also for i = 1, 2.

479   6            C ONCLUSIONS
480
481   The policy of equal treatment as blindness, often referred to in machine learning as fairness as
482   unawareness, has long been critiqued by social scientists. In this work, we argue that existing fairness
483   metrics fail to adequately address these criticisms. To address this gap, we introduce a novel metric
484   that aims to more effectively capture these concerns. While related work applied Demographic Parity,
485   we have provided theoretical and experimental evidence that this fairness metric does not adequately
      measure fairness. Our notion of Explanation Disparity is more fine-grained, accounting for the usage


                                                                                                                9
486
      of attributes by the model via explanation distributions. Consequently, Explanation Disparity implies
487   Demographic Parity, but the converse is not necessarily true, which we confirmed theoretically and
488   experimentally.
489
490
      This paper seeks to improve the understanding of how theoretical concepts of fairness from
      liberalism-oriented political philosophy align with technical measurements. Rather than merely
491
      comparing one social group to another based on disparities within decision distributions, our
492
      metric of Explanation Disparity considers differences through the explanation distribution of all
493   non-protected attributes, which often act as proxies for protected characteristics. Implications warrant
494   further techno-philosophical discussions.
495
496
      Limitations: Political philosophical notions and policies are more complex than we can account for in
      this paper. Our research has focused on tabular data using Shapley values, which allow for theoretical
497
      guarantees but may differ from their computational approximations. It is possible that alternative
498
      AI explanation techniques, such as feature attribution methods, logical reasoning, argumentation, or
499   counterfactual explanations, could be useful and offer their unique advantages to definitions of equal
500   treatment. It is important to note that employing fair-ML techniques does not necessarily ensure
501   fairness in socio-technical systems based on AI, as stated in Kulynych et al. (2020). See impact
502   statement in Appendix I for further information and implications.

892            I MPLICATIONS IN P RACTICE
893
894   In this section, we present the philosophical intuitions behind different notions of fairness illustrated
895   with an applied case study.1 Next, we collect the technical requirements that fairness metrics must
896   meet to align with equal treatment as blindness criticism and to be viable for the practical applicability
897   of fairness metrics in real-world scenarios where data constraints and accountabilty on the results are
898   often important. Finally, we conduct a broader survey of fairness metrics and relate them to these
899   requirements, examining how each metric addresses the alignment and practical challenges.
900
901   A.1       A N OVERVIEW OF N OTIONS OF FAIRNESS IN D ISTRIBUTIVE J USTICE
902
903   To illustrate the difference between equal opportunity, equal outcomes, and equal treatment, we
904   consider the hypothetical use case of the admission process of a university.
905   In June 2023, the U.S. Supreme Court struck down race-conscious admission programs at some
906   universities (Killenbeck and Killenbeck, 2022)2 , ruling these as discriminatory against certain racial
907   groups. This decision marked a significant shift from previous policies that used race as one of several
908   factors in a holistic admissions process to promote a diverse student body3 . While the ruling does
909   not completely forbid universities from considering applicants’ racial features, it emphasizes a more
910   colorblind approach to admissions. This move towards equal treatment focuses less on achieving
911   equal outcomes (diverse representation) and more on not considering race as an admissions factor.
912
      Now we discuss these notions within the context of fair machine learning. We now compare to the
      different notions from distributive justice commonly discussed in the literature.
913
914      1
           We do not claim to cover comprehensively different philosophical approaches to such a case study. While
915   a comprehensive philosophical treatise may require a whole book, we only want to illustrate some issues, on
916   which we are basing our arguments.

      Equal Outcomes. This notion requires that the distribution of acceptance rates is similar, independently
919   of the student (cf. Definition 2.1). This could mean setting targets or quotas to ensure representation
920   from various groups, regardless of their individual academic credentials. For instance, if 20% of
921   applicants are from a certain ethnicity, the university might aim to have 20% of their admits from
922   that ethnicity as well. While this promotes a diverse student body, it can overlook individual merit
923   and potentially lead to tension between equity and academic standards. Particularly given the recent
924   ruling of the US Supreme Court, considering ethnicity to match the proportion of applicants from
925   that ethnicity would likely be considered illegal4 . It’s also important to note that outcomes can have
926   similar rates due to random chance.
927   Equal Opportunity. In this approach, the university would ensure that students from different
928   backgrounds have an equal chance of being admitted if they have high potential or qualifications. For
929   instance, students from under-resourced high schools are given the same opportunity as those from
930   well-funded schools if they demonstrate the same exceptional talent or potential. The university may
931   implement this strategy by adjusting admission criteria based on educational opportunities tied to
932   race. Policies following this notions can lead to challenges like overcompensating for disadvantages
933
      or unintentionally lowering standards for certain groups.
934   Equal Treatment. In this scenario, the university ensures that every application is evaluated based on
935   uniform criteria such as academic achievement, extracurricular involvement, and personal essays,
936   without bias towards the applicant’s background. This approach means that factors like a student’s
937   socioeconomic status, high school’s reputation, geographic location, or other attributes not chosen at
938   birth (protected attributes) would not directly influence the admission procedure.
939   In the university admission case, each approach to fairness in admissions — equal outcome, equal
940   opportunity, and equal treatment — has its merits and issues. Equal outcomes, strive for a representa-
941   tive student body, assuming that all groups have inherently the same academic capabilities. Equal
942   opportunity aims to level the playing field based on the potential of the candidates. Equal treatment
943   focuses on ignoring protected attributes aiming to achieve a uniform and unbiased evaluation process.
944   We leave the normative discussion of which fairness paradigm should be pursued by policy to the
945
      discourse in the social sciences and the broad public.

      If we aim to measure the impact of equal treatment as blindness policy in the scenario involving
949   college admissions, liberal-oriented political scientists argue that the university should ensure that

952   means that a student’s protected attributes, such as ethnicity, socioeconomic status, or geographic
953   location, should not influence their likelihood of admission. From an application standpoint, this
954   approach also necessitates practical measures to ensure that the systems can be effectively monitored
955   and maintained once deployed.

963           the consequences of the decisions (Driver, 2022). This criterion evaluates discriminatory

1040   Fairness Through Unawareness. This bias mitigation method was initially proposed by Grgic-
1041   Hlaca et al. (2018) and is often used as a baseline.
1042
       Definition A.1. (Fairness Through Unawareness). An algorithm is fair if no protected attributes Z is
1043   explicitly used as an input of the algorithm.
1044
1045   Any mapping f : X → Y for which Z ̸∈ X satisfies such a definition. It has a clear shortcoming as
1046   features in X can contain discriminatory information, known as proxy features for discrimination.
1047   Further, the research requirement of detecting (R4) Proxy discrimination is not met. One of the
1048   main contributions requirements for the notion of equal treatment and technical contributions of
1049   Explanation Disparitiy is that it captures statistical relations of all the features with the protected
1050
       attribute. In our approach, the model does not consider the protected attribute to be present in the
       covariates X, therefore implying fairness through unawareness. Furthermore, in Section 4.1 of the
1051
       main body, we discuss the limitations of analyzing the Shapley values of the protected attribute.

1053
       Demographic Parity. To define a quality metric, one must collect domain requirements and
1054   formalise the metric such that its mathematical properties match the requirements. The domain
1055   requirements in this paper are given by philosophical foundations (cf. Appendix A). From the
1056   mathematical perspective, we have shown that Explanation Disparity is a more aligned notion to
1057   equal treatment than Demographic Parity to the liberal notion of equal treatment.
1058
       A refined metric introduced by legal scholars aiming to align with the European Court of Justice “gold
1059
       standard” (Wachter et al., 2021) is Conditional Demographic Parity, which extends Demographic
1060   Parity by fixing one or more attributes.
1061
       Definition A.2. (Conditional Demographic Parity (CDP)). A model fθ achieves conditional demo-
1062
       graphic parity if P (fθ (X)|Xi = τ, Z = z) = P (fθ (X)|Xi = τ ), where τ is any fixed value.
1063
1064   CDP differs from DP only in the sense that one or more additional covariate conditions are added.
1065   CDP, therefore, is not able to detect proxy discrimination (R4) nor capable to account for the sources
1066   behind discrimination (R5). Our notion for Explanation Disparity aims to align further with liberal-
1067   oriented equal treatment, and this is only achieved if the contributions of all features to the prediction
1068   are equal for each of the protected subgroups.

1070   Individual Fairness. We say that a model f achieves individual fairness if similar individuals
1071   receive similar predictions:
1072                                    ∀x, x′ d(f (x), f (x′ )) ≤ L · d(x, x′ )
1073
1074   Where L > 0 is a constant and the two d(·, ·)’s are distance functiosn between models’ outputs and

1076   From a philosophical perspective, individual fairness is closest to the liberal point of view. However,
1077   it fails the requirements of liberalist arguments. Liberalism argues for meritocracy, i.e., disparate
1078   treatment may be considered fair if it is based on varying efforts or preferences of individuals but
1079   unfair if it is based on protected characteristics that individuals did not choose. E.g., it’s fair to hire
       someone because of better grades, but it is unfair if these grades depend on ethnicity. The definition

1080
       of individual fairness does not consider protected characteristics or proxies thereof failing research
1081   requirement (R1) of group discrimination.. While individual fairness may be easily combined with
1082   blindness or adjusted definitions of similarities (Fleisher, 2021; Yurochkin et al., 2020), the treatment
1083   of proxies to protected characteristics is hard to avoid, rendering the alignment of an AI model with
1084   distributive justice values difficult. Therefore, individual fairness is not a popular metric — unlike
1085   group fairness metrics (Fleisher, 2021).

1087   Counterfactual Fairness. Counterfactual fairness, as defined by Kusner et al. (2017) captures the
1088   intuition that a decision is fair towards an individual if it is the same in (i) the actual case and (ii) in a

1091
1092            ∀x ∈ X ∀z, z ′ ∈ Z P (fθ (xZ=z )|X = x, Z = z) = P (fθ (xZ=z′ )|X = x, Z = z)

       where xZ=z and xZ=z′ are the instances x in the (counterfactual) worlds where Z = z and Z = z ′

       respectively.

1099   equivalent to demographic parity, therefore failing the same requirements (R4), proxy discrimination
1100   and (R5) explanation capabilities. Moreover, this fairness definition fails on our requirement (R3), of
1101   not needing background knowledge – since counterfactuals can only be computed for a given causal
1102   graph.

1195   C.1   E XPLAINABILITY AND FAIR S UPERVISED L EARNING
1196
1197   The intersection of fairness and explainable AI has been an active research topic in recent years.
1198   The work most close to our approach is Lundberg (2020) where Shapley values are aimed at testing
1199   for demographic parity (we provide a formal comparison in section A.3.2). Similarly, Chang et al.
1200   (2023) proposes a method that uses the expected feature importance and computes subgroups which
1201
       maximize feature importance disparity. Their work differs with respect to Lundberg (2020) and our
       work, in that they look for finding those groups while our work assumes the groups as given.
1202
1203   Stevens et al. (2020) present an approach based on adapting the Shapley value function to explain
1204   model unfairness. They also introduce a new meta-algorithm that considers the problem of learning
1205   an additive perturbation to an existing model in order to impose fairness. In our work, we use the
1206   theoretical Shapley properties to provide fairness auditing guarantees. Our Equal Treatment Inspector
1207
       is not perturbation-based but uses Shapley values to project the model to the explanation distribution,
       and then measures disparities. It also allows us to pinpoint the specific features driving such a
1208
       violation.
1209
1210   Grabowicz et al. (2022) present a post-processing method based on Shapley values aiming to detect
1211   and nullify the influence of a protected attribute on the output of the system. For this, they assume
1212   there are direct causal links from the data to the protected attribute and that there are no measured
1213
       confounders. Our work does not use causal graphs but exploits the theoretical properties of the
       Shapley values to obtain fairness model auditing guarantees.
1214
1215   Begley et al. (2020) propose a meta-algorithm for applying fairness interventions to already-trained
1216   models, wherein one trains a perturbation to the original model, rather than a new model entirely
1217   by adapting the Shapley value function w.r.t. demographic parity. In our work, we do not adopt the
1218   Shapley value function, but train a meta-algorithm in the testing phase, there is no need for labelled
1219
       data, nor is our method based on perturbations.
1220   Other lines of work have studied the relationship between explanation quality and population sub-
1221   groups. For example,Dai et al. (2022), explores group-based disparities in explanation quality,
1222   focusing on metrics such as fidelity, stability, consistency, and sparsity. Aïvodji et al. (2019), in-
1223   troduces the concept of “fairwashing”, where rule-based explanation methods can be manipulated
1224   to justify unfair model decisions, making them appear fair. Slack et al. (2020) demonstrate how
1225
       machine learning algorithms can be deliberately designed so that explanation methods like LIME and
       SHAP obscure the model’s explicitly unfair behavior, making it harder to detect biases. While these
1226
       works focus on individual or local explanations, our research examines distributions of explanations,
1227
       where individual differences might be masked by overall metric aggregation. For example, Figure 10
1228   shows that SHAP and LIME yield similar results despite their distinct explanation mechanisms, and
1229   Appendix E analyzes various Shapley value approximations, which still produce comparable results.
1230   Investigating errors in calculating explanation distributions remains an avenue for future research.
1231
       Few works have researched fairness using other explainability techniques such as counterfactual
1232
       explanations (Kusner et al., 2017; Manerba and Guidotti, 2021; Mutlu et al., 2022). We do not focus
1233
       on counterfactual explanations but on feature attribution methods that allow us to measure unequal
1234   feature contribution to the prediction. Further work can be envisioned by applying explainable AI
1235   techniques to the Equal Treatment Inspector, or by constructing the explanation distribution out of
1236   other techniques.

1240   The use of classifiers as a statistical tests of independence W ⊥ Z for a binary Z’s has been
1241   previously explored in the literature (Lopez-Paz and Oquab, 2017). The approach relies on testing
       accuracy of a classifier trained to distinguish Z = 1 (positives) from Z = 0 (negatives) given


                                                         23
1242
       W = w. In the null hypothesis, i.e., the distributions of positives and negatives are the same, no
1243   classifier is better than a random answer with accuracy 1/2. This assumes equal proportion of positive
1244   and negative instances. Our approach builds on this idea, but it considers testing the AUC instead
1245   of the accuracy. Thus, we remove the assumption of equal proportions5 . We also show in Section
1246   G.1 that using AUC may achieve a better power than using accuracy. Chakravarti et al. (2023) have
1247   also used C2ST together with the AUC, with their implementation using bootstrap and asymptotic
1248   methods. Their work does not provide a formal proof as we do. Also, we use the Brunner–Munzel
1249   test, which has a better power; see Section G.1 for an experimental comparison.
1250   Liu et al. (2020) propose a kernel-based approach to two-sample test classification. Alike work
1251   has also been used in Kaggle competitions under the name of “Adversarial Validation” (Ellis, 2023;
1252   Guschin et al., 2018; Barrabés et al., 2023), a technique which aims to detect which features are
1253   distinct between train and leaderboard datasets to avoid possible leaderboard shakes. Other works
1254   have integrated predicting the protected attribute in their pipeline. Edwards and Storkey (2016)
1255   remove statistical imparity from images by using an adversary that tries to predict the relevant
1256   sensitive variable from the model representation and censoring the learning of the representation of
1257
       the model and data on images and neural networks. Our approach does not aim to censor or mitigate
       bias on the learned representation but to propose a measure of fairness that is more aligned with the
1258

1307   Many works discuss the application of Shapley values for feature attribution in ML models (Strumbelj
1308   and Kononenko, 2014; Lundberg et al., 2020; Lundberg and Lee, 2017; Lundberg et al., 2018).
1309   However, the correct way to connect a model to a coalitional game, which is the central concept of
1310   Shapley values, is a source of controversy, with two main approaches: an interventional (Aas et al.,
1311   2021; Frye et al., 2020; Zern et al., 2023), and an observational (Sundararajan and Najmi, 2020; Datta
1312   et al., 2016; Mase et al., 2019) formulation of the conditional expectation, see Eqs. (4) and (5).
1313   In the following experiment, we compare the impact of the two approaches on our Equal Treatment
1314   Inspector to measure Explanation Disparity. We benchmark this experiment on the four prediction
1315   tasks based on the US census data (Ding et al., 2021) and use linear models for both the fθ (X) and
1316   gψ (S(fθ , X)). We calculate the two variants of Shapley values using the SHAP linear explainer.6
1317   The comparison will be parametric to a feature perturbation hyperparameter. The interventional
1318   SHAP values break the dependence structure between features in the model to uncover how the model
1319
       would behave if the inputs was changed (as it was an intervention). This option is said to stay “true to
       the model”, meaning it will only give allocation credit to the features that the model actually uses.
1320
       On the other hand, the full conditional approximation of the SHAP values respects the correlations of
1321
       the input features. If the model depends on one input that is correlated with another input, then both
1322   get some credit for the model’s behaviour. This option is said to say “true to the data”, meaning it
1323   only considers how the model would behave when respecting the correlations in the input data (Chen
1324   et al., 2020). We measure the difference between the two approaches by looking at the AUC and the
1325   linear coefficients of the inspector gψ , for this case only for the pair of values White-Other of the

       H                 LIME AS AN A LTERNATIVE TO S HAPLEY VALUES
1675
1676
       Def. 3.2 of ED is parametric in the explanation function. We used Shapley values for their theoretical
1677
       advantages (see Appendix B). Another widely used feature attribution technique is LIME (Local
1678
       Interpretable Model-Agnostic Explanations). The intuition behind LIME is to create a local linear
1679   model that approximates the behavior of the original model in a small neighbourhood of the instance
1680   to explain (Ribeiro et al., 2016a;b), whose mathematical intuition is very similar to the Taylor-
1681   Maclaurin series. This section discusses the differences in our approach when adopting LIME instead
1682   of the SHAP implementation of Shapley values. First of all, LIME has certain drawbacks:
1683
1684                     • Computationally Expensive: Its current implementation is more computationally expensive
1685                       than current SHAP implementations such as TreeSHAP (Lundberg et al., 2020), Data SHAP
1686                       (Kwon et al., 2021; Ghorbani and Zou, 2019), or Local and Connected SHAP (Chen et al.,
1687                       2019b). This problem is exacerbated when producing explanations for multiple instances
1688                       (as in our case). In fact, LIME requires sampling data and fitting a linear model, which
1689                       is a computationally more expensive approach than the aforementioned model-specific
1690
                           approaches to SHAP. A comparison of the runtime is reported in the next sub-section.
1691                     • Local Neighborhood: The randomness in the calculation of local neighbourhoods can lead
1692                       to instability of the LIME explanations. Works including Slack et al. (2020); Alvarez-Melis
1693                       and Jaakkola (2018); Adebayo et al. (2018) highlight that several types of feature attributions
1694                       explanations, including LIME, can vary greatly in their provided explanations.
1695
                         • Dimensionality: LIME requires, as a hyperparameter, the number of features to use for the
1696
                           local linear model. For our method, all features in the explanation distribution should be
1697
                           used. However, linear models suffer from the curse of dimensionality. In our experiments,
1698                       this is not apparent, since our synthetic and real datasets are low-dimensional.
