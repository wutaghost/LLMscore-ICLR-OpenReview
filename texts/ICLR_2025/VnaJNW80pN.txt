011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Combinatorial optimization (CO) problems are fundamental across various do-
014            mains, with many sharing similarities in optimization objectives, decision variables,
015
               and constraints. Many traditional algorithms perform well on related problems
               using similar solution strategies, highlighting the commonality in solving different
016
               problems. However, most machine learning approaches treat each CO problem in
017
               isolation, failing to capitalize on the underlying relationships between problems. In
018            this paper, we investigate the potential to learn generalized solving strategies that
019            capture the shared structure among different CO problems, enabling easier adapta-
020            tion to related tasks. To this end, we propose to first divide the model architecture
021            into three components: a header, an encoder, and a decoder; where The header and
022            decoder address problem-specific inputs and outputs, while the encoder is designed
023            to learn shared strategies that generalize across different problems. To ensure
024            this, we enforce alignment in the optimization directions of the encoder across
025            problems, maintaining consistency in both gradient directions and magnitudes to
026
               harmonize optimization processes. This is achieved by introducing the additional
               problem-specific rotation matrices and loss weights to steer the gradients, which are
027
               updated via a gradient consistency loss. Extensive experiments on six CO problems
028
               demonstrate that our method enhances the model’s ability to capture shared solving
029            strategies across problems. We show that the learned encoder on several prob-
030            lems can directly perform comparably on new problems to models trained from
031            scratch, highlighting its potential to support developing the foundational model for
032            combinatorial optimization. Source code will be made publicly available.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Combinatorial optimization (CO) problems, which involve optimizing discrete variables under
038   specific objectives, are a fundamental class of problems in computer science, operations research,
039
      and beyond, serving widespread applications in practical scenarios Hong et al. (2010); Mironov &
      Zhang (2006); Ganzinger et al. (2004). However, due to their inherent computational complexity,
040
      e.g. NP-hardness, solving efficiency poses significant challenges and requires exhaustive human
041
      efforts to design solving heuristics. Traditionally, this has necessitated the development of problem-
042   specific heuristics, requiring exhaustive human effort and domain expertise. Recently, advances in
043   machine learning (ML) have demonstrated the potential to automatically learn solving heuristics
044   from data, improving both solution quality and speed when the problem instances fall within certain
045   distributions (Bengio et al., 2021; Kool et al., 2018; Joshi et al., 2019; Kwon et al., 2020; Sun &
046   Yang, 2023; Li et al., 2023). In addition, learning can help quickly uncover new heuristics for new
047   problems or new instance distributions where experts are not there.
048   Despite their complexity, many CO problems share common structures, exhibiting similarities in
049   optimization objectives, decision variables, or constraints (Kool et al., 2018; Kwon et al., 2020). This
050   shared nature extends even further in the realm of NP-complete (NPC) problems, where reductions
051   between problems reveal a deeper computational equivalence (Cook, 2023; Karp, 2010). The presence
052   of these commonalities suggests that techniques designed for one CO problem may have broader
053   relevance, offering the potential for more generalizable and adaptable solutions. Historically, classical
      heuristics (Papadimitriou & Steiglitz, 2013; Dorigo et al., 2006) have demonstrated this versatility,

      To this end, we introduce gradient-consistent neural combinatorial optimization (GCNCO) to enforce
079   the consistency of the optimization trajectories across tasks and promote learning generalizable
080   strategies. Gradients are the driving force behind model optimization, guiding it through the landscape
081   of a given task (Ruder, 2016; LeCun et al., 2015). If a models optimization trajectory, reflected in
082   the gradient directions and magnitudes, remains consistent across tasks, it suggests that the model
083   is learning a shared, underlying solution strategy that is applicable across different CO problems.
084   Gradient consistency ensures that when optimizing for one problem, the models learned parameters
085   improve performance not just for that specific task but also for others. This alignment of gradients
086   across tasks creates a unified approach to solving CO problems, preventing the model from overfitting
087
      to one particular task and instead promoting a general-purpose strategy.
088   Specifically, we first introduce a header-encoder-decoder framework to allow the model to process
089   different problems in a unified manner, where the header and decoder handle problem-specific inputs
090   and outputs, and the encoder is designed to learn a common strategy that generalizes across multiple
091   CO problems. Then, we propose to align the optimization directions of the encoder across different
092   problems, ensuring consistency in both gradient directions and magnitudes during training. The
093
      encoder gradients of different tasks are steered by newly introduced task-specific feature rotation
      matrices and loss weights. The feature rotation matrices rotate the optimization landscape and thus
094
      adjust the gradient direction, while the loss weights modulate the gradient magnitude. The whole
095
      framework follows a bi-level optimization process where the original models are optimized through
096   the solving objective of different problems, and the additional rotation matrices and loss weights are
097   optimized through a gradient-consistent loss reflecting the gradient homogenization. This approach
098   ensures that the encoder learns strategies that are not only effective for individual tasks but also
099   applicable across a range of CO problems.
100
      Our experiments cover six combinatorial optimization problems: Travelling Salesman Problem (TSP),
101
      Vehicle Routing Problem (VRP), Split Delivery VRP (SDVRP), Orienteering Problem (OP), Prize
102   Collecting TSP (PCTSP), and Stochastic PCTSP (SPCTSP). First, we analyze the relationships
103   between tasks by studying the gradient alignment across problems. We then demonstrate the ef-
104   fectiveness of our method in learning a general solving strategy by evaluating its performance on
105   both training tasks and new, unseen problems under zero-shot evaluation and finetuning settings.
106   Remarkably, we show that the learned encoder, when applied to new problems, achieves results
107   comparable to models trained from scratch, highlighting the potential of our approach to serve as a
      foundational model for combinatorial optimization.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Neural Combinatorial Optimization. Recent advancements in machine learning for combina-
111   torial optimization (CO) have introduced innovative solvers that can be broadly categorized into
112   constructive and improvement-based approaches. Constructive methods, including autoregressive
113   techniques Khalil et al. (2017); Kool et al. (2018); Kwon et al. (2020); Hottung et al. (2021); Kim
114   et al. (2022), sequentially determine decision variables to build complete solutions, while non-
115   autoregressive strategies Joshi et al. (2019); Fu et al. (2021); Geisler et al. (2022); Qiu et al. (2022);
116   Sun & Yang (2023) generate soft-constrained solutions in a single pass, subsequently refined through
117   post-processing for feasibility. In contrast, improvement-based solvers d O Costa et al. (2020); Wu
118   et al. (2021); Chen & Tian (2019); Li et al. (2021); Hou et al. (2023) focus on iteratively enhancing
119
      existing solutions using local search operators to optimize the objective. The promise of generative
      modeling in CO lies in its robust representational abilities and effective distribution estimation,
120
      framing problem-solving as a conditional generation task to learn solution distributions tailored to
121
      specific instances Hottung et al. (2021); Cheng et al. (2022); Sun & Yang (2023); Du et al. (2023);
122   Zhang et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023).
123
124   Multi-task learning. Multi-Task Learning (MTL) seeks to boost the effectiveness of multiple tasks
125
      by training a unified model that captures the shared insights across these tasks. A plethora of studies
      have tackled MTL from different angles, such as striking a balance in the loss functions of diverse
126
      tasks (Mao et al., 2021; Dai et al., 2023), developing mechanisms for module sharing (Javaloy &
127
      Valera, 2021), and leveraging meta-learning (Wang et al., 2021). In an effort to enhance the efficiency
128   of MTL and reduce the detrimental effects of negative transfer (Jiang et al., 2024), some research has
129   turned to task-grouping strategies (Fifty et al., 2021), aiming to discern task relationships and facilitate
130   learning within these groups to minimize negative transfer between conflicting tasks. MTL has found
131   widespread application in many fields (Allenspach et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024). Nonetheless,
132   there is a scarcity of research on applying MTL to solve COPs.
133
      Multi-task Learning for CO. The unified model capable of simultaneously addressing multiple
134   CO problems is still in its early stages. The ability of neural solvers to generalize across tasks has
135   garnered increasing interest. Ibarz et al. (2022) introduce a generalist neural algorithmic learner a
136   single graph neural network processor designed to execute a broad range of algorithms for classic
137   polynomial problems. For more computationally complex CO problems, Wang & Yu (2023) propose
138   a multi-armed bandit approach to solve multiple CO problems by alternating optimizing different
139   problems. For specialized problems like Vehicle Routing Problems (VRPs), Lin et al. (2024) suggest
140   training a shared backbone model for VRPs, which can then be fine-tuned efficiently for different
141   VRP variants using linear projections. Additionally, Zhou et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2024); Berto
142
      et al. (2024) apply attribute composition to achieve (zero-shot) generalization across various VRP
      variants. While these approaches treat a set of problem variants as extensions of a single-task model
143
      with varying attributes, this paper explores learning generalized strategies across different problems,
144
      aiming to prune the single-task model for the generalized part rather than expanding it.

149   Adopting the conventions established in (Karalias & Loukas, 2020; Wang et al., 2022), we define
150   G as the collection of CO problem instances represented by graphs G(V, E) ∈ G, where V and
151   E denote the nodes and edges respectively. CO problems can be broadly classified into two types
152

      edges and node-decision problems that determine nodes. Let x ∈ {0, 1}N denote the optimization
153
      variable, where each entry indicates whether it is included in the solution. For edge-decision problems,
154
      N = n2 and xi,j indicates whether Ei,j is included in x. For node-decision problems, N = n and xi
155   indicates whether Vi is included in x. The feasible set Ω consists of x satisfying specific constraints
156   as feasible solutions. A CO problem on G aims to find a feasible x that minimizes the given objective
157   function l(·; G) : {0, 1}N → R≥0 :

181

182   the learned strategies being effective across multiple tasks, which may potentially be effective for
183   new, unseen tasks. Furthermore, this model can serve as a pre-trained model, where its performance

      To equip the model with the ability to handle various problems, we follow Wang & Yu (2023) to
236
      adopt separate headers and decoders for each problem to handle problem-specific inputs and outputs,
237   and the entire model follows a header-encoder-decoder framework, where the headers and decoders
238   consist of a single layer each, while the encoder, with significantly larger parameters, is designed to
239   learn the core solving strategy.
240
      The headers preprocess the raw input of different combinatorial optimization problems, converting
241
      information such as coordinates, demands, rewards, and penalties into unified vector representations.
242
      The encoding steps of the shared encoder are consistent with the Transformer (Vaswani, 2017), where
243   the input is first mapped to Q, K, V by passing through the weight matrices Wq , Wk , Wv of linear
244   layers. Then, multi-head attention is used to capture the correlations between input nodes. The
245   output is combined with the input through residual connections and layer normalization, followed by
246   processing with a feed-forward neural network to extract features for each task. The decoding steps
247   in the Decoder differ from the Transformer decoder, combining multi-head attention and single-head
248   attention. Before selecting actions in the final step, attention scores and embedding vectors are
249   generated based on the current problem state (such as the current node, remaining capacity, etc.)
250   using multi-head attention. These intermediate variables are then processed through single-head
251
      attention to compute the probability of an action rather than generating a continuous sequence. This
      simplifies the action selection process, directly outputting the probability distribution for action
252
      selection, making it more suitable for specific optimization tasks.

331   4.4   OVERVIEW
332
333
      Based on the header-encoder-decoder architecture, when optimizing the objective function, we
      additionally attach rotation matrices for different tasks to transform the features obtained from the
334
      encoder. The transformed features are then passed through a problem-specific decoder to obtain the
335
      results, and the model is optimized using Eq. 9. On the other hand, in the same step, we update
336   the parameters of the rotation matrix using Eq. 12, optimizing the rotation matrix to ensure that the
337   gradient directions remain consistent across each problem.
338
339
      In this paradigm, we train the model on data from several problems simultaneously, aiming for the
      encoder to learn common solving strategies across these problems. The learned strategies are expected
340
      to have the potential to solve other similar problems. Therefore, after training, when we encounter a
341
      new problem, we do not need to retrain the entire model from scratch. Instead, we can freeze the
342   encoder, hoping that the abstracted solving strategies will already provide good performance. In this
343   case, we only need to retrain the problem-specific single-layer header and decoder. On the other hand,
344   we can also continue to finetune the encoder on different tasks, allowing it to gain more specialized
345   solving abilities for the tasks, thereby improving its performance.

      In this section, we conduct a series of experiments to evaluate the performance of our proposed
350
      method. Specifically, we analyze the relationship between different combinatorial optimization (CO)
351   tasks through gradient similarity, assess the in-distribution solving performance, and investigate the
352   model’s generalization ability across tasks, both in a zero-shot setting and with finetuning. The
353   experiments are carried out on six distinct CO problems, including the Travelling Salesman Problem
354   (TSP), Vehicle Routing Problem (VRP), Split Delivery VRP (SDVRP), Orienteering Problem (OP),
355   Prize Collecting TSP (PCTSP), and Stochastic PCTSP (SPCTSP).
356
357   5.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
358
359   Datasets. For our TSP, VRP, PCTSP, and OP problems, the common aspect in training and testing
360   instances is the uniform sampling of NN node coordinates within the unit square [0, 1]2 . The
361
      differences lie in their additional information and objectives. Unlike TSP, other problems explicitly
      sample a starting point.While VRPs introduces demand at each node and vehicle capacity constraints,
362
      PCTSP assigns rewards and penalties to nodes and OP needs the prizes to nodes and the specific type

      of the prize. All settings mentioned above is a standard procedure as adopted in Kool et al. (2018);

      6    C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
528
529
530   This paper presents a novel approach to learning shared-solving strategies across different combina-
531
      torial optimization (CO) problems by enforcing gradient consistency along the optimization process.
      Built upon the header-encoder-decoder architecture which explicitly separates problem-specific com-
532
      ponents (header and decoder) from a generalized problem-agnostic encoder. The encoder is designed
533
      to learn and generalize solving strategies across a diverse set of CO problems by maintaining gradient
534   consistency through the use of problem-specific rotation matrices and loss weights. The effectiveness
535   is demonstrated through extensive experiments on six distinct CO tasks, showing that the proposed
536   approach not only performs well on in-distribution tasks but also exhibits strong generalization
537   capabilities in zero-shot and finetuned cross-problem scenarios. Our results underscore the potential
538   of the learned encoder to support the development of foundational models for CO, as it allows for
539   adaptation to new CO problems without requiring retraining from scratch. This opens up avenues for
      future work, including extending the method to larger data and exploring specific fine-tuning methods.
