009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Input-gradient-based feature attribution methods, such as Vanilla Gradient, Inte-
012            grated Gradients, and SmoothGrad, are widely used to explain image classifiers by
013            generating saliency maps. However, these methods struggle to provide explana-
014            tions that are both visually clear and quantitatively robust. Key challenges include
015            ensuring that explanations are sparse, stable, and faithfully reflect the model’s
016            decision-making. Adversarial training, known for enhancing model robustness,
017            have been shown to produce sparser explanations with these methods; however,
018
               this sparsity often comes at the cost of stability. In this work, we investigate the
               trade-off between stability and sparsity in saliency maps and propose the use of a
019
               smoothing layer during adversarial training. Through extensive experiments and
020
               evaluation, we demonstrate this smoothing technique improves the stability and
021            faithfulness of saliency maps without sacrificing sparsity. Furthermore, a quali-
022            tative user study reveals that human evaluators tend to distrust explanations that
023            are overly noisy or excessively sparse—issues commonly associated with expla-
024            nations in naturally and adversarially trained models, respectively and prefer ex-
025            planations produced by our proposed approach. Our findings offer a promising
026            direction for generating reliable explanations with robust models, striking a bal-
027            ance between clarity and usability.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Input gradient-based explanation methods highlight the features most influential to a model’s deci-
033
      sion by calculating the gradient of the model’s output with respect to its input, visualized as saliency
      maps in images. One of the earliest approaches, Vanilla Gradient (VG) (Simonyan et al., 2014),
034
      computes gradients across input pixels, ranking features by their gradient magnitude. While prior
035
      studies have shown that input-gradients can capture relevant information regarding a model output
036   (Samek et al., 2016), VG suffers from noisy saliency map. Hence, various methods like Integrated
037   Gradient (IG) (Sundararajan et al., 2017), and SmoothGrad (SG) (Smilkov et al., 2017) have been
038   proposed that modifies the input-gradient approach to reduce saliency map noise and improve the
039   visual quality of the explanations.
040
      However, quality explanations require more than visual appeal. Explanations should be compre-
041
      hensible to users and satisfy quantitative measures to ensure their practical utility. Key properties
042   include sparsity, which ensures explanations focus on the most relevant features by discarding ir-
043   relevant ones (Chalasani et al., 2020); stability, which guarantees consistent explanations across
044   small input perturbations (Alvarez-Melis & Jaakkola, 2018); and faithfulness, ensuring that the ex-
045   planations accurately reflect the model’s actual decision-making process (Rong et al., 2022). These
046   attributes are essential for explanations to be trustworthy and actionable in real-world applications.
047
      In this work, we demonstrate a way to enhance above-mentioned properties of explanations in input-
048   gradient based methods. We consider three representative input-gradient based methods (Vanilla
049   Gradient (VG), Integrated Gradient (IG), and SmoothGrad (SG)) and first demonstrate that the sta-
050   bility of their explanations is closely tied to the model’s sensitivity to input perturbations. Adver-
051   sarial training (Goodfellow et al., 2015), a technique commonly used to improve model robustness,
052   results in explanations that are sparser, aligning with previous studies (Chalasani et al., 2020; Et-
053   mann et al., 2019). However, we observe that this increased sparsity comes at the cost of reduced
      stability in explanations. To mitigate this trade-off, we introduce a smoothing layer applied during

089   2    R ELATED WORK
090
091   As highlighted by Ilyas et al. (2019), explanations that are meaningful and faithful to the model’s
092   decision-making process cannot be pursued independently from the training of the model, a principle
093   central to our approach. Below we discuss such related works.
094
      Improving saliency maps by training modification: Previous studies have proposed several mod-
095
      ifications to model training to improve saliency maps. For instance, Kim et al. (2019) introduce
096   layer-wise thresholding during backpropagation, while Dombrowski et al. (2019) suggest soft-plus
097   activations as an alternative to ReLU for refining saliency maps. Wicker et al. (2023) develop a
098   framework for certifying the robustness of explanations through training constraints. Meanwhile,
099   Chenyang & Chan (2023) propose training object detectors by ensuring explanation consistency
100   within same object and distinctions between different objects. In contrast, we do not make such
101   modifications, and enhance the quality of explanations by applying simple smoothing filters during
102   adversarial training.
103   Study of saliency maps in robust models: Some previous works have also explored saliency map
104   quality in robust models (Etmann et al., 2019; Zhang & Zhu, 2019; Chalasani et al., 2020; Mangla
105   et al., 2020; Shah et al., 2021), typically evaluating sparsity, or visual quality. Chalasani et al. (2020)
106   show that adversarial training with L∞ attacks leads to sparse saliency maps, and theoretically
107   demonstrate that training a 1-layer network by encouraging stability of explanations is equivalent
      to adversarial training, but do not present results on multi-layer networks. Etmann et al. (2019) ex-


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

      plain the interpretability of robust models by demonstrating alignment between image and saliency
109   maps, which works well for smaller datasets like MNIST but does not scale to larger datasets like
110   ImageNet. Zhang & Zhu (2019) argue that adversarially trained models produce shape-biased rep-
111   resentations, resulting in sparser saliency maps. In contrast, we approach the quality of saliency
112   maps via the stability of the input-gradient explanation methods and establish a theoretical con-
113   nection with model sensitivity, and propose adversarial training with feature-map smoothing as the
114   mitigation of sparsity-stability tradeoff.

116   3     M ETHOD
117
118   Preliminaries: Consider a single-layer DNN with the form F (x) = H(⟨w, x⟩), where H is a
119   differentiable scalar-valued activation function (e.g., sigmoid), ⟨w, x⟩ is the dot product between
120   the weight vector w and input x ∈ Rd . The Vanilla Gradient (VG) method (Simonyan et al., 2014)
121   measures the sensitivity of the model output F (x) with respect to each feature of the input x. This
122   is given by computing the gradient of the output F (x) with respect to the input x. The Integrated
123   Gradients (IG) method (Sundararajan et al., 2017) averages the gradients along a straight-line path
124   from a baseline input x′ (often a zero vector) to the actual input x. SmoothGrad (SG) (Smilkov
125   et al., 2017) improves on any gradient-based explanations like VG or IG by adding random noise to
126
      the input x multiple times, calculating the explanations for each noisy version, and then averaging
      the results.

248   Setup: We evaluate our approach on three datasets: FMNIST (Xiao et al., 2017), CIFAR-10

250   The variants include: 1) naturally trained (N), 2) adversarially trained (A), 3) adversarial training
251   with mean-filter smoothing (M1), 4) adversarial training with median-filter smoothing (M2), 5) ad-
252   versarial training with Gaussian-filter smoothing (G), 6) adversarial training with embedded filter
253   smoothing (E), and 7) adversarial training with non-local Gaussian smoothing (NG). Following the
254   setup from Chalasani et al. (2020), we use LeNet (LeCun et al., 1998) for FMNIST and Wide-ResNet
255
      (Zagoruyko & Komodakis, 2016) for CIFAR-10. We use ResNet-18 He et al. (2016)for ImageNette.
      For adversarial training, we apply perturbations under the L∞ norm using the PGD attack (Madry
256
      et al., 2018). The models are trained with ϵ = 0.1 for FMNIST and CIFAR-10, and ϵ = 1/255 for
257
      ImageNette, as these values yielded the best performance across our evaluations. We also achieved
258   optimal results by adding the smoothing block after the first convolutional or residual block. Full
259   details of our datasets and training methodology are provided in Appendix A, and we discuss the
260   impact of altering the smoothing filter’s position in Appendix E. We also discuss the effect of robust
261   training strategy on saliency map quality for a different network architecture in Appendix F.

      4.2 R ESULTS AND DISCUSSION
283
284   1. On the sparsity and stability of saliency maps: Similar to Chalasani et al. (2020), we compare
285   the sparsity and stability improvement of saliency maps with respect to the naturally trained model
286
      (N). Specifically, for a given training method (M), we compute the following metrics that quantify
      the improvement in sparseness (dG), relative input stability (dRIS), relative output stability (dROS),
287
      and relative representation stability (dRRS) of the explanation method ϕ(.) ∈ {V G, IG, SG}:
288
      dG[ϕ(x)] = GM [ϕ(x)] − GN [ϕ(x)], dRIS[ϕ(x)] = RIS M [ϕ(x)] − RIS N [ϕ(x)], dROS[ϕ(x)] =
289   ROS M [ϕ(x)] − ROS N [ϕ(x)] and dRRS[ϕ(x)] = RRS M [ϕ(x)] − RRS N [ϕ(x)].
290
291   As illustrated in Table 1, across all datasets-FMNIST, CIFAR-10 and ImageNette-robust models
292
      consistently achieve higher positive dG values for all three explanation methods (VG, IG, SG), in-
      dicating that these methods produce sparser saliency maps in robust models than naturally trained
293
      models. Notably, the highest sparsity gains in explanations are observed in models utilizing non-
294
      local smoothing filters. On FMNIST and CIFAR-10, the NG models (non-local gaussian) attain the
295   highest sparsity across all explanation methods, and on ImageNette dataset, model E (embedded
296   gaussian) achieves the highest sparsity for VG, IG and SG. However, this increase in sparsity comes
297   at the expense of stability, as most robust models exhibit reduced stability in their explanations, sug-
298   gesting an inverse relationship between the sparsity and stability of saliency maps. For example: NG
299   models, while achieving high sparsity for explanations, show significant drops in dRIS, dROS, and
300   dRRS, indicating that their explanations may be more sensitive to input perturbations or variations
301   in model representations. Notably, models M1 and M2 provide a promising middle-ground. On
302   FMNIST and CIFAR-10, explanations consistently achieves the highest stability in M2 across all
303
      methods, while still maintaining sparsity gain. On ImageNette, M1 offers the best stability across
      explanation methods. These results suggest that the use of local smoothing filters like mean and me-
304
      dian filters during adversarial training can preserve the stability of saliency maps while maintaining
305
      a degree of sparsity.
306
307   2. On the faithfulness of explanation: Faithfulness metrics that involve pixel removal and measur-
308   ing model prediction changes (such as insertion/deletion (Petsiuk et al., 2018)) introduces artifacts
309
      and cause a distribution shift in the perturbed inputs. Retraining based approaches like ROAR
      (Hooker et al., 2019) addresses this problem but is computationally expensive. ROAD (Rong et al.,
310
      2022) addresses both concerns in faithfulness evaluation by measuring model accuracy on the test
311
      set as pixels are iteratively removed using a nosily linear imputation strategy. We adopt the MoRF
312   (Most Relevant First) removal strategy but ROAD demonstrates consistent results with both MoRF

      4.3                Q UALITATIVE A NALYSIS
481
482   Our quantitative studies demonstrate that saliency maps in adversarially trained models are sparse
483   but at the expense of stability. Incorporating local feature-map smoothing improves stability of
484   saliency maps without drastically compromising sparsity, balancing these two aspects. In this sec-
485   tion, we analyze how well end-users comprehend saliency maps from different model training strate-
      gies based on the level of sparsity.


                                                                                                             9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      We conducted an experiment with 65 graduate students (Ph.D./ Masters), each with at least a year of
487   experience in computer vision1 . The objective was to determine whether the information conveyed
488   by saliency maps was sufficient for understanding and trusting the underlying model behavior. Par-
489   ticipants were shown saliency maps using Vanilla Gradient from three models—naturally trained,
490   adversarially trained, and adversarially trained with feature-map smoothing (median filter)—for 10
491   images across FMNIST and CIFAR-10 datasets, resulting in 60 image-saliency pairs. The saliency
492   maps were presented in random order, and participants were unaware of the model that generated
493   them. Afterward, they rated each saliency map using the Hoffman satisfaction scale (Hoffman et al.,
494   2023), responding to two key questions: 1) “Does the explanation provide sufficient information?”
495
      and 2) “Do you trust the model’s classification based on this saliency map?” Ratings were on a scale
      of 1 (strongly disagree) to 5 (strongly agree). Finally, participants were asked to compare saliency
496
      maps from all three models side by side and select the most comprehensible explanation, providing
497
      free-text justifications for their choices.
498
499   Results: We assessed the comprehensibility of the saliency maps based on two metrics: sufficiency
500   and trust. For the naturally trained model, participants rated sufficiency at an average of 2.08 (± 0.75)
501
      and trust at 2.02 (± 0.82), indicating that the noisy maps from this model were generally considered
      untrustworthy. In contrast, adversarially trained models fared better, with sufficiency scoring 2.99
502
      (± 0.93) and trust 3.08 (± 0.90), as participants found these maps clearer and more aligned with the
503
      images. The feature-map smoothed adversarial model scored the highest, with sufficiency at 3.33
504   (± 1.03) and trust at 3.14 (± 1.01). Participants appreciated the reduction in noise and highlighted
505   the clarity and relevance of the explanations. When comparing saliency maps directly, 56% of
506   participants preferred the maps from the feature-map smoothed model, 29% favored the adversarial
507   model, and only 15% selected the naturally trained model. The majority cited reasons such as
508   “highlighting important features without excessive detail” and “close enough to the image with the
509   least noise”.
510   To statistically validate the results, we performed Wilcoxon signed-rank test (Woolson, 2007) and
511   one-way ANOVA (Cuevas et al., 2004) on the sufficiency and trust metrics across the three models.
512   The p-values were extremely small (< 0.001), confirming significant differences between the models
513   in terms of both metrics. This shows that the different training strategies lead to distinct levels of
514   comprehensibility and trustworthiness in saliency maps. Details of qualitative study and results are
515   provided in Appendix I.

      5    L IMITATIONS
519
520
      Our experiments are conducted on three popular datasets such as FMNIST, CIFAR-10, and Ima-
      geNette. As model complexity and dataset size grow, especially with higher class counts, adversar-
521
      ial training becomes increasingly difficult (Zhang et al., 2019). Maintaining both high accuracy and
522
      robustness in such settings presents a significant challenge. Additionally, while we explored several
523   local and non-local smoothing filters, the choice of the optimal filter remains largely empirical and
524   task-dependent.

526
527   6    C ONCLUSION
528
      In this paper, we explore the connection between model training strategies and quality of explana-
529
      tions, and propose a simple modification to adversarial training to improve the comprehensibility
530
      of saliency maps. Through a comprehensive study, we established that the quality of saliency maps
531   is tied to the sensitivity of a model, with adversarially trained models producing sparser but unsta-
532   ble explanations. Incorporating local feature-map smoothing during adversarial training enhances
533   stability and faithfulness without sacrificing sparsity. Our work underscores that meaningful and
534   faithful explanations are tied to the model training strategy. By shedding light on the trade-offs be-
535   tween robustness of a model and saliency map quality, we advocate for the designing models that
536   strike a balance between performance and saliency map comprehensibility.

728   A.1    A DVERSARIAL TRAINING
729
      Adversarial training (Goodfellow et al., 2015) is a machine learning technique that involves train-
730
      ing a model in the presence of adversarial examples. Adversarial examples are inputs specifically
731   designed to mislead or deceive the model, causing the model to make incorrect predictions. The
732   goal of adversarial training is to improve the robustness and generalization of a model against such
733   perturbed examples. To perform adversarial training, we generate adversarial examples that are pro-
734   duced from natural samples x ∈ Rd by adding a perturbation vector δ ∈ Rd . The perturbation
735   vector differs based on the type of attack employed. We use the PGD (Madry et al., 2018) attack
736   to obtain adversarial perturbations. PGD is an iterative attack where the perturbation is computed
737   multiple times with small steps. The hyper-parameters of PGD attack in our adversarial training:
738   for FMNIST and CIFAR-10, ϵ ∈ {0.01, 0.03, 0.06, 0.1}, attack step size = ϵ/10, and number of
739
      iterations = 40; for ImageNette ϵ = 1/255, step size = 0.00784 and number of iterations = 20. Other
      training hyperparameters are kept as explained in Appendix A.

846   of explanations using faithfulness estimate (Alvarez Melis & Jaakkola, 2018), Softmax Information

849   than their naturally trained counterparts, particularly on datasets like CIFAR-10 and ImageNette.
850   This aligns with the findings of Shah et al. (2021), which showed that naturally trained models fail
851   to capture the most discriminative features, often due to feature leakage.
852   However, while adversarial training appears to mitigate the feature leakage issue, and improves the
853   faithfulness of explanations, the underlying mechanisms are still not fully understood. One hypoth-
854   esis is that adversarial training encourages models to rely on more robust, generalizable features,
855   which better reflect the decision-making process across adversarial and clean inputs. However, fur-

      explanations.

       G    C ONDITIONS A FFECTING THE T IGHTNESS OF S TABILITY B OUNDS

974
       The stability bounds presented in Section 3.1 serve as indicators of the relationship between model
975    sensitivity and attribution stability. However, these bounds are inherently approximate and depend
976    on several factors, including model architecture, the input data distribution, and the type of pertur-
977    bation applied. Here, we discuss some conditions under which these bounds may become tighter or
978    looser.
979
980          1. Model Nonlinearity and Activation Function: The nonlinearity of the model, particu-
981
                larly the choice of activation function H, influences the bounds’ tightness. For activation
                functions with bounded gradients, such as sigmoid or tanh, the change in H ′ (⟨w, x⟩) is lim-
982
                ited, leading to more consistent attributions across small perturbations and therefore tighter
983
                stability bounds. Specifically, for sigmoid, H(z) = 1+e1−z and H ′ (z) = H(z)(1 − H(z)),
984
                both of which remain bounded as H(z) approaches 0 or 1. Conversely, for ReLU activation,
985             H(z) = max(0, z) with H ′ (z) = 1 when z > 0 and 0 otherwise, the gradient can change
986             abruptly across input perturbations. Thus, for perturbations where x is shifted across the
987             activation boundary, H ′ (⟨w, x⟩) may vary significantly, producing looser bounds.
988
             2. Magnitude and Type of Input Perturbations: The type and scale of input perturbations
989             can also impact bound tightness. For small perturbations, such as Gaussian noise with
990             n ∼ N (0, σ 2 ), the output change is typically small, and stability bounds remain tight.
991             However, larger perturbations, such as stronger adversarial attack, often result in more
992             significant output shifts |F (x′ ) − F (x)|, leading to looser bounds.
993          3. Smoothness of Model Parameters: Weight regularization techniques, such as weight de-
994             cay, result in smoother gradients, reducing the sensitivity of F (x) to input changes. For in-
995             stance, regularized models with smaller gradient norms tend to have tighter stability bounds
996             as H ′ (⟨w, x⟩) · w varies less across the input space. Consequently, the bounds for VG, SG,
997             and IG become tighter, as regularization reduces model sensitivity.
998          4. Dataset-Specific Characteristics: Datasets with high intraclass variability introduce more
999             variable responses to perturbations, increasing |F (x′ )−F (x)|. As a result, stability bounds
1000            may become looser due to the variability in F (x) across samples.

1336   K.3   FAITHFULNESS
1337
1338   Faithfulness estimate Alvarez Melis & Jaakkola (2018): The faithfulness metric measures the
1339   influence of attributed features on model prediction. If the features attributed to an explanation
1340   method truly capture the model behavior, the influence should be high. Influence is measured with
1341
       a correlation metric where a given image is iteratively modified to compute the correlation between
       the sum of attributions and the difference in model prediction. We use the implementation of the
1342
       faithfulness estimate available in Quantus Hedström et al. (2023).
1343
1344   Performance Information Curves (PIC) Kapishnikov et al. (2019): Performance Informa-
1345   tion Curves (PIC) is analogous to the area under Receive Operating Characteristics (ROC) curves,
1346   proposed by Kapishnikov et al., to measure the quality of saliency maps. There are two variants of
1347   PIC: Area Under Softmax Information Curve (SIC) and Area Under Accuracy Information Curve
1348   (AIC). To measure PIC, we take a blurred version of a given image and then unblur the pixels
1349   by adding features that are deemed important by an attribution method. We measure the entropy
       of the unblurred image and model performance and then map the model performance result as a


                                                           25
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1350
       function of the entropy. The two variants of the PIC, AIC, and SIC, differ in the model performance
1351   metric used to compute the area under the curve. AIC uses the accuracy of images and SIC uses the
1352   proportion of the softmax. We use the implementation shared by the authors of the original paper
1353   PAIR (2021).
1354
1355   K.4    ROAD: R EMOVE AND D EBIAS RONG ET AL . (2022)
1356
1357   ROAD measures the accuracy of a model on the provided test set at each step of an iterative process
1358   of removing k most important pixels. Removal of pixels is done with a noisy linear imputation to
1359   avoid out-of-distribution samples.
1360   We use the MoRF (Most Relevant First) removal strategy implementation of the ROAD evaluation
1361   available in Quantus Hedström et al. (2023). Given a network F and an input sample, an attribution
1362   method assigns an importance value to each input feature for the sample. The features are then
1363   ordered in decreasing order of importance for MoRF. At each iteration, k most important features
1364   are removed and the model accuracy is measured. We set k = 5 in our experiments. We prefer a
1365   sharper drop in accuracy for a better explanation.
1366   We use ROAD instead of Insertion/Deletion Petsiuk et al. (2018) or ROAR Hooker et al. (2019)
1367   because Insertion/Deletion introduces artifacts and results in a distribution shift of perturbed inputs,
1368   and ROAR requires an expensive model retraining.
1369
1370   K.5    S IMILARITY A DEBAYO ET AL . (2018)
1371
1372   Similarity measures the structural similarity between saliency maps of original and perturbed sam-
1373   ples, given the same model prediction Adebayo et al. (2018). We measure the similarity of saliency
1374   maps using the structural similarity index (SSIM). For each image x, we add Gaussian noise
1375
       (N (0, σ)) and generate its noisy version x’ such that the model prediction is consistent. We then
       compute the saliency map of x and x’ and measure the structural similarity between the maps.
