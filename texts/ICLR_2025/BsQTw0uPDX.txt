011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            This work introduces Hierarchical Preference Optimization (HPO), a novel
014            approach to hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) that addresses non-
015
               stationarity and infeasible subgoal generation issues when solving complex
               robotic control tasks. HPO leverages maximum entropy reinforcement learning
016
               combined with token-level Direct Preference Optimization (DPO), eliminating the
017
               need for pre-trained reference policies that are typically unavailable in challenging
018            robotic scenarios. Mathematically, we formulate HRL as a bi-level optimization
019            problem and transform it into a primitive-regularized DPO formulation, ensur-
020            ing feasible subgoal generation and avoiding degenerate solutions. Extensive ex-
021            periments on challenging robotic navigation and manipulation tasks demonstrate
022            HPO’s impressive performance, where HPO shows an improvement of upto 35%
023            over the baselines. Furthermore, ablation studies validate our design choices, and
024            quantitative analyses confirm HPO’s ability to mitigate non-stationarity and infea-
025            sible subgoal generation issues in HRL.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Reinforcement learning (RL) encounters significant challenges in sparse-reward environments, par-
030   ticularly in complex robotic control tasks (Nair et al., 2018). Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning
031   (HRL) (Sutton et al., 1999) helps to deal with sparse reward issues by enhancing exploration and
032   introducing temporal abstraction (Nachum et al., 2019). However, off-policy HRL methods (Levy
033   et al., 2018; Nachum et al., 2018) face their own challenges. The first (C1) is non-stationarity caused
034   by evolving lower-level policies, which destabilizes the higher-level reward function (Chane-Sane
035   et al., 2021). The second (C2) is the high-level policy’s tendency to generate subgoals that are
036   infeasible for the lower-level policy to achieve.
037   Preference-based learning (PbL) approaches like Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback
038   (RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017) have been successful in solving complex tasks. However, inte-
039   grating PbL directly with HRL is non-trivial. Singh et al. (2024) proposed an RLHF-based method
040   to mitigate non-stationarity in HRL by first learning a reward model from preference data and then
041   using RL to train the high-level policy. However, the approach in Singh et al. (2024) introduces an
042   expensive and potentially unstable RL step which can still result in infeasible subgoal predictions
043   for the lower-level primitives.
044   Recently, direct preference optimization (DPO) methods (Rafailov et al., 2024b) have been intro-
045   duced, which learn policies directly from preference data, bypassing the need for RL. Leveraging
046   DPO to learn the high-level policy in HRL could efficiently mitigate non-stationarity. However,
047   naively applying DPO requires effective pre-trained reference policies, which are often unavailable
048   in robotics scenarios. Additionally, such approaches may still produce subgoals that are infeasible
049   for the lower-level policy to execute.
050   In this work, we propose Hierarchical Preference Optimization (HPO), a hierarchical approach that
051   optimizes the higher level policy using token-level DPO objective (Rafailov et al., 2024a), and the
052   lower level primitive policy using RL. Since HPO learns higher level policy from preference data, it
053   avoids dependence on changing lower level primitive and thus mitigates non-stationarity (C1). We
      derive token-level DPO objective in a maximum entropy RL setting, to eliminate the requirement

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
097
098
099
      Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning. HRL is an elegant framework that promises the intuitive
      benefits of temporal abstraction and improved exploration (Nachum et al., 2019). Prior research
100
      work has focused on developing efficient methods that leverage hierarchical learning to efficiently
101
      solve complex tasks (Sutton et al., 1999; Barto and Mahadevan, 2003; Parr and Russell, 1998; Di-
102   etterich, 1999). Goal-conditioned HRL is an important framework in which a higher-level policy
103   assigns subgoals to a lower-level policy (Dayan and Hinton, 1992; Vezhnevets et al., 2017), which
104   executes primitive actions on the environment. Despite its advantages, HRL faces challenges owing
105   to its hierarchical structure, as goal-conditioned RL based approaches suffer from non-stationarity in
106   off-policy settings where multiple levels are trained concurrently (Nachum et al., 2018; Levy et al.,
107   2018). These issues arise because the lower level policy behavior is sub-optimal and unstable. Prior
      works deal with these issues by either simulating optimal lower primitive behavior (Levy et al.,


                                                                                    2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      2018), relabeling replay buffer transitions (Nachum et al., 2018; Singh and Namboodiri, 2023b;a),
109   or assuming access to privileged information like expert demonstrations or preferences (Singh et al.,
110   2024; Singh and Namboodiri, 2023a;b). In contrast, we propose a novel bi-level formulation to
111   mitigate non-stationarity and regularize the higher-level policy to generate feasible subgoals for the
112   lower-level policy.
113
      Behavior Priors. Some prior work relies on hand-crafted actions or behavior priors to accelerate
114
      learning (Nasiriany et al., 2021; Dalal et al., 2021). While these methods can simplify hierarchical
115   learning, their performance heavily depends on the quality of the priors; sub-optimal priors may lead
116   to sub-optimal performance. In contrast, ours is an end-to-end approach that does not require prior
117   specification, thereby avoiding significant expert human effort.
118
      Preference-based Learning. A variety of methods have been developed in this area to apply re-
119
      inforcement learning (RL) to human preference data (Knox and Stone, 2009; Pilarski et al., 2011;
120
      Wilson et al., 2012; Daniel et al., 2015), that typically involve collecting preference data from human
121   annotators, which is then used to guide downstream learning. Prior works in this area (Christiano
122   et al., 2017; Lee et al., 2021) first train a reward model based on the preference data, and subse-
123   quently employ RL to derive an optimal policy for that reward model. More recent approaches
124   have focused on improving sample efficiency using off-policy policy gradient methods (Haarnoja
125   et al., 2018) to learn the policy. Recently, direct preference optimization based approaches have
126   emerged (Rafailov et al., 2024b;a; Hejna et al., 2023), which bypass the need to learn a reward
127   model and subsequent RL step, by directly optimizing the policy with a KL-regularized maximum
128   likelihood objective corresponding to a pre-trained model. In this work, we build on the foundational
129
      knowledge in maximum entropy RL (Ziebart, 2010), and derive a token-level direct preference op-
      timization (Rafailov et al., 2024b;a) objective regularized by lower -level primitive, resulting in an
130
      efficient hierarchical framework capable of solving complex robotic tasks.

135   MDP Setup. We formulate the problem using a Markov Decision Process (MDP), defined as
136   (S, A, p, r, γ). Here, S represents the state space, and A denotes the action space. The transi-
137   tion dynamics are governed by the stochastic probability function p : S × A → ∆(S), which
138   maps each state-action pair to a probability distribution over subsequent states. The reward func-
139
      tion, r : S × A → R, provides a scalar reward based on the current state and action, and γ ∈ (0, 1)
      is the discount factor that modulates the importance of future rewards. At each time step t, the
140
      agent follows a policy π : S → ∆(A), which defines a probability distribution over actions at
141
      given the current state st . After taking action at , the agent receives a reward rt = r(st , at ), and the
142   environment transitions to a new state st+1 according to the transition probability p(·|st , at ).

166   Challenges of HRL While HRL offers advantages over traditional RL, such as better sample effi-
167   ciency through temporal abstraction and enhanced exploration (Nachum et al., 2018; 2019), it also
168   faces significant challenges. This paper focuses on two key issues:
169   C1. Training instability due to non-stationarity in off-policy HRL, and
170   C2. Suboptimal performance due to the generation of infeasible subgoals by the higher-level policy.
171
      Off-policy HRL suffers from non-stationarity because the behavior of the lower-level policy changes
172
      over time (Nachum et al., 2018; Levy et al., 2018). This makes previously collected higher-level
173   transitions in the replay buffer outdated, reducing their effectiveness. Furthermore, the higher-level
174   policy may suggest infeasible subgoals for the lower-level policy (Chane-Sane et al., 2021), hinder-
175   ing learning and decreasing overall performance. Thus, despite its theoretical advantages, HRL often
176   underperforms in practice (Nachum et al., 2018; Levy et al., 2018).
177
      Preference-based learning (PbL) methods such as RLHF (Christiano et al., 2017; Ibarz et al., 2018;
178
      Lee et al., 2021) and DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024b) leverage preference data to solve complex tasks.
179   Prior work (Singh et al., 2024) leverages RLHF to mitigate non-stationarity in HRL using pref-
180   erences. However, directly applying PbL techniques to HRL remains challenging. We now first
181   discuss PbL approaches and then discuss their key limitations when directly applying to HRL.

      3.1.1      L IMITATIONS OF DIRECTLY APPLYING P B L TO HRL
217
218   Directly using RLHF: Prior approaches leverage the advancements in PbL to mitigate non-
219   stationarity (Singh et al., 2024) by utilizing the reward model rϕH learned using RLHF as higher
220   level rewards instead of environment rewards rπHL used in vanilla HRL approaches, which depend
221   on the sub-optimal lower primitive. However, such approaches may lead to degenerate solutions by
222   generating infeasible subgoals for the lower-level primitive. Additionally, such approaches require
223   RL as an intermediate step, which might cause training instability (Rafailov et al., 2024b).
224   Directly using DPO: In temporally extended task environments like robotics, directly extending DPO
225   to the HRL framework is also non-trivial due to three reasons: (i) such scenarios deal with multi-step
226   trajectories involving stochastic transitions models, (ii) efficient pre-trained reference policies are
227   typically unavailable in robotics, (iii) similar to RLHF, such approaches may produce degenerate
228   solutions when the higher level policy subgoal predictions are infeasible.

230   4      HPO : H IERARCHICAL P REFERENCE O PTIMIZATION
231
232   To deal with the challenges of HRL and utilizing PbL in HRL framework (3.1.1), we introduce
233   HPO : Hierarchical Preference Optimization, a novel approach that leverages primitive regularized
234   DPO to mitigate non-stationarity and infeasible subgoal generation issues in HRL. The rest of this
235   section proceeds as follows: we first critically analyze the key source of non-stationarity in HRL
236   and formulate HRL as a bi-level optimization problem. Utilizing this formulation, we then derive
237   a primitive-regularized token-level DPO objective that overcomes the limitations of non-stationarity
238   and infeasible subgoals generation in HRL. Finally, we analyze the HPO objective and gradient, and
239   provide the final algorithm.

392   In our empirical analysis, we ask the following questions:
393   (1) How well does HPO perform against baselines?: How well does HPO perform in complex
394
      robotics control tasks against prior hierarchical and non-hierarchical baselines?
      (2) Does HPO mitigate HRL limitations?: How well does HPO mitigate the issues of non-
395
      stationarity and infeasible subgoal generation in HRL?
396
      (3) What is the impact of our design decisions on the overall performance?: Can we concretely
397   justify our design choices through extensive ablation analysis?
398
399   Task details: Here, we explain the experimental setup and test beds for comparing HPO against
400
      baselines. We assess HPO on four robotic navigation and manipulation environments: (i) maze
      navigation, (ii) pick and place (Andrychowicz et al., 2017), (iii) push, and (iv) franka kitchen
401
      environment (Gupta et al., 2019). These are formulated as sparse reward scenarios, where the agent
402
      is only rewarded when it comes within a δ distance of the goal. Due to this, these environments are
403   hard where the agent must extensively explore the environment before coming across any rewards.
404   As an example: in franka kitchen task, the agent only receives a sparse reward after achieving the
405   final goal (e.g. successfully opening the microwave and then turning on the gas knob).
406
      Environment details: Since in our goal-conditioned RL setting, the final goals are randomly gener-
407
      ated, and this further increases the task complexity. As empirically demonstrated later, these chal-
408
      lenges prevent previous baselines from performing well, making these environments ideal test beds
409   for evaluating the advantages of efficient hierarchical preference-based hierarchical policy learning.
410   Unless otherwise stated, we maintain consistency across all baselines to ensure fair comparisons.
411   Finally, for the more complex sparse reward tasks such as pick and place, push, and franka kitchen,
412   we assume access to one human demonstration and incorporate an imitation learning objective at
413   the lower level to accelerate learning. However, we do not use demonstrations in the maze task.
414   However, we apply the same assumption consistently across all baselines to ensure fairness. Further
415   implementation and environment details are provided in Appendix A.3 and A.5, and the implemen-
416   tation code is provided in the supplementary.

525   6     C ONCLUSION
526
527   In this work, we introduce Hierarchical Preference Optimization HPO, a novel hierarchical approach
528
      that employs primitive-regularized direct preference optimization DPO to mitigate the issues of non-
      stationarity and infeasible generation in HRL. HPO employs token-level DPO to efficiently learn
529
      higher level policy using preference data, and RL to learn the lower level primitive policy. Since
530
      the DPO formulation avoids the dependence on changing lower level primitive, it mitigates non-
531   stationarity. We formulate HRL as a bi-level optimization objective to insure that the higher level
532   policy generates feasible subgoals, and avoids degenerate solutions. Based on strong empirical
533   results, We believe that HPO is an important step towards learning effective control policies for
534   solving complex sparse robotics scenarios.

826   A.5.1    M AZE NAVIGATION TASK
827
828   In this environment, a 7-DOF robotic arm gripper must navigate through randomly generated four-
829
      room mazes. The gripper remains closed, and both the walls and gates are randomly placed. The
      table is divided into a rectangular W × H grid, with vertical and horizontal wall positions WP and
830
      HP selected randomly from the ranges (1, W − 2) and (1, H − 2), respectively. In this four-room
831
      setup, gate positions are also randomly chosen from (1, WP − 1), (WP + 1, W − 2), (1, HP − 1),
832   and (HP + 1, H − 2). The gripper’s height remains fixed at table height, and it must move through
833   the maze to reach the goal, marked by a red sphere.
834
835
      For both higher and lower-level policies, unless stated otherwise, the environment consists of con-
      tinuous state and action spaces. The state is encoded as a vector [p, M], where p represents the
836
      gripper’s current position, and M is the sparse maze representation. The input to the higher-level
837
      policy is a concatenated vector [p, M, g], with g representing the goal position, while the lower-level
838   policy input is [p, M, sg ], where sg is the subgoal provided by the higher-level policy. The current
839   position of the gripper is treated as the current achieved goal. The sparse maze array M is a 2D
840   one-hot vector, where walls are denoted by a value of 1 and open spaces by 0.
841
      In our experiments, the sizes of p and M are set to 3 and 110, respectively. The higher-level policy
842
      predicts the subgoal sg , so its action space aligns with the goal space of the lower-level primitive.
843
      The lower-level primitive’s action, a, executed in the environment, is a 4-dimensional vector, where
844   each dimension ai ∈ [0, 1]. The first three dimensions adjust the gripper’s position, while the fourth
845   controls the gripper itself: 0 indicates fully closed, 0.5 means half-closed, and 1 means fully open.

848   A.5.2    P ICK AND PLACE AND P USH E NVIRONMENTS
849
      In the pick-and-place environment, a 7-DOF robotic arm gripper is tasked with picking up a square
850
      block and placing it at a designated goal position slightly above the table surface. This complex
851
      task involves navigating the gripper to the block, closing it to grasp the block, and then transporting
852   the block to the target goal. In the push environment, the gripper must push a square block towards
853   the goal position. The state is represented by the vector [p, o, q, e], where p is the gripper’s current
854   position, o is the block’s position on the table, q is the relative position of the block to the gripper,
855   and e contains the linear and angular velocities of both the gripper and the block.
856
      The higher-level policy input is the concatenated vector [p, o, q, e, g], where g denotes the target
857
      goal position, while the lower-level policy input is [p, o, q, e, sg ], with sg being the subgoal provided
858   by the higher-level policy. The current position of the block is treated as the achieved goal. In our
859   experiments, the dimensions for p, o, q, and e are set to 3, 3, 3, and 11, respectively. The higher-level
860   policy predicts the subgoal sg , so the action and goal space dimensions align. The lower-level action
861   a is a 4-dimensional vector, where each dimension ai falls within the range [0, 1]. The first three
862   dimensions adjust the gripper’s position, and the fourth controls the gripper itself (0 for closed, 1 for
863   open). During training, the block and goal positions are randomly generated, with the block always
      starting on the table and the goal placed above the table at a fixed height.
