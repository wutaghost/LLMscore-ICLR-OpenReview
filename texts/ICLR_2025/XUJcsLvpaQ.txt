                                                  A BSTRACT
011
               Estimating potential outcomes for treatments over time based on observational data
012
               is important for personalized decision-making in medicine. Yet, existing neural
013
               methods for this task either (1) do not perform proper adjustments for time-varying
014            confounders, or (2) have a problematic adjustment strategy. In order to address
015            both limitations, we introduce the G-transformer (GT). Our GT is a novel, neural
016            end-to-end model which adjusts for time-varying confounders in order to estimate
017            conditional average potential outcomes (CAPOs) over time. Specifically, our GT is
018            the first neural model to perform fully regression-based iterative G-computation
019            for CAPOs in the time-varying setting. We evaluate the effectiveness of our GT
020            across various experiments. In sum, this work represents a significant step towards
021            personalized decision-making from electronic health records.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Causal machine learning has recently garnered significant attention with the aim to personalize
027   treatment decisions in medicine (Feuerriegel et al., 2024). Here, an important task is to estimate
028
      conditional average potential outcomes (CAPOs) from observational data over time (see Fig. 1).
      Recently, such data has become prominent in medicine due to the growing prevalence of electronic
029
      health records (EHRs) (Allam et al., 2021; Bica et al., 2021) and wearable devices (Battalio et al.,
030
      2021; Murray et al., 2016).
031
032   Several neural methods have been developed for estimating CAPOs over time. However, existing
033   methods suffer from one of two possible limitations (see Table 1): 1 Methods without proper
034
      adjustments for time-varying confounding (Bica et al., 2020; Melnychuk et al., 2022; Seedat
      et al., 2022) exhibit significant bias, as they do not target the correct estimand. Hence, these
035
      methods have irreducible estimation errors irrespective of the amount of available data, which renders
036
      them unsuitable for medical applications. 2 Existing methods that perform proper time-varying
037   adjustments (Li et al., 2021; Lim et al., 2018) have a problematic adjustment strategy. Here,
038   the causal adjustments are based on the estimation of either the distributions of all time-varying
039   covariates, or on inverse propensity weighting at several time steps in the future. While the former is
040   impracticable when granular patient information is available, the latter suffers from strong overlap
041   violations in the time-varying setting. To the best of our knowledge, there is no method that can
042   address both 1 and 2 .
043   To fill the above research gap, we propose the G-transformer (GT), a novel, neural end-to-end
044   transformer that overcomes both limitations of existing methods. Our GT builds upon G-computation
045   (Bang & Robins, 2005; Robins & Hernán, 2009). However, unlike existing neural models that
046   perform G-computation (Li et al., 2021), our GT is based on an iterative regression scheme and does
047   not require estimating any probability distribution. As a result, our GT has two clear strengths: it
048   performs 1 proper adjustments for time-varying confounding, and it is 2 fully regression-based
049   with low-variance pseudo-outcomes.
050   Our contributions are three-fold:1 (1) We introduce the first neural end-to-end method for estimating
051   CAPOs over time with 1 proper adjustments for time-varying confounding, while 2 avoiding

      Identifiability: In order to estimate the causal quantity in Eq. (1) from observational data, we
150   make the following identifiability assumptions (Robins & Hernán, 2009; Robins et al., 2000) that
151   are standard in the literature (Bica et al., 2020; Li et al., 2021; Lim et al., 2018; Melnychuk et al.,
152   2022; Seedat et al., 2022): (1) Consistency: For an observed sequence of treatments Āt = āt , the
153   observed outcome Yt+1 equals the corresponding potential outcome Yt+1 [āt ]. (2) Positivity: For
154   any history H̄t = h̄t that has non-zero probability P(H̄t = h̄t ) > 0, there is a positive probability
155   P(At = at | H̄t = h̄t ) > 0 of receiving any treatment At = at , where at ∈ {0, 1}da . (3) Sequential
156   ignorability: Given a history H̄t = h̄t , the treatment At is independent of the potential outcome
157   Yt+δ [at:t+δ−1 ], that is, At ⊥ Yt+δ [at:t+δ−1 ] | H̄t = h̄t for all at:t+δ−1 ∈ {0, 1}δ×da .

161   or by using propensity score clipping. Further, with growing amounts of observational data, this
      becomes less of a restriction. Finally, relaxations of (iii) ignorability are typically studies in sensitivity

390   4.4   A DVANTAGES OVER EXISTING APPROACHES
391
392
      In the following, we explain the differences of our GT compared to (i) CT (Melnychuk et al., 2022)
      and (ii) G-Net (Li et al., 2021), and (iii) RMSNs (Lim et al., 2018). Importantly, our method has an
393
      entirely different learning algorithm that allows for proper adjustments in an end-to-end approach
394
      through iterative regressions.
395
396   Our GT vastly differs from CT (Melnychuk et al., 2022). Recall that CT does not perform proper
397   adjustments for time-varying confounding. In particular, CT targets E[Yt+τ | Ht = ht , At:t+τ =
398
      at:t+τ ], which is not the CAPO (Frauen et al., 2024). Hence, it targets an incorrect estimand, leading
      to irreducible bias. Therefore, deploying it to medical scenarios would be irresponsible. In contrast,
399
      our GT leverages iterative regression based on the G-computation to correctly target the CAPO
400
      (Prop. 2). To achieve this, we propose a new generation-learning approach inside our GT.
401
402   Our GT is also vastly different from G-Net (Li et al., 2021). In order to estimate a τ -step-ahead
403   CAPO, G-Net requires (i) a dy -dimensional regression as well as estimating the entire distribution of
404
      a (τ − 1) × (dy + dx )-dimensional confounding variable. That is, it needs to estimate all moments
      of a high-dimensional random variable. In contrast, our GT only requires τ regressions of a dy -
405
      dimensional outcome and, hence, only needs to estimate the first moment of a much lower dimensional
406
      random variable. Compared to G-Net, our estimation strategy is unproblematic as it does not fit
407   unnecessary nuisance. We provide a detailed comparison in Supplement F.
408
409   Finally, RMSNs (Lim et al., 2018) also rely on pseudo-outcome regressions. However, their pseudo-
410
      outcomes are constructed via inverse propensity weighting, which leads to pseudo-outcomes with
      larger variance than ours:
411
412
413   Proposition 3. Pseudo-outcomes constructed via inverse propensity weighting have larger variance
414   than pseudo-outcomes in our G-transformer.
415
416   Proof. See Supplement E.

758   Estimating CAPOs in the static setting: Extensive work on estimating potential outcomes focuses
759   on the static setting (e.g., Alaa & van der Schaar, 2017; Frauen et al., 2023b; Johansson et al., 2016;
760   Louizos et al., 2017; Melnychuk et al., 2023; Yoon et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2020)). However,
761   observational data such as electronic health records (EHRs) in clinical settings are typically measured
762   over time (Allam et al., 2021; Bica et al., 2021). Additionally, treatments are rarely applied all at
763   once but rather sequentially over time (Apperloo et al., 2024). Therefore, the underlying assumption
764   of these methods prohibitive and does not properly reflect medical reality. Hence, static methods
765   are not tailored to accurately estimate potential outcomes when (i) time series data is observed and
766
      (ii) multiple treatments in the future are of interest.
767   Additional literature on estimating CAPOs over time: There are some non-parametric methods for
768   this task (Schulam & Saria, 2017; Soleimani et al., 2017; Xu et al., 2016), yet these suffer from
769   poor scalability and have limited flexibility regarding the outcome distribution, the dimension of
770   the outcomes, and static covariate data; because of that, we do not explore non-parametric methods
771   further but focus on neural methods instead.3
772   Survival analysis: Some works in survival analysis (Andersen & Perme, 2010; Andersen et al.,
773   2017; Su et al., 2022) employ pseudo-outcomes, which is similar to our approach. However, these
774   works are different in that they are aimed at survival outcomes and not CAPOs for sequences of
775   treatments. Further, they do not consider neural networks as estimators. Additionally, (Andersen
776   et al., 2017) only considers a single, static treatment, and (Andersen & Perme, 2010) only uses
777   linear estimators. Finally, (Su et al., 2022) focuses on average causal effects and is therefore not
778
      applicable to personalized medicine.

      outcomes, whereas Q-learning is rooted in reinforcement learning to derive policies that maximize
      long-term rewards. We show more details on the two in the following:

      B    D ISCUSSION ON E STIMATING O UTCOMES FOR S EQUENCES OF
865
866
           T REATMENTS IN M EDICAL S CENARIOS
867
      In this study, we present a novel neural network, the G-transformer, for estimating conditional average
868
      potential outcomes (CAPOs) from observational data such as electronic health records (EHRs). Our
869
      GT addresses a crucial question in personalized medicine: “What would the outcome be for patient X
870   if they were administered treatments A, B, and C sequentially over the next 5 days, given their unique
871   clinical history?” Unlike many existing methods that focus on static or single-point interventions
872   (Alaa & van der Schaar, 2017; Johansson et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2020), our method is specifically
873   designed to handle the sequential nature of treatments in medical practice – a feature that is both
874   realistic and necessary, as treatments are rarely applied all at once but rather sequentially over time
875   (Apperloo et al., 2024). With the growing availability of large-scale observational data from EHRs
876   (Allam et al., 2021; Feuerriegel et al., 2024; Bica et al., 2021) and wearable devices (Battalio et al.,
877   2021), there is an increasing need for robust methods that estimate the effect of multiple treatments,
878
      given the individual patient history.
879   Our framework builds on three key assumptions: (i) consistency, (ii) positivity, and (iii) sequential
880   ignorability (see Section 3). These assumptions are the standard assumptions for estimating CAPOs
881   over time (Bica et al., 2020; Li et al., 2021; Melnychuk et al., 2022; Seedat et al., 2022). Notably,
882   compared to other methods that rely on even stricter assumptions, such as additional Markov or
883   independence assumptions (Özyurt et al., 2021), our assumptions are less restrictive. Furthermore,
884
      these assumptions are the dynamic analogues of the standard causal inference assumptions in static
      settings (Alaa & van der Schaar, 2017; Muandet et al., 2021; Johansson et al., 2016). Importantly,
885
      methods for the static setting implicitly impose unrealistic assumption that treatments occur only once
886
      and that covariates and outcomes remain static over time. Such limitations can introduce significant
887   bias in sequential decision-making contexts. In contrast, our approach models the time-varying nature
888   of clinical interventions and patient evolution, making it less restrictive and far more aligned with
889   real-world medical scenarios.
890
      Further, we argue that these assumptions are both plausible and practical in medical applications.
891
      First, consistency is generally satisfied as long as EHR data is accurately and systematically recorded.
892
      Second, positivity can be ensured through thoughtful data pre-processing, such as filtering obser-
893   vations or applying propensity clipping. Additionally, as the scale of observational datasets grows,
894   this assumption becomes less restrictive. Third, the sequential ignorability assumption is a standard
895   assumption in epidemiology (Little & Rubin, 2000), and studies in digital health interventions may
896   satisfy this assumption by design. Furthermore, advances in sensitivity analysis (Frauen et al., 2023b;
897   Oprescu et al., 2023) and partial identification frameworks (Duarte et al., 2023) offer complementary
898   pathways to relax this assumption. That is, these literature streams are orthogonal to our work. In
899   practice, our GT thus integrates into established workflows that include point estimation, uncertainty
900   quantification, and sensitivity analysis.
901   From a practical perspective, our GT addresses key challenges in estimating CAPOs for sequences of
902   treatments. Specifically, our GT provides a neural end-to-end solution that adjusts for time-varying
903   confounding. On top, it neither relies on large-variance pseudo-outcomes (Prop. 3) nor on estimating
904   high-dimensional probability distributions. Therefore, we are convinced that our GT is an important
905   step towards reliable personalized medicine.

1192   which is leveraged by RMSNs (Lim et al., 2018).

       J   A RCHITECTURE OF G- TRANSFORMER
1567
1568
       In the following, we provide details on the architecture of our GT.
1569
1570   Multi-input transformer: The multi-input transformer as the backbone of our GT is motivated by
1571   (Melnychuk et al., 2022), which develops an architecture that is tailored for the types of data that
1572
       are typically available in medical scenarios: (i) outcomes Y¯t ∈ Rdy ×t , covariates X̄t ∈ Rdx ×t , and
       treatments Āt ∈ {0, 1}da ×t . In particular, their proposed transformer model consists of three separate
1573
       sub-transformers, where each sub-transformer performs multi-headed self-attention mechanisms on
1574
       one particular data input. Further, these sub-transformers are connected with each other through
1575   in-between cross-attention mechanisms, ensuring that information is exchanged. Therefore, we build
1576   on this idea as the backbone of our GT, as we detail below.
1577
1578
       Our multi-input transformer zθ (·) consists of three sub-transformer models zθk (·), k = 1, 2, 3, where
1579
