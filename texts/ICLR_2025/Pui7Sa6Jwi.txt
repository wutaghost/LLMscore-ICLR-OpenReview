011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Visual offline RL aims to learn an optimal policy for visual domains, solely from
014            the pre-collected dataset comprised of actions taken on visual observations. Prior
015            works on visual RL typically learn a dynamics model by extracting a latent state
016
               representation. However, the learned representation would contain factors irrele-
               vant to control when there are distractions in the visual observations. These nui-
017
               sance factors introduced by the distraction further exacerbates the difficulties of
018
               learning a good policy in the offline RL setting. In this work, we formalize the
019            visual offline RL setting as a Partially Observable Markov Decision Process with
020            exogenous variables (ExoPOMDP) and identify these problems with previous ap-
021            proaches under an information-theoretic lens. To overcome these challenges, we
022            propose CLEAR (Controllable Latent State ExtrActoR) for visual offline RL,
023            which learns the dynamics model of a succinct agent-centric state representa-
024            tion that is consistent with the underlying ExoPOMDP. We empirically demon-
025            strate that CLEAR is able to outperform baselines on the DeepMind Control Suite
026            with various types of distractions and perform consistently well across these dis-
027
               tractions. We further provide qualitative analysis on the results showing that our
               approach successfully disentangles the distraction factors from the agent-centric
028
               state representation.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Offline Reinforcement Learning (Offline RL) (Lange et al., 2012; Levine et al., 2020) aims to learn
034
      policies solely from a fixed dataset of trajectories without any further access to the environment.
035
      While many offline RL algorithms have been proposed (Fujimoto et al., 2019; Kumar et al., 2020;
036   Fujimoto & Gu, 2021), much of the recent progress has been limited to datasets which assume access
037   to the underlying state of the environment (Fu et al., 2020). However, many datasets collected from
038   real-world scenarios (e.g. autonomous driving (Yu et al., 2020) and robotics (Vuong et al., 2023))
039   consists of visual observations rather than state information. In these partially observable settings,
040   extracting representations which capture the underlying state of the environment becomes critical to
041   learn good policies.
042   However, inferring the ground-truth state from a sequence of image observations and learning a
043   good offline RL policy is non-trivial. This is due to the fact that visual observations often contain
044   complex distractions (e.g. background screens playing video advertisements or birds flying in the
045   sky) which are irrelevant to the control task at hand (illustrated in Figure 1). The generalization
046   challenges of offline RL (Fujimoto et al., 2019; Kumar et al., 2019) are further exacerbated by the
047   presence of these distractions since they may spuriously correlate with the task. Thus, one of the
048   keys to successful visual offline RL is to learn succinct agent-centric representations that capture the
049   ground-truth state which are free from these distractions.
050   To address the challenge of partial observability in visual RL, one of the standard approaches
051   is to learn the latent state dynamics model by maximizing the likelihood of the observed trajec-
052   tory (Hafner et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2020; Hafner et al., 2020; Hwang et al., 2023). However, as
053   we show through the experiments, we find that the learned representations still contain superfluous
      information irrelevant to control in the presence of distractions.

      In this work, we provide an information-theoretic framework for addressing this problem and learn-
      ing distraction-free representations. We start by formalizing the visual RL problem as a Partially
068
      Observable Markov Decision Process with exogenous variables (ExoPOMDP). Under ExoPOMDP,
069
      we identify the main reasons why a latent state representation extracted by learning a single dynam-
070   ics model, despite having an information bottleneck term, cannot be minimal when observations
071   contain distractions. Specifically, previous approaches (Hafner et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2020) maxi-
072   mize the lower bound of an objective which maximizes predictive information while imposing the
073   Markov property under an information-theoretic lens (Hwang et al., 2023). We show that in Ex-
074   oPOMDPs, the learned representations may still contain superfluous information irrelevant for con-
075   trol. To overcome these shortcomings of previous approaches, we propose CLEAR (Controllable
076   Latent State ExtrActoR), which models both the agent-centric latent state dynamics as well as the
077   distractions through separate encoders whose representations are disentangled. To train CLEAR,
078
      we introduce a regularized objective which additionally encourages the learned agent-centric state
      representation to be influenced or controlled by actions. Through this information-theoretic per-
079
      spective, CLEAR provides a principled representation learning procedure that is consistent with the
080
      underlying ExoPOMDP.
081
082   Finally, we conduct experiments on a series of datasets with various degrees of distractions on the
083   DeepMind Control Suite (Tassa et al., 2018), closely following the settings in (Lu et al., 2023;
084
      Islam et al., 2023). We show empirically that our method performs consistently well across these
      distractions and outperforms baselines especially for more dynamic distractions. We further provide
085
      qualitative results showing that our approach successfully disentangles the distraction variables from
086
      agent-centric ones.

      Latent Dynamics Models To address the challenge of partial observability in visual RL, prior
      works learn latent variable models by maximizing the lower-bound of the log-likelihood of the ob-
290
      served trajectory that recovers the latent state dynamics (Hafner et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2020; Hafner
291
      et al., 2020; Hwang et al., 2023). The learned model then can be applied to extract representa-
292   tion for model-free RL (Lee et al., 2020; Hwang et al., 2023), planning (Hafner et al., 2019), and
293   model-based RL (Hafner et al., 2020). However, as we have seen in Section 2.2, the representa-
294   tions extracted through this approach can include superfluous information which is problematic for
295   learning good policies in offline RL.
296
297   Task-Relevant Representations To eliminate the distraction (superfluous information) from the
298   representation, prior works have incorporated the concept of task-relevance. DRIBO (Fan & Li,
299   2022) extracts task-relevant representation via the multi-view information bottleneck (Federici et al.,
300   2020), obtaining two views by data augmentation. However, generating two views by data augmen-
301   tation does not guarantee the mutual redundancy assumption necessary for their method. TiA (Fu
302
      et al., 2021) takes a similar approach to ours and attempts to learn two sets of representations. How-
      ever, TiA uses two identical encoders and uses reward to differentiate between task-relevant and
303
      task-irrelevant feature. Using reward to identify task-relevance is problematic since rewards may be
304
      sparse and/or dependent only on the subset of the agent state. Denoised MDP (Wang et al., 2022)
305   goes one step further by modeling three sets of representations and categorising features based on its
306   task-relevance and controllability. However, it still only uses reward and makes the problem under-
307   determined since they rely solely on the reward to separate three sets of representations. RePo (Zhu
308   et al., 2023) avoids observation reconstruction by only predicting the reward to obtain task-relevant
309   representations, and thus inherits the same problems faced by TIA in utilizing reward. This issue
310   has been studied in detail in the ablation studies in (Hafner et al., 2020).
311
312   Control-Relevant Representations Finally, another line of work uses the notion of control-
313   relevance to remove the distractions. The single-step inverse dynamics (predicting action at time
314   t given observations at time t and t + 1) has been empirically observed to be effective to learn a
315
      representation for control (Agrawal et al., 2016; Pan et al., 2022; Brandfonbrener et al., 2023; Paster
      et al., 2021). Intuitively, the representation only needs to capture features that are necessary to pre-
316
      dict the action given the transition. However, Rakelly et al. (2021) showed that the representations
317
      learned via inverse dynamics is not sufficient for control. To resolve this issue, the multi-step inverse
318   dynamics (predicting action at time t given observations at time t and t + k where k is a hyperparam-
319   eter) has been proposed (Efroni et al., 2022; Lamb et al., 2023). ACRO (Islam et al., 2023) utilizes
320   the multi-step inverse dynamics in the context of offline RL. InfoGating (Tomar et al., 2023) extends
321   ACRO by learning a sparse mask to mask out the irrelevant part of visual observation. However, the
322   multi-step inverse dynamics does not fully resolve the problem (Levine et al., 2024) and is an inher-
323   ently ill-posed problem since there are multiple actions that can achieve the same transition. Unlike
      previous approaches, we derive the single-step inverse dynamics from an information-theoretic per-

      spective and use it as a regularization term instead of the main objective. InfoPower (Bharadhwaj
325   et al., 2022) learns a latent state dynamics and avoids reconstruction by using contrastive loss while
326   regularizing the model with inverse dynamics prediction. However, using contrastive loss tends to
327   perform poorly in practice when compared to the reconstruction loss (Hafner et al., 2020). Similar
328   to our method, Iso-Dream (Pan et al., 2022) utilizes an additional encoder and regularizes its model
329   using inverse dynamics. However, their approach uses three latent variables with one regularization
330   term. This makes the model underspecified and prone to local optimas which can negatively affect
331   offline RL performance as we show in the experiments, similar to Denoised-MDPs (Wang et al.,
332   2022). On the other hand, we derive our method in a principled manner using mutual information
333
      to reflect the underlying ExoPOMDP resulting in a more general method in the sense that ours is
      simpler and we can employ other types of bounds.

      5       E XPERIMENTS
337   Datasets To validate the effectiveness of CLEAR against various levels of distractions, we evaluate
338   our algorithm on the DeepMind Control Suite (Tassa et al., 2018), which is a standard benchmark
339   in visual offline RL (Lu et al., 2023; Islam et al., 2023). Since v-d4rl (Lu et al., 2023) only provides
340   datasets for image observations with static backgrounds, we construct our own set of datasets which
341   also includes dynamic distractions.
342   For each dataset, we generate four levels of varying difficulties of distractions by adjusting the types
343   of distractions present in the observation. The easy level has a static background which is used in
344   the original observations (Clean). For the medium level, we introduce correlated distractions by
345   using, as the background, a single video which repeats for every episode (SV) and four videos which
346   change every episode (MV). Lastly, for the hard level, we make a 2 × 2 grid where we put the agent
347   that we can control on the top-left of the grid. For the rest of the grid, we put similar agents which
348   are controlled by a random uniform policy (2 × 2). Figure 1 shows a sample of the Cheetah-Run
349
      dataset with Video distraction and Walker-Walk dataset with 2 × 2 Grid distractions. We provide
                                                                                                             2
350   more details about the dataset construction as well as some samples of the dataset in Appendix G.
351   We evaluate on three sets of environments, namely Hopper-Hop, Walker-Walk, and Cheetah-Run.
352   In order to ensure a fair comparison, we collect medium-expert datasets which have been shown to
353   be an appropriate level for the baselines to perform well (Lu et al., 2023).
354
355   Baselines Following the prior work (Lu et al., 2023), we use TD3+BC (Fujimoto & Gu, 2021)
356   as the offline RL algorithm to evaluate the learned representations for all baselines as well as for
357   CLEAR. We include SLAC (Lee et al., 2020), TiA (Fu et al., 2021), InfoPower (Bharadhwaj
358   et al., 2022), Iso-Dream (Pan et al., 2022), Denoised MDP (Den-MDP) (Wang et al., 2022), and
359   RePo (Zhu et al., 2023) as baselines which learn the latent state dynamics. Although TiA, Iso-
360   Dream, Den-MDP, and RePo were originally proposed as model-based methods, we can use the
361
      variational posterior to extract representations similar to what was done in (Wang et al., 2022).
      Additionally, we include DrQ-v2 (Yarats et al., 2022), ACRO (Islam et al., 2023), and InfoGat-
362
      ing (Tomar et al., 2023). These additional methods do not learn the latent state dynamics but instead
363
      take a stack of consecutive frames as a state. Lastly, we train TD3+BC using the ground-truth
364   state as an upper-bound on the performance to normalize the score. A score of 100 means that it
365   performs as good as using the ground-truth state. We provide further implementation details and
366   hyperparameters in Appendix H.

461
462
      To show how informative the learned state representation is about the ground-truth state, we perform
      linear regression to predict the ground-truth state using the pretrained frozen encoder. In addition
463
      to the original 400k timestep dataset for training, we collect an additional 100k timesteps as the
464
      validation set. Table 2 shows the average mean squared error (MSE) of predicting the ground-truth
465   state on the validation set.
466
467   The result is consistent with our hypothesis. While SLAC predicts the ground-truth state well on
468
      the Clean setup, its prediction gets worse as distractions are introduced in the observation hinting
      the negative effect of superfluous information. CLEAR reliably has low MSE across environments
469
      and distractions. Additionally, the case where SLAC, Iso-Dream, ACRO, and InfoGating have high
470
      average normalized score in Table 1 translates to its representation having low MSE hinting the
471   representation has high information regarding the ground-truth state. However, the reverse is not
472   true (i.e. low MSE does not necessarily translate to good offline RL performance) as can be seen
473   in SLAC 2 × 2 Grid in Hopper and Cheetah, Den-MDP in Hopper and Walker, and Iso-Dream in
474   Hopper. Thus, it further supports our claim that superfluous information (i.e. information about
475   distractions) makes learning good policies in offline RL more difficult.

485   Furthermore, we find that the inverse dynamics regularization term helps stabilize the training pro-
      cedure and improve overall performance.

514   6    C ONCLUSION
515
516   In this work, we presented CLEAR, which takes an information-theoretic approach to learning suc-
517   cinct agent-centric representations for visual offline RL. We introduced ExoPOMDPs and identified
518   the shortcomings of previous approaches which learn latent state dynamics, namely the existence of
519   superfluous information in the learned representations. CLEAR mitigates these issues through a sep-
520   arate encoder for learning the agent-centric and exogenous representations, trained by a regularized
521   objective derived from the graphical model of the ExoPOMDP. We quantitatively and qualitatively
522   validated our approach on the DeepMind Control Suite with varying levels of distractions. CLEAR
523
      outperformed previous baselines and demonstrated its ability to disentangle the agent-centric repre-
      sentations from the distraction factors, even with dynamic distractions.

1211   Table 10 shows the hyperparameters that we use for the reported score in the main experiments.
1212   SLAC (Lee et al., 2020) models a single latent variable and optimizes its ELBO with additional
1213   reward prediction. We follow the implementation of SLAC by factorizing the variable as explained
1214   in the Appendix B of the paper. The latent variables have 32 and 256 dimensions for z1 and z2 ,
1215   respectively. The decoder qϕ (ot ∣ŝt ) is parameterized as an independent Gaussian for each pixel
1216   whose variance is fixed to a constant. We search the variance between {0.4, 0.1, 0.04}.
1217   TiA (Fu et al., 2021) models two latent variables and regularize it via reward prediction. However,
1218   their method assumes both latent variables are controllable (i.e. affected by action). Additionally,
1219   reward prediction makes the learned representation to be task-dependent and is problematic since
1220   reward may be sparse or depends only on the subset of the agent state. TiA has two hyperparameters,
1221   namely λRadv which controls the adversarial reward regularizer and λOs which controls distractor-
1222   model-only reconstruction. We refer readers to (Fu et al., 2021) for the details of the objective.
1223   We follow the original implementation which parameterizes both encoders as RSSM (Hafner et al.,
1224
       2019). We pick 30 and 200 dimensions for the stochastic and deterministic variables, respectively.
       We search λRadv between {20k, 30k} and λOs between {0.25, 1.5, 2.0}.
1225
1226   DenoisedMDP (Wang et al., 2022) models multiple latent variables based on its controllability
1227   and task relevance. We use the official implementation which uses the Figure 2b variant of the
1228   paper. It has two hyperparameters, α which weights the KL divergence of the controllable repre-
1229   sentation and β which weights the KL divergence of the rest. We search over {1., 2.} for α and
1230
       {1., 0.5, 0.25, 0.125} for β.
1231   Iso-Dream (Pan et al., 2022) models three latent variables with a single regularization (inverse
1232   dynamics prediction) on the controllable representation. We set all KL weights to 1, following the
1233   paper’s reported hyperparameters. However, we tuned the image decoder variance and found the
1234   optimal hyperparameters to match those used in CLEAR (ours).
1235   RePo (Zhu et al., 2023) models a single latent variable and avoids observation reconstruction al-
1236   together. Instead, it reconstructs reward to extract task-relevant information. The issue is similar
1237   with TiA since reward may be sparse or depends only on the subset of the agent state. Similar to
1238   TiA, we use RSSM as the encoder with 30 and 200 dimensions for the stochastic and deterministic
1239   variables, respectively. In the original implementation, the weight between reward prediction and
1240   KL divergence is learned. However, we found that it does not perform well. Instead, we search over
       {1, 10 , 10 , 10 , 10 , 10 , 10 , 10 } for the KL weight and {1., 0.1, 0.04, 0.01} for the vari-
