010                                               A BSTRACT
011
012            In many real-world applications, data distributions are often subject to transla-
013            tion shifts caused by various factors such as changes in environmental conditions,
014            sensor settings, or shifts in data collection practices. These distribution shifts
015            pose a significant challenge for measuring the similarity between probability dis-
016
               tributions, particularly in tasks like domain adaptation or transfer learning. To
               address this issue, we introduce a new family of distances, relative-translation
017
               invariant Wasserstein distances (RWp ), to measure the similarity of two probability
018
               distributions under distribution shift. Generalizing it from the classical optimal
019            transport model, we show that RWp distances are also real distance metrics defined
020            on the quotient set Pp (Rn )/ ∼ and invariant to distribution translations, which
021            forms a family of new metric spaces. When p = 2, the RW2 distance enjoys more
022            exciting properties, including decomposability of the optimal transport model and
023            translation-invariance of the RW2 distance. Based on these properties, we show
024            that a distribution shift, measured by W2 distance, can be explained in the bias-
025            variance perspective. In addition, we propose two algorithms: one algorithm is a
026            two-stage optimization algorithm for computing the general case of RWp distance,
027
               and the other is a variant of the Sinkhorn algorithm, named RW2 Sinkhorn algo-
               rithm, for efficiently calculating RW2 distance, coupling solutions, as well as W2
028
               distance. We also provide the analysis of numerical stability and time complexity
029
               for the proposed algorithms. Finally, we validate the RWp distance metric and the
030            algorithm performance with two experiments. We conduct one numerical valida-
031            tion for the RW2 Sinkhorn algorithm and demonstrate the effectiveness of using
032            RWp under distribution shift for similar thunderstorm detection. The experimental
033            results report that our proposed algorithm significantly improves the computational
034            efficiency of Sinkhorn in practical applications, and the RWp distance is robust to
035            distribution translations.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
039   Optimal transport (OT) theory and Wasserstein distance (Peyré & Cuturi, 2020; Janati et al., 2020a;
040   Villani, 2009) provide a rigorous measurement of similarity between two probability distributions.
041   Numerous state-of-the-art machine learning applications are developed based on the OT formulation
042   and Wasserstein distances, including domain adaptation, score-based generative model, Wasserstein
043   generative adversarial networks, Fréchet inception distance (FID) score, Wasserstein auto-encoders,
044   distributionally robust Markov decision processes, distributionally robust regressions, graph neu-
045
      ral networks based objects tracking, etc (Shen et al., 2017; Pinheiro, 2017; Courty et al., 2017b;
      Damodaran et al., 2018; Courty et al., 2017a; Arjovsky et al., 2017; Heusel et al., 2017; Tolstikhin
046
      et al., 2017; Clement & Kroer, 2021; Shafieezadeh-Abadeh et al., 2015; Chen & Paschalidis, 2018;
047
      Yu et al., 2023; Sarlin et al., 2019). However, the classical Wasserstein distance has major limitations
048   in certain machine learning and computer vision applications. For example, a meteorologist often
049   focuses on identifying similar weather patterns in a large-scale geographical region Wang et al. (2023);
050   Roberts & Lean (2008); Dixon & Wiener (1993), where he/she cares more about the “shapes” of
051   weather events rather than their exact locations. The weather events are represented as images or point
052   clouds from the radar reflectivity map. Here the classical Wasserstein distance is not useful since the
053   relative location difference or relative translation between two very similar weather patterns will add
      to the Wasserstein distance value. Another example is the inevitable distribution shift in real-world

054
      datasets. A distribution shift may be introduced by sensor calibration error, environment changes
055   between train and test datasets, simulation to real-world (sim2real) deployment, etc. Motivated by
056   these practical use cases and the limitations of Wasserstein distances, we ask the following research
057   question:
058
      Can we find a new distance metric and a corresponding efficient algorithm to measure the similarity
059
      between probability distributions (and their supports) regardless of their relative translation?
060
061   To answer this research question, we introduce the relative translation optimal transport (ROT)
062   problem and the corresponding relative-translation invariant Wasserstein distance RWp . We then
063   focus on the general case result when p ∈ [1, ∞) and the quadratic case (p = 2) by identifying
064
      two exciting properties of the RW2 distance. We leverage these properties to design a variant of
      the Sinkhorn algorithm to compute RW2 distance, coupling solutions, as well as W2 distance. In
065
      addition, we provide analysis and numerical experiment results to demonstrate the effectiveness of
066
      the new RW2 distance against translation shifts. Finally, we show the scalability and practical usage
067   of the RW2 in a real-world meteorological application.
068
069   Contributions. The main contributions of this paper are highlighted as follows: (a) we introduce a
070   family of new similarity metrics, relative-translation invariant Wasserstein (RWp ) distances, which
071   are real distance metrics like the Wasserstein distance and invariant to the relative translation of
072   two distributions; (b) we identify two useful properties of the quadratic case RW2 to support our
073   algorithm design: decomposability of the ROT problem and translation-invariance of both the ROT
074
      problem solution and the resulted RW2 ; (c) we show the non-convexity of general ROT problem
      and propose a two-stage algorithm for computing the general RWp distances; and (d) we propose
075
      an efficient variant of Sinkhorn algorithm, named the RW2 Sinkhorn, for calculating RW2 distance,
076
      coupling solutions as well as W2 distance with significantly reduced computational complexity and
077   enhanced numerical stability. Empirically, we report promising performance from the proposed
078   RW2 distance when the relative translation is large, and the RW2 Sinkhorn algorithm in illustrative
079   numerical examples and a large-scale real-world task for similar weather detection. Figure 1 shows
080   our major findings in this work.

111   Related work. Optimal transport theory is a classical area of mathematics with strong connections
112
      to probability theory, diffusion processes and PDEs. Due to the vast literature, we refer readers to
      (Villani & Society, 2003; Ambrosio et al., 2005; Villani, 2009; Oll, 2014) for comprehensive reviews.
113
      Computational OT methods have been widely explored, including Greenkhorn algorithm (Altschuler
114
      et al., 2017), Network Simplex method (Peyré & Cuturi, 2020), Wasserstein gradient flow (Mokrov
115   et al., 2021; Fan et al., 2022), neural network approximation (Chen & Wang, 2023). Significant
116   research has also been conducted on Wasserstein distances, such as the sliced Wasserstein distance
117   (Nguyen & Ho, 2023; Mahey et al., 2023; Nguyen & Ho, 2022), Gromov-Wasserstein distance
118   (Sejourne et al., 2021; Le et al., 2022; Alvarez-Melis et al., 2019), etc. Other important topics include
119   Wasserstein barycenter (Guo et al., 2020; Vaskevicius & Chizat, 2023; Korotin et al., 2022; Lin et al.,
120   2020; Korotin et al., 2021) and unbalanced optimal transport (Nguyen et al., 2024; Chizat, 2017).
121   Among these foundational areas, information geometry (Amari, 2016; Liero et al., 2018; Janati et al.,
122   2020b) and the Wasserstein-Bures metric (Chen et al., 2015; Bhatia et al., 2019; Peyré & Cuturi,
123   2020; Malagò et al., 2018) are closely related to our work, as both provide tools for measuring
124   variances. However, it is important to note key differences. Unlike information geometry, which
125   typically employs measures such as Bregman divergence or statistical information, our approach
126   utilizes the energy transport cost as the primary metric. Additionally, while the Wasserstein-Bures
127   metric specifically focuses on Gaussian distributions and the W2 metric, our research extends to more
128   general distributions and considers broader classes of p-norm metrics, offering a more comprehensive
129
      framework for analysis.

135   2.1   O PTIMAL T RANSPORT T HEORY
136
137   The optimal transport theory focuses on finding the minimal-cost transport plans for moving one
138
      probability distribution to another probability distribution in a metric space. The core of this theory
      involves a cost function, denoted as c(x, y), alongside two probability distributions, µ(x) and ν(y).
139
      The optimal transport problem is to find the transport plans (coupling solutions) that minimize the cost
140
      of moving the distribution µ(x) to ν(y), under the cost function c(x, y). Although the cost function
141   can take any non-negative form, our focus will be on those derived from the p-norm, expressed as
142   ∥x − y∥pp for p ∈ [1, ∞), since the optimal transport problem is well-defined (Villani, 2009).
143

145   formulate the optimal transport problem as a functional optimization problem, detailed below:

254   3.2   RWp METRIC SPACES
255
      One advantage of this family of distances is that it defines a new family of metric spaces (Pp (Rn )/ ∼
256
      , RWp ). These spaces differ from the conventional metric spaces (Pp (Rn ), Wp ) (Villani, 2009), as
257   the distances here are solely influenced by the “shape” of the variances, independent of their means.
258
259   Classical Lp models show that the L1 norm exhibits enhanced robustness to outliers, making it more
260
      appropriate for noisy data applications (Jolliffe, 2002; Zou et al., 2004). In contrast, the L2 norm does
      not induce sparsity, thereby reducing its effectiveness in feature selection. Similarly, RW1 distance is
261
      anticipated to offer greater robustness in the presence of noise, whereas RW2 distance is expected to
262
      perform more balanced in cleaner datasets.

511   6   C ONCLUSIONS
512
      In this paper, we introduce a new family of distances, relative-translation invariant Wasserstein
513   (RWp ) distances, for measuring the pattern similarity between two probability distributions (and their
514   data supports). Generalizing from the classical optimal transport model, we show that the proposed
515   RWp distances are real distance metrics defined on the quotient set Pp (Rn )/ ∼ and invariant to the
516   translations. When p = 2, this distance enjoys more useful properties, including decomposability of
517   the ROT model and translation-invariance of coupling solutions and RW2 . Based on these properties,
518   we show a distribution shift, measured by W2 distance, which can be explained from the perspective of
519   bias-variance. In addition, we propose our algorithm for general RWp distances and RW2 Sinkhorn
520   algorithm, for efficiently calculating RW2 distance, coupling solutions, as well as W2 distance.
521
      We provide the analysis of numerical stability and time complexity for the proposed algorithms.
      Finally, we validate the RWp distance and the algorithm performance with illustrative and real-world
522
      experiments. The experimental results report that our proposed algorithm significantly improves the
523
      computational efficiency of Sinkhorn in practical applications with large translations, and the RW2
524   distance is robust to distribution translations.

       C.3   T HE OPTIMAL RELATIVE TRANSLATION
1243
1244   In the following, we show that the optimal relative translation is not always the same as the difference
1245   between the means of two distributions when p ̸= 2.
1246
       Assume the underlying space is in two-dimensional space and source and target distribution µ and
1247
       ν are formed by {x1 = (3, 0), x2 = (0, 0), x3 = (0, 3)} and {y1 = (−3, 0), y2 = (0, 0), y3 =
1248   (0, −3)} with equal masses, respectively.
1249
1250   Consider the case when p = 1. Since the mass center (centroid) of distribution µ and ν in terms
1251
       of L1 norm are µ̄ = (0, 0) and ν̄ = (0, 0), if we take their difference as a translation, we can get
1252   W1 (µ, ν) = (3+3+6)
                         3    = 4. However, this translation is not optimal, since when the translation
1253   s0 = (−3, −3), the total transport cost is W1 (µ + s0 , ν) = (3+3)

1254   optimal translation might not be the difference between the means of two distributions, when p ̸= 2.
