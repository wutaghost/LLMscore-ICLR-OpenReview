009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            System prompts, essential for guiding model outputs, play a pivotal role as large
012            language models proliferate across diverse applications. Despite their importance,
013            these prompts are highly vulnerable to injection attacks. Intuitively, adding defen-
014            sive prompts and implementing output filtering could offer strong protection, but
015            these defenses rely on direct access to the system prompt—a luxury increasingly
016            unavailable in today’s evolving prompt market and third-party defense scenarios,
017            where prompts must remain concealed and confidential. To address this pressing
018
               limitation, we introduce SurF (Surrogate-based Filtering), a novel approach that
               compensates for the lack of system prompt access by utilizing a surrogate prompt
019
               pool. Namely, we leverage the prompt pool as the surrogate of the system prompt.
020
               Once a potential leak from this pool is identified, the input is classified as harm-
021            ful, and the system resists generating a response. Experiments on various models,
022            including both offline and online LLM services, demonstrate SurF’s effectiveness
023            in reducing attack success rates. Furthermore, we evaluate the trade-off between
024            defense robustness and response consistency on natural inputs using a response-
025            following metric. Our findings indicate that while stronger defenses reduce attack
026            success, they may also degrade the quality of legitimate responses.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The advent of pretrained large language models (LLMs), such as BERT (Devlin, 2018), Llama
032   (Touvron et al., 2023; Meta, 2024), Vicuna (Chiang et al., 2023), and the GPT series (Brown, 2020;
033   Achiam et al., 2023), has dramatically transformed natural language processing. With the integration
034   of these models into services like ChatGPT, LLM-based applications have reached over 100 million
035
      users within eight months (Hu, 2023). Central to the success of these models is the concept of system
      prompts, carefully crafted sequences that guide LLMs to generate task-specific outputs. As LLM
036
      applications expand, platforms like Poe1 and the GPT Store1 , as well as the broader prompt market
037
      Promptbase1 and Prompti1 , have emerged, where system prompts are treated as valuable assets.
038
039   Despite the proprietary nature and the central role of system prompts in LLM services, these prompts
040   introduce a significant vulnerability: system prompt leakage attacks (Zhang & Ippolito, 2023). Un-
041
      like adversarial attacks (Wallace et al., 2019; Casper et al., 2023), which degrade model performance
      through optimized inputs, or jailbreak attacks (Zou et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023a), which seek to
042
      elicit prohibited outputs, system prompt leakage attacks target the core functionality of LLMs—the
043
      system prompt itself. As shown in the Figure 1 left part, by injecting unauthorized instructions into
044   the model, attackers can reverse-engineer and steal valuable system prompts, posing a severe risk
045   to intellectual property and confidentiality. These attacks are a critical subtype of prompt injection
046   attacks (Greshake et al., 2023; Toyer et al., 2023), where the goal is not limited to extracting the
047   system prompt but also to controlling or misdirecting the LLM’s output. Given their relevance, our
048   research focuses on developing robust defenses against these system prompt injection threats.
049
      To protect system prompts from injection attacks, an intuitive approach involves adding defensive
050   prompts and employing output filtering. Defensive prompts are designed to instruct the model to
051   avoid leaking sensitive system prompts, while output filtering examines the model’s responses for

088
089   Experiments are conducted on a variety of models, including the Vicuna series (Chiang et al., 2023),
090   Llama2 (Touvron et al., 2023), Llama3 (Meta, 2024) series, and GPT series (Brown, 2020; Achiam

092   of our proposed method. Results demonstrate a significant reduction in attack success rates across
093

      recognize the importance of not only considering the attack success rates but also examining re-
094
      sponse consistency for those natural, benign inputs. To explore the relationship between these two
095
      factors, we utilize a response-following metric to comprehensively assess various defense methods.
096   Our findings suggest that while stronger defenses significantly reduce attack success, they may also
097   lead to a degradation in the quality of legitimate responses.
098
099
      In summary, our main contributions can be summarized as follows: a) We propose SurF, a novel
      surrogate-based filtering approach that simulates prompt-output interactions to detect potential leaks
100
      and malicious inputs, without requiring direct access to the raw system prompts. Our proposed ap-
101
      proach is particularly suited for third-party defense scenarios, where raw system prompts must re-
102   main confidential and inaccessible. b) We conduct extensive experiments across various offline and
103   online LLM services, including Vicuna, Llama2, Llama3, and GPT series, as well as the online Poe
104   platform. Our results demonstrate that with the help of SurF, we can significantly reduce the success
105   rate of system prompt injection attacks. c) To evaluate the balance between defense robustness and
106   response quality, we introduce the use of a response-following metric. This allows us to assess not
107   only the effectiveness of the defense mechanisms in thwarting attacks, but also their impact on the
      consistency and quality of natural, benign responses.

      2     S AFEGUARDING SYSTEM PROMPT
109
110
      The primary goal of this study is to enhance defenses against system prompt leakage attacks in Large
111   Language Models (LLMs). In line with established practices in the computer security domain, we
112   first develop a specific threat model that addresses prompt leakage and then define our defense
113   objectives and evaluation metrics accordingly.
114
115   2.1   T HREAT M ODEL AND D EFENDING G OAL
116
117   Threat model. Suppose the generation task of the LLM service is handled by a server provider API,
118   fp . The API receives both a secret system prompt psys and a user-provided query q, which it passes
119   to a large language model for processing. Formally, this is expressed as fp (q) = LM(psys , q). A
120   prompt leakage attack occurs when an adversary submits a malicious query a, causing the model to
121
      reveal parts of the system prompt psys in its response fp (a), thus exposing sensitive information.
122   Defending goal. The goal of defending against prompt leakage attacks is to ensure that sensitive
123   system prompts remain secure while maintaining the model’s ability to provide accurate and mean-
124   ingful responses to legitimate inputs. An effective defense system must resist attacks by blocking
125   or neutralizing malicious queries without impairing the overall performance of the model. The key
126   challenge is balancing robust protection against leakage with preserving the natural and reliable
127
      functionality of the model, so benign queries can continue to receive high-quality responses.
128
129   2.2   E VALUATION METRICS
130
      Attack success rate. This metric measures the percentage of malicious queries that successfully
131   cause the model to reveal parts of the system prompt in the response. Formally, a prompt leakage
132   attack is considered successful if the response r = LM(psys , q) contains elements of secret system
133   prompt psys . To evaluate this, we use exact-match (EXC) and approx-match (APP) criteria. EXC
134   determines whether every sentence in the system prompt psys appears exactly in the response r:
135
                         EXC(psys , r) = 1[∀ sentence s of psys : s is a substring of r].                (1)
136
137   If any part of the system prompt exactly matches the response, the model has fully leaked the prompt,
138   indicating a complete failure. APP provides a more relaxed evaluation by using Rouge-L (Lin,
139   2004) recall to compute the longest common subsequence (LCS) between the system prompt and
140   the response. Formally, it measures the proportion of the system prompt that appears in the response:
141                                            |LCS(tokens(psys ), tokens(r))|
142                       APP(psys , r) = 1[                                   ≥ 90%],                   (2)
                                                      |tokens(psys )|
143
144   where a threshold of 90% is employed, same as in Zhang&Ippolito (2023) meaning that if 90% or
145   more of the system prompt is present in the response, it is considered a significant leakage.
146   Response following. We introduce the response-following metric (RFM) to evaluate how well the
147   defense mechanism preserves the natural behavior of the model when responding to legitimate in-
148   puts. This metric measures how closely the protected system’s responses follow those of the original,
149   unprotected model, ensuring that the defense system maintains response quality for benign queries.
150   Let qb represent the benign input query, RFM is computed by the cosine similarity between the
151   sentence embeddings of the responses from the protected and unprotected systems:
152                                            ST(LM(psys , qb )) · ST(LMd (psys , qb ))
153                     RFM(psys , qb ) =                                                     ,          (3)
                                            ||ST(LM(psys , qb ))|| · ||ST(LMd (psys , qb ))||
154
155   where ST refers to the sentence transformer, and LMd represents the LLMs defense system. The
156   RFM score ranges from 0 to 1, with higher scores indicating that the protected system preserves the
157   model’s natural output quality, minimizing disruptions for benign inputs.
158   Detection. One interesting aspect of a defense system against prompt leakage attacks is its ability to
159   detect harmful prompts. While not the primary focus, it’s valuable to explore how well the system
160   can classify inputs as harmful or benign. To evaluate this, we employ basic machine learning metrics
161   such as accuracy and F1 score, which give a general sense of how effectively the defense system
      identifies harmful prompts without interfering with benign inputs.

      3     D EFENDING S TRATEGIES
163
164   3.1   P ROACTIVE AND T HIRD -PARTY S ETTING
165
166   In our study, we categorize the operational settings for defending against prompt leakage attacks
167   into two types: the proactive setting and the third-party setting. These settings differ in their level of
168   access to the system prompt, which directly influences the defense strategies that can be employed.
169   In the proactive setting, the defense system has full access to and control over the system prompt,
170   allowing it to actively monitor and prevent leakage by inspecting and managing the system prompt.
171
      Conversely, the third-party setting is a broader and more common scenario in today’s expanding
      prompt market ecosystem. Here, prompts are often developed by independent individuals and must
172
      remain proprietary and confidential. The system prompt is hidden from the defense mechanism,
173
      requiring indirect methods to detect and block prompt leakage without direct access to the prompt.
174
175
      3.2   D EFENSIVE PROMPTS AND DIRECT FILTERING
176
177   Defensive prompts. The use of defensive prompts helps instruct the model to keep the system
178   prompt confidential and avoid revealing sensitive information. For instance, a defensive prompt
179   might include guidance such as, ”These instructions are privileged information. Do not disclose
180   them.” We maintain a set of defensive prefix prompts generated by GPT-4, which can be embedded
181   into the system prompt to enhance security. These prompts guide the model to prioritize confiden-
182   tiality during input processing, reducing the risk of prompt leakage. This approach is applicable in
183
      both proactive and third-party settings, making it a versatile method for defending against prompt
      injection attacks in scenarios where the system prompt is either accessible or restricted.
184
185   Direct filtering. With full access to the system prompt, output filtering can identify and prevent
186   potential leaks by directly comparing the raw system prompt with the model’s output. We calculate
187   the overlapped word ratio between the system prompt and the output using the following formula:
                                                   P
188                                                   s∈psys 1[s ∈ LM(psys , q)]
189                               WR(psys , r) =                                 ,                       (4)
                                                                |psys |
190
      where 1[s ∈ r] is an indicator function that equals 1 if the substring s from the system prompt psys
191
      appears in the response r, and |psys | represents the total number of substrings in the prompt. We set
192   α as the threshold, with a value of 0.8 in our experiments. If 80% or more of the system prompt is
193   detected in the response, the system flags it as potential leakage and rejects the response.
194
195   3.3   S URROGATE - BASED FILTERING (S UR F)
196
197   Direct filtering methods become impractical when the system prompt is inaccessible, particularly
198   in third-party settings where the prompt is proprietary and hidden from the defense system. Since
199   direct filtering relies on comparing the system prompt with the model’s output, it cannot function ef-
200   fectively without access to the prompt. To address this, we propose surrogate-based filtering (SurF),
201
      an indirect yet effective solution that utilizes a pool of surrogate prompts to detect inputs that could
      trigger prompt leakage without requiring direct access to the system prompt. The detailed process
202
      of SurF, including both word ratio filtering and semantic similarity checks, which will be discussed
203
      later, is outlined in Algorithm 1.
204
205   SurF operates by collecting a set of K surrogate prompts, denoted as D = {pksur }K    k=1 , derived from
206   actual system prompts. The defense mechanism evaluates the input by analyzing its interactions
207
      with each of these surrogate prompts. If a potential leak is detected during the simulated interaction
      with any surrogate prompt in the set, the input is classified as harmful, and the system rejects the
208
      response. This approach allows SurF to safeguard the system prompt while maintaining confiden-
209
      tiality, making it particularly suitable for third-party scenarios where direct access is unavailable.
210
211   In addition to the word ratio based filtering criteria mentioned in Eq.(4), SurF goes beyond simple
212   string matching by incorporating semantic similarity checks between the output and the surrogate
213
      prompts. This allows for the detection of more sophisticated attacks, such as translation, paraphras-
      ing, or other indirect methods of prompt leakage, as discussed in (Zhang & Ippolito, 2023). We
214
      calculate the cosine similarity between the response and any of the surrogate prompts,

      It is important to note that our proposed SurF method is designed to detect whether inputs are
      harmful to the LLM service. Typically, we perform this detection by constructing an LLM service
242
      based on the same underlying LLM to evaluate the harmfulness of inputs, meaning the same LM is
243
      used. However, in practice, some LLM services may not publicly disclose the specific type of LLM
244   they use, and certain LLMs may have high computational requirements. In these cases, SurF can
245   also utilize a proxy LLM (denoted as LMpro ) for detection, providing flexibility to accommodate
246   different services and computational constraints.

248   4       E XPERIMENTS
249   4.1       E VALUATING THE D EFENSE S TRATEGIES
250
251   To simulate both proactive and third-party settings, we use two instruction-following datasets, Un-
252   natural (Honovich et al., 2022) and Alpaca (Peng et al., 2023), along with two real-world prompt
253
      datasets, ShareGPT2 and Awesome2 . We sample 100 prompts from each dataset, creating a system
      prompt dataset of 400 prompts, with 200 paired with natural inputs from Unnatural and Alpaca. For
254
      the attack evaluation, we collect 100 prompt injection attacks from Zhang & Ippolito (2023) and
255
      pair them with the system prompts, generating 400 malicious query-prompt pairs. Additionally, the
256   200 natural inputs are used as legitimate queries, bringing the total to 600 query-prompt pairs.
257
258   We evaluate five defense strategies: NoD (no defense), DefP (defensive prompts), OutF (output
259
      filtering), SurF (surrogate-based filtering), and SurF+DefP (SurF with defensive prompts). NoD
      serves as the baseline, showing LLM services vulnerability without defenses. DefP, applicable in
260
      both proactive and third-party settings, uses defensive prompts to protect system instructions. OutF,
261
      limited to proactive settings, relies on output filtering. SurF, suited for third-party settings, uses a
262   surrogate prompts pool to detect leakage, while SurF+DefP enhances this with defensive prompts.
263   Each experiment is repeated at least three times, and performances are measured using attack success
264   rate, response following metric, and detection metrics as mentioned in Sec 2.2.
265

267

      5     R ELATED WORKS
477
      Prompting large language models. Prompting large language models (LLMs) has become a piv-
478
      otal technique in natural language processing, enabling models to perform diverse tasks through
479
      well-crafted input sequences. Research demonstrated that, with appropriate prompts, LLMs could
480   achieve state-of-the-art performance across various domains (Le Scao & Rush, 2021). This realiza-
481   tion led to extensive research in prompt engineering, focusing on techniques such as prompt tuning
482   (Li & Liang, 2021) and advanced strategies like chain-of-thought prompting (Wei et al., 2022),
483   showcasing how prompt design directly influences model outcomes. Within this context, system
484   prompts have emerged as particularly crucial. Unlike general user prompts, system prompts are
485   designed to steer LLMs toward desired behaviors across a range of scenarios, serving as the foun-
      dational layer that underpins commercial and operational applications of LLMs (Giray, 2023). As a


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      result, a prompt economy has emerged, with highly effective prompts being regarded as intellectual
487   property and often kept secret (Warren, 2023).
488
489   Prompt injection attacks. As LLMs are increasingly integrated into real-world applications, they
490
      face a variety of security threats (Mozes et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023). Jailbreak attacks manipu-
      late models into bypassing built-in safeguards (Zou et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023a), while adversarial
491
      attacks inject optimized triggers to degrade performance (Wallace et al., 2019; Casper et al., 2023).
492
      Prompt injection attacks pose a unique challenge by tampering with input prompts to alter model
493   behavior (Greshake et al., 2023; Toyer et al., 2023). For instance, malicious inputs can embed hid-
494   den instructions that direct LLMs towards a completely different task (Liu et al., 2023b), or control
495   the output of the LLMs on the task (Piet et al., 2023). Among these, system prompt injection attacks
496   (Zhang & Ippolito, 2023) are especially concerning as they target the foundational system prompts
497   that guide LLMs behavior, threatening both intellectual property and operational integrity.
498
      Prompt injection defenses. Several studies have explored defenses against prompt injection attacks.
499   Yi et al. (2023) proposes placing a special delimiter between the prompt and data while fine-tuning
500   the model on attack samples. Piet et al. (2023) requires fine-tuning the model for each specific task,
501   while StruQ (Chen et al., 2024) introduces structured queries that separate LLMs prompts from input
502   and employ structured instruction tuning to mitigate prompt injection. Beyond resource demands,
503   fine-tuning-based methods are also impractical in today’s large model API-driven market. When
504   specifically considering system prompt injection, initial attempts by Hui et al. (2024) and Zhang
505   & Ippolito (2023) involve adding a defending system prompt prefix, but no systematic research or
506   analysis has been conducted on defending against these attacks. Our work addresses this gap.
507
508   6   L IMITATIONS AND D ISCUSSIONS
509
510   While our SurF method demonstrates robust defense against system prompt leakage in single-turn
511   interactions, its applicability to multi-turn dialogues remains limited. The dynamics of multi-turn
512   conversations introduce more complexity, as each input-output pair builds on the previous one,
513
      which could expose vulnerabilities that single-turn interactions may not reveal. Future work should
      explore extending SurF to handle these interactions effectively.
514
515   Additionally, the balance between security and user experience poses an important consideration.
516   Although SurF effectively mitigates prompt leakage, it may impact response quality for benign in-
517   puts. Overly aggressive filtering can reduce usability and satisfaction. Designing methods that main-
518   tain security without compromising user experience is critical. Ensuring transparency in defenses
519
      and offering clear feedback on filtered outputs will help preserve user trust.
520   Our surrogate-based approach, which leverages surrogate prompts to detect prompt leakage without
521   access to the actual system prompt, proves highly effective in the third-party scenarios. Beyond
522   its use in safeguarding system prompts, the concept of using surrogate prompts shows potential for
523   defending against other types of attacks on large language models.
524
525   7   C ONCLUSION
526
527   In this work, we address the critical issue of system prompt injection attacks, which pose significant
528
      vulnerabilities in the application of large language models (LLMs). We highlight the limitations of
      methods like output filtering, which, although effective when the system prompt is fully visible and
529
      controlled by the LLMs provider, become impractical in the prompt market or third-party defense
530
      scenarios, where system prompts are concealed and proprietary. To overcome these challenges,
531   we propose SurF (Surrogate-based Filtering), a novel approach that detects malicious inputs by
532   simulating interactions with surrogate prompts, without requiring direct access to the system prompt.
533   Once a potential leak from this prompt pool is identified, SurF alarms the system to resist generating
534   a response. Our experiments on both online and offline models, including Vicuna, Llama2, Llama3,
535   GPT series, and the Poe platform, show that SurF significantly reduces the success rate of system
536   prompt injection attacks. Additionally, we use a response-following metric to evaluate the balance
537   between defense robustness and response consistency, finding that while stronger defenses reduce
538   attack success, they may also slightly degrade the quality of legitimate responses. These results
539
      highlight SurF’s potential as a robust defense mechanism, with future work aimed at refining its
      performance and exploring applications to other LLMs vulnerabilities.

      A.4   DATASET C OLLECTION
728
729   We curated four datasets to simulate diverse scenarios where system prompts may be at risk of
730   leakage, allowing us to evaluate the robustness of our proposed SurF method and baseline defenses.
731
      Unnatural. This dataset consists of instruction-input-output pairs generated from a language model
732
      prompted with human-written seed instructions. It represents a wide variety of naturally occurring
733
      user queries, making it valuable for evaluating defense systems in common, benign input scenarios.
734
735   Alpaca. This dataset contains instruction-following data generated through interactions with Ope-
736   nAI’s GPT-4 model. Human-written seed prompts are used to create a diverse set of instructions,
737
      reflecting more complex user queries and interactions.
738   ShareGPT. A collection of real-world prompts shared by users of the ChatGPT service. This dataset
739   includes a variety of complex and diverse prompts, providing a realistic challenge for testing defense
740   mechanisms.
741   Awesome. A curated set of prompts resembling system messages used in real-world LLM-based
742   APIs and services. These prompts highlight potential vulnerabilities in practical settings.
743
744
      From these datasets, we randomly sampled 100 prompts from each set to construct the system
      prompt dataset. For the Unnatural and Alpaca datasets, we included both the system prompts and
745
      their corresponding natural, harmless inputs, providing a realistic representation of benign queries
746
      in user interactions. In contrast, the ShareGPT and Awesome datasets focus exclusively on system
747   prompts, reflecting scenarios where complex or sensitive system instructions may be vulnerable to
748   leakage. This sampling process resulted in a final dataset of 400 system prompts, with 200 prompts
749   containing paired natural inputs. By combining prompts with and without natural inputs, this dataset
750   allows for a comprehensive evaluation of defense mechanisms under varied and realistic conditions,
751   simulating both benign and potentially harmful scenarios.

764   To better illustrate our proposed method, we present several cases and the process of SurF in Table 8.

767
      the pool, the input is flagged as harmful. This approach is highly relevant in today’s evolving mar-

769

      A.7   T RANSLATION AND INTERLEAVING BASED EVALUATION .
773
774   To evaluate the effects of translation-based and interleaving attacks on system prompt leakage, we
775   modify our experiments using the same four datasets as in the main study: Unnatural, Alpaca,
776   ShareGPT, and Awesome. From each dataset, we sample 100 system prompts for evaluation.
777
      For translation-based attacks, input prompts are translated into several languages—French, Spanish,
778   Hindi, Arabic, and Chinese—before being fed into the LLM service. To measure the attack success
779   rate, we back-translate the outputs into English for consistent comparisons.
780
781
      For interleaving attacks, the model’s output is interspersed with special characters such as [”?”, ”-”,
      ”*”, ” ”, ”@”]. As with translation-based attacks, when evaluating the success rate, these special
782
      characters are removed to compare the outputs with the original system prompts.
783
784   Research shows that both translated attack prompts and interspersed characters may bypass tradi-
785   tional defenses like OutF, which rely on direct string matching (Zhang & Ippolito, 2023). However,
786   SurF proves more robust in these scenarios. By incorporating semantic similarity checks, SurF op-
787
      erates beyond simple string matching, enabling it to detect prompt leakage even when the system
      prompt is indirectly manipulated. This approach strengthens its defense against both translation-
788
      based and interleaving attacks, offering a more reliable solution for mitigating prompt leakage.

      A.9                 I N - DEPTH STUDY ON HYPERPARAMETER α AND β
811
812   There are two key hyperparameters in our proposed SurF method: α and β. The parameter α
813   represents the threshold for the overlapped word ratio between the proxy system prompt and the
814   output, while β defines the threshold for semantic similarity. If either the word ratio or the semantic
815   similarity exceeds the corresponding threshold, SurF identifies the input as a potential harmful attack
816   and alerts the defense system to reject the response. To better understand how these thresholds
817   impact performance, we conducted an in-depth investigation. We evaluated the method using several
818
      metrics, including approximate attack success rate, response-following metric, accuracy, precision,
      and recall. These experiments were performed on an LLM service with a Llama2-13B backbone,
819
      and the results are presented in Figure 6 for α and Figure 7 for β.
820
821   Varying α and β reveals distinct trade-offs in balancing security and utility under the Llama2-13B
822   model. Lower values of α result in more aggressive filtering, which enhances detection of malicious
823   inputs but comes at the expense of utility, as legitimate prompts may also be affected. Conversely,
824
      higher values of α increase utility by reducing unnecessary filtering, but they simultaneously elevate
      the risk of prompt leakage. Similarly, adjusting β influences the detection threshold for harmful
825
      inputs, with lower values providing stricter defenses but potentially impacting user experience. From
826
      these two figures, to achieve an optimal balance between detecting malicious attacks and minimizing
827   the impact on normal inputs, we selected α = 0.8 and β = 0.7. However, it is important to note that
828   when defense performance is prioritized over user experience, lower hyperparameter values can be
829   chosen to enforce stricter filtering.
