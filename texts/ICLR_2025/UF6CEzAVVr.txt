                                                      A BSTRACT
036
               Recent advancements in text-guided diffusion models have unlocked powerful im-
037
               age manipulation capabilities, yet balancing reconstruction fidelity and editability
038            for real images remains a significant challenge. In this work, we introduce Task-
039            Oriented Diffusion Inversion (TODInv), a novel framework that inverts and edits
040            real images tailored to specific editing tasks by optimizing prompt embeddings
041            within the extended P ∗ space. By leveraging distinct embeddings across different
042            U-Net layers and time steps, TODInv seamlessly integrates inversion and editing
043            through reciprocal optimization, ensuring both high fidelity and precise editability.
044            This hierarchical editing mechanism categorizes tasks into structure, appearance,
045            and global edits, optimizing only those embeddings unaffected by the current edit-
046
               ing task. Extensive experiments on benchmark dataset reveal TODInv’s superior
               performance over existing methods, delivering both quantitative and qualitative
047
               enhancements while showcasing its versatility with few-step diffusion model.

051
052   Text-guided diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022; Xue et al., 2024; Saharia et al., 2022) have
053   achieved significant success in synthesizing realistic images due to their controllability and diver-
      sity. Leveraging these effective text-guided diffusion models, numerous works have explored the

      generative priors of pre-trained diffusion models and successfully applied these capabilities to vari-
055   ous downstream tasks (Zhao et al., 2023; Qi et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Ji et al.,
056   2023; Baranchuk et al., 2022), particularly in text-driven image and video editing (Wu et al., 2023;
057   Chai et al., 2023; Qi et al., 2023; Tumanyan et al., 2023; Hertz et al., 2023; Khachatryan et al., 2023;
058   Saharia et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2023). These technologies enable users to edit images according to
059   their desires via text modification.
060
      When editing a real image x0 , many text driven image editing methods (Hertz et al., 2023; Cao
061   et al., 2023; Tumanyan et al., 2023; Parmar et al., 2023) require to invert x0 into the latent space
062   of a pre-trained diffusion model to obtain the corresponding latent codes {zt }1t=T , which is the in-
063   verse process of the diffusion model’s sampling procedure. There are two key aspects to this task:
064   the fidelity of the reconstruction and the editability of the latent codes (Garibi et al., 2024; Pan
065   et al., 2023). A naive approach to this task is Denoising Diffusion Implicit Models (DDIM) inver-
066   sion (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Song et al., 2021), which reverses the source image according to
067   the DDIM sampling schedule. However, applying DDIM inversion to text-guided diffusion models
068   often fails due to Classifier Free Guidance (CFG) (Ho & Salimans, 2022), which uses conditional
069
      text as input and magnifies the approximation error.
070   To eliminate the approximation error in DDIM inversion, many works (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015;
071   Mokady et al., 2023; Han et al., 2024; Miyake et al., 2023) align the differences between condi-
072   tional and unconditional trajectories to ensure that the source image is faithfully reconstructed. In
073   addition to aligning the two trajectories directly, several works reduce the approximation error at
074   each timestep by optimizing the latent codes. Specifically, AIDI (Pan et al., 2023), FPI (Meiri et al.,
075
      2023), and ReNoise (Garibi et al., 2024) introduce a fixed-point iteration process in each inversion
      step to obtain accurate latent codes. Furthermore, SPDInv (Li et al., 2024) optimizes latent codes
076
      directly based on the difference between two adjacent latent codes. Despite the progress made in
077
      fidelity reconstruction, the optimized latent codes often exhibit reduced editability (Garibi et al.,
078   2024; Parmar et al., 2023).
079

      Furthermore, we discern that various text-driven image editing tasks can be broadly categorized into
090   three distinct classes: structure editing, appearance editing, and structure-appearance (i.e., global)
091   editing. The modulation of appearance and structure is controlled by different layers within the
092   U-Net architecture during the diffusion process. This leads us to assert that varying levels of edit-
093   ing should correspondingly activate different tiers of text embeddings. These insights motivate the
094   creation of an inversion framework that dynamically integrates edit instructions in a hierarchical
095   manner, thereby ensuring both high fidelity and precise editability. In this paper, we propose a novel
096   Task-Oriented Diffusion Inversion (TODInv) framework designed to invert and edit real images tai-
097   lored to specific editing tasks. Our approach focuses on inverting to prompt embeddings in individ-
098
      ual layers. This method represents the input real image through a sequence of prompt embeddings,
      which can be effectively edited in downstream applications. In particular, we optimize the prompt
099
      embeddings within the extended prompt embedding space P ∗ (Alaluf et al., 2023). As illustrated in
100
      Fig. 2, unlike the original prompt space P, which shares the same embedding across different time
101   steps and U-Net layers, the P ∗ space employs distinct embeddings at different layers and time steps.
102   This extended space integrates the disentanglement and expressiveness of time and space, benefiting
103   our inversion in two key aspects:
104   i) The expressiveness of this latent space facilitates the minimization of inversion errors, signifi-
105   cantly enhancing reconstruction accuracy.
106   ii) Compared to the original P space, P ∗ space is more disentangled, which allows for more precise
107   optimization tailored to the specific editing type.

      To obtain a faithful reconstruction tailored to the target editing task, we optimize only those prompt
109   embeddings that are agnostic to the current editing, thereby minimizing approximation errors with-
110   out compromising editability. We conduct extensive experiments on benchmark datasets utilizing
111   various text-driven image editing technologies (Hertz et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2023; Tumanyan et al.,
112   2023). As shown in Fig. 1, the experimental results indicate that our method outperforms exist-
113   ing diffusion inversion techniques in both quantitative and qualitative evaluations. Additionally, our
114   method demonstrates strong performance with few-step diffusion models, further showcasing its
115   versatility and effectiveness.
116   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
117
118
      • We present TODInv, a novel diffusion inversion framework that seamlessly links and jointly opti-
        mizes inversion and editing processes, achieving both faithful reconstruction and high editability.
119
      • We introduce a task-oriented prompt optimization strategy, categorizing various editing tasks into
120
        three types. For each class of editing, we minimize the approximation error by optimizing specific
121     prompt embeddings that are irrelevant to the current editing.
122   • Extensive experiments on benchmark dataset demonstrate the effectiveness of our method over
123     state-of-the-art techniques. Our inversion model also supports few-step diffusion models.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
127
      Image Editing via Diffusion Models. Diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022; Saharia et al.,
128   2022; Ramesh et al., 2022) have made significant advancements in generating diverse and high-
129   fidelity images guided by text prompts. Leveraging these powerful models, numerous works have
130   harnessed their generative capabilities for text-driven image editing. For instance, Prompt-to-Prompt
131   (P2P) (Hertz et al., 2023) manipulates attention modules in Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022)
132   for localized and global edits. Plug-and-Play (PNP) (Tumanyan et al., 2023) adjusts spatial features
133   and self-attention modules for fine-grained edits, while Pix2pix-Zero (Parmar et al., 2023) retains
134   cross-attention maps for image-to-image translation. Recently, MasaCtrl (Cao et al., 2023) has en-
135   abled complex non-rigid editing by converting the self-attention module into mutual self-attention.
136
      Additionally, several works (Wu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Geyer et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024)
      have extended these methods to video editing. To apply these techniques to real images, inverting
137
      the images to the latent space of the diffusion model is a crucial first step.
138
139   Inversion in Diffusion Models. Early inversion methods for real image editing focused on Gener-
140   ative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Xu et al., 2023; 2021; Creswell & Bharath, 2018; Abdal et al.,
141   2019; 2020; Xia et al., 2023). The advent of diffusion models has shifted attention to diffusion-
142   based inversion methods, which can be categorized into Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models
143   (DDPM)-based (Huberman-Spiegelglas et al., 2024; Wu & De la Torre, 2023) and Denoising Dif-
144   fusion Implicit Models (DDIM)-based approaches (Garibi et al., 2024; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021;
145   Song et al., 2021; Pan et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Meiri et al., 2023). DDPM-based methods lever-
146   age the denoising process but require a large number of inversion steps (Wu & De la Torre, 2023;
147
      Huberman-Spiegelglas et al., 2024). DDIM-based methods introduce a deterministic DDIM sam-
      pler for inversion. However, when CFG is used, DDIM inversion often fails to achieve high-fidelity
148
      reconstruction (Mokady et al., 2023). To address these issues, several works (Mokady et al., 2023;
149
      Han et al., 2024; Miyake et al., 2023) align the conditional and unconditional trajectories by opti-
150   mizing the null text token or the prompt embedding. Concurrently, methods like EDICT (Wallace
151   et al., 2023) and BDIA (Zhang et al., 2023a) introduce invertible networks for inversion. PNPInv (Ju
152   et al., 2024) merges differences between reconstruction and editing branches, while NMG (Cho
153   et al., 2024) utilizes spatial context from DDIM inversion for faithful editing. Despite these ad-
154   vancements, existing methods still suffer from approximation errors in DDIM inversion, as the pro-
155   cess approximates latent xt using xt−1 . To eliminate these errors, techniques like AIDI (Pan et al.,
156   2023), FPI (Meiri et al., 2023), and ReNoise (Garibi et al., 2024) introduce fixed-point iteration
157   processes to optimize latent codes. SPDInv (Li et al., 2024) reformulates this iteration as a loss
158
      function. However, directly optimizing latent codes often results in reduced editability (Garibi et al.,
      2024; Parmar et al., 2023).
159
160   In contrast to existing solutions, our task-oriented inversion approach optimizes specific prompt em-
161   beddings in an extended prompt space for both inversion and editing, thereby avoiding the trade-off
      between faithful reconstruction and editability. While our method shares similarities with related

193   sion models, several works have analyzed the latent space of these models. Voynov et al. (Voynov

340   by the PIE-Bench dataset. Additionally, we introduce CLIP Similarity (Wu et al., 2021) to evalu-

      4.2   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
350
351
      We implement the proposed method in PyTorch on a PC with Nvidia GeForce RTX 3090. We use
352   Stable Diffusion V1.4 as our main text-guided diffusion model and set the CFG scale as 7.5. We
353   use the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) with the learning rate is set to be 0.001.
354   We categorize 9 editing types in PIE-Bench dataset into three classes. Particularly, the structure
355   editing contains Add Object, Delete Object, Change Content, and Change Pose. The appearance
356   editing contains Change Color, Change Material, and Change Style, and the global editing only
357   contains Change Background. Additionally, the U-Net of diffusion model has 4 resolution layer
358   scales, i.e., 64 × 64, 32 × 32, 16 × 16, and 8 × 8. Inspired by (Voynov et al., 2023), we take the
359   resolutions of 64 × 64 and 32 × 32 as appearance layers, and 16 × 16, 8 × 8 as structure layers. We
360
      set the maximization optimization steps K=10, and follow (Mokady et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024) set
      threshold δ as 5e−6 .

      5     C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
531
532
      In this paper, we present TODInv, a framework that inverts and edits a real image using diffusion
533
      models tailored to specific editing tasks. We categorize various editing tasks into three types, for
534   each kind of editing, we minimize the approximation error by optimizing specific prompt embed-
535   dings that are irrelevant to the current editing, achieving both faithful reconstruction and high ed-
536   itability. We conducted experiments on Stable Diffusion and SDXL-Turbo models, demonstrating
537   the effectiveness of our TODInv over state-of-the-art methods. The primary limitation of TODInv is
538   that it requires determining the editing types prior to inversion. However, this can be addressed by
539   using a large language model to easily determine the types. Please refer to the Appendix for detailed
      instructions.

              Yes, image editing tasks can indeed be broadly categorized into three classes based on the nature of the edits:
1089           1. Structure Editing:
1090                  Definition: This involves changing the shape, form, or structure of an object in the image without altering its fundamental appearance attributes (like
1091
                      color or texture).
                      Example: Editing a round yellow cake to a square yellow cake.
1092                  Applications: Architectural design changes, reshaping objects in design prototypes, altering body shapes in fashion imagery.
1093           2. Appearance Editing:
1094                  Definition: This involves altering the appearance attributes such as color, texture, or material of an object while keeping its structure the same.
1095                  Example: Editing a round yellow cake to a round red cake.
                      Applications: Changing colors of products in marketing images, altering textures in material design, modifying lighting effects.
1096
               3. Global Editing:
1097                  Definition: This encompasses changes that involve both structure and appearance simultaneously.
1098                  Example: Editing a round yellow cake to a square red cake.
1099                  Applications: Comprehensive image transformations for creative purposes, complete redesigns of products, significant alterations in artistic works.
1100          These categorizations help in understanding the nature and complexity of image editing tasks, and they guide the choice of tools and techniques to be
              employed for achieving the desired modifications.
