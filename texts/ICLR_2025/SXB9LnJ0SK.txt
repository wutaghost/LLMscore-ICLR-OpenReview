009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The application of formulas is a fundamental ability of humans when addressing
012            numerical reasoning problems. However, existing numerical reasoning datasets
013            seldom indicate explicitly the formulas employed during the reasoning steps. To
014            bridge this gap, we construct a dataset for formula-based numerical reasoning
015            called FormulaReasoning, which consists of 5,420 reasoning-based questions.
016            We employ it to conduct evaluations of LLMs with size ranging from 7B to over
017
               100B parameters utilizing zero-shot and few-shot chain-of-thought methods, and
               we further explore using retrieval-augmented LLMs provided with an external for-
018
               mula database associated with our dataset. We also experiment with supervised
019
               methods where we divide the reasoning process into formula generation, param-
020            eter extraction, and numerical calculation, and perform data augmentation. Our
021            empirical findings underscore the significant potential for improvement in existing
022            models when applied to our challenging, formula-driven FormulaReasoning.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Numerical reasoning constitutes one of the significant forms within natural language reason-
028   ing (Frieder et al., 2023). The study of numerical reasoning has seen substantial progress in recent
029   years, largely driven by the development of LLMs (OpenAI, 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Li et al.,
030   2023c) and specialized datasets (Wang et al., 2017; Dua et al., 2019; Amini et al., 2019; Cobbe
031
      et al., 2021a). Current datasets for numerical reasoning typically include simple, commonsense nu-
      merical questions that do not reflect the complexity of real-world problems. These datasets have
032
      not fully addressed the interpretability issue in numerical reasoning, as they often rely on implicit
033
      commonsense knowledge without explicit guidance knowledge during the reasoning process. This
034   issue becomes particularly evident when LLMs meet hallucination (Frieder et al., 2023; Bang et al.,
035   2023). Consequently, one might naturally ask “What knowledge could be used to guide numerical
036   reasoning process?”. Formulas exactly represent such knowledge that has been largely overlooked
037   in previous research but is frequently utilized in real-life applications.
038
      Take a question from the GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021a) as an example: “A robe takes 2 bolts of
039
      blue fiber and half that much white fiber. How many bolts in total does it take?”. This example only
040   requires the use of implicit commonsense mathematical knowledge to solve without domain-specific
041   formula. However, in our FormulaReasoning dataset, we require domain-specific formulas to guide
042   the numerical reasoning process, such as the formula used to calculate the heat absorption of an
043   object.
044
      Recently, Liu et al., 2023 constructed two formula-based datasets, Math23K-F and MAWPS-F.
045
      However, the formulas in these datasets primarily consist of commonsense formulas (such as to-
046   tal amount = unit amount × total number), and only 33.5% and 38.4% of the questions in these
047   datasets, respectively, require the use of formulas.
048
049
      To fill this gap, we constructed a dataset for numerical reasoning that requires the use of formu-
      las called FormulaReasoning. We annotated formulas for each question in FormulaReasoning. An
050
      example of FormulaReasoning is shown in Figure 1.1 The formula-based feature makes Formula-

126   2.1   N UMERICAL R EASONING DATASETS
127
      Numerical reasoning is one of the fundamental capabilities of natural language reasoning. The
128
      study of numerical reasoning in natural language has existed for several years. Numerous datasets,
129   such as DROP (Dua et al., 2019), GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021b), TSQA (Li et al., 2021) and
130   MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021), have introduced natural language numerical reasoning. Another
131   line of research focusing on numerical reasoning in natural language is math word problem (MWP).
132   MWP tasks typically provide a short passage (i.e., a question) and require the generation of an arith-
133   metic expression that can compute an answer. Representative datasets include MAWPS (Koncel-
134   Kedziorski et al., 2016), Math23K (Wang et al., 2017), MathQA (Amini et al., 2019), etc. Several
135   works focus on specialized domains where some of the questions in their datasets require numer-
136   ical reasoning. Examples include GeoSQA (Huang et al., 2019), which focuses on the geography
137
      domain, the STEM (Drori et al., 2023) dataset and the ScienceQA (Lu et al., 2022) which covers
      multiple disciplines in science and technology. The distinguishing feature of our FormulaReasoning
138
      is that the numerical reasoning questions within these datasets lack explicitly labeled formulas.
139
140   The recently introduced datasets (Liu et al., 2023) Math23K-F and MAWPS-F require formulas for
141   only 33.5% and 38.4% of the questions, respectively, and the formulas within these datasets are
142   all simple commonsense formulas (e.g., total cost = unit cost × total number). By contrast, our
143
      FormulaReasoning dataset collects questions from junior high school physics examinations, with
      every question accompanied by formulas. In addition, we also annotated a formula database for
144
      FormulaReasoning that can serve as an external knowledge base, used to assess retrieval-augmented
145
      systems.
146
147
      2.2   N UMERICAL R EASONING M ETHODS
148
149
      The methods for solving numerical reasoning have evolved from statistical approaches (Hosseini
150   et al., 2014; Kushman et al., 2014) to those based on rules and templates (Shi et al., 2015; Wang et al.,
151   2019) and further to methods based on deep learning models (Gupta et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2022;
152   Kim et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023a). In the past two years, with the rapid development of LLMs, LLMs
153   have demonstrated strong capabilities in resolving numerical reasoning questions. Consequently,
154   several methods aimed at enhancing the reasoning abilities of LLMs have been proposed, including
155   the notable Chain of Thoughts (CoTs) method (Wei et al., 2022), along with many subsequent variant
156   approaches (Kojima et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023b).
157   We established representative existing methods as baselines for FormulaReasoning, including
158   zero/few-shot CoTs prompting methods to LLMs ranging from 7B to over 100B parameters. We
159   trained a specialized formula retriever for retrieving formulas and explored retrieval-enhanced nu-
160   merical reasoning. We also divided the reasoning process into formula generation, parameter ex-
161   traction, and numerical calculation, and used data augmentation to enhance fine-tuned models with
      fewer than 7B parameters.

      3     DATASET C ONSTRUCTION
163
164
      We collected raw questions from Chinese junior high school physics examinations from 2015 to the
165   present. We had a total of five postgraduate volunteer students, and they all hold a bachelor’s degree
166   in science and engineering. We then annotated the reasoning steps and corresponding formulas for
167   each question. This process involved a combination of manual annotation and the assistance of
168   LLMs to improve the efficiency of annotation. Each question is associated with an explanation of
169   the reasoning steps in natural language with a symbolic representation of the reasoning steps using
170   formulas, including the values and units for all the parameters within the formulas. Finally, we
171   compiled all the formulas and we merged those expressing the same meaning to create a formula
172   database. We describe this process to construct FormulaReasoning in detail below.
173
174   3.1    P REPROCESSING
175
      We crawled 18,433 junior high school physics examination questions in China from 2015 to the
176
      present from public sources, including only those with free-text answers and excluding multiple-
177
      choice and true/false questions. Each raw question contains a question text and an explanation text
178   that includes the reasoning steps. We eliminated questions requiring diagrams.
179
180   Subsequently, we filtered the questions by assessing the presence of numerical values within the
181
      explanation and confirming that the final answer was numerical. Utilizing a regular expression-
      based approach, we extracted the final numerical answer, including its unit, from the explanation.
182
      We found that for 487 questions, the regular expressions did not return results, so we manually
183
      annotated the positions of their answers in the text explanations. Following the preprocessing phase,
184   we compiled an initial dataset comprising 6,306 questions.

205   3.2    F ORMULA N ORMALIZATION
206
207
      We found that the reasoning steps (i.e. the explanation) in the obtained raw dataset lacked a normal-
      ized format and were expressed quite casually. Some formulas mixed parameter names (e.g., “mass
208
      of water”) and symbols (e.g., “mwater ”), while others simply provided calculations in numerical
209
      form without parameter names or symbols. In order to ensure that all explanations adopted a nor-
210   malized form of formulas, we normalized the formula annotations in the explanations. An example
211   can be found in Table 2. In this process, we need to identify the formulas used within the original
212   explanations and to correct any formatting issues. Manually undertaking such tasks would require
213   significant effort. However, since the process is not open-ended, but rather structured and verifi-
214   able, we could automatically, e.g., using a LLM, extract formulas from the explanations, calculate
215   each step, and compare the result with the given answer to ensure the accuracy of this normalization
      process.

      Specifically, to enhance the efficiency of the annotation, we adopted a coarse-to-fine annotation
217   approach with the help of a LLM2 . We first prompted the LLM in a few-shot manner to generate
218   accurate explanations of the reasoning process. Then, we used few-shot prompts to guide the LLM in
219   correcting minor errors within the normalized explanations, including formatting errors in formula
220   annotations and inaccuracies in the parameters used during computations. Both prompts can be
221   found in Appendix A.1.1. Next, we will provide a detailed description of this process.
222
      Initially, we introduced the question along with its original explanation and the corresponding an-
223   swer to guide the LLM through few-shot prompting to revise the original explanation. We observed
224   that the ability of the LLM to revise explanations towards normalized explanations remained sat-
225   isfactory. To assess the correctness of the revised explanations, we extracted formulas from these
226   explanations and then computed the answer using the numbat tool3 . In addition to providing expla-
227   nations, we also required the LLM to present the values, symbols, and units of each parameter in the
228   formulas in the form of a table. An example is shown in Figure 1.
229   At this stage, we checked the correctness of the formula format in the explanations by automatic
230   rules, including whether there were omissions in parameter names, parameter symbols, or corre-
231   sponding units, and these issues were all correctable. Therefore, if our program detected that the
232   LLM had not successfully generated an accurate normalized explanation, we used few-shot prompt-
233   ing to identify and correct these specific errors. More details can be found in Appendix A.1.1. We
234   observed that the questions which remained incorrect despite multiple attempts by the LLM were
235   of notably poor quality, including missing important reasoning steps, unclear question formulation,
236   and so on. Some examples of these questions can be found in Appendix A.1.2. These questions
237
      were removed from our dataset. Following this step, our dataset contains a remaining total of 5,420
      questions.

295
296   In this section, we explore several methods for handling the questions within FormulaReasoning,
297   including prompting LLMs using zero-shot and few-shot chain-of-thought (CoT, Wei et al., 2022;
298   Kojima et al., 2022), and training a formula retriever to retrieve formulas to be incorporated into
299   LLM prompts. Additionally, we employed two approaches to enhancing the reasoning abilities of
300   fine-tuned models with fewer than 7B parameters. The first approach involved dividing the reasoning
301   process into distinct steps: formula generation, parameter extraction, and numerical calculation. The
302   second approach leveraged data augmentation to improve the models’ reasoning ability.

      We divided FormulaReasoning into into subsets for training, id (in-distribution) test, and ood (out-

      formulas in the id test must appear in the training set, whereas in the ood test, each question involves
308
      at least one formula that has not been seen in the training set. This division is designed to evaluate

      4.2.3    F ORMULA R ETRIEVER
332
333   We trained a formula retriever on the training set. Specifically, we encoded each question using the
334   Chinese-BERT-wwm-base (Devlin et al., 2019; Cui et al., 2021) model to obtain the CLS vector
335   of the question. Each formula in the formula database was represented by a randomly initialized
336   vector. During training, we calculated the cosine score between the question vector and the formula
337   vector. The retriever was then trained with in-batch negatives and contrastive learning loss (Gao
338   et al., 2021). Subsequently, for each question in the id test, we retrieved the top five formulas with
339
      the highest scores and included them in the prompt to observe the change in the performance of the
      LLM when provided with relevant formulas. More details can be found in Appendix A.2.3.

      4.2.4    S UPERVISED F INE - TUNED M ODELS
342
343   We found that directly prompting models possessing fewer than 7B parameters failed to produce
344   satisfactory outcomes (for example, ChatGLM3-6B attained merely 8.99 points in a zero-shot set-
345   ting). Therefore, we conducted supervised fine-tuning of models with fewer than 7B parameters,
346   yet discerned that, dissimilar to larger models (such as GLM-4-plus), smaller models did not exhibit
347   proficient performance in numerical extraction and calculation. In order to augment the reason-
348   ing capabilities of smaller models, we explored two approaches for improvement. We conducted
349   experiments on Qwen-1.8B (Bai et al., 2023) and ChatGLM3-6B (Zeng et al., 2022).
350
351   Chain-of-Thought Supervised Fine-Tuning (CoT-SFT) We decomposed the reasoning process
352
      into several steps. First, we instructed the model to generate the formulas required to solve the ques-
      tion. Subsequently, the parameter names within the formulas were extracted, allowing the model to
353
      retrieve the corresponding values and units from the context. Next, the formulas and the associated
354
      parameter values were provided to a calculator to obtain the final result. This approach relieved the
355   model from numerical calculation, allowing it to concentrate on the reasoning aspect.
356
357
      Data Augmentation (DA) We augmented the training dataset with the assistance of larger models.
358   Firstly, we utilized a few-shot approach to prompt a LLM (Qwen-max) to generate new question-
359   answer pairs. The correctness of the computation process generated by the LLM was meticulously
360   verified using a calculator. Subsequently, the formulas generated by the model were extracted and
361   normalized. More details could be found in Appendix A.2.1.
362

      6     C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
512
513   We introduced FormulaReasoning, a dataset for formula-based numerical reasoning. We annotated
514   the reasoning steps with formulas for each question with both manual and LLM-assisted efforts. Fur-
515   thermore, we constructed a formula database after merging formulas with similar meanings, serving
516   as an external knowledge base for subsequent retrieval-based/augmented approaches. We evalu-
517
      ated FormulaReasoning across various sizes of LLMs, supervised fine-tuned models, and retrieval-
      augmented LLMs, demonstrating its challenging nature as an unresolved task. Our findings indicate
518
      substantial room for improvement of existing models on formula-based numerical reasoning, thus
519
      motivating future research efforts.
520
521   In the future work, we plan to utilize the formula knowledge from FormulaReasoning to improve the
522   numerical reasoning capabilities of LLMs. Possible approaches include enhancing reasoning abili-
523
      ties through knowledge-driven methods, preference learning methods based on formula feedback.
524   One limitation of this work is that our evaluation results reported in the paper were obtained from the
525   original Chinese version of FormulaReasoning. We have employed a combination of LLM-based
526   translation and manual review to release an English version of FormulaReasoning. Currently, we
527   provide a preview English version in our GitHub repository, and we will release the official English
528   version of FormulaReasoning after completing the manual review process. Another limitation is
529
      that, our dataset is limited to the domain of physics. Although junior high school physics is not
      overly complex and can be understood by most people which would benefit evaluation efforts, it is
530
      still possible to explore formula-based question answering data in other domains.

758   A.2.1    DATA AUGMENTATION (DA) FOR F ORMULA R EASONING
759
760   There have been several studies utilizing large language models (LLMs) for data augmentation (Ding
761   et al., 2024). The data generated in these related works (Zheng et al., 2023; Whitehouse et al., 2023)
762   primarily focus on daily conversations or sentiment analysis and do not require rigorous numerical
763
      calculations. Some research on data augmentation involving numerical calculations (Shum et al.,
      2023) employs LLMs to generate solutions to questions to aid in training, rather than creating com-
764
      plete questions. In contrast to these approaches, our work generates complete questions that involve
765
      numerical calculations (particularly formula calculations), along with automatic improvement and
766   selection to ensure data quality.
767
768   In order to enhance the capabilities of models, we use LLM to generate more data for fine-tuning.
769
      We divide the process of data generation into the following several steps.
770   First, we randomly generated 17,000 prompts. Each prompt was obtained by stacking five question-
771   answer pairs sampled form training set. At the end of the prompt, LLM was required to generate the
772   sixth question-answer pair. Second, we normalized the generated formulas. Except for the absence
773   of manual review, the remaining steps were consistent with those in Section 3.2. At last, we unitized
774   the calculator to check whether the calculation process in the data generated by the LLM is correct,
775
      and discarded the generated data with incorrect calculation processes. After the above steps, we
      finally retained more than 2500 questions.
776
777   We found that mixing the newly generated data into the original training set did not always bring
778   positive improvement, perhaps because the newly generated data has not undergone manual re-
779   view. We found that randomly selecting a small portion of the newly generated data can enable
780   the model to have performance improvement. We set several different mixing ratios selected from
781
      {5%, 10%, 15%, 20%, 2%, 30%, 35%, 40%}. We fine-tuned each model using the augmented data
      set. After training for a fixed number of steps (150k and 200k), we selected the checkpoints with the
782
      smallest loss among models of different mixing ratios.
