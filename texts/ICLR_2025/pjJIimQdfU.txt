009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Self-supervised learning (SSL) on graph-structured data has attracted considerable
012            attention recently. Masked graph autoencoder, as one promising generative graph
013            SSL approach that aims to recover masked parts of the input graph data, has shown
014            great success on various downstream graph tasks. However, existing masked graph
015            autoencoders fail to consider the degrees of difficulties of recovering the masked
016            edges that often have different impacts on the model performance, resulting in
017            suboptimal node representations. To tackle this challenge, in this paper, we propose
018            a novel curriculum based self-supervised masked graph autoencoder that is able
019
               to capture and leverage the underlying degree of difficulties of data dependencies
               hidden in edges, and design better mask-reconstruction pretext tasks for learning
020
               informative node representations. Specifically, we first design a difficulty measurer
021
               to identify the underlying structural degree of difficulties of edges during the mask-
022            ing step. Then, we adopt a self-paced scheduler to determine the order of masking
023            edges, which encourages the graph encoder to learn from easy parts to difficult
024            parts. Finally, the masked edges are gradually incorporated into the reconstruction
025            pretext task, leading to high-quality node representations. Experiments on several
026            real-world node classification and link prediction datasets demonstrate the superi-
027            ority of our proposed method over state-of-the-art graph self-supervised learning
028            baselines. This work is the first study of curriculum strategy for masked graph
029            autoencoders, to the best of our knowledge.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Graph structured data is ubiquitous in the real world, such as social networks, citation networks,
035   e-commerce networks, etc. Recently, graph neural networks (GNNs) have gained considerable
036   attention for learning representations pertinent to graph-structured data and have shown great success
037   in supervised learning (Xu et al., 2019) or semi-supervised learning (Kipf & Welling, 2017; Hamilton
038   et al., 2017), where task-specific labels are necessary to train GNNs as the supervision information.
039
      However, collecting numerous task-specific annotations is a challenging endeavor in practice.
040   Self-supervised learning (SSL), as one type of unsupervised methods that are popular in computer
041   vision and natural language processing (Chen et al., 2020; He et al., 2020), has also revolutionized
042   the field of graph learning due to its promising performances in learning informative representations
043   through well-designed pretext tasks without relying on labels. Current graph SSL approaches can
044   be broadly categorized into two classes, i.e., contrastive and generative approaches. Specifically,
045
      contrastive graph SSL has firstly emerged as the prevailing method, adopting the instance discrim-
      ination as the pretext task, e.g., DGI (Veličković et al., 2019), MVGRL (Hassani & Khasahmadi,
046
      2020), and BGRL (Thakoor et al., 2022), etc. However, although there are some inspiring recent
047
      works exploring augmentation-free strategies like AFGRL (Lee et al., 2022b) and IGCL (Li et al.,
048   2023a), most classical contrastive graph SSL methods heavily rely on well-designed heuristic graph
049   augmentation, so that their performances can substantially degenerate when the graph augmentation
050   is not compatible with downstream tasks (Zhang et al., 2021b). Generative graph SSL approaches can
051   naturally tackle this problem by adopting one simple yet effective strategy as the pretext task, which is
052   to directly reconstruct the missing parts of the input graph data, whose representative methods include
053   GPT-GNN (Hu et al., 2020b), GraphMAE (Hou et al., 2022), S2GAE (Tan et al., 2023). They achieve
      promising performances without relying on the high-quality hand-crafted graph augmentation.

      Despite the noticeable success, the existing generative graph SSL literature fails to consider the
055   influence of the difficulties of pretext tasks during training process and merely treats all data samples
056   for training equally, which can lead to suboptimal performances. Intuitively, the pretext tasks should
057   be designed from dealing with easy data samples first and gradually to harder samples. This is
058   because tackling much more difficult data samples at an early training stage will inevitably make
059   the initial GNN encoder, with randomly initialized parameters, struggle to finish the pretext tasks.
060   Similarly, feeding over easy data samples at the later training stage will also bring limited benefits to
061   the GNN encoder. However, it remains unexplored to design tailored easy-to-hard pretext tasks for
062   more powerful representations, which poses the following challenges.
063
                 • It is technically difficult to design tailored reconstruction tasks to encourage the GNNs to
064
                   capture informative patterns of the input graph into representations.
065
066              • It is challenging to derive a proper principle to quantify the difficulty of reconstruction
067                samples for training the GNNs.
068              • It is also non-trivial to design a feasible schedule strategy to gradually exploiting data
069                samples for reconstruction by explicitly considering the training status of GNNs.
070
071   To tackle these challenges, we propose the Curriculum Masked Graph AutoEncoder (Cur-MGAE1 )
072
      to capture and leverage the underlying degree of difficulties of data dependencies hidden in edges and
      design better mask-reconstruction pretext tasks for learning informative representations. The proposed
073
      method is capable of learning GNNs for informative representations in a tailored easy-to-hard
074
      meaningful order to incorporate training data samples for reconstruction pretext tasks. Specifically, we
075   first propose a tailored structure-aware edge reconstruction task, namely recovering purposely masked
076   edge from the other unmasked graph structure. By the pretext tasks, the GNN encoder can extract
077   useful patterns from the input graph into the output representations. Then, we advocate for a self-
078   supervised method to select the top K easiest edges that the GNN encoder anticipates to reconstruct,
079   so that the difficulties of reconstruction samples can be quantified effectively. Furthermore, we
080   propose a tailored self-paced learning strategy to gradually exploit the edges for reconstruction as the
081   training processes. The task of recovering the missing structures for informative node representations
082   and selecting appropriate edges for training are integrated into a joint training framework. Finally,
083
      the GNN encoder can be trained by feeding data samples in a meaningful order to select proper data
      samples in pretext tasks, whose difficulties are well aligned with the GNN’s training status, leading
084
      to more powerful node representations and showing better performances in downstream tasks.
085
086   We theoretically analyze the convergence guarantee of this tailored training paradigm by proving
087   the properties of the avoidance of saddle points and the second-order convergence. Extensive
088   experiments on various real-world node classification and link prediction benchmarks demonstrate
089
      that our proposed model can achieve significant performance gains against state-of-the-art approaches.
090   The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
091
092              • We propose a novel method to train the GNN encoder by feeding the data with a tailored
093                easy-to-hard meaningful order to design pretext tasks. To the best of our knowledge, this is
094
                   the first attempt to study curriculum graph self-supervised learning.
095              • We propose a joint framework to recover the missing edges of the input based on the
096                unmasked graph structure and schedule the training edges for reconstruction in a proposed
097                self-paced learning manner, so that the GNN encoder can be trained more effectively.
098              • Theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the proposed Cur-MGAE model
099                and extensive experiments show that our proposed method significantly outperforms the
100                state-of-the-art graph SSL approaches, covering both contrastive and generative approaches.

123   Graph Self-Supervised Learning. Graph self-supervised learning (SSL) techniques (Liu et al.,
124   2022; You et al., 2020; Peng et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2023b) primarily branch into
125   contrastive and generative methodologies. Recently, contrastive graph SSL first becomes popular.
126   Some methods mainly explore negative sampling strategies like corruption-based negative pair
127   construction in DGI (Veličković et al., 2019), and in-batch negatives in methods like GCA (Zhu
128   et al., 2021). In these methods, graph augmentation is vital for the efficacy of contrastive learning.
129
      Nevertheless, the understanding of augmentation in graph contexts lacks clarity and theoretical
      grounding, making their label-invariance and optimality questionable. On the other hand, generative
130
      SSL focuses on replenishing omitted portions of input data, bifurcating into autoregressive and
131
      autoencoding models. While historically generative approaches lag in performance compared to
132   their contrastive counterparts, there are notable works in autoregressive graph models, such as GPT-
133   GNN (Hu et al., 2020b). In autoencoder space, models like GAE and VGAE (Kipf & Welling,
134   2016) set early benchmarks. More recent methods are developed such as GraphMAE (Hou et al.,
135   2022), GraphMAE2 (Hou et al., 2023), GigaMAE (Shi et al., 2023), SeeGera (Li et al., 2023e),
136   RARE (Tu et al., 2023), S2GAE (Tan et al., 2023) and Bandana (Zhao et al., 2024), whose key idea is
137   to reconstruct the masked parts based on the left unmasked graph structures. However, these methods
138   largely ignore the influence of the difficulties of self-supervised tasks and simply treat all training
139   samples equally, which can lead to suboptimal performances on downstream tasks. One of the recent
140
      methods, AUG-MAE(Wang et al., 2024) designs an easy-to-hard adversarial masking strategy on
      nodes to provide hard-to-align samples, which improves the alignment performance.

      Curriculum Learning. Curriculum Learning (CL) is an approach where training progresses from
145   simpler to more complex tasks, mimicking the manner in which humans often approach learning
146   through courses taught in school (Bengio et al., 2009; Wang et al., 2021a). The foundational
147   algorithm in this domain is termed "Baby Step" (Spitkovsky et al., 2010), responsible for dictating
148   the complexity and sequential order of data inputs. Subsequent advancements led to the inception of
149   the self-paced method (Kumar et al., 2010), designed to autonomously curate data samples based
150   on training loss metrics. In addition, numerous automatic CL frameworks have emerged, such as
151   transfer teacher (Hacohen & Weinshall, 2019), reinforcement learning teacher (Zhao et al., 2020),
152   as well as others tailored to specific datasets, models, and objectives (Sinha et al., 2020). Notably,
153
      there’s also been an integration of CL with areas like disentangled recommendations (Chen et al.,
      2021), combinatorial optimization (Zhang et al., 2022b), neural architecture search (Zhou et al.,
154
      2022), and video grounding (Lan et al., 2023). Some works also introduce CL into graphs (Li
155
      et al., 2023c), e.g., GNN-CL (Li et al., 2024), CurGraph (Wang et al., 2021b), etc. Central to the
156   CL paradigm are mechanisms that evaluate the intricacy of data samples and orchestrate training
157   regimens, determining either the sequence or the relative importance of data segments. However,
158   these methods generally need labels as the supervised information for training encoder and ignore
159   the self-supervised scenarios where the labels are not available. More importantly, the existing
160   methods consider the training samples as independent samples, while we focus on more challenging
161   structure-aware curriculum learning where samples are inter-connected with each other so that they
      can not be treated as independent samples.

      3.2   C OMPLEXITY- GUIDED C URRICULUM M ASKING
248
249   Since the roles of different edges in a graph could be different, randomly sampling the mask edges to
250   construct a perturbed graph may introduce optimization difficulties during the reconstruction process.
251   For example, a perturbed graph with little structural information may be too hard for the GNN to
252   reconstruct the original structures at a early training stage, where the encoded node embedding may
253   not be well-trained. To address the issue, we propose a complexity-guided curriculum masking
254   module to select edges from easy to hard, so that the optimization process can be smoothed.
255   Specifically, we try to identify which edges are explicitly important to the graph and gradually
256   filter them out to increase the learning difficulty. We formally defined the edge difficulty as: the
257   difficulty score measuring how hard for the current model to predict the edge correctly. To quantify
258   the difficulty of each edge in a given graph, we first use the current model to reconstruct the original
259   graph as: Are = DEC(ENC(H, A)). The predicted structures Are reflect the edges that are expected
260   to be inherently drawn from the current model, where the edge probabilities can be viewed as the
261
      model’s confidence for the edge occurrence. Intuitively, lower edge confidence indicates that the
      edge is more complex to be reconstructed by the current model. So we propose to leverage structural
262
      complexity to estimate the hardness of the edge for curriculum learning. Specifically, we calculate the
263
      residual errors as: R = A − Are . The residual errors could also potentially serve as a representation
264   of the degree of confidence the model possesses regarding the existence of an edge between two
265   nodes. It measures the distance between the predicted structure and the original structure, which tells
266   how difficult the edges are for the current model. Consequently, a smaller residual error could be
267   interpreted as an indication of a more pronounced significance of the edge existence. This implies that
268   such edges are relatively easier to learn and comprehend within the context of the model. Moreover,
269   by implementing a masking technique on the smallest K edges, the complexity of the task for the
      graph learning model can be substantially reduced. This is due to the fact that the model will be

      3.3   S ELF - PACED M ASK S CHEDULER
275
276   In this section, we propose a self-paced mask scheduler to progressively and autonomously incorporate
277   an increasing number of edges throughout the training process. One potential solution could be
278   incrementally increasing the value of K during the training process. However, identifying and
279   updating an appropriate K value within the process is non-trivial. Moreover, the selection of edges
280   directly correlates to a discrete optimization problem within an exceedingly vast topological space (1
281   for selecting and 0 for not selecting). This factor significantly complicates the optimization process.
282   To address this issue, we first relax each item in S(t) into [0, 1], and transform the problem into a
283   continuous constrained optimization problem. Specifically, we regard the constraint as a Lagrange
284
      multiplier, and introduce a regularization term into the loss function as follows: f (S; λ, A) =
285
      λ||S(t) ⊙ A − A||. S(t) is the edge selection matrix at training iteration t in the self-paced scheduler,
      which has the same dimensionalities as the adjacency matrix A. To optimize the loss function, the
286
      edge selection matrix S(t) is relaxed to be continuous. Each element in the optimized S(t) will
287
      belongs to [0, 1], and then, a cutoff threshold of 0.5 will be applied for converting the continuous
288
      value in edge selection matrix S(t) back into discrete binary value, or formally written as {0, 1}, so
289
      that the selected edges for training at step t is A(t) = S(t) ⊙ A, where ⊙ means the element-wise
290
      multiplication. The regularization term in the process of optimization, endeavors to mask as many
291
      edges as possible, contingent upon the parameter λ. By gradually increasing the coefficient λ,
292

293   adjacency matrix A. Therefore, it can be regarded as scheduling the training edges in an easy-to-hard

532
533   In this paper, we propose a novel curriculum-based graph pretraining strategy utilizing masked graph
534
      autoencoder named Cur-MGAE. Our proposed method can measure the difficulty of recovering
      masked edges, which encourages the model to learn from easy parts to difficult parts. The learned
535
      graph representations are more informative. Theoretical analysis of the convergence properties is
536
      provided in detail. Extensive experimental results on node classification and link prediction tasks
537   demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method over state-of-the-art graph SSL baselines.

1155
1156
       Effectiveness of split ratio. Split ratio is an important parameter for adding randomness in edged
       selection for overcoming overfitting. A small split ratio represents more edges are selected randomly.
1157
       Specifically, when split ratio is zero, the model selects edges randomly. When split ratio is 1.0, the
1158
       model completely relies on difficulty-based strategy instead. As shown in Figure 4, with a defined
1159   mask ratio, instead of 0 or 1, a proper split ratio can help with balancing exploitation and exploration,
1160   and achieves better result, which is also shown in our ablation studies.
1161
1162   Effectiveness of mask ratio. Mask ratio defines how many edges can be masked at the maximum.
1163   It can be shown in Figure 4 that evidently a smaller mask ratio does not help with the increase of
1164   model generalizability (e.g. ω < 0.7), while considering other parameters ω = 0.8 is a good value to
1165   reach higher performance.
1166
1167   Effectiveness of initial λ. λ influences the pace in which our curriculum model increments the
1168   number of selected edges. During training, λ is increased to force the model mask more and
1169   more edges. Thus, the starting point of such parameter will affect the training pace and is of great
1170
       importance. As shown in Figure 4, for most small datasets like Cora, a relatively small initial λ
       (λ = 1) is good for digesting the data and shows better performance. However, as this parameter
1171
       continues decreasing (λ < 1), the performance will be disturbed because of the lack of data. Due to
1172
       space limit, the sensitivity experiment on large datasets is not shown here, but in our practice, a larger
1173   initial λ (λ = 4) will help the model get more data quickly and perform better.
