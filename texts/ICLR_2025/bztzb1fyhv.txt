008                                                       A BSTRACT
009            To display high dynamic range (HDR) images on low dynamic range (LDR)
010            screens, tone mapping operations (TMO) are required to compress the dynamic
011            range. Recently, deep learning-based TMO methods have shown promising per-
012            formance. However, these methods often fail to deliver satisfactory results in
013            local areas, and generating image-level TMO on down-sampled low-resolution
014
               images leads to loss of details. To address this issue, we propose a learnable Differ-
               ential Pyramid Representation Network (DPRNet), a comprehensive framework
015
               capable of recovering details and manipulating global and local tone mappings.
016
               Specifically, we construct a Global Tone Perception module to modulate the image
017            globally. Then the Local Tone Tuning generates TMO coefficients at the patch
018            level to refine the local tones. Furthermore, we propose a Learnable Differen-
019            tial Pyramid module to capture multi-scale high-frequency components, coupled
020            with an Iterative Detail Enhancement progressively refining image resolution and
021            image details. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our method significantly
022            outperforms state-of-the-art methods, improving PSNR by 2.58 dB in the HDR+
023            dataset and 3.31 dB in the HDRI Haven dataset respectively compared with the
024            second-best method. In addition, our method has the best generalization ability in
025
               the unsupervised image and video TMO. We provide an anonymous online demo at
               https://xxxxxx2024.github.io/DPRNet/.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
050   High dynamic range imaging has revolutionized the way we capture and represent real-world scenes,
051   preserving rich luminance details across varied intensity ranges. However, the challenge of effectively
052   rendering these HDR images on conventional low dynamic range (LDR) display devices remains due
053   to inherent hardware constraints. In this context, tone mapping (TM) algorithms emerge as a critical
      bridge, narrowing the dynamic range disparity between HDR content and LDR screens.

054
      Recent methods based on deep neural networks (DNNs) [1, 2, 3] have made impressive progress in
055   tone mapping performance. These methods [4, 5, 6, 7] can be broadly classified into two categories:
056   global and local tone mapping, based on their operational patterns. Global tone mapping [2, 8]
057   focuses on learning the dense pixel-wise mapping or transformation between input HDR image
058   and output LDR image pairs, including pixel-level and image-level tone mapping. These methods,
059   without considering neighboring pixels, ignore spatial transformations and intensity correlations
060   between pixels, resulting in over-compression of bright areas or loss of detail in dark areas, see Fig. 1.
061   Conversely, local tone mapping [9, 10], patch-level TMO, can dynamically adjust the tones of local
062   regions based on local luminance features. Despite their local adaptivity, these methods suffer from
063
      halo artifacts and exhibit substantial computational demands, particularly for high-resolution images.
064   On the other hand, we observe that the customized software, e.g., photoshop and Photomatix, often
065   includes both global and local tone mapping operations [11, 12], where the global TMO is realized
066   by logarithmic compression, and the local TMO is realized by local laplacian pyramids. However,
067   these methods lack flexibility in dealing with various image contents. Therefore, integrating local
068   and global TMO in a unified learning framework is expected.
069   Furthermore, to reduce computational resources, existing methods [5, 7, 13] usually manipulate
070   tone mapping at a low resolution after image downsampling. This method introduces a trade-off,
071   where lower resolution processing inevitably leads to a compromise in image detail and quality.
072   To address this problem, some methods [4, 14] have introduced reversible pyramid decomposition
073   [15] to maintain and recover high-frequency details through multi-scale decomposition. However,
074   the extraction of high-frequency components from HDR images is inherently challenging due to
075
      their extensive dynamic range and complex texture structure. The strong contrast and rich lighting
      variations of HDR images make it difficult for traditional manual features to effectively capture these
076
      details [4, 7]. Especially in extremely bright or dark areas, image details are often compressed or lost.
077
      Fig. 1 illustrate that the enhancement results of the existing methods suffer from severe loss of detail.
078
079   To address these challenges, we propose a learnable Differential Pyramid Representation Network
080   (DPRNet), a comprehensive framework designed to recover details and manipulate global and local
081
      tone mappings. Our method involves the decomposition of tone mapping into three distinct yet
      interrelated subtasks: i) global tone perception, which manipulates the overall tonality and preserves
082
      image visual consistency, ii) local tone tuning, which refines the brightness and contrast of specific
083
      regions in a patch-based manner; iii) high-frequency extraction and reconstruction, which restores
084   image resolution while enhancing detail fidelity. To achieve comprehensive tone mapping, we design
085   a Global Tone Perception module for the global modulation of image and then construct a Local
086   Tone Tuning module to refine the local tone. To compensate for detail loss during downsampling, we
087   propose a Learnable Differential Pyramid module to capture multi-scale high-frequency components,
088   coupled with an Iterative Detail Enhancement strategy that facilitates a progressive enhancement of
089   detail while restoring image resolution. This framework enables the refinement of each level of the
090   pyramid images to produce multi-scale LDR images for supervision, thereby enabling the network to
091   learn the mapping between HDR and LDR images across multiple scales.
092   Our contributions are summarized as follows:
093
            • We propose an innovative end-to-end network, the Differential Pyramid Representation
094
              Network (DPRNet), capable of detail recovery and the simultaneous manipulation of global
095           and local tone mappings.
096
            • We introduce the Learnable Differential Pyramid module for adaptive high-frequency extrac-
097
              tion, coupled with an Iterative Detail Enhancement module to progressively restore image
098
              resolution and detail.
099
100         • Extensive experiments demonstrate that our method significantly outperforms state-of-the-
101
              art methods, improving PSNR by 2.58 dB in the HDR+ dataset and 3.31 dB in the HDRI
              Haven dataset respectively compared with the second-best method. In addition, our method
102
              has the best generalization ability in the unsupervised image and video TMO.

      2    R ELATED WORKS
106   2.1 L EARNING - BASED TONE MAPPING METHODS
107   Recent advancements in tone mapping have leveraged deep learning techniques to address the
      nonlinear mapping from HDR to LDR images. Hou et al. [2] applied CNNs to tone mapping tasks,


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      establishing a foundation for subsequent research. He et al. [8] developed a conditional sequential
109   retouching network for effective image retouching. Similarly, Cao et al. [1], Rana et al. [16], and
110   Panetta et al. [17] explored generative adversarial networks (GANs) for pixel-precise mapping.
111   Despite these efforts, issues such as halo effects, noise, and local area processing remain challenging.
112   To combat this, Hu et al. [18] combined tone mapping with denoising, incorporating a discrete cosine
113   transform module for noise removal and improved image quality. Zhang et al. [19] manipulated
114   tone in the HSV color space, which significantly reduced halos and preserved detail. Although these
115   learning-based methods have achieved remarkable results, most of them are either global or local
116   mappings, struggling to achieve satisfactory tone mapping results.
117
      2.2   3D LUT- BASED ENHANCEMENT METHODS
118
119   In pursuit of computational efficiency and performance, emerging tone mapping methods employed
120   3D LUT to render image brightness and color. Zeng et al. [5] developed an adaptive 3D LUTs
121   prediction network that merges multiple foundational 3D LUTs. Zhang et al. [13] presented a
122
      compressed representation of 3D LUTs, reducing parameters. Wang et al. [20] studied pixel-level
      fusion based on 3D LUTs, extending the method to a spatially aware variant. Yang et al. [21] proposed
123
      effective ICELUT for extremely efficient edge inference. Yang et al. [22] introduced the AdaInt
124
      mechanism, which adaptively learns non-uniform sampling intervals in the 3D color space for more
125   flexible sampling point allocation. Furthermore, Yang et al. [7] proposed SepLUT, addressing the
126   shortcomings of 1D LUTs in color component interaction while alleviating the memory consumption
127   issues of 3D LUTs. Despite these efforts, these methods necessitate an initial down-sampling step to
128   reduce the network computation, and in the case of 4K images, even need 16 times down-sampling.
129   As a result, this leads to loss of image details and degradation of image quality. Zhang et al. [4]
130   attempted to use the Laplacian pyramid to compensate for the loss of high-frequency detail, but
131   effectively capturing the high-frequency component of HDR images remains a challenge.

      3.1   M OTIVATION
135
136   Combining the strengths of both methods achieves a critical balance: global tone mapping ensures
137
      overall consistency in luminance, while local tone mapping preserves fine detail and improves local
      contrast in challenging lighting conditions. As a result, existing customized software often includes
138
      both global and local tone mapping operations. However, few works have integrated global and local
139
      tone mapping strategies into a unified learning framework. Comprehensive tone mapping not only
140   solves the limitations of global tone mapping, i.e., enhancing detail retention in complex lighting
141   scenes but also mitigates the problems of localized tone mapping, such as artifacts and inconsistencies.
142   On the other hand, paradigms based on frequency domain decomposition have been widely used
143   for various image-processing tasks [4, 23, 24]. Previous methods typically utilized pyramids [15]
144   with fixed convolutional kernels to manually extract features. However, limited by complex lighting
145   environments, traditional manual features often struggle to capture high-frequency details effectively.
146   Therefore, adaptively extracting high-frequency details is a critical step in tone mapping.
147
148
      3.2   F RAMEWORK OVERVIEW
149   To handle global and local tone mapping while preserving high-frequency details, we propose an
150   end-to-end framework, the Differential Pyramid Representation Network (DPRNet). The pipeline of
151   the proposed DPRNet is shown in Fig. 2. Our framework is composed of four components: global
152   tone perception, local tone tuning, learnable differential pyramid, and iterative detail enhancement.
153
      Given an HDR image X ∈ RH×W ×3 , our aims to generate an LDR image T0 ∈ RH×W ×3 . We
      begin to manipulate tone mapping from downsampled low-resolution images Ln , optimizing for
154
      computational complexity. Subsequently, we employ the Global Tone Perception (GTP) to manipulate
155
      the global tone and preserve image visual consistency. To refine the local regions, we built the Local
156   Tone Tuning (LTT) module to refine the brightness and contrast of different regions in a patch manner.
157   The combination of GTP and LTT allows our framework to maintain the overall tone of an image
158   while adaptively enhancing local contrast and details. To compensate for the loss of detail due
159   to downsampling, we introduce a Learnable Differential Pyramid for adaptively extracting high-
160   frequency information from complex scenes. Finally, we utilize the high-frequency details captured
161   by the LDP to refine the texture layer by layer while restoring the resolution through iterative detail
      enhancement modules.

183   3.3 G LOBAL T ONE P ERCEPTION
184   Global tone mapping typically applies a uniform mapping function to manipulate global brightness
185   and contrast. We mathematically find that these pixel-independent operations can be approximated
186   or formulated by multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs). For global brightness adjustment, given an input
187   image X, the global brightness is described as the average value of its luminance map:
188

191   brightness is to multiply a scalar for each pixel:
192                                                               X′ Y (x, y) = αXY                                                                                        (2)

194   where X Y is the adjusted pixel value, α is the scalar, and (x, y) indicates the pixel location in an

196                                                              Y = R(WT A + b),                                                                                          (3)

      and biases respectively, and R(·) is the activation function. Similarly, contrast adjustment refers to
      differences in luminance or color maps, the operation of which can also be expressed in MLPs [8]. To
200
      this end, we build an MLP-based Global Tone Perception module for adjusting the global brightness
201
      and contrast of an image and ensuring tonal consistency.
202
203   Specifically, given an input image X, we begin to obtain the lowest resolution LF component Ln
204   of size 2Hn × 2Wn through multiple Gaussian down-sampling operations. Then, Ln is fed into the
205
      GTP module, which globally modulates the tones at the pixel level. The GTP is composed of three
      full convolutions, conditional network [8], and ReLU activation functions. The conditional network
206
      consists of three convolutions, ReLU activation, and an average pooling. The conditional network
207
      outputs a conditioning vector, which is used for global pixel-level modulation of the intermediate
208   features of the base network, generating the lowest resolution global mapping image LGn.

246   3.5 L EARNABLE DIFFERENTIAL PYRAMID
247   Compared to general images, HDR images are more difficult to extract high frequency due to a
248   wider range of luminance, complexity of high-frequency details, and color accuracy and consistency.
249   Existing methods struggle to recover these details and colors effectively, see in Fig. 8. We introduce
250   a learnable differential pyramid module, inspired by scale-invariant feature transform [28, 29], for
251   adaptively extracting high-frequency information from HDR images. The differential pyramid
252
      exhibits two key properties [29, 28] that make it suitable for tone mapping tasks. 1) The features
      within the differential pyramid remain stable under scale transformations, rotations, and illumination
253
      variations, enabling effective tone mapping across varying conditions. 2) The differential pyramid,
254
      constructed via filtering and difference operations, efficiently captures image edges, corners, and
255   textures, which are crucial for preserving details.
256
257   In detail, for the input image X, we first apply a 3 × 3 convolution to obtain the initial feature map F0 .
258
      Subsequently, we generate a lower-resolution feature map F1 through three consecutive convolutions
      and a maximum pooling operation. We then compute the difference between three convolutions
259
      to obtain three sets of differential features, which are concatenated together and fed into a residual
260
      network to generate the first high-frequency feature H0 . This process iterates, with differential feature
261   maps generated through successive convolutions and pooling, which are concatenated and fed into
262   the residual network to predict HF components. Through n − 1 iterations, we obtain the complete
263   differential pyramid XHF = [H0 , H1 , . . . , Hn−1 ] that contains multi-scale high-frequency features
                                                                                                  H       W
264   adaptively learned from the input HDR image, tapering resolutions from H × W to 2n−1            × 2n−1 .n
265   denotes the number of pyramid levels (n=3 in our framework).

      various lighting conditions. We select 456 image sets for training and 114 for testing. Similar to the

319
      The HDR Survey dataset consists of 105 HDR images, with no ground truth, and is one of the
320
      benchmarks for HDR tone mapping evaluations. [1, 16, 17, 34, 35]. The UVTM video dataset, also

      5 C ONCLUSION
531
      This paper proposes a learnable Differential Pyramid Representation Network (DPRNet), a compre-
532
      hensive framework capable of recovering details and manipulating global and local tone mappings.
533
      We construct a Global Tone Perception module to modulate the image globally. Then the Local
534   Tone Tuning generates TMO coefficients at the patch level to refine the local tones. Furthermore, we
535   propose a Learnable Differential Pyramid module to capture multi-scale high-frequency components,
536   coupled with an Iterative Detail Enhancement progressively refining image resolution and image
537   details. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our method significantly outperforms state-of-the-
538   art methods, improving PSNR by 2.58 dB in the HDR+ dataset and 3.31 dB in the HDRI Haven
539   dataset respectively compared with the second-best method. In addition, our method has the best
      generalization ability in unsupervised video TMO.

      F   L IMITATIONS AND DISCUSSION
825
826
      Our method does have limitations. For the MIT FiveK dataset, our method is unable to obtain
      SOTA results, and we have performed some analyses on this. Firstly, FiveK was shot by a DSLR
827
      camera, which was not processed by advanced ISP pipeline and lacked raw denoising and YUV
828
      denoising, resulting in the dataset containing serious noise. These noises have a great influence on
829   our high-frequency extraction. Second, some reference images in the FiveK dataset suffer from
830   overexposure or oversaturation, which poses a challenge to the enhancement method, as mentioned
831   by Zhang et al [4]. Thirdly, there are inconsistencies in the reference images adjusted by the same
832   professional photographer, leading to differences between the training and test sets.
833
      As a future work, we plan to build a unified model to tackle more visual enhancement tasks by
834
      integrating denoising, and tone mapping.
