011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We propose HyDance, a transformer based diffusion network utilizing both the
014            temporal and frequency domain representations of dance sequences for music-
015            driven dance generation. Existing dance generation methods primarily use tempo-
016
               ral domain representations of dances, which results in the network losing the fre-
               quency domain characteristics of the dance sequences. This manifests in overly
017
               smooth generated dance sequences, resulting in dance movements that lack dy-
018
               namism. From an aesthetic perspective, such overly smooth movements are per-
019            ceived as lacking expressiveness and the sense of power. To address this issue, we
020            propose HyDance, which incorporates independent temporal feature encoders and
021            frequency domain feature encoders. The model employs a shared-weight hybrid
022            feature encoder, enabling the complementary extraction of motion information
023            from both domains. By introducing compact frequency domain representation
024            into the dance generation framework, our method mitigates the oversmoothing
025            problem in generated dance sequences and achieves improved spatial and tempo-
026            ral alignment in the generation results. Experiments show that our method gener-
027
               ates more expressive dance movements than existing methods and achieves better
               alignment with the music beats.

048   1   I NTRODUCTION
049
050   In the field of human motion synthesis, dance synthesis represents a specialized research direction
051   worthy of exploration. As an art form, dance contains rich semantic information derived from
052   human aesthetics, and a method capable of effectively generating high-quality dance sequences that
053   conform to specified conditions possesses a wide range of applications, including content creation
      areas such as video production and game cutscenes.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Furthermore, in terms of motion characteristics, dances feature a diverse range of movements. Com-
055   pared to common human motions such as walking or running, the differences between various dance
056   movements are more pronounced, and transitions between motions are more frequent. This includes
057   a substantial number of transitions between high-frequency and low-frequency dance movements,
058   which presents a greater challenge to the generation capabilities of the model.
059
      In this paper, we aims to generate detailed 3D dance sequences that conform to human aesthetic stan-
060
      dards of dance movements, given a piece of music. In recent years, with advancements in generative
061   model technologies, researchers have made significant progress in the field of dance sequence syn-
062   thesis. However, previous work primarily relies on the temporal joint rotation representation during
063   model training, which limits the model’s performance due to the sparsity of motion representation.
064   As a result, the generated dance sequences often exhibit overly smooth characteristics, lacking the
065   fine dynamics present in real dance performances. In the field of motion representation learning,
066   in addition to directly using joint rotation representations, structured representations learn to extract
067   essential features from the motions, thereby creating a more compact feature space. Such represen-
068   tations generally help to improve the dynamic performance of the motion sequences generated by
069
      the model. Recent work named PAE (Starke et al., 2022) proposes using an autoencoder structure,
      introducing frequency domain transformations as inductive bias to capture the periodic features of
070
      human motion from unstructured motion sequences. This approach has achieved good results in
071
      synthesis tasks with relatively simple motion categories, producing flexible and lively motions with
072   smooth transitions between high and low-frequency actions. However, for dances, encoding only
073   the periodic features of the dance sequences is not sufficient to fully restore the intricate details of
074   the dance performance.
075
      In terms of model design, while the capabilities of transformer models have been validated across
076
      many fields, recent research(Piao et al., 2024; Park & Kim, 2022; Guo et al., 2023; Tian et al.,
077
      2023) has highlighted certain learning biases inherent in transformer models. This issue specifically
078   refers to the fact that the self-attention mechanism tends to capture low-frequency features in the
079   data and can easily overlook high-frequency features. Researchers, through in-depth studies of the
080   mechanisms of transformers, have found that self-attention layers often exhibit low-pass filtering
081   characteristics, tending to capture tokens that appear consistently in the context. For dance sequence
082   data, high-frequency motion features typically appear at the transitions between dance movements
083   or in the detailed movements of limbs within a segment of dance. Since these movements are not
084   choreographically designed and the transitions between dance movements can vary widely, they
085   do not frequently occur in dance sequences. This means that high-frequency features of dance
086
      sequences usually do not affect the overall direction or general trajectory of the dance movements
      but do impact the expressiveness and the sense of power in the dance movements. The relatively low
087
      energy of high-frequency features in the dance generation results demonstrates the oversmoothing
088
      of dance in the frequency domain.
089
090   To address this issue, we propose HyDance, a novel dance generation framework that combines
091   frequency domain representation with temporal joint rotation representation of dance sequences to
092
      generate high-quality dance sequences. We follow PAE to obtain the motion manifolds of dances
      and then use it to build the frequency domain representations of dance sequences. Because of the
093
      more compact feature space in learned motion manifolds of dances, integrating this frequency do-
094
      main representation helps the model reduce the probability of sampling meaningless samples. To
095   compensate for the limitations of frequency domain representation in encoding non-periodic dance
096   movement features, we design the model to accept both frequency domain motion representation and
097   temporal joint rotation motion representation, thereby allowing the model to acquire complemen-
098   tary motion information from both representations. The generated dance sequences achieve richer
099   transitions and are more expressive compared to existing methods.
100
      To summarize, our contributions include the following:
101
102
103        1) We introduce the frequency domain representation of dance sequences into the dance gen-
104
              eration framework, proposing HyDance, a dance generation framework that combines fre-
              quency domain and temporal representations for high-quality dance generation.
105
106
107        2) A Dual-Domain Hybrid Encoder that helps the dance generation framework effectively
              utilize the additional information to optimize the dance generation results.

            3) Experiments demonstrate that our method generates dances that better align with human
109            aesthetic standards, featuring more expressive movements and more natural transitions.

      2.1   H UMAN M OTION S YNTHESIS
118
119   Human motion synthesis is a topic of significant interest among researchers in computer vision.
120   Earlier motion synthesis methods typically used GAN models. (Habibie et al., 2022) employs mo-
121   tion matching techniques to acquire reference motion sequences and refines these motion sequences
122   through a GAN model to synthesize body pose sequences that conform to speech. (Hernandez et al.,
123   2019) uses past motion sequences and leverages a GAN model to predict subsequent motion se-
124   quences, achieving consistent body poses and root translations in the synthesized motion sequences.
125
      Additionally, VAEs have been widely used in many works for motion synthesis. (Guo et al., 2022)
      proposes using motion snippet codes as motion representations within the model and employs a
126
      VAE model to achieve text-to-motion generation.
127
128   In recent years, as diffusion models have demonstrated powerful generation capabilities, there has
129   been significant progress in numerous works that employ these models. (Tevet et al., 2023) achieves
130   Text-Conditioned Motion Synthesis with a Diffusion framework and obtains high-quality motion
131
      generation results. (Dabral et al., 2023) uses a diffusion-based motion generation framework and
      can leverage textual or musical conditioning information to control the generation process, offering
132
      multimodal generation capabilities. (Chen et al., 2023) first maps motions to low-dimensional latent
133
      codes using a VAE and then uses these latent codes as input to a diffusion model, thereby achieving
134   motion synthesis with lower computational requirements. (Zhang et al., 2024b) separately constructs
135   global trajectory and local pose modules, leveraging the generation capabilities of diffusion models
136   to achieve 3D motion reconstruction from monocular video frames.

140   2.2   M USIC D RIVEN DANCE G ENERATION
141
142
      Synthesizing dance sequences that match the style and rhythm of given music and have high
143
      motion quality is a topic of significant interest among researchers. Various methods, including
144
      sequence-based models, GANs, VAEs, and diffusion models, have been proposed to tackle this
145   task. Sequence-based dance generation models usually employ transformer-based models to gen-
146   erate dance sequences in an autoregressive manner. (Li et al., 2021) utilizes a transformer-based
147   model, taking initial short motion sequences and music features as input, and generates dance se-
148   quences by predicting the next motion frame-by-frame in an autoregressive manner. (Sun et al.,
149   2022) introduces a bank of latent codes to constrain generated dance sequences to remain close to
150   ground truth dance sequences, thereby reducing error accumulation due to the autoregressive gener-
151   ation approach in sequence-based models and alleviating the motion freezing problem when gener-
152   ating long dance sequences. (Kim et al., 2022) uses a GAN architecture with a transformer encoder
153
      as the discriminator and a transformer decoder as the generator, enabling style-controlled dance gen-
      eration through the use of style codes. (Siyao et al., 2022) first uses a VQ-VAE to map motions to a
154
      codebook, and then builds a GPT-based model to arrange motion codes according to control infor-
155
      mation and convert them into dance sequences. (Tseng et al., 2023) introduces a Diffusion model for
156   dance generation, and (Li et al., 2024) achieves a balance between global choreographic patterns and
157   local motion quality through a coarse-to-fine two-step diffusion framework. (Zhang et al., 2024a)
158   divides the complete noisy motion sequence into short segments and uses a cross-attention mecha-
159   nism to fuse information from preceding and following short segments during the denoising process,
160   generating more coherent dance sequences. (Bhattacharya et al., 2024) achieved the generation of
161   dance sequences that closely align with musical beats by separately generating short sequences of
      beat poses corresponding to the music beats and repetition poses between these beat pose sequences.

181   to transform the original joint rotation representations of dance sequences into latent variables w
182
      that have m channels. It then calculates the latent parameterization f, a, b, s for each latent channel
      using a differentiable FFT layer. During the decoding process, these latent parameterization along
183

186   Then, we use stacked 1D convolutional layers to decode the latent variables back into motion se-
187   quences. After the training of frequency domain feature extractor is completed, we use a sliding
188   window approach to apply the extractor to the full dance sequence, obtaining the latent parameteri-
189   zation f, a, b, s of motion clips within each window. The latent parameterization is then used as the
190   encoding result for the center motion frame of each window. Through this approach, we encode the
191   original motion sequence into a structured frequency domain representation.
192   Representation. For a music sequence of L frames, we follow (Li et al., 2021) and extract music
193   features m ∈ RL×35 using librosa (Mcfee et al., 2015). The extracted music features consist of 35
194   channels, including a 1-dim envelope, 20-dim MFCC, 12-dim chroma, 1-dim one-hot peaks, and
195   1-dim one-hot beats.
196   For dance sequences, we use both temporal and frequency domain motion representations. For the
197   temporal domain representation, we follow (Tseng et al., 2023) and represent the motion sequence
198   as d ∈ RL×151 , which includes: (1) 4-dim contact label indicating whether the heels and toes of the
199   left foot and right foot are in contact with the ground; (2) 3-dim root translation; (3) 144-dim joint
200   rotation, represented in a 6-DOF (Zhou et al., 2019) format. For the frequency domain represen-
201   tation, we represent the dance sequences as df ∈ RL×32×4 , which includes 32 channels of latent
202   variables. Each latent channel contains four parameters f, a, b, s per motion frame, representing the
203   latent parameterization of the motion sequence segment within the window centered on the current
204
      frame.

316   Dataset. In our experiments, we tested our method using the open source dataset AIST++ (Li et al.,
317   2021), which contains paired music and dance sequences. The AIST++ dataset contains 1,408 dance
318   sequences recorded in joint rotation and root translation representations. The dance sequences in the
319   AIST++ dataset vary in length, ranging from 7.4 seconds to 48 seconds.
320   Implementation details. In our experiments, the length of both motion representations and music
321   features is 128 frames, with a frame rate of 30 frames per second, corresponding to a duration of
322   approximately 4.3 seconds. We used the Adan optimizer during training and set the learning rate
323   to 1e-4. The training of the model consists of two stages. Prior to training the dance generation
      framework, we first train the frequency domain feature extractor with the same data used for the

      dance generation framework. Once the frequency domain feature extractor is trained, we freeze
325   its weights and then train the dance generation framework. During this training stage, we employ

327   our model has 64.5M trainable parameters and was trained on four NVIDIA 3090 GPUs for 2000
328   epochs, with a batch size of 32.

      Physical plausibility. We adopt the Physical Foot Contact(PFC) score(Tseng et al., 2023) as one
360   of the metrics to measure the quality of the dance sequences. The motivation behind the PFC score
361   is that high-quality dances should possess good physical plausibility. The PFC score is based on
362   the observation that, in dances, the acceleration of the dancer’s center of mass must be supported
363   by contact with the ground through either one or both feet. Because dance movements differ from
364   general human movements and often involve foot sliding, which can be an element of choreography,
365   the PFC score provides a better evaluation of the physical plausibility of dance sequences compared
366   to simply calculating foot sliding related metrics, and reflects the quality of the generated dance
367   sequences. As shown in the Table 1, HyDance achieves an improvement of 0.278 over EDGE in the
368
      PFC metric, and shows improvements of 0.996, 0.496, and 0.166 respectively over FACT, Bailando,
      and BADM. This demonstrates that HyDance produces dance sequences with better motion quality.

371   to measure the alignment of the generated dance sequences with the beat of the music. The BAS

373   where dance beats are defined by the local minima of joint velocities.

      FID results. Fréchet Inception Distance(FID) is a widely used metric for the overall evaluation of
      generative models. FID quantifies the difference between the empirical distribution of the gener-
454
      ated results and the ground truth distribution. Previous works(Li et al., 2021; Siyao et al., 2022) on
455

456   et al., 2021) proposed extracting different motion features from dance sequences to compute FIDk

458   ric for assessing dance sequence generation quality is questionable. Through experiments, EDGE
459   demonstrated that although some generated dance sequences achieved the best performance in the
460   FIDg metric, they received pretty low ratings in user evaluations, suggesting that this metric may
461   not reliably reflect the quality of dance generation results on the AIST++ dataset. For the FIDk
462   metric, EDGE found through evaluations of models trained for different durations that, although the
463   user evaluation performance of the dance generation results gradually improved, the FIDk metric
464
      did not show a similar trend. Therefore, the FID metric may not reliably reflect the quality of dance
      generation results on the AIST++ dataset. We present the comparison results of our method with

522   5     C ONCLUSION
523
524
525
      In this paper, we propose HyDance. We argue that the high-frequency features in dance sequences
      contain numerous motion details, which are important for determining whether the final dance per-
526
      formance is close to that of a human dancer. We find that previous works focus only on the temporal
527
      features of dance, neglecting the dynamic features in the frequency domain, which leads to generated
528   dances lacking dynamic performance and expressiveness. Therefore, we combine both frequency
529   domain and temporal features to generate dance movements. To fuse these features more effectively,
530   we introduce the HyDance network. Our method is validated on public datasets, achieving state-of-
531   the-art performance, and ablation experiments further demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed
532   modules.
533
      Limitations and future work. Although our method generates dance sequences with improved ex-
534   pressiveness and details, we also note in our experiments that the root node displacement trajectories
535   in the dance sequences generated by our method are not adequately represented. We believe that the
536   possible reason for this issue is that the frequency-domain feature extractor in the framework focuses
537   more on local periodic features of the dances, leading to poor capture of the root node displacement
538   characteristics. Therefore, a meaningful direction for future work could be to explore better ways to
539   encode dance sequences, ensuring that both global displacement features and local dance movement
      features are well captured.
