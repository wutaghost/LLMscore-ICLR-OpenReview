009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Graph neural networks (GNN) suffer from large computational and memory costs
012            in processing large graph data on resource-constrained devices. One effective
013            solution to reduce costs is neural network quantization, replacing complex high-bit
014            operations with efficient low-bit operations. However, to recover from the error
015            induced by lower precision, existing methods require extensive computational costs
016            for retraining. In this circumstance, we propose TopGQ, the first post-training
017            quantization (PTQ) for GNNs, enabling an order of magnitude faster quantization
018            without backpropagation. We analyze the feature magnitude of vertices and observe
019            that it is correlated to the topology regarding their neighboring vertices. From these
020
               findings, TopGQ proposes to group vertices with similar topology information
               of inward degree and localized Wiener index to share quantization parameters
021
               within the group. Then, TopGQ absorbs the group-wise scale into the adjacency
022
               matrix for efficient inference by enabling quantized matrix multiplication of node-
023            wise quantized features. The results show that TopGQ outperforms SOTA GNN
024            quantization methods in performance with a significantly faster quantization speed.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Graph neural networks (GNNs) attract a great
031   amount of attention due to their ability to process
032   diverse unstructured data. They have achieved
033
      success in many areas such as recommendation
      systems (Pal et al., 2020; Fan et al., 2019; Zhang
034
      et al., 2023), molecular interaction (Wale et al.,
035
      2008; Borgwardt et al., 2005), transportation net-
036   works (Bai et al., 2020; Cao et al., 2020), and social
037   network analysis (Qiu et al., 2018; Arazzi et al.,
038   2023). However, GNNs often suffer from substan- Figure 1: Comparing duration of existing GNN
039   tial computational and memory costs due to increas- quantization methods against TopGQ.
040   ing demands for processing large graphs, especially on resource-constrained devices.
041
      One promising direction to circumvent this issue is neural network quantization (Choukroun et al.,
042   2019; Zhao et al., 2019; Choi et al., 2021), which reduces the computation and memory requirements
043   of GNN inference by utilizing reduced numerical precision for computations. However, despite
044   its advantages, quantizing GNNs is considered difficult due to the extremely diverse vertex feature
045   magnitudes caused by the message-passing of the GNN algorithm. Since the quantization process is
046   known to be highly sensitive to the magnitude outliers (Wei et al., 2022), such diversity in aggregated
047   features in GNNs results in high quantization errors.
048   To handle the magnitude outlier problem, several methods (Tailor et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2022) have
049   been proposed to adopt quantization-aware training (QAT). However, QAT methods accompany
050   significant computation and memory costs for the quantization process, requiring excessive resources
051   and time larger than full-precision pretraining target GNN architecture. Figure 1 reports the time it
052   takes to quantize common GNN architectures using prior GNN quantization methods (Tailor et al.,
053   2020; Zhu et al., 2022). Measured in wall-clock time, the quantization time easily exceeds 100
      minutes, and even up to 4.9 days (ogbn-products, Degree-Quant) when the graph size increases.

054
      Real-world graphs, such as traffic networks (Jiang & Luo, 2022; Sharma et al., 2023) or social
055   media graphs (Gao et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2023), are continuously updated, which requires multiple
056   compressing of the GNNs to keep the application up-to-date. Also, in safety-critical applications such
057   as edge fraud detection in financial transactions (Lu et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2018), a
058   rapid update is required to avoid safety crisis, and fast quantization of GNN can be necessary.
059
      To this end, we propose TopGQ, the first accurate post-training quantization (PTQ) framework for
060
      GNNs addressing the aforementioned issues of existing GNN quantization methods. TopGQ does
061   not involve any form of gradient computation or parameter updates, which makes the proposed
062   method significantly faster compared to the baseline and requires low memory consumption. Instead,
063   TopGQ focuses on the local topological information of the graphs to determine accurate quantization
064   parameters. As a result, our method TopGQ shortens the quantization time by an order of magnitude,
065   making GNN quantizations much faster and more efficient.
066
      The key to achieving high performance is to well utilize the local topology of the graphs. We observe
067   that the existing method relying on the indegree of the vertices is insufficient to capture the diversity of
068   the feature magnitudes. Instead, we propose topology-based node grouping. Because the magnitude
069   of a node feature is determined by local neighbors, we arrange the vertices into several quantization
070   groups that share similar indegree and local Wiener index. In the process, we devise an efficient
071   algorithm for computing the local Wiener index. Lastly, we additionally provide scale absorption
072   method to enable efficient integer matrix multiplication of node-wise quantized feature matrix. The
073   experimental results show that TopGQ outperforms the existing SOTA method for GNN quantization
074   with up to 358× speedups with better or comparable accuracy.
075   Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
076
077          • We show that the magnitudes of node features in GNN are correlated with local topological
078            information from degree centrality and Wiener index.
079          • We propose a topology-based node grouping, which groups vertices with similar topological
080            characteristics to reduce quantization error from high feature magnitude variance of GNN.
081          • We propose scale absorption to enable efficient integer arithmetic of node-wise quantized
082            GNN operation by absorbing node-wise scale into an adjacency matrix.
083
             • We propose the first PTQ method for GNN, which outperforms the existing training-based
084
               quantization method with meaningful margins while bringing up to 358× less quantization
085            time compared to the baselines.

116   simple yet effective uniform integer quantization as with scale (s) and zero-point (z) as follows:
117

121   tion, and ⌊·⌉ is rounding operator. Symmetric quantization has the representation range centered with
122   zero (z = 0), while asymmetric quantization uses z = s · xmin + 2k−1 . Also, each row or column
123   may have different quantization parameters (s, z), which are calculated independently according to
124   quantization dimension, called row-wise and column-wise quantization, respectively.
125
      There are two mainstream types of quantization: Post-training quantization (PTQ) and quantization-
126
      aware training (QAT). On the one hand, PTQ goes through a calibration process which adjusts the
127   scale, zero-point, and rounding directions using only a small set of data. Conversely, QAT methods
128   directly apply gradient-based training to explicitly reduce the quantized network’s target loss. The
129   major discrepancy between them is that QAT generally incorporates updating weight parameters,
130   while PTQ methods focus on quantization parameters without weight updates and is much faster.

149   3     R ELATED W ORK
150
151   GNN Quantization can efficiently reduce extensive computational costs and memory requirements
152   of graph neural networks (Kipf & Welling, 2016; Xu et al., 2019; Veličković et al., 2018), as they
153
      suffer from large size of real-world graphs. Degree-Quant (Tailor et al., 2020) is the first work to
      quantize GNN, using QAT to allow high-degree vertices to retain full-precision features at training
154
      and be quantized later for inference. EPQuant (Huang et al., 2022) focuses on reducing high memory
155
      costs incurred by processing large graph data with product quantization, along with quantized
156   GNNs. SGQuant (Feng et al., 2020) and A2 Q (Zhu et al., 2022) are also QAT methods targeting
157   GNN architectures, but they differ in that they allow mixed-precision to assign higher bitwidth
158   to high-magnitude vertices. SMP (Wang et al., 2023) tackles the oversmoothing problem when
159   quantizating deep GNNs with a customized message propagation, and (Eliasof et al., 2023) utilizes
160   wavelet transformations in traditional image compression to quantize GNNs. Notably, existing GNN
161   quantization methods adopt QAT, i.e., incorporating gradient-based iterative weight updates, which
      require significant computational overheads (Figure 1).

163   the structural information (Ji, 2019; Zhang & Lu, 2020; Hu et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2018; You et al.,

      5     M ETHODOLOGY
200
201
      The goal of TopGQ is to rapidly perform quantization with PTQ, while retaining QAT-like perfor-
202
      mance on GNNs. For this, we propose topology-based node grouping which captures the local
203
      topology information into GNN quantization. In this process, we propose a new algorithm to
204   accelerate the computation of the local topology for each node in the graph. Then, we propose
205   scale absorption which allows for efficient integer arithmetic while still preserving the accuracy of
206   node-wise quantization.

      5.1   Q UANTIZATION WITH T OPOLOGY- BASED N ODE G ROUPING
209
210   Figure 3a shows the group generation strategy of TopGQ. Due to the nature of the aggregation phase
211   (Equation (3)), it is evident that the feature magnitudes of a vertex depend greatly on how many and
212   which vertices the features are being aggregated from. As discussed in Section 4, node indegree is
213   a suboptimal measure because it only accounts for a limited amount of information. Instead, we
214   propose to examine the topology of the local subgraph surrounding each vertex, and group the vertices
215   with the same or similar local topology. Within the same group, we can expect that the vertices
      aggregate similar values and thus sharing a quantization scale leads to minimal quantized errors.

501   To further identify the advantages of the localized Wiener Index, we compare it against other centrality
502   measures, such as betweenness centrality, closeness centrality, and Katz centrality. The results are
503   shown in Table 7. The other centrality measures depict suboptimal performance compared to localized
504   Wiener Index. We believe that the result stems from the unique expressiveness of the localized Wiener
505   index in capturing local compactness of a node within k-hop neighbors: A small value of a node
506
      indicates a dense connectivity within its neighbors, and relatively rapid propagation of features via
      message passing. Therefore, TopGQ can effectively group node features with distinctive ranges, as

      shown in Figure 2 in the paper, leading to enhanced quantization quality.

532   7     C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this paper, we propose TopGQ, the first post-training quantization method for GNNs. TopGQ
535   proposes to group vertices that share similar topological structure, which is measured using an
536   adaptation of Wiener index to capture the local topology around each node. For this, TopGQ proposes
537   a new algorithm that reduces the overhead of computing localized Wiener index for each node. Then,
538   TopGQ proposes the scale absorption method, which merges the scale parameters of quantization
539   groups to the adjacency matrix for efficient computation. The extensive experimental results show
      that TopGQ outperforms baselines while having orders of magnitude faster quantization.

736   Here, we present a comprehensive analysis regarding the trade-offs of quantization and provide
737
      a theoretical analysis of computation cost and storage consumption. The experimental results are
      shown in Tables 8 and 9.

739   As shown in Table 8, TopGQ finds a good balance between reducing quantization time and preserving
740   accuracy, while other choices in FP32, Degree-Quant, TopGQ demonstrate disadvantages in either

742   Degree-Quant alleviates this cost via quantization, the long quantization time is required to obtain

      contribution. Nevertheless, we provide TopGQ with QAT settings and compare the results with the

      original TopGQ, shown in Table 14 and Table 15.

890   As we discussed in the main body of the paper, the better performance of TopGQ compared to
891   QAT baselines is from better consideration of topological structure. In other words, the superior

898   the low-bit format is sufficient to handle the original model complexity. We cite some papers that
899   exhibit the mentioned occasion in their experiments. For example, some of Wu et al.; Shomron et al.
