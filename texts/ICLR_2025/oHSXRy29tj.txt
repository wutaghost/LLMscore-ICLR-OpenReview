009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Overlap and hierarchy are two prevalent phenomena in clustering, and usually
012            coexist in a single system. There are several studies on each of them separately,
013            but it is unclear how to characterize and evaluate the hybrid structures yet. To
014            address this issue, we initiate the study of hierarchical overlapping clustering
015            on graphs by introducing a new cost function for it. We show the rationality of
016            our cost function via several intuitive properties, and develop an approximation
017            algorithm that achieves a provably constant approximation factor for its dual
018            version. Our algorithm is a recursive process of overlapping bipartition based
019            on local search, which makes a speed-up version of it extremely scalable. Our
020
               experiments demonstrate that the speed-up algorithm has good performances in
               both effectiveness and scalability on synthetic and real datasets.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025
      Clustering is a major task in data mining and has a wide range of applications in many areas. Two
026   fundamental categories of clustering have attracted in-depth study recently. The first is hierarchical
027   clustering (HC) which requires a recursive partitioning of a graph into smaller clusters to form a
028   cluster tree Dasgupta (2016); Li & Pan (2016); Cohen-Addad et al. (2019); Charikar & Chatziafratis
029   (2017); Moseley & Wang (2017); Naumov et al. (2021). The other is overlapping clustering (OC)
030   that allows data points to belong to multiple clusters Orecchia et al. (2022); Zhang et al. (2007);
031   Shen et al. (2009); Chen et al. (2010); Nicosia et al. (2009); Whang et al. (2016); Li et al. (2017);
032   Yang & Leskovec (2012a). These two structures are widely present in the real world, and the hybrid
033   structure of hierarchical overlapping clustering (HOC) that allows for the presence of overlaps
034
      among hierarchical clusters better reflects real-world scenarios. For instance, in social networks, an
      agent may belong to several different groups, which can form larger communities with overlapping
035
      structures based on different themes. In cooperation networks, the coauthors of a paper can be thought
036
      of as a small cluster, which may belong to more than one research area due to the topic. This hybrid
037   structure in fact poses a significant challenge to the study of clustering. There are many works for
038   HC and OC separately, but we lack research on HOC. In this paper, we address this problem.
039
040

      and OC. Similarly, a proper cost function is helpful to evaluate the quality of HOC, which transforms
041
      the HOC problem to an optimization task. In this paper, we propose a new cost function for HOC,
042
      and present an approximation algorithm for it in some reasonable condition. Our contributions are
043   summarized as follows:

121   scalable to large graphs with tens of millions of edges, and is the main competitor in our experiments.

      In this section, we formulate our cost function for HOC. First of all, we briefly introduce the
      underlying idea. HOC can be represented by a directed acyclic graph, called HOC graph, that is a
131
      natural generalization of HC tree. Inspired by Dasgupta‚Äôs cost function for HC, we extend the LCA of
132
      an edge to its minimal common ancestor set, and introduce the belonging factor to measure the degree
133   by which a node, a cluster, or an edge belongs to an ancestor. Intuitively, for a similarity-based graph,
134   a quality HOC graph should involve edges of heavy weights into clusters that are small and as far
135   down from the root of the HOC graph as possible. Overlapping is desirable when a node has strong
136   connections to more than one cluster simultaneously, in which case, the belonging factor allows to
137   suppress the cost contributed by the edges incident to the node. This is the crucial idea of our cost
138   function for HOC.

269   overlapping clusters without any restriction, and thus proper constraints on D are necessary. However,
      we need to be very careful in formulating the constraints. In fact, there is a trivial solution that allows

      two endpoints of each edge to form a cluster, which achieves the minimum cost 2w(E). Treating
271   two endpoints of each edge as a cluster is in fact an intuitive way for overlapping cluster settings, but
272   due to the large number of clusters, it is meaningless. This is quite different from the optimization

      Definition 2.10 (k-HOC problem). The k-HOC problem on a similarity-based graph G is defined as
278   minD H D (G) for which the width of the HOC graph D is at most k.
279
280   To better understand this problem, let us consider a non-overlapping HC tree first. Here, the width
281   means the largest number of bottom and smallest non-overlapping clusters that contain the leaves
282   directly. These can be considered as a set of key clusters that are closest to the leaves on the tree.
283   Similarly, on an HOC graph, since the longest anti-chain blockades all paths from leaves to the root,
284   the width measures intuitively the number of the incomparable key clusters that contain the leaves.
285

288   multiple clusters since it could be a nice way to construct a k-HOC graph by recursively calling 2-OC
289   algorithm in a top-down fashion. In Section 3, our algorithm for k-HOC proceeds in this way.

525   5    C ONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE WORK
526
527   Conclusions. In this paper, we study the problem of hierarchical overlapping clustering from the
528   aspects of cost function, algorithm and scalability. We propose a cost function and give some basic
529   properties. We provide an approximation algorithm that achieves constant factor for the dual version
530
      of k-HOC problem. A speed-up version of our algorithm based on some easy heuristics during local
      search has good performances in HOC reconstruction and good scalability.
531
532   Future work. There are many directions worth further study. The first is about approximation
533   algorithm for the primal k-HOC problem for k > 2. Although we know the complementary
534   relationship between the primal and the dual problems, the approximation guarantees are quite
535   different. The second is about variant versions of the HOC problem, e.g., having other constraints on
536
      HOC graphs and alternative definitions of node-to-node and edge-to-node belonging factors. These
      flexible settings may adapt to different application scenarios.

       C.1    OSBM AND OUR SETTINGS
1351
1352   OSBM is specified by a k √ó k symmetric matrix Z, where each element is a natural number, and a
1353   pair of real numbers p1 , p2 (0 ‚Č§ p1 ‚Č§ p2 ‚Č§ 1). Zij (i Őł= j) represents the number of overlapping
1354   nodes between the i-th and the j-th clusters, and Zii represents the number of nodes in the i-th cluster
1355   that do not participate in overlap. Denote by C1 , ...Ck the planted overlapping clusters. p1 represents
1356   the inter-link probability between each pair of clusters, while p2 represents the intra-link probability
1357   within each cluster. For two nodes in the overlapping parts, we have two independent samples, and
1358
       the edge is present if any of them generate an edge. In other words, the probability of edge presence
       between any two nodes in the overlapping parts is 1 ‚ąí (1 ‚ąí p2 )2 .
1359
1360   In our experiments, we assume that each node belongs to at most two clusters. Thus, the total number
1361   of nodes is the sum of entries in the upper triangle of Z. For simplicity of implementation, all clusters
1362   are of the same size and have the same size of overlaps between clusters. For a 2-level hierarchical
1363   structure, we choose three probability values 0 ‚Č§ p1 ‚Č§ p2 ‚Č§ p3 ‚Č§ 1, in which p1 is the inter-link
1364
       probability between clusters on the first level, p2 is the inter-link probability between clusters on the
       second level, and p3 is the intra-link probability within each cluster. So now, the probability of edge
1365
       presence between any two nodes in the overlapping parts is 1 ‚ąí (1 ‚ąí p3 )2 .
