009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In this paper, we study the problem of uncertainty estimation and calibration for
012            LLMs. We begin by formulating the uncertainty estimation problem, a relevant yet
013            underexplored area in existing literature. We then propose a supervised approach
014            that leverages labeled datasets to estimate the uncertainty in LLMs’ responses.
015            Based on the formulation, we illustrate the difference between the uncertainty
016
               estimation for LLMs and that for standard ML models and explain why the hid-
               den neurons of the LLMs may contain uncertainty information. Our designed
017
               approach demonstrates the benefits of utilizing hidden activations to enhance un-
018
               certainty estimation across various tasks and shows robust transferability in out-
019            of-distribution settings. We distinguish the uncertainty estimation task from the
020            uncertainty calibration task and show that better uncertainty estimation leads to
021            better calibration performance. Furthermore, our method is easy to implement
022            and adaptable to different levels of model accessibility including black box, grey
023            box, and white box.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Large language models (LLMs) have marked a significant milestone in the advancement of nat-
028
      ural language processing (Radford et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022; Bubeck
029
      et al., 2023), showcasing remarkable capabilities in understanding and generating human-like text.
030   However, their tendency to produce hallucinations—misleading or fabricated information—raises
031   concerns about their reliability and trustworthiness (Rawte et al., 2023). The problem of whether
032   we should trust the response from machine learning models is critical in machine-assisted decision
033   applications, such as self-driving cars (Ramos et al., 2017), medical diagnosis (Esteva et al., 2017),
034   and loan approval processes (Burrell, 2016), where errors can lead to significant loss.
035
      This issue becomes even more pressing in the era of generative AI, as the outputs of these models
036   are random variables sampled from a distribution, meaning incorrect responses can still be produced
037   with positive probability. Due to this inherent randomness, the need to address uncertainty estima-
038   tion in generative AI is even greater than that in other machine learning models (Gal & Ghahramani,
039   2016; Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017; Guo et al., 2017; Minderer et al., 2021), and yet there has
040   been limited research in this area (Kuhn et al., 2023; Manakul et al., 2023; Tian et al., 2023).
041
      In this work, we aim to formally define the problem of uncertainty estimation for LLMs and propose
042   methods to address it. As shown in Figure 1, uncertainty estimation for LLMs can be broadly defined
043   as the task of predicting the quality of the generated response based on the input. In this context,
044   “quality” typically refers to aspects such as confidence, truthfulness, and uncertainty. Assuming
045   access to a universal metric for evaluating the confidence of the output, the goal of uncertainty
046   estimation is to produce a confidence score that closely aligns with this metric. Given the inherent
047   randomness in LLMs, where incorrect responses can still be generated with positive probability,
048   uncertainty estimation serves as a crucial safeguard. It helps assess the reliability of responses,
049   enhance the trustworthiness of the model, and guide users on when to trust or question the output.
050   It is also worth noting that calibration is closely related and can be viewed as a subclass of uncer-
051   tainty estimation, where the metric corresponds to the conditional probability in the individual level.
052   Most studies on uncertainty estimation or calibration in language models focus on fixed-dimensional
053   prediction tasks (i.e., the output of the LLM only has one token limited in a finite set), such as senti-
      ment analysis, natural language inference, and commonsense reasoning (Zhou et al., 2023; Si et al.,

      This work explores a simple supervised method motivated by two ideas in the existing literature on
076
      LLMs. First, prior work on uncertainty estimation for LLMs primarily focused on designing uncer-
077
      tainty metrics in an unsupervised way by examining aspects like the generated outputs’ consistency,
078   similarity, entropy, and other relevant characteristics (Lin et al., 2023; Manakul et al., 2023; Kuhn
079   et al., 2023; Hou et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2022; Kuhn et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024). The absence
080   of the need for knowledge of the model’s weights enables their application to some black-box or
081   gray-box models. Second, a growing stream of literature argues that hidden layers’ activation values
082   within the LLMs offer insights into the LLMs’ knowledge and confidence (Slobodkin et al., 2023;
083   Ahdritz et al., 2024; Duan et al., 2024). It has shown success in other fields of LLMs, like hallucina-
084   tion detection (CH-Wang et al., 2023; Azaria & Mitchell, 2023; Ahdritz et al., 2024). Based on this
085   argument, white-box LLMs, which allow access to more of LLMs’ inner values, such as logits and
086
      hidden layers, are believed to have the capacity to offer a more nuanced understanding and improved
      uncertainty estimation results (Verma et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Plaut et al., 2024).
087
088   Both of the above approaches, however, have key limitations. For the unsupervised metrics, given
089   the complexity of LLMs’ underlying architectures, semantic information may be diluted when pro-
090   cessing through self-attention mechanisms and during token encoding/decoding. For the second
091   idea, the requirements of hidden layer features restrict its application to close-source/black-box
092
      LLMs. In this paper, we combine the strengths of these two ideas by proposing a general super-
      vised learning method and pipeline design that address these limitations. Specifically, to incorporate
093
      more features (e.g., hidden layers) in estimating the uncertainty, we train an external uncertainty es-
094
      timation model in a supervised way to estimate the uncertainty/confidence of the response generated
095   from an LLM (target LLM). As the quality of the response reveals to what extent we should believe
096   the response is correct, we formulate this supervised uncertainty estimation problem as a regression
097   task and prepare the labels in the training dataset by measuring the response’s quality. To extend our
098   method to black-box LLMs, we allow the semantic features of the question-response pair to come
099   from another language model (tool LLM). The overall pipeline of this method is shown in Figure 2.
100
      Our contributions are four-fold:
101
102   - First, we formally define the task of uncertainty estimation, while some of the existing literature ei-
103   ther does not distinguish uncertainty estimation and uncertainty calibration or misuses and confuses
104
      the terminologies of uncertainty and hallucination.
105   - Second, we adopt a supervised method for uncertainty estimation that is intuitive, easy to imple-
106   ment, and executable even on black-box LLMs. Leveraging supervised labels from the uncertainty
107   metric, our approach sets an upper bound for the performance of all unsupervised methods, repre-
      senting the highest achievable performance for these approaches.

      1.1   R ELATED LITERATURE
133
134   The uncertainty estimation and calibration for traditional machine learning is relatively well-studied
135   (Abdar et al., 2021; Gawlikowski et al., 2023). However, with the rapid development of LLMs,
136   there is a pressing need to better understand the uncertainty for LLMs’ responses, and measuring
137   the uncertainty from sentences instead of a fixed-dimension output is more challenging. One stream
138   of work has been focusing on unsupervised methods that leverage entropy (Malinin & Gales, 2021),
139
      similarity (Fomicheva et al., 2020; Lin et al., 2022), semantic (Kuhn et al., 2023; Duan et al., 2023),
      logit or hidden states’ information (Kadavath et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024; Su et al., 2024; Plaut
140
      et al., 2024) to craft an uncertainty metric that helps to quantify uncertainty. For black-box models,
141
      some of the metrics can be computed based on multiple sampled output of the LLMs (Malinin &
142   Gales, 2021; Lin et al., 2023; Manakul et al., 2023; Chen & Mueller, 2023); while for white-box
143   models, more information such as the output’s distribution, the value of the logit and hidden layers
144   make computing the uncertainty metric easier. We also refer to Desai & Durrett (2020); Zhang
145   et al. (2021); Ye & Durrett (2021); Si et al. (2022); Quach et al. (2023); Kumar et al. (2023); Mohri
146   & Hashimoto (2024) for other related uncertainty estimation methods such as conformal prediction.
147   We defer more discussions on related literature, in particular, on the topics of hallucination detection
148   and information in hidden layers of LLMs, to Appendix A.

150   2     P ROBLEM S ETUP
151
152   Consider the following environment where one interacts with LLMs through prompts and responses:
153   An LLM is given with an input prompt x = (x1 , x2 , ..., xk ) ∈ X with xi ∈ V representing the i-th
154   token of the prompt. Here V denotes the vocabulary for all the tokens. Then the LLM randomly
155   generates its response y = (y1 , y2 , ..., ym ) ∈ Y following the probability distribution
156                                                yj ∼ pθ (·|x, y1 , y2 , ..., yj−1 ).
157
158
      Here the probability distribution pθ denotes the distribution (over vocabulary V) as the LLM’s out-
      put, and θ encapsulates all the parameters of the LLM. The conditional part includes the prompt x
159
      and all the tokens y1 , y2 , ..., yj−1 generated preceding the current position.
160
161   We consider using the LLM for some downstream NLP tasks such as question answering, multiple
      choice, and machine translation. Such a task usually comes with an evaluation/scoring function that


                                                                        3
162
      evaluates the quality of the generated response s(·, ·) : Y × Y → [0, 1]. For each pair of (x, y),
163   the evaluation function rates the response y with the score z := s(y, ytrue ) where ytrue is the true
164   response for the prompt x. The true response ytrue is usually decided by factual truth, humans, or
165   domain experts, and we can assume it follows a distribution condition on the prompt x. It does not
166   hurt to assume a larger score represents a better answer; z = 1 indicates a perfect answer, while
167   z = 0 says the response y is off the target.
168
      We define the task of uncertainty estimation for LLMs as the learning of a function g that predicts
169   the score
170                                         g(x, y) ≈ E [s(y, ytrue )|x, y]                                (1)
171   where the expectation on the right-hand side is taken with respect to the (possible) randomness
172   of the true response ytrue , and for notational clarity, we omit the dependence of ytrue on x. We
173   emphasize two points on this task definition: The uncertainty function g takes the prompt x and y
174   as its inputs. This implies (i) the true and predicted uncertainty score can and should depend on the
175   specific realization of the response y, not just x (Zhang et al., 2021; Kuhn et al., 2023), and (ii) the
176   uncertainty function g does not require the true response ytrue as the input.
177   We note that a significant body of literature explores uncertainty estimation and calibration in lan-
178   guage models (Zhou et al., 2023; Si et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2022; Desai & Durrett, 2020). They
179   primarily focus on classification tasks where outputs are limited to a finite set of tokens (i.e., y con-
180   tains only one element). In contrast, our work extends this to allow free-form responses, and the
181   ability to handle variable-length outputs aligns more closely with current advancements in LLMs.

233

234

236   and extract the corresponding hidden layers’ activations of the LLM.
237   Grey-box features: Entropy- or probability-related outputs.          The entropy of a discrete distribution

      4.1   U NCERTAINTY ESTIMATION V. S . UNCERTAINTY CALIBRATION
277   So far in this paper, we focus on the uncertainty estimation task which aims to predict the quality of
278   the response to reveal whether the LLM makes mistakes in its response or not. There is a different but
279   related task known as the uncertainty calibration problem. In comparison, the uncertainty calibration
280   aims to ensure that the output from the uncertainty estimation model for (1) conveys a probabilistic
281   meaning. That is, g(x, y) is defined as the probability that y is true. This is compatible with our
282   method by replacing the quality s(y, ytrue ) with 1 {y ∈ Ytrue }, where Ytrue is a set containing all the
283   possible true responses. Another aspect of the relation between our uncertainty estimation method
284   and uncertainty calibration is that our method can be followed by any recalibration methods for ML
285
      models to form a pipeline for calibration. And intuitively, a better uncertainty estimation/prediction
      will lead to a better-calibrated uncertainty model, which is also verified in our numerical experiments
286
      in Appendix C.

      4.2   W HY HIDDEN LAYERS AS FEATURES ?
289
290   In this subsection, we provide a simple theoretical explanation for why the hidden activations of
291   the LLM can be useful in uncertainty estimation. Consider a binary classification task where the
292   features X ∈ Rd and the label Y ∈ {0, 1} are drawn from a distribution P. We aim to learn a model
293   f : Rd → [0, 1] that predicts the label Y from the feature vector X, and the learning of the model
294   employs a loss function l(·, ·) : [0, 1] × [0, 1] → R.
295   Proposition 4.1. Let F be the class of measurable function that maps from Rd to [0, 1]. Under the
296   cross-entropy loss l(y, ŷ) = y log(ŷ) + (1 − y) log(1 − ŷ), the function f ∗ that minimizes the loss
297                                         f ∗ = arg min E [l(Y, f (X))]
298                                                 f ∈F
299   is the Bayes optimal classifier f ∗ (x) = P(Y = 1|X = x) where the expectation and the probability
300   are taken with respect to (X, Y ) ∼ P. Moreover, the following conditional independence holds
301                                              Y ⊥ X | f ∗ (X).
302
303
      The proposition is not technical and it can be easily proved by using the structure of f ∗ (X) so
304   we refer the proof to Berger (2013). It states a nice property of the cross-entropy loss that the
305   function learned under the cross-entropy loss coincides with the Bayes optimal classifier. Note that
306   this is contingent on two requirements. First, the function class F is the measurable function class.
307   Second, it requires the function f ∗ learned through the population loss rather than the empirical
308   loss/risk. The proposition also states one step further on conditional independence Y ⊥ X | f ∗ (X).
309   This means all the information related to the label Y that is contained in X is summarized in the
310   prediction function f ∗ . This intuition suggests that for classic uncertainty estimation problems, when
311   a prediction model fˆ : Rd → [0, 1] is well-trained, the predicted score fˆ(X) should capture all the
312   information about the true label Y contained in the features X, without relying on the features of
313   X. This indeed explains why the classic uncertainty estimation and calibration methods only work
314   with the predicted score fˆ(X) for re-calibration, including Platt scaling (Platt et al., 1999), isotonic
315
      regression (Zadrozny & Elkan, 2002), temperature scaling (Guo et al., 2017), etc.
316   When it comes to uncertainty estimation for LLMs, which is different from calibration and LLMs’
317   structure is much more complex, we will no longer have conditional independence, and that requires
318   additional procedures to retrieve more information on Y . The following supporting corollary states
319   that when the underlying loss function ˜l does not possess this nice property (the Bayes classifier
320   minimizes the loss point-wise) of the cross-entropy loss, the conditional independence will collapse.
321   Corollary 4.2. Suppose the loss function ˜l satisfies

331

332

333
      ML models, the cross-entropy loss, which is commonly used for training the model, is aligned
334   toward the uncertainty calibration objective. When it comes to uncertainty estimation for LLMs,
335   the objective can be different from calibration, and the LLMs are often pretrained with some other
336   loss functions (for example, the negative log-likelihood loss for next-token prediction) on diverse

342   it also explains why we do not see much similar treatment in the mainstream uncertainty estimation
343

351   LLMs. For our numerical experiments, we mainly consider three open-source LLMs, LLaMA2-
352   7B (L-7B) (Touvron et al., 2023), LLaMA3-8B (L-8B)(AI@Meta, 2024) and Gemma-7B (G-7B)

356   Tasks and Datasets. We mainly consider three tasks for uncertainty estimation, question answer-

359   the labeled datasets for these tasks are in the form of {(xi , yi,true )}ni=1 where xi can be viewed as
360   the prompt for the i-th sample and yi,true the true response. We adopt the few-shot prompting when
361   generating the LLM’s response yi , and we use 5 examples in the prompt of the multiple-choice task
362   and 3 examples for the remaining natural language generation tasks. This enables the LLM’s in-
363
      context learning ability (Radford et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2023) and ensures the LLM’s responses
      are in a desirable format. We defer more details of the few-shot prompting to Appendix D.1.
364
365   Benchmarks. We compare our approach with a number of the state-of-the-art benchmarks for the
366   problem. Manakul et al. (2023) give a comprehensive survey of the existing methods and compare
367   four distinct measures for predicting sentence generation uncertainty. The measures are based on
368   either the maximum or average values of entropy or probability across the sentence, including Max
369
      Likelihood, Avg Likelihood, Max Ent, and Avg Ent (denoted as MaxL, AvgL, MaxE, AvgE) defined
      in Table 5. We note that each of these measures can be applied as a single uncertainty estimator, and
370
      they are all applied in an unsupervised manner that does not require additional supervised training.
371
      In particular, in applying these measures for the MMLU dataset, since the answer only contains one
372   token from {A, B, C, D}, we use the probabilities and the entropy (over these four tokens) as the
373   benchmarks which represent the probability of the most likely choice and the entropy of all choices,
374   respectively. Kuhn et al. (2023) generate multiple answers, compute their entropy in a semantic
375   sense, and define the quantity as semantic entropy. This semantic-entropy uncertainty (SU) thus can
376   be used as an uncertainty estimator for the LLM’s responses. Tian et al. (2023) propose the approach
377   of asking the LLM for its confidence (denoted as A4U) which directly obtains the uncertainty score
      from the LLM itself.

378
      Our methods. We follow the discussions in Section 3.3 and implement three versions of our pro-
379   posed supervised approach: black-box supervised (Bb-S), grey-box supervised (Gb-S), and white-
380   box supervised (Wb-S). These models have the same pipeline of training the uncertainty estimation
381   model and the difference is only on the availability of the LLM. For the Bb-S method, we use the
382   Gemma-7B as the model qθ to evaluate the uncertainty of LLaMA2-7B/LLaMA3-8B pθ (treated as
383   a black-box), and reversely, use LLaMA2-7B to evaluate Gemma-7B. The supervised uncertainty
384   model ĝ is trained based on the random forest model (Breiman, 2001). Details on the feature con-
385   struction and the training of the random forest model are deferred to Appendix D.2.
386   Performance metrics. For the model evaluation, we follow Filos et al. (2019); Kuhn et al. (2023)
387   and compare the performance of our methods against the benchmark using the generated uncertainty
388   score to predict whether the answer is correct. The area under the receiver operator characteristic
389   curve (AUROC) metric is employed to measure the performance of the uncertainty estimation. As
390   noted in Section 4.1, AUROC works as a good metric for uncertainty estimation whereas for uncer-
391   tainty calibration, we follow the more standard calibration metrics and present the results in Section
392   C.

526   6   C ONCLUSIONS
527
528
529   In this paper, we study the problem of uncertainty estimation and calibration for LLMs. We follow
530   a simple and standard supervised idea and use the labeled NLP datasets to train an uncertainty
531
      estimation model for LLMs. Our finding is that, first, the proposed supervised methods have better
      performances than the existing unsupervised methods. Second, the hidden activations of the LLMs
532
      contain uncertainty information about the LLMs’ responses. Third, the black-box regime of our
533
      approach (Bb-S) provides a new approach to estimating the uncertainty of closed-source LLMs.
534   Lastly, we distinguish the task of uncertainty estimation from uncertainty calibration and show that
535   a better uncertainty estimation model leads to better calibration performance. One limitation of our
536   proposed supervised method is that it critically relies on the labeled data. For the scope of our paper,
537   we restrict the discussion to the NLP tasks and datasets. One future direction is to utilize the human-
538   annotated data for LLMs’ responses to train a supervised uncertainty estimation model for open-
539   question prompts. We believe the findings that the supervised method gives a better performance
      and the hidden activations contain the uncertainty information will persist.

743   A    M ORE R ELATED L ITERATURE
744
745   Hallucination detection. Recently, there is a trend of adopting uncertainty estimation approaches
746   for hallucination detection. The rationale is that the information of the value of logits and the hid-
747
      den states contain some of the LLMs’ beliefs about the trustworthiness of its generated output. By
      taking the activations of hidden layers as input, Azaria & Mitchell (2023) train a classifier to predict
748
      hallucinations, and Verma et al. (2023) develop epistemic neural networks aimed at reducing hal-
749
      lucinations. Slobodkin et al. (2023) demonstrate that the information from hidden layers of LLMs’
750   output can indicate the answerability of an input query, providing indirect insights into hallucination
751   occurrences. Chen et al. (2024) develop an unsupervised metric that leverages the internal states
752   of LLMs to perform hallucination detection. More related works on hallucination detection can be
753   found in CH-Wang et al. (2023); Duan et al. (2024); Xu et al. (2024). While there is a lack of a
754   rigorous definition of hallucination, and its definition varies in the above-mentioned literature, the
755   uncertainty estimation problem can be well defined, and our results on uncertainty estimation can
      also help the task of hallucination detection.


                                                        14
756
      Leveraging LLMs’ hidden activation. The exploration of hidden states within LLMs has been
757   studied to better understand LLMs’ behavior. Mielke et al. (2022) improve the linguistic calibration
758   performance of a controllable chit-chat model by fine-tuning it using a calibrator trained on the
759   hidden states, Burns et al. (2022) utilizes hidden activations in an unsupervised way to represent
760   knowledge about the trustfulness of their outputs. Liu et al. (2023) show that LLMs’ linguistic
761   outputs and their internal states can offer conflicting information about truthfulness, and determining
762   whether outputs or internal states are more reliable sources of information often varies from one
763   scenario to another. By taking the activations of hidden layers as input, Ahdritz et al. (2024) employ
764   a linear probe to show that hidden layers’ information from LLMs can be used to differentiate
765
      between epistemic and aleatoric uncertainty. Duan et al. (2024) experimentally reveal the variations
      in hidden layers’ activations when LLMs generate true versus false responses in their hallucination
766
      detection task. Lastly, Li et al. (2024) enhance the truthfulness of LLMs during inference time by
767
      adjusting the hidden activations’ values in specific directions.
768
769   We also remark on the following two aspects:
770
771          • Fine-tuning: For all the numerical experiments in this paper, we do not perform any fine-
772            tuning with respect to the underlying LLMs. While the fine-tuning procedure generally
773
               boosts the LLMs’ performance on a downstream task, our methods can still be applied for
               a fine-tuned LLM, which we leave as future work.
774
775          • Hallucination: The hallucination problem has been widely studied in the LLM literature.
776            Yet, as mentioned earlier, it seems there is no consensus on a rigorous definition of what hal-
777            lucination refers to in the context of LLMs. For example, when an image classifier wrongly
778            classifies a cat image as a dog, we do not say the image classifier hallucinates, then why
779            or when we should say the LLMs hallucinate when they make a mistake? Comparatively,
780
               the uncertainty estimation problem is more well-defined, and we provide a mathematical
               formulation for the uncertainty estimation task for LLMs. Also, we believe our results
781
               on uncertainty estimation can also help with a better understanding of the hallucination
782
               phenomenon and tasks such as hallucination detection.

      B.1   L AYER COMPARISON
792
793   For general LLMs, each token is associated with a relatively large number of hidden layers (32 layers
794   for LLaMA2-7B for example), each of which is represented by high-dimensional vectors (4096 for
795   LLaMA2-7B). Thus it is generally not a good practice to incorporate all hidden layers as features
796   for the uncertainty estimation due to this dimensionality. Previous works find that the middle layer
797   and the last layer activations of the LLM’s last token contain the most useful features for supervised
798   learning (Burns et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024; Ahdritz et al., 2024; Azaria & Mitchell, 2023).
799   To investigate the layer-wise effect for uncertainty estimation, we implement our Wb-S method
800   with features different in two aspects: (i) different layers within the LLM architecture, specifically
801
      focusing on the middle and last layers (e.g., LLaMA2-7B and LLaMA3-8B: 16th and 32nd layers
      out of 32 layers with 4096 dimensions; Gemma-7B: 14th and 28th layers out of 28 layers with
802
      3072 dimensions); and (ii) position of token activations, including averaging hidden activations
803
      over all the prompt/answer tokens or utilizing the hidden activation of the last token. The second
804   aspect makes sense when the output contains more than one token, so we conduct this experiment
805   on the natural language generation tasks only. Figure 3 gives a visualization of the comparison
806   result. While the performances of these different feature extraction ways are quite similar in terms
807   of performance across different tasks and LLMs, activation features from the middle layer generally
808   perform better than the last layer. This may come from the fact that the last layer focuses more on
809   the generation of the next token instead of summarizing information of the whole sentence, as has
      been discussed by Azaria & Mitchell (2023).

903   In Section 4.1, we distinguish the two tasks of uncertainty estimation and uncertainty calibration.
904   Throughout the paper, we have been focused on improving the performance on the task of uncer-
905   tainty estimation – to predict when the LLM is uncertain about its response. Generally, a better
906   uncertainty estimation model leads to one with better calibration performance. The calibration (or
907   recalibration) of the uncertainty estimation model can be indeed reduced to the classic ML setting
908   which does not involve the LLM. Table 4 gives the calibration performance and we see an advan-
909
      tage of our supervised methods over benchmark methods consistent with the AUROC performance
      in Table 1. We adopt the histogram binning method here because we find that the temperature scal-
910
      ing method and the Platt scaling method will give all predicted scores concentrated within a small
911
      range such as [0.2, 0.6]. We also do not exclude the possibility that the other calibration methods can
912   give even better performance. The point to make here is that uncertainty estimation and uncertainty
913   calibration are two closely related tasks. Note that (i) a better uncertainty estimation model leads to
914   a better calibration performance and (ii) the LLMs are pretrained and not designed for these NLP
915   tasks in the first place (see Section 4.2) so that there is no uncertainty score readily available (as the
916   predicted probabilities for the image classifiers); we emphasize the importance of an extra uncer-
917   tainty estimation procedure as our supervised one so to extract the uncertainty information from the
      inside of the LLMs.

1186   In Section B, we show the advantage of utilizing the hidden activations of the answer from the
1187   middle layer of the LLM to estimate the uncertainty in Wb-S. In this section, we further discuss the
       impact of employing the hidden activations from the question in the Wb-S.


                                                              22
1188
       The motivation stems from the observation that within the transformer architecture, although the
1189   hidden activation of a question’s last token (referred to as the question’s activation) is forwarded
1190   to obtain the hidden activation of the answer’s last token (referred to as the answer’s activation),
1191   implying that the answer’s activation incorporates the question’s activation information, it has been
1192   discovered that concatenating the question’s activation with the answer’s activation offers additional
1193   insights into the answer’s uncertainty (Duan et al., 2024). We would like to further investigate the
1194   effectiveness of incorporating the question’s activation along with the answer’s activation into the
1195   supervised setting.
1196   We experiment with three feature combinations in our supervised setting: (i) Question: we use
1197   the hidden activation of the last token of the question from the middle layer, incorporated with the
1198   entropy- or probability-related features of the question (10 features in total listed in the right column
1199   of Table 5) if it is a natural language generation task, otherwise incorporated with all the features
1200   in Gb-S; (ii) Answer: we use the hidden activation of the last token of the answer from the middle
1201   layer incorporated with all the features used in Gb-S; (iii) Question-Answer: we use the last-token
1202   hidden activation of both the question and answer from the middle layer and all the features in Gb-S.
1203
       We compare their performance with Gb-S in Figure 8 and present the following observations.
1204   Question itself cannot capture enough uncertainty information. From Figure 8, we observe
1205   that the method Bb-S consistently outperforms Question across all these tasks. This implies that
1206   incorporating the features relating to the question only cannot provide enough information about the
1207   uncertainty of the answer. This aligns with the inferior performance of the sample-based method
1208   (Kuhn et al., 2023) we tested in the earlier sections. In these methods, the uncertainty score is used
1209
       to estimate the language model’s uncertainty about the question. This result implies that uncertainty
       cannot be captured in the question by the language model without generating the answer.
1210
1211   Question’s hidden activation cannot help to generate more uncertainty information Again from
1212   Figure 8, by comparing the performance of Answer and Question-Answer, we find that the inclu-
1213   sion of question’s activation has little impact on improving the performance. This shows that the
1214   uncertainty from the question might have already been well encoded in the last token activation of
1215
       the answer.

1287   In this section, we show some examples of the wrong answers the LLM generated and explore how
1288   different methods understand the LLM’s uncertainty. The wrong answers are selected from those
1289   samples where the LLM makes wrong predictions.
1290   Since we let the LLM output the greedy answer, which could be wrong, we expect an ideal un-
1291   certainty estimation model to output a high confidence score when the LLM generates the correct
1292   answer, and give a low confidence score when the LLM outputs the wrong answer. By looking at
1293   different wrong answers generated by the LLM, we note that although our approach sometimes gives
1294   a high confidence score on a wrong answer generated by the LLM, at other times it shows desirable
1295   properties such as giving higher uncertainty scores to better answers, and giving low confidence
       score when LLM does not know the answer.


                                                                                               24
1296
       Our illustrative examples are generated as follows: For questions where the LLM’s greedy response
1297   is incorrect, we also extract the correct answer from the dataset and additional answers randomly
1298   generated by the LLM with lower probabilities than the greedy answer. Along with these answers,
1299   we also compute the answers’ corresponding metrics and features so that we can observe how they
1300   behave with different outputs. We conduct this experiment in the test dataset of TriviaQA, in which
1301   both the question and answer are short. We summarize the ways that our uncertainty estimation
1302   model behaves as follows:
1303
1304
             • Confidently support a wrong answer. The LLMs are confident that the wrong greedy
               answer is true and assign a high confidence score. Moreover, the LLMs give low uncer-
1305
               tainty scores to the correct answers, suggesting a lack of knowledge about these questions.
1306
               We give an example of LLaMA2-7B and Gemma-7B in Figure 11 and 12. Note that in
1307           both examples, our method assigns a low uncertainty score to the correct answer and a
1308           much higher uncertainty score to the wrong answer. In contrast, the unsupervised grey-box
1309           methods assign higher uncertainty scores to the correct answer.
1310
             • Confidently reject a wrong answer. We give examples from LLaMA2-7B and Gemma-
1311           7B in Figure 13 and 14. The uncertainty estimation model gives a higher score to the true
1312           answer or answers that are better than the wrong answer. This means that for these ques-
1313           tions, our model actually knows which answer is better and can assign uncertainty scores
1314           accordingly. In contrast, the unsupervised methods tend to assign much higher uncertainty
1315           scores to the greedy (wrong) answer.
1316         • Unconfident about any answer. Due to the lack of knowledge, the LLM may not know
1317           the true answer. We show the examples in Figure 15 and 16. From these examples, we can
1318           see that the model assigns almost the same uncertainty scores to these generated answers,
1319           including the true answer. In this scenario, the uncertainty estimation model is uncertain
1320           about the correctness of any answer. Furthermore, it is interesting to note that the unsuper-
1321           vised methods exhibit similar behavior, assigning almost similar scores to other answers
1322           as well, albeit with much higher uncertainty scores. This differs from the previous two
1323
               cases, where the unsupervised method behaved differently from our uncertainty estimation
