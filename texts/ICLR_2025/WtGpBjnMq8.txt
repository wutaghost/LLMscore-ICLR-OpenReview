011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Source-free active domain adaptation (SFADA) addresses the challenge of adapt-
014            ing a pre-trained model to new domains without access to source data while min-
015
               imizing the need for target domain annotations. This scenario is particularly rele-
               vant in real-world applications where data privacy, storage limitations, or labeling
016
               costs are significant concerns. Key challenges in SFADA include selecting the
017
               most informative samples from the target domain for labeling, effectively lever-
018            aging both labeled and unlabeled target data, and adapting the model without re-
019            lying on source domain information. Additionally, existing methods often strug-
020            gle with noisy or outlier samples and may require impractical progressive label-
021            ing during training. To effectively select more informative samples without fre-
022            quently requesting human annotations, we propose the Propensity-driven Uncer-
023            tainty Learning (ProULearn) framework. ProULearn utilizes a novel homogene-
024            ity propensity estimation mechanism combined with correlation index calculation
025            to evaluate feature-level relationships. This approach enables the identification
026
               of representative and challenging samples while avoiding noisy outliers. Addi-
               tionally, we develop a central correlation loss to refine pseudo-labels and create
027
               compact class distributions during adaptation. In this way, ProULearn effectively
028
               bridges the domain gap and maximizes adaptation performance. The principles
029            of informative sample selection underlying ProULearn have broad implications
030            beyond SFADA, offering benefits across various deep learning tasks where identi-
031            fying key data points or features is crucial. Extensive experiments on four bench-
032            mark datasets demonstrate that ProULearn outperforms state-of-the-art methods
033            in domain adaptation scenarios.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   In recent years, convolution neural networks have achieved remarkable success across a wide range
039
      of computer vision tasks, including image classification, object detection, and semantic segmen-
      tation. However, the performance of these models often degrades significantly when faced with
040
      substantial domain shifts between training and testing distributions. This degradation is particularly
041
      problematic in real-world applications where we need to transfer a pre-trained model from a source
042   domain to different downstream target domains. Moreover, obtaining human annotations for large
043   datasets or new domains is time-consuming and expensive. To address these challenges, researchers
044   have proposed various unsupervised domain adaptation (UDA) techniques (Long et al., 2018; Xu
045   et al., 2019). These methods aim to adapt models trained on a labeled source domain to unlabeled
046   target domains, thereby reducing the need for extensive manual annotation in the target domains.
047
      However, traditional UDA approaches require simultaneous access to both source and target domain
048   data during the adaptation process. This requirement induces several practical challenges, including
049   increased storage and training resource demands as well as privacy concerns associated with sharing
050   or storing source domain data. To overcome these limitations, we focus on a more practical setting
051   known as source-free active domain adaptation (SFADA). In this scenario, the model is initially
052   trained on the source domain, but during adaptation, only the target domain data is available. Ad-
053   ditionally, the active learning component allows for selective annotation of a small subset of target
      domain samples to maximize adaptation performance while minimizing labeling costs.

072   The active learning aspect of SFADA is particularly significant, as it addresses a fundamental chal-
073
      lenge in machine learning: identifying the most informative data points or features for model train-
      ing and adaptation. This challenge extends far beyond domain adaptation, influencing areas such
074
      as medical image analysis, natural language processing, and robotics. By developing more sophis-
075
      ticated methods for informative sample selection, we can improve model efficiency, reduce data
076   requirements, and enhance performance across a wide range of deep learning applications.
077
078   Current methods in this field (Li et al., 2022; 2023b; Du & Li, 2023) normally utilize techniques such
079
      as pseudo-labeling, self-training, or adversarial learning to leverage the knowledge encoded in the
      pre-trained source model and adapt it to the target domain with minimal supervision. They often se-
080
      lect active samples where the model has high predictive uncertainties. However, these samples have
081
      a great probability of belonging to noise or outliers of the class, which can influence the prediction of
082   neighbor samples and lead to performance degradation during adaptation. In addition, some of the
083   active domain adaptation methods (Xie et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2023; Du & Li, 2023) progressively
084   request human annotation during the training process, which is not practical and inconvenient.
085
      To improve the domain adaptation efficacy and address existing challenges on informative sample
086
      selection without frequently requesting human annotations, we propose a Propensity-driven Uncer-
087
      tainty Learning framework (ProULearn). This framework incorporates informative sample selection,
088   pseudo-labeling, and correlation alignment for domain adaptation. At its core, ProULearn utilizes a
089   homogeneity propensity estimation mechanism and correlation index to evaluate the grouping con-
090   dition and model prediction confidence of each data point. This approach aims to select samples
091   that are grouped with others with low prediction confidence, enabling the model to learn the target
092   domain information it currently lacks. Our framework further enhances performance by combin-
093   ing the homogeneity propensity score with correlation relationships of samples to different class
094   centroids, facilitating accurate pseudo-label assignment. To improve class distinction, we introduce
095   a central correlation loss that encourages the model to concentrate on class centroids, resulting in
096
      more compact and easily distinguishable class clusters. The effectiveness of our approach is visually
      demonstrated in Figure 1, which presents t-SNE visualizations (Van der Maaten & Hinton, 2008) of
097
      the data distribution before and after adaptation. Initially, most of the selected active samples (red
098
      stars) are shown to be strategically grouped with other samples in high data density areas. Following
099   the adaptation process, we observe a significant concentration of most clusters around these selected
100   samples, illustrating the model’s successful learning from these key data points and its improved
101   domain adaptation capabilities. The contributions of our work can be summarized as follows:
102
103          • We propose a novel ProULearn (Propensity-driven Uncertainty Learning) framework that
104            addresses the challenges of source-free active domain adaptation tasks. ProULearn inte-
105            grates three key components: informative sample selection, pseudo-labeling, and correla-
106
               tion alignment, to effectively learn target domain features in cross-domain scenarios.
107          • An innovative sample selection mechanism is developed within ProULearn, which com-
               bines homogeneity propensity estimation and correlation index calculation. This mecha-

118   2     R ELATED W ORKS ON ACTIVE L EARNING
119
120   Active learning is getting great attention nowadays. Compared with traditional supervised learning
121   strategies which heavily rely on human annotations, active learning aims to utilize the pre-trained
122   model on unlabeled downstream tasks or domains with selective training samples. It is a more practi-
123   cal training scene which greatly reduces the annotation costs and time. People adopt active learning
124
      concepts in various deep learning tasks including image or text classification (Kim et al., 2021;
      Seo et al., 2022), object detection (Su et al., 2020; Yuan et al., 2021), person re-identification (Liu
125
      et al., 2019; Teng et al., 2023), etc. Recently, people starting to introduce active learning into do-
126
      main adaptation tasks to reduce the labeling cost for transferring the model from one domain to
127   another (Prabhu et al., 2021; Mathelin et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2023). Sun et al. (2023) progressively
128   requested and augmented active samples as well as their expanded neighbour to refine the network.
129   Xie et al. (2022) alleviated the domain gap by using regularization terms. Meanwhile, it incorporates
130   domain characteristics and instance uncertainty into the active sample selection process.
131
      Recently, source-free domain adaptation (SFDA) has gathered some attention. It aims to transfer a
132
      pre-trained model from a source domain to target domains without access to the source domain data.
133   A more detailed introduction to SFDA can be found in the Appendix. While this approach offers
134   potential benefits in reducing annotation efforts during adaptation, current research has encountered
135   challenges in achieving substantial improvements. Given these constraints, our paper focuses on a
136   more practical and effective paradigm: source-free active domain adaptation (SFADA). This strategy
137   incorporates active learning techniques to enhance model performance and adaptability.

      3.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
143
      Source-free active domain adaptation (SFADA) addresses the challenges of adapting a pre-trained
144
      model from a source domain S to a target domain T without access to the original source data. In
145   the source domain, we have a labeled dataset S = (Xs , Ys ) used to train the initial model under
146   supervision. The adaptation phase focuses solely on the target domain T , which consists of a small
147   set of labeled data Tl and a large set of unlabeled data Tu . The complete target domain is thus T =
148   Tl ∪ Tu . The key constraint in SFADA is the limited labeling budget for Tl , which is expressed as a
149   percentage B%, determining the size of Tl . This setting presents two major challenges: (1) selecting
150   the most informative samples from Tu for labeling as Tl to maximize adaptation effectiveness; (2)
151   leveraging both labeled and unlabeled target data to adapt the model effectively.
152   To effectively select informative samples and adapt the pre-trained model to the target domain, we
153   proposed a Propensity-driven Uncertainty Learning (ProULearn) framework to address the SFADA
154   tasks. The overall training process can be found in Figure 2. In the following sections, details of
155   selecting samples, pseudo-labeling, and training process are introduced.
156

186   Homogeneity propensity estimation. To effectively identify the grouping condition of each sam-
187   ple from a global perspective, we introduce a homogeneity propensity estimation mechanism (HPE)
188   inspired by the isolation forest algorithm (Liu et al., 2008). The algorithm is first introduced for
189   abnormal data detection and we develop our HPE inspired by its structure to assign a homogene-
190   ity score to each sample. Our approach offers advantages over traditional unsupervised clustering
191
      methods, such as K-means (Lloyd, 1982) or HDBSCAN (Campello et al., 2013), which rely heavily
      on initial distributions and local cluster relationships. The HPE mechanism can capture the sam-
192
      ple grouping condition from the whole training set’s perspective and is particularly valuable when
193
      dealing with large domain gaps, where initial distributions may be inaccurate.
194
195   The HPE mechanism begins by constructing an ensemble of g separation trees. Each tree is built
196   using a random subset of the data. At each node of the tree, a random feature m is selected, and a
197
      split value v is randomly chosen between the minimum and maximum values of the selected feature
      in the current subset. The data is then split into left and right child nodes based on whether their
198
      value for feature m is less than or greater than v. As shown in Figure 3, this process continues until
199
      a maximum depth is reached or a node contains only one sample. Each node stores the depth in the
200   tree, the feature index used for splitting, and the split value. The splitting process ensures that the tree
201   adapts to the local density of the data, as the split value is chosen uniformly between the minimum
202   and maximum values of the selected feature in the current subset. This allows the tree to create more
203   splits in regions of high density and fewer splits in regions of low density. Consequently, samples
204   in dense regions typically have longer path lengths, reflecting their homogeneity, while samples in
205   sparse regions have shorter path lengths, indicating their potential anomalous nature.
206   The maximum depth of the tree is set to rmax = log2 (sample size). This depth limit prevents over-
207   fitting by avoiding unnecessarily deep trees that might capture noise rather than true data patterns
208   while allowing the tree to capture the essential structure of the data. The logarithmic relationship en-
209   sures that the tree depth scales reasonably with the sample size without increasing model complexity.
210   This design effectively handles datasets of varying sizes without manual tuning.

      5.1    H YPER - PARAMETER S ENSITIVITY A NALYSIS
473
474   As described in Section 3, our method incorporates several hyper-parameters to facilitate the selec-
475   tion of active samples, including the number of trees g in the HPE mechanism and the number of
476   neighbors K considered for each sample in the correlation index calculation. To better understand
477   how to determine the optimal hyper-parameters, we conducted extensive experiments on the Office-
478   31 dataset, varying g within the range [100, 400] and K within [4, 16]. The average performances
479   of Office-31 under different hyper-parameter combinations are recorded in Figure 4a.
480   As the figure shows, the maximum average performance occurs when g is 200 and K is 8 for
481   the Office-31 dataset. We applied a similar strategy to other datasets to determine their optimal
482   parameters. We found that setting the tree number g to 200 works well across all datasets, as this
483   parameter is more related to the feature output dimension rather than specific dataset properties. Our
484   empirical studies reveal that the number of trees g plays a crucial role of the HPE mechanism. When
485   g is too low, the estimation becomes unstable since the selection of sample and feature is random,
      leading to unreliable selection. Conversely, when g is too high, it may overfit to noise in the data.

      6     C ONCLUSION
531
      In this paper, we presented ProULearn, a novel Propensity-driven Uncertainty Learning framework
532
      for source-free active domain adaptation (SFADA). Our approach addresses the critical challenges
533
      in SFADA through innovative techniques for sample selection and model adaptation, comprising
534   a homogeneity propensity estimation mechanism and correlation relationship learning. ProULearn
535   effectively identifies informative samples while mitigating the impact of noisy outliers, and locates
536   these samples before training begins, eliminating the need for acquiring human labels during the
537   adaptation process. This proactive approach, combined with our training strategy that leads to more
538   compact and discriminative class distributions in the target domain, significantly enhances the practi-
539   cality of domain adaptation. Experiments across four benchmark datasets demonstrate ProULearn’s
      consistent superiority over state-of-the-art methods in various domain adaptation scenarios.

      A     R ELATED W ORKS ON S OURCE - FREE D OMAIN A DAPTATION
706
      Source-free domain adaptation (SFDA) has been of great interest to researchers in recent years. It
707
      aims to adapt a pre-trained model on the source domain to other domains without access to source
708   domain data. People apply this concept to many downstream tasks to solve real-world problems, for
709   example, fine-grained image classification (Wang et al., 2020; Pan et al., 2024a), object detection (Li
710   et al., 2021; VS et al., 2023), video analysis (Xu et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023a), etc. A large propor-
711   tion of SFDA methods transfer the source domain knowledge to the target domains by exploring
712   data properties (Li et al., 2024) like utilizing pseudo-labels (Liang et al., 2020), entropy minimiza-
713   tion, and contrastive learning strategies. Xia et al. (2021) proposed an adaptive adversarial network

715   the target domains. Shen et al. (2023) introduced a selective pseudo-labeling and feature alignment

      strategy for domain adaptation. Yi et al. (2023) tackled the SFDA problem by learning with noise

815   B.4   D ISCUSSIONS
816
817   The proposed ProULearn framework represents a significant advancement in SFADA through sev-
818   eral key innovations. First, our HPE mechanism, combined with correlation-based feature rela-
819   tionships, introduces a novel approach to sample selection that considers both global data struc-
820   ture and local feature distributions, enabling more reliable identification of informative samples and
821
      pseudo-label assignment. Unlike traditional methods that rely heavily on initial distributions or re-
      quire progressive labeling during training, ProULearn performs one-time sample selection before the
822
      adaptation process, making it particularly practical for real-world applications where continuous hu-
823
      man annotation is infeasible. Second, our correlation index fundamentally differs from conventional
824   distance metrics by capturing feature-level distribution relationships rather than spatial proximity,
825   making it more robust to domain shifts. This correlation-based approach, when integrated with HPE,
826   has demonstrated superior performance compared to traditional clustering methods. Furthermore,
827   the newly proposed central correlation loss and weighted cross-entropy loss incorporate these com-
828   ponents, creating more compact and discriminative class distributions. This cohesive integration of
829   novel components forms a comprehensive framework tailored for SFADA challenges, while the un-
830   derlying principles of our sample selection strategy offer broader applicability for various computer
831   vision tasks where identifying informative samples or features is crucial.

838   ImageNet-1K pre-trained weights. The hyperparameter g is set to 200 across all datasets as it pri-
839   marily depends on feature dimensionality, while K is tuned based on dataset characteristics: smaller
840   values (K=8) for Office-31 and Office-Home where classes have fewer samples, and larger values
841   for DomainNet-126 (K=40) and VisDA-2017 (K=84). These values were determined through ex-

      tensive experimentation as in the ablation studies (Section 5 in the paper) to optimize performance.
      Experiments are conducted on RTX A5000 GPUs using the PyTorch library (Paszke et al., 2019).

845   adaptation, the fine-tuning iteration is set to 30 epochs. The backbone and classifier’s learning rate

847   Xia et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023). The bottleneck layer’s learning rate is 10 times higher than the
848
      backbone. Optimization is performed using SGD with a 0.9 momentum. We conduct experiments
      mainly based on a 5% budget for active sampling, the same as other SFADA benchmarks. Batch

      sizes are set to 64 for Office-31 and 128 for other datasets. In addition, we also conduct experiments

      with 1% as well as 10% budget and the results are presented in Appendix Section E.1.
