009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Sparse coding aims to exploit the latent linear structure of the input data, trans-
012            forming dense data into sparse data, thereby improving data processing efficiency.
013            However, many real-word signals cannot be expressed linearly, rendering the tra-
014            ditional sparse coding algorithms ineffective. One potential solution is to expand
015            the dimensions of data. In this paper, we verify that the feature mapping of Radial
016            Basis Function (RBF) kernel contains infinite dimensional information, and it does
017            not significantly increase the computational complexity. Based on this, we pro-
018            pose to explore the l1 -norm regularization sparse coding method with RBF kernel,
019            and provides a solution with convergence guarantees by leveraging the principle
020
               of coordinate descent. Additionally, to accelerate the optimization process, we in-
               troduce a novel two-stage acceleration strategy, based on theoretical analysis and
021
               empirical observations. Experimental results demonstrate that the two-stage ac-
022
               celeration strategy can reduce processing time by up to 90%. Furthermore, when
023            the data size is compressed to about 2% of its original scale, the NMAE metric of
024            the proposed method reaches as low as 0.0824 to 0.2195, achieving a significant
025            improvement of up to 47% compared to traditional linear sparse coding methods
026            and 36% compared to other kernel sparse coding techniques.

062   While increasing the dimensionality of input data can enhance its discriminability, facilitating sparse
063   coding analysis, it also inevitably introduce problems, including indeterminate forms and scales of
064   data dimensionality enhancement, and high computational complexity. It is known that kernel trick
065   provides an effective way to expand data dimensions with reducing computational complexity, and
066   there are various categories of kernel functions (Gao et al., 2010). To select a good kernel function,
067   we must be aware that the richer the dimensional information contained in the feature map, the better
068   the performance of the corresponding kernel function. Therefore, this article delves into the specific
069
      form of feature mapping corresponding to the Radial Basis Function (RBF) kernel and analyzes its
      potential to demonstrate great performance. Ultimately, we solidify our decision to employ the RBF
070
      kernel-based approach as a means to tackle the challenge of enhancing sparse coding efficiency.
071
072   Contributions. The main contributions of this work include:
073   • We verify that the feature mapping of the Radial Basis Function (RBF) kernel contains infinite di-
074   mensional information, and it does not significantly increase the computational complexity. Based
075
      on this, we explore the l1 -norm regularized sparse coding method with RBF kernel, and provides a
      solution with convergence guarantees by leveraging the principle of coordinate descent.
076
      • To reduce the computational complexity of the standard kernel sparse coding method based on
077
      coordinate descent, a novel two-stage acceleration strategy is introduced. This strategy focuses on
078   updating the initially predicted nonzero weight factors, and effectively predicts zero weight factors
079   to skip the computation of the corresponding intermediate variables.
080   • Our experiments demonstrate that the proposed two-stage acceleration strategy significantly re-
081   duces processing time by up to 90%. Additionally, our method outperforms both traditional linear
082   sparse coding and other kernel sparse coding methods. Specifically, when the data size is com-
083   pressed to about 2% of its original scale, the NMAE metric of the proposed method reaches as low
084   as 0.0824 to 0.2195, achieving a significant improvement of up to 47% compared to traditional linear
085   sparse coding methods and 36% compared to other kernel sparse coding techniques.

412   parameter λ. Specifically, as stated in (9), the larger the value of λ, the greater the interval [−λ, λ]

      In this paper, we have addressed the limitations of traditional sparse coding algorithms when dealing
      with nonlinear real-world signals. By leveraging RBF kernel to implicitly increase the dimensional-
473
      ity of the original data to enhance its separability, our proposed kernel sparse coding method enables
474
      more effective sparse representations and provides a solution with convergence guarantees based on
475   the principle of coordinate descent. To further optimize the computational efficiency, we introduced
476   a novel two-stage acceleration strategy. The strategy is theoretically underpinned by the insight that
477   updates to zero weights can be skipped and empirically supported by the observation that the po-
478   sitions of nonzero weights are relatively stable. This innovation allows the optimization process to
479   be significantly accelerated. Experimental results validate the effectiveness of our approach. The

      A.1   R ELATED WORK
651
652   Sparse coding serves as a pivotal branch within deep learning (Yang et al., 2009), endeavoring to
653   discover a sparse representation of input data in the form of a linear combination of basic atoms. This
654   pursuit achieves multiple objectives: data compression, enhancement of computational efficiency,
655   and uncovering salient features of the data, thereby finding widespread applications across diverse
656   domains.
657
      However, recent studies (Pimentel-Alarcón et al., 2017; Fan et al., 2021; Fan & Udell, 2019) have
658   indicated that many real-world signals do not possess an inherent linear structure, hence traditional
659   linear sparse coding methods may fail to be effective (Yang et al., 2016). Consequently, a few
660   scholars have proposed the use of kernel tricks to implicitly elevate the dimensionality of the input
661   data, thereby enhancing the applicability of sparse coding methods to complex data. Kernel sparse
662   coding is an extension of linear sparse coding. Existing research on kernel sparse coding problems
663   can be roughly divided into two categories based on the form of constraint on the sparse matrix in
664   their mathematical models: the l0 norm-based approach and the l1 norm-based approach.
665   The kernel sparse coding problem formulated with the l0 norm is inherently NP-hard and can only
666   be solved by using heuristic algorithms. For instance, the Kernel Orthogonal Matching Pursuit
667   (KOMP) (Nguyen et al., 2013) algorithm iteratively selects dictionary atoms that exhibit the highest
668   correlation with the current residual during the sparse optimization process until a predetermined
669   sparsity level is achieved. Alternatively, the Linearized Proximal Method (LPM) (Quan et al., 2016)
670   leverages block coordinate descent, initially obtaining a closed-form solution through the proximal
671   gradient method, followed by a brute-force selection of the weights with the largest absolute values
672
      to satisfy the sparsity constraint. These heuristic algorithms are collectively noted for their rapid
      sparse optimization capabilities. However, they fall short by lacking convergence guarantees,
673
      which means they cannot assure the attainment of the optimal solution and are susceptible to getting
674
      trapped in local optima.
675
676   In contrast, the l1 norm offers a convex alternative that is easier to handle mathematically but still
677   presents challenges due to its non-differentiability. The Kernel Feature-sign Search (KFSS) (Gao
678
      et al., 2013) algorithm addresses this issue by taking an active approach to guessing the signs of
      the coefficient weights. This strategic method is based on the observation that the sign of a coef-
679
      ficient is closely related to the correlation between the dictionary atom and the residual. By cor-
680
      rectly guessing the signs, KFSS can simplify the optimization problem and work towards finding
681   an analytical solution that satisfies the sparsity requirement. The First-Order Smooth Optimization
682   (FOSO) (Kim, 2014) algorithm tackles the non-differentiability of the l1 norm by approximating
683   it with smooth functions. This approximation allows the use of conventional gradient-based opti-
684   mization techniques, which are otherwise inapplicable to non-differentiable problems. The smooth
685   approximation serves as a surrogate that enables the derivation of an analytical solution while still
686   promoting sparsity in the solution. Both KFSS and FOSO contribute to the broader field of kernel
687   sparse coding by providing methods that can yield sparse solutions with convergence guarantees.
688   However, they face challenges in terms of the scalability of optimization complexity and com-
689
      putational speed. Therefore, this paper aims to explore a new fast kernel sparse coding method
      with convergence guarantees.

      A.4     FKSC ALGORITHM
757
758   The FKSC method with a two-stage acceleration strategy is described in Algorithm 2. The core
759   strategies of FKSC includes: Stage 1: Prioritizing updates of initially predicted nonzero elements
760   (lines 3-11); Stage 2: Safely skipping updates for zero elements in w (lines 14-16).
761
      Computational complexity analysis. Assuming the FKSC method requires T1 rounds of iterations
762   in Stage 1 and T2 rounds of iterations in Stage 2, while the KSC method requires T rounds of
763   iterations for the weight vector to converge.
764
765
      In Stage 1 of FKSC, assuming that in the 1st round of iteration, there are k1 elements are predicted
      to be nonzero and require T1 iterations to converge. Thus, the total computational complexity of
766
      Stage 1 is O(p + k1 pT1 ), where O(p) represents the total computational complexity of calculating
767
      the bounds in the 1st round of iteration, and O(k1 pT1 ) represents the total computational complexity
768   of the k1 predicted nonzero elements over T1 rounds of iteration.
769
770   In Stage 2 of FKSC, assuming that the entire weight vector requires T2 iterations to converge, and
771
      on average, k0 elements are predicted to be zero in per round of iteration. Therefore, the total com-
      putational complexity of Stage 2 is O(pT2 (p − k0 + 1)), where O(pT2 ) is the total computational
772
      complexity of calculating the bounds throughout the process, and O((p − k0 )pT2 ) is the total com-
773
      putational complexity of the remaining (p − k0 ) elements, excluding the predicted k0 zero elements,
774   to undergo T2 rounds of iterations.
775
776   Hence, the total computational complexity of the entire FKSC algorithm is O(p(1 + k1 T1 + T2 (p −
777
      k0 + 1))). If no acceleration strategies are adopted and the standard coordinate descent algorithm is
      used for updating iterations directly, then the overall computational complexity would be O(p2 (T1 +
778
      T2 )). Generally speaking, T is not much different from T1 + T2 , then the two-stage acceleration
779
      strategy has the potential to speed up the processing time of kernel sparse coding.

843
      The comparison algorithms for KSC and FKSC include
844
845   • CD (Fujiwara et al., 2016): the coordinate descent algorithm for traditional linear sparse coding
846   problem, based on l1 regularization in the original input space.
847   • KFMC (Fan & Udell, 2019): the explicit solution approach for kernelized matrix factorization
848   problems, with the weight matrix regularized by the Frobenius norm.
849
      This method results in a weight matrix that is not strictly sparse, but contains many weight factors
850
      that are very small. In this paper, for each column of the weight matrix, we preserve weight factors
851   with significant absolute values while setting the others to zero to meet a predetermined sparsity
852   level, thereby facilitating a comparison with our proposed method.
853
854
      • KSR-L2,1 (Qian et al., 2023): the explicit solution approach for kernel sparse representation, with
      the weight matrix being constrained via L2,1 matrix norm.
855
856   Ideally, this method yields a weight matrix that is row-sparse, but the overall sparsity is not high
857   enough, or in other words, there are not enough zero elements. Therefore, to enable a comparative
858   analysis with our proposed method, for each column of the weight matrix, we retain only those
859   weight factors with substantial absolute values in accordance with a preset sparsity level, while
860
      nullifying the others to zero.
861   • LPM (Quan et al., 2016): the linearized proximal method, employed for addressing kernel sparse
862   coding problems with l0 regularization, initiates by leveraging the proximal gradient approach to
863   secure a closed-form solution. Subsequently, it selects and retains the weight factors with the most
      significant absolute values, aligning with a predefined sparsity level.
