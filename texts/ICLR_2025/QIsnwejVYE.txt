009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Neural operator methods have achieved significant success in the efficient simu-
012
               lation and inverse problems of complex systems by learning a mapping between
013
               two infinite-dimensional Banach spaces. However, existing methods still exhibit
014            room for optimization in terms of robustness and modeling accuracy. Specifi-
015            cally, existing methods are characterized by sensitivity to noise and a tendency
016            to overlook the importance of sparse observations in new domains. Therefore,
017            we propose a robust latent neural operator based on the variational autoencoder
018            framework. In this method, an encoder based on recurrent neural networks ef-
019            fectively extracts sequential information and dynamical characteristics embedded
020            in the domain-specific sparse observations. Subsequently, a neural operator in
021            latent space and a decoder facilitate the modelling of the original system. Addi-
022
               tionally, for certain higher-dimensional systems, opting for a lower-dimensional
               latent space can reduce task complexity while still maintaining satisfactory model-
023
               ing performance. We conduct experiments across several representative systems,
024
               and the results validate that our method achieves superior modeling accuracy and
025            enhanced robustness compared to the state of the art baseline approaches.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Modeling and forecasting complex dynamical systems have emerged as pivotal research themes
032   within the scientific machine learning domain, witnessing the development of numerous efficacious
033   methods and tools (Tang et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2023a). These advancements play a crucial role
034   across various domains of complex systems research, not only aiding in the understanding of the
035   intricate behaviors and evolutionary patterns of real-world systems, but also laying the groundwork
036   for downstream tasks such as system prediction (Li et al., 2024b;a), structure inference (Brugere
037   et al., 2018; Lorch et al., 2022), change point detection (Bjørheim et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023),
038   tipping point prediction (Bury et al., 2021; Grziwotz et al., 2023), and system control (Bertsekas,
039
      2019; Wang et al., 2023b).
040   Traditional artificial models, such as prevalent ordinary differential equation (ODE) and partial dif-
041   ferential equation (PDE) systems, facilitate the modeling of original systems through determinate
042   rules. However, these methods often struggle to precisely model more intricate behaviors in real-
043   world systems. This gap underlines an urgent need for data-driven and machine learning approaches
044   to explore the dynamics. To identify critical terms within the dynamical equations, the Sparse Iden-
045
      tification of Nonlinear Dynamical Systems (SINDy) (Brunton et al., 2016; Kaiser et al., 2018) ap-
      proach is commonly employed. Subsequently, benefiting from the universal approximation capabil-
046
      ity of deep neural networks (Hornik, 1991), an increasing number of researchers are now focusing
047
      on neural network tools. For instance, Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and their extended ar-
048   chitectures (Memory, 2010; Cho et al., 2014; Suárez et al., 2024) have proven to be effective for
049   processing sequential data; Neural Ordinary Differential Equations (NODEs) (Chen et al., 2018)
050   can handle data with irregular intervals, thereby enabling continuous modeling; Graph Neural Net-
051   works (GNNs) (Murphy et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023) are capable of efficiently managing data with
052   graph structures. Although these approaches have achieved success in their specific domains, they
053   necessitate retraining of the neural networks when environmental parameters change. Consequently,
      it is imperative to explore methods for modeling a family of systems.

054
      In response to the above challenge, neural operator methods have been proposed in recent years (Lu
055   et al., 2021; Li et al., 2020). These methods represent an emergent technology within the domain of
056   deep learning, aimed at establishing a machine learning model capable of learning mappings from
057   functional spaces to functional spaces. Specifically, neural operators can effectively handle con-
058   ditions involving infinite-dimensional variations (including parametric functions, initial conditions,
059   and boundary conditions, etc.) as inputs, and directly output future system states, thereby achiev-
060   ing modeling of a family of dynamical systems. Particularly for PDE systems, neural operators
061   present significant advantages over traditional numerical resolution methods, such as finite element
062   and finite difference methods. These advantages include enhanced solution speeds while maintain-
063
      ing accuracy, and more efficient handling of various inverse problems (Zhao et al., 2022; Wang &
      Wang, 2024).
064
065   However, existing neural operator approaches still have room for optimization in terms of noise
066   robustness and modeling accuracy. For instance, traditional neural operator methods may under-
067   perform when the observational data is contaminated with noise. Moreover, in new testing envi-
068   ronments, we might observe system states at several moments, which are often irregularly spaced.
069
      Current approaches overlook the crucial role of these sparse observational data from new domains,
      particularly the sequential information and dynamical characteristics embedded within them. There-
070
      fore, we introduce a more robust latent neural operator approach, termed as Robust Latent Neural
071
      Operator (RLNO), grounded in the variational autoencoder (VAE) framework. Specifically, the pri-
072   mary contributions of our work are as follows.
073
074          • RLNO utilizes more domain-specific information compared to traditional neural operators.
075            When tested in new domains, our approach not only leverages sparse observational state
076            information but also excavates and utilizes the dynamic information embedded within these
077            sequential observations.
078          • RLNO represents a novel approach grounded in VAE framework, incorporating an RNN-
079            based encoder. This method effectively harnesses the strengths of RNNs and neural oper-
080            ators to more adeptly extract and utilize the dynamical information within sparse observa-
081            tions from new domains.
082          • RLNO inherits characteristics from neural operator approaches, significantly surpassing the
083            computational efficiency observed in RNN-based and Neural ODE-based methods. Fur-
084            thermore, we design the OPERATOR-RNN encoder, which can encode unevenly spaced
085            observations into the initial value distribution of the latent space more efficiently.
086          • RLNO can select a smaller latent space dimension, thereby reducing the complexity of
087            operator learning, and facilitating the neural operator’s ability to capture essential features
088            with limited training data. Consequently, our approach can be extended to modeling tasks
089            of higher-dimensional complex systems.

      2   R ELATED WORK

093
      Recent years have seen broad interest and rapid advancements in the area of neural operators. Two
094
      of the most seminal contributions in this domain are the development of Deep Operator Network
095
      (DeepONet) introduced by Lu et al.(Lu et al., 2021), and Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) proposed
096   by Li et al.(Li et al., 2020). These methods leverage the neural network model Gθ to learn the
097   operator mapping G † : U → S, where U and S are two infinite-dimensional Banach spaces. Here,
098   our research focuses on the DeepONet framework, which primarily consists of two components: the
099   trunk network and the branch network. Specifically, the trunk network accepts an arbitrary position
100   y as input and outputs the vector c = {c1 , c2 , · · · , cm }. Concurrently, the branch network takes l
101   discrete observations {u(x1 ), u(x2 ), · · · , u(xl )} of the sampled function u(x) as input and outputs
102   the vector b = {b1 , b2 , · · · , bm }. Subsequently, integrate the trunk and branch networks through
103   the inner product of c and b, thereby estimating the state value at point y. This can be represented
104
      as Gθ (u)(y) = c · b. Here, y represents a position within the domain of G † (u), which could be a
      temporal variable or spatial location, among others.
105
106   Furthermore, in light of specific application scenarios, a series of improved neural operator methods
107   have been successively introduced. For instance, Jin et al. proposed a multi-input neural operator
      based on tensor products (Jin et al., 2022), and Wang et al. introduced a physics-informed neural op-

506   5   C ONCLUDING R EMARKS
507
508
      In this work, we introduce a novel neural operator method named RLNO, grounded in the framework
509
      of VAEs. This method initially encodes the original system states into a latent space. Subsequently,
510
      the future states of these latent variables are predicted by modeling a neural operator Gθ . Finally, a
511   decoder maps the variables from the latent space back to the data space, thus enabling the modeling
512   of the original system. In this process, our model is trained by maximizing the evidence lower bound,
513   and the Gaussian prior assumption within the model enables our method to more effectively handle
514   observations with noise. We conduct experiments across several parametric ODE and PDE systems,
515   and the results demonstrate that our RLNO method surpasses state-of-the-art baseline methods in
516   terms of noise robustness and modeling accuracy.
517   To enhance the encoding of sparse observational data, our RLNO method incorporates an
518   OPERATOR-RNN encoder. This encoder not only inputs multiple observational data points but
519   also encodes temporal interval information of sequential data, thereby exhibiting superior modeling
520   performance in irregularly spaced observations compared to traditional RNN encoders. Moreover,
521   while both the ODE-RNN encoder and the RNN-Decay encoder are capable of encoding tempo-
522   ral interval information, their performance is inferior to our method, and the ODE-RNN encoder
523   incurs a high computational cost in PDE systems. This assertion was validated through meticu-
524   lously designed ablation studies, indicating that our method can more effectively utilize sparse and
525
      spaced observational data for operator learning. Additionally, we conducted a parameter sensitivity
      analysis on the dimensionality of the latent space. Results indicate that lower-dimensional latent
526
      states achieve impressive modeling accuracy. This dimensionality reduction in the latent space
527
      decreases the task’s complexity, facilitating the neural operator’s ability to capture essential fea-
528   tures with limited training data. Consequently, this approach can be extended to modeling tasks of
529   higher-dimensional complex systems. These findings are consistent with the conclusions drawn in
530   (Kontolati et al., 2024).
531
      Certainly, the RLNO method exhibits certain limitations that warrant further investigation. For
532
      instance, when the length of the recognition network Tenc is sufficiently large, the predictive perfor-
533
      mance of our method does not significantly improve. This suggests that the RNN-based encoders
534   may gradually forget information from more distant observations. Thus, developing an encoder with
535   long-term memory capabilities could further enhance the modeling capacity of RLNO. Moreover,
536   current neural operator methods typically excel in modeling a family of dynamical systems under
537   relatively simple sampling distribution scenarios (e.g., Gaussian random fields). Therefore, future
538   efforts are required to devise more effective neural operators capable of modeling systems within
539   more complex sampling distributions. This is of significant practical importance for replacing inef-
      ficient numerical simulations with efficient neural operators.

852            ablation studies and comparative experiments with classic baseline methods convincingly

               support this conclusion.
854          • Third, an RNN-based encoder (OPERATOR-RNN) can efficiently leverage additional ob-

856            neural operator approaches fail to achieve this. In fact, this can be attributed to the fact that

      First, we consider the original version of the Deep Operator Network method (Lu et al., 2021), herein
865   referred to as DeepONet. This technique is structured around a branch network and a trunk network.
866   The branch network processes l equidistantly distributed sampling points from the parameter func-
867   tion u(x, t), generating a m-dimensional vector b = {b1 , . . . , bm }. Conversely, the trunk network
868   accepts the temporal and spatial variables, t and x, respectively, yielding another m-dimensional
869   vector c = {c1 , . . . , cm }. Consequently, the prediction of the system’s state for any given set of
870   inputs is articulated as follows:
