046                                             A BSTRACT
047
048           Distilling latent diffusion models (LDMs) into ones that are fast to sample from
049           is attracting growing research interest. However, the majority of existing methods
050           face two critical challenges: (i) They need to perform long-time learning with a
051           huge volume of real data. (ii) They routinely lead to quality degradation for gener-
052           ation, especially in text-image alignment. This paper proposes the novel Training-
053           efficient Latent Consistency Model (TLCM) to overcome these challenges. Our
              method first accelerates LDMs via data-free multistep latent consistency distilla-

054
               tion (MLCD), then data-free latent consistency distillation is proposed to guaran-
055            tee the inter-segment consistency in MLCD at a low cost. Furthermore, we intro-
056            duce bags of techniques e.g., distribution matching, adversarial learning, and pref-
057            erence learning, to enhance TLCM’s performance at few-step inference without
058            any real data, TLCM demonstrates a high level of flexibility by enabaling adjust-
059            ment of sampling steps within the range of 2 to 8 while still producing competitive
060            outputs compared to full-step approaches. As the name suggests, TLCM excels in
061            training efficiency in terms of both computational resources and data utilization.
062            Notably, TLCM operates without reliance on a training dataset but instead em-
063
               ploys synthetic data for the teacher itself during distillation. With just 70 training
               hours on an A100 GPU, a 3-step TLCM distilled from SDXL achieves an impres-
064
               sive CLIP Score of 33.68 and an Aesthetic Score of 5.97 on the MSCOCO-2017

               5K benchmark, surpassing various accelerated models and even outperforming the
066            teacher model in human preference metrics. We also demonstrate the versatility
067            of TLCMs in applications including image style transfer, controllable generation,
068            and Chinese-to-image generation.

071   1   I NTRODUCTION
072
073   Diffusion models (DMs) have made great advancements in the field of generative modeling, becom-
074   ing the go-to approach for image, video, and audio generation (Ho et al., 2020; Kong et al.; Saharia
075   et al., 2022). Latent diffusion models (LDMs) further enhance DMs by operating in the latent im-
076   age space, pushing the limit of high-resolution image and video synthesis (Ma et al., 2024; Peebles
077   & Xie, 2023; Podell et al.; Rombach et al., 2022). Despite the high-quality and realistic samples,
078   LDMs suffer from frustratingly slow inference–generating a single sample requires tens to hundreds
079   of evaluations of the model, giving rise to a high cost and bad user experience.
080   There is growing interest in distilling large-scale LDMs into more efficient ones. Concretely, pro-
081   gressive distillation (Lin et al., 2024; Meng et al., 2023; Salimans & Ho, 2023) reduces the sampling
082   steps by half in each turn but finally hinges on a set of models for various sampling steps. InstaFlow
083   Liu et al., UFO-Gen Xu et al. (2024b), DMD (Yin et al., 2024b), and ADD Sauer et al. (2023) target
084   one-step generation, yet losing or weakening the ability to benefit from more (e.g., > 4) sampling
085   steps. Latent consistency models (LCMs) Luo et al. (2023) apply consistency distillation (Song
086   et al., 2023) on LDMs’ reverse-time ordinary differential equation (ODE) trajectories to conjoin
087
      one- and multi-step generation, but the image quality degrades substantially, especially in 2-4 steps.
      HyperSD (Ren et al., 2024) applies consistency trajectory distillation (Kim et al., 2023) in segments
088
      of the ODE trajectory, yet suffers from a substantial performance drop in text-image alignment. Be-
089
      sides, all these methods rely on a huge volume of high-quality data and long training time, hindering
090   their applicability to downstream scenarios with rare compute and data.
091
092   Before presenting our proposal, it’s essential to note that one-step generation may not always be the
093
      optimal choice in practical applications—empirically, sampling with 2-4 steps introduces less than
      40% additional computational time compared to one step but can notably enhance the upper limit of
094
      sampling quality. Moreover, some practical applications typically have a low tolerance for quality
095
      degradation and hence can accept a moderate number of sampling steps. Thereby, this paper aims
096   to develop a unified model with 2-8 sampling steps that can deliver competitive quality comparable
097   to full-step counterparts. We propose Training-efficient Latent Consistency Models (TLCMs) to
098   achieve this at the expense of minimal computation and training data. Technically, we introduce
099   data-free multistep latent consistency distillation (MLCD) for fast training at a low cost. After
100   MLCD, we propose a data-free latent consistency distillation (LCD) term for global consistency. To
101   enhance LCD, we enforce the consistency of TLCM at sparse predefined timesteps instead of the
102   entire timestep range, which reduces the learning difficulty of LCD and accelerate convergence. A
103   multistep solver is further explored to unleash the potential of teacher in LCD. Besides, we train a
104
      latent LPIPS model to constrain the perceptual consistency of the distilled model in latent space. To
      optimize TLCM’s performance at few-step inference, we explore preference learning, distribution
105
      matching, and adversarial learning techniques for regularization in data-free manner.
106
107   We have performed comprehensive empirical studies to evaluate TLCMs. We first assess the image
      quality on the MSCOCO-2017 5K benchmark. We observe the TLCM distilled from SDXL (Podell

128   2   R ELATED WORKS
129
130   Diffusion models. (DMs) (Ho et al., 2020; Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Song & Ermon, 2019; 2020;
131   Song et al., b) progressively add Gaussian noise to perturb the data, then are trained to denoise
132   noise-corrupted data. In the inference stage, DMs sample from a Gaussian distribution and per-
133   form sequential denoising steps to reconstruct the data. As a type of generative model, they have
134
      demonstrated impressive capabilities in generating realistic and high-quality outputs in text-to-image
      generation (Podell et al.; Rombach et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022), video generation (Peebles &
135
      Xie, 2023). To enhance the condition awareness in conditional DMs, the classifier-free guidance
136
      (CFG) (Ho & Salimans, 2021) technique is proposed to trade off diversity and fidelity.
137
138   Diffusion acceleration. The primary challenges that hinder the practical adoption of DMs is the
139   slow inference involving tens to hundreds of evaluations of the model.
140   Early works like Progressive Distillation (PD) (Salimans & Ho, 2023) and Classifier-aware Dis-
141   tillation (CAD) (Meng et al., 2023) explore the approaches of progressive knowledge distillation
142   to compress sampling steps but lead to blurry samples within four sampling steps. Consistency
143   models (CMs) (Song et al., 2023), consistency trajectory models (CTMs) (Kim et al., 2023) and
144   Diff-Instruct (Luo et al., 2024) distill a pre-trained DM into a single-step generator, but they do not
145   verify the effectiveness on large-scale text-to-image generation.
146   Recently, the distillation of large-scale text-to-image DMs has gained significant attention.
147   LCM (Luo et al., 2023) extends CM to text-to-image generation with few-step inference but
148   synthesizes blurry images in four steps. InstaFlow (Liu et al.), UFOGen (Xu et al., 2024b),
149   BOOT (Gu et al., 2023), SwiftBrush (Nguyen & Tran, 2024), DMD (Yin et al., 2024a), and Dif-
150   fusion2GAN Kang et al. (2024) propose one-step sampling for text-to-image generation but are
151   unable to extend their sampler to multiple steps for better image quality.
152   More recently, SDXL-Turbo (Sauer et al., 2023), SDXL-Lighting (Lin et al., 2024), and Hy-
153   perSD (Ren et al., 2024) are proposed to further enhance the image quality with few-step sam-
154   pling but their training depends on huge high-quality text-image pairs and expensive online training.
155   Our method not only enables the generation of high-quality samples using a 2 8 steps sampler but
156   also enhances model performance with more inference cost. Furthermore, our training strategy is
157   resource-efficient and does not require any images.
158   Human preference for text-to-image model. ImageReward (IR) (Xu et al., 2024a) and Aesthetic
159   Score (Schuhmann) are proposed to evaluate the human preference of text-to-image model. Multi-
160   dimensional Preference Score (MPS) (Zhang et al., 2024) improves metrics by learning diverse
161   preferences. To optimize TLCM towards human preference, we incorporate effective reward learn-
      ing into TLCM to directly guide model tuning.

238   4       M ETHODOLOGY
239
240   Our target is to accelerate LDM into few-step model, with performance competitive to long-iteration
241   teacher. The learning procedure should be executed with cheap cost in data-free manner. In this
242   section, we propose a novel and unified Training-efficient Latent Consistency Model (TLCM) with
243   2-8 step’s inference. We begin by introducing data-free multistep latent consistency distillation.
244
      Subsequently, we discuss data-free latent consistency distillation to enforce global consistency of
      TLCM. Lastly, we explore various techniques to promote TLCM’s performance in data-free manner.
245
      The overview of our training pipeline is presented in Figure 2.

      4.1       DATA - FREE M ULTISTEP L ATENT C ONSISTENCY D ISTILLATION
248
249   We consider distilling representative pre-trained LDMs, e.g., SDXL (Podell et al.). Previous
250   LCM Luo et al. (2023) has distilled SDXL into few-step model, but it results in the big perfor-
251   mance drop, since it is hard to learn the mapping between an arbitrary state of the entire ODE
252   trajectory to the endpoint. Drawing inspiration from MCM, we split the entire range [0, T ] into M
253   segments, and then only enforce consistency at each separate segment. To speed up convergence,
254   we change the skipping step (skip) =1 in MCM into 20. The EMA module is removed to save mem-
255   ory consumption. Let zt denote the states at timestep t in the latent space. We abuse ϵθ0 (zt , c, t)
256   and fθ (zt , c, t) to denote the pre-trained LDM and target model respectively, where c refers to the
257
      generation condition. We formulate the multistep latent consistency distillation (MLCD) loss as:
258                        Lmlcd = ∥gθ (ztm , tm , tsstep , c) − nograd(gθ (ztn , tn , tsstep , c)∥22 ,             (7)

262

277   4.2   I MPROVED DATA - FREE L ATENT C ONSISTENCY D ISTILLATION
278
279   After a round of distillation on M segments, TLCM can naturally produce high-quality samples
280   through M -step sampling. However, it is empirically observed that the performance decreases when
281   using fewer steps, which is probably because of the larger discretization error caused by long sam-
282
      pling step sizes. To alleviate this, we advocate explicitly teaching TLCM to capture the mapping
      between the states that cross segments. Upon this goal, we propose data-free latent consistency
283
      distillation to promote the model to be consistent across the predefined timesteps.
284
285   We do not compile TLCM to keep consistency across the entire timestep range [0, T ] since it is hard
286   to learn the mapping that transforms any point along trajectory into real data. Instead, we improve
287   raw LCD through only keeping consistency at the predefined M timesteps, which makes LCD much
288
      easier to learn the mapping. Naturally, the skipping step skip is changed to T /M . The big skip
      offers an additional advantage which further accelerates model convergence. Benefiting from the
289
      pre-trained TLCM, we can fast yield clean data ẑ0 via few-step (q-step) sampler, such as 4 steps,
290
      eliminating the requirement of real data. The latent state ẑtm is obtained by adding noise to ẑ0 in
291   the forward diffusion process, where tm ∈ {tsstep }M
                                                         s=1 . We formulate this procedure as
292
293                             ẑtm = F D(T LCM (ϵ, T, c), tm ),     ϵ ∈ N (0, I),                       (9)

295
      solver is used to estimate latent state ẑtn from ẑtm . Raw LCD adopts one-step solver to predict
296   ẑtn . We argue that it restricts the capability of the teacher due to discretization error, especially

305   4.3   I NCORPORATING BAG OF T ECHNIQUES INTO TLCM IN DATA - FREE M ANNER
306
307   Latent LPIPS. Typical LCD directly adopts mean square error loss (Lmse ) to enforce consistency in
308   the latent space, but it can not capture perceptual features. LPIPS (Zhang et al., 2018) can extract the
309   features matching human perceptual responses. Meanwhile, it has been widely used as an effective
310   regression loss across many image translation tasks. Thereby, we aim to integrate LPIPS into our
311   distillation pipeline to enhance TLCM’s performance. However, LPIPS is built in the pixel space,
312
      and hence we have to reconstruct latent codes to pixel space to use LPIPS. To reduce training time,
      we train a latent LPIPS (L-LPIPS) model, which computes perceptual features in latent space. Latent
313
      LPIPS model adopts VGG network by changing the input to 4 channels and removing the 3 max-
314
      pooling layers, as the latent space in LDM is already 8× downsampled. The model is trained from
315   scratch on BAPPS dataset (Zhang et al., 2018). Base on L-LPIPS, the outputs of the model gθ
316   and fθ are first fed into L-LPIPS model, whose outputs are used to calculate consistency loss via
317   Equation (7) or Equation (10).
318
      MPS optimization. Since TLCMs transform the points on the trajectory to clean samples x̂0 , we
319
      can naturally directly maximize the feedback of the scorer on the sample s(x̂0 , c). Considering
320
      multi-dimensional preference score (Zhang et al., 2024) can measure diverse human preferences,
321   we leverage it to improve TLCM towards human preference. Formally, we optimize the following
322   MPS loss (Lmps ):
323
                           Lmps = max(s0 − s(x̂0 , cpos ), 0) + max(s(x̂0 , cneg ), 0),                  (11)

474   To analyze the key components of our method, we make a thorough ablation study to verify the

478   free method is able to accelerate LDM with good quality. Changing Llcd−s to single-step denoising
479   MLCD Lmlcd−s , all metrics are improved. This result verifies that MLCD has a stronger capability
480   to accelerate LDM than LCD. This is because it is hard for data-free LCD to enforce consistency
481   across the entire timestep range while data-free MLCD alleviates this by performing LCD within
482
      predefined multiple segments.
483   Denoising strategy. We can observe from Table 2 that Lmlcd−m substantially enhances the perfor-
484   mance of Lmlcd−s , verifying that the proposed multistep denoising strategy is critical to perform
485   data-free MLCD. The probable reason is our multistep MDS yield better initial latent codes, where
      the latent codes have better quality with smaller timesteps.

522   In this paper, we propose Training-efficient Latent Consistency Model (TLCM), a novel approach for

      style transfer, controllable generation, and Chinese-to-image generation.
