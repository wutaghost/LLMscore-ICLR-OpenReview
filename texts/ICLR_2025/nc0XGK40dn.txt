011                                               A BSTRACT
012
               This paper presents a novel approach to addressing the underexplored challenge
013
               of human pose prediction in dynamic target domains that simultaneously con-
014            tain in-distribution (ID) and out-of-distribution (OOD) data. Existing test-time
015            adaptation (TTA) techniques predominantly focus on OOD data, neglecting the
016            fact that ID data, which closely resembles the training distribution, is often en-
017            countered during real-world deployment, leading to significant degradation in ID
018            performance. To address this, we introduce In-Distribution Knowledge Reten-
019            tion (IDKR), a continual TTA framework designed to preserve critical knowledge
020            about ID data while adapting to unseen OOD sequences. Our method introduces
021            an ID-informative subgraph learning strategy that leverages the structural charac-
022
               teristics of human skeletal data to compute a structural graph Fisher Information
               Matrix (SG-FIM). Unlike prior work, IDKR simultaneously considers both node
023
               and edge features in the skeletal graph, with edge features, representing the invari-
024
               ant bone lengths between parent-child joint pairs, being essential for maintaining
025            structural consistency across poses. These edge features are key to extracting re-
026            liable SG-FIM parameters, enabling the model to retain parameters critical for ID
027            performance while selectively updating those needed for OOD adaptation. Ex-
028            tensive experiments on multiple benchmark datasets demonstrate that IDKR con-
029            sistently outperforms state-of-the-art methods, particularly in scenarios involving
030            mixed ID and OOD data, setting a new standard for robust human pose prediction
031            in dynamic environments.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
      3D human pose prediction (HPP) is a fundamental task in computer vision and machine intelligence,
036   with applications in human-robot interaction, and robotics (Lou et al., 2024; Yan et al., 2024). The
037   goal of HPP is to predict future human poses based on a sequence of observed 3D poses.
038
039
      State-of-the-art HPP methods predominantly follow a data-driven deep learning approach, where
      models are trained on large-scale datasets and directly applied to unseen target data (Guo et al.,
040
      2023; Chen et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023). However, a key challenge for these models is domain
041
      shift, where a model trained on a source domain performs poorly when deployed in a new, unseen
042   target domain. Domain shifts occur due to differences in body shapes, proportions, and motion
043   patterns between the training and test data (Liang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023). To address this
044   issue, recent studies (Cui et al., 2023b;a) have proposed TTA techniques, which dynamically adjust
045   the pre-trained model during inference to better accommodate target domain shifts.
046
      Despite these advancements, current TTA methods exhibit two notable limitations when applied to
047
      real-world HPP scenarios:
048
049         • Unrealistic Stationary Target Domain Assumption: Existing TTA-based HPP methods
050           (Cui et al., 2023a;b) assume that the distribution shift between the source and target do-
051
              mains remains static, which is unrealistic in real-world dynamic environments.
052         • In-Distribution and Out-of-Distribution Data in the Target Domain: Current TTA
053           methods (Wang et al., 2020; 2022; Brahma & Rai, 2023) do not adequately address the
              coexistence of in-distribution (ID) data, which resembles the training data, and out-of-


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
               distribution (OOD) data, which differs from it. This results in suboptimal performance
055            when both ID and OOD data are present in the target domain.
056
057   It is noteworthy that in real-world HPP deployments, the operational environment is dynamic, with
058
      individual behavioral patterns evolving over time, thus leading to a non-stationary target domain
      (Wang et al., 2022; Sanyal et al., 2023). This ongoing change of the target domain results in a
059
      perpetual distribution shift, which is not adequately addressed by existing TTA-based HPP meth-
060
      ods. More importantly, in practical settings, alongside the OOD data, the deployment phase will
061   inevitably encounter ID sequences that closely align with the distribution of the source domain (Sun
062   et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022b; Mao et al., 2024), as evidenced in Appendix-B. However, existing
063   methods often assume that the target domain is exclusively OOD, thus overlooking the presence of
064   ID sequences, which limits their generalization performance.
065
      To address these challenges, we propose a novel continual TTA framework, IDKR, which is specifi-
066
      cally designed to handle evolving target domains that contain both ID and OOD data. Our framework
067   not only enhances performance on OOD data but also retains key parameters critical for ID recog-
068   nition, ensuring robust performance across both types of motion sequences. Our approach builds
069   on the insight that human motion can be represented as a graph, where nodes correspond to human
070   joints and edges represent skeletal connections. Recent advances in Graph Invariant Learning (GIL)
071   have demonstrated the effectiveness of extracting informative subgraph structures for downstream
072   tasks by filtering out irrelevant elements (Chen et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2022c). For example, in
073   distinguishing between walking and running, the movements of the legs and arms provide more
074   significant discriminatory information than the torso. Building on this, we integrate the Graph In-
075
      formation Bottleneck (GIB) framework (Wu et al., 2020) into GIL theory, proposing a novel method
      for in-distribution subgraph learning that compresses the original skeleton graph into an informative
076
      subgraph relevant to the ID labels (You et al., 2020). Once the ID-informative subgraph is identi-
077
      fied, we incorporate this structure into the model’s continual adaptation process. The in-distribution
078   subgraph is used to guide the model’s parameter updates during test-time adaptation. By aligning
079   the model’s focus on this subgraph, we ensure that the knowledge specific to ID data is preserved
080   throughout the adaptation process, even as the model learns to handle OOD data.
081
      Considering the skeletal topology of motion sequence, instead of the standard Fisher Information
082
      Matrix (FIM), we introduce an innovative ID-parameter estimation strategy–Structural Graph Fisher
083
      Information Matrix (SG-FIM), which quantifies the importance of model parameters based on both
084   node and edge features within the ID-informative subgraph. The edge features, which represent the
085   invariant bone lengths between parent and child joints, are crucial for maintaining structural consis-
086   tency across different poses. This invariance is essential for accurately extracting SG-FIM, allowing
087   the model to retain parameters critical for ID data while updating others for OOD adaptation. The
088   SG-FIM serves as an ID knowledge retention regularization, integrated into the TTA optimization
089   process via self-supervised loss (Tian & Lyu, 2024; Brahma & Rai, 2023), ensuring that ID-specific
090   knowledge is preserved while the model adapts to the evolving target domain. This allows our ap-
091   proach to dynamically adapt to both ID and OOD data, achieving optimal predictive performance in
092
      a variety of deployment scenarios.
093   Our contributions can be summarized as follows: 1) We introduce the first framework that explic-
094   itly addresses the challenge of mixed ID and OOD target domains in HPP, overcoming a critical
095   limitation of current TTA approaches. 2) We propose a novel IDKR that leverages ID-informative
096   subgraph learning and SG-FIM to identify and retain ID-specific parameters while adapting to OOD
097   data. 3) Extensive evaluations show that IDKR significantly outperforms state-of-the-art HPP mod-
098
      els, particularly in non-stationary target domains containing both ID and OOD data.

101
102   2.1   H UMAN P OSE P REDICTION (HPP)
103   Early approaches to HPP primarily rely on RNN-based models, treating the task as a sequence-to-
104   sequence generation problem by mapping historical poses to future predictions (Ruiz et al., 2018;
105   Gui et al., 2018). While RNNs capture temporal correlations, they exhibit limitations such as static
106   pose prediction and discontinuities between frames. To address these shortcomings, graph convo-
107   lutional networks (GCNs) gain prominence due to their ability to model the semantic relationships
      within 3D skeleton structures (Dang et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022d; Chen et al., 2024a). Recent works

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      also emphasize the effectiveness of transformer-based architectures (Martı́nez-González et al., 2021;
109   Aksan et al., 2021; Dai et al., 2023) and multilayer perceptrons (MLP) (Guo et al., 2023; Bouazizi
110   et al., 2023) in capturing long-term dependencies, offering greater flexibility in motion prediction.
111
112
      Despite these advancements, most methods operate under the assumption that training and test data
      come from the same distribution, which is unrealistic in real-world applications where target do-
113
      mains often exhibit domain shifts (Brahma & Rai, 2023; Yuan et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023). To
114
      address this discrepancy, recent studies (Cui et al., 2023b;a) introduce TTA techniques, which are
115   constrained by their assumption of a stationary target domain. As a result, they struggle to handle
116   continuously evolving environments or mixed ID and OOD samples within the target domain. Our
117   approach explicitly tackles these limitations by providing a framework capable of adapting to both
118   ID and OOD data in dynamic settings, improving the model’s generalization across domains.
119
120   2.2   T EST- TIME A DAPTATION (TTA) AND C ONTINUAL TTA (CTTA)
121
      TTA represents a widely adopted source-free domain adaptation technique (Wang et al., 2020; Liu
122   et al., 2021a; Gong et al., 2022a; Su et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Sreenivas et al., 2024), enabling
123   pre-trained models to fine-tune themselves on individual test samples, thereby customizing their
124   predictions post-deployment. In the context of HPP, TTA-based approaches (Cui et al., 2023a;b)
125   have achieved promising results. However, standard TTA often assumes that the target distribution
126   is static, which is unrealistic in dynamic real-world HPP settings.
127
      Continual TTA (CTTA) extends TTA by allowing the model to adapt incrementally to dynamic target
128   domains (Gong et al., 2022b; Sójka et al., 2023; Sanyal et al., 2023; Tian & Lyu, 2024). Methods
129   like CoTTA (Wang et al., 2022) use a teacher-student framework with random restoration to mitigate
130   catastrophic forgetting, while PETAL (Brahma & Rai, 2023) employs probabilistic modeling to
131   stabilize updates during inference. However, most existing CTTA methods overlook the coexistence
132   of ID and OOD data in the target domain, leading to performance degradation on ID sequences,
133   particularly during long-term adaptation. Our method addresses this gap by explicitly identifying
134   and preserving ID-specific parameters during adaptation, ensuring sustained performance on ID data
135   throughout continual adaptation processes.
136
137
      2.3   O UT- OF -D ISTRIBUTION (OOD) D ETECTION
138   OOD detection is crucial for distinguishing ID from OOD data in the target domain. These methods
139   can be broadly classified into supervised and unsupervised categories, with supervised approaches
140   being particularly relevant in our case, as all source data are labeled as ID during training (Sun et al.,
141   2022; Li et al., 2022b; Mao et al., 2024). However, identifying ID samples in graph-structured data,
142   such as human skeletons, presents unique challenges for conventional OOD detection techniques.
143   To address it, we propose a GIL-based OOD detection method that leverages the inherent graph
144   structure of human skeletons. GIL allows us to compress subgraphs based on ID-specific infor-
145   mation (Li et al., 2022c; Chen et al., 2024b). Unlike traditional OOD detection methods, which
146   typically require explicit OOD labels, our approach does not depend on these labels. Instead, we
147   focus on extracting ID-informative subgraphs that guide the subsequent identification and retention
148   of ID-specific parameters, ensuring more robust performance across both ID and OOD data.
149
150   2.4   G RAPH I NVARIANT L EARNING (GIL)
151   GIL aims to capture invariant relationships between graph features and labels while filtering out spu-
152   rious correlations. Recent research in GIL, particularly in causal learning (Wu et al., 2022; Liu et al.,
153   2022) and graph manipulation (Li et al., 2022c; 2023), has demonstrated its effectiveness in OOD
154   generalization and detection. In particular, the Graph Information Bottleneck (GIB) framework has
155   been shown to learn robust graph representations by optimizing mutual information between sub-
156   graphs and their corresponding labels (Wu et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2024).
157   Building on these advances, our approach uses GIB to extract the most informative subgraph that
158   captures ID patterns. This subgraph plays a crucial role in retaining in-distribution knowledge during
159   continual adaptation, ensuring that the model maintains high performance on ID data while adapting
160   to evolving OOD data. Unlike prior methods, which fail to retain ID-specific knowledge, our frame-
161   work guarantees the continual relevance of ID parameters, providing superior performance across
      both ID and OOD samples in real-world scenarios.

         Let fΘ(0) denote the underlying human pose prediction (HPP) model, where Θ ∈ RP (Ma et al.,
252
         2022; Dang et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2023; Lou et al., 2024). During the continual test-time adapta-
253
         tion (CTTA) process, for a given sample x(t) and the adapted model fΘ(t−1) from the previous step,
254      the Structural Graph Fisher Information Matrix (SG-FIM) is designed to identify and retain key pa-
255      rameters for in-distribution (ID) sequences. This ensures the preservation of ID-specific knowledge
256      while dynamically updating other parameters, leading to the model’s adaptation fΘ(t) ← fΘ(t−1) .
257
         SG-FIM extends the classical Fisher Information Matrix (FIM) (Vedantam et al., 2021; Tian & Lyu,
258
         2024; Brahma & Rai, 2023) by incorporating the graph structure and features of the human skeleton.
259
         Unlike traditional FIM, which measures parameter sensitivity on regular data, SG-FIM is tailored to
260      the characteristics of human motion data, as follows:
261
262              • Graph topology consideration: While FIM generally overlooks the data’s inherent struc-
263                ture, SG-FIM explicitly accounts for the geometric topology of the human skeleton, reflect-
264                ing the structural relationships between joints and their temporal dependencies.
265              • Edge feature integration: SG-FIM is able to analyze both node (joint) and edge (bone)
266                features. The edges, representing the fixed bone lengths between parent and child joints,
267                are particularly important in human motion, as these lengths remain invariant regardless
268                of pose. This invariance provides a stable reference for motion prediction, improving the
269                accuracy of SG-FIM in estimating ID-specific parameters. Traditional graph methods often
                   ignore such constraints, making SG-FIM uniquely suited for skeletal data.

      3.4   C ONTINUAL A DAPTATION WITH ID KNOWLEDGE RETENTION
325
326   After obtaining the diagonal SG-FIM, we propose a novel weighted Fisher regularization termed
327   In-Distribution Knowledge Retention (IDKR). This mechanism is specifically designed to mitigate
328   catastrophic forgetting, particularly addressing the performance degradation on in-distribution (ID)
329
      motion sequences (Wang et al., 2024). By introducing a regularization term based on SG-FIM into
      the TTA optimization process, we ensure that the parameters critical for ID sequences are preserved
330
      during continual adaptation (Tian & Lyu, 2024; Saharia et al., 2022; Press et al., 2024).
331
332   The diagonal SG-FIM reflects the contribution of each parameter to the model’s predictive ability on
333   ID data: larger values indicate parameters that have a greater impact on ID performance and should

      5            C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this work, we address a more realistic TTA scenario for human pose prediction, wherein both in-
531
      distribution and out-of-distribution motion sequences are present in the target deployment domain.
      To tackle this challenge, we propose a novel continual TTA method incorporating an in-distribution
532
      knowledge retention mechanism. Our approach utilizes the Graph Information Bottleneck frame-
533
      work to compress the most informative subgraph relative to in-distribution for any target skeleton
534   sequence, which facilitates filtering out irrelevant elements or structures. Using this subgraph, we
535   calculate a structural graph fisher information matrix to identify parameters that significantly con-
536   tribute to the prediction of in-distribution sequences. It is then constructed an in-distribution knowl-
537   edge retention regularization, which is integrated into the TTA optimization process to control the
538   preservation of in-distribution parameters during continual TTA. Extensive experiments demonstrate
539   that the proposed IDKR outperforms state-of-the-art methods across various real-world experimen-
      tal setups, thereby evidencing its practicality and effectiveness.

760
761   In this sub-section, we provide the detailed optimization process of the graph information bottleneck
762   (GIB) as in Eq. 2. Recently, graph information bottleneck (GIB) has been integrated into GIL theory,
763
      where the bottleneck is engineered to compress the original graph information, preserving what is
      useful for detecting the ID. Inspired by this, given a graph G and its label Y, our objective is to
764
      learn a compressed subgraph Z that maximizes the mutual information with the ID label Y while
765
      containing minimal mutual information with the original graph G. For the sake of convenience, we
766   re-write the GIB objective in Eq. 2 as follows:
767
768                                        max I(Z, Y) − αI(Z, G).                                     (12)
                                             Z
769
770
      α is the Lagrange multiplier. Once the invariant subgraph Z is obtained, it can be fed into a GNN
      model gψ to extract the latent node and edge feature.
771
772   It is difficult to directly optimize the mutual information I(Z; Y) due to the intractability of the
773   marginal distribution p(Z) and p(G) (Shen et al., 2021; Higgins et al., 2016; Covert et al., 2023).
774   Therefore, for the sake of convenience, we propose to maximize the lower bound of the mutual
775   information to simplify the optimization process (Cha et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022a). To be specific,
776
      we introduce a variational distribution q(Z|G), controlled by the learnable parameter ϕ, which given
      the graph G, denotes the distribution of the compressed subgraph Z. Then, our objective is to seek
777
      a distribution q(Z|G) that maximizes I(Z; Y) while minimizing I(Z; G). For this purpose, we
778
      introduce a variational lower bound of the mutual information, which can be formulated as:
779
780                       I(Z; Y) ≥ Eq(Z|G) [log p(Y|Z) − DKL (q(Z|G)||p(Z))] ,                        (13)
781
      where DKL (q(Z|G)||p(Z)) is the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the variational distribution
782

1116   G     L IMITATION
1117
1118   The proposed IDKR model is designed to tackle the distribution shift challenges in human pose
1119   prediction tasks, particularly in scenarios involving a mixture of ID and OOD data. Our approach is
1120   grounded in the concept of retaining ID-specific knowledge during continual Test-Time Adaptation
1121   (TTA). This is achieved by compressing the most informative subgraph relative to the in-distribution
1122   for any given target skeleton sequence. The methodology behind our IDKR model involves extract-
1123
       ing the invariant subgraph from the original human skeleton graph and utilizing this ID-informative
       subgraph to identify the most relevant parameters for in-distribution sequences.
1124
1125   To effectively preserve ID-specific knowledge, the model incorporates a gradient computation pro-
1126   cess, which introduces some computational overhead. Specifically, on a single NVIDIA RTX 4090
1127   GPU with PyTorch 2.1.0, our model requires approximately 121ms to adapt to a new target se-
1128   quence. This computational overhead is relatively small, allowing our method to achieve state-of-
1129
       the-art performance in terms of Mean Per Joint Position Error (MPJPE). Although this adaptation
       time is slightly longer than the standard HPP method—siMLPe (84.3ms), it is faster than the state-
1130
       of-the-art H/P-TTP method (130ms). Therefore, we believe that the proposed method is still efficient
1131
       enough for practical applications, taking only 121 ms to predict a 1000 ms motion sequence.
