                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Data loss and corruption are common incidents that often lead to catastrophic
012
               consequences in both theoretical and experimental facets of data analytics. The
013
               aspiration to minimize the impacts of such consequences drives the demand for
014            the development of effective data analytic tools and imputation methods to replace
015            missing, corrupted, or artifacted data. The focus of this paper is on multivariate
016            time series imputation, for which we develop a dynamical systems-theoretic deep
017            learning approach. The central idea is to view a multivariate time series as a
018            trajectory of a dynamical system. Then, we construct a deep reservoir computing
019            architecture to model the temporal evolution of the system by using existing data
020            in the time series. In particular, this architecture is composed of a cascade of echo
021            state network (ESN) layers with diminishing reservoir sizes. We then propose a
022
               layer-by-layer training scheme, which gives rise to a deep learning-based time
               series imputation algorithm. We further provide a rigorous convergence analysis of
023
               this algorithm by exploiting the echo state property of ESN, and demonstrate the
024
               imputation performance as well as the efficiency of the training process by utilizing
025            both synthetic and real-world datasets arising from diverse applications.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      With the rapid advancement in sensing and data storage technology, acquiring multivariate time series
031
      data has become easily achievable and increasingly popular across various fields, including medicine,
032   economics, and climate science(Wu et al., 2017; Fernández-Gómez et al., 2017; Nguyen et al., 2020).
033   Accompanying the abundance of data is a common occurrence of data irregularity, where partial
034   recordings, such as sparse snapshots or multiple segments, are lost or damaged due to software
035   corruption or hardware impairment. Missing data undoubtedly poses a significant challenge in data
036   analytics tasks and may eventually lead to bottlenecks that hinder progress in scientific research.
037   Driven by the critical need to minimize the impact of missing data, imputation—concerned with
038   replacing missing data with appropriate substituted values—has garnered prominent attention in the
039   realm of machine learning (ML).
040   The predominant focus of research in data imputation lies in crafting statistics-driven interpolation
041   strategies and ML-enabled methods to deduce the values of absent data points, especially when those
042   points are isolated, sparse, or randomly omitted. Yet, in the realm of multivariate time series, missing
043   data can span consecutive (long) snapshots and multiple spatial dimensions. Imputing such data
044   exceeds the capacities of many current techniques, necessitating the creation of novel methodologies.
045
046   Our contributions. In this work, we propose a novel dynamical systems-theoretic deep learning
047   architecture for imputation of multivariate time series with isolated, sparse, or randomly omitted data
048   samples as well as time series with missing data spanning consecutive (long) snapshots and multiple
049   spatial dimensions. Specific contributions of this work are summarized below.
050   (1) We construct a deep reservoir computing network (RCN) architecture, composed of multiple
051   layers of echo state network (ESN) layers with diminishing reservoir size to learn the underlying
052   dynamical system generating the multivariate time series. (2) We propose a layer-by-layer training
053   scheme for the proposed deep RCN, which gives rise to the DL-DRCN algorithm for multivariate
      time series imputation. (3) Rigorous convergence analyses of the DL-DRCN algorithm are provided.

059   2    R ELATED W ORKS
060
061
062   Missing value imputation problems have been widely studied over the past decades, with comprehen-
063   sive insights provided in various works, such as (Schafer & Graham, 2002; Little & Rubin, 2002;
064   Fang & Wang, 2020; Osman et al., 2018; Lin & Tsai, 2020; Emmanuel et al., 2021). The main
065
      body of works concerning adding and removing datapoints from time series focuses on regularizing
      irregularly sampled data. This refers to instances where the sampling rate is not uniform across the
066
      entire time series (Li & Marlin, 2020; Cao et al., 2018; Shukla & Marlin, 2021; Weerakody et al.,
067
      2021). Some of the regularizing techniques proposed in these works have also been tailored to tackle
068   time-series imputation problems, where the missing data are filled in by mechanisms that adhere to
069   certain assumptions on the distribution of the time series (Moritz et al., 2015; Kim et al., 2023; Little
070   & Rubin, 2002; Azur et al., 2011; Fortuin et al., 2020). Interpolation is a popular non-probabilistic
071   times-series imputation technique, e.g., linear interpolation (Shukla & Marlin, 2018; Junninen et al.,
072   2004) and spline interpolation (Zhang, 2016; Junninen et al., 2004), where the values of missing
073   data are estimated by fitting the observed data to linear and higher-order polynomials, respectively.
074   However, these traditional methods often struggle to deliver satisfactory performance when applied
075   to multivariate time series characterized by high spatial dimensions.
076   Due to their extraordinary computing power, various neural network-based multivariate time series
077   imputation methods have been proposed, which have also achieved significant successes (Kidger
078   et al., 2020; Che et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2018; Luo et al., 2018; Shan et al., 2021). These include
079   methods that utilize static, deep generative models, such as generative adversarial network (GAN)
080   (Gong et al., 2024; Luo et al., 2018), attention networks (Shukla & Marlin, 2021; Du et al., 2023),
081   graph neural networks (Cini et al., 2022), and diffusion models (Tashiro et al., 2021; Alcaraz &
082
      Strodthoff, 2023; Rasul et al., 2021), as well as dynamic, recurrent neural networks (RNN) (Liu et al.,
      2019; Cao et al., 2018; Che et al., 2018; Yoon et al., 2019; Weerakody et al., 2021; Lipton et al., 2016).
083
      For instance, gated recurrent unit (GRU) models have gained in popularity in multivariate time-series
084
      imputation due to their capability to infer missing patterns and integrate them into observed data
085   (Che et al., 2018; Cho et al., 2014; Chung et al., 2014; Kim et al., 2023). Typical examples of
086   GRU-integrated imputation techniques include filling missing values with zero (GRU-zero), the mean
087   of observed data (GRU-mean), the last observed values (GRU-forward), and an exponential decay
088   mechanism between observations (GRU-D) (Che et al., 2018). Furthermore, a GAN-based framework
089   is introduced, which employs a modified GRU to capture the complex dynamics of multivariate time
090   series (Luo et al., 2018).
091   More recently, Neural Ordinary Differential Equation (Neural ODE) (Chen et al., 2018; Habiba &
092   Pearlmutter, 2020) has been demonstrated as an efficient framework for the purpose of multivariate
093   time series imputation. Different from traditional RNNs with discrete sequences of hidden layers,
094   Neural ODEs are continuous-depth models whose outputs can be computed by solving ODEs. This
095   characteristic of Neural Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs) has been demonstrated to offer
096   improved imputation performance and greater computational efficiency when compared to RNN-
097   based methods. Further, ODE-RNNs have been introduced to further enhance the data imputation
098   capabilities of Neural ODEs, exhibiting a notable advantage in handling irregularly sampled time
099
      series (Rubanova et al., 2019). Nevertheless, there is scarce literature addressing the imputation of
      multivariate time series with significant gaps, such as consecutive missing data spanning the temporal
100
      dimensions across various spatial dimensions. This gap persists despite increasing concern across
101
      diverse fields (Liu et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2022; Velasco-Gallego & Lazakis, 2022). In this work,
102   we aim to address this gap by integrating techniques in dynamical systems theory with reservoir
103   computing networks (RCNs), particularly echo state networks (ESNs) (Jaeger, 2007; 2005; Millea,
104   2014; Luko!evičius, 2012; Lu et al., 2017). We will begin by introducing ESNs from the perspective
105   of dynamical systems theory in the next section and introduce a novel deep RCN architecture in
106   section 4.1, which is fundamentally different from the deep ESN structures proposed in (Gallicchio
107   et al., 2017; Jaeger, 2008) or other variations of RC frameworks as discussed in (Gauthier et al., 2021;
      Li et al., 2024).

116   3.1   M ULTIVARIATE T IME S ERIES I MPUTATION FROM DYNAMICAL S YSTEM V IEWPOINTS
117
118   Notations. We represent a multivariate time series in terms of a matrix X1:T = [x1 , . . . , xT ] →
119   Rd→T , where the tth column xt → Rd is the tth snapshot and the ith row xi → R1→T is the ith
120   component of the mutivariate time series. We further assume that in each snapshot xt , at least one
121   component is not missing. The imputation task considered in this work is to infer the missing values
122   in some components of X1:T , denoted by Y1:T = [y1 , . . . , yT ] → Rq→T , by using the data in the
123
      complemented components U1:T = [u1 , . . . , uT ] → Rp→T with p = d ↑ q. We also allow U to
      contain missing data under the condition that ut and yt do not simultaneously have missing values
124
      for all t = 1, . . . , T . We further associate the imputation target Y with mask M1:T → Rq→T , whose
125
      (i, j)-entry is defied as mij = 0 if yij , the (i, j)-entry of Y1:T , is missing and mij = 1 otherwise.
126
127   Missing scenarios. The scenarios of missing data can be classified into two types: (1) data points
128   are absent without discernible patterns, referred to as random missing scenario, which is similar
129   to the case proposed in (Alcaraz & Strodthoff, 2023); (2) some components have missing data in
130   consecutive snapshots, referred to block missing.
131
132   Dynamical systems-theoretic approach to time series analytics. The main idea is to think of
133   the time series Y , that is, the imputation target, is a trajectory of a dynamical system in the form
134   of yt = f (yt↑1 , ut ). Of course, if the equation, specifically the function f : Rp ↓ Rq ↔ Rp ,
135   modeling the system is discerned, the missing data in Y can be directly restored by evolving the
136   system. From this perspective, the time series imputation task can be formulated as a dynamical
137
      system model learning task, for which recurrent neural networks (RNNs), owing to their dynamical
      system characteristic, stand out as the prime candidate for the learning tool.

143   to as the reservoir, whose state, denoted as rt → RN , is determined by the dynamical system
144   rt = !! (rt↑1 , ut ), where N is the reservoir dimension, equivalently the number of neurons in the

148   the number of snapshots in the given time series. The output layer projects the reservoir state to a
149
      low-dimensional space Rq with q ↗ N by using the readout map ŷt = h(rt ), which will be trained

470   6    C ONCLUSION
471
472   In this paper, we present a novel RCN-based method for analyzing multivariate time series data.
473   By utilizing the dynamical system characterization of RCNs, we propose a deep RCN architecture,
474   composed of a sequence of ESN layers, to learn the systems generating the data so that the missing
475
      data can be recovered by the temporal evolution of the learned systems. To improve the computational
      efficiency, the reservoir size of an ESN layer is decreased compared to that of its predecessor, and the
476
      deep RCN is trained layer-by-layer, which gives rise to an iterative multivariate time series imputation
477
      algorithm. We further provide the computational analysis as well as the convergence proof of the
478   algorithm, and demonstrate its excellent performance by using both synthetic and real-world data.
479
480   Limitations and future works. Since the ESP is required to be satisfied while training the proposed
481   DL-DRCN to eliminate the impact of the choices of the initial reservoir states on the imputation
482   result, the proposed algorithm may not perform satisfactorily if the missing data are located at the
483   beginning of the time series. On the other hand, the convergence of the algorithm requires that
484   the dimension of the reservoir state in each ESN layer is set to be greater than a lower bound (the
485   dimension of minimal realization ESN). Since this requirement is highly dependent on data and varies
      across different scenarios, this design parameter often relies on empirical experiments.


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      In this work, the deep RCN is trained by using the observed components of multivariate time series.
487   The model requires the input and the imputed target sequences to contain no overlapping time slots
488   where data is missing. In principle, the algorithm is still applicable if there are overlapping missing
489   data in both input and target time series. This will be investigated in our future works. In addition, the
490   requirement mentioned above, that is, the reservoir dimension is greater than the spatial dimension
491   of the time series is primarily due to the use of linear ESN output layers for projecting the desired
492   outputs to the reservoir spaces. In the future, we will design ESNs with nonlinear readout maps in the
493   output layers to waive this requirement, which gives another approach to improve the computational
