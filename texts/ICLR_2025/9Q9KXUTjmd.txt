011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Federated Learning (FL) can be coordinated under the orchestration of a central
014            server to build a privacy-preserving model without collaborative data exchange.
015
               However, participant data heterogeneity leads to local optima divergence, affect-
               ing convergence outcomes. Recent research focused on global sharpness-aware
016
               minimization (SAM) and dynamic regularization to enhance consistency between
017
               global and local generalization and optimization objectives. Nonetheless, the es-
018            timation of global SAM introduces additional computational and memory over-
019            head. At the same time, the local dynamic regularizer cannot capture the global
020            update state due to training isolation. This paper proposes a novel FL algorithm,
021            FedTOGA, designed to consider optimization and generalization objectives while
022            maintaining minimal uplink communication overhead. By linking local perturba-
023            tions to global updates, global generalization consistency is improved. Addition-
024            ally, linking the local dynamic regularizer to global updates increases the percep-
025            tion of the global gradient and enhances optimization consistency. Global updates
026
               are passively received by clients, reducing overhead. We also propose neighbor-
               hood perturbation to approximate local perturbation, analyzing its strengths and
027
               working principle. Theoretical analysis shows FedTOGA achieves faster con-
028
               vergence O(1/T ) on the non-convex function. Empirical studies demonstrate that
029            FedTOGA outperforms existing algorithms, with a 1% accuracy increase and 30%
030            faster convergence, achieving state-of-the-art.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   The widespread connectivity of mobile terminals has dramatically propelled the development of in-
036   dustries related to big data. However, the massive data throughput has led to communication link
037   congestion and increased privacy risks. Consequently, to safeguard data privatization and localiza-
038   tion, FL McMahan et al. (2017) has garnered significant attention as a distributed machine learning
039
      (ML) method that avoids the need for data exchange. Nonetheless, due to the variations in data
      distribution among participants Fan et al. (2022; 2024c), conflicts in local optimization targets arise,
040
      potentially causing the global loss function to converge to a sharp local minimum Woodworth et al.
041
      (2020). As illustrated in Figures 1a-1c, with an increase in local heterogeneity, there is a steep rise
042   in the sharpness of the global model loss. Moreover, due to the limitations of uplink bandwidth to
043   the global server Speedtest (2024), FL employs a вАЬComputation-Then-AggregationвАЭ (CTA) strategy
044   Zhang et al. (2020), which utilizes multiple rounds of local training and partial participation to al-
045   leviate communication bottlenecks. However, by increasing synchronization intervals and reducing
046   participation rates, the discrepancy between local and global models will be significantly amplified
047   Wang et al. (2020); Li et al. (2020b).
048   In response to these challenges, most studies address global consistency issues via the Empirical
049   Risk Minimization (ERM) Malinovsky et al. (2020). However, when handling highly heteroge-
050   neous datasets, global solutions may become trapped in steep local minima, rendering it difficult
051   to provide reliable estimates Sun et al. (2023b) and potentially causing the optimizer to stagnate.
052   Consequently, recent innovations have leveraged Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) Foret et al.
053   (2021), which seeks to identify a flatter minimum by minimizing the perturbed loss of the model,
      thereby enhancing generalization capabilities. FedSAM was introduced by incorporating SAM into

080   additional local gradient ascent computations, while reducing computational overhead may lead to
081   insufficient local generalization. In both methods, the difference in local perturbation scales may
082   be significant when facing clients with prolonged disconnections, thereby disrupting generalization.
083   Apart from addressing the consistency of perturbation generalization, local objective optimization
084   has also been intensely studied, as seen in dynamic regularize algorithms Zhang et al. (2020); Acar
085   et al. (2021); Sun et al. (2023b). However, its performance degrades significantly as the local interval
086   expands. This is because the CTA strategy makes local fail to capture the global updates state.
087   To achieve a reliable, stable, and consistent global model, we propose a novel algorithm named
088   FedTOGA, as illustrated in Fig.1f. FedTOGA initially guides global update gradients to merge
089   with local perturbations, thereby enhancing local generalization consistency. Simultaneously, it
090   employs global updates to correct the local dynamic regularizer, reinforcing consistency with the
091   global optimization objective. This approach significantly improves performance, even under ex-
092   treme conditions characterized by highly heterogeneous data or limited client participation. Due
093
      to the communication interval, the universal SAM optimizer applied on the global server cannot
      precisely capture the perturbations occurring during local updates on client devices. We introduce
094
      the global update gradient as an approximation to maintain local consistency, replacing the global
095
      perturbation estimation used in FedSMOO Sun et al. (2023b). Furthermore, the universal dynamic
096   regularizer dynamically adjusts the optimization direction based on the client drift estimated from
097   dual variable statistics; however, it neglects the global gradientвАЩs changing trends. Hence, we con-
098   sider the global stationary condition and propose leveraging the global update gradient to correct
099   the local dynamic regularizer, further aligning with global and local objectives. At the same time,
100   to further reduce the computational overhead, we propose the neighborhood gradient perturbation.
101   When the interval between local training sessions on the client side exceeds one, the client simulates
102   the current perturbation by utilizing cached gradients stored in a gradient register, thereby alleviating
103   computational costs. Unlike FedCM Xu et al. (2021) and MoFedSAM Qu et al. (2022), the global
104
      update is not treated as a trade-off term with the local perturbation, meaning that their coefficients
      do not sum to one. As the active local clients converge, they ultimately reach a globally stationary
105
      state characterized by a smooth loss landscape.
106
107   Theoretically, FedTOGA can achieve a rapid convergence rate of O(1/T ) in non-convex settings.
      Extensive evaluations were conducted on the CIFAR10/100 datasets, demonstrating that FedTOGA

444   Performance with compared benchmarks. As shown in Tables 1 and 2, the proposed FedTOGA
445   algorithm performs excellently on various heterogeneous datasets regarding convergence speed and
446   final achieved accuracy. Table 1, which details the test accuracy of the LeNet model, demonstrates
447
      that FedTOGA significantly outperforms other algorithms with different heterogeneous data con-
      ditions. Specifically, under the Dirichlet-0.1 setting on the CIFAR10 dataset, FedTOGA attains an
448
      accuracy of 82.05%, marking a significant improvement of over 7.37% compared to vanilla FedAvg
449
      and a 1.99% increase over the second-highest baseline accuracy. Similar results are observed in
450   Table 2 for the ResNet18 model, FedTOGA outperforms all current baseline algorithms. As seen
451   in Table 3, FedTOGA also exhibits a significant advantage in terms of convergence speed. When
452   reaching 80% accuracy, FedTOGA converges 3.6√Ч faster than FedSAM and 1.2√Ч faster than the
453   second-best algorithm. Similarly, when reaching 82% accuracy, FedTOGA converges 4.7√Ч faster
454   compared to FedSAM and 1.5√Ч faster than the second-best algorithm. This indicates that FedTOGA
455   achieves the target accuracy with significantly reduced computation and communication overhead
456   compared to other methods.
457   Impact of heterogeneity. We use the Dirichlet and Pathological methods for data partitioning. For
458   the Dirichlet distribution, we adopt with variance coefficients u of 0.1 and 0.6. We use coefficients
459   c of 3 and 6 for the Pathological distribution. As shown in Tables 1 and 2, increased data hetero-
460   geneity leads to decreased accuracy across all algorithms. However, FedTOGA exhibits the smallest
461   accuracy drop. Specifically, for the Resnet18 model, as u changes from 0.6 to 0.1 under the Dirich-
462   let distribution on the CIFAR10 dataset, FedSAMвАЩs accuracy decreases from 81.46% to 77.03%,
463   a 4.43% reduction. At the same time, the second-best algorithm, FedSMOO, shows a drop from
464
      86.08% to 81.80%, a 4.28% reduction. In contrast, FedTOGAвАЩs accuracy declines from 86.99% to
      83.16%, a 3.83% drop. Similar trends are observed under the Pathological distribution, underscoring
465
      FedTOGAвАЩs superior stability and accuracy across varying levels of data heterogeneity.
466
467   Impact of partial participation. We fix all hyperparameters except the client participation rate
468   to assess its effect on accuracy. As illustrated in Table 2, a reduction in the client participation
469   rate from 10% to 5% results in a modest decline in accuracy across all algorithms. For instance,
470
      on the CIFAR10 dataset, under the challenging pathological distribution with c = 3, FedTOGAвАЩs
      accuracy decreases marginally from 84.55% to 83.35%, a reduction of just 1.40%, while FedSMOO
471
      experiences a sharper decline from 82.79% to 81.01%, a reduction of 1.78%. Similarly, under the
472
      Dirichlet distribution with u = 0.1, FedTOGAвАЩs accuracy decreases from 86.99% to 85.21%, a
473   decrease of 1.78%, whereas FedSMOOвАЩs accuracy drops from 86.08% to 84.96%, a reduction of
474   1.12%. Despite these reductions, FedTOGA consistently outperforms other algorithmsвАЩ accuracy,
475   highlighting its robust generalization capability and stability.

478   7     C ONCLUSION
479
480   In this paper, we propose a novel FL algorithm, FedTOGA, which, for the first time, estimates global
481   perturbations by combining global training gradients and enhances the local dynamic regularizer.
482   This ensures local clients can effectively align with the global generalization and optimization ob-
483   jectives. FedTOGA facilitates the efficient search for globally consistent flat minima and accelerates
484   convergence without incurring additional local storage or uplink communication overhead. Theo-
485   retical analysis guarantees that FedTOGA achieves a fast convergence rate of O(1/T ). Extensive
      experiments were conducted to verify its efficiency and remarkable performance.

      In this section, we review the contributions of related works.
707
708   Federated Learning FL gained widespread attention upon its introduction due to its data-exchange-
709   free nature. FedAvg McMahan et al. (2017) As its foundational framework, it allows for collabora-
710
      tive modeling without exchanging data Stich (2019). However, due to various irresistible factors, the
      data of cooperative devices show heterogeneous distribution, which makes the modeling effective-
711
      ness suffer. Therefore, many studies based on empirical loss minimization have been proposed to
712
      solve this problem. FedProx Li et al. (2020a) employs a simple and intuitive practice, ensuring that
713   the local model is not far from the global model. Specifically, regular terms are introduced during
714   local training to limit the distance between the local and global models. SCAFFOLD Karimireddy
715   et al. (2020), Mime Karimireddy et al. (2021) uses control variables for local updates. However, it
716   requires greater communication overhead. FedDyn Acar et al. (2021), FedPD Zhang et al. (2020)
717   considers the inconsistency of the local optimal point with the global optimal point to be a fun-
718   damental dilemma, which aligns the locally optimal solution to the global optimal solution via a
719   dynamic regularizer. FedPA Al-Shedivat et al. (2021) removes bias from client updates by estimat-
720   ing a global posterior. FedDC Gao et al. (2022) takes decoupled local and global updates to mitigate
721
      heterogeneity. Furthermore, recent research has shown that local model bias is similar to catas-
      trophic forgetting in continuous learning Lee et al. (2022; 2024); Shoham et al. (2019); Wang et al.
722
      (2023), Clients overriding previously important parameters to learn a new task resulted in disrupting
723
      pretask performance. Some studies have mitigated global knowledge collapse by task recall Re-
724   buffi et al. (2017); Dong et al. (2022). Server momentum-based Sun et al. (2023a) algorithms also
725   play an important role in FL. Zaheer et al. (2018) investigates the convergence failure of ADAM
726   in certain non-convex settings and develops an adaptive optimizer, YOGI, which aims to improve
727   convergence. Reddi et al. (2021) integrates it in a FL framework. FedAvgM Hsu et al. (2019) us-
728   ing Momentum Qian (1999), while FedACG Kim et al. (2024) utilizes NAG Nesterov (1983). And
729   FedCM Xu et al. (2021) mitigates local heterogeneity by using the proximity global update gradient
730   applied to the client momentum. FedLADA Sun et al. (2023d) combines local ADAM with FedCM
731   to dynamically modify local deviations.
732   Sharpness-aware Minimization. Many studies Hochreiter & Schmidhuber (1994); Dinh et al.
733   (2017) have pointed out that flat minima imply superior generalization performance, which pos-
734   sesses greater robustness to model perturbations. In order to minimize sharpness Keskar et al.
735   (2017), Foret et al. (2021); Becker et al. (2024) proposed sharpness-aware minimization (SAM),
736   and many works Li & Giannakis (2023); Mueller et al. (2023) were carried out. Specifically, SAM
737   only captures the sharpness of specific small batches of data, and VaSSO Mueller et al. (2023) aims
738
      to address this issue. Our FedTOGA can also help solve this problem to some extent. First, we add
                                     t
      neighborhood perturbations gћГi,kвИТ1    to help the local SAM optimizer perceive the amount of neigh-
739
740   boring batch data perturbations (optionally), and global update perturbations via вИЖtвИТ1 . In addition,
741
      m-SAM Andriushchenko & Flammarion (2022) can be considered to be closely related to federated
      sharpness minimization. m-SAM Andriushchenko & Flammarion (2022) quantifies the sharpness
742
      of batches across m training point batches, averaging out multiple disjoint batches in the generated
743
      Updates. The neighborhood global perturbation proposed by our FedTOGA alleviates the problem
744   of local perturbation isolation in the FL paradigm and can be applied to all existing algorithms.

      FedSAM and gets some results. And FedSpeed Sun et al. (2023c) uses SAM to optimize FedDyn

754   timizes global sharpness and reduces computation. As we have seen, FedSAM Qu et al. (2022);

880   A.3   N EIGHBOURHOOD P ERTURBATION STRATEGY
881
      How Neighbourhood Perturbation works? Recall the fact that when the local iteration interval is
882
      greater than 1, the gradient register needs to be cleared by (optimizer.zero grad()) each time the
883   gradient computation is performed. This is to prevent the accumulating gradient from causing errors.
884   Recognizing that using the neighborhood gradient variables in the registers does not need substantial
885   additional overhead, we use the neighboring gradients temporarily stored in the registers to simulate
886   the current perturbation gradient. We give an example of a local gradient computation to help better
887   understand the workflow. We initialize the model ќЄ0 and a gradient register G = [вИЕ]. After the
888   first calculation of the gradient (loss.backward()), the register is updated G = [g1 = вИЗf (ќЄ0 )].
889   If SGD is used, ќЄ1 = ќЄ0 вИТ ќЈg1 , followed by clearing the register G = [вИЕ] before computing the
890   second gradient. If SAM is used, then the perturbation is computed as ќі1 = ѕБ вИ•gg11 вИ• , then the gradient
891   register is emptied, and after the gradient is computed again as gћГ1 = вИЗf (ќЄ + ќі1 ), perform model
892   update ќЄ1 = ќЄ0 вИТ ќЈgћГ1 . So the register status changes to G = [вИЕ] вЖТ [g1 ] вЖТ [вИЕ] вЖТ [gћГ1 ]. When
893   neighborhood perturbation is enabled, gradient calculation and clearing before SAM are no longer
894
      required. Therefore, the register status changes to G = [g0 ] вЖТ [вИЕ] вЖТ [g1 ]. The client will directly
      use the previously calculated gradient of the gradient register as the perturbation variable. You can
895
      observe the gradient calculation and cache change process in Fig. 2.
896
897   How does Perturbation Fusion work? With the above technical means of neighborhood per-
898   turbation, we can easily merge it in the perturbation computation by not emptying the gradient
899   cache before computing the perturbation, then the gradient cache state will change to G = [gћГ0 ] вЖТ
900
      [gћГ0 + g1 ] вЖТ [вИЕ] вЖТ [gћГ1 ].
901   What is LOOKAHEAD? LOOKAHEAD Zhang et al. (2019) uses a fast-slow-step mechanism,
902   where a retrospective is performed every K steps forward. The idea behind this is to take a step in
903   the direction of the current gradient update and then use a set of additional weights (called вАЬslow
904   weightsвАЭ) to take a step in the same direction but on a longer time scale. These slow weights
905   are updated less frequently than the original weights, effectively creating a вАЬlook aheadвАЭ into the
906
      future of the optimization process. Incorporating N-perturbation techniques forms a Lookahead-like
      updating mechanism that helps the optimizer escape local minima and saddle points more efficiently,
907
      leading to faster convergence. Experiments on the fusion of N-P with existing algorithms in Sec.B.7.
