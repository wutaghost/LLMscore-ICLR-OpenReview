               Discrete dynamical systems are principled models for real-world cascading phe-
               nomena on networks, and problems for learning dynamical systems have garnered
012
               considerable attention in ML. Existing studies on this topic typically assume that
013
               the training data is noise-free, an assumption that is often impractical. In this
014            work, we address this gap by investigating a more realistic and challenging set-
015            ting: learning discrete dynamical systems from data contaminated with noise. We
016            present efficient noise-tolerant learning algorithms that provide provable perfor-
017            mance guarantees, and establish tight bounds on sample complexity. We show
018            that, even in the presence of noise, the proposed learner only incurs a marginal
019            increases in training set size to infer a system. Notably, the number of training
020            samples required in the noisy setting is the same (to within a constant factor) as
021            the upper bound in the noise-free scenario. Further, the number of noisy training
022
               samples used by the algorithm is only a logarithmic factor higher than the best-
               known lower bound. Through experimental studies, we evaluate the empirical
023
               performance of the algorithms on both synthetic and real-world networks.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Background and Motivation. Discrete dynamical systems serve as formal models for various real-
030   world diffusion processes on networks, including the spread of rumors, information, and diseases
031   (Battiston et al., 2020; Ji et al., 2017; Lum et al., 2014; Sneddon et al., 2011; Schelling, 2006;
032   Laubenbacher & Stigler, 2004; Kauffman et al., 2003). For dynamical systems in the real world,
033
      however, one often cannot readily obtain a full system specification. Thus, learning unknown com-
      ponents of dynamical systems is an active research area (Chen et al., 2021; Chen & Poor, 2022;
034
      Conitzer et al., 2022; 2020; Rosenkrantz et al., 2022; He et al., 2016; Narasimhan et al., 2015;
035
      Dawkins et al., 2021; Adiga et al., 2019).
036
037   In essence, a discrete dynamical system consists of an underlying network over which a contagion
038   spreads. Vertices in the network are entities such as individuals, and edges denote their relation-
039
      ships. To model a cascade, each vertex has a contagion state and an interaction function. As the
      contagion spreads, the states of vertices evolve in discrete time steps based on the mechanism of
040
      interaction functions. Thus, the interaction functions play an important role in the system dynamics
041
      as they model the behavior of individuals. One concrete example is the threshold function, a classic
042   model for social contagions (Granovetter, 1978; Watts, 2002; Li et al., 2020; Trpevski et al., 2010;
043   Rosenkrantz et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2021). Here, each individual adopts a contagion (e.g., believes
044   a rumor) only when the number of its neighbors adopting this contagion reaches a tipping point.
045
      Our work focuses on learning discrete dynamical systems where the interaction functions are un-
046
      known. Notably, existing methods for learning interaction functions (e.g., (Adiga et al., 2019; Qiu
047
      et al., 2024a)) were developed under one critical assumption: the training data is noise-free. How-
048   ever, it is widely recognized that real-world data are often contaminated by noisy labels (i.e., clas-
049   sification noise) (Sarfraz et al., 2021; Natarajan et al., 2013; Cesa-Bianchi et al., 1999; Gupta &
050   Gupta, 2019; Kearns & Vazirani, 1994; Angluin & Laird, 1988). In particular, the presence of noise
051   in training data can significantly degrade the prediction accuracy of learning algorithms that are not
052   noise-tolerant. Nevertheless, noise-tolerant learning for discrete dynamical systems has not received
053   attention in the literature. In this work, we address this gap with a systematic study of learning dis-
      crete dynamical systems under classification noise.

      Problem description. There is a target ground-truth dynamical system where all the interaction
055   functions of vertices are unknown. A learner must infer the missing functions from only snapshots
056   of the system’s dynamics (provided in a training set) which specify the evolution of vertices’ states.
057   Moreover, the training set is contaminated by classification noise (Angluin & Laird, 1988) such that
058   in each snapshot of the dynamics, the updated state of each vertex is incorrect with some (unknown)
059   probability. Our goal is then to design noise-tolerant algorithms that learn all the unknown interac-
060   tion functions and recover a system that models the behavior of the true system, with performance
061   guarantees under the Probably Approximately Correct (PAC) model (Valiant, 1984).
062   Challenges. Empirical risk minimization (ERM) is often a promising method under the PAC frame-
063   work. However, the results in (Adiga et al., 2017) (Theorem 4 on page 132) show that in our setting,
064   ERM cannot be done efficiently unless P = NP. In particular, one cannot even efficiently approxi-
065   mate the problem of constructing a system that is consistent with the maximum number of training
066   samples. The second difficulty is that, in our noise setting (see Section 2), the probability of a multi-
067   class label (a vector of the states of vertices) being incorrect in the training set asymptotically goes
068   to one as the system size increases. In other words, almost surely all the multi-class labels in the
069
      training set are wrong.
070   Given the importance of the problem and the challenges, we aim to answer the following questions
071   about learning discrete dynamical systems: 1. Is efficient learning possible under classification
072   noise? 2. How many additional samples do we need compared to the noise-free case?
073   Our contributions. Despite the challenges, we answer both questions. In particular, we show that
074   one can still efficiently learn discrete dynamical systems under high classification noise. Notably,
075   the number of training samples required is the same (to within a constant factor) as the upper bound
076   in the noise-free scenario, and it is only a log factor higher than the best-known lower bound.
077
       • Efficient learnability. Formally, we propose two efficient noise-tolerant algorithms V-ERM and
078
         VisRange with respective theoretical and empirical advantages. Both algorithms achieve the
079      PAC guarantee: w.p. at least 1 − δ, the prediction error is at most ϵ, for any ϵ, δ > 0. However,
080      Algorithm V-ERM uses O(n2 log (n)) training samples, whereas Algorithm VisRange uses
081      only O(n log n) samples.
082      From a theoretical perspective, VisRange wins: the corresponding bound O(n log (n)) on the
083      sample complexity is close to optimal; it matches (to within a constant factor) the information-
084      theoretic upper bound in the noise-free scenario (Haussler, 1988; Laird, 2012). That is, the
085      algorithm VisRange is strongly noise-tolerant, with only a marginal increase in the number of
086      samples used in comparison with the noise-free case. Further, its sample complexity is only a
087
         factor O(log n) larger than the general lower bound in the noisy setting (Angluin & Laird, 1988).
088    • Experimental evaluation. We conduct an experimental study of the algorithms over both real
089      and synthetic networks. Our results highlight a drastic difference in their empirical behaviors:
090
         VisRange exhibits a phase transition w.r.t. the error rate when the size of the training set reaches
         a critical threshold. This phenomenon is expected from our theoretical analysis. In contrast, V-
091
         ERM shows a steady increase in learning accuracy as more training samples are given. Overall,
092
         our experiments underscore that V-ERM is the preferred empirical algorithm due to its simplicity
093      and consistent performance. We then further explore the property of V-ERM w.r.t. different
094      model parameters such as network size, density, and error rates.
095

103   (2012); Natarajan et al. (2013); Bshouty et al. (2002); Diakonikolas et al. (2019)).

118   2.1   D ISCRETE DYNAMICAL SYSTEMS
119
120   A discrete dynamical system over domain B = {0, 1} is defined as a pair h∗ = (G, F), where (i)
121
      G = (V, E) is an underlying undirected graph with n vertices (e.g., individuals in a social network);
      (ii) F = {fv : v ∈ V} is a set of interaction functions, where fv is the function for v ∈ V.
122
123   Interaction functions. Each vertex in G has a state from domain B representing its contagion state
124   (e.g., inactive or active). Starting from the initial states of vertices, a system h∗ evolves over discrete
125   time, with vertices updating their states synchronously using the interaction functions. Specifically,
126   for any interaction function fv , the inputs are the current states of v’s neighbors; the output of
127
      fv is the next state of v. In this work, we focus on dynamical systems with threshold interaction
      functions. Such systems are fundamental models for the spread of social contagions such as rumors
128
      and information (Granovetter, 1978; Watts, 2002; Li et al., 2020; Trpevski et al., 2010; Rosenkrantz
129
      et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2021).
130
131   Formally, each vertex v ∈ V has an integer threshold τv∗ ∈ [0, degv + 1], where degv is the degree
132   of v in G. At each time t ≥ 1, the function fv computes v’s state at the next time-step t + 1 as
133
      follows: fv outputs (state) 1 if the number of state-1 vertices in v’s neighborhood at time t is at least
      τv∗ ; fv outputs (state) 0 otherwise. In the rumor-spreading example, a person’s belief changes when
134
      the number of neighbors believing in the rumor reaches a certain tipping point. An example of a
135
      threshold dynamical system is shown in Figure 4 in Appendix A.2.
136
137   Configurations. A configuration C of a system h∗ is a length-n binary vector specifying the con-
138   tagion states of all vertices; here, C[v] is the state of v under C. Thus, one can view the evolution
139
      of system h∗ as a time-ordered trajectory of configurations. In a trajectory, a configuration C ′ is the
      successor of C if the system evolves from C to C ′ in a single time-step, denoted by C ′ = h∗ (C).

145   are unknown. In this case, the hypothesis class H consists of all systems with the same graph
146   as h∗ , over all possible threshold assignments for vertices in V. By observing the noisy snapshots
147   of system dynamics (given in a training set), the learner’s goal is to infer the missing interaction
148
      functions and learn a system h ∈ H that closely approximates the behavior of the true h∗ .

152   multi-class learning context. Let N = {ηv : v ∈ V(G)} be a collection of unknown noise rates

166   Note that ηv is often fixed w.r.t n. Thus, we sometimes omit the terms involving ηv (or η̄) in our
167   bounds for clarity, and focus on the expressions with the dominating terms given as a function of n.
168   Remark 1. In our learning under noise model, the learner is not involved in generating the training
169   set. That is, the learner does not generate training data or sample the data; rather, the data are
170   provided to our model. Therefore, it does not have control over how the noise is applied to each
171
      vertex, and it also does not know which vertex’s state is wrong in any training sample. Further,
      the learner does not have the exact value of each noise parameter ηv , v ∈ V. It is important to
172
      note that our multi-class learning model is different from the standard Random Classification Noise
173
      (RCN) model. In particular, in the RCN model, strictly less than half of the labels are incorrect
174   in expectation. On the other hand, in our setting, the probability of a multi-class label (i.e., the
175   successor of a configuration) in the training set being incorrect (i.e., altered by noise) asymptotically
176   goes to one as n increases. In other words, almost surely all the labels in the training set are wrong.
177   Remark 2. In real-world scenarios, the training set T can be viewed as a collection of snapshots of
178   the true system dynamics: these snapshots can exhibit correlations (w.r.t. the system dynamics), as
179   one snapshot might be the immediate predecessor of another in a trajectory. Consequently, learning
180   from a trajectory of the system evolution can be cast as a special case of our setting. Specifically,
181   suppose T consists solely of configurations on a trajectory P . Then the underlying sampling dis-
182   tribution (unknown to the learner) is such that only configurations on P are sampled with positive
183   probability, while all other configurations are sampled with probability P . In this work, we present
184   learners that work under arbitrary sampling distributions, including the one mentioned above.
185   Remark 3. We now discuss the model parameters where assumptions are made:
186   1. Noise rate ηv < 1/2. As highlighted on Page 6, Section 2.1 of (Angluin & Laird, 1988), under
187      the Random Classification Noise Model, when the noise rate equals 1/2, the errors in the noise
188      process destroys all information about the underlying true hypothesis in the training set. As
189      a result, nothing can be learned in a meaningful manner and noise-tolerant learning becomes
190      impossible if ηv = 1/2. This is also discussed in (Kearns & Vazirani, 1994).
191      Next, when ηv > 1/2, note that the problem is equivalent to our ηv < 1/2 case due to symmetry.
192      In particular, in the ηv > 1/2 regime, one can simply take the complement of the noisy label
193      for each individual vertex, which leads to an error of 1 − ηv < 1/2. This is also discussed
194      in (Angluin & Laird, 1988). Due to these reasons, in our setting (and also in the existing work
195      on random classification noise model), having ηv < 1/2 represents the most general form.
196   2. Graph is known. As shown in (Qiu et al., 2024a), when the graph is unknown, one cannot
197      efficiently learn the interaction function of dynamical systems even in the noise-free scenario.
198      It immediately follows that the problem remains intractable in our noisy case. Therefore, if
199      efficient noise-tolerant learning of interaction functions is possible, one needs to assume that the
200
         underlying graph structure is known.
201   Learning from the noisy training set. Even though the training set T is from the noisy oracle O,
202   the aim of the learner remains to find an appropriate hypothesis w.r.t the true (unknown) distribution
203   D. Given a new C ∼ D, a learned hypothesis h should predict the true successor C ′ (i.e., h∗ (C)) with
204   high probability. Formally, we use errD (h) = Pr C∼D [h(C) ̸= h∗ (C)] to denote the error admitted
205   by h. Note that in our setting, a prediction is considered incorrect if h(C) and h∗ (C) disagree on the
206   state of at least one vertex; that is, we want to predict the output states of all vertices correctly.
207   Following the PAC model, for any parameters ϵ, δ > 0, a learner should find a hypothesis h ∈ H
208   such that with probability at least 1 − δ over T ∼ Oq , the error errD (h) ≤ ϵ. The minimum number
209   of training examples required by any learner to achieve the above PAC-guarantee is known as the
210   sample complexity of the class H.

      One immediate question is whether the sample complexity established in the previous section can be
301
      improved. We now answer the question with a problem-dependent analysis and improve the bound
302
      to matches the general upper bound in noise-free scenarios. Toward this end, we present the
303   algorithm VisRange that uses O(1/ϵ · n log (n/δ)) training samples under our high-noise setting,
304   which is only a constant factor larger than the general upper bound for learning under the noise-free
305   scenario (Haussler, 1988). That is, despite the presence of noise, the algorithm achieves efficient
306   learnability without needing many more samples compared to the noise-free scenario. Further, our
307   established upper bound is only a factor O(log n) larger than the best-known lower bound (Aslam
308   & Decatur, 1996). Due to space limit, all proofs appear in Appendix A.4.
309   We first discuss a simplified version of the algorithm for ease of understanding. This simpler algo-
310   rithm uses O(1/ϵ · ∆n log (n/δ)) training samples, where ∆ is the maximum degree of the graph.

      4.2   A LGORITHM S CALABILITY
376   We remark on the scalability of our algorithms to large networks. First, the running time of all the
377   proposed algorithms is O(n∆2 q), where n is the number of vertices, ∆ is the maximum degree,
      and q is the training set size. More importantly, all algorithms are inherently parallelizable at


                                                           7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      vertex level. This significantly improve the scalability of the model, effectively reducing the time
379   complexity by a factor of n, giving a runtime of O(∆2 q).
380
381   Further, we have proved that, when the noise level increases, the sufficient number of samples for the
382
      algorithm does not grow w.r.t n, the dominant term. Consequently, in noisy real-world systems, only
      a minimal additional training set is required to handle increased noise. These features collectively
383
      make our algorithms scalable to larger and more complex systems.
384
385   Dependency on n. We now discuss how the dependency on n in the sample complexity (even
386   before parallelization) can be further relaxed. In particular, consider a scenario where only a σ
387   number of vertices has unknown interaction functions, and we want to learn these vertices. Then,
388
      by the mechanism of the proposed algorithms, our techniques can be naturally extended where the
      factor n in our sample complexity analysis can be all replaced by σ. Consequently, when σ is low
389
      (e.g., only a small fraction of vertices, say log (n), are to be learned), the corresponding number of
390
      samples is significantly reduced, and as σ approaches n, the bounds approach our bounds.
391
392   Extension to other loss. We discuss an extension of our algorithm to a natural loss function based
393   on Hamming weights, such as the one given in the the PMAC model (Balcan & Harvey, 2011). In
394
      this model, instead of trying to predict the states of all vertices correctly, the notion of a successful
      prediction is relaxed: we allow at most β fraction of the states of the n vertices to be wrong. This
395
      new setting implies that the Hamming distance between the predicted configuration and the true
396
      configuration can be at most βn. Importantly, our algorithms can be extended to this context without
397   any modification. Further, the resulting sample complexity bound is only increased by an extra
398   multiplicative factor 1/β and 1/β 2 for Algorithm 1 and Algorithm 2,3, respectively.

424   Remark 7. With everything in place, we now discuss the advantages of V-ERM (i.e., Algorithm 1)
425   and VisRange (i.e., Algorithm 3) from both theory and practice perspectives:
426    - Theory. With more problem-dependent mechanism and analysis as given for VisRange, one
427      can significantly improve the upper bound (by a multiplicative factor of n) of the sufficient
428      training size from the bound derived by V-ERM. Specifically, there exists a problem instance
429      (e.g., a graph G and a distribution D) such that VisRange requires multiplicative Ω(n) fewer
430      samples than V-ERM to achieve the same error rate. Further, VisRange provides an important
431      (and somewhat surprising) theory insight: despite the presence of classification noise, one can
         still efficiently learn threshold dynamical systems using the same number of training samples (to


                                                            8
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
                     within only a constant factor) as the noise-free case. Further, the number of required samples is
433                  only a factor O(log n) higher than the lower bound.
434
435
           - Practice. A distinct nature of VisRange is that, one expects to see a drop in the error rate only
             when the size of the training set q reaches a critical threshold (i.e., a phase transition). Before
436
             q reaches that threshold, however, the behavior of the algorithm can be unpredictable as the
437
             high-probability guarantee is not yet satisfied. On the other hand, such a critical threshold on the
438          training set size is not inherent to V-ERM. In particular, as more training samples are given, the
439          corresponding loss for V-ERM always decreases correspondingly. Thus, V-ERM is the preferred
440          method in practice due to both its simplicity and smoother performance. As a result, we focus
441          on V-ERM for our empirical evaluations, as shown in the next section.

763   A.1   A DDITIONAL REMARKS
764
765   Remarks on sample complexity analysis. It is expected that the sample complexity for learning
766   under noise would be higher than the noise-free case, since noise introduces higher variances in the
767   data and higher uncertainty in the hypothesis space. Further, noise leads to misleading patterns in
768   the training process. For a simple example, in the training set, we often observe that under the same
769
      input to a vertex v’s (deterministic) interaction function, the outputs are sometimes 0 and sometimes
      1 (due to noise). Such uncertainties require more training samples for an algorithm to unpack useful
770
      information about the true hypothesis.
771
772   Impact of the theoretical findings. Below we address additional impact of the findings from both
773   theoretical and practical perspectives.
774   1. Theory. Existing work on learning threshold discrete dynamical systems focuses on the noise-
775      free setting; the problem of efficient learning under noise has remained open. We fill this gap
776      and establish that one can efficiently learn threshold discrete dynamical systems under high
777      classification noise. Further, our proposed algorithms are strongly noise-tolerant, with only a
778      marginal increase in the number of samples used in comparison with the noise-free case. Overall,
779      we establish a theoretical foundation for learning threshold discrete dynamical systems in the
780      presence of noise.
781   2. Practice. Our results provides practical methodologies on learning real-world large-scale com-
782      plex systems (e.g., social, multi-agent, infrastructure systems).
783      Practical applications. As discussed in the paper, discrete dynamical systems are widely used to
784      model dynamic processes in various fields such as biology, social sciences, and network analysis.
785      Our results provide efficient techniques for robust modeling and inference in these domains,
786      under the realistic setting where training data is noisy (which is often the case in fields like
787      social science). This robustness w.r.t noise is crucial for practical applications when (i) perfect
788
         data are rare, and still, (ii) reliable decisions are needed.
789      Training data. Despite the presence of noise, the increase in the required number of samples
790      for our proposed algorithm is marginal. As a result, even when the noise level rises in real-world
791
         applications, practitioners do not need to excessively increase data collection efforts while main-
         taining model accuracy. This efficiency is particularly beneficial in fields where data collection
792
         is expensive, such as social science which often involves extensive surveying and field research.
793
794
         Model simplicity. The algorithms are in the classic ML domain, and they are inherently sim-
         pler and interpretable compared to deep learning methods. This simplicity translates into more
795
         straightforward implementation and less intricate engineering effort. As a result, practitioners
796
         can quickly deploy these algorithms in real-world applications. Further, because the learning
797      process employs gradient-free optimization, the extensive computation associated with back-
798      propagation is not a bottleneck for the algorithms, making them accessible to a broader range
799      of users with limited computational resources. These are also reflected in our code and experi-
800      mental evaluation which use only standard (single thread) CPUs. Consequently, the transparent
801      training process can easily be interpreted since it involves only a small number of parameters.
802      Scalability. As discussed in the main paper, our proposed algorithms can learn the interaction
803      function for each vertex independently. Therefore, the algorithms can be easily scaled across
804      batches of vertices given the necessary computational resources, making the algorithms suitable
805      for deployment in settings where analysis over large-scale networks is required.

856    - Training set. The training set contains pairs of n-dimensional vectors. For each pair, the in-

858      computed from the input features collectively.
859      Formally, a training set T of size q consists of q pairs of n-dimensional vectors, denoted by

863      (i.e., hypothesis) h∗ ∈ H′ . Here, each Wj′ [i] ∈ {0, ..., k − 1} is the label for the ith entity,
         i = 1, ..., n, where there are k ≥ 2 possible labels. We do not restrict the actual form of h∗ .
