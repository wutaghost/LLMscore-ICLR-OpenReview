011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            In this work, we explore the theoretical properties of conditional deep generative
014            models under the statistical framework of distribution regression where the re-
015            sponse variable lies in a high-dimensional ambient space but concentrates around
016
               a potentially lower-dimensional manifold. More specifically, we study the large-
               sample properties of a likelihood-based approach for estimating these models.
017
               Our results lead to the convergence rate of a sieve maximum likelihood estimator
018
               (MLE) for estimating the conditional distribution (and its devolved counterpart)
019            of the response given predictors in the Hellinger (Wasserstein) metric. Our rates
020            depend solely on the intrinsic dimension and smoothness of the true conditional
021            distribution. These findings provide an explanation of why conditional deep gen-
022            erative models can circumvent the curse of dimensionality from the perspective
023            of statistical foundations and demonstrate that they can learn a broader class of
024            nearly singular conditional distributions. Our analysis also emphasizes the impor-
025            tance of introducing a small noise perturbation to the data when they are supported
026            sufficiently close to a manifold. Finally, in our numerical studies, we demonstrate
027
               the effective implementation of the proposed approach using both synthetic and
               real-world datasets, which also provide complementary validation to our theoreti-
028
               cal findings.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Conditional distribution estimation provides a principled framework for characterizing the depen-
034
      dence relationship between a response variable Y and predictors X, with the primary goal of es-
035
      timating the distribution of Y conditional on X through learning the (conditional) data-generating
036   process. Conditional distribution estimation allows one to regress the entire distribution of Y on
037   X, which provides much richer information than the traditional mean regression and plays a central
038   role in various important areas ranging from causal inference (Pearl, 2009; Spirtes, 2010), graphical
039   models (Jordan, 1999; Koller and Friedman, 2009), representation learning (Bengio et al., 2013),
040   dimension reduction (Carreira-Perpinán, 1997; Van Der Maaten et al., 2009), to model selection
041   (Claeskens and Hjort, 2008; Ando, 2010). Their applications span across diverse domains such as
042   forecasting (Gneiting and Katzfuss, 2014), biology (Krishnaswamy et al., 2014), energy (Jeon and
043   Taylor, 2012), astronomy (Zhao et al., 2021), and industrial engineering (Simar and Wilson, 2015),
044
      among others.
045   There is a rich literature in statistics and machine learning on conditional distribution estimation
046   including both frequentist and Bayesian methods (Hall and Yao, 2005; Norets and Pati, 2017). Tra-
047   ditional methods, however, suffer from the curse of dimensionality and often struggle to adapt to the
048   intricacies of modern data types such as the ones with lower-dimensional manifold structures.
049   Recent methodologies that leverage deep generative models have demonstrated significant advance-
050   ments in complex data generation. Instead of explicitly modeling the data distribution, these ap-
051   proaches implicitly estimate it through learning the corresponding data sampling scheme. Com-
052   monly, these implicit distribution estimation approaches can be broadly categorized into three types.
053   The first one is likelihood-based with notable examples including Kingma and Welling (2013),
      Rezende et al. (2014), Burda et al. (2015), and Song et al. (2021) . The second approach, based


                                                       1
054
      on adversarial learning, matches the empirical distribution of the data with a distribution estimator
055   using an adversarial loss. Representative examples include Goodfellow et al. (2014), Arjovsky et al.
056   (2017), and Mroueh et al. (2017), among others. The third approach, which is more recent, reduces
057   the problem of distribution estimation to score estimation through certain time-discrete or contin-
058   uous dynamical systems. The idea of score matching was first proposed in Hyvärinen and Dayan
059   (2005) and Vincent (2011). More recently, score-based diffusion models have achieved state-of-the-
060   art performance in many applications (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Nichol and Dhariwal, 2021; Song
061   et al., 2020; Lipman et al., 2022).
062   On the theoretical front, recent works such as Liu et al. (2021), Chae et al. (2023), Altekrüger et al.
063   (2023), Stanczuk et al. (2024), Pidstrigach (2022) , and Tang and Yang (2023) demonstrate that dis-
064   tribution estimation based on deep generative models can adapt to the intrinsic geometry of the data,
065   with convergence rates dependent on the intrinsic dimension of the data, thus potentially circum-
066   venting the curse of dimensionality. Such advancement has naturally motivated us to employ and
067   investigate conditional deep generative model for conditional distribution estimation. Specifically,
068   we explore and study the theoretical properties of a new likelihood-based approach to conditional
069
      sampling using deep generative models for data potentially residing on a low-dimensional mani-
      fold corrupted by full-dimensional noise. More concretely, we consider the following conditional
070
      distributional regression problem:
071
                                                Y |X = V |X + ε,                                          (1)
072                                           p
      where X serves as a predictor in R , V |X represents the (uncorrupted) underlying response
073
      supported on a manifold of dimension d ≤ D, Y |X represents the observed response, and
074   ε ∼ N(0, σ∗2 ID ) denotes the noise residing in the ambient space RD . Our deep generative model fo-
075   cuses on the conditional distribution V |X by using a (conditional) generator of the form G∗ (Z, X),
076   where G∗ is a function of a random seed Z and the covariate information X. This approach is
077   termed ‘conditional deep generative’ because the conditional generator is modeled using deep neu-
078   ral networks (DNNs). Observe that, when d < D, the distribution of G∗ (Z, X) is supported on
079   a lower-dimensional manifold, making it singular with respect to the Lebesgue measure in the D-
080   dimensional ambient space. We study the statistical convergence rate of sieve MLEs in the condi-
081   tional deep general model setup and investigate its dependence on the intrinsic dimension, structure
082
      properties of the model as well as the noise level of the data.

084   1.1   L IST OF CONTRIBUTIONS
085
      We briefly summarise the main contributions made in this paper.
086
087   • To the best of our knowledge, our study is the first attempt to explore the likelihood-based ap-
088     proach for distributional regression using a conditional deep generative model, considering full-
089     dimensional noise and the potential presence of singular underlying support. We provide a solid
090     statistical foundation for the approach by proving the near-optimal convergence rates for this pro-
091     posed estimator.
092   • We derive the convergence rates for the conditional density estimator of the corrupted data Y
093     with respect to the Hellinger distance and specialize the obtained rate for two popular deep neural
094
        network classes: the sparse and fully connected network classes. Furthermore, we characterize
        the Wasserstein convergence rates for the induced intrinsic conditional distribution estimator on
095
        the manifold (i.e., a deconvolution problem). Both rates turn out to depend only on the intrinsic
096
        dimension and smoothness of the true conditional distribution.
097
      • Our analysis in Corollary 2 suggests the need to inject a small amount of noise into the data when
098
        they are sufficiently close to the manifold. Intuitively, this observation validates the underlying
099     structural challenges in related manifold estimation problems with noisy data, as outlined by Gen-
100     ovese et al. (2012).
101   • We show that the class of learnable (conditional) distributions of our method is broad. It encom-
102     passes not only the smooth distributions class, but also extends to the general (nearly) singular
103     distributions with manifold structures, with minimal assumptions.

105   1.2   OTHER RELEVANT LITERATURE
106
107   The problem of non-parametric conditional density estimation has been extensively explored in
      statistical literature. Hall and Yao (2005), Bott and Kohler (2017), and Bilodeau et al. (2023) directly


                                                         2
108
      tackle this problem with smoothing and local polynomial-based methods. Fan and Yim (2004)
109   and Efromovich (2007) explore suitably transformed regression problems to address this challenge.
110   Other notable approaches include the nearest neighbor method (Izbicki et al., 2020; Bhattacharya
111   and Gangopadhyay, 1990), basis function expansion (Sugiyama et al., 2010; Izbicki and Lee, 2016),
112   tree-based boosting (Pospisil and Lee, 2018; Gao and Hastie, 2022), and Bayesian optimal transport
113   flow Chemseddine et al. (2024) among others.
114
      In the context of conditional generation, we highlight recent work by Zhou et al. (2022) and Liu
115   et al. (2021). In Zhou et al. (2022), GANs were employed to investigate conditional density estima-
116   tion. While this work offers a consistent estimator, it lacks statistical rates or convergence analysis,
117   and its focus is on a low-dimensional setup. In Liu et al. (2021), conditional density estimation
118   supported on a manifold using Wasserstein-GANs was examined. However, their setup does not ac-
119   count for smoothness across either covariates or responses, nor do they address how deep generative
120   models specifically tackle the challenges of high-dimensionality. Moreover, their assumption that
121   the data lies exactly on the manifold can be restrictive. Our study shares some commonalities with
122   the work of Chae et al. (2023), as both investigate sieve maximum likelihood estimators (MLEs).
123
      However, the fundamental problems addressed and the methodologies employed differ significantly,
      and our work involves technical challenges that span multiple scales. While Chae et al. (2023) con-
124
      centrates exclusively on unconditional distribution estimation, our theoretical analysis necessitates
125
      much more nuanced techniques due to the conditional nature of our setup. This shift is noteworthy
126   because it demands a more refined analysis of entropy bounds, considering two potential sources
127   of smoothness - across the regressor and the response variables. Furthermore, our setting accom-
128   modates the possibility of an infinite number of x values, which gives rise to a dynamic manifold
129   structure, further compounding the intricacy of the problem at hand.

414   2.4.3   C ONDITIONAL DISTRIBUTION ON MANIFOLDS
415
416   In this part, we extend Lemma 2 and provide the existence of the generator when the conditional
417   distribution is supported on a compact manifold with dimension d∗ ≤ D. Due to space constraints,
418   we provide only a sketched proof here; the detailed proof can be found in Appendix D. Specifically,
419   we first present arguments for the existence of the generator when Y is covered by a single chart.
420   We then extend this to the multiple chart case using the technique of partition of unity.
421   In the simpler case when there exists a single (Y, φ) covering Y, where φ : B1 (0d∗ ) → Y is a
422   homeomorphism, we assume φ ∈ Hβmin +1 . In this case, we use the change of variable formula
423   to transfer the measure on B1 (0d∗ ) (unit ball in Rd∗ ) from Y. Following Lemma 2, we can find
424   a transport map g ∈ Hβmin mapping from Z × X to B1 (0d∗ ). The map g ◦ φ then serves as our
425   generator.
426   In the general case where the compact manifold Y needs to be covered by multiple charts, demon-
427   strating the existence of a transport or push-forward map is challenging because Y is not uniformly
428   convex. Suppose that {(Uk , φk )}K k=1 forms a cover of Y. Due to the compactness of Y, the number
429   of charts K is finite. Analogous to the single chart scenario, we first construct gk ◦ φk to transport
430   the measure on each chart. We then patch these local transport maps together to construct a global
431   transport map; see Appendix D for full details. As a result, following Corollary 1, the convergence
      rate for density estimation shall be given by ε∗n ≍ n−(βmin −dα)/(2βmin +d) .

523   4     D ISCUSSION
524
525   We investigated statistical properties of a likelihood-based conditional deep generative model for
526   distribution regression in a scenario where the response variable is situated in a high-dimensional
527   ambient space but is centered around a potentially lower-dimensional intrinsic structure. Our anal-
528   ysis established favorable rates in both the Hellinger and Wasserstein metrics which are dependent
529   on only the intrinsic dimension of the data. Our theoretical findings show that the conditional deep
530
      generative models can circumvent the curse of dimensionality for high-dimensional distribution re-
      gression. To the best of our knowledge, our work is the first of its kind.
531
532   Given the novelty of emerging statistical methodologies with intricate structural considerations in
533   the study of deep generative models, there exist numerous paths for future exploration. Among
534   these potential directions, we are particularly interested in investigating controllable generation via
535   penalized optimization methods, studying statistical properties of deep generative models trained
536
      via matching flows, as well as delving into the hypothesis testing problem within the framework of
      deep generative models, among others. Another interesting direction is to explore residual neural
537
      network structure for modeling time series of distributions with interesting temporal dependence
538
      structures.
