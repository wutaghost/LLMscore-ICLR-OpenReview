009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have shown impressive capabilities
012            in image understanding and generation. However, current benchmarks fail to accu-
013            rately evaluate the chart comprehension of MLLMs due to limited chart types and
014            inappropriate metrics. To address this, we propose ChartBench, a comprehensive
015            benchmark designed to assess chart comprehension and data reliability through
016
               complex visual reasoning. ChartBench includes 42 categories, 66.6k charts, and
               600k question-answer pairs. Notably, we do not provide data point annotations on
017
               charts explicitly, which requires models to derive values by leveraging inherent
018
               chart elements such as color, legends, and coordinate systems. We also design
019            an enhanced evaluation metric named Acc++ to evaluate MLLMs without ex-
020            tensive manual or costly LLM-based evaluations. Furthermore, we propose two
021            baselines based on the chain of thought and supervised fine-tuning to improve
022            model performance on unannotated charts. Extensive experimental evaluations
023            of 18 open-sourced and 3 proprietary MLLMs reveal their limitations in chart
024            comprehension and offer valuable insights for further research.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Given the groundbreaking advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) Radford et al. (2021);
030   Brown et al. (2020); Chowdhery et al. (2023); Touvron et al. (2023a), Multimodal Large Language
031   Models (MLLMs) Li et al. (2023c); Liu et al. (2023e); Zhu et al. (2023) have become the leading
032   approach in multimodal learning, which exhibit excellent visual semantics understanding perfor-
033
      mance OpenAI (2023); Wang et al. (2023b). However, existing MLLMs face challenges in effectively
      reading, comprehending, and summarizing articles that contain embedded charts Masry et al. (2022);
034
      Han et al. (2023); Li & Tajbakhsh (2023). Unlike natural images, which are typically interpreted
035
      based on discernible objects, relative positions, or interactions, charts convey nuanced semantic
036   meanings through visual-grounded logic, such as trend lines or color-coded legends. They present
037   detailed and intricate data narratives in visual formats, making it essential to evaluate MLLMs’ chart
038   comprehension ability and data reliability in understanding these visual representations.
039
      Previous works Masry et al. (2022); Methani et al. (2020); Kantharaj et al. (2022a); Xia et al. (2024);
040
      Chen et al. (2024a) have attempted to address this issue but have encountered some limitations. 1)
041
      They primarily focus on 3 regular chart types (i.e., line, bar, and pie charts), neglecting more intricate
042   formats such as scatter or combination charts, which are equally prevalent in real-world scenarios.
043   Robust MLLMs should be able to adeptly handle a diverse range of chart types. 2) They heavily
044   rely on datapoint annotation on charts or meta table data as textual prompts Masry et al. (2022);
045   Han et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2024a) to generate content, allowing models to easily obtain candidate
046   answers while ignoring the charts’ visual-grounded logic. This will cause MLLMs to struggle with
047   unannotated charts in real-world applications. 3) Current evaluation metrics like judgment or multi-
048   choice question cannot avoid lucky guesses and thus result in overestimated baseline performance,
049   which requires refinement to enhance assessment objectivity and precision.
050   To address these limitations, we introduce ChartBench, which comprehensively evaluates the perfor-
051   mance of MLLMs on a wider variety of chart types, including both annotated and unannotated charts.
052   As summarized in Tab. 1, ChartBench includes over 68k charts and more than 600k high-quality
053   instruction data, covering 9 major categories and 42 subcategories of charts. ChartBench has 5 chart
      question-answering tasks to assess the models’ cognitive and perceptual abilities. Each subclass

084   We further improve the Acc+ metric introduced by MME Fu et al. (2023a), where MLLMs can only
085
      score if they correctly answer a query from both affirmative and negative views. The negative query
      is typically generated by simply negating the affirmative statement, usually by adding not before
086
      the verb. However, the semantic differences between these two forms are substantial and do not
087
      effectively prevent the model from making lucky guesses. To address this, we propose generating
088   the negative query by randomly replacing the ground truth value from the same meta table, named
089   Acc++. This approach generates two views with similar representational and semantic embedding
090   features, thereby reducing instances of lucky guessing. If the model fails to accurately interpret the
091   chart’s visual information, it will provide identical responses and fail to get the Acc++ score.
092
      The evaluation of 18 mainstream open-source and 3 closed-source models shows that current MLLMs
093
      cannot effectively understand complex charts, especially those without data annotations, raising
094   concerns about the reliability of their data interpretation. Detailed examinations on ChartBench reveal
095   the reasons behind the suboptimal performance of MLLMs on charts, highlighting ChartBench’s
096   meticulous curation to explore the nuances of chart reasoning. We introduce two simple yet effective
097   baselines based on the chain of thought (CoT, Fig. 4) and supervised fine-tuning (SFT) to improve
098   MLLMs’ performance on ChartBench, aiming to inspire more innovative proposals in the future.
099
      Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
100
        a) We introduce ChartBench, a large-scale dataset with over 42 types of charts, 66k charts,
101         and 600k instructions. It primarily includes charts without data point annotations, assessing
102         MLLMs’ ability to reason through visual elements instead of OCR.
103     b) We refine the Acc+ metric and value matching criteria to effectively reduce random guesses
104         and provide more robust evaluation results of 18 open-sourced and 3 closed-sourced MLLMs.
105     c) We propose two efficient baselines based on the chain of thought and supervised fine-tuning,
106         inspiring more methods to enhance MLLMs’ understanding of unannotated charts.
107     d) Extensive experiments reveal existing MLLMs’ inadequacies in chart comprehension, high-
            lighting potential directions for future optimization.

      2     R ELATED W ORKS

110   2.1   M ULTIMODAL LLM S
111
112   Current LLMs (Vaswani et al., 2017; Radford et al., 2018; Brown et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2022;
113   Chowdhery et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023a;b; Cai et al., 2024) successfully bridge the multimodal
114   areas via instruction tuning (Ouyang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2022). The connectors
115   are proposed to align visual and text modality to train MLLMs Chen et al. (2022); Alayrac et al.
116
      (2022), e.g., Q-Former (Li et al., 2023c) or MLP Bai et al. (2023b). Mini-GPT4 (Zhu et al., 2023;
      Chen et al., 2023a), mPLUG-Owl (Ye et al., 2023b), and InstructBLIP (Dai et al., 2023) extend
117
      language-only instruction tuning to multimodal tasks using Q-Former. LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023e;d)
118
      maps visual features into the LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023a) embedding space by a linear layer,
119   while concurrently fine-tuning with LLaMA. The closed-source Baidu ERNIE BaiDu and GPT-
120   4 (OpenAI, 2023) further show satisfactory image understanding capabilities. Despite the impressive
121   achievements of existing MLLMs (Ding et al., 2021; Du et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023; Bai et al.,
122   2023b; Chen et al., 2023b; Lin et al., 2023) in common multimodal tasks like VQA (Antol et al., 2015)
123   and image captioning (Vinyals et al., 2015), their focus tends to be on general image understanding,
124   neglecting the specialized task of comprehending chart data in domain-specific contexts (Masry et al.,
125   2022; Li & Tajbakhsh, 2023; Han et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023c; Xia et al., 2023). Existing research
126   can be divided into two categories. 1) two-stage methods mainly transform multimodal queries into
127
      text QAs by extracting table information as prompt Lee et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023b;a); Xia et al.
      (2024). 2) end-to-end approaches adopt chart-question pair data to align and supervised fine-tune
128
      the MLLMs Han et al. (2023); Carbune et al. (2024); Meng et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2023c); Ye et al.
129
      (2023a); Liu et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2023a); Zhuowan et al. (2024); Yan et al. (2024); Chen et al.
130   (2024a); Zhang et al. (2024). Although these efforts have improved the chart understanding ability
131   of MLLMs, there are still limited benchmarks to properly evaluate their performance on the charts,
132   especially unannotated ones.
133
134
      2.2   M ULTIMODAL B ENCHMARKS
135
136
      MLLMs have been fully evaluated on numerous traditional benchmarks (Goyal et al., 2017; Hudson
137   & Manning, 2019; Xu et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023c; Fu et al., 2023a; Yu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b;
138   Liu et al., 2023f), while largely ignoring the requirement for complex visual chart understanding
139   and reasoning. HallusionBench (Guan et al., 2023) exposes the susceptibility of formidable models
140   like GPT-4V (OpenAI, 2023) and LLaVA-1.5 (Liu et al., 2023d) to severe hallucinations when
141   confronted with complex charts. VisText (Tang et al., 2023) introduces a benchmark to incorporate
142   multi-level and fine-grained chart labeling, covering aspects such as chart construction, summary
143   statistics, relations, and complex trends. SciCap (Hsu et al., 2021), Chart2Text (Kantharaj et al.,
144   2022b), AutoChart (Zhu et al., 2021), and ChartSumm (Rahman et al., 2023) address chart-to-text
145
      summarization tasks. ChartQA Masry et al. (2022) and PlotQA Methani et al. (2020) are currently
      mainstream benchmark datasets for evaluating the chart comprehension abilities of MLLMs, which
146
      focus on three commonly encountered chart types. Chartllama Han et al. (2023) and ChartX Xia
147
      et al. (2024) expand the range of available chart types, while ChartY Chen et al. (2024a) significantly
148   expands the number of regular chart types with LLMs. However, these benchmarks have limited
149   chart types, and their charts are always accompanied by detailed datapoint annotations, which allow
150   MLLMs to obtain candidate answers via simple OCR. Comparatively, the advantages of ChartBench
151   stem from its larger scale, more diverse chart types, richer plot styles, and high proportion of
152   unannotated charts.

      3.2                    AUTOMATIC I NSTRUCTIONS G ENERATION
204
      ChartBench consists of 5 tasks, encompassing perception and conception Fu et al. (2023a) tasks.
205   Perception tasks primarily entail perceiving and processing raw data to extract valuable features and
206   information. Conversely, conception tasks involve processing and comprehending abstract concepts
207   and higher-level information.
208
209   Chart Type Recognition (CR, Fig. 2a) task aims to evaluate the MLLMs’ capability to identify
210
      chart types accurately. Determining the chart type is the simplest but most basic step in the chain of
      thought, which determines the steps and logic to analyze the chart elements. The model is required to
211
      choose the correct candidate chart categories from both positive and negative views.
212
213   Value Extraction (VE, Fig. 2b) task aims to assess whether MLLMs can correctly extract the relevant
214   values when confronted with complex visual logic. Without annotated data, MLLMs are required
215   to rely on legends, axes, and corresponding graphical elements to provide answers. If the extracted
      numbers are not accurate, the analysis or summary of the MLLM will be incredible.

310
      ChartBench primarily evaluates MLLMs’ ability to understand unannotated charts. We propose two
311
      simple yet effective baselines that significantly improve MLLMs’ performance.
312
313   ChartCoT. As shown in Fig. 4, we propose effective baselines based on Chain of Thought Wei et al.
314   (2022) to enhance the visual reasoning capability without model tuning. As shown in Fig. 4b, we
315   design a series of questions that decompose user inquiries and employ prompts to mimic human visual
316   reasoning for chart recognition. Additionally, we enable MLLMs to generate their own CoT (Fig. 4c)
317   or seek assistance from stronger LLMs to generate CoTs (Fig. 4d). This approach significantly aids
318
      MLLMs in understanding charts, particularly in cases where visual logic is more complicated.
319   Supervised Fine-tuning. We conduct a two-stage supervised fine-tuning (SFT) based on Qwen-
320   VL-Chat and Internlm-XComposer-v2. In the first stage, we perform alignment training with chart
321   and CSV pairs to update the connector parameters. In the second stage, we utilize instruction and
322   chart pairs to fine-tune the LLM branch with LoRA Hu et al. (2021). Considering that charts are
323   not complex images compared to natural images, we keep the visual encoder frozen during the SFT
      process. Please refer to Appendix D for detailed experimental settings.

499   6    D ISCUSSION
500
501
      Instruction Following. Some models encounter difficulties in following instructions. For instance,
502   mPLUG Ye et al. (2023b) provides overly detailed responses to explain its decision. LLaVA-v1.6 has
503   difficulty accurately understanding the instructions when the dictionaries extracted by OneChart Chen
504   et al. (2024a) are too lengthy. Models like Shikra Chen et al. (2023b) often simply reiterate the
505   original question. Meanwhile, models like CogVLM Wang et al. (2023b) produce hallucinatory
506   responses unrelated to the query. Therefore, instruction design greatly impacts the performance of
507   models because the same model can yield vastly different results with different prompt templates.
508
      MLLM Performance. MLLMs exhibit several common deficiencies in chart comprehension. 1)
509
      Since MLLMs are typically trained on images and descriptive statements, they prioritize giving
510   descriptive responses to charts over numbers. This is the opposite of human graph recognition, where
511   specific elements are identified first, followed by the final answer. 2) Some MLLMs fail to effectively
512   follow complex instructions, which hinders their application of intricate CoT strategies. 3) Data
513   hallucinations that occurred in VE and NQA tasks show that the data extracted by models is not yet
514   entirely reliable, leading to errors when answers involve specific numbers.
515
      CoT v.s. SFT. Both CoT and SFT effectively improve MLLMs’ capabilities, but their impacts vary.
516
      CoT shows greater improvement for weaker MLLMs (e.g., 6.48% for Qwen-VL-Chat v.s. 1.94% for
517
      Internlm-XComposer-v2 in Tab. 7). The main improvement of CoT comes from unannotated charts,
518   and Qwen-VL-Chat benefits more than Internlm-XComposer-v2. As a result, CoT provides limited
519   improvement for MLLMs that already exhibit high performance on annotated charts. Enhancing
520   performance on unannotated charts through CoT remains a challenging task. In contrast, as shown
521   in Tab. 8, SFT provides more significant improvements for the more powerful model Internlm-
522   XComposer-v2 compared to Qwen-VL-Chat (Avg. gain 15.65% v.s. 13.01%, respectively). The
523   improvements are comparable for both annotated and unannotated charts (∆ -0.66% v.s. +0.60%,
524   respectively). This indicates that existing models are required to enhance the fundamental ability to
525   understand unannotated charts, and researchers should prioritize such data during MLLM training.
526   Limitations. 1) ChartBench is required to evaluate more models, and we will continue to follow
527   the rapidly evolving area. 2) Models are highly sensitive to prompt templates, and thus the best
528   prompt template for each model is required to be explored further. 3) The training methods and
529   model architectures for chart perception and reasoning are worth further exploration.
530
531
      7    C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we introduce ChartBench to evaluate the chart comprehension abilities of MLLMs.
534   ChartBench significantly expands chart types and requires MLLMs to infer numbers using visual
535   cues like color or legends. We propose improved Acc+ for accurate, automated assessments, avoiding
536   manual effort or costly LLM evaluations. We further offer two effective baselines to show how
537   the chain of thought and supervised fine-tuning ameliorate MLLMs on charts. Our evaluation of
538   21 mainstream MLLMs reveals their limitations in chart interpretation and provides some insights
539   for further directions. We aim to highlight the MLLM’s ability to understand charts without data
      annotations. ChartBench and its code will be publicly available for research.

852   A.2    D ESIGN P RINCIPLE
853
854   ChartBench has two fundamental design principles. 1) Wider range of chart types. ChartBench expands the
855
      3 common chart types (line, bar, and pie) Masry et al. (2022); Methani et al. (2020); Chen et al. (2024a) to
      representative 9 chart types in the real world (see Tab. 2 and thumbnails in Appendix H). In the train and test
856   sets, conventional charts account for 61.4% and 54.8%, respectively, while the newly added charts account
857   for 38.6% and 45.2%. ChartBench further divides 9 major categories into 42 subcategories, allowing for a
858   more detailed analysis of MLLM performance. 2) More intuitive visual logic. Unlike existing benchmarks,
859   ChartBench primarily focuses on perception and visual logical reasoning. It emphasizes evaluating the ability to
860
      extract value from unlabeled charts rather than simple OCR or localization tasks. We assess MLLMs’ core visual
      reasoning skills directly without converting charts into textual descriptions for further textual reasoning. Previous
861   benchmarks mainly provided annotated charts, which led to some approaches extracting tables first and then
862   transforming the problem into purely text-based logic. In contrast, ChartBench includes a larger proportion of
863   unlabeled charts, accounting for 84.96% and 76.20% in train and test splits, respectively, in Tab. 2. MLLMs must
      accurately extract values based on color or line shape to identify categories and their corresponding coordinate

905
906   ChartBench primarily focuses on the following evaluation aspects: 1) Bar charts are the most common and
907   have been the focus of ChartQA and ChartLLaMA. ChartBench includes basic variations such as horizontal
908   and vertical bar orientations, data complexity (single and multiple groups of data), and different representations
909
      (regular, percentage, stacked, and 3D bar charts). 2) Line charts are commonly used chart types to reflect
      data trends. ChartBench includes error line charts as well as regular single or multiple groups, with or without
910   annotations line charts. 3) Pie charts primarily show the data proportional distribution. ChartBench includes
911   single, nested, doughnut pie charts, and irregular sector charts. 4) Radar charts have a straightforward
912   distribution structure and are used to represent multiple attributes of an entity. ChartBench incorporates diverse
913   data complexities (single or multiple groups) and representations (with or without fillings). 5) Box charts
914
      primarily depict the statistical distribution of a substantial volume of data points. ChartBench collects horizontal
      and vertical box plots, as well as authentic candlestick charts depicting real stock prices. 6) Scatter charts mainly
915   depict the distribution of discrete data. ChartBench includes simple single or multi-group scatter plots, 3D
916   bubble plots, and scatter plots with interpolated smoothing lines. 7) Area charts employ color fillings to visually
917   convey the magnitude and distribution of data. ChartBench encompasses single or multiple groups area plots,

1059   Online website generated charts:
1060
1061       1. Resolution Concerns: The images from the online websites have high resolution, making the text difficult
1062          to read on smaller user mobile screens during the human surveys. This issue doesn’t appear on larger
1063
              monitors.
1064       2. Lack of Data Point Labels: Some comparative questions involving charts without data point labels rely
1065
              solely on the length of bars for comparison. When the values are close, users find it difficult to make

1066          the model to determine the absolute size of the bars.

1069
1070
           1. Percentage Accumulation Charts: Some themes are not intuitive, like a percentage distribution chart for

1071          subsets (50*4). We add percentage information in the titles and along the y-axis to clarify.
1072

1073          in four subsets (50*4). We adjust the padding to ensure all text is positioned away from the chart to avoid
1074          obstruction.
1075       3. Dual Axis Charts: We use color to convey the correspondence between data points and their respective
1076          axes. However, in some cases, the contrast is insufficient, making it hard for users to distinguish between
1077          them. This issue affects two subsets (50*2). We update the color map, removing low-contrast styles such

       Using the ChartBench data, we propose an SFT baseline. Here, we introduce the basic setup of our training
1258
       process. Considering the imbalance between the Acc++ and NQA content in the instruction data, we manually
1259   balance these two types of data to prevent the model from developing a prediction bias.
1260
       Qwen-VL-Chat. We perform SFT for 3 epochs using instructions. We keep the parameters of the vision
1261
       encoder frozen and use LoRA to update only the LLM branch. Training is conducted with DeepSpeed’s Zero2
1262   configuration in half-precision bf16, with a weight decay of 0.05. The optimizer is AdamW with adam_beta2
1263   set to 0.98. The input image resolution is 448 × 448, the batch size is 1, and the learning rate is 2e − 5. The
1264   entire training process consumes 12 A100 GPU days. We do not perform alignment training for the connector
1265   because Qwen-VL’s connector is small and can be updated along with the LLM parameters.
1266   Internlm-XComposer-v2. We use the chart-CSV pair for alignment training over 2 epochs, freezing the
1267   parameters of the ViT Encoder and LLM, and only updating the connector. Then, we perform 1 epoch of
1268   supervised fine-tuning using the chart instruction data, updating both the connector and the LLM branch with
       LoRA. We set a learning rate of 1e − 5 and the AdamW optimizer (adam_beta2=0.95). DeepSpeed’s Zero2
1269
       configuration is employed, with half-precision bf16 for parameter updates. The input image resolution is
1270   490 × 490, and the batch size is set to 1. This experiment approximately requires 15 A100-GPU days.

       Accuracy is the most widely used evaluation criterion for true/false or multiple-choice questions, but it has
1447   inherent limitations. Firstly, for difficult questions, accuracy struggles to distinguish between genuine answers
1448   and random guesses, both of which can yield performance close to the baseline (e.g., 50% for true/false questions,
1449   25% for four-choice questions). Secondly, accuracy places high demands on data scale. In the case of the
1450   accuracy metric, if the test sample approaches infinity, the performance of random guessing would converge to
1451
       the baseline. Conversely, with a small data scale, random guessing might produce results significantly higher than
       the baseline. Although ChartBench provides 16.8K judgment QA pairs (consisting of 8.4K original questions
1452   and their counterparts), this quantity still cannot completely eliminate the occurrence of the situations above
1453   (e.g., the accuracy of MiniGPT-v2 on Node chart in Tab. 18).
1454
       In Tab. 18 and Tab. 19, we present the results using Accuracy (abbreviated as Acc.) as the metric. Overall,
1455   Internlm-Xcomposer-v2 continues to demonstrate the best performance, consistent with the trend shown by
1456   Acc++ in Tab. 3. However, there are differences between accuracy and Acc++ in terms of specific details.
1457   InternLM-Xcomposer achieves 55.70% accuracy in Tab 18, while its Acc++ performance is just 15.49% (Tab. 3),
       indicating that a significant portion of its correct answers are the result of random guessing. This is further


                                                                            27
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1458
       confirmed by the CoR metric in Tab. 5. From Tab. 19, it can be observed that accuracy does not effectively
1459   differentiate between tasks of varying difficulty, as it shows results close to the baseline of 50% across all 5 tasks.
1460   Compared with Tab. 4, it is evident that the VE and GC tasks are notably more challenging, as they require
1461   MLLMs to rely on more visual cues for reasoning. The above analysis demonstrates that the improved Acc++
1462   metric enables more robust evaluations.
1463   Our improved metric, Acc++, effectively addresses the two limitations of accuracy mentioned above. The Acc++
1464   metric requires MLLMs to provide accurate judgments for both positive and negative perspectives regarding the
1465   base assertions. This innovative metric offers two distinct advantages. Firstly, it ensures consistency between
       positive and negative queries, with the only difference being the Ground Truth value. This precautionary
1466
       approach reduces the chance of lucky guesses resulting from random choices, as MLLMs may produce identical
1467   responses for both query types if they fail to comprehend the chart. Secondly, the GT values for negative queries
1468   are derived from other data within the same chart, eliminating unrealistic scenarios and enhancing the validity
1469   of the evaluation process. Generally, the expected probability of random guessing is 25% for vanilla Acc++.
1470   However, for the MLLM that has insufficient chart recognition capabilities, the CoR tends to be 100%, and thus
       the Acc++ tends to be 0% instead of 25% baseline. This characteristic enables Acc++ to accurately reflect the
1471
       model’s chart comprehension ability even when the dataset is small in size.

1509   encompasses solely three chart types, viz., line, bar, and pie. To ascertain the indispensability of ChartBench and
1510   the rationality of our benchmark design and evaluation, we initially scrutinize the vanilla accuracy (Acc.) on
1511   ChartQA. We employ the test-split in ChartQA for evaluation, circumventing the prompt engineering process,
       and directly utilizing the original query without any modification as the prompt input to MLLMs. Thereafter,

       we evaluate the correctness of the results utilizing rule-based and regular expression matching. For numerical
1513   questions, we employ the relax accuracy metric akin to ChartQA, signifying that the difference between the

1515   the zero-shot Acc regarding chart types and dataset split. Conspicuously, for bar charts, we report the average

       The motivation behind ChartBench is to evaluate the understanding capability of MLLMs regarding charts. While
1545
       MLLMs have exhibited high performance on previous benchmarks, they still encounter significant hallucination
1546   issues in practical applications due to the unreliable nature of the data they extract from charts. ChartBench aims
1547   to truly reflect MLLM’s ability to interpret visual data and approach or even surpass human-level performance.
1548   Therefore, we have provided evaluation results of human performance on ChartBench.
1549   To ensure a fair and objective evaluation, we conduct an online survey, which consists of 10 randomly selected
1550   subcategories from ChartBench for each questionnaire. 1 chart and 4 assertions are selected from each sub-
1551   category for respondents to assess their accuracy. To obtain reliable evaluation results, the survey participants
1552   mainly consist of undergraduate and graduate students with chart reading ability, as well as other researchers
       in the campus and company. We encourage participants to use large-screen devices for better chart display
1553
       and kindly request their patient and diligent responses. On average, it takes approximately 15 minutes and 17
1554   seconds to complete each survey. To avoid cases of random guessing, we still employ the Acc++ evaluation
1555   metric. Incomplete responses are discarded, and we ensure that each subcategory has valid answers. In total, we
1556   have collected 68 valid surveys.
1557   Tab. 22 presents the results of human evaluations, revealing some insightful observations. Firstly, the VE task
1558   appears to be more challenging compared to other tasks. The human eye faces challenges in determining the
1559   values of unmarked data points. While the coordinate system offers potential inference, excessively fine granu-
1560   larity can diminish respondents’ confidence. Secondly, there is not a significant variation in human performance
       across different chart types. Once individuals grasp the correct interpretation methods for charts, they can
1561
       demonstrate similar proficiency across each chart category. Thirdly, even in some relatively straightforward

1676   In this section, we devise several leaderboards to evaluate the performance of diverse MLLMs across multiple

1678   categories.
1679   In Tab. 23 & 24 & 25 & 26, we present the leaderboards of MLLMs on ChartBench, which includes 3 regular
1680   types of charts and 6 extra types of charts, utilizing the Acc++ metric. Additionally, we showcase the Acc++
1681   and CoR leaderboards of MLLMs for 4 chart comprehension tasks while also displaying their rankings on w/i
1682   and w/o annotation data.

1684   G.1    L EADERBOARDS ON C HART T YPE
1685
       Tab. 23 presents an overview of MLLMs’ performance across various chart types, along with the overall Acc++
1686   metric. Generally, the current MLLMs exhibit a constrained ability in chart recognition, encountering notable
1687   challenges. For specific chart types, such as radar or combination charts, certain MLLMs achieve close to
1688   0% in Acc++, signaling their difficulty in extracting crucial information from charts and their insensitivity
1689   to both positive and negative queries. It’s essential to highlight that the Acc++ metric tends toward 0% in
       situations of random guessing, as elaborated in Sec. 3.4. Particularly, Qwen-VL-Chat and mPLUG-Owl-bloomz
1690
       showcase commendable proficiency in recognizing charts, a capability likely attributed to their precise tuning
1691   with chart data. However, their performance in this aspect falls below what has been reported in ChartQA. This
1692   discrepancy can be traced back to their reliance on OCR skills rather than robust visual logical reasoning. In the
1693   context of ChartBench, where the proportion of annotated charts is notably low, these models face a significant
1694   challenge. The majority of queries in ChartBench necessitate MLLMs to employ visual logical reasoning, a
       task that proves quite demanding for models like Qwen-VL-Chat and mPLUG-Owl-bloomz. On the other hand,
1695
       VisualGLM and Shikra exhibit subpar performance, potentially due to their smaller LLM size and less robust
1696   visual encoding branch. While MLLMs generally demonstrate satisfactory performance on regular charts, there
1697   remains considerable room for improvement, particularly in handling more intricate graphics.

1699   G.2    L EADERBOARDS ON TASK T YPE
1700
1701   Tab. 24 outlines the performance of MLLMs on perception and conception tasks introduced in Sec. 3.2. Most
       MLLMs exhibit notable success in the CR task, showcasing their proficiency in recognizing fundamental chart
1702
       types. Notably, LLaVA-v1.5, mPLUG-Owl-bloomz, and Qwen-VL-Chat demonstrate substantial advantages
1703   in the VC and GC conception tasks, leveraging their chart-tuned data. The most challenging task, VE, serves
1704   as a key distinction between ChartBench and ChartQA. Unlike basic OCR, the VE task requires a series of
1705   visual and textual logical reasoning steps to arrive at the correct answer. Despite strong overall performance,
1706   models such as BLIP2 and ChartLlama face difficulties in the VE task. This underscores the importance
       of prioritizing and enhancing the visual logical reasoning capabilities of these MLLMs. In terms of model
1707
       comparison, closed-source models outperform their open-source counterparts, partly attributed to their larger
1708   model size and broader data coverage.

1710   G.3    L EADERBOARDS ON CoR M ETRIC
1711
1712   Tab. 25 showcases the CoR metric, which signifies the portion of the chart that the MLLM fails to comprehend
1713
       entirely. Qwen-VL-Chat exhibits the highest Acc++, albeit with a lower CoR compared to models like MiniGPT-
       v2. The top-performing MiniGPT-v2 demonstrates a CoR of 55.06%, underscoring the prevalence of random
1714   guessing cases for open-source models due to their challenges in accurately understanding charts. In the case of
1715   closed-source MLLMs, although GPT-4V outperforms ERNIE in terms of Acc++, their CoR values are similar.
1716   A more detailed examination reveals that ERNIE excels in challenging VE tasks, which happen to be the weaker
1717   area for GPT-4V.
