011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            We study reinforcement learning for global decision-making in the presence of
014            local agents, where the global decision-maker makes decisions affecting all local
015            agents, and the objective is to learn a policy that maximizes the joint rewards
016
               of all the agents. Such problems find many applications, e.g. demand response,
               EV charging, and queueing. In this setting, scalability has been a long-standing
017
               challenge due to the size of the joint state space which can be exponential in the
018
               number of agents. This work proposes the SUBSAMPLE-Q algorithm, where the
019            global agent subsamples k ≤ n local agents to compute a policy in time that
020            is polynomial in k. We show  √ that this learned policy converges to the optimal
021            policy on the order of Õ(1/ k + ϵk,m ) as the number of subsampled agents k
022            increases, where ϵk,m is the Bellman noise. Finally, we validate our theoretical
023            results through numerical simulations in demand-response and queueing settings.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Global decision-making, where a global agent makes decisions that affect a large number of local
029   agents, is a classical problem that has been widely studied in many forms (Foster et al., 2022; Qin
030   et al., 2023; Foster et al., 2023) and can be found in many applications, e.g. network optimization,
031   power management, and electric vehicle (EV) charging (Kim & Giannakis, 2017; Zhang & Pavone,
032   2016; Molzahn et al., 2017). A critical challenge is the uncertain nature of the underlying system,
033
      which is often difficult to model precisely. Reinforcement Learning (RL) has demonstrated an im-
      pressive performance in a wide array of applications, such as the game of Go (Silver et al., 2016),
034
      autonomous driving (Kiran et al., 2022), and robotics (Kober et al., 2013). More recently, RL has
035
      emerged as a powerful tool for learning to control unknown systems (Ghai et al., 2023; Lin et al.,
036   2023; 2024a;b), and thus holds significant potential for decision-making in multi-agent systems,
037   including global decision making for local agents.
038
039
      However, RL for multi-agent systems becomes intractable as the number of agents increases, due to
      the curse of dimensionality. For instance, classical RL algorithms, such as tabular Q-learning and
040
      temporal difference learning, require storing a Q-function (Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis, 1996; Powell,
041
      2007) that scales with the size of the state-action space. Even if the individual agents’ state space
042   is small, the global state space can take values from a set of size exponentially large in the number
043   of agents. When the system’s rewards are not discounted, reinforcement learning for multi-agent
044   systems is provably NP-hard (Qu et al., 2020a; Blondel & Tsitsiklis, 2000), and this scalability issue
045   has been observed in a variety of settings Guestrin et al. (2003); Papadimitriou & Tsitsiklis (1999). A
046   promising line of research over recent years focuses on networked instances, where interactions are
047   restricted to local neighborhoods of agents (Lin et al., 2020; 2021; Qu et al., 2020b; Jing et al., 2022;
048   Chu et al., 2020). This approach has led to scalable algorithms where each agent only considers the
049   agents in its neighborhood to derive approximately optimal solutions. However, these results do not
050
      apply to our setting, where one global agent interacts with many local agents. This can be viewed
      as a star graph, where the neighborhood of the central decision-making agent is large.
051
052   Beyond the networked formulation, another exciting line of work addressing this intractability is
053   mean-field RL (Yang et al., 2018). Mean-field RL assumes that all agents are homogeneous in
      their state and action spaces, enabling interactions to be approximated by a representative “mean”

055   number of agents, and learns an approximately optimal policy where the approximation error decays
056   with the number of agents (Gu et al., 2021; 2022a). However, mean-field RL does not directly
057   transfer to our setting since the global decision-making agent violates the homogeneity assumption.
058   Moreover, when the number of local agents is large, storing a polynomially-large Q-table (where the
059   polynomial’s degree depends on size of the state space for a single agent) can still be infeasible. This
060   motivates the following fundamental question: can we design a fast and competitive policy-learning
061   algorithm for a global decision-making agent in a system with many local agents?
062   Contributions. We answer this question affirmatively. Our key contributions are outlined below.
063

      Related Work. This paper relates to two major lines of work which we describe below.
143
144   Multi-agent RL (MARL). MARL has a rich history, starting with early works on Markov games
145   used to characterize the decision-making process (Shapley, 1953; Littman, 1994), which can be
146   regarded as a multi-agent extension of the Markov Decision Process (MDP). MARL has since been
147
      actively studied (Zhang et al., 2021) in a broad range of settings, such as cooperative and competitive
      agents. MARL is most similar to the category of “succinctly described” MDPs (Blondel & Tsitsiklis,
148
      2000), where the state/action space is a product space formed by the individual state/action spaces of
149
      multiple agents, and where the agents interact to maximize an objective function. Our work, which
150   can be viewed as an essential stepping stone to MARL, also shares the curse of dimensionality.
151
152   A line of celebrated works (Qu et al., 2020b; Chu et al., 2020; Lin et al., 2020; 2021; Jing et al., 2022)
153
      constrain the problem to networked instances to enforce local agent interactions and find policies that
      maximize the objective function, which is the expected cumulative discounted reward. By exploiting
154
      Gamarnik’s spatial exponential decay property from combinatorial optimization (Gamarnik et al.,
155
      2009), they overcome the curse of dimensionality by truncating the problem to only search over the
156   policy space derived from the local neighborhood of agents that are at most κ away from each other
157   to find an O(ρk+1 )-approximation of the maximized objective function for ρ ∈ (0, 1). However,
158   since their algorithms have a complexity that is exponential in the size of the neighborhood, they
159   are only tractable for sparse graphs. Therefore, these algorithms do not apply to our decision-
160   making problem, which can be viewed as a dense star graph (see Appendix A). The recently popular
161   work on V-learning (Jin et al., 2021) reduces the dependence of the product action space to an
      additive dependence. However, since our work focuses on the action of the global decision-maker,


                                                              3

162
      the complexity in the action space is already minimal. Instead, our work focuses on reducing the
163   complexity of the joint state space which has not been previously accomplished for dense networks.
164
165   Mean-Field RL. Under assumptions of homogeneity in the state/action spaces of the agents, the
166
      problem of densely networked multi-agent RL was partially resolved in Yang et al. (2018); Gu
      et al. (2021; 2022a;b); Subramanian et al. (2022) which approximates √ the learning problem through
167
      mean-field control, where the approximation error scales as O(1/ n). To overcome the problem
168
      of designing algorithms on probability measure spaces, they study MARL under Pareto optimality
169   and use the (functional) strong law of large numbers to consider a lifted state/action space with a
170   representative agent, where the rewards and dynamics of the system are aggregated. Cui & Koeppl
171   (2022); Hu et al. (2023); Carmona et al. (2023) introduce heterogeneity to the mean-field approach
172   using graphon mean-field games; however, there is a loss of topological information when using
173   graphons to approximate finite graphs, as graphons correspond to infinitely large adjacency matrices.
174   Additionally, graphon mean-field RL imposes a critical assumption of the existence of graphon
175   sequences that converge in cut-norm to the problem instance. Another mean-field RL approach that
176   partially introduces heterogeneity is in a line of work considering major and minor agents. This has
177
      been well studied in the competitive setting (Carmona & Zhu, 2016; Carmona & Wang, 2016). In
      the cooperative setting, Mondal et al. (2022); Cui et al. (2023) are most related to our work, as they
178
      collectively consider a setting with k classes of homogeneous agents, but their mean-field analytic
179
      approaches do not converge to the optimal policy upon introducing a global decision-making agent.
180   Furthermore, these works require Lipschitz continuity assumptions on the reward functions which
181   we relax in our work. Finally, the algorithms underlying mean-field RL have a runtime that is
182   polynomial in n, whereas our SUBSAMPLE-Q algorithm has a runtime that is polylogarithmic in n.
183
      Other Related Works. A line of works has similarly exploited the star-shaped network in cooper-
184
      ative multi-agent systems. Min et al. (2023); Chaudhari et al. (2024) studied the communication
185
      complexity and mixing times of various learning settings with purely homogeneous agents, and
186   Do et al. (2023) studied the setting of heterogeneous linear contextual bandits to yield a no-regret
187   guarantee. We extend this work to the more challenging setting of reinforcement learning.

      6    C ONCLUSION , L IMITATIONS , AND F UTURE W ORK
514
515
      Conclusion. This work considers a global decision-making agent in the presence of n local homo-
516                                                                            est
      geneous agents. We propose SUBSAMPLE-Q which derives a policy πk,m           where k ≤ n and m ∈ N
517
      are tunable parameters, and show that πk,m converges to the optimal policy π ∗ with a decay rate of
                                               est
518         √
519
      O(1/ k + ϵk,m ), where ϵk,m is the Bellman noise. To establish the result, we develop an analytic
520   framework which constructs an adapted Bellman operator T̂k , shows a Lipschitz-continuity result
521   for Q̂∗k , generalizes the DKW inequality, and proves a probabilistic bound on Q-functions with dif-
522   ferent actions. Further, we extend this result to the non-tabular setting with infinite state and action
523
      spaces. Finally, we validate our theoretical result through numerical experiments.
524   Limitations and Future Work. We recognize several future directions. Firstly, this model studies
525   a ‘star-network’ setting to model a single source of density. It would be fascinating to extend this
526   subsampling framework to general networks. We believe expander-graph decompositions (Anand &
527   Umans, 2023; Reingold, 2008) are amenable for this. A second direction would be to find connec-
528   tions between our sub-sampling method to algorithms in federated learning, where the rewards can
529
      be stochastic, and to incorporate learning rates (Lin et al., 2021) to attain numerical stability. A third
      limitation of this work is that we have only partially resolved the problem for truly heterogeneous
530
      local agents by adding a ‘type’ property to each local agent to model some pseudoheterogeneity in
531
      the state space of each agent. Finally, it would be exciting to generalize this work to the online set-
532   ting without a generative oracle. For this, we conjecture that tools from recent works on stochastic
533   approximation (Chen & Theja Maguluri, 2022) and no-regret RL (Jin et al., 2021) might be valuable.

979    specific type of dense graph: the star graph (Figure 4). We briefly elaborate more on this connection
980
       below.
981    Definition A.21 (Star Graph Sn ). For n ∈ N, the star graph Sn is the complete bipartite graph K1,n .
982
983    Sn captures the graph density notion by saturating the set of neighbors for the central node. Fur-
984    thermore, it models interactions between agents identically to our setting, where the central node is
985    a global agent and the peripheral nodes are local agents. The cardinality of the search space simplex
986    for the optimal policy is |Sg ||Sl |n |Ag |, which is exponential in n. Hence, this problem cannot be
987    naively modeled by an MDP: we need to exploit the symmetry of the local agents. This intuition
988
       allows our subsampling algorithm to run in polylogarithmic time (in n). Further, works that lever-
       age the exponential decaying property that truncates the search space for policies over immediate
989
       neighborhoods of agents still rely on the assumption that the graph neighborhood for the agent is
990
       sparse Lin et al. (2021); Qu et al. (2020a;b); Lin et al. (2020); however, the graph Sn violates this
991    local sparsity condition; hence, previous methods do not apply to this problem instance.

1818   This section extends our result to non-tabular settings where the global agent’s state space Sg can
1819   be a compact infinite set, and the global agent’s action space Ag and each local agent’s state space
1820
       Sl is a finite set. In order to solve this problem, we make assumptions on the underlying MDP. A
       common assumption made is the linearity of value functions with respect to some known features
1821
       (Sutton et al., 1999b; Chen & Theja Maguluri, 2022; Min et al., 2023).
1822
1823   At a high-level, this section learns the non-tabular function Q̂est
                                                                       k,m using function approximation
1824   methods from Golowich & Moitra (2024) under assumptions of Linear Bellman completeness, and
1825   using the triangle inequality to bound the performance between the optimal policy and the subsam-
1826   pled policy learned via sampling and linear function approximation.
1827   Typically, existing works in the literature assume the existence of a map ϕ such that ϕ : S ×
1828   A → Rd , where d is the dimension of the embedding ϕ. The weakest assumption made on the
1829   value function is that Q is linear: for some w ∈ Rd , Q(s, a) = ⟨w, ϕ(s, a)⟩ for all (s, a) ∈ S ×
1830   A. However, it is conjectured that it is impossible to computationally learn a near-optimal policy
1831   under this assumption. Therefore, in accordance with Golowich & Moitra (2024), we make the
1832   stronger assumption that the underlying Markov decision process on the subsampled Q-function,
1833   Q̂est
         k,m , satisfies Linear Bellman completeness. This class of Linear Bellman completeness captures
1834   a variety of function classes: for instance, it subsumes the set of linear MDPs and MDPs with low
1835   Bellman-Eluder (BE) dimension, which in turn contains rich subclasses such as functions with low
       Eluder dimension or low Bellman rank.

1880   Discussion F.1 (Tighter Endpoint
                                      √    Analysis). Our theoretical result shows that V π (s) − V πk,m
1881   decays on the order of O(1/ k + ϵk,m ). For k = n, this bound is actually suboptimal since Q̂∗k
1882   becomes Q∗ . However, placing |∆| = n in our weaker TV bound in Lemma C.7, we recovers a
1883   total variation distance of 0 when k = n, recovering the optimal endpoint bound.
1884   Discussion F.2 (Choice of k). Discussion 3.6 previously discussed the tradeoff in k between the
1885   polynomial
             √       in k complexity of learning the Q̂k function and the decay in the optimality gap of
1886   O(1/ k). This discussion promoted k = O(log n) as a means to balance the tradeoff. However,
1887   the “correct” choice of k truly depends on the amount of compute available, as well as the accuracy
1888   desired from the method. If the former is available, we recommend setting k = Ω(n) as it will yield
1889
       a more optimal policy. Conversely, setting k = O(log n), when n is large, would be the minimum k
       recommended to realize any asymptotic decay of the optimality gap.
