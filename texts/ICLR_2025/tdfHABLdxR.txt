011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Representation learning and unsupervised skill discovery can allow robots to ac-
014            quire diverse and reusable behaviors without the need for task-specific rewards. In
015            this work, we use unsupervised reinforcement learning to learn a latent represen-
016
               tation by maximizing the mutual information between skills and states subject to a
               distance constraint. Our method improves upon prior constrained skill discovery
017
               methods by replacing the latent transition maximization with a norm-matching
018
               objective. This not only results in a much a richer state space coverage compared
019            to baseline methods, but allows the robot to learn more stable and easily control-
020            lable locomotive behaviors. We successfully deploy the learned policy on a real
021            ANYmal quadruped robot and demonstrate that the robot can accurately reach ar-
022            bitrary points of the Cartesian state space in a zero-shot manner, using only an
023            intrinsic skill discovery and standard regularization rewards.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Learning dynamic motions for legged robots typically requires a combination of complex reward
029   engineering, imitation learning and curriculum learning. This can be limiting in two ways: firstly,
030   reward engineering can be a tedious and time-consuming process, which does not scale well to
031   learning a large range of behaviors; secondly, it constrains the problem through the injection of
032   strong inductive bias. For example, in locomotion it is common to have a reward for moving directly
033
      towards the goal — behavior, which can be very sub-optimal if there are any obstructions between
      the robot and the target. In contrast, skill discovery, or options learning, is a subset of representation
034
      learning that presents a promising solution to this problem. It encourages the agent to autonomously
035
      explore its environment and learn diverse behaviors with an intrinsic motivation reward (Salge et al.,
036   2013; Choi et al., 2021). The main advantage of this approach is that the agent can learn general
037   behaviors that not only do not require as much handcrafting and reward engineering but can also be
038   easily reused for downstream tasks.
039
      Skills in a latent representation are typically learned by maximizing an information theoretic ob-
040
      jective, such as the mutual information between skills and state trajectories (usually with a dis-
041
      criminator) (Eysenbach et al., 2018; Choi et al., 2021), or diversity in terms of expected features
042   and state distributions under different skills (Zahavy et al., 2022; Laskin et al., 2022; Cheng et al.,
043   2024a). However, one of the main limitations of prior methods is that they define mutual informa-
044   tion through the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence, which does not consider the degree to which
045   different behaviors are distinguishable (Park et al., 2023b). To learn “useful” behaviors —for exam-
046   ple state-traversing locomotive behaviors— it is common to constrain the encoder to focus only on
047   certain parts of the observation space, which also injects a strong inductive bias into the problem.
048   Prior work (Park et al., 2021; 2023b) has proposed maximizing the latent transitions in the skill
049   space under a Lipschitz constraint. While such methods can learn skills spanning a great part of
050
      the state-space without additional inductive bias, the maximization objective leads to only acquiring
      overly aggressive high-velocity motions, which cannot be deployed on most robotics hardware.
051
052   In this work, we explore the application of skill discovery via representation learning to the domain
053   of quadrupedal locomotion. We introduce an enhanced constrained skill-learning objective that fa-
      cilitates the robot’s acquisition of an extensive repertoire of locomotion skills. Our work builds upon

      2.2   R ELATED WORK
112
113   Legged Locomotion: Reinforcement learning has shown great results in recent years when applied
114   to legged locomotion, both for blind (Hwangbo et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2020; Kumar et al., 2021;
115   Margolis et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2023; Gangapurwala et al., 2023) and for perceptive locomotion
116   (Miki et al., 2022; Loquercio et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023; Gangapurwala et al., 2022). The standard
117   approach is to frame the learning problem as a velocity-following task, where the robot learns to
118   track user-specified velocity commands under noise and various disturbances. One disadvantage of
119
      this method is that the policy often converges to a single gait behavior, unless additional rewards (Fu
      et al., 2022) or neural network structures (Yang et al., 2020) are included. Recently, more works have
120
      explored learning navigation and locomotion, resulting in a goal-conditioned policy that can reach
121
      various points in the Cartesian state space while allowing the robot to adapt its velocity depending
122   on the environment. Due to the sparsity of the task rewards, many works implement additional dense
123   exploratory rewards (Rudin et al., 2022a; Zhang et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 2024a), or task-specific
124   curricula (Li et al., 2024; Atanassov et al., 2024). Cheng et al. (2024b), for example, use a simpler
125   velocity-based reward but requires pre-specified oracle waypoints for the robot to follow as it learns
126   to navigate more challenging environments. Other parkour works (Zhuang et al., 2023; Hoeller et al.,
127   2023) require learning several manually defined skills, each with its own set of task-based rewards
128   that require separate design and tuning. To summarize, legged locomotion by reinforcement learning
129   needs heavy tuning of task-specific rewards, which requires domain knowledge and human efforts.
130   Unsupervised Skill Discovery: To address the challenges of sparse reward problems that require
131   strong and structured exploration, many works have proposed encouraging curious and exploratory
132   behavior through intrinsic motivation. There are many methods for representing intrinsic motivation
133   in reinforcement learning agents, which can be broadly grouped into three information-theoretic
134   objectives - maximizing expected information gain, novelty, and empowerment (Salge et al., 2013;
135   Jung et al., 2012) (also referred to as options learning (Sutton et al., 1999; Bacon et al., 2016; Jong
136
      et al., 2008) and skill discovery) (Aubret et al., 2023). Of these three methods, skill discovery is
      particularly attractive as it promises to encourage exploration throughout training and to learn a
137
      reusable representation or set of behaviors for downstream tasks. Learning state-traversing behav-
138
      iors has also been done through goal-conditioned RL (GCRL) (Kaelbling, 1993; Pong et al., 2020;
139   Durugkar et al., 2021; Cho et al., 2023), and in fact Choi et al. (2021) have linked these two ideas
140   together under the name of variational GCRL. Some approaches (Bacon et al., 2016; Harb et al.,
141   2017) learn both the skills and a high-level skill selector jointly. However, as discussed in (Eysen-
142   bach et al., 2018), this can lead to a modal collapse where suboptimal options are picked less often,
143   and as such, not improved further. Many skill discovery methods then approach this separately - by
144   first learning skills, and then training a high-level meta controller which executed skills for a certain
145   number of time steps. Some options frameworks (Bacon et al., 2016; Harb et al., 2017; Li et al.,
146   2022) learn termination functions for each skill instead.
147   To learn diverse behaviors, prior works commonly use the mutual information (MI) between skills
148   and states, I(z; s) as the optimization objective (Choi et al., 2021). Intuitively, this encourages the
149   agent to learn to associate different states (or trajectories of states, i.e. behaviors) with different
150   skills. The MI can be expressed in two ways - the first being in terms of the skill-conditioned state
151   entropy and the entropy of the states, i.e. I(z; s) = H(s) − H(s|z) (Liu & Abbeel, 2021; Sharma
152   et al., 2020a;b; Laskin et al., 2022); or alternatively as I(z; s) = H(z) − H(z|s), where the skill
153
      entropy H(z) depends on the choice of distribution z ∼ p(z), typically chosen as uniform and is
      therefore constant. As maximizing H(z|s) directly is intractable, a variational approximation is
154
      used with the following lower bound:

185   (Peng et al., 2021) to learn discrete diverse skills from a motion dataset. Similarly, ASE (Peng et al.,
186   2022) uses an encoder-based representation to learn continuous high-dimensional skills given a mo-
187   tion dataset, but constraints the latent space to lie on the surface of an N-dimensional hypersphere.
188   One major disadvantage of the common formulations is the lack of exploration incentive (Laskin
189   et al., 2022). In fact, as long as the discriminator can distinguish between skills there is no benefit
190   to exploring more diverse states (Park et al., 2021). With a large enough discriminator, even a small
191   difference in the states could lead to accurate skill predictions. This causes many unsupervised skill
192
      discovery methods to produce distinguishable skill-conditioned trajectories that cover a reasonably
      small part of the state-space. To tackle this, for locomotion it is common to add a high level of task
193
      inductive bias into the problem, for example by limiting the discriminator input to the XY position of
194
      the body (Eysenbach et al., 2018; Sharma et al., 2020b). Alternatively, giving structure the problem
195   by imitating a reference motion dataset (Peng et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023) or an optimal pretrained
196   policy (Zahavy et al., 2022; Cheng et al., 2024a) can also significantly help with converging to more
197   diverse behaviors. Recently, Park et al. (2021) proposed an alternative formulation to align the tran-
198   sitions in a learned latent space with the corresponding skills and maximize their norm. Crucially,
199   they then add a constraint on the norm of these latent transitions being a lower bound on a chosen
200   distance metric, such as the Euclidean (LSD) (Park et al., 2021) or the temporal distance (METRA)
201   (Park et al., 2023b) between a transition pair in the original state space. This constraint ensures that
202   skills that cover large latent distances also cover large state space distances under the chosen metric,
203
      leading to much larger coverage and more useful skills.
204   Unsupervised skill discovery has mainly been studied in relation to standard problems (such as the
205   MuJoCo robotic environments) in the domain of reinforcement learning. Few skill discovery meth-
206   ods have been applied to robotics and tested in the real world. Sharma et al. (2020a) applied an off-
207   policy version of DADS (Sharma et al., 2020b) for real-world reinforcement learning on a quadruped
208   robot. Recently, Cheng et al. (2024a) applied DOMiNO (Zahavy et al., 2022) to goal-conditioned
209
      quadrupedal locomotion, where the discrete skills are used to produce diverse trajectories that reach
      the same goal point. However, their approach requires an optimal pretrained policy, that is already
210
      capable of reaching the goal.

292   3.2   R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING S ETUP
293
294   Observations, actions and control framework. For the policy observations, we use a concatenated
295
      array of current joint positions and velocities, base linear and angular velocities, base quaternion and
      the previous actions. While some previous skill discovery works (Eysenbach et al., 2018; Sharma
296
      et al., 2020b) require constraining the discriminator input based on the task (e.g. to only the XY
297
      base position for locomotion tasks), this is not the case for our method, similarly to (Park et al.,
298   2021). For this reason, we use the full observations, i.e. the base linear vb and angular ωb velocities,
299   base quaternion q̄b , joint positions qj and velocities q̇j , and the base position pb . The action space
300   consists of desired joint positions for the 12 joints on the ANYmal robot relative to a nominal
301   standing configuration. These are updated at 50 Hz and summed up with the nominal joint positions
302   and passed at 400 Hz to low-level higher frequency PD controllers.
303
      Reward design. To slightly bias the skill discovery towards feasible motions that can be applied
304   to the real robot without damaging the hardware, we provide several extrinsic rewards commonly
305   used in legged locomotion. These are energy conservation and smoothness terms, together with
306   feet air time (to prevent high-frequency stepping), flat orientation and nominal base height rewards,
307   and unwanted contact penalties. We note that these extrinsic rewards are only necessary to make
308   the motions smoother and more aesthetically pleasant. In the supplementary material we conduct
309   an ablation with and without those extrinsic regularization rewards, and show that they are not
310   crucial for learning the diverse behaviors. In comparison to quadruped locomotion acquired by pure
311   reinforcement learning, we use far fewer reward terms since we don’t need any task-specific rewards.

315
      We demonstrate the ability of our approach to discover a broad range of skills which are used to lo-
316
      comote to desired goal poses. These learned skills are used to achieve zero-shot goal-tracking in the
317
      real world. We compare our method with two state-of-the-art baselines: (i) Lipschitz-constrained
318   skill discovery (LSD) (Park et al., 2021) with the objective shown in (8) and a Euclidean distance
319   constraint; (ii) Metric-aware abstraction (METRA) (Park et al., 2023b) with the same objective
320   as LSD and a temporal distance constraint. As in the baselines, we choose a 2D skill space, and
321   therefore a 2D latent space for the encoder ϕ(·), and sample the skills uniformly from within a circle
322   of radius ||zmax || = 50. We choose this value because for a 300 step episode it results in a per-step
323   latent transition of ∼ 0.17, which is the maximum magnitude achieved by LSD on the same task.

      The encoder learns to compress its input into the latent space in a way that preserves both the
      magnitude and the direction of the corresponding skills. In this case, this results in mapping the
478
      base linear velocities and the base position and ignoring the remaining dimensions of the input states.
479
      The reason for this is that the encoder has a limited latent space dimensionality and those are the
480   dimensions that can achieve the largest transition magnitudes. An interesting hypothesis is that by
481   increasing the latent dimensionality, the encoder should use a larger part of its input state space, and
482   subsequently be able to perform zero-shot goal tracking on the rest of its state space. To investigate
483   this, we trained our approach with a 3-D latent (and skill) space. In Fig. 8 we condition the policy
484   on tracking different base positions with an arbitrary desired yaw. As can be seen in the figure, the
485   agent can successfully move to the desired point with the required heading simultaneously, which

      5   C ONCLUSION
496
497
      In this work, we presented a novel approach that uses unsupervised skill discovery to learn robust
498   locomotion skills. We showed that these cover a much larger part of the state space when contrasted
499   to baseline methods. We introduced an augmented mutual information objective function, so that an
500   encoder learns to map input states to a latent space and predict both the direction and magnitude of
501   the sampled skills. By combining this with a constraint on the Euclidean distance between states, as
502   in previous work, our method resulted in a much richer state space coverage. In addition, we show
503   that our approach can scale well beyond Euclidean norm to other distance metrics, such as temporal
504   distance. Furthermore, we demonstrate that it can learn useful behaviors to navigate complex en-
505   vironments, such as a 2-D maze. While these skills can be used for various downstream tasks, in
506
      this work, we showed how the robot can accurately reach desired Cartesian positions in a zero-shot
      manner. Our method achieves this without any task-relevant rewards, unlike other works which re-
507
      quire carefully engineered dense exploratory rewards. Compared to the baselines (Park et al., 2021;
508
      2023b) we achieve a much more uniform skill-conditioned state distribution. In contrast with other
509   intrinsic motivation works that require an imitation dataset (Peng et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023) or a
510   pretrained optimal policy (Zahavy et al., 2022; Cheng et al., 2024a), our method can learn a diverse
511   set of motions with very little prior structure.
512
      Limitations and future work. In this work we used up to 3-D continuous skill spaces, which allow
513
      for control of both position and orientation of the robot. However, since our formulation is inherently
514
      more challenging to learn than the LSD and METRA baselines, as we learn both magnitude and
515   direction, the encoder is more difficult to train for larger skill dimensions. A possible explanation
516   is that the encoder input does not contain enough information to predict high dimensional skills.
517   For example, some states like the pitch and roll cannot be changed much without resulting in the
518   robot falling; other states like those describing the joint and base motion are co-dependent and
519   intertwined for complex articulated systems like quadruped robots. Nevertheless, in future work,
520   we will investigate whether more complex model structures can be leveraged to train accurately for
521   even higher continuous skill dimensions.
522   An interesting research direction would be to evaluate the performance at more downstream tasks
523   than just goal-tracking. Exploring the application of unsupervised skill discovery methods to loco-
524   manipulation tasks is an exciting possibility. Prior work has shown that such methods can learn
525   useful skills in robot manipulator setups, and investigating the kind of behaviors the agent acquires
526   for simultaneous locomotion and manipulation is a promising research direction.
527   Reproducibility Statement: To contribute towards reproducible research, we have included the
528   hyperparameters and rewards necessary to reproduce our work. Our RL set-up is based on the
529   SKRL (Serrano-Muñoz et al., 2023) library and the environments are based on the Isaac Sim Orbit
530   framework (Mittal et al., 2023). We will make our code base publicly accessible upon acceptance.

948        A.6                I NTRINSIC AND EXTRINSIC REWARD ABLATIONS
949
950       The regularization rewards that we propose are necessary for a successful hardware deployment.
951       However, they can add a degree of structure to the problem, which makes the task of skill discovery
952
          easier. To better evaluate the effect of our proposed algorithm (rather than that of the extrinsic
          regularization rewards), we compare several ablations:
953
954                           • Baseline - Our proposed method.
955
                              • Variant 1 - Intrinsic rewards only.
956
957                           • Variant 2 - Intrinsic rewards with body-contact termination.
958                           • Variant 3 - Intrinsic rewards with body-contact termination and contact avoidance extrinsic
959                             rewards.
960
961       As can be seen from Fig. 13, Variant 1 which uses only the intrinsic rewards and no termination
962       conditions has the lowest performing behavior. Qualitatively, the behavior consists of the robot
963
          falling down and rolling around a bit, resulting in the small maximum traveled distance. Just the
          addition of a termination condition on body-ground contacts (Variant 2) enables the robot to learn
964
          a good locomotive policy. For highly articulated robots like the ANYmal, after falling down it
965
          is very challenging to either move or recover to the nominal pose. Without any external bias, if
966       the robot converges to this local optimum it becomes very hard for it to explore enough so that it
967       learns to stand up and then locomote. Finally, by rewarding a lack of hip and shank contacts, as in
968       Variant 3, we can get a much better policy with a similar loss to our proposed Baseline. The main
969       differences between Variant 3 and Baseline is that the former is much less robust and stable, and
970       covers a smaller distance in each episode.

1009        A.7                  U SING OTHER DISTANCE METRICS FOR THE CONSTRAINT
1010
1011       In this work, we used Euclidean distance as the constraint in the optimization objective, as shown in
1012       Eq. 10. While this works well for locomotive behaviors, one can expect that there are certain envi-
1013       ronments and tasks where Euclidean distance might be suboptimal. In literature, several works have
1014       proposed using other distance metrics, such as a controllability-aware distance Park et al. (2023a),
1015       temporal distance (METRA) Park et al. (2023b), or a language-based distance function Rho et al.
1016       (2024). The Temporal distance between two states s0 and state sT is the minimum number of en-
1017
           vironment steps required to transition between them. To show that our approach can be combined
           with different distance metrics, we evaluate its performance using the temporal distance constraint
1018
           as in METRA, and otherwise keep the same norm-matching objective. We constrain the magnitude
1019
           of the skills to in the range [0, 1]1 and set the constraint to be ||ϕ(s′ ) − ϕ(s)|| ≤ 1 as in METRA,
1020       which has been shown to be equivalent to ||ϕ(sT ) − ϕ(s0 )|| ≤ dtemporal . By selecting a magnitude
1021       of, for example, 0.5, we encourage the agent to achieve half the temporal distance achieved under a
1022       magnitude of 1. As can be seen in Fig. 14, our method can also achieve competitive performance
1023
1024          1
                During training we sample magnitudes in the range [0, 1.5] rather than [0, 1] as we found it biases the
1025       approach towards learning behaviors with larger temporal distances due to the implicit temporal distance max-
           imization.

1066   A.8   C AN WE LEARN STATE - TRAVERSING BEHAVIORS IN MORE COMPLEX ENVIRONMENTS ?
1067
1068   In this section we evaluated the skills learned in complex maze environment (see Fig. 15), where the
1069   robot cannot simply locomote in arbitrary directions, using METRA, and our approach with both
1070   temporal and Euclidean distance for the constraint. We used the same training setup, with the agents
1071
       initialized near the center of the maze with a longer episode duration of 20 seconds. We use the same
       observations for the encoder as in the rest of the experiments, but add the base position and height
1072
       scans around the robot to the policy. As can be seen from Fig. 16, all three variations show relative
1073
       success at traversing a large part of the maze. However, the behaviors learned by our method cover
1074   a more uniform area of the maze, unlike METRA where for all skills the agents saturate at different
1075   corners of the maze.
1076   It is interesting to note that not all areas of the maze are visited by either method. A possible
1077   explanation is that the agent might have to traverse the same path under different skills to reach new
1078   areas of the maze. As this is suboptimal per-step (since it would make it difficult to align the latent
1079   encodings with the skills), the agent might never explore enough to discover the distant areas of the
       maze.
