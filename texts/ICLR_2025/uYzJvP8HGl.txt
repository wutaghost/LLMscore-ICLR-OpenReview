011                                                  A BSTRACT
012
013            Existing simulation environments in the field of multi-agent reinforcement learn-
014            ing (MARL) either lack authenticity or complexity. The data generated by these
015
               environments significantly deviate from the requirements of the real world, hinder-
               ing the practical application of MARL. To address this issue, we propose Unreal
016
               Multi-Agent Playground (UMAP), a highly extensible, physics-based 3D simula-
017
               tion environment implemented on the Unreal Engine. UMAP is user-friendly in
018            terms of deployment, modification, and visualization, and all its components are
019            open-sourced1 . Based on UMAP, we design a series of MARL tasks featuring het-
020            erogeneous agents, large-scale agents, multiple teams, and sparse team rewards.
021            We also develop an experimental framework compatible with algorithms rang-
022            ing from rule-based to MARL-based provided by third-party frameworks. In the
023            experimental section, we utilize the designed tasks to test several state-of-the-art
024            algorithms. Additionally, We also conduct a physical experiment to demonstrate
025            UMAP’s potential in sim-to-real applications, which is a significant advantage due
026
               to the high extensibility and authenticity of UMAP. We believe UMAP can play
               an important role in the MARL field by evaluating existing algorithms and helping
027
               them apply to real-world scenarios, thus advancing the field of MARL.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) has demonstrated remarkable potential in many practi-
033   cal fields, including swarm robotic control (Kalashnikov et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2020), autonomous
034   vehicles (Peng et al., 2021b), and video games (Vinyals et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2022). However, a
035   peculiar phenomenon can be observed in the field of MARL (Oroojlooy & Hajinezhad, 2023): al-
036   though numerous new algorithms are claimed to achieve state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance every
037
      year, algorithms actually utilized in real-world applications tend to be classic MARL algorithms or
      extensions of single-agent reinforcement learning (SARL) algorithms, such as IQL (Tan, 1993) and
038
      IPPO (Schulman et al., 2017). Some studies even find that the performance of SARL algorithms
039
      in certain multi-agent scenarios outperforms that of some MARL algorithms (Papoudakis et al.).
040   This indicates that the development of MARL has encountered a bottleneck, with many algorithms
041   performing well only in specific simulated tasks but struggling to be applied in real-world scenarios.
042
043
      One of the keys to breaking through this bottleneck lies in the data of MARL. As a data-driven ap-
      proach, MARL depends on high-quality data for the design and evaluation of its algorithms. How-
044
      ever, if the data distribution is far from that of real-world problems, current developments fail to
045
      align with practical needs. As a learning approach driven by rewards, the data of MARL originates
046   from various simulation environments, and that’s where the problem lies.
047
048   The existing simulation environments of MARL are either overly simplistic and lack authenticity,
049
      or limited to low-complexity decision-making, thereby failing to fully reflect the unique challenges
      of MARL. For instance, MAgent (Zheng et al., 2018) and GoBigger (Zhang et al., 2022) have
050
      the capability to support large-scale multi-agent and multi-team training respectively, but the state
051
      transitions in these environments are simply achieved through interaction rules among particle-like

165   3.1   BASIC C ONCEPTS IN UMAP
166
167   Multi-agent simulation can demonstrate great diversity in different domains. In order to break the
168   limitations of existing environments, it is necessary to introduce a few new concepts that align with
169   human intuition as well as the requirements of multi-agent simulation.
170
      Agents and Teams: Agents are the basic decision-making units in the environments. UMAP intro-
171
      duces a new concept “team” to distinguish agents with different goals. UMAP supports numbers
172   of teams, where teams may engage in competition or cooperation. Each team possesses its own
173   independent goal and is equipped with a separate learning-based (or rule-based) algorithm.
174
175
      Tasks and Scenarios: Tasks corresponds to POMGs defined in Section 2. The properties of tasks
      in UMAP include the types and numbers of agents, their team affiliations, as well as each agent’s
176
      state, observations, reward functions, etc. A scenario can give rise to a series of tasks, which typi-
177
      cally share similar reward functions, implying that the objectives to be achieved by the multi-agent
178   systems are the same.
179
180   Maps: Maps in UMAP determine where the task takes place. A map can be a small room, or a city
181
      full of buildings. It is a great advantage that UMAP decouples the concept of tasks and maps, as
      users can conveniently deploy a task in new maps (as long as the agent has the appropriate size and
182
      a suitable position initialization function).
183
184   Entities: Entities are objects in simulation that do not make decisions but still has important func-
185   tionality. For instance a street lamp or a dynamic obstacle. A shared characteristic of these objects
186   is that they must be removed or reinitialized when an episode ends or a new episode starts.
187   Events: We define an event system to simplify the reward crafting procedure. For instance, an event
188   will be generated when an agent is destroyed or an episode is ended. When it is time to compute
189   next-step reward, these events will provide convenient reference.

      3.2      U TILIZING UMAP TO CUSTOMIZE TASKS
217
218
      UMAP employs a hierarchical five-layer architecture, where each layer builds upon the previous one.
219
      From bottom to top, the five layers are: native layer, specification layer, base class layer, advanced
220
      module layer, and interface layer. Users only need to focus on the advanced module layer and
221   the interface layer. In most cases, modifying the basic functions and configuration parameters in
222   the interface layer is sufficient to alter all elements of tasks.
223
224
      Figure 2 shows the internal structure of each layer of UMAP and the task elements affected by each
      submodule. Specifically, the native layer includes 3D assets from the Unreal community and the
225
      Unreal Engine, some part of which have been optimized for MARL compatibility. The specification
226
      layer consists of UMAP’s underlying systems and programming specifications, all implemented in
227   C++. The base class layer includes all basic classes implemented using Blueprints7 . These three
228   layers form the foundation of UMAP.
229
230
      The advanced module layer, also based on Blueprints, allows for the modification of agents’ phys-
      ical properties such as appearances, perceptions and kinematics, thereby enabling the development
231
      of various agents. This layer also facilitates the development of environmental entities and maps.
232
      The top layer is the interface layer, implemented in Python and compliant with the gym standard. It
233   includes basic functions like reset, step, and done. Additionally, it supports customizable observa-
234   tions and reward functions. This layer also allows for the selection of maps and agents. More details
235   about the UMAP architecture can be found in Appendix C.
236
      Thanks to the hierarchical architecture of UMAP, users can easily customize tasks through simple
237
      operations via top layers. Here we provide a detailed explanation of how each element of a task8 is
238
      customized within UMAP.
239
240   Agent Set. Within the interface level of UMAP, the agent selection module enables users to specify
241   the types, numbers, and associated teams of agents.
242   State Space. The global state is composed of the states of individual agents and the environmental
243   state. Customization of the environmental state can be achieved by selecting different maps and
244   modifying them along with related entities. The state of the agents can be customized through the
245   agent init function in the advanced module layer and the agent component module in the interface
246   layer.
247   Observation Space and Observation Function. UMAP transmits global information from the UE
248   side to the Python side, where the make obs function in the interface layer is used to construct
249   the agents’ observations. Direct modification of this function allows for the customization of each
250   agent’s observation space and function. Moreover, modifying agents’ properties, such as the obser-
251   vation range, can also change their observations. Additionally, UMAP supports more sophisticated
252   agent observation simulation mechanisms, such as masking entities blocked by walls, which can be
253   implemented through the agent perception module in the advanced module layer.
254   Action Space. UMAP supports continuous actions, discrete actions, and hybrid actions. Users can
255   assign a built-in action set to each agent via the agent init function in the interface layer. Fur-
256   thermore, a deeper customization of agent actions can be achieved through the agent action-related
257   modules in the advanced module layer.
258   Transition Function. Similar to the state space, the transition function in UMAP is comprised
259   of local transitions of all agents and environmental transitions. The latter can be modified through
260   map-related and entity-related modules. Local transitions of agents can be customized by modifying
261   the agent init function and the step function, or more deeply through the agent component modules
262   and agent controller modules, such as agent kinematics.
263
      Reward Function. UMAP constructs rewards using global information and an event system. Users
264   can customize the agents’ rewards by modifying the make reward function, which supports team
265   and individual rewards, as well as sparse and dense reward structures.

      4   HMAP
271
272
      To facilitate the deployment of algorithms for UMAP and fully utilize its capabilities, we also open-
273
      source our experimental framework HMAP. HMAP is a multi-agent experimental framework with
274   decoupled Core-Task-Algorithm components. Currently, HMAP integrates environments such as
275   UMAP, SMAC (Samvelyan et al., 2019), MPE (Mordatch & Abbeel, 2017), DCA (Fu et al., 2022),
276   and OpenAI Gym (Brockman, 2016), and supports a wide range of algorithms. This includes rule-
277   based algorithms (most of them are opponent policies for UMAP tasks), single-agent reinforcement
278   learning algorithms like DQN (Mnih et al., 2015) and SAC (Haarnoja et al., 2018), as well as MARL
279   algorithms such as MAPPO (Yu et al., 2022) and HAPPO (Zhong et al., 2024). Furthermore, HMAP
280   is compatible with third-party frameworks, supporting all algorithms from PyMARL2 (Hu et al.,
281   2021) and HARL (Zhong et al., 2024).
282   The unique feature of HMAP is its support for multi-team training. By thoroughly decoupling
283   algorithms from tasks, HMAP employs its core as a “glue module”, enabling any algorithm module
284   to control teams within any task module. Moreover, the observations, actions, and reward data
285   for each algorithm are processed separately and efficiently, ensuring that the policy executing and
286   training for each team are independent. HMAP accommodates sequential and parallel updates of
287   multiple team policy according to hardware performance variations, with the update sequence having
288
      no adverse impact on the effectiveness of algorithm training.
289   HMAP’s highly modular design presents three key benefits. Firstly, it enables modification of script-
290   based opponent policies, which are treated as algorithm modules, in contrast to SMAC and GRF
291   where such policies are hardcoded and immutable. Secondly, it enables teams controlled by multiple
292   algorithms to interact within the same scenario, facilitating the evolution and training of algorithms
293   from different frameworks under the same task. Thirdly, it is user-friendly, as all experimental
294
      configurations based on HMAP can be implemented through a single JSON file. Upon completing
      the configuration, users can initiate the training task with just one line of code. More details of
295
      HMAP can be found in Appendix D.

      Metal Clash. This scenario is designed for heterogeneous and large-scale multi-agent tasks. As
325   illustrated in Figure 3(a), the scenario involves a competition between two teams of agents. Each
326   team can be controlled by either rule-based or learning-based algorithms. Metal Clash provides
327   three types of basic agents: missile cars, laser cars, and support drones. The properties of each basic
328   agent, such as maximum speed and health points (HP), are encapsulated as configurable parameters.
329   Users can easily modify these parameters, creating a variety of heterogeneous agent types beyond
330   the original three. Additionally, the number and types of agents in each team can be freely changed,
331   altering the characteristics and difficulty of the tasks.
332   Based on this scenario, we develop a series of tasks with heterogeneous or large-scale features.
333   In each task, the ally team, controlled by MARL algorithms, competes against an opponent team
334   controlled by rule-based algorithms. Both teams have the same types and numbers of agents (in
335   the following part, we only describe the composition of the ally team). We denote a task where
336   the team consists of x support drones, y laser cars, and z missile cars as metal clash xsd ylc zmc.
337   Accordingly, we develop four heterogeneous tasks: metal clash 5sd 5lc, metal clash 5sd 5mc,
338   metal clash 5lc 5mc, and metal clash 2sd 4lc 4mc.
339   For large-scale tasks, we develop two homogeneous tasks, metal clash homo 50 and
340   metal clash homo 100, which include 50 and 100 laser cars, respectively. In addition, there
341   are two large-scale heterogeneous tasks, metal clash hete 50 and metal clash hete 100. In
342   metal clash hete 50, each team has 10 support drones, 20 laser cars, and 20 missile cars. In
343   metal clash hete 100, the number of each type of agent is doubled compared to metal clash hete 50.
344   Tower Challenge. This scenario is designed for sparse team rewards in a multi-agent cooperative
345   setting. As shown in Figure 3(b), it includes a defense tower and several agents. The goal of all the
346   agents is to destroy the tower cooperatively. The tower’s defenses cover a much larger area than any
347   single agent can attack, making individual efforts ineffective. The entire team receives a positive
348   reward only if the they destroy the tower, there are no rewards or penalties in other cases.
349   Users can adjust the difficulty by modifying the tower’s HP and the number of agents. Based on this
350   scenario, we design two tasks named tower challenge easy and tower challenge hard. Each task
351   involves eight agents, with the harder task featuring a tower HP which is twice that of the easy one.
352
      Flag Capture. This scenario is designed for multi-team gaming. As depicted in Figure 3(c), it
353
      involves several teams competing to capture a flag. The closest agent can pick up the flag, and
354
      their teammates must defend it from other teams. At the end of each episode, the team that held
355   the flag the longest wins. Since all teams start with the same number of agents, capturing the
356   flag first doesn’t guarantee victory. Success requires balancing power and strategic cooperation
357   among all teams. We develop 4 tasks based on this scenario. The first two tasks, flag capture script
358   and flag capture double script, correspond to two-team and three-team tasks, respectively. In each
359   of these tasks, only one team is controlled by the tested MARL algorithm, while the remain-
360   ing teams are controlled by scripts. Similarly, for the last two tasks, flag capture mappo and
361   flag capture double mappo, the script-based algorithms are replaced with MAPPO. Both the tested
362   algorithm and the MAPPO algorithm(s) start training from scratch.
363   Landmark Conquer. This scenario is specifically developed to demonstrate the potential of sim-to-
364   real transfer using UMAP. As illustrated in Figure 3(d), all agents and entities are derived from repli-
365   cas of the physical environment described in Section 6.4. In this scenario, the challengers, consisting
366   of two unmanned ground vehicles (UGVs) and one unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV), are tasked with
367   capturing any landmark protected by guardians. Compared to the challengers, the guardians possess
368   higher attack power and HP. The scenario includes several obstacles and walls, as well as two target
369
      locations. If the UAV remains above any landmark for a specified duration, the capture is considered
      successful, resulting in a victory for the challengers.

492   6.4   P HYSICAL E XPERIMENT
493
      We conduct this experiment to demonstrate the potential of UMAP in bridging the sim-to-real gap.
494   Firstly, we construct a real-world experimental setup, which consists of a motion capture system,
495   a communication system, several autonomous UGVs and UAVs, and a number of physical entities.
496   Subsequently, we develop the landmark conquer scenario through UMAP, wherein the entities are
497   proportionally replicated from the physical setup, and the kinematics of the unmanned units are also
498   recreated. Ultimately, we develop an algorithm-UMAP-hardware framework, with details presented
499   in Appendix G.
500   During the training phase, the algorithmic side, represented by HMAP, interact with UMAP to train
501   policies within the simulated scenarios. In the execution phase, the physical system relay global
502   information captured by the motion capture system and first-person view data from the vehicles’
503   cameras to UMAP. UMAP then update its internal environment with this information and transmit
504   the filtered observational data to HMAP. The algorithm within HMAP generate action commands
505   based on these observations, which are conveyed to UMAP. UMAP execute virtual state transitions
506   based on these commands, and concurrently transmit the decomposed action information to the
507   real-world setup for execution by the autonomous vehicles/drones.
508   Figure 5 presents snapshots from both the virtual and the real-world scenarios. The experimental re-
509   sults indicate that the whole system can successfully replicate the policies of the multi-agent system
510   from the virtual environment within the physical setup.

526   7     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
527
528   In this paper, we introduce UMAP, a powerful, highly extensible UE-based MARL simulation en-
529   vironment. Utilizing UMAP, we design a series of base tasks which include features such as het-
530   erogeneity, large scale, sparse team rewards, and multi-team. Additionally, we develop a multi-
531   agent experimental framework compatible with UMAP, named HMAP. With the tasks developed on
532   UMAP and the algorithm modules within HMAP, we provide a thorough report and discussion on
533   several SOTA MARL algorithms, encompassing both value-based and actor-critic-based methods.
534   Finally, we replicate a task in a real-world setting, demonstrating UMAP’s potential to bridge virtual
535   algorithms with real-world applications.
536   However, UMAP is not perfect. One limitation is that the sim-to-real demonstration so far is rela-
537   tively simple and requires global real-world information to construct pretended local information. In
538   the future, we plan to develop a comprehensive, plug-and-play sim-to-real toolkit based on UMAP.
539   This toolkit will help map real-world requirements into UMAP’s virtual environment, thereby ad-
      vancing the practical application of MARL to the next level.

729   B    R ELATED W ORK
730
731   The simulation environments for MARL can be broadly categorized into two types: those with
732   physics engines and those without. Here, physics engines refer to a suite of tools capable of simulat-
733   ing the physical laws inherent in real-world tasks (Templet, 2021). Given that game engines also aim
734
      at reincarnating the real-world elements into the digital world (Vohera et al., 2021), environments
      leveraging game engines are classified under the physics engine category.
735
736   Among the environments without physics engines, MPE (Mordatch & Abbeel, 2017) utilizes a sim-
737   ple rule-based particle world to simulate multi-agent tasks such as predator-prey and cooperative
738   navigation. MAgent (Zheng et al., 2018), grounded in a grid world, facilitates simulations involv-
739   ing the aggregation and combat of pixel-block agents, notable for its ability to support large-scale
740
      multi-agent settings. The two environments mentioned above are based on the state transition laws
      of particle worlds and particle interactions. Although they are completely open-source and their task
741
      elements are relatively easy to modify, their scenarios are overly simplistic and lack realism.
742
743   Hanabi (Bard et al., 2020) provides a multiplayer card game scenario, which is commonly used
744   in MARL research based on opponent modeling. However, the overly narrow theme prevents it
745   from further simulating tasks involving heterogeneity, large scale, and mixed strategies. Neural
746
      MMO (Suarez et al., 2021) is developed in a 3D grid world derived from massively multiplayer
      online games, supporting large-scale multi-agent simulations over long time horizons.
747
748   Gobigger (Zhang et al., 2022), based on a ball world concept, stands out for enabling simulations
749   involving collaboration and competition among multiple teams. However, similar to MPE and MA-
750   gent, their particle-based 2D environments fall significantly short of simulating the real-world com-
751   plexities of 3D scens.
752   JaxMARL (Rutherford et al., 2024) integrates numerous MARL environments together and has re-
753   implemented these environments using JAX technology, enabling them to support efficient, GPU-
754   based parallel computing. However, to support pure GPU parallelism, some environments in JAX-
755   MARL have lost their original CPU-based underlying physical engines. Moreover, as a collection
      of environments that integrates multiple basic environments, it does not support multi-team multi-


                                                       14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      algorithm training, nor does it support controllable time-flow simulation and cross-platform real-
757   time rendering.
758
759   As for the environments with physics engines, GRF (Kurach et al., 2020) is built upon the Gameplay-
760
      Football simulator (Schuiling, 2017), creating a highly realistic football match setting that allows
      agents to simulate the behaviors of human players. However, it does not support large-scale sce-
761
      narios, multi-team training, and mixed multi-agent gameplay. Moreover, although its environment
762
      interface and underlying engine are open-source, the underlying engine is not suitable.
763
764   SMAC (Samvelyan et al., 2019) and SMACv2 (Ellis et al., 2022) are developed based on the popu-
765   lar video game StarCraft II, constructing a multi-agent micromanagement environment where each
766
      agent controls individual units to complete adversarial tasks. Despite their widespread use, the fact
      that their underlying games and engines are not fully open-source limits further expansion, confining
767
      their built-in tasks to battle-type game scenarios only.
768
769   Hide-and-Seek (Baker et al., 2019) has set up a series of multi-agent curriculum learning scenarios,
770   such as hide and seek, based on a 3D engine. However, its theme is too singular, making it im-
771   possible to simulate tasks involving heterogeneity, large scale, multiple teams, etc., and it does not
772
      allow for customization of all task elements. Hok3v3 (Liu et al., 2023), specifically designed for
      heterogeneous multi-agent tasks, is based on the Honor of Kings engine, with agent action spaces
773
      consistent with those of human players engaging in the real game. However, it only supports het-
774
      erogeneous multi-agent scenarios (3VS3) and does not have an open-source underlying game and
775   related engine.
776
777   MAMuJoCo (Peng et al., 2021a) is developed using the Mujoco physics engine (Todorov et al.,
778
      2012), where multiple agents each control different joints to collaboratively manage the movements
      of a single robot. However, all of the multi-agent scenarios are fully cooperative and do not support
779
      large-scale multi-agent tasks.
780
781   Marathon Environment (Booth & Booth, 2019) is developed using the Unity3D engine, support-
782   ing multiple agents learning complex movements such as running and backflipping. The built-in
783   tasks are relatively simple and are unable to simulate large-scale, multi-team, and mixed multi-agent
784
      gameplay tasks. Moreover, its underlying engine, Unity3D, is not fully open-source, thus preventing
      comprehensive modifications from the bottom to the top layer.
785
786   It is evident that environments without physics engines are adept at simulating challenging tasks
787   designed to push the limits of existing algorithms. In contrast, environments equipped with physics
788   engines offer greater potential for real-world applications but are constrained in terms of academic
789   flexibility. Our goal is to develop an environment that not only has practical application potential
790
      but also fully leverages scalability, ultimately leading to the creation of UMAP.

794   C.1   A RCHITECTURE OF UMAP
795
796   UMAP utilizes a hierarchical design that consists of five layers, all of which are open source. As
797   shown in Figure 2, the first layer of UMAP is the native part of the Unreal Engine, including the
798   physics engine, rendering engine, AI engine, and a range of 3D assets. We build the entire UMAP
799   based on the open-source version of UE, making modifications to some of the native modules.
800
      For instance, the original AI detection system for agents in UE was very inefficient in large-scale
      scenes. UMAP optimizes the detection of multiple entities by incorporating tensor operations and
801
      eliminating redundant checks.
802
803   The second layer of UMAP comprises the underlying systems and programming specification, all
804   implemented in C++. The time control system and task system in this layer ensure the correct initia-
805   tion and termination of simulation episodes, guaranteeing the precision of simulation time steps and
806
      the reproducibility of experimental results. Other components of this layer define the specification
      for all base classes, communication, and debugging within UMAP.
807
808   The third layer consists of three fundamental classes implemented using Blueprints. The agent class
809   defines all entities that can be controlled by algorithms, while the entity actor corresponds to all
      environmental entities that do not make decisions. Classes derived from these two form all the


                                                       15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      entity elements within a task scenario. The abstract class acts as a bridge, connecting the underlying
811   systems to the highest Python-based layer, facilitating communication, debugging, action updates,
812   and observation feedback.
813
814
      The fourth layer of UMAP consists of advanced functional modules, implemented using Blueprints.
      These modules allow for the modification of various attributes of agents, including appearance,
815
      perception, action sets, movement, and navigation, enabling the development of diverse types of
816
      agents. Moreover, leveraging the abundant resources in the Unreal community, the map construction
817   module facilitates the creation of new maps and even the importation of real-world maps. The
818   entity construction module aids in developing complex environmental entities, such as altering the
819   kinematic model of missiles launched by drones.
820
      The fifth layer serves as the interface for interaction between UMAP and algorithms, all imple-
821
      mented in Python. This interface adheres to the gym (Brockman, 2016) specification, encompassing
822
      basic functions like reset, step, and done, and supports the customization of agent-level observation
823   and reward functions. Attributes such as agent size, initial position, detection range, and health are
824   directly encapsulated within the agent initialization function, allowing for easy modification. As
825   shown in Figure 6, the selection of agents, tasks, and maps in UMAP are independent. Users can
826   customize the types, numbers, and teams of agents in a task and switch maps flexibly.
827
      From the perspective of designing and utilizing a MARL simulation environment, users need only
828
      focus on the fourth and fifth layers of UMAP. In most cases, users can directly customize MARL
829   tasks by modifying the built-in scenarios and agent parameters through the fifth layer. If there is
830   a need to develop new scenarios or further develop existing ones, users can also easily develop
831   through the graphical programming approach provided in the fourth layer. UMAP’s hierarchical
832   design significantly reduces the burden of customizing tasks.

1086   F    UMAP’ S E FFICIENCY AND C OMPUTATIONAL R ESOURCE C ONSUMPTION
1087
1088   As a simulation environment based on a 3D physical engine, UMAP boasts high simulation effi-
1089   ciency. It is well-designed to adapt to and fully utilize various types of computing resources. UMAP
1090
       can be deployed on computing systems entirely devoid of GPUs for algorithm training and sup-
       ports the full utilization of uneven computing resources. It can operate in a single-threaded manner
1091
       as well as support multiple parallel environments. Additionally, with the feature of time dilation
1092
       factors, UMAP can not only improve simulation efficiency by increasing the number of parallel
1093   processes but also control the simulation speed of each process to make full use of computing re-
1094   sources (using more CPU utilization under the same memory and GPU memory), a functionality not
1095   available in other simulation platforms.
1096
       In this section, to verify UMAP’s efficiency and adaptability to various computing resources, we
1097
       conducted a series of experiments on UMAP’s efficiency index and resource consumption indices.
1098
       The efficiency index adopted was FPS, i.e., the number of virtual timesteps run in a real second;
1099   the resource consumption indices included CPU utilization, memory occupancy, and GPU memory
1100   occupancy. All experiments were conducted on a Linux server equipped with an AMD7742 CPU
1101   (maximum frequency 2.25GHz) and NVIDIA RTX3090 GPUs. To ensure fairness, all experiments
1102   tested the QMIX algorithm on the metal clash 5sd 5mc task. The data points for all indices were
1103   obtained by averaging the results of five experiments. At the beginning of each experiment, the
1104   server was maintained in an idle state, executing only the essential system processes.
1105   Figure X shows the indices under a fixed number of parallel environments at 8, with varying time
1106   dilation factors. Figure Y shows the indices with a time dilation factor of 32, under varying numbers
1107   of parallel environments. From these two figures, the following conclusions can be drawn:
1108
1109         1. With a constant number of parallel environments, FPS and CPU utilization are roughly
1110            proportional to the time dilation factor, but this proportional relationship degrades into a
1111            positive correlation when the time dilation factor reaches a certain threshold (limited by the
1112            CPU’s clock speed).
1113         2. With a constant number of parallel environments, changing the time dilation factor almost
1114            does not affect memory occupancy and GPU memory occupancy.
1115
             3. With a constant time dilation factor, CPU utilization is roughly linearly related to the num-
1116            ber of parallel environments, while FPS is roughly logarithmically related; memory and
1117            GPU memory occupancy are positively correlated with the number of parallel environ-
1118            ments.
1119
1120   The above conclusions mean that under limited memory resources, training efficiency can be im-
1121   proved by increasing the time dilation factor to fully utilize CPU resources; similarly, under limited
1122   CPU computing resources, reducing the time dilation factor and increasing the number of processes
1123   can avoid the waste of computing resources.
1124   In fact, when the number of processes is 8 and the time dilation factor is 32, training 1024 episodes
1125   on the metal clash 5sd 5mc task takes less than 2 minutes. This means that under such parameter
1126   settings, this server can simultaneously support 50 such tasks (each with 20 agents) and complete all
1127   training tasks (100k episodes) within 3 hours. In special cases, the number of parallel processes can
1128   be further increased to improve training efficiency. When the number of processes reaches 128 and
1129   the time dilation factor is set to 32, the FPS can reach 1000+, and the training task can be completed
1130
       in about an hour.
1131   It is important to emphasize that FPS here counts the number of virtual UMAP timesteps per real
1132   second. Considering this is a simulation of 20 agents, and each timestep in UMAP undergoes 1280
1133   frames of calculations for environmental dynamics and kinematics to maintain fine state transitions
       (details in Appendix C.2), this is already highly efficient computation.
