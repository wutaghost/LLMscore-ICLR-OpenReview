009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural combinatorial optimization (NCO) models have achieved remarkable per-
012            formance, yet their learned underlying representations remain largely unclear.
013            This hinders real-world application, as industrial stakeholders may want a deeper
014            understanding of NCO models before committing resources. In this paper, we
015            make the first step towards interpreting NCO models by investigating embeddings
016
               learned by various architectures through three probing tasks. Specifically, we an-
               alyze representative and state-of-the-art attention-based models, including AM,
017
               POMO, and LEHD, on the representative Traveling Salesman Problem and Ca-
018
               pacitated Vehicle Routing Problem. Our findings reveal that NCO models encode
019            linear representations of Euclidean distances between nodes, while also capturing
020            additional knowledge that help avoid making myopic decisions. Furthermore, we
021            show that architectural choices affect the ability of deep models to accurately rep-
022            resent Euclidean distances and to incorporate non-myopic decision-making strate-
023            gies. We also verify to what extent NCO models understand the feasibility of con-
024            straints. Our work represents an initial effort to interpret NCO models, enhance
025            understanding of why certain architectures outperform others, and demonstrate
026            probing as a valuable tool for analyzing their internal mechanisms.
027
028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Recently, learning-based neural combinatorial optimization (NCO) methods have achieved remark-
031   able performance on classic combinatorial optimization problems, such as routing, that is com-
032   parable to, or even surpasses, specialized heuristic algorithms designed for these problems (e.g.,
033   Concorde (Applegate et al., 2006), LKH3 (Helsgaun, 2017), HGS (Vidal, 2022), etc.). However, the
034   underlying reasons behind these impressive results, particularly the nature of the knowledge learned
035   by these neural models, remain largely unexplored and unclear.
036   Due to this lack of understanding, current research often relies on final performance metrics, such
037   as average objective function values, to retrospectively assess the strengths and weaknesses of dif-
038   ferent NCO architectures. This retrospective evaluation approach, however, may lack rigor and
039   precision. Various external influences, such as differing inference strategies (e.g., greedy, sampling,
040   beam search, or specialized methods like Random Re-Construct from Luo et al. (2023)), can lead to
041   significant differences in performance (Zhou et al., 2024). This obscures the assessment of the true
042   representational capacity of NCO models and hinders understanding of how effectively they capture
043   decision-supporting information. Misinterpreting the model architecture, in turn, can negatively
044
      affect future model design. Therefore, addressing this gap is crucial.
045   To address this gap, for the first time, we bring the tool, probing, from the computer vision (CV)
046   and natural language processing (NLP) fields to the NCO field, so as to more directly explore the
047   representational capacity of neural network embeddings (Alain & Bengio, 2016; Adi et al., 2016;
048   Belinkov, 2022). Probing involves training auxiliary prediction tasks using the embeddings learned
049   by a pre-trained deep learning model. In the context of NLP, for example, if a simple model, partic-
050
      ularly a linear model, can be trained to predict linguistic information about a word (e.g., its part-of-
      speech tag) or a pair of words (e.g., their semantic relation) from the embeddings, we can reasonably
051
      conclude that the embeddings encode this information (for more details, see Liu et al. (2019)).
052
053   Unlike NLP tasks, which naturally have intuitive subtasks (e.g., part-of-speech tagging, semantic re-
      lation tagging) suitable for probing, combinatorial optimization (CO) problems typically lack such


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      directly applicable subtasks. To deal with this, we designed probing tasks tailored to evaluate the
055   representational capacity of NCO models in specific CO problems and constructed corresponding
056   datasets. To demonstrate the effectiveness of probing in NCO, we selected two groups of NCO
057   models with similar architectures (both based on the transformer (Vaswani, 2017) structure) but
058   with contrasting design principles. In addition to uncovering certain knowledge embedded in their
059   representations through probing, we also compared the differences in this knowledge captured by the
060   embeddings, which result from the structural variations between the two groups of models. Specif-
061   ically, one group consists of the classical models AM (Kool et al., 2018) and POMO (Kwon et al.,
062   2020), while the other is their successor LEHD (Luo et al., 2023), which introduces a contrasting
063
      architecture—the light encoder heavy decoder (LEHD) model structure, proposed as a potentially
      more effective alternative to the heavy encoder light decoder (HELD) structure of the earlier models.
064
065   Experimental results in Luo et al. (2023) show that the LEHD model indeed outperforms AM and
066   POMO in solving the traveling salesman problem (TSP) and capacitated vehicle routing problem
067   (CVRP), specifically in terms of the objective function value, as measured by the average traveling
068   distances of the routing solutions. Luo et al. (2023) attribute this to the LEHD’s ability to bet-
069
      ter capture the dynamic relationships between nodes of varying sizes. Through our probing tasks,
      we provide additional and more direct evidence identifying specific factors that might make the
070
      LEHD structure superior to the HELD one. These factors include improved perception of Euclidean
071
      distances between nodes, a stronger ability to avoid myopic decision-making, and a more robust
072   capability to capture information related to constraints. Unlike final performance metrics, which
073   are influenced by inference strategies, these factors offer strong support for the design idea behind
074   LEHD models. This provides subsequent researchers with a clearer basis for determining whether to
075   incorporate such structural choices in their designs, enhancing confidence in these design decisions.
076
      Contribution. Our contributions are as follows: (1) For the first time, we pioneer the use of probing
077
      in the NCO field to explore and understand the embeddings learned by NCO models. (2) Akin
078   to other impactful probing research in non-NLP fields that lack natural subtasks for probing (Li
079   et al., 2022; Gurnee & Tegmark, 2023), we design targeted probing tasks and create corresponding
080   datasets. (3) We provide evidence that NCO models are capable of capturing knowledge relevant to
081   decision-making in routing problems. (4) By analyzing the differences in the knowledge learned,
082   we shed light on why state-of-the-art models achieve superior performance.
083
      Overall, we take an important first step in unveiling the internal mechanisms of NCO model em-
084   beddings through probing techniques. We demonstrate how probing, as a toolkit, can be used to
085   verify why models are effective in CO problems and gain insights into model architecture design.
086   This analysis toolkit supports future work in understanding the representations of black-box NCO
087   models, providing more direct evidence beyond final problem results for performance exploration.

090   2    P ROBING TASKS
091
092   Since combinatorial optimization problems do not have suitable subtasks to serve as probing tasks,
093   targeted task design is necessary for the specific CO problem being explored. Using the TSP problem
094
      as an example, we propose two probing tasks to investigate NCO models: whether the model can
      perceive the Euclidean distance between nodes (probing task 1); and whether the model can learn
095
      to avoid constructing solutions in a myopic manner, such as greedily connecting to the nearest node
096
      (probing task 2). Additionally, we also introduce a probing task using the CVRP problem, examining
097   whether NCO models can capture constraints (probing task 3).
098
099
      Probing task 1: Euclidean distance When solving routing problems in Euclidean space, the
100
      Euclidean distance between nodes is a critical piece of information for all solution methods. For
101
      instance, a simple greedy algorithm for solving the TSP starts at an arbitrary node, computes the
102   Euclidean distance between the current node and all unvisited nodes, and selects the nearest one as
103   the next destination. This process is repeated until all nodes are visited, returning to the starting node
104   to form a Hamiltonian cycle. In traditional methods, whether using exact approaches (mathematical
105   programming) that rely on the distance matrix of nodes as input or approximate (heuristic) methods
106   (Reinelt, 2003; Liu et al., 2023), the Euclidean distance between any two nodes must be precomputed
107   or computed on the fly. Therefore, for a TSP solver, recognizing the Euclidean distances between
      nodes is essential. Based on this, we aim to explore whether a trained learning-based NCO model

      can capture this critical Euclidean distance between the current node and any of the candidate nodes
109   in its representations.

      Probing task. We define probing task 2 as a binary classification task, where the probing model
148
      is trained to determine whether the current node (e.g., ni ) should be linked to node nj . Node nj
149
      could either be a myopic choice that leads to a local optimum or the node connected to ni in the
150   global optimal solution. To assess whether the NCO models make myopic decisions by choosing
151   the nearest Euclidean distance, we construct data points as illustrated in Figure 2.

153
      Dataset. First, we randomly generate an instance with N nodes, input it into a mathematical pro-
      gramming model, and use the Gurobi (Gurobi Optimization, LLC, 2024) solver to obtain the theo-
154
      retical optimal solution, as shown in Figure 2(a). Next, starting from each node, we use a greedy
155
      algorithm to generate N solutions and select the best one (as illustrated in Figure 2(b), gradually
156   comparing the next node selected by the greedy algorithm with the optimal solution. For example,
157   in the instance shown in Figure 2, when the current node is node 4, the optimal solution selects
158   node 3, whereas the greedy algorithm selects the nearest one, node 5. Ultimately, we obtain two
159   data points for this instance: node 4 connected to node 3 represents the optimal choice, labeled as a
160   positive example (i.e., the feature is [h4 , h3 ] and the label is 1), while node 4 connected to node 5
161   represents the myopic choice of the greedy algorithm, labeled as a negative example (i.e., the feature
      is [h4 , h5 ] and the label is 0).

      Domain knowledge. Unlike the relatively straightforward probing tasks and datasets in CV and
163   NLP, probing in the CO field requires incorporating domain-specific knowledge. For instance, in this
164   dataset, there may be multiple optimal solutions. Suppose one of them includes node 4 connected
165   to node 5, which would render a label of 0 incorrect. To verify this, we add a constraint to the
166   mathematical model that forces the connection between nodes 4 and 5. The new optimal solution
167   obtained under this constraint is worse than the original solution without the constraint. Similarly,
168   for data labeled 1, we add a constraint preventing the connection between nodes 4 and 3, and the
169   resulting solution is also worse. This confirms that both labels are valid.

207   Probing Model For all three probing tasks, we use a linear model for the corresponding regression
208   and classification tasks. Specifically, we train a simple linear fully connected (FC) layer for both
209   classification and regression tasks. If this linear model can accurately predict the probing tasks based
210   on the embeddings from the NCO models, it indicates that the knowledge relevant to the probing
211   tasks can be easily extracted from the embeddings (Alain & Bengio, 2016; Liu et al., 2019). This
212
      also suggests that the NCO model, from which the embeddings for the probing tasks are derived,
      has the ability to encode this knowledge in its representations after training.
213
214   It is important to note that a linear model cannot directly capture the nonlinear relationship of Eu-
215   clidean distance. For probing task 1 (the Euclidean distance regression task), a linear model would
      have no explanatory power if the input only consists of the nodes’ coordinate information. In the

251   Datasets In line with the problem settings of AM, POMO, and LEHD, we select 20 nodes as a
252   small-scale instance and 100 nodes as a relatively large-scale instance. This setup allows us to use
253   pre-trained models and extract embeddings from models corresponding to these scales. We will
254
      make all the code and datasets publicly available for future research.
255   Routing instances. For probing task 1 and probing task 2, we generate 10,000 TSP instances with
256   20 nodes and 10,000 instances with 100 nodes, respectively, following the method introduced in
257   AM (Kool et al., 2018), which was subsequently used by both POMO and LEHD. Since probing
258   task 2 requires global optimal solutions and greedy solutions to create the probing dataset labels,
259   we use Gurobi to solve the optimal solution for these 20,000 instances. For the greedy solution, we
260
      perform a greedy algorithm starting from each node. Then, we generate the datasets for these 20,000
      instances following the method described in Section 2.
261
262   For probing task 3, we similarly generate 10,000 instances with 20 nodes and 10,000 instances with
263   100 nodes following the method used in AM. The aim of this paper is to pioneer the application of
264   probing to the study of NCO models. Therefore, as an initial exploration, we have conducted only
265   one constraint-related probing task on the CVRP. We believe that future probing research on CVRP
266
      can offer deeper insights into how NCO models handle constraints. If further labeling of CVRP
      solutions is required, we recommend using the HGS (Vidal, 2022) to solve CVRP instances.

317
318   For Probing Task 1, we examine the ability of the three NCO models to linearly represent the Eu-
319   clidean distances between pairs of nodes (specifically, the current node and any unvisited node) dur-
320   ing decision-making, by training linear probes and evaluating their performance. First, we demon-
321   strate that all three NCO models can linearly capture the Euclidean distances between nodes. Then,
322   through comparison, we find that LEHD performs better in accurately perceiving Euclidean dis-
323   tances than POMO and AM, particularly in the robustness of its representation extraction method,
      as the inclusion or exclusion of interaction terms has little impact on LEHD’s probing performance.

      Existence. As shown in the ”w/o ints.” rows of AM-Init in Table 1 for both 20-node and 100-
329
      node examples, the values indicate that the initial embeddings of AM fail to capture the nonlinear
330
      relationship of Euclidean distance (with R2 values close to 0). These embeddings are derived by
331   mapping the raw features, specifically the 2D coordinates in the TSP, through a linear projection
332   into the shared dimensional space of the encoder and decoder (128 dimensions for all three models
333   discussed in this paper). In Section 3, we explained that the R2 of a Euclidean distance regression
334   model using node coordinates as input is zero because it cannot capture the nonlinear nature of
335   Euclidean distance. As a result, the initial embeddings essentially retain the properties of the raw
336   features and similarly fail to linearly capture Euclidean distances. The phenomenon of an R2 value
337   of zero for the initial embeddings can be observed across all NCO models.
338   However, after passing through the NCO model, the R2 values for AM, POMO, and LEHD in-
339   crease to 0.2529, 0.1981, and 0.9421, respectively, for 20-nodes example. Even when considering
340   interaction terms, the R2 values for all three models’ embeddings after the encoder or decoder are
341   significantly higher than those of the initial embeddings, approaching 1 in 100-node example. This
342   indicates that the representations in these NCO models contain linearly decodable Euclidean dis-
343   tance information, meaning they have learned how to linearly represent Euclidean distances.
344   Comparison of HELD and LEHD. In the results shown in Table 1, for both the 20-node and 100-
345   node instances, LEHD’s approach—using a single encoder layer followed by multi-layer attention
346   calculations between the current node and other nodes in the decoder—outperforms the embedding

      As Layers Deepen. By comparing the results (including both node-scale instances and whether
379   interaction terms are used) of the same model across different layers, we find that the ability of the
380   embeddings to perceive Euclidean distances decreases as the number of attention layers increases
381   in all three models. Notably, after six attention layers, POMO shows a more significant decline
382   in Euclidean distance perception compared to AM, which has the same structure but only three
383   attention layers. This suggests that while deeper attention layers may enhance other decision-making
384   capabilities (as discussed in the next section), the model’s ability to perceive distances diminishes.
385   In subsequent research based on AM/POMO models, some models introduce node distance infor-
386   mation to enhance performance: either by explicitly incorporating distance information to adjust
387   the model’s output (Wang et al., 2024), or by designing distance-aware attention mechanisms (Zhou
388   et al., 2024). Through probing experiments, we verify that these approaches introduce Euclidean
389   distance to mitigate its perception deficiency as the number of layers increases in NCO models.
390   This provides important guidance for future improvements to AM and POMO-based models.

392   5.2   R ESULTS AND DISCUSSION FOR P ROBING TASK 2
393
394   Through Probing Task 2, we explore whether NCO models can learn to avoid making decisions
395   based solely on distance. First, we demonstrate that the embeddings of all three NCO models exhibit
396   the ability to avoid myopic decision-making. Then, by comparison, we find that LEHD performs
397   better in this regard than POMO, with POMO outperforming AM. This result aligns with their per-
398   formance on the objective function values in the routing problem and further supports the conclusion
399
      from Probing Task 1 regarding the impact of model structure on performance.
400   Existence. We use the “AM-Init” results as a baseline reference, with AUC values consistently at
401   0.5, indicating that the initial embeddings cannot linearly extract the knowledge needed to distin-
402   guish which nodes are connected to the current ones in the global optimal solutions (namely, the
403   optimal edges). To confirm that Probing Task 2 is not relying on Euclidean distances for node dif-
404   ferentiation, we further examine the initial embeddings with interaction terms, whose AUC values
405
      remain close to 0.5, suggesting they still fail to distinguish between global optimal or greedy edges.
      In contrast, in Probing Task 1, the initial embeddings with interaction terms achieve an R2 above
406
      0.7, indicating that the initial embeddings with interaction terms have linear explanatory power for
407
      Euclidean distances. This observation confirms that the two probing tasks are fundamentally differ-
408   ent. It also implies that if the embeddings in an NCO model can be linearly distinguished in Probing
409   Task 2, the model has learned to avoid myopic decision-making and capture the knowledge needed
410   to find the global optimal solution.
411
      Comparison. The results in Table 1 show that all three NCO models possess the ability to avoid
412
      myopic decision-making, and this ability improves as the number of attention layers increases. Ad-
413
      ditionally, for the 20-node instance, LEHD’s performance in this regard matches POMO (both with
414   an AUC of 0.86) and slightly outperforms AM (0.83). In the 100-node instance, however, LEHD
415   outperforms the others with an AUC of 0.85, compared to 0.80 for POMO and 0.76 for AM. These
416   results are fully consistent with their performance on the optimization problem outcomes. More
417   detailed results can be found in Figure 8 in Appendix C.3.
418
      Furthermore, by observing the results of “AM-Enc-l3-w/c” and ”AM-Enc-l3-w/g”, which show
419
      almost no difference from ”AM-Enc-l3” and remain lower than LEHD, we further support the con-
420   clusion from Probing Task 1: LEHD’s heavy decoder structure captures more relevant decision-
421   supporting information compared to AM and POMO, which rely on context embeddings.

424
425
      Through Probing Task 3, using the capacity constraint in the CVRP problem as an example, we
426   demonstrate that probing can be applied to study the ability of NCO models to represent constraints.
427   We observe that, while all three NCO models can capture the linear (additive) relationship between
428   node demands, this ability weakens with an increasing number of layers, similar to the perception of
429   Euclidean distances. This observation is particularly noteworthy in the Hadamard product probing
430   input, [hi ⊙ hj ]. As discussed in Section 2, we simulate attention calculations using this Hadamard
431   product input. Many NCO models, including AM and POMO, calculate a compatibility score by
      attention calculations before applying the output Softmax. In this context, the R2 values for the final

      output layer decrease significantly compared to the first layer, as shown in the ”w/ ints.” rows in Table
433   1. In some results, R2 even drops to the 0.1-0.35 range, indicating that these NCO models may no
434   longer accurately capture whether the demand exceeds vehicle capacity and are likely relying on
435   masking to impose final output modifications and constraints.
436
      This probing task raises an interesting research question for designing NCO models to handle con-
437
      straints: should additional constraint-related information be incorporated into NCO models, similar
438
      to how distance information is added in studies (Wang et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024)? Future work
439   can further explore this by designing and implementing more probing tasks to deepen the under-
440   standing of how NCO models handle constraints.

495   5.5   ROBUSTNESS C HECK
496
497   To further validate the robustness of probing as a tool for analyzing NCO models and the probing
498   tasks we designed, we demonstrate Probing Task 1 for distance perception in non-Euclidean space.
499   Specifically, we selected MatNet (Kwon et al., 2021), a state-of-the-art model designed for solving
500
      asymmetric TSP (ATSP).
501   We use MatNet’s row embeddings and column embeddings for pairs of nodes as features, and the
502   distances between the corresponding nodes in the distance matrix as labels to construct the probing
503   dataset. For example, the row embeddings of node i and node j are used as features, with the
504   corresponding label being the value in the distance matrix at the intersection of row i and column j,
505   denoted as dist(i, j). Similarly, the column embeddings of node i and node j are used as features,
506
      with the label being dist(j, i).
507   The results are shown in Figure 11. As the number of layers in MatNet increases, the ability of
508   its embeddings to perceive distances improves, with the R2 rising from less than 0.2 in the first
509   layer to approximately 0.5 in the final layer. Additionally, we conduct supplementary comparison
510   experiments. In the first experiment, serving as a baseline, the embeddings of node i and node j are
511   used as inputs, but the labels are replaced with random distance values unrelated to both nodes from
512
      the distance matrix. The resulting probing R2 is -0.0232, indicating that the probe could not learn
      any distance information from random labels based on the embeddings. In the second experiment,
513
      we swap the labels between row and column embeddings, assigning the row embeddings of node i
514
      and j with the label dist(j, i) and vice versa. The resulting probing R2 is 0.2532. Comparing these
515   results, we conclude the following: MatNet’s dual-attention structure effectively learns information
516   from the asymmetric distance matrix. Furthermore, regardless of whether the embeddings of two
517   nodes are correctly aligned, they can still partially represent distance information. However, the
518   model’s ability to capture correct distance information between two nodes is significantly stronger
519   than its ability to capture incorrect distance information, with R2 values of approximately 0.5 versus
520   0.2, respectively.

      6     C ONCLUSION
523
524
525
      Using the probing method, we are the first to reveal the representational capabilities of NCO mod-
      els, thereby deepening the understanding of the internal mechanisms of these black-box models.
526
      Through three probing tasks, we find that both classical and state-of-the-art attention-based NCO
527
      models can perceive Euclidean distances and have learned to avoid making myopic decisions based
528   solely on distance. Although the ability to perceive Euclidean distances decreases as the number of
529   attention layers increases, the models simultaneously acquire more knowledge to avoid shortsighted
530   decision-making. Similarly, the ability of these NCO models to perceive linear constraints weakens
531   with deeper layers. This finding offers insights and supports for future works focused on improving
532   NCO models’ ability to handle constraints. Additionally, by comparing the performance of differ-
533   ent models, we uncover that the best-performing SOTA models possess a stronger representational
534   capacity for capturing decision-relevant knowledge.
535   We believe that with the design and introduction of more probing tasks in the future, the reliability
536   and interpretability of NCO models will further improve, increasing their potential for real-world
537   applications. Additionally, our exploration of combining combinatorial optimization knowledge
538   with probing to understand model representations in the NCO domain provides a method to enhance
539   the potential of deep learning applications in other areas. This approach is expected to promote the
      broader application of deep learning models in scientific and engineering fields.

      A    R ELATED W ORK
649
650
651
652
      Neural (deep learning-based) methods have been applied to various combinatorial optimization
      problems for several years (Khalil et al., 2017; Bengio et al., 2021). With the rapid advancement
653
      of deep learning (DL), an increasing number of approaches have been introduced to address these
654
      classical problems in operations research (OR). In the context of the routing problem discussed in
655   this paper, researchers have explored methods such as graph convolutional networks (GCNs) (Kool
656   et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023), pointer networks with recurrent neural networks (RNNs) (Bello
657   et al., 2016; Nazari et al., 2018), diffusion-based approaches (Sun & Yang, 2023), and attention
658   mechanisms (Kool et al., 2018; Lu et al., 2019; Kwon et al., 2020; Ma et al., 2021; Luo et al., 2023),
659   which are the primary focus of this study.
660
      Although NCO methods have seen rapid development in academia, industries remain cautious about
661   deploying them to replace classical OR methods. This is because these DL-based methods are per-
662   ceived as black-box models, lacking the reliability and interpretability of traditional OR approaches.
663   As a result, even though some NCO models have achieved strong performance on certain instances,
664   they are still met with skepticism. For example, Santana et al. (2023) raises concerns about the
665   overuse of GNNs, noting that the improvements achieved by GNN-based methods over traditional
666   distance-related approaches were minimal. To address this, we are the first to unveil the inner work-
667   ings of NCO models, aiming to enhance understanding of their internal mechanisms.
668   The probing method used in this paper was initially applied to understand the representations of
669   DL models in computer vision (Alain & Bengio, 2016) and natural language processing (Adi et al.,
670   2016). Beyond traditional DL tasks, probing has also demonstrated effectiveness in other domains,
671   such as exploring world representations (Li et al., 2022; Gurnee & Tegmark, 2023) in large language
672   models, auditory representations (Shah et al., 2021; Ngo & Kim, 2024), and studying the quality of
673   unsupervised reinforcement learning representations (Zhang et al., 2022).
674
675
676
677
      B    AM, POMO, LEHD
678
679
680
681
      AM is one of the earliest and most successful learning-based models for routing tasks. It is pio-
      neering in introducing the widely popular Transformer architecture to combinatorial optimization
682
      problems, inspiring a multitude of subsequent models. POMO, as a notable example, retains a
683
      structure fundamentally similar to AM (with minor differences, such as in context embedding) but
684   introduces a novel reinforcement learning (RL) training method.
685
686   AM not only introduces the Transformer architecture but also makes significant contributions to the
687
      model design for routing problems. A notable idea is AM’s context embedding in the decoder, which
      focuses on the current node and the starting node (for TSP problems). Although many later models
688
      do not adopt this exact context embedding design, the core idea of focusing on these two key nodes
689
      remains. For example, even though LEHD’s decoder design differs from AM’s, it fundamentally
690   considers how to represent information from these two critical nodes.
691
692   Specifically, the difference between LEHD and AM lies in their architectural design. Figure 3 illus-
693
      trates the architecture of both models. In Figure 3(a), AM uses a multi-layer encoder to learn how to
      represent node information based on their input features (coordinates), while the decoder performs
694
      a single attention computation on the node representations generated by the encoder, producing a
695
      global ”glimpse” for decision-making without updating the node embeddings. This is known as the
696   ”Heavy Encoder Light Decoder” structure. In contrast, LEHD adopts a ”Light Encoder Heavy De-
697   coder” structure, where the encoder uses only a single attention layer to learn node representations,
698   while the decoder, at each step, re-learns the embeddings of the current node, destination node, and
699   candidate nodes through multiple attention layers. In LEHD, as shown in Figure 3(b), hs and hd
700   represent the embeddings of the current node (referred to as the starting node in LEHD) and the
701   destination node, while the node embeddings located in the middle are filtered in LEHD to exclude
      previously visited nodes.

1046   C.6        JSSP PRECEDENCE CONSTRAINT
1047
1048   In addition to the routing problem analyzed earlier, we also apply probing to test the precedence
1049   constraints in the Job-shop Scheduling Problem (JSSP). For JSSP, we evaluate a classic learning
1050
       model Zhang et al. (2020), which is based on a graph neural network. The datasets for this probing
       task are constructed as follows: we extract embeddings for all nodes, pair two node embeddings that
1051
       satisfy the precedence constraint with a label of 1 ([hi ,hj ]-1), and pair two node embeddings that
1052
       violate the constraint with a label of 0 ([hm ,hn ]-0). As an ablation, we also construct an alternative
1053   dataset where pairs that satisfy the precedence constraint are incorrectly labeled as 0: [hi ,hj ]-1,
1054   [hn ,hm ]-0.
1055
1056
       The results show that for the correct dataset, the probing model achieves an AUC of 1, while for
       the ablation dataset, the AUC is 0.5. This indicates that the NCO model effectively captures the
1057
       precedence constraint information between nodes in its embeddings. Here, we provide an initial
1058
       demonstration of how probing can explore the NCO model’s perception of constraints in the JSSP.
1059   In the future, more sophisticated probing tasks can be designed to further analyze how the NCO
1060   model perceives constraints and incorporates them into its decision-making process, thereby offering
1061   deeper insights into the design of NCO models.
