010
                        User prompts for generative AI models are often underspecified or open-ended,
011
                        which may lead to sub-optimal responses. This prompt underspecification problem
012                     is particularly evident in text-to-image (T2I) generation, where users commonly
013                     struggle to articulate their precise intent. This disconnect between the user’s vision
014                     and the model’s interpretation often forces users to painstakingly and repeatedly
015                     refine their prompts. To address this, we propose a design for proactive T2I agents
016                     equipped with an interface to actively ask clarification questions when uncertain,
017                     and present their understanding of user intent as an interpretable belief graph
018                     that a user can edit. We build simple prototypes for such agents and verify their
019                     effectiveness through both human studies and automated evaluation. We observed
020
                        that at least 90% of human subjects found these agents and their belief graphs
                        helpful for their T2I workflow. Moreover, we use a scalable automated evaluation
021
                        approach using two agents, one with a ground truth image and the other tries to
022
                        ask as few questions as possible to align with the ground truth. On DesignBench, a
023                     benchmark we created for artists and designers, the COCO dataset (Lin et al., 2014)
024                     and ImageInWords (Garg et al., 2024), we observed that these T2I agents were able
025                     to ask informative questions and elicit crucial information to achieve successful
026                     alignment with at least 2 times higher VQAScore (Lin et al., 2024) than the standard
027                     single-turn T2I generation. Demo: https://youtu.be/HPgJ4xPRnto

      1    I NTRODUCTION
055
056   A fundamental challenge in the development of AI agents is how to foster effective and efficient multi-
057   turn communication and collaboration with human users to achieve user-defined goals, especially
058   when faced with the common issue of vague or incomplete instructions from humans. We focus
059   specifically on text-to-image (T2I) generation, where recent advancements (Baldridge et al., 2024;
060   Betker et al., 2023; Podell et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023) have enabled the creation of stunning images
061   from complex text descriptions. However, users often struggle to describe the image they would like
062   to generate in a way that T2I systems can fully understand. This leads to unsatisfactory results and
063
      repeated iterations of prompts.
064   The prompt underspecification problem arises from the inherent ambiguity of natural language, the
065   different assumptions that humans make and the vast space of potential images that can be generated
066   from a single prompt (Hutchinson et al., 2022). Imagine a prompt generate an image of a rabbit next
067   to a cat. This seemingly simple prompt leaves many important aspects underspecified: What kind of
068   rabbit? What color is the cat? What is their relative positions? What is the background? While a
069
      T2I model can generate an image with a rabbit and a cat in it, it is unlikely that the image captures
      the specific details a specific user has in mind. For example, people in Holland might assume it is
070
      common for rabbits to have lop ears, but people in New England might expect to see cottontail rabbit
071
      with straight ears. The combination of all these factors can lead to a frustrating cycle of trial-and-error,
072   with the user repeatedly refining their prompt in an attempt to steer the model towards the desired
073   output (Vodrahalli & Zou, 2024; Huang et al., 2024; Sun & Guo, 2023).
074
075
      Instead of relying on passive T2I models that simply generate images based on potentially vague
      user instructions, we pursue a quest for agency in T2I generation. The T2I agents should actively
076
      engage with human users to provide a collaborative and interactive experience for image creation. We
077
      envision that these T2I agents will be able to (1) express and visualize their beliefs and uncertainty
078   about user intents, (2) allow human users to directly control their beliefs beyond just text descriptions,
079   and (3) proactively seek clarification from the human user to iteratively align their understanding
080   with what the human user intends to generate.
081
      In this work, we develop simple prototypes of such agents. At the core of those agent prototypes, we
082
      build in a graph-based symbolic belief state, named belief graph, for agents to understand its own
083
      uncertainty about possible entities (e.g., rabbit) that might appear in the image, attributes of entities
084   (e.g., rabbit’s color), relations between entities and so on. Given a user prompt, we use an LLM
085   and constrain its generation to the graph structure of beliefs, which include probability estimates on
086   the appearance of entities and the possible values for attributes and relations. Figure 1 illustrates
087   the interface and features of the prototypes. In particular, the agent can ask questions based on its
088   uncertainty. For example, a very simple strategy is to find the most uncertain attribute of an entity
089   (e.g., rabbit’s color) and use an LLM to phrase a question about the attribute (e.g., What is the color
090   of the rabbit?). The agent can also guide users to directly edit items in the graph.
091   To evaluate the utility of our agent prototypes, we conduct both human studies and automatic evalua-
092   tions. The human studies aim to understand how helpful simple T2I agents can be, and evaluate how
093   good the agents’ questions are. We develop automatic evaluation pipelines to assess the effectiveness
094   and efficiency of the T2I agents when interacting with simulated users with underspecified prompts
095   answering questions based on their pre-fixed intents.
096   We found that over 90% human subjects expect proactive clarifications to be helpful, and 58% think
097   this question asking feature of agents could deliver value to their work very soon, or immediately. We
098   create a hand-curated benchmark called DesignBench which contains aesthetic scenes with multiple
099   entities and interactions between entities; it also contains both a short and long caption. DesignBench
100   also features diversity between photo-realism, animation and multiple styles allowing a robust testing
101   with the use case of artists and designers in mind. This benchmark will be released with this paper.
102   We run automatic evaluations on both the COCO dataset (Lin et al., 2014) and DesignBench. We
103   found that our agents can achieve at least 2 times higher VQAScore (Lin et al., 2024) than the
104
      traditional single-turn T2I generation within 5 turns of interaction.
105   Our contributions: (1) the first interpretable and controllable belief graph used for T2I, (2) novel
106   design and prototypes for T2I agents that adaptively ask clarification questions and present belief
107   graphs; (3) a new automatic evaluation pipeline with simulated users to assess question-asking skills
      of T2I agents; and (4) DesignBench: a new T2I agent benchmark. Appendix A details the novelty.

      2    R ELATED WORK
109
110   From the very outset of artificial intelligence, a core challenge has been to develop intelligent agents
111   capable of representing knowledge and taking actions to acquire knowledge necessary for achieving
112   their goals (McCarthy & Hayes, 1969; Minsky, 1974; Moore, 1985; Nilsson, 2009; Russell & Norvig,
113   2016). Our work is an attempt to address this challenge for intelligent T2I agents.
114
      In machine learning and statistics, efficient data acquisition has been extensively studied for
115
      many problems, including active learning (Settles, 2009), Bayesian optimization (Garnett, 2023),
116   reinforcement learning (Kaelbling et al., 1996; Sutton, 2018) and experimental design (Chaloner &
117   Verdinelli, 1995; Kirk, 2009). We reckon that T2I agents should also be capable of actively seeking
118   important information from human users to quickly reduce uncertainty (Wang et al., 2024b) and
119   generate satisfying images. In §D, we detail the implementation of action selection strategies for our
120   T2I agents.
121
      In human-computer interaction, researchers have been extensively studying how to best enable
122   Human-AI interaction especially from user experience perspectives (Norman, 1994; Höök, 2000;
123   Amershi et al., 2019; Cai et al., 2019; Viégas & Wattenberg, 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Yang et al.,
124   2020; Kim et al., 2023). Interface design for AI is becoming increasingly challenging due to the
125   lack of transparency (Viégas & Wattenberg, 2023; Chen et al., 2024), uncertainty about AI capability
126   and complex outputs (Yang et al., 2020). We aim to build user-friendly agents, and an indispensable
127   component is their interface to enable them to effectively act and observe, as detailed in §G.
128   Interpretebaility. Surfacing an agent’s belief overlaps with interpretability as both aim to understand
129   model or agent’s internal. Some methods leverage LLM’s natural language interface to surface their
130   reasoning (e.g., chain of thought (Wei et al., 2023a)), sometime interactively (Wang et al., 2024a).
131   While these approaches makes accessible explanations, whether the explanations represents truth has
132   been questioned (Lanham et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2023). Some studies indicate
133   explanations generated by the LLMs may not entail the models’ predictions nor be factually grounded
134   in the input, even on simple tasks with extractive explanations (Ye & Durrett, 2022).
135   Text-to-Image (T2I) generation. Text-to-image prompts can be ambiguous, subjective (Hutchinson
136   et al., 2022), or challenging to represent visually (Wiles et al., 2024). Different users often have distinct
137   requirements for image generation, including personalization (Wei et al., 2024), style constraints
138   (Wang et al., 2023), and individual interpretations (Yin et al., 2019). To create images that better align
139   with users’ specific needs and interpretations, it is essential to actively communicate and interact with
140   the user to understand the user’s intent.
141   Multi-turn T2I. Current multi-turn T2I systems typically focus on multi-turn user instructions.
142   Huang et al. (2024); Sun & Guo (2023) propose multi-modal interactive dialogue systems which
143   passively respond to user’s natural language instructions. Mini DALL·E 3 (Lai et al., 2023) builds an
144   interactive T2I framework with an LLM in the loop to have a dialogue with the user via text chat and
145   improve image generation and editing based on the entire conversation. Vodrahalli & Zou (2024)
146   collected and analyzed a dataset of human-AI interactions where users iteratively refine prompts for
147   T2I models to generate images similar to goal images (goal images are only visible to users). This
148
      may require users to actively try prompts to understand model behaviors. On the contrary, our work
      aims to reduce the burden on the user by actively asking questions to understand user intents.
149
150   A core challenge in multi-turn T2I is consistency (Cheng et al., 2024a;b; Zeqiang et al., 2023).
151   Hu et al. (2024) introduce Instruct-Imagen, which is a model that follows complex multi-modal
152   instructions. AudioStudio (Cheng et al., 2024a) is a multi-turn T2I framework aimed at subject
153   consistencies while generating diverse and coherent images. These consistency improvement methods
154
      can be integrated into T2I agents but it is beyond the scope of this work. Our key focus is on the
      sequential decision making capability of agents to elicit user intents.

158
159   The belief graph in our work is closely related to symbolic world representations.
160   World states. In classical AI, researchers use symbolic representations to describe the world
161   state (McCarthy & Hayes, 1969; Minsky, 1974; 1988; Pasula et al., 2007; Kaelbling & Lozano-Pérez,
      2011). For example, in the blocks world (Ginsberg & Smith, 1988; Gupta & Nau, 1992; Alkhazraji

162
      et al., 2020), a state can be
163
164              is block(a) ∧ is red(a) ∧ on table(a) ∧ is block(b) ∧ is blue(b) ∧ on(b, a),
165
      describing that there are a red block and a blue block, referred to as a and b, block a is on a table, and
166
      block b is on a. Such world states must include entities (e.g., a and b), their attributes (e.g., position
167   on table, characteristics is block) and relations (e.g., on(b, a)) which are critical for enabling a
168   robot to know and act in the world.
169
170
      In linguistics, Davidson (1965; 1967b;a) introduce logic-based formalisms of meanings of sentences.
      The semantics of a sentence is decomposed to a set of atomic propositions, such that no propositions
171
      can be added or removed from the set to represent the meaning of the sentence. (Cho et al., 2023)
172
      propose Davidsonian Scene Graph (DSG) which represent an image description as a set of atomic
173   propositions (and corresponding questions about each proposition) to evaluate T2I alignment.
174
175   We borrow the same concept as symbolic world representations and scene graphs, except that the
176
      agent needs to represent an imaginary world. The image generation problem can be viewed as taking
      a picture of the imaginary world. The world state should include all entities that are in the picture,
177
      together with their attributes and relations.
178
179   Belief states. Term “belief state” (Nilsson, 1986; Kaelbling et al., 1998) has been used to de-
180
      scribe a distribution over states. E.g., for block a, we might have p(on table(a)) = 0.5 and
      p(¬on table(a)) = 0.5, which means the agent is unsure whether the block is on a table. To repre-
181
      sent the T2I agent’s belief on which image to generate, we need to consider the distribution over
182
      all possible “worlds” in which the picture can be taken. This distribution can be described by the
183   probabilities that an entity appears in the picture, an attribute gets assigned a certain value, etc.

186   4     P ROACTIVE T2I AGENT DESIGN
187
188   We provide high-level principles and design that guide our agent how to behave and interact with
189   users to generate desired images from text through multi-turn interactions. The goal of the agent is to
190
      generate images that match the user’s intended image as closely as possible with minimal back-and-
      forth, particularly in cases with underspecified prompts and the agent needs to gather information
191
      proactively. This requires a decision strategy on information gathering to trade off between the cost of
192
      interactions and the quality of generated images. The formal problem definition can be found in §B.
193
194   We equip the agent with the ability to gather information in two ways: ask clarification questions
195   (§4.1) and express its uncertainty and understanding in a way that users can edit (§4.2). Once a piece
196
      of information is collected from a user, the agent also need to update its questions and uncertainty
      (§4.3). To enable all these agent behaviors, we need to situate the agent in an interface to effectively
197
      communicate with users (§G). In the following, we introduce the design of the above components
198
      under the interface, to ensure information efficiency for T2I generation.
199
200   4.1     W HAT KIND OF QUESTIONS SHOULD BE ASKED ?
201
      We explain considerations in question asking and examples of strategies in this section.
202
203   4.1.1    P RINCIPLES
204
205   We identify the following principles for an agent to ask the user questions about the underspecified
206
      prompt and their intended image: (i) Relevance: The question should be based on the user prompt.
      (ii) Uncertainty Reduction: The question should aim to reduce the agent’s uncertainty about the
207
      attributes and contents of the image, the objects, the spatial layout, and the style. (iii) Easy-to-Answer:
208
      The question should be as concise and direct as possible to ensure it is not too difficult for the user to
209   answer. (iv) No Redundancy: The question should not collect information present in the history of
210   interactions with the user. The Relevance and No Redundancy principles are self-explanatory, we
211   detail the other two principles below.
212
      The Uncertainty Reduction principle aims to let agent elicit information about various characteris-
213
      tics of the desired image, which the agent is unsure of.
214
215   First, the agent needs to know what characteristics of images are important. Some examples include:
      (i) Attributes of the subjects, such as breed, size, or color, with questions like What kind of rabbit?


                                                          4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      What color is the cat?; (ii) Spatial relationships between the subjects, such as proximity and relative
217   position (Are the rabbit and cat close to each other? Are they facing each other?); (iii) Background
218   information, such as location, style and time of day (Are they in a park or at home?); and (iv) Implicit
219   entities that might not be explicitly mentioned in the initial prompt but are relevant to the user’s vision
220   (Are there any other animals or people present?).
221
      Second, the agent needs to know its own uncertainty about those characteristics. In the agent’s belief,
222
      the uncertainty is explicit. One strategy is to form questions about the image characteristics that the
223   agent is most uncertain about. We discuss more in §D.2.
224
225   Third, the agent needs to update its own uncertainty once the user gives a response to its question
226
      (a.k.a. transition in §4.3). Then, it can construct questions again based on its updated uncertainty
      estimates. This iterative clarification process allows the agent to progressively refine its understanding
227
      of the user’s intent and generate an image that more accurately reflects their desired output.
228
229   The Easy-to-Answer principle aims to reduce users’ effort to respond to questions. One way is
230   to have the agent provide some answer options, where options are what the agent believes likely to
231   appear. E.g., What color is the cat? (a) Black (b) Brown (c) Orange (d) Other (please specify).
232   4.1.2 E XAMPLES OF QUESTION - ASKING STRATEGIES
233
      Given the agent belief constructed from the user prompt (more details in §4.2), several basic ap-
234
      proaches can be employed following the above principles. We construct simple agents with the
235   following strategies, which are implemented and used in our experiments.
236
237     • Ag1 (§D.5): Rule-based question generation, which leverages predefined rules or heuristics to
238
      identify salient attributes, entities, or relationships that require clarification. For example, an LLM
      could be used to estimate the importance and likelihood of different components within the belief,
239
      and a heuristic could be applied to prioritize the most crucial elements for questioning.
240
        • Ag2 (§D.6): Belief-guided question generation, which involves using natural language to
241   represent the current understanding encapsulated in the belief. This representation, along with the
242   conversation history, is provided as input to an LLM, guiding it to generate clarification questions.
243     • Ag3 (§D.7): Direct question generation, which write the above question-asking principles in a
244   prompt for an LLM to generate a question.
245

281   4.3     T RANSITION
282   The agent belief undergoes a transition whenever the agent receives new information through user
283   feedback, either user answers from the agent question or user interactions with the graph-based
284   belief interface (Figure 1). This transition process integrates information from the initial user prompt,
285   the conversation history, interaction and the previous belief to generate an updated belief of the
286   user’s desired image. Two possible approaches include: (i) Generate a comprehensive prompt that
287   summarizes all interactions and information gathered thus far. This merged prompt is then used to
288
      re-generate the belief, effectively incorporating the new information into a refreshed representation.
      (ii) Leverage natural language to describe the accumulated information, including the initial prompt,
289
      conversation history, and user interactions. This descriptive summary is then provided as input to an
290
      LLM, instructing it to generate an updated belief based on the provided context. We use (i) for all
291   agents in §4.1.2 and the implementation details can be found in §D.3.

300

301
      conversation starts with an arbitrarily chosen image to represent the goal image from a T2I model
302   that the user has in mind2 . Along with this ground truth image, a user has a detailed prompt in mind

304   simulate the user, where the questions are answered based on the ground truth prompt and the belief
305   graph generated from the ground truth prompt. We run the agent-user conversation for a total of 15
306   turns3 and compute different metrics at the end of each turn. More details of the simulated user can
307   be found in the appendix, including the prompts provided to the LLM when simulating the user are
308   provided. Figure 2 part b shows the multi-turn set up that we use in our results.

      Our multi-turn agents aim to facilitate the generation of complex images, a process that often requires

      multiple subjects, interactions, backgrounds, and styles. Each dataset consists of tuples: (I, p0 , c, bgt ),
358   where I represents the target image, p0 is an initial (basic) prompt describing only the primary
359   elements of the scene, c is a ground truth caption providing a detailed description of I, including
360   spatial layout, background elements, and style, and bgt is the ground truth belief graph constructed
361   via parsing c. The initial prompt p0 is intentionally less detailed than c to necessitate multi-turn
362   refinement. This framework allows us to assess the agent’s ability to guide the user towards the target
363   image I starting from a simplified prompt.
364   Existing image-caption datasets primarily focus on simple scenes (Deng et al., 2009; Krizhevsky
365   et al., 2009; Deng, 2012) or focus on very specific categories (Liu et al., 2016; Liao et al., 2022). With
366   the aim for complex realistic images for testing the robustness of the Agents, we evaluate over the
367   validation split of the Coco-Captions dataset (Chen et al., 2015). Five independent human generated
368   captions are provided for each image in the dataset. These captions are often short and describe the
369   basic elements contained in the image and the interactions between objects or persons in the image.
370   We therefore select the shortest of the five human-generated captions and use this as a starting prompt
371   p0 . We then use Gemini 1.5 Pro to expand the starting prompt by adding more details of the attributes
372
      of the entities in the image as well as the style and image composition which results in the ground
      truth caption. We also use the ImageInWords (Garg et al., 2024) dataset which takes a diverse set of
373
      realstic and cartoon images and has human annotators create dense detailed captions that describe
374
      attribute and relationships between objects in the image. In ImageInWords evaluations we use the
375   long human annotation as the ground truth caption.

387   5.1.3    M ETRICS
388
      The outputs produced by the agent include a final generated image, a final caption and a final belief
389   graph. We evaluate the agents across these modalities and evaluate their alignment to the ground
390   truth image I, c and bgt , using the following metrics.
391
392
      Text-Text Similarity: We use 2 metrics for comparing the ground truth caption and the generated
      caption: 1) T2T – embedding-similarity computed using Gemini 1.5 Pro5 and 2) DSG (Cho et al.,
393
      2024) adapted to parse text prompts into Davidsonian scene graph using the released code.
394
395   Image-Image Similarity (I2I): We compute cosine similarity between the groundtruth image and the
396   generated image from the agent prompt. We use image features from DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2024)
397   model following prior works.
398   Text-Image Similarity: We compare the ground truth prompt with the generated image (T2I) using
399   the VQAScore (Lin et al., 2024) metric. We use the author released implementation of the metric and
400   use Gemini 1.5 Pro as the underlying MLLM. More details about the T2I metrics can be found in §E.
401
      Negative log likelihood (NLL): We construct the ground truth state of the image in the form of a
402   belief graph but with no uncertainty. We then approximately compute the NLL of the ground truth
403   state given the belief of the agent at each turn, by assuming the independence of all entities, attributes
404   and relations, and summing their log probabilities6 .

501   5.4    H UMAN S TUDY
502
503   In order to get real user feedback, we performed a human survey with the objective of understanding
504
      user frustrations to validate whether our potential solutions could help with their use of T2I models.
      We gathered data from 143 participants who all identified to be regular T2I users (at least once a
505
      month). Participants were presented with four hypothesized frustrations (prompt misinterpretation,
506
      many iterations, inconsistent generations, incorrect assumptions) and three potential mitigating
507   features (clarifications, entity graph, relationship graph; more details in Appendix §H).
508
509   As reported in Table 4 (in Appendix), the results confirmed the prevalence of hypothesized frustrations
510
      amongst users, with 83% experiencing occasional, frequent, or very frequent frustration due to
      prompt iterations, followed by 70% for misinterpretations, 71% for inconsistent generations, and 60%
511
      experiencing frustration due to incorrect assumptions. Most acutely 55% of participants reported
512
      frequent or very frequent frustration due to the prompt iteration frequency necessary. In Table 2, we
513   report the mitigation features that are likely to help. Clarifications reported the highest likelihood
514   to help current workflows (91% could / likely / very likely to be helpful), followed by entity graphs
515   (88% could / likely / very likely to be helpful) and relationship graphs (86% could / likely / very
516   likely to be helpful). Clarifications were expected to deliver value immediately / very soon by 58%.
517
      Overall these suggest strong user desire for & likelihood for success of features that reduce iterations
518
      and mitigate misinterpretations in T2I generation. Full explanations of the hypothesized frustrations,
519   mitigation and responses splits are in §H. All respondents were compensated for their time as per
520   market rates, and were recruited by our vendor to ensure diversity across age, gender, and T2I usage
521   in terms of models, frequency and purpose (work and non work).

524   6     D ISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION
525
526   This work introduces a design for agents that assist users in generating images through an interactive
527   process of question-asking and belief graph refinement. By dynamically updating its understanding of
528   the user’s intent, the agent facilitates a more collaborative and precise approach to image generation.
529   Modular design. Our agent prototypes are highly modular: the agents use frozen T2I models to
530   generate images based on the prompts that the agent updated. Therefore when a better off-the-shelf
531   T2I model becomes available, it can be directly plugged into the agents and the system will achieve
532   better performance without any additional adaptation7 .
533   Future work. Alternative to the modular design, one can explore generating images directly from
534   belief graphs and fine-tuning LLM/VLMs on text/image trajectories that include asking questions.
535   These may require a) collecting data such as gold-standard trajectories or annotations on the quality of
536   trajectories of human-agent conversations and b) new approaches to fine-tune the model on multi-turn
537   trajectories of images and text, which can potentially improve the performance of the agent.

      E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542   Our proposed T2I agents are equipped with better tools (belief graphs) for interpretability and
543   controllability. Presenting the agent’s belief graph can be a generalizable method for AI transparancy,
544   which is an important factor given the increasing complexity of modern AI models.
545   By asking clarification questions, our proposed agents may enable a more customizable and person-
546   alized content creation experience. Because different groups of people may perceive harmfulness
547   of contents differently, learning more about the user through clarification questions can potentially
548   mitigate risks of generating contents that can be offensive to each specific user.

      A    N OVELTY AND CONTRIBUTIONS
865
866
      In this section, we emphasize the novelty and contributions of this work.
867
868        1. System design of proactive T2I agents:
869              • Novel human-agent interaction modalities: Prior to our work, human users typically
870                interact with current T2I systems by giving additional instructions or refining the
871                prompt. To the best of our knowledge, our work is the first to propose a proactive T2I
872                agent system that is able to ask clarification questions and present its belief graph for
873                the user to edit.
874              • Novel human-agent interaction interface: We designed a new interface to best enable
875                the clarification and belief graph interaction modalities. We have not seen these features
876                in any T2I, or other generative media apps that are publicly live to date, signifying to
877
                   us total uniqueness. Our human studies showed that at least 85
                 • Novel design of different T2I agents that enable the proposed interaction modalities.
878
                   Please see Section 4 of the paper for the full details of the design principles and
879
                   construction of those T2I agent prototypes (Ag1, Ag2, Ag3).
880
881
           2. Our belief graph significantly differs from the classic belief state in the following ways:
882              • Hardcoded predicates v.s. Automatically-generated predicates: Traditionally, con-
883
                   structing classic symbolic belief states requires a pre-defined set of predicates such
                   as “on(a, b)”, “is red(a)”, “at position(robot, x, y, z)” and it is non-trivial to learn new
884
                   predicates that can be used and generalized to new tasks (Pasula et al., 2007; Xia et al.,
885
                   2019). Typically the pre-defined set of predicates are written by system developers and
886                hardcoded into classic AI systems (Fikes & Nilsson, 1971).
887              • Our belief graph does not require any pre-defined set of predicates. Instead, we propose
888                to construct symbolic beliefs using a sequential in-context learning (ICL) method with
889                LLMs. This method first generates a list of entities together with their descriptions
890                conditioned on the user description of image; then, we add each entity to the context,
891                and let the LLM generate a list of attributes and values (this step is done in parallel
892                across entities); and finally, we add all entities to the context and let the LLM generate
893                relations and their attributes. Our method can be generalized across a wide range
894
                   of T2I tasks and achieve high performance (see our comprehensive results on Coco,
                   Imageinwords, DesignBench). We have included a pseudo code for this method in the
895
                   paper.
896
                 • Application to planning v.s. T2I: To the best of our knowledge, classic symbolic belief
897                states are mostly used for robot planning, and we are the first to use symbolic beliefs
898                to assist T2I tasks. Data structure for symbolic states / belief states – Set v.s. Graph:
899                Because of the application to planning, a symbolic world state is usually implemented
900                and stored as a set or list of literals (atoms or negation of atoms where atoms are
901                instantiated predicates (Alkhazraji et al., 2020; Garrett et al., 2020a;b) so that whenever
902                an action is applied, the agent can apply transition by adding and deleting items in the
903                set according to the precondition and effect of the action.
904              • For T2I tasks, it is more convenient to use a graph to represent the world state associated
905
                   with an image, since entities and relations naturally form a set of nodes (entities)
                   and edges (relations between entities). Each component of the graph can also have
906
                   probabilities, making it easy to turn a world state into a belief state using the same data
907
                   structure. Hence we represent T2I agent beliefs using graphs. The agent can directly
908                update the graph for each transition instead of going through a set or list.
909              • Interpretability and controllability: The graph structure makes our agent belief more
910                interpretable than traditional belief states, since we can visualize and progressively
911                disclose the graph to the human user. Moreover, each node or relation in the belief graph
912                has associated descriptions, making it easy for the user to understand and potentially
913                edit every component of the belief graph. In our human studies, about 85% of raters
914                found the belief graph useful. To the best of our knowledge, our work is the first to use
915                the graph-based belief state for human-AI interaction.
916        3. Automated evaluation of T2I agents: We propose a novel automated evaluation approach for
917           T2I agents using self-play. The agent interacts with a simulated user that has access to the
              original image and its long caption. See Section 5.1 (and C.4) for the full details of how the


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
               simulated user is constructed. This evaluation pipeline is easy to use and can help the future
919            development of T2I agents.
920
            4. DesignBench: We envision that a significant fraction of T2I users are artists and designers,
921
               and it is important to ensure that T2I agents are evaluated for these use cases. Hence we
922
               create DesignBench, featuring photo-realism, animation and multiple styles with short
923            and long captions. DesignBench can be directly plugged into our automated evaluation to
924            streamline the evaluation process.

      B    F ORMALISM OF THE AGENT AND ITS OBJECTIVE
928
929
      We define an interactive T2I agent as a ⟨B, A, O, τ, π⟩ tuple, where we have
930          • S: a representation space of images,
931          • B: a space of agent beliefs,
932          • A: a space of actions that the agent can take,
933          • O: a space of agent observations of the user,
934          • transition function τ : B × A × O 7→ B for updating beliefs given new interactions,
935          • action selection strategy π : B 7→ A, which specifies which action to take given a belief.
936   For each user-initiated interaction, we assume that there exists a specific intent s ∈ S, where S is
937   the space of all possible user intents. For a T2I task, we assume that the intent is the image the user
938   would like to generate, and the intent stays the same throughout the interaction with an agent. We
939   discuss more about the validity of this assumption in §6.
940
      Each type of T2I agents can have a unique user intent representation, belief representation, construc-
941   tion of the action space, and user interface design to obtain observations of users.
942
943   In §4, we show the examples for these components.
944   We use a score function, f : B × S 7→ R, to evaluate the alignment between an agent belief and a
945   user intent at any turn of the interaction. Function f can only be evaluated in hindsight once the user
946   intent is revealed. The agent does not have direct access to function f since the user intent is hidden
947   from the agent. However, the agent may construct a probabilistic distribution over function f based
948   on its belief about the user intent. The goal of the agent is to maximize function f with as few turns
949   of interaction with the user as possible.

       D.1    I MPLEMENTATION OF S TATE
1243
1244   Our agent’s state is represented in two complementary forms: (i) Merged prompt: This is a natural
1245   language representation that summarizes the entire conversation history up to the current turn. It
1246   provides a comprehensive textual overview of the user’s requests, feedback, and any clarifications
1247   exchanged with the agent. (ii) Belief: This is a symbolic representation derived from the merged
1248   prompt. It parses the natural language text into a structured format, capturing key elements like
1249   entities, attributes, relationships, and associated probabilities. This structured representation facilitates
1250
       more precise reasoning and decision-making by the agent.
1251   Prompt Merging. An LLM (§D.11) summarizes the latest interaction, encapsulating the agent’s
1252
       question and the user’s response into a concise textual representation. This step distills the essential
       information exchanged during the interaction. Another LLM (§D.12) merges the summarized
1253
       interaction with the existing merged prompt, which contains the accumulated information from
1254
       previous interactions. This creates an updated prompt that reflects the evolving understanding of the
1255   user’s intent.
1256
       Belief Parsing. See an example of the belief state fig. 9. We employ three specialized parsers
1257
       trained via in-context learning (ICL): entity parser (§D.8) analyzes the user prompt to identify and
1258   extract a list of relevant entities.; attribute parser (§D.9) takes user prompt and an entity as the input to
1259   extract a list of attributes associated with that entity; relation parser (§D.10) takes the user prompt and
1260   a list of entities as input and identifies relationships between those entities. Each entity is associated
1261   with meta information like name, importance to ask score, description, probability of appearing, a list
1262   of attributes like color, position, etc 8 . Each attribute contains meta information like name, importance
1263   to ask score, a list of possible values for the attribute along with their associated probabilities, etc.
1264   Each relation includes meta information such as: name, description, spatial relation, importance to
1265   ask score, entity 1 and entity 2, whether the relation is bidirectional, etc.

1356   D.2    I MPLEMENTATION OF ACTION
1357
1358   From an information theoretic perspective, an optimal action is the one that maximizes the information
1359   gain between the observation and the belief, i.e. at = arg maxa H(oi−1 ; bi−1 | a) − H(oi ; bi | a).
1360   However, directly optimizing this objective can be computationally challenging. Therefore, we
1361   explore several heuristic strategies to effectively reduce uncertainty:
1362          • Maximize the overall heuristic importance score (M HIS):This strategy focuses on max-
1363            imizing the overall importance score of the entities, attributes, and relations within the
1364            belief. We further ask a question regarding an attribute or relation by maximizing the overall
1365            heuristic importance score. The score can be modeled as:
1366
1367                        maxe,a,c,r (IS(e) ∗ IS(a) ∗ P (e) ∗ Ent(c), IS(r) ∗ P (r) ∗ Ent(c))                 (1)
1368            Here IS, P, Ent represents importance to ask score, probability of appearing, and entropy of
1369            the probabilities respectively and e, a, c, r represents entity, attribute, candidate list, relation
1370            respectively.
1371          • Ask Important Clarification Question based on belief (AICQB ): This strategy leverages
1372            the structured information within the belief. We provide the LLM with the user prompt, con-
1373            versation history, and the current belief, utilizing an ICL prompt (§D.14) to guide question
1374
                generation. The LLM then formulates a clarification question aimed at eliciting information
                about key features of the image, naturally prioritizing those with higher Importance to ask
1375
                score within the belief.
1376
              • Ask Important Clarification Question directly (AICQbase ): This strategy relies on the
1377            LLM’s inherent ability to identify important aspects of the user prompt and conversation
1378            history. The LLM (§D.13) generates an important clarification question based on its implicit
1379            understanding of the user’s needs, without explicitly relying on the structured information
1380            in the belief.
1381
       Ag1 employs M HIS strategy for question generation. This strategy leverages the importance scores
1382
       assigned to entities, attributes, and relations within the belief state. It identifies the element with the
1383   highest heuristic importance score and formulates a question aimed at eliciting further information
1384   about that specific element. The question is then verbalized using the LLM described in Section
1385   §D.15.
1386
       Ag2 utilizes the parsed belief state as the basis for question generation. It employs the AICQB
1387
       strategy, which leverages the structured information within the belief state to generate targeted
1388
       clarification questions.
1389
1390   Ag3 relies solely on the conversation history for question generation. It employs the AICQbase
1391   strategy, which leverages the LLM’s ability to understand the ongoing dialogue and identify key areas
1392
       requiring further clarification.

1395
       Belief Updating. Both Ag1 and Ag2 require belief updating to incorporate new information gained
1396
       during the interaction. At each turn, we perform prompt merging to create a comprehensive prompt
1397
       that summarizes the conversation history. This merged prompt is then used for belief parsing to
1398   obtain an updated belief state. For Ag2, this updated belief state directly informs the subsequent
1399   interaction. For Ag1, it incorporates additional post-processing mechanisms to enhance memory and
1400   prevent redundant questioning: (i) Redundancy elimination: If an attribute or relation has already
1401   been addressed in the conversation history, the corresponding user response is assigned as the sole
1402   candidate with a probability of 1.0, and its importance score is set to 0. This prevents the agent from
1403   repeatedly asking about the same information. (ii) Information retention: If an attribute or relation
       from the conversation history is absent in the updated belief state, it is explicitly added. This ensures

1409
1410   To simulate end-to-end agent-user interactions, we implement a user simulator that mimics human
1411   question-answering behavior. This simulator operates as follows:
1412          • It generates a belief state based on a ground truth prompt, representing the user’s intended
1413            image. This serves as the simulator’s internal representation of the desired image.
1414          • Mirroring the AICQB strategy, the simulator takes the ground truth prompt, conversation
1415            history, and its current belief state as input. It then leverages an ICL prompt (see §D.14) to
1416            generate a response to the agent’s question. This ensures that the simulator’s answers are
1417            consistent with its internal belief state and the ongoing conversation.

1419   D.5    AG 1: H EURISTIC S CORE AGENT
1420
1421   The Heuristic score agent leverages the importance scores and probabilities within the belief state
1422   to guide its question-asking strategy. The underlying principle is to identify and inquire about the
1423   entity, attribute, or relation that exhibits both high importance and high uncertainty. This aligns with
1424   the uncertainty reduction principle discussed in §4.1.1, which emphasizes minimizing uncertainty
1425   through targeted questioning. To achieve this, we define a heuristic importance score as formulated
1426   in Equation 1, and the agent then selects the attribute or relation with the highest heuristic importance
1427
       score as the focus of its inquiry. To facilitate easy answering, we utilize an LLM to generate user-
       friendly questions with multiple-choice options. For example, the agent might ask: What color of
1428
       the rabbit do you have in mind? a. black , b. white, c. brown. d. unkown. If none of these options ,
1429
       what color of the rabbit do you have in mind?. This format allows users to simply select the most
1430   appropriate option or provide their own answer if needed.
1431
1432   Here’s a summary of Ag1’s implementation: (i) State Representation: The agent’s state comprises
1433
       the merged prompt and the current belief state. (ii) Select Action: M HIS strategy is employed to
       identify the attribute or relation of interest based on the heuristic importance score. (iii) Verbalize
1434
       Action: An LLM (§D.15) is used to generate a clear and concise question about the selected attribute
1435
       or relation. (iv) Answer Question: The user simulator provides an answer to the agent’s question,
1436   mimicking human response behavior. (v) Transition: The agent updates the merged prompt with
1437   the new information, re-generates the belief state based on the updated prompt, and applies the
1438   post-processing logic outlined in §D.3 to ensure consistency and prevent redundancy.

1442
1443
       The Ag2 agent incorporates the belief state into its decision-making process but adopts a different
       approach compared to Ag1. Instead of relying on a heuristic score, Ag2 leverages the full capacity
1444
       of an LLM to generate questions. It provides the LLM with comprehensive information, including
1445
       the merged prompt, belief state, and conversation history, allowing the LLM to formulate the most

1796   E    BACKGROUND
1797
1798   T2I Evaluation. Inception Score (Salimans et al., 2016) and Frechet Inception Distance (Heusel
1799   et al., 2018) are popular metrics to measure the fidelity of generated images, i.e. the similarity of
1800   the generated images to real ones. Improved precision and recall (Kynkäänniemi et al., 2019) allows
1801
       to analyse the sample quality and the coverage independently. Since text prompts are used to guide
       image generation in T2I models, image-prompt alignment is an important evaluation metric which can
1802
       be classified as embedding-based such as CLIPScore (Hessel et al., 2022), ALIGNScore (Zha et al.,
1803
       2023), VQA-based metrics such as TIFA (Hu et al., 2023), DSG (Cho et al., 2023) abd VQAScore
1804   (Lin et al., 2024) and captioning based metrics like LLMScore (Lu et al., 2023). Approaches such
1805   as PickScore (Kirstain et al., 2023), ImageReward (Xu et al., 2023) and HPS-v2 (Wu et al., 2023)
1806   finetune models on human ratings to devise a metric that aligns with human preferences. Recently,
1807   diversity of generated images (Naeem et al., 2020) is becoming an important metric of measurement
1808   to track progress, especially in the geo-cultural context (Kannen et al., 2024; Hall et al., 2024).
1809   Diversity can be used to quantify the under-specification in the input prompt: more specific the
1810   prompt, the less diverse are the generated set of images across different seeds (Kannen et al., 2024).
1811   Prompt expansion is a widely known technique to improve image generation (Betker et al., 2023).
1812   ImageinWords (Garg et al., 2024) proposes to obtain high-quality hyper-detailed captions for images,
1813   which significantly improve quality of image generation. Datta et al. (2024) present a generic prompt
1814   expansion framework used along Text-to-Image generation and show an increase in user satisfaction
1815   through human study. Our work can be viewed as a method to adaptively expand a T2I prompt based
1816   on user feedback. Samples from the agent belief can be used to construct expanded prompts.

2026         1. Clarifications: The model would ask specific clarifying questions about uncertainties in the
2027            prompt. These details would then be incorporated into subsequent iterations. For example:
2028            ”Is the cat playing with: 1. a ball of wool, or 2. a tennis ball?”
2029
2030         2. Graph of Prompt Entities: A visual representation of all entities in the prompt as a graph,
2031            allowing users to see and edit attributes of each entity. E.g., seeing that the model has
2032            assigned ”round,” ”small,” and ”wooden” as attributes to ”table” and allowing the user to
2033            change them to ”square” and ”metal.”
2034
2035
             3. Graph of Prompt Relationships: A visual representation of relationships between entities
2036            in the prompt, allowing users to see and edit these relationships. E.g., seeing that ”donut” is
2037            ”next to” ”coffee” and allowing the user to change the relationship to ”on top of.”
2038
