011                                                     A BSTRACT
012
013                Recent advancements in multimodal representation learning for electrocardiogram
014                (ECG) have moved onto learning representations by aligning ECG signals with
015                their paired free-text reports. However, current methods often result in suboptimal
016
                   alignment of ECG signals with their corresponding text reports, thereby limiting
                   diagnostic accuracy. This is primarily due to the complexity and unstructured
017
                   nature of medical language, which makes it challenging to effectively align ECG
018
                   signals with the corresponding text reports. Additionally, these methods are unable
019                to handle arbitrary combinations of ECG leads as inputs, which poses a challenge
020                since 12-lead ECGs may not always be available in under-resourced clinical envi-
021                ronments. In this work, we propose the Knowledge-enhanced Multimodal ECG
022                Representation Learning (K-MERL) framework to address these challenges. K-
023                MERL leverages large language models (LLMs) to extract structured knowledge
024                from free-text reports, enhancing the effectiveness of ECG multimodal learning.
025                Furthermore, we design a lead-aware ECG encoder to capture lead-specific spatial-
026                temporal characteristics of 12-lead ECGs, with dynamic lead masking. This novel
027
                   encoder allows our framework to handle arbitrary lead inputs, rather than being
                   limited to a fixed set of full 12 leads, which existing methods necessities. We evalu-
028
                   ate K-MERL on six external ECG datasets and demonstrate its superior capability.
029
                   K-MERL not only outperforms all existing methods in zero-shot classification
030                and linear probing tasks using 12 leads, but also achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA)
031                results in partial-lead settings, with an average improvement of 16% in AUC score
032                on zero-shot classification compared to previous SOTA multimodal methods1 .

035   1       I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Recent advancements in deep learning have enabled automated classification of cardiovascular disease
038   (CVD) using electrocardiograms (ECGs), one of the most crucial diagnostic tools. However, most
039   methods are supervised, requiring large amounts of annotated data, which is costly and demands
040   prohibitively extensive expert effort in annotation (Liu et al., 2023b; Huang & Yen, 2022). To
041
      address this challenge, self-supervised multimodal learning has recently emerged as an effective
      approach for learning representative ECG features from accompanied free-text clinical reports (Li
042
      et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; 2023g). To this end, MERL (Liu et al., 2024) recently introduced the first
043
      comprehensive benchmark using the largest dataset MIMIC-ECG (Gow et al.) for pretraining, and six
044   datasets (Wagner et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2018; Zheng et al., 2022; 2020) for evaluating downstream
045   task performance, including zero-shot classification and linear probing.
046
047
      Despite outperforming signal-only self-supervised approaches, multi-modal approaches, including
      MERL (Liu et al., 2024), still have notable drawbacks: They directly align ECG signals with reports,
048
      introducing unnecessary noise due to the free-text nature of the reports, and failing to fully exploit the
049
      rich cardiac knowledge contained within the text. Additionally, they encode ECG in a lead-agnostic
050   manner, overlooking the unique spatial and temporal characteristics of the individual 12 ECG leads.
051   Moreover, they require all 12 leads to be available as input, limiting their ability to generalize across
052   different lead combinations. This raises important practical concerns since full 12-lead ECG data is
053

055   for rapid assessments in emergencies, and limited resource in pre-hospital care environments (Bray
056   et al., 2021; Swor et al., 2006; Quinn et al., 2020; Nonogi et al., 2008; Kotelnik et al., 2021; Zhang &

      To overcome the challenges listed above, we make the following contributions: (1) We propose a
059

060
      which extracts cardiac-related entities from free-text ECG reports, converting unstructured reports
061   into structured knowledge to enhance self-supervised ECG multimodal learning. To the best of
062   our knowledge, this is the first work to leverage structured cardiac entities extracted from clinical
063   reports to improve ECG multimodal learning. (2) To effectively capture and leverage the lead-specific
064   spatial and temporal characteristics of 12-lead ECGs, we explore various tokenization and positional
065   embedding techniques. In particular, we design lead-specific tokenization and lead-specific spatial
066   positional embeddings, enabling the framework to capture the distinctiveness of each lead. (3) To
067   enable our framework to handle arbitrary combinations of input leads, we introduce a dynamic lead
068   masking strategy. In addition, we propose an independent segment masking strategy to further capture
069
      lead-specific temporal patterns. (4) Our K-MERL framework demonstrates superior performance
      in zero-shot classification and linear probing on multiple downstream datasets in various lead
070
      combinations, from a single lead to all 12 leads.

075   2.1   ECG R EPRESENTATION L EARNING .
076
      Recently, ECG self-supervised learning (eSSL) has shown promise in learning ECG representations
077
      from unannotated signals (Lai et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2020; Sangha et al., 2024). Contrastive
078   methods such as CLOCS (Kiyasseh et al., 2021) and ASTCL (Wang et al., 2023) explore temporal
079   and spatial invariance, while generative techniques (Zhang et al., 2022; Sawano et al., 2022; Na et al.,
080   2023; Jin et al., 2024) focus on masked segment reconstruction. However, both approaches often
081   lack clinical domain knowledge and are limited to single-modality settings, restricting the quality of
082   learned representations.
083
      Multimodal learning has shown success in multiple biomedical applications (Liu et al., 2023a;c;
084   Wan et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023f;e;d; Chen et al., 2023). However, ECG signals pose unique
085   challenges due to their complex spatial-temporal structure, necessitating well-tailored modeling.
086   As a result, few studies have explored multimodal ECG learning. (Lalam et al., 2023; Yu et al.,
087   2024) demonstrated the effectiveness of combining ECG and EHR data using large language models
088   (LLMs) to rewrite textual reports. However, their work is restricted to private datasets, making
089   reproducing and comparisons challenging. Other works such as (Li et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023g)
090   explored multimodal ECG learning for zero-shot classification. However, their methods were over
091   simplistic: They align signals with text without sufficiently capturing the distinctiveness of individual
092
      ECG leads, and rely on naive category names as prompts, which fail to capture relative patterns,
      leading to suboptimal performance. Their limited evaluations on small datasets also fall short of
093
      fully assessing multimodal ECG learning in real-world scenarios. Additionally, works such as (Zhao
094
      et al., 2024; Wan et al., 2024) focus on ECG-to-text generation tasks, but their results are not publicly
095   accessible, making reproducing and comparisons difficult.
096
097   MERL (Liu et al., 2024) is the first open-source study to demonstrate the potential of ECG multimodal
098
      learning in zero-shot classification and linear probing across diverse datasets. Therefore, we mainly
      compare our work to MERL. However, like other methods, MERL relies on all 12 ECG leads as
099
      input and cannot handle arbitrary lead combinations, limiting its applicability in real-world clinical
100
      scenarios where all 12 leads may not always be available (Jahrsdoerfer et al., 2005; Madias, 2003;
101   Fontana et al., 2019; Maheshwari et al., 2014)

136   2.3   C HALLENGE IN PARTIAL L EADS ECG I NPUT.
137
138
      Currently, full 12 leads ECG data dominates publicly accessible ECG datasets (Gow et al.; Ribeiro
      et al., 2020; Junior et al., 2023). However, in real clinical scenarios, obtaining a standard 12 leads
139
      ECG can be excessive and often requires advanced clinical knowledge, which may not always
140
      be readily available (Chamadiya et al., 2013; Alizadeh Meghrazi et al., 2020; Dai et al., 2016).
141   This makes partial-lead ECG data both crucial and common for practical applications. Despite
142   its importance, partial leads issue is often overlooked and remain unaddressed in existing ECG
143   multimodal representation learning studies. To handle partial lead inputs across various downstream
144   tasks, in this work, we design lead-specific processing and dynamic lead masking strategies that
145   enable our model to accept any combination of ECG leads as input. adaptable to various clinical
146   scenarios (Jahrsdoerfer et al., 2005; Madias, 2003; Fontana et al., 2019; Maheshwari et al., 2014). We
147   evaluate our model on extensive downstream tasks with partial lead inputs, demonstrating its ability
148   to recognize and adapt to the lead-specific nature of ECG signals.

239   Using a fixed number of masked leads limits the model’s flexibility in handling arbitrary lead inputs.
240

241

242
      of unmasked lead indices, denoted as ˆl, and mask the remaining leads. This approach ensures the
243
      model is exposed to diverse combinations of unmasked and masked leads during pretraining. The
244
245
      resulting ECG signal with the selected unmasked leads is denoted as el̂i .

250   unmasked lead signal el̂i , we randomly select masked token indices Hl̂ based on a masking proportion

      3.4   M INING C ARDIAC - RELATED E NTITIES FROM R EPORT
257
      In this section, we introduce the structured knowledge extraction process for handling free-text
258   ECG reports. The pipeline is illustrated in Fig 3. Since each ECG report provides descriptions of
259   cardiac-related entities, as shown in the leftmost part of Fig. 3, our goal is to extract all positive
260   cardiac-related entities mentioned in the report as structured knowledge to enhance the supervision
261   signals for ECG multimodal learning.
262
      Extracting Cardiac-related Entities. Unlike existing biomedical multimodal learning approaches
263
      from the radiology domain, which rely on knowledge graphs to extract structured knowledge
264   from reports (Zhang et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023), we directly query an LLM with the following
265   prompt:     ‘Please extract all positive Cardiac-related Entities from
266   the given ECG report. Output format is [Entity1, Entity2, ...]’.
267   There are two main reasons for this approach. First, there is no off-the-shelf knowledge graph
268   (KG) specifically focused on ECG, making it impractical to use KG-based methods for extracting
269   structured knowledge. Second, since we are only extracting existing terms from the free-text
      report, we can easily verify that the extracted cardiac-related entities are present and positive,


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      ensuring no non-existent terms are generated by the LLM. Moreover, (Zhang et al., 2023)
271   has already demonstrated that a general-purpose LLM can effectively extract existing medical
272   terms from free-text reports independently of any external knowledge database. To ensure
273   accuracy, after each extraction operation, we query the LLM with: ‘Please verify the
274   extracted cardiac-related entities as existing and positive in the
275   given report. Output format is YES or NO’, and only retain the cardiac-related
276   entities with a ‘YES’ response. After this stage, we obtain a total of 341 unique cardiac-related
277   entities in the whole dataset..
278   Merging Duplicated Cardiac-related Entities. After extracting all cardiac-related entities from
279   whole dataset, we observe that many names share the same semantics but are expressed differently, as
280   shown in the second part of Fig 3. This variation arises because different clinical protocols generate
281   ECG reports in different styles, even though they describe the same cardiac-related entities. To
282   address this, we query the LLM with: ‘Please merge the cardiac-related entities
283   that have the same semantics but different expressions. Here are
284   <all Cardiac-related Entities>. Output format is JSON, where the
285
      key is the original name and the value is the merged name.’ After this
      stage, we obtain a total of 252 unique cardiac-related entities in the whole dataset..
286
287   Aggregating Subtypes into Superclasses. Since cardiac-related entities are organized in a
288   clear hierarchical structure (Arnaout et al., 2016; Okshina et al., 2019), for example, as shown
289   in the rightmost part of Fig 3, ‘anterior myocardial infarction’ and ‘inferior
290   myocardial infarction’ are subtypes of the superclass ‘Myocardial infarction’
291
      (Brieger et al., 2000), we query the LLM with the following prompt: ‘Please detect
      all the superclasses present in <all Cardiac-related Entities>.
292
      Output format is JSON, where the key is the superclass name and
293
      the values are the cardiac-related entities that belong to this
294   superclass.’
295
296   After this stage, we identify 25 superclasses of cardiac-related entities. By the end of the process,
297
      we obtain a list of 277 unique cardiac-related entities for the entire dataset. The list of these entities
      is represented as Q = {q1 , q2 , . . . , qQ }, where Q = 277. For each ECG report ti , we create a label
298
      vector of length 277, where the positions corresponding to present and positive cardiac-related entity
299
      are set to 1, and all other positions are set to 0. This results in a binary label vector for each report,
300   which we denote as yi ∈ {0, 1}277 .

354
      et al., 2018) contains 6,877 12-lead ECG records, sampled at 500 Hz, annotated with 9 distinct labels.

358   datasets used for downstream tasks is presented in Tab 3.

360   4.2.1       I MPLEMENTATION .
361
      Zero-shot Classification. For zero-shot classification, we freeze the entire model and use the original
362
      category names from the dataset as entity queries Q for input to the cardiac query network, FCQ .
363
      The ECG signals are converted into ECG feature with FE , serving as the key and value inputs for
364   FCQ . The output of FCQ provides the predicted probabilities for each category.
365   Linear Probing. For linear probing, we keep the ECG encoder FE frozen and only update the
366   parameters of a randomly initialized linear classifier. We conduct linear probing with {1%, 10%,
367   100%} of the training data. This configuration is used consistently across all linear probing tasks.
368   Further implementation details are provided in the Tab 4.
369   Partial Lead Setting. In the partial lead setting, we follow the lead order from the MIMIC-ECG
370   dataset (Gow et al.): [I, II, III, aVF, aVR, aVL, V1, V2, V3, V4, V5, V6], progressively expanding
371   the input from a single lead to all 12 leads in sequence. In contrast, since MERL (Liu et al., 2024)
372
      requires a full 12-lead input, we pad the missing leads with zeros to maintain the 12-lead format.

      6       C ONCLUSION
529
      We present K-MERL, a knowledge-enhanced ECG multimodal learning framework capable of
530
      processing arbitrary lead inputs. First, we mine cardiac-related entities as structured knowledge from
531   ECG free-text reports using a general LLM, without relying on external domain-specific resources.
532   Next, we align ECG features with these cardiac-related entities to integrate this knowledge into the
533   ECG multimodal learning. Additionally, we introduce lead-specific processing and lead&segment
534   masking strategies to capture the spatial-temporal patterns unique to each ECG lead, enabling the
535   model to handle varying lead inputs. Our experiments on six downstream ECG classification tasks,
536   along with extensive ablation studies, demonstrate K-MERL’s superior zero-shot and linear probing
537   performance compared to existing ECG multimodal and self-supervised learning methods.
