008                                                A BSTRACT
009
010            Transformer-based large language models and vision transformers have achieved
011
               remarkable performance, but at a high energy cost. Nonlinearities (e.g., GELU,
               softmax) have regions where the magnitude of the gradient is small, which means
012
               that errors in pre-nonlinearity inputs result in small output error. We propose
013
               Nonlinearity-Aware Quantization (NAQ), which involves computing the FC layer
014            outputs and attention scores at low precision, predicting the magnitude of the
015            gradient of the nonlinearity, and recomputing the pre-nonlinearity if the gradient
016            magnitude is large. With future hardware support, models with NAQ would avoid
017            up to 62% of full precision pre-nonlinearity computation and it would achieve up
018            to 29% reduction in energy consumption, with small effects on model performance.
019
020
021   1   I NTRODUCTION
022
023   The transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) is the basis of large, pre-trained models that
024   have achieved remarkable performance on language and vision modelling tasks, fuelling the recent
025
      excitement around generative AI. However, their growing energy consumption is a significant
      challenge, requiring the costly construction of new power plants to power this demand (Hiller, 2024).
026
      This increasing energy cost is hindering the availability and adoption of AI, such as on mobile
027
      platforms like smartphones and autonomous drones, requiring offloading of the most powerful models
028   to cloud servers. A recent study shows model inference consumes 70% of AI infrastructure power
029   capacity compared to 10% for experimentation and 20% for training (Wu et al., 2022). This motivates
030   the need for solutions that reduce the inference energy consumption of existing, trained models.
031
      Common to all transformer architectures are building blocks of linear layers followed by an activation
032
      function (e.g., GELU) and attention weight computation followed by softmax. We find that some of
033
      this pre-nonlinearity (pre-activation and pre-softmax) computation can be computed at low precision
034   with little effect on model performance, because nonlinearities have regions where the magnitude
035   of the gradient is small. Fig. 1 (left) shows gradient of various element-wise, nonlinear activation
036   functions plotted against pre-activation input value, and it shows that there are regions where the

061
      pre-computing the gradient to determine how to quantize weights and inputs is not an option. In other
062
      words, the dilemma is that we need to know the result of the pre-nonlinearity computation before
063   we know how to quantize the inputs to the pre-nonlinearity computation. We break this dependency
064   loop by performing low-precision computation to gather information about the gradient magnitude,
065   and then recomputing where the gradient magnitude is large. Since low-precision computation uses
066   much less energy than full precision, the energy savings outweigh the cost of recomputation. We
067   call this technique NAQ, and it specifically targets pre-activation fully-connected (FC) layer and the
068   pre-softmax attention score computation. While prior works have also proposed hardware solutions
069   predicting when the input to ReLU will be negative to avoid computation that will end being set to
070   0 (Akhlaghi et al., 2018; Kim et al., 2021a), their assumptions break down when applied to other
071
      nonlinearities (§A). In contrast, our insights lead to a proposal called NAQ, which improves the
      energy efficiency for computation preceding all activation functions and softmax.
072
073   We make the following contributions:
074
075          • We observe that common nonlinearities in transformers (e.g., GELU and softmax) have
076            regions of small gradient magnitude.
077          • We propose a technique called Nonlinearity-Aware Quantization (NAQ) that gathers in-
078            formation about the gradient with a low-precision pass before selectively recomputing
079            pre-nonlinearity values where the gradient is predicted to be large.
080
             • We apply NAQ on vision transformers (ViTs) and large language models (LLMs) quantized
081
               using GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2022) and PTQ4ViT (Yuan et al., 2022), resulting in avoiding
082            up to 62% of full precision pre-nonlinearity computation and achieving up to 29% reduction
083            in energy consumption, with small effects on model performance.

099
      in this paper, we tackle a less explored goal of decreasing energy used in computation.

139   2.3     G RADIENT OF N ONLINEARITIES
140
      When the magnitude of the gradient of the output of a nonlinearity is small for a given value in a
141
      tensor, then that value would be a prime candidate for lower precision representation and computation,
142
      since the error from low precision computation would result in less output error. In other words,
143   quantization error would be mitigated by the low gradient magnitude. This is similar to the idea of
144   “sensitive values” in SqueezeLLM (Kim et al., 2023), where values that are estimated to be likely to
145   impact the loss of the network through a Hessian heuristic are represented in full-precision. In our
146   case, we consider “sensitive values” in terms of their direct effect on the output of the nonlinearity
147   by estimating the gradient. In this subsection, we analyze nonlinearities to determine when the
148   magnitude of their gradient is small with respect to elements of the pre-activation tensor and inputs to
149   linear transformations that produce the pre-activation tensor.

228   The maximum quantized representation is 2N −1 − 1 because one bit is used as sign bit. For simplicity,
229                                                        x
      we use a symmetric quantization function f (x) = M     , where M is the maximum representable value.
230   However, the quantization function can become more complex with the addition of zero-point (Jacob
231   et al., 2018), group-wise quantization (Shen et al., 2020), and non-uniform quantization (Kim et al.,
232   2023).
233
      Quantization introduces quantization error through the rounding process, but computation with
234   quantized values uses much less energy. Energy consumption for multiplications scales quadratically
235   (a multiplication with half the bits will take one quarter the energy), while energy consumption
236   for additions, scales linearly (Horowitz, 2014). This relationship is illustrated in (Horowitz, 2014),
237   where the author lists the energy consumption of computing operations at different bitwidths. Using
238   this relationship, we estimate that at 45nm technology, an INT4 multiply-add costs 0.065pJ while
239   a 16-bit floating point multiply-add costs 1.5pJ and INT8 multiply-add costs 0.23pJ, so an INT4
240   multiply-add uses 23.1× less energy than FP16 and 3.5× less than INT8. The relative difference in
241   energy consumption holds at other process nodes (Stillmaker & Baas, 2017). This highlights the
242
      tradeoff between energy consumption and model performance, where we aim to limit the model
      performance degradation as energy consumption is quadratically decreased.

      3   N ONLINEARITY-AWARE Q UANTIZATION
279
280   We propose Nonlinearity-Aware Quantization (NAQ) to exploit the small gradient magnitude regions
281   in nonlinearities (§2.3) in order to reduce the energy consumption of pre-nonlinearity computa-
282
      tion (§2.5). In §2.3, we described a dependency loop where we need the nonlinearity gradients to
      selectively quantize the inputs, but we need to compute the pre-nonlinearity values from these inputs
283
      to determine the nonlinearity gradients. NAQ predicts when the gradient is large via low-precision
284
      computation and then uses the prediction to recompute at full precision when the gradient magnitude
285   is large, thus breaking the dependency loop.
286
287   NAQ addresses energy efficiency because computing at low-precision uses much less energy than full
288
      precision (§2.4) and we only recompute a fraction of the total computation at full precision. However,
      recomputation adds latency as the critical path includes the original full precision computation
289
      and additional low-precision computation. For this reason, NAQ is not ideal for latency-sensitive
290
      applications, but NAQ is suited to energy-constrained applications, such as data centers that would
291   like to lower energy costs and mobile platforms that are limited by battery life.
292
293   Quantization and dequantization are performed according to Eqn. 8 and Eqn. 9, respectively. Fixed
294
      point low-bitwidth computation is performed in the dot product and addition in the FC layer (Fig. 4
      (left)), and in attention score computation (Fig. 4 (right)) if the parameters are quantized by model
295
      compression. In §4.1, we describe building on top of quantization-based model compression works.

366   3.4   H ARDWARE I MPLEMENTATION
367
368   A limitation of NAQ is that there is not current hardware support to achieve energy efficiency
369
      gains. However, commodity hardware and computer architecture research is not far from being
      able to support NAQ. NVIDIA GPUs have support INT4 computation since 2019, with throughput
370
      around double INT8 (Dave Salvator & Emmart, 2019). Assuming the same power demand for
371
      INT4 compared to INT8, this would mean the energy per computation scales linearly, as opposed to
372   quadratically like we believe is possible with specialized hardware. While commodity GPUs do not
373   directly support the arbitrary sparse computation during full-precision recomputation, it is possible
374   to repack the sparse computation into the 2:4 structured sparsity pattern (Choquette, 2023), though
375   this would come with some data movement overheads in repacking. Recent computer architecture
376   works (Zhang et al., 2020b; Srivastava et al., 2020) have shown one to two orders of magnitude
377   improvements in throughput and energy efficiency compared to GPUs. We hope this study motivates
      further research and development of better support for sparsity.

      Results for PTQ4ViT models with NAQ are shown in Fig. 7 and Fig. 8. While over 40% of full
      precision pre-nonlinearity computation can be avoided with small degradations in accuracy, NAQ
491
      struggles to reduce energy consumption. At most, it achieves a 10% energy savings. The reason
492
      this is the case is that full-precision computation for PTQ4ViT is INT8, which uses just 3.5× more
493   energy than INT4 computation. The additional energy for INT4 computation that is discarded and
494   recomputed means the energy efficiency gains are more modest.

      More detailed results with the quantization thresholds and more detailed breakdown of computation
      avoided can be found in App. C.

499   5   C ONCLUSION
500
501   In this work, we propose NAQ, which exploits the small gradient magnitude regions of nonlinearities
502   in the transformer architecture. Where the gradient magnitude is small, quantization error can be
503   tolerated. Under NAQ, all pre-nonlinearity computation is performed at low bit-width, which provides
504
      information about the which pre-nonlinearity elements correspond to high gradient magnitude, so
      those can be re-computed at full precision. We evaluate NAQ by building on top of prior model
505
      compression work and we find that with hardware support, models with NAQ would avoid up to
506
      62% of full precision pre-nonlinearity computation and it would achieve up to 29% reduction in
507   energy consumption, with small effects on model performance. We hope our work stimulates further
508   research into the interplay between the choice of activation function and accelerated approximate
509   computing.

      A    R E LU- BASED A PPROACHES
649
650
      In this section, we discuss in more depth the problems with applying ReLU-based prior approaches
651   to transformers, as well quantitative results of trying to do so.
652
653   We consider SnaPEA (Akhlaghi et al., 2018), a representative early termination technique that exploits
654
      characteristics of ReLU in CNNs with the following two observations: (1) the ReLU function output
      zero for any negative input, so if one can predict which pre-activations will be negative, then one
655
      can skip computing the value of that negative pre-activation; and (2) many CNNs have repeating
656
      sequences of convolution followed by ReLU, which means that the input to most convolution layers
657   are strictly non-negative. SnaPEA (Akhlaghi et al., 2018) orders weights from most positive to
658   most negative, then computes and accumulates partial products serially until the partial product
659   becomes negative. From observation (2), the partial products must decrease monotonically as they
660   are computed, and from observation (1), it outputs zero once the partial product becomes negative.
661
      The two assumptions underlying ReLU-based Early Negative Termination both fall apart in trans-
662
      formers. Observation (1) fails for non-RELU activation functions, because it is not enough to make a
663   binary (output = 0 or something else) prediction when negative pre-activation values map to non-zero
664   negative post-activation values. We swept a range of predicted single values and none achieve the
665   performance of NAQ, despite the benefit of an unrealistic oracle predictor. Even if the transformer
666   used ReLU like OPT (Zhang et al., 2022) does, an early termination method would run into problems
667   with observation (2) not being true. As shown in Fig. 2, the inputs into the pre-activation computation
668   are not the output of ReLU and thus are not strictly non-negative, violating that assumption underlying
669   the early termination methods.
670
671   B    TANH AND S IGMOID ACTIVATION F UNCTIONS
672
673   Tanh and sigmoid activation functions are not commonly used in transformer models, but they play
674   an important role in the history of neural networks. Indeed, the Universal Approximation Theorem
675   underlying all neural networks is proven using the sigmoid activation function (Hornik et al., 1989).
676   Tanh and sigmoid are different from the other activations because their gradient ∂ Act(z
                                                                                          ∂zi
                                                                                              i)
                                                                                                 is symmetric
677   around the y-axis with low gradient for large magnitude (absolute value) inputs. This contrasts to the
678   other activations that are asymmetric, as shown in Fig. 1. Having low gradients for large magnitude
679   inputs (regardless of sign) makes tanh and sigmoid helpful in combatting the problem of outliers
680   in transformers (Xiao et al., 2023). Outliers are difficult to quantize as they can require more bits
681   to accurately represent the dynamic range, but the low gradient for these outliers means that larger
682   quantization error in representing these values would have a relatively small impact on the output.
683   Implementing NAQ on a neural network with tanh or sigmoid would require the quantization threshold
684   TtextQP to be applied to the absolute value of QP (§3.1) to predict the activation function gradient.
685   This small change should suffice in enabling NAQ support for tanh and sigmoid. Further exploration
686   of this would be warranted if tanh and sigmoid regain popularity.
