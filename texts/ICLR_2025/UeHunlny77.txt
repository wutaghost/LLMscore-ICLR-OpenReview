009                                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Long-context models (LCMs) have made remarkable strides in recent years, offer-
012            ing users great convenience for handling tasks that involve long context, such as
013            document summarization. As the community increasingly prioritizes the faithful-
014            ness of generated results, merely ensuring the accuracy of LCM outputs is insuffi-
015            cient, as it is quite challenging for humans to verify the results from the extremely
016            lengthy context. Yet, although some efforts have been made to assess whether
017            LCMs respond truly based on the context, these works either are limited to spe-
018
               cific tasks or heavily rely on external evaluation resources like GPT-4. In this
               work, we introduce L-CiteEval, a comprehensive multi-task benchmark for long-
019
               context understanding with citations, aiming to evaluate both the understanding
020
               capability and faithfulness of LCMs. L-CiteEval covers 11 tasks from diverse
021            domains, spanning context lengths from 8K to 48K, and provides a fully auto-
022            mated evaluation suite. Through testing with 11 cutting-edge closed-source and
023            open-source LCMs, we find that although these models show minor differences in
024            their generated results, open-source models substantially trail behind their closed-
025            source counterparts in terms of citation accuracy and recall. This suggests that
026            current open-source LCMs are prone to responding based on their inherent knowl-
027            edge rather than the given context, posing a significant risk to the user experience
028            in practical applications. We also evaluate the RAG approach and observe that
029
               RAG can significantly improve the faithfulness of LCMs, albeit with a slight de-
               crease in the generation quality. Furthermore, we discover a correlation between
030
               the attention mechanisms of LCMs and the citation generation process.

049   1   I NTRODUCTION
050
051   The rapid development of Long-context Models (LCMs) provides users with numerous conve-
052   niences in resolving long-context real-world tasks, such as code analysis (Zhu et al., 2024) and
053   long document summarization (Reid et al., 2024). Recently, the community has gradually intensi-
      fied its efforts to enhance the faithfulness of generative artificial intelligence (Manna & Sett, 2024),


                                                                              1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      which is of paramount importance for LCMs. This is because tasks that involve long context usu-
055   ally require LCMs to respond based on the provided context rather than relying solely on models’
056   intrinsic knowledge. Therefore, there is an urgent need for a benchmark to verify whether LCMs
057   truly leverage context for responding and reflect those models’ capability on long-context tasks.
058
      To date, substantial efforts have been made to develop benchmarks for evaluating LCMs. These
059
      endeavors aim to achieve several key objectives: (1) ensuring that the benchmarks include a com-
060
      prehensive range of task scenarios and varying context lengths; (2) employing automated metrics
061   to guarantee the reproducibility of evaluations; (3) incorporating an appropriate volume of test
062   data to maintain evaluation efficiency; and (4) offering sufficient interpretability (e.g., providing
063   evidence to support the responses). As shown in Fig. 1, taking three representative long-context
064   benchmarks as examples: LongBench (Bai et al., 2023) primarily evaluates the accuracy of LCMs’
065   responses across a range of realistic and synthetic tasks, with a context length of up to 24K tokens;
066   Ruler (Hsieh et al., 2024) focuses on using synthetic data to test LCMs’ capabilities in information
067   retrieval over long sequences, with context lengths exceeding 48K tokens; and LongCite (Bai et al.,
068   2024) assesses whether models respond based on the content within the context, employing GPT-4 as
069
      a judge. These benchmarks, based on their purpose, can be roughly divided into two categories: (1)
      evaluating long-context understanding and (2) assessing model faithfulness. The former evaluates
070
      model outputs using large volumes of test data to infer LCMs’ capabilities but lacks interpretability
071
      to the generated results. The latter are mainly based on short-context datasets (e.g., in LongCite, the
072   maximum sequence length only reaches 32K, comprising just 5.88% of the benchmark) and rely on
073   external resources like GPT-4 to judge faithfulness, making the evaluation results hard to reproduce.
074
075
      In this work, we introduce L-CiteEval, a comprehensive multi-task benchmark for long-context
      understanding with citations. As shown in Fig. 2, given the question and long reference context,
076
      L-CiteEval requires LCMs to generate both the statements and their supporting evidence (citations).
077
      There are 5 major task categories, 11 different long-context tasks, with context lengths ranging from
078   8K to 48K in L-CiteEval. To address the timeliness and the risk of data leakage in testing (Ni et al.,
079   2024; Apicella et al., 2024), we incorporate 4 latest long-context tasks as the subsets in L-CiteEval,
080   ensuring that the evaluation remains up-to-date and robust. Different from previous benchmarks
081   for long-context understanding that primarily assess LCMs based on their predicted answers, L-
082   CiteEval evaluates model performance based on both the generation quality (whether the predicted
083   answer is correct) and citation quality (whether the provided citations can support the corresponding
084   answer). To extend the context length of short-context data, we design a rigorous data construction
085   pipeline to extend the sequence length and mitigate the perturbation introduced from the additional
086
      context. Additionally, to facilitate the ease of use and ensure reproducibility, L-CiteEval offers an
      automatic evaluation suite. Considering that the prediction from LCMs can be influenced by both
087
      the task difficulty and the context length, we propose two benchmark variants: L-CiteEval-Length
088
      and L-CiteEval-Hardness. These two variants strictly control the variables within the evaluation,
089   focusing solely on context length and task difficulty to assess LCMs’ capabilities.
090
091   We test 11 cutting-edge and widely-used LCMs, including 3 closed-source and 8 open-source
092
      models, which feature different sizes and architectures. We also explore whether the Retrieval-
      Augmented Generation (RAG) technique can improve the faithfulness of LCMs. Evaluation results
093
      indicate that there is a minor difference between open-source and closed-source models regarding
094
      generation quality, while open-source models substantially trail behind their closed-source counter-
095   parts in terms of citation quality. Utilizing the RAG technique exhibits a notable improvement in
096   the faithfulness of open-source models, but it slightly impacts the generation quality. Furthermore,
097   we reveal a correlation between the model’s citation generation process and its attention mecha-
098   nism (i.e., retrieval head (Wu et al., 2024)), demonstrating the validity of our benchmark and offering
099   insights for future evaluations of LCM faithfulness and the development of advanced LCMs.

132

136   world tasks and synthetic tasks for long-context understanding with citations. By requiring LCMs
137   to provide evidence to support their predictions, we can also mitigate the challenge of being unable
138   to test whether LCMs respond based on their intrinsic knowledge or the provided context.

140   2.2   C ITATION G ENERATION
141
      The citation generation task aims to verify whether the model predictions are supported by the refer-
142   enced source (Li et al., 2023a). To evaluate the citations generated by models, Rashkin et al. (2023)
143   first proposed attributed to identified sources (AIS) evaluation framework to measure the faithful-
144   ness of the model outputs. Then, some works began to improve the AIS framework in different
145   tasks (such as single-document QA (Bohnet et al., 2022) and fact checking (Honovich et al., 2022))
146   and domains (such as science (Funkquist et al., 2022) and commerce (Liu et al., 2023)). To enhance
147   the evaluation precision of citations within the generated text, Qian et al. (2023); Kamalloo et al.
148   (2023); Li et al. (2023c) made great contributions based on the QA tasks. With the advancement of
149   generative AI, citation generation has begun to require models themselves to generate citations that
150
      support their predictions (Gao et al., 2023). More recently, Bai et al. (2024) introduced LongCite,
      which represents the first attempt at citation generation in long context question-answering tasks.
151
      Compared with LongCite, L-CiteEval is (1) more comprehensive – it covers a wider range of tasks,
152
      supports longer context lengths, and strictly categorizes tasks by length intervals; (2) more repro-
153   ducible – it relies entirely on automatic evaluation metrics without reliance on GPT-4 or human
154   judgments; and (3) more efficient – the task and data distribution are well-designed in L-CiteEval,
155   enabling users to utilize a limited amount of testing data to reflect the LCMs’ capabilities.

      Automatic Evaluation During the verification stage, the model response is evaluated from two
188   aspects: the response quality and citation quality. As shown in Tab. 1, for response quality, we
189   employ different evaluation metrics tailored to each specific task, e.g., Precision (Prec.) and Re-
190   call (Rec.) for QA tasks and Rouge-L (Lin, 2004) for summarization tasks. As for citation quality,
191   following Gao et al. (2023), we adopt Citation Recall (CR) to reflect whether the model statements
192   are fully supported by the citations; Citation Precision (CP ) to detect irrelevant citations; and cita-
193   tion F1 score to represent the overall citation performance. Besides, we report citation number N to
194   show how many citations the model uses to support its output. Different from previous works that
195   utilize an NLI model (Gao et al., 2023) to automatically determine whether citations support the cor-
196
      responding statements, we adopt a long-context NLI model deberta-base-long-nli (Sileo, 2024), to
      better align with long-context scenarios. We describe the calculation of CR and CP in Appendix B.

201   There are 5 main categories in the L-CiteEval benchmark: Single-document QA, Multi-document
202
      QA, Summarization, Dialogue understanding, and Synthetic tasks, covering both realistic and syn-

208

209

212   data (Dseed and Dpad ) into citation chunks of approximately equal size, with sentences as the basic
213   unit. As mentioned above, we utilize different citation chunk sizes for different tasks. For tasks in-
214   volving concentrated information, e.g., single-document QA, we employ smaller chunk sizes, while
215   for tasks involving dispersed information, e.g., summarization, we use larger chunk sizes. We report
      the citation chunk size for each dataset in Table 1.

300   4      E XPERIMENTS
301
302   We conduct experiments with 11 latest LCMs, including 3 closed-source LCMs and 8 open-source
303
      LCMs, each with a context window size of at least 128K tokens, encompassing different parame-
      ters (ranging from 3B to 70B) and model architectures (dense model and MoE model). The statistic
304
      of LCMs is shown in Tab. 2. We provide one demonstration within the prompt for each task to
305
      make the model’s output format more standard, i.e., one-shot learning during the inference time,
306   and employ the same instruction for every LCM. Demonstration of model prediction, question, and
307   instruction for each task is shown in Appendix F. We benchmark all the LCMs with L-CiteEval and
308   then select 6 representative LCMs (including 1 closed-source LCMs and 4 open-source LCMs) to

477   Given outstanding performance retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) on long-context understand-

479   understanding in citation generation tasks. Furthermore, we will analyze the relevance between the

482   5.1       I MPACT OF RAG FOR L ONG - CONTEXT U NDERSTANDING WITH C ITATIONS

485   segments related to the question within the context. For each question, we select the top 32 citation
      segments with the highest retrieval scores and concatenate these segments as input to the LCMs.

509   5.2   R ELEVANCE BETWEEN C ITATION G ENERATION AND ATTENTION M ECHANISM
510
      Recently, Wu et al. (2024) highlighted that LCMs can accurately identify token-level salient infor-
511
      mation within the context. We explore whether the process of citation generation by LCMs is also
512   reflected in the attention mechanisms. Let the ground truth citation segment within the context be
513   denoted as gj . Following Wu et al. (2024), we can use the retrieval score to determine whether the
514   LCM’s attention focuses on the segment containing gj when generating the citation for gj . We find
515   the positions that receive the most attention from all the attention heads. If a position is located in the
516   segment containing gi and the model’s output citation is exactly gi , or if neither matches, we con-
517   sider this a “correct retrieval”. Otherwise, it is an “incorrect retrieval”. We conduct the experiments
518   on two tasks (HotpotQA and 2WikiMultihopQA) with two strong LCMs (Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct
519   and GLM-4-9B-Chat). We plot the number of citations generated by the models and the number of
520
      citation segments identified by the attention heads in Fig. 6. Ideally, if all citation positions exhibit
      “correct retrieval”, each data point would be distributed along the diagonal (i.e., the green dot line in
521
      6). We utilized Pearson correlation analysis to calculate the correlation coefficient (r) between the
522
      generated citations and those retrieved by the attention mechanism, finding all the correlation values
523   exceed 0.7. This reveals the underlying mechanism by which we can leverage the model’s citation
524   output to verify whether the model is truly responding based on the given context.

527   6     C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this paper, we introduce L-CiteEval, a multi-task benchmark for long-context understanding with
530   citations. There are 5 major task categories, 11 different long-context tasks, with context lengths
531
      ranging from 8K to 48K in L-CiteEval. For reproducibility of evaluation results and the ease of
      use, we develop an automatic evaluation suite. Additionally, considering the multitude of variables
532
      that affect model generation results, we developed two benchmark variants: L-CiteEval-Length and
533
      L-CiteEval-Hardness, which evaluate the LCMs from the context length and task difficulty aspects.
534   Experiments on 11 cutting-edge and widely used LCMs indicate that open-source LCMs are prone to
535   generating responses based on their intrinsic knowledge rather than the context, while closed-source
536   LCMs tend to provide more explanations, which significantly reduces generation accuracy. We also
537   find that RAG technology can significantly enhance the faithfulness of open-source LCMs, although
538   it may lead to some loss in generation quality. Furthermore, we reveal a correlation between the
539   model’s citation generation process and its attention mechanism, demonstrating the validity of the
      citation generation approach and providing insights for future evaluations of LCM faithfulness.

      A    L IMITATION AND F UTURE W ORK
811
812
      In this paper, we present a comprehensive multi-task benchmark for long-context understanding with
813   citation. Throughout our work, we identify two significant issues in the long-context evaluation field
814   that not only represent the limitations of this paper but also indicate directions for future research:
815
816           • Evaluation: Current evaluation metrics still heavily rely on human judgment or model
817             outputs. While automated metrics offer convenience, they often fail to accurately reflect
818             model performance. For instance, when testing the generation quality of closed-source
819             models, we found that their tendency to produce excessively long content resulted in sig-
820             nificantly lower accuracy, even compared to open-source models. This issue arises because
821
                traditional automated metrics cannot adaptively extract correct answers. Therefore,
                we should explore methods to combine external tools for precise matching, such as using
822
                RAG to extract answers (Ru et al., 2024).
823
824           • Currently, many benchmarks are facing serious data leakage issues Apicella et al. (2024),
825
                which is not just a problem in the long-text evaluation domain but across the entire eval-
                uation field. An effective solution is to continuously update the data through anonymous
826
                submissions to prevent data leakage. Therefore, in our future work, we will continue to re-
827
                fine L-CiteEval by creating an anonymous system that dynamically adjusts tasks and data
828             to mitigate the risk of data leakage.
829
830
              • Currently, the data in L-CiteEval is still limited. While we believe that using less data
                can enhance evaluation efficiency, it can also lead to issues with data distribution bias.
831
                Therefore, in future work, we will propose an L-CiteEval-Ultra version, which will cover
832
                a broader range of data distributions and larger testing datasets to provide a more compre-
833             hensive evaluation of LCMs.
