009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Active learning reduces annotation costs by selecting the most informative sam-
012            ples, however fixed batch sizes used in traditional methods often lead to ineffi-
013            cient use of resources. We propose Adaptive BAtch Size using Reinforced Active
014            Learning, a novel approach that dynamically adjusts batch sizes based on model
015            uncertainty and performance. By framing the annotation process as a Markov De-
016            cision Process, the proposed method employs reinforcement learning to optimize
017            batch size selection, using two distinct policies: one targeting precision and bud-
018            get, and the other for adapting the batch size based on learning progress. The pro-
019            posed method is evaluated on both CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100 and MNIST datasets.
020
               The performance is measured across multiple metrics, including precision, accu-
               racy, recall, F1-score, and annotation budget. Experimental results demonstrate
021
               that the proposed method consistently reduces annotation costs while maintain-
022
               ing or improving performance compared to fixed-batch Active Learning methods,
023            achieving higher sample selection efficiency without compromising model qual-
024            ity.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Active Learning (AL) reduces the effort required for image classification by selecting the most
031   informative samples for labeling (Zhan et al., 2022). However, labeling large datasets can be a costly
032   and time-consuming process, especially when expert knowledge is required (Mahmood et al., 2022).
033
      Additionally, poor-quality annotations can negatively impact machine learning (ML) performance,
      further increasing the complexity and cost of AL. Reinforcement Learning (RL) (Takezoe et al.,
034
      2023; Ren et al., 2020) offers a promising solution to these challenges by dynamically adjusting
035
      batch sizes and optimizing sample selection strategies. RL operates by learning from feedback,
036   rewarding actions that enhance performance and efficiency, making it well-suited for sequential
037   decision-making tasks, such as sample selection in AL, where outcomes are uncertain and evolve
038   over time. Despite these advantages, RL has not yet been widely used to optimize batch sizes in
039   AL. Most RL-based methods focus on broader decision-making tasks, leaving a gap in the dynamic
040   adjustment of batch sizes, which could potentially improve AL’s efficiency.
041
      Reinforced Active Learning (RAL) (Takezoe et al., 2023; Ren et al., 2020) merges RL with AL by
042   enabling an agent to select the most relevant data based on rewards and penalties. This approach has
043   been successfully applied in fields such as robotics (Kober et al., 2023), natural language processing
044   (NLP) (Fang et al., 2017), and computer vision (Le et al., 2021), where it has improved learning effi-
045   ciency by focusing on the most useful data. In RAL, an RL agent autonomously replaces traditional
046   AL strategies such as uncertainty-based (Cao & Tsang, 2021; Kim et al., 2020; Sinha et al., 2019),
047   diversity-based (Sener & Savarese, 2017), or hybrid methods (Ash et al., 2019). Rather than rely-
048   ing on fixed rules, the agent dynamically adapts its sample selection based on learned experiences,
049   optimizing the learning process by concentrating on the most informative data.
050   Although the above-mentioned methods improve AL efficiency, mostly rely on fixed batch sizes or
051   predefined strategies, which can limit their adaptability and resource optimization. The proposed
052   method, Adaptive BAtch Size using Reinforced Active Learning (ABAS-RAL), addresses these
053   limitations by using RL to dynamically adjust batch sizes based on real-time feedback, resulting in
      more efficient and effective sample selection. The key contributions of this work are:

064   2.1   ACTIVE L EARNING
065
066   AL selects the most informative data points for labeling to enhance learning efficiency and reduce
067   costs, especially when labeled data is scarce. Uncertainty-based AL (Cao & Tsang, 2021; Kim et al.,
068
      2020; Sinha et al., 2019) targets data points with high uncertainty, while hybrid (Ash et al., 2019)
      and interpolation-based (Parvaneh et al., 2022) methods combine strategies or focus on samples
069
      near labeled points. ALFA-Mix (Parvaneh et al., 2022) and LOC (Mahmood et al., 2022) are specific
070
      methods that improve AL by evaluating label variability and optimizing data collection to balance
071   costs and performance.

073
      2.2   R EINFORCED ACTIVE L EARNING
074
075   RL has advanced AL by dynamically selecting the most informative samples. Key contributions
076   include Fang et al. (2017) for NLP, Woodward & Finn (2017) for image classification, Pang
077   et al. (2018) for meta-learning, and Sun & Gong (2019) for image selection. Wassermann et al.
078   (2019) introduced contextual-bandit models, Liu et al. (2019) developed a human-in-the-loop model,
079   and Wang et al. (2020) applied RL to medical imaging. Casanova et al. (2020) focused on seman-
080   tic segmentation, Hanane et al. (2022) on multi-agent RL for medical segmentation, and Ahmad
081
      et al. (2021) on selecting informative image regions. Recent work includes Nilsson et al. (2021) on
      lifelong visual perception, Slade & Branson (2022) on deep RL for medical image classification,
082
      Cui et al. (2022) on human activity recognition, Katz & Kravchik (2022) on online stream-based
083
      meta AL, Dodds et al. (2023) on molecular design, and Chun (2023) on data-efficient classification
084   frameworks.

      2.3   A DAPTIVE BATCH S IZE A PPROACHES
087
088   Research on adaptive batch sizes in AL includes Hacohen et al. (2022) on budget and query strate-
089   gies, Chakraborty et al. (2014) on optimizing batch size and selection criteria, and Ishibashi & Hino
090   (2017) on error stability-based stopping. Cai et al. (2017) focused on Expected Model Change Maxi-
091   mization to select informative samples. Konyushkova et al. (2018) developed a universal AL strategy
092   using RL and MDP frameworks. Fazakis et al. (2019) combined AL with semi-supervised learning
093   for efficient data utilization. Recent advancements by Haghighatlari et al. (2020) and Fauld (2022)
094
      include EMCM (Expected Model Change Maximization) and AB-EMCM for adaptive batch size in
      regression tasks.
095
096   ABAS-RAL utilizes RL to dynamically determine the optimal batch size for visual classification
097   tasks. The proposed method uses RL to adaptively select the batch size in a single, efficient step.
098   This is achieved with minimal additional cost or computational overhead, as RL agent not only
099   selects the batch size but also utilizes the classifier to fill it effectively. This approach ensures that
100
      batch sizes are tailored in real-time to maximize learning efficiency and performance, addressing
      the limitations of existing methods and advancing adaptive batch size techniques in a practical,
101
      classification-focused manner.

104   3     A DAPTIVE BATCH SIZE SELECTION USING RL
105
106   The introduced RL-based approach uses an agent, called BatchAgent, which learns to select the
107   optimal number of samples to annotate. The BatchAgent operates within a defined state-space,
      choosing actions (batch sizes) and receiving rewards based on improvements in model performance.

      3.1   F ORMULATING AL AS A M ARKOV D ECISION P ROCESS
112
113   Following state-of-the-art approaches (Konyushkova et al., 2018) we use the deep Q-network
114   (DQN) (Mnih et al., 2013) method and modify AL process based on this methodology. Based on
115   the work of Konyushkova et al. (2018), the proposed method also takes advantage of the benefits of
116   Q-learning (Watkins & Dayan, 1992). BatchAgent is trained with the goal of being able to identify
117   an optimal batch of samples at each iteration of an AL episode.
118
119   3.2   RL STATES
120
      At the beginning of AL training, a subset V (state data) is designated from the unlabeled data pool
121
      U0 and then update U0 to exclude V , resulting in U0 \ V . At each iteration t, the state representation
122
      is defined as a vector st consisting of the sorted margin scores m(xi ) for each xi in V . The margin
123   scores, computed based on the classifier’s predictions for each sample in the state data V , are calcu-
124   lated as the difference between the highest and the second-highest predicted class probabilities for
125   each sample of the state data V , i.e., mi (xi ) = P (y ∗ |xi ) − P (y ∗∗ |xi ), where y ∗ and y ∗∗ are the
126   most likely and the second most likely class labels for sample xi . Sorting the margin scores ensures
127   the input is consistent and structured, allowing RL agent to easily identify the most uncertain sam-
128   ples. This helps the agent to make better learning decisions, as an unsorted input would be harder to
129   interpret and generalize. The state representation captures essential details, like the mean prediction
130   and the level of classifier confidence, and is a simple form derived from the classifier and the dataset.

188   3.5      RL ACTION SELECTION
189
      DQN selects actions that maximize the Q-value, guiding the agent to choose batch sizes that yield
190
      the highest cumulative reward. By updating Q-values based on received rewards, DQN optimizes
191   the annotation process for efficient and effective learning.
192
193   At each iteration t, the action at is defined as selecting k samples for annotation, where k ∈
194
      {1, 2, . . . , ⌊0.1 × |Ut |⌋}, and |Ut | is the total number of available unlabeled samples. Limiting
      the action space to 10% of the dataset per iteration encourages thorough data exploration, prevents
195
      overfitting, and ensures efficient use of annotation resources. This approach also provides more
196
      frequent feedback, helping the agent to quickly identify informative samples and refine the model
197   iteratively.

      3.6      RL AGENT DECISION PROCESS
200
201   To better illustrate the decision-making process of RL agent, Fig. 1 provides a flowchart that outlines
202   each step from state representation to reward calculation. RL agent begins by training the classifier
203   on the currently labeled dataset L. It then evaluates the uncertainty of samples in the unlabeled pool U
204   to represent the current state s. Based on this state, the agent selects an optimal batch size (action a)
205   for annotation. The most uncertain samples are then forwarded to the human annotator for labeling,
206   after which the datasets are updated. The agent receives a reward based on the improvement in
207   precision relative to the annotation budget, guiding future batch size selections.

211   Warm-start episodes are crucial for initializing the AL algorithm and training the BatchAgent. Dur-
212   ing these episodes, the BatchAgent tests different batch sizes to learn their effects on performance.
213
      An episode ends when:
214
                 • Two consecutive rewards decrease, or
215
                 • All samples have been used.

217   longer improving. Stopping here avoids wasting time on ineffective batches, allowing the model to

219   process are outlined in Algorithm 1.

      3.8    P OLICY
243
244   The BatchAgent’s policy determines the batch size for each annotation episode, aiming to balance
245   exploration of new batch sizes with exploitation of existing knowledge to maximize precision. An
246   episode concludes when:
247
248          • Target Precision is achieved, ensuring optimal performance without over-training or

255   3.9    P ROCEDURE
256
257   As described in Algorithm 2, the BatchAgent is trained with the goal of being able to identify an
258
      optimal batch of samples at each iteration of an AL episode. Specifically, during model training, a
      predefined number of epochs are executed, consisting of a predefined number of episodes.
259
260   These steps repeat until a terminal state sT is reached. Using the best precision score p as the
261   best precision score that was achieved from the warm-start episodes, the target precision is defined.
262   Using the smallest achieved budget from the warm-start episodes, the target budget is defined. Thus,
263   the terminal state sT is reached when the target precision and the target budget are achieved.
264   By selecting and labeling samples, updating the classifier, and calculating rewards in each step, the
265   BatchAgent improves its performance and adjusts to changes in the model’s predictions.

      4.5                       D ISCUSSION
481
482   The experimental results demonstrate that ABAS-RAL is an efficient AL method across various
483   datasets. Key findings are:
484
485                             1. Precision and Cost Efficiency: ABAS-RAL consistently achieves precision scores com-
                                   parable to or higher than other methods like RS and fixed-batch approaches. Importantly,


                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                               9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
               it requires significantly less annotation budget across CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and MNIST,
487            making it particularly useful when data annotation is expensive. ABAS-RAL’s ability to
488            maintain high performance with fewer labeled samples highlights its efficiency.
489
            2. Dynamic Batch Size: Unlike fixed-batch methods (RS, US, and ALFA-Mix), which often
490
               require a large portion of the dataset, ABAS-RAL dynamically adjusts batch sizes based on
491
               the model’s learning needs. This flexibility allows ABAS-RAL to reach target performance
492            without excessive annotation, making it more resource-efficient.
493
494   In all experiments, including CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and MNIST, ABAS-RAL outperforms other
495   approaches, particularly in scenarios with limited annotation budgets. Its ability to maintain high
496   precision while minimizing costs makes ABAS-RAL an ideal choice for AL tasks that demand both
497   performance and resource efficiency.
