009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) are trained on a substantial amount of docu-
012            ments that contain extensive world knowledge. However, it is still not well-
013            understood how knowledge is acquired via autoregressive pre-training and ex-
014            tracted via question-answering. This lack of understanding greatly hinders effec-
015            tive knowledge learning, especially for continued pre-training on up-to-date infor-
016
               mation, as this evolving information often does not have diverse repetitions like
               foundational knowledge. In this paper, we focus on understanding and improv-
017
               ing LLM knowledge learning. We found and verified that knowledge learning
018
               for LLMs can be deemed as an implicit supervised task hidden in the autore-
019            gressive pre-training objective. Our findings suggest that knowledge learning for
020            LLMs would benefit from methods designed to improve generalization ability for
021            supervised tasks. Based on our analysis, we propose to diversify training docu-
022            ments’ formats as data augmentation to grow in-distribution samples. This data
023            augmentation method does not present the risk of altering the facts embedded in
024            documents as text paraphrasing. We also introduce sharpness-aware minimization
025            as an effective optimization algorithm to better improve generalization. Moreover,
026            we adapt our method to instruction tuning for generalization to various phrasings
027
               of questions. Extensive experiment results validate our findings and demonstrate
               our methods’ effectiveness in improving knowledge learning in both the continued
028
               pre-training and instruction tuning stages. This paper offers new perspectives and
029
               insights to interpret and design effective strategies for LLM knowledge learning.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Large language models (LLMs) are pre-trained on large-scale datasets encompassing extensive
035   knowledge, enabling them to demonstrate remarkable performance on knowledge-intensive tasks
036   (Brown et al. (2020); OpenAI (2023); Chowdhery et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2022); Touvron et al.
037   (2023a;b); Gemini Team (2023); Yang et al. (2024)). Pre-trained LLMs are able to answer ques-
038   tions on various domains, like “Who was the first president of America?”, or “What is the second
039
      highest mountain in the world?”. However, it is still unclear how LLMs can acquire knowledge
      from the training corpus and answer these questions. This insufficient understanding severely lim-
040
      its the efficiency of knowledge acquisition, particularly when it comes to continued pre-training on
041
      newly updated information. Unlike foundational or textbook knowledge, which typically benefits
042   from diverse repetitions and widespread coverage, evolving information often lacks such extensive
043   variation, making it more challenging for LLMs to learn (Kandpal et al. (2023); Jiang et al. (2024);
044   Allen-Zhu & Li (2024)). Therefore, we hope to better understand the mechanism for autoregressive
045   language model knowledge learning and enhance it.
046
      We approach this problem by carefully examining the training objective and inference for-
047
      mula for autoregressive language models. Assuming pre-training on the document d =
048   “Elon M usk was raised in South Af rica.”, people might ask questions querying
049   knowledge stored in the bold tokens, which we refer to as knowledge tokens. For exam-
050   ple, successful elicitation of Elon Musk’s hometown via question-answering is possible only if
051   P (South|tokens preceding South in q) and P (Af rica|tokens preceding Af rica in q)
052   are high enough, where q = “Question : W here did Elon M usk grow up? Answer :
053   South Af rica”. Then we analyze how autoregressive pre-training on documents increases this
      conditional probability. The training objective of autoregressive language models can be formally

054
      interpreted as minimizing the negative log-likelihood (NLL) loss over a dataset, where each sam-
055   ple is constructed such that each token in a document serves as the target label, and its preced-
056   ing tokens are used as the input context. Therefore, we hypothesize document training samples
057   and questions that share the same knowledge tokens as labels come from the same distribution.
058   In this way, minimizing NLL loss for document samples would generalize to questions. In the
059   example d and q above, it means that increasing P (South|tokens preceding South in d)
060   and P (Af rica|tokens preceding Af rica in d) would generalize and thereby increases
061   P (South|tokens preceding South in q) and P (Af rica|tokens preceding Af rica in q). How-
062   ever, this is not obvious from the human perspective, as document training samples and questions
063
      differ in structure: one is a declarative sentence, while the other is in the form of QA. Therefore, we
      construct a human biography dataset including different attributes similar to Allen-Zhu & Li (2024).
064
      During training, we observe that the accuracy of predicting attribute knowledge tokens given ques-
065
      tions as inputs increases along with the accuracy conditioned on the training document sequence.
066   We also observe that integrating paraphrased documents in training can further improve the accuracy
067   of question answering. These observations verifies our hypothesis that document training samples
068   and questions that share the same knowledge token labels come from the same distribution and thus
069   LLM knowledge learning is implicitly a supervised problem.
070
      As we have demonstrated that LLM knowledge learning is supervised, we then explore meth-
071
      ods to improve the generalization ability. According to our perspective, with a single document
072   demonstrating the knowledge, LLM knowledge learning is a 1-shot supervised learning problem.
073   With paraphrased documents, LLM knowledge learning is a few-shot supervised learning problem.
074   Therefore, the first and foremost task is to increase the number of in-distribution samples since gen-
075   eralization for few-shot learning is extremely difficult. However, reliable rephrasing documents by
076   humans is expensive and time-consuming, while LLM rephrasing might alter facts in documents
077   (Ding et al. (2024)). Witnessing texts can be presented in different formatting without affecting
078   embedded facts, we propose modifying documents’ formats as data augmentations. By presenting
079   the same training document with different spacing or padding, the number of in-distribution sam-
080
      ples for knowledge learning is increased. Then, with adequate in-distribution samples synthesized
      using our data augmentation, we utilize sharpness-aware minimization (SAM, Foret et al. (2021))
081
      as the optimization method to better improve generalization. Last, we identify the importance of
082
      generalizing to diverse questions that share the same answer, an aspect critical for effective knowl-
083   edge extraction but overlooked in instruction tuning (Wang et al. (2024); Bukharin & Zhao (2024)).
084   Thus we also integrate our methods into the instruction tuning phase to enhance the generalization
085   ability for question-answering. Specifically, we use SAM as the optimization method and augment
086   the questions of the training QA pairs. In this way, the instruction-tuned model can respond more
087   accurately to different rephrasings of the questions in the training QA pairs, and apply analogous
088   QA patterns for similar questions about all pre-training documents.
089   We evaluate our methods on our constructed biography dataset similar to Allen-Zhu & Li (2024),
090   and the Wiki2023 dataset (Jiang et al. (2024)). Results show that our approach leads to nontrivial im-
091   provement of generalization ability in both the continued pre-training and instruction tuning phases.
092   In addition, detailed ablations validate our finding that generalization matters for LLM knowledge
093   acquisition and extraction. The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
094
095          • We hypothesize and verify that LLM knowledge learning is implicitly a supervised learn-
096            ing problem. This novel perspective provides a solid foundation and systematic way for
097
               analyzing and improving knowledge learning ability.
098          • To improve the generalization ability for LLM knowledge learning, we propose to generate
099            in-distribution training samples by diverse document formatting. This automatic augmen-
100            tation method mitigates the risk of altering facts in documents, in contrast to rephrasing.
101
               We further apply SAM as the optimizer to enhance the generalization ability.
102          • We point out the importance of generalization on different questions that share the same
103            answer, an aspect critical for effective knowledge extraction but previously neglected in the
104            instruction tuning phase. We use SAM and apply our data augmentation to the question
105
               part of QA pairs used in instruction tuning, to enhance the knowledge extraction ability.
106          • Extensive experimental results and ablation studies validate the supervised nature of LLM
107            knowledge learning and demonstrate our methods’ effectiveness in improving knowledge
               learning in both the continued pre-training and instruction tuning phases.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Understanding of LLM Knowledge Learning. There are several works trying to understand how
111
      LLMs learn knowledge from documents and retrieve them in question answering. Akyürek et al.
112   (2022) tries to detect training documents important for question-answering for pre-trained LLMs.
113   A number of works find connections between the frequency of certain knowledge appearing in pre-
114   training documents and its question-answering ability (Kandpal et al. (2023); Akyürek et al. (2022);
115   Petroni et al. (2019); Kassner et al. (2020); Wei et al. (2021); Févry et al. (2020); De Cao et al.
116   (2021)). Recently, Allen-Zhu & Li (2024) and Ovadia et al. (2024) empirically observe that adding
117   rephrased documents describing the same knowledge in the pre-training phase helps knowledge
118   extraction after conducting instruction tuning. These works primarily try to explain LLM knowledge
119   learning by summarizing observed patterns and their analysis is only confined to LLMs after the
120
      instruction tuning stage. Our work, on the other hand, provides a more rigorous and systematic
      explanation. Our analysis covers both the continued pre-training and instruction tuning stages.
121
122   Continued LLM Knowledge Learning. As the pre-trained knowledge stored in LLMs quickly
123   becomes outdated, adapting up-to-date information into LLMs becomes a critical problem. The
124   primary approach to tackling this problem is through continued pre-training on documents contain-
125   ing up-to-date knowledge (Ovadia et al. (2024); Jiang et al. (2024); Jang et al. (2022)). However,
126
      straightforward autoregressive pre-training on new corpus usually cannot lead to effective knowl-
      edge acquisition. This is likely due to the lack of diverse demonstrations of the same knowledge
127
      like foundational or textbook knowledge (Allen-Zhu & Li (2024); Jiang et al. (2024); Ovadia et al.
128
      (2024); Cheng et al. (2024)). Therefore, some works focus on rephrasing documents to alleviate
129   this issue (Cheng et al. (2024); Allen-Zhu & Li (2024); Ovadia et al. (2024)). However, paraphras-
130   ing documents manually can be expensive and tedious, while paraphrasing by LLM might not be
131   reliable as facts and knowledge inside documents could be changed in this process (Ding et al.
132   (2024)). Therefore, we aim to avoid the risk of changing facts embedded in documents while en-
133   abling effective knowledge acquisition. Another line of work tries to include QA data together with
134   or before adapting to new documents (Allen-Zhu & Li (2024); Jiang et al. (2024)). However, these
135   methods introduce new difficulties in finding effective arrangements and proportions of QA data
136   and documents. To induce effective knowledge extraction during inference, instruction tuning on
137
      annotated QA pairs after training on raw documents has recently become a common practice (Sanh
      et al. (2022); Wei et al. (2022); Mishra et al. (2022); Iyer et al. (2022); Kopf et al. (2023); Zhou
138
      et al. (2023); Sun et al. (2023b;a)). Current instruction tuning methods generally focus on diversify-
139
      ing the domain of QA pairs so that questions from different areas can be answered after instruction
140   tuning (Bukharin & Zhao (2024); Wang et al. (2024)). However, the diversity of questions with the
141   same answer is largely overlooked. We identify that different users might pose the same question
142   using different wordings and phrases, and therefore propose to also take this into consideration for
143   instruction tuning.
144
      Data Augmentation for Natural Language Processing. There is a rich literature on data augmen-
145
      tation techniques used for natural language processing (Chen et al. (2021); Ding et al. (2024); Wei
146   & Zou (2019)). A popular type of traditional data augmentation method is synonym substitution,
147   which replaces words in documents with their synonyms according to some pre-defined dictionaries
148   (Kolomiyets et al. (2011); Yang (2015); Zhang et al. (2015)). Another popular class of traditional
149   data augmentation method is inserting, replacing, deleting, and swapping words in documents (Wei
150   & Zou (2019); Iyer et al. (2022); Niu & Bansal (2018); Miao et al. (2020)). In the era of LLMs,
151   paraphrasing documents or synthesizing data using LLMs become increasingly popular (Ding et al.
152   (2024); Sharma et al. (2023); Nair et al. (2023)). However, these data augmentation methods gen-
153   erally modify the semantics of original documents, and some tokens where the knowledge and facts
154
      reside have the risk of being altered (Ding et al. (2024)). Therefore, we opt to avoid such risk and
      design our data augmentation method for LLM knowledge learning.

      4     M ETHODS
282
      Sec. 3 has posed LLM knowledge learning as a supervised learning problem. This section explores
283
      methods to improve the generalization ability for LLM knowledge learning. As knowledge tokens of
284   a document are usually unknown, the methods developed are applied to all tokens in the document.
285
286   4.1     DATA AUGMENTATION VIA FORMATTING
287
288   As we analyzed in Sec. 3.3, knowledge learning without enough rephrased documents can be ex-
289   tremely difficult due to insufficient in-distribution samples with knowledge token labels. However,
290   paraphrasing documents manually can be expensive and laborious, while paraphrasing using LLM
291   might not be reliable. It is not easy to ensure that knowledge or facts in documents are not altered
292   by LLM during paraphrasing (Ding et al. (2024)). Moreover, certain expressions and terminologies
293
      are irreplaceable and must be used in their exact form. Nor should mottoes and poems be rephrased
      when they are in training documents. Therefore, it is crucial to develop methods to reliably increase
294
      in-distribution samples with knowledge token labels without paraphrasing.
295
296   We may often encounter variations in the formatting used to present texts, such as whether to indent
297   the beginning of a paragraph and whether to use spaces or tabs as indentations for codes. There are
298   also variations for using single-space or double-space spacing in the era of typewriters (Wikipedia
299
      contributors (2024)). These formatting differences, while altering some of the format tokens, do not
      affect the semantic meaning and knowledge of the text itself. Therefore, given a training document
300
      d, we propose to apply the following formatting-based data augmentations:
301
302           • Wrapping. Augmented documents are created by wrapping document d with quotes, aster-
303             isks, brackets, or parentheses. This is used to imitate the case that the document is quoted,
304             highlighted, or appears in a Markdown document.
305           • Left padding. Augmented documents are created by padding spaces, tabs, or pound signs
306             to the left of the document d. This is to mimic the scenarios of d appearing in a document
307             written using Markdown or as a paragraph in a paper.
308           • Random space insertion. Augmented documents are created by randomly inserting addi-
309             tional spaces adjacent to original spaces in d. This simulates the case that the training doc-
310             ument is presented using different spacing and includes some unintentional extra spaces.
311
      Therefore, for a training document being “Elon Musk was raised in South Africa.”, some examples
312
      of its augmentations are as follows:

328   4.2   S HARPNESS - AWARE MINIMIZATION
329
330   With our proposed data augmentation methods increasing in-distribution samples with knowledge
331   token labels, we can further enhance the generalization ability by applying generalizable optimiza-
332   tion or regularization methods designed for traditional supervised problems. Recently, Foret et al.
333   (2021) developed the Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) to improve the generalization ability
334   of DNN for supervised problems, which has achieved substantial generalization improvement on
335
      widely studied supervised learning problems like image classification (Baek et al. (2024); Chen
      et al. (2022); Foret et al. (2021)). We adopt this technique for the LLM knowledge learning task.
336
337   Given a training document d, let B = S(d), and according to SAM we solve the following problem:
338
                                        min max LB (θ + ϵ) + λ∥θ∥22 ,                                    (4)
339                                      θ   ∥ϵ∥2 ≤ρ
340   where ρ ≥ 0 is a given perturbation radius, λ is a small positive regularization constant. The
341   objective is to find a minimizer with the neighborhood where the loss does not increase too much.
342   According to SAM, the inner maximization problem in Eq. (4) is solved approximately at ϵ̂ =
343   ρ∇LB (θ)/∥∇LB (θ)∥2 by the first-order Taylor expansion. Then, the objective function of Eq. (4)
344   changes to LB (θ + ϵ̂) + λ∥θ∥22 , on which the gradient descent is performed.

346   4.3   A DAPTATION TO INSTRUCTION TUNING
347
348   Instruction tuning has recently become a common practice to make LLMs follow human instruc-
349   tions and perform question-answering (Sanh et al. (2022); Wei et al. (2022); Ouyang et al. (2022)).
350
      Instruction tuning computes the negative
                                           P     log-likelihood loss only on tokens in the answer with the
      question as the context: La = − t log P (at |q, a<t ). The QA pairs used in instruction tuning
351
      are derived from specific documents in the pre-training dataset. After instruction tuning, LLM can
352
      make analogies and perform similar QAs on other documents seen during the pre-training phase. We
353   identify that different users might pose variations of the same question to LLM, thus generalization
354   on diverse questions sharing the same answer is crucial. Therefore, we use SAM and apply our data
355   augmentation only to the context questions for instruction tuning. In this way, LLM would be able
356   to respond accurately to different rephrases of a question seen during instruction tuning, consistently
357   eliciting the same correct answer. As instruction tuning would make LLM apply analogous QA pat-
358   terns for other documents seen during pre-training, we expect the generalization ability can also be
359   brought to other pre-training documents. As prior works on instruction tuning generally focus on
360   the diversity of QA pairs from different domains and tasks while ignoring the diversity of questions
361
      with the same answer (Bukharin & Zhao (2024); Wang et al. (2024)), we hope our exploration can
      bring more insights.

      6   C ONCLUSION
524
525   In this paper, we try to understand how LLMs acquire knowledge through autoregressive pre-training
526   and retrieve the knowledge in question-answering. We found and verified that the knowledge learn-
527   ing for LLM is an implicitly supervised problem. We found that for certain knowledge tokens in
528   documents that might serve as answers to questions, minimizing the negative log-likelihood loss on
529   samples with prefixed document sequences as input and the next knowledge tokens as labels would
530   generalize to samples with questions as input and the same knowledge token as labels. Thus, we ver-
531
      ified that knowledge learning for LLMs is indeed a supervised problem. We subsequently propose a
      formatting-based data augmentation method to increase in-distribution samples via presenting train-
532
      ing documents in different formats, which does not have the risk of altering knowledge and facts
533
      embedded in documents as paraphrasing. We also introduce the sharpness-aware minimization as
534   the optimizer to better improve generalization ability. Then we extend our analysis to the instruc-
535   tion tuning phase and point out the importance of generalization on different questions with the same
536   answer for effective knowledge extraction, which is overlooked by previous works. Extensive exper-
537   iments and ablation studies validate our finding of the supervised nature of LLM knowledge learning
538   and demonstrate our methods’ effectiveness in improving knowledge acquisition and extraction for
539   both continued pre-training and instruction tuning phases. We hope our work can provide insights
      to better understand and develop effective methods for LLM knowledge learning.

856   B     D ISCUSSION ON THE REVERSAL CURSE
857
858   The reversal curse is an empirical observation that LLMs trained on “A is B” fail to learn “B is A”
859   (Berglund et al. (2024)). We do not aim to alleviate the reversal curse problem in this paper, however,
860   our proposed perspective to view LLM knowledge learning as a supervised learning problem can
861   explain the existence of the reversal curse. Assume the training sentence is “<bos> A is B” and its
862   reverse sentence is “<bos> B is A”, where <bos> is the special begin-of-sentence token indicating
863   the beginning of a sentence. During training, the input “<bos>” has “A” as the label. In the reverse
      sentence, the input “<bos> B is” has “A” as the label. “<bos>” and “<bos> B is” are too different
