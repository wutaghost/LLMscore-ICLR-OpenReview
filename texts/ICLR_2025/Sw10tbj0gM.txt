011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The problem of imputing multivariate time series spans a wide range of fields,
014            from clinical healthcare to multi-sensor systems. Initially, Recurrent Neural Net-
015
               works (RNNs) were employed for this task; however, their error accumulation
               issues led to the adoption of Transformers, leveraging attention mechanisms to
016
               mitigate these problems. Concurrently, the promising results of diffusion models
017
               in capturing original distributions have positioned them at the forefront of current
018            research, often in conjunction with Transformers. In this paper, we propose replac-
019            ing time-oriented Transformers with State-Space Models (SSM), which are better
020            suited for temporal data modeling. Specifically, we utilize the latest SSM vari-
021            ant, S6, which incorporates attention-like mechanisms. By embedding S6 within
022            Mamba blocks, we develop a model that integrates SSM, Graph Neural Networks,
023            and node-oriented Transformers to achieve enhanced spatiotemporal representa-
024            tions. Implementing these architectural modifications, previously unexplored in
025            this field, we present Time series Imputation with Bi-directional mamba blocks
026
               and diffusion models (TIMBA). TIMBA achieves superior performance in almost
               all benchmark scenarios and performs comparably in others across a diverse range
027
               of missing value situations and three real-world datasets. We also evaluate how
028
               the performance of our model varies with different amounts of missing values and
029            analyse its performance on downstream tasks. In addition, we provide the original
030            code to replicate the results.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   The issue of missing values is widely recognized and can arise from various causes, including dif-
036   ficulties in data collection, problems with recording mechanisms, or human errors. In the field of
037   Multivariate Time Series (MTS) analysis, where multiple variables are recorded, sometimes with
038   irregular frequency, this problem is even more pronounced. It affects many fields, from defective
039
      sensor records that transmit erroneous data (Wu et al., 2020), to the Internet of Things (IoT) where
      these failures are even more common (Ahmed et al., 2022), and even clinical contexts (Moor et al.,
040
      2020). Properly filling these gaps is crucial, as failure to do so can distort the data distribution and
041
      reduce model performance (Solı́s-Garcı́a et al., 2023).
042
043   In recent years, this problem has been tackled using Deep Learning (DL) techniques, employing
044   Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) to capture temporal representations and Graph Neural Networks
045
      (GNNs) for spatial ones (Cini et al., 2022). However, the error accumulation problem in RNNs has
      led to their replacement by Transformers (Tashiro et al., 2021), which use attention mechanisms to
046
      avoid these issues. Furthermore, the latest state-of-the-art results have been achieved using diffusion
047
      models (Tashiro et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023), which excel at modeling multiple distributions in the
048   original data. Additionally, the use of State-Space Models (SSMs) has expanded in recent years,
049   thanks to their discretization, as presented in S4 (Gu et al., 2022b). These models, reminiscent of
050   RNNs, have shown a high capacity to handle time series and long sequences, such as those found
051   in speech processing, text, and even DNA sequencing (Gu et al., 2022b). This architecture has been
052   briefly explored in the context of multivariate time series imputation (MTSI) (Alcaraz & Strodthoff,
053   2023). Recently, S6, an improvement over S4 that incorporates an attention-like mechanism, was
      developed to potentially eliminate error accumulation issues, as demonstrated in selective copy tasks


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      and speech processing (Gu & Dao, 2023). However, the S6 and Mamba blocks have not yet been
055   tested in the context of MTSI.
056
057   Starting from the state-of-the-art models for MTSI, CSDI and PriSTI (Tashiro et al., 2021; Liu et al.,
058
      2023), in this article we replaced time-oriented Transformers in their architecture with S6 layers
      embedded within bi-directional Mamba blocks (Gu & Dao, 2023), carefully maintaining a similar
059
      parameter count for fair comparability. This combination results in our proposed method, Time
060
      series Imputation with bi-directional Mamba Blocks and Diffusion models (TIMBA).
061
062   Our main contributions are as follows: 1) We propose TIMBA, a model that replaces the time-
063   oriented transformers in state-of-the-art diffusion models with bi-directional Mamba blocks, an ap-
064
      proach not previously explored to the best of our knowledge. 2) Through an extensive benchmark
      using three real-world datasets, we demonstrate that TIMBA consistently achieves superior perfor-
065
      mance in almost all scenarios and performs comparably in others when compared to state-of-the-art
066
      models 3) Additionally, we expand the model’s analysis by presenting an ablation study, evaluating
067   its sensitivity to different missing rates, and using it for data imputation followed by an evaluation
068   on a downstream task. The rest of the paper is organized as follows: In Section 2, we review related
069   work in the literature. Section 3 introduces the mathematical background supporting our approach.
070   Section 4 presents TIMBA. In Section 5, we discuss the benchmark, experimental setup, and results
071   obtained. Finally, Section 6 provides the conclusion. Additional information about the code and
072   data can be found in Appendix A.

075   2   R ELATED WORKS
076
077
      Multivariate time series imputation MTSI has been explored through various approaches. Tra-
078
      ditional solutions such as mean, zero, or linear imputation have been utilized to address missing
079   values (Moor et al., 2020). However, these methods often distort the original data distribution, thus
080   degrading the data quality. In contrast, Machine Learning (ML) offers a range of simple to sophis-
081   ticated techniques, including k nearest neighbors (Beretta & Santaniello, 2016), linear predictors
082   (Seaman et al., 2012), matrix factorization (MF) (Cichocki & Phan, 2009), vector autoregressive
083   model-imputation (VAR) (Bashir & Wei, 2018), and multivariate Gaussian processes (MGP) (Li &
084   Marlin, 2016), which have improved outcomes in many cases.
085   With the advent of DL, MTSI applications were soon discovered. Initially, Recurrent Neural Net-
086   works (RNNs) (Suo et al., 2019; Lipton et al., 2016) excelled due to their robust capacity to extract
087   accurate temporal representations, crucial for effective imputation. Advancements in RNNs led to
088   the adoption of architectures such as Bi-directional RNNs (BiRNNs), exemplified by BRITS (Cao
089   et al., 2018), which analyze time series data both forwards and backwards, thereby enhancing impu-
090   tation capabilities. However, recent years have seen the rise of Transformers as a dominant architec-
091   ture, highlighting the limitations of RNNs. The attention mechanism in Transformers is particularly
092   effective in identifying significant or real samples in time series, addressing the error accumulation
093
      issues prevalent in RNNs, as evidenced by several studies (Tashiro et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023).
094   Lastly, the role of GNNs, which are based on graph theory, must be noted for their profound ability to
095   extract spatial relationships among variables. Many researchers argue that for accurate imputation,
096   it is essential to derive robust spatio-temporal representations, hence the integration of RNNs or
097   Transformers with GNNs has become a focal point (Cini et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023). To further
098   enhance the effectiveness of MTSI, generative models and State-Space Models have emerged as
099
      powerful tools, offering unique advantages in handling complex data distributions and dynamics.
100
101
      Generative models for imputation Recently, there has been growing interest in applying genera-
102   tive methods to MTSI due to their ability to learn the original data distribution, making them highly
103   suitable for addressing this issue. Variational Autoencoders (VAE) have been used in this context
104   for several years (Fortuin et al., 2020). However, interest in generative techniques for imputation
105   exploded following the introduction of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), with the ground-
106   breaking work of Generative Adversarial Imputation Networks (GAIN) (Yoon et al., 2018) marking
107   a significant milestone. Following this development, numerous GAN applications emerged in MTSI,
      often incorporating RNN layers to analyze temporal sequences (Luo et al., 2018; Miao et al., 2021).


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Nevertheless, the issue of model collapse in GANs (Thanh-Tung & Tran, 2020), coupled with the
109   emergence of Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs) and their superior ability to cap-
110   ture diverse and high-quality distributions, has led to a shift away from GANs. Research such as
111   CSDI by Tashiro et al. (2021) demonstrated how DDPMs, with the right conditional information,
112   could efficiently address MTSI challenges. Further studies, such as those by Yun et al. (2023), con-
113   firmed that unconditional DDPMs might fail, whereas Liu et al. (2023) enhanced DDPMs’ ability to
114   generate better conditional information through specialized layers, furthering their effectiveness in
115   MTSI applications.
116
117
      State-Space Models State-Space Models (SSMs) (Hangos et al., 2006), derived from control the-
118
      ory to model system dynamics, have been successfully adapted to the Deep Learning (DL) realm
119
      as Structured State Space Models (S4) (Gu et al., 2022b). These models, similar to RNNs, have
120   recently demonstrated their proficiency in modeling diverse sequences, leading to their application
121   in text, audio, and even video-related problems (Gu et al., 2022a). Like RNNs, however, they lack
122   an attention mechanism, which is crucial for avoiding error accumulation during tasks such as im-
123   putation.
124
      Nevertheless, the latest iteration, Selective State Space Models (S6), has incorporated an attention
125
      mechanism into the SSM structure, sparking a surge in applications. These models have been em-
126   ployed in selective copy tasks, language modeling, DNA modeling, and audio modeling and gen-
127   eration (Gu & Dao, 2023), occasionally outperforming traditional transformers. Specifically, in the
128   field of time series imputation with DDPMs, the application of S4 has so far been limited to the
129   study by (Alcaraz & Strodthoff, 2023), while S6 remains unexplored.

      3.1   M ULTIVARIATE TIME SERIES IMPUTATION
136
137   A multivariate time series (MTS) consists of Nt variables or channels recorded at different time
138
      instants denoted by t. Consequently, these data can be represented as Xt ∈ RNt ×d , where each row
      i contains the d-dimensional vector xit ∈ Rd , associating the ith variable with the time instant t.
139
      Additionally, a matrix Mt ∈ {0, 1}Nt ×d is used, where each entry contains a 0 if the corresponding
140
      value in Xt is missing, and a 1 if it is an observed data point.
141
142   Considering this, we define Xft as the unknown ground truth variable-measure matrix, i.e., the com-
143   plete time series without missing data; Xct denotes the time series imputed by the model, and X the
144   series imputed by linear interpolation technique.
145
      Finally, given that our work will focus on graph-based models, we model the time series as a se-
146   quence of graphs following the approach established by Cini et al. (2022). In this approach, each
147   instant t is defined as a graph Gt with Nt nodes at each time instant. Moreover, the graph is defined
148   as Gt = ⟨Xt , At ⟩, where Xt represents the data matrix and At represents the adjacency matrix at
149   instant t. However, this paper only considers the approach in which the graph topology does not
150   change over time, thus A is constant for each t.

      4.2   M ODEL ARCHITECTURE
241
242   Our architecture builds upon the foundational design by Tashiro et al. (2021) and incorporates en-
243   hancements introduced by Liu et al. (2023). Referring to Figure 1, and following the information
244   flow from left to right, we begin with the Conditional Feature Extraction Module (CFEM) (Liu et al.,
245   2023). This module is responsible for generating conditional information that aids other components
246   in improving imputation quality. The CFEM takes X and A as inputs and processes temporal data
247   with bidirectional Mamba blocks, replacing the original transformers—a modification we propose
248
      and will justify subsequently. Spatial data is processed using transformer and Message Passing Neu-
      ral Network (MPNN) blocks, inspired by Wu et al. (2019). This information is then refined with an
249
      MLP to produce H pri , as illustrated in the CFEM bubble of Figure 1. The operation is defined as:
250
      H pri = CFEM(X , A).
251
252   Afterwards, a series of Noise Estimation Modules (NEM) (Tashiro et al., 2021) is employed to esti-
253   mate the final noise. These modules receive H pri , the diffusion time embedding t, A, and H in . For
254
      the initial NEM, H in is a concatenation of noisy Xt and X . The output generates two information
      flows: one as input for the next NEM block (acting as H in ) and another as H out , serving as the
255
      initial noise estimation. Outputs from each NEM are aggregated and processed by a convolutional
256
      layer to finalize the noise estimation. The internal workings of these blocks are akin to CFEM, as
257   depicted in the NEM bubble in Figure 1, and described by: H out = NEM(H in , H pri , A, t).
258
259   Now, we will discuss our decision to replace the transformer blocks, which focus on the temporal
260
      dimension, with Mamba blocks. Originally, this architecture utilized transformers for the temporal
      processing of information. However, despite their known advantages in MTSI, transformers lack
261
      an intrinsic inductive bias for temporal data. In contrast, Mamba blocks provide this bias while
262
      incorporating attention mechanisms, making them more suitable for accurately capturing temporal
263   relationships. Therefore, we propose replacing time-focused transformers with Mamba blocks.
264
265   Furthermore, the mechanism within Mamba blocks’ S6 layers resembles that of RNNs. This similar-
266
      ity allows us to adopt a BiRNN-like approach, where each Mamba block processes time series bidi-
      rectionally. Inspired by the Vision Mamba block (Zhu et al., 2024), which processes image patches
267
      in both directions, we have modified this approach to work effectively with NEM and CFEM blocks.
268
269   Our bidirectional block, depicted in Figure 2, processes a data tensor. If it is a NEM block, it
      concatenates H pri and applies layer normalization. Dropout is employed for regularization, and

      5.1   DATASETS
317
318   We employed datasets identical to those used in Cini et al. (2022), which established benchmarks
319   later used as comparison metrics (Tashiro et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023). The AQI-36 dataset (Yi
320   et al., 2016) comprises air quality data from 36 stations in Beijing, recorded hourly over a year,
321   with 13.24% missing values. We also utilized the METR-LA and PEMS-BAY datasets (Li et al.,
322   2018), documenting traffic in Los Angeles and the San Francisco Bay Area, respectively. METR-
323   LA includes data from 207 sensors over four months with an 8.10% missing rate, while PEMS-BAY
      covers 325 sensors over six months with 0.02% missing values, both sampled every five minutes.

      Consistent with the Cini et al. (2022) benchmark, we replicated the adjacency matrices using thresh-
325   olded Gaussian Kernels on geographical distances, as suggested in (Li et al., 2018) and (Wu et al.,
326   2019). The same training, validation, and testing splits were used: 70%, 10%, and 20% of the data,
327   respectively, with identical seeds. For AQI-36, testing was conducted in February, May, August, and
328   November, as originally designated, with 10% validation samples using the same seed.
329
      Evaluation of these datasets includes three scenarios with synthetic missing data. In the ”Block
330
      missing” scenario, 5% of the data was randomly masked, and 1-4 hour blocks were masked at each
331   sensor with a 0.15% probability; in the ”Point missing” scenario, 25% of the values were randomly
332   masked; in the last scenario, missing values are simulated with the same distribution as the original
333   data.

337
338   We compared our proposed method against established benchmark methods originally presented in
339
      the GRIN article (Cini et al., 2022), and subsequently extended by CSDI (Tashiro et al., 2021) and
      PriSTI (Liu et al., 2023). To ensure the accuracy of our comparison, we replicated the experiments
340
      of CSDI and PriSTI using the parameters reported in their original papers. The only modification
341
      made was the adjustment of small differences found in the training and validation splits of the AQI-
342   36 dataset between CSDI, PriSTI and GRIN to maintain a fair comparison. The evaluated methods
343   are:
344
345
      1) Statistical techniques: MEAN (using historical mean values), DA (daily averages), and KNN
      (geographical proximity). 2) Machine learning algorithms: Lin-ITP (linear interpolation), MICE
346
      (White et al., 2011) (multiple imputation), VAR (vector autoregressive model), and KF (Kalman
347
      Filter). 3) Matrix factorization strategies: TRMF (Yu et al., 2016) (temporal regularized ma-
348   trix factorization) and BATF (Chen et al., 2019) (Bayesian augmented tensor factorization). 4)
349   Autoregressive models: BRITS (Cao et al., 2018) (bidirectional RNN), MPGRU (one-step-ahead
350   GNN-based predictor similar to DCRNN (Li et al., 2018)), GRIN (Cini et al., 2022). 5) Generative
351   models: CSDI (Tashiro et al., 2021), PriSTI (Liu et al., 2023), V-RIN (Mulyadi et al., 2021) (VAE
352   with quantified uncertainty), GP-VAE (Fortuin et al., 2020) (VAE combined with Gaussian process),
353   and rGAIN (Yoon et al., 2018) (GAN-based imputation with a recurrent structure).

      5.3   E XPERIMENTAL SETTINGS
356
357
      For DDPMs, 100 imputations were generated per missing value, with the median serving as the final
358
      imputation. Additionally, all experiments have been repeated 3 times using different seeds. During
359   training, four synthetic missing data generation techniques were used:
360
361   1) Point strategy: A random [0, 100]% of data was masked per batch. 2) Block strategy: Se-
362
      quences of missing values of length [L/2, L] were generated with a [0, 15]% probability, plus an
      additional 5% missing. 3) Historical strategy: Real imputation masks already present in the data
363
      were used. 4) Hybrid strategy: Each training sample was masked using the point strategy as a first
364
      option, or block or historical strategies as a second option with a 50% probability.
365
366   For the ”Point missing” scenarios described in Section 5.1, the point strategy was used. For ”Block
367   missing” scenarios, the hybrid strategy was applied with the block strategy as the secondary option.
368
      For AQI-36, the hybrid strategy with the historical strategy as the secondary option was employed.
369   It is important to highlight that for the benchmark comparison in Section 5.4.1, the CSDI, PriSTI,
370   and TIMBA models were trained for the number of epochs specified in their original papers: 200
371   epochs for the AQI-36 dataset and 300 epochs for the Traffic datasets. However, for the remaining
372   experiments discussed in Section 5.4, the training epochs were limited to 50 due to time constraints
373   during the execution of the experiments.
374   Finally, as in the original work by Tashiro et al. (2021), the model was trained with a learning rate
375   of 10−3 , which was reduced to 10−4 after 75% of the training epochs, and again to 10−5 after
376   90%. Consistent with Tashiro et al. (2021), a quadratic scheduler was used for the noise scheduling.
377   Detailed hyperparameter settings, as well as information on training and inference times, and code
      reproducibility, are provided in Appendix A.

508   5.4.5   L IMITATIONS
509
      Analyzing the limitations of our model, it should be noted that our method does not assume any
510
      distribution in the missing values beyond their occurrence through a stationary process. Throughout
511
      the paper, we mainly focus on the missing at random (MAR) scenario (Rubin, 1976). Beyond this,
512   we make no assumptions about the amount or length of the missing data.

515   6   C ONCLUSIONS
516
517   In this paper, we presented TIMBA, a model that replaces the time-oriented transformer layers in
518   state-of-the-art diffusion models for multivariate time series imputation with bidirectional Mamba
519   blocks. Our extensive benchmark with three real-world datasets demonstrates that TIMBA either
520
      surpasses or performs comparably to the current state-of-the-art. Additionally, we showed that
      TIMBA can scale effectively with longer temporal sequences, generally achieving better results
521
      as the number of time steps per sample increases.
522
523   For future work, it would be valuable to explore ways to further reduce training and inference time,
524   potentially through the application of latent diffusion models or denoising diffusion implicit models.
525   Additionally, it would be interesting to apply our architecture to time series forecasting problems.

      by compressing real nodes into a defined number of virtual nodes k.
