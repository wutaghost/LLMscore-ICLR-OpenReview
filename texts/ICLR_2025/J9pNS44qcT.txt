011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Sparse reward environments consistently challenge reinforcement learning, as
014            agents often need to finish tasks before receiving any feedback, leading to lim-
015
               ited incentive signals. This issue becomes even more pronounced in multi-agent
               systems (MAS), where a single reward must be distributed among multiple agents
016
               over time, frequently resulting in suboptimal or inconsistent learning outcomes.
017
               To tackle this challenge, we introduce a novel approach called Collaborative
018            Multi-dimensional Course Learning (CCL) for multi-agent cooperation scenar-
019            ios. CCL features three key innovations: (1) It establishes an adaptive curriculum
020            framework tailored for MAS, refining intermediate tasks to individual agents to
021            ensure balanced strategy development. (2) A novel variant evolution algorithm
022            creates more detailed intermediate tasks. (3) Co-evolution between agents and
023            their environment is modeled to enhance training stability under sparse reward
024            conditions. In evaluations across five tasks within multi-particle environments
025            (MPE) and Hide and Seek (Hns), CCL demonstrated superior performance, sur-
026
               passing existing benchmarks and excelling in sparse reward settings.

028   1   INTRODUCTION
029
030   Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) has shown promising results in addressing various challenges
031   within Multi-Agent Systems (MAS), including applications in robotics (Abbass et al., 2021)per-
032   rusquia2021multi, gaming (Rashid et al., 2020), and autonomous driving (Shalev-Shwartz et al.,
033   2016). However, in sparse reward environments, reinforcement learning methods still face limi-
034   tations regarding incentivization and learning efficiency, as agents typically receive feedback only
035   upon task completion. This feedback mechanism dramatically increases agents’ difficulty in effi-
036   ciently exploring their environment and extracting meaningful insights from their actions.
037   In order to deal with the challenge of sparse reward, several methods have been proposed to improve
038   the exploration efficiency, including reward reshaping (Ng et al., 1999; Hu et al., 2020), learning
039   from demonstrations (Ross et al., 2011), policy transfer (Duan et al., 2017), and curriculum learning
040   (Florensa et al., 2017; 2018). The core goal of these strategies is to enhance the agent’s exploration
041   ability by reinforcing the reward signal during training. Although these methods have achieved
042   remarkable results in single-agent environments, their application in MAS faces many challenges.
043   In MAS, the interactive decision-making of multiple agents often enhances environment dynamics
044
      and the sharp expansion of state space, which often weakens the effectiveness of the above strategies.
045   However, it is worth noting that most of the above methods have shown satisfactory performance
046   performance in single-agent tasks. However, once entering the environment of a Multi-Agent Sys-
047   tem (MAS), the coexistence of multiple decision-making entities inevitably leads to the reduction
048   of environmental stability (Bloembergen et al., 2015; Buşoniu et al., 2010) and the rapid expansion
049   of state space (Hernandez-Leal et al., 2019). Such changes often considerably weaken the original
050
      effectiveness of these strategies and may even make them invalid.
051   This paper proposes a new framework called Coevolving Multidirectional Curriculum Learning
052   (CCL), which aims to deal with cooperative decision-making problems in sparse-reward MAS.
053   CCL innovatively combines automatic Curriculum learning (ACL) technology, which automatically
      generates and prioritizes intermediate tasks to minimize the dependence on and bias from exist-


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      ing knowledge. Unlike traditional ACL methods, CCL is uniquely designed to optimize two key
055   dimensions in MAS:
056
057   1. Refining Reward Granularity to Enhance Agent Interaction: CCL implements finer-grained
058
      control by focusing on the unique perspective of individual agents during task generation. By lever-
      aging a variational evolutionary algorithm, CCL can precisely decompose complex intermediate
059
      tasks in MAS, enabling balanced and optimized strategy development for each agent while simulta-
060
      neously working toward the overall objective.
061
062   2. Cooperative Iteration to Enhance Training Stability: CCL employs an evolutionary algorithm
063   based on cooperative co-evolution (Antonio & Coello, 2017), allowing for the simultaneous ad-
064
      vancement of intermediate task evolution and agent policy development. This synchronization en-
      sures task difficulty aligns with the agent’s current skill level, preventing inefficient training caused
065
      by either too complex or too simple tasks. As a result, this approach facilitates the co-evolution of
066
      tasks and agent capabilities, significantly improving training stability and efficiency.
067
068   In a comprehensive experimental evaluation, CCL exhibits outstanding performance, surpassing ex-
069   isting curriculum learning approaches and achieving industry-leading results across five cooperative
070
      multi-agent tasks with sparse rewards.Further ablation experiment analysis reveals that compared
      with the traditional evolutionary methods in the field of MAS, the variational individual-perspective
071
      evolutionary algorithm adopted by CCL shows significant advantages, which verifies its unique
072
      value in improving the cooperation efficiency and effect of multi-agent systems.

089
090   In the reinforcement learning framework, the reward signal is a vital feedback mechanism that helps
091   the agent assess its actions at each time step. By utilizing the Bellman equation (Kaelbling et al.,
092   1996), the agent can develop a policy to maximize long-term cumulative rewards. As the reward
093   function defines the core objective of the learning task and provides a quantitative measure of the
094   agent’s behavior, it is crucial to design a well-structured and compelling reward system. It is essen-
095   tial to recognize that agents may still follow suboptimal or inefficient action paths even with a clearly
096   defined reward function. However, a well-designed reward function can significantly facilitate the
097   agent’s ability to learn and converge toward an optimal policy (Dewey, 2014).
098   However, designing an appropriate dense reward function faces many challenges in the complex
099   MAS environment. This is mainly because the setting of dense rewards is often limited by re-
100   searchers’ prior knowledge, making it challenging to comprehensively cover all possible interaction
101   scenarios and dynamic changes. In contrast, the sparse reward setting provides a more flexible and
102   effective solution, which only provides a single reward signal when MAS achieves a predefined crit-
103   ical goal state g (Booth et al., 2023), to get rid of the excessive dependence on the prior knowledge
104
      of researchers and effectively overcome the limitations of dense reward design.
105   In non-sparse reward settings, at each time step t, the agent observes its current state st ∈ S and
106   selects an action at ∈ A based on its policy π(at |st ). The chosen action results in a transition
107   to a new state st+1 , determined by the environment’s transition dynamics p(st+1 |st , at ), and an
      associated reward rt is obtained from the reward function r(st , at , st+1 ). The sequence of states,

      3     RELATED WORK
183
184
      3.1   CURRICULUM LEARNING
185
186   To deal with the exploration problem caused by sparse rewards, the field of reinforcement learning
187   has introduced a variety of strategies, including Reward Shaping (Laud, 2004), Intrinsic Motivation
188   (Barto, 2013), and Curriculum Learning (Bengio et al., 2009). Among them, reward shaping and
189   intrinsic motivation both supplement the original task reward information in the way of gain; that is,
190   they increase the density of the reward in an additional way. Curriculum learning, on the other hand,
191   takes a different divide-and-conquer strategy. It breaks the complex subject task into a series of more
192
      manageable subtasks to solve and sorts them according to certain logic to achieve the goal step by
      step. Given the high similarity between this step-by-step training mode and curriculum design in
193
      education (Abbass et al., 2021; Rohde & Plaut, 1999; Elman, 1993), the strategy is vividly termed
194
      Curriculum Learning.
195
196   Within the field of reinforcement learning, the core of the curriculum learning framework can be
197   deconstructed into three pillar elements (Narvekar et al., 2017): first, the generation of tasks; second,
198
      the ranking strategy for these tasks; and finally, the application of transfer learning. These key
      components’ construction process can be guided by the power of automated generators (Florensa
199
      et al., 2017) and expert domain knowledge. However, given the biases or limitations that may be
200
      implied in expert domain knowledge (Wang et al., 2019; Cobbe et al., 2019), the schedule Adaptive
201   Automatic Course Learning (ACL) method shows superior performance in the face of complex and
202   changing scenarios. The CCL method discussed in this paper is one in the frontier field of ACL.
203
204
      The core challenges of ACL focus on the selection of metrics and the optimization of computational
      efficiency. Specifically, ACL aims to achieve immediate and accurate evaluation (Ren et al., 2018;
205
      Wu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024) of intermediate tasks without the assistance of expert knowledge.
206
      However, the current methods are generally rough in metrics, making it challenging to deal com-
207   prehensively with the complex policy hierarchy in MAS. In addition, whether it is a comprehensive
208   evaluation of all intermediate tasks or the replay technology based on regret mechanism (Samvelyan
209   et al., 2023; Parker-Holder et al., 2022), it may bring high computational cost. Therefore, in prac-
210   tical applications, ensuring the quality of tasks while reasonably controlling the computational cost
211   has become an urgent problem that needs to be solved.
212
213   3.2   EVOLUTIONARY REINFORCEMENT LEARNING
214
215   Evolutionary Algorithm (EA) is a technique for solving optimization problems based on the natural
      evolutionary mechanism. Starting from a set of initial candidate solutions (i.e., population), they


                                                             4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      retain the best individuals through the selection process based on the fitness evaluation of each solu-
217   tion and then use mutation and recombination operations to continuously generate new populations
218   in the iterative process to approach the optimal solution (Beyer & Schwefel, 2002) gradually.
219
220
      Given the excellent performance shown by reinforcement learning and evolutionary algorithms in
      their respective fields, the research on their integration has always been the focus of academic at-
221
      tention (Miconi et al., 2020; Pagliuca et al., 2020; Jianye et al., 2022). The introduction of an
222
      evolutionary algorithm aims to make up for the critical shortcomings faced in the process of rein-
223   forcement learning, such as the problem of long-term reward information (Samvelyan et al., 2023;
224   Parker-Holder et al., 2022) backtracking and the lack of strategy diversity (Long et al., 2020). How-
225   ever, this integration path is not smooth, and a series of challenges accompany it. On the one hand,
226   the high computational cost caused by the large population size (Wang et al., 2019) becomes a
227   non-negligible obstacle. On the other hand, how to effectively retain and utilize the environmen-
228   tal information to prevent its loss in the evolutionary operation and encoding stage of evolutionary
229   reinforcement learning is also an urgent problem to be solved.

      5.2   A BLATION S TUDIES
355
      Adaptive Mutation Step: As mentioned earlier, an adaptive mutation step size provides more flex-
356   ibility than a fixed step size, especially for more straightforward expansion tasks. Our ablation
357   studies evaluated three conditions: adaptive mutation step size, fixed mutation step size, and no mu-
358   tation. While the mutation operation generally increases strategy diversity within the population,
359   an unsuitable mutation step size in sparse reward environments can adversely affect CCL’s perfor-
360   mance. Notably, with the adaptive mutation step size, the mutation operation’s effectiveness in CCL
361   matches that of crossover and variation techniques focusing solely on individual perspectives, as
362   shown in Fig. 3.
363   Non-linear Factor in Fitness Function: As shown in Fig.4, the sigmoid fitness function delivers
364   better performance than the linear form f˜ = −k|r − 0.5|. This improvement stems from the sigmoid
365   function’s properties: as the agent’s success rate approaches 0 or 1, the task’s suitability to the agent’s
366   abilities decreases exponentially. Specifically, when the success rate is exactly 0.5, the fitness value
367   remains consistently at 0.5. This approach effectively integrates nonlinear elements into the success
368   rate distribution, enabling the fitness function to more accurately represent the relationship between
369   task difficulty and the agent’s skill level.
