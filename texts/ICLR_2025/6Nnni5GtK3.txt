009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011              Visual prompting (VP) is a new technique that adapts well-trained frozen models
012              for source domain tasks to target domain tasks. This study examines VP’s benefits
013              for black-box model-level backdoor detection. The visual prompt in VP maps class
014              subspaces between source and target domains. We identify a misalignment, termed
015              class subspace inconsistency, between clean and poisoned datasets. Based on
016              this, we introduce BP ROM, a black-box model-level detection method to identify
017
                 backdoors in suspicious models, if any. BP ROM leverages the low classification
                 accuracy of prompted models when backdoors are present. Extensive experiments
018
                 confirm BP ROM’s effectiveness.

021   1       I NTRODUCTION
022
023   Deep neural networks (DNNs) are commonly used in complex applications but require extensive
024   computational power, leading to significant costs. Users often access these models through online
025   platforms like BigML model market1 and ONNX zoo2 , or via Machine Learning as a Service (MLaaS)
026   platforms. However, DNNs can include backdoors (Gu et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2018b; Tang et al.,
027   2021; Qi et al., 2023b; Nguyen & Tran, 2021; Chen et al., 2017), which manipulate model responses
028   to inputs with specific triggers (like certain pixel patterns) while functioning correctly on other inputs.
029
      In backdoor attacks, attackers embed these triggers in the training data, leading the model to associate
      the trigger with a particular outcome and misclassify inputs containing it.
030
031   Why Black-Box Model-Level Detection. Black-box backdoor detection, which uses only black-
032
      box queries to the suspicious model (i.e., the model to be inspected), is gaining attention. This
      detection method is divided into input-level (Li et al., 2021c; Qiu et al., 2021; Gao et al., 2022; Liu
033
      et al., 2023; Qi et al., 2023c; Zeng et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023; Hou et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024; Mo
034
      et al., 2024) and model-level (Huang et al., 2020; Dong et al., 2021; Guo et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2019;
035   Wang et al., 2024) techniques. Input-level detection identifies trigger samples in an infected model,
036   while model-level detection determines if a model contains backdoors. Input-level detection relies
037   on the model having backdoors; otherwise, its accuracy drops significantly. For example, as shown
038   in Table 1, TeCo (Liu et al., 2023) and SCALE-UP (Guo et al., 2023), state-of-the-art input-level
039   detectors, show AUROCs of 0.8113 and 0.7877, respectively, on a BadNets-infected model (Gu et al.,
040   2017), but only 0.4509 and 0.5103 on a clean model. If a model is clean, many legitimate samples
041   may be misclassified as triggers, reducing the model’s practical utility. Thus, model-level detection
042   should be performed first. If backdoors are found but the model must still be used, input-level
043
      detection should then be applied to each input.
044
      Design Challenge. Despite its importance, black-box model-level detection faces two main chal-
      lenges. First, unlike input-level detection, which benefits from the presence of an infected model,
045
      model-level detection has limited ground truth, relying on only a few clean samples. Second, it needs
046
      a stable feature to differentiate between clean and infected models across various backdoor types,
047   which is difficult to find. For instance, B3D (Dong et al., 2021) targets trigger localization but is
048   mainly effective for patch-based triggers. Similarly, AEVA (Guo et al., 2022) may struggle with
049   larger triggers due to its dependence on adversarial peak analysis.
050
      Our Design. Visual prompting (VP) (Bahng et al., 2022; Jia et al., 2022) allows a frozen, pre-trained
051   model from a source domain to correctly predict samples from a target domain by applying a visual

079   In an infected model, the target class subspace in the feature space is adjacent to all other class

138   Backdoor Attack Methods. Badnets (Gu et al., 2017) introduced the first backdoor attack on
139   DNNs, with many following works adopting its approach to poison training datasets. Backdoor
140
      attacks are categorized by trigger appearance into universal (Chen et al., 2017; Gu et al., 2017; Zeng
      et al., 2021), where all triggers are identical, and sample-specific (Li et al., 2020; Nguyen & Tran,
141
      2020; Salem et al., 2022), where triggers vary per sample. Subsequent developments include invisible
142
      backdoors (Doan et al., 2021; Li et al., 2020; Nguyen & Tran, 2021), which are harder to detect by
143   human inspection, and clean-label backdoors (Zhao et al., 2020; Ning et al., 2021; Shafahi et al., 2018;
144   Turner et al., 2018), which stealthily poison target class samples without label changes. Additionally,
145   anti-defense attacks (Qi et al., 2023b) circumvent detection by preventing latent separation.
146
147
148   Backdoor Detection Methods. Backdoor detection is categorized into white-box and black-box.
149   White-box detection (Liu et al., 2018a; Wu & Wang, 2021; Li et al., 2021a; Xia et al., 2022; Du et al.,
150   2020; Li et al., 2021b; Huang et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2019; Hu et al., 2022; Tao et al., 2022; Wei
151   et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2024) requires access to a poisoned training set or model
152
      parameters. Some methods identify backdoors, while others remove them. However, it is unsuitable
      for MLaaS applications and safety-critical deployments (e.g., autonomous vehicles).
153
154   Black-box detection only requires access to the suspicious model, making it more applicable. It is
155   divided into input-level and model-level. Input-level detection (Li et al., 2021c; Qiu et al., 2021; Gao
156   et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023; Qi et al., 2023c; Zeng et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023; Xian et al., 2024; Ma
157   et al., 2022; Pan et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024; Hou et al., 2024; Xu
158
      et al., 2024) distinguishes trigger samples from benign ones. Since infected models act benign except
      for trigger samples, they can be used safely if detection works per input. However, this can result in
159
      high false positives, rejecting many benign samples if the model is clean, as shown in Table 1.
160
161   This paper focuses on model-level detection (Huang et al., 2020; Dong et al., 2021; Guo et al., 2022;
      Xu et al., 2019; Shi et al., 2024; Xiang et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2023; Rezaei et al., 2023; Wang et al.,

      2024), which identifies backdoors in suspicious models and serves as front-line detection before
163   input-level methods.

166   3     BACKGROUND K NOWLEDGE
167
168   Both visual prompting (VP) (Chen et al., 2023; Bahng et al., 2022; Jia et al., 2022) and model
169   reprogramming (MR) (Tsai et al., 2020; Chen, 2024; Elsayed et al., 2019; Neekhara et al., 2022)
170
      enable a frozen pre-trained model for one task to perform a different target domain classification task
      by deriving a visual prompt for inputs from the target domain. Initially, MR was considered an attack
171
      that misused cloud services (i.e., MLaaS) to perform undocumented tasks (Elsayed et al., 2019). VP
172
      was recently introduced in (Bahng et al., 2022). Although VP and MR share the same concept, VP
173   focuses exclusively on images. VP has been extended to image inpainting (Bar et al., 2022), antibody
174   sequence infilling (Melnyk et al., 2023), and differentially private classifiers (Li et al., 2023b). In
175   this paper, VP and MR are used interchangeably, with the visual prompt in VP corresponding to the
176   trainable noise in MR. More formally, VP/MR proceeds with four steps (Chen, 2024).
177
      1. Initialization: Let fS (·) and DT = {(xT , yT )} be the source model (the model trained from the
178
      source domain dataset) and the target domain dataset, respectively. Randomly initialize θ and w
179   (defined below).
180
181   2. Visual prompt padding: Obtain the prompted input sample x̃T = V (xT |θ), where θ is the visual
182   prompt. A common method for V (·) is to resize xT and add the visual prompt (trainable noise)
183   around it. Although x̃T visually differs from the source domain, it can still be used as input for the
184
      source domain classifier. Figure 1a illustrates this with xT as “3” from MNIST, θ in the middle, and
      V (·) resizing xT and padding it with θ.
185
186   3. Output mapping: Obtain the target task prediction via ŷT = O(fS (x̃T )|w), where w represents
187   the trainable parameters for output label mapping. This step is optional for VP/MR. In our experiment,
188   we omitted this step.
189   4. Prompted model training: Optimize θ and w by minimizing a task-specific loss L(ŷT , yT ) on DT .
190
191   After executing the four-step procedure, we obtain the prompted model fT = O ◦ fS ◦ V from fS (·)
192
      with optimized θ∗ and optionally w∗ . This results in ŷT = O(fS (V (xT |θ∗ ))|w∗ ).
193
194   4     S YSTEM M ODEL
195
196   Threat Model. We consider two roles: attacker and defender. The attacker’s goal aligns with
197   previous work (Gu et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2017; Tang et al., 2021; Qi et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2018b).
198   Specifically, the attacker poisons the training dataset by injecting trigger samples. The DNN model
199   (e.g., an image classifier) trained on this poisoned dataset behaves normally with clean inputs but
200   always predicts an attacker-specified target class for inputs with a trigger. Essentially, an all-to-one
201   backdoor is implanted, mapping all trigger inputs to a specific target class.
202
203   Defender’s Goal and Capability. The defender’s goal is to detect if a suspicious model is back-
204   doored, primarily measured by AUROC (see Section 6). The defender has limited abilities: no access
205
      to the poisoned dataset, model structure, or parameters. In MLaaS applications, detection involves
      only black-box queries on the model to obtain confidence vectors. The defender also has a small
206
      reserved clean dataset DS (1%, 5%, 10% of the test dataset in our experiment) to aid detection.

      We overview the experimental setup, including datasets, model architectures, attack methods, and
327   defense baselines, consistent with recent works (Qi et al., 2023c; Guo et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2020;
328   Liu et al., 2023). We then present the experimental results and hyperparameter study.
329
330   6.1      E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
331
332   Datasets and Model Architectures. We use five image datasets: CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky, 2009),
333   GTSRB (Stallkamp et al., 2011), and STL-10 (Coates et al., 2011), Tiny-ImageNet (Le & Yang, 2015),
334   and ImageNet (Russakovsky et al., 2015). For a suspicious model fS (·) trained on CIFAR-10, GTSRB,
335   Tiny-ImageNet or ImageNet, we first train shadow models fi ’s using an α% (α ∈ {1, 5, 10}) subset
336   of the corresponding test set as DS . Then, we apply VP on fi ’s using STL-10 as DT to obtain the
337   corresponding prompted models f˜i ’s. We experiment with ResNet18 and MobileNetV2 architectures,
338
      training models on each DSi and DTtrain using standard procedures. For the meta-classifier fmeta , we
      use a random forest with 10,000 trees to detect backdoors based on confidence vectors. We mainly
339
      use Area Under the ROC Curve (AUROC) and F1-score to measure the detection effectiveness of
340
      backdoor detection methods. Our experiments were performed on a workstation equipped with a
341   16-core Intel i9 CPU (64GB RAM) and an RTX4090 GPU.
342
343   Attack Methods and Defense Baselines. We evaluate BP ROM against 9 backdoor poisoning
344   attacks from the Backdoor Toolbox3 , including classical dirty label, clean label, sample-specific
345   trigger, and adaptive attacks. Default hyperparameters are used to ensure at least 98% attack success
346   rate. We compare BP ROM with 10 backdoor defenses either from Backdoor Toolbox or from their
347   official code. Default hyperparameters are used for each defense.
348
349   6.2      E XPERIMENTAL R ESULT
350
351   We know from class subspace inconsistency that a prompted model’s accuracy degrades if the
352   suspicious model is backdoored. We conducted experiments with backdoor attacks using varying
353
      trigger sizes (4 × 4, 8 × 8, 16 × 16 pixels) and poisoning rates (5%, 10%, 20% of training data) to
      further examine the impact of class subspace inconsistency on prompted model accuracy. For each
354
      experiment, we generated a backdoor-infected model and prompted it for a new task on STL-10.
355
      These experiments also cover adaptive attacks, where BP ROM maintains high performance, achieving
356   an AUROC of 1 even at low poison rates (e.g., 0.2% for BadNets on CIFAR-10; see Section 6.4).

533   7     C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
534
535   We present BP ROM as a novel VP-based black-box model-level backdoor detection method. BP ROM
536   relies on class subspace inconsistency, where the prompted model’s accuracy degrades if the source
537   model is backdoored. This inconsistency is common in various backdoor attacks due to feature space
538   distortion from the poisoned dataset. Our experiments show BP ROM effectively detects all-to-one
539   backdoors. However, it struggles with all-to-all backdoors, as their feature space distortion is more
      controllable by the attacker. Addressing this limitation is left for future work.
