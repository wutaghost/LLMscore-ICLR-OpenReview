009                                                        ABSTRACT
010
011                Large language models (LLMs) with extended context windows are gaining attention.
012                However, whether these resource-intensive LLMs indeed surpass simpler Retrieval Aug-
013                mented Generation (RAG) techniques remains debatable. We precisely delineate differ-
014                ences between long-context LLMs and RAG methods, emphasizing the long-context
015                reasoning abilities of LLMs. Existing benchmarks for long-context models often focus
016                on information retrieval, hindering the assessment of reasoning over extended contexts.
017                We introduce the FACTOR benchmark (Factoring Analysis of Complexity and Textual
018                Context in Reasoning). FACTOR consists of two suites of tasks, covering both the
019
                   symbolic and real-world facets of reasoning evaluation. Both suites are carefully created to
                   delineate task complexity and context length when evaluating LLMs. We present detailed
020
                   evaluations of popular LLMs on FACTOR. Besides accuracy scores, we also model the
021
                   relationship between accuracy and task complexity. A simple but consistent log-linear
022                relationship works surprisingly well across various models. From the log-linear relation-
023                ship, two explainable parameters, the slope or Complexity Decay Factor (CDF) and the
024                y-intercept or Contextual Decay Offset (CDO), are shown to offer separate and insightful
025                measures of the models’ complex reasoning and long context innate ability. Our findings
026                highlight distinct failure modes linked to task complexity and context length, underscoring
027                the unique reasoning capabilities of long-context LLMs unattainable by RAG methods.1

      1       INTRODUCTION
030
031   Recently, large language models (LLMs) with extended context windows have gained attention in real-world
032   applications (Achiam et al., 2023; Team et al., 2024). With the advent of next-generation models (Dubey et al.,
033   2024), context lengths of up to 128K tokens are becoming the new norm. Despite these advancements, a
034
      persistent debate exists (Li et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024) regarding whether sophisticated and resource-intensive
      long-context LLMs genuinely offer advantages over more straightforward and cost-effective Retrieval
035
      Augmented Generation (RAG) techniques. This paper aims to precisely delineate the differences between
036
      what LLMs can accomplish with extended context capabilities and what is attainable through RAG methods.
037   We contend that long-context LLMs possess unique long-context reasoning abilities that are inherently
038   challenging for RAG-based methods to replicate.
039
      While existing benchmarks, such as RULER (Hsieh et al., 2024) and ∞Bench (Zhang et al., 2024), cover
040
      a range of tasks and are becoming the new paradigm for evaluating the long-context models, they often
041
      fail to capture the fundamental distinctions between the reasoning capabilities of long-context LLMs and
042   the retrieval strengths of RAG methods. Specifically, existing benchmarks exhibit two key limitations that
043   inadvertently favor RAG-based approaches: (1) they heavily present tasks focused on tasks that assess the
044   model’s retrieval ability for long context (key characteristics being the complexity independent of context
045   length) and (2) even though some tasks indeed see complexity increases with context length, e.g. Variable
046   Tracking (Hsieh et al., 2024), they are often still too simple, and doesn’t require model’s reasoning ability
047   to solve. We empirically showed that simple RAG techniques easily get perfect scores on the Variable Tracing
048   tasks. Using the MPnetv2 Song et al. (2020) as the encoder and Llama3.1 8B Instruct Dubey et al. (2024)
049   model as the generator, the system achieves 100% and 98% accuracy on 131k and 1M context length with
050
      only 1024 actual context during the retrieval. Experiments show that solving VT is viable through simple
      backtracking only the occurrence of the query variable without grasping the entire problem setting and states
051
      of other variables. We contend that benchmarks hide the unique but impressive reasoning advantages that

      Therefore, in this paper, we introduce the FACTOR (Factoring Analysis of Complexity and Textual COntext
      in Reasoning) benchmark, designed to systematically evaluate language models’ long context complex
077
      reasoning ability through carefully curated synthetic tasks that require models’ grasp of the entire problem to
078
      succeed. Similar to previous benchmarks, FACTOR disentangles task complexity and context length, each can
079   be independently varied. Specifically, the FACTOR benchmark relies on a suite of synthetic task generators
080   by adjusting the following two key knobs for independently controlling task complexity and context length.
081
082   • Number of Variables (Task Complexity): Defines the complexity of the reasoning task via the number
083     of interdependent variables. For most evaluations, the number of variables is limited to less than 40.
084   • Length of Filler Text (Context Length): To independently control the task complexity, we insert the
085     question prompt with text irrelevant to the necessary portion of logic arguments, referring to them as Filler
086     Tex. Filler text lengths are selected from predefined lengths: 0, 4K, 8K, 16K, 32K, 64K, and 128K tokens.
087
088   FACTOR consists of three subsets: Easy, Medium, and Hard. Easy subset consists of symbolic reasoning
089   tasks, where the model evaluated is asked to deduce from variables in ”Vx” for integer x. For Medium
090   and Hard, two of context in natural language and contains rich hidden operations from semantics. In all
091   three suites, the model is presented with long chains of variables, and they can only provide correct output
092
      when they correctly capture the relationship of all variables that appeared. The rest of the context length
      is filler text. Computation operators are limited to grade school level similar to GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021).

103

105   Offset (CDO) captures the model’s baseline performance at the given context length. We can separately
106   conclude both the model’s reasoning ability and the long-context tracking ability from CDF and CDO.
107   Specifically, benefiting from FACTOR design to isolate complexity and context length, we found that most
      LLMs generally exhibit the following two patterns.

      Our contribution can be summarized as follows:
136
      • Revealing a Log-Linear Accuracy Pattern in Long-Context Reasoning: In Section 5, we show that
137
        the relationship between task accuracy and complexity can be modeled using a simple and consistent
138     log-linear model.
139
140   • Identifying Mechanisms Behind Performance Decay: Also in Section 5, we uncover two primary
141     mechanisms causing the decay of performance in long-context reasoning: the degradation of logical
142     reasoning ability, quantified by the Complexity Decay Factor (CDF), and the decline in baseline
143
        performance with longer contexts, represented by the Contextual Decay Offset (CDO).
144   • Reproducing Failure Modes Through Fine-Tuning Strategies: In Section ??, our experiments show
145     that the observed failure modes can be reproduced using different fine-tuning methods—course learning
146     (as in Llama models) and mixed sequence length training (as in GPT-4o-mini). This highlights the impact
147     of training methodologies on models’ abilities to handle complex reasoning over long contexts.
148
      • Unveiling Limitations via Repeated Sampling: Further in Section ??, we investigate inference-time
149
        strategies like repeated sampling and find that, although they have potential to improve overall performance,
150
        inherent biases limit models’ abilities to indefinitely extend their reasoning capabilities. The longer the
151     context, the more challenging it becomes to recover performance levels seen with clean context training.

155   2.1     LONG-CONTEXT LANGUAGE MODELS
156   Various works related to the Long-context Language Model have been proposed. Flash attention(Dao et al.,
157   2022), Flash attention2(Dao, 2024), Ring attention(Liu et al., 2023a), and Tree attention(Shyam et al., 2024)
158   significantly reduced the memory footprint and communication overhead for processing long context in
159   engineering level across multiple nodes. Architectural level innovations such as sparse attentions represented
160   by sliding window attention(Beltagy et al., 2020), are also widely used to reduce the overhead caused by the
161   increasing sequence length. New training strategies, such as gradually extending the training context length
      in the final stages of pretraining have been applied to support a long context window(Dubey et al., 2024).

      2.2   LONG CONTEXT BENCHMARKS AND TASKS
163
164   There have been a quite a few works benchmarking long-context language models. Existing comprehensive
165
      benchmarks like ∞bench(Zhang et al., 2024) cover realistic tasks including document QA, summary, and
      synthetic tasks including information retrieval, expression calculation, extending the context length in the
166
      benchmark to over 200k tokens. ∞bench(Zhang et al., 2024) does have mathematical reasoning tasks,
167
      however the most relevant math.calc part seems to be too difficult for SOTA models to work out. Synthetic
168   tasks often offer more control and are less affected by parametric knowledge in comparison with realistic
169   tasks. One comprehensive synthetic benchmark is RULER(Hsieh et al., 2024), a synthetic benchmark with
170   tasks including retrieval, variable tracking and so on, offering some controls over context length and task
171   complexity. Experiments with various complexities were done, but it does not provide a quantitative analysis
172   of complexity and context length on the correctness of the task, let alone isolate two separate patterns of
173   performance decay. Other benchmarks usually focus on simple retrieval(Github, 2023; Liu et al., 2023b),
174   fact reasoning(Kuratov et al., 2024), the impact of long context on natural language reasoning(Levy et al.,
175   2024) and other real-world knowledge involved tasks.
176
177   2.3   SYNTHESIZED DATASETS FOR LONG-CONTEXT REASONING
178   Synthesized tasks are simple to build and absolutely deterministic, data contamination safe, but highly
179   effective to evaluate certain aspects of LLM performance. Its use in long-context benchmarks is profound.
180   Needle-in-the-haystack Kamradt (2023), a pioneering long-context synthesized task, now becomes the go-to
181   task for evaluating LLM long-context retrieval ability. On the other hand, LLM reasoning benchmarks also
182   see recent efforts in synthesized tasks. Mirzadeh et al. (2024) recently proposes to use build synthesized
183
      dataset upon GSM8K Cobbe et al. (2021) to study the robustness of LLM reasoning. Part of our work draws
      a strong inspiration from a series of works (Ye et al. (2024a), Ye et al. (2024b)) which systematically
184
      studies the intricacies of decoder transformers in solving grade-school level problems. Following their
185
      footsteps, we carefully redesign the process of generating the problems so current LLMs can solve without
186   training, and together with thoughtful steps in noise addition, we effectively construct effective reasoning
187   benchmarks for the long-context community.

255   4     THE FACTOR BENCHMARK
256
      We introduce the FACTOR (Factoring Analysis of Complexity and Textual COntext in Reasoning)
257
      benchmark. In the design, we intentionally disentangle the effects of task complexity and context length on
258   language model performance, providing a systematic framework for evaluating reasoning over long contexts.
259   FACTOR is subdivided into three different subsets Easy, Medium, and Hard. The task division is classified
260   based on the hierarchical depth of the semantics. The Easy subset consists of assignments and operations acting
261   on symbols, like ”Vi” for integer i. Therefore, every operation in this subset is explicit, meaning the model
262   directly follows the instruction to perform operations. More details in 4.1. The medium and hard are questions
263   that have the maximum hierarchical depth of 2 and 3 respectively. Detailed definition and construction in 4.2.
264
265   4.1   SYMBOLIC PORTION
266   Tasks are generated by first creating a set of variables {v0,v1,...,vN }, where N represents the task complexity.
267   Mathematical relationships among these variables (e.g., vi =vj ±1) are then established to form a dependency
268   graph. Consistent values that satisfy all relationships are assigned. These relationships are embedded within
269   filler text, the randomly generated text irrelavant to the logic components, to create contexts of varying lengths,
      representing different context lengths. To distinguish the variable relationships from the filler text, they are


                                                                5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      enclosed within triple angle brackets <<< and >>>, and further enclosed within @ symbols to separate
271   them from the surrounding content. This ensures that the relationships are clearly identifiable and not affected
272   by the filler text.
273
      To avoid falling back to Variable Tracking-like tasks that use backtracking for answer generation, we choose
274
      not to allow the model to calculate only the value of certain query variables. Instead, as shown in the example,
275
      we ask the model to output all the variables of a query value, which none can be a valid answer. In practice,
276   our approach works well in differentiating between strong and weak reasoning abilities across models. No
277   partial answer is allowed, so to consistently do the question correctly, the model is asked to traverse through
278   the entire computation graph.

294   4.2     COMMONSENSE PORTION
295   Then, we attempt to map the computation graph into the real-world context. Similar to the symbolic datasets,
296   we strictly control the difficulty level of each problem by the number of binary operations (two variables each)
297   needed to get to the final answer. Our method draws strong inspiration from a previous study Ye et al. (2024a).
298   Defining Hierarchical Depth - The key to designing the commonsense reasoning benchmark similar to
299   Cobbe et al. (2021) is to craft the hidden operations. The difficulty of hidden operations is controlled by
300   the hierarchical structure of the context. For example, the context of ”Animals in the Location” contains
301   two classes of objects (”Animals” and ”Location”) where Animals is the possession of Location. It is a
302   structure with hierarchical depth two. Given a context of two layers of objects, we have the most fundamental
303   hidden operation: addition. For example, ”The number of Beverly Forest’s Fox is 2. The number of Beverly
304   Forest’s Wolves is 3. Assuming there isn’t any other type of animal in Beverly Forest.” The total number
305
      of animals in Beverly Forest equals the sum of the first two objects, even though there isn’t any description
      of addition in the text. Context of higher hierarchical depth usually contains all possible relationships of
306
      the previous plus the newly emerged relationship pattern, making them harder to deduce.
307
308   On the other hand, adding ”number of children” to the previous context increments the hierarchical depth
309   to three. Continuing the previous sentence, ”The average number of children of Beverly Forest’s Wolves
310
      is 3, while the average number of children of Beverly Forest’s Fox is 2. What is the total number of Beverly
      Forest’s animal children?” To compute that we need to multiply each animal count with their average count
311
      of children and sum up all the animals. Similarly, the chain of operations isn’t narrated explicitly in the
312
      text, but it is intuitive for humans to apply. As an example, for depth of three, it contains the direct operations
313   in the Easy subset as well as the addition hidden operation, plus the sum of multiply. We naturally designate
314   problems with a depth of two to be in the Medium subset, and a depth of three to be in the Hard subset.
315   Below we present an example with FACTOR HARD where blue signifies abstract variables that appear
316   starting from Medium (depth 2), whereas cyan signifies abstract variables that only appear starting from
317   Hard (depth 3). Commonsense reasoning problems mostly contain depth 2 and depth 3 relationships (Ye
318   et al. (2024a)), where deeper hierarchy is rare even in normal natural language.
319   Making the solution - Similar to the Easy subset, we start from the randomly generated computational
320   graph, which is a DAG, and then attach numbers, forming abstract parameters to the graph. Out from the
321   computational graph, we randomly select a variable as the query variable, then perform a topological sort of the
322   computation graph. For the nodes on the topological sort list that aren’t pointed to by other variables, we initial-
323   ize their initial values and continue going through the sort list until the query variable. The entire chain is the
      solution, while the value of the query variable is the answer. Below we present a full example of FACTOR Hard.

337   4.3     REVERSE REASONING PATH
338
      Our problem construction mandates the use of a topological sort list, so all the hidden operations we added are
339   constructive, always either addition or multiplication. However, to enable hidden operations of subtraction and
340   division naturally through our generation pipeline, we also designate half of the problem in the benchmark
341   to be of the reverse reasoning path. For the reverse reasoning path, we first still construct the topological
342   list first. Then we initialize the previous query variable at the end of the sort list. Then, we assign the other
343   side of the sorted list, variables that no other nodes point to as the query variable. Therefore, since we go
344   in the complete opposite direction of the normal forwarding logic consisting of addition and multiplication,
345   we now have natural subtraction and division.
346   Overview of Commonsense subsets - For both the medium and hard subsets, we have half of the problem
347   in the normal forward, and half in the reverse path. Also, we deploy three different contextual templates
348   to diversify the problem text. These templates are ”location-animal-children”, ”city-school-teacher”, and
349   ”festival-movie-nomination”.
350
351   4.4     NOISE ADDITION
352   Adding noise is essential to enriching the length of the problem in the long context regime. For FACTOR,
353   we mainly explore two different directions of making the noise. For FACTOR Easy, we follow conventional
354   long context benchmarks and generate random noise to increase the context length. On the other hand,
355   for FACTOR Medium and Hard, Benefiting from our commonsense reasoning problem generator, we can
356   generate noise statements that are in the same format and semantics as the essential logic statements. In
357
      practice, we also encourage the noise statements to be noise variables pointed by essential variables to enlarge
      the connection between the core graph and the noise graph. This tight connection is the key to why RAG
358
      cannot solve the problem effectively. On the other hand, long context LLM isn’t sensitive to close noise.

      5     EVALUATION ON PRETRAINED MODELS

384   5.1    EVALUATION METRICS
385
      Besides just getting the accuracy, we also care about modeling the relationships between the accuracy and
386
      the complexity, thanks to the FACTOR design to isolate complexity and context length.
387
388   Two-Phase Accuracy Behavior - Models generally exhibit a characteristic two-phase behavior (Figure
389   3 (a)) in accuracy as the number of variables N increases (see Figure 3). Phase 1: For small values of N,
390
      models maintain near-perfect accuracy, effectively handling tasks with low complexity. Phase 2: Beyond
      a critical complexity threshold (Neff), accuracy declines exponentially with increasing N, indicating rapid
391
      degradation in performance for more complex tasks. This pattern suggests a limit to the task complexity
392
      that models can handle before performance significantly deteriorates to close zero, where it plateaus.
393
394   It is intuitive to fit the accuracy versus operations using logistic regression that can fit all two phases elegantly.
395
      Also, another candidate is a stepwise function with a constant for phase 1 following a exponential decay
      function. The only difference is at the transition between the two phases, where the logistic regression predicts
396
      a smooth transition, while exponential decay is a sharper one. Later, these two functions are mathematically
397
      similar, even close to identical when x gets better. We compare these two methods rigorously on O1-mini’s
398   hehavior, where we found that the step-wise gives 0.006 MSE score from ops 0-150, compared with 0.013
399   from logistic regression, suggesting that the true transition from top LLM is sharp. On the other hand, due to
400   lack of reasoning ability, most LLMs barely have the phase 1 displayed. Therefore, the focus of our analysis
401   now is on the decay stage, where the two candidates are very similar. We proceed with exponential decay
402   function as the modeling tool for later analysis because of its surprisingly strong vicinity to our collected
403   data. Extensive studies and fitting to justify using exponential function is presented in Appendix B.
404   Evaluation Metrics Definition - In Phase 2, accuracy A decreases exponentially with increasing N. By
405   taking the natural logarithm of accuracy, we linearize this decay:
406
                                                 log(A)=CDF×N +CDO
407
408   Where Complexity Decay Factor (CDF) is the negative slope of the line (CDF<0), representing the rate
409   at which accuracy decays with increasing task complexity; Contextual Decay Offset (CDO) is the intercept
410   of the line, capturing the baseline performance level influenced by context length. Our two-phase model
411   explains well the pattern of decreasing model accuracy with task complexity, and this model does not predict
412   accuracy outside the 0-1 range.
413   From these parameters, we define the Effective Complexity Neff, indicating the maximum task complexity the
414   model can handle before significant performance degradation. It is calculated as the value of N when the extrap-
415   olated log(A)=0 (i.e., when accuracy A=1):Neff =− CDO CDF . However, if the CDO is negative, the extrapolated
416   Neff becomes negative, which is not meaningful since we cannot have a negative number of variables. In such
417   cases, the two-phase behavior is not observed—the model’s accuracy declines from the outset without an initial
418   phase of high accuracy. Therefore, for negative CDO values, we focus on the exponential decay characterized
419   by the CDF and CDO. A more detailed description of data processing can be found in the Appendix D.

524   6    CONCLUSION
525
526
      In this paper, we introduced the FACTOR benchmark, a novel framework designed to systematically evaluate
      the complex reasoning abilities of large language models (LLMs) over long contexts. A key innovation
527
      of our work is the modeling of performance over task complexity, moving beyond traditional scalar evaluation
528
      metrics to capture the dynamic two-phase behavior in accuracy as complexity increases. Specifically, we
529   observed that LLMs maintain high accuracy up to a certain complexity threshold, after which performance
530   declines exponentially. By characterizing this behavior through the Complexity Decay Factor (CDF) and
531   the Contextual Decay Offset (CDO), we provided a nuanced understanding of how task complexity and
532   context length independently affect model performance.
533
      Our analysis revealed that these metrics not only quantify the degradation of logical reasoning ability and
534   baseline accuracy but also highlight the limitations of current LLMs in handling complex reasoning tasks
535   over extended contexts. Furthermore, we demonstrated that different fine-tuning strategies can reproduce
536   these failure modes, emphasizing the significant impact of training methodologies on model capabilities.
537   By modeling the two-phase behavior in accuracy rather than relying on a single performance score, the
538   FACTOR benchmark offers a more detailed and insightful evaluation framework. This approach allows
539   researchers to identify specific areas for improvement in LLMs and guides future developments in creating
      more robust language models capable of complex reasoning over long contexts.

       C    TASK GENERATION AND DISTRIBUTION
1023   In this section, we provide a detailed explanation of the process used to generate the synthetic tasks in the
1024   FACTOR benchmark. These tasks are meticulously designed to evaluate the reasoning abilities of language
1025   models over varying levels of task complexity and context length while ensuring that these two factors are
       independently controlled.


                                                            19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       C.1     TASK GENERATION PROCESS
1027
1028   The generation of each task involves several steps: creating variables and relationships, generating the payload,
1029
       preparing the context, inserting the payload into the context, and forming the question prompt. This process
       is carefully constructed to introduce variability and prevent models from relying on simple heuristics or
1030
       memorization.
1031
1032   C.1.1    VARIABLE AND RELATIONSHIP CREATION
1033
1034   To control task complexity, we vary the number of variables N, where each variable is denoted as vi for
1035   i=0,1,...,N −1. We establish interdependent relationships among these variables by generating a directed
1036   forest—a collection of trees representing dependencies.
1037   We start by randomly shuffling the variables to introduce variability. For each variable vi where i≥k (with
1038   k being a randomly selected parameter between 1 and N), we randomly select a parent variable vp from the
1039   set {v0,v1,...,vi−1}. This process creates directed edges from vp to vi, establishing dependency relationships.
1040   To define the mathematical relationships between the variables, we assign simple operations to each edge.
1041   These operations are randomly chosen from{no operation, +1, −1}. The use of these simple operations
1042   ensures that the calculations required to solve the tasks remain within the realm of basic arithmetic, preventing
1043   models from facing overly complex computations.
1044
1045   Variable Value Assignment After establishing the relationships, we assign integer values to the variables
1046   while satisfying the dependencies. For root variables (those without parents), we assign random integer
1047
       values between 0 and 10. This range is chosen to keep the numerical values small, again to prevent the
       need for complicated calculations.
1048
1049   For each child variable vi, its value is computed based on its parent’s value and the assigned operation:
1050
1051                                        
                                            value(vp)+1, if the operation is +1
1052
                                  value(vi)= value(vp)−1, if the operation is −1
1053                                        
                                             value(vp),   if there is no operation
1054
1055   This approach maintains simplicity in the calculations required, ensuring that the tasks assess the models’
1056   reasoning abilities rather than computational prowess.
1057
1058   C.1.2    PAYLOAD GENERATION
1059
       The payload consists of the variable relationships formatted as textual statements. Each relationship is
1060
       expressed as an assignment statement, enclosed within triple angle brackets <<< and >>> to clearly
1061
       distinguish them from the filler text. These statements are further enclosed within single @ symbols when
1062   inserted into the context.
1063
1064
       An example of a payload statement is:
1065
1066                                      <<<assign vi =vp [operation]>>>
1067
1068
       where [operation] is either + 1, - 1, or left empty if there is no operation. The payload statements
       are shuffled to present the relationships in a non-sequential order, adding to task complexity by preventing
1069
       models from relying on the order of presentation.
1070
1071   C.1.3    CONTEXT PREPARATION
1072
1073   To control the context length independently, we generate filler text of specified lengths. The filler text is
1074   irrelevant to the variable relationships and serves to increase the context length, simulating scenarios where
1075   critical information is embedded within large amounts of unrelated data.
1076   The filler text is created by randomly selecting words from a predefined list related to computational topics,
1077   such as ”algorithm,” ”data,” ”performance,” and so on. These words are concatenated to form sentences,
1078   with occasional punctuation added to simulate natural language text. The filler text may also include random
1079   sentences or phrases inserted between the payload statements to further obscure the relationships and mimic
       real-world text where key information is interleaved with irrelevant content.


                                                              20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       C.1.4    PAYLOAD INSERTION
1081
1082
       The payload of variable relationships is inserted into the filler text at random positions. The filler text is
1083   first tokenized into sentences using the NLTK library’s sentence tokenizer. Insertion points are determined
1084   based on specified intervals, ensuring that the payloads are dispersed throughout the context rather than
1085   clustered together.
1086
       Each payload statement, enclosed within @ symbols and <<<>>> brackets, is inserted at the selected
1087   points. The maximum group size parameter controls how many payload statements can be inserted together
1088   at a single insertion point, adding another layer of variability. Additionally, filler content may be placed
1089   between payload statements, further increasing the challenge by requiring the model to discriminate between
1090   relevant and irrelevant information.
1091
1092
       C.1.5    QUESTION PROMPT FORMATION
1093
1094
       Finally, we generate the question prompt that instructs the model on the task to perform. The prompt includes:
1095
1096   - Delimiters indicating the beginning and end of the text. - An explanation that the relationships enclosed
1097   by <<< and >>> are independent mathematical equations that are all true simultaneously, and not
1098
       sequential assignments in a programming language. - A task instruction, asking the model to determine
       which variable(s), if any, are equal to a randomly selected target value. The target value is chosen from
1099
       among the variable values or from values slightly outside the range of assigned values, which may result
1100
       in no variables matching the target, thereby introducing unpredictability. - A request for the model to show
1101   step-by-step reasoning and conclude with the final answer in a sentence.
1102
1103
       This structure ensures that the model must process and reason over the entire context, filtering out irrelevant
       information and correctly interpreting the relationships to arrive at the answer.
1104
1105
1106
1107
       C.2     TASK DISTRIBUTION
1108   We generate tasks across a wide range of complexities and context lengths to create a comprehensive
1109   evaluation suite.
1110
1111   C.2.1    COMPLEXITY LEVELS
1112
1113   The number of variables N varies from 1 to 39, covering a broad spectrum of task complexities. For each
1114   value of N, we generate multiple tasks with different configurations to ensure diversity. The parameter
1115   k, controlling the number of trees in the forest, is randomly selected for each task and ranges from 1 to N.
1116
1117
       C.2.2    CONTEXT LENGTHS
1118
1119
       Context lengths are set to predefined values: 0 (no filler text), 1K, 2K, 4K, 8K, 16K, 32K, 64K, and 128K
1120
       tokens. Our benchmark in the main context only include subsets of 0 and 4K or more. For each context
1121   length, filler text is generated accordingly, and the payloads are inserted as described. The inclusion of
1122   different context lengths allows us to evaluate how models handle tasks when key information is embedded
1123   within varying amounts of irrelevant text.
1124
1125
1126
       C.2.3    SAMPLE SIZES
1127
       For each combination of N and context length, we generate 50 distinct task instances. This results in a
1128
       total of 1,950 tasks for each context length setting (39 values of N times 50 tasks). The large sample size
1129
       ensures that our evaluation is statistically robust and that any observed trends are not due to random chance.

1160   C.4     ENSURING DIVERSITY AND AVOIDING DATA LEAKAGE
1161
1162
       To prevent models from exploiting patterns or memorizing specific instances, we employ several strategies:
1163   - Variable names are randomly shuffled for each task instance. - Relationships are presented in a random
1164   order and interleaved with filler content. - Operations assigned to edges are randomly selected from simple
1165   options to maintain calculation simplicity while adding variability. - Target values for the query are randomly
1166   chosen and may not correspond to any variable value in the task, introducing the possibility that no variables
1167
       meet the condition.
1168   These measures create a wide variety of task instances, reducing the likelihood of data leakage or overfitting.
1169   Models must genuinely understand and reason through each task rather than relying on memorization or
1170   pattern recognition.

1172   C.5     SUMMARY
1173
       The task generation process in the FACTOR benchmark is carefully designed to independently control
1174
       task complexity and context length while preventing models from relying on shortcuts. By varying the
1175
       number of variables and their interdependencies, we manipulate task complexity. By inserting filler text
1176   of specified lengths and interleaving filler content between payload statements, we manipulate context length
1177   and complexity. The use of simple operations and small integer values ensures that the focus remains on
1178   reasoning rather than computation.
1179
       This systematic approach allows us to create a diverse set of tasks that robustly evaluate models’ reasoning
1180
       abilities over long contexts. By disentangling the effects of task complexity and context length on model
1181   performance, the FACTOR benchmark facilitates a deeper understanding of models’ strengths and limitations
1182   in reasoning over extended textual inputs, guiding future improvements in language model development.

       D.1     TWO-PHASE ACCURACY BEHAVIOR AND DATA SELECTION
1189
1190   Our experiments reveal a characteristic two-phase accuracy behavior as task complexity N increases:
1191   (1) Phase 1: High accuracy plateau where models perform well on simpler tasks, typically with accuracies
1192   close to 100%.
1193   (2) Phase 2: Exponential decay of accuracy as task complexity exceeds a certain threshold Neff.
1194
1195
       To model the rate of accuracy decline in Phase 2, we perform linear regression on the natural logarithm
       of accuracy versus task complexity N. It is essential to focus on data from Phase 2 only, to avoid distortion
1196
       from the Phase 1 plateau.
1197
1198
       D.2     DATA SELECTION CRITERIA
1199
1200   Selecting appropriate data ranges is crucial for accurate regression. We use different accuracy ranges for
1201
       different models and experimental stages to focus on Phase 2 data:
1202   (1) Pretrained Models: Accuracy between [0.1,0.9]. (2) Train-from-Scratch Models (Pretraining Stage):
1203   Accuracy between [0.1,0.8]. (3) Fine-tuned Models: Accuracy between [0.2,0.8]. (4)Repeated Sampling
1204   Experiments: Accuracy between [0.02,0.8].
1205   These ranges exclude Phase 1 data (high accuracy plateau) and avoid extremely low accuracies that can
1206   lead to numerical instability.

1520   G     TRAINING DATA GENERATION
1521
1522   This section details the generation of the training data used in both the pretraining and posttraining phases.
1523
       Our approach carefully controls task complexity and context length to effectively train the models for
       evaluating their reasoning abilities on the FACTOR benchmark.
1524
1525
       G.1     PRETRAINING DATASET
1526
1527   The pretraining dataset comprises 3 million synthetic Question-Solution pairs. It is designed to teach the
1528   model fundamental reasoning skills over a range of task complexities and shorter context lengths.
1529
       G.1.1     TASK COMPLEXITY DISTRIBUTION
1530
1531   To control task complexity, we vary the number of variables N in each synthetic example. The value of
1532   N is determined by:
1533
1534
1535
                                        N =min(N1,N2), N1,N2 ∼Uniform(1,29)
1536   where Uniform(1,29) denotes a discrete uniform distribution over integers from 1 to 29 (excluding 30).
1537   By taking the minimum of two independently sampled values, we bias the distribution toward smaller values
1538   of N, emphasizing simpler tasks while still including examples with higher complexities up to N =29.
1539
1540   G.1.2     FILLER CONTEXT LENGTH DISTRIBUTION
1541
       The length of the filler context Lpretrain is sampled from a log-normal distribution to introduce variability
1542
       in context lengths while maintaining manageable sequence sizes for pretraining. Specifically, we use:
1543
1544
                                                  2
1545                                 lnL∼N (µlnL,σlnL ), Lpretrain =exp(lnL)−Lmin
1546
       where: µlnL =5, σlnL =2.5,Lmin =100, Lmax =1000
1547
1548   After sampling lnL, we compute L, round it to the nearest integer, and clip it to the range [Lmin,Lmax]. We
1549   then subtract Lmin to adjust the lengths to start from zero. This results in a distribution of filler context lengths
1550
       biased toward shorter lengths but including a range up to 900 tokens.
1551
1552
       G.2     POST-TRAINING DATASET
1553   The post-training dataset consists of 60,000 synthetic long-context Question-Solution pairs. It is used to
1554   fine-tune the pretrained model, enhancing its ability to handle extended contexts and complex reasoning tasks.
1555   We employ three different training strategies to investigate their effects on model performance:
1556
1557           1. Gradually Increasing Context Length: The dataset is divided into four equal parts, each
1558              containing 15,000 examples. Each part corresponds to filler context lengths that are progressively
1559              increased. Specifically, the filler context length distribution in each stage is scaled by factors of 2,
1560              4, 8, and 16 times the pretraining filler context length Lpretrain, respectively. By gradually increasing
1561              the context length, the model is incrementally adapted to handle longer sequences, reaching the
1562
                  maximum context length in the final stage.
1563           2. Mixture of Different Context Lengths: In this strategy, the examples from all four stages of
1564              the gradually increasing context length dataset are combined and shuffled. Training on this mixed
1565              dataset exposes the model to different context lengths simultaneously, which may encourage better
                  generalization across various sequence lengths.

               3. Training Directly with Full Context Length: The model is trained starting from a filler context
1567              length distribution scaled by 16×Lpretrain. This approach tests the model’s ability to handle long
1568              contexts without prior adaptation through shorter sequences.
