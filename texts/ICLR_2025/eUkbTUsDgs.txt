                                                  A BSTRACT
013
014
               As general-purpose tools, Large Language Models (LLMs) must often reason
015
               about everyday physical environments. In a question-and-answer capacity, under-
016
               standing the interactions of physical objects may be necessary to give appropriate
017            responses. Moreover, LLMs are increasingly used as reasoning engines in agen-
018            tic systems, designing and controlling their action sequences. The vast majority
019            of research has tackled this issue using static benchmarks, comprised of text or
020            image-based questions about the physical world. However, these benchmarks do
021            not capture the complexity and nuance of real-life physical processes. Here we
022            advocate for a second, relatively unexplored, approach: ‘embodying’ the LLMs
023            by granting them control of an agent within a 3D environment. We present the
024            first embodied and cognitively meaningful evaluation of physical common-sense
025
               reasoning in LLMs. Our framework allows direct comparison of LLMs with other
               embodied agents, such as those based on Deep Reinforcement Learning, and hu-
026
               man and non-human animals. We employ the Animal-AI (AAI) environment, a
027
               simulated 3D virtual laboratory, to study physical common-sense reasoning in
028            LLMs. For this, we use the AAI Testbed, a suite of experiments that replicate
029            laboratory studies with non-human animals, to study physical reasoning capabili-
030            ties including distance estimation, tracking out-of-sight objects, and tool use. We
031            demonstrate that state-of-the-art multi-modal models with no finetuning can com-
032            plete this style of task, allowing meaningful comparison to the entrants of the 2019
033            Animal-AI Olympics competition and to human children. Our results show that
034            LLMs are currently outperformed by human children on these tasks. We argue
035            that this approach allows the study of physical reasoning using ecologically valid
036
               experiments drawn directly from cognitive science, improving the predictability
               and reliability of LLMs.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
040
041
      Large Language Models (LLMs) can do your physics homework, but might not find their way to the
042   classroom. While LLMs have made great strides in several areas, including writing code (Champa
043   et al., 2024), solving maths problems (Frieder et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2023b), and answering general
044   knowledge questions (Wang et al., 2024), it remains unclear what they know and understand about
045   the physical world.
046
      Physical common-sense reasoning is the capacity to perceive, understand, and predict the behaviour
047
      of objects in an environment. This includes an understanding of the physical rules governing space
048   and objects in that environment, and how they interact to determine the outcome of events or actions.
049   In cognitive science, physical common-sense reasoning is also referred to as intuitive or folk physics
050   (Kubricht et al., 2017). In LLMs, this capability has typically been evaluated using task- or image-
051   based benchmarks involving short vignettes describing a physical scene, perhaps accompanied by an
052   image if the model is multi-modal, with questions about the objects and their interactions (Buschoff
053   et al., 2024; Bisk et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2023b). Benchmark scores are then aggregated to produce
      the final estimate of an LLM’s capability. While this traditional approach has provided insight into


                                                        1
054
      some aspects of physical reasoning, it misses many definitive features of physical common sense
055   reasoning - that is, the capacity to perceive, understand, and predict the behaviour of objects in a
056   physical environment, and use that knowledge to take appropriate actions.
057
058
      Beyond this, traditional benchmarks suffer from a number of shortcomings (Hernández-Orallo,
      2017). First, these benchmarks lack ecological validity—when deployed, LLM agents will not
059
      be interacting with well-described, clean vignettes with clear questions and uniquely identifiable
060
      answers. Instead, they will be interacting with a complex, noisy world where the correct answer,
061   or action, is not always easily discriminated. Second, these benchmarks lack established construct
062   validity (Borsboom et al., 2004; Cronbach & Meehl, 1955)—they have not been validated indepen-
063   dently as good measures of physical common-sense reasoning by, for example, running experiments
064   with humans or animals. Third, these benchmarks are static, meaning that the test items are fixed.
065   When these benchmarks are released, there is a risk that new models will be trained on test items,
066   contaminating the benchmark and thus rendering any results invalid, since models have been trained
067   to predict the answer rather than to exhibit any emergent physical common-sense reasoning (Xu
068   et al., 2024). Finally, benchmarks of physical common-sense reasoning are large and general—it is
069
      often unclear which aspects of physical common-sense reasoning they are targeting for evaluation.
      This is problematic because this type of reasoning is multifaceted, comprising everything from un-
070
      derstanding inertia, gravity, and the solidity of objects, to reasoning about the concepts of causality,
071
      quantity and time (Lake et al., 2017; Shanahan et al., 2020). Traditional benchmarks do not allow us
072   to precisely answer questions about what LLMs know about their physical environments and how
073   they use that knowledge to take actions in them.
074
075
      In this paper, we introduce LLMs in Animal-AI (LLM-AAI), a framework for conducting robust
      cognitive evaluations of the physical common-sense reasoning capabilities of LLM agents in a 3D
076
      virtual environment. Our framework allows us to test LLMs’ physical common sense reasoning
077
      by embodying LLMs within Animal-AI—a virtual laboratory environment designed for the devel-
078   opment of systematic cognitive test batteries with a particular emphasis on physical common-sense
079   reasoning (Voudouris et al., 2023). Our approach situates LLMs in a physically realistic environment
080   (ecologically valid), draws on testing materials that have been independently validated on humans
081   and other animals (construct valid), capitalises on the variance of physical phenomena to produce
082   difficult, dynamic tests (non-static), and tests a range of components of physical common-sense
083   reasoning (precise evaluation target). A further strength of the LLM-AAI framework is that it facil-
084   itates comparison between human, animal, and multiple types of artificial intelligence systems on
085   directly comparable tests. Here, we present the first evaluation of physical common-sense reasoning
086
      in LLMs using experiments drawn from research testing these capabilities in non-human animals,
      and compare their performance to Reinforcement Learning (RL) agents and human children.
087
088   The paper proceeds as follows: First, we review the recent literature on LLM agents and physical
089   common-sense reasoning evaluations. Second, we introduce the Animal-AI environment and the
090   Animal-AI Olympics—a competitive cognitive benchmark drawing on experiments from compara-
091   tive psychology. Third, we introduce the LLM-AAI framework and describe the results from two
092
      experiments, where we evaluate the performance of three state-of-the-art LLMs (Claude Sonnet 3.5,
      GPT-4o, and Gemini 1.5 Pro) on the Animal-AI Olympics, in comparison to RL agents and human
093
      children, using different prompting strategies. Finally, we discuss these results and future work
094
      developing the LLM-AAI framework.

097   2   R ELATED W ORK
098
099
      In machine learning and natural language processing, there is increasing interest in whether LLMs
100
      possess the capacity to perceive, understand, and predict the behaviour of objects in their environ-
101
      ment, which has come to be known in the literature as physical common-sense reasoning (Bisk et al.
102   2020; Buschoff et al. 2024; Sap et al. 2020; Storks et al. 2019; Wang et al. 2023b; see also ‘world
103   models’, e.g., Matsuo et al. 2022). This capacity has been studied extensively in the cognitive sci-
104   ences, where it is often called intuitive or folk physics (Bates et al., 2019; Battaglia et al., 2012;
105   Chiandetti & Vallortigara, 2011; Povinelli, 2003; Smith et al., 2018). Physical common-sense rea-
106   soning is multifaceted, ranging from understanding the properties and affordances of objects (Rutar
107   et al., 2024) to tracking occluded objects (Voudouris et al., 2022b; 2024), using tools (Shanahan
      et al., 2020), and predicting the effects of gravity and momentum (Buschoff et al., 2024; Jassim


                                                         2
108
      et al., 2024; Povinelli, 2003). One approach to studying physical common-sense reasoning in Large
109   Language Models is through the administration of text-based descriptions of physical scenes, some-
110   times accompanied by images in the case of multi-modal LLMs, about which the model must answer
111   some questions. The Physical Interaction: Question Answering (PIQA) benchmark (Bisk et al.,
112   2020) consists of over 16K items that follows this approach using only text-based questions. LLMs
113   are asked how they might achieve certain goals, such as Make an outdoor pillow and they are given
114   two potential solutions, in this case, Blow into a {trash bag, tin can} and tie with a rubber band.
115   Clearly, the answer is trash bag, given what we know as humans about the properties of trash bags
116   and tin cans. Aroca-Ouellette et al. (2021) extend PIQA to over 18K question-answer pairs in the
117
      PROST benchmark, and Wang et al. (2023b) scale up even further to over 160K items in the NEW-
      TON benchmark. The results from these three benchmarks indicate that physical common-sense
118
      reasoning is not yet at human-level in text-only LLMs. In the multi-modal context, Buschoff et al.
119
      (2024) develop a suite of tasks inspired by cognitive science to study physical common-sense among
120   other things. In their design, multi-modal prompts including task descriptions and visual stimuli are
121   combined, and LLMs are tasked with providing a numerical judgment or rating about the described
122   physical scene. For example, in the block towers task, LLMs are presented with pictures of stacks of
123   coloured blocks, and asked to provide a binary judgment about whether the ‘tower blocks’ are stable
124   or not. In their results, they found that only OpenAI’s GPT-4V was able to make correct judgments
125   above the level of chance on this task. In a similar vein, Jassim et al. (2024) present the Ground-
126   ing And Simulated Physics (GRASP) benchmark, but in this case images are replaced with videos
127   generated by a physics simulator. For every video, models are asked whether they think that the
128
      physical scene depicted is plausible, and they can only give a binary answer. Videos depict scenes
      in which objects appear to change size, colour, or shape spontaneously, disappear when occluded,
129
      or lack inertia or momentum. Their results also indicate that current LLMs that can process videos
130
      do not answer questions about these visual scenes above the level of chance.
131
132   An alternative approach to studying physical common-sense reasoning in LLMs is to grant them
133   control of an agent, such that they are embodied in a real-world environment. Previous work has
134
      explored different approaches to LLM embodiment in both physical and digital environments. In
      the field of robotics, LLMs have been used to generate high-level action plans that are executed
135
      in real-world settings (Ahn et al., 2022; Driess et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2022). However for such
136
      forms of deployment to be safe and reliable, it is important to establish whether LLM’s apparent
137   understanding of the physical world translates into appropriate behaviour when faced with real-
138   world physical constraints (Ahn et al., 2022). Evaluating LLMs in ‘real-world’ contexts offers
139   a high degree of ecological validity, but presents significant challenges: these approaches require
140   extensive additional training, and face bottlenecks related to cost, safety and development speed in
141   robotics. Hence, there is much to be gained from taking incremental steps towards true embodiment.
142   One such step involves embedding LLMs as agents within virtual environments.
143   While there has been recent progress towards embodied LLM agents (Li et al., 2024), there has
144   been no work, to our knowledge, on providing a robust framework for evaluating their physical
145   common-sense reasoning. In the remainder of this section, we briefly review research on LLM
146   agents before comparing it to our approach. LLM agents have been implemented and evaluated
147   in a wide variety of game environments (Hu et al., 2024), ranging from co-operative games like
148   OverCooked (Agashe et al., 2023; Gong et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a) to strategy
149   games like StarCraft II (Ma et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2024). Many of these games do not directly
150   require good physical common-sense, because they involve simplistic visual and physical scenes
151
      with limited action spaces—their focus tends to be on evaluating how LLMs interact with other
      agents. In open field environments, there have been implementations of LLMs in Minecraft (Chen
152
      et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2022; Feng et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Stengel-Eskin et al., 2024; Wang
153
      et al., 2023c;d;a; Yuan et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2023b; Zhao et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2023) and
154   Crafter (Du et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023c; Zhang & Lu, 2024), although again
155   the physical reality of these environments is heavily limited by their simplicity - indeed, Crafter is
156   a 2D world (Hafner, 2021). Most closely aligned to our work are those LLM implementations in
157   VirtualHome (Huang et al., 2022; Xiang et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024), which has a realistic physics
158   engine (Puig et al., 2018). In all cases, however, the focus has been on developing LLMs that
159   can outperform humans or other AI agents, rather than developing a framework for more precise
160   evaluation of physical common-sense reasoning.
161



                                                        3
162
      This paper is the first example of a novel framework and proof-of-concept results demonstrating
163   that LLMs can be evaluated on ecologically valid, complex tasks of physical common-sense reason-
164   ing. Furthermore, our approach allows meaningful direct comparisons to be drawn between LLMs
165   and other agents, both biological (e.g. children) and non-biological (e.g. Reinforcement Learning
166   agents).

      3     T HE A NIMAL -AI E NVIRONMENT
169
170
      The Animal-AI (AAI) environment (Beyret et al., 2019; Crosby et al., 2019; Voudouris et al., 2023)
171
      is a physically realistic 3D simulation based on the Unity ML-Agents framework (Juliani, 2018),
172   designed to be used by researchers from AI and cognitive science to assess nonverbal physical
173   common sense reasoning in embodied agents. The goal of the environment is to offer a tool for
174   interdisciplinary research at the intersection of AI and cognitive science, with a particular focus
175   on comparative and developmental psychology. All experiments in AAI consist of a 40×40 arena,
176   populated with a single agent (spherical with diameter 1) and a variety of different objects.
177
178   3.1   T HE A NIMAL -AI T ESTBED AND O LYMPICS
179
180   AAI was first released in 2019 as part of the Animal-AI Olympics Competition, in which over 60
181   entrants competed to produce agents that could solve a series of unseen tasks inspired by com-
182
      parative psychology research (Crosby et al., 2020), thus favouring the development of agents that
      could perform robustly out-of-distribution on tests of physical common sense reasoning. After the
183
      competition was completed, these tasks were released as the Animal-AI Testbed to further stim-
184
      ulate interdisciplinary research between AI and comparative psychology. The Animal-AI Testbed
185   contains 300 distinct tests (with 3 variants of each; n=900 tasks) that test the full breadth of ca-
186   pabilities that underpin physical common-sense reasoning, including navigating around obstacles,
187   making spatial inferences, tracking occluded objects, and causal reasoning. The aim in every task is
188   to maximise total reward at the end of the episode. The environment contains spheres of different
189   colours and sizes: yellow spheres increase reward, as do green spheres, which also end the episode;
190   red spheres decrease reward and end the episode. In all cases, the magnitude of the reward change
191   is proportional to the size of the sphere. Touching red ‘death zones’ leads to a decrease in reward of
192   −1 and also ends the episode. Reward decreases at a constant rate starting from 0 on each timestep,
193
      thus favouring efficient action sequences. Entering orange ‘hot zones’ leads to a doubling in reward
      decrement. A variety of movable and immovable blocks are present in the environment, including
194
      tunnels and opaque and transparent walls. While the colours and textures of objects in AAI are
195
      simplified, their physical interactions are close enough to those of the real world to appear identical.
196   This is because AAI uses the physics engine provided in Unity: Every object has mass, volume,
197   and static and dynamic friction coefficients, meaning that their movements are governed by laws of
198   momentum, inertia, friction (including air resistance), and gravity.
199
      The Animal-AI Testbed is arranged into 10 levels of 90 tasks of roughly increasing difficulty
200
      (Voudouris et al., 2022a) which probe different aspects of physical common-sense reasoning. For
201
      example, level 1 (Food Retrieval) tests the ability of the agent to navigate towards rewarding green
202   and yellow spheres, level 2 (Preferences) tests the ability to distinguish objects that give different
203   rewards, and level 3 (Static Obstacles) tests the ability to navigate around and over immovable solid
204   objects, such as walls, ramps, and tunnels. The most complex levels test sophisticated physical
205   common-sense reasoning abilities: level 8 (Object Permanence and Working Memory) tests whether
206   agents understand that objects continue to exist when they are occluded, while level 10 (Causal
207   Reasoning) tests the ability to understand cause and effect through the use of tools that can be used
208   to achieve certain goals. These levels are described further in the Appendix in Table 1. Examples of
209   the tests from each level used in this paper are presented in Figure 1.

      We consider three state-of-the-art multi-modal Large Language Models. Our selection was based
286   on a convenience sample, guided by the inclusion criterion that models must be multi-modal with
287   a large context window (>64k), and the exclusion criterion that models must not be too costly to
288   run inference on. We evaluated Claude 3.5 Sonnet, GPT-4o, and Gemini 1.5 Pro. We ran all
289   experiments with temperature 0, but noticed that model responses can vary nevertheless. Therefore,
290   we ran three trials of each model on each task.

292   4.3   E XPERIMENTS
293
294   In this study, we use a subset of the Animal-AI Testbed containing four randomly selected tasks
295   from the ten levels (n=40), replicating the design of Voudouris et al. (2022a), in which 59 children
296   aged 6-10 completed the same subset of 40 tasks. This allows direct comparison of LLM agents with
297   human children, and non-human entrants to the Animal-AI Olympics Competition (Crosby et al.,
298
      2020).
299   We conduct two experiments to explore LLM performance in this setting. Our first experiment
300   includes a prompt that explains the environment and possible actions to the LLM, and assesses
301   three models on 40 AAI Testbed tasks. Our second experiment provides the LLM with an in-context
302   example of the completion of a simple ‘tutorial’ level, which we assess on a subset of the 40 instances
303   assessed in Experiment 1.
304   When we encountered errors from API calls that persisted after three retries, we discarded the current
305   trial data and relaunched that trial run.
306
307   4.4   E XPERIMENT 1: R E ACT P ROMPTING
308
309   First, we designed a simple prompt that provides the core information needed to navigate and collect
310   rewards in the AAI Testbed. To improve the LLM’s decision-making, we incorporated the ReAct
311   (Reasoning and Acting) framework (Yao et al., 2022) into our prompt design. The ReAct approach
312   combines reasoning and acting by allowing the model to generate reasoning traces alongside actions,
313   which can improve performance on agentic tasks (Yao et al., 2022). By integrating ReAct, we
314
      encourage the LLM to first reason about the environment—identifying visible objects and their
      spatial relationships relative to the agent—before producing action scripts.
315
316   Our prompt begins by setting the context: The LLM is informed that it is a player in a game set in a
317   square arena with a white fence, tasked with collecting green and yellow ball rewards as quickly and
318   efficiently as possible using a basic scripting language. The prompt details the kinds of objects the
319   LLM will encounter, their key properties, and instructions on how to write scripts using the com-
320
      mands Think, Go, and Turn. It includes examples to illustrate correct usage of these commands
      and provides guidelines to avoid common mistakes.
321
322   To aid the LLMs in navigating the environment efficiently, we incorporated expert tips on movement
323   distances and turning angles. For instance, we explain that moves of 1 to 10 steps cover small dis-
      tances, while moves of 10 to 20 steps cover larger distances. We also provide strategic guidance on


                                                        6
324
      how to approach the task using the Think command to describe the current state of the environment
325   and plan its actions, and subsequently using either Go or Turn to move within the environment.
326
327   Lastly, the prompt warns about potential obstacles such as red lava puddles, holes, blue paths, purple
328
      ramps, transparent walls, pushable grey blocks, and immovable objects like walls and arches. It
      provides instructions on how to identify and interact with these obstacles, emphasizing caution to
329
      prevent the agent from dying or becoming trapped. The full prompt is provided in Appendix C.
330
331   Armed with this prompt, each LLM is evaluated on the 40 tasks performed by children in Voudouris
332   et al. (Voudouris et al., 2022a). The LLM is not presented with previous action scripts from other
333   episodes, meaning it approaches each task as if it is interacting with the AAI Testbed for the first
334
      time.
335
336   4.5              E XPERIMENT 2: S UPERVISED I N -C ONTEXT L EARNING
337
338   When children played the tasks in the AAI Testbed, they received a short two-minute video to de-
339   scribe “the game”—that is, to introduce the AAI environment, its objects and controls. To emulate
340   this, we designed an example level in AAI that introduced the same information presented in the
341   video, including a sequence of scripts for solving the level and ‘Think’ actions to explain obser-
342
      vations. These were incorporated into the prompt above. LLMs are thus provided with images of
      objects they may encounter, as opposed to just textual descriptions, and an ‘expert example’ (shown
343
      in Appendix D), before they are tasked with controlling the agent. We call this supervised in-context
344
      learning.
345
346   Due to the increased cost of passing additional images and text, we conducted this experiment on
347   a subset of tasks. We focused on the first three levels of the AAI Testbed as they provide a better
348
      opportunity to observe meaningful differences given LLMs’ poor performance on later levels in
      Experiment 1.

385   but performance drops off quickly in more challenging tasks. The LLMs’ performance never ex-
386   ceeds that of the top 10 agents submitted to the Animal-AI competition. It could be argued that this
387   comparison will always favour the RL agents, who had been specifically trained for the environ-
388   ment, if not for the specific tasks. However, the same cannot be said for the human children, whose
389   performance also exceeded that of the LLMs across the board. These results indicate that LLMs
390   may still lack the physical common-sense reasoning abilities possessed by human children.

      6     D ISCUSSION
433
434
      The LLM-AAI framework tests the out of the box physical reasoning capabilities of LLMs by allow-
435
      ing them to perceive and interact with the Animal-AI environment via the ReAct prompting method
436
      (Yao et al., 2022). While previous work has explored the capabilities of LLMs in virtual environ-
437   ments, none have used them to develop a framework for testing physical common-sense reasoning in
438   LLMs. Our results show that this method LLMs can not only be assessed in this way, but that when
439   this is done it allows meaningful comparisons to be made with other biological and non-biological
440   intelligences.
441
      Evaluations in LLM-AAI have synergies with other efforts in evaluating and training LLMs. In
442
      evaluation, several LLM testbeds can be seen as targeting facets of the Animal-AI Testbed such
443   as spatial reasoning (Ranasinghe et al., 2024), numerosity (Trott et al., 2017; Villa et al., 2023)
444   and tool use (Tian et al., 2023). Evaluations in LLM-AAI complement such efforts, but also adds
445   the increased challenge of interacting in a 3D environment, which has less direct correspondence
446   with the linguistic prompt. Furthermore, where a 3D environment has been used at the learning
447   stage (Dagan et al., 2023; Zellers et al., 2021; Driess et al., 2023; Xiang et al., 2024), an LLM-AAI
448   approach can be used to ensure the robustness of a model’s physical common-sense.
449   For humans, an understanding of the physical world is built from countless embodied interactions
450   with objects in their environment (Thelen, 2000). It is from these interactions that humans construct
451   intuitive theories of the causal relationships that exist in their external world (Goddu & Gopnik,
452   2024; Gopnik & Schulz, 2004; Tenenbaum et al., 2011), and ground the symbolic concepts contained
453   in language (Lakoff & Johnson, 2008; Wolff, 2007). To date, there has been much debate as to the
454   potential for ‘disembodied’ systems such as LLMs to have a ‘meaningful’ understanding of the
455   physical world, or even a ‘world model’ (Bender & Koller, 2020; Mitchell, 2021; Shanahan, 2010).
456   The LLM-AAI framework allows us to make headway on these debates, with our initial results
457
      suggesting that LLMs still have some way to go before they can compete with their embodied
      counterparts.
458
459
460   6.1   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
461
462   The LLM-AAI framework satisfies an important demand in the field of LLM evaluation. It provides
463   a methodology and way forward for evaluations of physical common-sense reasoning using inde-
464   pendently developed tests from cognitive science (construct valid) that measure specific components
465
      of physical common-sense (precise evaluation target), in a physically realistic environment (ecolog-
      ically valid) with real-world dynamics (non-static). These tests identify the capabilities and failure
466
      modes of contemporary multi-modal LLMs, aiding researchers to identify how training curricula
467
      and model architectures can be improved to achieve better performance. Furthermore, LLM-AAI
468   enables direct, cognitively meaningful, comparisons between LLMs, deep reinforcement learning
469   (DRL) agents, humans, and other animals. Our results in this paper demonstrate that out-of-the-box
470   systems can produce meaningful results on the Animal-AI competition. Nevertheless, there remain
471   a number of extensions to how LLMs interact with AAI through our framework that could improve
472   LLM performance. These extensions remedy some of the limitations of this current work and serve
473   as the basis for future research.
474   Sensing the environment. In LLM-AAI, at every conversation turn, the tested LLM receives a
475   single 512 x 512-pixel image of the environment. This image is captured after the LLM’s action
476   script is executed. The number of environment time-steps that unfold during the execution depends
477   on the action script. For example, if the LLM uses the Turn(180) command, more environment
478   time-steps will go by than if the LLM uses the Turn(25) command. Despite this difference
479   in time-steps, in both cases a single image observation is sent to the LLM. While this observation
480   routine allows larger agent-displacements with fewer API calls (and hence reduced costs), it can also
481   cause the LLM to miss important environment information. For example, the agent may execute a
482
      Turn(180) script meaning that it misses the goal that is placed 90° to its right.
483   Locomotion and control. The control scheme used in the study, although theoretically sufficient
484   for completing levels, is a relatively coarse way of controlling an agent in the environment compared
485   to both children and AAI Olympics competition entrants, who could all provide a single action after
      every timestep. The additional challenge of writing action scripts manifests in the game-play of


                                                        9
486
      the LLMs. For example, in many cases, the LLM almost aligns itself with the goal but misses it
487   slightly. This could result in the LLM finding itself beyond the goal and having to take extra turns
488   to reorient itself before trying again. Future work could experiment with alternatives to the control
489   scheme employed in this paper, such as allowing the LLM to control the agent frame-by-frame, or
490   fine tuning a model to turn natural language descriptions of the action into environment commands.
491
      Capability limitations. This study aimed to assess LLMs out of the box on the Animal-AI Testbed.
492
      This ensures that the evaluation is not contaminated as LLMs have not been explicitly trained to
493   solve these tests. However, it might be that the challenge of controlling the agent in the environment
494   is so large that this dominates the cognitive challenge on some tasks. By comparing the LLMs’
495   Think responses with their in-world actions on selected levels (see Capability Case Studies in
496   Appendix A), we describe a specific example (object permanence) where low-level navigational
497   demands may have limited LLMs’ performance, among other indicative failures (in affordance un-
498   derstanding and numerical magnitude comparison) that may shed light on the the behavioural mech-
499   anisms underlying our results. Future work could ensure LLM performance is not constrained by
500   low-level perceptual or navigational demands by fine-tuning multi-modal LLMs on the observa-
501
      tions and action scripts of an agent successfully completing simple navigation tasks. This would
      overcome the problem of calibrating action scripts to the environment, and allow our tests to more
502
      accurately reveal the cognitive capabilities of LLMs. An alternative approach would be to embed
503
      LLMs as components of a larger control and memory system (Wang et al., 2023a; Sumers et al.,
504   2023) to attempt achieve better performance on the Animal-AI Testbed.
505
506   Cost. The scaling cost of longer experiments rendered some experiments financially unfeasible. For
507
      example, human participants completing the same tasks as the LLM would have had the ability to
      learn over the course of the 40 arenas; this could be replicated in LLMs by attempting all 40 arenas
508
      in a single context window. However, the large number of tokens this generates is too costly. Due
509
      to financial limitations, the tested LLMs were also restricted to using, at most, 30 action-scripts, and
510   therefore API calls, per episode. In contrast, human participants and DRL agents were only restricted
511   by the arena’s time-limit, rather than a maximum number of executed actions. This constraint was
512   especially penalising for LLMs in arenas with multiple goals and those that required many finely
513   controlled movements and adjustments; such sequences inflated the number of action-scripts needed
514   to complete the level. Future work will increase or remove the action-script limit and assess the
515   change in performance.
516   Towards cognitively-driven evaluation. The levels in the Animal-AI Testbed are inspired by the
517   rich tradition of developing non-verbal tests of capacities in cognitive science. Since there exists a
518   large number of tests and experimental paradigms, they cannot be condensed into a single testbed
519   such as ours. More targeted LLM-AAI evaluations using the tests from Voudouris et al. (2022b) for
520   object permanence or Rutar et al. (2024) for object affordances, will allow assessors to make more
521   precise statements about physical common-sense reasoning capabilities, and produce comparisons
522   with the humans and DRL agents that have been evaluated on these tests.
523
524
      7   C ONCLUSION
525
526
      We have introduced LLM-AAI, a framework for evaluating the physical common-sense reasoning
527
      capabilities of LLMs in a 3D environment. Using the diverse tasks of the Animal-AI Testbed, we
528   have presented results from an initial assessment, showing that LLMs are capable of completing
529   tasks using LLM-AAI, but may lack the physical common-sense reasoning capabilities of humans.
530   We hope that these results will inspire researchers to embrace embodied evaluations as a powerful
531   addition to the LLM evaluation toolbox.

      Beyond the overall pass-rates of models, our evaluations in LLM-AAI also generated a rich dataset
813   of behaviours and Think actions which can be used to investigate the reasons for LLM performance
814   further. In this section we assess LLM performance in some cognitive domains.
815
816   A.1    A FFORDANCE UNDERSTANDING
817
818   Affordance understanding is “the cognitive capability to identify what action-possibilities exist with
819   a particular object or set of objects, given an agent’s specific physical properties and capacities”
820   (Rutar et al., 2024). Our results demonstrate interesting failures of affordance understanding in
821   LLM agents.
822   In arena ‘10-22-03’ of the AAI testbed the reward is on a platform. To reach the reward the agent
823   must push a block to bridge the gap between the platform and a ramp that faces it, and then climb
824   the ramp 1 . To do this, the agent must have an understanding of two sets of affordances: That certain
825   blocks can be pushed, and that ramps can be climbed from a certain side.
826   No models considered using the pushable block, but only GPT-4o consistently noted its existence,
827   indicating that for the others their vision may not have been sensitive enough to detect it. However,
828   all LLMs acknowledged the existence of the ramp. For example Gemini 1.5 Pro stated ‘I see a
829   purple ramp to my right and the blue path is still visible’. Of the three models tested only Claude
830   Sonnet 3.5 noticed the ramp and attempted to climb it, for example stating ‘I need to climb this
831   ramp to explore what might be on the other side’. However, it did not consider the fact that ramps
832   must be climbed from a particular side and so failed to climb it. The fact that LLMs were able to
833   recognise the ramp, but only one realised its affordance of being climbable, without realising that
834
      this affordance is only available from one side, indicates that robust affordance understanding is still
      a significant challenge for models.
835
836
837
      A.2    O BJECT PERMANENCE
838   Object permanence is “the understanding and belief that objects continue to exist even when they
839   are not directly observable” (Voudouris et al., 2022b), and the presence of this capability is a founda-
840   tional milestone in human cognitive development (Piaget, 2013; Baillargeon et al., 1985). Although
841   LLM performance on object permanence tasks was generally poor (see Figure 3, Level 8), verbal re-
842   port from the models suggests that these failures may be due to low-level navigational or perceptual
843   difficulties, rather than failures of object permanence per se.
844   In arena ‘08-03-03’, two yellow rewards descend from above before being hidden behind a series of
845   walls on the other side of the arena. To solve this task, agents must reason that although the rewards
846   are no longer visible, they nonetheless continue to exist, and can be discovered by searching for
847   them behind the wall.
848
      All LLMs reported that they were searching for the rewards that they had seen previously, while
849
      GPT-4o and Claude Sonnet 3.5 made explicit comments relating to the continual existence of the
850   rewards despite them no longer being in view. For example, GPT-4o states: ‘I can no longer see the
851   yellow balls. They might be behind the grey blocks ahead. I will turn to the right to get a better
852   view.’ And then, after series of poorly executed actions, GPT-4o continues: ‘I still cannot see the
853   yellow balls. They must be behind the grey blocks. I will ... move closer to investigate.’
854
      In contrast to the other models, Claude Sonnet 3.5 showed some success retrieving the reward (see
855
      Figure 3, Level 8) and their verbal reports also suggest a coherent strategy. At the start, Claude
856   comments: ‘There appears to be grey block structures in front of me, which might be obscuring the
857   view of the balls.’ After moving closer, Claude continues: ‘It seems the balls might be behind these
858   structures. I need to move forward and to the right to try to get around these obstacles and locate the
859   yellow balls.’
860
      Given that these verbalisations are being provided in response to dynamic visual input in an embod-
861
      ied environment, rather than as part of a purely linguistic interaction, they make a more robust case
862

864
      for the presence of a generalisable object permanence capability that future work could investigate
865   systematically.

      A.3   N UMERICAL MAGNITUDE COMPARISON
868
869   Numerical magnitude comparison is the ability to determine which one of two numbers has the
870   greater magnitude.
871
      In our experiments, failures in numerical magnitude comparison arose when the LLMs attempted
872
      to track the change in their health. In LLM-AAI, at every turn, before the LLM agent provides a
873   new action script, the environment states the agent’s current health value. An example of this might
874   be: ‘Your remaining health is 83.4’, which is passed as user content to the LLM assistant. The
875   agent may then infer, from this health value, whether it has collected a reward while executing its
876   last action script, by comparing the value with the one it was told one turn before. Misjudging this
877   difference in health may lead to misinterpreting whether or not a reward has been collected.
878
      All three tested LLMs showcased occasional errors in comparing previous and current health values.
879   The following example illustrates the most common flow in which this issue was observed. In arena
880   ‘05-09-01’, GPT-4o attempts to collect a yellow reward in its view. The LLM is provided with a
881   health reading of 63.3 followed by one of 59.7. Clearly, the health has decreased as the agent has not
882   collected the reward. Surprisingly, however, its following Think command content—‘My health
883   has increased, confirming the collection’—showcases an inability to correctly compare the numbers
884   63.3 and 59.7. Similar inaccuracies were observed for Claude Sonnet 3.5 and Gemini 1.5 Pro. In one
885   example, Claude Sonnet 3.5 explicitly verbalised that a health decrease was an increase: In arena
886   ‘04-16-01’, after missing the green reward, Claude stated ‘I have successfully collected the green
887
      ball as my health has increased from 84.2 to 35.4’. This rarer example illustrates how numerical
      mistakes may also lead to the LLM forgetting some basic rules of the environment. Namely, that if
888
      it had collected the green reward, the episode would have ended.
889
890   Our goal was not to conduct a statistical study of the occurrence of this failure mode or to compare
891   numerical magnitude comparison in different LLMs. Rather, we demonstrate that this ability can be
892   crucial to completing physical common sense tasks.
