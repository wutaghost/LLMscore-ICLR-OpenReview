009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Targeted data poisoning poses a critical adversarial threat to machine learning sys-
012            tems by enabling attackers to manipulate training data to induce specific, harmful
013            misclassifications. Among these threats, backdoor attacks are particularly perni-
014            cious, embedding hidden triggers in the data that lead models to misclassify only
015            those inputs containing the trigger, while maintaining high accuracy on benign
016            samples. In this paper, we propose Gradient Storm, a novel technique that facili-
017            tates the simultaneous execution of multiple backdoor attacks, while necessitating
018            only minimal modification to the training dataset. Our contributions are twofold:
019
               First, we introduce a method for designing adversarial poisons in modular com-
               ponents, each tailored based on a distinct region of the model’s parameter space.
020
               Second, we present a framework for conducting multi-trigger attacks, where each
021
               trigger causes misclassification from a specific source class to a distinct target
022            class. We evaluate the efficacy of Gradient Storm across multiple convolutional
023            neural network architectures and two benchmark datasets, demonstrating its ro-
024            bustness against eight different poisoning defense mechanisms. Additionally, we
025            show that poisons crafted for one model can be effectively transferred to other
026            models, demonstrating that our attack remains effective even in black-box set-
027            tings.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) have been successfully employed in various fields, including com-
033   puter vision, speech recognition, and natural language processing (e.g., Redmon & Farhadi (2018);
034   Baevski et al. (2020); Brown et al. (2020)). Despite their high performance, training or fine-tuning
035   these networks often requires task-specific datasets and significant processing power, which may
036   not always be accessible to all users. As a consequence, users may rely on third-party providers for
037   obtaining the necessary resources. This dependency introduces a risk where adversaries could em-
038   bed malicious samples into the datasets, leading to models that exhibit unintended behaviors, such
039
      as misclassification. These attacks are known as data poisoning, as described by Muñoz-González
      et al. (2017). Backdoor attacks, introduced by Gu et al. (2019), represent a specific form of data
040
      poisoning designed to embed a trigger within the model during the training phase. The resulting
041
      model behaves normally on benign inputs but misclassifies any image containing the trigger as the
042   target class during inference. A notable demonstration by Liu et al. (2018b) in a driving simulator
043   environment showed how a roadside billboard used as a trigger could cause an autonomous driving
044   system to fail, resulting in accidents.
045
      Early methods to incorporate triggers into models involved patching source class images with trig-
046
      gers and re-labeling them with a target class label (Gu et al. (2019); Chen et al. (2017)), making
047
      them easily identifiable by human observers. To visually hide triggers, Barni et al. (2019) added an
048   invisible sinusoidal signal to target class images, causing the model to associate the signal with that
049   class. Zhong et al. (2020) increased the stealthiness of attacks by adding imperceptible changes to
050   a small portion of the training data, resulting in clean-label attacks where images appear to match
051   their labels. Saha et al. (2020) extended this approach to transfer learning scenarios by injecting
052   poisoned samples into the fine-tuning dataset. Ning et al. (2021) proposed disguising poisoned im-
053   ages as noisy ones, further complicating their detection in the training data. Inspired by gradient
      matching techniques from Geiping et al. (2020), Souri et al. (2022) introduced the Sleeper Agent


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      attack, which embeds triggers during neural network training from scratch in a black-box setting,
055   where the attacker is unaware of the model architecture and training routine.
056
057   Despite extensive research dedicated to embedding a single trigger within models, the exploration
058
      of multi-trigger backdoor attacks in image classification remains limited, encompassing efforts that
      focus on methods to generate input-specific triggers (Nguyen & Tran (2020); Doan et al. (2022))
059
      as well as those utilizing multiple static triggers (Xue et al. (2024); Gong et al. (2021)). This study
060
      introduces Gradient Storm, an enhanced version of the Sleeper Agent attack (Souri et al. (2022)),
061   which is capable of injecting multiple backdoor triggers into neural networks simultaneously during
062   the training phase. Our main contributions are:
063
064          • Stronger Noisy Gradients: We strengthen the Sleeper Agent attack introduced by Souri
065            et al. (2022) through dividing the optimization process into multiple rounds each corre-
066            sponding to a different region of the parameter space.
067          • Multiple Triggers, Targets, and Attack Types: Our attack poisons a victim model with
068            backdoors of different kinds, each activated by its own trigger, and corresponding to a
069            unique source and target pair.
070
      This paper is organized as follows. Section 2 discusses related work on backdoor attacks and de-
071   fenses. Section 3 describes our attack scheme in detail. Section 4 presents the experimental results
072   demonstrating the effectiveness of our proposed method. Finally, Section 5 concludes our work.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
076
      Data poisoning attacks initially aimed at reducing the classification performance of traditional ma-
077
      chine learning models, such as Support Vector Machines (SVM) (Biggio et al. (2012)). Unlike these
078
      indiscriminate attacks, targeted attacks focus on altering model outputs for specific test instances
079   (Mozaffari-Kermani et al. (2014); Nelson et al. (2008)). Subsequent works have introduced clean-
080   label poisons, which appear visually normal, to minimize the likelihood of detection (Shafahi et al.
081   (2018)). In the context of targeted clean-label attacks, poisons have been specifically designed to
082   manipulate models in both transfer learning (Zhu et al. (2019); Aghakhani et al. (2021)) and from-
083   scratch training scenarios (Huang et al. (2020); Jagielski et al. (2021); Geiping et al. (2020)).
084
      Backdoor attacks are a targeted data poisoning approach, designed to bind a static pattern (known
085   as the trigger) to a target class in classification tasks. Researchers initially considered the trigger to
086   be another image blended with (Chen et al. (2017)) or patched to (Gu et al. (2019); Wenger et al.
087   (2021)) the original one. Subsequent studies explored the use of less conspicuous triggers to activate
088   the backdoor, including weak sinusoidal signals (Barni et al. (2019)), reflections (Liu et al. (2020)),
089   a warping effect (Nguyen & Tran (2021)), or a color space shift for all pixels (Jiang et al. (2023)).
090   Research has also been directed towards increasing the variety of triggers. Gong et al. (2021), draw-
091   ing on the mathematical formulation of the multi-trigger attack by Ji et al. (2018), have developed
092   a method utilizing multiple triggers, each designed to activate specific neurons in outsourced cloud
093
      environments. Furthermore, researchers have proposed an alternative type of attack involving gen-
      erative networks capable of producing triggers dynamically for any given input, thereby causing it
094
      to be classified into any desired class (Nguyen & Tran (2020); Doan et al. (2022)). These meth-
095
      ods result in a lack of control over the trigger and potentially significant computational expense for
096   designing the triggers. Recently, Xue et al. (2024) employed DCT Steganography to embed a text
097   trigger in the channels of an RGB image. Researchers have also investigated multi-trigger backdoor
098   attacks in the context of graph neural networks (Wang et al. (2024); Xu & Picek (2023)), neural code
099   models (Li et al. (2023)), and deep image compression networks (Yu et al. (2023)).
100
      To counter backdoor attacks, the most elementary defense mechanism involves identifying and ex-
101
      cluding poisoned data from the training dataset, often requiring prior knowledge of the proportion of
102   poisoned samples. For instance, Chen et al. (2019) proposed clustering samples to identify poisoned
103   clusters, while Tran et al. (2018) used singular value decomposition to define outliers in the feature
104   space. Researchers have also developed methods to remediate backdoored models, such as pruning
105   neurons with abnormal activations and fine-tuning (Liu et al. (2018a)), reconstructing and mitigating
106   triggers (Wang et al. (2019)), and using knowledge distillation to focus on original features (Li et al.
107   (2021b)). Additionally, Wu & Wang (2021) utilized clean data to reveal trigger patterns and prune
      related neurons, and Zeng et al. (2022) formulated backdoor removal as a minimax problem solved


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      through unlearning. Robust training algorithms are another approach to mitigating backdoor attacks.
109   Li et al. (2021a) increased the loss of misleading samples to prevent backdoor learning, while Hong
110   et al. (2020) used differentially private SGD, and Borgnia et al. (2021) suggested data augmentations
111   preserving differential privacy. Liu et al. (2022) added noise to training data to perturb examples
112   without performance degradation, and Gao et al. (2023) developed an adaptive data-splitting method.
113   These approaches are generally effective but often assume access to clean data or control over the
114   entire training process, conditions that may not always be feasible.

116   3     M ETHOD
117
118   Following the threat model introduced by Gu et al. (2019), we consider an attacker who supplies a
119   victim with poisoned data for the purpose of training a model from scratch. Given that the victim
120   may have the ability to inspect the data for anomalies with the assistance of expert analysts, it is
121   crucial for the attacker to ensure that the data does not appear to be conspicuously manipulated.
122   Therefore, inspired by Souri et al. (2022), we assume that the attacker is restricted to making minor
123   perturbations to a limited subset of the data. Although Souri et al. (2022) utilized these perturba-
124   tions to embed a single trigger in the resulting model for activating the attack later, we extend their
125
      approach to embed multiple triggers. This extension allows for the potential activation of various
      types of attacks during inference. Building on the assumptions in Souri et al. (2022), we assume that
126
      the attacker does not have knowledge of the architecture of the final model or the learning algorithm
127
      employed. Consequently, we design the poisons using a surrogate model. In Section 4.3, we demon-
128   strate that these poisons can effectively embed a backdoor in models with different architectures.

286   4.1   C OMPARISON TO OTHER ATTACKS
287
288
      We conduct a comparative analysis of the proposed Gradient Storm (GS) attack against several state-
      of-the-art backdoor attacks, including Blended (Chen et al. (2017)), Label-Consistent (LC) (Turner
289
      et al. (2019)), Refool (Liu et al. (2020)), Hidden Trigger Backdoor Attack (HTBA) (Saha et al.
290
      (2020)), and Sleeper Agent (SA) (Souri et al. (2022)). In all implementations, the ℓ∞ norm of the
291   perturbations is constrained to a maximum of 16/255. ResNet18 (He et al. (2016)) is utilized as the
292   target model across all attack scenarios, with an 8 × 8 RGB image serving as the trigger. All models
293   are trained for 80 epochs using the Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) optimizer with a momentum
294   of 0.9, a weight decay of 5e-4, and an initial learning rate of 0.01. The batch size is set to 128, and
295   all images are normalized to the range [0,1] before being standardized by subtracting the dataset
296   mean and dividing by the standard deviation.
297   To ensure a fair comparison, ResNet18 is also employed as the surrogate model for both the SA and
298   GS attacks. In the GS attack, we set S = 4 and R = 2, optimizing the poisons with T = 0.006,
299   followed by model retraining. For the SA attack, we distribute the optimization process evenly
300   across four retraining periods.
301
      The HTBA attack, originally designed for transfer learning scenarios that require a fixed feature
302
      extractor, necessitates a distinct approach. The network is initially trained on clean data for 80
303   epochs. Poisons are subsequently generated based on the feature space representations extracted
304   from the trained network. These poisons are then substituted for their benign counterparts in the
305   original dataset, which is subsequently used to train a new model from scratch. The effectiveness
306   of these poisons is evaluated in this from-scratch training scenario. To ensure fairness in the Refool
307   attack, which requires a candidate reflection set, we utilize the same RGB image alongside three
308   rotated versions at angles of 90, 180, and 270 degrees.
309   We present the experimental results using the following two metrics:
310
311          • Benign Accuracy (BA): The classification accuracy of the model on clean, unseen data
312            during the inference stage.
313
             • Attack Success Rate (ASR): The percentage of inputs containing the trigger, also unseen
314            during training, that are successfully misclassified into the target class as intended by the
315            attack.
316
317   The results of this comparative analysis are presented in Tables 1 and 2. For all experiments, the
318   source and target classes were selected randomly.

      Spectral Signatures: Proposed by Tran et al. (2018), this method involves initially training a neural
341   network on poisoned data to extract a learned representation for each input within each class. Singu-
342   lar Value Decomposition (SVD) is then performed on the representations of each class to compute
343   an outlier score for each sample. The network is subsequently retrained after removing outliers, and
344   this process is repeated over multiple iterations. The final model, which is expected to be free of
345   backdoors, is then returned.
346
      Activation Clustering: Chen et al. (2019) observed that while backdoor and target samples are
347   classified identically by the poisoned network, the underlying reasons for this classification differ.
348   Building on this observation, they clustered the learned representations of samples within each class
349   and found that, for the target class of backdoor attacks, activations tend to separate into two clusters
350   of approximately equal size. They then conducted cluster analysis to identify the poisoned cluster,
351   which was subsequently removed from the training process.
352
      DeepKNN: Peri et al. (2020) proposed a method that involves analyzing the nearest neighbors of
353   each sample in the feature space and removing those for which the model assigns a label that differs
354   from the majority of their neighbors. This approach has been empirically tested against targeted
355   clean-label data poisoning attacks.
356
      Gradient Shaping: Hong et al. (2020) observed that gradients computed in the presence of poisoned
357
      data exhibit significantly higher ℓ2 norms and orientations that diverge substantially from those of
358
      clean gradients. To address this, they employed Differentially Private SGD (DPSGD) to constrain
359   gradient magnitudes and reduce discrepancies in their orientations during model training.
360
361   Anti-Backdoor Learning (ABL): Li et al. (2021a) empirically identified a critical vulnerability in
362
      backdoor attacks: models tend to learn backdoored data significantly faster than clean data, with
      stronger attacks resulting in even more rapid convergence on the backdoored data. Leveraging this
363
      observation, they developed a method to isolate poisoned examples in the early stages of training
364
      and subsequently disrupt the correlation between backdoor examples and the target class in later
365   training stages.
366
367   DP-InstaHide: Building on the work of Zhang et al. (2018), who introduced MixUp as a powerful
368
      data augmentation technique, Borgnia et al. (2021) demonstrated that combining these augmenta-
      tions with random additive noise can effectively neutralize poisoning attacks with minimal accu-
369
      racy trade-off. Additionally, they provided a theoretical proof that, under certain assumptions, their
370
      method satisfies differential privacy.
371

464   5     C ONCLUSION
465
466   In this study, we introduced Gradient Storm, a powerful and innovative method for executing mul-
467   tiple backdoor attacks in machine learning models with minimal data manipulation. Our approach
468   demonstrates significant advancements in the design of adversarial poisons, allowing for precise
469   control over specific regions of the parameter space and enabling the successful execution of multi-
470   trigger attacks. Through comprehensive evaluation across two convolutional neural network archi-
471   tectures and two benchmark datasets, Gradient Storm has proven to be highly effective, achieving an
472   attack success rate exceeding 90% in both single-trigger and multi-trigger scenarios. Additionally,
473   the method shows strong resilience against a range of poisoning defense mechanisms, underscor-
474
      ing its potential impact on the security and trustworthiness of AI systems. These findings highlight
      the need for continued research into more robust defense strategies to counteract such sophisticated
475
      adversarial threats.
