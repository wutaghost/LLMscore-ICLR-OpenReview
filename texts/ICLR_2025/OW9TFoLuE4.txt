009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Monitoring and recognizing patterns in continuous sensing data is crucial for many
012            practical applications. These real-world time-series data are often nonstation-
013            ary, characterized by varying statistical and spectral properties over time. This
014            poses a significant challenge in developing learning models that can effectively
015            generalize across different distributions. In this work, based on our observation
016
               that nonstationary statistics for time-series classification tasks are intrinsically
               linked to the phase information, we propose a time-series domain generalization
017
               framework, PhASER. It consists of three key elements: 1) Hilbert transform-based
018
               phase augmentation that diversifies non-stationarity while preserving discrimina-
019            tory semantics, 2) separate magnitude-phase encoding by viewing time-varying
020            magnitude and phase as independent modalities, and 3) phase-residual feature
021            broadcasting by incorporating phase with a novel residual connection for inherent
022            regularization to enhance distribution invariant learning. Extensive evaluation on 5
023            datasets from sleep-stage classification, human activity recognition, and gesture
024            recognition against 13 state-of-the-art baseline methods demonstrate that PhASER
025            consistently outperforms the best baselines by an average of 5% and up to 11% in
026            some cases. Moreover, PhASER’s principles can also be applied broadly to boost
027
               the generalizability of existing time-series classification models.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Time-series data play a ubiquitous and crucial role in numerous real-world applications, such as
032   continuous monitoring for human activity recognition (Li et al., 2020), gesture identification (Ozdemir
033   et al., 2020), sleep tracking (Kemp et al., 2000), and more. Continuous time series often exhibit
034   non-stationarity, i.e., the statistical and spectral properties of the data evolve over time. Another
035   inherent challenge is the distribution shift due to the underlying sensing properties or subject-specific
036   attributes, commonly referred to as domain shift, which directly degrades the performance of time-
037   series models in real-world applications. Thus, developing methods for more generalizable pattern
038   recognition in nonstationary time series classification is crucial.
039   Most existing methods (Ragab et al., 2023a;b; He et al., 2023) tackle distribution shifts in time-series
040   applications via domain adaptation, assuming accessible target domain samples. Yet, obtaining data
041   from unseen distributions in advance is not always feasible. To overcome this challenge, a few
042   works (Gagnon-Audet et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2022) applied standard domain generalization (DG)
043   algorithms (Volpi et al., 2018; Sagawa et al., 2019; Parascandolo et al., 2020) to temporally-varying
044   time-series data, but reported a significant performance gap when compared with visual data. Recent
045
      research on DG tailored for time series explores latent-domain characterization (Lu et al., 2023; Du
      et al., 2021), augmentation strategies (Iwana & Uchida, 2021; Li et al., 2021), preservation of non-
046
      stationarity dictionary (Liu et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2021c), and utilization of spectral characteristics of
047
      time series (He et al., 2023; Yang & Hong, 2022; Kim et al., 2021a). While successful in some cases,
048   these methods have their limitations. Latent-domain characterization heavily relies on the hypotheses
049   of latent domains, limiting its broader applicability. Augmentation strategies (shift, jittering, masking,
050   etc.) for time series may not be universally applicable and can impair the task (Iwana & Uchida, 2021).
051   For instance, in physiological signal analysis, morphological alterations from augmentations are
052   harmful, and time-slicing is unsuitable for periodic signals. Advanced augmentation techniques like
053   spectral perturbations (time-frequency warping, decomposition techniques, etc.) are usually heavily
      parametric (Wen et al., 2021) and application-specific. Other approaches specific to preserving

078   Our Approach and Contributions. We propose a novel Phase-Augmented Separate Encoding and
079   Residual (PhASER) framework to achieve domain-generalizable classification for nonstationary
080   real-world time series. Figure 1 illustrates an overview of PhASER, which includes three key modules.
081   First, we diversify the non-stationarity of source domain data through an intra-instance phase shift,
082   by leveraging the generality and non-parametric nature of Hilbert Transform (HT) (King, 2009) to
083   introduce a phase-shift-based augmentation. Next, we apply a novel strategy to encode the time-
084   varying magnitude and phase responses separately for enhanced integration of the time-frequency
085   information. Finally, we design an effective broadcasting mechanism with a non-linear residual
086
      connection between the phase-encoded embedding and the backbone representation to learn domain-
      invariant and generalizable (He et al., 2020; Marion et al., 2023) task-specific features (He et al.,
087
      2016). We experiment with 13 baselines on 5 datasets to quantitatively demonstrate PhASER’s
088
      superiority in learning generalizable representations, even in challenging scenarios like transferring
089   from one domain to multiple domains. Additionally, we provide design insights through ablation
090   analysis, explore PhASER’s applicability to other architectures, and present qualitative visualizations
091   of its learned representations.

158   Overview of PhASER. As shown before in Figure 1, our proposed PhASER framework begins with
159   an augmentation module that utilizes the Hilbert Transform to generate out-of-phase augmentations
160   for time series. These augmentations not only diversify non-stationarity (temporal data statistics)
161   but also preserve category-discriminatory semantics for classification tasks. Next, the short-term
      Fourier Transform (STFT) is employed to obtain temporal magnitude and phase responses. Two

      4    R ELATED W ORKS
505
506
      Nonstationary Time-Series Analysis. In real-world scenarios, nonstationary time-series data pose
      challenges for forecasting and classification (Esling & Agon, 2012; Ismail Fawaz et al., 2019; Dama &
507
      Sinoquet, 2021). While various solutions exist, including Bayesian models, normalization techniques,
508
      recurrent neural networks, and transformers, systematic works addressing non-stationarity’s impact
509   on time-series classification are limited (Liang, 2005; Chen & Sun, 2021; Liu et al., 2023b; Chang
510   et al., 2021; Passalis et al., 2019; Tang et al., 2021; Du et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022; Wang et al.,
511   2022a). Our study is the first to rigorously address the impact of non-stationarity on time-series
512   out-of-distribution classification, complementing empirical findings from prior works (Zhao et al.,
513   2020; Tonekaboni et al., 2020; Eldele et al., 2023).
514   Domain Generalizable Learning. While domain generalizable learning is well-established in visual
515   data (Wang et al., 2022b), applying it to time-series data poses unique challenges. Traditional ap-
516   proaches like data augmentation (Wang et al., 2021) and domain discrepancy minimization (Zhang &
517   Chen, 2023; Li et al., 2018) face limitations in time series due to less flexible augmentation and broader
518   domain concepts (Wen et al., 2021; Wilson et al., 2020). Some studies explore domain-invariant
519
      representation learning (Lu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023) and learnable data transformation (Qin
      et al., 2023). We highlight the non-stationarity of time series and its role in domain discrepancy,
520
      drawing on evidence from the visual domain regarding the importance of phase (Kim et al., 2023;
521
      Xu et al., 2021). A handful of works hint at phase’s role in domain-invariant learning in time-series
522   applications (Lu et al., 2022a), and there is evidence in traditional signal processing that phase-only
523   information is sufficient to reconstruct a signal (Masuyama et al., 2023; Jacques & Feuillen, 2020;
524   2021). Inspired by these insights, we propose a novel phase-driven framework with an augmentation
525   module and a phase-anchored representation learning to address non-stationarity and minimize
526   domain discrepancy.

529
530
      PhASER achieves domain generalization without explicit domain characterization or accessing target
531   domain samples, by diversifying non-stationarity and anchoring design to signal phase information.
532   Our evaluation is currently limited to categorical tasks due to a scarcity of publicly available datasets
533   with distinct domain definitions for continuous tasks like regression. Our future work aims to develop
534   a universal representation for generalization across various tasks in dynamic conditions.

1023   Augmented Dickey Fuller (ADF) Test. This is a statistical tool to assess the non-stationarity of a
1024   given time-series signal. This test operates under a null hypothesis H0 where the signal has a unit-root.
1025   The existence of unit-root is a guarantee that the signal is non-stationary (Said & Dickey, 1984). To
       reject H0 , the statistic value of the ADF test should be less than the critical values associated with a


                                                         19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       significance level of 0.05 (denoted by p, the probability of observing such a test statistic under the
1027   null hypothesis). Throughout the paper, for multivariate time series, the average ADF statistics across
1028   all variates are reported. Besides, since this is a statistical tool to evaluate non-stationarity for each
1029   instance of time-series data, we provide an average of this number across a dataset to give the reader
1030   a view of the degree of non-stationarity.

       Phase Augmentation. In this work, we are particularly interested in learning representations robust
1032
       to temporal distribution shifts. Incorporating a phase shift in a signal is a less-studied augmentation
1033   technique. One of the main challenges is that real-world signals are not composed of a single
1034   frequency component and accurately estimating and controlling the shifting of the phase while
1035   retaining the magnitude spectrum of a signal is difficult. To solve this, we leverage the analytic
1036   transformation of a signal using the Hilbert Transform. The key advantages of this technique
1037   are maintaining global temporal dependencies and magnitude spectrum, no exploration of design
1038   parameters and being extendible to non-stationary and periodic time series.
1039   Lets walk through a simple example for a signal, x(t) = 2cos(w0 t) which can be written in
1040   the polar coordinates as x(t) = eiw0 t + e−iw0 t . Applying the HT conditions from Equation 4,
1041   HT(x(t)) = 2sin(w0 t). Essentially, HT shifts the signal by π/2 radians. We conduct this instance-
1042   level augmentation for each variate of the time series input. The aim is to diversify the phase
1043   representation. We use the scipy (Virtanen et al., 2020) library to implement this augmentation.
1044
       STFT Specifications. Non-stationary signals contain time-varying spectral properties. We use STFT

1332   ization and SiLU (Elfwing et al., 2018) activation function. This style of architecture is closely
1333   adapted from the basic building blocks in BCResNet (Kim et al., 2021a). After average pooling
1334   the FTem can assume any backbone as per the requirements of the application. As demonstrated
1335   previously in Section B, the choice of backbone is not central to our design here. We find that some
1336   applications(like WISDM and GR) benefit from attention-based temporal encoding more than others.
1337   For the attention-based version of FTem we used a multi-headed attention based on a transformer
1338   encoder (Vaswani et al., 2017). Regarding positional encoding, we used a simple sinusoid-based
1339   encoding and added it to the sequence representation rDep . However, arriving at the best positional
1340
       encoding for numerical time-series data is an active area of research (Kazemi et al., 2019; Tang et al.,
       2023; Mohapatra et al., 2023) given its uniqueness compared to typical natural language inputs and
1341
       further optimizations can be carried out. For the the convolution-based FTem we simply use a kernel
1342
       of size (1 × 3) in a 2D convolution layer to conduct temporal convolutions.
1343
1344   For the classification head, gCls , we apply 2D convolution layers to have the number of output channels
1345   equal to the number of classes in an application, followed by softmax operation. Interestingly, if the
1346
       choice of FTem remains convolutional the entire network can be implemented in a purely convolutional
       form allowing applicability to real-time problems. The model sizes across the different datasets range
1347
       from 40k-100k trainable parameters (based on the number of variates, temporal encoding etc.) which
1348
       is modest and can be further tuned for resource-constrained applications by adjusting the c parameter.

1414   D.5   P HASE - DRIVEN NST RANS
1415
       Non-stationary transformer, NSTrans (Liu et al., 2022), applies a destationarizing attention around
1416
       the transformer block. Since it is typically used for forecasting tasks, it comprises of encoder
1417   and a decoder module. For adapting this model to classification we update the design to conduct
1418   normalization and denormalization around the encoder block. We use this modified version of
1419   NSTrans as the FTem module in PhASER and observe significant improvement in performance as
1420   shown in Figure 4.
1421
       Note: The poor performance of the Nonstationary transformer can be attributed to two main reasons:
1422
1423   (1) Originally, the Nonstationary transformer was designed for forecasting time-series tasks and
1424   employs an encoder-decoder style architecture. To successfully apply the core module of the
1425   Nonstationary transformer (Liu et al., 2022), stationarization-destationarization, the input-output
1426
       space needs to remain consistent. This consistency is naturally ensured in an encoder-decoder
       design. However, in our classification applications, we only utilize the encoder module. Although we
1427
       maintain the input-output dimensions, the semantics of the latent space and input space are not the
1428
       same. Hence, destationarization is not very successful.
1429
1430   (2) Nonstationary transformer inputs consist of raw time-series data with positional encoding. Given
1431   the fine-grained nature of current tasks, such an approach can be more data-hungry as they try
1432
       to establish a relation (attention) among every time step. Therefore, it may not perform well on
       short-range classification tasks that focus on domain generalization. This indicates a limitation in
1433
       its direct usage for optimizing a categorical objective function using only the encoder part with a
1434
       classification head.

       D.7.1    T RADITIONAL AUGMENTATION
1477
1478   For time series, brute augmentations like scaling, reverting, cropping, and jittering may not be always
1479   suitable as they may alter the morphological properties that are important for the task. Even more
1480   advanced techniques like frequency-time warping and additive noise, need deliberate characterization
1481   of the signal’s frequency response to meaningfully provide an augmented view while retaining the
1482
       task-relevant semantics. This is one of the key motivating factors for us to explore a general-purpose
       augmentation strategy that diversifies the non-stationarity in a signal without altering its task-specific
1483
       semantics (magnitude and frequency responses).
1484
1485   To demonstrate the use of traditional augmentations with PhASER for human-activity recognition,
1486   we incorporate the following augmentations proposed by past works (Qin et al., 2023; Um et al.,
1487   2017) on the HHAR dataset.
1488
               • Rotation - incorporating arbitrary rotation matrices to simulate different sensor locations.
1489
1490           • Permutation - random temporal perturbation for fixed window within each sample (Um
1491
                 et al., 2017).
1492           • Circular Time-shift - shifting the signal by a random time interval, constrained by a pre-
1493             defined maximum time-shift parameter (20% of the sample length in this case) for each
1494
                 sample. The shifted time points from the trailing edge are wrapped around and padded to
                 the leading edge of the signal
1495
1496   We incorporate these augmentations in place of the Hilbert augmentation and apply the PhASER. We
1497   also run an experiment with identical settings with no augmentations and illustrate in Figure 5. These
1498   results are indicative that arbitrary augmentations in the time domain do not necessarily diversify
1499   the non-stationarity of a signal. Hence, PhASER principles like residual connections to re-introduce
1500   nonstationary dictionary as phase-projection and broadcasting (using gRes ) do not bode well here,
1501   and even the performance of a no-augmentation scenario is sometimes better than the traditional
1502   temporal augmentations for domain-generalization tasks in this case. However, in the future, we
1503   may encounter applications where established augmentation strategies, in combination with Hilbert
1504
       augmentation, might be the best choice. In this work, we aim to propose a more generic framework
       that can benefit most time-series classification tasks to achieve better generalizability.
