                                                   A BSTRACT
024            When a human undertakes a test, their responses likely follow a pattern: if they
025            answered an easy question (2 ⇥ 3) incorrectly, they would likely answer a more
026            difficult one (2 ⇥ 3 ⇥ 4) incorrectly; and if they answered a difficult question
027            correctly, they would likely answer the easy one correctly. Anything else hints at
028            memorization. Do current visual recognition models exhibit a similarly structured
029            learning capacity? In this work, we consider the task of image classification and
030
               study if those models’ responses follow that pattern. Since real images aren’t
               labeled with difficulty, we first create a dataset of 100 categories, 10 attributes, and
031
               3 difficulty levels using recent generative models: for each category (e.g., dog)
032
               and attribute (e.g., occlusion), we generate images of increasing difficulty (e.g., a
033            dog without occlusion, a dog only partly visible). We find that most of the models
034            do in fact behave similarly to the aforementioned pattern around 80-90% of the
035            time. Using this property, we then present a new way to evaluate those models’
036            image recognition ability. Instead of testing the model on every possible test image,
037            we create an adaptive test akin to GRE, in which the model’s performance on the
038            current round of images determines the test images in the next round. This allows
039            the model to skip over questions too easy/hard for itself, and helps us get its overall
040            performance in fewer steps.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
043   Imagine a math teacher grading a student’s answer sheet, and finds that they got the answer of (2 ⇥ 3)
044   wrong but the answer for (2 ⇥ 3 ⇥ 4) right. The teacher will rightly wonder whether the student
045   properly learnt the concept of multiplication or whether they memorized the answer to the more
046   difficult question. This is because there is a characteristic way in which humans learn any concept:
047   if they cannot answer an easy question, they very likely cannot answer a more difficult one. And
048   conversely, if they can answer a difficult question, they most certainly can answer an easier one as
049   well. Neural networks are also trained to learn concepts to perform a task. Do they also learn those
050
      concepts in a similarly characteristic way?
051   In this work, we study this question for the task of image classification. Our goal is to see if modern
052   visual recognition systems (e.g., ConvNext (Liu et al., 2022), ViT Dosovitskiy et al. (2020)) have that
053   human-like behavior to easy/hard-to-classify images. Since there does not exist any real data labeled
      with ground-truth difficulty, we propose to generate one instead. Recent image generative models

054
      have become capable of generating very high quality images (Rombach et al., 2022), good enough to
055   be used in training recognition models (Yu et al., 2023; Azizi et al., 2023), and we believe that they
056   are good enough to be used for our evaluation. With the aid of recent large language and generative
057   models (GPT-4 Achiam et al. (2023) + DALL-E 3 Ramesh et al. (2021)), we design a prompting
058   system to generate descriptions of images of three levels of difficulty. For example, an image of a
059   fully visible dog and an image of dog only partly visible can be considered to be image descriptions
060   of an easy- and hard-to-classify images respectively. We use DALL-E 3 to take in these different
061   difficulty level prompts and generate images while faithfully preserving the desired attributes. Fig. 1
062   (left) gives an example.
063   Once we have the easy, medium and hard-to-classify test images, we record if the model predicts the
064   class correctly or incorrectly. Fig. 1 (right) depicts the 8 possibilities of model’s behavior (green/red
065   represent correct/incorrect response). If the model truly learns to classify images by developing
066   the aforementioned notion of easy/hard concepts, then its responses should fall under 4 out of the
067   8 possibilities highlighted in a red box. Our first key finding is that, for most of the current visual
068   recognition models, their responses do indeed fall under the 4 highlighted categories around 80-90%
069
      of the time. This result hints that even without an explicit supervision, visual recognition models
      learn to learn things in a structured way.
070
071   While an intriguing result in its own, we believe that this can have applications, especially in the
072   way we evaluate models. We take inspiration from how students are often tested using standardized
073   tests, like the Graduate Record Examination (GRE), for admissions into U.S. universities. These tests
074   are adaptive in nature, where questions in the next round depend on how well the student does in
075
      the current one. So, for example, if the student cannot solve easy-medium questions, there is not
      much point giving them difficult questions in the next round; i.e., one can reliably predict that they
076
      will get zero points for those hard questions. We develop a similar GRE-type test to evaluate visual
077
      recognition models on the generated dataset proposed above. The test is broken up into multiple
078   rounds. In the first round, the model is shown images of medium difficulty on average. Its score
079   in this round determines the distribution of easy/medium/hard questions in the next round. That
080   is, similar to GRE, we can skip over images that are too easy/hard for the model to classify. Thus,
081   instead of evaluating the model on every possible image in the test set, this way of dynamically
082   selecting the images helps approximate that total score of the model on the whole set using only 25%
083   of the test images.
084   Additionally, the newly proposed dataset can have usefulness in and of itself. We generate images
085   from 100 categories taken from ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009). For each category, we consider
086   10 attributes. Within each attribute, we generate 12 images for 3 levels of difficulty, bringing the
087   total number of images to 36,000. However, different from standard benchmarks like the ImageNet
088   validation set, these 36k images are labeled with attribute value, difficulty, in addition to the ground-
089   truth class. This can enable analysing models on a much finer level (e.g., ResNet-50 struggles to
090   detect dogs from a side view).
091   In summary, our work has the following contributions. We present a new method to study the learning
092   dynamics of modern visual recognition systems using the concept of example difficulty. To do this,
093   we create a new test set of synthetic images labeled with class, attribute, and difficulty level. Our
094   results indicate that most of the models do in fact develop a semantically meaningful notion of
095   example difficulty while learning visual concepts, without having access to any external supervision.
096   Using this newly found property, we develop a multi-round adaptive test, inspired by GRE, which
097   steers the future test images according to a model’s ongoing performance. This facilitates skipping
098
      over too easy/hard questions, and helps assess a model’s performance using a fraction of test images.

136   static benchmarks have leaked cues, reducing their effectiveness (Mayilvahanan et al., 2023). To
137   address this and to reduce the cost of manual collection, recent work generates synthetic images

139   images, while Spawrious (Lynch et al., 2023) leverages Stable Diffusion to generate datasets with

      spurious correlations. PUG (Bordes et al., 2024) offers photorealistic data using Unreal Engine, and

141
      approach stands out by systematically incorporating both difficulty levels (Easy, Medium, Hard) and
142
      specific attributes using a detailed prompting system with the powerful generative model DALL-E 3,
143   unlike other benchmarks that focus on either a single difficulty level, have limited attributes (e.g.,

145
146
      Adaptive model evaluation. Inspired by computerized adaptive testing (CAT) (Van der Linden
      & Glas, 2000), used in exams like the GRE, we develop algorithms for adaptive testing in image
147
      classification benchmarks. Similar to CAT, which does not require all questions to be answered
148
      and instead efficiently assesses examinees with fewer questions based on examinee responses, our
149   framework evaluates classification models by selecting a subset of samples, reducing computational
150   demands while maintaining assessment accuracy. Prabhu et al. (2024) presents an efficient evaluation
151   framework to maintain a lifelong benchmark by leveraging dynamic programming to rank and sub-
152   select samples, whereas our approach generates unseen images using DALL-E, ensuring models are
153   tested on genuinely new data, mitigating the risk of data leakage, and enhancing the reliability of
154   generalization evaluation.

      3    V ISION M ODELS ’ B EHAVIOR ON E ASY ! H ARD I MAGES
157   Humans learn concepts in a progressive way - it is only possible for them to solve hard questions if
158   they have the ability to solve easier ones first (Zacks & Tversky, 2001; Newtson, 1973). We want to
159   study if visual recognition models trained for image classification also learn concepts in a similar way.
160   To do this, we first explain in Sec. 3.1 our process of creating a test data having images of various
161   difficulty (akin to questions for humans). Then, in Sec. 3.2, we present a method to analyze a model’s
      response to such images to see if it mimics a human-like way of learning concepts.

179   3.1     DATASET CREATION
180
      For the task of image classification, the standard data format is images paired with their ground truth
181
      labels D = {(x1 , y1 ), (x2 , y2 ), ...}. However, for our purpose, not only do we need the information
182
      that yi is the ground-truth label of xi , but we also need to know how difficult it is for xi to be classified
183   as yi . Hence, our first task is to formalize this notion of difficulty for our problem.
184
185   3.1.1    U NDERSTANDING SAMPLE DIFFICULTY
186
187   Consider Fig. 2 and observe the group of three images in the top right corner. All of the images have
188   ‘golden retriever’ as the main subject. Yet, we, as humans, can intuit that it is easiest to correctly
189   classify the content of the left most image and most difficult to do it for the right most one. This
190
      is because from left to right, the dog is getting more occluded. Other triplets show similar easy !
      hard progression for other attributes. The important point from these figures is that the difficulty of a
191
      sample is best understood in relation to a particular attribute type (e.g., occlusion). However, to the
192
      best of our knowledge, no dataset of real images contains human annotations indicating difficulty
193   about a sample in that way.
194
195   Hence, given the recent advances in large language and image generative models, we propose to
196
      synthesize images with the desired attributes. Text-to-image generative models can now generate very
      high quality images (Rombach et al., 2022; OpenAI, 2024), so good that they have even been used to
197
      train image classification models and shown promise (Yu et al., 2023; Azizi et al., 2023). Furthermore,
198
      using the text medium, we can use very precise language to describe more easy/hard-to-classify
199   images. Hence, we propose to use these generative models to design an evaluation setup.

249   3.2   H IERARCHICAL LEARNING SCORE
250
      We now describe how to use this dataset of easy/medium/hard-to-classify images to study whether
251
      models learn concepts hierarchically. If we consider the combination of each class with its attribute
252   type, there will be a total of 100 ⇥ 10 = 1000 combinations ({Golden retriever, occlusion} is one
253   such example). For each such class-attribute combination, we create 12 triplets, each having one easy,
254   one medium, and one hard image. Hence, there are a total of 12000 triplets of test images.
255
      For each visual recognition model that is of interest to us, we test it on these images and record whether
256
      their prediction matches the ground-truth object category. The model’s response on each triplet
257
      will follow one of 8 patterns depending on whether it gets the easy/medium/hard image correctly
258   or incorrectly classified; these are shown in Fig. 1 right (red/green colors mean correct/incorrect
259   prediction respectively). We compute the percentage of each of these 8 patterns over all 12,000
260   triplets. Now, if we consider the following human-like hierarchical learning principle - A model
261   should correctly answer a harder question only if it can answer all easier questions, we see that
262   only 4 out of those 8 patterns satisfy the requirement (highlighted with red box). We call them
263   ‘principle-following patterns’, and define the hierarchical-learning score to be the percentage of
264   model’s responses on 12000 triplets that fall under principle-following patterns. The more a model
265   follows this principle, the higher its hierarchical-learning score should be.

267   2020), (ii) ConvNext (Liu et al., 2022) and (iii) ResNet-101 (He et al., 2016) - each trained on
268   ImageNet1k (Deng et al., 2009) and LAION (Schuhmann et al., 2022) using cross-entropy and CLIP
269   objective (Radford et al., 2021), respectively. This gives us a total of 6 trained models which we test
      using the setting described above. The results are shown in Fig. 4 (left), where we plot the percentage

320   learning gets tested. Many standardized tests like the GRE are adaptive in nature. Based on how
321   well the student is doing at any point, future questions are adapted to match the student’s ability.
322   For example, if a student is struggling to answer questions like (2 ⇥ 3) and (4 ⇥ 5), there is not
323   much point in testing them on (22 + 32 ) ⇥ (4 + 5). We can reasonably predict that they will get zero
      points for the latter question, and hence can avoid testing them on all possible (easy/medium/hard)

344   has a test set of 36k images uniformly spread over 100 object categories, 10 attribute types, and 3
345
      levels of difficulty. Given that visual recognition models also seem to learn in a hierarchical way, we
      investigate a similar GRE-type test for them, using which we can get a good estimate of model’s
346
      overall performance without needing to test it on every single easy/medium/hard image.
347
348   To test a model, we start by iterating over all the classes and attribute types. In each such class +
349   attribute type combination (there are 1000 of these), there are 3 difficulty levels, each with 12 images,
350   i.e., 3 ⇥ 12 = 36 test images. The goal is to predict how a model performs on these 36 images without
351
      needing to evaluate it on all 36 images. Hence, we conduct a test borrowing principles from GRE.
      Instead of the model being tested on all images in the same round at once, we consider two rounds of
352
      test images. In the first round, we randomly select 1 easy, 2 medium, and 1 hard image. The average
353
      difficulty is medium. The model is tested on these 4 images, and we assign it a score based on how
354   well it did. Similar to GRE, one gets more points for solving harder images. We assign the model 1,
355   2, and 4 points for correctly classifying an easy, medium, and hard image, respectively; and 0 points
356   for misclassification. In the next round, the model is tested on 5 new images. The distribution of these
357   new images depends on the model’s total score in the first round (min/max score is 0/9 respectively).
358   The mapping from total score to second round images’ distribution is shown in Fig. 5. Similar to
359   the first round, the model gets assigned points for correctly classifying images. All throughout the
360   process, the model is tested only on x easy, y medium, and z hard test images, where x + y + z = 9.
361   So, after the end of the second round, we can collect the following information about the model: (i)
362
      its total score, and (ii) classification accuracies on easy/medium/hard images.
363   Once the model has gone through all the class/attribute combinations, and we have collected these
364   information from each session (first + second round), there are multiple ways to combine that
365   information to analyze the model. We can get an attribute level score by averaging each session’s
366   score across all the 100 classes. Similarly, we can get attribute level accuracy, either overall across
367   easy/medium/hard questions or per difficulty, also averaged across the classes. This is helpful if we
368
      want to study which attributes a model struggles at, discussed more in Sec. 5. Obviously, one can get
      a global score/accuracy, averaged across attributes dimension, for the model. The key part in all of
369
      these analyses is that they can be done using only a fraction of the 36k images. In the next section,
370
      we discuss how accurate our predictions (score/accuracy) can be when compared against those same
371   values computed over the whole set.

      5.3   H OW CLOSELY DOES A DAPTIVE E VALUATION FOLLOW F ULL E VALUATION ?
450
451   We next evaluate our GRE-style adaptive evaluation discussed in Sec. 4, comparing to what the
452   performance would be with the standard way of evaluating a model on the entire dataset across the
453   ten attributes and three difficulty levels.
454   To measure classification performance of a model, in addition to standard classification accuracy, we
455   compute a GRE-style score to reward getting more difficult examples correct: Score = (correcteasy ⇥
456   1) + (correctmedium ⇥ 2) + (correcthard ⇥ 4), i.e., 1, 2, and 4 points for correctly classifying an
457   easy, medium, and hard image; and 0 points for misclassification. Note, one advantage of using a
458   GRE-style score is its ability to differentiate between models with similar accuracy. For example,
459   when two models, such as ConvNext-B and CLIP-ViT-B16, achieve close accuracy scores (70.2 and
460   69.8, respectively) in Table 2, the GRE-style score (59.2 vs. 57.6) can highlight the differences by
461   showing which model is more successful in handling more challenging questions.
462   Our full generated test set has a total of 36,000 images—comprising 12 images for each combination
463   of 100 classes, 10 attributes, and 3 difficulty levels. We refer to the evaluation results from this
464   complete set as ‘Static 12,’ which serves as our ground truth (full evaluation baseline). To validate
465   our adaptive testing method, we first establish a baseline called ‘Static 3.’ This baseline is created by
466   randomly selecting 3 images out of the 12 for each difficulty level. This produces a subset containing
467   100 (classes) × 10 (attributes) × 3 (difficulty levels) × 3 (images) = 9,000 images. Our adaptive
468
      testing then selects 9 samples across the three difficulty levels for each attribute and class, allowing
      the number of selected images for each difficulty level to vary. This also results in a subset of 100
469
      (classes) × 10 (attributes) × 9 (images) = 9,000 images, matching the size of the Static 3 subset, but
470
      with a different difficulty distribution than that of Static 3. We then evaluate the classifier on these
471   subsets, aiming to achieve results that correlate with the full test set (‘Static 12’) while using fewer
472   images and potentially maintaining smaller errors compared to the Static 3 strategy.
473
474
      We compare the performance of Static 3 and our adaptive testing with the full evaluation (Static 12) in
      Table 2. This evaluation is repeated three times, and the average error is reported. The results indicate
475
      that our adaptive testing provides accurate performance detail estimates using fewer test images than
476
      the full set and achieves smaller score and accuracy errors compared to the Static 3 strategy. This
477   confirms that based on the hierarchical learning pattern of common image classification models, it is
478   possible to perform adaptive testing to expedite the evaluation process.

506   6     D ISCUSSION AND LIMITATIONS
507
508   The typical way one evaluates a visual recognition model is to compute its average top-1 accuracy
509   over a standard test set (e.g., ImageNet). However, there have been concerns that models can get
510   over optimized on such benchmarks (Recht et al., 2019). We believe that our work can help in a
511   better assessment of models in two ways. First, since we can always generate (on-the-fly) a new fresh
512
      test dataset for evaluation, there is much less chance for a model overfitting, even if one is simply
      interested in classification accuracy. Second, the hierarchical learning score measures a different
513
      dimension of learning ability; it is less about how accurate a model is, but more about whether that
514
      capability is grounded in some fundamental learning principle, or in memorizing examples.
515
516   However, this score is not immune to extreme cases. If the model to be tested has completely
517   overfitted to test images, then it can get {X, X, X} for most of them and hence get a high hierarchical
518
      learning score. Finally, while our approach of using DALL-E 3 to generate a synthetic dataset offers a
      novel way to study model behavior across varying difficulty levels, it has certain limitations. DALL-E
519
      sometimes struggles with generating images for unfamiliar classes, such as “African Hunting Dog,”
520
      often producing images of other dog breeds labeled incorrectly, which can lead to noisy annotations.
521   Additionally, for complex or uncommon prompts like “A carousel in an amusement park, almost
522   entirely hidden behind a festival tent,” DALL-E may generate simplified images, such as a festival
523   tent near a carousel but without the intended occlusion, resulting in unintended “easy” sample. These
524   issues could introduce bias into our dataset, potentially affecting the balanced evaluation of models.
525   We manually reviewed and removed some of these examples, but future work could explore more
526   advanced generative models to address these challenges.
527
528   7     C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this work, we explored whether modern visual recognition models exhibit human-like learning
531
      behaviors when classifying images of varying difficulty. By leveraging advanced generative models
      (GPT-4 and DALL-E 3), we generated a synthetic dataset annotated with class, attribute, and difficulty
532
      level to test this concept. Our findings reveal that most models do, in fact, demonstrate a structured
533
      understanding of example difficulty, even without explicit supervision. This insight opens up new
534   possibilities for model evaluation, leading us to develop a multi-round adaptive test inspired by
535   standardized exams like the GRE. This adaptive testing method significantly reduces evaluation time
536   by selecting test images based on the model’s ongoing performance, providing a comprehensive
537   assessment using only a subset of the dataset. Additionally, the synthetic dataset itself, with its detailed
538   annotations, offers a valuable resource for more granular model analysis. Our work contributes a
539   novel perspective on understanding learning dynamics in visual recognition models and proposes an
      efficient, dynamic approach to their evaluation.
