009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Gaussian process regression (GPR) is a popular nonparametric regression method
012            based on Bayesian principles that, unlike most machine learning techniques, pro-
013            vides uncertainty estimates for its predictions. Recent GPR research has focused
014            on enhancing robustness to model misspecification but has often neglected im-
015            provements to the underlying methods for computing bounds. In addition, current
016            GPR methods rely heavily on scaling posterior standard deviations and assume
017            well-specified models, both of which reduce GPR’s adaptability and accuracy. To
018
               address these limitations, we draw inspiration from the chaining method (Tala-
               grand, 2014), and derive chaining bounds for the prediction intervals of GPR, of-
019
               fering a more flexible and accurate approach to handling model uncertainty. Our
020
               experimental results validate our theoretical findings, and demonstrate that our
021            method outperforms existing approaches on synthetic and real-world datasets.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   For many applications, especially those requiring safety assurances, obtaining reliable uncertainty
027   estimates is crucial. In this regard, Gaussian process regression (GPR), a flexible non-parametric
028   Bayesian method, is becoming increasingly popular in machine learning fields such as learning-
029   based control methods. GPR assumes that the observed data is generated by a Gaussian process
030   (GP) with independently and identically distributed Gaussian noise. The GP can be fitted to training
031   data and be used to generate predictions along with their associated uncertainty estimates.
032   Bounds are a way to measure uncertainty in GPR, and both rigorous and practical bounds are the
033   goals of existing research. Wu & Schaback (1993) use the classic method of Fourier transforms to
034   achieve such bounds. By exploiting the properties of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS),
035   Schaback (1999) derives uniform error bounds with faster convergence rates. Relying on an upper
036   bound of the maximum information gain, Srinivas et al. (2009; 2012) and Chowdhury & Gopalan
037   (2017) successively improve methods for frequentist uncertainty bounds.
038   Given the severe consequences of incorrect hyperparameter specification, recent research has shifted
039   focus to improving robustness. Lederer et al. (2019) introduce probabilistic Lipschitz constants to
040   reduce reliance on prior knowledge. Fiedler et al. (2021) include an error term to modify an objective
041   bound function and improve its resilience to noise. Capone et al. (2022) improve robustness by
042   calculating error bounds based on a given range of hyperparameters. Recently, Papadopoulos (2024)
043   utilizes conformal prediction to calibrate prediction intervals for robustness.
044   However, this recent line of GPR methods primarily focuses on enhancing robustness but remains
045   constrained by their reliance on scaling the posterior standard deviation, without fundamentally
046   improving their approach to deriving GPR bounds. These approaches also often assume a well-
047   specified model and heavily depend on hyperparameters, which limits their adaptability to new
048   domains and can result in inaccurate error estimates. To address these limitations, we draw inspi-
049   ration from chain-based techniques (Talagrand, 2014), and propose a chaining-based method. By
050
      decomposing the problem into smaller, more refined stages, our method enables more effective error
      control and improved robustness, especially in complex domains.
051
052   In our work, we introduce not only general bounds but also tailored, rigorous bounds for commonly
053   used covariance functions in GPR, such as the Radial Basis Function (RBF) and Matérn kernels.
      These bounds deepen the theoretical understanding of the kernels’ behaviors and enhance their ver-

055   the superior performance of our method on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

061   2.1   G AUSSIAN P ROCESS R EGRESSION
062
063   Gaussian process regression (GPR) serves as a robust, non-parametric Bayesian approach to regres-
064   sion (Williams & Rasmussen, 2006). A Gaussian process (GP) defined over an index set or input set
065   T is characterized by a collection of random variables, such that any finite subset has a multivariate
066
      normal distribution. In practice, a GP is used to define a distribution over a family of functions
      {f } that could describe the data, and we write f (x) ∼ GP(m(x), K(x, x′ )) to indicate that the
067
      function f (x) is sampled from its corresponding GP. A GP is fully specified by its mean function
068
      m(x) (which represents the average value of the functions in the family at each point x) and its
069   covariance function K(x, x′ ) (which reflects the extent to which the values of the functions in the
070   family vary together at the points x and x′ ). Popular covariance functions include the radial basis
071   function Kernel (RBF) kernel and Matérn kernel. For simplicity, it is often assumed without loss of
072   generality that m(x) ≡ 0.

075   2.2   C HAINING
076
077   Chaining is a mathematical technique consisting of a succession of steps that provide successive
078   approximations of an index space (T, d), where T is an index set or input set, and d is a metric on
079   T . Its fundamental idea is to group variables Xt that are nearly identical and approximate them at
080
      successive levels of granularity (Talagrand, 2014). By doing this, we achieve more effective bounds,
      especially in cases where many variables are similar (Asadi & Abbe, 2020). This approach mitigates
081
      the risk of large errors that can arise from such correlations.
082
083   To illustrate, consider a stochastic
                                 P         process (Xt )t∈T , and the difference between Xt and Xt0 is ex-
084   pressed as Xt − Xt0 = n≥1 (Xt − Xt−1 ). When many variables Xt in T are nearly identical,
085   strong correlations between them can obscure the true variation in the process. Grouping similar
086   variables together helps reduce this redundancy by allowing us to approximate these highly corre-
087   lated variables with a representative value, thereby simplifying the analysis and making the process
088
      easier to interpret and work with. A more detailed explanation can be found in Appendix A.
089   For n ≥ 0, we select a subset Tn , and for each t ∈ T , we choose an approximation πn (t) from Tn .
090   Using these πn (t) points, we obtain the corresponding Xπn (t) variables, which serve as successive
091   approximations of Xt . We start by assuming that T0 contains only one element t0 , and thus π0 (t) =
092   t0 for all t ∈ T . The core relation is:
093
                                                  X
094                                 Xt − Xt0 =          Xπn (t) − Xπn−1 (t) .
095                                               n=1
096
097   This equality holds because, for sufficiently large n, πn (t) equals t, meaning that beyond a certain
098   point, the approximation stops, and the series becomes a finite sum. Specifically, as n increases, the
099   sets Tn become progressively finer, eventually covering all points in T . Once Tn contains t, we have
100   πn (t) = t, so no new information is added by further terms in the series. As a result, the infinite
101   series truncates to a finite sum. This ensures convergence in practical settings where the process Xt
102   is fully captured after a finite number of terms.
103   The efficacy of this approach is rooted in the fact that for each approximation πi (t), the variables
104   Xt − Xπi (t) are smaller than Xt − Xt0 , making their supremum easier to handle. This stepwise
105   refinement converts the intractable global bound estimation into manageable local problems, simpli-
106   fying the overall calculation. Exponential decay is employed to tighten the bounds through gradual
107   decomposition and layer-by-layer control, thus avoiding the complexity and error accumulation typ-
      ically associated with global estimation. A more detailed explanation is provided in Appendix A.

      The concept of bounds in Gaussian process regression (GPR) originates from the confidence inter-
      vals intrinsic to Gaussian processes. This concept is later extended by the bandit literature (Srinivas
113
      et al., 2009) to include frequentist uncertainty bounds, which utilize the principle of maximum in-
114
      formation gain. Building on this, Srinivas et al. (2012) introduce the use of the reproducing kernel
115   Hilbert space (RKHS) norm for computing bounds in the bandit setting. Thereafter, Chowdhury
116   & Gopalan (2017) significantly advance this line of research, but their work continued to rely on
117   upper bounds given by maximum information gain. To address computational costs, Bartels et al.
118   (2023) propose probabilistic bounds with minimal computational overhead by leveraging interme-
119   diate computations performed by the Cholesky decomposition.
120
      Another closely related concept is error bounds, which refer to the absolute gap between the pre-
121   dicted and ground-truth values. In this case, the predicted value plus and minus the error bounds can
122   be regarded as upper and lower bounds respectively. Since the regression produced by radial basis
123   function (RBF) interpolation is equivalent to the GP posterior mean with noiseless training data,
124   classical methods use Fourier transform techniques to derive such error bounds for functions in the
125   reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) associated with the interpolation kernel (Wu & Schaback,
126   1993). By further exploiting RKHS properties, uniform error bounds with faster convergence rates
127   are derived by Schaback (1999).
128   The aforementioned methods assume the accurate specification of the GPR model, using empirical
129   or heuristic approaches to determine its appropriate hyperparameters. However, the misspecification
130   of model hyperparameters can have serious consequences. As a result, recent research has focused
131   on improving the robustness of GPR. Lederer et al. (2019) introduce probabilistic Lipschitz con-
132   stants to reduce prior knowledge, estimating errors on a finite grid and extending them to the entire
133   input space. Fiedler et al. (2021) modify their bound function by introducing an error term based on
134   the work of Chowdhury & Gopalan (2017). Capone et al. (2022) address hyperparameter misspec-
135
      ification by proposing a method to calculate error bounds based on a given hyperparameter range.
      More recently, Papadopoulos (2024) uses conformal prediction to calibrate prediction intervals using
136
      a nonconformity measure to evaluate the degree to which a candidate is unusual or nonconforming.
137
138   However, these methods focus solely on robustness to model misspecification and noise, while their
139   underlying approach remains limited to scaling the posterior standard deviation for each instance,
140   without introducing new computational strategies for their bounds. Additionally, existing methods
141
      are limited by their reliance on the assumption that the model is well-specified in terms of parameters
      (e.g., the length scale) and hyperparameters (e.g., noise parameters (Fiedler et al., 2021) and the
142
      hyperparameter space (Capone et al., 2022)). This reliance reduces adaptability and often leads to
143
      an over- or under-estimation of the bounds. Unlike these methods, our chaining technique mitigates
144   GPR’s reliance on the global posterior mean and hyperparameter tuning.
145
146   The method proposed by Capone et al. (2022) primarily addresses errors resulting from model mis-
147
      specification, but it may be less effective when dealing with datasets that have been subjected to
      added noise. While Fiedler et al. (2021) considers such errors, their approach relies heavily on
148
      the selection of the noise-level hyperparameter and is still constrained by the underlying concept
149
      of scaling the posterior error. This means that even if the posterior error estimation is accurate, a
150   significant bias in the posterior mean could prevent an adequate coverage of the prediction intervals.
151   (We provide examples and more detailed explanations of these issues in Appendix B.2.) In contrast,
152   our chaining technique ameliorates these issues.
153
      In highly concentrated datasets, such as those involving temporally or spatially continuous data
154
      (e.g., temperature time-series), adjacent data points tend to exhibit strong correlations. Traditional
155
      methods typically rely on global kernel functions to compute the mean across data points, which
156   makes it difficult to effectively capture such localized correlations. In contrast, chaining methods
157   gather highly correlated data points by defining different layers of approximation, allowing for a
158   layer-by-layer refinement that better controls errors. Additionally, in high-dimensional and complex
159   datasets, where distances between points vary significantly, chaining methods are more adept at
160   capturing local variations, thereby preventing error accumulation and yielding tighter bounds. (We
161   provide examples and more detailed explanations in the Appendix B.3.) In contrast to traditional
      methods, our technique inherits the aforementioned benefits of chaining to mitigate such problems.

      Chain-based methods in machine learning have recently started to gain attention, with Chaining
163   Mutual Information (CMI; (Asadi et al., 2018)) being an example. CMI is a technique that uses
164   mutual information to quantify the shared information between two random variables.This approach
165   has been applied to derive bounds on the expected generalization error of supervised learning al-
166   gorithms, based on the regularity of the loss function (Clerico et al., 2022). Additionally, CMI has
167   been employed in the context of hierarchical coverings of neural networks to establish risk bounds
168   for neural networks (Asadi & Abbe, 2020). While all these methods apply chaining to the principle
169   of mutual information, in the specific domain of GPR, the covariance function is a more appropri-
170   ate tool for measuring dependency because it directly defines the structure of the Gaussian process.
171
      Therefore, in our work, we apply chaining directly to the covariance functions.

175   We now present our primary technical contributions. A key observation is that existing methods for
176   uncertainty bounds in Gaussian processes remain largely focused on posterior-based approaches,
177   while chaining techniques have yet to be fully explored in this context. Chaining systematically
178
      approximates the upper bound through hierarchical refinements, leveraging incremental estimates
      between data points. This approach offers greater flexibility and robustness without relying on prior
179
      assumptions, especially in high-dimensional spaces. Additionally, chaining excels at capturing lo-
180
      cal variations in non-smooth processes by refining estimates at each layer. We shall demonstrate
181   that chaining provides rigorous upper bounds through the use of increment and metric entropy tech-
182   niques, guaranteeing uniform convergence even under noisy conditions and complex metric spaces.

440
441   The performance of our proposed approach is evaluated using standard metrics for prediction inter-
442   vals, as described by (Khosravi et al., 2010).
443
444   • Prediction Interval Coverage Probability (PICP). This metric evaluates the percentage of test
445     observationsPnthat lie within the bounds of the prediction intervals (PIs) . It is calculated as
446     PICP = n1 i=1 ci , where ci = 1 if the output at point i lies within the bounds [L(Xi ), U (Xi )],
447     and ci = 0 otherwise. Here, L(Xi ) and U (Xi ) denote the lower and upper bounds of the ith PI.
448   • Normalized Mean Prediction Interval Width (NMPIW). PIs that are too wide provide little
449     useful information, so the NMPIW metric quantifies the width of the PIs as:
450                                                1
                                                      Pn
451                                                n   i=1 (U (Xi ) − L(Xi ))
                                      NMPIW =                                 ,
452                                                            R
453     where R is the range of the target variable. NMPIW expresses the average PI width as a percentage
454     of the target range.
455   • Coverage Width-Based Criterion (CWC). This is the primary evaluation metric because it bal-
456     ances the conflicting goals of achieving narrow PIs (low NMPIW) and high coverage (high PICP).
457     (Note that a good PICP score can be trivially achieved at the expense of NMPIW (by using overly
458     wide PIs) and vice versa (by using overly narrow PIs). Hence either PICP or NMPIW alone is
459     insufficient to completely reflect the goodness of bounds.) CWC is defined as:

463

      Next we review at a high level the scheme of the chaining bound method.
597
598   The goal is to bound EY where Y = supt (Xt − Xt0 ). We introduce a “ood set” Ωu for a given
599   parameter u ≥ 0, which excludes undesirable events. As u becomes large, P (Ωcu ) becomes small.
600
      When Ωu occurs, we bound Y , say Y ≤ f (u), where f is an increasing function on R+ .
                               Z ∞                         Z ∞

612   In practice, we will always have P (Ωcu ) ≤ L exp(−u/L) and f (u) = A + uα B, yielding the bound:
613                                               EY ≤ A + K(α)B.
614
615   At the heart of this example is the introduction of a “good set” Ωu , which confines undesirable events
616   to a small probability. As the parameter u increases, the probability of bad events Ωcu decreases
617   exponentially. This allows for effective error control within the “good set,” avoiding the coarse
618   global error estimates typically used in traditional methods.
619   Furthermore, by controlling tail probabilities and utilizing exponential decay bounds, such as
620   P (Ωcu ) ≤ L exp(−u/L), along with the function f (u) = A + uα B, the chaining method en-
621   sures that the final error remains well-controlled. This level of probabilistic precision, achieved by
622   breaking the problem into layers and managing each incremental error independently, prevents the
623   overestimation of total error that is common in traditional approaches.

709   to even worse predictions in other cases. However, the posterior mean is still too low, causing a
710   slight underestimation of the true value. Although the model accounts for significant uncertainty, its
711   reliance on the posterior mean skews the prediction bounds.
712
      In contrast, our chain method does not rely on fixed noise parameters. Instead, it progressively
713
      refines the posterior estimate by breaking the process down into layers, with each layer capturing
714
      different local variations in the data. This multi-scale approach reduces the noise’s impact on the
715   posterior mean by spreading the uncertainty across multiple levels. As a result, the chain method
716   produces a prediction interval that is not only more accurate but also successfully encompasses the
717   true value. The broader uncertainty range reflects a more realistic variance estimation, avoiding
718   the overly tight bounds seen in other methods, which tend to shrink the variance too much and
719   underestimate the true uncertainty.
