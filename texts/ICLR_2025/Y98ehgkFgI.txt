009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper introduces Network-based Active Inference (NetAIF), a novel robotic
012            framework that enables real-time learning and adaptability in dynamic, un-
013            structured environments. NetAIF leverages random attractor dynamics and the
014            Free Energy Principle (FEP) to simplify trajectory generation through network-
015            topology-driven attractors that induce controlled instabilities and probabilistic
016            sampling cycles. This approach allows robots to efficiently adapt to changing
017            conditions without requiring extensive pre-training or pre-calculated trajectories.
018
               By integrating learning and control mechanisms within a compact model architec-
               ture, NetAIF facilitates seamless task execution, such as target tracking and valve
019
               manipulation. Extensive simulations and real-world experiments demonstrate Ne-
020
               tAIF’s capability to perform rapid and precise real-time adjustments, highlight-
021            ing its suitability for applications requiring high adaptability and efficient control,
022            such as robotics tasks in the energy and manufacturing sectors.

027   1.1   OVERCOMING AUTOMATION C HALLENGES WITH A DVANCED L EARNING M ETHODS
028
029   The World Energy Employment 2023 report by the IEA highlights a significant shift towards clean
030   energy jobs, which now surpass fossil fuel employment, driven by a 40% rise in clean energy in-
031
      vestment over the past two years. Despite economic and geopolitical challenges, the energy sector
      has seen growth in employment, particularly in solar PV, wind, EVs, and battery manufacturing.
032
      However, a shortage of skilled labor remains a key challenge, underscoring the need for targeted
033
      training and policy support to develop a workforce suited for the energy transition (IEA, 2023).
034
035   In response to these labor challenges, automation is playing an increasingly critical role in advancing
036   the clean energy sector. Robotics, in particular, offers a promising solution to enhance operational
037
      efficiency and safety. However, to maximize the potential of robotics in complex and dynamic en-
      vironments, sophisticated learning methods are required. One such approach, Deep Reinforcement
038
      Learning (DRL), has emerged as a leading candidate for enabling autonomous robotic systems in
039
      tasks like control, manipulation, and decision-making. Yet, despite its potential, DRL faces notable
040   barriers to widespread adoption in the energy sector.
041
042
      1.2   D EEP R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING (DRL)
043
044   DRL combines the decision-making power of reinforcement learning (RL) with the pattern recogni-
045   tion capabilities of deep learning (DL). This allows robots to learn and adapt through trial and error,
046   improving performance over time. DRL is increasingly explored for enabling autonomy in control
047   and manipulation tasks in real-world environments by training agents to recognize complex patterns
048   in data and make informed decisions.
049   However, DRL requires large amounts of data and time for agent training, as well as expert-designed
050   reward functions to guide learning. Creating these reward functions demands substantial knowledge
051   and engineering resources, as they must accurately capture desired outcomes, agent actions, and
052   constraints. Poorly defined reward functions can lead to suboptimal or unsafe behavior (Sutton &
053   Barto, 2020). Thus, while powerful, DRL may not always be the most practical or cost-effective
      approach for every application.

      1.3   ACTIVE I NFERENCE AS A N EXT-G ENERATION L EARNING M ETHOD
055
056   Active Inference (AIF) is an advanced framework from neuroscience that is now being applied
057   in robotics to help agents minimize surprisal—the unexpectedness of observations—without rely-
058   ing on traditional reward-based approaches like deep reinforcement learning (DRL). The goal of
059   the agent is to reduce surprisal, mathematically expressed as − log p(o), where p(o) represents the
060   probability of an observation o.
061   Since directly minimizing surprisal is often impractical, the agent minimizes variational free energy
062   F, which serves as an upper bound on surprisal:
063
064
                                F = Eq(st ) [log q(st ) − log p(ot , st )] ≥ − log p(ot )
065
066
      In this expression, q(st ) is the approximate posterior over states st , and p(ot , st ) is the joint likeli-
067   hood of observing ot given the state st . By minimizing F, the agent balances accuracy (matching
068   observations) and complexity (keeping the model simple), continuously refining its predictions and
069   actions to reduce prediction error.
070
      While AIF holds significant promise for creating adaptive robotic systems, its real-world deployment
071
      faces challenges due to the complexity of model design and high computational demands (Lanillos
072
      et al., 2021). Nonetheless, its potential to enhance flexibility, durability, and adaptability makes it a
073   powerful alternative to traditional DRL techniques
074
075
      1.4   N ETWORK - BASED ACTIVE I NFERENCE (N ETAIF)
076
077   To overcome the limitations of both DRL and traditional AIF approaches, we propose Network-
078   based Active Inference (NetAIF), a novel framework that leverages network dynamics to simplify
079   trajectory calculations and enhance efficiency. Rooted in key AIF principles such as entropy and
080   surprise minimization, NetAIF builds on the Free Energy Principle (FEP), which posits that systems
081   self-organize by minimizing surprisal or prediction error. By harnessing the inherent dynamics of a
082   network, NetAIF computes trajectories more efficiently than traditional AIF methods, reducing the
083   need for complex mathematical models while enabling agents to adapt to dynamic environments in
084
      real-time. This streamlined approach makes NetAIF highly suitable for real-world robotic applica-
      tions, offering significant improvements in both speed and computational cost.

202   2.2    T HE R ANDOM ATTRACTOR
203
204   To represent the NESS behavior in NetAIF, Random Dynamical Systems (RDS) are employed,
205   providing a framework to understand complex systems driven by stochastic processes. In particular,
206
      random pullback attractors (Caraballo & Han, 2016), also known as stochastic basins of attraction,
      describe how NetAIF’s state evolves over time in response to environmental uncertainty. Expressed
207
      as φ(t, ω, x), where t is time, ω represents randomness, and x is the state variable, these attractors
208
      characterize regions in the state space where the system tends to settle. The random attractor A(ω)
209   pulls trajectories towards it, ensuring that NetAIF remains adaptive and stable within its NESS
210   framework, despite external randomness.
211

243   This convergence process can be understood as a stochastic diffusion in parameter space, driven by
244   increasing the amplitude of random fluctuations on parameters (e.g., connection weights) in regions
245
      of high free energy. As the system approaches free energy minima, these random fluctuations are
      attenuated, resulting in a more stable and precise arm trajectory. Such system dynamics can be
246
      described by a stochastic differential equation (SDE) in the form of a Langevin equation (Karl,
247
      2019):
248                                                          √
                                        dx = −∇F (x) dt + 2Γ dW
249
250   where x represents the system’s parameters, F (x) is the free energy landscape, Γ is the diffusion
251   coefficient, and W is a Wiener process. This equation captures the interplay between the determin-
252   istic drift towards free energy minima and the stochastic exploration of the parameter space, which
253   ultimately shapes the arm’s trajectory.
254   It is worth noting that the optimization process in NetAIF is inherently local because free energy is
255   an extensive quantity, meaning that the system’s total free energy is the sum of the free energies of
256   its individual components. The variational free energy, which approximates the true free energy, is
257   calculated using local prediction errors. Some predictions are clamped with high precision, fixed, or
258   strongly influenced by the desired outcomes, defining the attracting set, which represents the desired
259   sensor inputs or the target state of the system. Minimizing variational free energy by reducing local
260
      prediction errors guides the network model towards the attracting set.
261   This local optimization process enables the system to efficiently navigate the free energy landscape
262   without requiring global computations or information propagation across the entire network. By
263   iteratively updating its local components based on prediction errors and external control laws, the
264   system converges towards the desired states.
265   The roots of this learning scheme can be traced back to early formulations of self-organization in
266   cybernetics (Ashby, 1947) (Ashby, 1956) and are connected to stochastic thermodynamics (Ao,
267   2008) (Seifert, 2012). These connections highlight the consistency of the design principle with the
268   fundamental concepts underlying the FEP. This principle drives the network model to minimize pre-
269   diction errors, guiding the entire network towards a stable regime, resulting in smooth and efficient
      arm movements.

      5   CONCLUSIONS
531
532
      The Network-based Active Inference (NetAIF) model presents an efficient approach to real-time
533
      adaptive intelligence in robotics, utilizing random attractor dynamics and the Free Energy Prin-
534   ciple (FEP) to enable rapid adaptation to unpredictable environments without requiring extensive
535   pre-training or high computational resources. Its real-time feedback ensures precise control and
536   flexible adaptation, making it ideal for industries like energy, where cost-efficiency and adaptabil-
537   ity are crucial. Unlike Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL), which demands significant training
538   and computational power, NetAIF offers a more streamlined, cost-effective solution for tasks such
539   as inspections and maintenance. For a comparison with DRL methods, see the companion paper
      (Anonymous, 2024).
