009                                                      A BSTRACT
010
011            While deep neural networks are highly effective at solving complex tasks, their
012            computational demands can hinder their usefulness in real-time applications and
013            with limited-resources systems. Besides, it is a known fact that, for many down-
014            stream tasks, off-the-shelf models are over-parametrized. While classical struc-
015            tured pruning can reduce the network’s width, the computation’s critical path,
016            namely the maximum number of layers encountered at forward propagation, ap-
017            parently can not be reduced.
018
               In this paper, we aim to reduce the depth of over-parametrized deep neural net-
               works: we propose an eNtropy-basEd Pruning as a nEural Network depTH’s
019
               rEducer (NEPENTHE) to alleviate deep neural networks’ computational burden.
020
               Based on our theoretical finding, NEPENTHE leverages “unstructured” pruning
021            to bias sparsity enhancement in layers with low entropy to remove them entirely.
022            We validate our approach on popular architectures such as MobileNet, Swin-T and
023            RoBERTa, showing that, when in the overparametrization regime, some layers are
024            linearizable (hence reducing the model’s depth) with little to no performance loss.
025            The code will be publicly available upon acceptance of the article.

066   to guarantee a-priori no performance loss (given that they impose a target shallow model) or avoid
067   substantial perturbations. This motivates the exploration of designing an iterative pruning strategy,
068   aimed at reducing the model’s depth while preserving optimal performance.
069   In this work, we present NEPENTHE, an approach that iteratively attempts to remove layers from a
070   DNN. More specifically, given the large use of rectifier activation functions such as ReLU, GELU,

092   2   R ELATED W ORKS
093
094   Neural Network Pruning. Neural network pruning has gained considerable attention in recent
095   years due to its potential to enhance model performance and reduce over-fitting. Its goal is to re-
096   duce a cumbersome network to a smaller one while maintaining accuracy by removing irrelevant
097   weights, filters, or other structures, from neural networks. While structured pruning removes entire
098   neurons, filters, or channels Tartaglione et al. (2021); He and Xiao (2023); Lin et al. (2020), un-
099
      structured pruning algorithms remove weights without explicitly considering the neural network’s
      structure Han et al. (2015). Magnitude-based pruning, where the importance score to prune pa-
100
      rameters is based on their magnitude Han et al. (2015); Louizos et al. (2018); Zhu and Gupta
101
      (2017), and gradient-based pruning, where the ranking or the penalty term is a function of the gra-
102   dient magnitude (or to higher order derivatives) Lee et al. (2019); Tartaglione et al. (2022), are the
103   main types of unstructured pruning approaches. Blalock et al. (2020) compared the effectiveness
104   of these approaches and concluded that, in general, gradient-based methods are less accurate than
105   magnitude-based methods. Moreover, Gale et al. (2019) showed that simple magnitude pruning
106   approaches achieve comparable or better results than complex methods, making them a good trade-
107   off between complexity and competitiveness. Computationally-wise, it is broadly known that, in
      general-purpose hardware setup, structured pruning can provide larger benefits, in terms of both


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      memory and computation, than unstructured approaches, despite the achieved sparsity rate can be
109   substantially lower Bragagnolo et al. (2021).
110
111   Entropy-Guided Pruning. Some works have already tried to propose entropy-based approaches
112
      to guide pruning. For convolutional neural networks, Luo and Wu (2017) put forward an iterative
      filter pruning strategy in which the importance of each filter is calculated by their entropy-based
113
      channel selection metric. To recover performance, the pruned model is then fine-tuned. Also for
114
      CNNs, Hur and Kang (2019) suggested an entropy-based method that determines dynamically dur-
115   ing training the threshold by considering the average amount of information from the weights to
116   output. Moreover, Min et al. (2018) proposed a two-stage filter pruning framework, first intra-layer
117   and then extra-layer. Given that the entropy is a measure of disorder, evidently, it identifies filters
118   that mutually have low entropy: these can be considered redundant and for instance, can be removed
119   from the model. These approaches, despite reducing the layer’s width, are not designed to tackle
120   our aim: removing entire layers to reduce DNNs’ depth.
121   Neural Network Depth Reduction. Towards neural network depth reduction, Chen and Zhao
122   (2019) inspect the possibility of having a layer-wise pruning method based on feature represen-
123   tation, a-posteriori employing a retraining strategy that utilizes knowledge distillation. This work
124   reinforces the possibility of designing a layer-pruning algorithm. Endorsing this, Dror et al. (2022)
125   proposed a method that learns whether non-linear activations can be removed, allowing the folding
126   of consecutive linear layers into one. More specifically, ReLU-activated layers are replaced with
127   PReLU activations, showcasing a regularized slope. Post-training, the PReLUs almost linear are
128   removed, and the layer can be folded with its subsequent one. Ali Mehmeti-Göpel and Disselhoff
129
      (2023) proposes a similar channel-wise approach that enables reducing more non-linear units in the
      network while maintaining similar performance. While these works sought to shrink the neural net-
130
      work’s depth by working at the activation level and forcing it to stay either linear or non-linear, our
131
      approach does not directly enforce any of that. In rectifier-activated networks, we perform a targeted
132   unstructured pruning that off-line favors either the neuron’s shutdown or the use of its linear part.
133   By prioritizing pruning connections in low-entropy layers, Liao et al. (2023) also develop an un-
134   structured entropy-guided pruning method which reduces DNNs’ depth. Nevertheless, EGP only
135   enables the removal of a limited number of layers, notwithstanding its effectiveness. Indeed, the
136   accuracy drastically decreases when several layers are removed. This will be confirmed in Sec. 4 by
137   contrasting this approach with NEPENTHE.

505   4.5     L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
506
      NEPENTHE is a successful approach to alleviate deep neural networks’ computational burden by
507
      decreasing their depth. Nevertheless, this method also presents some limits.
508
509   Due to its iterative nature, NEPENTHE leads to longer training time: the more iterations, the higher
510   the training time. However, compared to Iterative Magnitude Pruning (IMP) we observe that our
511   entropy-based term introduces a negligible overhead (in Table 13, Appendix. D.3, a little bit more
512
      than 1 minute per iteration). Including the entropy in the minimized objective function could be a
      way to design a one-shot approach, which would be more efficient at training time. Nevertheless,
513
      this approach is not directly suitable as it relies on a non-differentiable expression and is therefore
514
      left as future work.
515
516   We acknowledge that our approach should extend to larger models such as large language models
517   to provide insights into its scalability and effectiveness in more complex scenarios. Due to time
518
      and resource limitations, we focused our experiments on the tested models. To break the limitation
      coming from the training cost, we consider replacing full retraining in each iteration with shorter
519
      fine-tuning. We performed tests on different models on CIFAR-10 dataset, in which we employed a
520
      short fine-tuning process that focused only on the final stage of training. We refer to this approach
521   as NEPENTHE-finetuning. As shown in Table 7, even though the ability of NEPENTHE-finetuning
522   to remove layers and preserve performance is not as remarkable as NEPENTHE. NEPENTHE-
523   finetuning is still functional. This result shows that our method has the potential to be extended
524   to larger language models, and our approach is scalable and effective in more complex situations.
525   Further exploration and refinement of this approach are left for future work.
526
527
      5     C ONCLUSION
528
529
      In this work, we have presented NEPENTHE, an iterative unstructured approach towards layer re-
530   moval in rectifier-activated deep neural networks. Leveraging on some theoretical results showing
531   that unstructured pruning has the potential to reduce the neural network’s depth, an entropy-based
532   weighting mechanism has been designed to select parameters to prune from the network toward
533   depth reduction and attempt to preserve high performance in the considered tasks. Experiments
534   were conducted on popular architectures, including the Transformer-based Swin-T and architec-
535   tures for NLP like BERT and RoBERTa, showcasing the potential of NEPENTHE to reduce the
536   number of layers in the model concretely. This work has a practical impact even in computation
537   on parallel architectures such as GPUs or TPUs, as it inherently reduces the critical path forward
538
      propagation undergoes.
