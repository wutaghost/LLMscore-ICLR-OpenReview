047                                             A BSTRACT
048           Video personalization methods allow us to synthesize videos with specific con-
049           cepts such as people, pets, and places. However, existing methods often focus
050           on limited domains, require time-consuming optimization per subject, or support
051           only a single subject. We present VideoAlchemy—a video model equipped with
052           built-in multi-subject, open-set personalization capabilities for both foreground
053           objects and backgrounds, eliminating the need for time-consuming test-time opti-
              mization. Our model is built on a new Diffusion Transformer module that fuses


                                                      1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
               each reference image conditioning and its corresponding subject-level text prompt
055            with cross-attention layers. Developing such a large model presents two main
056            challenges: dataset and evaluation. First, as paired datasets of reference images
057            and videos are extremely hard to collect, we opt to sample video frames as ref-
058            erence images and synthesize entire videos. This approach, however, introduces
059            data biases issue, where models can easily denoise training videos but fail to gen-
060            eralize to new contexts during inference. To mitigate these issues, we carefully
061            design a new automatic data construction pipeline with extensive image augmen-
062            tation and sampling techniques. Second, evaluating open-set video personaliza-
063
               tion is a challenge in itself. To address this, we introduce a new personalization
               benchmark with evaluation protocols focusing on accurate subject fidelity assess-
064
               ment and accommodating different types of personalization conditioning. Finally,
065
               our extensive experiments show that our method significantly outperforms exist-
066            ing personalization methods, regarding quantitative and qualitative evaluations.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
070
071
      Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Song & Ermon, 2019) have enabled
072
      us to synthesize realistic videos with natural motions given a simple text prompt (Singer et al., 2023;
073   Blattmann et al., 2023b; Brooks et al., 2024; Ho et al., 2022; Menapace et al., 2024). This level
074   of quality and realism paves the way for personalization—the ability to generate videos containing
075   specific objects and people rendered in the unseen context or background. Multiple video personal-
076   ization methods have been proposed to generate content with specific people or pets, but they remain
077   limited in the level of control they provide. Some focus on human faces (He et al., 2024; Ma et al.,
078   2024), some support only a single subject (Jiang et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024; Wu
079   et al., 2024), with others supporting only foreground control (Wang et al., 2024c). Moreover, some
080   of these works require costly and lengthy test-time optimization (Wei et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024).
081   In this paper, we present VideoAlchemy, a video generation model with extensive personalization
082   capabilities. In contrast to existing methods, VideoAlchemy supports multiple subjects and open-
083   set entities, including both foreground objects and background. Importantly, our optimization-free
084   method does not require fine-tuning to incorporate new concepts. In Figure 1, we show videos
085   personalized for two subjects across three different backgrounds. Our video model is built on new
086   Diffusion Transformer modules tailored for personalization. Each module uses two cross-attention
087
      layers: one to integrate the text prompt describing the entire image and another to incorporate the
      embeddings of each reference image. We employ object-level fusion, blending the text description
088
      of each object with its corresponding image embeddings to achieve multiple subject conditioning.
089
090   But how can we collect data to train our model? Ideally, it requires a dataset of images and videos
091   with many subjects, each captured under varying lighting, background, and pose. Unfortunately,
092   collecting such a dataset for open-set entities is challenging at best and impossible at worst. Al-
093
      ternatively, we can extract the reference images and target video clips from the same video. This
      approach, however, comes with a significant drawback—factors unrelated to identity still have a very
094
      high correlation across different video frames. While this correlation helps the model denoise train-
095
      ing videos accurately, the model often struggles to synthesize diverse videos with unseen lighting,
096   background, and poses. To address these biases, we carefully design a data construction pipeline to
097   automatically extract object segments from target videos. Additionally, we craft a personalization-
098   specific data augmentation and conditional subject sampling strategy during training to ensure the
099   model focuses on the object identity of the reference images.
100
      Another challenge we are facing is the lack of a suitable benchmark for evaluating multi-subject
101
      video personalization. Commonly, we evaluate video personalization results by computing a simi-
102   larity score between the generated video and reference images (Ruiz et al., 2023a; Ye et al., 2023;
103   Jiang et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024). Unfortunately, this metric does not apply to multiple entities, as
104   it cannot focus on each subject. To address these limitations, we introduce MSRVTT-Personalization,
105   a comprehensive and robust evaluation protocol for personalization tasks. MSRVTT-Personalization
106   facilitates evaluation across various conditioning modes, including face-crop conditioning, condi-
107   tioning on single or multiple arbitrary subjects, and conditioning on foreground and background.
      Different from image-level similarity, we use object segmentation algorithm to localize each con-

      Diffusions Model for Video Generation. Diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Song &
121
      Ermon, 2019; Ho et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022; Ho et al., 2022) have demonstrated impres-
122   sive capabilities in generating realistic content in recent years. Building on the power of diffusion
123   models, several subsequent studies have explored their applications in text-conditioned video syn-
124   thesis (Saharia et al., 2022; Singer et al., 2023; Blattmann et al., 2023b; Zhou et al., 2022; Luo et al.,
125   2023; Guo et al., 2024; Menapace et al., 2024; Brooks et al., 2024). ImagenVideo (Saharia et al.,
126   2022) and Make-A-Video (Singer et al., 2023) propose a cascade of temporal and spatial upsam-
127   plers for video generation. VideoLDM (Blattmann et al., 2023b) adopts a latent diffusion paradigm
128   where a pretrained latent image generator and latent decoder are finetuned to generate temporally
129   coherent videos. Differently from previous models based on the U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015)
130
      architecture, SnapVideo (Menapace et al., 2024) adapts the FiT (Chen & Li, 2023) and scales up to
      billion-parameters size. More recently, SORA (Brooks et al., 2024) employs the Diffusion Trans-
131
      former (DiT) (Peebles & Xie, 2023) and shows a tremendous leap in high-resolution, long video
132
      synthesis. While these studies demonstrate significant advancements in video synthesis, the use of
133   text prompts alone confines the generated content to what can be described textually.
134
135   Personalized Image Generation. This task aims at adapting and customizing generative models
136
      to a set of desired subjects using a few input images (Ruiz et al., 2023a; Gal et al., 2023a; Kumari
      et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2024; Tewel et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b; Ostashev et al.,
137
      2024). For example, DreamBooth (Ruiz et al., 2023a) optimizes the weights of the entire text-to-
138
      image model for a reference subject. Textual Inversion (Gal et al., 2023a) learns a text embedding
139   of the reference subject and uses the embedding to generate novel images. Custom Diffusion (Ku-
140   mari et al., 2023) learn to compose multiple concepts, each represented by the text embedding and
141   cross-attention weights. However, these optimization-based models require finetuning pre-trained
142   weights or optimizing a text embedding for every new concept, which is inevitably slow and prone
143   to overfitting. Recently, more studies have explored encoder-based methods to reduce test-time fine-
144   tuning (Shi et al., 2024; Ye et al., 2023; Arar et al., 2023; Gal et al., 2023b; Wei et al., 2023b; Li et al.,
145   2023; Valevski et al., 2023; Ruiz et al., 2023b). IP-adapter (Ye et al., 2023) learns a lightweight de-
146   coupled cross-attention mechanism for image conditioning. InstanceBooth (Shi et al., 2024) trains
147
      an image encoder to convert reference images into textual tokens and introduces adapter layers to
      retain identity details. Our model also trains an image encoder for faster personalization, but we
148
      focus on video personalization with multiple subjects.
149
150   Personalized Video Generation. Inspired by the success in image personalization, several works
151   have explored these techniques for videos (Zhang et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2024;
152   Wang et al., 2024c; Long et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024; He et al., 2024; Fang
153
      et al., 2024). Among them, DreamVideo (Wei et al., 2024) employs an optimization-based strategy,
      training an image adapter to capture the subject’s appearance and a motion adapter to model dynam-
154
      ics. StoryDiffusion (Zhou et al., 2024) instead adopts an optimization-free approach by introducing a
155
      consistent self-attention mechanism and employing a semantic motion predictor to synthesize videos
156   with smooth transitions and consistent subjects. Nonetheless, most existing video personalization
157   methods focus on limited domains. Some models are limited to face personalization (He et al.,
158   2024; Ma et al., 2024) or single subjects from specific categories (Zhang et al., 2024; Jiang et al.,
159   2024; Wei et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024), while the other focuses solely on fore-
160   ground objects (Wang et al., 2024c). In contrast, our work introduces a video model with extensive
161   personalization capabilities, supporting the customization of multiple open-set entities across both
      foreground and background. Closely related to our work, VideoDrafter (Long et al., 2024) achieves

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
188
189   Given a text prompt and a set of images conceptualizing each entity word in the prompt, our goal is
190
      to learn a video generative model conditional on both text and image inputs. We first elaborate on
      the collection of the training dataset in Section 3.1, and provide the details of the model architecture
191
      in Section 3.2. Lastly, we discuss the issue of training data biases and our solution in Section 3.3.
192
193
194   3.1     DATASET C OLLECTION
195
      As shown in Figure 2, we curate the training dataset upon video and caption pairs with three steps.
196
      In the first step, we use a large language model (Jiang et al., 2023) to retrieve entity words from the
197
      given caption. Specifically, we define three types of entity words: subjects (e.g., human or animal),
198   objects (e.g., car, jacket), and backgrounds (e.g., room, beach). Each subject or object entity word is
199   expected to appear in the video. Next, we use the retrieved entity words to filter the training dataset
200   with the following criteria: (1) we remove videos containing any subject entity word in plural form
201   (e.g., a group of people, multiple dogs), as they introduce ambiguity in model personalization; (2)
202   we also remove videos without any subject entity words, as their dynamics are often dominated by
203   camera movements rather than significant foreground motion. Appendix A.2 details this process.
204   In the second step, we construct reference images that feature subjects and objects. We first select
205   three frames from the video’s beginning, middle, and end (at the 5%, 50%, and 95% percentiles),
206   which might capture the target subject or object with varying poses and different lighting conditions.
207   Next, we apply GroundingDINO (Liu et al., 2023a) on each frame to detect the bounding boxes.
208   These bounding boxes are then used by SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023) to segment the mask regions
209   corresponding to each entity. Additionally, for the reference images that depict humans, we apply
210   face detection (Wang et al., 2024a) to extract face crops.
211   Lastly, we create a clean background image by removing the subjects and objects. Since SAM (Kir-
212   illov et al., 2023) occasionally produces imprecise boundaries, we dilate the foreground mask. Next,
213   we use an inpainting algorithm (Rombach et al., 2022) to obtain a clean background image. We use
214   the background entity word as the positive prompt and “Any human or any object, complex pattern
215   and texture” as the negative prompt. To ensure consistency of the background, we only use the
      middle frame to obtain a single background image for each video sequence.

240   3.2     V IDEO P ERSONALIZATION M ODEL
241
242   In Section 3.1, we annotate each video and prompt pair with a sequence of reference images and
243   their corresponding entity words. Next, we train VideoAlchemy by learning to denoise the training
244   video using the conditions of text prompt, reference images, and conditional entity words. Figure 3
245   illustrates the model architecture of VideoAlchemy, a deep cascade of Diffusion Transformer (DiT)
246   blocks (Peebles & Xie, 2023). Different from vanilla DiT designs, our module supports personal-
247
      ization by fusing the information from both text and image conditioning. Our DiT block includes
      three main operations: one multi-head self-attention (Vaswani, 2017) and two following multi-head
248
      cross-attention respectively for text and reference image conditioning.
249
250
251   Binding of Image and Word Concept. In the task of multi-subject, open-set personalization, the
252   video model can be conditioned on different subjects, each of which can be represented by one or
253   a few reference images. Therefore, it is critical to provide the model binding information between
254
      text tokens and image tokens. We provide these binding in the form of personalization embeddings
      f = Concat(f1 , ..., fN ), where fn encompasses the information from both the reference image and
255
      the representative entity word and N is the number of conditional reference images. Specifically,
256
      to produce the embeddings fn , we first encode the image as the image tokens xn ∈ Rl×c by a
257   shared and frozen image encoder. Next, we retrieve the word tokens wn from the text embeddings
258   (encoded from the text prompt), and flatten wn to a 1-D embedding. Considering the number of
259   tokens of an entity word varies, we zero-pad or crop the word embeddings to a consistent length.
260   To bind the information of both the image and word tokens, we repeat the flattened word tokens for
261   l times and concatenate them with the image tokens along the channel axis. Lastly, after a linear
262   projection module, we apply a residual connection with the image tokens xn and add a learnable
263   index embedding to produce the embeddings fn .
264
265   Personalization Conditioning. The personalization embeddings f are later used to compute cross
266   attention with video latent tokens. Note that IP-adapter (Ye et al., 2023) encodes conditional text
267   and image into an unified embeddings space through CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and employs a
268   single decoupled cross-attention to compute both conditioning at the same time. In contrast, our
269   model encodes the text and image using separate models. Empirically, we find that using distinct
      cross-attention for each modality can handle the tokens from different distributions more effectively.

      3.3    U NDESIRABLE T RAINING DATA B IASES
287
288   In Section 3.2, we learn VideoAlchemy by denoising the entire video from the selected and masked
289   video frames. Empirically, we observe that this training strategy leads the model to learn unintended
290   biases presented in the reference image (ref ). We list some noticeable biases as follows:
291
            • If ref is high-resolution, the model generates a large object close to the camera.
292         • If ref has been photoshopped, the model replicates the subject without introducing motion.
293         • If ref is occluded, the model generates random objects occluding the subject.
294         • If ref is cropped, the model places the subject at edge to make it cropped by the boundary.
295         • The model tends to generate the subject with the same pose and lighting conditions as ref.
296         • If multiple ref s represent the same subject with similar poses, the model generates a subject
297           with small motion.
298   During training, our model learns to exploit these biases since they are beneficial at denoising train-
299   ing video. Nonetheless, they are not applicable during inference. Such domain gap between training
300   and inference usually results to unnatural composition of the objects or undesirable video dynam-
301   ics. To alleviate these unfavorable biases, we apply a sampling rule to randomly select reference
302   images for conditioning and adopts data augmentations on the reference images. Specifically, we
303   add downscaling and Gaussian blurring to fix the bias on image resolution, apply color jittering and
304   brightness adjustment to mitigate the bias on lighting condition, and adopt random horizontal flip
305   and image shearing and rotation to alleviate the bias on the pose of reference subject.
306   The core concept is to guide the model focusing on the identity of the reference images instead
307   of learning the unintended information leakage from the properties or composition of the input
308   reference images. We detail the training augmentations and the sampling of the conditioning subjects
309   and images in Appendix A.3.

      4.1    MSRVTT-P ERSONALIZATION B ENCHMARK
319
320   Existing personalization frameworks (Ruiz et al., 2023a; Ye et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2024; Zhou et al.,
321   2024) assess the preservation of the subject appearance by measuring the similarity (Deng et al.,
322   2019; Radford et al., 2021; Oquab et al., 2024) between the reference image and the entire output
323   image or video frames. However, these metrics are limited to single-subject conditioning and fail to
      focus specifically on the target subject. To solve this issue, we present MSRVTT-Personalization, a


                                                          6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      framework designed to provide a more comprehensive and accurate evaluation for the tasks of per-
325   sonalization. It supports various conditioning scenarios: face-crop conditioning, single or multiple
326   arbitrary subject conditioning, and both foreground and background conditioning.
327
328
      We construct the testing dataset upon MSR-VTT (Xu et al., 2016) and process the dataset with three
      steps. First, we use TransNetV2 (Souček & Lokoč, 2020) to split a long video into multiple clips
329
      based on shot boundary detection and apply the in-house captioning algorithm to generate more a
330
      detailed caption for each clip. In the second step, we follow Section 3.1 to produce the annotations
331   for each video-caption pair. Lastly, to ensure the quality of the benchmark, we manually select the
332   samples that meet the following four criteria:
333
            • The video sample is not an animation of an image without any object motion.
334
            • The video sample does not include extensive texts.
335
            • The retrieved subjects and objects cover all of the main subjects and objects in the video.
336         • Inpainting of the background image does not introduce any additional random objects.
337
      To increase the data diversity, we select only one clip from each long video and collect 2, 130 clips in
338
      total, forming the testing samples of the benchmark. Figure 4 shows a test sample with its annotation.
339
      To perform an extensive evaluation, we compute four metrics:
340
341         • Text-Sim: the average cos-sim between the text prompt and the synthetic video frames.
342
            • Video-Sim: the pairwise cos-sim between the target and the synthetic video frames.
            • Subject-Sim: the pairwise cos-sim between the input reference images and the synthetic sub-
343
              ject image segmented from the video frames.
344
            • Face-Sim: the pairwise cos-sim between the input face crops and the synthetic face images
345           cropped from the video frames.
346           (cos-sim stands for cosine similarity)
347
348
      With more details, we follow the default setting in Torchmetrics (2024) and use CLIP ViT-L/14 (Rad-
      ford et al., 2021) embeddings for the Text-Sim and Video-Sim. For the Subject-Sim, we follow Ruiz
349
      et al. (2023a) and Wei et al. (2024) and use DINO ViT-B/16 (Caron et al., 2021) embeddings for the
350
      evaluation. For the Face-Sim, we use ArcFace R100 (Deng et al., 2019) embeddings to better extract
351   identity features than general image encoders. To detect the target subjects from the synthetic video
352   frames, we utilize Grounding-DINO Swin-T (Liu et al., 2023a) with the confidence score threshold
353   of 0.4. To detect the synthetic face crops, we employ YOLOv9-C (Wang et al., 2024a) with the
354   confidence score threshold of 0.2 and the IoU score threshold of 0.4. For the video frames with
355   missing subjects or face crops, we assign a similarity score of 0. The testing dataset and the eval-
356   uation protocol will be made publicly available and can serve as a comprehensive personalization
357   benchmark in the future.

      4.2    Q UANTITATIVE E VALUATION
362
363   In this section, we quantitatively evaluate VideoAlchemy and compare it with the state-of-the-art
364   personalization frameworks on MSRVTT-Personalization.
365
      Experimental Setup. Given that various personalization frameworks utilize different types of con-
366   ditional images as inputs, we develop two modes: the subject mode and the face mode, which
367   respectively use the entire subject images or only the face crops as inputs. For the subject mode,
368   we collect 1, 736 testing videos that have exactly one subject in the video and compare them with
369   ELITE (Wei et al., 2023a) and VideoBooth (Jiang et al., 2024). For the face mode, we collect
370   1, 285 testing videos that have exactly one subject containing face crops and compare them with
371   IP-Adapter-FaceID+ (Ye et al., 2023) and PhotoMaker (Li et al., 2024).
372   For image personalization models, including ELITE (Wei et al., 2023a), IP-Adapter-FaceID+ (Ye
373   et al., 2023), and PhotoMaker (Li et al., 2024), we use StableVideoDiffusion (Blattmann et al.,
374   2023a) Img2Vid-XT-1-1 to animate the output images as videos. Since most frameworks only sup-
375   port single-image input, we randomly sample one subject image or face crop for conditioning. For
376   PhotoMaker (Li et al., 2024), as an exception, we also provide the results using all available face
377   crops for conditioning. Additionally, we report the results of our model with the inclusion of back-
      ground conditioning.

510   First, using DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2024) to encode the reference images yields significantly higher
511   fidelity, which is consistently demonstrated in Table 1 to 3 and Figures 5 and 6 (see the “car’s grill

513   to minimize the self-supervised training objective (Chen et al., 2020) and capture unique features in
514   an image. Therefore, DINOv2 embeddings retain rich visual details, which helps maintain subject
515   details. Second, the model using CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) achieves better text-video alignment,
516   as shown in both the quantitative and qualitative evaluations (see the “BBC logo and the license
517   plate” in Figure 5). We assume that CLIP learns to bridge visual and textual modalities, guiding its
518
      embeddings to focus on details typically described in the text prompt. This helps the model generate
      text-aligned videos. Finally, based on the results in Table 3, the model using CLIP embeddings
519
      provides better video quality when conditioned on an entire subject image. In contrast, the model
520
      adopting DINOv2 embeddings results in higher video quality when conditioned on a face crop.
521   We speculate that CLIP embeddings may convey more high-level semantic information which can
522   simplify the video synthesis when conditioned on an relatively complex subject image. On the other
523   hand, for a face crop image that contains fewer semantics features, richer detail presented in DINOv2
524   embeddings can enhance the generation of photorealistic faces.

530   In this paper, we present a new video personalization model, VideoAlchemy, which demonstrates a
531
      significant advancement in video personalization by addressing the limitations of existing methods.
      Our method supports multi-subject, open-set personalization capabilities for both foreground and
532
      background without the need for time-consuming test-time optimization. Through our approach
533
      to dataset construction and augmentation engineering, we have largely mitigated challenges related
534   to data biases, enabling our model to better generalize to real-world settings. Furthermore, we in-
535   troduce a comprehensive personalization benchmark, which supports the measurement of subject
536   fidelity under various conditioning and scenarios. We hope that this benchmark could facilitate
537   robust evaluation for varying personalization approaches and settings. Finally, we experimentally
538   validate that VideoAlchemy outperforms existing methods in both quantitative and qualitative mea-
539   sures. We believe our findings pave the way for future research in video synthesis and open up new
      possible applications in entertainment, advertisement, and education.

      A.1     T RAINING DATASETS AND U NDESIRABLE S AMPLES F ILTERING
813
814   Our personalization training dataset is built on Panda-70M (Chen et al., 2024) and other in-house
815   video-caption datasets. However, we observe that the quality of the video samples is noisy and the
816   training dataset contains several data distributions that are not ideal for generation. We classify the
817   undesirable training samples into four categories:
818         • Still foreground image: sample which is an animation of a static image.
819         • Slight motion: sample with tiny camera movement and static foreground object.
820         • Screen-in-screen: sample with an image or video overlaying on a background image or video.
821         • Computer screen recording: sample which computer screen recording (excluding PC game).
822
      We find that training on this data can make our personalization model generate trivial videos by sim-
823   ply replicating the input reference images and pasting them onto a static background without intro-
824   ducing any motion, especially when there is varying illumination across the reference images. To ad-
825   dress this, we train a video classification model to filter out these undesirable samples. Specifically,
826   we randomly sample 40K videos from the training dataset and manually annotate them with class
827   labels to indicate whether the sample is desirable, and if not, which category of undesirability it falls
828   into. Using the labels, we finetune VideoMAE (Tong et al., 2022) for video classification. Moreover,
829   as we target generating videos that are free of shot boundaries, we apply TransNetV2 (Souček &
830   Lokoč, 2020) to detect videos containing shot boundaries. We only retain the desirable and shot-free
831
      video samples for training.

833   A.2     R ETRIEVAL OF E NTITY W ORDS FROM THE P ROMPT
834
835   In Section 3.1, we utilize a large-language model (Jiang et al., 2023) (LLM) to retrieve entity words
836
      from the prompt. In more detail, we use the prompt template in Figure 7 as an instruction.
837
838    Given an image caption, please retrieve the word tags that indicate background, subject, and visually separable objects.
839    [Definition of background] the background spaces that appear in most of the image area.
       [Definition of subject] human or animal subjects that appear in the image
840
       [Definition of object] the entities that appear in part of the image and can be visually separated with each other.
841
       All of the word tags need to strictly follow two rules below:
842    1) word tag is a noun without any quantifier.
843    2) word tag is an exact subset of the caption. Do not modify any characters, word and symbols.
844    Here are some examples, follow this format to output the results:
845    ### Caption: A woman in a mask and coat, with long brown hair, shows a small green-capped bottle to the camera.
846    ### Output: {'background': [''], 'subject': ['woman'], 'object': ['mask', 'coat', 'long brown hair', 'green-capped bottle’]}
847    (More examples)
848
849                              Figure 7: Prompt template for entity word retrieval.
850
851   Given the video caption, the LLM agent is expected to return a string in the dictionary format, where
852   the values are the list of entity words retrieved from the text prompt. We apply the following steps
853   to process the output:
854         • Remove the sample if the output string is not in the valid dictionary format.
855         • Remove the sample if any entity word is not a sub-string of the text prompt.
856         • Reclassify the entity words according to the pre-defined rules. For example, “cloud” is not a
857           visually separable object that should be classified into a background entity word.
858         • Remove the sample with no subject entity word, as we observe that the motion of these sam-
859           ples is typically trivial camera movements and lacks meaningful foreground motion.
860         • Remove the sample with the subject entity word in the plural form, as this will introduce
861
              ambiguity when applying the localization algorithm.
862   To this end, we curate a training dataset comprising 37.8M videos. To illustrate the diversity of
863   conditioning subjects within the dataset, we plot a word cloud of entity words from 10K randomly
      sampled training videos in Figure 8.

897   A.3     T RAINING AUGMENTATIONS AND C ONDITIONAL I MAGES S AMPLING
898
899
900   In Section 3.3, we propose to prevent the model from learning the undesirable training data biases
901   by adding image augmentations and randomly sample the conditional subjects and reference images
902   for training. Table 4 lists the training augmentations and the hyperparameter setting. While augmen-
903   tations can fix some biases from reference images, empirically, we find that the model can also learn
904   the unintended biases from the composition of reference images. Specifically, if we always use all
905
      available reference images as conditions during training, the model can generate the target subject
      with some properties correlated to the number of reference images (ref ) during inference. Using the
906
      text prompt “A dog is running” as an example:
907
908         • If having 0 ref, the model generates a tiny or heavily occluded dog.
909         • If having 1 ref, the model generates a dog running out of the view of the video.
910
            • If having 3 ref s of a similar pose, the model generates a dog running in slow-motion.
911   To avoid the model learning the biases from the composition of reference images, we apply a special
912   rule to sample the conditional subjects and their reference images during training. It includes the
913   following five steps:
914         • Randomly sample the number of the conditional subjects from 1 to 3.
915         • Randomly sample the conditional subjects with replacement.
916         • For each subject, randomly sample the number of conditional reference images from 1 to 3.
917         • For each subject, randomly sample the conditional reference images with replacement.
            • Randomly including the background conditional with a probability of 50%.

      B.1     M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
963
964
      Our framework is a latent-based diffusion model, using MAGVIT (Yu et al., 2023) and DiT (Pee-
965
      bles & Xie, 2023) as the autoencoder and the video generation backbone respectively. We detail
966   the hyperparameters of our model architecture in Table 5. To accelerate the model, we utilize the
967   positional embeddings and self-attention in RoPE (Su et al., 2024) and adopt flash attention (Dao
968   et al., 2022) and fused layer normalization (2018). We implement the models with three different
969   image encoders, including CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2024), MAE (He
970   et al., 2022), where the backbone and other details are listed in Table 6. We find that using the
971   patch tokens as the image embeddings can retain more localized properties of the reference images
      and result to higher fidelity compared to the class token. Moreover, aligned with the observation


                                                             18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       from Liu et al. (2024), we notice that CLIP’s patch tokens from the second last transformer block
973    can yield better preservation of the subject details than the ones from the last block.

       B.2   M ODEL T RAINING
976
977    We present the training details of the model in Table 7. We train the model in three stages. In the
978    first stage, we fix the autoencoder and train the video generation backbone without cross-attention
979    for personalization conditioning for 490K steps with a 10K-step warmup. In the second stage,
980    we introduce the personalization conditioning modules and finetune them while keeping the video
981    generation backbone and image encoder fixed for 20K steps. In the final stage, we finetune both
982    the video generation backbone and the personalization conditioning modules, keeping the image
983
       encoder fixed, for 50K steps with a 5K-step warmup. We use the AdamW (Loshchilov, 2017)
       optimizer with a constant learning rate of 1e−4 . To achieve stable training, we set β = [0.9, 0.99], a
984
       weight decay of 0.01, gradient clipping with the value of 0.05. We randomly drop the text prompt or
985
       subject image conditioning with a probability of 10% and set them to zero to support classifier-free
986    guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2022).
987
988    To enable the generation of high-resolution and long-duration videos while ensuring efficient model
989
       training, we train our model on videos of varying resolutions and lengths. Table 7 lists the batchsizes
       and sampling weights for the training videos across different resolutions and lengths. The batchsizes
990
       are set to balance the training time for each step with different attributes. We apply the fixed fram-
991
       erate of 24. Our model supports generating videos up to 16 seconds in length at 512p × 288p
992    resolution, and up to 5 seconds at 1024p × 576p resolution.
993
994    We implement our model in PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019) and perform all experiments on Nvidia
995
       80GB A100 GPUs.

997    B.3   M ODEL I NFERENCE
998
       We utilize a rectified flow sampler (Liu et al., 2023b) with classifier-free guidance (Ho & Salimans,
999
       2022) (CFG) for sampling. The choice of CFG scale can significantly impact the performance of
1000   diffusion models. While our model performs best with a CFG scale of 8 for text conditioning, we
1001   find that applying such a high CFG scale for subject image conditioning can cause the model to
1002   embed reference images directly into the video, without introducing natural motion and appearance
1003   variation. To address this, we apply CFG twice within each sampling step: once for text conditioning
1004   with a CFG scale of 8 and once for subject image conditioning with a scale of 2.5. We use 128 de-
1005   noising steps for quantitative evaluations and 256 steps for qualitative visualizations, with the same
1006   CFG interval (Kynkäänniemi et al., 2024) of [0.15, 0.5]. Additionally, we apply time shifting (Esser
1007   et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2024) to align the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) across different resolutions.

       D    L IMITATIONS
1189
1190
       Undesirable Training Data Bias. In Section 3.3, we address the issue of undesirable image biases
1191   by introducing augmentations and random sampling with replacement during training. However,
1192   some image biases, such as facial expressions and body postures, remain unresolved. As a result,
1193   our framework may generate subjects with similar facial expressions or postures as the reference
1194   images. Figures 12 and 13 show that existing personalization frameworks (Wei et al., 2023a; Jiang
1195   et al., 2024) with the same reconstruction-based learning strategy also exhibit this issue, which
1196   remains a challenge for future work.
1197   Taking Masked Images as Inputs. Our model personalizes video synthesis using segmented ref-
1198   erence image inputs. It requires users to provide masked images during inference and additional
1199   efforts may be needed if the localization algorithms do not segment the correct subject. Pasting the
1200   subject image segment to a random background image can be employed on the training dataset to
1201   address this issue.
1202
       Oversaturation. In Appendix B.3, we adopt classifier-free guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2022) (CFG)
1203   twice in each denoising step to achieve different CFG scales for text and personalization condi-
1204   tionings. However, we empirically observe that our model occasionally generates highly saturated
1205   samples, which is a persistent issue (Saharia et al., 2022; Kynkäänniemi et al., 2024) in diffusion
1206   models when strong CFG is used for sampling. For future work, we plan to explore sampling tech-
1207   niques like static or dynamic thresholding (Saharia et al., 2022) to address this issue.
1208
       Unnatural Composition for Multiple Subject Conditioning. When users input multiple subjects
1209   for conditioning, the synthetic videos sporadically exhibit unrealistic compositions and scales among
1210   the different subjects. This behavior can be interpreted as the relative minority of videos with multi-
1211   ple subjects in the training dataset. We are considering creating a training dataset featuring a higher
1212   frequency of video samples with multiple subjects for future work.
1213
       Unsupported Measure on Video Quality. Same as CLIP similarity score (Torchmetrics, 2024),
1214
       MSRVTT-Personalization does not assess visual quality. Users must rely on alternative evaluations,
1215   such as user studies, to compare the visual quality.
