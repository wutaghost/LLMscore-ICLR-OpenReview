009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Modular object-centric representations are key to unlocking human-like reasoning
012            capabilities. However, addressing challenges such as object occlusions to obtain
013            meaningful object-level representations presents both theoretical and practical
014            difficulties. We introduce a novel multi-view probabilistic approach that aggregates
015            view-specific slots to capture invariant content information while simultaneously
016            learning disentangled global viewpoint-level information. Our model resolves
017            spatial ambiguities and provides theoretical guarantees for learning identifiable
018
               representations, setting it apart from prior work focusing on single-view settings and
               lacking theoretical foundations. Along with our identifiability analysis, we provide
019
               extensive empirical validation with promising results on both benchmark and
020
               proposed large-scale datasets carefully designed to evaluate multi-view methods.
021

023   1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025   The ability to capture the notion of objectness in learned representations is believed to be a critical as-
026   pect for developing situation-aware AI systems with human-like system-1 reasoning capabilities (Lake
027   et al., 2017). Recent advances in object-centric representation learning have shown great potential in
028   this direction (Locatello et al., 2020b; Kori et al., 2023; Löwe et al., 2024). The goal of object-centric
029   learning (OCL) is to enable agents to learn representations of respective objects in an observed
030   scene in the context of their environment, as opposed to learning global representations as in the
031   case of traditional generative models such as variational auto-encoders (Kingma & Welling, 2013).
032   Object-centric approaches enable agents to learn spatially disentangled representations, which is an
033
      important step in compositional scene generation (Bengio et al., 2013; Lake et al., 2017; Battaglia
      et al., 2018; Greff et al., 2020) and understanding of causal (and physical) interactions between the
034
      objects (Marcus, 2003; Gerstenberg et al., 2021; Gopnik et al., 2004).
035
036   Most of the recent progress in OCL has been limited to learning scene representations from single-
037   viewpoints (Locatello et al., 2020b; Engelcke et al., 2021; Singh et al., 2021; Kori et al., 2023; Chang
038   et al., 2022; Seitzer et al., 2022; Löwe et al., 2024). While these approaches may learn meaningful
039
      object-specific representations, they face insurmountable challenges due to spatial ambiguities;
      learning from single viewpoints cannot capture effective representations due to partially or fully
040
      occluded objects. Li et al. (2020) previously proposed an intriguing approach to address some of
041
      the spatial ambiguities. They take a view-conditional OCL perspective, which makes their approach
042   reliant on the availability of paired viewpoint conditioning and corresponding images. Here, we take
043   a step forward, exploring multi-view object-centric learning (MVOCL), allowing us to exploit objects’
044   inherent geometry and semantics to establish correspondences across views.
045
      Another issue with many of the earlier OCL methods (including (Li et al., 2020)) is that they lack
046
      rigorous formalisation of their underpinning explicit and implicit assumptions; Kori et al. (2024);
047
      Brady et al. (2023); Lachapelle et al. (2023) make an effort to formalise these assumptions and
048   provide conditions under which these methods result in learning identifiable slot representations.
049   Similarly, formalisations in MVOCL are unexplored, and the theoretical guarantees under which the
050   partially or fully occluded slot representations are identifiable have not been studied before. In this
051   work, we consider learning the joint distribution over all viewpoints, as opposed to view-conditional
052   OCL (Li et al., 2020); our model provides the additional advantage of not being dependent on
053   camera/viewpoint information. Inspired by Kori et al. (2024); Kivva et al. (2022), we take the
      perspective of imposing latent structure to achieve identifiable slot representations under viewpoint

      4      T HEORETICAL A NALYSIS

      In this section, we leverage the properties of the MV PSA method proposed in Section 3 to theoretically
273   demonstrate the learning of identifiable representations under challenging viewpoint ambiguities. In
274   summary, we show three main results; firstly, we show that aggregate content representations (c)
275   are identifiable without supervision (up to an equivalence relation). Secondly, we show that these
276   representations are invariant to the choice of viewpoints under viewpoint sufficiency. Finally, we
277   show that the trained model results in an approximate representational equivariance up to an affine
278   transformation, i.e., for any two viewpoints sub-sets A, B ⊆ [V ] ∋ A ̸= B, the resulting content
279   distribution pA (c) can be recovered by pB (c) up to an affine transformation.

      Identifiable representation learning. Learning meaningful representations from unlabeled data has
353
      long been a primary objective of deep learning (Bengio et al., 2013). Several approaches, such as
354
      those proposed by (Higgins et al., 2017; Kim & Mnih, 2018; Eastwood & Williams, 2018; Mathieu
355   et al., 2019), relied on independence assumptions between latent variables to learn disentangled
356   representations. However, Hyvärinen & Pajunen (1999); Locatello et al. (2019) demonstrated the
357   provable impossibility of unsupervised methods for learning independent latent representations from
358   i.i.d. data. Which is tackled by restricting mixing functions to conformal maps (Buchholz et al., 2022)
359   or volume-preserving transformations (Yang et al., 2022), or with additional data assumptions (Zim-
360   mermann et al., 2021; Locatello et al., 2020a; Brehmer et al., 2022; Ahuja et al., 2022; Von Kügelgen
361   et al., 2021), or by imposing structure in the latent space as in nonlinear Independent Component
362   Analysis (ICA) (Hyvarinen et al., 2019; Khemakhem et al., 2020a;b), resulting in identifiable models.
363
      In the context of nonlinear ICA, Dilokthanakul et al. (2016) introduced a VAE model with a GMM
      prior, and Willetts & Paige (2021) empirically demonstrated the effectiveness of the GMM prior,
364
      which was later rigorously proven by Kivva et al. (2022). Kori et al. (2024) use this notion of latent
365
      GMM in the context of OCL, achieving identifiability guarantees for object-centric representations.
366   Here, we use this notion in the context of multiview object-centric representations, tackling the issues
367   with spatial ambiguities and uncertainties in bindings.
368
369
      Multiview nonlinear ICA. It has been noted that addressing the challenge of nonlinear Independent
      Component Analysis (ICA) can involve incorporating a learnable clustering task within the latent
370
      representations, thereby imposing asymmetry in the latent distribution (Willetts & Paige, 2021; Kivva
371
      et al., 2022). Moreover, the study by Gresele et al. (2020) delves into multiview nonlinear ICA,
372   particularly in scenarios involving corrupted observations, where they aim to recover invariant repre-
373   sentations while accounting for certain ambiguities. Along similar lines, Daunhawer et al. (2023);
374   Von Kügelgen et al. (2021) explore the concept of style-content identification using contrastive learn-
375   ing, focusing on addressing the multiview nonlinear ICA problem. Here, we work along similar lines
376   by emphasising the learning of invariant content and identifiable object-centric representations. We
377   achieve this by formulating a reconstruction objective where the enforced invariance and equivariance
      stem from the underlying probabilistic graphical model rather than relying on a contrastive learning

403   Object-centric learning. Extending nonlinear ICA from representation learning to object-specific

416   representations (Lachapelle et al., 2023; Brady et al., 2023; Kori et al., 2024), we formally investigate
417   the modelling assumptions and their implications for achieving identifiability guarantees in the
418

      7         C ONCLUSION & D ISCUSSION
516
      Understanding when object-centric representations are both unambiguous and identifiable is essential
517
      for developing large-scale models with provable correctness guarantees. Unlike most existing work
518
      on identifiability, which largely focuses on single-view setups, we offer identifiability guarantees
519   in multi-view scenarios. Building upon the approach by Kori et al. (2024), we use distributional
520   assumptions for latent slot and view representations, drawing inspiration from mixture model-based
521   structures. To achieve this, we propose a model that is viewpoint-agnostic and does not require
522   additional view-conditioning information.
523
      Our model specifically guarantees the identifiability of view-specific slot representations, viewpoint-
524
      invariant content representations, and view representations, all without the need for additional
525   supervision (up to an equivalence relation). We visually validate our theoretical claims using
526   illustrative 2D data points. We then empirically demonstrate the model’s identifiability properties on
527   multiple object-centric benchmarks, highlighting its ability to resolve view ambiguities in imaging
528   applications. Furthermore, we showcase the scalability of our approach on large-scale datasets and
529   more complex decoders using realistic datasets and transformer decoders, respectively, demonstrating
530   its capacity to scale effectively with both data volume and decoder complexity.
531   Limitations & future work. We recognize that our assumptions, particularly regarding the viewpoint
532   sufficiency, are strong and may not always hold in practice. However, we did not observe limiting
533   effects of this assumption on the proposed MV-M OV I D dataset. A more extensive analysis of this
534   assumption and its implications in real-world applications is left for future work. We would also
535   highlight that the weak injectivity of the mixing function may not always hold for different types
536   of architectures. While generally applicable, the piecewise-affine functions we use may not always
537   capture valid assumptions for real-world problems, e.g., when the model is misspecified. Nevertheless,
538   to the best of our knowledge, our theoretical results on multi-object, multi-view identifiability are
539
      unique and capture key concepts in multi-view object-centric representation learning, opening various
      new avenues for future research.

871
872   As pointed out in Lachapelle et al. (2023), softmax-based masks do not truly fall under the category
873   of additive decoders due to the competition between masks for groups of pixels. This implies that
874   the additive decoders studied in Lachapelle et al. (2023) are not expressive enough to represent the
875
      “masked decoders” typically employed in object-centric representation learning. The issue arises
      from the normalization of alpha masks, and care must be taken when extrapolating the findings from
876
      Lachapelle et al. (2023) to the models used in practice.
877
878   Although sigmoid-based masks satisfy the condition of additivity during inference, it is important
879   to note that in our setting the model is still trained using softmax normalization. The effect of
