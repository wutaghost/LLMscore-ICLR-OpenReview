025                                                A BSTRACT
026
027                  In this work, we investigate the problem of creating high-fidelity photorealistic
028                  3D avatars from only a single face image. This task is inherently challenging due
029                  to the limited 3D cues and ambiguities present in a single viewpoint, further com-
030                  plicated by the intricate details of the human face (e.g., wrinkles, facial hair). To
031                  address these challenges, we introduce PSHead, a coarse-to-fine framework that
032                  optimizes 3D Gaussian Splatting for a single image, guided by a mixture of object
033                  and face prior to generate high-quality 3D avatars while preserving faithfulness to
034
                     the original image. At the coarse stage, we leverage diffusion models trained on
                     general objects to predict coarse representation by applying score distillation sam-
035
                     pling losses at novel views. This marks the first attempt to integrate text-to-image,
036
                     image-to-image, and text-to-video diffusion priors, ensuring consistency across
037                  multiple views and robustness to variations in face size. In the fine stage, we uti-
038                  lize pretrained face generation models to denoise the rendered noisy images, and
039                  use them as supervision to refine the 3D representation. Our method outperforms
040                  existing approaches on in-the-wild images, proving its robustness and ability to
041                  capture intricate details without the need for extensive 3D supervision.

044   1       I NTRODUCTION
045
046    Creating photorealistic 3D avatars is a key challenge in computer graphics, with applications in
047   movies, games, virtual or augmented reality, and the metaverse. There is growing interest in creating
048   digital avatars from a single image, as it is easily obtainable. While humans can intuitively infer 3D
049   shapes and textures from a quick glance, thanks to their vast knowledge of the natural world, tackling
050
      this task algorithmically is far more difficult. The main challenge lies in the limited 3D cues and
      inherent ambiguities present in a single viewpoint, compounded by the rich and intricate details of
051
      the human face (e.g., wrinkle, facial hair), making the task even more difficult.
052
053   Some attempts have been made to generate 3D heads from a single reference image, but their perfor-
      mance and flexibility are severely constrained by the training datasets. They typically utilize small


                                                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      scale 3D head datasets (Zheng et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024a) or large-scale 2D images (Chan et al.,
055   2022a; An et al., 2023). However, challenges in capturing and processing data often result in reduced
056   quality and diversity (in terms of identity, race, age et al.) in the training datasets, which in turn neg-
057   atively impacts the accuracy of generated reconstructions, particularly when the reference image is
058   captured in the wild. Additionally, the normalization preprocessing steps (e.g., align-cropping) in
059   (Chan et al., 2022a; An et al., 2023) prohibit them from handling inputs of varying scales, such as
060   head only, head and neck, or head and shoulders images.
061   Recently, significant progress has been made in text and image-to-3D object generation, largely
062   driven by diffusion models pretrained on large-scale datasets that encode object priors (Saharia
063   et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022). The typical approach involves optimizing a 3D representation
064   by aligning its 2D renderings from random angles with diffusion prior (Poole et al., 2023). While
065   these methods have been successfully applied to text-to-3D avatar generation (Cao et al., 2024; Han
066   et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024), adapting them for image-to-3D avatar generation is non-trivial and
067   requires additional efforts. The primary difficulty lies in achieving fidelity: the generated 3D mod-
068   els must closely match the identity of the reference image, while also being realistic at the same
069
      time, rather than relying on the more general guidance of a rough text prompt. However, most
      existing text-to-image (T2I) (Ruiz et al., 2023), image-to-image (I2I) (Liu et al., 2023), and text-
070
      to-video (T2V) (Wang et al., 2023b) diffusion models are not specifically trained on face images,
071
      limiting their ability to capture fine facial details or maintain identity-preserving characteristics.
072   Face-specific diffusion models often have limitations: they either lack scale (He et al., 2024), rely
073   on synthesized data (Wang et al., 2023a), or focus solely on frontal or profile views with facial land-
074   mark constraints (CrucibleAI, 2023), and are therefore not directly applicable to image-to-3D avatar
075   task. Despite these limitations, Text-to-3D avatar generation under T2I guidance has demonstrated
076   a strong ability to handle a wide range of inputs, from close-up shots of the face (Han et al., 2024;
077   Liu et al., 2024) to full-body characters (Cao et al., 2024). This suggests that diffusion models pos-
078   sess valuable 3D knowledge about the human structure. Motivated by this, we argue that carefully
079   leveraging existing diffusion models holds strong potential to solve the image-to-3D avatar task.
080   In this work, we propose a head-specific generative method PSHead that lifts a single frontal face
081   image to an accurate and faithful 3D gaussian splatting (3D-GS) reconstruction (Kerbl et al., 2023),
082   with a particular focus on preserving the subject’s identity and recovering details in the reference
083   image (e.g., face, hair, neck, and shoulders). We adopt a coarse-to-fine strategy. At the coarse stage,
084   we incorporate Score Distillation Sampling (SDS) (Poole et al., 2023) guidance from multiple types
085   of diffusion models to leverage their unique strengths. These models include a subject-specific T2I
086
      model finetuned via DreamBooth (Ruiz et al., 2023), capturing person-specific characteristics such
      as hair style; an I2I model (Liu et al., 2023) to generate novel views with camera rotations cover-
087
      ing a full 360◦ space and the reference image, providing plausible multiview SDS guidance; and a
088
      T2V model (Wang et al., 2023b) to generate novel views as consecutive frames in video, enhanc-
089   ing multiview consistency via a temporal cross-attention mechanism. The combined SDS loss from
090   object diffusion models allows us to learn a 3D-GS with coarse geometry and a noisy appearance,
091   lacking high-quality details, especially in the face and hair. To address this, we incorporate a re-
092   finement stage, where additional 2D facial priors from models trained on face datasets are used to
093   refine the representation and enhance facial detail. Specifically, we prioritize enhancement by using
094   landmark-guided ControlNet (CrucibleAI, 2023) to denoise the entire face image, with particular
095   focus on refining face geometry and applying a face super-resolution model (Zhou et al., 2022) to
096   increase resolution in facial regions. Additionally, we use the personalized T2I model to effectively
097
      denoise rest views, ensuring consistency across different angles.
098   To summarize, we make the following contributions: (1) We propose PSHead, a method that learns
099   a 360◦ photographic 3D-GS representation for a reference image with varying face sizes; (2) We
100   leverage a mixture of diffusion priors to generate a coarse representation of the input face, providing
101   insights into how each prior contributes to the process; (3) We refine the coarse representation in an
102   innovative way by introducing 2D face priors to enhance more detailed representation.

142   Single Face to 3D. The task of converting a single face to a 3D model can be divided into two
143   categories based on the usage of different face generation models. The first approach, 3D-GAN
144   inversion, involves initially training a 3D-GAN on a large-scale 2D face dataset and then learning
145   the latent code for a specific face image. EG3D (Chan et al., 2022b) exemplifies this method, with
146
      subsequent studies enhancing inversion performance through the integration of symmetry priors
      (Yin et al., 2023), refinements (Bhattarai et al., 2024), and other techniques (Trevithick et al., 2023).
147
      The second approach focuses on generating a 3D avatar starting from a text prompt, where a face
148
      image is generated using T2I diffusion models, and a 3D model is learned with supervision from
149   the generated image and guidance from the diffusion model. This method tends to prioritize textual
150   descriptions over the input image, producing a 3D model that aligns more closely with the text
151   description (Wu et al., 2024). However, these methods often rely heavily on the preprocessing steps
152   used during the training of the 3D-GAN, making it difficult to generalize to arbitrary facial inputs.

207   3.2       PSH EAD
208
       Our goal is to generate a high-fidelity 3D head model parameterised θ, that preserves the iden-
209
      tity and appearance of the person in a frontal reference image Iref . To achieve this, we leverage
210   prior knowledge embedded in models pretrained on both general objects and faces to optimize θ
211   from coarse to fine. In the coarse stage, we use a mixture of SDS losses provided by personalized
212   T2I, generic I2I, and T2V diffusion models to optimize a coarse 3D-GS. The reference view re-
213   construction is also involved to supervise training. In the fine stage, we utilize personalized T2I,
214   a shape-guided face controlnet module, and a pretrained face super-resolution model to denoise
215   and improve novel views, and subsequently apply reconstruction loss using the enhanced images to
      refine 3D representation. Figure 2 provides a visual diagram of PSHead, illustrating these processes.

      which helps simplify and improve the geometry reconstruction process (Yariv et al., 2020).

226   The second group includes novel views of the object, where we uniformly sample 25 views around
227   the azimuth angle from 0◦ to 360◦ . These novel views are optimized under the guidance of prior
228   models to improve the overall training process and reconstruction quality.
229   Specifically, we investigate three types of diffusion priors: T2I (Ruiz et al., 2023), I2I Liu et al.
230   (2023), and T2V Wang et al. (2023b). T2I focuses on how text descriptions influence individual
231   novel view generation, I2I examines how a reference image propagates to the generation of another
232   view, and T2V explores multi-view generation in a sequential manner. To fully harness the poten-
233   tial of these diffusion models, we have made several improvements and modifications, effectively
234   conditioning them to enhance the 3D-GS process.
235   Personalized Text-to-Image (T2I) SDS. Text-to-3D avatar generation, which creates a 3D head
236   avatar using descriptive text through SDS loss, has shown promising performance (Han et al., 2024;
237   Chen et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024). However, when applied directly to the task of image-to-3D
238   generation, it often results in a mismatch between the generated 3D avatar and the identity of the
239   reference image. This is due to the inherent ambiguity of text – a picture is worth a thousand words.
240   To facilitate the understanding of visual characteristics of a given image, we propose combining
241   descriptive text with a personalized T2I diffusion model.
242   Specifically, we utilize BLIP (Li et al., 2022) to describe a face from three key aspects: gender,
243   clothing, and hair style. Additionally, we deploy DreamBooth (Ruiz et al., 2023), to personalize
244   T2I model to encode reference image through few-shot tuning, which helps to reduce the excessive
245   imagination typically seen in 2D diffusion models. To generate the necessary inputs for fintuning, we
246   follow (Huang et al., 2024) to augment the single input image with five different backgrounds, and
247   create a gallery of “man” and “woman” images for regularization. After optimization, the subject-

      specific appearance is encoded within a unique identifier token “[V]”. For instance, the description

531   6           C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
532
      We propose PSHead that utilizes diffusion priors via SDS to generate coarse representation for a
533
      single reference image, which is then refined using facial priors to enhance the rendered images.
534   Benefits from general diffusion, PSHead is robust across varying face sizes. As the first effort
535   to integrate T2I, I2I, and T2V diffusion models into a single framework, we analyze the function
536   of each model, hoping to inspire future work to adopt similar designs. While PSHead improves
537   performance and expands the scope of 3D avatar generation, it has certain limitations. Due to the
538   lack of a hair super resolution module, usage of T2I model results in the hair details appearing
539   less defined and lacking individual strands. Additionally, accessories like earrings and transparent
      eyeglasses are difficult to synthesize (see Figure 7).

716   age cosine similarity score using ArcFace across viewpoints (−45◦ to 45◦ ) relative to the reference
717   image. If facial landmarks are undetectable in an image from a particular viewpoint, its score is
718   set to 0. The ArcFace model used here differs from the one in Eq.11, as we use ResNet50 trained
719   on WebFace600K, whereas the model in Eq.11 is ResNet100 trained on MS1MV2. ArcFace model
720   used here is different from Eq. 11 (ResNet50@WebFace600K vs ResNet100@MS1MV2).
