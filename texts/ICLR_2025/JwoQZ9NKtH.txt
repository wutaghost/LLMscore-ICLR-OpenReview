                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               In this paper, we propose a novel approach to fair machine learning, the Cauchy-
010
               Schwarz fairness regularizer, which minimizes the Cauchy-Schwarz divergence
011            between the prediction distribution and sensitive attributes. While existing methods
012            effectively reduce bias as indicated by low values on specific fairness metrics, they
013            frequently struggle to achieve a balanced performance across various fairness defi-
014            nitions. For example, many approaches may successfully attain low demographic
015            parity yet still demonstrate significant disparities in equal opportunity. Theoretical
016            studies have shown that the Cauchy-Schwarz divergence provides a tighter bound
017            compared to the Kullback-Leibler divergence and gap parity, suggesting its po-
018            tential to improve fairness in machine learning models. Our empirical evaluation,
019
               conducted on four tabular datasets and one image dataset, demonstrates that the
               Cauchy-Schwarz fairness regularizer achieves a more balanced performance across
020
               fairness metrics while maintaining satisfactory utility. It outperforms existing
021
               fairness approaches, providing a superior trade-off between fairness and utility.
022            In addition, the Cauchy-Schwarz fairness regularizer is a versatile, plug-and-play
023            fairness regularizer that can be easily integrated into various machine learning
024            models to promote fairness.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Machine learning models are increasingly adopted in high-stakes decision-making scenarios, such as
030   credit scoring (Petrasic et al., 2017), the job market (Hu & Chen, 2018), healthcare (Grote & Keeling,
031   2022), and education (Bøyum, 2014; Kizilcec & Lee, 2022). Despite their success, these models
032   are often prone to generating prediction disparities among different demographic groups, including
033
      genders, ages, skin colors, and regions, particularly when no interventions are introduced during
      the training process (Mehrabi et al., 2021; Dwork et al., 2012; Barocas et al., 2017). Such biased
034
      algorithms can have detrimental impacts on individuals’ lives, especially for disadvantaged groups.
035
      This inherent bias in the data complicates the pursuit of fairness in machine learning models (Jiang &
036   Nachum, 2020). Consequently, the growing concern over fairness has garnered significant attention
037   from researchers, who are striving to achieve equitable predictions across demographic groups based
038   on various fairness notions (Hsu et al., 2022; Chai et al., 2022; Reddy et al., 2021).
039
      Many debiasing methods incorporate a fairness regularizer that aims to minimize the differences in
040
      prediction distributions across various sensitive groups. These prediction distributions are typically
041
      assessed using metrics such as gap parity, Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence, and the Hilbert-Schmidt
042   Independence Criterion (HSIC). While these methods can effectively enhance performance on certain
043   fairness metrics, they often fail to maintain a balanced level of fairness. For instance, as shown in
044   Figure 1, the DP regularizer successfully achieves low demographic parity (DP), indicated by the
045   closely aligned prediction distributions for female and male groups. However, it does not significantly
046   improve equal opportunity (EO), as evidenced by the considerable disparity between these two
047   distributions. This finding is further corroborated by the T-SNE plots, which illustrate that the
048   embeddings for the female (blue points) and male (red points) groups are indistinguishable across
049   all data points, particularly for Y = 0/1. In contrast, for the positive class data points (Y = 1),
050
      the embeddings exhibit a more discernible pattern, with the blue points clustered in a specific area
      (circled) rather than being evenly distributed, as depicted in the T-SNE plots for all classes.
051
052   This indicates that training a model solely to optimize its objectives is insufficient for achieving
053   fairness in test data. Previous studies have shown that a machine learning model that achieves a 0
      disparity in training may still fail to maintain low disparity on test data, indicating an inability to

098   2.1   FAIR M ACHINE L EARNING
099
100   There are various notions of fair machine learning, with group fairness being one of the most
101   extensively studied concepts in recent years. In this study, we focus specifically on group fairness
102   and introduce additional notions in Appendix F.2.
103   Group fairness seeks to ensure that machine learning models treat different demographic groups
104   equitably, where groups are defined based on sensitive attributes such as gender, race, and age. This
105   concept is rooted in the notion of statistical parity, as discussed in prior studies (Feldman et al., 2015;
106   Zemel et al., 2013). Specifically, group fairness requires that the proportion of individuals in a given
107   group receiving positive (or negative) outcomes aligns with the overall proportion of those outcomes
      in the entire population.

      The literature on group fairness presents a variety of concepts related to fairness. Each definition
109   focuses on distinct statistical measures aimed at achieving balance among subgroups within the data.
110   In this paper, we specifically consider demographic parity and equalized opportunity as fair metrics.
111
112
      Demographic Parity. Demographic Parity (DP) (Zafar et al., 2017; Feldman et al., 2015; Dwork
113   et al., 2012) mandates that the predicted outcome Ŷ be independent of the sensitive attribute S,
114   expressed mathematically as Ŷ ⊥ S. Most of the existing literature primarily addresses binary
115
      classification and binary attributes, where Y ∈ {0, 1} and S ∈ {0, 1}. Similar to the concept of equal
      opportunity, the metric evaluating the DP fairness is defined by:
116
117                               △DP = |P (Ŷ = 1|S = 0) − P (Ŷ = 1|S = 1)|.                               (1)
118   A lower value of △DP signifies a fairer classifier. Both Disparate Impact (DP) and Equal Opportunity
119   (EO) metrics can be effectively extended to problems involving multi-class classifications and multiple
120   sensitive attribute categories. This can be achieved by ensuring that Ŷ ⊥S for DP and Ŷ ⊥S|Y for
121   EO.
122
      Equal Opportunity (EO). Equal Opportunity (EO) (Hardt et al., 2016) mandates that a classifier
123
      achieves equal true positive rates across various subgroups, striving towards the ideal of a perfect
124
      classifier. The corresponding fairness measurement for EO can be articulated as follows:
125
126                         △EO = |P (Ŷ = 1|Y = 1, S = 0) − P (Ŷ = 1|Y = 1, S = 1)|.                       (2)
127   A low △EO indicates that the difference in the probability of an instance in the positive class being
128   assigned a positive outcome is relatively small for both subgroup members.

228   3.2   W HY IS THE C AUCHY-S CHWARZ D IVERGENCE M ORE E FFECTIVE FOR E NSURING
229         FAIRNESS ?
230
      The Cauchy-Schwarz Divergence is particularly well-suited for promoting fairness due to several key
231
      reasons:
232
233   (1) Closed-form solution for the mixture of Gaussians. The CS divergence has several advantageous
234   properties, one of which is that it provides a closed-form solution for the mixture of Gaussians (Kampa
235
      et al., 2011). This particular property has facilitated its successful application in various tasks,
      including deep clustering (Trosten et al., 2021), disentangled representation learning (Tran et al.,
236
      2022), and point-set registration (Sanchez Giraldo et al., 2017).
237
238   (2) CS Divergence has a tighter error bound than the KL divergence.
239   Proposition 2. For any d-variate Gaussian distributions p ∼ N (µp , Σp ) and q ∼ N (µq , Σq ), where
240   Σp and Σq are positive definite, the following inequality holds:
241
242
                             DCS (p; q) ≤ DKL (p; q) and DCS (p; q) ≤ DKL (q; p).                       (10)
243
244   The proof can be found in Appendix A.3.
245   (3) CS divergence can provide tighter bounds than MMD and DP when the distributions are far
246   apart or when the scale of the embeddings varies significantly. Based on the analysis presented
247   in Remark 1, we know that CS divergence employs cosine distance, while MMD relies on Euclidean
248   distance. In addition, DP Equation (6) utilizes a mean disparity, which is a Manhattan distance for the
249   mean estimations of two distributions. CS divergence measures the angle between two distributions
250   in the feature space, focusing on the difference in direction rather than magnitude. In cases where the
251
      distributions have significantly different variances or scales, MMD and DP may yield a large distance
      even if the distributions are aligned in the feature space. In contrast, CS divergence normalizes
252
      this comparison, resulting in a more accurate measure of similarity and thereby providing a tighter
253
      generalization bound. This normalization enhances the robustness of CS divergence, preventing
254   MMD and DP from overestimating the discrepancy due to its reliance on an unnormalized distance
255   measure.

      6        C ONCLUSION
512
      In this paper, we introduce a novel fair machine learning method known as the Cauchy-Schwarz (CS)
513
      fairness regularizer. While existing methods effectively reduce bias in machine learning models, they
514
      often struggle to maintain balanced fairness across different fairness metrics, such as Demographic
515   Parity (DP) and Equal Opportunity. For instance, many existing approaches can achieve very low DP
516   but may still exhibit relatively high EO. We demonstrate that our proposed Cauchy-Schwarz fairness
517   regularizer achieves superior and balanced fairness performance without compromising utility. This
518   is accomplished by minimizing the Cauchy-Schwarz divergence between the prediction distribution
519   and the sensitive attributes. Through analyzing the relationship between the CS divergence and other
520   distance measurements, we found that the CS divergence provides a tighter bound than both the
521   Kullback-Leibler divergence and the Maximum Mean Discrepancy, as well as the mean disparity
522   (used in DP regularizer). This holds true particularly when the distributions are significantly different
523
      or when there is substantial variation in the scale of the embeddings. This leads to improved fairness
      performance in practical scenarios.

      Remark 1. CS divergence measures the cosine distance between µp and µq in a Reproducing Kernel
827
      Hilbert Space, while MMD utilizes Euclidean distance.
828
829
      Proof. Let H be a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) associated with a kernel κ(xpi , xqj ) =
830
      ⟨f (xpi ), f (xqj )⟩H (Yu et al., 2024). The mean embeddings of two distributions p and q in H are
831                            PN1                            PN2
832   denoted by µp = N11 i=1           f (xpi ) and µq = N12 j=1   f (xqj ) in H, respectively. The CS divergence
833   defined by Equation (14) can thus be written as:
834
                             De CS (p; q) = −2 log ⟨µp , µq ⟩H = −2 log DCOS (µp , µq )
835                                                   ∥µp ∥H ∥µq ∥H
836
837   Here, ⟨·, ·⟩H denotes the inner product in the RKHS, and ∥ · ∥H represents the norm induced by the
838   inner product. The mean embeddings µp and µq are elements of H. Thus, the CS divergence is
839
      computed based on the cosine distance DCOS between µp and µq .
840   Similarly, the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) between distributions p and q defined in Equa-
841   tion (7) can be written as:
842                               MMD2 (p, q) = ∥µp − µq ∥2H = DEUC (µp , µq ).
843
844   Thus, the MMD measures the Euclidean distance between the mean embeddings of p and q in the
845   RKHS H, i.e., the µp and µq .

       D      BASELINES D ETAILS
1151
       We consider four widely used fairness methods: DP, MMD, HSIC, and PR. Specifically, DP and HSIC
1152
       minimize the demographic parity and Hilbert-Schmidt Independence Criterion, correspondingly.
1153
       MMD learns a classifier that optimizes the Mean Maximum Discrepancy. PR optimizes the Kullback-
1154   Leibler divergence. We also include base models MLP and RN for tabular data and image data,
1155   correspondingly.
1156
1157
       • DP: It is a gap regularization method for demographic parity (Chuang & Mroueh, 2020). As these
         fairness definitions cannot be optimized directly, gap regularization is differentiable and can be
1158
         optimized using gradient descent.
1159   • MMD: The Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) (Gretton et al., 2012) is a metric used to measure
1160     the distance between probability distributions. Previous research has leveraged MMD to enhance
1161     fairness in machine learning models, specifically in variational autoencoders (Louizos et al., 2016)
1162     and MLPs (Deka & Sutherland, 2023). In this paper, we build on the methodologies from earlier
1163     works (Zhao & Meng, 2015) to compute the MMD baseline.
1164   • HSIC: It minimizes the Hilbert-Schmidt Independence Criterion between the prediction accuracy
1165     and the sensitive attributes (Gretton et al., 2005; Baharlouei et al., 2020; Li et al., 2019).
1166
       • Prejudice Remover (PR) (Kamishima et al., 2012) (Prejudice Remover) minimizes the mutual
         information between the prediction accuracy and the sensitive attributes.

1227   In this section, we first review relevant prior studies, beginning with an overview of algorithmic
1228   fairness in machine learning. We then narrow our focus to regularization-based in-processing methods,
1229   which are central to our approach.
1230
1231   F.1   A LGORITHMIC FAIRNESS IN M ACHINE L EARNING
1232
1233
       The importance of fairness in machine learning has grown significantly as the demand for unbiased
       decision-making models for individuals and groups increases. This is especially critical in high-
1234
       stakes applications where the consequences of biased decisions can be severe. Fairness is commonly
1235
       categorized into three main types: Individual fairness (Yurochkin et al., 2019; Mukherjee et al.,
1236   2020; Yurochkin & Sun, 2020; Kang et al., 2020; Mukherjee et al., 2022), which aims to ensure that
1237   similar individuals are treated similarly; Group fairness (Hardt et al., 2016; Verma & Rubin, 2018;
1238   Li et al., 2020; Ling et al., 2023), which focuses on achieving fairness across predefined subgroups,
1239   often defined by sensitive attributes such as gender or race; Counterfactual fairness (Kusner et al.,
1240   2017; Agarwal et al., 2021; Zuo et al., 2022), which seeks to ensure fairness by considering how
1241   decisions would hold under alternative scenarios. Given the widespread adoption of group fairness
       metrics in real-world applications and the increasing development of in-processing techniques for


                                                           23
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1242
       deep neural network models, we focus on benchmarking these methods to ensure group fairness in
1243   neural networks, particularly for tabular and image data.
1244
1245   Various techniques for mitigating bias in machine learning models can be categorized into three
1246
       main approaches: pre-processing, in-processing, and post-processing. Pre-processing methods
       focus on addressing biases present in the dataset itself to ensure that the trained model exhibits
1247
       fairness (Kamiran & Calders, 2012; Calmon et al., 2017a). For instance, these techniques may
1248
       involve rebalancing the dataset or modifying the data collection process (Calmon et al., 2017b).
1249   In-processing methods, on the other hand, adjust the training objectives by incorporating fairness
1250   constraints directly into the learning process (Kamishima et al., 2012; Zhang et al., 2018; Madras
1251   et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2022; Buyl & De Bie, 2022; Alghamdi et al., 2022; Shui et al., 2022;
1252   Mehrotra & Vishnoi, 2022). This approach aims to ensure that the model learns fair representations
1253   during training. Finally, post-processing methods modify the predictions made by classifiers after
1254   the model has been trained, with the goal of promoting fairness across different groups (Hardt et al.,
1255   2016; Jiang et al., 2020; Tsaousis & Alghamdi, 2022). By categorizing these techniques, we can
1256   better understand the different strategies available for mitigating bias in machine learning systems.
1257
1258   F.2   R EGULARIZATION - BASED I N -P ROCESSING M ETHODS
1259
       In this paper, we explore three types of regularization-based in-processing methods. First, Gap
1260
       Regularization Chuang & Mroueh (2020) streamlines the optimization process by offering a smooth
1261
       approximation of real-world loss functions, which are typically non-convex and difficult to optimize
1262   directly. This category includes methods such as DP, EO, and EOD. Second, the Independence
1263   approach integrates fairness constraints into the optimization, aiming to mitigate the influence of
1264   protected attributes on model predictions while maintaining overall performance. Notable examples
1265   of this approach include PR (Kamishima et al., 2012) and HSIC (Li et al., 2019). Lastly, adversarial
1266   debiasing seeks to minimize utility loss while hindering an adversary’s ability to accurately predict
1267   the protected attributes. This approach encompasses methods like ADV (Zhang et al., 2018; Louppe
1268   et al., 2017; Beutel et al., 2017; Edwards & Storkey, 2015; Adel et al., 2019) and LAFTR (Madras
1269   et al., 2018).
