009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Sequential recommendation aims to predict the next click for a particular user based
012            on their historical interacted item sequences. Recently, diffusion-based methods
013            have achieved the state-of-the-art performance in sequential recommendation.
014            However, they fail to effectively utilize the rich semantic information embedded in
015            items during the diffusion process to accurately guide the generation, leading to sub-
016            optimal results. To address this limitation, we designed SDR EC, a Semantic-aware
017            Diffusion model for sequential Recommendation. Our model introduces a novel
018
               architecture, the Semantic Fusion Layer, which leverages the embedding
               table from the encoder to incorporate item semantics into the diffusion process
019
               through an attention mechanism. Together with the well-designed contrastive and
020
               generative losses, SDR EC effectively utilizes the item semantics in diffusion model,
021            unleashing the potential of sequential recommendation. Our experiments show
022            that SDR EC has over 10% relative gain with superior efficiency compared with
023            existing methods.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Sequential recommendation aims to mine the user‚Äôs behavior patterns from historical interaction
029   sequences and predicts the next item that the user is most likely to click in the future. It has attracted
030   widespread attention due to its high commercial value in many business scenarios, such as streaming
031   media (Covington et al., 2016), e-commerce (Chen et al., 2019), and social networking (Zhou et al.,
032   2018). Since sequential recommendation needs to identify the most suitable item from the existing
033
      item set based on a user‚Äôs historical interactions, the effectiveness of the recommendations hinges on
      the deep understanding of the semantics of the items (e.g., the categories that a movie belongs to) and
034
      the modeling of user interests (e.g., what kinds of movies does the user like).
035
036   In order to efficiently capture the item semantics and user preferences, various types of methods
037   have been proposed for sequential recommendation (Hidasi & Karatzoglou, 2018; Yuan et al., 2019;
038   Sun et al., 2019; Xie et al., 2021; Ren et al., 2020). Recently, diffusion model (Ho et al., 2020)
039
      has shown remarkable results in generation tasks from Computer Vision (CV) (Dhariwal & Nichol,
      2021; Rombach et al., 2022) and Natural Language Processing (NLP) (Li et al., 2022; Gong et al.,
040
      2022). It defines a sequence of Gaussian distributions (i.e., Markov chain) instead of a single one in
041
      VAEs, granting it powerful fitting capabilities (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Vahdat & Kautz, 2020).
042   Moreover, it addresses the training instability in adversarial learning in GANs (Salimans et al., 2016),
043   making it easier to converge. Diffusion model corrupts inputs with random noises iteratively in the
044   forward process. Under the guidance of some conditions, it can learn the distribution more deeply by
045   removing noise and reconstructing the input. Thanks to its strong ability to fit complex distributions
046   and to generate diverse outputs, it has been achieved the state-of-the-art performance in sequential
047   recommendation (Wang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024).
048   Despite their advancements, there remain some problems for existing diffusion recommenders.
049   Diffusion model was initially designed for generative tasks, allowing the model to create content
050   randomly to some degree as long as it satisfies the provided conditions. In contrast, recommendation
051   tasks require precise retrieval of suitable items that a user is likely to click in the future (Lin et al.,
052   2023), which requires a thorough understanding of the semantics of each item. This highlights the
053   need to effectively integrate item semantics at each diffusion step to accurately guide the generation
      process. Unfortunately, existing diffusion recommenders rely solely on user preferences as conditions

055   these methods introduce noise into user sequences and then pass them to Transformers or MLPs for

081   To address the problems mentioned above, we propose a novel Semantic-aware Diffusion model for
082   sequential Recommendation named SDR EC. The model is structured around an Encoder-Decoder
083
      architecture (refer to (b) in Figure 1). The encoder receives clean user sequences and explicitly
      capture the semantic relationships between items by contrastive learning. It also discerns user
084
      preferences from historical interactions, generating a conditional signal that effectively encodes
085
      user interests. Guided by this conditional signal, the decoder iteratively recovers the next item
086   distribution from a noisy one. Before passing the noisy item distribution to the denosing network, we
087   introduced a Semantic Fusion Layer that leverages the semantic embedding table from the
088   encoder to transfer semantic information of items into the input distribution. Inspired by the attention
089   mechanism, the embedding table is weighted by the noisy input distribution, enriching the semantic
090   context while preserving the inherent randomness in diffusion model. Therefore, the reverse process
091   of diffusion model can refer to the rich semantics embedded in item embeddings to accurately guide
092   the generation. To sum up, the contributions of this work are as follows:
093   ‚ÄĘ We propose a novel diffusion recommender SDR EC, which can effectively utilize the item seman-
094     tics through the Semantic Fusion Layer, where the noisy input distribution is enriched by
095     the semantic information from the item embedding table, improving the accuracy of the reverse
096     process in the diffusion model.
097   ‚ÄĘ SDR EC adopts an Encoder-Decoder architecture. Equipped with well-designed contrastive and
098     generative loss, it can efficiently learn the item semantics and model user preferences simultane-
099     ously.
100   ‚ÄĘ We conduct extensive experiments to demonstrate impressive improvements over the baselines.
101     Meanwhile, our model is more efficient than baselines, which is favorable for online serving.

106
107   Given a user‚Äôs historical interacted item sequence arranged in chronological order i1 , i2 , ¬∑ ¬∑ ¬∑ , im ,
      sequential recommendation aims to capture the user preferences from that and forecasts the next

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      item i‚čÜ that the user is likely to engage with in the future. The success of this task depends on a
109   deep understanding of item semantics and accurate modeling of user interests. Due to the sequence
110   format of the user‚Äôs historical interactions and the discriminative nature of this task (i.e., distinguish
111   between items that users are interested in or not), combing sequential models with contrastive learning
112   to capture the semantics of items and mine user interests becomes a straightforward idea. Such
113   methods include Convolutions Neural Networks (CNN) (Tang & Wang, 2018; Yuan et al., 2019)
114   and Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN) (Hidasi & Karatzoglou, 2018; Hidasi et al., 2015). Recent
115   advances with Transformer-based methods (Kang & McAuley, 2018; Sun et al., 2019) have pushed
116   their performance even further.
117   However, in practical scenarios, users‚Äô interests are dynamic and evolving over time (Sachdeva et al.,
118   2019; Li et al., 2023). To capture such diversity and uncertainty of user behaviors, generative models
119   have been introduced for sequential recommendation, such as VAE-based (Sachdeva et al., 2019; Xie
120   et al., 2021) and GAN-based (Bharadhwaj et al., 2018; Ren et al., 2020) methods. These kinds of
121   approaches can also stimulate new interests for users and discover more business opportunities. How-
122   ever, these models suffer from intrinsic limitations such as the instability of GANs (Salimans et al.,
123
      2016) and the limited representation capacity of VAEs (Vahdat & Kautz, 2020). Such deficiencies
      hinder the deep modeling of complex user behaviors and item semantics.

      2.3   D IFFUSION R ECOMMENDERS
156
157   With the merits of diffusion model‚Äôs tractability and strong representation capability (Sohl-Dickstein
158   et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020), recent studies have explored integrating diffusion models into sequential
159   recommendation and achieved state-of-the-art performance (Wang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Yang
160   et al., 2024). For example, DiffRec (Wang et al., 2023) modifies the noise scale in diffusion model
161   to ensure personalized recommendations, DiffuRec (Wang et al., 2023) injects uncertainty into
      item representations and reconstruct them by diffusion model in order to capture users‚Äô multi-level

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      interests, DCDR (Lin et al., 2023) proposes to use step-wise discrete operations to add noise during
163   the diffusion process, RecFusion (BeŐĀneŐĀdict et al., 2023) adopts a binomial Markov diffusion process
164   to fit the discrete recommendation datasets, DreamRec (Yang et al., 2024) proposes to generate the
165   oracle item via diffusion model without any discriminative information.
166
      Despite their success, these methods have not effectively leveraged item semantics during the diffusion
167
      process to generate high-quality recommendations. Originally, diffusion models were designed for
168
      generative tasks, allowing for some randomness in content creation as long as the provided conditions
169   were met. However, recommendation tasks require the precise retrieval of items that are likely to
170   engage the user (Lin et al., 2023), which necessitates a deep and comprehensive understanding of
171   item semantics to guide generation accurately. Unfortunately, current diffusion recommenders rely
172   solely on user historical sequences as conditions, lacking a global awareness of item semantics.
173   Furthermore, the noise introduced to the user sequences impairs the model‚Äôs ability to accurately
174   capture the semantic relationships between items, further reduces the quality of recommendations.
175   To illustrate how item semantics affect the recommendation quality, we extract data from Movielens-
176   1M (Harper & Konstan, 2015) as an example. Since category is an inherent attribute of each movie,
177   we can use categories to express the semantics of movies. We count the number of each category that
178   appears together with Drama movies (Category 6). As Figure 2(a) shows, Comedy, Romance, Drama,
179   Action, and Thriller categories (Category 2, 5, 6, 7, and 9) often appear together, suggesting that
180   Drama movie contains multiple semantics. Figure 2(b) shows the category counts of movies clicked
181   by a user and (c) shows in a click timeline view. We can see that this user has a strong interest in
182   Comedy, Drama, Action, and Thriller (Category 2, 6, 7, and 9), which aligns the semantic correlations
183
      confirmed from Figure 2(a). He also occasionally explores other categories like Adventure and
      Romance (Category 3 and 5), indicating his preferences are dynamic and diverse.

456   Ablation Study To verify the effectiveness of each design choice of SDR EC, we perform four
457   ablation experiments, shown in Table 3. The removal of the contrastive loss (w/o discriminative
458   learning) leads to a significant decline in results. This is mainly due to the lack of constraints
459   on item representation learning results in inaccurate item semantics, and thus the direction of
460   the denoising process becomes blurred. After replacing the Semantic Fusion Layer to a
461   linear layer which simply reduces the dimension from N to D (w/o Semantic Fusion Layer), the
462   performance drops substantially due to the lack of a comprehensive awareness of item semantics

      in enhancing recommendation performance. Besides, the absence of unconditional training and

465
      in recommendation performance. However, for Beauty dataset, whether unconditional training is
466
      introduced has little impact on performance. This is because we trained relatively fewer steps on this

527   5            C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
528
529   In this paper, we propose SDR EC, a semantic-aware diffusion model for sequential recommendation
530   which can efficiently leverage the item semantics during the diffusion process. Inspired by the
531   attention mechanism, we designed the Semantic Fusion Layer. In this layer, the embedding
532   table is weighted by the noisy input distribution, allowing the reverse denoising process aware
533   of the item semantics comprehensively. Combined with contrastive learning, which constraints
534   the embedding table to learn discriminant information, SDR EC will better extract item semantics
535   contained in the embeddings. Experiments demonstrate promise gain compared with existing methods.
536
      Furthermore, as SDR EC shows efficient inference speed, it is friendly to online services.
537   However, there is still a limitation for SDR EC. The current model structure is based on a fixed
538   candidate set, which is not suitable for handling new items in real recommendation scenarios. We
539   believe that advanced methods for cold start scenarios will mitigate this problem, which also provides
      new research opportunities for future work.
