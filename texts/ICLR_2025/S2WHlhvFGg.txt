011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Predicting drug-target interactions (DTIs) is essential for advancing drug discov-
014            ery. This paper presents a unified mathematical framework (MoleProLink) for
015            unsupervised domain adaptation in drug-target interaction (DTI) prediction, in-
016            tegrating measure theory, functional analysis, information geometry, and opti-
017            mal transport theory. We introduce the novel concept of DTI-Wasserstein dis-
018            tance, incorporating both structural and chemical similarities of drugs and tar-
019            gets, and establish a refined bound on the difference between source and tar-
020
               get risks. Our information-geometric perspective reveals the intrinsic structure
               of the DTI model space, characterizing optimal adaptation paths as geodesics
021
               on a statistical manifold equipped with the Fisher-Rao metric. We develop a
022
               spectral decomposition of the DTI-DA transfer operator, providing insights into
023            the modes of information transfer between domains. This leads to the intro-
024            duction of DTI-spectral embedding and DTI-spectral mutual information, al-
025            lowing for a more nuanced understanding of the adaptation process. Theoret-
026            ical contributions include refined bounds on DTI-DA performance, incorporat-
027            ing task-specific considerations and spectral properties of the feature space. We
028            prove the existence of an optimal transport map for DTI-DA and derive a novel
029            information-theoretic lower bound using DTI-mutual information. Empirical eval-
030            uations demonstrate the superiority of our approach over existing methods across
031
               multiple benchmark datasets, showcasing its ability to effectively leverage data
               from diverse sources for improved DTI prediction. Our anonymous gitHub link:
032
               https://anonymous.4open.science/r/MoleProLink-EF30

      This paper presents a unified mathematical framework for unsupervised domain adaptation Fang

049   cepts from measure theoryBottazzi (2023), functional analysisWillem (2023), information geom-

051   the fundamental challenge of transferring knowledge from a source domain with abundant labeled
052   data to a target domain where labeled data is scarce or unavailable, a scenario common in drug dis-
053   covery Sadybekov & Katritch (2023) where new chemical entities or previously unexplored target

      Central to our framework is the novel concept of DTI-Wasserstein distance, which extends the clas-
055   sical Wasserstein metricHosseini-Nodeh et al. (2023) to incorporate both structural and chemical
056   similarities of drugs and targets. This innovation allows for a more nuanced quantification of the
057   discrepancy between source and target domains in the context of DTI prediction. Building upon this,
058   we establish a refined bound on the difference between source and target risks, providing theoretical
059   guarantees for domain adaptation performance.
060
      Our approach leverages the power of information geometry to reveal the intrinsic structure of the
061   DTI model space. By equipping the statistical manifold of DTI models with the Fisher-Rao metric,
062   we characterize optimal adaptation paths as geodesics on this manifold. This geometric perspec-
063   tive offers profound insights into the nature of domain adaptation in DTI prediction and guides the
064   development of more effective adaptation strategies.
065
      A key contribution of our work is the spectral decomposition of the DTI-DA transfer operator,
066
      which provides a detailed understanding of the modes of information transfer between domains.
067   This leads to the introduction of DTI-spectral embedding and DTI-spectral mutual information,
068   concepts that allow for a more granular analysis of the adaptation process. Our unified variational
069   formulation connects geometric, transport-theoretic, and information-theoretic perspectives on DTI-
070   DA, culminating in an equivalence theorem between the variational and geometric formulations.
071
      The theoretical foundations laid in this paper have significant practical implications. We derive
072
      refined bounds on DTI-DA performance that incorporate task-specific considerations and spectral
073   properties of the feature space. These bounds not only provide performance guarantees but also
074   offer insights into the design of more effective domain adaptation algorithms for DTI prediction.
075
076
      Furthermore, we prove the existence of an optimal transport map for DTI-DA under suitable reg-
      ularity conditions, a result that underpins the theoretical validity of our transport-based adaptation
077
      approach. We also derive a novel information-theoretic lower bound using DTI-mutual information,
078
      which sheds light on the fundamental limits of domain adaptation in this context.
079
080   Our work builds upon and significantly extends previous efforts in domain adaptation for DTI pre-
081   diction. While earlier approaches often relied on shallow transfer learning techniques or focused
082
      solely on feature-level adaptation, our framework provides a comprehensive treatment that consid-
      ers the deep structure of the DTI prediction problem. We address limitations of existing methods,
083
      such as their inability to capture the complex interplay between chemical structure and biological
084
      function, or their failure to account for the geometry of the DTI model space.
085
086   Empirical evaluations demonstrate the superiority of our approach over existing methods across mul-
087   tiple benchmark datasets, showcasing its ability to effectively leverage data from diverse sources for
088
      improved DTI prediction. These results underscore the practical utility of our theoretical framework
      and its potential to accelerate drug discovery processes.
089
090   In summary, this paper presents a rigorous mathematical framework for unsupervised domain adap-
091   tation in DTI prediction, offering both theoretical insights and practical advancements. By bridging
092   the gap between abstract mathematical concepts and the concrete challenges of drug discovery, our
093   work lays the foundation for a new generation of domain adaptation algorithms in pharmaceuti-
094
      cal research. The implications of this research extend beyond DTI prediction, potentially influ-
      encing fields such as protein structure prediction, molecular property estimation, and personalized
095
      medicine, where domain adaptation plays a crucial role in leveraging diverse datasets for scientific

      discovery.

114   2.2   F RAMEWORK OVERVIEW
115
116   Our framework begins with a preprocessing module, where we represent drugs and proteins us-
117   ing Residual2vec and molecular graphs, respectively. Residual2vec is an adaptation of Word2vec,
118
      treating the residue sequence as a document and dividing the amino acid sequence into fixed-length
      fragments (k-mers) as words. In the drug encoder module, we incorporate a Graph Transformer to
119
      capture biologically significant information. We introduce Centrality Encoding to assess node im-
120
      portance within the molecular graph and implement a novel Spatial Encoding to learn the structural
121   relationships between atomic nodes. This module ultimately employs multi-head attention to de-
122   rive hidden embedding representations for the entire molecular graph. The protein encoder module
123   utilizes a standard Transformer architecture, comprising self-attention mechanisms, interactive at-
124   tention layers, and fully connected networks. In the final module, tailored to the binary classification
125   task, we integrate a multi-head attention mechanism with a single linear layer to learn embeddings
126   that represent interactions, thereby determining whether a protein interacts with a target.

      4   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
521
522
      This paper presents a comprehensive and unified mathematical framework for unsupervised domain
523   adaptation in drug-target interaction (DTI) prediction, integrating advanced concepts from measure
524   theory, functional analysis, information geometry, and optimal transport theory. Our work signifi-
525   cantly advances the field of DTI prediction by addressing the critical challenge of distribution shift
526   between different experimental settings, drug classes, or target families. The cornerstone of our
527   framework is the novel DTI-Wasserstein distance, which extends the classical Wasserstein metric to
528   incorporate both structural and chemical similarities of drugs and targets. This innovation allows for
529   a more nuanced quantification of domain discrepancies in DTI prediction, leading to more effective
530   adaptation strategies. The refined bound on the difference between source and target risks, derived
531
      from this distance, provides theoretical guarantees for domain adaptation performance and offers
      insights into the fundamental limits of knowledge transfer in DTI prediction. Our information-
532
      geometric perspective, which equips the statistical manifold of DTI models with the Fisher-Rao
533
      metric, reveals the intrinsic structure of the DTI model space. By characterizing optimal adaptation
534   paths as geodesics on this manifold, we provide a deep geometric understanding of the domain adap-
535   tation process in DTI prediction. This insight not only enhances our theoretical understanding but
536   also guides the development of more effective adaptation algorithms. Empirical evaluations across
537   multiple benchmark datasets demonstrate the superiority of our approach over existing methods,
538   showcasing its ability to effectively leverage data from diverse sources for improved DTI predic-
539   tion. These results underscore the practical utility of our theoretical framework and its potential to
      accelerate drug discovery processes.
