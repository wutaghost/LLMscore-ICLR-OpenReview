009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Contrastive language-image pre-training (CLIP) has revolutionized computer vision
012            by integrating natural language understanding with image analysis, enabling zero-
013            shot classification without prior training on specific classes. However, recent efforts
014            to improve the performance of frozen CLIP models through prompt tuning and
015            adapter mechanisms have introduced additional system complexity and training
016            requirements, thus undermining CLIP’s inherent efficiency in zero-shot knowledge
017            transfer. In this paper, we propose to address two common challenges in zero-
018
               shot classification using CLIP: 1) the misalignment between textual and image
               embeddings, and 2) the long-tailed distribution of CLIP’s training dataset. Our
019
               approach, CLIP-Enhance, is motivated by a re-interpretation of CLIP zero-shot
020
               classification as a clustering problem on a hypersphere using a von Mises-Fisher
021            mixture model. Inspired by the DINO self-supervised learning framework, we
022            optimize this mixture model to simultaneously improve the alignment of textual
023            and image embeddings as well as represent data distribution disparities between
024            training and evaluation datasets. Empirically, we show that jointly optimizing for
025            both embedding alignment and concentration via self-supervised learning improves
026            CLIP zero-shot classification significantly across multiple benchmark datasets. We
027            also show empirically how CLIP-Enhance mitigates problems (1) and (2), as well
028            as its robustness to limited data through a series of additional experiments.

      Advancements in neural network architectures (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020; He et al., 2015; Krizhevsky
      et al., 2012) and the availability of large scale datasets (e.g. Imagenet (Deng et al., 2009b), COCO (Lin
034
      et al., 2014), SA-1B (Kirillov et al., 2023), Laion (Schuhmann et al., 2022)) have produced extraor-
035
      dinary results on supervised vision understanding tasks with closed-set assumptions (Carion et al.,
036   2020; Touvron et al., 2021; Kirillov et al., 2023; Girshick, 2015). Despite their success in these
037   scenarios, such techniques are often less effective when encountering new, unseen classes, as they
038   rely on annotated data and often lack the ability to attain general visual representations. Inspired by
039   humans’ ability to infer novel visual categories based on text descriptions, recent research focused
040   on multi-modal representation learning has established new possibilities for zero-shot, multi-modal
041   classification. In this context, zero-shot classification consists of leveraging an available multi-modal
042   feature representation to perform closed-set classification on an unlabelled dataset for which textual
043   descriptions of the classes is available.
044   In particular, vision-language pre-training frameworks, such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and
045   subsequent similar work (e.g. ALIGN (Jia et al., 2021), BLIP (Li et al., 2022b), Florence (Yuan et al.,
046   2021)), have established themselves as methods for supervised visual representation learning. These
047   methods encode both image and text data to the same embedding space under the assumption that
048   pre-training on large-scale noisy image-caption (text) pairs allows models to learn diverse visual
049   concepts that are easily transferred to downstream tasks. For example, for zero-shot classification,
050
      embeddings generated from text describing each novel class can then be compared to embeddings
      generated from the images that are being classified.
051
052   While models such as CLIP represent a significant advancement in multi-modal representation
053   learning, using CLIP embeddings directly for zero-shot classification performs remarkably poorly
      on many simple datasets such as MNIST (see Table 1). Moreover, many methods designed to use

099   2    R ELATED W ORK
100
101
      Supervised pre-training on large-scale image classification tasks (e.g. ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009b))
102   and transferring to downstream tasks has been widely adopted (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014;
103   Girshick et al., 2014; He et al., 2022; Devlin et al., 2018; Dosovitskiy et al., 2020). However,
104   constructing datasets at scale is challenging due to collection and labeling costs. Recently, vision-
105   language pre-training (CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), ALIGN (Jia et al., 2021), BLIP (Li et al., 2022b),
106   Florence (Yuan et al., 2021)) has emerged as a promising alternative for visual representation learning.
107   Because the learned representations from CLIP and other models are general, additional work is often
      required to achieve state-of-the-art performance on specific downstream tasks. These efforts largely


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      fall into one of three categories depending on the compute and data resources available: fine-tuning
109   models, few-shot learning, and zero-shot learning. Fine-tuning CLIP or similar models uses the
110   general representations learned initially to jump-start learning patterns for a specific application
111   (Wortsman et al., 2022; Han et al., 2024; Nam et al., 2024; Mao et al., 2024; Qiu et al., 2021).
112   However, although fine-tuning is much more affordable than re-training large models entirely from
113   scratch, it still requires substantial data and compute resources.
114
      In an effort to more fruitfully exploit CLIP’s low-cost transferability, some methods have attempted
115   to leverage few-shot learning to improve CLIP’s performance on specific datasets. These methods
116   typically either focus on prompt tuning, where text inputs are learned or modified to produce text
117   embeddings that better align with the corresponding image embeddings, or adapter methods, which
118   learn a small adapter to modify the embeddings. For example, CoOp (Zhou et al., 2022d) proposes
119   learning prompts for textual inputs to optimize classification in a few-shot setup, and CoCoOp (Zhou
120   et al., 2022a) extends CoOp by adding context to prompt learning.
121   While CoOp and CoCoOp address the problem in prompt token space, a second class of approaches
122   directly adapts the visual-text embedding space, allowing more fine-grained adjustments than prompt-
123   based methods resulting in superior performance. CLIP-Adapter (Gao et al., 2024) appends an
124   adapter module to produce adapted multi-modal features for both text and image modalities which
125   are better aligned, and Tip-Adapter (Zhang et al., 2021) greatly reduces training cost by constructing
126   a key-value cache model from the given set of labelled images in the few-shot setup. PromptKD (Li
127   et al., 2024) also aims to learn prompts but additionally learns a non-linear image embedding projector
128   to improve alignment by minimizing KL-divergence between student and teacher networks. However,
129
      the teacher model is trained initially in a supervised manner on few-shot, labelled data. Overall, these
      adaptation and prompt tuning methods often involve additional learnable parameters and still require
130
      extra training data, which undermine the core principle of CLIP’s efficient zero-shot recognition.
131
132   In zero-shot learning, there have been few published approaches to improve CLIP’s baseline perfor-
133   mance. CALIP (Guo et al., 2023) proposes a parameter-free attention module, which guides visual
134   and textual representations to interact with each other and explore cross-modal informative features
135
      via attention. CALIP blends image features with textual-aware signals and the textual features
      with visual-guided signal for better adaptive zero-shot classification. Although they both improve
136
      CLIP zero-shot, CLIP-Enhance and CALIP are not mutually exclusive and, in theory, may be used
137
      simultaneously. It is still an open question as to whether or not improvements in performance due to
138   CALIP and CLIP-Enhance arise from the same underlying data instances.
139
140   The most directly related work to our own is TPT (Shu et al., 2022), however there are significant
141
      differences. First, TPT optimizes prompt embeddings by minimizing the entropy among predictions
      on augmented views of each individual test sample, whereas we optimize for the entire test dataset.
142
      This allows our method to leverage additional knowledge that TPT cannot fully exploit. Second, TPT
143
      relies on entropy minimization, while we frame the problem as knowledge distillation, which has
144   been demonstrated to be more effective (Caron et al., 2021). Furthermore, we formulate the clustering
145   task using von Mises-Fisher distributions, which accounts for the intra-class spread of embeddings,
146   something other methods, including TPT, cannot do. Overall, CLIP-Enhance offers several benefits
147   compared to other approaches in addition to higher accuracy, including the ability to adapt to the
148   whole dataset at hand by addressing the inference problem directly and greater ease of use due to not
149   having to fine-tune hyperparameters for each different dataset.

152
153   In this section, we begin by establishing CLIP zero-shot classification as a clustering problem.
154   Next, we introduce CLIP-Enhance and show how this clustering can be modeled as a von Mises-
155   Fisher mixture model. Finally, we describe our self-supervised approach to distill and align the
156   aforementioned vMF mixture model.
157
158   3.1   Z ERO SHOT CLASSIFICATION USING CLIP
159
160   In zero-shot classification, the objective is to classify a set of novel images into a set of potentially
161   unseen classes C = {0, 1, . . . , |C| − 1} without any prior training on those specific classes. CLIP
      addresses this challenge by framing the classification task as a problem of similarity measurement


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      between textual and visual feature representations, where each class descriptor δi (e.g., “Cat”,
163   “Elephant”) is represented by a text embedding vector. These vectors are obtained by encoding class
164   descriptors using CLIP’s text encoder, typically in the form of a structured prompt such as “This is
165   an image of a [δi ]”. This process produces an embedding vector wi ∈ Rd for each class i. These
166   vectors are then normalized to create text-based class reference vectors ŵi = wi/∥wi ∥ on the unit
167   hypersphere. Similarly, an image I is encoded by CLIP’s image encoder to obtain an embedding
168   x, which is then normalized to x̂ = x/∥x∥ on the unit hypersphere. The prediction for image I is
169   the class i with the largest cosine similarity si , which is given by the dot product si = ⟨ŵi , x̂⟩. We
170   denote by Wtext ∈ R|C|×d the matrix whose rows are ŵi . The class prediction for an image I is thus
171   given by arg maxi Wtext x̂.
172
173   3.2     CLIP-E NHANCE
174
      We aim to address two main problems. First, text embedding vectors and image embedding vectors
175
      for a given class often have lower than desired cosine similarity. To tackle this issue, we enhance the
176
      textual descriptors by applying knowledge distillation to the clustering process, thereby improving
177   accuracy. Second, CLIP’s training data exhibits a long-tailed distribution which leads to unequal
178   concentrations of image vectors for different semantic classes on the hypersphere. This causes cosine
179   similarity to perform worse as a discriminator for classes with extreme (high or low) concentrations.
180   Inspired by previous work on integrating SSL and vMF distributions (Govindarajan et al., 2022; Scott
181   et al., 2021; Karpukhin et al., 2024) we propose to address these two problems in CLIP zero-shot
182   classification using a von Mises-Fisher mixture model, which provides a principled and flexible
183   approach to modelling representations of various classes.
184

233   3.2.2   K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION
234
235
      While normalized CLIP representations, given by rows of Ŵtext , provide crucial prior information
      about mean directions of the vMF distribution for each class, they do not represent the optimal
236
      vectors for computing image similarity, nor do they seem to encode any useful information about
237
      concentrations of each class representations. According to our vMF mixture model interpretation,
238   the matrix W can encode both direction and concentration. To learn the optimal mixture model
239   parameters, we propose to use a knowledge distillation pipeline inspired by DINO (Caron et al., 2021).
240
241
      The teacher and student vMF mixture models are parametrized by matrices Wt and Ws , respectively,
      as in Eq. 5. For simplicity, we write Pt and Ps for PWt and PWs , respectively. We initialize all models
242
      using the same concentration κ0 since we assume no prior in class concentrations, thus, initially
243
      Ws = Wt = κ0 Ŵtext . While we are eventually learning different concentrations for each class, by
244
      initially fixing identical concentration for all classes, the student and teacher model’s predictions agree
245   with that of zero-shot CLIP (Sec. 3.1) at initialization, as noted after Eq. 5. We use the same value
246   for κ0 in all our experiments and this choice is briefly discussed in the implementation details § 4.3.
247
248
      Given an image I, we generate a set of diverse student views v ∈ V , by repeatedly copying and
      applying different augmentaiton such as random resized cropping to the image I. Similarly, we
249
      create a single teacher view It . For each student view v ∈ V , the CLIP vision encoder provides a
250
      normalized representation x̂v ; likewise, for the teacher, x̂t for It . These embedding vectors are then
251   fed to the student and teacher models, which produce outputs Ps (x̂v ) and Pt (x̂t ), respectively.
252
253   In our context, both the student and the teacher act on representations obtained from CLIP’s vision
254
      encoder, whose parameters are frozen. The student model is trained to minimize the Kullback-Leibler
      divergence between a subset of predictions on its most confident views V conf ⊆ V and the teacher’s
255
      prediction on It . More precisely, given a single image I ∈ D, student parameters Ws are updated
256
      using stochastic gradient descent with respect to the following loss function:

      5.1   I NCREASED A LIGNMENT THROUGH K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION
441
442   In this section, we make a first step towards providing empirical evidence for our claim from the
443   introduction about the anisotropic character of the distribution of image embeddings around the
444   average text embedding for a given class. Specifically, we compute a metric we call ‘alignment’,
445   which measures how image representations for each class are aligned with the corresponding reference
446   vector. More precisely, given a matrix W ∈ RC×d and a class i in a dataset D, we compute the
447   average cosine similarity between the row wi – which is our reference direction for class i – and
448   each normalized representations of images in that class. By linearity of the dot product, this is equal
449   to the cosine between the mean direction µi of image representations for class i with the row wi of
450
      W , whose corresponding angle we denote by αi . Finally the alignment of W on D is obtained by
      averaging angles αi over classes i.
451
452   In Table 2, we report for each dataset the alignment for CLIP Zero-Shot using Ŵtext and for CLIP-
453   Enhance using the matrix Wt learned from Ŵtext using Algo 1. We observe that while the alignment
454   may seem low in all cases given the high dimensionality (d = 1024) of representations, our knowledge
455   distillation method consistently improves the initial alignment of CLIP zero-shot given by embbedings
456   of text descriptions. Although, the absolute difference in degree is relatively small, this seems
457   significant given the high dimension of the representations. This therefore seems to indicate that
458   on average the image representations of each class are distributed closer to the directions learn
459
      by CLIP-Enhance than to initial representations obtained from class descriptions. This increased
      alignment explains partly the performance improvement obtained by CLIP-Enhance.

513   6   C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
514
515   This paper proposes a novel method, CLIP-Enhance, for improving CLIP zero-shot classification,
516   by reformulating it as von Mises-Fisher mixture model. CLIP-Enhance leverages CLIP’s invariant
517   representation space to optimize this von Mises-Fisher mixture model in order to mitigate inherent
518   misalignment between embedding vectors of different data modalities as well as model the non-
519
      uniformity of embedding concentrations between different classes. We show the relative efficacy of
      CLIP-Enhance compared to standard CLIP zero-shot classification as well as other state-of-the-art
520
      methods on a number of standard datasets. We also show that CLIP-Enhance provides performance
521
      gains even under constrained conditions, where only a small fraction of the test set is available or
522   training is conducted over a small number of epochs.
523
524   While providing a significant improvement over CLIP zero-shot, CLIP-Enhance does have some
525
      limitations. Perhaps the most obvious, and the most widely shared limitation amongst all CLIP
      fine-tuning, prompt engineering, adapter, or post-processing methods is that we are limited by
526
      the quality of CLIP’s original representation space. First, the relatively poor performance of our
527
      initialization using representations of class descriptions may impede our knowledge distillation step.
528   Second, the quality of CLIP image representations may limit the performance that methods like
529   CLIP-Enhance can achieve. Additionally, although we show improvements even with small amounts
530   of data, CLIP-Enhance still does significantly better when given access to more data, and it is likely
531   that this tradeoff could be further improved. We see a similar trend with epochs trained, where longer
532   training runs provide better performance. Possibly, performing knowledge distillation with a small
533   amount of data on which the initial vMF classifier shows better accuracy would lead to interesting
534   results, but this would require an efficient proxy for the uncertainty of multi-modal models like CLIP.
535   Overall, zero-shot classification in this context remains a very challenging task.
536   In future work we plan to investigate extensions to few-shot learning as well as exploring a greater
537   number of SSL algorithms which may further close the gap between zero-shot CLIP-Enhance and
538   linear probe classifiers trained on the full dataset. It may also be possible to use the output of
539   algorithms like CLIP-Enhance to evaluate or improve the CLIP’s representation space, creating a
      closed-loop system, though this possibility has yet to be seriously explored.

      A.1    S UPERVISED BASELINES :
759
760   In 5 we evaluate the performance of supervised baselines including image-center clustering and
761   a linear probe, These supervised baselines establish classification upper bounds for the particular
762   dataset, indicate the extent to which the data is easily separable, and generally give some insight
763   into the difficulty of the dataset. We establish an upper bound for classification performance using a
764   linear probe, which are widely regarded as a strong baseline for assessing the potential of pre-trained
765
      representations for a specific task, especially in scenarios with limited data available for training the
      classifier (e.g., zero-shot learning) (Tian et al., 2020).
766
767   Next, we perform classification using image mean clustering, where cluster directions are computed
768   by averaging the image embeddings belonging to each class within the dataset. These image cluster
769   directions are then used to classify the test set. the aim of this is to establish the magnitude of
770   alignment error, which refers to the discrepancy in image embeddings and textual embeddings
771
      describing the same image, do not point in the same direction. this also co-relates with lower
      alignment within text embedding vector and image embedding vectors. for example in MNIST where
772
      its hard to differentiate the image of digits through a textual description on a image of digit ( image
773
      of number five vs image of number four) , we see a huge performance gain when using Image mean
774   cluster center but in Datasets such as Food101 , CIFAR100 where the classes are quite different even
775   in the text domain( apple pie vs hot dog, CAT vs DOG). Finally, we also explore non-linear decision
776   boundaries, by studying the performance of K nearest neighbours, where classification is done by
777   finding the most common class among the K nearest neighbours( Top K neighbours with the highest
778   cosine similarity).

900   A.3.1    R EDEFINING A LIGNMENT
901
902   CLIP’s training objective aims at a relative alignment of captions embeddings with embeddings of
903
      their corresponding image embeddings (Radford et al., 2021). More precisely, for a random batch
      from CLIP’s training dataset, the symmetric cross entropy is minimized when 1): every caption
904
      embedding is more are aligned (larger cosine similarity) with its corresponding image embedding
905
      than with any other image embedding 2) every image embedding is more are aligned (larger cosine
906   similarity) with its corresponding caption embedding (positive pair) than with any other caption
907   embedding (negative pairs). Moreover, since “a temperature parameter which controls the range
908   of the logits in the softmax is directly optimized during training” – which would appear to take
909   approximately the value exp(τ ) = 100 [1], which is the maximal value allowed during training
910   to prevent instability (Radford et al., 2021) – a small difference between the cosine similarity of
911   a positive pair compared to negative pairs leads to cross entropy loss which is roughly zero. For
912   instance, for the training batch size (b = 32, 768), a positive cosine similarity of 0.5 = cos(60◦ ) for
913   negative cosine similarities of 0.225 ≈ cos(77◦ ) obtains a cross entropy loss (when logits are scaled
914
      by 100) which is smaller than the smallest representable number (with Pytorch, Float32).
915   Hence, CLIP’s training does not aim at aligning text embeddings and their corresponding images in the
916   sense that the angle between them is small (cosine almost 1). On the contrary, in CLIP’s multimodal
917   embedding space, the angle between a text representation and its corresponding image representation
      can be as large as 60◦ (cosine 0.5) as long as negative pairs are less aligned, namely with an angle


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      larger than 77◦ . Therefore, we conclude that the quality of CLIP’s multimodal embeddings really
919   resides in their RELATIVE alignment, not in the actual alignment of positive pairs, an observation
920   that is also supported by (Liang et al., 2022).
921
922
      Furthermore, we consider that this relative alignment highly depends on the training distribution.
      When leveraging these representations for a downstream classification task, we aim at a relative
923
      alignment of text-based class reference embeddings with respect to the image distribution. A specific
924
      balanced classification task like CIFAR10 is expected to differ greatly from the long tail distribution
925   of CLIP’s training dataset, resulting in a poor relative alignment. Our approach aims at adapting in
926   an unsupervised manner the initial text-based class reference embeddings to improve their relative
927   alignment on the image dataset. By endowing each class reference with a concentration parameter and
928   formulating the classification problem as a von Mises–Fisher mixture model, our approach achieves
929   this goal as reflected by the improved accuracy. 2
