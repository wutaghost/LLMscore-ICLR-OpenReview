009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The performance of Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) on downstream tasks im-
012            proves substantially when examples of visual-text relationships are incorporated
013            as context, with performance gains increasing as the number of examples and
014            the context window size grow. However, collecting high-quality training sets for
015            In-Context Learning (ICL) to retrieve multimodal examples is not trivial, particu-
016
               larly in specialized domains like healthcare, remote sensing, finance, and scientific
               research, due to the significant costs of manual labeling and strict privacy regu-
017
               lations. In this paper, we introduce Active In-Context Learning (AICL), a novel
018
               paradigm that eliminates the need for traditional training sets in multimodal ICL.
019            AICL dynamically selects and annotates a small, highly informative set of samples
020            in real-time during the query phase of LMMs. This active set evolves throughout
021            querying, with the most relevant examples being continuously retrieved from it to
022            optimize LMM performance on new data, without relying on pre-existing training
023            sets. To construct an optimal active set, we propose Spectral-based Representative
024            Sampling, which applies spectral clustering in the early query phase to select sam-
025            ples that are early, class-balanced, and representative, ensuring the active set cap-
026            tures key features of the data distribution and reduces data bias. To fully leverage
027
               the active set, we propose Similarity-enhanced TopK Prompt Construction, which
               retrieves the most relevant multimodal examples using a TopK similarity strategy
028
               and integrates the visual similarities between the multimodal examples and the
029
               query samples directly into the text prompts. By incorporating this similarity in-
030            formation, LMMs can better grasp the relationships, leading to more accurate and
031            context-aware predictions. Experimental results on 10 specialized datasets and
032            four LMMs show that our method significantly enhances LMMs’ generalization
033            performance. For example, in medical diagnosis tasks, our method, using only 10
034            annotated samples in the active set, outperforms existing ICL methods that rely on
035            2,000 annotated training samples.

132   2.1   I N - CONTEXT L EARNING
133
      In-Context Learning (ICL) has emerged as a powerful strategy in natural language processing (Lu
134
      et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2024; Akyürek et al., 2024; Wies et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2022), driven by
135   advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) such as GPT-3 (Mann et al., 2020). For instance,
136   ICL can use a set of review-sentiment examples to predict the sentiment of new reviews (Dong
137   et al., 2022). ICL offers a more feasible solution compared to fine-tuning, which requires modify-
138   ing model parameters—a process often impractical due to intellectual property restrictions or the
139   computational expense of retraining large models. By contrast, ICL requires only examples from
140   a relevant task, allowing LLMs to make predictions without altering the underlying model. This
141   flexibility is particularly useful in specialized domains where data privacy and regulatory concerns
142   prevent the free exchange of annotated datasets (Dong et al., 2022; Min et al., 2022).
143   Multimodal ICL has gained significant attention in the multimodal domain for its ability to enhance
144   the generalization of Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) in specialized fields such as healthcare and
145   visual interpretation (Zhou et al., 2024; Ferber et al., 2024). One key strength of multimodal ICL is
146   its ability to retrieve demonstration examples from accessible training sets to improve performance.
147   The pioneering Flamingo study (Alayrac et al., 2022) highlighted the crucial role of selecting high-
148   quality examples for tasks like image generation and interpretation (Bar et al., 2022; Wang et al.,
149
      2023; Liu et al., 2021). However, collecting high-quality training sets remains a challenge. To ad-
      dress this, we introduce a paradigm for constructing an optimal active set during test time, enabling
150
      models to handle real-time queries without depending on pre-existing training sets.
151
152
153   2.2   ACTIVE L EARNING
154
      Active Learning (AL) seeks to improve model performance by selectively labeling a small yet highly
155
      informative subset of samples (Michael, 2006; Settles, 2009). Traditional AL methods typically rely
156   on metrics derived from model outputs, such as (1) Uncertainty (Siddiqui et al., 2020; Holub et al.,
157   2008), including entropy; (2) Diversity (Brinker, 2003; Agarwal et al., 2020; Sener & Savarese,
158   2018), such as CoreSet; and (3) combined uncertainty and diversity strategies (Ash et al., 2019;
159   Yang et al., 2015), such as BADGE. This paper seeks to enhance the generalization of LMMs at test
160   time. Our approach differs in two key aspects: first, LMMs in our lightweight ICL framework often
161   act as black-box models, making it difficult to extract conventional uncertainty or diversity metrics.
      As a result, we propose spectral-based representative sampling to construct an optimal active set.


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Second, due to the complexity and size of LMMs, fine-tuning is often impractical, which is why
163   we focus on developing the similarity-enhanced TopK prompt construction for more effective ICL
164   example utilization, further boosting model performance without modifying underlying parameters.

169   3.1   ACTIVE I N -C ONTEXT L EARNING
170
      Active In-Context Learning (AICL) aims to enhance the generalization of LMMs by constructing
171
      an ‘active set’ at test time, removing the need for a pre-existing training set. Let’s start by clarifying
172   the distinction between multimodal in-context learning and AICL.
173
174   Multimodal In-context Learning needs a pre-existing multimodal training set, Ds =
175
      {(xn , yn )}N
                  n=1 , comprising N pairs (e.g., an image and its label). Given a query image xq from
      a test set Dq , a text question xt (which could ask if the image belongs to a specific class or pose a
176
      free-form question about it) and a set of candidate answers Y = {y1 , y2 , ...ym } (where Y could be
177
      class labels or a set of free-text phrases), multimodal in-context learning (ICL) is formulated as:
178
179                                        yq = f (P, xq , xt ) | yq ∈ Y ,                                  (1)
180
      here, P = {(x1 , y1 ), ..., (xk , yk ) ∈ Ds } denotes the multimodal ICL examples, chosen as K samples
181
      from the training set, and K ≪ N .
182
183   Active In-Context Learning (AICL) differs from multimodal ICL by building an ‘active set’ Dl
184   from streaming data batches, with the batch size represented as bz . Instead of relying on a pre-
185
      existing training set, AICL selects ICL examples from this dynamically constructed active set. The
      expert labeling budget is defined as B, where B ≪ |Dq | and B ≪ |Ds |, reflecting the high cost of
186
      expert annotation. Formally, when streaming data batch Dqi arrives in the i-th batch and |Dl | < B,
187
      indicating available annotation resources, AICL needs to efficiently select the most valuable active
188
      samples from Dqi for expert annotation. These samples, once annotated, are incorporated into the
189
      active set Dl . Subsequently, for a given query image xiq within Dqi , accompanied by a text question
190
      xt , and a set of candidate answers Y , AICL is formulated as:
191
192                                        yq = f (P a , xiq , xt ) | yq ∈ Y ,                              (2)
193
      here, P a = {(x1 , y1 ), ..., (xk , yk ) ∈ Dl } denotes the multimodal ICL examples, chosen as K sam-
194
      ples from our active set.
195
196   In practice, once active samples are selected from Dqi , and added to the active set, each query sample
197   in Dqi is processed using this set to select ICL examples for prediction. Only after the entire batch
198   is completed, the next batch Dqi+1 is introduced. If the annotation budget has not been exhausted,
199   active sample selection continues for Dqi+1 , and its samples are sequentially predicted; if the budget
200   is depleted, predictions proceed directly using the existing active set. This cycle repeats until all
201   batches are processed.
202
      Challenges. According to the definition, AICL needs to address two critical factors that enhance the
203
      generalization of LMMs. (1) Active selection: Efficiently identifying informative active samples
204   from streaming data batches and constructing the active set is crucial. Our experiments (Fig. 4(a))
205   show that selecting ICL samples using the TopK strategy from different active sets leads to varying
206   performance gains, with performance differences between sets reaching up to 16.9% in downstream
207   tasks. To enhance stability and performance, AICL requires effective sampling strategies to select
208   optimal active samples. (2) ICL example utilization: Selecting and utilizing relevant ICL examples
209   from the active set is equally important. Our experiments (Fig. 4(b)) demonstrate that random ICL
210   example selections yield marginal benefits, with performance increasing only about 2% on datasets
211   like EuroSAT and COVID-CT after annotating 10% of test samples. AICL must carefully select and
212
      leverage relevant ICL examples to significantly improve performance in the ICL process.
213   To tackle these challenges, we identify three key characteristics for optimal active samples: early
214   selection, class balance, and representativeness. We introduce a Spectral-based Representative Sam-
215   pling module to identify these samples and a Similarity-enhanced TopK Prompt Construction mod-
      ule to effectively select and utilize relevant ICL examples from the active set.

      3.2     T HREE CHARACTERISTICS OF OPTIMAL ACTIVE SAMPLES
252
253   Early Selection. As shown in Fig. 5 (a), selecting active samples early significantly improves per-
254   formance. Active samples chosen from the first batch outperformed those selected from the third
255   batch by 22.8%, 23.2%, and 20.5% for different domains (hosp0, hosp1, and hosp2), respectively.
256   This demonstrates the importance of beginning sample annotation as soon as the initial batch of data
257   is received to develop a well-prepared active set (Dl ). Delaying this step leads to poorer perfor-
258   mance, as the early querying samples lack sufficient ICL examples for effective reference.
259   Class Balance. Fig. 5 (b) demonstrates that a balanced class distribution in the active set (Dl )
260   greatly improves performance. In binary classification, the ‘5:5’ ratio achieved a performance gain
261   of 38.6%, 32.6%, and 35.5% over the extreme ‘10:0’ ratio in different domains, respectively. This
262   demonstrates the necessity of maintaining a balanced class distribution to prevent bias in the selec-
263   tion of ICL examples. When the active set is imbalanced, the model struggles to correctly identify
264   underrepresented classes due to the absence of relevant ICL examples.
265   Representativeness. As shown in Fig. 5 (c), selecting samples closer to their class centroids sig-
266   nificantly improves performance. Samples closer to the centroids outperformed those farther away
267   by 26.7%, 24.2%, and 14.0% for different domains, respectively. This highlights the importance of
268   selecting representative samples to maintain the quality of the active set. Centroid-proximate sam-
269   ples provide clearer class separation and more accurate information, enhancing the model’s ability
      to interpret downstream queries and improving overall performance in LMMs.

468   bution vector of class quantities within the selected active samples, indicating class balance. A

470   class balance. CS quantifies representativeness as CS = Avg(s), where s measures the similarity
471   of class-specific samples to their class centers, with higher averages suggesting closer proximity to
472   class centers and greater representativeness, exemplified by s = [1.0, 1.0] in binary classifications
473   using cosine similarity. For analytical clarity, we compared the combined metric: Values = CE×CS

      5   C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
528
529   To address the challenge of acquiring high-quality training sets for In-context Learning, we intro-
530   duced a novel paradigm, Active In-context Learning (AICL). AICL dynamically selects and la-
531
      bels informative samples to form an active set, thus eliminating the need for traditional training
      sets. We utilized Spectral-based Representative Sampling to construct this optimal active set, em-
532
      ploying spectral clustering to ensure the samples are early, class-balanced, and representative. Our
533
      Similarity-enhanced TopK Prompt Construction module further refines the similarity relations be-
534   tween selected samples and query inputs in both image and text modalities, enhancing the model’s
535   understanding. This paradigm not only surpasses standard zero-shot performance with minimal
536   annotation but also outperforms existing active learning baselines. Experimental results across mul-
537   tiple datasets and models confirm our method’s enhanced generalization capabilities. This paper
538   focuses on a relatively static scenario where the downstream data distribution remains unchanged
539   over time. Future work will explore dynamic scenarios where the distribution evolves, enhancing
      the LMMs’ ability to understand and adapt to new and changing tasks.

           A.3   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
843
844        In our experimental setup, we employ the CLIP (vit-large-patch14-224-clip-laion2b) configuration
845        to extract features from each dataset sample, which are subsequently used for clustering and the
846        TopKPR process. In the VQA task, we leverage CLIP’s text encoder to extract textual features from
847        the ‘question’ field and combine these with image features to select the top-K ICL samples. We
848        adjust the budget B for active samples based on dataset size: for datasets containing fewer than
849        200 samples, the budget is set at 10; for those with more than 200 samples, it is increased to 20,
850
           ensuring that active samples represent approximately 10% of the total streaming query samples.
           Correspondingly, the batch size is established at 32 for smaller datasets and increased to 64 for
851
           larger datasets. The TopKPR strategy, as outlined in Eq. 7, is implemented to select ICL example
852
           samples with the parameter K set to 1, motivated by the relatively low token count in the input
853        examples which enhances computational efficiency and reduces costs. We explore a static scenario
854        with consistent query distributions, using the initial batch as a representative sample to approximate
855        the entire dataset. Thus, we set v to 1 for adequate ICL referencing.

897   B.1    ICL EXAMPLES WITH VARIOUS S HOTS .
898
899   To examine the impact of in-context learning (ICL) examples within the SimTopKPC framework, we
900
      conducted an empirical study using the first three domains of the Camelyon17 dataset, adjusting the
      number k in TopKPR from 1 to 5 per domain. Fig. 11(a) shows that optimal performance is attained
901
      at k = 1. Increasing k reduces method efficacy due to the limited availability of actively labeled
902
      examples. An excessive number of in-context samples often leads to class mismatches between
903   selected and query samples, where similar text prompts from SimTopKPC may be detrimental. In
904   our setup, employing one in-context sample (k = 1) (Fig. 11(a)) optimally reduces the computational
905   load on large multimodal models by minimizing extensive image token processing, thus conserving
906   computational resources and prediction time.
907
908
      B.2    H YPERPARAMETER v IN ACTIVE SAMPLE SELECTION .
909
910
      In all experiments, we use the entire labeling budget (B) in the first batch Dq1 , with no updates to
911
      the active set in subsequent batches. This design is based on two key considerations: (i) In this
912   paper, we focus on a static scenario, where the distribution of downstream query samples remains
913   unchanged. As the first batch is typically considered representative of the overall dataset, selecting
914   active samples from this batch approximates the full data stream distribution, providing sufficient
915   ICL references. (ii) Experiments (Fig. 11(b)) show that allocating the full labeling budget to the
916   first batch improves performance by 3% on average compared to dynamic updates, confirming that
917   early selection provides adequate ICL support for the entire data stream. Delaying labeling reduces
      the model’s ability to understand earlier batches due to the lack of sufficient ICL examples.

921   We perform a comparative analysis of the application of active samples for fine-tuning small models
922   versus their utilization in in-context learning to augment the generalization capabilities of large
923   models. As depicted in Fig. 11(c), on the Camelyon17 dataset, the Gemini 1.5 Flash model, which
924   employs in-context learning, achieves a performance improvement of approximately 5% over the
925   gains observed in the ResNet50 model. This result validates the efficacy of our AICL paradigm,
926
      affirming its superiority in enhancing the generalization capabilities of large models compared to
      traditional updates of smaller models.
