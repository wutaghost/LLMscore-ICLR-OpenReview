011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            This paper introduces Network-based Active Inference (NetAIF), a novel frame-
014            work that integrates random attractor dynamics and the Free Energy Principle
015            (FEP) to improve trajectory generation and control in robotics. NetAIF optimizes
016
               the intrinsic dynamics of neural networks, enabling robots to quickly adapt to
               dynamic and complex real-world environments with minimal computational re-
017
               sources and without the need for extensive pre-training. Unlike traditional learn-
018
               ing methods that rely on large datasets and prolonged training periods, NetAIF
019            offers a more efficient alternative.
020
               In real-world scenarios, such as Photovoltaic (PV) panel inspections, NetAIF
021            demonstrates its ability to execute dynamic tasks with both high efficiency and
022            robustness. The system excels in unpredictable environments while maintaining
023            a low computational footprint. These capabilities make NetAIF a promising so-
024            lution for industrial applications, offering cost-effective, adaptive robotic systems
025            that can reduce operational expenses and enhance performance, particularly in
026            sectors like energy, where adaptability and precision are crucial.

030
031   1.1   OVERCOMING AUTOMATION C HALLENGES WITH A DVANCED L EARNING M ETHODS
032
      The World Energy Employment 2023 report by the IEA highlights a significant shift towards clean
033
      energy jobs, which now surpass fossil fuel employment, driven by a 40% rise in clean energy in-
034   vestment over the past two years. Despite economic and geopolitical challenges, the energy sector
035   has seen growth in employment, particularly in solar PV, wind, EVs, and battery manufacturing.
036   However, a shortage of skilled labor remains a key challenge, underscoring the need for targeted
037   training and policy support to develop a workforce suited for the energy transition (IEA, 2023).
038
      In response to these labor challenges, automation is playing an increasingly critical role in advancing
039
      the clean energy sector. Robotics, in particular, offers a promising solution to enhance operational
040   efficiency and safety. However, to maximize the potential of robotics in complex and dynamic en-
041   vironments, sophisticated learning methods are required. One such approach, Deep Reinforcement
042   Learning (DRL), has emerged as a leading candidate for enabling autonomous robotic systems in
043   tasks like control, manipulation, and decision-making. Yet, despite its potential, DRL faces notable
044   barriers to widespread adoption in the energy sector.
045
046   1.2   D EEP R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING (DRL)
047
048   DRL combines the decision-making power of reinforcement learning (RL) with the pattern recogni-
049   tion capabilities of deep learning (DL). This allows robots to learn and adapt through trial and error,
050
      improving performance over time. DRL is increasingly explored for enabling autonomy in control
      and manipulation tasks in real-world environments by training agents to recognize complex patterns
051
      in data and make informed decisions.
052
053   However, DRL requires large amounts of data and time for agent training, as well as expert-designed
      reward functions to guide learning. Creating these reward functions demands substantial knowledge

      and engineering resources, as they must accurately capture desired outcomes, agent actions, and
055   constraints. Poorly defined reward functions can lead to suboptimal or unsafe behavior (Sutton &
056   Barto, 2020). Thus, while powerful, DRL may not always be the most practical or cost-effective
057   approach for every application.

      1.3   AIF AS A N EXT G ENERATION L EARNING M ETHOD
060
061   Active Inference (AIF) is a groundbreaking framework in neuroscience, offering a unified approach
062   to understanding adaptive systems, including brain functions, and is gaining traction in fields like
063   machine learning and robotics (Friston et al., 2006; Parr, 2019; Millidge, 2020; Lanillos et al., 2021).
064   In robotics, AIF is reshaping control and learning by minimizing surprise rather than relying on
065   reward-based mechanisms like DRL. Unlike DRL, which requires fixed environments, AIF utilizes
066   a dynamic generative model, continuously adapting to changing surroundings through a feedback
067   loop of prediction, perception, and action. This approach addresses the exploration-exploitation
068
      dilemma more fluidly by incorporating uncertainty directly into decision-making.
069   While AIF holds significant promise for creating adaptive robotic systems, its real-world deployment
070   faces challenges due to the complexity of model design and high computational demands (Lanillos
071   et al., 2021). Nonetheless, its potential to enhance flexibility, durability, and adaptability makes it a
072   powerful alternative to traditional DRL techniques
073
074   1.4   N ETWORK - BASED ACTIVE I NFERENCE (N ETAIF)
075
076   To overcome the limitations of both DRL and traditional AIF approaches, we propose Network-
077   based Active Inference (NetAIF), a novel framework that leverages network dynamics to simplify
078
      trajectory calculations and enhance efficiency. Rooted in key AIF principles such as entropy and
      surprise minimization, NetAIF builds on the Free Energy Principle (FEP), which posits that systems
079
      self-organize by minimizing surprisal or prediction error. By harnessing the inherent dynamics of a
080
      network, NetAIF computes trajectories more efficiently than traditional AIF methods, reducing the
081   need for complex mathematical models while enabling agents to adapt to dynamic environments in
082   real-time. This streamlined approach makes NetAIF highly suitable for real-world robotic applica-
083   tions, offering significant improvements in both speed and computational cost.

      vironment through blanket states. Blanket states consist of sensory states, which gather external

158   In NESS, the system is never fully at rest but continuously adapts to changing inputs from the envi-
159   ronment, minimizing prediction errors in real time. The feedback between sensory and active states
160   ensures that the system remains stable yet flexible, adjusting its actions and beliefs to maintain op-
161   timal performance even in uncertain or complex environments. This aligns with Bayesian inference
      principles, as NetAIF constantly updates its beliefs in response to new sensory inputs and envi-

165   NetAIF also replaces traditional activation functions with a discrete weight-assigning mechanism,
166
      designed to reset node weights and maintain NESS. By leveraging the constant interaction between
      sensory and active states, NetAIF remains in a state of continuous exploration, avoiding local min-

      ima and ensuring that it adapts dynamically to new challenges. This stochastic function enhances
168
      the network’s ability to explore different states, preventing it from being trapped in local optima.

170   Additionally, NetAIF integrates learning and control, guiding motor outputs with clear task-specific
171   control laws. These laws break tasks down into sub-goals, such as aligning objects, allowing even
172
      non-experts to define behaviors without deep control theory knowledge. For instance, in a valve
      manipulation task, control instructions guide the network to minimize errors by aligning the vector
173

      ment, making the system more intuitive and effective for real-world applications. This user-friendly

177   An effective way to understand this is through an analogy: the control (vector) law acts like a road,

179
      while staying on the road, the random attractor dynamically explores within the constraints set by the
180
      control law, ensuring the robot adapts to changing conditions while maintaining the most efficient
181   trajectory. This approach allows for greater flexibility and precision in the robot’s movement.

203   PV panel inspection.

205   2.2    T HE R ANDOM ATTRACTOR
206
207   To represent the NESS behavior in NetAIF, Random Dynamical Systems (RDS) are employed,
208
      providing a framework to understand complex systems driven by stochastic processes. In particular,
      random pullback attractors (Caraballo & Han, 2016), also known as stochastic basins of attraction,
209
      describe how NetAIF’s state evolves over time in response to environmental uncertainty. Expressed
210
      as φ(t, ω, x), where t is time, ω represents randomness, and x is the state variable, these attractors
211   characterize regions in the state space where the system tends to settle. The random attractor A(ω)
212   pulls trajectories towards it, ensuring that NetAIF remains adaptive and stable within its NESS
213   framework, despite external randomness.

      This convergence process can be understood as a stochastic diffusion in parameter space, driven by
      increasing the amplitude of random fluctuations on parameters (e.g., connection weights) in regions
246
      of high free energy. As the system approaches free energy minima, these random fluctuations are
247
      attenuated, resulting in a more stable and precise arm trajectory. Such system dynamics can be
248   described by a stochastic differential equation (SDE) in the form of a Langevin equation (Karl,
249   2019):
250
                                                             √
                                        dx = −∇F (x) dt + 2Γ dW
251
252   where x represents the system’s parameters, F (x) is the free energy landscape, Γ is the diffusion
253   coefficient, and W is a Wiener process. This equation captures the interplay between the determin-
254
      istic drift towards free energy minima and the stochastic exploration of the parameter space, which
      ultimately shapes the arm’s trajectory.
255
256   It is worth noting that the optimization process in NetAIF is inherently local because free energy is
257   an extensive quantity, meaning that the system’s total free energy is the sum of the free energies of
258   its individual components. The variational free energy, which approximates the true free energy, is
259   calculated using local prediction errors. Some predictions are clamped with high precision, fixed, or
260
      strongly influenced by the desired outcomes, defining the attracting set, which represents the desired
      sensor inputs or the target state of the system. Minimizing variational free energy by reducing local
261
      prediction errors guides the network model towards the attracting set.
262
263   This local optimization process enables the system to efficiently navigate the free energy landscape
264   without requiring global computations or information propagation across the entire network. By
265   iteratively updating its local components based on prediction errors and external control laws, the
266
      system converges towards the desired states.
267   The roots of this learning scheme can be traced back to early formulations of self-organization in
268   cybernetics (Ashby, 1947) (Ashby, 1956) and are connected to stochastic thermodynamics (Ao,
269   2008) (Seifert, 2012). These connections highlight the consistency of the design principle with the
      fundamental concepts underlying the FEP. This principle drives the network model to minimize pre-

277
      PV farms, covering extensive areas spanning numerous hectares, traditionally rely on the keen eyes
278
      of professional inspectors to identify damages or issues on the panels. This method of manual in-
279   spection, while thorough, is both time-intensive and laborious given the vastness of the installations.
280   The complexity of the task is exacerbated by the dynamic nature of PV farms, which face continual
281   changes due to weather patterns, the undulating terrain, the encroachment of wildlife, and the unpre-
282   dictable intrusion of vegetation. These factors contribute to noisy data and unforeseen challenges for
283   any automated systems (such as robotic solutions) that might be employed to streamline the process.
284   Furthermore, the variability in panel aging, weather fluctuations, and diverse panel types necessitate
285   robot controllers with an increased level of adaptability and flexibility.
286   To address these challenges, we have implemented an inspection system using a robotic arm
287   equipped with sensors designed to detect PV panel defects. This system had to meticulously extend
288   to a pre-determined distance from a panel, position its sensors to be perpendicular to the panel’s
289   surface, and systematically survey the panel sections for any signs of deterioration or damage.

      5   CONCLUSIONS
519
520
      The Network-based Active Inference (NetAIF) model offers a novel, efficient approach to real-time
521
      adaptive intelligence in robotics by leveraging random attractor dynamics and the Free Energy Prin-
522   ciple (FEP) to enable robots to adapt to unpredictable environments without extensive pre-training
523   or significant computational resources. Its real-time feedback processing ensures precise control
524   and flexible adaptation, making it ideal for cost-sensitive industries like energy, where adaptability
525   and precision are critical. Unlike Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL), which requires substantial
526   training and computational power, NetAIF provides a computationally efficient, cost-effective solu-
527   tion for tasks such as inspections and maintenance. For a comparison with DRL methods, see the
528   companion paper (Anonymous, 2024).
