                                                 A BSTRACT
013
014
               Genetic diseases can be classified according to their modes of inheritance and
015
               their underlying molecular mechanisms. Autosomal dominant disorders often re-
016            sult from DNA variants that cause loss-of-function, gain-of-function, or dominant-
017            negative effects, while autosomal recessive diseases are primarily linked to loss-
018            of-function variants. In this study, we introduce a graph-of-graphs approach that
019            leverages protein-protein interaction networks and high-resolution protein struc-
020            tures to predict the mode of inheritance of diseases caused by variants in autoso-
021            mal genes, and to classify dominant-associated proteins based on their functional
022            effect. Our approach integrates graph neural networks, structural interactomics
023            and topological network features to provide proteome-wide predictions, thus of-
024
               fering a scalable method for understanding genetic disease mechanisms.
025

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Human genetic diseases result from variants that disrupt protein function through diverse molecular
029
      mechanisms, which play a critical role in determining their mode of inheritance (MOI) (Zschocke
030   et al., 2023). In autosomal dominant (AD) disorders, a single copy of a mutated gene can result in
031   disease, often through loss of function (LOF) due to haploinsufficiency (HI), where the remaining
032   wild-type allele cannot compensate for the lost function (Veitia, 2002). Dominant disorders can
033   also result from non-LOF mechanisms, such as gain of function (GOF), where the mutant protein
034   acquires a new or altered function, and the dominant-negative (DN) effect, where the mutant isoform
035   interferes with the normal function of the wild-type protein (Backwell & Marsh, 2022). In contrast,
036   autosomal recessive (AR) disorders require variants in both gene copies, predominantly involving
037   LOF mechanisms, such as missense variants that destabilize protein structure or nonsense variants
038
      leading to truncated, non-functional proteins.
039   Previous studies on MOI prediction have introduced computational tools such as DOMINO (Quin-
040   odoz et al., 2017), which utilizes linear discriminant analysis (LDA) to predict whether a protein
041   is associated with AD disorders by integrating various features such as genomic data, conserva-
042   tion, and protein interactions. MOI-Pred (Petrazzini et al., 2021), on the other hand, focuses on
043   variant-level predictions, specifically targeting missense variants associated with AR diseases.
044   More recent research has aimed at predicting the functional impact of variants in specific genes.
045   LoGoFunc combines gene-, protein-, and variant-level features to predict pathogenic GOF, LOF,
046   and neutral variants (Stein et al., 2023). Another study explored the structural effects of variants,
047   finding that non-LOF variants tend to have milder impacts on protein structure (Gerasimavicius
048   et al., 2022). Additionally, a recent study employed three support vector machines (SVM) to predict
049   protein coding genes associated with DN, GOF, and HI mechanisms (Badonyi & Marsh, 2024).
050   In this study, we present a comprehensive approach for predicting the MOI for all proteins encoded
051   by autosomal genes, as well as elucidating the functional effect of variants underlying AD genetic
052   disorders (Figure 1). Our framework combines graph neural networks (GNNs) (Zhou et al., 2021)
053   with structural interactomics by creating a graph-of-graphs (D’Agostino & Scala, 2014), utilizing
      both protein-protein interaction (PPI) network and high-resolution protein structures. For MOI pre-

      diction, we model proteins as nodes within the PPI network, incorporating topological and protein-
055   level features for classification. For molecular mechanism prediction, we represent each protein as
056   a graph of amino acid residues, leveraging structure-based features to classify the functional effect
057   as HI, GOF, or DN. This integrated approach enables proteome-wide prediction of inheritance pat-
058   terns and provides mechanistic insights into AD diseases, offering a novel, scalable framework for
059   understanding genetic disorders.

      For the sake of flow and conciseness, we refer to ”proteins associated with a autosomal dominant
061   (recessive) disorders” as AD (AR) proteins. Similarly, we use DN (GOF/LOF) proteins instead of
062   ”proteins associated with DN (GOF/LOF) molecular disease mechanisms”.

087
088   2.1   DATA COLLECTION
089
090   Mode of inheritance We collected the MOI data from the Gene Curation Coalition (GenCC)
091   (DiStefano et al., 2022) as well as the Online Mendelian Inheritance in Man (OMIM) (Hamosh,
092   2002). For GenCC records, we kept records with definitive, strong, or moderate gene-disease clinical
093
      validity. We focused on autosomal proteins, due to intrinsic differences in MOI for X chromosome
      proteins. Proteins were accordingly labeled as AD, AR, or ADAR (both dominant and recessive).
094
095
      Molecular mechanism We collected the functional effect of AD proteins from Badonyi & Marsh
096
      (2024). This is a curated set of AD proteins labeled with their known functional effects, including
097
      DN, GOF, and HI.
098
099
      PPI network To make a comprehensive PPI network, we combined the interaction from four
100   resources: STRINGdb with interaction score ≥ 0.7 (Szklarczyk et al., 2022), BioGRID (Oughtred
101   et al., 2020), the Human Reference Interactome (HuRI) (Luck et al., 2020), and Menche et al. (2015),
102   which resulted in a network with 17,248 nodes, and 375,494 edges.
103
104   Protein graph We downloaded the predicted structures of all human proteins from the AlphaFold
105   database (Varadi et al., 2023). We then used Graphein (Jamasb et al., 2022) to construct a residue
106   graph per protein based on the protein structures. In such residue graphs, nodes are amino acids and
107   edges are various interaction between them, including peptide bonds, aromatic interaction, hydrogen
      bonds, disulfide bonds, ionic interactions, aromatic-sulfur interactions, and cation-π interactions.

      Protein features We annotated all proteins with 78 features. Based on their definition, we clus-
109   tered the features into three groups: 1) structure and function 2) conservation and constraint 3)
110   expression and regulation. The complete list of the protein features is available at A.1.
111
112   Residue features For the residue graphs, we annotated the nodes (i.e. amino acids) with 73 fea-
113   tures. We grouped them into four clusters based on their description: 1) structure and function 2)
114   sequence 3) biochemical 4) evolutionary. The complete description of residue features can be found
115   in A.2.

117   2.2   M ODEL DEVELOPMENT
118
119   Study design In this study, MOI is predicted by classifying PPI network nodes, while functional
120
      effect prediction is performed as a graph classification task. In both models, we considered multi-
      label classification, where inputs can have more than one label. For all the following steps, we used
121
      PyTorch Geometric library (Fey & Lenssen, 2019).
122
123

      In all the models, we used 2 hidden layers with 128 and 64 units. The output layer dimension is
      two for MOI models (AD and AR), and three for functional effect models (DN, HI, and GOF). We
189
      used dropout (Srivastava et al., 2014) and weight decay (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) to mitigate the
190
      chance of over-fitting.
191
192
      Training and evaluation We trained each model using a binary cross entropy loss on 80% of
193   the data for maximum 100 epochs, and used early stopping based on validation loss to avoid over-
194   fitting. We evaluated each selected model on 10% of the unseen test data using F1 , precision, and
195   recall scores.
196
      We benchmarked the performance of our model against previous state-of-the-art approaches. For
197
      MOI prediction, we compared our model with DOMINO (Quinodoz et al., 2017), which predicts
198
      the probability of a protein’s association with dominant disorders (pAD). We used our MOI test set
199   and excluded any proteins present in DOMINO’s training data. Since no threshold was provided,
200   we classified proteins as AD if pAD > 0.6, AR if pAD < 0.4, and ADAR otherwise.
201
202
      For functional effect prediction, we compared our model with the models from Badonyi & Marsh
      (2024), which include three separate SVM models (DN vs LOF, GOF vs LOF, and LOF vs non-
203
      LOF). We combined the test sets from these models and used the pre-calculated probabilities to
204
      evaluate performance in a multi-label classification setting.
205
206
      Explanation To study the importance of features, we utilized Integrated Gradients (Sundararajan
207   et al., 2017) using Captum (Kokhlikyan et al., 2020). Since this method works per sample, we
208   applied it on correctly predicted samples in the test sets. We included samples with only one label
209   for further interpretability. Finally, we averaged feature attributions across selected samples, and
210   scaled them by dividing to the maximum attribution.

      Enrichment analysis To study further the predictions, we used GSEApy (Fang et al., 2022) to
217   perform enrichment analysis (Khatri et al., 2012), which is a statistical method used to determine
218   whether known biological functions or processes are over-represented in a protein list of interest (e.g.
219   AD proteins). In this method, the enrichment significance is calculated based on the hypergeometric
220   distribution, where p-value is the cumulative probability of observing at least k proteins of interest
221   annotated to a specific protein set. The formula for the p-value is given by:
222

227   where N is the total number of proteins in the background distribution, M is the number of proteins
228   in that distribution annotated to the gene set of interest, n is the size of the list of proteins of interest,
229   and k is the number of proteins in that list which are annotated to the gene set.
230
      For proteins predicted as only AD or AR, we used DisGeNET (Piñero et al., 2019) as reference
231
      to investigate the enrichment of AD or AR proteins in certain diseases. For AD proteins predicted
232   as DN, HI, or GOF, we used Gene Ontology (Ashburner et al., 2000; Aleksander et al., 2023) to
233   understand their functional landscape.

      4   D ISCUSSION
409
410   In this work, we introduce a novel framework that integrates GNNs with structural interactomics
411
      to predict both the MOI and the functional effect of mutated proteins in genetic disorders. By
      leveraging PPI network and high-resolution protein structures, we offer a graph-of-graphs approach
412
      that addresses two critical aspects of genetic disease prediction. This allows us to not only classify
413
      proteins as AD or AR but also predict whether AD diseases manifest through HI, GOF, or DN
414   mechanisms.
415
416   Our framework demonstrated good performance in predicting MOI, with the GAT model achiev-
417
      ing the best recall for identifying AD and AR proteins. Notably, we found that proteins predicted
      as AD were strongly enriched in cancer pathways, while AR proteins were predominantly associ-
418
      ated with mitochondrial and neurodevelopmental disorders. In terms of functional effects, the GCN
419
      model effectively classified HI, GOF, and DN proteins based on structural features. Feature attribu-
420   tion analysis revealed that DN proteins were associated with high MoRFchibi scores (Malhis et al.,
421   2016), which might indicate regions involved in protein-protein interactions, potentially at inter-
422   faces. HI proteins were linked to the presence of topological domains, while GOF proteins were
423   associated with features related to the amino acid composition of buried residues.
424
      While our approach offers a comprehensive view of inheritance patterns and functional effects, there
425
      are several limitations. First, the availability of high-quality structural data for all human proteins
426   is still limited, which could restrict the accuracy of our predictions (Bertoline et al., 2023). Addi-
427   tionally, our reliance on existing PPI network data may introduce biases, as not all interactions are
428   equally well-characterized across different tissues or biological contexts (Ziv et al., 2022). Further-
429   more, the imbalance in labeled training data may impact the model performance on these classes. Fi-
430   nally, although our method captures the functional effect of AD proteins, it does not extend to other
431   modes of inheritance or interactions that may occur at a multi-variant or epistatic level (Phillips,
      2008).

432
      Moving forward, there are several avenues for expanding this work. First, incorporating tissue-
433   specific PPI networks and expression data could enhance the precision of our predictions, especially
434   for proteins with context-dependent functions (Ziv et al., 2022). Additionally, expanding the model
435   to account for more complex inheritance patterns, such as polygenic traits and epistasis, could pro-
436   vide a more comprehensive understanding of genetic disease (Boyle et al., 2017). Finally, improving
437   the interpretability of models in biological contexts remains essential to derive more actionable in-
438   sights from the predictions (Chen et al., 2024b).
