011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive performance
014            across various natural language processing (NLP) tasks owing to the strong ca-
015            pability of following instructions. To further accelerate the integration of LLMs
016
               into our society, it is essential to have LLMs follow many instructions as accu-
               rately as humans do. This study reveals that LLMs unexpectedly struggle to fol-
017
               low all instructions simultaneously as the number of instructions increases. First,
018
               to validate our claim, we introduce ManyIFEval, a large-scale benchmark dataset
019            comprising task prompts with up to ten objectively verifiable instructions. Sec-
020            ond, we conduct experiments based on ManyIFEval with GPT-4o, Claude-3.5,
021            Gemini-1.5, Gemma2, and Llama3.1, demonstrating that as the instruction count
022            rises, the models’ ability to follow individual instruction deteriorates gradually
023            but constantly. As a result, the models’ ability to follow all the instructions signif-
024            icantly drops: the success rate of all the instructions is precisely explained by the
025            success rate of individual instructions to the power of total number of instructions.
026            We refer to it as the “curse of instructions”. Third, to remove the curse without
027
               retraining models, we propose an inference-time strategy that enhances perfor-
               mance through iterative self-refinement. We demonstrate that instruction-level
028
               chain-of-thought reasoning significantly improves their capability to detect and
029
               correct instruction-following errors. Notably, our method has improved the suc-
030            cess rate of following ten instructions by GPT-4o from 15% to 31% and Claude
031            3.5 Sonnet from 44% to 58%. We also show that precision is more important than
032            recall in feedback: just telling LLMs that they are not following all the instructions
033            also improves self-refinement success. Our findings highlight a fundamental lim-
034            itation of instruction-following ability and suggest a future direction for building
035            trustworthy LLMs that can coexist with human society. 1

038   1   I NTRODUCTION
039
040   Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive performance across various natural
041   language processing tasks (OpenAI, 2024b; Google, 2023). When utilizing LLMs for solving di-
042
      verse tasks, prompting (Brown et al., 2020; Vatsal & Dubey, 2024; Sahoo et al., 2024) has become a
      prevalent approach. Prompting involves providing textual instructions to guide the model’s behavior
043
      towards desired outcomes. This instruction-following ability mainly comes from instruction tuning
044
      process (Wei et al., 2022a; Zhang et al., 2023; Chung et al., 2024).
045
046   As LLMs continue to evolve, their ability to follow multiple instructions simultaneously becomes
047   crucial for broader applications. In human society, individuals perform activity under numerous laws
048
      and organizational norms. For LLMs to be more reliable and widely applicable in human contexts,
      they must adhere to the various rules. Moreover, in domain-specific applications, LLMs need to
049
      comply with established guidelines and regulations. For instance, legal applications require adher-
050
      ence to thousands of statutes (Guha et al., 2023), while medical applications must follow numerous
051   clinical guidelines (Singhal et al., 2023; Kasai et al., 2023; Hager et al., 2024).

      However, the extent to which models can adhere to multiple instructions simultaneously remains
071
      unclear. To address this question, we construct ManyIFEval, a large-scale benchmark dataset com-
072   prising task prompts with up to ten objectively verifiable sets of instructions. These instructions
073   are designed with a difficulty level such that major models can successfully follow them in most
074   cases. Unlike existing benchmarks that typically provide only a few instructions at a time (Wang
075   et al., 2022b; Wei et al., 2022a; Conover et al., 2023), our benchmark scales up to ten concurrent
076   instructions by extending IFEval (Zhou et al., 2023) .
077
      Our experiments reveal that as the number of instructions increases, models struggle to adhere to
078   all of them by following a simple rule of instructions. Specifically, we conduct experiments based
079   on ManyIFEval with various type of models, including closed models (GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024a),
080   Claude 3.5 Sonnet(Anthropic, 2024), and Gemini 1.5 Pro (Gemini Team, 2024)) and open models
081   (Gemma2 (Gemma Team, 2024) and Llama3.1 (Llama Team, 2024)). The experiments demonstrate
082   that as the instruction count rises, the models’ ability to follow individual instruction deteriorates
083   gradually but constantly. Consequently, the models’ ability to follow all the instructions significantly
084   drops by following a simple rule: the success rate of all the instructions is precisely explained by the
085   success rate of individual instructions to the power of total number of instructions. We refer to it as
086
      the “curse of instructions”.
087   To alleviate the curse of significant performance drop without additional training, we explore
088   inference-time strategies (Brown et al., 2024; Welleck et al., 2024) that enhance performance
089   through iterative self-refinement (Madaan et al., 2023). We find that having the model refine its
090   response by feedback from itself leads to improved performance. When creating this feedback, it
091   is beneficial to use Chain-of-Thought reasoning (Wei et al., 2022b; Kojima et al., 2022) for each
092
      instruction. This approach enhances performance by increasing inference time without the need
      for model retraining, making it applicable to models available only through APIs and avoiding the
093
      substantial costs associated with retraining model weights. Notably, our method has improved the
094
      success rate of the following ten instructions by GPT-4o from 15% to 31% and Claude 3.5 Sonnet
095   from 44% to 58%. We also show that recall is more important than precision in feedback: just telling
096   LLMs that they are not following all the instructions also improves self-refinement success.

119   2.1     B ENCHMARKS FOR E VALUATING M ULTIPLE I NSTRUCTION -F OLLOWING P ERFORMANCE
120
121   Instruction-following is a critical capability of large language models (LLMs), and various bench-
122   mark datasets have been proposed to evaluate this aspect (Wang et al., 2022b; Li et al., 2023; Zheng
123
      et al., 2023; Hayati et al., 2024; He et al., 2024b; Jiang et al., 2024; He et al., 2024a). Our study
      focuses on the relationship between instruction-following performance and the complexity of in-
124
      structions, specifically measured by the number of instructions within a prompt. While several
125
      benchmarks investigate this relationship from different angles, our work uniquely emphasizes the
126   impact of instruction count on model performance.
127
128   For instance, FollowBench (Jiang et al., 2024) incrementally adds up to five constraints (e.g. Sit-
129
      uation, Format, Style) to a single prompt, organizing data according to the number of added con-
      straints. FollowBench also includes a subset called ”Mixed Constraints,” which, like our work, adds
130
      constraints of different types to simulate real-world use cases. However, their Mixed Constraints pri-
131
      marily involve only tasks that need to edit or transform input sentences. Their findings indicate that
132   instruction-following performance deteriorates as the number of constraints increases. However, the
133   experiment was conducted with a limited number of instruction count and sample size.
134
135
      ComplexBench (He et al., 2024a) includes not only ”And”-type instructions—independent instruc-
      tions that the model must satisfy simultaneously, similar to our benchmark—but also ”Chain” in-
136
      structions that require sequential execution and ”Selection” instructions that involve conditional
137
      branching. ComplexBench demonstrates that complex instructions, such as those in the ”Selection”
138   and ”Chain” categories, pose significant challenges for LLMs in terms of compliance.
139
140   IFEval (Zhou et al., 2023), the basis of our ManyIFEval, consists of a set of 25 categories of verifi-
141
      able instructions and 541 prompts to which 1 to 3 instructions are assigned. These prompts are first
      rephrased with few-shot prompting to increase the diversity of wording, and then manually checked
142
      to ensure that there are no conflicts between the instructions. Furthermore, the method of judg-
143
      ing compliance is programmatic and deterministic, and analysis of the evaluation results leads to
144   insights on instructions that are difficult to comply with and to comparisons among various LLMs.
145
146   Regarding the quality of the datasets, it is crutial to have a wide range of complexity and abun-
147
      dant samples for each number of instructions to verify the relationship between the increase in the
      number of instructions and LLM’s ability to follow plural instructions. As shown in Table 1 and
148
      Figure 2, because FollowBench and IFEval have the small variation in the number of instructions,
149
      it can be difficult to adequately verify the capability of following multiple instructions of LLMs.
150   Furthermore, although samples with 14 instructions are included in ComplexBench, such samples
151   exceedingly rare and the majority of samples are concentrated within the range of 3 to 6, which
152   means that the number of instructions is not balanced across samples. Contrary to them, ManyIFE-
153   val includes samples with up to 10 instructions and 100 samples are contained for each instruction
154   count. Therefore, our benchmark is preferable because of the sufficient variation in the number of
155   instructions and the balanced samples.
156   Regarding evaluation methods, approaches to assess instruction-following can be broadly catego-
157   rized into human evaluation, model-based automatic evaluation, and program-based automatic eval-
158   uation. Human judgment is costly and time-consuming. While model-based evaluations (Zhang
159   et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2023), which is partially adopted by FollowBench and ComplexBench, can
160   be influenced by the evaluating model’s biases and limitations (Wang et al., 2024; Shen et al., 2023;
161   Zheng et al., 2023). Program-based methods, which evaluates instruction-following performance by
      programmable rules, offer a cost-effective and objective alternative, allowing for accurate assess-

      2.2     M ETHODS TO I MPROVE I NSTRUCTION -F OLLOWING A BILITY
188
189
      LLMs are primarily pre-trained on large corpora of web documents to predict subsequent words,
190   without specific training to respond to human textual instructions. To enhance their ability to in-
191   terpret and follow human instructions, instruction tuning (Wei et al., 2022a; Zhang et al., 2023) is
192   performed as the most representative method for post pre-training. These methods train models
193   on datasets consisting of instructions paired with appropriate responses, such as FLAN (Wei et al.,
194   2022a), Alpaca (Taori et al., 2023), and Dolly (Conover et al., 2023).
195
      More recent studies (Xu et al., 2023; He et al., 2024a) have highlighted that existing datasets often
196   feature only few instructions for each task. The studies have synthesized more complex instruc-
197   tion datasets using LLMs themselves, reporting improvements in instruction-following performance
198   through fine-tuning with these intricate data. While the target instruction count in the studies is
199   limited, this perspective is crucial for enabling models to handle multiple instructions.
200
      However, retraining LLMs is often impractical due to the substantial computational resources re-
201
      quired and the risk of catastrophic forgetting, where retraining may degrade the model’s perfor-
202   mance on previously learned tasks. Additionally, for LLMs accessed via commercial APIs, users
203   are often restricted to access to model parameters and to finetune LLMs.
204
205
      Therefore, we explore performance enhancement methods that do not require retraining and are
      applicable to API-accessible models by focusing on inference strategies. Techniques such as gen-
206
      erating multiple outputs and selecting the most suitable one through majority voting (Wang et al.,
207
      2022a), or self-refinement (Madaan et al., 2023), have demonstrated performance improvements
208   across various tasks. This study focuses on self-refinement for improving the instruction following
209   ability of multiple instructions, where LLMs first produce an initial response and execute a loop of
210   error detection and refinement by themselves.

233   3.1   DATASET
234
235   As shown in figure 3, each instance in ManyIFEval consists of a free-form task prompt, such as
236   ”Write a blog post about a trip to Japan.”, accompanied by a set of instructions that must be satisfied
237
      when performing the task. To systematically examine the impact of the number of instructions on
      performance, we created ten instances per task prompt, varying the number of instructions from one
238
      to ten.
239
240   Considering all possible combinations of which instructions to include and the order in which they
241   appear would result in an enormous number of instances, leading to prohibitive inference costs
242   during evaluation. Therefore, to maintain computational feasibility while ensuring a comprehensive
243
      assessment across various models, we limited the dataset to ten instances per task prompt, each with
      a different number of instructions ranging from one to ten.
244
245   The task prompts and instructions were extracted from the existing IFEval benchmark. In the ex-
246   traction process, we selected task prompts that remain feasible even when additional instructions
247   are provided, and instruction groups that are non-conflicting and can be simultaneously satisfied.
248   Through manual verification, we curated a total of 216 task prompts from an initial set of 541.
249
      These were divided into 110 for training, 100 for testing, and 6 for few-shot prompting.
250   There are a total of 15 instructions categorized into the following six types:
251
252    • Keywords: Specifying keywords to include or exclude.
253    • Length Constraints: Specifying the length of the answer.
254
       • Detectable Format: Requiring bullet point formatting.
255
256    • Change Cases: Specifying the use of uppercase or lowercase letters.
257    • Start with / End with: Instructions to begin or conclude with quotation mark.
258    • Punctuation: Prohibiting the use of commas.
259
260   Details of these instruction types are provided in Appendix B. For instruction types like Change
261   Cases and Length Constraints, we select one variant each, ensuring that up to ten non-conflicting
262   instructions are chosen from the total of 15.
263
      The compliance with each instruction can be objectively verified programmatically, allowing us to
264   determine success or failure in instruction following. Importantly, the selected 15 instructions are
265   designed such that, when only one instruction is given with the task prompt, GPT-4o(gpt-4o-2024-
266   05-13) achieves high success rate. GPT-4o achieves a success rate of over 90% for nine instructions,
267   over 80% for four instructions, over 60% for two instructions. In other words, these instructions
268   are designed with a difficulty level such that major models can successfully follow them in most
269   cases: we excluded individually challenging instructions from our benchmark to focus on observing
      changes in performance as the number of instructions increases. By constructing the dataset with in-

368   5.1   O UR M ETHOD
369
370   To alleviate the “curse of instructions”, we explore a method to enhance instruction-following perfor-
371   mance of LLMs without modifying their parameters. Given the enormous number of parameters in
372   LLMs, fine-tuning or retraining is computationally expensive, and some models are accessible only
373   via APIs, limiting the ability to adjust their internal weights. Therefore, we investigate inference-
374
      time strategies that can improve performance without altering the underlying model.
375   We propose a method of iterative self-refinenment in which the model itself evaluates whether the
376   model successfully follow each instruction and then corrects its answers based on this evaluation.
377   An overview of the proposed method is illustrated in Figure 5. Specifically, given a task description
      and instructions to follow within a prompt, our method lets the model first generate an initial answer

467   (self-refinement, self-feedback, Chain-of-Thought and feedback per instruction) are combined. In-
468
      terestingly, just giving self-feedback does not improve the performance (See the performance change
      from “refinement w/o feedback” to “refinement w/ feedback”). This result indicates that the quality
469
      of self-feedback is important for the performance improvement. See Appendix D for the results of
470
      the other models.

473
474   Based on the finding that the quality of self-feedback is important for the performance improvement,
475   we further analyze what characteristics are important for the self-feedback. First, we conduct the
476   following additional baseline experiments:
477
478    • refinement w/ oracle: uses an external oracle feedback mechanism that can precisely determine
479           whether each instruction was successfully followed. Specifically, this method (1) generates
480           an initial answer; (2) uses the oracle verifier to assess success or failure in following each
481           instruction; (3) lists the instructions that were not followed and prompt the model to correct
482           its answer accordingly. We repeat steps (2) and (3) for T times.
483    • refinement w/ all false: informs the model that it failed to follow all instructions. Specifically,
484           this method (1) generates an initial answer; (2) prompt the model with all the instructions
485           to inform the model that it failed to follow all the instructions and correct its answer ac-
              cordingly. We repeats steps (2) for T times.

524   6   CONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
525
526   We hava introduced ManyIFEval, a large-scale benchmark dataset comprising task prompts with
527   up to ten objectively verifiable instructions. We conducted experiments based on ManyIFEval with
528   various models, demonstrating that as the instruction count rises, the models’ ability to follow indi-
529   vidual instruction deteriorates gradually but constantly. As a result, the models’ ability to follow all
530
      the instructions significantly drops by the “curse of instructions”. To remove the curse without re-
      training models, we proposed a inference-time method that enhances performance through iterative
531
      self-refinement by feedback with instruction-level chain-of-thought reasoning. Our Experiments
532
      showed that our method drastically improved the performance of following multiple instructions.
533
534   It is important to note that the instructions used in our study were designed to be simple and allowed
535   for objective, binary evaluations of success or failure in instruction following. These instructions
536
      did not encompass more abstract or nuanced directives, such as those found in legal texts, where
      judgments may have gradations and are less clear-cut. Addressing the challenges posed by such
537
      complex instructions remains an area for future work. Developing methods for LLMs to handle
538
      abstract instructions with subjective success criteria will be crucial for advancing their applicability
539   in real-world scenarios that demand nuanced understanding and interpretation.

      C    E FFECT OF M ULTIPLE UNIQUE INSTRUCTIONS ON PERFORMANCE
811
812
      Previous studies have shown that increasing the input token length can negatively impact a model’s
813
      reasoning capabilities (Levy et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). To determine whether the observed
814   degradation was due to the increased number of instructions or simply the longer input sequences,
815   we conducted an experiment where we repeated the same instructions to match the input length of
816   prompts with multiple unique instructions. By measuring instruction-following performance with
817   repeated instructions, we isolated the effect of input length from the effect of instruction variety.
818   As shown in Figure 8, the degradation in performance with repeated instructions was less severe
819   compared to when the number of unique instructions increased. In some cases, models even demon-
820   strated improved instruction-following when instructions were repeated. These findings suggest
821   that the negative impact on instruction-following performance is more attributable to the increased
822
      number of unique instructions rather than the overall input length, highlighting a limitation in the
      models’ capacity to handle multiple instructions simultaneously.
