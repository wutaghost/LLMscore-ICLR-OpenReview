011                                                     A BSTRACT
012
013                Learning a precise robotic grasping policy is crucial for embodied agents op-
014                erating in complex real-world manipulation tasks. Despite significant advance-
015
                   ments, most models still struggle with accurate spatial positioning of objects to be
                   grasped. We first show that this spatial generalization challenge stems primarily
016
                   from the extensive data requirements for adequate spatial understanding. How-
017
                   ever, collecting such data with real robots is prohibitively expensive, and relying
018                on simulation data often leads to visual generalization gaps upon deployment. To
019                overcome these challenges, we then focus on state-based policy generalization
020                and present ManiBox, a novel bounding-box-guided manipulation method built
021                on a simulation-based teacher-student framework. The teacher policy efficiently
022                generates scalable simulation data using bounding boxes, which are proven to
023                uniquely determine the objects’ spatial positions. The student policy then utilizes
024                these low-dimensional spatial states to enable zero-shot transfer to real robots.
025                Through comprehensive evaluations in simulated and real-world environments,
026
                   ManiBox demonstrates a marked improvement in spatial grasping generalization
                   and adaptability to diverse objects and backgrounds. Further, our empirical study
027
                   into scaling laws for policy performance indicates that spatial volume generaliza-
028
                   tion scales positively with data volume. For a certain level of spatial volume, the
029                success rate of grasping empirically follows Michaelis-Menten kinetics relative to
030                data volume, showing a saturation effect as data increases.1

      1       I NTRODUCTION
034   Robotic manipulation in dynamic environments is pivotal for advancing modern robotics (Fu et al.,
035   2024). Equipping robots with precise grasping capabilities in unstructured settings not only ex-
036   tends their utility beyond traditional static scenarios but also enhances their real-world effective-
037   ness, especially in industrial and domestic settings. A robot policy that achieves spatial general-
038   ization—defined as the ability of a manipulation model to complete tasks regardless of the target
039   object’s position within a defined spatial volume—would be particularly practical. Such generaliza-
040   tion is vital when deploying robots in diverse environments, such as different households, where they
041   must handle various objects on different platforms. The capability to perform successfully across
042
      these varied spatial conditions is essential, as objects in real-world settings are rarely fixed in place,
      and effective manipulation demands adaptability to a broad range of spatial challenges.
043
044   Recent works have made notable progress in robotic manipulation in multi-task and multi-
045   environment scenarios, which is largely driven by end-to-end training methodologies (Ahn et al.,
046   2022; Shridhar et al., 2022; 2023; Zitkovich et al., 2023). Notably, some large embodied foundation
047   models for manipulation, such as those fine-tuned from Vision-Language Models (VLMs) for multi-

      task robot manipulation tasks (Brohan et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2024), have made significant strides.

096   tiveness of a robot in dynamic settings hinges on the robustness of its policy execution. A well-
097   generalized policy empowers the robot to autonomously adjust its actions in real-time, effectively
098   compensating for visual inaccuracies and adapting to changes in object positions or environmental
099   dynamics. Such adaptability is crucial for achieving task success and maintaining system stability,
100   especially in the face of imperfect or noisy perception data. To further enhance the robot’s ability to
101   manage unpredictable scenarios (e.g. Sec. B.2) and ensure reliable real-world performance, we shift

      2   R ELATED W ORK
128
129   Learning from Simulation. Simulators provide an efficient and scalable way to expand embodi-
130   ment datasets, offering a cost-effective means for parallel data collection and faster iteration cycles.
131   There are three main approaches for leveraging simulation in robotic control: 1) training reinforce-
132   ment learning (RL) agents through interaction within the simulator (Huang et al., 2023; Radosavovic
133   et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2024), 2) using imitation learning based on expert demonstrations gener-
134   ated in simulation (Xie et al., 2020; Tan et al., 2024), and 3) employing a teacher-student framework,
135   where privileged information from simulation trains a teacher policy to guide a student policy (Lee
136
      et al., 2020a; Geng et al., 2023; Zhuang et al., 2023), as demonstrated in the ManiBox framework.
      These methods reduce the reliance on expensive real-world data collection.
137
138   Various techniques have been developed to bridge the Sim2Real gap, including realistic simula-
139   tors (Todorov et al., 2012; James et al., 2020; Makoviychuk et al., 2021), domain randomization (To-
140   bin et al., 2017; Peng et al., 2018; Zhuang et al., 2023), and vision-based policy generalization (Geng
141   et al., 2023; Tan et al., 2024; Ying et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2024). These approaches enable robots
142
      to generalize across diverse visual and physical conditions, which are critical in real-world environ-
      ments. For example, domain randomization allows policies trained in varied simulated conditions
143
      to transfer effectively to real-world scenarios (Tobin et al., 2017; Peng et al., 2018). While real-
144
      world datasets such as Open X-Embodiment (Padalkar et al., 2023) provide 1M demonstrations,
145   they are much smaller than datasets in other domains like language and images (Lehmann et al.,
146   2015; Mühleisen & Bizer, 2012; Weyand et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2019). Thus, simulation-generated
147   datasets play a vital role in compensating for the limitations of real-world data collection.
148
149
      Visual Generalization for Robotic Manipulation. Visual generalization is fundamental for
150   robotic manipulation in dynamic environments, where robots must handle varying visual inputs
151   and changing conditions, such as object positions and lighting variations. Vision-based policy gen-
152   eralization, which leverages both simulated and real-world data, addresses these challenges. By
153   simulating diverse visual scenarios, robots can better generalize to new environments and tasks. For
154   instance, multi-task and multi-object manipulation studies have extended behavior cloning to handle
155   a wide range of visual inputs, improving generalization to unseen objects and scenes (Vuong et al.,
156   2023; Fang et al., 2023). Additionally, language-conditioned policies (Brohan et al., 2022; Zitkovich
157   et al., 2023; Shridhar et al., 2023) and imitation learning from human videos (Chen et al., 2021; Nair
158
      et al., 2023) enable robots to interpret and manipulate objects using both visual and language cues.
159   However, scaling real-world demonstrations to improve generalization remains challenging due to
160   resource constraints. Simulation-based learning mitigates this by offering large, diverse visual data.
161   Domain randomization techniques (Tobin et al., 2017; Peng et al., 2018) have proven effective in
      generating varied visual conditions, enabling models to generalize more robustly across real-world

195   3.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
196
      For robotic manipulation, especially within dynamic environments, achieving spatial generalization
197
      (i.e., the ability to effectively perform tasks across varied spatial configurations) is essential for us-
198
      ability and overall efficacy. We formulate robotic manipulation as a partially observable Markov
199   decision process (POMDP) (Cassandra et al., 1994), by considering the robot’s limited access to
200   complete state information, the necessity to make decisions under uncertainty, as well as the in-
201   herently stochastic nature of its interactions with the environment. Formally, the decision-making
202   process is characterized by the tuple M = (S, A, O, T , R, γ), where S represents the state space,
203   including all configurations of the robot and its environment; A denotes the action space, containing
204   all possible robot actions; O is the observation space, which reflects the limited state information
205   accessible to the robot; T defines the state transition dynamics, T (st+1 |st , at ); R is the reward
206   function, providing feedback based on the state-action pair R(st , at ); and γ is the discount factor.
207   In real-world scenarios, the robot receives partial observations ot ∈ O through an observation func-
208   tion, which provides incomplete and potentially noisy information about the true state st . The obser-
209   vation can be represented as ot ∼ O(st ). The robot’s objective is to find a policy π that maps obser-
210   vations (or histories of observations) to actions at ∼ π(at |τt ), where τt = (o0 , a0 , o1 , a1 , . . . , ot )
211   is the history up to time t. The policy is expected to maximize the expected cumulative reward:

      for manipulation tasks is computationally expensive, as it requires maintaining a belief distribution

219   state is constantly evolving and uncertain (Kaelbling et al., 1998; Kochenderfer, 2015).

      novel bounding-box-guided manipulation method that offers significant advantages in spatial gener-

225   by using low-dimensional bounding box states, the state-based student policy can accelerate learning
226   and effectively transfer from simulation to the real world.

228
      a high-dimensional state space in simulation, which can be computationally intensive. The student
229   policy must derive optimal actions from incomplete observations and adjust to real-world variations

231   strategies for overcoming these challenges are discussed in the following sections.

254   Environment Setup. To ensure efficient data generation and policy training, we utilize Isaac
255   Lab (Mittal et al., 2023), which supports large-scale parallelism environments. The scene includes
256   basic elements like a platform, objects, and a robot, arranged simply to facilitate future domain ran-
257   domization. The robot’s goal is to grasp the object on the table and move it to a designated location.
258

      view RGB cameras, into the simulator and ensure its dynamics are consistent with the real world.
259
260   State. For training efficiency, our teacher policy is state-based and can access full state information
261   st . As the robot lacks visual input, we include privileged information like the object’s position in
262   the state information. The privileged information accelerates RL training and enables the agent to
263   learn more efficient and generalized policies.
264   Domain Randomization. To improve the generalization of the teacher policy and enhance the
265   spatial diversity of the generated data, we adopt several domain randomization techniques:
266           • objective position (x, y, z): To generalize the grasping policy to the whole space, we ap-
267             ply the domain randomization for all three dimensions of x, y, and z, ensuring that the
268             x-coordinate and y-coordinate of the object, which is chosen randomly, can cover a large
269             range. Moreover, the height of the platform is randomized to ensure that the z-coordinate

277   3.3   S IMULATION DATA G ENERATION
278
279   We utilize the teacher policy to automatically generate data for training a student policy that can be
280   deployed in the real world across different objects, grasping spatial positions, and backgrounds.
281   Data Volume Estimation. To guide the data volume needed for policy generalization to a specific
282   spatial volume, we now analyze the sample efficiency for generalizing in a b × b × b cube. Based on
283   the classical learning theory, the sample complexity of uniform convergence property is in proportion
284   to the VC dimension of the model hypothesis set (Shalev-Shwartz & Ben-David, 2014). Extending
285   previous results about generalization bounds for multi-task learning (Crammer & Mansour, 2012),
286   we can have the following results:
287   Lemma 1 (Details are in Appendix A.1). For training a grasp agent that can generalize spatially
288   within a cube whose size is b×b×b via imitation learning, under some mild assumptions
                                                                                              in Crammer
289   & Mansour (2012), the VC dimension is at most proportional to d log Cb3 d , where d is the VC
290   dimension of the hypothesis set for grasping the fixed point and C is a constant independent of d, b.
291
      This lemma demonstrates that the data required for policy generalization to a certain volume grows,
292
      thus it is necessary to collect data from the simulator. This result gives us a rough estimation of how
293
      much data we need to collect with the increasing of the spatial cube needs to generalize.
294
295   In light of this Lemma 1, we need a large scale of trajectories to train a student policy feasible
296   under full spatial range. This calls for conciseness in data generation and collection, otherwise it
297
      will place a heavy burden on student policy training. On the other hand, since the teacher policy
      trained with privileged information (i.e. object positions) cannot directly transfer from simulation
298
      to reality (Sim2Real), the student policy needs visual observations that implicitly encode object
299
      position information. However, there remains a significant Sim2Real gap between visual images in
300   the simulator and those in the real world because the vast complexity of real-world environments
301   cannot be fully simulated by the simulator (e.g. various backgrounds and objects). Additionally,
302   vision-based policy is difficult to train on a large scale and requires data augmentation.
303
      To address this Sim2Real gap and enable robust spatial generalization, we propose using an individ-
304
      ual vision module to extract the bounding box from camera views as a consistent, low-dimensional,
305
      and complete representation of the object’s spatial properties, such as position and size.
306
307   Using Bounding Boxes to Provide Complete 3D Information about the Object. To efficiently
308   generate a large amount of simulator data that aligns with real-world visual images, the vision mod-
309
      ule must be robust, mature, and efficient. Considering open-vocabulary detection, we selected
      YOLO-World (Cheng et al., 2024b) as the frozen 2D visual feature extraction module. This pro-
310
      vides us with the bounding box of the objects as the 2D low-dimensional visual feature, which is
311
      consistent and low-dimensional. Notably, the inference time of YOLO-World for a single image is
312   around 30ms , ensuring the data generalization efficiency and real-time inference in the real world.
313
314   In addition, to ensure that these multi-view 2D low-dimensional visual features can offer complete
315
      information about object position and size, we present a lemma: Suppose the object is a sphere, the
      3D information of the object can be uniquely determined by the bounding boxes from two cameras:
316
317   Lemma 2 (Details and proofs are in Appendix A.2). Given: (1) Two pinhole cameras with known
318   intrinsic parameters Ki and extrinsic parameters (Ri , ti ); (2) A sphere of unknown radius r is fully
319   visible in both camera images without occlusion; (3) Normalized image coordinates of the bounding
320
      boxes of the sphere in both images: Camera i: (u1min , vimin , uimax , vimax ), the image dimensions are
      known (width Wi and height Hi for each camera).
321
322   Then the 3D coordinates of the sphere’s center C in the world coordinate system and the radius r
323   of the sphere can be uniquely determined using the known camera parameters, and the normalized
      bounding box coordinates, provided that the cameras are not in degenerate configurations.

335   While the teacher policy and data generation are completed in the simulator, the student policy is
336   deployed on a real robot and relies only on partial observations in the real world. The student policy
337   needs to ensure high generalizability in real-world environments. Consequently, real-world varia-
338   tions, such as sensor noise and dynamic environments, pose challenges not encountered in the simu-
339   lation environment to our student policy. For instance, an open-vocabulary detection model(namely
340   YOLO-World (Cheng et al., 2024b) in our practice) can fail to recognize real-world target objects
341   at times because of background distraction or robot gripper occlusion, while the recognition of the
342
      simulated target object rarely fails.
343   In this context, we introduce the random mask to bridge this sim-to-real gap and further improve
344   the robustness of our policy. In detail, when distilling our student policy from the simulation data
345   D, for each trajectory, we randomly mask some bboxes(i.e., set them to 0) with a probability pre-
346   defined as random mask ratio, indicating that this camera does not recognize the target object.
347   This approach replicates the real-world scenarios where the open-vocabulary detection model fails
348
      to generate the bounding box.
349   On top of this expected unpredictable failure and partial observability, our policy requires encoding
350   sufficient history information (Hafner et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2020b; Ying et al., 2023). For this
351   reason, we use RNN for our student policy:

529   5   C ONCLUSION
530
      In this work, we present ManiBox, a novel bbox-guided manipulation method, using a simulation-
531
      based teacher-student framework to enhance the spatial generalization of grasping policies. To ad-
532
      dress the challenge of the generalization gap in vision-based models from simulation to real-world
533   deployment, we propose a generalizable, state-based student policy that is innovatively equipped
534   with bbox positions. This policy is trained on scalable simulation data generated by the teacher
535   policy. Extensive experiments in both simulated and real-world environments demonstrate that
536   ManiBox significantly outperforms existing methods in adapting to diverse spatial positions, ob-
537   ject configurations, and background variations. Moreover, our theoretical and empirical analyses
538   reveal that the generalization of spatial volume scales positively with the data volume, and the suc-
539   cess rate follows Michaelis-Menten kinetics for specified spatial volumes. These findings pave the
      way for the development of robust spatial generalization across a wide range of embodied systems.

1268
       We train our teacher policy using PPO with 8,192 parallel environments in Isaac Lab (Mittal et al.,
1269   2023). With a carefully designed reward function and applied PPO techniques, the training is com-
1270   pleted in around 40 minutes, reaching approximately 2,000 training iterations. The task involves
1271   training a robot to grasp an apple randomly placed on a liftable table and transport it to a designated
1272   target position (Figure 18).

1284   The advantages of using a teacher policy instead of direct visual RL training can be summarized in
1285   three key aspects. First, the teacher policy relies solely on robot and object information as input,
1286   significantly reducing the dimensionality of the state space. Second, the incorporation of privileged
1287
       information and tailored reward functions for critical steps offers more focused guidance during
       training, thereby narrowing the exploration space. Lastly, visual RL suffers from limited paralleliz-
1288
       ability, which results in lower overall training efficiency.

1548
1549   We employ the student policy for sim-to-real transfer, where the inputs to the policy included pro-
1550
       prioception orob
                     t   which consists of joint positions, and the visual input ovis
                                                                                  t , represented by bounding
       boxes. The output action is the desired joint position for the next step. Thus, the primary sim-to-real
1551
       gaps exist in the joint position and bounding box dimensions.
1552
1553   For joint positions, we develop a real-to-sim code that maps the joint positions obtained from tele-
1554   operation on a real-world robot to a simulated robot. We evaluate whether the actions performed
1555   at specific target positions were consistent between the real-world and simulated environments. For
1556
       example, when teleoperating to grasp an apple at a precisely measured location, both the real-world
       and simulated apples are set at the same coordinates. We used the main arm of the real-world robot
1557
       to observe whether the simulated robot could successfully grasp the apple.
1558
1559   Special handling is required for the gripper, as there are discrepancies between the URDF model
1560   of the gripper and its real-world counterpart. In the simulation, the gripper has two joints, while in
1561   reality, it functions as a single joint, leading to differences in value interpretation. To address this,
1562
       we collect 10 data sets for fitting and utilize function interpolation to map the simulated gripper joint
       positions to those of the real-world gripper. This approach ensures that the state space and action
1563
       space are aligned between the simulation and the real environment.

       G    L IMITATIONS AND D ISCUSSIONS
1621
1622
       One of the limitations of ManiBox is that the accuracy of the obtained bounding box depends on
1623   the capability of the preceding visual model. As the YOLO-World sometimes may not be able to
1624   accurately partition the bounding box, ManiBox may grasp the wrong positions due to the wrong
1625   input bounding box. Also, the bounding box is mainly for objects with convex shapes and may be
1626   insufficient for handling flexible objects and fluids. In the future, a promising direction is to apply
1627   more powerful visual models like Grounding DINO and capture more semantic visual signals like
