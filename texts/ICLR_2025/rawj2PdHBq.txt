009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011                Medical Vision-Language Pre-training (MedVLP) has made significant progress
012                in enabling zero-shot tasks for medical image understanding. However, training
013                MedVLP models typically requires large-scale datasets with paired, high-quality
014                image-text data, which are scarce in the medical domain. Recent advancements
015                in Large Language Models (LLMs) and diffusion models have made it possible
016                to generate large-scale synthetic image-text pairs. This raises the question: Can
017
                   MedVLP succeed using purely synthetic data? To address this, we use off-the-
                   shelf generative models to create synthetic radiology reports and paired Chest X-ray
018
                   (CXR) images, and propose an automated pipeline to build a diverse, high-quality
019
                   synthetic dataset, enabling a rigorous study that isolates model and training settings,
020                focusing entirely from the data perspective. Our results show that MedVLP models
021                trained exclusively on synthetic data outperform those trained on real data by 3.8%
022                in averaged AUC on zero-shot classification. Moreover, using a combination of
023                synthetic and real data leads to a further improvement of 9.07%. Additionally,
024                MedVLP models trained on synthetic or mixed data consistently outperform those
025                trained on real data in zero-shot grounding, as well as in fine-tuned classification
026                and segmentation tasks. Our analysis suggests MedVLP trained on well-designed
027                synthetic data can outperform models trained on real datasets, which may be limited
028
                   by low-quality samples and long-tailed distributions1 .

      1       I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In medical image analysis, learning representative features typically requires labor-intensive and
033   costly image annotations (Ronneberger et al., 2015; Liu et al., 2023b). Medical Vision-Language
034   Pre-training (MedVLP) addresses this challenge by aligning vision and language content using paired
035   datasets of images and clinical reports, reducing the need for manual annotations (Radford et al.,
036   2021; Zhang et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023a). However, existing MedVLP models rely
037   heavily on large-scale, high-quality paired data (Liu et al., 2023e), which is scarce in practice. Real-
038   world datasets often contain noisy data, such as low-quality images and unpaired image-text samples,
039   degrading model performance (Xie et al., 2024; Bannur et al., 2023). Recent advancements in
040
      Large Language Models (LLMs) and diffusion models enable the generation of large-scale synthetic
      image-text datasets, offering an alternative to traditional data collection. Although these techniques
041
      have shown promise in medical tasks, they are primarily used as auxiliary support for real data via
042
      augmentation (Chen et al., 2024a; Yao et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022; Qin et al., 2023), and are often
043   limited to single-modality settings. To the best of our knowledge, no studies have fully explored
044   the potential of using synthetic multimodal data for MedVLP or considered training exclusively on
045   synthetic data (Liu et al., 2023e).
046

049                synthetic images and text generated with quality and distribution control using off-the-shelf
050                models, without relying on real data or manual curation.
051              • We successfully demonstrate that MedVLP models trained on our SynCXR dataset, con-
052                taining only synthetic data, outperform those trained on real data. Moreover, combining

092   Representation Learning with Synthetic Data. Synthetic data has been widely employed across
093
      various deep learning fields (Rossenbach et al., 2020; Varol et al., 2017; Jahanian et al., 2022; Zhou
      et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2020; Li et al., 2023). In visual representation learning, synthetic data has
094
      improved model performance in a range of tasks (Richter et al., 2016; Ros et al., 2016; Chen et al.,
095
      2019; Johnson-Roberson et al., 2017; Yuan et al., 2024; Shmelkov et al., 2018). Recent efforts have
096   also focused on using synthetic data from text-to-image models to augment real-world data during
097   training (Azizi et al., 2023; Sariyildiz et al., 2023; He et al., 2023). For example, (Yu et al., 2023)
098   introduced a framework to generate synthetic images to diversify existing datasets. Notably, methods
099   utilizing text-to-image generative models (Rombach et al., 2022) have demonstrated that synthetic
100   images guided by real captions can effectively train self-supervised models, achieving performance
101   comparable to that of real images (Tian et al., 2023b).
102   Further advancements like SynCLR (Tian et al., 2023a) have focused on visual representation learning
103   using only synthetic images, generated with conditioning on various categories. Meanwhile, other
104   recent works (Fan et al., 2023; Sharifzadeh et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2024) have explored joint image and
105   text generation for enhanced vision-language pretraining (VLP). However, only one study, SynthCLIP
106   (Hammoud et al., 2024), investigates VLP exclusively with synthetic data, and even that work is
107   limited to natural images. To date, no research has explored the potential of MedVLP trained solely
      on synthetic data.

133
      performance (Wu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Phan et al., 2024a), which limits their scalability
      and accessibility.
134
135   Synthetic Data for Medical Image Tasks. Given the scarcity of annotated data, high costs, and
136   privacy concerns in medical data collection, synthetic data has been explored to support various
137   medical image tasks (Koetzier et al., 2024). However, most prior work focuses on image modality and
138   supervised learning (Chen et al., 2024a; Yao et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022; Qin et al., 2023), using
139
      synthetic data solely as augmentation for real datasets (Khosravi et al., 2024; Ktena et al., 2024). Few
      studies have evaluated models trained entirely on synthetic medical data (Wu et al., 2024). Recent
140
      efforts have generated synthetic text and images for MedVLP (Xie et al., 2024), but still restrict
141
      synthetic data usage to augmentation. Consequently, the full potential of synthetic data in MedVLP
142   remains largely unexplored.
143
144   In this work, we generate both synthetic CXR images and reports, then training a MedVLP model
145
      solely on synthetic data. We conduct an extensive evaluation of the impact of large-scale synthetic
      medical data on MedVLP, exploring its performance across various downstream tasks.

150   3.1   E XPLORING I MPERFECTIONS IN R EAL DATA
151
152   For MedVLP, the most commonly used dataset is MIMIC-CXR (Johnson et al., 2019a;b), a collection
153   of chest x-ray (CXR) images paired with their corresponding textual reports. after following the
154   preprocessing steps outlined in previous works (Zhang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022; Huang et al.,
155   2021), this dataset provides a total of 213,384 image-text pairs for pre-training. And all images must
156   be frontal views according to the preprocessing steps outlined in (Huang et al., 2021).
157   Previous work on VLP with natural images (Xu et al., 2023b) has shown that data quality, including
158   image fidelity and long-tailed distribution, significantly impacts model performance. However, the
159   quality of MedVLP datasets remains underexplored due to ambiguity in defining medical image
160   quality, stemming from diverse imaging protocols. Additionally, quantifying data distribution is
161   complex, as radiology reports often describe patterns across multiple anatomical regions rather
      than distinct categories.To address these challenges, we develop a systematic pipeline to thoroughly

      CXR Image Generation. After generating the synthetic radiology reports, we aim to generate paired
302   CXR images conditioned on the synthetic reports. Since general text-to-image (T2I) models (e.g.,
303   Stable Diffusion) are not designed for CXR image generation and demonstrate poor performance, as
304   shown in (Liu et al., 2023e; Bluethgen et al., 2024), we select RoentGen6 (Bluethgen et al., 2024),
305   the most recent and validated CXR-specific T2I model, verified by clinicians, as our image generator.
306   We use RoentGen’s (Bluethgen et al., 2024) official pretrained weights to generate images. Following
307   their implementation, we use only the ‘IMPRESSION’ section from the synthetic reports as the text
308   prompt for the T2I model. The generation process is controlled using the official hyperparameters
309   provided by RoentGen, where the classifier-free guidance (CFG) is set to 4 and the number of
310
      denoising steps is set to 50.
311   To prevent the synthetic images from exhibiting the same issues found in the real dataset (as discussed
312   in Sec. 3.1), we apply a similar curation procedure. First, we use the MLLM to filter synthetic images,
313   and then we compute the similarity of visual features between synthetic images and the problematic
314   samples identified from the real dataset. If the visual similarity exceeds a threshold δ = 0.5, we
315   regenerate the images by re-querying the T2I model with the same text prompt until they pass the
316
      curation procedure.
317   We generate 200,000 synthetic CXR images, each paired with a corresponding synthetic report, using
318   only general-purpose, open-source models (e.g., Llama3.1 (AI@Meta, 2024), InternVL2 (Chen et al.,

320   et al., 2024)). No annotated CXR images or MedVLP models pre-trained on specific CXR image-text
321   datasets are used in this process. This ensures our approach is adaptable and can easily incorporate
322
      future advancements in general-purpose models. We refer to this dataset as SynCXR.

      3.3       S YNTHETIC DATA T RAINING FOR M EDVLP
325
326   Finally, we use the synthetic dataset, SynCXR, to train a MedVLP model and explore how effectively
327   a model can learn from pure synthetic data. Since there are many existing methods for MedVLP, we
328   select simple baseline models like ConVIRT (Zhang et al., 2020) and GLoRIA (Huang et al., 2021)
329   for the following reasons:
330
      ConVIRT (Zhang et al., 2020) jointly trains vision and text encoders on paired medical images and
331   reports using global contrastive learning.
332
333   GLoRIA (Huang et al., 2021) extends ConVIRT by incorporating both global and regional contrastive
334
      learning to train the encoders on paired medical images and reports.
335   These models are open-source, straightforward, and minimize the influence of external factors on
336   evaluating synthetic data for MedVLP. For retraining these two methods on our synthetic dataset,
337   SynCXR, we strictly use their official codebases78 . More complex models may introduce unnecessary
338   complications.
339   Excluding Complex Models. Recent models like BioViL (Boecking et al., 2022) and BioViL-T
340   (Bannur et al., 2023) lack publicly available training code, making them impractical for re-training
341   with synthetic data. Knowledge-enhanced MedVLP models such as MedKLIP, KAD, and MAVL (Wu
342   et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Phan et al., 2024b) rely on external tools and human-annotated data
343   to incorporate additional knowledge, making direct implementation with synthetic data challenging
344   and introducing unnecessary variables.

353   4.1       D OWNSTREAM TASK DATASETS AND C ONFIGURATIONS
354
      For downstream tasks, we evaluate the effectiveness of synthetic data for MedVLP across four tasks.
355
      Details on the datasets and implementation are provided in Appendix, Sec A.
356
357   Zero-shot Medical Image Classification. Following the guidelines in (Phan et al., 2024b; Wu et al.,
358   2023), we perform this task on seven datasets: CheXpert (Saporta et al., 2022), ChestXray-14 (Wang
359   et al., 2017), PadChest-seen, PadChest-unseen, PadChest-rare (Bustos et al., 2020), RSNA (Shih
360
      et al., 2019), and SIIM (Steven G. Langer & George Shih, 2019), using the dataset splits from (Phan
      et al., 2024b). Evaluation metrics include AUC, F1, and ACC.
361
362   Zero-shot Medical Image Visual Grounding. In line with (Phan et al., 2024b), this task is conducted
363   on the RSNA (Shih et al., 2019), SIIM (Steven G. Langer & George Shih, 2019), and Covid-19 Rural
364   (Desai et al., 2020) datasets, using official splits and metrics. Grounding performance is evaluated
365   with IoU, and Dice score.
366   Medical Image Fine-tuned Classification. As described in (Phan et al., 2024b), we use the RSNA
367   (Shih et al., 2019), SIIM (Steven G. Langer & George Shih, 2019), Covid-19 CXR-2 (Pavlova et al.,
368   2022), and ChestXray-14 (Wang et al., 2017) datasets. During fine-tuning, all model parameters,
369   including the pre-trained vision encoder and linear classifier, are updated. The AdamW optimizer is
370   applied with a learning rate of 1 × 10−4 , batch size of 64, and training runs for 50 epochs. Evaluation
371   follows the AUC score protocol in (Huang et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Zhou et al.).
372   Medical Image Fine-tuned Segmentation. This task uses the RSNA (Shih et al., 2019), SIIM (Steven
373   G. Langer & George Shih, 2019), and Covid-19 Rural (Desai et al., 2020) datasets, following
374   preprocessing from (Wang et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2021). U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015) is used
375   for fine-tuning, freezing the pre-trained vision encoder and updating only the decoder parameters.

403
404   Since the MIMIC-CXR dataset already includes several diseases present in downstream tasks, as
405
      mentioned in (Phan et al., 2024b; Zhang et al., 2023), we split the zero-shot classification task into
      seen and unseen categories, strictly following (Phan et al., 2024b). Note that all experimental results
406
      for ConVIRT and GLoRIA pre-trained with real data (MIMIC-CXR) are directly referenced from
407
      (Phan et al., 2024b) to ensure a fair comparison.
408
409   Zero-shot Classification on Seen Diseases. Tab 1 shows the zero-shot classification performance
410   on seen diseases. Across all datasets, both MedVLP methods pretrained on SynCXR (our purely
411
      synthetic dataset) consistently outperform or achieve comparable performance to their counterparts
      pretrained on real datasets, with an average improvement of 4.7% in AUC and 4.53% in F1 scores.
412
      Furthermore, the methods pretrained on the mixed dataset, which directly combines real and synthetic
413
      data, achieve even greater improvements, with 10.08% AUC and 7.62% F1 scores on average
414   across all datasets and methods. This demonstrates that the SynCXR dataset effectively enables
415   MedVLP models to learn representative cross-modal features, enhancing their zero-shot classification
416   capability.
417
      Zero-shot Classification on Unseen Diseases. Tab 2a reports the zero-shot classification performance
418
      on unseen diseases. Similar to the results for seen diseases, MedVLP models pretrained on the
419
      synthetic dataset consistently outperform those pretrained on real data, with an average improvement
420   of 2.96% AUC and 0.51% F1 scores. Additionally, models pretrained on the mixed dataset show
421   substantial gains over those trained on real data, with 7.39% AUC and 1.52% F1 scores on average.
422   This indicates that the SynCXR dataset, generated with meticulous quality control and balanced
423   distribution, can increase the generalizability of MedVLP models for unseen diseases prediction.
424
      Zero-shot Visual Grounding. We further evaluate the effectiveness of synthetic data in improving
425
      MedVLP models’ local visual understanding capabilities through zero-shot grounding tasks. Tab
426   2b presents the performance of zero-shot grounding on RSNA (Shih et al., 2019), Covid-19 Rural
427   (Desai et al., 2020), and SIIM (Steven G. Langer & George Shih, 2019). Across all datasets, MedVLP
428   models pretrained on the SynCXR dataset achieve superior performance compared to those trained
429   on the real dataset, with an average increase of 1.42% IoU and 0.97% Dice scores. The mixed dataset
430   further enhances performance, with 4.06% IoU and 2.92% Dice scores on average. This demonstrates
431   that the SynCXR dataset not only benefits global cross-modal feature learning but also improves local
      visual understanding for MedVLP models.

524   6        C ONCLUSION
525
526
      In this work, we tackle the question: Can MedVLP succeed using purely synthetic data? Our
      findings demonstrate that the answer is: Yes. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first study to
527
      comprehensively explore the potential of synthetic data for MedVLP models. We also identify key
528
      limitations in existing real-world datasets and introduce SynCXR—a synthetic dataset of 200,000
529   image-text pairs generated without any manual quality checks. Our findings show that MedVLP
530   models trained on purely synthetic data outperform those trained on real data. Moreover, combining
531   synthetic and real data further boosts model performance, demonstrating the potential of synthetic
532   data to overcome limitations in real-world datasets. We systematically analyze key factors in SynCXR
533   and validate its effectiveness through extensive ablation studies. In summary, we show that MedVLP
534   achieves strong performance using a purely synthetic image-text dataset and benefits significantly
535   from a combination of real and synthetic data. We believe this work will inspire the community
536   to fully leverage synthetic data and mitigate the challenges posed by noisy and limited real-world
537
      datasets.

      A.1        DATASET D ETAILS
813
814   In this section, we provide details on all datasets used. The dataset splits are publicly accessible at12 .
815
      ChestX-ray14 (Wang et al., 2017) includes 112,120 frontal X-rays from 30,805 patients, labeled for
816
      14 diseases. We use the official split and partition it into 80%/10%/10% for train/validation/test.
817
818   PadChest (Bustos et al., 2020) includes 160,868 X-rays from 67,000 patients, annotated with over
819   150 findings. As in (Phan et al., 2024b), three subsets are built based on PadChest: 14 common
820   diseases as PadChest-seen, rare diseases from the NORD database13 as PadChest-rare, and the
821
      remaining diseases as PadChest-unseen. We use the official split provided by (Phan et al., 2024b).
822   RSNA (Shih et al., 2019) contains over 260,000 frontal X-rays annotated with pneumonia masks. We
823   divide it into training (60%), validation (20%), and test (20%) sets for segmentation and classification
824   tasks (Huang et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2023).
825   CheXpert (Irvin et al., 2019) contains 224,316 chest X-rays from 65,240 patients at Stanford Hospital,
826   with an official validation set of 200 studies and a test set of 500 studies, both annotated by board-
827   certified radiologists. Our evaluation on the five observations in the official test set follows protocols
828   from earlier studies (Tiu et al., 2022b; Irvin et al., 2019).
829
      SIIM (Steven G. Langer & George Shih, 2019) consists of over 12,000 frontal X-rays annotated with
830
      pneumothorax masks, split into training (60%), validation (20%), and test (20%) sets.
831
832   COVIDx CXR-2 (Wang et al., 2020) includes 29,986 X-rays from 16,648 COVID-19 patients,
833   divided into training (70%), validation (20%), and test (10%) (Pavlova et al., 2022).
834   COVID Rural (Desai et al., 2020) contains over 200 X-rays with segmentation masks, divided into
835   training (60%), validation (20%), and test (20%).

837   A.2        I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
838
839   Zero-shot Image Classification. The CXR images undergo a two-step preprocessing: resizing to
840   256 × 256, followed by center cropping to 224 × 224. As per (Huang et al., 2021), pixel values
841   are normalized to [0, 1]. The processed image is passed through a visual encoder and projector to
842
      generate the image embedding v̂i . Simultaneously, the text prompts are processed through a text
843   encoder to obtain text embeddings l̂i . Classification is based on cosine similarity between image
844
      and text embeddings. If the similarity between the image embedding and the positive prompt (e.g.,
      disease) is higher than that with the negative prompt (e.g., No disease), the classification is positive,
845
      and vice versa. The prompt design follows (Tiu et al., 2022a) for both ConVIRT and GLoRIA.
846
847   Zero-shot Visual Grounding. For this task, we follow the BioViL pipeline as described in (Phan
848   et al., 2024b), since ConVIRT (Zhang et al., 2020) and GLoRIA (Huang et al., 2021) do not provide
849   code for visual grounding. This pixel-level classification task relies on the similarity between text
850
      embeddings and the dense visual feature map from the final convolutional layer. The cosine similarity
      generates a similarity map, resized to match the image, and used as segmentation results for grounding
851
      evaluation.
852
853   Medical Image Fine-tuned Classification.
854   For fine-tuning, we follow the experimental setup from (Phan et al., 2024b), updating both the visual
855   encoder and linear layer. Images are resized to 256 × 256, and data augmentation is applied as
856   recommended in (Zhang et al., 2023). We use the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of 1 × 10−4 ,
857   batch size of 64, for 50 epochs on a single A100 GPU. Early stopping is applied, with a learning rate
858   of 5e-4 and batch size of 8. AdamW is configured with β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.999, and weight decay of
859   1e-6.

861   SIIM (Steven G. Langer & George Shih, 2019), and Covid-19 Rural (Wang et al., 2020) datasets, we
