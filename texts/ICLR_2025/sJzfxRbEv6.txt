011                                           Abstract
012
013           Graph neural networks (GNNs) have increasingly adopted transformer ar-
014           chitectures to capture long-range dependencies. However, integrating struc-
015           tural information into graph transformers remains challenging, often neces-
016           sitating complex positional encodings or masking strategies. In this paper,
017
              we propose the Moiré Graph Transformer (MoiréGT), which introduces a
              novel focused attention mechanism that eliminates the need for explicit
018
              graph positional encodings. Our model eﬀectively captures structural con-
019
              text without additional encodings or masks by adjusting attention scores
020           based on a learnable focus function of node distances. We theoretically
021           demonstrate that multiple attention heads with diﬀerent focus parameters
022           can implicitly encode positional information akin to moiré patterns. Exper-
023           iments on 3D molecular graphs show that MoiréGT achieves significant per-
024           formance gains over state-of-the-art models on the QM9 and PCQM4Mv2
025           datasets. Additionally, our model achieves competitive results on 2D graph
026           tasks, highlighting its versatility and eﬀectiveness.

029   1   Introduction
030
031   Graph neural networks (GNNs) have incorporated the transformer architecture, leverag-
032
      ing its success in other domains (Ying et al., 2021). Graph transformers (GTs) demonstrate
      strong performance on tasks involving node relationships (Dwivedi & Bresson, 2020), primar-
033
      ily due to their global attention mechanism. In graph contexts, the permutation invariance
034
      of attention is advantageous, as graphs are concerned with topological relationships and are
035   invariant under the SE(3) group (Bronstein et al., 2021).
036
037   However, GTs that rely solely on global attention without incorporating structural adjust-
038
      ments often perform poorly (Kreuzer et al., 2021). This is similar to how fully connected
      layers can fail when lacking inductive biases. Incorporating graph structural information is
039
      crucial for eﬀective learning. While positional encodings have been employed to inject struc-
040
      tural information (Dwivedi et al., 2021), designing encodings that uniquely and meaningfully
041   represent topological positions while preserving invariance properties is challenging.
042
043   Masking techniques can hide irrelevant nodes and implicitly convey structural information,
044
      but it’s limiting the global receptive field of attention mechanisms. This leads us to ask: Can
      we develop a method that leverages attention while preserving structural context, eﬀectively
045
      combining the benefits of positional encoding and masking?
046
047   We introduce the Moiré Graph Transformer (MoiréGT), a graph transformer with a
048   focused attention mechanism. This allows the model to focus on specific ranges of nodes
049   relative to nodes of interest by adjusting attention scores within the self-attention layer. We
050
      use a non-linear focus function with two learnable parameters—shift and width—to empha-
      size nodes within the focus area while filtering out others. Our theoretical analysis shows
051
      that moiré patterns with phase distortions can eﬀectively encode positional information.
052
053   Experimentally, we demonstrate significant performance gains on 3D graphs—QM9
      and PCQM4Mv2—and we also tested our model on 2D graphs to show its versatility.

055
056         • We introduce the focus mechanism, an explicit filtering mechanism based on spatial
057           relationships, providing a strong inductive bias for graph learning tasks.
058         • Our model eliminates the need for a virtual node, reducing computational overhead.
059
            • Extensive experiments demonstrate the eﬃciency and eﬀectiveness of our model,
060
              achieving state-of-the-art results on challenging benchmarks with fewer parameters
061
              than competing models.

064   2     Related Works
065
066   Graphormer (Ying et al., 2021) adds centrality encoding into node features and employs
067   learnable spatial and edge encodings to adjust attention values. Unlike Graphormer, we
068
      filter and emphasize node features solely based on topological distance, providing a simpler
      and more distinguishable method for each node.
069
070   GraphGPS and TokenGT (Rampášek et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2022) utilize orthonor-
071   mal graph Laplacian eigenvectors to represent nodes’ topological locations, which is not
072   suitable for transductive graph learning tasks due to the need for eigen decomposition of
073   the adjacency matrix and large input feature dimensions. Our method does not require
074
      eigendecomposition and can use any node features.
075   GRPE (Park et al., 2022) employs learnable positional encodings derived from the shortest
076   path distance to adjust attention scores, a subset of the approach used in Transformer-M.
077
      Transformer-M (Luo et al., 2022) incorporates degree encodings and sums of 3D distance
078   encodings added to the input node features. It also uses shortest path distance, edge, and
079   3D distance encodings within attention. Despite their complex structure, they did not use
080   this information for filtering as we do, leading to larger parameter numbers without clear
081   performance gains from each component.
082
      EGT (Hussain et al., 2022) adjusts global attention using edge embeddings, eﬀectively infer-
083
      ring edge data. However, without filtering, their global attention lacked suﬃcient inductive
084   bias, resulting in models 5 to 10 times larger than others and requiring clipping to stabilize
085   training.
086
087
      Moreover, these models often require a virtual node to make predictions, adding compu-
      tational overhead due to its global connectivity. In contrast, our model does not require a
088
      virtual node.

110   matrix D and has two learnable parameters—shift (µ) and width (σ)—which allow the

112

113
      to selectively emphasize or mask certain nodes based on their structural distance. This
114
      adjustment eﬀectively incorporates structural information without the need for explicit po-
115   sitional encoding or an additional learnable projection matrix.

120   parameter controls the spread or range of the focus function, adjusting how narrowly or
121   broadly the model attends to nodes around µ. The shift parameter adjusts the center of the
122   focus function, eﬀectively shifting the function along the distance axis.

179   Using the log of the focus function prevents numerical instabilities that could arise from
180   very small values of f (Dij ), allowing for more stable computation and smoother gradients
181   during backpropagation.
182
      Adding Self-loop: To prevent the focus function from diminishing self-attention, especially
183
      when µ is large, we add an identity matrix scaled by a learnable weight to the distance
184   matrix, as D′ = D + Wself I. This ensures that each node maintains a significant attention
185   score for itself.
186
187
      Masking to Prevent Unintended Global Nodes: Zero-padded nodes might uninten-
      tionally create global nodes when padding graphs to create uniform batch sizes. To mitigate
188
      this, we implement masking that prevents attention and feed-forward network computations
189
      to and from these padded nodes.
190
191   Masking Incorrect Distances: In an adjacency matrix context, zero typically indicates
192   no connection, making zero-padding a natural choice. However, this can lead to incorrect
193
      focusing values if the focus function interprets these zeros as valid distances. To address this
      issue, we assign a very large value (e.g., 106 ) to padded nodes’ distances in the matrix D,
194
      preventing any unintended influence from padded elements.
195
196   Clamping µ: To ensure numerical stability and meaningful focusing behavior, we clamp
197   µ to values greater than a minimum threshold µ′ = max(µmin , µ), where µmin = 0.5. This
198   prevents the focus function from becoming undefined or overly narrowed.

200   3.3     MoiréGT Architecture
201
      The Moiré Graph Transformer (MoiréGT) architecture consists of multiple Moiré Layers
202
      that implement the focus mechanism (Figure 2).
203
204   Input and Output Fully Connected Layers: MoiréGT incorporates fully connected
205   layers at both the input and output sides. The input layers preprocess raw node features,
206
      while the output layers map the transformed features to the desired output space. We also
      employed max pooling for the graph-level inference tasks to make embedding tensor before
207
      the output FC layers.
208
209   Moiré Layer: Each Moiré Layer consists of the focused attention mechanism integrated
210   within the self-attention framework. The layer begins by projecting the input node features
211   X into query, key, and value matrices. The focused attention is computed using the adjusted
212
      attention scores, incorporating the focus function as described in Equation equation 2. This
      allows the model to emphasize nodes within certain distance ranges dynamically.
213
214   Feed-Forward Network and Residual Connections: Following the attention mecha-
215   nism, each layer includes a feed-forward network (FFN). Residual connections are employed
      around both the attention and FFN sublayers to prevent over-smoothing problems.

      Overall Architecture: Multiple Moiré Layers are stacked to form the full MoiréGT model,
217   allowing for hierarchical representation learning over the graph. Each layer can have multi-
218   ple attention heads with diﬀerent focus parameters, enabling the model to capture diverse
219   structural patterns akin to the formation of moiré patterns.

252   3.4   Theoretical Foundation and Analysis
253
      The Moiré Graph Transformer (MoiréGT) is inspired by moiré patterns, which arise when
254
      overlapping patterns with slight diﬀerences create complex interference. By integrating
255
      distance-based focus functions into the attention mechanism, MoiréGT implicitly encodes
256   positional information, capturing complex graph structures without explicit positional en-
257   codings. The overlapping of multiple attention heads with varying focus parameters enables
258   the model to capture local and global structural information eﬀectively. For a detailed the-
259   oretical analysis, please refer to the Appendix.

429   5     Conclusion
430
431   We have introduced MoiréGT, a novel graph transformer architecture that eliminates the
      need for explicit positional encodings by leveraging a focused attention mechanism inspired


                                                   8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      by moiré patterns. Our approach removes the reliance on global node tricks and complex
433   positional encodings to infer graph-level properties and node positions, respectively.
434
435   By integrating self-loops and carefully designed focus functions, we stabilize the attention
436
      mechanism and gradients while eﬀectively capturing structural information through implicit
      positional encoding. Our theoretical analysis demonstrates how the superposition of multiple
437
      focus functions can encode complex graph structures akin to the formation of moiré patterns.
438
439   Our experiments on 3D molecular datasets show that MoiréGT achieves state-of-the-art
440   results, significantly outperforming existing models in predicting molecular properties. The
441   model also demonstrates promising performance on 2D graphs.
442   While MoiréGT excels in tasks where physical node locations or meaningful distance mea-
443   sures are available, it underperforms in settings where such information is absent. This
444   suggests that the current focus mechanism relies heavily on distance information, which
445   may not be suitable for all types of graph data.
446   Future work could explore extending the focus mechanism to incorporate topological features
447   or integrating eﬀective positional encoding methods in these settings. We believe that further
448   research can extend MoiréGT’s applicability to a wider range of graph learning tasks.
