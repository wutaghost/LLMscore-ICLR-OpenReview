031                                               A BSTRACT
032
033            This paper introduces gamified adversarial prompting (GAP), a framework that
034            crowd-sources high-quality data for visual instruction tuning of large multimodal
035            models. GAP transforms the data collection process into an engaging game, in-
036            centivizing players to provide fine-grained, challenging questions and answers
037            that target gaps in the model’s knowledge. Our contributions include (1) an ap-
038            proach to capture question-answer pairs from humans that directly address weak-
039            nesses in a model’s knowledge, (2) a method for evaluating and rewarding players
040            that successfully incentivizes them to provide high-quality submissions, and (3) a
041
               scalable, gamified platform that succeeds in collecting this data from over 50,000
               participants in just a few weeks. Our implementation of GAP has significantly im-
042
               proved the accuracy of a small multimodal model, namely MiniCPM-Llama3-V-
043
               2.5-8B, increasing its GPT score from 0.147 to 0.447 on our dataset, approaching
044            the benchmark set by the much larger GPT-4V. Moreover, we demonstrate that
045            the data generated using MiniCPM-Llama3-V-2.5-8B also enhances its perfor-
046            mance across other benchmarks, and exhibits cross-model benefits. Specifically,
047            the same data improves the performance of QWEN2-VL-2B and QWEN2-VL-7B
048            on the same multiple benchmarks.

079
      Large multimodal models (LMMs) have shown impressive capabilities in visual question answering
080   tasks, demonstrating an ability to understand and reason about visual content (Liu et al., 2023a;
081   Dai et al., 2023). However, these models often struggle with fine-grained visual details, factual
082   accuracy, and complex reasoning, particularly when faced with challenging, objective questions (Li
083   et al., 2023c; Yu et al., 2023).
084
      Supervised fine-tuning (SFT) remains a critical yet challenging phase for LLMs and LMMs, espe-
085
      cially in visual question answering. SFT includes instruction tuning, where models are optimized
086   to follow natural language instructions. Visual instruction tuning builds on this by fine-tuning mod-
087   els to handle both visual inputs and textual instructions such as questions or commands related to
088   an image. While visual instruction tuning has led to improvements in LMMs, its effectiveness is
089   constrained by the quality and specificity of the training data (Liu et al., 2023a; Li et al., 2023a).
090
      Our gamified adversarial prompting (GAP) method transforms visual instruction tuning by incen-
091
      tivizing players to come up with challenging, fine-grained questions that target specific weaknesses
092   in a given LMM. The GAP approach focuses on improving factual accuracy and reasoning capabili-
093   ties, particularly for objective, verifiable information more than for subjective preferences or general
094   alignment.
095
      The contributions of this paper include an approach to automatically evaluate and reward player
096
      submissions with high accuracy inspired by the reCAPTCHA approach (Ahn et al., 2008). This
097
      evaluation scheme ensured we were able to scale GAP to over 50,000 players (Figure 2) in just a
098   few weeks, while ensuring high data quality and player engagement.

178   Model Analysis and Robustness Recent work has highlighted the importance of thorough model
179   analysis. Studies have shown that while models achieve human parity on many benchmarks, they
180   can be brittle when faced with out-of-domain (Brown et al., 2020; Talmor & Berant, 2019; Fisch
181   et al., 2019) or adversarial examples (Jia & Liang, 2017; Rajpurkar et al., 2018; Yuan et al., 2019;
182   Ribeiro et al., 2018). Yuan et al. (2023); Raina et al. (2024) observed these issues in LLMs as well.
183   Contrast sets (Gardner et al., 2020) and counterfactual data (Kaushik et al., 2020) have been used
184   to examine model consistency, revealing limitations in current state-of-the-art models. Our dataset
185   generated through GAP can be visualized as a contrast set in relation to the pre-existing knowledge
186   base of an LMM.

      When it comes to data, quality beats quantity (Zhou et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023b). Given an LMM
191
      M , an image i and question q related to the image and the expected answer a, the model response is
192
      denoted r = M (i, q). The tuple (i, q, a) is defined as an informative sample for M if the response
193   r is not factually equivalent to the expected answer a.
194
195   While LMMs are stochastic and generate different responses to the same query, generating a factu-
196
      ally incorrect response is usually a sign of a gap in a model’s knowledge.
197
198   Gameplay One session for one player proceeds as follows:
199
           1. Player sees an image
200
201        2. They ask a question which they believe AI would answer incorrectly
202        3. Player evaluates the AI’s response, they have to mark it ”Correct” or ‘Wrong‘
203
204
           4. If they mark it ‘Wrong‘ they move to the next image, otherwise they can ask more ques-
              tions. Player automatically moves to the next image after 120 secs.
205
206
      For every session, a player sees 10 images and has at most 120 seconds for each image. When
207   the player finishes 10 images, or the session resets after 6 hours, they earn points based on their
208   performance. We time-limit every image to create a sense of urgency, while 10 images per session
209   keeps casual gamers motivated, since they can spend limited time on the game but still play for the
210   cash reward.

256   Evaluation and Scoring In each session a player interacts with five images from the tainted
257   dataset and five from the untainted one. Users are unaware of this split. For each question asked
258   concerning an image from the tainted set, we randomly instruct the model to change its answer sub-
259   tly, introducing a minor inaccuracy. This process ensures that the incorrect answers are plausible
260   and closely related to the truth, making the challenge more nuanced. Participants are scored based
261
      on their ability to identify these deliberate mistakes.
262   Our goal is to obtain questions on the untainted dataset where the model answers incorrectly. We
263   want to encourage players to ask difficult questions where the model gives an incorrect response, but

      GAP-VQA Dataset and Experiment Design To create GAP-VQA, we randomly sampled 3, 683
379   question-image pairs, filtering them using a threshold θ = 0.8 (Equation 3) to ensure a high propor-
380   tion of adversarial examples. While this filtering may include some non-adversarial questions, these
381   do not negatively impact the model; instead, they contribute to the overall diversity of the dataset
382   while having a less pronounced benefit.
383
      Our labeling process combines human expertise with AI refinement to produce high-quality, accurate
384
      answers for these visual questions. Initially, trained in-house labeling experts provide answers based
385   on the given images, ensuring a high baseline of accuracy and leveraging human understanding of
386   context and nuance. Subsequently, we employ Llama 3.1 8B (Dubey et al., 2024) to refine these
387   answers, correcting errors, improving clarity, and maintaining consistency across the dataset.
388
      GAP-VQA is divided into two subsets: GAP-VQA-train, consisting of 2,683 question-answer pairs,
389
      and GAP-VQA-val, comprising 1,000 question-answer pairs. We carry out three separate types of
390
      evaluations:
391
392
          Experiment Type     Description
393
            Same dataset      Fine-tune MiniCPM-Llama3-V-2.5-8B on GAP-VQA-train, then assess ac-
394
                              curacy on GAP-VQA-val. This provides a direct measure of how well the
395                           model learns to generalize across the GAP-VQA dataset.
396         Cross dataset     Evaluate the fine-tuned model on other benchmarks, such as MME, OCR-
397                           Bench, MMBench, and TextVQA. This tests how improved accuracy trans-
398                           fer to standardized multimodal tasks.
399         Cross model       Fine-tune QWEN2-VL-2B and QWEN2-VL-7B on GAP-VQA-train.
400                           Evaluate these models on GAP-VQA-val and other benchmarks. This de-
401                           termines whether the GAP dataset for one model benefits another model,
402                           indicating its potential for broader AI advancement.

407   We choose LoRA over full fine-tuning of the language and vision encoders to facilitate faster exper-
408   imentation, and to reduce GPU memory requirements while mitigating catastrophic forgetting.

413   The GAP-VQA-train dataset includes a diverse set of questions, divided into 13 groups (Table 8 in
414   the Appendix). These questions assess varying levels of image understanding, from simple tasks
415   like object naming and description to more complex ones like identifying anomalies or explaining
416   cultural events. They evaluate object recognition, spatial reasoning, counting, reading text in images,
417   recognizing emotions, estimating time or season, and interpreting art styles. This broad range of
418   questions helps LMMs improve their generalization capabilities.
419
420   Model Performance on the GAP-VQA-val Dataset We evaluated the performance of various
421   models on the GAP-VQA-val dataset using GPT-4 as an evaluator. A comprehensive prompt was
422   used to assess the model’s answers based on criteria including existence, position, count, and color
423   accuracy, with scores ranging from 0 to 1, where 1 indicates perfect alignment. The evaluation
424   prompt is detailed in Appendix A.2.
425
      The GAP-VQA dataset was designed to challenge the MiniCPM model by targeting its weaknesses,
426   guiding the fine-tuning process for better alignment. As shown in Table 4, MiniCPM-Llama3-V-
427   2.5-8B achieved major improvement post-fine-tuning, with a score increase of +0.300 (from 0.147
428   to 0.447). This indicates the dataset’s effectiveness in helping capture complex multimodal rela-
429   tionships. In comparison, Qwen2-VL-2B and Qwen2-VL-7B saw more modest improvements of
430   +0.116 and +0.043, respectively, suggesting Qwen2’s architecture was less suited for the dataset’s
431   complexity. The smaller size of these models, and less aligned pre-training, likely contributed to the
      weaker improvements.

      Cross Dataset Evaluation Our GAP-VQA approach demonstrates that targeted knowledge gap

      filling significantly enhances model generalization (Table 5). By training on carefully curated data

463   models to develop more robust and transferable skills. The enhanced performance on benchmarks
464   like MME, TextVQA, and MM-Vet, whose contents differ substantially from the training data, un-
465   derscores the effectiveness of our method in promoting generalization.

      Cross Model Evaluation Our cross-model evaluation reveals a compelling phenomenon: address-
468   ing knowledge gaps in one model architecture leads to improvements across different model sizes
469   and architectures. This suggests a previously under-explored commonality in multimodal model
470   deficiencies. We observe that GAP-VQA training, initially designed for MiniCPM, significantly
471   enhances both Qwen2-VL-7B and Qwen2-VL-2B models across various benchmarks.
472
      The effectiveness of GAP-VQA across different model architectures suggests fundamental, shared
473
      gaps in multimodal understanding. For instance, both 7B and 2B models show marked improve-
474   ments in LLaVA Bench and MME, with the smaller 2B model exhibiting substantial relative gains.
475   Furthermore, we note that while enhancing performance on targeted tasks, our method preserves or
476   improves capabilities on specialized benchmarks like OCRBench and DocVQA. This indicates that
477   GAP-VQA complements existing model strengths while addressing common weaknesses.

483   of AI model improvement. Our results demonstrate substantial improvements in VQA across multi-
484   ple benchmarks and models. The implications of GAP extend beyond immediate performance gains,
485   suggesting a new paradigm for continuous, scalable AI improvement that leverages human creativity
      and engagement.

513   GAP offers advantages over methods that rely on AI self-assessment or using one AI model to evalu-
514
      ate another. Such approaches can be efficient, but they risk perpetuating or even amplifying existing
      biases and errors. Furthermore, in cases where AI models are trained using outputs from other mod-
515
      els without proper attribution or permission, ethical concerns arise regarding the appropriation of
516
      intellectual property. Our human-in-the-loop approach sidesteps these issues by leveraging human
517   cognition and diverse perspectives to drive model improvement. By relying on human evaluation
518   within a gamified framework, we respect legal and ethical boundaries while providing a more trans-
519   parent method for model improvement. This ensures that our model’s growth is built on a foundation
520   of original, human-verified data, rather than potentially restricted or problematic AI-generated con-
521   tent.

903
      image analysis incorporates player ability, image difficulty, time pressure, and fatigue to estimate
904

907

1035   Model Formulation Let P (Sijk ) be the probability that question k submitted by player i for
1036   image j successfully reveals an AI mistake:
1037
1038
                                 P (Sijk ) = σ(αAi − βDj + τ (1 − tijk /T ) − γFij )                       (19)
1039
1040
1041
       where:
1042
                • σ(x) = 1+e1−x is the logistic function, mapping R → (0, 1)
1043
1044            • Ai ∈ [0, 1] is the ability score of player i
1045
                • Dj ∈ [0, 1] is the difficulty score of image j
1046
1047            • tijk ∈ [0, T ] is the time taken by player i to ask question k for image j
1048
                • T > 0 is the total allowed time per image (e.g., 120 seconds)
1049
1050            • Fij ∈ [0, 1] is the fatigue factor for player i when reaching image j
1051            • α, β, τ, γ > 0 are scaling parameters
1052
1053
       Parameter Explanation and Domain Reasoning
1054
1055            • Ai ∈ [0, 1]: Player ability score. Higher values indicate greater skill in identifying AI mis-
1056              takes. The [0,1] range allows for intuitive interpretation and comparison between players.
1057
1058            • Dj ∈ [0, 1]: Image difficulty score. Higher values represent more challenging images. The
1059
                  [0,1] range facilitates comparison across images and allows for intuitive difficulty scaling.
1060            • α, β > 0: Scaling parameters for player ability and image difficulty, respectively. These
1061              allow the model to adjust the relative importance of these factors.
1062
                • tijk ∈ [0, T ]: Time taken for each question. Bounded by 0 and the maximum allowed time
1063
                  T.
1064
1065            • T > 0: Total allowed time per image, a positive constant defining the time limit.
1066            • τ > 0: Time pressure scaling parameter. This global parameter adjusts the impact of time
1067              pressure across all players and images.
1068
1069
                • Fij ∈ [0, 1]: Fatigue factor. We propose modeling fatigue as:
1070
1071                                                  Fij = 1 − e−λ(j−1)/N                                 (20)
1072
1073              where λ > 0 is a fatigue rate parameter and N is the total number of images. This formu-
1074              lation ensures Fij starts at 0 and asymptotically approaches 1 as j increases, capturing the
1075
                  cumulative effect of fatigue.
1076            • γ > 0: Fatigue scaling parameter, adjusting the overall impact of fatigue on performance.
1077
1078   The term (αAi − βDj ) represents the core interaction between player ability and image difficulty.
1079   The time pressure term τ (1 − tijk /T ) increases the success probability for quicker responses, while
       the fatigue factor γFij decreases it as the session progresses.
