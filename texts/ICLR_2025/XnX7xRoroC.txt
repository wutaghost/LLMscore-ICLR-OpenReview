009                                                      A BSTRACT
010
011             Dataset distillation compresses a large dataset into a small synthetic dataset such
012             that learning on the synthetic dataset approximates learning on the large dataset.
013             Training on the distilled dataset can be performed in as little as one step of gradi-
014             ent descent. We demonstrate that distillation is generalizable to different tasks by
015             distilling reinforcement learning environments into one-batch supervised learn-
016             ing datasets. This demonstrates not only distillation’s ability to compress a re-
017
                inforcement learning task but also its ability to transform one learning modality
                (reinforcement learning) into another (supervised learning). We present a novel
018
                extension of proximal policy optimization for meta-learning and use it in distil-
019
                lation of a multi-dimensional extension of the classic cart-pole problem, all Mu-
020             JoCo environments, and several Atari games. We demonstrate distillation’s ability
021             to compress complex RL environments into one-step supervised learning, explore
022             RL distillation’s generalizability across learner architectures, and demonstrate dis-
023             tilling an environment into the smallest-possible synthetic dataset.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   As deep learning increases in popularity and is used on larger learning tasks, training costs impose
029   a greater cost to the deep learning community and the planet. Methods to reduce training costs are
030   more necessary than ever. We propose a method that can reduce reinforcement learning to a single
031
      step of gradient descent using an extension of dataset distillation.
032   Dataset distillation1 is a technique in which a large dataset is compressed into a synthetic dataset,
033   learnable in as few as a single step of gradient descent (Wang et al., 2018). The performance of
034   a model when trained on the synthetic dataset should approximate the performance of that model
035   when trained on the original dataset. By compressing the original dataset, distillation greatly re-
036   duces the cost of training models; in this work, we demonstrate one-step learning made possible
037
      with distillation. The compression provided by distillation can be used for low-resource training, in-
      terpretability, and data anonymization (Dong et al., 2022). The reduction of training costs resulting
038
      from this compression can accelerate searches such as hyperparameter searches, neural architecture
039
      searches, and high-performance initialization searches, and can produce inexpensive ensembles.
040
041   We propose a generalization of dataset distillation, which we call task distillation. Using techniques
042   similar to dataset distillation, we posit that any learning task can be distilled into a compressed syn-
043
      thetic task. Task distillation allows for transmodal distillations, reducing a more complex learning
      task, such as a reinforcement learning environment, to a simpler learning task, such as a supervised
044
      classification dataset.
045
046   In order to advance task distillation beyond supervised-to-supervised distillation, we explore distill-
047   ing reinforcement learning environments into supervised learning datasets, which we call RL-to-SL
048   distillation. We choose to produce synthetic supervised datasets because supervised learning is the
049
      bread-and-butter of machine learning, with decades of research going into improving SL. We con-
      sider transforming an arbitrary learning task into supervised learning to be an important step to
050
      simplify machine learning. We select RL environments to distill because reinforcement learning has
051
      the added complexity of requiring exploration of the environment. Exploration can cause a host of

      2     BACKGROUND

      2.1   DATASET D ISTILLATION
080
081   Dataset distillation was originally proposed by Wang et al. (2018). This method utilizes meta-
082   gradients to learn a synthetic dataset, based on how well a predictive model trained on the synthetic
083   data performs on the true dataset. This process is split into two nested learning loops: in inner
084   learning, a newly-initialized predictive model is trained on the synthetic dataset. In outer learning,
085   the trained predictive model is tested against the true dataset, and the loss is backpropagated through
086   the inner-learning process to the synthetic dataset. The synthetic dataset is updated with gradient
087   descent, and the updated synthetic dataset is used to train a new predictive model in the next iteration.
088   The goal of dataset distillation is to produce a synthetic dataset {Xd , Yd }θ such that predictive
089   models sampled from a given distribution λϕ ∈ Λ can train on the synthetic dataset to reach high
090   performance on a targeted training task T0 . The original formulation defines a fixed synthetic label
091   vector Yd ; however, in this work we use the soft label formulation of Sucholutsky & Schonlau
092   (2021b), in which Yd is a parameterized vector of real numbers that can be learned along with the
093   synthetic data instances. The sampled learner is trained on the synthetic dataset using an appropriate
094   loss measure; we utilize mean-squared error between the prediction and soft labels. This inner
095
      optimization must be differentiable, as outer learning requires backpropagating through the inner
      optimization. The trained model can be tested against a sample of T0 with an appropriate loss
096
      metric (generally one that would be used in learning T0 directly) to produce the outer meta-loss.
097
098   It is expected that the distillation’s performance is on average less than or equal to the performance
099   of the average model trained directly on the task. This is an expected trade-off for high compression
100   and low training time, as the compressed dataset cannot contain all the information found in the full
101
      task. However, closely approaching this performance is vital for distillation’s use cases.
102   We visualize a task-agnostic version of this formulation of dataset distillation in Figure 1.

175   3     RL- TO -SL D ISTILLATION
176
177   The learner, or distillation-trained predictive model, must be capable of performing on both the true
178   task T0 (i.e. act as an RL agent) and the distilled task Td (i.e. act as a classifier). In RL-to-SL
179   distillation, a learner λ must be capable of both producing a policy p̂ for acting in the outer RL
180
      environment E0 based on a state observation: p̂ = λ(s) for all state observations s in E0 , and label
      prediction in the inner synthetic SL task based on a data instance: ŷ = λ(x), for all x ∈ Xd . Once
181
      distillation is complete, a new learner λ ∈ Λ trained on {Xd , Yd }θ should solve the environment
182
      E0 , or achieve an acceptable episodic reward on E0 (without any direct training on E0 ).
183
184   We define the synthetic data instances and labels to match the dimensionality of the states and
185   actions of the RL environment, respectively. Thus, training the learner, the RL policy network, on
186
      the synthetic task is equivalent to learning to classify synthetic states into an action or regressing a
      state-dependent policy. However, the distiller is not guaranteed to generate data instances that are in
187
      or near the state space, nor label a reasonably state-like instance with a reasonable policy. The only
188
      constraint on the distiller is how well the dataset it produces can train the learners to perform on E0 .
189
190   The distiller only learns information pertinent to task learning. Thus, when we discuss distilling an
191   environment, we mean that the learning trajectories and policies are distilled, not other aspects such
192
      as transition and reward functions.

194   3.1   P ROXIMAL P OLICY M ETA -O PTIMIZATION
195
      We consider PPO to be a great candidate for the distillation outer-learning objective due to the ben-
196
      efits of its trust-region protections. However, a direct adaptation of dataset distillation using PPO
197   as the outer objective is not sufficient to utilize these trust region protections. PPO’s trust region is
198   created by the clip function, which sets the gradient to 0 when the change in policy throughout learn-
199   ing on a given RL trajectory exceeds a threshold set by ϵ. This is because once clipping is applied,
200   infinitesimal changes will not affect the result. This requires that the policy network πϕ be altered
201   throughout training on the RL trajectory. However, only the synthetic dataset is changed by the
202   policy loss during distillation. Thus, we alter dataset distillation such that the agents’ initializations
203   do not change throughout training on a given trajectory (i.e. over a single PPO epoch) to ensure that
204   the only changes reflected in the change in policies is due to optimization on PPO loss, rather than
205
      factors such as parameter initialization or model architecture.
206   We propose a new formulation for task distillation using PPO as the outer objective, and introduce
207   it as Proximal Policy Meta-Optimization (PPMO) for RL-to-SL distillation. This incorporates the
208   policy trust region protections of PPO.

214   inner-training, πϕ0 , defines the policy that is run on the RL environment E0 , creating an RL trajectory

313   4.2   M INIMUM D ISTILLATION S IZE
314
315   Distillation compresses a learning task, transforming it from many-shot learning to finite k-shot
316   learning. k, the number of distilled instances to produce, is an important hyperparameter in distil-
317   lation. Our experiments have determined that varying k has only a small effect on the quality of
318   the distillation, past a certain threshold. Increasing k increases the computation costs of the distilla-
319   tion training non-negligibly, as all k instances are updated in each outer learning step and a distiller
320
      with a larger k requires more epochs on average to converge. However, the end-learning costs are
      negligibly increased, so long as k fits into a single batch on the provided hardware.
321
322   There is, however, a minimum threshold kmin , such that distillation fails when k < kmin . This
323   number is determined by the geometry of the label space (Sucholutsky & Schonlau, 2021a). The
      distilled instances and labels must distinguish the classes or actions from each other. This can be


                                                          6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      done in fewer instances than the number of classes using soft labels. Using soft labels, the minimum
325   number of instances to distinguish the classes of a label space with c discrete values is:
326
327                                                    kmin = ⌈c/2⌉ + 1                                               (3)
328                                                                                                         2
      This allows for what Sucholutsky & Schonlau (2021a) call “less than one”-shot learning, or learning
329   on less than one instance per class.
330
331   Equation 3 was one of multiple demonstrated by Sucholutsky & Schonlau (2021a). We validate this
332
      equation experimentally on ND cart-pole and Atari. Distilling to k ≥ kmin instances results in a
      successful distillation—the expected reward for ∀λ ∈ Λ trained on the distillation is approximately
333
      equal to the reward achieved by λ after direct RL training. As an example, 2D cart-pole distillation
334
      achieves a mean reward of 350 when k = 512 and 365 mean reward when k = kmin = 3. When
335   k < kmin , the distillation is unsuccessful in teaching learners to solve the task, resulting in low
336   average rewards consistent with random performance in cart-pole. 2D cart-pole distilled to k =
337   kmin − 1 = 2 achieves an average of 40.6 reward.
338
      The equation kmin = ⌈c/2⌉ + 1 determines how many instances are required to distinguish the
339
      classes of the action space, assuming all actions are necessary. In ND cart-pole, all actions are
340
      needed to solve the task, and all actions are performed approximately at the same frequency. Thus,
341   the distilled dataset must provide distinction between each action in its soft labels. That is, there
342   must be at least one instance in which the action probability label is high, and one where it is low,
343   relative to the other action probabilities in the label.

347   5       ATARI E NVIRONMENTS
348
349   We distill Atari environments to demonstrate RL-to-SL distillation scaling to more complex and
350   difficult reinforcement learning tasks. The Atari environments have a much larger state and action
351   space than cart-pole, as well as more complex reward functions, providing a significant challenge.
352   We have chosen a small selection of Atari tasks for brevity. We have avoided selecting tasks that
353   are difficult for a standard PPO agent to learn, as finding tasks that can be learned by distillation
354   but not by direct-task learning is beyond the scope of this work. As Atari tasks are mostly open-
355   ended reward maximization problems, we determine the success of the distillation by comparing
356   the average reward achieved by randomly sampled agent initializations from Λ trained on a fully
357   distilled dataset {Xd , Yd }θ , against the average reward achieved by randomly sampled learners from
358   Λ trained directly on the environment using PPO. This is in line with the distillation ratio metric
359
      proposed by Sucholutsky & Schonlau (2021b), which also compares performance after direct-task
      learning versus distilled-task learning.
360
361   For the Atari experiments, we define Λ as the distribution of random initializations of a single
362   architecture αa , derived from the standardized PPO implementation of Huang et al. (2022), as with
363   parameter initialization and all PPO hyperparameters. For the sake of feasibility, we limit the amount
364   of training time for the baseline RL experiments. While it may be possible for the agents to achieve
365
      a higher reward given more training time, it is difficult to determine whether an RL agent has fully
      converged, or if it has reached a temporary plateau in performance; thus, we consider time limits
366
      and apparent convergence are a reasonable early-stop heuristic. In order to provide a more fair
367
      comparison to the converged full distillation results of Centipede, we trained the Centipede agent
368   to 8000 epochs, giving it the same approximate number of update steps as in the fully distilled
369   experiment. Those results are reported, yet note that the reward appeared to plateau at 1000 epochs.
370   To provide a baseline for our RL agents themselves, we ensured our baseline PPO agents reached
371   comparable performance to the results of DQN agents in the work of Mnih et al. (2015).
372
      Due to the increase in complexity in Atari environments versus cart-pole environments, the cost of
373
      distillation increases greatly as well. It may be unreasonable to commit the large amount of required

401       5.1   E NCODER ROLLBACK FOR VARIABLE D IFFICULTY
402
403       Due to the expense of distilling Atari games, we provide a method to scale the complexity of dis-
404
          tillation training, providing evidence for the feasibility of full distillations. We utilize an encoder
          network to simplify the complex state spaces without reducing the dimensionality of the synthetic
405
          dataset. This formulation with the encoder as all but the final layer is defined and explored by Zhou
406
          et al. (2022) as a combination of dataset distillation and neural feature regression. While they use
407       this formulation to train downstream task learners, we expand this method for difficulty scaling.
408
409       An encoder network ε with parameters ξ can be utilized to transform the learner model’s input into
410
          a smaller space. We use our standard 5-layer Atari learner architecture αa and split it along layer
          l, such that the encoder εlξ consists of layers [0, l) of αa and the learner λlϕ consists of layers [l, 5).
411
          The encoder’s output is fed directly into the learner, just as if they were still a single network. The
412
          difference between learner and encoder lies in training: the learner’s parameters are still updated by
413
          inner learning on the distiller’s synthetic task Td , and the learner is resampled from Λl each outer
414       iteration. The encoder, however, is only initialized at the start of training and is trained through outer
415       meta-learning on T0 , rather than inner learning Td . The encoder is trained alongside the distiller and
416       the auxiliary critic network, rather than with the learner.
417
          We provide the encoder formulation of generalized task distillation as Algorithm 2. The encoder is
418
          initialized with the distiller and, unlike the learner, is not reinitialized in the outer loop. The encoder
419
          produces encodings of the data associated with both the synthetic task (line 3) and the real task (line
420       5), which in RL-to-SL distillation includes the synthetic data instances and the environment’s states,
421       respectively. The learner predicts on the encoded data, rather than directly on the tasks’ data (lines
422       4, 6). While the learner’s parameters are updated through inner learning, the encoder’s parameters
423       are updated through outer learning, alongside the distiller (line 8). Note that the distiller could create
424       synthetic data in the encoded space, rather than utilizing the encoder in inner learning. We utilize
425       the encoder in inner learning, as that allows the distiller’s output to be the same dimensionality
426       throughout our encoder rollback experiments; thus, we can ensure that the distiller’s output space is
427       large enough to hold all information needed at any value of l.
428       While distilling with an encoder limits the use of the distillation to training only new learners that
429       utilize that encoder, it does allow scaling the difficulty of distillation for tasks where a full distillation
430       may be too computationally expensive. When l = 0, the encoder is 0 layers long, and thus training is
431       equivalent to distillation without using an encoder, which we refer to as full distillation. When l = 5

      5.2   E XPERIMENTS
460
461
      In the Atari experiments, a 5-layer agent (with architecture αa ) is used. These agents are standard for
462   PPO learning and are capable of learning the tested environments directly. We demonstrate that the
463   environments can also be learned with an encoder with l = 4, where the learner consists of a single
464   linear probe, and for decreasing values of l, at the cost of an increase in computational complexity.
465   We test 100 agents on 100 episodes for each distillation experiment.
466
      In general, as l decreases, the number of outer epochs to convergence increases, though the amount
467
      by which it increases varies across the environments and network layers. This makes it difficult
468   to accurately predict the cost of a full distillation; however, this method does provide a smoother
469   increase in computation cost per step than jumping from reinforcement learning to distillation. We
470   were able to fully distill Centipede, due to its relatively cheap cost of full distillation. The other
471   environments quickly became prohibitively expensive for our resources; however, we assert that full
472   distillations of these environments is possible given sufficient resources (see Table 2).
473
      While these experiments provide evidence for the feasibility of full distillations of these environ-
474   ments, the costs may be prohibitive. However, distillation frontloads computation costs and allows
475   for cheaper training on downstream tasks—as low as one optimization step per model. Given a large
476   enough search space for neural architecture search, distillation is a cost-effective solution. Training
477   Atari agents is expensive, and each agent must explore the environment on its own. Distilling an
478   Atari environment allows for training these agents in a single optimization step without exploration.
479
      For example, our distillation of the Atari Centipede environment took approximately 8 times the
480   number of epochs to train than direct-RL on Centipede. The meta-optimization and inner training
481   slightly increase the cost of each distillation epoch: on our hardware using one NVIDIA A100 GPU,
482   distillation took on average 3.73 seconds per epoch versus 3.25 seconds per epoch for RL; distillation
483   training took 1.15 times longer per epoch than direct RL training. The benefits of distillation come in
484   training multiple models on the trained distiller. Training on the synthetic dataset is relatively cheap,
485   taking one step of SGD on a single batch of data without any interaction with the environment.

509   6   C ONTINUOUS E NVIRONMENTS
510
511   Due to its importance in fields such as robotics, we distill continuous RL environments. We use
512   the MuJoCo-v4 (Multi-Joint dynamics in Contact) environments (Todorov et al., 2012), which have
513   continuous state and action spaces. Continuous PPO uses the state to predict the mean value of each
514   action and learns a fixed log standard deviation for each action, independent of the state. The distilled
515   dataset is treated similarly to the discrete environments. The distilled state trains the learner’s mean
516
      action, while an action-agnostic standard deviation is learned. In addition, due to the continuous
      action space, we opted to distill to k = 64 instances, rather than determining kmin through trial-
517
      and-error. We trained all models for 24 hours on 4 GPUs. Note that some models, such as the
518
      Inverted Double Pendulum and Walker2D may not have converged. We report our results in Table
519   3. The success of this method suggest that distillation can be used on a wide variety of RL tasks.

522   7   D ISCUSSION
523
524   We have demonstrated distillation of cart-pole, Atari, and MuJoCo environments into compact,
525
      single-batch datasets. We have demonstrated its benefits in reducing training costs. While the up-
      front expenses of distillation are not insignificant, for applications that require training many models,
526
      the per-model speedup is significant. The cost may be reduced by using better reinforcement learn-
527
      ing algorithms, better hyperparameters, and parallelization. The performance of distillation-trained
528   models may be improved with a longer distillation process or a robust hyperparameter search. Per-
529   forming more complex experiments on Atari and MuJoCo, such as k-shot learning on different
530   learner distributions, could further increase our understanding of the principles explored in our cart-
531   pole experiments; decreased distillation costs would increase the feasibility of such experiments.
532
      Distillation has been proposed for use in downstream tasks such as data anonymity (Dong et al.,
533
      2022) and neural architecture search (Wang et al., 2018), and could be viable for other search prob-
534   lems, such as finding high-performance models or good parameter initializations. In addition, meth-
535   ods that require multiple end-task models, such as ensembling, may benefit from distillation. How-
536   ever, distillation has not yet been widely adopted for these use cases, likely due to applications being
537   an afterthought in distillation research and to the computational expense of distillation. Generalized
538   task distillation, and RL-to-SL distillation in particular, opens avenues for real-world application by
539   expanding the types of problems that distillation can be used on. We intend for our future work to
      examine use cases of distillation to prove its viability for real-world applications.

      In an effort to create a reinforcement environment with a controllable state and action space, we have
684   expanded the classic control cart-pole problem from a 1 degree-of-freedom problem to an arbitrary
685   N degree-of-freedom problem. This problem contains N dimensions in which the cart can be moved
686   and in which the pole can fall. The observation state consists of N sets of quadruples: the cart’s
687   position, the cart’s velocity, the pole’s angle, and the pole’s angular velocity in the corresponding
688   dimension; or as 4 vectors of size N . We provide a diagram showing two views of 2D cart-pole in
689   Figure 2
690   At each timestep, a force of magnitude F is applied to the cart in a direction selected by the agent.
691   To limit the action space to 2N , we limit the applied force to ±F along one of the N axes, resulting
692   in a force of 0 along all other axes (other than gravity pulling the pole downward). This is similar to
693   the classic 1D cart-pole problem, where the agent can select to apply a force of ±F to the cart along
694   the dimension of the track. Another way of framing ND cart-pole is as N independent cart-pole
695   environments, in which the agent applies force ±F in exactly one environment per timestep, and
696   the other environments receive a force of 0. If the agent fails in any of the parallel cart-poles, it
697   fails in all. Like in standard cart-pole, each iteration provides a reward of +1 until the agent fails.
698
      In all ND cart-pole experiments, we terminate the environment once an accumulated reward of 500
      is reached, an arbitrary reward ceiling often used in standard cart-pole (as unbounded cart-pole can
699
      lead to extremely long episodes).
700

      does not appear to be linear. With a sufficiently high N , the agent cannot act in all dimensions

897   E     D IFFUSION AS A D ISTILLATION BASELINE
898
899   In order to provide a baseline for the distillation process, we trained a diffusion model to learn the
900
      state-action distribution of an expert Centipede RL agent, and used the data generated from the
      diffusion model to train RL agents.
901
902   We trained a single diffusion model until convergence on a dataset of 50,000 state-action pairs from
903   the policy of the RL agent reported in our results.
904   We trained RL models on the diffusion-generated dataset. First, we trained an RL agent on 1600
905   generated pairs (taking approximately 19 hours, approximately doubling the time requirements of
906   distillation training). This agent was tested over 1000 episodes on Centipede and got an average
907   reward of 2067.
908
      We then tested a cheaper method of generating a single-batch dataset of 32 instances (2 per action
909
      class) and allowing the models to train until convergence. This more than triples the data usage of
910   distillation. We tested this on 5 agents over 1000 episodes each and got an overall mean reward of
911   1708; the best performing model got a mean reward of 1980.
912
913
      The Centipede distillation, with a mean reward of 8083 far surpassed these baselines. It beat the first
      in compression size and time, including distillation training. The second method was much faster to
914
      train than to train a distillation, but the end-training of distillation is significantly faster and uses less
915
      data. These results demonstrate that distillation training is not easily replaceable with generative AI
916   methods trained on a near-optimal state-action distribution.
