009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Fine-tuning has become a popular approach to adapting large foundational mod-
012            els to specific tasks. As the size of models and datasets grows, parameter-efficient
013            fine-tuning techniques are increasingly important. One of the most widely used
014            methods is Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA), with adaptation update expressed as the
015            product of two low-rank matrices. While LoRA was shown to possess strong per-
016            formance in fine-tuning, it often underperforms when compared to full-parameter
017            fine-tuning (FPFT). Although many variants of LoRA have been extensively stud-
018            ied empirically, their theoretical optimization analysis is heavily under-explored.
019
               The starting point of our work is a demonstration that LoRA and its two extensions,
               Asymmetric LoRA and Chain of LoRA, indeed encounter convergence issues. To
020
               address these issues, we propose a general optimization framework that rigorously
021
               analyzes the convergence rates of LoRA-based methods. Our approach inherits the
022            empirical benefits of LoRA-style heuristics, but introduces several small but impor-
023            tant algorithmic modifications which turn it into a provably convergent method.
024            Our framework serves as a bridge between FPFT and low-rank adaptation. We
025            provide provable guarantees of convergence to the same solution as FPFT, along
026            with the rate of convergence. Additionally, we present a convergence analysis for
027            smooth, non-convex loss functions, covering gradient descent, stochastic gradient
028            descent, and federated learning settings. Our theoretical findings are supported by
029            experimental results.

032   1     I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Many real-world Deep Learning (DL) applications require adapting a large pre-trained model to
035
      specific tasks in order to improve its performance (Church et al., 2021). This process, known as
      fine-tuning, involves adjusting the model from its pre-trained state to better handle the nuances of
036
      particular tasks or domains. Fine-tuning is a specialized form of transfer learning, where knowledge
037
      gained during pre-training is adapted for new, specific applications (Vrbančič & Podgorelec, 2020).
038
039   Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning. While fine-tuning all model parameters has been effective, mod-
040   ern models with billions of parameters pose significant challenges due to their scale. Full-parameter
041
      fine-tuning is often computationally impractical with standard resources. To address this challenge,
      Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) (He et al., 2021) has emerged as a solution, focusing on up-
042
      dating a subset of parameters only (Richtárik & Takáč, 2016), or adding task-specific modules (Xu
043
      et al., 2023). PEFT reduces computational costs by modifying fewer parameters or adding external
044   modules, enabling more efficient resource use and lowering storage requirements. This approach
045   significantly reduces both training time and computational demands, making it a practical solution
046   for adapting large models to new tasks (Han et al., 2024).
047
048   1.1   L OW-R ANK A DAPTATION (L O RA)
049
050   One of the most popular PEFT methods is Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021). The core
051   idea behind LoRA is that fine-tuning large pre-trained models can be effectively achieved by utilizing
052   lower-dimensional parameter spaces (Li et al., 2018; Aghajanyan et al., 2020). Instead of updating
053   all parameters of a large and potentially dense matrix associated with the weights of a linear layer,
      LoRA works with the product of two trainable low-rank matrices, which significantly reduces the

055   is added to the pre-trained model weights.
056
057   In LoRA (Hu et al., 2021), the weight adaptation is represented as the product of two low-rank
058
      matrices (and a scalar multiplier), resulting in the final model

062   rank and its scaling factor. Typically, since the dimensions of (particularly deep learning) models
063   are enormous, we have rank r ≪ min{m, n}. This approach saves computational resources and
064   minimizes the risk of overfitting or catastrophic forgetting (Biderman et al., 2024). Hence, LoRA has
065   become a lightweight and efficient technique for adapting large models to various tasks, particularly
066   in resource-constrained environments (Sun et al., 2022). It is important to note that W 0 remains
067   fixed and does not receive updates, while A and B are optimized during the training process. The
068   scaling factor α serves as a “step size” for the adaptation, and it is normalized by rank r. The matrix

      (2024).

      1.2   C HAIN OF L O RA (COLA)
074
      While LoRA offers significant computational advantages in practice, it remains less effective than
075   full-parameter fine-tuning (FPFT) if efficiency is not a major concern (Biderman et al., 2024). To
076   balance efficiency and performance, Xia et al. (2024) proposed an iterative method called Chain of
077   LoRA (COLA). Essentially, COLA simply means the successive application of several LoRA updates.
078
      Chain of LoRA (COLA) constructs a sequence of LoRA modules through an iterative process of
079
      parameter fine-tuning, merging, and extending. The chain length is defined by the number of op-
080
      timized LoRA modules. COLA’s central concept involves applying LoRA adaptations iteratively T
081   times. COLA can be summarized as training a LoRA module, merging the updates with the fixed pa-
082   rameters, reinitializing the LoRA matrices, and repeating the process (Xia et al., 2024). The resulting
083   model can be represented by:
084                                                        T −1

085                                        W = W0 +             B A,
                                                         r t=0
086
087   where At and B t indicate the low-rank matrices in the t-th block in the chain, which are typically
088   initialized in the same manner as in standard LoRA. The motivation behind COLA is that standard
089   LoRA may clearly fail to find the optimal adaptation since such an adaptation may not in general
090   be of a low rank. To address this, COLA proposes using a sequence of low-rank matrix decomposi-
091   tions to approximate a middle-to-high-rank update. The hypothesis is that this sequence of updates
092
      can provide a better approximation than a single LoRA adaptation and may be easier to optimize
      compared to learning the optimal adaptation from scratch.

      2.2   N O R EASONABLE T HEORY FOR L OW-R ANK A DAPTATION
126
127
      We claim that a satisfying theoretical understanding of prevalent fine-tuning methods based on low-
128
      rank updates, such as LoRA and COLA, is lacking.
129
130          • First, as already noted by Sun et al. (2024), the LoRA re-parameterization of the domain
131            effectively transforms a smooth Lipschitz loss into a non-smooth Lipschitz loss, which
132
               poses additional theoretical challenges to those related to proper handling of the low-rank
               structure of the updates. While this hints at a possible source of issues with providing a
133
               good theory for methods based on low-rank adaptation, this observation does not on its
134
               own mean that a good theory is impossible to obtain.
135          • More importantly, the existing theoretical analysis of COLA (Xia et al., 2024) replaces low-
136            rank optimization over matrices A and B with full-rank matrix optimization (∆W ). This
137            makes the theoretical analysis irrelevant at worst and unsatisfactory at best as it completely
138            ignores to model and to explain the key component of LoRA: low-rank updates.
139          • Third, it is known that LoRA can be highly sensitive to the choice of the hyper-parameters
140            (Khodak et al., 2021; Kuang et al., 2024). A good theory should be able to explain or
141            remove this issue. No such theory exists, to the best of our knowledge.
142          • Finally, and this is the true starting point of our exploration in this work, we observe that
143
               COLA may simply fail to converge to the optimal solution. We give a simple example (with
               3×3 matrices) of this divergence behavior in Section 3. Hence, COLA is merely a heuristic.
144
               Providing a fix is an open problem – the problem we address in this work.
145
146   While clearly LoRA and COLA are enormously useful in practice, these methods remain mere heuris-
147   tics since they do not come with solid theoretical backing. This is problematic and raises valid
148   concerns about the robustness and reliability of LoRA-type methods in scenarios beyond current
149
      datasets, models and practice.

151   2.3   C ONTRIBUTIONS
152
153   To address the aforementioned fundamental issues of LoRA-type heuristics, and to firmly ground the
154   fine-tuning-via-low-rank adaptation line of work in a theoretically sound algorithmic framework,
155   we propose a new generic low-rank adaptation framework for which we coin the name Randomized
156   Asymmetric Chain of LoRA (RAC-LoRA); see Algorithm 1.
157          • Similarly to COLA (Xia et al., 2024), our method is iterative: we perform a chain of low-
158            rank updates (see Step 2 in Algorithm 1). In each step of the chain, one matrix (e.g., A)
159            is chosen randomly from a pre-defined distribution, and the other (e.g., B) is trainable (see
160            Step 3 in Algorithm 1). Which of these two update matrices is chosen randomly and which
161            one is trainable is decided a-priori, and hence our method is asymmetric in nature, similarly
               to AsymmLoRA (Zhu et al., 2024). We propose two options, depending on which matrix is


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
               trainable and which one is chosen randomly: in Option 1, A is trainable, and in Option 2,
163            B is trainable.
164          • In order to make our framework flexible, we offer a variety of strategies for updating the
165            trainable matrix in each step of the chain. This is possible since in each such step we
166            formulate an auxiliary optimization subproblem in the trainable matrix, and once can thus
167            chose essentially any optimizer for approximately solving it (see Step 4 in Algorithm 1).
168            We theoretically analyze several such optimizers within our RAC-LoRA framework, includ-
169            ing Gradient Descent (GD) in Appendix C (however, we include and describe the theorems
170            in Section 5.2), Random Reshuffling RR in Appendix D, and Stochastic Gradient Descent
171
               (SGD) in Appendix E. See Table 1 for a quick overview. Our analysis applies to the smooth
               nonconvex regime, in which we prove fast sublinear (i.e., O(1/T )) convergence rates to a
172
               stationary point, and fast linear (i.e., O(e−T )) rates to the globally optimal solution under
173
               the Polyak-Łojasiewicz (PL) condition.
174          • The update is applied (see Step 5 in Algorithm 1), and the method moves on to the next
175            step of the chain.

234    Loss of Lipschitz smoothness. Lipschitz continuity of the gradient is a commonly invoked as-
235

236
       too rapidly, which in turn guarantees a controlled behavior of the optimization process, and plays
237
       a key role in establishing convergence rates and in providing stability guarantees for various opti-
238    mization algorithms (Nesterov, 2004; Sun, 2020). A formal definition follows.

245    tation. Specifically, even if the original function f (W ) is Lipschitz smooth, meaning that the gradi-

248    In particular, the function f (W 0 + BA) is not Lipschitz smooth with respect to the set of variables
249

250
       is provided in Theorem 2 of the work by Sun et al. (2024), highlighting the challenges in extending
251
       standard gradient-based methods to such adaptations.

517   7       C ONCLUSION
518
519   In this work, we introduced RAC-LoRA, a framework for parameter-efficient fine-tuning that enables
520   interpolation between low-rank adaptation and full parameter fine-tuning. Motivated by the conver-
521   gence challenges of LoRA, we propose the iterative algorithm RAC-LoRA and provide convergence
522
      guarantees across various settings, including gradient descent, stochastic gradient descent, and ran-
      dom reshuffling. We extended this framework to the federated learning setup, where RAC-LoRA has
523
      advantages over competing algorithms in terms of communication efficiency. Finally, we validate
524
      our theoretical results empirically in both convex problems, such as linear and logistic regression,
525   and non-convex problems, such as MLPs and LLMs, finding that its chaining procedure is advanta-
526   geous in settings where standard low-rank adaptation approaches (such as LoRA and AsymmLoRA)
527   fail to capture the richness of a full-parameter fine-tuning.

       D     A NALYSIS OF RAC-L O RA WITH R ANDOM R ESHUFFLING
1027
1028   The previous results were obtained using full gradients. However, this approach is impractical in
1029   deep learning settings, where calculating full gradients is often infeasible. To analyze stochastic
1030   methods, we assume a finite sum structure for the loss function:
1031
1032                                          "                                   #
                                                                   N
1033                                                 0          1 X        0
                                     min          f (W + ∆W ) =       fi (W + ∆W ) ,                                  (6)
1034                             ∆W ∈Rm×n                       N i=1
1035
1036   where each function fi represents the individual loss function for one sample and N is total number
1037   of datapoints. Next, we analyze a practical variant of stochastic gradient descent (SGD) known
1038   as Random Reshuffling (RR), which involves sampling without replacement. In this method, the
1039
       dataset is shuffled according to a permutation, ensuring that each training sample is used exactly
       once during each epoch.
1040
1041   RR is a variant of SGD in which each data point is used exactly once per epoch, also known as SGD
1042   with sampling without replacement. Many efforts have been made to explain why gradient methods
1043   with reshuffling perform so well in practice, across different types of problems. The convergence
1044   rates for incremental gradient methods with random reshuffling in convex optimization were first
1045
       explored by Nedić & Bertsekas (2001) and later by Bertsekas (2011). In recent years, a lot of focus
       has shifted toward strongly convex problems, with studies showing that RR can outperform SGD.
1046
       For example, Recht & Ré (2012) were among the first to analyze this for quadratic least squares
1047
       problems.
1048
1049   Researchers have also managed to improve results and remove some of the earlier assumptions,
1050   such as second-order smoothness, as seen in works by Jain et al. (2019), Safran & Shamir (2021)
1051
       and Mishchenko et al. (2020). These studies introduced a new way to account for the random
       permutation’s variance, making it easier to analyze both convex and strongly convex cases. There
1052
       have even been extensions into non-convex settings, with results under the PL condition (Ahn et al.,
1053
       2020; Nguyen et al., 2021) and general non-convex smooth cases (Lu et al., 2022; Mishchenko et al.,
1054   2020; Malinovsky et al., 2023c). More recently, tighter lower bounds for strongly convex and PL
1055   functions have been developed (Cha et al., 2023).
1056
1057
       In recent years, there’s also been growing interest in applying these reshuffling techniques to dis-
       tributed and federated learning, which is crucial for training large-scale, decentralized models (Yun
1058
       et al., 2021; Malinovsky et al., 2023b; Sadiev et al., 2022b; Mishchenko et al., 2022a; Cho et al.,
1059
       2023; Malinovsky & Richtárik, 2022; Malinovsky et al., 2023a; Horváth et al., 2022)
1060
1061   To analyze stochastic methods, we need to make assumptions about the variance. The standard
1062   assumption is that the variance is bounded:

       In the previous section, we introduced the Random Reshuffling (RR) method, where each data point
       is used exactly once during each epoch, also known as sampling without replacement. This method
1301
       has demonstrated strong empirical performance across various optimization tasks. However, in this
1302
       section, we shift our focus to the RAC-LoRA framework, where Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD)
1303   is applied with a more general, arbitrary data sampling procedure, allowing for broader flexibility in
1304   how data is selected and used during training.
1305
1306
       The analysis of general sampling schemes in SGD has garnered significant attention in the litera-
       ture, particularly in understanding its impact on convergence rates and optimization performance
1307
       across different problem classes. For strongly convex functions, general sampling methods have
1308
       been rigorously studied in works such as Gower et al. (2019), which provide detailed convergence
1309   guarantees and bounds. In the case of general convex optimization problems, Khaled et al. (2023)
1310   offer a thorough analysis of the performance of SGD under various sampling strategies. Further-
1311   more, for non-convex settings, both Khaled & Richtárik (2020) and Demidovich et al. (2023) have
1312   explored how general sampling procedures influence the convergence behavior and optimization
1313   efficiency of SGD, shedding light on its applicability to a wide range of machine learning tasks.
1314
       In the following sections, we build on these foundational studies to examine how the flexibility of
1315   general sampling in the RAC-LoRA framework can lead to improved convergence in certain scenar-
1316   ios, while also maintaining robust performance across different convexity settings.

1539   We consider the following optimization problem with a double finite-sum structure:
1540
                                                           M     N
1541                                                    1 X 1 X
1542
                            min     f (W 0 + ∆W ) =                 fm,i (W 0 + ∆W ),                    (15)
                         ∆W ∈Rm×n                       M m=1 N i=1
1543
1544   where M is the total number of clients and N is the number of data points on each client. In
1545   the context of Federated Learning, each client maintains its own local loss function fm , which
1546   also follows a finite-sum structure, reflecting the client’s local data. This formulation captures the
1547   decentralized nature of the learning process, where each client performs computations based on their
1548   local dataset.
1549   Federated Learning (FL) (Konečný et al., 2016; Kairouz et al., 2021) is a distributed machine learn-
1550   ing framework that enables multiple devices or clients to collaboratively train a shared model without
1551   sending their raw data to a central server. In contrast to traditional machine learning, where data is
1552   centralized for model training, Federated Learning allows each client to train a local model using its
1553   own data. The clients then share only the updated model parameters with a central server or aggre-
1554   gator. The server aggregates these updates to form a new global model, which is then redistributed
1555   to the clients for further iterations of the process (Konečný et al., 2016). Local Training (LT) is a
1556
       key component of Federated Learning (FL), in which each participating client conducts several local
       optimization steps before synchronizing their model parameters with the central server.
1557
1558   The analysis of LT marked a significant advancement by eliminating the need for data homogeneity
1559   assumptions, as demonstrated by Khaled et al. (2019; 2020). However, later studies by Woodworth
1560   et al. (2020b) and Glasgow et al. (2022) revealed that LocalSGD (also known as FedAvg) has no
1561   communication complexity advantage over minibatch SGD in heterogeneous data settings. Addi-
1562
       tionally, Malinovskiy et al. (2020) analyzed LT methods for general fixed-point problems, while
       Koloskova et al. (2020) explored decentralized aspects of LT.
1563
1564   Although removing the data homogeneity requirement was a major breakthrough, the results were
1565   somewhat discouraging, as they indicated that LT-enhanced GD, or LocalGD, exhibits a sublinear
       convergence rate, which is worse than the linear convergence rate of vanilla GD (Woodworth et al.,


                                                         29
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1566
       2020a). The impact of server-side step sizes was further explored by Malinovsky et al. (2023b) and
1567   Charles & Konečnỳ (2020).
1568
1569   Subsequent LT methods aimed to achieve linear convergence by addressing client drift, which had
1570
       hindered earlier approaches. Scaffold, introduced by Karimireddy et al. (2020), was the first to
       successfully mitigate client drift and achieve a linear convergence rate. Similar methods were later
1571
       proposed by Gorbunov et al. (2021). Although this was a significant breakthrough, these methods
1572
       still have slightly higher or equal communication complexity compared to vanilla GD.
1573
1574   Mishchenko et al. (2022b) recently introduced the ProxSkip method, a simple yet effective approach
1575   to Local Training that achieves provable communication acceleration in the smooth strongly convex
1576
       regime, even with heterogeneous data. In a follow-up article, Malinovsky et al. (2022) expanded
       on ProxSkip, presenting a broad variance reduction framework. Condat & Richtárik (2022) further
1577
       applied ProxSkip to complex splitting schemes involving the sum of three operators in a forward-
1578
       backward setting. Additionally, Sadiev et al. (2022a) and Maranjyan et al. (2022) improved the
1579   computational complexity of ProxSkip while preserving its communication efficiency. Condat et al.
1580   (2023) introduced accelerated Local Training methods allowing client sampling based on ProxSkip,
1581   while Grudzień et al. (2023a;b) proposed an accelerated method using the RandProx approach with
1582   primal and dual updates.
1583
       In practice, Federated Learning faces a fundamental challenge: it is often infeasible for all clients to
1584
       communicate and aggregate updates with the central server simultaneously due to limitations such
1585   as network bandwidth, client availability, or resource constraints. Therefore, rather than requiring
1586   all clients to participate in every round of communication, we adopt a strategy in which only a
1587   randomly selected subset of clients is involved in each aggregation step. This approach relies on
1588   uniform sampling of the clients, ensuring that the selection process is unbiased over time.
1589
       The method operates as follows: in each communication round, the central server sends the current
1590
       global model, denoted by W t , along with the sampled matrix, to the clients chosen to participate in
1591   the current cohort. Each client in this cohort trains a local learnable matrix using an optimization
1592   algorithm (e.g., stochastic gradient descent) based on their local data. After completing the local
1593   updates, the clients send their computed updates (i.e., changes in model parameters) back to the
1594   central server.
1595
       Once the server receives these updates, it aggregates them (e.g., by averaging the updates) to produce
1596
       an updated global model. In addition to the aggregation, the server may perform an additional server-
1597   side update step to further refine the model before broadcasting it in the next round. This iterative
1598   process of local training, communication, and aggregation continues until convergence is achieved
1599   or a predefined stopping criterion is met.
