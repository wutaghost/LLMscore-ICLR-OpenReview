009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Foundational vision-language models have shown impressive performance on
012            various downstream tasks. Yet, there is still a pressing need to update these models
013            later as new tasks or domains become available. Ongoing Continual Learning
014            (CL) research provides techniques to overcome catastrophic forgetting of previous
015            information when new knowledge is acquired. To date, CL techniques focus only
016            on supervised training sessions. This results in significant forgetting yielding
017            inferior performance to even the prior model zero shot performance. In this work,
018
               we argue that test-time data hold great information that can be leveraged in a
               self-supervised manner to refresh the model’s memory of previously learned tasks
019
               and, hence, greatly reduce forgetting at no extra labeling cost. We study how
020
               unsupervised data can be employed online to improve models’ performance on
021            prior tasks upon encountering representative samples. We propose a simple yet
022            effective student-teacher model with gradient-based sparse parameter updates and
023            show significant performance improvements and reduction in forgetting. This could
024            alleviate the role of an offline episodic memory/experience replay buffer.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Foundation models in computer vision have shown impressive performance on various downstream
030   tasks and domains, which renders them a key building block of various solutions, including generative
031   vision language models Li et al. (2022); Chen et al. (2023); Bommasani et al. (2021). However,
032   naively adapting pretrained models to changes in data distribution or new tasks faces the well-known
033
      catastrophic forgetting phenomena McCloskey & Cohen (1989), where new learning sessions interfere
      with what a model has previously acquired. To overcome catastrophic forgetting, Continual Learning
034
      (CL) has emerged as a branch of machine learning to enable models to continuously adapt to evolving
035
      distributions of training samples or supervision signals over time. A variety of approaches have been
036   proposed to mitigate catastrophic forgetting, such as regularization-based methods Kirkpatrick et al.
037   (2017); Maltoni & Lomonaco (2019); Schwarz et al. (2018), external memory approaches Lopez-Paz
038   & Ranzato (2017); Li & Hoiem (2017), and dynamic model architecture techniques Shin et al. (2017);
039   Singh et al. (2024). Most works focus on training models from scratch, which might fail with large
040   pretrained models. The rise of foundation models has fueled interest in combining CL with the
041   strengths of pretrained models. Han et al. (2021); Radford et al. (2021); Ridnik et al. (2021); Caron
042   et al. (2021); Oquab et al. (2023); Radford et al. (2021).
043   Despite the increased attempts to efficiently improve foundational models performance on new
044   streams of data Ermis et al. (2022); Pelosin (2022); Wang et al. (2022e); Smith et al. (2023); Janson
045   et al. (2022); Zhou et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023a); Wang et al. (2022b); Ding et al. (2022); Goyal
046   et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2022d), forgetting is still a significant problem in applications of continual
047   learning Wang et al. (2024); Prabhu et al. (2023). Continual learning systems often process large
048   volumes of unsupervised data throughout their lifecycle. We argue they must learn continuously,
049   regardless of supervision. However, most works focus on supervised training, leaving models static
050
      during testing.
051   Consider an embodied agent with a Vision Language Model (VLM) that must handle new objects,
052   layouts, or skills while still recognizing previously learned tasks during evaluation. To overcome
053   catastrophic forgetting and accumulate knowledge, we propose leveraging test-time data to reinforce
      the model’s understanding of prior tasks. Test-time data reflects the distribution most relevant to the

078   We consider a scenario where a model undergoes continual supervised training, with unsupervised
079   data available during deployment between training phases, offering a chance to mitigate forgetting. To
080
      ensure practical computational overhead, we constrain unsupervised adaptation to an online approach.
      Additionally, stringent data privacy constraints during deployment Verwimp et al. (2023) require
081
      online algorithms that discard samples immediately after processing.
082
083   Test-Time Adaptation (TTA) Sun et al. (2020) and Continual Test-Time Adaptation (CoTTA) Wang
084   et al. (2022a) are related research areas that focus on leveraging test-time data for dynamic model
085   adaptation. These areas focus on adapting the model towards unknown distribution shifts using
086
      test-time data, while our formulation aims to use test-time data to control the model forgetting without
      any assumption of distribution shifts from training to test data.
087
088   To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to explore how test-time data can be leveraged in a
089   continual learning setting to reduce forgetting. We consider the foundation model CLIP Radford
090   et al. (2021) for our experiments since it has been shown to encompass an extensive knowledge base
091   and offer remarkable transferability Rasheed et al. (2023); Pei et al. (2023). It undergoes through
092
      supervised and unsupervised sessions, leveraging the unsupervised data to control forgetting.
093   We propose an effective approach based on student-teacher models with sparse parameter selection
094   based on gradient values. Student and teacher models suggest labels for test data, and the predictions
095   from the most confident model are used to update the student model, where the teacher is updated in
096   an exponential moving average, adding a stability component to the learning process. We show that
097   such a simple approach achieves significant improvements on all studied sequences. Our approach
098
      is stable in class incremental learning (CIL), especially in the challenging setting where no replay
      buffers are used, which in many cases can be a critical bottleneck.
099
100   Our contributions are as follows: 1) We propose a new setting for continual learning where test-time
101   data can be leveraged, especially in the challenging scenario of CIL-CL. 2) We investigate different
102   baselines for this setting. 3) We propose a novel approach that illustrates the utility of test-time data
103   in supervised continual learning and the significant reduction in forgetting without any external replay
104
      buffer.

107   on different components of our approach, Section 5, put forth some limitations of our work, Section 6
      and conclude in Section 7.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
135
136
      Continual Learning: It considers learning in an incremental manner where training data is received
137
      at various time steps (sessions). The typical problem is catastrophic forgetting McCloskey & Cohen
138
      (1989) of previously learned information. We refer to De Lange et al. (2021) for a survey on class
139   incremental learning where different classes are learned at distinct sessions, a setting we consider
140   in this work. Weight regularization methods Aljundi et al. (2018); Kirkpatrick et al. (2017) and
141   functional regularization Li & Hoiem (2017); Asadi et al. (2023) direct the training to stay optimal
142   for tasks of previous sessions via various regularization terms. Experience Replay French (1999) is
143   usually deployed where samples of previous training session data are replayed during new sessions to
144   reduce forgetting. In this work, we consider continual learning with limited or no replay. Our work
145   is orthogonal to other continual learning methods and can be combined with any CL method in the
146   supervised training sessions.
147   Continual Learning from Pre-trained Models: Due to the abundance of powerful pre-trained
148   models Radford et al. (2021); Oquab et al. (2023); Brown et al. (2020), continual learning that
149   begins with a pre-trained model is becoming a popular paradigm. Recent methods like Koh et al.
150   (2022); Boschini et al. (2022) have utilized a Teacher-Student framework for knowledge distillation
151   on previously seen tasks. However, these methods utilize an additional buffer to mitigate catastrophic
152   forgetting. This often entails significant memory Zhou et al. (2022); Prabhu et al. (2023). Additionally,
153
      such methods often face an outdated logit problem, as the memory-stored logits are not updated
      to preserve information on previous tasks. Boschini et al. (2022) addresses this issue by updating
154
      logits stored in the past using task boundary information (e.g., input’s task identity) during training,
155
      but it may not always be available, especially in task-free CL setups. However, foundation models
156   Radford et al. (2021); Oquab et al. (2023) often have a reasonable initial performance on novel tasks,
157   indicating some pre-existing knowledge relevant to these tasks. Zhang et al. (2023b) utilizes this
158   property and preservers generic knowledge by modifying only a small set of parameters based on
159   gradient scoring mechanism. However, this method also suffers from recency bias since the gradient
160   scores are computed for the current task, and only those sparse parameters are updated based on
161   current task scores. Moreover, none of the methods utilize test data in continual learning scenarios
      and leave a strong potential for self-supervised techniques to capture robust feature representations.

      Test Time Adaptation (TTA): TTA has been extensively studied in recent years, focusing on adapting
163   a pre-trained model based on test data. Typically, the goal is to enhance performance on the test data
164   used for adaptation. However, our focus is on using this data to mitigate forgetting of past tasks.
165   Various methods have been proposed for TTA, including those leveraging self-supervised learning
166   Sun et al. (2020), batch normalization Nado et al. (2020); Vianna et al. (2024), entropy minimization
167   Wang et al. (2020); Niu et al. (2023), and pseudo labeling Chen et al. (2022); Li & Hoiem (2017). It
168   is important to note that our method is not merely a modification, a novel variation, or a combination
169   of existing TTA approaches. Unlike typical TTA methods, which primarily address data corruption
170   and show limited benefits when changes are restricted to label distributions, our approach leverages
171
      unsupervised data from previous tasks without requiring it to be corrupted to deliver its advantages.
172   Continual Test-Time Adaptation: Recent work has studied the setting of performing Online Test-
173   Time Adaptation where the distribution of test-time data is changing over time Wang et al. (2022a).
174   This is distinct from the proposed setting as we focus on the setting where the model is updated with
175   supervised data while the test-time data is leveraged to control forgetting on the supervised tasks.

178
179
      We introduce a novel setting for continual learning that leverages test-time data, particularly in
180   the challenging context of Class Incremental Continual Learning (CIL-CL). As depicted in Figure
181   1, this setting allows the deployed model to recover lost knowledge from distributions spanning
182   all previously encountered tasks after each supervised learning session. The model adapts to the
183   current task’s classes while minimizing forgetting of earlier tasks’ classes. Our proposed approach
184   demonstrates how test-time data can significantly reduce forgetting in supervised continual learning,
185   achieving this without relying on an external replay buffer.

242   We propose a simple yet effective method for continual test-time learning, Double Smoothing via
243
      Affine Projected Parameters, aka DoSAPP. Our approach combines two key components: 1) sparse
      and local updates: to reduce forgetting, maintain generalization, and ensure efficient updates, and 2)
244
      teacher-student framework to promote stability in online updates and minimize forgetting. In the
245
      continual test time learning, we can identify two distinct phases of learning, as outlined below.

      PARAMETERS

250   Our primary objective is to swiftly accumulate new knowledge without catastrophically forgetting
251   the generic knowledge both at training and test time. To achieve this, we opt for updating only a
252   small subset of selected parameters. It has been suggested by Zhang et al. (2023b) that for a generic
253   pretrained model like CLIP and a given task, relevant parameters can be identified before training,
254   and updating only those parameters would result in a reduced forgetting of previous knowledge.
255   Further Geva et al. (2020) suggested that MLP blocks in a transformer model emulate key-value
256   neural memories, where the first layer of MLP acts as memory keys operating as pattern detectors.
257   This suggests that for updating knowledge of previously known "patterns", it might be sufficient to
258
      update only the first MLP layer parameters. Thus we limit candidate parameters to the first MLP layer
      parameters of each transformer block in the CLIP model Zhang et al. (2023b). From these candidate
259
      parameters of the first MLP layer of each transformer, we select top-K (K=c) parameters. This results
260
      in efficient training without loss of previously acquired knowledge as all other layers remain frozen.
261

263   as a score of how relevant a parameter is, the larger the gradient magnitude the larger the expected
264
      decrease in loss after small changes to that parameter. We refer to the model being optimized as
      MS . Upon receiving supervised data, we first estimate the most relevant parameters, θ m such that

      To ensure stability later during online updates and reduce forgetting, we utilize a Student-Teacher
      framework Tarvainen & Valpola (2017); Koh et al. (2022); Boschini et al. (2022) where the student
280
      model is denoted by MS (θ S ) and the teacher model is denoted by MT (θ T ).
281
282   During both train and test time, teacher model MT parameters θ T move with exponentially moving
283   average (EMA) of student model parameters θ S . Normally in a teacher-student framework, all
284   teacher model parameters move similarly toward the student parameters with a single smoothing
285
      parameter (momentum). However, in Tables 1 and 3, we show that a single smoothing parameter is
      insufficient and yields poor performance. Indeed, in our case, most of the student model parameters
286
      remain frozen, and only a small portion is updated. We propose that the teacher model’s parameters
287
      corresponding to the student-frozen parameters should move at a different pace than those selected
288   for updates. Therefore we use dual smoothing parameters (referred to as momentum parameters)
289   based on the affine transformation of the binary mask m to adapt the teacher parameters θ T .

465   accuracy. This shows that our approach retains the knowledge on the first task as well as adapts to the
466   current task, with strong generalization performance.

469   5     A BLATION S TUDY
470
471   In this section, we quantitatively analyze the effect of different components of our proposed method
472   DoSAPP. We evaluate the effects of each component incrementally, as seen in Table 3. Starting with
473   only a student and teacher model setup, we subject it to TTL data and this forms our baseline. Next,
474   we compare with localized sparse updates for the first MLP layer of each of the transformer blocks.
475   This gives an increase in performance in 4 out of 5 datasets. It is to be noted that the momentum used
476   to update the teacher model is according to Eq. 3. We then take the union of supervised task masks
477
      to use them at the TTL phase, but this deteriorates performance since the masked parameters and
      unmasked parameters are updated with a single momentum. Finally, we add our dual momentum
478
      approach, which gives the best performance. We also subject our approach to a more challenging
479
      scenario where the tasks in TTL phases are class-imbalanced. Here we sample each task from a
480   symmetric Dirichlet distribution whose concentration parameter is the length of each task. This
481   causes a high imbalance of classes within each task, and sometimes, even absence of certain classes.
482   This imbalanced case is of particular importance since, in real settings, test suites are often skewed.
483   This is done by randomly sampling classes from a Dirichlet distribution. Although the performance
484   is inferior to the balanced case, it should not be interpreted as a drawback. This is because the model
485   should adapt more to the classes that are seen often in TTL phases and loss of performance on rarely
      seen classes is but natural.

500   We highlight the innovative aspect of our approach, which leverages unsupervised test data—readily
501
      available in production environments, to enhance continual learning. Unlike our method, existing
      continual learning (CL) techniques are not inherently designed to incorporate unsupervised test data,
502
      making them less adaptable to this scenario. Indeed, naive approaches to using the unsupervised
503
      data alongside existing methods proved unfruitful in our preliminary analysis. To illustrate this, we
504   combined the best-performing CL method (compared to ours), SPU, with a simple pseudo-labeling
505   baseline, namely SPU + test-time data (Du ). The model is updated with SPU-learned masks using
506   a standard self-labeling approach on test-time data, using the max logit of the model as the label.
507   Further, we integrate RMT Döbler et al. (2023), one of the most recent Test Time Adaptation methods,
508   with SPU Zhang et al. (2023b) and SparsCL Wang et al. (2022c), and observed that our proposed
509   method DoSAPP outperforms all of them as shown in Table 5. This highlights that TTA methods,
510   when fused with continuous supervised training pipeline, cause the model to significantly lose
511   knowledge. As there are long sequences of distinct tasks, it becomes difficult for any TTA method to
512
      adapt to these ever-changing source distributions. Our method mitigates this issue by intuitive usage
      of dual momentum over masked parameters. We further observe that the TTA method gives inferior
513
      performance for almost all datasets in comparison to self-labeling, proving that these methods are
514
      not suitable for deploying under continuous supervised learning and expanding tasks. Further in
515   Appendx A.3 and A.4, we demonstrate the superiority of our method in adapting to noise present
516   in the unsupervised test data and the effect of the proportion of test data Du on the performance of
517   DoSAPP.

      6    L IMITATION
521
522
523   DoSAPP is a robust algorithm which can be potentially applied to any CL technique for unsupervised
524   adaptation of Test Time Data. However, since it utilizes the test data, its primary bottleneck becomes
525   the quality of test data, especially if it’s highly skewed. Another limitation is the increase in the
526   computational budget due to two deployed models: Student-Teacher framework. We address this by
527
      leveraging the efficient sparse parameter selection method.

530   7    D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this work, we discuss how to leverage test-time data to improve models’ representation of previous
533
      tasks, mimicking human learning and striving for real intelligent agents. In summary, to the best of our
534   knowledge, we are the first to explore test-time learning to control forgetting. We show that test-time
535   data can provide a great source of information when leveraged correctly. Our method, DoSAPP, was
536   able to significantly improve over the zero-shot performance of CLIP when continually learning a
537   dataset without any replay and with no specific CL method applied at the supervised training session.
538   DoSAPP is stable due to sparse parameter updates and the weighted EMA teacher-student framework.
539   Further, during TTL, the max-logit in distribution scores makes it more robust to class imbalance
      than other strategies.
