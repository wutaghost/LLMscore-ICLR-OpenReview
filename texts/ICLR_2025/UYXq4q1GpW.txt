009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Unhealthy eating habits are a major contributing factor to public health problems
012            such as the globally rising obesity rate. One way to help solve this problem is
013            by creating systems that can suggest better food choices in order to improve the
014            way people eat. A critical challenge with these systems is making sure they offer
015            1) suggestions that match what users like, while also 2) recommending healthy
016
               foods. In this paper, we introduce a novel food recommender system that provides
               healthy food recommendations similar to what the user has previously eaten. We
017
               used collaborative filtering to generate recommendations and re-ranked the recom-
018
               mendations using a novel health score and a BERT embedding similarity score.
019            We evaluated our system on human subjects by conducting A/B testing on several
020            methods deployed in a web application.
021

023   1     I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Unhealthy eating habits are a major concern worldwide, leading to various health issues such as
026
      obesity, heart disease, and diabetes Gracia (2020); Gonçalves et al. (2021). Numerous studies have
      highlighted the long-term effects of poor diet choices, emphasizing the urgent need for dietary im-
027
      provements across populations Kandel (2023); Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (2023);
028
      Morris et al. (2016). As awareness about the impact of food on health grows, there has been a no-
029   ticeable shift towards healthier eating habits. People are increasingly moving towards foods that not
030   only satisfy their taste buds, but are also healthier.
031
032
      In this context, recommendation systems have a critical role to play. By guiding users towards
      healthier food choices and showing the users the nutritious value of each food items, these systems
033
      can influence eating behaviors. Therefore, in this paper, we present an experiment in which we
034
      develop a system that matches food recommendations to a user’s personal taste and also introduces
035   healthier alternatives. This approach aims to facilitate a gradual shift in eating patterns, promoting
036   a sustainable and healthy diet.
037
038   1.1   M OTIVATION
039
040   In recent years, numerous food recommendation systems Gao et al. (2022); Meng et al. (2020);
041   Pecune et al. (2020); Toledo et al. (2019); Zitouni et al. (2022) have been developed to predict
042   individual preferences and guide food choices based on specific criteria. While these systems have
043   shown relative success in understanding user preferences through historical interactions, there are
044
      still some improvements that can be done:
045          • Incorporating Nutritional Information: Many existing food recommender systems Gao
046            et al. (2022); Toledo et al. (2019); Zitouni et al. (2022) do not integrate comprehensive
047            nutritional data. The system proposed in this paper addresses this gap by integrating de-
048            tailed nutritional profiles for each food item. It considers essential nutrients like protein,
049            fiber, and vitamins while also taking into account harmful components such as sodium and
050            sugars. This dual focus ensures that the recommendations not only cater to users’ taste pref-
051            erences, but also align with their overall health goals, thus avoiding the recommendation of
052
               unhealthy food items.
053          • Semantic Embeddings for Similar Foods: Previous systems have not fully leveraged the po-
               tential of semantic embeddings to capture the relationships between different food items.

               By using BERT, our system can understand and identify foods with similar flavor profiles
055            and culinary attributes. This allows for the recommendation of potentially healthier alterna-

             • User Control and Customization: Our system allows users to customize their recommenda-

070   1.2   O BJECTIVES
071
      The primary objective of this paper is to develop a recommender system that effectively aligns with
072
      individual dietary preferences while promoting healthier eating choices. This objective is under-
073   pinned by several key aims, which collectively address both the technological challenges and the
074   practical applications of improving dietary habits through advanced computing techniques.
075
076         1. Personalization of Recommendations: The first aim is to personalize food recommenda-
077            tions to individual tastes and dietary requirements. This involves analyzing user data to
078            understand preferences and eating patterns, which will then inform the system’s sugges-
079            tions. The system must be able to adapt to user feedback and evolve over time, ensuring
080            that the recommendations remain relevant and appealing to each user.
081         2. Integration of Nutritional Guidelines: Another critical aim is to integrate comprehensive
082            nutritional guidelines into the recommendation process. This involves not only suggesting
083            foods based on taste compatibility, but also ensuring that these recommendations align with
084            healthful eating practices.
085         3. Usability: The system should be user-friendly and accessible to a broad audience. This
086            means designing an interface that is intuitive and engaging. While the focus of this paper
087            is on the backend system, future work will study frontend UI/UX design and usability.
088         4. Evaluation: Finally, the system must include mechanisms for continuous evaluation and
089            improvement. This involves setting up A/B testing to gather user responses and system
090            performance data. These insights will be used to refine the algorithms and decide which
091            models to incorporate in the deployed production system.
092
093   By achieving these objectives, this research aims to bridge the gap between nutritional science and
094
      consumer technology, creating a tool that not only makes healthier eating easier, but also more
      desirable. The ultimate goal is to contribute to public health outcomes by leveraging technology to
095
      influence and improve dietary behaviors on a large scale.

      2     DATA & P REPROCESSING
163
164
      To build our recommender system, we extracted training data from the meal descriptions provided by
165   the COCO Nutrition Database Korpusik et al. (2014; 2016; 2019), which captured 41,424 users’ food
166   preferences. Using a convolutional neural network for semantic tagging Korpusik et al. (2017a);
167   Korpusik & Glass (2017); Korpusik et al. (2017b); Korpusik & Glass (2018; 2019), we identified
168   and extracted individual food items (both the natural language and the matching USDA food codes)
169   that users had eaten. Our data pre-processing yielded a total of 11,548 different foods.
170
      Next, we needed nutritional information for each food item to calculate health scores. For this,
171   we used the USDA Food and Nutrient Database for Dietary Studies (FNDDS) U.S. Department of
172   Agriculture, Agricultural Research Service (2023), which contains a comprehensive list of nutrients
173   such as protein, saturated fats, dietary fiber, sodium, potassium, etc. We used the FNDDS as our
174   food database, which we used to find similar and healthier alternatives to a given food item.
175
      Since our recommendation algorithms, Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) Gower (2014) and
176
      Embarrassingly Shallow AutoEncoders (EASE) Steck (2019), rely on collaborative filtering tech-
177   niques, creating a user-item interaction matrix was a necessary first step. We translated the food
178   preferences data into a matrix where users were represented by rows, and food items were repre-
179   sented by columns.

      3.1     S TEP 1—C OLLABORATIVE F ILTERING FOR P RELIMINARY F OOD R ECOMMENDATIONS
204
205   In our recommendation system, we investigated two collaborative filtering algorithms for the first
206   pass at recommending foods: Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) Gower (2014) and Embarrass-
207   ingly Shallow AutoEncoders (EASE) Steck (2019). Both models give personalized recommenda-
208   tions based on a user-item matrix.

210   3.1.1     SVD M ODEL
211
212
      SVD is a common technique in collaborative filtering to generate recommendations. This method
      factors the user—item matrix into three matrices that uncover latent features underlying the interac-
213
      tions between users and items.
214
215   We have incorporated Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) Kiefer & Wolfowitz into our SVD model
      to optimize the prediction of how much a user would like a particular food item. This iterative

      3.2     EASE M ODEL
232
233   To capture the co-occurrence and interaction strength between different items, we first computed the
234   Gram matrix G from the given user-item matrix X as follows:
235
236                                                   G = XT X                                             (4)
237
238   To prevent overfitting and to make the matrix invertible, we added a regularization parameter λ to
239   each of the diagonal elements of the Gram matrix as follows:
240
241
                                                    Gii = Gii + λ                                          (5)
242
243   An essential minimization step in the EASE model involves inverting the Gram matrix G to solve
244   problems of the form Gx = b. This process can become computationally expensive as the number
245
      of users and items increases. To mitigate this, we employed matrix preconditioners, denoted as P,
      which have demonstrated their efficiency in various applications to improve the matrix inversion pro-
246
      cess Bergamaschi et al. (2003); Mas et al. (2015); Colley et al. (2017). In this approach, the original
247
      problem Gx = b is transformed into the preconditioned system P−1 Gx = P−1 b. Specifically, we
248   used the Jacobi preconditioner, which leverages only the diagonal components of the Gram matrix,
249   significantly reducing the computational overhead of matrix inversion. The Jacobi preconditioner P
250   is computed as follows:
251                                                P = D(G)                                               (6)
252
253   where the function D(X) constructs a diagonal matrix from the diagonal elements of X. As the
254   size and complexity of the data increase, more advanced solvers, such as the adaptive Algebraic
255
      Multigrid method Hu et al. (2019) and multilevel sparsifiers Hu & Lin (2024), can be employed to
      further enhance efficiency.

      3.3     S TEP 2—A DDING H EALTHIER O PTIONS WITH A H EALTH S CORE AND S EMANTIC
              E MBEDDINGS
289
290   While the preliminary recommendations provided by the collaborative filtering step are similar to
291   foods that the user has previously eaten, these recommendations are not necessarily healthier than
292   what the user tends to eat, which is an important aspect of a food recommender system intended
293   to improve diet. To address this shortcoming, we implemented a mechanism with a weighted score
294   balancing similarity to what the user has previously eaten and a health score inspired by FDA guide-
295   lines. While we used equal weights for both parts, this weight could easily be adjusted by the user
296   to either emphasize healthiness or likeability more.
297   Tables 2 and 3 show a comparison of food items based on their similarity to Lucky Charms Cereal,
298   using two different methods: BERT and Word2Vec Mikolov et al. (2013). We chose to use BERT
299   over Word2Vec as it looks at the whole sentence to understand the meaning of words given their
300   context, whereas Word2Vec looks at words individually and uses fixed embeddings without con-
301   text. Note that the second most similar food to Lucky Charms Cereal (after itself) using BERT is
302   Chocolate Lucky Charms Cereal, whereas the most similar using Word2Vec is Honey Nut Cheerios
303   Cereal, which is less similar semantically.
304
305
              1. Embedding Generation: Each food item description is processed through the BERT
                 model to generate vector embeddings. These embeddings capture the semantic content
306
                 of each item in a high-dimensional space.
307
308           2. Clustering Food Items: We use the K-means clustering algorithm to group food items into
309              clusters based on their semantic similarity. Each cluster comprises items that are contextu-
310
                 ally related.
311           3. Finding Similar Items:
312                 • Cluster Selection: We select the food item’s cluster.
313

315                   cluster.
316

317                   most similar to the queried item, providing users with the most contextually related
318                   food alternatives.
