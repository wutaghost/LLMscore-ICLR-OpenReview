009                                                       A BSTRACT
010
011                Dense embedding-based text retrieval—retrieval of relevant passages from knowl-
012                edge databases (KDBs) via deep learning encodings—has emerged as a pow-
013                erful method attaining state-of-the-art search results and popularizing the use
014                of Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG). Still, like other search methods,
015                embedding-based retrieval may be susceptible to search-engine optimization
016                (SEO) attacks, where adversaries promote malicious content by introducing ad-
017                versarial passages to KDBs. To faithfully assess the susceptibility of such systems
018
                   to SEO, this work proposes the GASLITE attack, a mathematically principled
                   gradient-based search method for generating adversarial passages without relying
019
                   on the KDB content or modifying the model. Notably, GASLITE’s passages (1)
020
                   carry adversary-chosen information while (2) achieving high retrieval ranking for
021                a selected query distribution when inserted to KDBs. We extensively evaluated
022                GASLITE, testing it on nine advanced models and comparing it to three baselines
023                under varied threat models, focusing on one well-suited for realistic adversaries
024                targeting queries on a specific concept (e.g., a public figure). We found GASLITE
025                consistently outperformed baselines by ≥140% success rate, in all settings. Par-
026                ticularly, adversaries using GASLITE require minimal effort to manipulate search
027                results—by injecting a negligible amount of adversarial passages (≤0.0001% of
028                the KDB), they could make them visible in the top-10 results for 61-100% of
029
                   unseen concept-specific queries against most evaluated models. Among other
                   contributions, our work also identifies several factors that may influence model
030
                   susceptibility to SEO, including the embedding space’s geometry.1

      1       I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      The rise of deep learning text encoders (Devlin et al., 2019; Reimers & Gurevych, 2019) has popu-
036
      larized the use of learned representation (a.k.a., embeddings) for semantic retrieval (Karpukhin et al.,
037   2020; Lin et al., 2022) in systems that rank relevant text passages from large knowledge databases
038   (KDBs) via vector similarity. Such retrieval systems have proven effective for knowledge-intensive
039   tasks (Ram et al., 2023; Lewis et al., 2020), enabling real-world applications such as search engines
040   (e.g., Meilisearch) and retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) (e.g., Google AI, Cursor).
041
      Still, the popularity of dense embedding-based retrieval raises security concerns due to their reliance
042
      on public KDBs (e.g., Wikipedia or Reddit corpora) vulnerable to poisoning by adversaries (Carlini
043   et al., 2024). A fundamental risk in search systems, and specifically in embedding-based retrieval, is
044   Search-Engine Optimization (SEO), seeking to promote potentially malicious content’s ranking for
045   certain query distributions (Sharma et al., 2019). E.g., attackers may target search results to spread
046   misinformation, or RAG to inject prompts misleading generative models (Greshake et al., 2023).
047

048
      merely poisoning the KDBs via inserting a few carefully crafted samples (without training). These

050   re-rankers, (Song et al., 2020a; Raval & Verma, 2020; Liu et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023) and text
051   embedding-based retrievers (Zhong et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2024; Shafran et al., 2024). However,
052   these attacks target over-simplistic, single-query search, while common SEO does not control the

      2    BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
101
102   Embedding Models and Retrieval Task. Dense sentence embeddings (i.e., learning-based repre-
103   sentations; Cer et al. (2018); Reimers & Gurevych (2019)) have been shown useful in downstream
104
      tasks such as semantic retrieval (Karpukhin et al., 2020), often after a contrastive fine-tuning stage
      (Gao et al., 2021; Izacard et al., 2021), with popular applications for search and RAG (App. A). Con-
105
      cretely, given an embedding model (R), a retrieval KDB (corpus of text passages P = {p1 , p2 , . . . }),
106
      and a query (q), R retrieves the k most relevant passages using vector similarity (e.g., dot product):
107
                               Top-k(q | P, R) := arg sort({EmbR (p) · EmbR (q) | p ∈ P})[−k :]

108   where EmbR (·) the R’s embedding of a given text. This scheme provides an efficient (retrieving
109   cached KDB embeddings via a forward pass followed by matrix multiplication), and flexible (inter-
110   changeable KDB) relevance ranking system. We focus on undermining such systems by inserting a
111   few adversarial passages into KDBs (Fig. 1).
112   Crafting Textual Adversarial Examples. While adversarial examples in computer vision (Szegedy
113   et al., 2014; Biggio et al., 2013) mislead neural network by slightly modifying inputs, generating
114   them in the discrete text domain is more challenging (Carlini et al., 2023). HotFlip (Ebrahimi et al.,
115   2018) pioneered gradient-based methods for text, inspiring work on text adversarial examples for
116   misclassification (Wallace et al., 2019), triggering toxic text generation (Jones et al., 2023), and
117   bypassing LLM alignment (Zou et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023). Building on HotFlip’s mathematical
118   foundation, we propose GASLITE (§4), a gradient-based method for crafting optimized adversarial
119
      passages, experimentally showcasing its superiority in retrieval attacks, even compared to powerful
      LLM discrete optimizers like GCG (Zou et al., 2023).
120
121   Poisoning Attacks. Differently than data poisoning attacks, that contaminate training data (Biggio
122   et al., 2012; Shafahi et al., 2018), KDB poisoning attacks insert a small amount of fully-controlled
123   new samples, without retraining the model (Zhou et al., 2020; Zhong et al., 2023). Our attack, like
124   others of the latter type, targets models that use datasets at inference time, specifically, we poison
125
      textual KDB in embedding-based retrieval.
126   Attacking Text Retrieval via KDB Poisoning. Recent work has demonstrated the feasibility of re-
127   trieval KDB poisoning (Zhong et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2024; Shafran et al., 2024) and its utilization
128   for misleading LLMs (Greshake et al., 2023; Kumar & Lakkaraju, 2024), particularly in the context
129   of RAG. Yet, these studies either employ baseline methods for KDB poisoning or assume retrieval is
130   successfully misled (often due to focusing on the generative RAG component), and are limited in tar-
131
      geted retrievers and query distributions. We focus on retrieval vulnerabilities, proposing GASLITE,
      which markedly outperforms past attacks (§6) allowing reliable assessment of retrievers’ robustness.
132
      Moreover, this work offers and employs more stringent threat models relevant to practical SEO (§3).

      3       T HREAT M ODEL
135
136   Our threat model considers an attacker targeting an embedding-based retrieval model, aiming to
137   promote information by inserting strategically-crafted passages into the retrieval KDB.
138   Attacker Goal. The attacker aims to promote malicious information (info) for queries distributed
139   in DQ̌ and retriever model R. Specifically, the attacker aspires to: (1) maximize visibility of the
140   adversarial passage in top-ranked results for targeted queries (∼ DQ̌ ); (2) ensure the passage is
141   informative, conveying attacker-chosen content. Additionally, the attacker may prioritize stealth by
142   imposing constraints on the crafted passages (e.g., fluency) to evade potential defenses (App. G).
143   Attacker Capabilities. The attacker can poison the retrieval KDB (a set P) by inserting adversarial
144                                (1)  (2)
      text passages, Padv := {padv , padv , . . . } whose amount (|Padv |) defines the attack budget, with
145
      |Padv | ≪ |P| (e.g., |Padv | = 10−5 × |P|). Such poisoning capability is also assumed in prior
146   work (Zhong et al., 2023; Shafran et al., 2024; Zou et al., 2024) and is practical, as many retrieval-
147   aided systems rely on textual KDBs from untrusted sourced (Carlini et al., 2024), including, e.g.,
148   large public corpora (e.g., Wikipedia, open-source documentations, or even Reddit comments2 ),
149   app-specific sources (e.g., customer-service records), and other attacker-created content (e.g., web
150   pages). We emphasize that our attacker controls adversarial passages’ text, per realistic settings, not
151   the input tokens to models, as assumed in prior work (Zhong et al. (2023); see App. I).
152   We further assume white-box access (i.e., attackers can access R’s weights) to examine retrievers’
153   worst-case behavior, justified by the widespread use of open-source, leading models (Muennighoff
154   et al., 2023), with some used in real-world applications (e.g., Meilisearch). Moreover, worst-case
155   attacks can serve at the basis of defenses (Madry et al., 2018), and black-box attacks (see App. H.7).
156   However, we emphasize that attackers cannot control model weights or training, nor do they have
157   read access to the KDB P, as it often dynamically changes (e.g., Wikipedia is constantly updated).
158   Targeted Query Distribution (DQ̌ ) and Attacker’s Knowledge. We consider three levels of at-
159   tacker knowledge about targeted queries, reflecting different SEO settings. Later, we evaluate all
160   three variants (§5–6) and focus on the second, arguing it better reflects typical SEO scenarios.

      1. “Knows All” Targeted Queries. The attacker targets a known, finite set of queries Q (i.e.,
163      DQ̌ := Uniform({q1 , . . . , q|Q| })). Prior attacks (Zou et al., 2024; Shafran et al., 2024; Song

165
      2. “Knows What” Kind of Queries To Target. The attacker aims to poison a specific concept,
166      namely, to target queries related to a particular theme. DQ̌ has potentially infinite support, with
167      the attacker possessing (or synthetically generating) only a sample of queries Q.
168      We propose and focus on this setting as it aligns with common SEO goals, where targeted queries
169      typically depend on the promoted content or attack context. For instance, when spreading mis-
170      information about a public figure in user-facing search, the relevant victims are users submitting
171      queries related to the figure. Similarly, when targeting RAG, attackers aim for the indirect prompt
172      injection string to be retrieved for queries related to the attack context (e.g., queries about sched-
173      ules and meetings when targeting a personal calendar RAG) (Greshake et al., 2023).
174   3. “Knows (Almost) Nothing” About Targeted Queries. The attacker indiscriminately targets
175      a broad query distribution DQ̌ with significant lexical and semantic variations. The attacker is
176      only given a sample of queries Q and seeks to generalize to unseen queries. This variant was also
177
         evaluated by Zhong et al. (2023).

      4.2    O PTIMIZATION W ITH GASLITE
221
222   To systematically optimize toward the objective in Eq. 2, we introduce GASLITE (Gradient-based
223   Approximated Search for maLIcious Text Embeddings), a multi-coordinate ascent gradient-based
224   algorithm that iteratively refines textual triggers to maximize the similarity between adversarial pas-
225   sages and target queries within the embedding space (Eq. 2). We now describe GASLITE (Alg. 1),
226
      outlining the critical design decisions, and demonstrate its superiority as an optimizer for attacking
      retrieval compared to prior optimizers (Fig. 2), with more comprehensive evaluation to follow (§6).
227
228   While continuous optimization of Eq. 2 is straightforward, involving only the computation of the
229   query centroid, the challenge lies in finding a text padv , in a discrete space, that satisfies this ob-
230   jective. Prior attacks on NLP models (§2) have addressed this by leveraging gradient information
231   to reduce the search space. Specifically, Ebrahimi et al. (2018) proposed a well-established mathe-
232
      matical scheme, which we employ in our attack and is at the foundation of several LLM jailbreaks
      (GCG (Zou et al., 2023), ARCA (Jones et al., 2023)) and retrieval attacks (Cor.Pois. (Zhong
233
      et al., 2023)). This scheme: (1) computes the gradient of the objective w.r.t. the input to estimate a
234
      linear approximation (i.e., first-order Taylor) over all tokens; (2) uses this approximation to identify
235   promising candidates for token substitutions (i.e., ones that are likely to increase the objective); and
236   (3) evaluates the exact objective for these candidates (via forward passes), and performs the best
237   substitution evaluated.

307   2013; Zou et al., 2024; Shafran et al., 2024), we use stuffing—i.e., filling the trigger with
308   sample queries (App. E). Third, we employ Cor.Pois. attack (Zhong et al., 2023) as a strong
309
      baseline, using the original implementation, while allowing the attack to operate under its more
      permissive threat model where the attacker can access the KDB. For fair evaluation, all methods
310
      perform query partitioning using k-means (§4.1) and share trigger length (ℓ = 100).

312   Metrics. As informativeness is inherent in the attacks (info serves as a prefix in crafted passages),
313   we measure the attack success in terms of visibility. To this end, we adopt the well-established metric
314   of appeared@k (Zhong et al., 2023; Song et al., 2020a), measuring the proportion of queries for
315
      which at least one adversarial passage (padv ∈ Padv ) appears in the top-k results; we typically set
      k=10, per common search apps (e.g., the first page of Google search commonly displays 10 results),
316
      taking measurements over held-out queries (except in Knows All attacks in §6.1).

      7.3   L IMITATIONS
511
512   While GASLITE demonstrates improved speed compared to previous attacks on text models
513   (Fig. 2a), similarly to other gradient-based attacks, it remains compute-intensive, requiring approx-
514   imately one hour on a GTX-3090 (24GB VRAM) per run (App. H.8). This computational demand
515   has constrained the maximum evaluated attack budget, although, in certain scenarios (e.g., §6.3), in-
516   creasing the budget could potentially enhance attack performance (§7.1), underscoring both the po-
517   tential and risks of more efficient attacks. Additionally, like other text-domain attacks, GASLITE’s
518   success can be mitigated by defenses such as perplexity filtering to eliminate non-fluent passages
519   (Jain et al., 2023). Still, an effort can be made to bypass these defenses, as demonstrated with
520
      GASLITE in App. G, and noted in recent LLM jailbreak research (Paulus et al., 2024).

      8     C ONCLUSION
523
524
525
      This work highlights the potential risks in embedding-based search through our method GASLITE
      (§4), surpassing prior approaches (§4.2, §6). Our extensive evaluation across nine widely used
526
      retrievers and three different threat models, demonstrates embedding-based search’s susceptibility
527
      to SEO via KDB poisoning. In particular, promoting concept-specific information with GASLITE
528   can be done efficiently (requiring <0.0001% poisoning rate) and with high success (Fig. 3, §6.2),
529   nearing the performance of a strong hypothetical attack (perfect in §7.1). Considering other
530   SEO settings, we find single-query attacks possible with optimal success (Tab. 1, §6.1), as opposed
531   to indiscriminately targeting a set of diverse queries, which we find more challenging and budget-
532   demanding, albeit possible (Fig. 5, §6.3). Furthermore, we observe some models are consistently
533   more vulnerable to attacks than others; we identify factors potentially affecting model susceptibility
534   to KDB poisoning (§7.2), including embedding space geometry and the anisotropy phenomenon.
535   Future work may explore further constraining retrieval attacks (e.g., requiring fluency), following
536   our initial defense-bypass results (App. G). Additionally, as our formulation reduces KDB poisoning
537   attacks to controlled embedding-inversion to text (Eq. 2), recent advances in such methods (Morris
538   et al., 2023) may enable the development of more efficient attacks. Lastly, our insights into model
539   susceptibility variance (§7.2) may provide a foundation for further exploration of embedding-based
      retrieval robustness.

542
      Our paper proposes a practical attack against embedding-based search via KDB poisoning, demon-
543   strated on widely-used models. While our work aims to advance the security of embedding-based
544   text retrieval systems, we recognize the potential for misuse. After careful consideration, we believe
545   the benefits of publishing this research outweigh the potential risks for several reasons.
546
      First, by disclosing the existence of such attacks we aim to promote awareness and transparency
547
      about the limitations of embedding-based retrieval, allowing users and stakeholders to make in-
548
      formed decisions about usage of such systems, and encouraging more cautious integration in sen-
549   sitive applications including weighting the trustworthiness of sources used as retrieval KDBs. Sec-
550   ond, the availability of such attacks offers researchers a valuable tool to assess model robustness
551   and evaluate different defense strategies; this can accelerate the development of effective mitiga-
552   tions. Finally, publicizing the attack methodology and source code establishes a foundation for
553   further research building upon it; this includes deeper exploration and interpretation of the underly-
554   ing vulnerabilities in NLP models (as showcased in §7.2), and discovery and evaluation of defenses
555   (similarly to §G).
556   Possible Mitigations. There are several ways to potentially mitigate the proposed attack. First,
557   previous work leverages attack artifacts, such as the text peculiarity or anomalous ℓp -norm, to detect
558   adversarial passages (Jain et al., 2023; Zhong et al., 2023). Indeed, our study (App. G) finds these
559   defenses to degrade attack performance, albeit adaptive variants (e.g., GASLITE that crafts fluent
560   adversarial passages) circumvent these more successfully (App. F.4). Second, as our attack focuses
561   on dense retrieval, incorporating sparse retrieval such as BM25 (Robertson & Zaragoza, 2009) into
562
      the KDB search method (e.g. via filtering, or hybrid ranking) may degrade attack success.

885   achieving the highest objective, and use the modified t for the indices to follow. We observe that
886   this both accelerates the attack and improves the optimization (App. F). Acceleration stems from
887   re-using the gradient (i.e., the linear approximation) that is calculated once per iteration but used for
888   multiple coordinate steps, thus reducing the amount of required backward passes per substitution.

892
      Per §4, our formalized objective (Eq. 2), and its corresponding optimizer (GASLITE) are aimed for
893
      a budget of a single adversarial passage (|Padv |=1). To generalize this to attacks of larger budget we
894
      partition the available query set (a set of embedding vectors) and attack each query subset separately
895   with a single-passage budget (App. D.1). We prove that a method for finding the optimally visible
896   partition is NP-Hard (App. D.2), and choose k-means (Lloyd, 1982) as our partitioning method after
897   empirically finding it superior relative to other methods (App. D.3).

      D.1   ATTACKING WITH MULTI - PASSAGE BUDGET
900
901
      Given a set of available queries Q and a budget size B, we attack Q with multiple instances of
902   GASLITE, each crafts a different adversarial passage for a different partition of queries (e.g., a k-
903   means cluster), as detailed in Alg. 2. Geometrically, this means we divide the attacked subspace
904   according to the allowed budget, placing adversarial passages in the different directions within it.
905   Subsequently, this process costs B runs of GASLITE and results with |Padv | = B adversarial
906   passages.

       G    D EFENSES AND ATTACKS AGAINST T HEM
1368
1369   We evaluate GASLITE against previously proposed detection-based baseline defenses (Jain et al.,
1370   2023; Alon & Kamfonas, 2023; Zhong et al., 2023)—specifically, perplexity-based and norm-
1371   based—and attempt relatively simple approaches for bypassing these with GASLITE.
1372   Concretely, for the detections we set a threshold of zero false-positives (e.g., highest perplexity of be-
1373   nign passages), and for the evasions we add an evasion term (e.g., an LM perplexity) to the optimized
1374   adversarial objective as well as trying simpler methods (more details in App. F.4). Demonstrating
1375   on two different models—picked for their high benign performance (E5 on fluency-based detection,
1376   and Contriever-MS for L2-based detection)—, we find evading detection possible, albeit comes with
1377   a price of a decrease in the attack success (Fig. 15). Future work may optimize directly under this
1378   setting, possibly employing recent advances in fluent LLM jailbreaks (Paulus et al., 2024).
1379   ℓ2 -Norm-based Detection. As noted by Zhong et al. (2023), we observe that attacking dot-product
1380   similarity models results with crafted passages of large ℓ2 norm; this can be used to identify anoma-
1381   lous adversarial passages, by filtering passages surpassing the maximal benign ℓ2 (Fig. 15d). En-
1382   riching the adversarial objective adding a term to penalize large-ℓ2 passages (GASLITE-L2) mostly
1383   fails to achieve low ℓ2 (Fig. 15d) resulting with a significant performance decrease (Fig. 15c).
1384   Fluency-based Detection. GASLITE, similarly to many other attacks (e.g., Zhong et al. (2023);
1385   Zou et al. (2023)), may result with non-fluent and non-sensical triggers, which can be exploited
1386   to identify adversarial passages via perplexity filtering (Jain et al., 2023; Alon & Kamfonas,
1387   2023) (Fig. 15b). Enriching the adversarial objective by adding a GPT2 (Radford et al., 2019)
1388   perplexity term (GASLITE-Flu) preserves roughly half of the attack success (Fig. 15a) while
1389   completely assimilating in the GPT2’s perplexity distribution of the benign passages (Fig. 15b).
1390   We find even simpler approaches to perform well (Fig. 15a), although they present slightly
1391   higher perplexity (Fig. 15b); these include limiting the trigger length to ℓ = 10 (GASLITE10),
1392
       and sampling token candidates, through the optimization, only from the top-1% logits of GPT2
       (GASLITE-FluLogits).

1849   these we prompt Claude-3.5-Sonnet 28 with the following few-shot template of 3 example queries:

       source queries (e.g., a sample of MSMARCO’s train set) and evaluate poisoning with those passages

1924   H.7            T RANSFERABILITY ACROSS M ODELS
1925   Applying GASLITE, a gradient-based method, requires access to model weights. However, prior
1926   work demonstrated success in transferring attacks on one model (that enables white-box access) to
1927   another model (with black-box access) (Szegedy et al., 2014; Zou et al., 2023). In what follows,
1928   we study the transferability of GASLITE under the different threat models. Note, however, that
1929   GASLITE was not explicitly optimized for transferability.
1930   To assess GASLITE’s transferability, we take the adversarial passages crafted for the different threat
1931   models (§6), and evaluate them as attacks against all models. Results in Fig. 21 show transferability
1932   occurs mainly within model families; for example, Contriever and Contriever-MS, or aMPNet and
1933   mMPNet (which share backbone architecture), or Arctic and E5 (Arctic was trained based on E5).
1934   Additionally, transferability is weaker in the more challenging threat models (e.g., Knows Nothing)
1935   that target a wide range of queries.

2014   I        O N P RACTICAL C ONSIDERATIONS IN P OISONING A KDB
2015
2016   Our threat model (§3), and accordingly, our evaluation (§6), assume the attacker can only insert text
2017   passages, which are then given as input text to the embedding model for retrieval. A weaker threat
2018   model may assume that attacker can control directly and exactly the retriever input tokens. In what
2019
       follows, we argue the latter threat model is more permitting and less realistic, nonetheless, our attack
       outperforms prior attack under it as well.
2020
2021   First, poisoning a retrieval KDB is mostly done via insertion of the malicious text (e.g., uploading
2022   code section to a public repository or paragraphs to Wikipedia), as a result the input tokens depend
2023   on the tokenization process of the embedding model. Perhaps surprisingly, this capability is strictly
2024   weaker than directly controlling the input tokens, in particular, for many tokenizers (e.g., BERT’s;
2025
       Devlin et al. (2019)), there exist token lists that cannot be reached from any text, as demonstrated in
       Tab. 14

2037   To cope under this practical setting, our attack includes a step we call retokenization (Alg. 1, Line
2038   10), that ensures the attack produces a passage with reversible tokenization; recall the attack op-
2039   timizes the input token list (§4.2). Specifically, in that step, we decode all the crafted candidates
2040   of adversarial passages, and discard those that re-tokenizing them results with a different token list
2041   than the one produced by the attack (i.e., encode(decode(x))! = x where x is the token list of a
2042
       candidate crafted by the attack). We observe, through ablation study, the positive contribution of
       this step to our method (+10% attack success rate; App. F). We note that allowing the evaluation of
2043
       our attack to be directly on the crafted input tokens does not affect the attack success, namely our
2044
       attack is invariant to allowing this stronger capability.
2045
2046   Differently, prior attacks benefit from allowing the non-realistic control of the adversarial passage
2047   input tokens. Specifically, we observe that evaluating Cor.Pois. directly on the crafted input
2048
       tokens results in an increased attack success rate (Cor.Pois. [on tok.] in Fig. 16), however,
       even under this permitting setting GASLITE still outperforms all prior attacks (while inserting text
2049
       passages).

2051   Finally, we note that even when the crafted input tokens are considered reversible, there could be
       more aspects relating the configuration of the tokenizer’s encoding that should be considered in a

2052
       successful attack. For example, a tokenizer’s encoding may escape default tokenization of special
2053   tokens (e.g., to avoid encoding user’s text such as [PAD] as a padding token, but rather as the string
2054   ”[PAD]”).29 30 In this example, the performance of an attack that produces a passage with special
2055   tokens is expected to deteriorate. Notably, we find Cor.Pois. (Zhong et al., 2023) tend to create
2056   such samples (e.g., Tab. 10), however, we did not apply such escaping during evaluation. In response
2057   to these kind of issues, in our method, we do not allow the use of special tokens and non-printable
2058   tokens for crafting the passage. Our results (§6) show that GASLITE-like attacks are possible even
2059   if the recommended security practices, such as special-tokens escaping, are applied.
