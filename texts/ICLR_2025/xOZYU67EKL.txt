009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Neuro-symbolic learning (NSL) aims to integrate neural networks with symbolic
012            reasoning approaches to enhance the interpretability of machine learning mod-
013            els. Existing methods mostly focus on the long dependency problem of sym-
014            bolic learning. The important challenge of complex categorization is largely over-
015            looked. To bridge this gap, we propose the Mixed Multinomial Distribution-based
016            NSL (MMD-NSL) framework. It seamlessly integrates the handling of long de-
017            pendency chains and complex semantic categorization within Knowledge Graphs
018
               (KGs). By introducing a continuous Mixed Multinomial Logic Semantic Dis-
               tribution, we extend traditional Markov Logic Networks (MLN) to incorporate
019
               context-aware semantic embeddings. Our theoretical innovations, including a bi-
020
               jective mapping between MLNs and continuous multinomial distributions, enable
021            the capture of intricate dependencies and varied contexts crucial for NSL tasks.
022            The framework leverages a bilevel optimization strategy, where a transformer-
023            based upper level dynamically learns mixing coefficients akin to attention mecha-
024            nisms, while the lower level optimizes rule weights for learning both context and
025            rule patterns. Extensive experiments on the DWIE benchmarking datasets demon-
026            strate significant advantages of MMD-NSL over four state-of-the-art approaches. It
027            achieves 10.47% higher F1-scores on average than the best-performing baseline
028            across 23 sub-datasets. It advances continuous probabilistic models for neuro-
029
               symbolic reasoning and complex relational tasks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Neuro-symbolic learning (NSL) d’Avila Garcez et al. (2012); Lamb et al. (2020); Besold et al. (2021)
034
      represents a promising frontier in artificial intelligence, aiming to integrate the robustness of neural
035
      networks LeCun et al. (2015); Schmidhuber (2015) with the interpretability and formal reasoning
036   capabilities of symbolic approaches Newell & Simon (1956); McCarthy (1960). By combining the
037   two, NSL seeks to address the limitations of traditional neural models, which often struggle with
038   transparency and the ability to handle structured, rule-based reasoning tasks.
039
      A key objective of NSL is to enhance question-answering (QA) systems by enabling logical reason-
040
      ing over complex logic steps. These reasoning processes resemble the “chain of thought” prompt
041
      approach Wei et al. (2022); Kojima et al. (2022) utilized by large language models (LLMs) Brown
042   et al. (2020); OpenAI (2023) to reason through problems step by step. As shown in Fig 1, NSL
043   leverages accumulated historical data to refine the reasoning process, moving beyond traditional
044   symbolic learning approaches that rely on directly learning hard rules. Instead, NSL emphasizes
045   the discovery of higher-level patterns in a differentiable framework, allowing for robust handling of
046   logically similar questions.
047
      Consider, for example in Fig 1, two related QA tasks: Under what circumstances do many doctors
048   appear at the schoolyard? and Under what circumstances do police choose to patrol at intersec-
049   tions? Both questions require reasoning about specific categories of people appearing in particular
050   locations. For the second question, reasoning paths might involve long dependency chains such as
051   Car accidents → Traffic jams → Conflicts among people → Police presence or Important events →
052   Crime prevention → Police presence. These examples highlight the complexity of reasoning over
053   such logical chains, as well as the importance of uncovering high-level generalizable patterns that
      can handle these dependencies.

085   Two significant challenges arise in this context. First, the reasoning process involves navigating a
086   vast search space of possible logical paths, making it impractical to enumerate all potential scenarios.
087   Second, the system must operate in a continuous semantic space that can effectively model and
088   categorize diverse entity types, such as ”doctors” and ”police,” while capturing their contextual
089   relationships. Addressing these challenges requires an approach that combines symbolic reasoning
090   with probabilistic and neural techniques.
091   This reasoning task can be reframed as a Knowledge Graph (KG) problem as shown in Fig 1, where
092   answering a question corresponds to finding equivalent logical paths within the graph. To reduce
093   the complexity of the search space, nodes in the KG (e.g., ”doctors” or ”schoolyard”) are mapped
094   into a Named Entity Recognition (NER) embedding space. This transformation allows the model
095   to capture high-level semantic relationships, enabling reasoning over multi-entity combinations and
096   providing a structured representation for logical reasoning.
097   In summary, addressing the challenges in NSL requires two critical capabilities: (1) managing long
098   dependency chains and (2) handling complex semantic categorization across diverse question
099   types involving NER combinations. While significant advances have been made in addressing long
100   dependency chains, existing approaches often overlook the complexity of semantic categorization.
101   This limitation restricts the ability of current NSL models to tackle real-world scenarios, where
102   entities and relationships often demonstrate diverse and context-sensitive semantics.
103   To overcome this gap, we propose the Mixed Multinomial Distribution-based NSL (MMD-NSL)
104   framework. MMD-NSL generalizes the modeling of logical rules and NER-based contexts into a
105   unified probabilistic framework, leveraging mixed multinomial logic semantic distributions. By
106   incorporating context-aware probabilistic reasoning, this approach bridges the gap between rule-
107   based reasoning and context-dependent categorization, enhancing the scalability and interpretabil-
      ity of NSL models. MMD-NSL accommodates both complex categorical contexts and long depen-

129   2    R ELATED W ORKS
130
131
      To model logic rules as latent variables in a softened form is a standard approach in many proba-
132
      bilistic NSL frameworks Bach et al. (2017); Dong et al. (2019); Manhaeve et al. (2018); Trouillon
133   et al. (2016) including softened logic operator Maene & De Raedt (2024) or softened reasoning ar-
134   chitecture Marra et al. (2021). It introduces flexibility by relaxing strict logical constraints, allowing
135   the model to handle uncertainties and partial truths.
136
      A significant line of research focuses on Markov Logic Network (MLN)-based NSL, which are a
137
      type of graphical probabilistic model. Although symbolic logic learning effectively captures com-
138
      plex long dependencies, its deterministic nature makes it less suited for uncertain scenarios. To ad-
139   dress this, MLNs Richardson & Domingos (2006) integrate logic with probabilistic graphical mod-
140   els by assigning weights to logical formulas, allowing for softened rule structures. Enhancements in
141   MLN-based models include Lifted Inference Braz et al. (2005); Singla & Domingos (2008); Sourek
142   et al. (2018); Wu et al. (2020), Lazy Inference Singla & Domingos (2006); Poon et al. (2008),
143   and Coarse-to-Fine Inference Kiddon & Domingos (2011), all aimed at improving efficiency and
144   scalability. Notable developments in this domain include Logic Tensor Networks (LTN) Donadello
145   et al. (2017), which relax logical operators into fuzzy logic using t-norm theory, and Neural Markov
146   Logic Networks (NMLN) Marra et al. (2019) and Relational Neural Machines Marra et al. (2020),
147
      which employ neural networks to model real-valued differentiable functions in probabilistic logic.
      In KG context, semantic logic learning focus on logic chain structure decomposition Cheng et al.
148
      (2022) and composition Cheng et al. (2023). All the above works can be classified under the chain
149
      structure-based NSL direction.
150
151   On the other hand, treating logic chains as samples drawn from a rule distribution represents a
152   distinct generative direction, often based on Multinomial Distributions (MD). This NSL direction
153
      leverages multinomial distributions to effectively model rules within neuro-symbolic frameworks.
      For example, Qu et al. (2020) proposed modeling the rule body in the NSL task as a sample drawn
154
      from a multinomial distribution, which can be parameterized by sequence models like Transformers.
155
      Consequently, many Transformer-based NSL models have gained significant attention in the litera-
156   ture Ru et al. (2021); Xing et al. (2023), where logic rules are learned as latent variables within a
157   multinomial distribution framework. Even though chain structure-based NSL direction have started
158   to concern context information of neighbors in subgraphs Han et al. (2023), distribution-based gen-
159   erative NSL approaches do not incorporate mixtures of distributions to account for category con-
160   texts. This gap highlights the need to extend these frameworks by integrating category contexts to
161   enhance the semantic richness and interpretability of NSL models. The proposed MMD-NSL frame-
      work bridges this gap.

358   3.4     A LGORITHM
359
360   The algorithm 1 adopts a bilevel optimization framework, where the lower and upper levels are
361   optimized iteratively to capture both semantic categories and logical dependencies within KG.
362   At the lower level, for each context Ck , the current transformer parameters ϕ (obtained from the
363   upper level) are used to generate candidate rule bodies. Specifically, the transformer encodes the
364   triplet ⟨Ck (h), rhead , Ck (t)⟩ into the embedding layer using unique indices. It then iteratively gen-
365   erates r1 to rL , step by step, to form a rule body r1 ∧ . . . ∧ rL . This transformer inference process
366   is performed multiple times to generate multiple candidate rule bodies. These candidate rule bodies
367   may include duplicates, which are filtered using a simple uniqueness function. The function ex-
368   tracts J unique rule bodies r1j ∧ · · · ∧ rLj
                                                    corresponding to the query rhead , while also returning the
369   count nj for each j-th unique rule body. The lower-level optimization focuses on maximizing the
370   log-likelihood of the Multinomial Logic Semantic Distribution PMD , as outlined in Eq. 14b. Specif-
371   ically, the context-specific weights θk are optimized by maximizing the summation of the weighted
372
      rule scores. This optimization step allows the model to learn the optimal weights θk⋆ (ϕ) for each
      rule, effectively capturing the logical dependencies within each context k in the KG G.
373
374   In the upper-level optimization, after updating the rule weights θk⋆ (ϕ) in the lower level, the goal is
375   to maximize the objective ℓ(ϕ, θ ⋆ (ϕ)), as given in Eq. 14a. This objective is designed to update the
376   transformer parameters ϕ, which encode the semantic embeddings and contextual representations.
377   The transformer model Tϕ embeds the input rule bodies r1j ∧ . . . ∧ rL   j

399
400   For evaluating our MMD-NSL, we use a KG relationship predictor as downstream task to assess
401   algorithm 1. Each relation query to be predicted is treated as a rule query (rule head). Our NSL
402   rule learner generates relevant rule bodies by sampling from the multiple NER-pair context within
403   the NSL model, instead of relying on exhaustive path searches through a large KG. These newly
404   discovered rule bodies provide a more accurate representation of the rule head (i.e., the relation
405   query), leading to more precise label predictions for the relation query.

418   4.2   M ODEL O PTIMIZATION S ETUP
419
420   At the upper level, the model employs a transformer-based architecture to learn a Context Logic
421   Semantic Distribution. The input to the transformer includes the NER combination and the rule
422   head, with the self-attention mechanism used to compute a continuous mixed coefficient represen-
423
      tation while encoding the rule query (rule head). This allows the model to represent categories and
      relations using unique numerical identifiers. The embedding layer for relations has dimensions of
424
      (256, 2R + 1), where R represents the total number of relations (65 in this case). Similarly, the
425
      NER category embedding layer has dimensions of (256, 10), corresponding to 10 distinct NER cat-
426   egories. The model architecture includes two encoding and decoding layers, with an output layer of
427   size (256, 2R + 1). The input to the transformer is constructed by concatenating the rule head and
428   rule body, each of size 4. If the input length is insufficient, padding symbols are added and masked
429   using a 4 × 4 positional mask. On average, each rule head generates 50 candidate rule bodies. These
430   candidates are filtered to remove duplicates and then passed to the lower-level model for further pro-
431   cessing. At the lower level, the model dynamically initializes weights for each training round. These
      weights are specifically tailored to the candidate rule bodies generated by the upper level, capturing

518   5   C ONCLUSIONS
519
520   In this paper, we introduced MMD-NSL, a novel framework for NSL that unifies the handling of
521   long dependency chains and complex semantic categorization within KG. By leveraging a continu-
522   ous Mixed Multinomial Logic Semantic Distribution, we extended traditional MLN to incorporate
523   context-dependent semantic embeddings. Our theoretical contributions, including the bijective map-
524   ping between MLNs and continuous multinomial distributions, establish a foundation for capturing
525   intricate dependencies and diverse contexts in NSL tasks. The framework employs a bilevel op-
526
      timization process, where the transformer-based upper level efficiently learns mixing coefficient
      analogous to attention mechanisms, and the lower level optimizes rule weights, allowing for effec-
527
      tive learning of both context and rule patterns. Experimental results show that MMD-NSL provides
528
      a more general and adaptable paradigm for NSL, outperforming traditional baselines in managing
529   complex relationships and multiple semantic contexts, thereby advancing continuous probabilistic
530   models for neuro-symbolic reasoning and complex relational tasks.
