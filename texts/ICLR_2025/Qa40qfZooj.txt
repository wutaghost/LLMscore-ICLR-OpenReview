009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               Bayesian neural networks (BNNs) have recently gained popularity due to their
012            ability to quantify model uncertainty in prediction. However, specifying a prior
013            for BNNs that accurately captures relevant domain knowledge is often extremely
014            challenging. In this work, we propose a framework for integrating general forms of
015            domain knowledge (i.e., any knowledge that can be represented by a loss function)
016            into a BNN prior through variational inference, while enabling computationally
017            efficient posterior inference and sampling. Specifically, our approach results in a
018            prior over neural network weights that assigns high probability mass to models
019            that better align with our domain knowledge, leading to posterior samples that also
020
               exhibit this behavior. In a semi-supervised learning setting, we show that BNNs
               using our proposed domain knowledge priors outperform those with standard priors
021
               (e.g., isotropic Gaussian, Gaussian process), successfully incorporating diverse
022
               types of prior information such as fairness, physics rules, and healthcare knowledge
023            and achieving better predictive performance. We also present techniques for
024            transferring the learned priors across different model architectures, demonstrating
025            their broad utility across many tasks.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   While recent advances in deep learning have led to strong empirical performance in many real-world
032   settings, it is crucial for deep learning models to faithfully represent the uncertainty in their predictions
033
      and avoid making incorrect predictions with high confidence, especially in safety-critical domains
      (e.g., healthcare, criminal justice). Unfortunately, prior works show that deep learning models trained
034
      via empirical risk minimization often make errors with high confidence at test time, especially on
035
      data points that differ from those observed in the training data distribution (Hendrycks & Gimpel,
036   2016; Hendrycks et al., 2021). Moreover, these models often inherit undesirable biases present in
037   their training data (Larson et al., 2016; Obermeyer et al., 2019), motivating the development of an
038   approach for incorporating prior knowledge into model training to mitigate such issues.
039
      A principled approach to achieving both good predictive performance and a faithful representation
040
      of predictive uncertainty is to use Bayesian neural networks (BNNs; MacKay, 1992; 1995; Neal,
041
      1996; Wilson & Izmailov, 2020; Papamarkou et al., 2024). In the Bayesian setting, selecting a good
042   prior is crucial, and its misspecification for BNNs can force the posterior distribution to contract to
043   suboptimal regions of the weight space (Grünwald & van Ommen, 2017; Gelman et al., 2017; 2020;
044   Fortuin, 2022), resulting in suboptimal posterior predictive performance. Ideally, the prior should
045   well-reflect what relevant domain knowledge (e.g., physics rules) specifies as plausible functions for
046   a given prediction problem and help mitigate any undesirable biases learned from the training data.
047
      However, the high-dimensionality of the weight space and the nontrivial connection between the
048   weight and function spaces make specifying a prior that reflects domain knowledge challenging
049   (Nalisnick, 2018; Fortuin, 2022). Due to such difficulties, uninformative priors that enable tractable
050   sampling and approximate inference are typically used in practice. The most widely used uninforma-
051   tive weight-space prior is the isotropic Gaussian prior (Hernández-Lobato & Adams, 2015). Recent
052   works propose to directly specify a function-space prior (e.g., via Gaussian processes (GPs)) to
053   encode functional properties such as smoothness and periodicity (Sun et al., 2019). However, existing
      forms of informative priors are not flexible enough to represent broader forms of domain knowledge.

074   In this work, we propose a novel approach for incorporating much more general forms of domain
075   knowledge into BNN priors in a semi-supervised learning setting. The key challenge lies in both
076   how to formulate and incorporate such knowledge into the prior and to ensure that this prior enables
077   to computationally tractable posterior inference and sampling. In particular, we focus on domain
078   knowledge for which we can formulate a loss function ϕ, such that it captures how well a particular
079
      model aligns with the given knowledge. We show that various forms of domain knowledge can be
      represented in this form (Section 5.1). For example, for a physics rule, we can define the loss function
080
      to measure how much a model’s prediction of the state of a physical system violates the law of
081
      conservation of energy. As another example, if we want a vision model to ignore the background of
082   an image, we can define the loss to be the norm of the gradient of the model’s prediction with respect
083   to the background pixels of the image. To obtain an informative prior that incorporates such domain
084   knowledge, we propose a variational inference approach to learn a low-rank Gaussian distribution
085   that puts higher probability mass on model weights with low values of the loss ϕ on unlabeled data.
086   The low-rank Gaussian structure of the informative prior enables computationally efficient posterior
087   inference. We emphasize that with existing approaches for specifying informative priors, it is not
088   clear how to incorporate similar forms of prior knowledge.
089   We demonstrate that using our learned informative priors for posterior inference in BNNs not
090   only ensures better alignment with domain knowledge (i.e., lower values of ϕ) but also improves
091   predictive performance across many datasets, where various forms of domain knowledge (e.g., feature
092   importance, clinical rules, fairness constraints) are available. Notably, our approach outperforms
093   BNNs that use an uninformative isotropic Gaussian prior, as well as those with more specialized—yet
094   unable to flexibly incorporate such general forms of domain knowledge—priors.
095   We also present various techniques, based on maximum mean discrepancy (Gretton et al., 2012) and
096   moment matching (with SWAG (Maddox et al., 2019)), for transferring a learned domain knowledge
097   prior across different model architectures to increase their overall utility. In general, a BNN prior
098   is architecture-specific, i.e., we cannot directly use a prior learned for one BNN in another (e.g.,
099   with a different number of hidden layers or units). While relearning a new prior every time is one
100   option, such an approach can be expensive and even infeasible in scenarios when we no longer have
101   access to the loss ϕ. For example, clinical rules derived from patient data in one hospital1 may not
102
      be accessible in another due to privacy. Our empirical results demonstrate that we can efficiently
      transfer our priors to different model classes, where models sampled from a transferred prior achieve
103
      significantly lower values of ϕ compared to models drawn from an isotropic Gaussian prior.
104

120   2    RELATED WORK
121
122   Learning with Domain Knowledge. Many researchers have focused on incorporating domain
123   knowledge or explanations into increasingly black-box deep learning models. Some approaches
124   directly regularize models to incorporate instances of such domain knowledge (Ross et al., 2017;
125   Rieger et al., 2020; Ismail et al., 2021). For example, Rieger et al. (2020) discourage models from
126   using spurious patches in images for skin cancer detection tasks by penalizing models that place
127   high feature importance on those patches. However, prior knowledge can also come in various
128
      forms beyond explanations, including rules from physics (de Avila Belbute-Peres et al., 2018; Seo
      et al., 2021), weak supervision (Sam & Kolter, 2022), invariance (Chen et al., 2020), explicit output
129
      constraints for particular regions of the input space (Yang et al., 2020) or desirable properties such
130
      as fairness (Zafar et al., 2017; Dwork et al., 2012). These works suggest that incorporating domain
131   knowledge can lead to models that are more robust and perform better out-of-distribution. Existing
132   work theoretically analyzes such simple incorporation of domain knowledge as constraints to show
133   benefits in sample complexity (Pukdee et al., 2023).
134
      In the Bayesian setting, existing works have studied directly regularizing posterior samples (Zhu
135
      et al., 2014; Huang et al., 2023), but none have extensively studied how to obtain BNN priors
136
      that incorporate forms of domain knowledge as broad as those aforementioned. Moreover, using
137   informative priors is more computationally efficient than posterior regularization, as we only need to
138   compute ϕ during the pretraining phase for the prior (see Section 4.2) and not for every posterior
139   sample. Our method thus scales better when sampling a large number of posterior samples, allowing
140   a more accurate approximation of the model posterior average.
141
142   Priors in Bayesian Neural Networks. As discussed above, the high-dimensionality of the weight
143   space for BNNs makes specifying a prior that reflects aforementioned forms of domain knowledge
144   challenging (Nalisnick, 2018; Fortuin, 2022). Prior works propose to encode functional properties
145   such as smoothness and periodicity by using a function-space prior (e.g., via GPs (Rasmussen &
146   Williams, 2005)) (Sun et al., 2017; 2019; Hafner et al., 2019; Tran et al., 2022), to encode output
147
      constraints for particular regions of the input space into a weight-space prior (Yang et al., 2020), or
      to use a set of reference models (e.g., simpler linear models) as priors to regularize the predictive
148
      complexity of BNNs (Nalisnick et al., 2021). Our work differs from prior work in that we address
149
      more general notions of domain knowledge, such as feature importance and fairness, which is difficult
150   to achieve with existing methods (e.g., how does one encode notions of fairness into a GP kernel?).
151
152   More recent works propose to leverage advances in self-supervised learning (Henaff, 2020; Chen
153
      et al., 2020) to learn more informative and expressive priors from auxiliary, unlabeled data. Sharma
      et al. (2023b) propose to learn an informative prior by fixing the parameters of the base encoder
154
      to the approximate maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimate from contrastive learning (Chen et al.,
155
      2020). Shwartz-Ziv et al. (2022) propose to use a temperature-scaled posterior from a source task as
156   a pretrained, informative prior for the target task, and empirically demonstrate that a BNN with an
157   informative prior consistently outperforms BNNs with uninformative priors (e.g., isotropic Gaussian)
158   and non-Bayesian neural network ensembles in predictive accuracy, uncertainty estimation, and data
159   efficiency. Other works look at the usage of priors incorporating knowledge from transfer learning
160   (Lee et al., 2024; Lim et al., 2024). We remark that, to the best of our knowledge, there are no other
161   existing BNN methods that allow incorporating general forms of domain knowledge into BNN priors.
      The most relevant prior work by Yang et al. (2020), which encodes information by upweighting

      models that satisfy particular constraints on their output space, can be seen as a specific instance of
163   our framework but is not easily applicable to most tasks considered in this paper.

166   3     PRELIMINARIES
167
168
      We consider a standard semi-supervised learning setting. Let X be an instance space and Y be a
      label space. Let D be a distribution over X × Y. We observe a training dataset of examples X =
169
      {(x1 , y1 ), ..., (xn , yn )} and unlabeled examples X ′ = {x′1 , ..., x′k } drawn from D and a marginal
170
      distribution DX , respectively. We consider a class of neural networks H = {hw |hw : X → Y},
171   which have weights w. Our goal is to learn a neural network h (or a distribution over possible neural
172   networks with a mean) that achieves the lowest loss, or
173
174                                      err(h) := E(x,y)∼D [ℓ(hw (x), y)],
175   where ℓ is the 0-1 loss for classification, and the ℓ1 or ℓ2 loss for regression.
176
177
      One approach to capture model uncertainty is via BNNs, which models a distribution over neural
      networks via a distribution overQweights, q(w). In practice, it is common to assume a standard
178
      isotropic Gaussian prior q(w) = i N (wi ; 0, σi2 ). This does not capture any prior knowledge about
179
      downstream tasks but is primarily used for its computational tractability. Given a prior q(w) over
180   neural network weights w and labeled data X, we can sample from the posterior distribution using
181   stochastic gradient Markov chain Monte Carlo methods such as Stochastic Gradient Hamiltonian
182   Monte Carlo (SGHMC) (Chen et al., 2014) and Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD)
183   (Welling & Teh, 2011). In this work, we mainly use SGLD in our experiments (Sections 5.1–5.2) but
184   also consider MultiSWAG (Wilson & Izmailov, 2020) in our ablations (Section 5.3).

      4     D OMAIN K NOWLEDGE P RIORS FOR BAYESIAN N EURAL N ETWORKS
187
188
      While existing methods tackle specific desirable properties of a network (e.g., smoothness), it is
189   unclear how to incorporate very general forms of domain knowledge into BNNs, as discussed in
190   Sections 1–2. We propose to achieve this by incorporating such information into a data-driven prior.
191
192   4.1   D OMAIN K NOWLEDGE L OSS
193
194   First, we define our notion of domain knowledge. We propose to represent this as a loss function that
195   measures the alignment of a particular model to our domain knowledge.
196
197   Definition 1 (Domain Knowledge Loss) A domain knowledge loss function can be expressed as
      ϕ : H × X → R, which takes inputs h ∈ H, x ∈ X and has ϕ(h, x) ≥ 0.
198
199
      We capture how well h satisfies our domain knowledge at a point x through this loss function, where a
200   lower loss value implies that h better satisfies the domain knowledge. This definition is quite general,
201   and it is possible to define the loss ϕ to capture various notions of domain knowledge including
202   physical rules and information about spurious correlations (see examples of these losses in Section
203   5.1). We remark that these notions of domain knowledge are functions of the random input data x,
204   and thus are difficult to directly encode in function space or via a kernel in a GP prior.
205
      Given this definition of domain knowledge, we want our models to achieve low values of this loss, e.g.,
206   Ex∼DX [ϕ(h, x)] ≤ τ , where τ is some threshold. We remark that this loss can be evaluated solely on
207   unlabeled data, which yields nicely to using this for pretraining or learning priors. Considering losses
208   that use information about labels could be potentially interesting, especially in the case of certain
209   fairness metrics, e.g., equal odds and disparate impact (Hardt et al., 2016; Mehrabi et al., 2021).
210
      In the frequentist setting, we can incorporate such domain knowledge by simply adding a regulariza-
211
      tion term based on this surrogate loss (Ross et al., 2017; Rieger et al., 2020; Pukdee et al., 2023). For
212   a loss function ℓ, this yields the regularized objective given by

      4.3    T RANSFERRING I NFORMATIVE P RIORS
271
272   A key limitation of the learned priors is that they are architecture-specific. To make them usable for
273   downstream tasks where other model architectures may be more suitable, it is important to identify
274   effective techniques for transferring these learned priors. Our proposed strategy is to match functions
275   drawn from the learned informative prior and a target prior distribution for the new model architecture.
276   Formally, let H1 = {hw | hw : X → Y} represent the hypothesis class of our original model
277   architecture, with a corresponding informative prior qψ1 (w). We want to learn an informative prior
278   for a different class of networks H2 = {hu | hu : X → Y}. We hope to learn a distribution
279   qψ2 (u) such that the distributions over H1 and H2 induced by w ∼ qψ1 (w) and u ∼ qψ2 (u) are
280   close. As we consider low-rank Gaussian priors, we can efficiently draw samples from qψ1 (w) and
281   qψ2 (u), and this motivates us to learn ψ2 such that the set of functions {hw1 , . . . , hwn } ⊆ H1 and
282   {hu1 , . . . , hun } ⊆ H2 are similar when wi ∼ qψ1 (w), ui ∼ qψ2 (u). Since the members of each
283   set are functions, it is difficult to compare them directly. If we have access to a set of unlabeled
284
      examples X ′ = {x′1 , . . . , x′m }, we can instead make sure that the evaluation of each function on
      X ′ are similar, i.e., W := {hw1 (X ′ ), . . . , hwn (X ′ )} and U := {hu1 (X ′ ), . . . , hun (X ′ )} are similar
285
      when h(X ′ ) = (h(x1 ), . . . , h(xm )) ∈ Rm .

358   porate notions of feature importance. We consider the task of ignoring background information,
359   which are spurious features, on the DecoyMNIST dataset (Ross et al., 2017), a variant of MNIST
360   (LeCun et al., 1998). On this task, a patch has been added in the background that correlates with
361   different labels at train and test time. Thus, models that learn to rely on these spurious features for
362   prediction can perform poorly at test time due to such distribution shift. Here, we consider the domain
363   knowledge of ignoring background pixels in making predictions, which can be expressed as
364                                       ϕbackground (h, x) = ||∇x h(x)||2b ,
365
366   where b denotes the feature indices that correspond to the background. On DecoyMNIST, we access
367
      these feature dimensions by looking at the uncorrupted data, which is not used during training. For
      other tasks, we can generate these background masks via a segmentation network.

      Clinical Rules for Healthcare Interventions. We demonstrate how our method can be used to
370
      incorporate clinical rules into the prior using the MIMIC-IV dataset (Johnson et al., 2023). We
371
      reproduce the binary classification task in Yang et al. (2020), where the goal is to predict whether
372   an intervention for hypotension management (e.g., vasopressors) should be given to a patient in
373   the intensive care unit, given a set of physiological measurements. As in Yang et al. (2020), we
374   incorporate the clinical knowledge that an intervention should be made if the patient exhibits: (i)
375   high lactate and low bicarbonate levels, or (ii) high creatinine levels, high blood urea nitrogen (BUN)
376   levels, and low urine output. We can express this knowledge as

382   Physics Rules for Pendulums. We demonstrate how our method can incorporate physical knowl-
383   edge into the prior on the double pendulum dataset (Seo et al., 2021; Asseman et al., 2018). We
384   consider a regression task where the goal is to predict the next state of double-pendulum dynamics
385   with friction from a given initial state x = (θ1 , ω1 , θ2 , ω2 ) where θi , ωi are the angular displacement

389                              ϕenergy_damping (h, x) = max(E(h(x)) − E(x), 0),

391
      where h(x) is the predicted next state and E(x) is a function that maps a given state x to its total
      energy. This loss penalizes predictions of states with higher total energy.

524   6       DISCUSSION
525
526
      We propose a framework to incorporate general forms of domain knowledge into the priors for BNNs.
527
      Empirically, we observe that this can improve the performance of BNNs across several tasks with
528   different notions of domain knowledge and leads to models that exhibit desirable properties. In
529   addition, we provide an effective approach to transfer informative priors across model architectures,
530   resolving an existing problem in the literature. Our results provide new insights into incorporating
531   domain knowledge into priors for Bayesian methods, which can be captured by optimizing a learnable
532   approximation through variational inference. As a whole, our results support the development of
533   open-source informative priors that practitioners can incorporate into their various specific use cases
534   to encode desirable model properties without the need to deal with ϕ directly, irrespective of the
535   desired model architecture. As such, this supports the foundations for pretraining in the Bayesian
536
      setting, by providing a framework to develop and transfer informative priors to new model tasks as
      desired, similar to pretrained weights or foundation models that are currently released as open-source.

1012   E.6      A BLATIONS ON M ODEL E NSEMBLE S IZE
1013
1014   With frequentist methods, computing a model ensemble approximately equivalent to a Bayesian
1015   model average (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017) requires performing potentially computationally
1016   expensive regularization with ϕ (or even pretraining, where domain knowledge can be incorporated
1017   as a notion of invariance for self-supervised learning). With a Bayesian approach on the other hand,
1018   we only need to learn the informative prior once and can generate multiple samples from the posterior
1019   using the learned prior. Given that sampling from the posterior is efficient as in our setting, this can
1020   be a significant benefit, specifically when computing this regularization or pretraining is costly.
1021   As such, we run ablation studies to evaluate how the model ensemble’s size impacts Banana’s
1022   downstream performance and the alternative approaches. We observe that increasing the ensemble
1023   size increases performance, until we observe diminishing returns after ensemble sizes of 15, on the
1024   DecoyMNIST task (Figure A3). This demonstrates that larger ensembles generally achieve better
1025   performance and supports the use of informative priors, which can more efficiently scale to posterior
       averages with larger ensembles when compared to other regularization-based approaches.
