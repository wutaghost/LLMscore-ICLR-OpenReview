009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Ensembling outputs from diverse sources is a straightforward yet effective ap-
012            proach to boost performance. Mixture-of-Agents (MoA) is one such popular en-
013            semble method that aggregates outputs from multiple different Large Language
014            Models (LLMs). This paper raises the question in the context of language mod-
015            els: is mixing different LLMs truly beneficial? We propose Self-MoA — an en-
016            semble method that aggregates outputs from only the single top-performing LLM.
017            Our extensive experiments reveal that, surprisingly, Self-MoA outperforms stan-
018
               dard MoA that mixes different LLMs in a large number of scenarios: Self-MoA
               achieves 6.6% improvement over MoA on the AlpacaEval 2.0 benchmark, and
019
               an average of 3.8% improvement across various benchmarks, including MMLU,
020
               CRUX, and MATH. Applying Self-MoA to one of the top-ranking models in Al-
021            pacaEval 2.0 directly achieves the new state-of-the-art performance ranking 1st on
022            the leaderboard. To understand the effectiveness of Self-MoA, we systematically
023            investigate the trade-off between diversity and quality of outputs under various
024            MoA settings. We confirm that the MoA performance is rather sensitive to the
025            quality, and mixing different LLMs often lowers the average quality of the mod-
026            els. To complement the study, we identify the scenarios where mixing different
027            LLMs could be helpful. This paper further introduces a sequential version of self-
028            MoA, that is capable of aggregating a large number of LLM outputs on-the-fly
029
               over multiple rounds, and is as effective as aggregating all outputs at once.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Large language models have made remarkable strides in improving performance across different
034
      domains, with notable examples such as GPT (Achiam et al., 2023), Gemini (Team et al., 2023),
035
      and Claude (Anthropic, 2023). Significant efforts have been directed toward increasing model size
036   and training data to boost capabilities. However, scaling at training time comes with steep costs,
037   while scaling computation during inference remains largely underexplored.
038
039
      A straightforward way to utilize test-time compute is ensembling, which aims to combine outputs
      of multiple LLMs (Wang et al., 2024a; Lin et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2024a).
040
      Among various ensembling approaches, Mixture-of-Agents (MoA) (Wang et al., 2024a) has gar-
041
      nered significant interest, achieving superior performance in challenging tasks such as instruction
042   following (Wang et al., 2024a), summarization, data extraction (OpenPipe, 2024), and real-world
043   code issue resolution (Zhang et al., 2024b). Specifically, MoA first queries multiple LLMs (pro-
044   posers) to generate responses, and then uses an LLM (aggregator) to synthesize and summarize
045   these responses into a high-quality response.
046
      Previous research highlights the significance of model diversity within the proposers for optimizing
047
      the performance of MoA, primarily focusing on strategies for ensembling a diverse set of individ-
048   ual models. We consider cross-model diversity as the variation among different models. However,
049   pursuing cross-model diversity may inadvertently include low-quality models, resulting in a quality-
050   diversity trade-off. While previous studies mainly concentrate on achieving a high cross-model di-
051   versity (Wang et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2024b), we adopt a holistic perspective on model diversity
052   by considering in-model diversity, which arises from the variability of multiple responses generated
053   by the same model. In-model diversity enables us to aggregate multiple outputs from an individual
      model. Intuitively, leveraging outputs from the best-performing individual model can more effec-

      tively navigate the quality-diversity trade-off by creating a higher-quality proposer mixture. Thus,
055   we propose Self-MoA as depicted in Figure 1b, which utilizes the same prompting template as MoA
056   but aggregates outputs that are repeatedly sampled from the same model, rather than from a set of
057   different models. To distinguish, we use Mixed-MoA to refer to MoA configurations that combine
058   different individual models when necessary.
059
      Surprisingly, we find that Mixed-MoA is usually sub-optimal compared with Self-MoA, especially
060
      when there exist significant quality differences among the proposers. Specifically, we revisit the
061   same experiment setting of MoA with six open-source instruction fine-tuned models as Wang et al.
062   (2024a). Compared with Mixed-MoA which aggregates all six models, Self-MoA on the strongest
063   model surpasses its mixed counterpart with merely half of the forward passes on the AlpacaEval 2.0
064   benchmark, showing a case of when intra-model diversity is more effective. Moreover, Self-MoA
065   combined with two best-performed models on AlpacaEval 2.0 consistently achieves a 2-3 point
066   gain and secures the top position on the leaderboard, which further confirms the effectiveness of
067   Self-MoA in this evaluation task.
068   To explore the limits of model diversity for MoA, we extend our experiments to a setting with
069   three specialized models, each excelling in a specific task. Specifically, we utilize Qwen2-7B-
070   Instruct (Bai et al., 2023) for common sense QA (MMLU-redux (Gema et al., 2024)), Qwen2-Math-
071   7B-Instruct (Bai et al., 2023) for mathematics (MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2020)), and DeepSeek-
072   Coder-V2-Lite-Instruct (Zhu et al., 2024) for coding (CRUX (Gu et al., 2024)). We compare Self-
073   MoA against a range of Mixed-MoA strategies, evaluating 13 combinations of individual models
074   based on their average performance across the three tasks. Our findings indicate that, even in this
075
      promising scenario for Mixed-MoA where each individual model excels in a specific subtask, only
      two Mixed-MoA strategies slightly outperform Self-MoA by 0.17% and 0.35%. Furthermore, if we
076
      have prior knowledge of the tasks and employ task-specific models as proposers for Self-MoA such
077
      as DeepSeek-Coder-V2-Lite-Instruct on CRUX or Qwen2-Math-7B-Instruct on MATH, Self-MoA
078   can significantly outperform the best Mixed-MoA.
079
080   To better understand the effectiveness of Self-MoA, we conduct a comprehensive investigation of the
081
      trade-off between quality and diversity in MoA, involving over 200 experiments. We use the Vendi
      Score (Dan Friedman & Dieng, 2023) to evaluate the diversity among the outputs of the proposers,
082
      while the average performance of the proposers serves as the measure of quality. In Section 3, we
083
      confirm that MoA performance has a positive correlation with both quality and diversity. Moreover,
084   we clearly show a trade-off along the achievable Pareto front of quality and diversity. Interestingly,
085   we find that MoA is quite sensitive to variations in quality, with optimal performance typically
086   occurring in regions characterized by high quality and relatively low diversity. This finding naturally
087   explains the effectiveness of Self-MoA, as it utilizes the strongest model as the proposer, ensuring
088   high quality in its outputs.
089
      Finally, we evaluate the performance of Self-MoA under increasing computational budgets. As the
090   number of outputs grows, the scalability of Self-MoA becomes constrained by the context length
091   of the aggregator. To address this issue, we introduce Self-MoA-Seq (Figure 1c), a sequential ver-
092   sion that processes samples using a sliding window, allowing it to handle an arbitrary number of
093   model outputs. Our findings show that Self-MoA-Seq performs at least as effectively as Self-MoA,
094   enabling scalable ensembling for LLMs with shorter context lengths without compromising final
095   performance.
096   Overall, our contributions are three-fold:
097
098          • We introduce Self-MoA, which leverages in-model diversity by synthesizing multiple out-
099            puts from the same model. Surprisingly, it demonstrates superior performance compared
100            to existing Mixed-MoA approaches, which emphasize cross-model diversity, across a wide
101            range of benchmarks.
102          • Through systematic experiments and statistical analysis, we uncover a core trade-off be-
103            tween diversity and quality among the proposers, emphasizing that MoA is highly sensitive
104            to proposer quality. This finding also explains the success of Self-MoA, which leverages
105            outputs from the highest-performing model, ensuring superior overall quality.
106          • We extend Self-MoA to its sequential version Self-MoA-Seq, which iteratively aggregates
107            a small amount of outputs step by step. Self-MoA-Seq unlocks LLMs that are constrained
               by the context length and enables computation scaling during inference.

127   2        I S E NSEMBLING D IFFERENT LLM S B ENEFICIAL ?
128
129   As introduced in Section 1, previous research primarily emphasizes cross-model diversity, which
130
      can inadvertently include low-quality proposers. In this work, we introduce Self-MoA (Figure 1),
      which uses a single top-performing model to generate multiple outputs and aggregate them to pro-
131
      duce the final result. Self-MoA leverages in-model diversity as repeated sampling often produces
132
      varied outputs. We propose our research question as follows:
133
134                            Does the benefit of MoA stem from cross-model diversity?
135                           Can we build a stronger MoA by utilizing in-model diversity?

314   We investigate factors that contribute to the strong performance of Self-MoA through careful exper-
315   iments. Previous studies have mainly focused on increasing model diversity within the group (Wang
316   et al., 2024a; Jiang et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2024b). However, searching for diverse models can
317   sometimes lead to including poorly performed models, resulting in a trade-off between diversity and
318   quality, where quality refers to how well each individual model performs in the group.
319   Therefore, we aim to identify the existence of a general relationship between MoA’s performance
320   and quality as well as diversity. Following Section 2, we evaluate MoA’s performance on MMLU,
321   CRUX, and MATH, which cover tasks requiring a wide range of capabilities. We vary the quality
322   and diversity with two orders of freedom: 1) combinations of individual models in proposers from
323   Section 2.2; and 2) sampling temperature. i.e., 0.5, 0.7, 1.0, 1.1, and 1.2. This results in a total of
      over 70 unique MoA proposer mixtures. We measure the quality and diversity as follows:

468   4     S CALING I NFERENCE C OMPUTE WITH S ELF -M OA
469
470   In previous sections, we have provided evidence that Self-MoA over one strong model is straight-
471   forward but effective. As the community is becoming more aware of scaling inference time comput-
472
      ing (Brown et al., 2024; Snell et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024), one natural question to ask is:
473
       Given a strong model, does Self-MoA’s performance scale with the number of repeated samples?
474
475   Intuitively, Self-MoA cannot scale indefinitely by simply increasing the computation budget for at
476   least three reasons:
477
478          • As more responses are sampled from a single model, the diversity among those samples
479            tends to plateau.
480          • Aggregating information from many samples is more challenging for LLMs compared to
481            handling a smaller number of samples.
482          • Every LLM has a context length limit (e.g., 8192 tokens for Gemma 2), which restricts the
483            number of responses an aggregator can process at once.
484
485   While the first limitation is inherent to repeated sampling, we address the latter two by introduc-
      ing Self-MoA-Seq, a sequential variant designed to manage large numbers of responses without

530   5   C ONCLUSION
531
532   In this paper, we introduce Self-MoA, an innovative approach that utilizes in-model diversity to
533   enhance the performance of large language models during inference. Our experiments demonstrate
534   that Self-MoA outperforms traditional Mixed-MoA strategies in many popular benchmarks, particu-
535
      larly when the proposer model quality varies. By aggregating outputs from a single high-performing
      model, Self-MoA effectively addresses the quality-diversity trade-off. We further identify the sce-
536
      narios where mixing LLM can be potentially beneficial and extend Self-MoA to the constrained
537
      context length setting. These findings highlight the potential of in-model diversity in optimizing
538   LLM performance and pave the way for further advancements in ensemble methods.

      A     R ELATED W ORK
757
758
      Ensembles of LLMs. Model ensembling aims to combine strengths from multiple models. Pre-
759
      vious studies have explored various methods to leverage a diverse set of models, including but not
760
      limited to prompting (Wang et al., 2024a), weight averaging (Lin et al., 2024; Ramé et al., 2024),
761   routing (Jiang et al., 2024b; Lu et al., 2023), training a generative fusion model (Jiang et al., 2023b),
762   and so on. Zhang et al. (2024a) argues that the fusion of specialized models with certain general
763   abilities could be a promising direction toward Artificial General Intelligence. Mixture-of-Agents
764   (MoA, Wang et al. (2024a)) first queries multiple LLMs to generate responses, then iteratively ag-
765   gregates these samples through several rounds of synthesis. MoA shows promising results on several
766   benchmarks, and its variants achieve superior performance on the AlpacaEval 2.0 leaderboard. Our
767   method is inspired by the prompt pipeline proposed in MoA. However, while existing MoA fo-
768   cuses on unleashing the strength from multiple different models (Wang et al., 2024a; Jiang et al.,
769
      2023b; Zhang et al., 2024b), we demonstrate the trade-off between diversity and quality within the
      proposers, highlighting that focusing solely on diversity may compromise overall quality and final
770
      performance.
771
772
773
      LLM Inference with Repeated Sampling. Previous studies have shown that combining model
      outputs from repeated sampling can yield a better response in various domains. In tasks with au-
774
      tomatic verifiers available, such as math (Hendrycks et al., 2021) and code (Chen et al., 2021),
775
      simply sampling LLMs multiple times can significantly improve the pass@k metric and hence boost
776   the success rate of solving the tasks (Roziere et al., 2023; Li et al., 2022; Brown et al., 2024). In
777   more general tasks without verification tools, we can conduct techniques like majority vote, self-
778   consistency, and best-of-n to choose the most promising one from candidate responses (Wang et al.,
779   2022; Chen et al., 2023b; Gui et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024). Therefore, repeated sampling is recently
780   regarded as one approach of scaling compute during inference time (Brown et al., 2024). In this
781   work, we identify the surprising effectiveness of repeated sampling in the context of MoA. Unlike
782   majority vote or best-of-N, Self-MoA asks LLMs to synthesize outputs generated from repeated
783   sampling, hence can further improve over each individual output.
784
785   Collaborative Agents There is a surge of interest in building agent systems based on verification,
786   critique, discussion, and refinement. For example, Stechly et al. (2023), Valmeekam et al. (2023),
787   and Madaan et al. (2024) use self-critique to iteratively refine outputs through a chain structure.
788   Madaan et al. (2024), Chen et al. (2024), and Wang et al. (2024a) explore the incorporation of
789
      multiple models to create a stronger agent that outperform each individual model. Du et al. (2023)
      incorporates multiple LLMs that propose and debate their individual responses over several rounds
790
      to reach a common final answer. Liang et al. (2023) proposes Multi-Agent Debate, which encourages
791
      divergent thinking during LLM debates to arrive at more informative conclusions and avoid rushing
792   to incorrect answers. Chen et al. (2023a) introduces RECONCILE, which adopts a confidence-
793   weighted voting mechanism for better consensus among LLM discussions. Interestingly, Wang
794   et al. (2024b) shows that a single model with carefully designed prompts can sometimes match the
795   performance of agent discussions. Moreover, agent discussions mainly outperform a single LLM
796   when the prompts are insufficient.

813   input a collection of samples along with a pairwise similarity function, and it outputs a single value

      The score is computed using a positive semi-definite similarity matrix K ∈ Rn×n as follows:
