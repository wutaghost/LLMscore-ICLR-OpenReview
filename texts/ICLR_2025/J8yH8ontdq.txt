                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               We introduce Audio-Agent, a multimodal framework for audio generation, edit-
012            ing and composition based on text or video inputs. Conventional approaches for
013            text-to-audio (TTA) tasks often make single-pass inferences from text descrip-
014            tions. While straightforward, this design struggles to produce high-quality audio
015            when given complex text conditions. In our method, we utilize a pre-trained TTA
016            diffusion network as the audio generation agent to work in tandem with GPT-4,
017            which decomposes the text condition into atomic, specific instructions, and calls
018            the agent for audio generation. Consequently, Audio-Agent generates high-quality
019            audio that is closely aligned with the provided text or video while also support-
020
               ing variable-length generation. For video-to-audio (VTA) tasks, most existing
               methods require training a timestamp detector to synchronize video events with
021
               generated audio, a process that can be tedious and time-consuming. We propose a
022
               simpler approach by fine-tuning a pre-trained Large Language Model (LLM), e.g.,
023            Gemma2-2B-it, to obtain both semantic and temporal conditions to bridge video
024            and audio modality. Thus our framework provides a comprehensive solution for
025            both TTA and VTA tasks without substantial computational overhead in training.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Multimodal deep generative models have gained increasing attention these years. Essentially, the
031   models are trained to perform tasks based on different kinds of input called modalities, mimick-
032   ing how humans make decisions from different kinds of senses such as vision and smell Suzuki &
033
      Matsuo (2022). Compared to other generation tasks such as image generation or contextual under-
      standing, audio generation is less intuitive as it is harder to precisely measure the generated sound
034
      quality using human ears. Additionally, previous works mainly focus on generating music-related
035
      audio, which is more structured compared to naturally occurring audio Copet et al. (2024); Mele-
036   chovsky et al. (2023). Some recent works have focused on generating visually guided open-domain
037   audio clips Chen et al. (2020); Zhou et al. (2018).
038
039
      Recent researches on audio generation are mainly focused on text-to-audio generation (TTA) and
      video-to-audio generation (VTA). For TTA task Xue et al. (2024); Kreuk et al. (2022), current
040
      datasets lack high-quality text-audio pairs. Existing datasets such as AudioCaps Kim et al. (2019)
041
      or Clotho Drossos et al. (2020) usually contain multiple event descriptions mixed into one single
042   sentence without fine-grained details and object bindings. This complicates training, particularly
043   when handling long continuous signals with complex text conditions Huang et al. (2023). We define
044   complex text conditions as long event descriptions containing a series of events without explicitly
045   describing the sound, such as “A man enters his car and drives away”. While previously not fully
046   studied, this type of condition is more realistic as it does not require any detailed specification in
047   terms of the characteristics of the audio result, offering more flexibility to the user and producer for
048   areas such as movie dubbing and musical composition. If we train these models from scratch, it
049   often demands extensive computational resources Liu et al. (2024); Ghosal et al. (2023).
050   The VTA task, or conditional Foley generation, remains unexplored until recently Wang et al.
051   (2024); Zhang et al. (2024b). One main challenge is that video clips typically contain excessive
052   visual information not always relevant to audio generation. Moreover, synchronization is hard be-
053   tween video and audio output, with recent solutions such as temporal masking Xie et al. (2024)
      proving inadequate for complex scenarios. Due to efficiency considerations, current methods often

075   and audio thus becomes the key to solving the problem.
076   While achieving state-of-the-art results, conventional approaches often perform inference in a single
077   pass based on a given text description. This approach struggles to produce high-quality audio when
078   faced with complex or lengthy text conditions. In this paper, we introduce Audio-Agent, which
079   breaks down intricate user inputs using GPT-4 into multiple generation steps. Each step includes
080   a description along with start and end times to effectively guide the audio generation process. Our
081   framework integrates two key tasks: Text-to-Audio (TTA) and Video-to-Audio (VTA). We leverage
082
      a pre-trained TTA diffusion model, Auffusion Xue et al. (2024), with essential adaptations, serving
      as the backbone for our generation process. In the TTA task, Auffusion focuses solely on generating
083
      simple, atomic text inputs. Our framework supports audio generation, editing, and composition, as
084
      illustrated in Figure 1. For the VTA task, we recognize that models such as GPT-4 and other large
085   language models lack sufficient temporal understanding of video clips. To address this problem,
086   we employ moderate fine-tuning to align the two modalities. We utilize the smaller Gemma2-2B-it
087   model, which has 2 billion parameters, and fine-tune an adapter and a projection layer to convert
088   visual inputs into semantic tokens. We then implement cross-attention guidance between the diffu-
089   sion layers of Auffusion. This approach eliminates the need for additional training on a temporal
090   detector, as the semantic tokens inherently contain time-aligned information.
091   The summary of our contributions is as follows: 1) we propose Audio-Agent which utilizes a pre-
092   trained diffusion model as a generation agent, for both TTA and VTA tasks; 2) For TTA, Audio-
093   Agent can handle complex text input, which is broken down into simple and atomic generation con-
094   ditions for the diffusion model to make inference on; 3) For VTA, we fine-tune an open-source LLM
095   (Gemma2-2B-it) to bridge the modality gap between video and audio modalities to align the un-
096   derlying semantic and temporal information. Through extensive evaluation, our work demonstrates
097   on-par results compared to the state-of-the-art task-specific models trained from scratch, while ca-
098   pable of producing high-quality audio given long and complex textual input. We hope our work can
099
      motivate more relevant works on multi-event long-condition TTA generation, which to our knowl-
      edge has not yet been fully explored despite its high potential in various content generations where
100
      high-quality audio is essential.

183   3.1     P RELIMINARIES
184
185   Audio Latent Diffusion Model Recent research adapted the successful latent diffusion models from
186   the image domain to the audio domain. A typical audio latent diffusion model such as Auffussion
187   first converts the audio wave into mel spectrogram, followed by VAE encoding into the relevant
188
      latent space. Inference is the reverse process, where the predicted latent is decoded by VAE and then
      converted back from mel spectrogram into audio wave through a vocoder such as HiFi-GAN Kong
189
      et al. (2020). The latent diffusion process can be regarded as the same as the standard latent diffusion
190
      model on image generation Rombach et al. (2022).
191
192   Semantic token AudioLM Borsos et al. (2023) was among the first to propose a two-stage method
193   for speech synthesis. In their method, the semantic tokens are derived from representations pro-
194   duced by an intermediate layer of w2v-BERT Chung et al. (2021). We choose an open-sourced
195   HuBERT Hsu et al. (2021) model to produce the semantic representation, since HuBERT can model
196   long-term temporal structure in a generative framework. Although only the smallest Hubert model
197   has its quantizer released and open-sourced, we found that the released small model is already
198   enough to assist the diffusion model in generating high-quality and temporally aligned predictions.
199
200   3.2     GPT-4 AS AN ACTION PLANNER FOR TTA TASK
201
202   Given a long, complex text condition, we ask GPT-4 to decompose the description into simple
203
      and atomic generation steps. GPT-4 has the freedom to decide how many steps to generate. We
      additionally restrict GPT-4 to keep the minimum number of necessary generation steps. This step
204
      instruction produces a good balance avoiding either extreme of being too abstract or too specific
205
      with unnecessary details. We also inform GPT-4 that the user may revise the text requirement in
206   subsequent conversations so that our framework can perform multi-turn conversational generation.
207   The output of GPT-4 consists of a JSON file, which contains a series of function calls of the agent
208   model with text description provided. In addition, to support variable length generation and multi-
209   event generation, GPT-4 also provides the start time and end time for each call which can overlap
210   with each other. After obtaining the generation result for each step, we add waveforms together
211   based on their time range. See Appendix A.1 for a prompt example.
212

235   To efficiently capture both visual and temporal information while compressing the video data, we
236   employ CLIP as a frame-wise feature tokenizer. CLIP is compatible with arbitrary frame sampling
237   strategies, enabling a more flexible frame-to-video feature aggregation scheme as noted by Cheng
238   et al. (2024). We pool the information within each frame to reduce the sequence size, resulting in a
239   vector f r of size N × D, where N is the number of frames and D is the CLIP hidden size. We set
240   the frame rate to 21.5 Hz and use CLIP ViT-L/14 by default.
241   Inter-frame information is crucial for the model to achieve temporal alignment. Previous meth-
242   ods Iashin & Rahtu (2021); Du et al. (2023) require extracting both RGB and optical flow informa-
243   tion within and across frames. In our design, we add a temporal connector after obtaining frame-
244   wise features. The temporal connector consists of a 1D convolution block and a projection layer.
245   The convolution block aggregates the inter-frame features together while preserving the temporal
246   order. The projection layer projects the features into LLM’s embedding space.

248   3.4   LLM FOR SEMANTIC TOKEN GENERATION ON VTA TASK
249
250   Semantic tokens allow us to represent continuous audio information in discrete semantic form. We
251
      denote the continuous audio ground truth as a ∈ RC×L , where C is the number of channels and
      L is the time of the audio clip times sample rate. The Hubert audio tokenizer applies the K-means
252
      algorithm to convert the representation into LLM-aware acoustic tokens. Specifically, we obtain the
253
      indices s ∈ {0, ..., 499}N from the audio by comparing it with the encoded audio with centroids,
254   and N is the sequence length.
255
256   During training and inference, we feed the model with encoded video embedding and caption, to-
257
      gether with the instruction prompt. To better differentiate the video input with text condition and
      instruction, we wrap the encoded video feature with special tokens as modality indicators. Specif-
258
      ically, we wrap the video caption with ⟨Caption⟩, ⟨/Caption⟩ indicators and video embedding in
259
      an embedded sequence of ⟨Video⟩, ⟨/Video⟩ indicators. In doing so, we avoid the possibility of
260   confusing the LLM with different kinds of information. See Appendix A.2.

513   5     C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
514
515   5.1   L IMITATION AND F UTURE W ORK
516
      Our framework experiences a drop in performance when given complex text conditions for the TTA
517
      task, which is more severe in other baseline methods. We believe it is a worthwhile direction in the
518   future for understanding long complex captions with improved fine-grained distinctions between
519   multiple events. We may also utilize the LLM’s versatility involving audio captioning tasks and
520   video captioning tasks. The above are worthwhile future directions to explore.
521
522   5.2   C ONCLUSION
523
524   In this paper, we present Audio-Agent, a multimodal framework for both text-to-audio and video-
525   to-audio tasks. Our model offers a conversation-based method for audio generation, editing and
526   composition, facilitating audio generation conditioned on multievent complex descriptions. For
527   the video-to-audio task, we propose an efficient method to achieve visual synchronization. Through
528   extensive experiments, we show that our model can synthesize high-fidelity audio, ensuring semantic
529
      alignment with input. Additionally, our work takes an initial, significant step toward multi-event
      long-condition TTA generation which has not been fully explored.
