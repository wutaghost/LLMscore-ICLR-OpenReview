009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent neural language models show impressive capabilities on a wide range of
012            tasks. However, it is not fully understood how the knowledge of the language is
013            encoded in these models. In this work, we focus on the simplest case of languages,
014            regular languages, and study language models trained on strings matching certain
015            regular expressions. We propose a method, dubbed LaMFA, to recover the full
016            knowledge of the regular language model by hardening it into a finite automaton.
017
               Such hardening is conducted by empirically partition the latent space of language
               models into finite states, and then recover a deterministic finite automaton by
018
               the estimated transition probabilities between these states. Through experiments
019
               on regular languages of varying complexity, we demonstrate that LaMFA can
020            effectively extract DFA that consistently replicate the performance of the original
021            language model. Notably, the extracted DFAs exhibit enhanced generalization
022            capabilities, achieving 100% accuracy even in out-of-distribution scenarios

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Recent progress on large language models (Brown et al., 2020; Chowdhery et al., 2022; OpenAI,
028   2023) has shown impressive capabilities of neural networks on a remarkably wide range of tasks
029
      such as chatbot (OpenAI, 2023), code generation (Chen et al., 2021), math word problem solv-
      ing (Lewkowycz et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023), theorem proving (Polu & Sutskever,
030
      2020; Jiang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b;a) and even tasks on other modalities such as image
031
      classification (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), text-to-image generation (Koh et al., 2024), VQA (OpenAI,
032   2023). Some postulate that certain large language models such as GPT-4 have made an important
033   step towards Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) (Bubeck et al., 2023).
034
035
      Impressive as their achievements are, the idea behind these large language models is strikingly simple.
      As all languages (and further all sorts of information) consist of sequences of tokens (characters, bits,
036
      etc) xi , it all boils down to model the decomposed joint distribution

                  Given a neural language model trained exclusively on strings conforming to a
                 regular expression, can one recover an equivalent automaton from it?
071
072
      If successful, such recovery would provide insights into how language models compress and represent
073
      linguistic knowledge, and qualify the knowledge they have acquired.
074
075   We focus on two prominent architectures: LSTM (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) and GPT
076   (decoder-only transformers) (Vaswani et al., 2017; Radford et al., 2019). We propose a hardening
077   process to convert a language model into an equivalent finite automaton, a method we term LaMFA
078
      (Language Model to Finite Automaton). Given a trained language model, we begin by sampling
      strings it generates. We then discretize the state space using clustering techniques such as k-means.
079
      For LSTMs, the state space is naturally defined as its latent space. For GPTs, we hypothesize that
080
      the latent space immediately preceding the final linear layer serves as the state space. Next, we
081   merge and denoise the states using the estimated transition matrix based on the existing partition,
082   thereby reducing potential redundancy. A DFA is then computed using the final state partition
083   and the transition matrix. An equivalent regex can further be obtained using the state elimination
084   method (Brzozowski & McCluskey, 1963).
085
      We conducted experiments on five different regular languages, varying in their circuit complex-
086
      ity (Arora & Barak, 2009) and context dependency, as shown in Table 1. Our experiments reveal
087   several key insights into the behavior of language models on regular languages. We find that all
088   models perform exceptionally well on languages with local context dependency, regardless of circuit
089   complexity. However, languages requiring global context pose significant challenges, especially for
090   LSTM models. Notably, LaMFA successfully extract DFA from the trained models, which often
091   demonstrate improved validity rates and strong generalization capabilities. In some cases, these
092   extracted DFA achieve high consistency with the original one, while in others, they encode more
093   states, particularly in larger models. These findings suggest a complex interplay between model
094   architecture, size, and the nature of the language being modeled.
095   The contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows.
096
097          • We conduct experiments on five regular languages with varying complexity, to investigate
098            how linguistic knowledge is encoded and compressed in language models.
099
100          • We propose a simple method, LaMFA, to recover the knowledge from trained language
101
               models and empirically show that it can effectively extract DFA of high consistency with
               the neural model;
102
103          • Our observations draw new insights of the complex interplay between model architectures,
104            language complexity, and the structure of extracted DFA;
105
106          • We argue that this pipeline potentially serve as a benchmark for improved interpretability of
107            language models. We release all codes as well as the checkpoints of language models in the
               experiments

      2   R ELATED W ORK

      Many efforts have been made on explaining the knowledge captured by the neural language model for
111
      safety or ethical concerns, and its further developing (Madsen et al., 2022). Given the complex nature
112   of both natural language and deep networks, existing explanation methods are based on knowledge
113   probing, i.e. inspect the existence of specific knowledge in the model through prediction tasks or
114   ablations (Tenney et al., 2019; Dalvi et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2020; Shi et al., 2016; Meng et al.,
115   2023; Madsen et al., 2022; Allen-Zhu & Li, 2024b). Such probing is conducted at different levels.
116   For example, Jiang et al. (2020) assess the storage of factual knowledge through automatic prompting.
117   Other existing works use predicting tasks to probe the existence of specific types of linguistic
118   information in the hidden layers (Shi et al., 2016; Tenney et al., 2019). Meng et al. (2023) identify
119   neurons associated with specific factual knowledge by causal interventions. Although probing helps
120
      in locating knowledge, the overall generating mechanism of the language model remains unexplained.
121   Recent works focus on assessing the expressive power of neural networks with their ability to
122   recognize formal languages. Theoretically, LSTMs have been demonstrated to be strictly more
123   powerful than regular languages, capable of perfectly emulating finite-state automata Merrill (2019).
124   Empirically, Gers & Schmidhuber (2001), Sennhauser & Berwick (2018) and Bhattamishra et al.
125   (2020b) have evaluated the potential of LSTMs to acquire context-free grammars. Regarding
126
      transformers, theoretical limitations have derived for different restricted form of transformers on
      recognizing formal languages of different circuit complexity (Hahn, 2020; Hao et al., 2022; Merrill
127
      et al., 2022; Merrill & Sabharwal, 2023; Li et al., 2024). For example, Merrill & Sabharwal (2023)
128
      show that log-precision transformers Merrill & Sabharwal (2024) are upper-bounded by uniform TC0 ,
129   i.e. they are only possible to compute formal grammars that can be simulated by a circuit in uniform
130   TC0 . Empirically, Bhattamishra et al. (2020a) examined LSTM and encoder-only transformers’ ability
131   to recognize regular languages and implement counter mechanisms. Liu et al. (2023) demonstrated
132   that transformers can learn automata with fewer layers than theoretically expected.
133
      The extraction of deterministic finite automata from RNNs that recognizing formal languages has
134
      been extensively studied over the past few decades. (Giles et al., 1991; Omlin & Giles, 1996; Das
135   & Mozer, 1993; Weiss et al., 2018; Michalenko et al., 2019). Early work by Giles et al. (1991)
136   and Omlin & Giles (1996) focus on simple second-order RNNs. More recently, (Weiss et al., 2018)
137   extended this study to more complex architectures such as GRU and LSTM. Our work builds upon
138   this foundation by further extending the extraction process to transformer-based models.
139
      A key distinction of our study is its focus on generative probabilistic language models, whereas
140
      previous works primarily examined RNNs and transformers trained on language recognition tasks,
141   which result in deterministic models. By investigating generative language models, our research
142   complements and expands upon this established body of work. Concurrent work by Allen-Zhu & Li
143   (2024a) aligns with this effort. They focus on a family of synthetic context-free languages exhibiting
144   hierarchical structures. By probing the trained model’s latent states quantify attention patterns, they
145   suggest that GPT models learn CFGs by implementing a dynamic programming-like algorithm. In
146   comparison, we focus on regular languages, which provide a simpler yet powerful framework for
147   analyzing model behavior, allowing us to precisely control the complexity and context dependency of
148   the input. By utilizing finite automata as our analytical tool, we can examine both RNN-based and
149
      transformer-based architectures through a unified lens.

      Deterministic finite automata. A deterministic finite automaton can be considered as a special
      Turing machine where the machine can only read from left to right (i.e. one-way) and cannot write in
188
      the tape (i.e. read-only). Formally, a DFA is defined as a 5-tuple (Q, V, δ, q0 , F ), consisting of (1) a
189
      finite set of states Q; (2) a finite set of input symbols called the alphabet (or vocabulary) V ; (3) a
190   transition function δ : Q × V → Q; (4) an initial state (or start state) q0 ∈ Q; (5) a set of accept states
191   (or final states) F ⊆ Q (often depicted with double circles). Some examples of DFA and regular
192   expressions are shown in Figure 1. These DFA/regex are also used in our experiments.
193
194   Equivalence of regular expressions and DFA. Both regular expressions and DFA specify each
195   a certain language L ⊆ V ∗ . It is a commonly known fact that regular expressions and DFA are
196   equivalent in the sense that they both specify all regular languages. Algorithms exist for converting
197   between regular expressions and DFAs, often utilizing non-deterministic finite automata (NFA) as an
198   intermediate step (Kleene, 1956; McNaughton & Yamada, 1960). This equivalence allows us to use
199
      these representations interchangeably in formal language theory and practical applications.

201   4    M ETHODOLOGY
202
203   The pipeline of our proposed method LaMFA is shown in Figure 2. LaMFA begins with a trained
204   language model. The language model is trained on a dataset of strings matching a given but unknown
205   regular expression r∗ using an auto-regressive loss akin to GPT. Then, we sample strings Xi using
206   the language model (considered as a generative network) and do clustering on the features of all
207
      substrings Xi [: t], i.e. the first t characters of Xi , in the latent space before the last linear layer. Next,
      each substring Xi [: t] is now attached to one center ci,t ∈ C of these clusters and we estimate a
208
209
      transition matrix P ∈ [0, 1]k×|V |×k using all triplets
210                                         (ci,t , Xi [t], ci,t+1 ) ∈ C × V × C,
211   where k := |C| is the number of clusters and |V | is the alphabet size. To mitigate the effect of the
212   randomness of the clustering algorithm and the noise, an additional merging and denoising procedure
213   is applied to merge redundant cluster classes in C and remove noisy transition patterns in P . A
214   DFA is then obtained using the estimated transition matrix and a corresponding regular expression
215   is computed using the classical state elimination method (Brzozowski & McCluskey, 1963). In the
      following, we give detailed introductions to the training settings and the LaMFA method.

236   4.1   T RAINING DATA G ENERATION
237
238
      We select 5 simple regular languages to generate datasets for training the language model: alter,
      mdY, end0, parity0, and div3. The DFA of some are visualized in Figure 1. A summary of
239
      the datasets can be found in Table 1. Specifically, alter consists of strings that alternate 0 and
240
      1, following the regex pattern 0(10)∗. mdY contains real date strings in the format mm/dd/yyyy,
241   matching the regex \d{2}/\d{2}/\d{4}. end0 includes strings ending with 0. parity0 contains
242   strings with an even number of 0s. div3 consists of binary integers divisible by 3.
243
244
      These languages vary in their circuit complexity of their grammars. alter and mdY belong to the
      complexity group AC0 , i.e. they can be recognized by constant-depth circuit families with polynomial
245
      size (Arora & Barak, 2009). However the rest 3 languages are not, thus belong to the complexity
246
      group TC0 . According to previous empirical and theoretical studies (Bhattamishra et al., 2020a;
247   Li et al., 2024), transformers struggles in recognizing regular languages outside AC0 . Thus it is
248   interesting to examining if similar conclusion can draw in language generation ability.
249
250
      Beyond circuit complexity, these languages exhibit varying degrees of context dependency. We
      define a language as having local context dependency if recognizing it requires only a constant-length
251
      context window. Conversely, languages with global context dependency necessitate information from
252
      the entire input sequence. Analysis of the regular expressions reveals that alter, mdY, and end0
253   exhibit local dependency, whereas parity0 and div3 require global context. For example, alter
254   can be recognized using a context window of merely two characters.
255
256
      We consider a random walk on the DFA graph to generate strings. It starts from the initial state
      and terminating only on the final states. For each episode of the random walk, the characters on
257
      all traversed edges then form a valid string accepted by the DFA. The only randomness we need to
258
      introduce is in the choice of the next character to read. For this, we apply a uniform distribution on all
259   possible actions/characters. Notably, there is an extra action ‘terminate’ in each final state. We follow
260   this data-generating process to generate 10000 examples for div3, 50000 examples for parity0
261   and end0. For mdY, we generate date strings from 01/01/1900 to 03/16/2023, in the m/d/Y format,
262   with 50000 examples. For alter, we generate all 44 possible examples under the constraint on
263   maximum length (≤ 88). Note that training data generated as above can contain repetitive strings.

419   5.2.1    L ANGUAGE MODEL PERFORMANCE
420
421   The results in Table 2 reveal that the context dependency feature of regular languages has a more
422
      significant impact on language models’ performance than circuit complexity. All models, regardless of
      their architecture, demonstrated exceptional performance on languages with local context dependency
423
      (i.e., alter, mdY, and end0), achieving an average accuracy of 98.34%. This high performance
424

427   particularly for LSTM models. While GPT-nano maintained relatively high performance with an
428
      average accuracy of 91.74% on these globally dependent languages, LSTM models showed a marked
      decrease in performance (53.37% on parity0, 41.25% on div3).
429
430   Among different architectures, GPT-nano consistently outperformed others, achieving valid rates
431   higher than 98% across all five datasets. It’s worth noting that neural language models, like most
      ‘soft’ algorithms, rarely achieve perfect (100%) accuracy.

496   5.2.3   G ENERALIZATION CAPABILITY
497
      LaMFA-DFA generally achieved better evaluation performance compared to the original neural
498
      models. For instance, recovered DFAs based on all three architectures reached 100% valid rate
499   and lower cross-entropy on the end0 dataset. Table 4 presents the out-of-distribution (OOD)
500   generalization evaluation results. For GPT-tiny and GPT-nano, the hardened models consistently
501   demonstrated higher OOD valid rates. For LSTM, on alter, end0, and mdY, LaMFA improved
502   the valid rate while maintaining comparable cross-entropy loss.
503
      Comparing OOD performance (Table 4) with in-distribution performance (Table 2), we noticed that
504
      both GPT-tiny and GPT-nano experienced significant drops in valid rate on parity0 (from 70.4 to
505   50.52, and 98.05 to 76.56, respectively). Interestingly, these observation align with previous DFA
506   extraction studies on language recognization RNNs (Giles et al., 1991; Das & Mozer, 1993), which
507   showed that extracted rules often exhibit better generalization ability than the original neural models.

510   6   C ONCLUSION AND DISCUSSIONS
511
512   This paper presents a pioneering study bridging probabilistic modeling with symbolic computation
513   models (automata). By examining trained language models on regular languages of varying complex-
514   ity, we demonstrate that context dependency is the dominant factor in language modeling complexity.
515   This insight offers new perspectives on regular language complexity and the expressiveness of lan-
516   guage models. Our proposed method, LaMFA, successfully extracted DFAs from trained models,
517   often showing consistency with the original models in terms of validity and cross-entropy loss. In
518   some cases, extracted DFAs captured more nuanced representations than the minimal ground truth
519
      DFA. LaMFA-extracted DFAs generally demonstrated better evaluation performance and improved
      out-of-distribution generalization compared to the original neural models, aligning with previous
520
      findings in DFA extraction studies. It marks a significant advancement in model interpretability
521
      and generalization. Our observations reveal a complex interplay between model size, language
522   complexity, and the structure of extracted DFA.
523
524   This research complements existing work on regular language recognition models and opens new
525
      avenues for studying language models through the lens of symbolic computation. By establishing
      this connection, we pave the way for future investigations that combining probabilistic and symbolic
526
      approaches in computational linguistics and machine learning. Furthermore, we argue that this
527
      pipeline—training models on multiple different regular languages and investigating the extracted
528   DFA—can potentially serve as a benchmark for analyzing language models of different architectures.
529   To facilitate future development and research in this area, we are releasing all codes and checkpoints
530   used in this study.
531
      However, it’s crucial to acknowledge the limitations of this study. First, our focus on formal languages,
532
      specifically regular languages, limits the direct generalization of our findings to natural language
533
      processing tasks, which involve far more complex linguistic structures and ambiguities. Second, while
534   our DFA extraction algorithm yielded promising results, there is potential for developing stronger,
535   more efficient algorithms that could extract even more accurate or compact automata representations.
536   Finally, our experiments were conducted on relatively small-scale models compared to the massive
537   language models currently at the forefront of AI research. Extending this work to larger-scale models
538   could reveal different behaviors or challenges, particularly in terms of computational feasibility
539   and the complexity of extracted automata. These limitations point to valuable directions for future
      research in this area.

      A    E XTENDED RELATED WORKS
757
758
      Probing knowledge in language models. Many efforts have been made on explaining the knowl-
759   edge captured by the neural language model for safety or ethical concerns, and its further develop-
760   ing (Madsen et al., 2022). Given the complex nature of both natural language and deep networks,
761   existing explanation methods are based on knowledge probing, i.e. inspect the existence of specific
762   knowledge in the model through prediction tasks or ablations (Tenney et al., 2019; Dalvi et al., 2022;
763   Jiang et al., 2020; Shi et al., 2016; Meng et al., 2023; Madsen et al., 2022; Allen-Zhu & Li, 2024b).
764   Such probing is conducted at different levels. For example, Jiang et al. (2020) assess the storage of
765   factual knowledge through automatic prompting. Other existing works use predicting tasks to probe
766   the existence of specific types of linguistic information in the hidden layers (Shi et al., 2016; Tenney
767
      et al., 2019). Meng et al. (2023) identify neurons associated with specific factual knowledge by causal
      interventions. Although probing helps in locating knowledge, the overall generating mechanism of
768
      the language model remains unexplained.
769
770
      Symbolic regression. Symbolic regression is the task of learning a symbolic representation from
771
      data. For example, physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) (Raissi et al., 2019) aim at discovering
772   the partial differential equations behind a given dataset. AI Feymann (Udrescu & Tegmark, 2020)
773   also tries to rediscover equations in physics from data using neural networks. Different from symbolic
774   regression, our method only relies on trained parameters and assumes no knowledge at all of the
775   training data.
776
777   Assessing neural networks with formal languages. Recent works focus on assessing the expres-
778   sive power of neural networks with their ability to recognize formal languages. Theoretically, LSTMs
779   have been demonstrated to be strictly more powerful than regular languages, capable of perfectly
780   emulating finite-state automata Merrill (2019). Regarding transformers, theoretical limitations have
781   derived for different restricted form of transformers on recognizing formal languages of different
782
      circuit complexity (Hahn, 2020; Hao et al., 2022; Merrill et al., 2022; Merrill & Sabharwal, 2023).
      For example, Hao et al. (2022) and Hahn (2020) have derived theoretical limitations for hard atten-
783
      tion transformers, where attention distributions focus all probability mass on a single index. Their
784
      findings indicate that AC0 , the class of languages recognizable by constant-depth circuit families,
785   serves as an upper bound for the formal languages that hard-attention transformers can recognize.
786   Notably, the formal language parity0 falls outside AC0 . Merrill & Sabharwal (2023) show that
787   log-precision transformers Merrill & Sabharwal (2024) are upper-bounded by uniform TC0 , i.e.
788   they are only possible to compute formal grammars that can be simulated by a circuit in uniform
789   TC0 . Empirically, Bhattamishra et al. (2020a) examined Transformers’ ability to recognize regular
790   languages and implement counter mechanisms. Liu et al. (2023) demonstrated that transformers can
791   learn automata with fewer layers than theoretically expected. Sennhauser & Berwick (2018) and
792   Bhattamishra et al. (2020b) have evaluated the potential of LSTMs to acquire context-free grammars.
793   In this work, we focus on probabilistic language models, i.e. neural networks trained with the
794   language modeling task, instead of recognition. Concurrent work by Allen-Zhu & Li (2024a) aligns
795   with this effort. They focus on a family of synthetic context-free languages exhibiting hierarchical
796   structures. By probing the trained model’s latent states quantify attention patterns, they suggest that
797   GPT models learn CFGs by implementing a dynamic programming-like algorithm. In comparison,
798   we focus on regular languages, which provide a simpler yet powerful framework for analyzing model
799
      behavior. This approach allows us to precisely control the complexity and context dependency of
      the input. By utilizing finite automata as our analytical tool, we can examine both RNN-based and
800
      transformer-based architectures through a unified lens. Furthermore, this approach enables us to build
801
      upon previous theoretical works, highlighting the crucial distinctions between language generation
802   and recognition tasks.
803
804   Finite automata extraction. The extraction of deterministic finite automata from RNNs has been
805   extensively studied over the past few decades. (Giles et al., 1991; Omlin & Giles, 1996; Das & Mozer,

807   & Giles (1996) focus on simple second-order RNNs. Giles et al. (1991) pioneered this field by
808   developing a dynamic clustering algorithm to extract production rules from trained second-order
809   RNNs. This method involved state clustering, transition mapping, and graph reduction to obtain
      minimal DFA representations. They proposed that in some cases this approach often resulted in
