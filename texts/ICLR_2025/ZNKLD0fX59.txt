                                                    A BSTRACT
031            In recent years, thanks to innovations in 3D scene representation, novel view syn-
032            thesis and photo-realistic dense 3D reconstruction from multi-view images, such
033            as neural radiance field (NeRF) and 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS), have garnered
034            widespread attention due to their superior performance. However, most works rely
035            on low dynamic range (LDR) images and representations of scenes, which limits
036            the capturing of richer scene details. Prior works have focused on high dynamic
037
               range (HDR) scene recovery, typically require repeatedly capturing of multiple
               sharp images with different exposure times at fixed camera positions, which is
038
               time-consuming and challenging in practice. For a more flexible data acquisi-
039
               tion, we propose a one-stage method: CasualHDR to easily and robustly recover
040            the 3D HDR scene from casual videos with auto-exposure (AE) enabled, even in
041            the presence of severe motion blur and varying exposure time. CasualHDR con-
042            tains a unified differentiable physical imaging model which jointly optimize (i.e.
043            bundle adjust) exposure time, camera response function (CRF), continuous-time
044            camera motion trajectory on SE(3), and the 3DGS-based HDR scene. Extensive
045            experiments demonstrate that our approach outperforms existing reconstruction
046            methods in terms of robustness and rendering quality. Three applications can be
047            achieved after the 3DGS HDR scene reconstruction: novel-view synthesis, image
048
               deblurring (deblur input images) and HDR editing (adjust the exposure time thus
               brightness of the input images).

051   1    I NTRODUCTION
052
053   Photo-realistic 3D scene reconstruction and Novel View Synthesis (NVS) are essential areas in com-
      puter vision with applications in VR/AR, autonomous driving, and embodied AI, offering immer-


                                                           1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      sive experiences for both humans and AI agents. Neural Radiance Fields (NeRFs) (Mildenhall et al.,
055   2020) have become a mainstream approach in NVS due to their high-quality rendering. The intro-
056   duction of 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) (Kerbl et al., 2023) further advanced the field. In contrast
057   to implicit representations like NeRFs, 3DGS uses explicit 3D Gaussian primitives, greatly improv-
058   ing training and rendering efficiency yielding high-quality images, making it a popular choice.
059
      However, most 3D reconstruction methods struggle with high-contrast inputs, assuming good-
060
      quality images with consistent exposure conditions and low dynamic range (LDR). Limited dynamic
061   range of the inputs hinders 3D scene representations from reconstructing fine details in high dynamic
062   range (HDR) environments, thereby restricting its further applications, e.g., 3D HDR content cre-
063   ation. Although 2D HDR contents (i.e. images and videos) have been standardized, consumed and
064   exploited in recent years (Hannuksela et al., 2015; ITU-R, 2018; Alakuijala et al., 2019), 3D HDR
065   free-viewpoint (volumetric) content is still a new concept with great potential value. Therefore, re-
066   constructing high dynamic range (HDR) scenes is of significant practical value for achieving better
067   visual effects and meeting the needs of downstream tasks.
068   Current 3D HDR reconstruction methods can be divided into two categories. The first category, e.g.
069   RawNeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2022) and LE3D (Jin et al., 2024) etc., takes in noisy RAW images,
070   aiming to reconstruct noise-free 3D HDR scenes. The second category, represented by HDR-NeRF
071   (Huang et al., 2022) , HDR-GS (Cai et al., 2024), HDR-Plenoxels (Jun-Seong et al., 2022) and
072   Cinematic Gaussians (Wang et al., 2024a), draws inspiration from HDR imaging (HDRI), using
073   multi-exposure LDR images at fixed positions as inputs to reconstruct the 3D HDR scene while
074   learning camera response function (CRF). However, the strict inputs and high reconstruction costs
075
      limit their flexibility and broader applications. The challenges include: 1) Data acquisition of RAW
      images and accurate exposure time is ususally expensive due to the use of professional equipment.;
076
      2) In low-light conditions, long exposure times increase the risk of motion blur from camera shake,
077
      reducing reconstruction quality; 3) The geometric consistency will be compromised if given inaccu-
078   rate camera pose initialization, as the camera poses are not being optimized. Thus, a key challenge
079   is reducing the cost of data acquisition, enabling high-quality 3D HDR scene reconstruction with
080   consumer-grade devices.
081
      Most modern consumer-grade cameras use auto-exposure during video recording, automatically
082
      adjusting exposure time based on ambient lighting. This expands the captured dynamic range in
083
      the video, making it possible for us to reconstruct 3D HDR scenes. However, naively applying
084   these videos to existing HDR 3D reconstruction methods presents several challenges: 1) Accurate
085   exposure times for individual frames are often unknown; 2) Auto-exposure can cause inconsistencies
086   in brightness between frames, leading to pose estimation errors in structure from motion (SfM)
087   frameworks; 3) In low-light conditions, longer exposure times combined with camera movement
088   during recording often cause severe motion blur.
089
      To address these challenges, we cannot assume that the camera is static during the exposure time
090   as in previous methods. Therefore, we must also account for camera motion during this period.
091   Through analyzing the physical imaging process, we found that both motion blur and brightness
092   variations are both directly related to the exposure time. For example, a longer exposure time lead
093   to more severe motion blur and higher image brightness. Thus, camera motion blur can serve as an
094   indicator of the exposure time, providing a useful constraint for joint optimization.
095
      Building on above reasoning, we propose a one-stage method called CasualHDR, which is an uni-
096   fied 3DGS-based HDR reconstruction framework that couples the physical imaging model with
097   camera motion representation, impoving the robustness and flexibility. In our designed unified
098   imaging model, the continuous-time camera trajectory on SE(3), exposure time, and camera re-
099   sponse function are jointly optimized and mutually constrained. Therefore, our approach does not
100   require ground truth exposure times as previous methods.
101
      Our method takes as input a casual video captured by a consumer-grade imaging device, where
102   each frame exhibits brightness variations and motion blur due to different unknown exposure times.
103   To evaluate the effectiveness of our method, we conducted experiments using synthetic datasets
104   generated by Blender and self-captured real-world datasets. The results demonstrate that our method
105   outperforms other approaches in 3D HDR reconstruction, achieving high-quality rendering.
106
      In summary, our contributions can be outlined as follows:

121   2.1   H IGH DYNAMIC R ANGE I MAGING
122
123   High Dynamic Range Imaging (HDRI) enhances luminosity beyond standard digital imaging by
124   merging multi-exposure LDR images from fixed poses. In video capture, alternating long and short
125
      exposures achieves similar effects. Recently, deep learning approaches have treated HDRI as an
      image domian translation task, designing networks to convert LDR to HDR images. However,
126
      camera disturbances often lead to ghosting artifacts. To address this, Gryaditskaya et al. (2015)
127
      proposed an adaptive metering algorithm to adjust exposure and reduce motion artifacts, while other
128   methods use spatial attention to mitigate motion blur. With the advent of 3D scene representations
129   such as NeRF and 3DGS, methods like (Huang et al., 2022; Jun-Seong et al., 2022; Cai et al., 2024;
130   Huang et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a) have emerged to reconstruct 3D HDR scenes and calibrate
131   CRFs simultaneously. While these methods are effective, they often rely on precise exposure times
132   and struggle with motion blur, highlighting the need for improved robustness and generalizability.
133
134   2.2   I MAGE DEBLURRING
135
136   Image deblurring aims to restore sharp images from blurred ones, and current techniques are catego-
137   rized into three main types. The first type uses hand-crafted priors, such as total variation and heavy-
138   tailed gradient priors, to constrain the solution space, solving for the blur kernel. However, these
139
      methods are limited as different blur kernels can produce similar blurred effects (Krishnan & Fergus,
      2009; Cho & Lee, 2009). The second type is deep learning-based, which achieves end-to-end im-
140
      age restoration by training on large datasets, with notable methods including MPRNet (Zamir et al.,
141
      2021) and Stripformer (Tsai et al., 2022). Despite their success, these 2D approaches sometimes
142   struggle with tasks requiring multi-view geometric consistency. The third type utilizes multi-view
143   blurred images to reconstruct the 3D scene representation and deblur the images while adhering
144   to geometric constraints. Pioneering works in this category include Deblur-NeRF (Ma et al., 2021),
145   PDRF (Peng & Chellappa, 2022), DP-NeRF (Lee et al., 2023a) and BAD-NeRF (Wang et al., 2023).
146   Deblur-NeRF, PDRF and DP-NeRF jointly learn blur kernels with the radiance field to approximate
147   the blurring process, while BAD-NeRF proposed a physical motion blur imaging model that jointly
148   recovers (i.e. bundle adjusts) the radiance field along with camera trajectories on SE(3). Following
149   BAD-NeRF, numerous emerging works (Lee et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2024a; Lee et al., 2024b;a; Sun
150
      et al., 2024; Chen & Liu, 2024; Oh et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b;
      Qi et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024) have proved the effectiveness of continuous SE(3) trajectory rep-
151
      resentations for modeling coupled camera motion and imaging characteristics in the process of joint
152
      3D reconstruction and multi-view image recovery.

      2.3   ROBUST N OVEL V IEW S YNTHESIS
155
156   Novel view synthesis involves generating images from arbitrary viewpoints using a series of images
157   with known poses. Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) has significantly advanced the field by recon-
158   structing the radiance field as the 3D scene representation to render images from new perspectives.
159   Building on NeRF, 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) was proposed, which uses explicit Gaussian
160   primitives to represent the 3D scene, significantly improving training and rendering speeds while
161   maintaining good image quality. Most novel view synthesis methods assume high-quality input data;
      however, when this assumption is violated—such as with blurry images, large exposure variations,

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      or inaccurate poses—the reconstruction quality degrades rapidly, producing artifacts.To address this,
163   NeRF-W (Martin-Brualla et al., 2021) and Gaussian-W (Zhang et al., 2024) attach an optimizable
164   appearance vector to each image, modeling varying appearances from internet-sourced images.
165   HDR-NeRF (Huang et al., 2022) reconstructs HDR 3D scenes using multi-view, multi-exposure
166   images with known precise exposure times, while HDR-HexPlane (Wu et al., 2024) extends this to
167   dynamic scenes, enabling fast reconstruction even with unknown exposure times. Other approaches,
168   such as Fu et al. (2024), tackle reconstruction with inaccurate or random poses, reducing reliance

      2024) is a concurrent work similar to ours, capable of using images with exposure inconsistencies
      and blur as input. However, it focuses on RGB-D SLAM, and its representations of trajectory and

      CRF differ from ours; Meanwhile, it cannot adjust the rendering exposure times fexibly as ours, as
173
      its CRF module follows Jun-Seong et al. (2022). These approaches improve robustness in handling
174   various forms of image degradation, enhancing the quality of novel view synthesis.

      4.1    DATASETS
317
      Synthetic datasets. We generated a synthetic dataset using Blender 3.6 with the Cycles engine,
318   featuring four distinct scenes: Factory, Pool, Cozyroom, and Trolley. Each scene contains 77 images,
319   with manually crafted Bézier camera trajectories. The dataset generation combines physical motion
320   blur imaging model (Wang et al., 2023) and tone mapping from HDR to LDR. For each scene,
321   images were assigned random exposure times, and captured using a continuous camera trajectory,
322   generating sharp HDR images, which were averaged over the exposure period to create motion-
323   blurred images. These HDR blurred images were then processed using the tone-mapping function
      from HDR-NeRF (Huang et al., 2022) to generate the corresponding LDR blurred images.

      Real datasets. Since current HDRI datasets consist of multiple images with known exposure times
325   captured from fixed viewpoints, which differs from our approach of using casual videos for HDR
326   scene reconstruction, we captured a challenging real-world dataset, CasualVideo, using the Intel
327   RealSense D455 and Google Pixel 8 Pro mounted on a DJI RS3 Mini gimbal. The dataset com-
328   prises two subsets: RealSense and Smartphone. RealSense contains four sequences: Yakitori, Toufu,
329   Toufu-vicon and Girls-vicon, where the the latter two sequences have ground truth camera poses
330   from the Vicon motion capture system. Smartphone contains two sequences: Building and Fish.
331   Due to the RealSense camera cannot provide the current exposure times when auto-exposure is
332   enabled, which is detrimental for baseline methods that require exposure time, we implemented our
333   own auto-exposure control with fixed aperture and gain (ISO) following Su & Kuo (2015) on both
334   camera devices. We also developed scripts to extract measured exposure times from the hardware
335   as ground truth labels. Additionally, we utilized the publicly available dataset, ScanNet (Dai et al.,
336   2017), which contains scenes recorded with the auto-exposure feature enabled (Bae et al., 2024), to
337   evaluate the performance of our method on real-world data.

      4.2   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
339
340   We implemented our method using PyTorch within the gsplat framework (Ye et al., 2024) with
341   MCMC strategy (Kheradmand et al., 2024). The optimization of HDR scene representation, implicit
342   CRF representation, camera motion trajectory, and exposure times was performed using the Adam
343   optimizer, with the learning rate for the Gaussian primitives kept consistent with gsplat. To balance
344   performance and efficiency, we set the number of virtual camera poses (i.e., n in Eq. 5) to 10. For
345   initialization, we used HLoc (Sarlin et al., 2019) instead of COLMAP Schönberger & Frahm (2016)
346   like in many other works to initialize camera poses and Gaussian primitives for the synthetic and
347
      our Realsense datasets, because we discover that learning-based SfMs performs more robustly in our
      challenging setting, as the change of exposure time breaks the photo-consistency across consecutive
348
      frames, meanwhile, overexposure and underexposure are challenging for hand-crafted feature detec-
349
      tion. For the ScanNet dataset (Dai et al., 2017) and our Smartphone datasets, DPV-SLAM (Lipson
350   et al., 2024) was used since HLoc (Sarlin et al., 2019) was unable to initialize due to the poor im-
351   age quality of the scenes. Synthetic dataset experiments were conducted on a single NVIDIA RTX
352   3090 (24G VRAM) GPU, while real dataset experiments were performed on a single NVIDIA RTX
353   A6000 (48G VRAM) GPU because the real datasets contain more images and require larger VRAM.

355   4.3   BASELINE M ETHODS AND EVALUATION METRICS
356
      To evaluate the robustness of our method in learning accurate scenes representation under poorly
357
      exposed conditions and server motion blur, we compared it against scene reconstruction methods
358   that handle brightness variations, e.g. HDR-NeRF (Huang et al., 2022), HDR-Plenoxels (Jun-Seong
359   et al., 2022), Gaussian-W (Zhang et al., 2024), as well as method for scene reconstruction from
360   blurred images, such as BAD-Gaussians (Zhao et al., 2024). In addition, 3D-GS (Kerbl et al., 2023)
361   implemented by gsplat (Ye et al., 2024) was included as the comparison baseline. The quality of im-
362   ages rendered from the learned scene is evaluated with commonly used metrics such as PSNR, SSIM
363   (Wang et al., 2004), and LPIPS (Zhang et al., 2018). Furthermore, to evaluate whether our method
364   effectively recovers camera motion trajectories, we compared it with pose estimation method, e.g.
365   HLoc (Sarlin et al., 2019), DPV-SLAM (Lipson et al., 2024), BAD-Gaussians (Zhao et al., 2024).For
366
      pose estimation accuracy, we utilize absolute trajectory error (ATE) with mean and std as the met-

      5    C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this paper, we introduce a novel method CasualHDR for reconstructing 3D HDR scenes from
489   videos casually captured with low-cost cameras, which often exhibit limited dynamic range and
490   motion blur. Our method can reconstruct 3D HDR scene and generate LDR images with given
491   specified exposures and camera poses, providing high robustness and flexibility. By leveraging the
492   auto-exposure capabilities of modern cameras, we incorporate the high dynamic range of captured
493   videos into a unified physical image formation model. This allows for the joint optimization with
494   exposure time, continous-time camera trajectory, and camera response function, enabling accurate
495   HDR scene reconstruction. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our method outperforms previ-
496   ous approaches in 3D HDR reconstruction.
