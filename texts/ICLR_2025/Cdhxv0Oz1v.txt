009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Out-of-distribution (OOD) detection is an anomaly-handling mechanism, for
012            which classification systems should detect outliers with true labels outside
013            the label space, distinguishing them from normal in-distribution (ID) data.
014            Advanced works suggest that gradient information preserves sufficient cues
015            to indicate the confidence of being OOD. However, we discover previous
016            gradient-based detection methods suffer from limited effectiveness mainly due
017            to over-parameterization. As gradient-based OOD scores derive from the over-
018
               parameterized weight space, a widely recognized cause for the suboptimal OOD
               detection performance, there are also some gradient components which lack nec-
019
               essary information, thereby impairing the performance in OOD detection. This
020
               observation motivates us to propose gradient rectification (GradRect), using fisher
021            information matrix to correct gradients in directions that are uninformative to dis-
022            cern the distribution change. Moreover, we connect GradRect with classical the-
023            ories in identifying influential observations, verifying that model fine-tuning with
024            outlier exposure can further improve GradRect. We conduct extensive experi-
025            ments on various OOD detection setups, revealing the power of GradRect against
026            state-of-the-art counterparts.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Deep classification systems often encounter out-of-distribution (OOD) data whose true labels are not
032   in the label space, and in such a situation classifiers cannot make right predictions as in-distribution
033
      (ID) data. This kind of phenomenon can lead to devastating results for many high-risk decision
      making applications (Li & Wechsler, 2005; Du et al., 2022; Shen et al., 2021). To address this issue,
034
      OOD detection aims to detect OOD cases to avoid making wrong predictions (Hendrycks & Gimpel,
035
      2016; Liu et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2022; Djurisic et al., 2022), which can remarkably improve the
036   reliability of deep learning in the open world (Wang et al., 2023).
037
038   OOD detection remains a challenging task, mainly owing to the calibration failures for modern deep
039
      models (Guo et al., 2017; Lee et al., 2018)—a well-trained ID classifier can make arbitrary-high soft-
      max confidence on OOD data, making it unreliable in OOD detection. Accordingly, post-hoc OOD
040
      detection (Liu et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2022; Djurisic et al., 2022) aims at devising more accurate
041
      OOD indicators, i.e. scoring functions, other than softmax confidence. Among them, gradient-based
042   scoring has received particular attentions (Huang et al., 2021; Igoe et al., 2022), which calculates
043   gradient magnitudes w.r.t. model parameters to detect OOD data. Generally, model parameters
044   should converge to local minimum for ID tasks with near-zero gradients after well training, while
045   not for OOD data. Hence, the gradient magnitudes should preserve sufficient information to separate
046   ID and OOD data, making gradient-based OOD detection a promising line of works.
047
      Despite promising performance has been reported, these methods still exhibit certain limitations
048   in practical applications. Inspired by the concept that deep models are susceptible to over-
049   parameterization (Sun & Li, 2022; Djurisic et al., 2022), we clip GradNorm with varying percent-
050   ages of gradient based on magnitudes and report the results of some OOD datasets on common
051   CIFAR-100 and ImageNet benchmarks in Figure 1 (a) and (b). It is worth noting that on certain
052   OOD datasets, as the percentage of clipped components increases, the performance of GradNorm
053   has a slight improvement and then drop accordingly. This observation leads us to conjecture there
      exist some uninformative components in gradient which are redundant and detrimental for OOD

074   To address this deficiency, this paper proposes gradient rectification (GradRect), a novel gradient-
075   based scoring function which can rectify the original gradient information to more informative direc-
076   tions for OOD detection. GradRect rectifies original gradient based on the fisher information matrix
077   (FIM) (Amari et al., 2019), which quantifies the amount of information carried for each direction in
078   the gradient space. By multiplying gradient features with the inverse of the FIM, the adverse effects
079
      of uninformative component will be eliminated and the rectified gradients will align to directions
      that are more informative for OOD detection in the gradient space, leading to more effective and
080
      stable results on OOD detection than previous methods.
081
082   Theoretically, FIM equals to the average Hessian matrix on ID tasks under mild assumptions when
083   using cross entropy loss as the objective for gradient calculation (Karakida et al., 2019). Then, the
084   expression of GradRect is the magnitude of the influence function given the particular input (Koh
085
      & Liang, 2017), which estimates its effect on model predictions. Such a connection lead to two
      benefits. (1) We can interpret GradRect from the perspective in identifying influential observations:
086
      fitting OOD data has much larger influence on model parameters than ID ones, and thus influence
087
      function (and GradRect, equivalently) can effectively discern ID and OOD data. (2) We can justify
088   that outlier exposure (Hendrycks et al., 2018), a particular fine-tuning approach, can further enhance
089   GradRect: making ID (OOD) data with small (large) loss values will shrink (enlarge) the observing
090   influence of ID (OOD) data, thus better separating GradRect and improving OOD detection.
091
      We conduct experiments and establish superior performance on common OOD detection bench-
092
      marks, including classical CIFAR (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) and challenging ImageNet (Deng et al.,
093
      2009) benchmarks. Extensive evaluations show that our GradRect not only achieves superior per-
094   formance over advanced OOD detection methods, but also exhibits greater stability across diverse
095   tasks and backbones than previous gradient-based methods as shown in Figure 1 (c) and (d). More-
096   over, we perform ablation study among diverse backbone models and tasks, further demonstrating
097   the stability of our method. We summarize our key contributions into four folds as follows:
098
099          • We offer new insights to make gradient-based scoring function more reliable in OOD de-
100            tection and present the FIM as a useful tool to estimate the amount of information gradient
101
               carried in each direction w.r.t. the parameter space.
102          • We propose GradRect, which corrects original gradients to more stable and reliable di-
103            rections through the FIM. Our proposed method compensates the drawbacks of instability
104            compared to previous gradient-based detection methods in literature. We hope our method
105
               can draw further attention on exploiting gradients for OOD detection.
106          • Comprehensive experiments are carried out and show the superior performance of
107            GradRect on various OOD detection benchmarks. Extensive experiments demonstrate the
               performance stability of GradRect on various model architectures and tasks.

115   We begin by introducing necessary notations. Denote X ✓ Rd the input space and Y = {1, 2, ..., C}
116   the label space, where d is the input dimension and C is the number of classes. Then, we discern
117   the ID joint distribution PXI YI over X ⇥ Y and the OOD distribution PXO over X . Therein, true
118   labels of OOD data (i.e., xO ⇠ PXO ) are not in Y, which are not predictable for the close-world
119   models. Additionally, we possess a predictor f✓ : X ! RC (i.e., logit outputs) parameterized by ✓,
120
      typically trained on ID data that are i.i.d. drawn from PXI YI to make correct predictions.
121   Out-of-distribution Detection. During test, we may encounter a mixed distribution PX of ID and
122   OOD, defined as PX = ↵PXI + (1 ↵)PXO with ↵ 2 (0, 1) a mixing parameter. We typically
123   employ the scoring function S : X ! R to detect OOD data from ID ones: if S(x) is larger than
124   a threshold ⌧ , we will take the corresponding x as an ID case; otherwise an OOD case. Then, the
125   question is how to find proper scoring functions for effective OOD detection.
126   Scoring Functions. We typically build the scoring functions upon our predictor f✓ . For example, as
127   a well-known baseline scoring function, maximum softmax prediction (MSP) (Hendrycks & Gimpel,
128   2016) takes the confidence of label predictions in discerning ID and OOD cases, namely,

140   As a promising designing criterion, many works (Liang et al., 2017; Huang et al., 2021) use gradi-
141   ent information, calculated by backward propagation, to design new scoring functions. Gradients
142   contain more information than outputs produced by forward propagation (Huang et al., 2021), po-
143   tentially making gradient-based methods a promising line of work towards effective OOD detection.

145   3.1   P REVIOUS M ETHOD : G RAD N ORM
146
147   As a seminal work in gradient-based OOD detection, the GradNorm (Huang et al., 2021) leverages
148   the gradient magnitudes in OOD scoring. It uses the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between
149   the softmax outputs and the uniform distribution u = [1/C, 1/C, . . . , 1/C] 2 RC as the objective,
150   further calculating its gradients w.r.t. model parameters, namely,

155   where KL is the KL divergence. Intuitively, gradient magnitudes should be larger than that for OOD
156   data, for the reason where the models have trained to its minimum for ID cases, but not for OOD.
157   The GradNorm uses higher dimension features than conventional MSP as its inputs, thus containing
158   more information in discerning ID and OOD features and leading to the improved detection perfor-
159   mance. However, high dimension features also contain more components that are useless for OOD
160   detection, causing the unstable and less-than-optimal results of GradNorm across different tasks.
161   Therefore, we raise the following question: Can we rectify the gradient features to discard those
      useless components to further improve gradient-based OOD detection?

224   We have demonstrated that GradRect can be used for post-hoc OOD detection given a well-trained
225   ID classification model, while further improvement with model fine-tuning is also of our interest.
226   Specifically, we study if we can suggest a specific model training scheme, of which the resultant
227   model can further improve GradRect in OOD detection.
228   Outlier Exposure. We find that conventional outlier exposure (OE) (Hendrycks et al., 2019), which
229   originally aims at maximizing the MSP score, can already be used to improve GradRect. Overall,
230   OE makes the model learn to discern ID and OOD data by using the OOD distribution PXO during
231   training. Specifically, OE adopts the learning objective as follows:

309   2018), SUN (Xu et al., 2015), Places365 (Zhou et al., 2017), and Texture (Cimpoi et al., 2014), which
310   are craft by (Huang & Li, 2021) with non-overlappping categories w.r.t. ImageNet. For the CIFAR

317   are both threshold-independent (Davis & Goadrich, 2006): the false positive rate of OOD data when
318   the true positive rate of ID data is at 95% (FPR95); and the area under the receiver operating
319   characteristic curve (AUROC), which is the probability of ID case having greater score than OOD,
320   depicting the relationship between true positive rate and false positive rate.

337   The large-scale ImageNet benchmark can provide clues about model performance in real-world
338   applications due to its large semantic space with about 1k classes. In Table 1, we compare the
339   results of GradRect with advanced methods on ImageNet and report performance for each OOD test

341   metrics, with 47.08% FPR95 and 88.88% AUROC. Compared to GradNorm (Huang et al., 2021),
342   GradRect improves the FPR95 by 7.62% and AUROC by 2.17%, demonstrating the effectiveness of
343
      our method in large-scale applications.
344   Moreover, as visualized in Figure 3, by mitigating the influence of uninformative components in
345   gradient, our proposed GradRect score produces better distinguished distributions, validating the
346   effect of gradient rectification in OOD detection. It is worth noting that performance differences
347   exist among various OOD datasets. We believe that the reason behind this disparity lies in the
348   fact that for OOD datasets with distributions that are further away from the ID data, the gradient
349
      information is less influenced by the quality of the model’s parameters .
350   It’s notable that there is the performance difference between various OOD datasets and we con-
351   sider the reason is that for those OOD datasets which have distribution more similar to ID data,
352   their gradient information is more affected by the quality of model’s parameters. Consequently, the

      In this section, we briefly review the related works in OOD detection and influence function.
467
468   OOD Detection. In real-world deployment, neural network need to figure OOD data which have
469   non-overlapping label space with training data instead of giving wrong predictions, which gives rise
470   to the importance of OOD detection. The phenomenon of the overconfidence in OOD data in neural
471
      network is first revealed in Nguyen et al. (2015). To solve this problem, a plethora of algorithms
      designed for the detection of OOD data have been proposed.
472
473   Based on whether the model needs to be fine-tuned, existing algorithms can be roughly categorized
474   into two types, post-hoc methods and fine-tuning methods. The post-hoc methods aim to detect OOD
475   data without additional training procedures and could be directly applied to any pre-trained models.
476   A main stream of work in this category is to devise a scoring function based on the intermediate or
477
      the final output of the model, such as OpenMax score Bendale & Boult (2015), Maximum Softmax
      Probability Hendrycks & Gimpel (2016), ODIN score Liang et al. (2017), Energy score Liu et al.
478
      (2020); Lin et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2021), Activation rectification (ReAct) Sun et al. (2021), and
479
      ViM score Wang et al. (2022). Another line of work focus on exploiting feature space to discern
480   OOD data, including Mahalanobis distance-based score Lee et al. (2018), non-parametric KNN-
481   based score Sun et al. (2022). The concept of utilizing gradient information to assist OOD detection
482   is first introduced by ODIN score Liang et al. (2017). Their method involves a pre-processing
483   which adds perturbations derived from gradient to input data, increasing the distinction between the
484   softmax scores of ID data and OOD data and resulting in better performance. More recently, Huang
485   et al. Huang et al. (2021) proposed to design scoring function based on gradient norms, which
      is motivated by the observation that ID and OOD data tend to exhibit different gradient patterns

509   In terms of the fine-tuning methods, they typically leverage auxiliary outlier datasets for a retraining
510
      process and apply specific regularization techniques to the model therein. For example, models are
      encouraged to give less confidence predictions Hendrycks & Gimpel (2016); Lee et al. (2018) or

512

514   the scope of this paper mainly focuses on post-hoc methods, superior over fine-tuning methods of
515   being easy to use and general applicability without modifying the training objective. The latter
516   property is especially desirable for the adoption of OOD detection methods in real-world production
517   environments, when the overhead cost of retraining can be prohibitive.
518
      Influence Function. Influence function is a classic method from the robust statistics literature which
519   aims at estimating the effect of removing an individual training point on a model’s parameters and
520   corresponding predictions without the cost of retraining the model Hampel (1974); Cook (1977).
521   The main concept of influence function is to study the impact on models through the lens of their
522   training data. Prior work mainly study influence function on linear models and recently it gains
523   attention in machine learning and can be efficiently approximated based on second-order optimiza-
524   tion techniques Agarwal et al. (2016); Koh & Liang (2017). There is a wide range of application
525   for influence function, such as explaining predictions Koh & Liang (2017), investigating model
526   bias Brunet et al. (2019); Wang et al. (2019), detecting adversarial attacks Cohen et al. (2020) and
527
      OOD generalization problem Ye et al. (2021).

529   6   C ONCLUSION
530
531   OOD detection is a important but challenging problem. We have proposed a relatively simple post-
532   hoc OOD detection method based on rectified gradient. Specifically, we correct gradient to the
533   directions which are more informative based on fisher information to discern the distribution dif-
534   ference between ID and OOD data most. We conduct a variety of ablation tests and verify our
535   effectiveness. Additionally, we analyze the experimental results thus gain insights into the underly-
536
      ing reasons behind the results.
537   Our method gives new state-of-the-art detection results on large-scale out-of-distribution settings
538   without requiring access to anything other than the training data itself. In the future, we will ex-
539   plore the usage scenarios of FIM for other scoring strategies and fine-tuning approaches in OOD
      detection.
