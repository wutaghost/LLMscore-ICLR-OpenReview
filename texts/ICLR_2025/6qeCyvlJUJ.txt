011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Deep neural networks can be exploited using natural adversarial samples, which
014
               do not impact human perception. Current approaches often rely on synthetically
               altering the distribution of adversarial samples compared to the training
015
               distribution. In contrast, we propose EvoSeed, a novel evolutionary strategy-based
016
               algorithmic framework that uses auxiliary Conditional Diffusion and Classifier
017            models to generate photo-realistic natural adversarial samples. We employ
018            CMA-ES to optimize the initial seed vector search, which, when processed by the
019            Conditional Diffusion Model, results in the natural adversarial sample
020            misclassified by the Classifier Model. Experiments show that generated adversarial
021            images are of high image quality, raising concerns about generating harmful
022            content bypassing safety classifiers. We also show that beyond generating
023            adversarial images, EvoSeed can also be used as a red-teaming tool to understand
024            classification systems’ misclassification. Our research opens new avenues for
025
               understanding the limitations of current safety mechanisms and the risk of
               plausible attacks against classifier systems using image generation.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Deep Neural Networks have achieved unprecedented success in various visual recognition tasks.
033   However, their performance decreases when the testing distribution differs from the training
034   distribution, as shown by Hendrycks et al. (2021) and Ilyas et al. (2019). This poses a significant
035   challenge for developing robust deep neural networks capable of handling such distribution shifts.
036   Adversarial samples and adversarial attacks exploit this vulnerability by manipulating images to alter
037   the original distribution.
038   Research by Dalvi et al. (2004) underscores that adversarial manipulations of input data often lead
039   to incorrect predictions from classifiers, raising serious concerns about the security and integrity
040   of classical machine learning algorithms. This concern remains relevant, especially considering
041   that state-of-the-art deep neural networks are highly vulnerable to adversarial attacks involving
042   deliberately crafted perturbations to the input (Madry et al., 2018; Kotyan & Vargas, 2022).
043   Various constraints are imposed on these perturbations, making them subtle and challenging to detect.
044   For example, L0 adversarial attacks such as One-Pixel Attack (Kotyan & Vargas, 2022; Su et al.,
045   2019) limit the number of perturbed pixels, L1 adversarial attacks such as EAD (Chen et al., 2018)
046   restrict the Manhattan distance from the original image, L2 adversarial attacks such as PGD-L2
047   (Madry et al., 2018) restrict the Euclidean distance from the original image, and L∞ adversarial
048   attacks such as PGD-L∞ (Madry et al., 2018) restricts the amount of change in all pixels. Some of
049   these attacks are of White-Box nature such as Madry et al. (2018); Chen et al. (2018), while others
050
      are of Black-Box nature such as Kotyan & Vargas (2022); Su et al. (2019); Chen et al. (2017).
051   While adversarial samples (Madry et al., 2018; Kotyan & Vargas, 2022; Su et al., 2019) expose
052   vulnerabilities in deep neural networks, their artificial nature and reliance on constrained input data
053   limit their real-world applicability. In contrast, the challenges become more pronounced in practical
      situations, where it becomes infeasible to include all potential threats comprehensively within the

084   The general noise-perturbed adversarial examples are specifically crafted by adding small, often
085   imperceptible perturbations to natural images to deliberately make models misclassify. These
086
      perturbations are designed to exploit model vulnerabilities, leading to misclassification. In contrast,
      Natural Adversarial Examples are real-world, unmodified, and naturally occurring examples that
087

088

090   In this context, we present EvoSeed, the first Evolution Strategy-based algorithmic framework
091   designed to generate Natural Adversarial Samples in an unrestricted setting as shown in Figure 2.
092
      Our algorithm requires a Conditional Diffusion Model G and a Classifier Model F to generate
      adversarial samples x for a given classification task. Specifically, it leverages the Covariance Matrix
093
      Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) at its core to enhance the search for adversarial initial
094
      seed vectors z ′ that can generate adversarial samples x. The CMA-ES fine-tunes the generation
095   of adversarial samples through an iterative optimization process based on the Classification model
096   outputs F (x), utilizing them as fitness criteria for subsequent iterations. Ultimately, our objective is
097   to search for an adversarial initial seed vector z ′ that, when used, causes our Conditional Diffusion
098   Model G to generate an adversarial sample x misclassified by the Classifier Model F and is also
099   close to the human perception, as shown in Figure 1.

104

179   3       E VO S EED - E VOLUTION S TRATEGY- BASED A DVERSARIAL S EARCH
180
181
      As illustrated in Figure 2, our algorithm contains three main components: a Conditional Diffusion
182   Model G, a Classifier model F , and the optimizer Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy
183   (CMA-ES). Following the definition of generating adversarial samples as an optimization problem
184   in Equation 4, we optimize the search for the adversarial initial seed vector z ′ using CMA-ES as
185   described by Hansen & Auger (2011). The main benefit of using CMA-ES over other black-box
186   optimizers is its ability to converge with fewer function evaluations, which is essential due to the
187   computational cost of generating images with a Diffusion Model (Stripinis et al., 2024; Loshchilov,
188   2017). We restrict the manipulation of z within an L∞ constraint parameterized by ε. This constraint
189   ensures that each value in the perturbed vector can deviate by at most ε in either direction from
190
      its original value. Further, we define a condition c that the Conditional Diffusion Model G uses to
      generate the image. This condition c is also used to evaluate the classifier model F . We present the
191
      pseudocode for the EvoSeed in the Appendix Section C.1.
192
193   In essence, our methodology leverages the power of the condition c applied to the Generative Model
194   G through a dynamic interplay with Classifier Model F , tailored to find an optimized initial seed
195   vector z ′ to minimize the classification accuracy on the generated image, all while navigating the
196
      delicate balance between adversarial manipulation and preserving a semblance of fidelity using
      condition c. This intricate interplay between the Conditional Diffusion Model G, the Classifier Model
197
      F , and the optimizer CMA-ES is fundamental in crafting effective adversarial samples.
198
199   Since high-quality image generation using diffusion models is computationally expensive, we divide
200   our analysis of EvoSeed into two parts: a) Qualitative Analysis presented in Section 4 to evaluate
201   the quality of adversarial images subjectively, and b) Quantitative Analysis presented in Section 5 to
202
      evaluate the performance of EvoSeed in generating adversarial images. We also present a detailed
      experimental setup and hyperparameters for the CMA-ES algorithm in the Appendix Section C.

372   To illustrate the use of EvoSeed in a partial-information setting, we apply the EvoSeed framework with

499   6     R ELATED W ORK
500
501   Generative models such as GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2020) and Diffusion Models (Sohl-Dickstein
502
      et al., 2015) have emerged as leading tools for content creation and the precise generation of high-
      quality synthetic data. Several studies have employed creativity to generate Adversarial Samples;
503
      some propose the utilization of surrogate models such as (Xiao et al., 2018a; Chen et al., 2023b;a;
504
      Lin et al., 2023; Jandial et al., 2019), while other advocates the perturbation of latent representations
505   as a mechanism for generating adversarial samples (Song et al., 2018; Zhao et al., 2018).
506
507   Many research over the past few years have used generative models to create adversarial samples,
508
      Xiao et al. (2018b) employs spatial warping transformations for their generation. Concurrently,
      Shamsabadi et al. (2020) transforms the image into the LAB color space, producing adversarial
509
      samples imbued with natural coloration. Song et al. (2018) proposes first to train an Auxiliary
510
      Classifier Generative Adversarial Network (AC-GAN) and then apply the gradient-based search
511   to find adversarial samples under its model space. Another research proposes Adversarial GAN
512   (AdvGan) (Xiao et al., 2018a), which removes the searching process and proposes a simple feed-
513   forward network to generate adversarial perturbations and is further improved by Jandial et al. (2019).
514   Similarly, Chen et al. (2023b) proposes the AdvDiffuser model to add adversarial perturbation to
515   generated images to create better adversarial samples with improved FID scores.
516
      Yet, these approaches often have one or more limitations such as, a) they rely on changing the
517   distribution of generated images compared to the training distribution of the classifier, such as (Xiao
518   et al., 2018b; Shamsabadi et al., 2020), b) they rely on the white-box nature of the classifier model to
519   generate adversarial samples such as (Song et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2023b), c) they rely heavily on
520   training models to create adversarial samples such as (Xiao et al., 2018a; Song et al., 2018; Jandial
521   et al., 2019), d) they rely on generating adversarial samples for specific classifiers, such as (Xiao
522   et al., 2018a; Jandial et al., 2019). Thus, in contrast, we propose the EvoSeed algorithmic framework,
523   which does not suffer from the abovementioned limitations in generating adversarial samples.
524   Recent work on image editing with diffusion models leverages the DDIM inversion process (Song
525   et al., 2020) to modify images by reversing the generative process and extracting the initial seed
526   vector. This allows for controlled manipulation with minimal distortion, preserving the image’s core
527   structure while enabling edits to attributes like style, texture, and content (Mokady et al., 2023; Pan
528   et al., 2023; Garibi et al., 2024; Parmar et al., 2023). Seed vector manipulation has thus become a key
529   method for photorealistic image editing. This article extends this approach by using the seed vector
530   to generate adversarial samples instead of traditional edits.
531
532   7     C ONCLUSIONS
533
534   This study introduces EvoSeed, a first-of-a-kind evolutionary strategy-based approach for generating
535   photorealistic natural adversarial samples. Our proposed framework employs an auxiliary Conditional
536   Diffusion Model, a Classifier Model, and CMA-ES to produce natural adversarial examples in a
537   general algorithmic setup. Experimental results demonstrate that EvoSeed excels in discovering high-
538   quality adversarial samples that do not affect human perception. Alarmingly, we also demonstrate how
539   these Conditional Diffusion Models can be maliciously used to generate harmful content, bypassing
      the post-image generation checking by the classifiers to detect inappropriate images.

      B.1   D IFFUSION M ODELS
867
868   The Diffusion Model is first proposed by Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015) that can be described as a
869   Markov chain with learned Gaussian transitions. It comprises of two primary elements: a) The
870   forward diffusion process, and b) The reverse sampling process. The diffusion process transforms
871   an actual distribution into a familiar straightforward random-normal distribution by incrementally
872   introducing noise. Conversely, in the reverse sampling process, a trainable model is designed to
873   diminish the Gaussian noise introduced by the diffusion process systematically.
874   Let us consider a true distribution represented as x ∈ R, where x can be any kind of distribution
875   such as images (Ho et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Ho et al., 2022; Ho & Salimans, 2022),
876   audio (Kong et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2022a;b; Kim et al., 2022), or text (Li et al., 2022). The
877   diffusion process is then defined as a fixed Markov chain where the approximate posterior q introduces
878   Gaussian noise to the data following a predefined schedule of variances, denoted as β1 , β2 . . . βT :
879

      We follow the definition from Song et al. (2018). Given that I represents a collection of images
911
      under consideration that can be categorized using one of the K predefined labels. Let’s consider
912   a testing classifier f : I → {1, 2 . . . K} that can give a prediction for any image in I. Similarly,
913   we can consider an oracle classifier o : O ⊆ I → {1, 2 . . . K} different from the testing classifier,
914   where O represents the distribution of images understood by the oracle classifier. An unrestricted
915   adversarial sample can defined as any image inside the oracle’s domain O but with a different output
916   from the oracle classifier o and testing classifier f . Formally defined as x ∈ O such that o(x) ̸= f (x).
917   The oracle o is implicitly defined as a black box that gives ground-truth predictions. The set O should
      encompass all images perceived as realistic by humans, aligning with human assessment.

938   B.3   C OVARIANCE M ATRIX A DAPTATION E VOLUTIONARY S TRATEGY (CMA-ES)
939
940   Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) (Hansen & Auger, 2011) is an
941   advanced evolutionary algorithm designed for optimizing complex, non-linear, and non-convex
942   functions in continuous domains. It is especially useful in black-box optimization problems where
943   derivative information is unavailable. CMA-ES operates by iteratively refining a population of
944
      candidate solutions. At each iteration, new solutions are generated by sampling from a multivariate
      normal distribution, whose mean and covariance matrix evolve over time. The core innovation of
945
      CMA-ES lies in its covariance matrix adaptation, which allows the algorithm to capture and exploit
946
      variable dependencies and correlations, effectively adjusting the search strategy to the problem
947   landscape.      This adaptation enables the algorithm to efficiently navigate complex and
948   high-dimensional spaces. Through continuous updating of the distribution, CMA-ES balances
949   exploration and exploitation, improving convergence toward optimal solutions without requiring
950   gradient information. The algorithm’s robustness and ability to self-adapt make it a powerful tool for
951   solving challenging optimization problems in various fields. By default, CMA-ES enforces
952   constraints using a smooth, piecewise linear and quadratic transformation into the feasible domain
953   resembling a sine function that ensures continuity, differentiability, and stability. This transformation
954   acts as the identity within the core interval and uses quadratic transformations near boundaries.

1378   F.4    A NALYSIS OF P ERFORMANCE OF E VO S EED WITH D IFFERENTIAL E VOLUTION
1379
       To understand the effect of using other black-box optimizer in our EvoSeed Framework, we
1380
       experiment with Differential Evolution (Storn & Price, 1997) and report the performance in Table 13.
1381   We also find that other black-box optimizers like SPSA (Spall, 1992), L-BFGS-B (Liu & Nocedal,
1382   1989), and TNC (Martens et al., 2010) did not converge, while other optimizers like DIRECT
1383   (Gablonsky & Kelley, 2001) have significantly more number of function evaluations than CMA-ES.
1384   Since gradient-based black-box optimizers fail to converge, we observe that estimating gradients
1385   around the seed vector (initial or pseudo-optimized) is challenging because nearby seed vectors often
1386   yield similar function evaluations, leading to insignificant gradient estimation. This similarity in
1387   function evaluations hinders the convergence of gradient-based optimization methods. In contrast,
1388   evolution-based optimization efficiently explores the search space by significantly altering the seed
1389
       vector. Simultaneously, it exploits by evolving new generations of the seed vector around the
       pseudo-optimal seed vector to enhance optimization.
