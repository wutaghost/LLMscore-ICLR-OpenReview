009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large-scale generative models have achieved remarkable success in a number of
012            domains. However, for sequential decision-making problems, such as robotics,
013            action-labelled data is often scarce and therefore scaling-up foundation models
014            for decision-making remains a challenge. A potential solution lies in leverag-
015            ing widely-available unlabelled videos to train world models that simulate the
016            consequences of actions. If the world model is accurate, it can be used to opti-
017            mize decision-making in downstream tasks. Image-to-video diffusion models are
018
               already capable of generating highly realistic synthetic videos. However, these
               models are not action-conditioned, and the most powerful models are closed-
019
               source which means they cannot be finetuned. In this work, we propose to adapt
020
               pretrained video diffusion models to action-conditioned world models, without
021            access to the parameters of the pretrained model. Our approach, AVID, trains an
022            adapter on a small domain-specific dataset of action-labelled videos. AVID uses
023            a learned mask to modify the intermediate outputs of the pretrained model and
024            generate accurate action-conditioned videos. We evaluate AVID on video game
025            and real-world robotics data, and show that it outperforms existing baselines for
026            diffusion model adaptation. Our results demonstrate that if utilized correctly, pre-
027            trained video models have the potential to be powerful tools for embodied AI.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Large generative models trained on web-scale data have driven rapid improvement in natural lan-
033
      guage processing (Brown, 2020; Touvron et al., 2023; Achiam et al., 2023), image generation (Rom-
      bach et al., 2022), and video generation (OpenAI, 2024). The potential for scaling to unlock progress
034
      in sequential-decision making domains, such as robotics, gaming, and virtual agents, has invoked a
035
      surge of interest in foundation models for decision-making agents (Reed et al., 2022), particularly
036   for robotics (Brohan et al., 2022; 2023). However, the quantity of action-labelled data in these do-
037   mains remains a significant bottleneck (Padalkar et al., 2023). This raises the question of to how
038   to utilize widely-available unlabelled videos to bootstrap learning (Baker et al., 2022; Bruce et al.,
039   2024). One promising approach is to use video data to learn a world model (Ha & Schmidhuber,
040   2018), a model the predicts the consequences of actions and acts as a learned simulator. Such a
041   model can be used to optimize decision-making for downstream tasks (Hafner et al., 2021).
042   Current image and video diffusion models are highly adept at generating text-conditioned syn-
043   thetic data (Podell et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023b; Blattmann et al., 2023). If actions can be ex-
044   pressed as natural language, these models have the potential to be used out-of-the-box for decision-
045   making (Kapelyukh et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2024b). However, in many real-world domains the core
046   challenge is optimizing low-level actions, such as joint angles in robotics, and therefore using natu-
047   ral language as the only interface is insufficient. To overcome this limitation, one option is to fine-
048   tune a pre-trained model to condition on low-level actions for a domain-specific dataset (Seo et al.,
049   2022). Another possibility is to apply existing adapter architectures such as ControlNet (Zhang
050
      et al., 2023a), to add action-conditioning by modifying the activations inside the original model.
      However, the parameters for state-of-the-art video diffusion models are usually not available pub-
051
      licly (LumaAI, 2024; OpenAI, 2024; RunwayML, 2024), which rules out these approaches.
052
053   In this work we address the problem of exploiting a pretrained video diffusion model to generate
      action-conditioned predictions without access to the parameters of the pretrained model. Inspired

054
      by the recent work from Yang et al. (2024b), we instead assume that we only have access to the
055   noise predictions of the pretrained diffusion model. We propose AVID, a domain-specific adapter
056   that conditions on actions and modifies the noise predictions of the pretrained model to generate
057   accurate action-conditioned predictions. To train the adapter, we assume that we have access to a
058   domain-specific dataset of action-labelled videos. The core contributions of our work are:
059
      • Proposing to adapt pretrained video diffusion models to action-conditioned world models, without
060     access to the parameters of the pretrained model.
061
      • Analyzing the limitations of the adaptation approach proposed by Yang et al. (2024b).
062
063   • AVID, a novel approach to adding conditioning to pretrained diffusion models. AVID applies a
064
        learned mask to the outputs of of a pretrained model, and combines them with conditional outputs
        learned by a domain-specific adapter.
065
066   We evaluate AVID on video game data, as well as real-world robotics data where we use a 1.4B
067   parameter model trained on internet-scale data as the pretrained model (Xing et al., 2023). Our
068   results show that our approach outperforms existing baselines, and demonstrates that AVID obtains
069   a considerable benefit from using the pretrained model, even with limited access to the model.
070   We advocate for providers of closed-source video models to provide access to intermediate model
071   outputs in their APIs to facilitate the use of adaptation approaches such as AVID.

      3.1   P ROBLEM S ETTING
111
112   In the context of video diffusion models each datapoint is a video, x = [x0 , x1 , . . . , xT −1 ], where
113   xτ indicates a video frame. Note that we use superscript indices, xτ , to indicate steps in time, and
114   subscript indices, xi , to indicate steps in a diffusion process. We assume that we have access to a
115   pretrained image-to-video diffusion model ϵpre , that is trained on web-scale data. Given an initial
116   image, x0 , the image-to-video model ϵpre generates a synthetic video x̂ = [x0 , x̂1 , . . . , x̂T −1 ].
117   We consider each video to be a sequence of observations generated by a partially observable
118   Markov decision process (POMDP) (Kaelbling et al., 1998), with corresponding action sequence
119   a = [a0 , a1 , . . . , aT −1 ]. For each domain, we assume access to a dataset of action-labelled videos,
120   D = {(x, a), . . .}. Given a new initial image, x0 , and action sequence a, the goal is to generate a
121   synthetic video x̂ that accurately depicts the ground-truth realization of the actions.
122
      To utilize the pretrained model ϵpre , we need to add action-conditioning to the model as accurate
123   videos cannot be generated without the actions. Adding a new conditioning signal to a pretrained
124   diffusion model is well-explored in previous works (Zhang et al., 2023a; Mou et al., 2024; Mokady
125   et al., 2023). However, most existing works assume access to the parameters of the pretrained model.
126   In this work, we assume that we do not have access to the parameters of the pretrained model.
127

158   3.3   A DAPTING V IDEO D IFFUSION M ODELS TO W ORLD M ODELS (AVID)
159
160   AVID is a new approach for diffusion model adaptation that does not require access to the pretrained
161   model. The motivation for AVID is that while pretrained image-to-video models can generate realis-
      tic videos, they cannot generate videos that are accurate with respect to a given sequence of actions.

      To achieve accuracy, the pretrained model must be guided towards the correct generation for the
      action sequence. However, as our experiments show, techniques such as classifier(-free) guidance
177
      do not perform well in this setting. AVID addresses this by training a lightweight adapter to adjust
178
      the output of the pretrained model to achieve accurate action-conditioned video predictions.
179
180   AVID trains an adapter that takes the output of the pretrained model as an input. AVID learns to
181   generate a mask, and uses this mask to combine the outputs of the pretrained model with those of
182
      the adapter. The final combined output is used to compute the standard denoising loss, and the
      adapter parameters are trained to optimize this loss. We train the AVID adapter using samples (x, a)
183
      from the action-labelled dataset. For the pretrained image-to-video model ϵpre we assume that we
184
      do not have to access to it’s parameters, θpre , but we can run inference using the model to obtain
185
      it’s noise predictions. To do this, we input a noisy video, xi ∈ RT ×h×w×c , initial image, x0 ∈
186   Rh×w×c , and diffusion step, i, to the pretrained image-to-video model to obtain its noise prediction,
187   ϵpre (xi , i, x0 ) ∈ RT ×h×w×c . The parameters of the pretrained model ϵpre are not modified.
188
189
      The adapter that we train is a 3D UNet (Ho et al., 2022c; Ronneberger et al., 2015) consisting of a
      sequence of spatio-temporal blocks with residual connections. Each spatio-temporal block consists
190
      of a 3D convolution, spatial attention, and temporal attention (Ho et al., 2022c). The UNet takes
191
      as input a tensor of shape RT ×h×w×3c . The input consists of the noisy video, xi , the output of the
192   pretrained model, ϵpre (xi , i, x0 ), and the initial image x0 repeated T times across the time dimension.
193   These three inputs are concatenated channel-wise to create the input tensor.
194
195
      The adapter is also conditioned on the diffusion step i and the sequence of actions a. For noisy video
      xi we embed the diffusion timestep according to a learned embedding table to get the diffusion step
196
      embedding ei . For each timestep τ of the noisy video xi we embed the corresponding action aτ
197
      to compute the action embedding eτa using an embedding table for discrete actions or a linear layer
198   for continuous actions. In each block, these two embeddings are concatenated and processed by an
199   MLP to compute the scale and shift parameters, γ τ and β τ , for the τ th frame. These parameters
200   scale and shift the feature maps of the τ th frame after each 3D convolution (Perez et al., 2018).
201
      To inject the correct action-conditioning into the predictions made by the pretrained model, the
202
      adapter needs to erase incorrect motions predicted by the pretrained model and add the correct
203
      motion. To facilitate this, the adapter outputs a tensor of shape RT ×h×w×(c+1) consisting of a mask,
204
      m ∈ RT ×h×w×1 which is bounded between 0 and 1 by a sigmoid layer, and the noise prediction
205   from the adapter ϵadapt . The mask is then used to combine the noise predictions from the pretrained
206   model and the adapter according to:

      4     E XPERIMENTS
222
223
      We evaluate AVID in two different domains with different pretrained base models. Details about the
224
      datasets are provided in Appendix B.1 and details about the pretrained models are in Appendix B.2.
225   As the focus of our work is on training lightweight adapters with limited compute, we compare
226   adapter models with limited parameters under a computational budget.
227
228   Procgen The pretrained model is a pixel-space image-to-video diffusion model (Ho et al., 2022c)
229   trained on videos sampled from 15 out of the 16 procedurally generated games of Procgen (Cobbe
230   et al., 2020), excluding the 16th game Coinrun. The adaptation approaches are trained using an
231
      action-labelled dataset sampled from Coinrun. At each timestep, the discrete action is one of 15
      possible keypad inputs, and the models are trained on sequences of 10 frames. Each adaptation
232
      approach is limited to 3 days of training on a single A100 GPU. We test three dataset sizes, Coin-
233
      run100k/500k/2.5M, evaluate the models on a held-out test set of initial frames and actions.
234
235   RT1 + DynamiCrafter In this benchmark, the pretrained model is DynamiCrafter (Xing et al.,
236   2023) which is currently one of the best performing image-to-video models in the VBench image-
237   to-video leaderboard (Huang et al., 2024). DynamiCrafter is a latent image-to-video diffusion model
238   that uses the autoencoder from Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) and a 1.4B parameter 3D
239
      UNet trained on web-scale video data. As DynamiCrafter is a latent diffusion model, we assume
      that we can run inference on the encoder and decoder as described in Section 3.3. For the action-
240
      labelled dataset we use the RT1 dataset (Brohan et al., 2022) which consists of real-world robotics
241
      videos. The action at each step is a 7 dimensional continuous vector corresponding to the movement
242   and rotation of the end effector and opening or closing of the gripper. The models are trained on
243   trajectories of 16 frames. Each adaptation approach is limited to 7 days of training on 4× A100
244   GPUs, and is evaluated using a held-out test set of ground truth trajectories.

      4.1   BASELINES
247
248   We compare AVID with several baselines, both with and without access to the parameters of the
249   pretrained model. Further details, including hyperparameter tuning, are in Appendix B.5.
250
      Full access to pretrained model parameters:
251
252   • Action-Conditioned Finetuning – tunes all of the parameters of the pretrained model on the action-
253     conditioned dataset. To add action-conditioning, we first compute an action embedding according
254     to Section 3.3. For Procgen and RT1, we concatenate and add the action embeddings with the
255
        time step embeddings respectively.
256   • Language-Conditioned Finetuning – finetunes the pretrained model using a language description
257     of each video. The language is embedded using CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and conditioned upon
258     using cross-attention following Xing et al. (2023).
259   • ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023a) – freezes the parameters of the pretrained model and makes a
260     trainable copy of its UNet encoder. The trainable part of the model is conditioned on a new signal,
261     and connected to the decoder in the original model via convolutions which are initialised to zero.
262
        In our work, we use ControlNet to add action-conditioning.
263   • ControlNet Small – ControlNet still has a large number of trainable parameters. ControlNet Small
264     freezes the pretrained model in the same way as ControlNet, but reduces the number of trainable
265     parameters to a similar amount to AVID by decreasing the number of channels in the layers of the
266
        UNet encoder. A learned projection at each layer projects the activations of the smaller model to
        match the number of channels in the pretrained base model.
267
268   No access to pretrained model parameters For a fair comparison to AVID, we evaluate the
269   following approaches which either do not leverage the pretrained model or, like AVID, assume
      access to only noise predictions from the pretrained model:

292   4.2   E VALUATION
293
294   For evaluation, we use a set of 1024 held-out test videos and their corresponding action sequences.
295   We generate 1024 synthetic videos by conditioning the models on each initial frame and action
296   sequence, and compare the generated videos against the ground truth using the following metrics:
297   • Action Error Ratio – To assess the consistency between the videos and the action sequences, we
298     train a model to predict actions from real videos. The Action Error Ratio is the ratio of errors
299     obtained by using this model on generated videos, divided by the error obtained on real videos.
300     More details are in Appendix B.3.
301   • FVD (Cobbe et al., 2019) – measures the similarity between feature distributions of generated and
302     real video sequences in the I3D network (Carreira & Zisserman, 2017), accounting for both spatial
303     quality and temporal coherence.
304   • FID (Heusel et al., 2017) – compares the feature distributions of all real and generated images in
305     the Inception-v3 network (Szegedy et al., 2015), measuring the quality and variety of the images.
306   • SSIM (Wang et al., 2004) – compares each ground truth and generated image, focusing on lumi-
307     nance, contrast, and structural information.
308   • PSNR (Hore & Ziou, 2010) – is computed using the mean squared error between each ground
309     truth and generated video, and thus computes the distance between the videos in pixel-space.
310   • LPIPS (Zhang et al., 2018) – compares the similarity between the features representations of each
311     ground truth and predicted image using the VGG network (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2015).
312
313
      We bold results within 2% of the best performance in each model size category. We also compute
      normalized metrics by normalizing each value between 0 and 1. Details are in Appendix B.4.

469
470   Diffusion for Decision-Making            Many works utilise diffusion models for generating ac-
471
      tions (Pearce et al., 2023; Janner et al., 2022). However, the focus of our work is on generat-
      ing action-conditioned synthetic data for world modelling (Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018), which can
472
      be used downstream for planning (Hafner et al., 2021). SynTHER (Lu et al., 2024) employs an
473
      unconditional diffusion model to generate synthetic data for reinforcement learning (RL). Other
474   works (Alonso et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024a; Rigter et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,
475   2024) utilise diffusion models to train action-conditioned world models. All of these works train
476   the diffusion model from scratch. In our work, the focus is on making effective use of a pretrained
477   video diffusion model to leverage web-scale pretraining. Most related is Pandora (Xiang et al.,
478   2024) which integrates an LLM and text-to-video diffusion model to generate videos conditioned on
479   actions described as natural language. While natural language is suitable for describing high-level
480   actions, it is inadequate for our goal of modelling low-level actions (McCarthy et al., 2024).
481   Video Pre-Training for World Models To address the scarcity of action-labeled real-world data,
482   several studies have investigated using unlabeled videos to enhance the efficiency of world model
483   training. Seo et al. (2022) and Wu et al. (2024) pre-train an autoregressive video prediction model,
484   which is later fine-tuned with action-labeled data. Mendonca et al. (2023) develop a world model
485   from videos of humans by defining a high-level action space that is shared between both embodi-
      ments. Another approach involves learning latent actions from videos (Bruce et al., 2024; Schmidt


                                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      & Jiang, 2024). Our work integrates video pre-training by utilizing a pre-trained video diffusion
487   model, with the distinction that we do not have access to the weights of the original model.
488
489   Adding Controllability to Diffusion Models Classifier guidance (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021) adds
490
      conditioning to a pretrained diffusion model using a separate classifier. Classifier-free guidance (Ho
      & Salimans, 2021) achieves stronger empirical performance but requires the model to be trained
491
      with conditioning signals, rendering it unsuitable for post-hoc application to a pretrained model.
492
493   Recent advancements include ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023a) and T2I-Adapter (Mou et al., 2024),
494   which introduce conditional control to pretrained diffusion models by freezing the original UNet
495   and injecting additive signals into the pretrained network. ControlNet-XS (Zavadski et al., 2023)
496
      extends this concept by increasing the interactions between the pretrained and adapter networks.
      Li et al. (2023) incorporates additional trainable layers into the frozen pretrained UNet. These
497
      techniques have been applied to introduce controls such as language (Xing et al., 2024), optical
498
      flow (Hu & Xu, 2023), and depth maps (Chen et al., 2023b) into video models. However, they are
499   not applicable in our context as they require access to the original model’s weights, which we assume
500   are inaccessible. Textual inversion (Gal et al., 2023) and null text inversion (Mokady et al., 2023)
501   optimize text embeddings to add controllability to text-conditioned diffusion models. However,
502   this requires backpropagation through the pretrained model which is not feasible in our setting.
503   Cascaded diffusion models (Ho et al., 2022a;b), train a sequence of diffusion models, which each
504   condition on the outputs of the previous diffusion model. However, the focus of these works is
505   improving the spatial and temporal resolution at each step, whereas our focus is on incorporating a
506   new conditioning signal, actions, that was absent in the pretrained model.
507   Compositional Generative Models Our work is related to compositional generative models, where
508   generative models are combined probabilistically (Liu et al., 2022; Nie et al., 2021; Du et al., 2020).
509   RoboDreamer applies this idea to world modeling by decomposing language conditioning into mul-
510   tiple components to generate several predictions that are then combined (Zhou et al., 2024). Yang
511   et al. (2024b) modifies the style of a pretrained video diffusion model by combining it’s output
512   with a domain-specific diffusion model that is trained independently. Unlike Yang et al. (2024b),
513
      the focus of our work is on adding action-conditioning to a pretrained model, and our experiments
      demonstrate that the approach proposed by Yang et al. (2024b) works poorly in our setting.
514

      6   D ISCUSSION
516
517   Limitations Adding the AVID adapter increases the inference compute required compared to the
518   pretrained model. AVID adapters are tailored to a specific pretrained model and therefore cannot be
519   composed with different models. Developing a method that works across different pretrained models
520   is an exciting direction for future work. AVID does not require access to pretrained model weights,
521   but it does require access to intermediate predictions during denoising, including the outputs of the
522   encoder and decoder in the case of latent diffusion. Many closed-source APIs do not provide access
523
      to these quantities, and therefore we advocate for model providers to provide API access to the
      outputs of the denoising model and autoencoder to facilitate more flexible use of their models.
524
525   In the RT1 domain we found that training an action-conditioned diffusion model from scratch re-
526   sulted in the best Action Error Ratio, despite the videos being less visually accurate. For some
527   downstream applications, action consistency might be the most important performance metric. If
528   this is the case, training from scratch may be the preferred approach for some domains.
529   Conclusion We introduced the novel problem of adapting pretrained video diffusion models to
530   action-conditioned world models without requiring access to the pretrained model’s parameters.
531   Our proposed approach, AVID, generates accurate videos with similar performance to ControlNet
532   variants without requiring access to the pretrained model parameters. AVID obtains superior over-
533   all performance to existing baselines that do not require access to the internals of the pretrained
534   model. Our results demonstrate that AVID benefits from the pretrained model by maintaining better
535
      consistency with the initial image across both pixel-space and latent diffusion models.
536   As general image-to-video diffusion models continue to advance in capability, our findings highlight
537   the considerable potential of adapting these models to world models that are suitable for planning
538   and decision-making. This work represents an initial step in that direction. In future research, we
539   aim to use synthetic data generated by AVID adapters for planning tasks. We also wish to explore
      using AVID adapters to add new conditioning signals to pretrained models other than actions.

1055   B.1           DATASET D ETAILS
1056
1057   Procgen Pretrained Model Dataset The pretrained model is trained on videos sampled from 15
1058
       out of the 16 procedurally generated games of Procgen, excluding the 16th game Coinrun. Because
       the games are procedurally generated, each level in each Procgen game has different visual charac-
1059
       teristics. To generate the dataset for the pretrained model, in each of the 15 games we sample an
1060
       episode of 1000 steps long from each of the first 2000 levels by executing uniformly random actions.
1061   This results in 2M frames from each game for a total dataset of 30M steps, each with a resolution of
1062   64 × 64. For training, we sample windows of 10 steps from the episodes. The pretrained model is
1063   trained on videos from this dataset for 12 days in a single A100.
1064
1065   Coinrun Datasets To train the Coinrun adapters, we generate an action-labelled dataset from the
1066   Coinrun game. At each timestep, the action is one of 15 discrete actions corresponding to keypad
1067   inputs. We sample episodes of 1000 steps from each of the first 100, 500, or 2500 levels by executing
1068   uniform random actions to create the Coinrun100k, Coinrun500k, and Coinrun2.5M datasets. For
1069   training, we sample action-labelled trajectories of 10 steps.
1070   For evaluation, we create a held-out evaluation set of ground truth trajectories of videos and actions.
1071   We use 1024 ground truth trajectories of 10 steps each, sampled by executing random action se-
1072   quences in levels sampled uniformly at random between level 10000 and 11000 of Coinrun. We use
1073   the same evaluation trajectories for all methods.
1074
1075   RT1 Dataset The RT1 dataset contains 87212 action-labelled episodes of a robot performing tasks
1076   such as picking up and placing objects, with a total of 3.78M steps. The action at each step is a 7
1077   dimensional continuous vector corresponding to the movement and rotation of the end effector and
1078   opening or closing of the gripper. The videos have a resolution of 320 × 512. For training, we use
1079   95% of the episodes, equating to 82851 episodes in the training dataset. We sample windows of 16
       steps from these episodes for training the models. For evaluation, we use 1024 trajectories of 16

1278
1279   • Language-Conditioned Finetuning – the language description that we use is: “Robot arm performs
1280     the task {task description} ” where {task description} is the description given in the RT1
1281     dataset.
1282   • Classifier Guidance (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021) – For the RT1 domain, since the actions are
1283     continuous, we discretise each action dimension into 256 bins uniformly following Padalkar
1284     et al. (2023) to train the classifier. We tune the weight of the guidance within w ∈
1285     {0.003, 0.01, 0.03, 0.1, 0.3}.
1286   • Product of Experts – we tune the weighting of the two models within λp ∈ {0.2, 0.4, 0.6}.
1287   • Action Classifier-Free Guidance – we tune the weighting within λa ∈ {0.3, 1.0, 3.0, 10.0}.
