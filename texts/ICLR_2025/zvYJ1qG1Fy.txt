009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            A significant challenge in representation learning is to capture latent semantics in
012            data mixing continuous, discrete, and even discretized observations (called mixed-
013            type data), encountering issues like inconsistent discoveries and redundant model-
014            ing. Recently, Bayesian flow networks (BFNs) offer a unified strategy to represent
015            such mixed-type data in the parameter space but cannot learn low-dimensional
016            latent semantics since BFNs assume the size of parameters being the same as
017            that of observations. This raises a new important question: how to learn latent
018
               semantics in parameter spaces rather than in observation spaces of mixed-type
               data? Accordingly, we propose a novel unified parameter space representation
019
               learning framework, ParamReL, which extracts progressive latent semantics in
020
               parameter spaces of mixed-type data. In ParamReL, a self-encoder learns latent
021            semantics from intermediate parameters rather than observations. The learned se-
022            mantics are then integrated into BFNs to efficiently learn unified representations
023            of mixed-type data. Additionally, a reverse-sampling procedure can empower
024            BFNs for tasks including input reconstruction and interpolation. Extensive ex-
025            periments verify the effectiveness of ParamReL in learning parameter space rep-
026            resentations for latent interpolation, disentanglement, time-varying conditional
027            reconstruction, and conditional generation. The code is available at https:
028            //anonymous.4open.science/r/ICLR25-F087/README.md.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      This work explores a new important question: How to learn latent semantics in parameter spaces
      rather than in observation spaces of mixed-type data comprising continuous, discrete, and even dis-
034
      cretized observations? We propose a novel unified parameter space representation learning frame-
035
      work that utilizes the parameter spaces rather than the observation spaces for mixed-type data.
036
037   Representation learning (Bengio et al., 2013) aims to discover low-dimensional latent semantics
038   from high-dimensional observations, widely applied in areas including computer vision (Li et al.,
039
      2023; Zhao et al., 2023a; Dong et al., 2023), and data analytics (Tonekaboni et al., 2022; Oublal
      et al., 2024). While the main focus has been on continuous-valued data (Kim & Mnih, 2018; Chen
040
      et al., 2018; Meo et al., 2024), it is more challenging to uncover semantics in discrete (Austin et al.,
041
      2021; Chen et al., 2023) and even discretized (Van Den Oord et al., 2017; Razavi et al., 2019)
042   data. However, existing efforts often encounter issues like inconsistent discoveries and redundant
043   modeling (Zhou et al., 2023; Krishnan et al., 2018). Recently, Bayesian flow networks (BFNs)
044   (Graves et al., 2023; Song et al., 2024; Xue et al., 2024) emerged as a promising deep generative
045   model. BFNs use multiple steps similar to diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021) to
046   refine parameters of an output distribution for reconstructing observations. Accordingly, BFNs offer
047   a unified strategy to handle mixed-type data while enabling fast sampling. However, they struggle
048   to capture low-dimensional latent semantics, raising the above open question.
049   Correspondingly, we propose a novel unified Parameter space Representation Learning framework,
050   ParamReL, which leverages the multi-step generative learning of BFNs for representation learning
051   on mixed-type data. ParamReL tackles this by performing representation learning in the parameter
052   space to extract high-level latent semantics. The key insight lies in progressively self-encoding the
053   intermediate parameters of BFNs, generating low-dimensional latent semantics step by step. Specif-
      ically, ParamReL adopts an architecture similar to BFNs but with two significant innovations: (1)

      a self-encoder encodes intermediate parameters into lower-dimensional latent semantics, capturing
055   gradual semantic changes throughout the multi-step generation process; and (2) a conditional de-
056   coder, which conditions on latent semantics and intermediate parameters, and forms the parameters
057   of an output distribution for simulating observations. Additionally, ParamReL involves a reverse-
058   sampling procedure customized for tasks like image reconstruction and interpolation. Variational
059   inference method is used in learning ParamReL, where mutual information is used to promote dis-
060   entangled latent semantic learning, resulting in distinct and meaningful representations.
061   We evaluate ParamReL in learning meaningful high-level latent semantics from both discrete and
062   continuous-valued observations on benchmark data. The sampling and reverse-sampling mecha-
063   nisms of ParamReL successfully perform tasks such as latent interpolation, disentanglement, time-
064   varying conditional reconstruction, and conditional generation. Notably, the self-encoder reveals
065   progressive semantics throughout flow steps, enabling ParamReL to generate semantics with im-
066   proved clarity, while maintaining high quality of sample generation.

069   2       U NDERSTANDING BAYESIAN F LOW N ETWORKS - A N A LTERNATIVE V IEW
070
071   Bayesian Flow Networks (BFNs) (Graves et al., 2023; Song et al., 2024; Xue et al., 2024) serve as
072   deep generative models with a primary objective to learn an output distribution for generating ob-
073   servations. The distribution’s parameters are learned by a neural network, which takes the posterior
074   parameters of observations of inputs. Here, we try to understand BFNs from an alternative parame-
075
      ter perspective since these (posterior) parameters play a key role in BFNs. BFNs involves concepts
      such as input distribution, sender distribution and receiver distribution, to introduce BFNs, making
076
      it less accessible to readers unfamiliar with BFNs. Interested readers may refer to Appendix A.1 and
077
      (Graves et al., 2023) for the original illustrations.
078
079   Figure 1 shows T steps of training and sample generation in BFNs, similar to diffusion models (Ho
080   et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021). To train BFNs, we minimize the divergence between the ground-truth
081
      data distribution and the evolving output distributions over T steps. At each step t ∈ {T, . . . , 1},
      an intermediate (posterior) parameter θ t is first updated using a Bayesian update function h(·) as
082
      θ t = h(θ t+1 , xt+1 ), where xt+1 is the observation at step t + 1. θ t is then fed into a neural network
083
      ψ(·) to form the parameters of output distribution, i.e., a decoder pO (xt |ψ(θ t ), for model training.
084   After training, these intermediate output distributions can be employed to simulate observations
085   during the sample generation process, replacing the actual observations at each step t.
086
087

      observations. For example, BFNs can use the mean of Gaussian distributions as parameter θ to
088
      model continuous data or use the event probabilities of categorical distributions as θ to study discrete
089
      data (see detailed settings for distributions in Table 2). However, BFNs cannot produce meaningful

115
116
      3.1      T HE PARAM R E L F RAMEWORK
117
      The framework and workflow of ParamReL are shown in Figure 2. ParamReL leverages the
118   parameter space for representation learning by extracting low-dimensional latent semantics from
119   high-dimensional mixed-type data. Different from BFNs in approximating data distribution p(x0 ),
120   ParamReL learns the joint distribution over observation x0 and a series of latent semantics {zt }Tt=1 ,
121   with |zt | ≪ |x0 |, ∀t ∈ {1, . . . , T }. That is, ParamReL seeks to reconstruct x0 while obtaining
122   meaningful low-dimensional latent semantics {zt }Tt=1 .
123
      Building on BFNs, ParamReL consists of four main components:
124
125          (1) A self-encoder, conditioning on the intermediate (posterior) parameters θ t to generate pro-
126              gressive latent semantics zt , described in Section 3.2.
127          (2) A conditional decoder, using a neural network on latent semantics zt and intermediate
128              parameters θ t to form the output distribution for subsequent steps, detailed in Section 3.3.
129          (3) A sampling and reverse-sampling process, facilitating tasks such as image reconstruction
130              and interpolation, outlined in Section 3.4.
131
             (4) A training and testing procedure, as discussed in Section 3.5, optimizing latent semantics
132              zt and ensuring effective model generalization.
133
134   Together, ParamReL forms a robust framework to capture and utilize latent semantics and to improve
135   the performance of tasks including unconditional image generation and reconstruction.

286   4   R ELATED W ORK
287
288   Recent advances have demonstrated that diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021) are
289   capable of generating high-quality data. Nonetheless, compared to the autoencoder framework, the
290   intermediate outputs in diffusion stages are high-dimensional and lack smoothness, making them
291   unsuitable for representation learning. Contemporary research focuses on encoding a conditional
292   latent space to acquire low-dimensional semantic representations. However, those observations-
293   based models (Preechakul et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023), such as VAEs and diffusion models,
294
      exhibit limitations when applied to discrete data.
295   Deep hierarchical VAEs have seen progress in capturing latent dependence structures for encoding
296   an expressive posterior, statistically or semantically. VQVAE-based (Van Den Oord et al., 2017;
297   Razavi et al., 2019) models have local-to-global features-based explanatory hierarchies at the image
298   level, forming a codebook-based discrete posterior. In (Sønderby et al., 2016; Tomczak & Welling,
299   2018), recursive latent structures in multi-layer networks form an aggregated posterior. NVAE (Vah-
300
      dat & Kautz, 2020) demonstrates that depth-wise hierarchies encoded by residual networks can ap-
      proximate the posterior precisely despite using shallow networks. Unlike the observation-based
301
      encoder, where the information flow between input and latent is maximized in encoding-decoding
302
      pipelines in the sample space, ParamReL uses progressive encoders in the parameter space to capture
303   the dynamic semantics.
304
305   Pre-trained diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022), (Baranchuk et al., 2021) have shown that the
306
      upsampling features from a U-Net can capture semantic information useful for downstream tasks.
      This discovery has sparked increasing research in leveraging these upsampling features of pre-
307
      trained diffusion models across various applications, including classification (Xiang et al., 2023;
308
      Mukhopadhyay et al., 2023), semantic segmentation (Baranchuk et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2023b),
309   panoptic segmentation (Xu et al., 2023), semantic correspondence (Tang et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,
310   2024; Luo et al., 2024; Hedlin et al., 2024), and image editing (Tumanyan et al., 2023; Hertz et al.,
311   2022). In most of these approaches, identifying the optimal denoising step and upsampling layer
312   is crucial for achieving high predictive performance. These approaches do not suggest fundamental
313   changes to model architectures or training methodologies, leaving the specific architectural com-
314   ponents and techniques for learning useful semantic representations unclear. ParamReL uses these
315   discoveries to construct efficient self-encoders.

      5.1   E VALUATION S ETUP
325
326   We conduct a two-fold comparison to evaluate the performance of ParamReL variants. Firstly, we
327   compare our parameter-based models (ParamReLc and ParamReLd) with established sample-based
328   representation learning baselines, including AE and VAE-based models such as β-VAE (Higgins
329   et al., 2017), infoVAE (Zhao et al., 2019), and diffusion-based models such as DiffAE (Preechakul
330   et al., 2022) and InfoDiffusion (Wang et al., 2023). These models represent key advancements in
331   the field: β-VAE introduce disentanglement into VAE, infoVAE incorporates MMD for balancing
332
      generation and representation, while DiffAE and InfoDiffusion explore the integration of AEs and
      VAEs into diffusion models to learn encodable latents and disentangled representations, respectively.
333
      Secondly, we compare the performance of ParamReLc and ParamReLd across various input distribu-
334
      tions for continuous and discrete data, respectively. The discrete datasets include binarized versions
335   of MNIST (bMNIST) (Deng, 2012), FashionMNIST (bFashionMNIST) (Xiao et al., 2017), while
336   the continuous datasets include CelebA (Liu et al., 2015), CIFAR10 (Krizhevsky & Hinton, 2009),
337   and Shapes3D (Burgess & Kim, 2018)1 . The detailed hyperparameter choices and experimental
338   configurations for each dataset are provided in Appendix C.3. This comparison allows for a detailed
339   examination of how different parameter space assumptions impact the representation learning of
340   discrete and continuous data.

418   Smooth Representation Learning for Latent Interpolation Latent space interpolation (Goodfel-
419   low et al., 2014; Higgins et al., 2017) is commonly used to validate the smoothness, continuity, and
420   semantic coherence of the learned latent semantics in generative models. Typically, two samples are
421   embedded into the latent space, and interpolating between the latent variables generates interpolated
422
      representations. The reconstructed outputs produced by the sampling process reveal the semantic
      richness of the latent space. Demonstration of the image interpolation is detailed in Appendix D.1.
423
424   As shown in Figure 14 in Appendix E.3, ParamReL achieves near-exact reconstruction, in contrast
425   to the downgraded performance of VAE variants such as (a) vanilla VAE, and (b) β-VAE. Compared
426   with diffusion models (c) DiffAE and (d) InfoDiffusion, ParamReL characterizes a smoother and
427   more consistent latent space with high-quality samples.
428   Disentanglement We perform latent traversals on the FFHQ and CelebA datasets to evaluate the
429   disentanglement properties of our trained ParamReL, as illustrated in Figure 5 and Figure 15 in
430   Appendix E.3. In this process, we modify one dimension of the learned latent semantics {zt }Tt=1
431   each step, and replace it with M evenly distributed numbers within a standardized range (e.g., −3
      to +3), while keeping the other dimensions fixed. After decoding these adjusted latent semantics,

      6   C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
470
      In this work, we introduce ParamReL, a novel unified parameter space representation learning
471
      framework, as a unified strategy to handle continuous, discrete and even discretized data. Unlike
472
      traditional encoder methods that map observations into static latent semantics, ParamReL employs a
473   self-encoder to derive progressively structured latent semantics from intermediate parameters at each
474   step of the generation process. This allows for more effective representation learning across different
475   data types. Our experiments on tasks including latent interpolation, disentanglement, time-varying
476   conditional reconstruction, and conditional generation validate the effectiveness of ParamReL. The
477   results demonstrate its superior ability to extract meaningful high-level semantics, leading to unified
478   representations and a clear semantic understanding of the underlying data.
479   While ParamReL shows promising results, our experiments reveal areas for potential expansion. (1)
480   The precision variables, which play a key role in the sampling process, could be further optimized to
481   reduce computational time and to improve efficiency. This was observed during the sampling stages
482   where slight inefficiencies in parameter updates are detected. (2) We noticed that employing a stan-
483   dard U-Net architecture without pre-training may limit the performance of ParamReL, particularly
484   in tasks involving complex data. Therefore, exploring the integration of a pre-trained U-Net model
485   into ParamReL could provide a significant boost in accuracy and representation quality. We will
      investigate these in the future work.

843   A.3     G ENERATIVE L ATENT VARIABLE M ODELS FOR R EPRESENTATION L EARNING
844
845   Latent Variable Models (LVMs) Everett (2013) which aim at learning the joint distribution p(x, z)
846   over data x and latent variables z present efficient ways for uncovering hidden semantics. In LVMs,
847   the joint distribution p(x, z) is usually decomposed as p(x, z) = p(x | z)p(z), where p(z) represents
848
      prior knowledge for inference Tschannen et al. (2018), thus facilitating learning the conditional
      distribution p(x | z). Among LVMs, Variational AutoEncoders (VAEs) Kingma & Welling (2014)
849
      and diffusion models Ho et al. (2020); Song et al. (2021) are two representative approaches Kwok
850
      & Adams (2012).
851
852   In VAEs, latent variables z is obtained through an encoder network qϕ (z | x), whereas observations
853   are reconstructed through a decoder network pθ (x | z), with ϕ and θ being the encoder and decoder
854
      parameters.
855   The dimensions of z are usually much smaller than those of x, denoted as |z| ≪ |x|, such that
856   redundant information is effectively removed and the most semantically meaningful factors are ab-
857   stracted Louizos et al. (2016). VAEs are popular for downstream tasks like disentanglement Higgins
858   et al. (2017); Yang et al. (2022); Hwang et al. (2023); Esmaeili et al. (2023), classification Takahashi
859   et al. (2022); Tonekaboni et al. (2022), and clustering Jiang et al. (2016); Xu et al. (2021).
860   On the other hand, diffusion models Ho et al. (2020); Song et al. (2021) first use T diffusion steps
861   to transform observation x into a white noise xT and then use T denoising steps to reconstruct the
862   observation. Diffusion models have obtained impressive performance in the fidelity and diversity
863   of generation tasks. However, they might be unable to obtain meaningful latent semantics since the
      dimensions of x and xT are the same as |x| = |xT |. Preechakul et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2023)


                                                                                         16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      have attempted to integrate a decodable auxiliary variable z to enable diffusion models to obtain
865   low-dimensional latent semantics. However, they have not overcome issues like the slow training
866   speed inherent to the diffusion and reverse processes.
