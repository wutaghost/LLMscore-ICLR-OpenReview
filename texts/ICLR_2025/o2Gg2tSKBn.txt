011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Accurate and transparent financial information disclosure is crucial in the fields
014            of accounting and finance, ensuring market efficiency and investor confidence.
015            Among many information disclosure platforms, the Chinese stock exchanges’ in-
016            vestor interactive platform provides a novel and interactive way for listed firms
017            to disclose information of interest to investors through an online question-and-
018            answer (Q&A) format. However, it is common for listed firms to respond to ques-
019
               tions with limited or no substantive information, and automatically evaluating the
               quality of financial information disclosure on large amounts of Q&A pairs is chal-
020
               lenging. This paper builds a benchmark FinTruthQA, that can evaluate advanced
021
               natural language processing (NLP) techniques for the automatic quality assess-
022            ment of information disclosure in financial Q&A data. FinTruthQA comprises
023            6,000 real-world financial Q&A entries and each Q&A was manually annotated
024            based on four key evaluation criteria: question identification, question relevance,
025            answer readability, and answer relevance. We benchmarked various NLP tech-
026            niques on FinTruthQA, including statistical machine learning models, pre-trained
027            language model and their fine-tuned versions, as well as large language models
028            (LLMs). Experiments showed that existing NLP models have strong predictive
029            ability for question identification and question relevance tasks, but are suboptimal
030
               for answer readability and answer relevance tasks. By establishing this bench-
               mark, we provide a robust foundation for the automatic evaluation of information
031
               disclosure, significantly enhancing the transparency and quality of financial re-
032
               porting. FinTruthQA can be used by auditors, regulators, and financial analysts for
033            real-time monitoring and data-driven decision-making, as well as by researchers
034            for advanced studies in accounting and finance, ultimately fostering greater trust
035            and efficiency in the financial markets.

039   1   I NTRODUCTION
040
041   Within the financial market, enhancing transparency and timeliness in information disclosure can
042   mitigate information friction, empower investors to make informed decisions, and foster market
043   efficiency. According to Lee & Zhong (2022), retail investors struggle to integrate the existing
044   public information, and increased online platform activities are associated with more active market
045
      transactions, such as greater trading volume, return volatility, and market liquidity. Novel investor
      interactive platforms (such as online forums established by the Shanghai Stock Exchange (SSE) and
046
      Shenzhen Stock Exchange (SZSE) ) offer a unique avenue for retail investors to engage directly with
047
      listed firms. In contrast to conventional modes of disclosure, such as mandatory disclosures and tra-
048   ditional voluntary disclosures like conference calls, which follow specific procedures and formats,
049   these novel online platforms enable direct, interactive, near real-time communication between firms
050   and ordinary investors through the format of Q&A. By analyzing the interactions on these platforms,
051   researchers can gain insights into how effectively companies communicate with investors, the types
052   of information that investors most value, and how the disclosure practices of listed firms may im-
053   pact their corresponding trading patterns in financial markets. This will enhance financial market
      regulation and provide deeper insights into corporate disclosure practices.

      Liu et al., 2021), with a primary emphasis on the English language. These efforts are predominantly

      3.3    DATA ANNOTATION
163
164   We focus on four key information disclosure quality evaluation criteria3 : question identification,
165   question relevance, answer relevance, and answer readability. These criteria are widely recognized
166   as crucial indicators of information quality and are important for investors to consider when evalu-
167   ating Q&A information. Specifically, the criteria in FinTruthQA are defined as follows:
168
169         1. Question identification: This criterion assesses whether the question from an investor is
170            a genuine question, as opposed to a suggestion or complaint. Each question is assigned a
171            binary label: Positive, indicating it is a real question, or Negative, indicating it is not. Data
172            entries with questions labeled as Negative are excluded from further evaluation under the
173            remaining criteria.
174         2. Question relevance: This criterion evaluates whether a question is pertinent to the invest-
175            ment topic, labeling it as Positive if relevant and Negative if not. It assesses whether the
176            question is related to the company’s shareholders, financial indicators, industry conditions,
177            or other investment-related topics.
178
            3. Answer readability: This criterion evaluates how easily an answer can be understood and
179
               whether it is well-written. The readability score ranges from 1 to 4, with 4 indicating the
180            highest level of readability—clear, concise, and free of ambiguity. A score of 3 indicates
181            moderate readability with some issues, while a score of 2 reflects significant readability
182            challenges. A score of 1 suggests the answer is unreadable, with major ambiguities, inco-
183            herent sentences, or content unrelated to the question.
184
185
            4. Answer relevance: This criterion evaluates whether an answer is relevant and informa-
               tive in response to the question. It assesses whether the answer provides sufficient detail
186
               and explanation to address the question. Answers are classified into three levels: Level 1
187
               indicates the answer is completely unrelated to the question (none of the questions is an-
188            swered); Level 2 indicates the answer is partially related (some questions are answered or
189            it is difficult to judge) ; and Level 3 indicates the answer fully responds to the question (all
190            questions are answered).
191
192   The importance of these criteria lies in their collective ability to enhance the quality and reliabil-
193   ity of information on investor communication platforms. The annotation process was carried out
194   in three phases: pre-annotation, formal annotation, and data verification and integration. In the
195   pre-annotation phase, we recruited six students from the Accounting Department at XXX Univer-
196   sity and conducted five rounds of annotation. After the last round, the inter-annotator agreement
197   (IAA) rate was calculated, and the four students with the highest average IAA rates (85.52%)—two
198
      undergraduates and two master’s students—were selected for the formal annotation phase. To en-
      sure annotation quality, the four selected annotators were evenly divided into two groups, with each
199
      group annotating the same data instances. Discrepancies were first discussed and resolved within
200
      the group, with unresolved issues escalated to the project leader for final decisions. After the formal
201   annotation, the project leader verified the data to ensure alignment with the latest annotation criteria
202   and completeness, before integrating the data and summarising key information, such as sample size
203   and score distribution. Figure 2 illustrates an example of the annotation. A detailed description of
204   the annotation process is listed in the Appendix Section B.

      3.4    Q UALITY CONTROL
207
208   For the answer relevance task, we observed that the raw annotations contained noise due to the

212   with implementation details, can be found in Appendix Section C.

261   4.1   M ODEL SELECTION
262
263   To fully evaluate the performance of different methods in FinTruthQA, we benchmarked several
264   models that cover Machine Learning (ML)-based, Pre-trained Language Model (PLM)-based, PLM
265   with continued Pre-Training (CPT)-based, and LLM-based approaches. Given the distinct nature
266
      and varying difficulty of the tasks, we applied tailored approaches to each accordingly.
267   ML-based. We modeled these tasks as text classification problems and employed various advanced
268   machine learning models, including Logistic Regression (LR)(Hosmer Jr et al., 2013), Support Vec-
269   tor Machine (SVM)(Hearst et al., 1998), K-nearest neighbor (KNN)(Peterson, 2009), and Random
      Forest (RF)(Breiman, 2001). For semantic feature extraction, we first utilized the Jieba toolkit


                                                                    5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270   4
        for Chinese word segmentation and then extracted weighted text representations using TF-IDF
271   Sparck Jones (1972). To achieve optimal performance, we used cross-validation and GridSearch to
272   rigorously explore the best hyperparameters. The best-performing classifiers and hyperparameters
273   varied across tasks, reflecting the distinct nature of each task. As a result, we performed task-specific
274   hyperparameter searches for each task individually.
275
      PLM-based. In our experiments, we found that the tasks of question identification and question
276
      relevance were relatively straightforward, with BERT achieving excellent performance on these
277   tasks. Therefore, our focus shifted to exploring the effectiveness of PLM-based methods on the
278   more challenging tasks of answer relevance and answer readability. Specifically, we investigated
279   a range of BERT-based models, including those specifically trained on financial text data, such as
280   financial news, research reports, and financial encyclopedia entries (FinBERT,5 Mengzi-fin (Zhang
281   et al., 2021), and DKPLM (Zhang et al., 2022)). Furthermore, we examined whether fine-tuned
282   PLMs on specific tasks, such as NLI (Natural Language Inference) and QA (Question Answering),
283   would offer any benefits for these tasks (SBERT-nli (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019) and RoBERTa-
284   extractive-qa (Liu et al., 2019)). Finally, we also examined the performance of a large BERT model
285
      (BERT (Large)).6 Notably, unless otherwise specified, all PLMs employed in this study are the base
      and Chinese versions. Links for these PLMs can be found in Appendix Section D.
286
287   PLM with CPT-based. General domain PLMs, trained on broad texts, often lack the specialized
288   knowledge required to handle the nuanced language and terminology in finance. This limitation is
289   especially pronounced in tasks like answer relevance, where understanding domain-specific jargon
290   and abbreviations is essential for accurate predictions. To bridge this gap, we curated a substantial
291
      volume of domain-specific texts and conducted continued pre-training on general PLMs. Specifi-
      cally, we collected over 900,000 Q&A pairs from the SSE and SZSE platforms by web scraping. The
292
      top-performing models from previous experiments were then selected for continued pre-training us-
293
      ing masked language modeling (MLM). This pre-training on a large-scale, domain-specific dataset
294   enhances the PLM’s ability to understand financial contexts, capture more specialized information,
295   and achieve better performance in downstream tasks.
296
297
      LLM-based. We further evaluate some advanced LLMs, including GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023),
      LLaMA-3.1 (Dubey et al., 2024), Qwen2 (Yang et al., 2024), and Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023), on the
298
      most challenging answer relevance task. Due to their in-context learning ability, LLMs can perform
299
      this task in a zero-shot manner, without requiring specific fine-tuning. Specifically, we provided
300   LLMs with task descriptions and evaluation criteria, instructing them to directly output relevance
301   scores (Direct score). Moreover, we incorporated the Chain of Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022)
302   technique, prompting the LLMs to provide specific reasoning along with the relevance scores (Score
303   with CoT). Full prompts and details are provided in Appendix Section F.

      4.3.1      TASK 1: Q UESTION IDENTIFICATION
358   In this task, we benchmarked several ML-based and PLM-based models, with their performances
359   summarized in Table 2. The ML-based models showed consistent results with minor variations.
360   Both TF-IDF + LR and TF-IDF + RF reached an accuracy of 93.70% and an F1 score of 96.66, with
361   closely aligned precision and recall. TF-IDF + SVM exhibited slightly lower accuracy but achieved
362   the highest recall among the ML models, demonstrating its strength in identifying relevant instances.
363   TF-IDF + KNN, while performing well with an accuracy of 92.67%, had the lowest precision and
364   F1 score in this group. On the other hand, the PLM-based BERT model outperformed all ML-based
365   methods, achieving the highest accuracy, precision, and F1 score. This result highlights the PLM
366
      model’s ability to capture deeper contextual nuances, which traditional frequency-based methods
      like TF-IDF struggle to achieve.

523   6    C ONCLUSION
524
525   In this study, we introduce FinTruthQA, a benchmark designed to evaluate advanced NLP tech-
526   niques for the automatic quality assessment of information disclosure in financial Q&A data. We
527   constructed a dataset of 6,000 real-world financial Q&A entries, each manually annotated based on
528   four key evaluation criteria of accounting. Experiments revealed the effectiveness of pre-trained
529   BERT models in tackling these tasks. In particular, we observed that models pre-trained on finan-
530   cial corpora, as well as those designed for related tasks such as QA, outperformed other variants of
531
      BERT and GPT-4. This emphasized the importance of domain-specific and task-oriented training
      in improving performance. Although existing NLP models showed reasonable performance on this
532
      benchmark, there is still significant room for improvement, especially in the task of answer rele-
533
      vance, which calls for better NLP models. Our benchmark is not only useful for post-hoc regulatory
534   assessments but also holds significant value in real-time applications. For instance, auditors, finan-
535   cial analysts, and corporate communication teams can leverage our framework for ongoing moni-
536   toring and assessment of information disclosure quality. This real-time application enhances market
537   efficiency by enabling quicker identification and correction of inadequate disclosures, thereby ben-
538   efiting all market participants, including retail investors. Moreover, our benchmark can serve as a
539   valuable resource for academic research in natural language processing, particularly in areas con-
      cerning the automated assessment of text quality.

      sure quality rather than conducting direct causal analyses. Users should be cautious when applying
718
      these models in contexts other than financial Q&A, as the models may have learned information
719   specific to the financial domain and Chinese market settings. Additionally, over-reliance on auto-
720   mated systems can lead to the neglect of human judgment and expertise. We recommend using
721   our models as supplementary tools to assist human analysts rather than replace them entirely. Al-
722   though our work aims to improve transparency and information quality in financial markets, users
723   should take appropriate precautions to avoid any potential negative societal impacts resulting from
724   misapplication of the models.

      B    A NNOTATION PROCESS
727
728
      The guideline of the general annotation process is depicted in Figure 5. The annotation process
729   consists of the following steps:
730
731         1. Pre-annotation: Prior to the formal annotation, multiple rounds of pre-annotation were
732            conducted to refine the annotation standards. Each round of pre-annotation involved 100
733            samples. Six annotators were trained and provided with the evaluation criteria and exam-
734            ples. After becoming familiar with the criteria, they rated the provided samples. After
735            each round, the inter-annotator agreement was calculated. Based on the inconsistent parts,
736            the relevant annotation rules were revised according to the discussion results. This process
737            continued for a total of 5 rounds. Eventually, four annotators with the highest consistency
738
               ratings were selected to move onto the formal annotation stage.
739         2. Formal annotation: The formal annotation process also used a multi-round iterative mode,
740            with each Q&A sample annotated by two annotators, A and B. Any uncertainties or dis-
741            agreements between annotators were recorded in Tencent documents. A and B initially
742            address their disagreement, and the project leader quantified the consistency of the annota-
743
               tion results, identifying and documenting the areas where inconsistencies and uncertainties
               were found. These issues were then discussed with experts to reach a consensus. After the
744
               discussion, A was responsible for modifying the annotation to form the final version of the
745
               annotation file.
746
747
            3. Data verification and integration: After completing the formal annotation, the project
               leader checked whether the annotated data conforms to the latest version of the annotation
748
               criteria, as well as whether there were missing scores and other issues. Once confirmed, all
749
               annotated data was integrated, and key information about the data set, such as sample size
750            and score distribution in each dimension, is summarized.
