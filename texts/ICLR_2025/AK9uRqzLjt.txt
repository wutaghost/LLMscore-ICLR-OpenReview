009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Identifying protein enzyme functions, crucial for numerous applications, is chal-
012            lenging due to the rapid growth in protein sequences. Current methods either
013            struggle with false positives or fail to generalize to lesser-known proteins and those
014            with uncharacterized functions. To tackle these challenges, we propose LLaPA:
015            a Protein-centric Large Language and Protein Assistant for Enzyme Commission
016
               (EC) number prediction. LLaPA uses a large multi-modal model to accurately
               predict EC numbers by reformulating the EC number format within the LLM
017
               self-regression framework. We introduce a dual-level protein-centric retrieval: the
018
               protein-level retrieves protein sequences with similar regions, and the chemical-
019            level retrieves corresponding molecules with relevant reaction information. By
020            inputting the original protein along with the retrieved protein and molecule into the
021            LLM, LLaPA achieves improved prediction accuracy, with enhanced generalizabil-
022            ity to lesser-known proteins. Evaluation on three public benchmarks show accuracy
023            improvements of 17.03%, 9.32%, and 38.64%. These results highlight LLaPA’s
024            ability to generalize to novel protein sequences and functionalities. Codes are
025            provided in the supplement.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Understanding the functions of protein enzymes is crucial for unraveling metabolic pathways (Fon-
030
      seca et al., 2019), diagnosing diseases (Hewitt et al., 2004; Voller et al., 1976), advancing personalized
031   medicine (Sookoian & Pirola, 2015), facilitating industrial applications (Victorino da Silva Amatto
032   et al., 2022; Bernal et al., 2018; Chapman et al., 2018; Basso & Serban, 2019), understanding biologi-
033   cal evolution (Campbell et al., 2016), and beyond. Recently, advances in biological technologies have
034   unveiled a vast array of enzyme protein sequences from organisms spanning the entire tree of life.
035   However, only a small fraction of the protein has been manually annotated (i.e., ∼ 0.3% (Boutet et al.,
036   2007) in UniProtKB (The UniProt Consortium, 2023) is manually annotated.) The computational
037   methods can bridge the sequence-annotation gap, but the critical assessment of protein function anno-
038   tation (CAFA) study found that ∼ 40% of the computation annotations are incorrect (Radivojac et al.,
039
      2013). Additionally, there exists a portion of proteins that are not similar enough to any characterized
      protein to infer function and their function remains unknown (Price et al., 2018a). Therefore, the
040
      functional annotation of understudied and promiscuous proteins remains an overwhelming challenge
041
      in protein science (Jeffery, 2018; Hult & Berglund, 2007).
042
043   In the past few years, the enzyme function annotation has been formulated as a multi-label classi-
044   fication tasks (Gligorijević et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2022; Ryu et al., 2019; Sanderson et al., 2023;
045
      Dalkiran et al., 2018), aiming to predict the Enzyme Commission (EC) number of annotated en-
      zymes (Webb & International Union of Biochemistry and Molecular Biology, 1992). The EC number
046
      is a classification ontology for the chemical reactions catalyzed by enzymes. However, the multi-label
047
      classification paradigm suffers from the limited and imbalanced training dataset. Recently proposed
048   CLEAN framework shows the retrieval-based framework can significantly surpass classification deep
049   learning frameworks, such as ProteInfer (Sanderson et al., 2023), DeepEC (Ryu et al., 2019), and
050   DEEPre (Li et al., 2018). Notably, it exhibits remarkable performance on EC numbers represented
051   by fewer than ten sequences, highlighting the superiority of contrastive learning over multi-label
052   classification in predicting enzyme function. However, the framework is not engineered to generalize
053   to proteins with novel functionalities, requiring a certain number of proteins with annotated EC
      numbers to maintain its generalizability. There are pioneers (Xu et al., 2023b; Gane et al.) aiming to

054
      harness the generalizability of LLM and combine LLM with a protein encoder to create an end-to-end
055   trained large multi-model model for various protein-related tasks. Despite these advancements, their
056   approach primarily emphasizes linking proteins with textual data, often overlooking biological priors.
057   This oversight restricts the model’s ability to offer interpretations from a biological standpoint—an
058   aspect that is essential for advancing biological research.
059
      In this paper, we introduce LLaPA, a protein-centric, framework for multi-modal large language
060
      models (MLLMs) training and inference. In detail, LLaPA enhances MLLMs for protein enzyme
061   understanding from two perspectives. ❶ Focusing on the Natural Language Prior, we first observed
062   that the LLM struggles to directly and accurately output EC numbers (i.e., “EC 3.4.11.4”) due to their
063   specific format—four numbers separated by periods. To counteract this limitation, we redesigned
064   the EC number format by replacing the period with another symbol that is distant from numbers in
065   the embedding space. ❷ Embracing the Biological Prior, we build a two-tiered protein-centric
066   retrieval engine, grounded in two fundamental biological insights: (1) At the protein-level, recognizing
067   the evolutionary conservation of functionally critical regions within protein sequences, our engine
068   retrieves a protein with similar regions as the reference to infer the query enzyme’s function. (2)
069
      At the chemical level, acknowledging the intrinsic link between an enzyme’s catalytic actions and
      its function, we leverage the retrieved protein to further identify a corresponding molecule. This
070
      molecule acts as an additional reference point, refining our EC number prediction capabilities. By
071
      querying a protein along with two retrieved entities - a protein and a molecule, LLaPA directly
072   predicts the corresponding Enzyme Commission numbers. Our contributions are summarized below:
073
074         ⋆ We introduce LLaPA framework, a cutting-edge framework specifically designed for protein
075           enzyme function prediction. LLaPA stands out by addressing the unique challenges in
076           protein enzyme function annotation through innovative training and inference strategies
077           tailored for multi-modal large language models (MLLMs).
078         ⋆ We identified how the traditional format of EC numbers can be problematic for accurate
079           predictions by large language models (LLMs). To address this, LLaPA introduces a new
080           encoding scheme that replaces periods with symbols that are more distinct in the embedding
081           space. This subtle change significantly improves EC number prediction accuracy, indicating
082           the format’s better compatibility with the LLMs’ self-regression paradigm.
083
            ⋆ LLaPA advances the field with its two-tiered retrieval engine, deeply rooted in biological
084           insights. This engine not only identifies proteins with evolutionary conserved, functionally
085           critical regions but also pairs these proteins with corresponding molecules. This dual
086           approach enhances the prediction of Enzyme Commission numbers, leveraging biological
087           priors at both the protein and chemical levels to refine the model’s predictive accuracy.
088
            ⋆ Our extensive testing across four public datasets confirms the effectiveness of our approach.
089
              For example, LLaPA achieves {17.03%, 9.32%, 38.64%} performance improvements on
090           Halogenase, Price, and New datasets over previous state-of-the-art (SOTA) approaches.

095   Large Language Model Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated considerable potential
096   in biology by leveraging vast biological datasets to advance research and understanding. Genomic
097   models such as BioBERT (Lee et al., 2020) and DNABERT (Ji et al., 2021) excel in sequence
098   annotation and gene function prediction. In proteomics, models like ESM-1b (Rives et al., 2019)
099
      improve protein sequence understanding, and TAPE (Rao et al., 2019) facilitates evaluation efficiency
      by providing a standardized benchmark. In drug development, AlphaFold3 (Callaway, 2024) proved
100
      superior in finding new drugs. Others, such as SciBERT (Beltagy et al., 2019), a leading language
101
      model, significantly improve the extraction and summarization of essential information. Recent
102   research focuses on integrating multimodal data (Zhang et al., 2023a) (Wang et al., 2024). For
103   example, LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024), which connects a vision encoder and an LLM, is the first attempt
104   to extend instruction-tuning to the language-image multimodal space. The BLIP (Li et al., 2022)
105   has demonstrated impressive performance in vision-language tasks and also achieved state-of-the-art
106   zero-shot performance when the models are directly applied to two video-language tasks. In addition,
107   enhancing model interpretability (Joshi et al., 2021) (Nhlapho et al., 2024), and improving prediction
      robustness (Yang et al., 2023) also attract a lot of attention.

131   Enzyme Function Prediction Enzyme function prediction plays a crucial role in the field of biology.
132   Several ways have been devised to forecast enzyme function, such as those relying on sequence
133   similarity (Zhang et al., 2017) (Desai et al., 2011) (Altschul et al., 1997), structural similarity
134   (Altschul et al., 1990), and protein homology (Zhang et al., 2017). InterPro (Paysan-Lafosse et al.,
135
      2022) signatures, position-specific scoring matrices (cheol Jeong et al., 2010), pseudo-amino acid
      composition (Chou, 2009), and machine learning techniques (Amidi et al., 2017) such as multi-label
136
      k-nearest neighbour (Huang et al., 2007) and SVM (Mohammad & Nagarajaram, 2011) are all good
137
      ways to figure out what multi-functional enzymes do. Furthermore, the deep learning frameworks that
138   integrate representation learning and classifier learning have shown significant promise in enzyme
139   function prediction, such as Proteinfer (Sanderson et al., 2023), DeepEC (Ryu et al., 2019), and
140   DEEPre (Li et al., 2018). A new paradigm was recently introduced by ProTranslator (Xu & Wang,
141   2022). It deems the process of using function descriptions to predict the amino acid sequence a
142   machine translation problem. This pattern was later expanded with a framework for multilingual
143   translation (Xu et al., 2023a). Additionally, (Yu et al., 2023) introduces a metric learning framework
144   designed to increase the distance between protein embeddings of differing functions and decrease
145   it for those with similar functions, achieving state-of-the-art (SoTA) performance. However, their
146
      approach relies solely on a simple triplet loss for contrasting samples and does not integrate biological
      priors to enhance generalization for functions without a defined EC number.

151   Overview LLaPA is a framework designed specifically for predicting the function of protein
152   enzymes, outputting the Enzyme Commission number based on the given protein sequence. First of
153   all, we reformulate the y that is more friendly for LLM prediction (Section 3.1). Then, for a protein
154   sequence x with n amino acids, LLaPA initially uses x to identify a reference protein sequence x′ ,
155   then retrieves the corresponding molecule m′ related to the catalytic reaction involve x (Section 3.2).
156
      As a result, LLaPA employs x, x′ , and m′ to predict the functional annotation y of x (Section 3.3).
157   Specifically, LLaPA inference adopts a similar design to LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023b;a). With x,
158   retrieved protein x′ and retrieved molecule m′ , LLaPA first apply the pre-trained protein encoder
159   E(·) to provide protein features zp = E(x) and z′p = E(x′ ). Next it uses the pre-trained molecular
160   encoder C(·) to obtain molecular features zm = C(m′ ). To process these features further, LLaPA
161   uses two projectors: Wp , which converts zp and zp′ into language embedding tokens hp and h′p , and
      Wm , which transforms zm into language embedding tokens hm . These projectors map information

      from proteins and molecules into the language token space, bridging biological and chemical prior
163   for protein enzyme function understanding. Finally, the query protein x, retrieved protein x′ and
164   molecule m′ along with corresponding instructions can combine together to obtain the EC number of
165   the query. For more details about overall pipeline, please refer to Algorithm 1.

204   3.2   I NTEGRATING B IOLOGICAL P RIOR K NOWLEDGE BY R ETRIEVAL E NGINE
205
      In this section, we introduce a novel two-tiered retrieval engine, a cornerstone of LLaPA integrates
206
      biological prior knowledge to prompt LLMs to predict the four digits of the EC number. This engine
207   is divided into two specialized modules: the first addresses the retrieval of reference protein sequences,
208   while the second concentrates on the identification of molecules relevant to chemical reactions.
209
210   Protein Prior Knowledge Module - Retrieval of Reference Protein Sequences. A fundamental
211   principle in understanding protein function is that regions of protein sequences important for function
212   tend to be conserved through evolution. Consequently, proteins sharing similar regions are likely to
213   possess similar enzymatic functions and may even catalyze the same reactions. Inspired by this insight
214   into protein functionality, we employ "mmseq2" (Steinegger & Söding, 2017), a comprehensive
215   software suite designed for the efficient searching and clustering of extensive protein and nucleotide
      sequence datasets based on significant protein-related knowledge. This tool enables us to identify


                                                           4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      the most closely related protein sequence as a reference, thereby aiding the model in accurately
217   predicting the four digits of the EC number. When given a protein, LLaPA utilizes "mmseq2" to find
218   the most similar protein sequence, x′ , within a specified protein database.
219
220
      Specifically, input the query protein sequence x to the “mmseq2”. It will search in the specified
      protein database and output the x′ with the highest sequence identify cutoff value in the protein
221
      database.
222
223
      Chemical Reaction Prior Knowledge Module - Retrieval of Corresponding Molecules. The
224
      simplest way to identify a catalytic reaction is by examining the reaction itself. This module
225   is designed to retrieve a molecule in SMILES format 1 that participates in the catalytic reaction
226   associated with a given protein. Yet, the task of retrieving the catalytic reaction based solely on
227   the protein sequence is exceedingly difficult. Fortunately, the "Protein Prior Knowledge Module"
228   presents an opportunity to bypass the direct retrieval of molecules by protein sequence. Therefore, we
229   employ the “rhea” (Bansal et al., 2022), an expert-curated knowledgebase of chemical and transport
230   reactions of biological interest, and the standard for enzyme and transporter annotation in UniProtKB.
231   Noticeably, the “rhea” necessitates the EC number to fetch the relevant catalytic reaction—the very
232   information we aim to predict.
233   During the training phase, as shown on the left side of Figure 1 (B), we directly input the
234   EC number (i.e., the label) of the protein sequence to the “Molecule Retrieval” module.
235   During the inference phase (the right side of Figure 1 (B)), the EC number of the input protein
236   sequence xu is unavailable. Therefore, we first input the protein sequence xu to the “Protein Prior
237   Knowledge Module” and get a protein sequence m′u . The protein sequence m′u from the protein
238   database has EC numbers. Consequently, we fed its EC numbers into the “Molecule Retrieval”
239
      module.
240   As depicted in Figure 1 (C), our “Molecule Retrieval” module operates as follows: (1) it randomly
241   selects one EC number from the input EC numbers; (2) it inputs the selected EC number into the
242   “rhea”, which then outputs the corresponding catalytic reaction; (3) it selects the first reactant molecule
243   in the catalytic reaction to be the output molecule m′ .
244   We emphasize that the retrieve logic in the inference phase is reasonable, as proteins with high
245   sequence identify cutoff values typically exhibit similar enzyme functions Gerlt et al. (2015); Yu et al.
246   (2023). Therefore, their molecules in the corresponding chemical enzyme reactions should possess
247   similar catalytic information. For instance, the protein “T1RRJ4” and its corresponding retrieved
248   protein “Q2XSC6” have the EC numbers “EC 4.2.3.10” and “EC 4.2.3.20”, respectively. Interestingly,
249   the first reaction molecule for both is “(2E)-geranyl diphosphate”. As depicted in Figure 1 (C), our
250   “Molecule Search” process randomly chooses an EC number when multiple are available; if only one
251
      EC number exists, that EC number is utilized.

329   Datasets. We selected the Swiss-Prot database (Boutet et al., 2007) as the source of our training
330   data, a subset of the extensive UniProt dataset known for its thorough human review and curated
331   annotations. Employing the data filtering approach described in (Yu et al., 2023), we initially secured
332   approximately 220K protein sequences. Subsequently, we clustered and subsampled these sequences
333   using mmseq2 (Steinegger & Söding, 2017), applying sequence identity cutoffs of 70% to effectively
334
      filter out homologous sequences. Our assessment of the LLaPA model’s competency in predicting
      EC numbers was performed across four well-regarded benchmarks: (1) New-392 (or New) (Yu et al.,
335
      2023), which includes 392 enzyme sequences that span 177 distinct EC numbers. (2) Price-149
336
      (or Price), a collection of protein sequences that were found to be inaccurately or inconsistently
337   labeled in reputable databases like the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) by
338   automated annotation methods. These sequences were later annotated with labels validated through
339   experiments by Price et al. (2018b). (3) Multi (Yu et al., 2023), a dataset comprising enzymes
340   associated with rare EC numbers, each represented no more than five times, with the dataset including
341   over 3, 000 samples and covering over 1, 000 distinct EC numbers. 4. Halogenase (Yu et al.,
342   2023), a dataset that encompasses various halogenases, either marked as uncharacterized and/or
343   hypothetical proteins in UniProt or bearing conflicting annotations in scholarly literature. Through
344   meticulous expert curation and subsequent experimental validations, all halogenase in the dataset
345
      were confidently annotated with EC numbers. The sequence identify between training set and
      testing set Halogenase, Multi, New-392, and Price-149 are 39.20%, 58.96%, 48.41%, and

      42.66%, respectively. Therefore, the performance improvement in Halogenase and Price-149

      can indicate the generalization enhancement.

524   5                 C ONCLUSION
525
526   This paper introduces LLaPA, a multi-modal framework developed to predict enzyme functions by
527   assigning Enzyme Commission (EC) numbers to protein sequences. Our work represents a pioneering
528   effort to synergize natural language priors (where punctuation such as "." in numbers can resemble
529   large numerical values to LLMs due to their proximity in the word embedding space) and biological
530   priors (emphasizing the evolutionary conservation of functionally critical regions within protein
531   sequences and the catalytic reactions of the corresponding enzymes) in a unified approach using
532   multi-modal large language models.
533   As a result, LLaPA achieves state-of-the-art performance across four public benchmarks, demon-
534   strating its superiority. This underscores the significance of the EC number format and suggests a
535   promising method for integrating biological insights through retrieval mechanisms with LLMs to
536   enhance our understanding of protein enzyme functions. Future directions include a broader explo-
537   ration of protein function, the integration of our proposed retrieval engine with reasoning capabilities
538   to further augment retrieval effectiveness, and propose large scale of protein dataset with secondary
539   structure then compare with structure-based protein predictors.
