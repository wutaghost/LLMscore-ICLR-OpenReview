009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
               Offline reinforcement learning methods, which typically train agents that make decisions
012            step by step, are known to suffer from instability due to bootstrapping and function ap-
013            proximation, especially when applied to tasks requiring long-horizon planning. To allevi-
014            ate these issues, in this paper, we propose a novel policy gradient approach by planning
015            an action sequence in a high-dimensional space. This design implicitly models tempo-
016            ral dependencies, excelling in long-horizon and horizon-critical tasks. Furthermore, we
017            discover that replacing maximum likelihood with cross-entropy loss in policy gradient
018            methods significantly stabilizes training gradients, leading to substantial performance im-
019            provements in long-horizon tasks. The proposed neural network-based solution features
020
               a simple architecture that not only facilitates ease of training and convergence but also
               demonstrates high efficiency and effective performance. Extensive experimental results
021
               reveal that our method exhibits strong performance across a variety of tasks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Offline reinforcement learning, which focuses on training agents using pre-collected static datasets without
      real-time environment interactions, has emerged as a promising approach, particularly in scenarios where
028
      online data collection is impractical or risky (Levine et al., 2020b). Unlike traditional reinforcement learn-
029
      ing, where agents learn through trial and error, Offline reinforcement learning leverages offline data to teach
030   agents step-by-step decision-making. Despite its potential, Offline reinforcement learning often encoun-
031   ters significant challenges related to the stability of the learning process, particularly when combined with
032   function approximation and bootstrapping (Mazoure et al., 2023).
033
      The primary issue arises from bootstrapping, where future estimates are used to update current predictions, in
034
      combination with function approximation, which generalizes over large state spaces (Sutton & Barto, 2018).
035
      Together, these factors propagate errors through iterative updates, which can result in divergence and lead to
036   ineffective policy learning. The iterative propagation of these errors, especially in long-horizon tasks, causes
037   inaccuracies to accumulate, eventually destabilizing the learning process and yielding suboptimal policies
038   (Szepesvari & Littman, 1999).
039
      To address these issues, recent works have proposed alternative approaches that avoid direct value estima-
040
      tion, opting instead for methods that focus on planning and optimizing action sequences over time (Ajay
041
      et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2021). This shift is particularly beneficial in long-horizon decision-making tasks,
042   which mitigates the instability inherent in dynamic programming methods, which are prone to compound-
043   ing approximation errors over long horizons. However, these prevailing methods are constrained by the
044   limitations of their underlying generative models, particularly in their reliance on accurately modeling the
045   trajectory distribution dynamics and state transitions.These models often need to capture the complexity
046   of high-dimensional or stochastic trajectories, and their reliance on large, diverse datasets for accurate state

                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


047
      representation and generalization exacerbates computational cost and model complexity, particularly in data-
048
      scarce environments. (Yang et al., 2022).
049
050   Recent studies also have shown that classification-based objectives, particularly cross-entropy loss, offer
051   a more stable alternative to regression-based losses in deep reinforcement learning, This is because cross-
052
      entropy encourages the model to learn finer distinctions and more detailed information about the data (Zhang
      et al., 2023). Cross-entropy loss is widely used in supervised learning tasks due to its ability to handle mit-
053
      igate overfitting and noisy data issues, However, although Farebrother et al. (2024) leveraged cross-entropy
054
      loss to improve the stability of model training, it does not fundamentally address the core issues related
055   to bootstrapping and value function estimation. Specifically, the method still relies on future estimates to
056   update current predictions, inherently propagating the approximation errors throughout the training process.
057
058
      Inspired by the approaches used in generative models, we propose a minimalistic model that transforms
      long-horizon planning into high-dimensional action outputs, which is called Action Sequence Planner (AS-
059
      Planner). Our model enhances policy optimization by focusing on the trajectory level, extracting relevant
060
      patterns from sub-optimal trajectories to refine decision-making in a more structured and efficient manner.
061   Unlike traditional generative models that often rely on complex architectures to model temporal dependen-
062   cies, our approach leverages a simple neural network to implicitly model the temporal structure by directly
063   outputting action sequences. This eliminates the need for complex trajectory modeling, making it possible
064   for even minimal multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) (Rumelhart et al., 1986) to handle temporal dynamics
065   effectively. ASPlanner implicitly captures temporal dependencies without relying on value estimation and
066   bootstrapping. By directly optimizing the policy, our method bypasses the need for future state estimations,
067   thus reducing the error propagation that typically occurs in traditional value-based methods.
068   In traditional policy gradient methods (Sutton et al., 1999), Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) is com-
069   monly used to maximize the likelihood of observed actions by minimizing the negative log-likelihood loss.
070   However, MLE often suffers from high variance, particularly in reinforcement learning scenarios where
071   step-by-step exploration can lead to unstable gradients and slow convergence (Rückstieß et al., 2008). To
072   address these issues, ASPlanner replaces the MLE objective with a cross-entropy loss function, which com-
073   pares the policy’s predicted action probabilities with a target distribution derived from normalized actions.
074   This substitution reduces the variance in gradient estimation, providing a more stable and effective approach
075
      to policy optimization in offline reinforcement learning environments. Extensive experiments showed that
      ASPlanner outperforms mainstream offline RL algorithms on long-horizon tasks. It significantly improves
076
      stability and policy performance in complex decision-making scenarios. Our contributions can be summa-
077
      rized as follows:
078
079          • We introduce a novel method that simplifies the complexity of long-horizon tasks by transforming it
080            into an action space complexity, making it easier to handle high-dimensional action spaces without
081            compounding errors from traditional value-based methods.
082
             • By employing a cross-entropy loss function instead of the maximum likelihood loss, we observed
083
               significant performance improvements across various datasets.
084
085          • Our model, consisting of a few MLP layers, demonstrates strong performance on long-horizon
086
               tasks and traditional control benchmarks, providing a lightweight and efficient solution for offline
               reinforcement learning challenges.

      2.1   M ARKOV D ECISION P ROCESS
095
096   A Markov Decision Process provides a formal framework for modeling decision-making problems where
097   outcomes are partly random and partly under the control of a decision maker. An MDP is defined by the
098   tuple (S, A, P, r, γ), where S is a set of states, A is a set of actions, P : S × A × S → [0, 1] ⊆ R is a state
099   transition probability function, where P (st+1 |st , at ) denotes the probability of transitioning from state st to
100   state st+1 in the t-th step after action at is taken, r(st , at ) ∈ R represents the immediate reward received
101   after taking action at in state st , with γ ∈ [0, 1) being the discount factor that determines the importance of
102   future rewards. The goal of reinforcement learning in an MDP is to find a policy π ∗ (a|s) that maximizes the
103
      expected cumulative reward over time.

      2.4   O FFLINE R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
135
136   In offline reinforcement learning, the policy is learned from a fixed dataset of past experiences without any

383
384
      Offline reinforcement learning has emerged as a powerful tool for learning policies from pre-collected
385
      datasets, particularly when real-time interactions with the environment are costly or unsafe. However, one of
386   the key challenges in offline RL is handling instability caused by bootstrapping and function approximation,
387   especially in long-horizon tasks.
388
389
      Bootstrapping and Function Approximation: Bootstrapping can lead to instability by propagating estima-
      tion errors through the learning process. To address this, Wang et al. (2022) developed the Bootstrapped and
390
      Constrained Pessimistic Value Iteration (BCP-VI) algorithm, which utilizes bootstrapping and pessimism
391
      to reduce suboptimality in offline RL with linear function approximation. Bai et al. (2022) proposed Pes-
392   simistic Bootstrapping for Offline RL (PBRL), which penalizes out-of-distribution (OOD) actions to mitigate
393   extrapolation error while generalizing beyond the offline data.
394
395
      Generative Modeling and Policy Optimization: Generative models have also been leveraged to tackle
      offline RL problems. Wei et al. (2021) developed Action-conditioned Q-learning (AQL), which incorporates
396
      generative modeling to improve policy approximation by mitigating distribution shift. Yin et al. (2023)
397
      further explored differentiable function approximation in offline RL, providing a theoretical framework for
398   understanding the statistical complexity of non-linear function approximators.
399
400   Cross-Entropy Methods for Stability: Cross-entropy loss has been increasingly used in RL to address
401
      instability in training. The work by Wen & Topcu (2020) introduced a constrained cross-entropy method
      for safe reinforcement learning, ensuring stability by transforming constrained optimization problems into
402
      unconstrained ones.
403
404   Robustness in Offline RL: Addressing the issue of out-of-distribution actions and policy divergence, Bai
405   et al. (2024) proposed a Monotonic Quantile Network (MQN), which improves the robustness of policy
406   learning by optimizing a worst-case criterion of returns. This method is especially effective in safety-critical
407
      applications where avoiding risky actions is paramount.
408
409   6   C ONCLUSION
410
411   This paper presented a novel approach to offline reinforcement learning that replaces maximum likelihood
412   estimation with a cross-entropy loss function to improve training stability and performance in long-horizon
413   tasks. Our method, ASPlanner, generates entire action sequences, reducing error propagation and enhancing
414
      decision-making by avoiding step-by-step planning.
415   Experiments on D4RL and Budget Allocation Environments showed that ASPlanner performs strongly
416   across many tasks, with faster convergence and improved stability compared to existing methods. By di-
417   rectly optimizing action sequences and leveraging cross-entropy loss, our approach effectively handles the
418   complexity of high-dimensional action spaces and long-term planning.
419   Given that our method relies on a simple neural network architecture with only a few layers, future work
420   can build on this minimalistic learning framework. We hope our work inspires further research and applica-
421   tions in both academia and industry, contributing to the development of more efficient offline reinforcement
422   learning methods.

645        Intuitively, the essence of MLE lies in evaluating the similarity between the actions predicted by the neural
646        network and the offline actions recorded in the dataset. The more similar these actions are, the smaller the
647        loss value becomes. Similarly, the CE loss function measures the divergence between the predicted and
648        target action distributions. By minimizing this divergence, we achieve a direct measure of how well the
649
           model’s predictions align with the offline data. From this perspective, the CE loss can be viewed as a natural
           extension of MLE, both conceptually and mathematically.

652        with the CE loss. This substitution is empirically validated through extensive experimentation. Our results

659
660   In this appendix, we formally describe the reinforcement learning environment used in this work, which we
661   refer to as the Budget Allocation Environment. The state at time t, denoted as St , is defined as a tuple of
662   three elements:
663                                      St = (timeLeftt , bgtLeftt , bgtCostt )
664   timeLeftt = Tt ∈ [0, 1] represents the normalized remaining time at time step t, where T is the total number
665   of decision steps in the environment. bgtLeftt = remainingBudget       ∈ [0, 1] represents the proportion of the
                                                                 budget
666
      remaining budget at time t. bgtCostt = At−1 ∈ [0, 1] records the proportion of the budget spent at the
667
      previous time step t − 1, and it is equal to the action taken at that step.
668
669   At each time step, the agent chooses an action At ∈ [0, 1], representing the proportion of the total budget
670   to allocate at time step t. The environment provides a reward Rt , where Rt ∈ [0, ∞], reflecting the value
671
      gained by allocating that portion of the budget.
672   The state transitions are governed by the following dynamics:
673
                                                       P (St+1 | St , At )
674
675   Specifically, the next state St+1 is determined by:
676
                                                                      1
677                                                timeLeftt+1 = t +
                                                                      T
678
679                                             bgtLeftt+1 = bgtLeftt − At
680                                                    bgtCostt+1 = At
681   Thus, the remaining time and remaining budget are updated based on the action taken at each time step,
682   while bgtCostt+1 records the action taken at the current time step, to be used in the next time step.
683
684
      The reward function R(St , At ) is defined as:
685                                             R(St , At ) = αt (At − βt )2
686
687   where αt is a time-dependent scaling factor, and βt represents the optimal budget allocation proportion for
688
      time step t. This quadratic reward structure indicates that the reward increases as the budget allocation At
      approaches βt , though the reward growth diminishes as the allocation deviates from this ideal.
689
690   The purpose of the Budget Allocation Environment is to simulate various budget spending scenarios and
691   to evaluate different allocation strategies over a fixed time horizon. By defining different reward func-
692   tions f (st , at ) and transition mechanisms T (st , at ), the environment can reflect diverse real-world spending
693   conditions and allow for the calculation of optimal strategies. The environment’s multi-dimensional and
694
      non-linear characteristics help isolate factors that might otherwise confound real-world analysis.
