009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
012            Long-tailed learning has garnered increasing attention due to its practical signifi-
013
               cance. Among the various approaches, the fine-tuning paradigm has gained con-
               siderable interest with the advent of foundation models. However, most existing
014
               methods primarily focus on leveraging knowledge from these models, overlook-
015
               ing the inherent biases introduced by the imbalanced training data they rely on. In
016            this paper, we examine how such imbalances affect long-tailed downstream tasks.
017            Specifically, we refer to the biases in foundation models and downstream tasks
018            as parameter imbalance and data imbalance, respectively. Through fine-tuning,
019            we observe that parameter imbalance plays a more critical role, while data imbal-
020            ance can be mitigated using existing re-balancing strategies. Moreover, we find
021            that parameter imbalance cannot be effectively addressed by current re-balancing
022            techniques, such as adjusting the logits, during training, unlike data imbalance.
023            To tackle both imbalances simultaneously, we constitute a causal structure graph
024
               and view the incomplete semantic factor as the confounder, which brings spurious
               correlations between input samples and labels. To resolve the negative effects of
025
               this, we propose a novel backdoor adjustment method that learns the true causal
026
               effect between input samples and labels, rather than merely fitting the correlations
027            in the data. Experimental results validate the effectiveness of our method.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
034   Real-world data often follows a long-tailed distribution, where the majority of instances are concen-
035   trated in head classes, leaving only a small number of instances for each tail class. This scarcity of
036
      samples makes generalization on such labels challenging, and naive learning on this data tends to
      introduce an undesirable bias toward dominant labels. Recently, with the advent of foundation mod-
037
      els, downstream performance can be significantly improved by fine-tuning these models on labeled
038
      data, often yielding superior results compared to training from scratch, while also minimizing train-
039   ing costs (Wang et al., 2022b;a). Consequently, there has been considerable interest in exploring
040   how to fine-tune foundation models and leverage their strong generalization capabilities to enhance
041   learning for tail classes.
042
      Recent works such as LIFT (Shi et al., 2024), LPT (Dong et al., 2022), and VL-LTR (Tian et al.,
043
      2022) demonstrate that properly fine-tuning foundation models like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021)
044
      can significantly enhance long-tail learning performance. VL-LTR improves visual recognition,
045   particularly for tail classes, by collecting class descriptions from the internet and jointly learning
046   both visual and text representations. LIFT, on the other hand, reveals that heavy fine-tuning hurts
047   and Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (Chen et al., 2022; Jia et al., 2022) (PEFT) based methods
048   can preserve as much information from the foundation model as possible, which is important for
049   the downstream imbalanced learning. However, these methods tend to overemphasize the use of
050   foundation models while overlooking their inherent biases, as shown in Fig. 1. Large-scale datasets
051   used to train foundation models, such as LAION (Schuhmann et al., 2021), also follow a long-
052   tailed distribution, which can negatively impact downstream tasks (Zhu et al., 2024; Wen et al.,
053
      2024). Therefore, the fine-tuned model is influenced by dual long-tailed distributions (upstream and
      downstream imbalance ), and only considering data imbalance is not proper.

087   causal inference to realize our backdoor adjustment. After applying our method, we achieve a more
088   balanced performance over all the classes on different datasets.

126
127   Downstream fine-tuning Fine-tuning techniques can be broadly categorized into full fine-tuning
128   and Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) (Jia et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022; Zaken et al., 2021;
129   Houlsby et al., 2019; Hu et al., 2021). PEFT refers to methods that adapt pre-trained models to
130   specific tasks while minimizing the number of parameters that require updating. When data samples
131   are limited, PEFT often outperforms full fine-tuning. Visual Prompt Tuning (VPT) (Jia et al., 2022)
132   introduces two variants, VPT-Shallow and VPT-Deep, which insert prompts into different trans-
133   former layers. In contrast, AdaptFormer (Chen et al., 2022) introduces lightweight modules that add
134
      only a small number of parameters, yet outperform fully fine-tuned models on various benchmarks.
      Despite their efficiency, PEFT methods focus on introducing fewer parameters while preserving the
135
      foundational model’s information. However, this also retains the model’s inherent limitations, such
136
      as imbalances. In this work, we provide an in-depth analysis of how the biases of foundation models
137   impact downstream tasks and propose a novel method that achieves promising performance.

      Notation We aim to solve a C-way classification problem with instances x ∈ X and labels y ∈
      Y = [C] = {1, . . . , C}, where X and Y denote the input space and output space. For pre-training,
143
      we denote DP as the distribution of the training set and we cannot access it at the fine-tuning phase.
144
      For downstream tasks, we denote DS and DT as the distribution for training and test, respectively.
145   In the context of long-tailed learning, the number of samples varies across classes, i.e., PS (Y =
146   1) ̸= PS (Y = 2) ̸= . . . ̸= PS (Y = C), where PS (Y ) denotes the class prior of DS . In contrast,

288   4.2    A NALYSIS OF ADJUSTMENT FOR PARAMETER IMBALANCE
289
290   Addressing parameter imbalance is a crucial task. Drawing inspiration from methods used to tackle
291   data imbalance, a natural approach to mitigating this issue is to adjust the output logits, similar to
292   the Logit Adjustment (LA) technique. The existing Generalized Logit Adjustment (GLA) method
293   addresses parameter imbalance by modifying the logits according to the estimated label priors. Thus,
294   in this section, we explore whether parameter imbalance can be effectively eliminated through the
295
      simple adjustment of output logits.

      5.2    BACKDOOR ADJUSTMENT
408
409   A more generalized fine-tuned model should be independent of incomplete semantic factor (i.e.
410   parameter imbalance), which inhibits confounding effects from C. Therefore, instead of estimating
411   P(Y = y | x), we use back-door criterion and estimate P(Y = y | do(x)), where do() is exploited
412   to cut off the connection from the C to X. Considering that each sample in X uniquely corresponds
413   to a balanced representation in B, which indicates that the mapping between X and B is injective.
414
      Thus there only exists a certain b such that P(b | x) = 1 and P(b′ | x) = 0 for b ̸= b′ . Then, we
      propose our back-door adjustment method:
415
                                 XX                                     X
416       P(Y = y | do(x)) =             P(Y = y | b, c)P(c)P(b | x) =     P(Y = y | b, c)P(c)
417
                                                                                                      (7)
                                b∈B c∈C                                       c∈C
418
419   For simplicity, we assume P(c) = 1/M , where M is the number of incomplete semantic factors.
420   Intuitively, P(Y = y | do(x)) can be estimated by fusing P(Y = y | b, c), where b represents
421   the balanced representation. For different incomplete semantic factors {c1 , c2 , · · · , cM }, we utilize
422   a re-balancing method, such as Logit Adjustment (LA), to compute a set of data balanced outputs
423   {P(Y = y | b, c1 ), P(Y = y | b, c2 ), · · · , P(Y = y | b, cM )}. These outputs can then be merged
424   using fusion weights, following Eq. 7, to obtain an unbiased estimation. Since iterating over all
425
      possible incomplete semantic factors is impractical, we approximate these factors using models like
      CLIP, OpenCLIP, and MetaCLIP, thereby addressing Eq. 7 and simplifying the process of balancing
426
      both parameter and data imbalances simultaneously.

      7     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we analyze how the bias of foundation models influences downstream imbalanced
      tasks. We formally define parameter imbalance and data imbalance to guide our analysis. After
532
      fine-tuning, while data imbalance can be effectively addressed, parameter imbalance persists, hin-
533
      dering further performance improvements. We analyze that the re-balancing based method cannot
534   give many benefits to the feature representation. To address this issue, we solve this problem from
535   another perspective and construct a causal structure graph, identifying the incomplete semantic fac-
536   tors generated by parameter imbalance as key confounders. Consequently, we propose a backdoor
537   adjustment method to mitigate the negative impact of this textcolor blueincomplete semantic fac-
538   tors. Experimental results demonstrate that our method enhances generalization performance across
539   various long-tailed datasets. In future work, we plan to extend our method to other tasks, such as
      object detection and segmentation under long-tailed distributions.

740   A.3   PARAMETER IMBALANCE AND DATA IMBALANCE
741
742   Previous methods have effectively addressed data imbalance by providing a clear definition of the
743   issue. Following the approach of OLTR (Liu et al., 2019), we categorize the classes into three
744   groups: “D-Many”, “D-Medium”, and “D-Few”. Specifically, classes with more than 100 training
745   samples are classified as “D-Many”, those with 20–100 samples are categorized as “D-Medium”,
746
      and classes with fewer than 20 samples fall into the “D-Few” group. This categorization allows for a
      systematic examination of the impact of data imbalance across varying levels of sample availability.
747
748   To address parameter imbalance, where access to pre-training data is unavailable, we adopt the
749   estimation method provided by GLA (Zhu et al., 2024) (Eq. 3) to estimate the label prior. Using this
750   estimation, we divide the classes uniformly into three groups: the top 30% of classes based on the
751   label prior are designated as “P-Many”, the next 30–60% as “P-Medium”, and the remaining 40%
752
      as “P-Few”. This approach provides a structured way to assess parameter imbalance across different
      subsets of classes.
753
754   By applying these criteria, any given dataset can be simultaneously split based on both parameter
755   imbalance and data imbalance. Importantly, a single class can belong to multiple groups across the
      two categorizations; for instance, a class may be grouped as “P-Many” while also being classified

760   B     D ETAILED DISCUSSIONS ON INCOMPLETE SEMANTIC FACTORS
761
762   We first provide the definition of confounders in the causal framework: When a third variable Z
763   influences both X and Y , we say that X and Y are confounded. Such a variable Z is referred
764
      to as a confounder of X and Y (Pearl, 2009). Specifically, it satisfies the fork structure X ←
      Z → Y . This latent common cause Z can create a spurious correlation between X and Y , making
765
      the observed statistical relationship P(Y |X) potentially misleading. To address this, the causal
766
      relationship P(Y |do(X)) is used as a replacement for the correlation P(Y |X).
767
768   Secondly, we explain why the incomplete semantic factor C in our problem setting can create a
769   backdoor path X ← C → Y , leading to a spurious correlation. The example in the first row in Fig.4
770
      explains why the parameter-imbalanced model D leads to an incomplete semantic factor C. For
      example, the semantic factor available to OpenCLIP is in the head of the object, while the semantic
771
      factor available to MetaCLIP is in the body of the object.
772
773   We emphasize that the semantic factors obtained due to model imbalance are incomplete. Therefore,
774   for rigor, we have modified the causal diagram in Fig. 5 to include the inaccessible semantic factor
775   U . From the perspective of data generation, C and U together constitute X, thus X ← C holds.
776
      On the other hand, the path C → Y indicates that the predicted label distributions follow their own
      parameter imbalance. A parameter-imbalanced model, OpenCLIP, consistently predicts a “head
777
      of the object” label distribution (i.e., making more accurate predictions on test samples where the
778
      head of the object is not occluded), regardless of the test distributions. Similarly, the parameter-
779   imbalanced model MetaCLIP exhibits a distinct “body of the object” preference across different test
780   distributions.
781
782
      The relationship between the confounding path X ← C → Y is unstable. When the training set
      model predicts the label with the help of the body semantics of the object, if the body of a sample
783
      of a certain class in the test example is occluded, the model will give an incorrect prediction. From
784
      this example, it can be seen that an ideal model needs to learn the causal relationship P(Y |do(X))
785   between the input and the label, rather than fitting the unstable confounding path X ← C → Y , in
786   order to achieve generalization between different distributions.

833
834
      AdaptFormer is a lightweight adaptation framework designed to enhance the efficiency and flexibil-
      ity of large pre-trained vision transformers (ViTs) in downstream tasks. By introducing a low-rank
835
      adaptation (LoRA) module within the feed-forward layers of the transformer, AdaptFormer allows
836
      for the modification of the model’s capabilities without requiring a full re-training of all parame-
837   ters. This approach preserves the pre-trained knowledge in the original transformer while enabling
838   it to adapt effectively to new tasks with minimal additional parameters. As a result, AdaptFormer
839   achieves strong performance in transfer learning tasks, providing a balance between computational
840   efficiency and task-specific adaptability.
841
      Let X be the input to the feed-forward layer, and the original weight matrix of the layer be
842
      W ∈ Rd×d . In AdaptFormer, an additional low-rank module is added as a modification. This
843   is represented by two matrices, A ∈ Rd×r and B ∈ Rr×d , where r ≪ d represents the low-rank
844   dimension. The output of the modified feed-forward layer is given by:
845
846
                                                 Y = W X + α(AB)X                                            (9)
847
      Here, α is a scaling factor that controls the influence of the low-rank adaptation. The term AB
848
      represents the low-rank adaptation added to the original weight transformation, allowing the model
849
      to adapt with a minimal number of additional parameters.

      C.3    V ISUAL P ROMPT T UNING
852
853   Visual Prompt Tuning is an innovative approach designed to adapt pre-trained vision models for
854   specific tasks by utilizing learnable visual prompts. Instead of fine-tuning all the parameters of a
855   large vision model, this method introduces a small set of visual prompts that can be learned during
856   the adaptation process. These prompts are essentially additional visual tokens that are concate-
857   nated to the input image embeddings, guiding the model to focus on relevant features for the target
858   task. This approach significantly reduces the computational burden associated with full model fine-
859   tuning while retaining the rich representations learned from extensive pre-training. By leveraging
860
      visual prompts, practitioners can achieve competitive performance in various computer vision ap-
      plications, such as image classification and object detection, with minimal adjustments to the model
861
      architecture.
