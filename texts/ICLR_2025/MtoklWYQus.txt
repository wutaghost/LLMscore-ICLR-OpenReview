009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            All-in-one image restoration tackles different types of degradations with a unified
012            model instead of having task-specific, non-generic models for each degradation.
013            The requirement to tackle multiple degradations using the same model can lead to
014            high-complexity designs with fixed configuration that lack the adaptability to more
015            efficient alternatives. We propose DyNet, a dynamic family of networks designed
016
               in an encoder-decoder style for all-in-one image restoration tasks. Our DyNet can
               seamlessly switch between its bulkier and lightweight variants, thereby offering
017
               flexibility for efficient model deployment with a single round of training. This
018
               seamless switching is enabled by our weights-sharing mechanism, forming the
019            core of our architecture and facilitating the reuse of initialized module weights.
020            Further, to establish robust weights initialization, we introduce a dynamic pre-
021            training strategy that trains variants of the proposed DyNet concurrently, thereby
022            achieving a 50% reduction in GPU hours. Our dynamic pre-training strategy elim-
023            inates the need for maintaining separate checkpoints for each variant, as all mod-
024            els share a common set of checkpoints, varying only in model depth. This ef-
025            ficient strategy significantly reduces storage overhead and enhances adaptability.
026            To tackle the unavailability of large-scale dataset required in pre-training, we cu-
027
               rate a high-quality, high-resolution image dataset named Million-IRD, having 2M
               image samples. We validate our DyNet for image denoising, deraining, and dehaz-
028
               ing in all-in-one setting, achieving state-of-the-art results with 31.34% reduction
029
               in GFlops and a 56.75% reduction in parameters compared to baseline models.
030            The source codes and trained models will be publicly released.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   The image restoration (IR) task seeks to improve low-quality input images. Despite several ad-
036   vancements in IR, diverse degradation types and severity levels present in images continue to pose
037   a significant challenge. The majority of existing methods (Tu et al., 2022; Zamir et al., 2022; Wang
038   et al., 2021; Zamir et al., 2021; 2020b; Chen et al., 2022) learn image priors implicitly, requiring
039
      separate network training for different degradation types, levels, and datasets. Further, these meth-
      ods require a prior knowledge of image degradation for effective model selection during testing,
040
      thereby lacking generality to cater diverse degradations.
041
042   All-in-one restoration aims to restore images with an unknown degradation using a single unified
043   model. Recent advancements, such as AirNet (Li et al., 2022) and PromptIR (Potlapalli et al.,
044   2023), have addressed the all-in-one restoration challenge by employing contrastive learning and
045
      implicit visual prompting techniques, respectively. Specifically, the state-of-the-art PromptIR (Pot-
      lapalli et al., 2023) employs an implicit prompting to learn degradation-aware prompts on the de-
046
      coder side, aiming to refine the decoder features. This approach, while intriguing, does not extend
047
      to the refinement of encoder features, which are left unprocessed. Despite the interesting idea of
048   implicit prompting, it poses a considerable challenge for deployment due to its poor computational
049   efficiency with 37M parameters and 243 GFlops to process a single 224×224 sized image. The prac-
050   tical application of such models is therefore challenging due to the high computational requirements,
051   especially in resource-constraint hand-held devices. However, choosing lightweight models invari-
052   ably leads to a trade-off between accuracy and efficiency (Zhang et al., 2020a; Li et al., 2023b). Most
053   of these models aim to reduce parameter counts by strided convolutions and feature channel split-
      ting (Kong et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023b; Kligvasser et al., 2018). Some strategies also operate within

074   To optimize all-in-one IR efficiency without sacrificing performance, in this paper, we propose a
075   novel weights-sharing mechanism. In this scheme, the weights of a network module are shared with
076   its subsequent modules in a series. This approach substantially reduces the number of parameters, re-
077   sulting in a more streamlined network architecture. We deploy the proposed weights-sharing mech-
078
      anism in an encoder-decoder style architecture named Dynamic Network (DyNet). Apart from the
      computational efficiency, the proposed DyNet provides exceptional flexibility as by merely changing
079

080
      switch between its bulkier and lightweight variants. Model variants share the same checkpoints,
081   differing only in depth, eliminating the need for separate checkpoints and saving disk space.
082
083   While DyNet offers great adaptability with orders of speedup, a common challenge with lightweight
084
      networks is the potential compromise on overall accuracy. To counteract this, we show that a
      large-scale pre-training strategy can effectively improve the performance of our lightweight models.
085
      By initializing the network with weights derived from the pre-training, the model benefits from a
086
      strong foundation, which can lead to enhanced performance even with a reduced parameter count.
087   However, large-scale pre-training is computationally intensive and requires significant GPU hours.
088   Therefore, we introduce an efficient and potent dynamic pre-training strategy capable of training
089   both bulkier and lightweight network variants within a single pre-training session, thereby achieving
090   significant savings of 50% in GPU hours. Complementing this, we have compiled a comprehensive,
091   high-quality, high-resolution dataset termed Million-IRD, consisting of 2 million image samples.
092   Our main contributions are as follows:
093
             • We propose DyNet, a dynamic family of networks for all-in-one image restoration tasks.
094
               DyNet offers easy switching between its bulkier and light-weight variants. This flexibility
095
               is made possible through a weight-sharing strategy, which is the central idea of our network
096            design, allowing for the efficient reuse of initialized module weights.
097
             • We introduce a Dynamic Pre-training strategy, a new approach that allows the concurrent
098

102

103            overlapping high-resolution patches, each sized 512×512, utilized for the pre-training of
104            variants of the proposed DyNet.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Image restoration (IR) seeks to restore images from their degraded low-quality versions, with the
111   restoration process varying considerably across different tasks. Research in IR has predominantly
112   concentrated on addressing single degradation challenges (Zamir et al., 2022; Ren et al., 2020; Dong
113   et al., 2020a; Zamir et al., 2020a; Ren et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2017b; Tsai et al., 2022; Nah et al.,
114   2022; Zhang et al., 2020b). Although significant progress has been made in single degradation
115   restoration techniques, research in multi-degradation restoration using a unified model is still rela-
116   tively under-explored. Multi-degradation restoration, however, provides more practical advantages
117   as the information about input degradation type is not required to select the task-specific model and
118   it also enhances model storage efficiency over single-degradation restoration.
119   The critical challenge in multi-task IR is developing a single model capable of addressing various
120   types of degradation and precisely restoring low-quality images. The IPT model (Chen et al., 2021),
121   for instance, relies on prior knowledge of the corruption affecting the input image, utilizing a pre-
122   trained transformer backbone equipped with distinct encoders and decoder heads tailored for multi-
123   task restoration. However, in blind IR, we do not have such prior information about the degradation.
124   In this direction, (Li et al., 2022) propose a unified network for multiple tasks such as denoising,
125
      deraining, and dehazing, employing an image encoder refined through contrastive learning. How-
      ever, it requires a two-stage training process, where the outcome of contrastive learning relies on
126
      selecting positive and negative pairs and the availability of large data samples. Further, motivated
127
      by easy implementation and broad applicability of prompts, PromptIR (Potlapalli et al., 2023) intro-
128   duces a concept where degradation-aware prompts are learned implicitly within an encoder-decoder
129   architecture for all-in-one IR. PromptIR uses implicit prompting to refine decoder features but does
130   not extend this refinement to the encoder features, leaving them unprocessed.
131
      Although the above-discussed methods tackle multi-task IR, they suffer from low efficiency due to
132
      a large number of parameters and substantial GFlops consumption. Consequently, deploying these
133
      models in environments where efficiency is crucial factor becomes difficult due to their demanding
134   computational needs. However, opting for lightweight models introduces an inevitable accuracy-
135   efficiency trade-off (Zhang et al., 2020a). The primary cause is that existing methods (Kong et al.,
136   2022; Li et al., 2023b; Kligvasser et al., 2018) focus on reducing parameter counts through tech-
137   niques like strided convolutions and feature channel splitting. Further, few approaches work in
138   frequency domain to reduce the computational burden associated with attention mechanisms (Zhou
139   et al., 2023) or non-local operations (Guo et al., 2021). Furthermore, methods like (Cui & Knoll,
140   2023; Xie et al., 2021) partition the feature space for efficient processing, while (Zhao et al., 2023;
141   Cui & Knoll, 2023) split the attention across dimensions to improve the computational efficiency.
142
      In contrast, our approach focuses on an efficient and scalable all-in-one image restoration model in-
      corporating a weights-sharing mechanism. We demonstrate that strategic fundamental adjustments
143
      to the network architecture result in substantial performance improvements.

146   3     P ROPOSED A PPROACH
147
148   Existing SoTA all-in-one image restoration (IR) approaches offer a high computational footprint and
149   do not provide the flexibility to change model complexity ”on-the-go” during deployment. Towards
150   more accurate, but lightweight and flexible architecture design for all-in-one IR, this work proposes
151
      a Dynamic Network Architecture (Sec. 3.1) and a Dynamic Pretraining Strategy (Sec. 3.2).
152
153   3.1   DYNAMIC N ETWORK (DY N ET ) A RCHITECTURE
154
      Our network architecture is based on a novel weight-sharing mechanism for IR models. This mech-
155
      anism allows the network module’s weights to be reused across subsequent modules in sequence,
156   thereby significantly reducing the total number of parameters and leading to a more efficient network
157   structure. We implement this weight-sharing approach within an encoder-decoder style architecture,
158   which we term the Dynamic Network (DyNet). Within DyNet, at each encoder-decoder level, mod-
159   ule weights are shared across a pre-specified number of subsequent modules. This not only enhances
160   computational efficiency but also grants remarkable flexibility to the architecture. By simply altering
161   the frequency of weight sharing, users can easily modify the network’s depth, seamlessly transition-
      ing between bulkier or lightweight variants of DyNet.

249   3.2   DYNAMIC P RE - TRAINING S TRATEGY
250
251   In recent times, large-scale pre-training has become a key strategy to improve given network per-
252
      formance. Initializing the network with pre-trained weights provides a robust foundation, boosting
      performance even with fewer parameters. However, this strategy is resource-intensive, demanding
253
      considerable computational power and GPU hours. Therefore, we propose a dynamic pre-training
254
      strategy that enables simultaneous training of multiple network variants with shared module weights
255   but varying depths. This allows for concurrent training of diverse models tailored to different com-
256   putational needs and task complexities, all leveraging a shared architecture. Through the proposed
257   dynamic pre-training strategy, we train both DyNet-L and DyNet-S variants of our DyNet concur-
258   rently in a single training session, achieving a 50% reduction in GPU hours.
259   DyNet-L and DyNet-S utilize the same underlying weights for transformer blocks, differing only
260   in the reuse frequency of these blocks at each encoder-decoder level. Thus, during training it-
261   erations, we randomly alternate between DyNet-L and DyNet-S ensuring the optimization of the
262   shared underlying weights as shown in Fig. 3. Moreover, we improve the generalization capability
263
      of our DyNet variants by adopting an input masking strategy akin to that proposed in masked auto-
      encoders (He et al., 2022). We randomly mask portions of an image and train the DyNet variants to
264
      reconstruct masked regions in a self-supervised fashion. The training dataset is described below.

      5.1   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
336
337   Dynamic Pre-training. For robust weights initialization, we conduct a dynamic pre-training for
338   two variants of our DyNet, i.e., DyNet-L and DyNet-S. Both variants share the same underline
339   weights but differ in their transformer block reuse frequency at each encoder-decoder level. For the
340   encoder-decoder levels 1 to 4, we set transformer block weights reuse frequencies of [4,6,6,8] for
341   DyNet-L and [2,3,3,4] for DyNet-S. We use all ∼8M patches of size 5122 from our Million-IRD
342   dataset for the dynamic pre-training. We randomly crop a 1282 region from each patch, employing a
343   batch size of 32. Each of these cropped patches undergoes a random augmentation to 50% of its area
344
      using either JPEG compression, Gaussian noise, or random noise with varying levels. Furthermore,
      we mask 30% of the patch, leaving the remaining portion unaltered. In total, 80% of the input
345
      patch undergoes modification, either through degradation or masking. For weight optimization, we
346
      use the L1 loss and Adam optimizer with parameters β1 = 0.9 and β2 = 0.999, setting the learning
347   rate to 1e-4 for 1 million iterations. Using the setup described above, we simultaneously pre-train
348   both DyNet-L and DyNet-S. At any given iteration, we randomly switch between the two variants.
349   Intriguingly, with each iteration, the shared underlying weights are optimized as shown in Fig. 3.
350   As a result, by the end of this single pre-training session, we get DyNet-L and DyNet-S sharing
351   the same trained underlying weights but differ in network depth, making them suitable for various
352   challenges, including robustness and efficiency.
353
354   Datasets. For the all-in-one task implementations, we adopt the training protocol from PromptIR
355
      and concurrently fine-tune the pre-trained DyNet-S and DyNet-L in a single session. Following
      PromptIR, we prepare datasets for various restoration tasks. Specifically, for image denoising, we
356
      combine the BSD400 (Arbelaez et al., 2011) (400 training images) and WED (Ma et al., 2016)
357
      (4,744 images) datasets, adding Gaussian noise at levels σ ∈ [15, 25, 50] to create noisy images.
358   Testing is conducted on the BSD68 (Martin et al., 2001) and Urban100 (Huang et al., 2015) datasets.
359   For deraining, we use Rain100L (Yang et al., 2020) with 200 training and 100 testing clean-rainy
360   image pairs. Dehazing employs the SOTS (Li et al., 2018) dataset, featuring 72,135 training and 500
361   testing images. To build a unified model for all tasks, we merge the datasets and deploy a dynamic
362   finetunning of the pre-trained DyNet for 120 epochs, then evaluate it across multiple tasks. The
363   resulting flexible DyNet allows ”on-the-go” adjustments to model depth, switching between DyNet-
364   L and DyNet-S without needing separate model weights. Both versions share the same underlying
365   weights, differing only in depth. For individual tasks, the pre-trained DyNet-L is fine-tuned for 120
366
      epochs on the corresponding task-specific training set.

530
531
      This paper introduces a novel weight-sharing mechanism within the Dynamic Network (DyNet) for
532
      efficient all-in-one image restoration tasks, significantly improving computational efficiency with
533
      a performance boost. By adjusting module weight-reuse frequency, DyNet allows for seamless
534   alternation between bulkier and lightweight models. The proposed dynamic pre-training strategy
535   simultaneously trains bulkier and lightweight models in a single training session. Thus saving 50%
536   GPU hours compared to traditional training strategy. The ablation study shows that the accuracy of
537   both bulky and lightweight models significantly boosts with the proposed dynamic large-scale pre-
538   training on our Million-IRD dataset. Overall, our DyNet significantly improves the all-in-one image
539   restoration performance with a 31.34% reduction in GFlops and a 56.75% reduction in parameters
      compared to the baseline models.

727   B     T RANSFORMER B LOCK D ETAILS
728
729   As described in Section 3.1 of the main manuscript, here, we have discussed the transformer block
730   utilized within our DyNet network architecture, detailing its sub-modules multi deconv head trans-
731   posed attention (MDTA) and gated deconv feed-forward network (GDFN). Initially, input features,
732   represented as R ∈ Hl × Wl × Cl , are processed through the MDTA module. Within this, Layer
733   normalization is used to normalize the input features. This is followed by the application of 1 × 1
734   convolutions and then 3 × 3 depth-wise convolutions, which serve to transform the features into
735   Query (Q), Key (K), and Value (V) tensors. A key feature of the MDTA module is its focus on
736   calculating attention across the channel dimensions, instead of the spatial dimensions, which sig-
737
      nificantly reduces computational demands. For channel-wise attention, the Q and K tensors are
      reshaped from Hl × Wl × Cl to Hl Wl × Cl and Cl × Hl Wl dimensions, respectively. This reshaping
738
      facilitates the computation of the dot product and leads to a transposed attention map of Cl × Cl
739
      dimensions. This process incorporates bias-free convolutions and executes attention computation
740   simultaneously across multiple heads. Following the MDTA Module, the features undergo further
741   processing in the GDFN module. Within this module, the input features are initially expanded by a
742   factor of γ through the use of 1 × 1 convolutions. Subsequently, these expanded features are pro-
743   cessed with 3 × 3 convolutions. This procedure is executed along two parallel pathways. The output
744   from one of these pathways is subjected to activation through the GeLU non-linear function. The ac-
745   tivated feature map is then merged with the output from the alternative pathway via an element-wise
746   multiplication.
