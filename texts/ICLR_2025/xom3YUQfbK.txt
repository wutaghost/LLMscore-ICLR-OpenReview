                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               In the current landscape of machine learning, we face a “model lake” phenomenon:
010
               a proliferation of deployed models often lacking adequate documentation. This
011
               presents significant challenges for model users attempting to navigate, differentiate,
012            and select appropriate models for their needs. To address the issue of differentiation,
013            we introduce Model Manager, a framework designed to facilitate easy comparison
014            among existing models. Our approach leverages a large language model (LLM) to
015            generate verbalizations of two models’ differences by sampling from two models.
016            We use a novel protocol that makes it possible to quantify the informativeness
017            of the verbalizations. We also assemble a suite with a diverse set of commonly-
018            used models: Logistic Regression, Decision Trees, and K-Nearest Neighbors. We
019            additionally performed ablation studies on crucial design decisions of the Model
020
               Managers. Our analysis yields pronounced results. For a pair of logistic regression
               models with a 20-25% performance difference on the blood dataset, the Model
021
               Manager effectively verbalizes their variations with up to 80% accuracy. The
022
               Model Manager framework opens up new research avenues for improving the
023            transparency and comparability of machine learning models in a post-hoc manner.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   The rapid increase in the number of machine learning models across various domains has led
030   to the saturation of these models, many of which are poorly documented and lack standardized
031
      evaluation metrics. This abundance creates a "model lake" (Pal et al., 2024), a vast and complex
      landscape where navigating and selecting models for specific tasks is increasingly challenging since
032
      it’s often a struggle to discern the strengths and weaknesses of these models. Several efforts have
033
      been made to improve model management and documentation. One example is ModelDB (Vartak
034   et al., 2016), which serves as a versioning system that tracks models’ metadata across successive
035   iterations (such as model configurations, training datasets, and evaluation metrics). ModelDB’s
036   primary focus is on ensuring reproducibility and traceability of models over time, allowing users
037   to track changes and reproduce past experiments. Similarly, Model Cards (Mitchell et al., 2019)
038   and Data Cards (Pushkarna et al., 2022), along with recent work on their automated generation
039   (Liu et al., 2024), offer valuable documentation on data characteristics, model architectures, and
040   training processes. While these methods provide critical insights into individual models and datasets,
041   they do not explicitly dive into verbalizing the differences in model predictions across the feature
042
      space. Addressing these limitations and providing interpretable verbalizations is essential for enabling
      more informed decisions when selecting or developing new and effective models. Yet, research
043
      aimed at systematically differentiating models remains sparse, leaving room for innovation in model
044
      transparency and comparison techniques.
045
046   Recently, Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown exceptional capabilities over a diverse range
047   of tasks (Hendy et al., 2023; Brown et al., 2020). Previous work has shown that LLMs can be
048
      leveraged to explain model behavior (Kroeger et al., 2023) and to develop explanation methods for
      other modules (Singh et al., 2023). These advancements motivate us to build a "Model Manager"
049
      framework that leverages LLMs to verbalize the model differences.
050
051   The Model Manager framework is designed to compare two models trained on the same dataset by
052   capturing and verbalizing their differences. It does so by serializing a representative sample of input
053   instances (from the dataset) and the corresponding model outputs in a JSON format. The serialization,
      along with a task description, is passed to the LLM through a zero-shot-based prompt. The LLM then


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025



054
      analyzes the patterns from the serialization, captures the inconsistencies in the predictions between
055
      the two models, and summarizes them in human-understandable texts.
056
057   The Model Manager framework is flexible. Since the framework primarily relies on comparing
058   input-output samples, it can be used with various model types and datasets. Additionally, the Model
059
      Manager is extensible. The framework allows the user to incorporate model-specific information, for
      example, textual descriptions of the structures of decision trees, which can improve the informative-
060
      ness of the verbalization — we present the effects via ablation studies in Section 6.
061
062   To evaluate the verbalization of Model Manager, we set up a novel protocol that is inspired by the
063   evaluation of natural language explanations (Kopf et al., 2024; Singh et al., 2023). Given the inputs,
064   the first model’s outputs, and the verbalization, we use an external LLM to infer the second model’s
065
      output. The accuracy of the inference is used to quantify the quality of the verbalization.
066   We test and compare the Model Managers utilizing state-of-the-art LLMs through a series of exper-
067   iments across different datasets, and model types (Logistic Regression, Decision Tree, K-Nearest
068   Neighbor). Our investigation reveals the following key findings:
069
070          • The framework can effectively verbalize differences between model-based learning algo-
071            rithms.
072          • Providing access to models’ internals (e.g., learned parameters) leads to more accurate
073            verbalizations.
074
075          • Obfuscating model-type information from our framework has no statistically significant
076            effect on its performance.
077
078
      We demonstrate that our work provides a valuable starting point for future directions in explainable ar-
      tificial intelligence (XAI) where LLMs can be used to manage models and enhance their transparency
079
      and comparability in a post-hoc manner.

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
083
084
085   Neuron-Level Semantics Research into the semantics of individual DNN components, particularly
086   neurons, has evolved significantly. Early investigations, such as those by Mu and Andreas (2020),
087
      focused on identifying compositional logical concepts within neurons. Building on this, Hernandez
      et al. (2022) developed techniques to map textual descriptions to neurons by optimizing pointwise mu-
088
      tual information. More recent approaches have incorporated external models to enhance explanations
089
      of neuron functions. For instance, Bills et al. (2023) conducted a proof-of-concept study using an
090   external large language model (LLM), such as GPT-4, to articulate neuron functionalities. However,
091   the perfection of these methods remains elusive, as noted by Huang et al. (2023). Evaluating the
092   effectiveness of these explanations is currently a vibrant area of inquiry, with ongoing studies like
093   those by Kopf et al. (2024) and Mondal et al. (2024).
094
095
      Model-Level Explanations Beyond individual neurons, the field is extending towards automated
096   explanation methods for broader model components. Singh et al. (2023) approaches models as opaque
097   "text modules," providing explanations without internal visibility. Our methodology diverges by
098   incorporating more detailed information about the models, which we believe enhances the accuracy
099   of explanations, a concept supported by Ajwani et al. (2024). Notably, our work aligns with Kroeger
100   et al. (2023), who employ in-context learning for prompting LLMs to explain machine learning
101   models. Our strategy differs as we emphasize zero-shot instructions.
102
103   Interpretable Feature Extraction Concurrently, there is a shift towards extracting interpretable
104   features directly from neurons. Techniques such as learning sparse auto-encoders have been explored
105   by Bricken et al. (2023). A significant advancement by Templeton et al. (2024) scales up these efforts
106   to newer architectures like Claude 3.5 Sonnet (Anthropic, 2024). Unlike previous methods, we do
107   not assume a predefined set of features for explanation, opting instead to use the LLM as a dynamic
      "model manager" to generate explanatory content.

125   Verbalization Techniques Another prevalent approach is the use of the language model head of
126   DNNs as a "logit lens," as demonstrated by nostalgebraist (2020). This method has been further
127   developed and diversified by researchers like Pal et al. (2023) and Belrose et al. (2023). The
128   PatchScope framework by Ghandeharioun et al. (2024) extends these techniques, incorporating
129   methods that modify the representations themselves. In our research, rather than utilizing the language
130   model head directly, we employ an external LLM to serve as the "model manager," providing a novel
131   means of interpreting and explaining model behaviors.
132
133   LLM Distinction Several approaches have emerged to differentiate between LLMs. One method,
134   LLM Fingerprinting, introduces a cryptographically inspired technique called Chain and Hash
135   (Russinovich and Salem, 2024). This approach generates a set of unique questions (the "fingerprints")
136   and corresponding answers, which are hashed to prevent false claims of ownership over models.
137   Complementing this, another method (Richardeau et al., 2024) proposes using a sequence of binary
138   questions, inspired by the 20 Questions game, to determine if two LLMs are identical. Unlike
139
      fingerprinting or binary distinction, our framework focuses on the behavioral aspect of models.
      Moreover, our current work does not aim to compare LLMs themselves; rather, we leverage LLMs as
140
      a tool to compare and verbalize the differences among other models.

      If a verbalization v accurately captures the differences between two models, it should facilitate an

                                              (1)

224                                                         (2)

            1. Mismatch Accuracy (Accmismatch ): It evaluates the cases where the outputs of M1 and M2

               disagree, i.e, Imismatch = {i | ŷeval,i ̸= ŷeval,i }. For these cases, the accuracy is computed as

270
      In the paradigm of model-based learning algorithms, we evaluate the efficacy of LLMs in verbalizing
271
      differences between two popular learning algorithms: (i) Logistic Regression (LR) and (ii) Decision
272   Tree ( DT). We specifically chose these algorithms because they are widely used, interpretable and
273   serve as good baselines in the development of LLM-based model management frameworks. To
274   demonstrate the significant challenge of evaluating instance-based learning algorithms, we quantita-
275   tively demonstrate the difficulties faced by LLMs in verbalizing the difference for (iii) the K-Nearest
276   Neighbors (KNNs) algorithm.
277
      To streamline our study, we stratified the experiments based on the percentage of differing outputs
278   between each pair of models, with three levels: (i) Level 1 (15% − 20%), (ii) Level 2 (20% − 25%),
279   and (iii) Level 3 (25% − 30%). To measure the differences between models, we computed the
280   percentage of differing outputs on the verb split. For each of these levels, we generated multiple
281   pairs of models for all three model types.
282
      To generate pairs of LR models with a specific percentage of differing outputs, we first train a
283
      base model using RandomizedSearchCV. Then we create multiple variations by adding randomly
284   generated noise to the base model’s coefficients. The noise is controlled by a modification factor m
285   (noise ∼ N (0, mβ)), where β represents the vector of the base model’s coefficients. We carefully
286   adjust m until the percentage of differing outputs between the base model and the modified model
287   reaches the desired level. Rather than limiting our comparisons to the base model obtained from
288   RandomizedSearchCV, we also compare the modified models against each other, identifying a diverse
289   collection of model pairs.
290   We follow a similar process for Decision Trees and KNNs, with the details provided in Appendix A.
291   For each model type and across all levels of output differences, we generate multiple base models
292   and corresponding modified models.
293
294
      Verbalizers: We include three state-of-the-art LLMs as LLMverb : Claude 3.5 Sonnet (Anthropic,
295
      2024), Gemini 1.5 Pro (Google, 2024), and GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024). For each of these LLMs, we set
296   the temperature as T = 0.1 in their respective API calls.
297
298
299
      Evaluator: We let LLMeval be the same model as LLMverb , to avoid the bias introduced when
      LLMs process the outputs of the other language models.

301
302   Ablation Study on the effects of including model’s internals: The access to the internals, com-
303   pared to solely relying on the representative samples, may help LLMverb understand (and therefore
304   verbalize) how the models make decisions. We hypothesize that providing such model-specific infor-
305
      mation enables LLMs to generate more accurate and faithful verbalizations. We examine the effect of
      incorporating the models’ internals on the performance of our framework in generating verbalizations.
306
      By internals, we refer to textual descriptions of a model’s learned structure or information about
307
      its inner workings. Different model types have different key pieces of information that they rely
308   upon to make predictions. For Logistic Regression, this entails providing the framework with the
309   learned coefficients. For Decision Trees, we provide a textual representation of the learned structure,
310   focusing on the decision rules and splits. Lastly, for completeness, we include KNNs, incorporating
311   the number of neighbors (K) and the distance metric, as these parameters define their behavior.
312
313
      Ablation Study on the effects of excluding model-type: The model-type is the name of the type
314   of the model (e.g., Logistic Regression, Decision Tree, or KNN). We study the impact of excluding
315   the model type when comparing models. We aim to evaluate if our framework can generate accurate
316   verbalizations based purely on the observed behavior, rather than the names.
317
      It should be noted that all our ablation studies are conducted using stratification Level 2 (20-25%) as
318
      the default configuration. Specific details about prompts can be found Appendix B.

438   KNNs appear more challenging for our framework due to their instance-based learning nature. Given
439   their reliance on specific local data points for predictions, we anticipate that our Model Manager
440
      struggles to effectively verbalize instance-based learning algorithms, and our observations support
      the anticipation. For Level 2 (20% − 25%) models on the Blood dataset, the Accmismatch scores were
441
      lower than 0.7, with Gemini lower than 0.6. On the Car and Diabetes datasets, the performance
442
      declines further, with Claude and GPT-4o failing to surpass 0.50 for Accmismatch . We include the
443   complete details of the KNN experiments in Table 6.

501   7   D ISCUSSION
502
503   Our results show promising trends when verbalizing the differences of parametric models (LR and
504   DT). The non-parametric KNN models, on the other hand, introduce more challenges, as indicated
505   by the lower Accmatch and Accmismatch . On one hand, these indicate that future Model Managers on
506   non-parametric models need to consider factors that describe the dataset. On the other hand, these
507   indicate that the Model Managers can be extended to verbalizing the differences between Deep Neural
508   Networks, especially incorporating approaches that describe the models’ internals (e.g., mechanistic
509
      interpretability). Considering the complex nature of DNNs, the developers for Model Managers on
      DNNs will have to consider a lot of intricate details.
510
511   The plug-and-compare flexibility of Model Manager allows potential upgrades to the Manager. When
512   newer, higher-capability LMs are developed, we can replace the LM in Model Manager with the
513   next-generation ones. The same flexibility applies to the prompting techniques and the expected tasks
514   (for example, comparing across more than two models).
515   A good resource manager does not just observe. Beyond verbalization, a fully-fledged Model Manager
516   should be able to automatically inspect the individual models, question the potential weaknesses, and
517   potentially recommend improvement methods, including but not limited to model merging, model
518   safeguarding, and model debiasing. A lot of future work is needed toward this goal, which we believe
519   deserves more attention from the field.
520
521
522   8   C ONCLUSION
523
524   In conclusion, the Model Manager framework establishes a foundational step toward automatic
525   management of machine learning models. The Model Manager verbalizes the difference between two
526   models. While it excels in identifying differences between parametric models, challenges remain with
527   non-parametric models like KNNs, highlighting the need for tailored strategies that accommodate the
528
      unique characteristics of various model types. This research sets the stage for future research in model
      management tools that can dynamically adapt to the evolving landscape of ML technologies. As we
529
      look to the future, integrating more sophisticated language models and expanding the framework’s
530
      capabilities will be essential in advancing the field towards more transparent, accountable, and
531   effective AI systems.

      A.1   A DDITIONAL E XPERIMENTAL D ETAILS
629
630   DT generation: For DTs, similar to the LR models, we first train a base model using
631   RandomizedSearchCV. To generate a modified DT, we introduce two levels of variation. First,
632   we randomly sample new hyperparameters from the defined space. This ensures that the modified
633   tree has a structure different from the base model. Second, we add noise to the splitting thresholds
634   of the nodes. The noise is normally distributed and controlled by a modification factor m (noise
635   ∼ N (0, m)) and is scaled to the level of thresholds (noise = τ ∗ noise ). We carefully adjust m until
636   the percentage of differing outputs between the base model and the modified model reaches the desired
637   level. Rather than limiting our comparison to the base model obtained from RandomizedSearchCV,
638
      we also compare the modified models against each other, identifying a diverse collection of pairs.
639
640   KNNs generation : In the case of KNNs, we first train a base model using RandomizedSearchCV.
641   To generate modified versions, we randomly sample new hyperparameters and compare the predictions
642   of the base model with each modified model, calculating the percentage of differing outputs until it
643   reaches the desired level. Additionally, we compare the modified models against each other to obtain
644
      a diverse collection of pairs.
