011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent transformer models have achieved state-of-the-art performance for visual
014            tasks involving high-dimensional data like 3D volumetric medical image segmen-
015
               tation. Hierarchical transformers (e.g. Swin Transformers) circumvent the compu-
               tational challenge of self-attention in high-dimensional data through shifted win-
016
               dow approach to learn token relations within progressively overlapping local re-
017
               gions, thus expanding receptive field across layers while limiting token attention
018            span in each layer within predefined windows. In this work, we introduce a novel
019            learning paradigm that captures token relations through progressive summariza-
020            tion of features. We leverage the compaction capability of discrete wavelet trans-
021            form (DWT) on high-dimensional features and learn token relation in multi-scale
022            approximation coefficients obtained from DWT. This approach enables efficient
023            representation of fine-grained local to coarse global contexts within each layer
024            of the network. Furthermore, computing self-attention on the DWT transformed
025            features significantly reduces the computational complexity, effectively address-
026
               ing the challenges posed by high-dimensional data in vision transformers. Our
               network competes favorably with current SOTA transformers (e.g. SwinUNETR)
027
               using three challenging public datasets on volumetric medical imaging: (1) MIC-
028
               CAI Challenge 2021 FLARE, (2) MICCAI Challenge 2019 KiTS, and (3) MIC-
029            CAI Challenge 2022 AMOS. Our DWT-based transformer termed as WaveFormer
030            consistently outperforms Swin-UNETR with improvement from 0.929 to 0.938
031            Dice (FLARE2021) and 0.880 to 0.900 Dice (AMOS2022). The source code and
032            pretrained models will be made available in the full paper submission.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   The Vision Transformer (ViT) architecture Dosovitskiy et al. (2020) has proven to be highly ef-
038   fective for visual recognition tasks due to its ability to model long-range relationships across non-
039
      overlapping image patches or tokens. However, ViT comes with significant computational costs,
      as its self-attention mechanism scales quadratically with input size. In addition, ViT generates
040
      low-resolution single-scale output features that are unsuitable for downstream tasks that require
041
      fine-grained analysis of high-resolution feature maps and global context understanding (Beal et al.,
042   2020; Fang et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2021; Zheng et al., 2021). These challenges are especially sig-
043   nificant for high-dimensional inputs such as 3D volumetric scans. Hierarchical backbones Wang
044   et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2021) offer a solution by reducing computational complexity through local
045   window attention applied to progressively smaller feature maps. While this alleviates some of the
046   computational burdens, it introduces a new limitation. The effective receptive field (ERF) becomes
047   constrained within each layer, even after techniques like neighborhood pooling Yang et al. (2021)
048   and shifted windows Liu et al. (2021) are applied. These methods attempt to expand the recep-
049   tive field in subsequent layers by gradually exposing tokens to previously unseen tokens, but the
050
      restriction within the individual layers remain.
051   Recent studies demonstrate that self-attention mechanisms in ViTs exhibit characteristics analo-
052   gous to a low-pass filter, as in, low-frequency components are crucial for the performance of ViT
053   models Bai et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2022b); Park & Kim (2022); Rao et al. (2021); Wang et al.
      (2020a; 2022a). In this work, we propose that it is feasible to achieve a multi-resolution feature

085   Specifically, we propose a novel wavelet-based transformer architecture that decomposes features
086   using DWT and computes windowed attention on the low-frequency components. Different level of
087
      decomposition enables attention at different resolutions, which allows the model to capture and ag-
      gregate essential local and global context at each stage. By prioritizing these compact low-frequency
088
      approximations, our method reduces the computational burden associated with high-resolution im-
089
      age analysis while preserving essential multi-resolution context. We validate our approach in 3D
090   volumetric segmentation benchmarks, including FLARE Ma et al. (2022), AMOS Ji et al. (2022)
091   and KiTS Heller et al. (2020b), where our model achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) mean dice score.
092   Additionally, our model demonstrates competitive results on classification with ImageNet-1k Deng
093   et al. (2009), highlighting its generalization ability across medical and natural image recognition
094   tasks. Our contributions can be summarized as below:
095
096   • We introduce WaveFormer, a novel transformer architecture that processes low-frequency approx-
097     imations of spatial images through DWT. This approach enables multi-resolution contextualiza-
098     tion of visual elements, resulting in a significant expansion of the effective receptive field while
099     maintaining superior computational efficiency compared to similarly sized models.
100
101   • Our model capitalizes on the high energy density present in low-frequency components, optimiz-
102     ing representation learning from natural and volumetric images. This novel integration of the
103     discrete wavelet transform opens new pathways for efficiently processing large-scale visual data.
104
105   • Our extensive experiments demonstrate that WaveFormer surpasses state-of-the-art performance
106     on 3D volumetric segmentation tasks, achieving superior mean dice scores on the FLARE, AMOS
107     and KiTS test sets. Additionally, our model achieves competitive accuracy on ImageNet-1k for
        natural image classification, all while reducing FLOP counts compared to other models in its class.

      2.1   R ECEPTIVE F IELD - C OMPUTATION S PECTRUM
111
112   Vanilla ViT Dosovitskiy et al. (2020) enjoys global receptive field by processing an entire sample as
113   patchified input tokens, incurring massive computational burden (O(N 2 )). In contrast, Stand-alone
114   Self-attention Ramachandran et al. (2019) reduces computation by attending within non-overlapping
115   local windows, limiting the receptive field to the window size. Various approaches aim to balance
116   the trade-off between computational cost and receptive field in transformer models. SWIN Liu et al.
117   (2021) uses shifting windows between consecutive self-attention blocks for cross-window interac-
118   tion, which adds complexity and limits global context. LinFormer Wang et al. (2020b) reduces com-
119
      putation via token projection, sacrificing fine-grained detail. Performer Choromanski et al. (2020)
      approximates attention with kernel methods, reducing computation to linear but yielding unreliable
120
      performance across tasks and modalities. Reformer Kitaev et al. (2020) hashes queries into buckets,
121
      risking sub-optimal grouping. Axial Attention Ho et al. (2019) processes 2D attention as sequential
122   1D attention, limiting global context capture. Longformer Zhang et al. (2021) and RegionViT Chen
123   et al. (2021) focus on regional tokens but add complexity and limit global efficiency. Biformer Zhu
124   et al. (2023) adapts to multi-scale contexts but has inconsistent performance. Focal AttentionYang
125   et al. (2021) combines fine and coarse features but struggles with scalability. Dilated Attention Has-
126   sani & Shi (2022) takes adaptively spaced tokens which allow a larger receptive field at a low cost,
127   but the resulting sparsity affects the attention granularity.
128
129   2.2   L EARNING IN F REQUENCY D OMAIN
130
131   Learning in the frequency domain has been explored in various tasks like image deblurring and
132   image inpainting, often by learning directly from the frequency components, or as an assistive rep-
133
      resentation alongside the spatial domain Xu et al. (2020); Wang & Sun (2022); Gueguen et al.
      (2018); Bai et al. (2022); Zou et al. (2021); Suvorov et al. (2022); Ehrlich & Davis (2019). Some
134
      works have leveraged frequency for model compression Kong et al. (2023) and channel description
135
      Qin et al. (2021). Based on energy under low-frequency coefficients, Wang et al. (2022b) performs
136   channel and token pruning to compress models. Yao et al. (2022) uses selective coefficient tokens
137   for attention. However, such pruning or selective token shortlisting may cause information imbal-
138   ance and redundancy. Additionally, the feature stacking and restoration in Yao et al. (2022) require
139   extra layers, diminishing the computational benefits of the wavelet transform.
140
      Compared to these works, our models’ strength comes from integrating wavelet into a multi-path
141
      hierarchical architecture. Each branch in our attention block independently attends to features at
142   different scales, capturing a broader range of patterns and scale invariance. Aggregating these
143   branches helps contextualize multi-resolution object properties. Our in-depth analysis shows that
144   such a multi-path network allows each path to develop distinct modeling abilities due to their differ-
145   ences in ERF.

      3     WAVE F ORMER : I NTUITION
149
      WaveFormer introduces a novel approach to hierarchical transformers by combining two key intu-
150
      itions: learning on compact representations and achieving local-to-global receptive field coverage.
151
      The first notion leverages the properties of the Discrete Wavelet Transform (DWT) and Parseval’s
152   theorem to establish the significance of low-frequency approximations in the context of learning.
153   This enables reduced computation while preserving essential global features. The second notion
154   consolidates extraction of multi-resolution token relations by using multi-level DWT, which seam-
155   lessly models local and global dependencies. Together, these two intuitions form the foundation of
156   our WaveFormer architecture, enabling efficient yet powerful token relation modeling.
157

      In conclusion, DWT offers three primary features that motivates our architecture:
206
207
      • Energy Compaction: As feature energy in transformer networks is mostly aligned towards the
208
        low-frequency spectrum, DWT enables the concentration of the signal energy into a few approxi-
209     mation coefficients at the coarsest scale (follows from Parseval’s Theorem).
210
211   • Computational Efficiency: By operating on wavelet coefficients at coarser scales, we reduce the
212
        computational burden without significant loss of important information.
213   • Multi-Resolution Representation: DWT provides a method for hierarchical decomposition of
214     data. In spatial context, shallower level of decomposition represents local details as deeper levels
215     tend to represent global structures. This enables another speculation for feature extraction at
        multiple scales, as discussed in Section 3.2 in more detail.

248   3.2      L OCAL - TO -G LOBAL R ECEPTIVE F IELD C OVERAGE
249
250   As mentioned above, our encoder network computes token relations on the compact approxima-
251   tion coefficients obtained from the Discrete Wavelet Transform (DWT). Figure 4a illustrates DWT
252   transformation on the input feature x, which is decomposed into multi-level low-frequency approx-
253   imations. At the coarsest level, global attention is applied, enabling the capturing of holistic rela-
254
      tionships among tokens. On other levels, the token relationship is computed locally using fixed-size
      window attention, where the window has the same shape as the spatial dimension of the coarsest-
255
      level feature. In this way, the attention mechanism efficiently captures multi-granular relationships
256
      spanning from local to global receptive fields as depicted in Figure 4a. This surpasses the limita-
257   tion of window attention and introduces a mechanism that learns token relation through multi-level
258   summarization of the input feature with low computational cost. Such a straightforward and effec-
259   tive approach to capturing token relationships at multiple resolutions has inspired us to develop a
260   wavelet-decomposition-based transformer network.
261
      In the context of our WaveFormer architecture, we apply DWT to the input feature map x to ob-
262
      tain a set of approximation coefficients Cj at multiple scales. Using these low-resolution wavelet
263   coefficients Cj , we capture global and local dependencies with reduced computation by applying
264   self-attention on the compact representations, enhancing efficiency without sacrificing accuracy.

      4.2      D ECODER FOR D OWNSTREAM TASK
318
319   For the downstream segmentation task, we follow the similar decoder architecture from Lee et al.
320   (2022); Hatamizadeh et al. (2021) that comprises a ”U-shaped” network overall. Multi-scale out-
321   put from different stages of the network is connected to the corresponding decoder layer via a skip
322   connection. First, the output feature from each stage I(i ∈ 1, 2, 3, 4) is passed through a residual
323   block comprised of two post-normalized 3 × 3 × 3 convolutional layers with instance normaliza-
      tion. This stabilizes further propagation of the feature. Note that the feature from stage 4 is also


                                                                                       6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      passed through a bottleneck residual layer to produce the final encoded feature. The feature is then
325   upsampled with a transpose convolution and concatenated with the previous stage features. The
326   concatenated feature will further be passed through a residual block to output the final feature for
327   that decoder layer (dark gray in Figure 4a). For final segmentation, the residual feature from in-
328   put patch is concatenated with the upsampled feature from the previous decoder layer and passed
329   through a residual block with 1 × 1 × 1 convolutional layer with a softmax activation to predict the
330   segmentation probabilities.

343
344   Following Lee et al. (2022), the model is evaluated in two scenarios for volumetric medical image
345
      segmentation: 1) directly supervised training on FLARE2021 and KITS2019 datasets, and 2) trans-
      fer learning with FLARE pre-trained wights on AMOS 2022 dataset. More detailed information
346
      on datasets and splits is provided in Appendix A.3. We performed 5-fold cross-validation on both
347
      FLARE and KITS while using the best fold model trained on FLARE to finetune on AMOS. Train-
348   ing details are provided in Appendix A.4. We evaluate WaveFormer against the current volumetric
349   transformer and ConvNet SOTA approaches for volumetric segmentation in a fully-supervised set-
350   ting. The dice similarity coefficient is used as the evaluation metric.
351   We further train the model on the natural image dataset Imagenet-1k for visual recognition tasks to
352   test the generalization capability of the representation encoded by the model. Training details on
353   Imagenet-1k are provided in Appendix A.5.
354   Furthermore, we performed ablation studies to investigate the effect of different-level wavelet de-
355   composition on the model’s capability to learn different-scale organs.

494   7   D ISCUSSION & F UTURE W ORKS
495
496   In this work, we proposed a frequency-level learning module as a general feature extractor and
497   adapted it into a generic encoder-decoder architecture for volumetric segmentation. Our findings in-
498   dicate that the process of learning from full-resolution feature maps can be effectively approximated
499   by computing multi-resolution token relationships in the frequency domain with fewer computation.
500   Two key factors contribute to WaveFormer’s performance. First, the Discrete Wavelet Transform
501   (DWT) enables selective retention of high-energy, low-frequency coefficients from 3D feature maps,
502   which minimizes redundancy when processing pairwise token relations. Second, the reduction in
503   spatial dimensions achieved by DWT facilitates attention across feature maps at different scales. The
504
      use of self-attention with constant-sized window captures local relationships at various granularities
      while also summarizing global relationships efficiently in a continuous token space.
505
506   In future work, we aim to further investigate optimal configurations for diverse datasets and tasks.
507   This includes exploring the role of high-frequency, low-information density coefficients, which were
508   omitted in the current implementation. Understanding how these high-frequency components con-
509   tribute to the learning process could unlock new avenues for fine-tuning WaveFormer’s architecture,
510
      potentially enhancing its utility across a broader range of vision applications.

512   8   C ONCLUSION
513
514   In this study, we introduced WaveFormer, a transformer-based architecture designed for high-
515   dimensional medical image segmentation. By utilizing a discrete wavelet transform-based self-
516   attention mechanism, WaveFormer efficiently fuses local and global token relations, leading to su-
517   perior segmentation performance on 3D volumetric datasets like FLARE2021 and AMOS2022. Our
518   approach reduces computational overhead while outperforming traditional methods, setting a bench-
519   mark for future research in visual transformers.
