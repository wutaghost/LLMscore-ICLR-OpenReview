010                                               A BSTRACT
011
012
               As large language models (LLMs) are increasingly relied on in AI systems, pre-
               dicting when they make mistakes is crucial. While a great deal of work in the field
013
               uses internal representations to interpret model behavior, these representations are
014            inaccessible when given solely black-box access through an API. In this paper,
015            we extract features of LLMs in a black-box manner by using follow-up prompts
016            and taking the probabilities of different responses as features to train reliable pre-
017            dictors of model behavior. We demonstrate that training a linear model on these
018            low-dimensional features produces reliable and generalizable predictors of model
               performance at the instance level (e.g., if a particular generation correctly answers
019
               a question). Remarkably, these can often outperform white-box linear predictors
020            that operate over a model’s hidden state or the full distribution over its vocabulary.
021            In addition, we demonstrate that these extracted features can be used to evaluate
022            more nuanced aspects of a language model’s state. For instance, they can be used
023            to distinguish between GPT-3.5 and a version of GPT-3.5 affected by an adversarial
024            system prompt that makes its answers often incorrect. Furthermore, they can
025
               reliably distinguish between different model architectures and sizes, enabling the
               detection of misrepresented models provided through an API (e.g., identifying if
026
               GPT-3.5 is supplied instead of GPT-4).

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated strong performance on a wide variety of tasks
032   (Radford et al.), leading to their increased involvement in larger systems. For instance, they are
033   often used to provide supervision (Bai et al., 2022) or as tools in decision-making (Benary et al.,
034
      2023; Sha et al., 2023). Thus, it is crucial to understand and predict their behaviors, especially in
      high-stakes settings. However, as with any deep network, it is difficult to understand the behavior
035
      of such large models (Zhang et al., 2021). For instance, prior work has studied input gradients or
036   saliency maps (Simonyan et al., 2013; Zeiler & Fergus, 2014; Pukdee et al., 2024)) to attempt to
037   understand neural network behavior, but this can fail to reliably describe model behavior (Adebayo
038   et al., 2018; Kindermans et al., 2019; Srinivas & Fleuret, 2020). Other work has studied the ability
039   of transformers to represent certain algorithms (Nanda et al., 2022; Zhong et al., 2024) that may be
040   involved in their predictions.
041   One promising direction in understanding LLMs (or any other multimodal model that understands
042   natural language) is to leverage their ability to interact with human queries. Recent work has demon-
043   strated that a LLM’s hidden state contains low-dimensional representations of model truthfulness
044   or harmfulness (Zou et al., 2023a). Other work studies learning sparse dictionaries and analyzing
      how these networks activate on certain, related input tokens (Bricken et al., 2023). While significant
045
      progress has been made on these fronts, these approaches all require white-box access to these models
046   (i.e., access to the model’s activations or hidden states). However, many of the best-performing LLMs
047   (Achiam et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023) lie beyond closed-source APIs, so these prior attempts to
048   understand model behavior cannot be applied. This raises the question, “How well can we model the
049   LLM’s behavior with only black-box access?”
050   In this paper, we propose to extract useful features in predicting model performance from black-box
051   LLMs by eliciting model responses by querying these LLMs about their outputs. In essence, after
052   receiving a generation or prediction from a LLM, we leverage the LLM’s ability to reason about
053   its own generated answer and meaningfully respond to follow-up questions, such as, “Are you able
      to explain your answer?” Our hypothesis is that the probability distribution over answers to these

093   In addition to predicting LLM performance at the example level, these extracted features are also

098   black-box setting. We also provide evidence that our approach is robust to variations in the system
099   prompt. Finally, we also demonstrate that our approach can be used to reliably distinguish between
100   different model architectures and model sizes; this can be useful in evaluating if cheaper or smaller
101
      models are falsely being provided through these closed-source APIs.

138   input), and we leverage a small amount of labeled data from the downstream task.
139
140   Extracting Features from Neural Networks Many other works have explored approaches to
      extract representations from neural networks (NNs). A related line of work looks to train NNs
141
      (specifically image classifiers) to extract a small set of discrete, interpretable concepts, which can
142   be passed through a linear probe to recover a classifier (Koh et al., 2020). In our case, we leverage
143   the ability of the LLM to understand language and can circumvent this need for training, extracting
144   features in a task-agnostic manner. Prior work has studied how to extract useful representations
145   for downstream tasks (Wang et al., 2023; Springer et al., 2024). Our approach significantly differs
146   in nature from these approaches, as we are looking to extract more compressed, low-dimensional
147
      features that reveal information about model behavior. Perhaps the most related work employs a
      similar strategy of asking questions, specifically to detect instances where a model is being untruthful
148
      (Pacchiardi et al., 2024). Our work significantly generalizes this approach towards the broader task of
149   predicting model behavior and performance.
150
151   Uncertainty Quantification in LLMs Finally, a related notion to our work is assessing the
152   calibration or ability of a language model to represent its own uncertainty (Xiong et al., 2023). Many
153   of the elicitation questions that we ask prompt the model to look at its answer and answer “Yes” or
154   “No”; this is related to the notion of a model’s ability to understand what it knows (Kadavath et al.,
155   2022) or reflect uncertainty in its own decisions. Our work is different, however, as we elicit these
156
      probabilities as a representation from such a model to train a simple, calibrated linear classifier.

196   3.1   E XTRACTING F EATURES BY F OLLOW- UP E LICITATION Q UESTIONS
197
198   To extract our black-box features, we prompt the model with a large number of elicitation ques-
199   tions. We consider a set of questions Q = {q1 , ..., qd } and some autoregressive language
      model, which models some distribution P over sequences of text. We also consider a dataset
200
      D = {(x1 , y1 ), ..., (xn , yn )}, where xi is a sequence of tokens and yi corresponds to a binary label,
201   for example, if the LLM has correctly answered the question xi . We define ai as the greedy response
202   from the LLM, or that ai = arg maxc P (c|xi ). Then, we construct our black-box representation as
203   some vector z = (z1 , ..., zd ), where each zj = P (yes|x ⊕ a ⊕ qj ), where ⊕ denotes concatenation.
204   In other words, dimensions of our representation correspond to the probability of the yes token
205   under the LLM (where the distribution is specified over the yes and no tokens), in response to the
206   question x, the greedy sampled answer a, and the elicitation question qj . The elicitation questions
      are detailed in Appendix L.2, but generally consist of simple self-inquiry questions such as “Do you
207
      think your answer is correct?” or “Are your responses free from bias?” This simple approach allows
208   us to add more information to representations by continuing to generate new follow-up questions. In
209   our paper, we find that working with a set of roughly 50 questions seems to be sufficient for strong
210   performance (see ablations in Section 4.5).
211
      In addition to these probabilities of responses to questions, we also append: (1) pre- and post-
212   confidence scores of the LLM, which are responses to asking the question before and after generating
213   a greedy sample from the model, and (2) the distribution over possible answers for the task, (for
214   open-ended QA tasks, we simply use the log probability of the greedy output). In our experiments
215   with GPT models, we append the sorted top-5 probabilities returned by the API. We train a linear
      predictor β to predict the label y (e.g., whether the model is correct or not) given our feature vector z.

      3.2   C ONSTRUCTING E LICITING P ROMPTS
217
218   To construct this set of eliciting questions Q, we specify a small number of questions that relate to
219   the model’s confidence or belief in its answer. We also use GPT4 to generate a larger number (40)
220   of questions. The questions and prompts used to generate the GPT4-generated questions are given
221   in Appendix L.2. As noted in prior work that uses similar questions for lie detection (Pacchiardi
222
      et al., 2024), a wide variety of questions seems to lead to more useful representations, capturing more
      information from the LLM.
223
224   We note that based on the specific nature of the question, the response (e.g., the probability of
225   responding yes) could define a weak predictor of whether the model is correct or not. This is
226
      reminiscent of the design of weak learners in boosting (Freund & Schapire, 1996) or weak labelers
      in programmatic weak supervision (Ratner et al., 2017; Smith et al., 2024; Sam & Kolter, 2023).
227
      However, to maintain our approach’s generality and to not restrict our approach to only a certain
228   type of elicitation questions, we treat these as abstract features for a linear predictor. We also note
229   that further work could perform discrete optimization over prompts to further improve the extracted
230   representation’s usability, through methods described in (Wen et al., 2024; Zou et al., 2023b; Chao
231   et al., 2023). However, one key appeal of the current approach is that it defines an extremely simple
232   classifier in a task-agnostic fashion. Performing optimization over these questions might lead to
233
      overfitting, and the resulting predictors on the outputs of these prompts require more complex analysis
      in deriving valid generalization bounds.

281   Baselines In our experiments, we compare against a variety of different baselines; two of which are
282   strong baselines that assume access to more information than our approach. These are RepE (Zou
283   et al., 2023a), which extracts the hidden state of the LLM at the last token position in its representation
284
      reading, and Full Logits, which uses the distribution over the LLM’s entire vocabulary. Both of
      these cannot be applied to black-box language models and should be seen as strong comparisons that
285
      assume more information than our approach. For instance, information from the full logits over the
286   complete vocabulary has been shown to reveal hyperparameter information of the LLM (Finlayson
287   et al., 2024). For this baseline for black-box models, we best approximate this with a sparse vector of
288   the top-k probabilities (if that is provided by the API).
289
      We also compare against pre-conf and post-conf scores, which are a univariate feature that corre-
290   sponds to the probability of the “yes” token under the language model to a prompt about the model’s
291   confidence either before (pre-) or after (post-) seeing the greedy (temperature 0) sampled response.
292   This is the same as extracting vanilla confidence scores from LLMs (Xiong et al., 2023). We also
293   compare against using the normalized probability distribution over the potential answer questions
294   (Answer Probs), which is similar to what is proposed in prior work that focuses on in-context
295
      learning (Abbas et al., 2024). These are individual components of our representations, so we highlight
      these comparisons are ablations, to study how much of an increase in performance we obtain by
296
      adding additional elicitation queries and concatenating them together.
297
298   We also compare against a version of semantic uncertainty (Kuhn et al., 2023) on the MCQ tasks,
      which looks to extract a more accurate quantification of uncertainty by grouping together semantically
299
      similar tokens for each potential answer. This baseline does not straightforwardly apply to open-
300   ended QA tasks, and we only present results for semantic uncertainty on the open-source models
301   as we do not have access to all of the GPT model’s token probabilities. We compare with other
302   uncertainty quantification approaches from (Xiong et al., 2023) (and the concatenation of them all) in
303   Appendix C.

      (Kwiatkowski et al., 2019), SQuAD (Rajpurkar et al., 2016), BoolQ (Clark et al., 2019), WinoGrande

348   4.2   D ISTINGUISHING B ETWEEN M ODEL A RCHITECTURES
349
      Next, we consider the setting of distinguishing between different model architectures in a black-box
350
      setting, purely via analyzing their outputs. This has a practical application; when using models given
351   through an API, our approach can be used to reliably detect whether a cheaper, smaller model is
352   being falsely provided through an API, as is the focus of prior work (Chen et al., 2023). Thus, given
353   a few historical known examples from different LLMs, we can develop reliable predictors to detect
354   which models are being provided through APIs. This has been studied by concurrent work (Gao et al.,
355   2024) in the framing of hypothesis testing.
356   We demonstrate that our black-box representations extracted by QueRE can be used to reliably
357   distinguish between different LLMs. We provide an experiment using a linear model to classify
358   which LLM from which each extracted representation was generated in Figure 4. We observe that
359   linear predictors using QueRE can often almost perfectly classify between LLMs of different sizes,
      while all other approaches struggle with this task. This suggests that the distributions learned by
360
      different LLMs behave in distinct ways, even when the same architecture and training objectives are
361   used and the only difference is the model size.

      4.3   D ETECTING A DVERSARIAL /H ARMFUL LLM S
364
365   Lastly, we demonstrate that our approach in extracting black-box representations from language
366   models can reliably distinguish between a clean version of the LLM and one that has been influenced
367   by an adversary. Detecting when models have been adversarially influenced is crucial for maintaining
368   robust and trustworthy AI systems. We provide an experiment where we add an adversarial system
      prompt to instruct GPT-3.5 to answer questions incorrectly. For instance, this system prompt drops
369
      the original performance of GPT-3.5 on BoolQ from 87% to 32% and on DHate from 77% to 28%.
370   We note that we can reliably detect when this a model has been influenced by an adversarial system
371   prompt by using QueRE with a simple linear probe (Figure 5), outperforming or matching all other
372   methods on all datasets. This is an extension of the finding to the work of MacDiarmid et al. (2024),
373   where they could reliably detect the presence of adversarial LLMs by training a linear model on the
374   hidden states. Our finding is more general in that we can construct this reliable linear classifier in a
375
      completely black-box fashion, without looking at model internals.
376   Building upon this result, we add an experiment to determine whether QueRE can reliably detect
377   between different versions of harmful versus helpful system prompts. We consider the following

      4.5   A BLATIONS
461
462   Sampling from the Black-Box LLM Achieves Comparable Performance As previously men-
463   tioned, we often do not have access to top-k probabilities through the closed-source API. While
464   we have provided asymptotic guarantees (in terms of both n and k) on the estimator learned via
465   logistic regression, we also are interested in the setting where we have a finite number of samples
466
      k. Therefore, we run an experiment where instead of using the actual ground-truth probability, we
      approximate this via an average of k samples from the distribution of the LLM. We report results
467
      using approximations via sampling from the distribution specified by GPT-3.5’s top-k log probs on
468   the BoolQ and DHate datasets. We observe not a significant drop (less than 2 points in AUROC)
469   in performance when using sampling, which implies that our method can be used in settings with
470   closed-source LLMs that do not give top-k probability access.
471
472   More Elicitation Questions Leads to Better Performance We study how much the number
473   of elicitation questions used directly impacts how much information is extracted in the black-box
474   representation. We randomly subsample the number of elicitation questions and report how much
475   the performance of our approach varies when only using this subset of questions. We observe that
476   on the BoolQ dataset with LLaMA2-70B and Mixtral-8x7B (Figure 8), we see that our predictive
      performance increases as we increase the number of elicitation prompts that we consider, with
477
      the rate of increase slowly diminishing with more prompts. We defer results on other datasets to
478   Appendix K.5, where we observe similar results. This demonstrates that we can achieve even stronger
479   performance with our method with more elicitation questions, even when they are LLM-generated.
480
481
      Elicitation Questions Versus Random Sequences of Language We also analyze the impact of
482   the importance of the particular choice of our elicitation questions (i.e., generated via GPT-4 in a
483   certain way) by running an ablation study where we feed random, unrelated sequences of coherent
484   natural language as inputs to the model. This new comparison (Random Sequences of Language)
485   evaluates how much unrelated sequences of natural language influence the distribution from the LLM
      and studies how useful this extracted information is for downstream tasks.

529   5     D ISCUSSION
530
531   We have provided a technique to extract black-box features from LLMs that are useful in predicting
532   downstream task performance and distinguishing between different model sizes or between models
533   that have been influenced by adversaries. For instance, this provides an approach to get non-vacuous
534
      generalization bounds in predicting the performance of LLMs. Furthermore, we also see the ability to
      extract useful and informative black-box representations as related to the notion of “explainability”.
535
      Extracting representations by asking a model questions eliciting is, in some sense, an evaluation of
536   its ability to meaningfully understand its own behavior and respond to prompts. However, we remark
537   that this is an imperfect comparison, as these extracted features are treated in an uninterpretable
538   manner (i.e., as features for a supervised learning model). In fact, our finding that responses to
539   random sequences can reveal information about model behavior aligns with prior work describing
      flaws in existing interpretability frameworks (Friedman et al., 2023; Singh et al., 2024).

1169   Understanding and Benchmarking LLMs A large body of work has focused on understanding
1170   the capabilities of LLMs. The field of mechanistic interpretability has recently evolved around
       understanding the inner workings of LLMs by uncovering circuits or specific weight activations
1171
       (Olsson et al., 2022; Nanda et al., 2022). This has developed a variety of potential hypotheses
1172   for how models learn to perform specific tasks (Zhong et al., 2024), as well as the tendencies of
1173   certain activations in a LLM to activate on certain types of inputs (Bills et al., 2023; Sun et al.,
1174   2024). Other works have studied model behavior by locating specific regions of a LLM that relate
1175   to certain concepts such as untruthfulness (Campbell et al., 2023) or honesty and ethical behavior
1176   (Zou et al., 2023a). Our work is different in that we only assume black-box access, with a similar
1177
       goal to extract information about model behavior. Finally, other work has attempted to study the
       abilities and performance of LLM via developing challenging benchmarks (Hendrycks et al., 2020),
1178
       also including those that use techniques from the cognitive sciences (Binz & Schulz, 2023) or by
1179   comparing with human similarity judgements (Coda-Forno et al., 2024). While these approaches
1180   look to benchmark and quantify performance in aggregate over tasks, our settings looks to predict the
1181   performance at the example level, for deciding when to trust or use LLMs in deployment.

1544   For our generalization details, we use PAC-Bayesian bounds over the linear models, as is outlined in
1545   the work of Jiang et al. (2019). Here, we consider a prior of weights specified about the origin, with a
1546   grid of variances of [0.1, 0.11, ..., 0.99, 1.0]. For the generalization experiments, we balance both the
1547   train and test datasets as we evaluate the accuracy of different predictors.
