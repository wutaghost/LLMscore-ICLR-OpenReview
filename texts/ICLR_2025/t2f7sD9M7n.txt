009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph neural networks (GNNs) have shown promise in learning the ground-state
012            electronic properties of molecules and crystalline materials, subverting compu-
013            tationally intensive density functional theory (DFT) calculations. Materials with
014            structural disorder, however, are more challenging to learn as they exhibit higher
015            complexity and a more extensive palette of local atomic environments, all of which
016            require large (10+ Å) cells to be accurately captured. In this work, we adapt effi-
017            cient equivariant GNN approaches to learn disordered materials’ electronic prop-
018            erties, represented by the Hamiltonian matrix (H). Since creating a large graph
019
               corresponding to the whole structure of interest would be computationally pro-
               hibitive, we introduce an augmented partitioning approach in which the graph
020
               is sliced into multiple partitions, each augmented with masked virtual nodes and
021
               edges. This method maintains correct atomic neighborhoods within a single mes-
022            sage passing layer, allowing for the network to learn the electronic properties of
023            amorphous HfO2 materials with 3,000 nodes (atoms), 500,000+ edges, and ∼28
024            million orbital interactions (non-zero entries of H).

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Predicting structure-property relationships in atomically resolved materials lends itself optimally
030   to graph-structured data. Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have proven capable of learning these
031   relationships while constrained by their underlying symmetries (Veličković et al. (2018)). Trained
032   networks have been able to learn molecular- and atomic-level quantities (Wang et al. (2023)), cir-
033
      cumventing otherwise computationally prohibitive simulations at the ab initio level. More recently,
      GNNs have been adapted to predict electronic properties, described by a discretized ground-state
034
      Hamiltonian matrix H. Rapid and accurate constructions of H can unlock in silico explorations of
035
      the large design space of electronic materials (Klinkert et al. (2020)).
036
037   The matrix H can be decomposed into sub-matrices Hi,j that encode the coupling between the sets
038   of atomic orbitals located on atoms i and j. These coupling terms are a function of the identity and
039
      relative coordinates of the local environment. Predicting such electronically resolved information
      introduces additional challenges over atomically resolved quantities, as the output data is equivariant
040
      under rotation. Existing work on electronic property prediction has mainly treated the cases of small
041
      molecules (Zhong et al. (2023); Yu et al. (2023b); Bai et al. (2021)) and well-ordered materials (Li
042   et al. (2022); Gong et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024a)). The graph representations of these structures
043   are fairly small - small molecules contain only a few atoms, and in crystalline materials all relevant
044   structural information can be captured within the smallest repeating unit cell.
045
      Many applications, however, require the computation of the electronic properties of materials with
046
      structural disorder, such as local or extended defects, or in an amorphous phase (Ducry et al. (2020);
047
      Kaniselvan et al. (2023); Strand et al. (2018)). These materials typically contain a limited number
048   of atomic species, from 2 to 5, with quasi-random distributions. Accurate ab initio simulations of
049   the resulting disordered atomic structures are only possible if large unit cells composed of hundreds
050   to thousands of atoms are used (Repa & Fredin (2023)). As a consequence, prohibitively expensive
051   computations must be performed with a density functional theory (DFT) tool to obtain their elec-
052   tronic properties. The prospect of applying deep-learning solutions to handle such materials is thus
053   particularly attractive. To be of practical relevance, however, they should be able to generalize to
      large scales.

054
      Here, we extend equivariant GNN approaches to learn and predict the electronic properties of ma-
055   terials in amorphous phases by fitting the sub-blocks of the ground-state Hamiltonian H in matrix
056   form. Our main contributions are:
057
058          • We develop an efficient GNN-based model for electronic property prediction, by combining
059            (1) the SO(2)-convolution approach detailed in Passaro & Zitnick (2023), (2) the equivari-
060            ant attention mechanism introduced in Liao et al. (2023), and (3) concepts from Gong et al.
061            (2023) and Wang et al. (2024a) to introduce learnable node/edge embeddings along with
062
               basis transformation layer to pre-process the targets and map predictions to the Hamiltonian
               output. We provide the code for this implementation in [the Supporting Materials].
063
064          • We propose an efficient augmented partitioning method that breaks down input graphs into
065
               small pieces and corrects atomic environments with masked virtual nodes and edges. This
               allows arbitrarily large graphs to be decomposed into independent partitions that can fit into
066
               GPU memory during training without compromising the achievable testing accuracy. Our
067
               approach enables the training and prediction of unfeasibly large systems including realistic
068            amorphous materials and heterostructures that can contain up to hundreds of thousands of
069            atoms in a unit cell.
070
071   We combine our model and augmented partitioning approach to treat a real example with practical
072   scientific relevance. Specifically, we consider hafnium dioxide (HfO2 ), one of the most technologi-
073   cally relevant amorphous oxides (Choi et al. (2011)). The theoretical study of defects and transport
074   properties in HfO2 is relevant to several research areas, from optimizing gate dielectrics for transis-
075
      tors (Strand et al. (2018)) to developing new resistive-switching technologies enabling in-memory
      computing (Kaniselvan et al. (2023)). With this, we achieve a prediction accuracy of 5.87 meV ,
076
      matching the eigenvalues of H to within 0.87% relative L1 error, on structures with 3,000 atoms,
077
      which require several (3.65) hours to compute using DFT. Our work advances applications of equiv-
078   ariant GNNs towards practical use cases in computational physics, chemistry, and materials science.

      2.2   D EVELOPMENT OF MODELS FOR THE PREDICTION OF ELECTRONIC PROPERTIES
143
      Only a few studies have attempted to directly predict the Hamiltonian matrix H rather than directly
144   fitting invariant quantities such as the total energy. The key is to constrain the solution space by
145   leveraging prior knowledge of physical symmetries, e.g., rotational equivariance of orbital blocks.
146
147
      Several works leverage local structure descriptors containing sufficient detail about each atom’s en-
      vironment. The mapping between these descriptors and the orbital blocks of the Hamiltonian was
148
      then learned through Kernel Ridge Regression (Hegde & Bowen (2017)) or multilayer neural net-
149
      works (Schütt et al. (2019); Gu et al. (2024)). Certain descriptors, such as Atomic Cluster Expansion
150   (ACE) (Drautz (2020)), can be extended to orbitally resolved data (Nigam et al. (2022)) and used
151   to predict the Hamiltonian matrix of crystalline materials (Zhang et al. (2022)). These approaches
152   achieved prediction accuracy of a few meV on small-molecule datasets. Rotational equivariance was
153   enforced through the descriptors (Zhang et al. (2022)) or data augmentation (Schütt et al. (2019)).
154   Initial GNN-based approaches, such as the network developed by Li et al. (2022), are intrinsically
155   invariant to the translation and permutation of the inputs. Information about the rotational equivari-
156   ance was incorporated by rotating to a pre-selected axis before training, which reduces the problem
157   to a rotationally invariant one.

159   Zhang et al. (2024); Batatia et al. (2023); Gong et al. (2023)), which requires maintaining SO(3)-
160   equivariance within the model. This means that all network operations f acting on input embedding

181
      advantage of the fact that when edges are rotated to align with a fixed axis (y or z depending on con-
182
      vention), the only non-zero spherical harmonic components are those of order m = 0. By keeping
183   track of the bond vectors and performing internal spherical rotations, complex SO(3) convolutions
184   can thus be reduced into SO(2) linear convolution operations (Passaro & Zitnick (2023)). Addition-
185   ally, under these conditions, the Clebsch-Gordan coefficients exhibit a predictable sparsity pattern
                                                                                 3
186   (non-zero only when m3 = ± m1 ). Altogether, the scaling reduces to O(lmax      ) (Wang et al. (2024a)),
187   and the network’s training speeds up, enabling the use of higher-order angular momenta (lmax ) and
188   more parameters to capture finer, more complex details of the surrounding environment. An ad-
189   vanced SO(2) convolution network was developed by Passaro & Zitnick (2023) (eSCN)) and further
190   expanded by Liao et al. (2023) (EquiformerV2) with the inclusion of equivariant attention and sep-
191
      arable activation layers. A subsequent implementation of this approach on Hamiltonians by Wang
      et al. (2024a) achieved better performance on custom crystalline 2D-material datasets compared to
192
      previous tensor field and invariant networks.

217   dsphere,n )/(Ne , (lmax + 1)2 , dsphere,e ), where Nn/e is the number of nodes/edges, dsphere,n/e

220   embeddings are initialized with the scalar distance between the two connecting nodes, expanded in
221   the chosen basis, here Gaussian functions. All other components are initially set to 0. The orbital
222   blocks representing the interaction between atoms are flattened into 1D tensors to form the labels
223   for each node/edge during the supervised training process. They are then converted from uncoupled
224   to coupled basis using a Wigner-Eckart transformation (Appendix A.1).
225   During training, each MP layer updates the node, and then the edge representations. Within the
226   node update block, each node i receives messages from each of its neighbors j, consisting of the
227   concatenated embeddings ni , nj , and that of the edge eji , rotated to align with the z-axis. After
228   SO(2) convolutions are performed on the input messages, the resulting output messages are rotated
229   back to their original orientations and aggregated onto the node i to update its embedding ni . The
230   updated node embeddings are then used to update the edges through a similar process, without the
231
      attention layer. Subsequently, the node embeddings in the next message-passing layer are updated
      with the new edge embeddings. A high-level overview of the network is presented in Fig. 3, and
232
      a more detailed outline can be found in Appendix A. During the inference phase, the output is
233
      converted back to the uncoupled basis through a Wigner-Eckart layer (Appendix A.1) to reconstruct
234   the Hamiltonian matrix H.
235

237
      lies in the use of gate activation layers instead of the S2 activation layer of EquiformerV2. Although

238
      ically perfect equivariance requires a large grid size, making it computationally expensive when
239
      implemented on large graphs (Passaro & Zitnick (2023)).

409   4.2.1    E FFECT OF AUGMENTED PARTITIONING ON PREDICTION ACCURACY
410
411   The augmented partitioning approach introduced in Section 3.2 allows for the subdivision of the
412   large graphs associated with the training of disordered materials by defining small slices (i.e., small
413
      tslice ) that can be treated sequentially on a single GPU or distributed across multiple GPUs and
      processed independently in parallel. Intermediate quantities do not need to be communicated during
414
      the forward/backward passes.
415
416   To establish that the network trained on augmented partitions does not suffer from loss of accu-
417   racy, we partition the same full graph into different numbers of slices with different thicknesses for
418   training. In each case, the total number of labeled atoms summed up across all the slices remains
419
      the same (3,000), while the total number of labeled edges reduces with increasing partitions. From
      Table 4 it can be seen that the prediction error is insensitive to partition size. Despite the different
420
      divisions ranging from 5 (tslice = ∼12 Å) to 54 (tslice = ∼1 Å) slices, ϵn and ϵe remain very close to
421
      the values obtained by training with the full graph (tslice = 52.346 Å). For small slices, the reduced
422   fraction of labeled connections along the x direction does not affect the accuracy as the remaining
423   data along the y and z directions is sufficient to train the network. The combined MAE loss from
424   all 3,000 atoms and 530,920 edges for the best performing case (tslice = ∼3 Å) is 0.2159 mEh , or
425   5.87 meV . This value is comparable to what a previous study obtained (2.2 meV ) using equivariant
426   GNNs for much smaller structures with ≤150 atoms per unit cell (Wang et al. (2024b)).

470   4.5   C OMPUTATIONAL COST
471
472   Compared to a naive full-batch training of the graph, our method using just 8 augmented slices
473   results in a 6.5× speedup per epoch (0.38 vs. 2.5 s), and a 7.2× decrease in memory consumption
474
      per rank (8.59 vs. 61.68 GiB). A more complete analysis is provided in Appendix G.2. This
      scaling behavior is limited only by the overhead introduced by the virtual nodes/edges, and the
475
      small computational load imbalance from partitioning. Further computational improvements could
476
      be achieved by combining the augmentation approach with optimized graph partitioning algorithms
477   (Karypis & Kumar (1998)) while leveraging periodicity.
478
479   The extension of GNN-based predictions to large material systems could potentially save tremen-
480
      dous amounts of computational time. While DFT calculations to obtain the H GT of small molecules
      (e.g., H2 O) take only a few seconds, the same operation for a-HfO2 structures made of 3,000 atoms
481
      is computationally ∼100× heavier (∼0.04 vs. ∼3.65 node hours, see Appendix G). More im-
482
      portantly, the GNN prediction unlocks the ability to consider much larger structures than the ones
483   considered here, the inference phase scaling with O(Natoms ) while DFT calculations are limited to
484         3
      O(Natoms    ). The model could also serve as an initial guess to DFT packages to reduce the number
485   of self-consistent field iterations that are required to obtain converged electron densities (Unke et al.
      (2021)).

      5     C ONCLUSION
487
488
      We adapted equivariant GNNs to learn the electronic properties of amorphous materials, and intro-
489
      duced an augmented partitioning approach to break down and train the large graphs encountered
490   when dealing with realistic structural disorder, without sacrificing accuracy. More generally, we
491   proposed a method to tackle the training of atomic systems that require large, highly connected, and
492   near-sighted graphs where a strictly local atomic environment is sufficient. The key is the addition of
493   virtual nodes and edges connected to relatively small partitions that mimic their neighborhood. The
494   method, demonstrated here on a-HfO2 , can be straightforwardly applied to other disordered materi-
495   als, or adapted to learn their other rotationally-equivariant attributes such as vibrational properties,
496   e.g., phonon dispersions (Fang et al. (2024)).
497   The resulting networks capture relevant properties of a-HfO2 in sufficient detail to achieve few-meV
498   accuracy and reproduce features of practical relevance, such as the energy eigenvalues. However,
499   the sub-millielectronvolt range is not currently reached, contrary to what has been demonstrated
500   with small-molecule datasets (Yu et al. (2023b); Unke et al. (2021)). This shortcoming can most
501   likely be attributed to a combination of greater dataset complexity in amorphous compounds, coarser
502   resolution of the training data (ϵSCF = 1 × 10−6 Eh ), and limited network size. Note that the
503   augmented partitioning approach is also a general method that can also be adapted for use in other,
504
      more expressive network architectures, since it is mainly applied during graph construction. Further
      data generation, parameter optimization, and enabling of networks with increased expressiveness
505
      will be the next steps.
506
507
508   5.1   O UTLOOK & A PPLICATIONS
509
      The ability to learn the electronic properties of complex disordered materials unlocks notable appli-
510
      cations in computational physics, chemistry, and materials science. Several compounds are used in
511   their amorphous phase, as they often exhibit different properties from their crystalline equivalents.
512   For example, a-SiO2 as dielectric layer has been a key enabler of the metal-oxide-semiconductor
513   technology (Nekrashevich & Gritsenko (2014)), IGZO, thanks to its large electron mobility, serves
514   as channel of flexible transistors (Kamiya et al. (2010)), a-HfO2 allows for the (non-)volatile storage
515   of information, when placed between two metallic electrodes (Chan et al. (2008)), and GST can be
516   used to write/store data optically (Pirovano et al. (2004); Kolobov et al. (2004)). Downscaling of
517   materials also reveals structural effects similar to disorder, e.g., defects (Wilhelmer et al. (2022)),
518   strain Parton & Verheyen (2006), or grain boundaries (Weitz et al. (2009)). All of them require large
519
      unit cells to be accurately described (Lany & Zunger (2008); Zhao et al. (2020)). Computationally
      expensive DFT calculations represent a bottleneck towards investigating the electronic properties of
520
      such systems in simulations. Recent advances in graph neural networks, combined with domain-
521
      specific innovations to train them, will enable such explorations.

839   In this section, we provide further details on the architecture adapted from EquiformerV2 (Liao et al.
840   (2023)) and DeepH2 (Wang et al. (2024a)), in addition to the network initialization description in
841   Section 3.1.
842   During message passing, input messages in the form of node embeddings of size (Nn , (lmax + 1)2 ,
843   dsphere,n ) and edge embeddings of size (Ne , (lmax + 1)2 , dsphere,n ) from surrounding neighboring
844   source nodes are passed into the target node. Note that for our network, dsphere,n and dsphere,e are
845   set to the same value dsphere , known as the number of spherical channels.
846   Inside the node update block, the source and target atom embeddings are concatenated together with
847   the edge embeddings of their connecting edge to form an input message of size (Ne , (lmax + 1)2 ,
848   3dsphere ). The message is multiplied by weights generated by the radial functions using scalar
849   embeddings (atomic numbers and distances), making it more receptive to small changes in environ-
850   ments. The dimension of these scalar embeddings are similarly set to dsphere .
851
      Afterwards, the input message is rotated to align with the z axis through the rotation block. The ro-
852   tation block contains rotation matrices that were pre-computed using the normalized edge vectors of
853   every edge in the graph. The rotated message is reshaped into m major order for linear convolutions
854   to be performed for each m = 0 to m = mmax with the number of l components for each m is given
855   by lmax − m + 1. mmax and lmax (mmax ≤ lmax ) are other hyperparameters that can be adjusted.
856   The convolutions produce an output embedding of size (ne , (lmax + 1)2 , dattn hidden ), where atten-
857   tion hidden channels dattn hidden is another hyperparameter. The message is then fed through the
858   gate activation layer, which adds non-linearity while preserving equivariance by applying separate
859   non-linearities to the l = 0 and l > 0 components.
860   Next, the non-linear message is passed through a second convolution layer that produces an output
861   embedding size of (ne , (lmax +1)2 , dattn value ∗Nheads ), which is then be reshaped into (ne , (lmax +
862   1)2 , Nheads , dattn value ). For each edge surrounding the target node, a set of dattn alpha attention
863   weights are then generated for each attention head, with the total number of heads being Nheads .
      This is used to generate the output vector alpha, which is reshaped along Nheads and multiplied with

      A.1    W IGNER E CKART L AYER
876
877
      Our implementation of this layer is similar to that found in Gong et al. (2023). A Hamiltonian block
878   representing the interaction between atom i and j consists can be split into different sub-blocks
879   representing interactions between orbital 1 of order l1 and orbital 2 of degree l2 as shown in Fig. 1.
880   Each block of size (2l1 + 1) × (2l2 + 1), is an equivariant tensor that comes from the tensor product
881   l1 ⊗ l2 for every pair of interacting orbitals in atom i and atom j. For example, an interaction block
882   between a p orbital (l = 1) and a d orbital (l = 2) has (2 × 1 + 1) × (2 × 2 + 1) = 3 × 5 elements.
883   We refer to this form as the uncoupled basis representation of the Hamiltonian block.
884   Before training, the Wigner-Eckart layer converts the Hamiltonian data from uncoupled basis to
885   coupled basis using Clebsch Gordan coefficients.
886
887
      l1 ⊗ l2 = |l1 − l2 | ⊕ ... ⊕ (l1 + l2 )
888   l1 ⊗ l2 is now represented by a direct sum of coupled sub-spaces with order ranging from |l1 − l2 | to
889   (l1 + l2 ). This is repeated for every possible type of orbital interaction between the atoms, to obtain
890   the final direct sum of all the sub-spaces needed for the network to reconstruct the Hamiltonian.
891   The largest possible (l1 + l2 ) value determines the minimum lmax hyperparameter needed for the
892   embeddings of the equivariant model. In the case of HfO2 in this paper, which uses the SZV basis
893   set, the highest order orbital is the d orbital of Hf. This means that the largest possible (l1 +l2 ) comes
894   from the interaction between two Hf d orbitals, and the lmax needs to be at least equal to (2+2) = 4.
895   This allows the predicted output of the model to be converted back into the full uncoupled basis using
896   the same layer, and reassembled into the full Hamiltonian matrix during inference.

1095   F.1   DATASET GENERATION
1096
1097   Atomic structures corresponding to materials in the amorphous phase can be produced through melt-
1098   quench Urquiza et al. (2021), seed-and-coordinate Youn et al. (2014), or ‘decorate and relax’ Tafen
1099   & Drabold (2003) approaches. To accurately reproduce long-range structural disorder, the struc-
1100   tures used must be large enough to avoid the creation of wavefunctions which repeat over periodic
1101   boundaries.
1102   The first step to computing the electronic properties of a-HfO2 is to generate the atomic structure
1103   of the material in its amorphous phase. To accurately capture the structural motifs underlying this
1104   phase and a realistic range of atomic coordination (eg, neighboring Oxygens for each Hafnium, and
1105   vice versa), we start from the crystalline m-HfO2 phase and perform melt-quench processes using
1106   Molecular Dynamics (MD), following a similar procedure as the ones described in Refs. Kaniselvan
1107   et al. (2023); Urquiza et al. (2021). We generate 3 independent structures of a-HfO2 using the
1108   QuantumATK toolkit Søren Smidstrup et al. (2020). As the first step, we run an NVT simulation
1109
       with the Langevin thermostat at 3000 K for 50 ps with a step size of 1 fs. We use the MTP-HfO2 -
       2022 potential, provided by the software. Next, we run an NPT simulation for 300 ps (and the same
1110
       1 fs step size), with an initial reservoir temperature of 3000K and a final temperature of 300K, for a
1111
       cooling rate of 9K/ps. Finally, we anneal structure evolve at 300K for 50 ps, using the same NVT
1112   Langevin thermostat as for the melting.
1113
1114   We then perform a structural relaxation with CP2K code Kühne et al. (2020) to correct for any dis-
1115
       crepancies between the relaxed bond lengths attained with the force field used for MD, and those
       obtained with DFT. Due to the computational cost of using a more complete DZVP basis set Vande-
1116
       Vondele & Hutter (2007), we use a simpler SZV basis VandeVondele & Hutter (2007) which uses 4
1117
       basis functions per Oxygen atom and 10 basis functions per Hafnium atom. The plane-wave cutoff
1118   is set to 500 Ry, while a cutoff of 60 Ry is used for mapping the Gaussian-type orbitals onto the grid.
1119   We use the PBE functional for the exchange-correlation energy Perdew et al. (1997). To accurately
1120   capture the band gap of a-HfO2 , we applied the Hubbard correction Anisimov et al. (1991) of U = 7
1121   eV to the 3d orbital of Ti and the Hubbard correction of U = 10 eV to the 2p orbital of O.

1221          • Computational overhead of the virtual nodes and edges: Individual nodes and edges of the
1222            graph can be repeated in labeled and virtual node lists. Treating the replicas introduces
1223            additional computational cost while training the network, which increases with Nt . This
1224            overhead is maximum with the use of very small slices (large Nt ), thus introducing a trade-
1225            off between parallelism and time per epoch.

1478       A PPROACH
1479
1480   The augmented partitioning approach relies on the correction of the partitioned sub-graphs through
1481   the introduction of virtual nodes and edges, such that partitioned training fully resembles full graph
1482   training. Here, we provide the theoretical foundations upon which our approach is built.
1483

1549
1550   To demonstrate the effects of long and short range perturbations in amorphous structures, we intro-
1551   duce a single perturbation at one chosen location in the structure and measure the mean absolute
1552   error of the onsite Hamiltonian blocks when compared to that of the unperturbed structure. The
1553   types of perturbations introduced include single oxygen atom translation, oxygen vacancy, and sub-
1554   stitution of an oxygen by a hafnium atom, which are plotted against distance from perturbation in
1555   Fig. 15 (a), (b) and (c) respectively. In all cases, the effect of the perturbation rapidly decays with
1556
       increasing distance.
1557   For the case of a 0.1 Å translation perturbation, the average onsite MAE at a distance of 8 Å away
1558   is given by 0.15 mEh . Considering the average value of an onsite Hamiltonian block (63 mEh ),
1559   the perturbation only affects the matrix elements by 0.24% overall. Similarly, for vacancy and sub-
1560   stitution perturbations, the matrix elements of atoms located 8 Å away only changed by 0.18% and
1561   0.12% respectively. This implies that for our chosen cutoff of 8 Å , perturbations occurring outside
1562
       of the radius surrounding the atom have a negligible effect on its Hamiltonian matrix elements. This
       also means that the electronic structure of that atom can be learned using information from the local
1563
       atomic environment.
1564
1565   This is also demonstrated through our study of sub-stoichiometric HfOx with randomly distributed
       vacancies. Despite training on independent slices, we ensure that every atom within that slice is
