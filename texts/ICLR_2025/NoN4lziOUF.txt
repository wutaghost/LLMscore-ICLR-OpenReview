009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In this paper, we investigate the characteristics that define a good representation
012            or model. We propose that such a representation or model should possess univer-
013            sality, characterized by: (i) discriminability: performing well on training samples;
014            (ii) generalization: performing well on unseen datasets; and (iii) transferability:
015            performing well on unseen tasks with distribution shifts. Despite its importance,
016
               current self-supervised learning (SSL) methods lack explicit modeling of universal-
               ity, and theoretical analysis remains underexplored. To address these issues, we
017
               aim to explore and incorporate universality into SSL. Specifically, we first revisit
018
               SSL from a task perspective and find that each mini-batch can be viewed as a
019            multi-class classification task. We then propose that a universal SSL model should
020            achieve: (i) learning universality by minimizing loss across all training samples,
021            and (ii) evaluation universality by learning causally invariant representations that
022            generalize well to unseen datasets and tasks. To quantify this, we introduce a
023            σ-measurement that assesses the gap between the performance of SSL model and
024            optimal task-specific models. Furthermore, to model universality, we propose the
025            GeSSL framework. It first learns task-specific models by minimizing SSL loss,
026            then incorporates future updates to enhance discriminability, and finally integrates
027
               these models to learn from multiple mini-batch tasks. Theoretical and empirical
               evidence supports the effectiveness of GeSSL.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Self-supervised learning (SSL) has revolutionized machine learning by enabling models to learn
033   meaningful representations from unlabeled data, thereby significantly reducing reliance on large
034   labeled datasets (Gui et al., 2024). SSL methods are generally divided into two categories: discrimi-
035   native SSL (D-SSL) and generative SSL (G-SSL). D-SSL approaches, such as SimCLR (Chen et al.,
036   2020a), BYOL (Grill et al., 2020), and Barlow Twins (Zbontar et al., 2021), focus on distinguishing
037   between different augmented views of the same image, learning representations by maximizing
038   the similarity between positive pairs and minimizing it with negative ones. In contrast, G-SSL
039
      methods like MAE (Hou et al., 2022) aim to reconstruct missing or corrupted parts of the input
      data, learning representations by capturing inherent visual structures and patterns. Both D-SSL and
040
      G-SSL have demonstrated remarkable performance, excelling in tasks such as unsupervised learning,
041
      semi-supervised learning, transfer learning, and few-shot learning. Their capacity to learn good
042   representations from unlabeled data has significantly advanced the field across diverse applications.
043
044   Whether using D-SSL or G-SSL methods, most research focuses on determining which factors, e.g.,
045
      network architectures (Caron et al., 2021), optimization strategies (Ni et al., 2021), prior assumptions
      (Ermolov et al., 2021), inductive biases (Grill et al., 2020), etc., lead to effective representations or
046
      models. However, a fundamental question persists: What exactly defines a “good” representation
047
      or model? To address this, the common practice is to evaluate the learned representations or models
048   on various downstream tasks, that is, if the performance is strong, the representation or model is
049   deemed good. Yet, a key challenge remains in understanding why certain approaches result in higher
050   performance. In other words, we often lack direct explanations of how specific methodological
051   choices influence the quality of the representation or model. For instance, why does an asymmetric
052   dual-branch network architecture in methods like BYOL enhance performance on downstream tasks?
053   Similarly, why does enforcing a uniform distribution on feature representations serve as an inductive
      bias for obtaining good representations or models in methods like SimCLR?

054
      To address the aforementioned challenges, this work shifts focus from considering SSL methods
055   and their subsequent developments in terms of “what to do” to directly exploring what constitutes a
056   good representation or model. We concentrate on the question: What characteristics should a good
057   representation or model possess? Inspired by the evaluation methods of most SSL and unsupervised
058   representation learning approaches (Chen et al., 2020a; Grill et al., 2020; Hou et al., 2022), we propose
059   that a good representation or model should satisfy three constraints: 1) Discriminability: For a single
060   task, the model should achieve the expected performance on the training set; 2) Generalizability:
061   For a single task, the trained model should generalize to unseen datasets while maintaining its
062   performance; 3) Transferability: The trained model should generalize to multiple different tasks
063
      while guaranteeing its performance. Based on these three constraints, we provide, for the first time in
      this paper, the formulation of a good representation or model—namely, Universality.
064
065   With the precise definition of “Universality” provided (i.e., Definition 3.1 within the main text),
066   another significant challenge is formalizing the properties of discriminability, generalizability, and
067   transferability within the SSL learning process. Notably, if a model can accurately predict all samples
068   of a task based on the learned representations, it possesses good discriminability, which is reflected
069
      in a low training loss. Furthermore, as shown by Schölkopf et al. (2021) and Ahuja et al. (2023),
      the causality of representations is a sufficient condition for generalizability. Finally, Ni et al. (2021)
070
      demonstrates that SSL and meta-learning are closely related, and meta-learning is an effective
071
      approach to modeling transferability (Finn et al., 2017a). Therefore, designing a new SSL paradigm
072   based on meta-learning can imbue the features learned by SSL methods with transferability. Based
073   on these insights, we propose a novel SSL framework called GeSSL to explicitly model universality
074   in the SSL learning process. Specifically, for discriminability, GeSSL employs the Kullback-Leibler
075   divergence to enable the SSL model to use the future state to distill the current state, thereby achieving
076   lower training loss. For generalizability, GeSSL extracts causal features by learning across multiple
077   tasks. For transferability, GeSSL introduces a bi-level optimization mechanism to formulate the
078   SSL learning behavior in a meta-learning style. In essence, GeSSL incorporates discriminability,
079   generalizability, and transferability into the SSL method from three dimensions: optimization
080
      objective, parameter update mechanism, and learning paradigm.
081   Our contributions: (i) We theoretically define SSL universality, encompassing discriminability,
082   generalizability, and transferability, and introduce a σ-measurement to quantify it (Sections 3.1,
083   3.2). (ii) We propose GeSSL, a novel framework that models universality through a self-motivated
084   target for discriminability, a multi-batch collaborative update mechanism for generalizability, and a
085   task-based bi-level learning paradigm for transferability (Section 3.3). (iii) Theoretical and empirical
086
      evaluations on benchmark datasets demonstrate the superior performance of GeSSL (Sections 4, 5).

      D-SSL methods typically have two main objectives: alignment and regularization (Chen et al., 2020a;
      Grill et al., 2020; Zbontar et al., 2021; Oord et al., 2018; Hjelm et al., 2018). The alignment objective
100
      maximizes the similarity between paired samples in the embedding space, while the regularization
101
      objective constrains the learning behavior via inductive biases. For example, SimCLR (Chen et al.,
102   2020a) enforces a uniform distribution over the feature representations. G-SSL methods (Hou et al.,
103   2022) can also be viewed as implementing alignment within a pair using an encoding-decoding
104   structure: sample x1i is input into this structure to generate an output that is made as consistent as
105   possible with sample x2i . Notably, alignment in D-SSL is often implemented using anchor points,
106   where one sample in a pair is viewed as the anchor, and the training process gradually pulls the other
107   sample towards this anchor. This concept of an anchor is also applicable to G-SSL, where x2i is
      treated as the anchor, and the training process involves constraining x1i to approach x2i .

      definition with theoretical support. Then, we propose the σ-measurement to help quantify universality.

      3.1   D EFINITION OF U NIVERSALITY
123
124   Wang et al. (2022) and Huang et al. (2021) theoretically proved that to obtain a good representation
125   or model, SSL methods need to constrain the feature of samples to be discriminative, that is,
126   compact within classes and separated between classes. However, this explanation does not clarify
127   why an SSL model trained on one dataset (e.g., ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009a)) can generalize
128   to different downstream tasks. Ni et al. (2021) explains the generalizability of SSL methods to
129   different downstream tasks from the perspective of tasks but does not address the discriminability and
130
      generalizability of SSL models themselves. These gaps motivated us to propose new understandings
      and insights into the effectiveness of SSL methods in this paper. Therefore, we first provide a
131
      definition of a good representation or model, namely, universality. This definition suggests that a good
132
      representation should possess the properties of discriminability, generalizability, and transferability.
133
134   Considering a single mini-batch of SSL as a multi-class classification task, as mentioned in Section 2,
135   we present the definition of universality as follows:

      For a specific mini-batch task, a model exhibits good discriminability if it can accurately predict all
      samples of the task based on the learned representations. This is reflected by the model fθ achieving
148
      the lowest loss on all samples of the task. Therefore, discriminability is a key component of learning
149
      universality. According to Ahuja et al. (2020), if a model achieves good performance across multiple
150   different tasks, it can be considered to have learned causal representations. Moreover, Schölkopf et al.
151   (2021) and Ahuja et al. (2023) conclude that causality in representations is a sufficient condition for
152   generalizability. Thus, a generalizable representation should be causally invariant across multiple
153   tasks, enabling the model to achieve very low loss on these tasks using the same representation.
154   Furthermore, since the training tasks and target tasks have no class-level overlap, the model’s ability
155   to perform well on unseen tasks demonstrates transferability. Consequently, evaluation universality
156   encompasses both generalizability and transferability.
157   For the differences and relation of learning and evaluation universality: (i) Differences: learning
158   universality refers to the rapid adaptation of the model to each task during training, referring to
159   discriminability, while evaluation universality refers to the performance of the trained model in
160   various tasks, referring to generalizability and transferability. The differences are reflected in the two
161   stages of training vs. evaluation, and the performance of each single task vs. all tasks. (ii) Relation:
      they cover all stages of training and testing, and jointly require the model to be close to universality.

      This paper introduces a fundamental concept of universality that surpasses previous works focused
163   on universal representations (Eastwood et al., 2023; Balazevic et al., 2024) and transferability (Hsu
164   et al., 2018; Ni et al., 2021) for two main reasons: (i) It incorporates both learning and evaluation
165   universality, thereby constraining the discriminability, generalizability, and transferability of SSL,
166   whereas prior studies focused solely on transferability. (ii) While earlier research emphasized task
167   performance, particularly in meta-learning (Hsu et al., 2018; Ni et al., 2021), this work evaluates the
168   model’s effects across diverse samples and tasks. More specifically, meta-learning operates under a
169   supervised framework, whereas GeSSL employs an unsupervised SSL approach. Additionally, the
170   outer model’s updates in meta-learning depend on the inner model’s performance, often constrained
171
      by minimal samples, such as in 5-way 1-shot tasks, which can hinder discriminability. Furthermore,
      generalization is assessed through query sets, potentially leading to overfitting on training tasks and
172
      undermining broader generalizability.

357   The theorem shows that any self-motivated target, even in the absence of noise, can drive model
358   updates towards better performance, i.e., as α and β become small or even zero, we get f˜θ − fθ ≤ 0
359   where f˜θ achieves performance improvements over previous fθ . By using KL divergence to quantify
360   the difference between the model’s output distributions, the theorem ensures that controlled gradient
361   updates gradually reduce the model’s deviation from the target distribution. As the parameter β
362   decreases, the KL divergence term diminishes, indicating the model’s steady convergence towards a
363   more optimal state. The proof of this theorem and more analyses are provided in Appendix B.

      analysis). We choose 5 image-based and 5 video-based tasks following Liu et al. (2022b). Figure 5
503
      shows the comparison results on image-based tasks. We can observe that the existing SSL model has
504
      limited universality with higher σ-measurement error, but is highly improved by introducing GeSSL.
505
506
507   6   R ELATED W ORK
508
509   SSL learns representations by transferring knowledge from pretext tasks without annotation. Follow-
510   ing Jaiswal et al. (2020); Kang et al. (2023), existing SSL paradigms methods can mainly be divided
511   into two types, i.e., discriminative SSL and generative SSL. The discriminative SSL methods, e.g.,
512   SimCLR Chen et al. (2020a), BYOL Grill et al. (2020), and Barlow Twins Zbontar et al. (2021),
513   mainly use stochastic data augmentation to produce two augmented views from the same input sample,
514   and then maximize the similarity of the same pair in the embedding space to learn representations. In
515   contrast, the generative SSL methods, e.g., MAE Hou et al. (2022) and VideoMAE Tong et al. (2022),
516
      mainly use an encoding-decoding structure to segment the input samples into multiple blocks, with
      some blocks being masked and the remaining blocks reassembled in their original positions to learn
517
      representation, and then use it to create a new sample. However, despite the empirical effect of the
518
      existing SSL methods has been proven, they still face challenges Jaiswal et al. (2020). For example,
519   SSL models generalize poorly (i) when data are scarce Krishnan et al. (2022), or (ii) in real life that
520   have a lot of noise Goyal et al. (2021). SSL models also result in overfitting or underfitting when
521   facing semantic inconsistency or ambiguous data Araslanov & Roth (2021); Li et al. (2020), e.g.,
522   the object orientation in rotation prediction is not fixed. Moreover, their performance is affected by
523   the matching between pretext and downstream tasks and may be difficult to transfer well Tendle &
524   Hasan (2021). The experiments in Section 5 and Section F also demonstrate it. Meanwhile, existing
525   theories on universality remain unclear. Previous SSL studies Oord et al. (2018); Hjelm et al. (2018);
526   Mizrahi et al. (2024); Tian et al. (2020b); Oquab et al. (2023) are generally framed as “employing
527
      certain methods to obtain a good representation” through experiments, without considering “what
      constitutes a good representation”. In this study, we address this gap by explicitly defining “a good
528
      representation” through formalized language, characterizing its core attributes as discriminability,
529
      generalizability, and transferability. We also propose corresponding learning objectives to enhance
530   feature interpretability, enabling constraining on the universality of representations or models.
531
532
533   7   C ONCLUSION
534
535   In this study, we explore the universality of SSL. We first unify SSL paradigms, i.e., discriminative
536   and generative SSL, from the task perspective and propose the definition of SSL universality. It
537   is a fundamental concept that involves discriminability, generalizability, and transferability. Then,
538   we propose GeSSL to explicitly model universality into SSL through bi-level optimization, which
539   introduces a σ-measurement-based self-motivated target to guide the model learn in the best direction.
      Extensive theoretical and empirical analyses demonstrate the superior effectiveness of GeSSL.

1169   Task Construction. We build tasks based on images with a batch size of B = 16. For data
1170   augmentation, we use the same data augmentation scheme as SimCLR to augment each image in the
1171   batch 5 times. In simple terms, we draw a random patch (224 × 224) from the original image, and
1172   then apply a random augmentation sequence composed of random horizontal flip, cropping, color
1173   jitter, etc.
1174   Architecture and Settings. We use C4-backbone, ResNet-18, and ResNet-50 backbones as our
1175   encoders for a fair comparison with different methods. The convolutional layers are followed by
1176   batch normalization, ReLU nonlinearity, and max pooling (strided convolution) respectively. The
1177   last layer is fed into a softmax classifier (a classification head). These architectures are pre-trained
1178   and kept fixed during training. We optimize our model with a Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD)
1179   optimizer, setting the momentum and weight decay values to 0.9 and 10−4 respectively. The specific
1180   adjustments of the experimental settings corresponding to different experiments are illustrated in
1181
       Section 5.2-Subsection 5.5 of the main text. In the ablation experiments, we adopt the experimental
       settings used in the corresponding dataset, i.e., the experiment of “Influence of λ” is conducted on
1182
       miniImageNet, so we adopt the experimental settings described in Section 5.5. All the experiments
1183
       are apples-to-apples comparisons and performed on NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPUs.

1261   E    BASELINES
1262
1263   In this section, we briefly introduce all baselines used in the experiments for comparison. We select
1264   fifteen representative self-supervised methods as baselines. These methods cover almost all the
1265   classic and SOTA self-supervised methods, including:
1266
1267         • SimCLR Chen et al. (2020a) learns visual representations by contrastive learning of aug-
1268           mented image pairs. It uses a neural network to maximize the similarity of positive pairs
1269
               and minimize the similarity of negative pairs.
1270         • MoCo v2 Chen et al. (2020b) improves MoCo Chen et al. (2020b), another contrastive
1271           learning method for visual representation learning. MoCo v2 introduces a momentum
1272           encoder, a memory bank, and a shuffling BN layer to handle limited batch size and noisy
1273           negatives. MoCo v2 also adopts SimCLR’s data augmentation and loss function to boost the
1274
               performance.
1275         • BYOL Grill et al. (2020) does not need negative pairs or a large batch size. It uses two
1276           neural networks, an online network and a target network, that learn from each other. The
1277           online network predicts the target network’s representation of an augmented image, while
1278           the target network is updated by a slow-moving average of the online network.
1279         • SimSiam Chen & He (2021) simplifies BYOL by removing the momentum encoder and the
1280           prediction MLP. It consists of two Siamese networks that map an input image to a feature
1281           vector, and a small MLP head that projects the feature vector to the contrastive learning
1282           space. SimSiam applies a stop-gradient operation to one of the MLP outputs, and uses a
1283           negative cosine similarity loss to maximize the similarity between the two outputs.
1284         • Barlow Twins Zbontar et al. (2021) learns representations by enforcing that the cross-
1285           correlation matrix between the outputs of two identical networks fed with different augmen-
1286           tations of the same image is close to the identity matrix. This encourages the networks to
1287           produce similar representations for the positive pair, while reducing the redundancy between
1288
               the representation dimensions.
1289         • DeepCluster Caron et al. (2018) is a clustering-based method for self-supervised learning. It
1290           iteratively groups the features produced by a convolutional network into clusters, and uses
1291           the cluster assignments as pseudo-labels to update the network parameters by supervised
1292           learning. DeepCluster can discover meaningful clusters that are discriminative and invariant
1293
               to transformations, and can learn competitive features for various downstream tasks.
1294         • SwAV Caron et al. (2020) uses online swapping of cluster assignments between multiple
1295           views of the same image to learn visual features. SwAV first computes prototypes (cluster
               centers) from a large set of features, and then assigns each feature to the nearest prototype.


                                                        24
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1296
               The assignments are then swapped across the views, and the network is trained to predict
1297           the swapped assignments.
1298
1299
             • DINO Caron et al. (2021) learns visual features by using a teacher-student architecture and
               a distillation loss. The teacher network is an exponential moving average of the student
1300
               network, and the distillation loss makes the student features similar to the teacher features.
1301
               DINO also applies a centering and sharpening operation to the teacher features, which
1302           prevents feature collapse and increases feature diversity.
1303
1304         • W-MSE Ermolov et al. (2021) learns features by using a weighted mean squared error (MSE)
1305
               loss, which assigns higher weights to the informative and less noisy features, and lower
               weights to the less informative and more noisy features.
1306
1307         • RELIC v2 Tomasev et al. (2022) learns visual features by predicting relative location of
1308           image patches. RELIC v2 divides an image into a grid of patches, and randomly selects a
1309           query and a target patch. The network is trained to predict the relative location of the target
1310
               patch with respect to the query patch, using a cross-entropy loss.
1311         • LMCL Chen et al. (2021) learns visual features by using a large margin cosine loss (LMCL).
1312           LMCL is a metric learning loss that makes the features of the same class closer and the
1313           features of different classes farther in the cosine space.
1314         • ReSSL Zheng et al. (2021) learns visual features by using a reconstruction loss and a
1315           contrastive loss. ReSSL applies random cropping and resizing to generate two views of the
1316           same image, and then feeds them to a reconstruction network and a contrastive network. The
1317           reconstruction network is trained to reconstruct the original image from the cropped view,
1318           while the contrastive network is trained to maximize the similarity between the features of
1319           the two views.
1320         • SSL-HSIC Li et al. (2021) learns visual features by using a Hilbert-Schmidt independence
1321           criterion (HSIC) loss. HSIC is a measure of statistical dependence between two random
1322           variables, and can be used to align the features of different views of the same image.
1323         • CorInfoMax Ozsoy et al. (2022) learns visual features by maximizing the correlation
1324           and mutual information between the features of augmented image pairs and the image
1325           labels. CorInfoMax aims to learn features that are both discriminative and consistent, and
1326           outperform previous methods on image classification and segmentation tasks.
1327
             • MEC Liu et al. (2022a) is a clustering algorithm that can handle large-scale data with limited
1328           memory by using a memory-efficient clustering (MEC) loss. MEC first samples a subset of
1329           features, and then performs k-means clustering on the subset. The cluster assignments are
1330           then propagated to the rest of the features by a nearest neighbor search.
1331
             • VICRegL Bardes et al. (2022) learns visual features by using a variance-invariance-
1332
               covariance regularization loss (VICRegL).
1333
1334   In addition, for the few-shot learning scenario, we choose six advanced unsupervised few-shot
1335   learning methods as comparison baselines.
1336
1337         • CACTUs Hsu et al. (2018) uses clustering and augmentation to create pseudo-labels for
1338           unlabeled data. It then trains a classifier on the labeled data and fine-tunes it on a few labeled
1339           examples from the target task.
1340         • UMTRA Khodadadeh et al. (2019) uses random selection and augmentation to create tasks
1341           with pseudo-labels from unlabeled data. It then trains a classifier on each task and adapts it
1342           to the target task using a few labeled examples.
1343
             • LASIUM Khodadadeh et al. (2020) uses latent space interpolation to generate tasks with
1344           pseudo-labels from a generative model. It then trains an energy-based model on each task
1345           and adapts it to the target task using a few labeled examples.
1346
             • SVEBM Kong et al. (2021) uses a symbol-vector coupling energy-based model to learn
1347
               from unlabeled data. It then adapts the model to the target task using a diffusion process.
1348
1349         • GMVAE Lee et al. (2021) uses a Gaussian mixture variational autoencoder to perform
               learning, and then adapts the model to the target task using a variational inference process.

       G.1    I NFLUENCE OF λ
1736
1737   This ablation study evaluates the effect of the hyperparameter λ in the self-motivated target. Recall
1738   that GeSSL explicitly models universality into self-supervised learning, and as mentioned in Section
1739   3.1 of the main text, universality involves two aspects, including: (i) learning universality, i.e.,
1740   the model fθ which learns universal representations during training, should achieve competitive
1741   performance on each task quickly with few data; (ii) evaluation universality, i.e., the trained fθ∗ , which
1742   has learned universal representations, should adapt to different tasks simultaneously with minimal
1743   additional data. Therefore, we hope that GeSSL can enable the model to achieve optimal results based
1744   on a few update steps. Our experimental setup constraints several conditions: (i) fast adaptation: keep
1745
       the update steps K of the inner-loop optimization in a small range of K ∈ [1, 15]; (ii) few data: use
       miniImageNet as the benchmark dataset, and follow the settings of few-shot learning experiments;
1746
       and (iii) performance evaluation: evaluate the effect of SimCLR + GeSSL, in addition to evaluating
1747
       the accuracy under different λ, we can also compare with the results of few-shot learning experiments
1748   (Subsection 5.5 and Table 4).
1749
1750   The results of the ablation experiment about “influence of λ” are presented in Table 5 of the main
1751
       text. Through further analysis, we derive two additional conclusions: (i) Combining with Table
       4 of the main text, regardless of the value of K, SimCLR + GeSSL consistently outperforms
1752
       SimCLR on miniImageNet, demonstrating the performance enhancement brought by GeSSL; (ii)
1753
       Considering Figure 2 of the main text, despite the introduction of universality constraints by GeSSL,
1754   the computational efficiency of SimCLR + GeSSL remains better than that of SimCLR, proving the
1755   efficiency improvement brought by GeSSL.

       G.2    M ODEL EFFICIENCY
1758
1759   This ablation study explores the efficiency of self-supervised models before and after applying GeSSL.

       From theoretical analysis, the key "optimal universality properties" for a metric in practical appli-
1871
       cations include: (i) the ability to accurately quantify subtle differences between distributions, (ii)
1872
       its utility in model optimization for stable and efficient convergence to the global optimum, (iii)
1873   applicability to various complex distributions, and (iv) computational efficiency. Accordingly, the
1874   superiority of KL divergence is reflected in three aspects Hershey & Olsen (2007); Goldberger et al.
1875   (2003); Shlens (2014), meeting these properties. Firstly, KL divergence is non-negative, and it is
1876   zero if and only if the two distributions are exactly the same, which is consistent with our intuitive
1877   understanding of difference Gong et al. (2021). It ensures the stability of KL divergence in handling
1878   subtle differences, meeting (i) and (iv). Secondly, KL divergence is a convex function, which means
1879   that optimizing it is more likely to converge to the global optimum, rather than getting stuck in
1880   the local optimum, particularly in high-dimensional problems Hershey & Olsen (2007). Thus, this
1881
       ensures that KL divergence meets (ii). Additionally, as an extension of information entropy, KL
       divergence quantifies information loss and uncertainty, making it effective across various applications
1882
       Goldberger et al. (2003), especially self-rewarding learning tasks, meeting (iii). In contrast, other
1883
       metrics have notable limitations. MSE, based on Euclidean distance, is sensitive to outliers and fails
1884   to account for non-negativity or normalization of probability distributions Marmolin (1986); Chicco
1885   et al. (2021); Lebanon (2010), limiting its effectiveness in (i) and (iii). Cross-entropy, a special case
1886   of KL divergence, struggles with continuous distributions or when the true distribution isn’t a one-hot
1887   vector De Boer et al. (2005); Botev et al. (2013), limiting its ability to finely measure complex distri-
1888   butions (i) and (iii). Lastly, while Wasserstein distance captures the overall shape difference between
1889   distributions, its high computational complexity and requirement for smoothness conditions make it
       less suited for high-dimensional cases Panaretos & Zemel (2019); Vallender (1974), hindering its

       fulfillment of (iv). Thus, KL divergence achieves the optimal balance between theoretical robustness
1891   and computational feasibility, aligning with the "optimal universality properties" and resulting in
1892   better model generalization and lower training costs.

       H     D IFFERENCES BETWEEN G E SSL AND M ETA -L EARNING
2032
2033
2034   In the main text, we have illustrated the differences between GeSSL and meta-learning and the
2035   advantages of GeSSL. In this section, we further elaborate on this and list different meta-learning
2036
       methods for comparison.
2037   Meta-learning Finn et al. (2017b); Wang et al. (2024b); Snell et al. (2017), often referred to as
2038   "learning to learn", has emerged as a prominent approach to improve the efficiency and adaptability
2039   of machine learning models, especially in scenarios with limited data. The fundamental idea behind
2040   meta-learning is to train models that can rapidly adapt to new tasks with minimal data by leveraging
2041   prior experiences gained from a range of related tasks.
2042   Few-shot Learning Khodadadeh et al. (2019); Jang et al. (2023): One of the primary areas where meta-
2043   learning has demonstrated substantial impact is in few-shot learning. Methods like Model-Agnostic
2044   Meta-Learning (MAML) Finn et al. (2017b) aim to find a set of model parameters that are sensitive
2045   to changes in the task, allowing for quick adaptation to new tasks with just a few examples. Variants
2046   of MAML, such as First-Order MAML (FOMAML) and Reptile Nichol & Schulman (2018), reduce
2047   the computational complexity of the original algorithm while maintaining competitive performance.
2048   Metric-based Approaches: Metric-based meta-learning methods, such as Matching Networks Sung
2049   et al. (2018) and Prototypical Networks Snell et al. (2017), learn an embedding space where similar
2050   tasks are closer together. These models perform classification by comparing the distance between
2051   new examples and a few labeled instances (support set) in this learned space, achieving remarkable
       results in few-shot classification tasks.


                                                           38
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


2052
       Memory-augmented Networks: Another line of research in meta-learning explores the use of external
2053   memory structures to facilitate rapid adaptation. Santoro et al introduced Memory-Augmented
2054   Neural Networks (MANNs) Santoro et al. (2016) that use an external memory to store and retrieve
2055   information about past tasks, enabling the model to perform well even in tasks with highly variable
2056   distributions.
2057
       Gradient-based Meta-learning: Beyond MAML, other gradient-based methods such as Meta-SGD
2058
       Li et al. (2017) and Learning to Learn with Gradient Descent have been proposed. These methods
2059   modify the way gradients are used during the training of the model, either by learning the initial
2060   parameters (as in MAML) or by learning the learning rates for different parameters, allowing for
2061   more efficient adaptation.
2062
       Bayesian Meta-learning: Bayesian approaches to meta-learning, such as Bayesian MAML Zhang
2063
       et al. (2021), offer a probabilistic framework for capturing uncertainty and improving generalization
2064
       to new tasks. These methods have been particularly useful in scenarios where task distributions are
2065   diverse, and the model needs to account for uncertainty in task inference.
2066
2067   Meta-learning for Reinforcement Learning: Meta-learning has also been successfully applied in the
2068
       domain of reinforcement learning (RL). Methods such as Meta-RL Yu et al. (2020) aim to train agents
       that can quickly adapt to new environments by leveraging the experience gained in previous tasks.
2069
       These approaches have shown promise in enabling RL agents to solve tasks with minimal exploration,
2070
       a crucial aspect for real-world applications where exploration can be costly or risky.
2071
2072   In summary, meta-learning has rapidly evolved as a versatile framework that enhances the ability of
2073   models to adapt quickly to new tasks, and operate efficiently in dynamic environments. Compared
2074
       meta-learning with the proposed GeSSL, we can see that the main difference is that meta-learning
       only considers transferability, and does not model discriminability and generalization. First, the
2075
       update of the outer model of meta-learning depends on the performance of the inner task-specific
2076
       model. Considering that the model is based on episode training mechanism, it is only based on one
2077   update on a specific task. Therefore, if the model update on a specific task is insufficient, then the
2078   outer model is likely to be difficult to achieve good results on the task, affecting the discriminability.
2079   Secondly, the generalization evaluation of the meta-learning model depends on its performance on the
2080   query set, which pushes the model to overfit on the training tasks, thereby diminishing the model’s
2081   ability to generalize.

       I   D ISCUSSION OF DATA I SSUES
2085
2086   First, we would like to point out that even if all mini-batch data are independently and identically
2087
       distributed (i.i.d.), it does not imply that the resulting tasks are homogeneous. On the contrary, under
       the i.i.d. assumption, task diversity can still be ensured in the following ways:
2088
2089
             1. Complexity of Data Distribution: The i.i.d. assumption does not require data to be simple
2090
                or homogeneous. The distribution can be complex, covering multiple classes and diverse
2091
                sample characteristics. For instance, in a multi-class task, data can come from various
2092            classes, with high complexity within each class. Therefore, even if the data are sampled
2093            from the same distribution, the distribution itself can be complex enough to ensure task
2094            diversity, reflected by a rich feature space (e.g., high-dimensional data or different input
2095            types).
2096
             2. Diversity Through Sampling: Each mini-batch can be composed of different samples
2097
                randomly drawn from the same distribution. This means that while the samples come from
2098            the same distribution, each mini-batch can have different sample combinations, with varying
2099            features and class ratios, presenting different learning challenges to the model.
2100
2101
             3. Data Augmentation: In many deep learning and self-supervised learning methods, data
                augmentation is used to create diverse training tasks. Even if samples are i.i.d., using different
2102
                augmentation techniques (e.g., cropping, rotation, color transformation) can provide the
2103
                model with diverse inputs.
2104
2105         4. SSL Task Construction: We treat each mini-batch as a multi-class task, with each original
                sample corresponding to a label. Thus, different tasks have different label distributions.


                                                          39
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


2106
                Even if samples within a task are i.i.d., the label distribution varies across tasks, ensuring
2107            diversity.
2108
             5. Theoretical Support: Theoretically, the i.i.d. assumption does not restrict task homogeneity.
2109
                Many theoretical works, such as Vapnik (1998) on statistical learning theory and Bengio et al.
2110
                (2013) on representation learning, discuss how samples drawn independently from the same
2111            distribution can train models while maintaining task diversity and achieving generalization.
2112            These studies show that even under the i.i.d. assumption, tasks can encompass different data
2113            patterns and diverse features.

2115   Even in cases of data scarcity or homogeneous tasks, not just data diversity, we have taken steps to
2116   ensure learning effectiveness:
2117
2118         1. Definition Perspective: In machine learning, data scarcity and homogeneous tasks can
2119
                lead to overfitting to specific tasks, causing the model to learn all information, including
                background, making it hard to adapt to other tasks. Therefore, if we constrain the model
2120
                to perform well on training data while maintaining effectiveness on unseen samples and
2121
                tasks, we can ensure its robustness. As mentioned in Section 3.1, the defined universality
2122            considers both learning and evaluation levels, covering discriminability, generalizability,
2123            and transferability, involving known samples, unknown samples, and unseen tasks. Thus,
2124            even with data scarcity and homogeneous tasks, this definition ensures that the model learns
2125            a universal representation to maintain effectiveness.
2126         2. Modeling Perspective: We further proposed GeSSL to model universality, including
2127            discriminability, generalizability, and transferability. As described in Section 3.4, for
2128            discriminability, GeSSL extracts key features from each mini-batch using limited data to
2129            achieve optimal performance. For generalizability, GeSSL ensures causal feature extraction
2130            during cross-task training. Finally, for transferability, GeSSL employs bi-level optimization
2131            to estimate the true task distribution from discrete training tasks. Thus, GeSSL models
2132            universality to ensure model effectiveness under limited data conditions.
2133         3. Empirical Perspective: Experiments across over 25 baselines, 16 datasets, and five settings
2134            for both discriminative and generative SSL demonstrate stable and significant performance
2135            improvements with GeSSL, including in few-shot and cross-domain scenarios. This empiri-
2136            cal evidence supports the effectiveness of our work in the face of data challenges.
