009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The delivery of free viewpoint videos (FVVs) is gaining popularity because of
012            their ability to provide freely switchable perspectives to remote users as immer-
013            sive experiences. While smooth view switching is crucial for enhancing user’s
014            experiences, FVV delivery faces a significant challenge in balancing traffic and
015            decoding latency. The typical approach sends limited viewpoints and synthesizes
016
               the remainings on the user, reducing traffic, but increasing decoding delays. Al-
               ternatively, sending more viewpoints reduces the delay, but requires more band-
017
               width for transmission. In this paper, we propose a novel FVV representation
018
               format, Free Viewpoint-Neural Representation for Videos (FV-NeRV), to address
019            this dilemma in FVV delivery. FV-NeRV reduces both traffic and decoding delay
020            even for content with a large number of virtual viewpoints by overfitting compact
021            neural networks to all viewpoints and pruning and quantizing the trained model.
022            Experiments show that FV-NeRV achieves a comparable or even superior traffic
023            reduction with a faster decoding speed compared to existing FVV codecs.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Free-viewpoint video (FVV) Guo et al. (2024); Tanimoto et al. (2011) is an emerging technique
028
      that allows freely switchable viewing experience even with the limited number of physical cameras.
029
      For this purpose, FVV generates video frames from any desired viewpoint utilizing a limited set of
030   texture and depth frames of multiple cameras and their positions Fehn (2004); Artois et al. (2023).
031   This technique enables us to create a new type of immersive experience in the field of, for example,
032   entertainment Amar et al. (2023), digital archive, and medical imaging Kitaguchi et al. (2024).
033
      Ensuring seamless view-switching between viewpoints is vital for enhancing user experiences; thus,
034
      users naturally demand as many viewpoints as possible. However, it is not necessarily the case
035
      that all viewpoints desired by users are pre-recorded, so there arises a need to synthesize and trans-
036   mit frames from viewpoints other than those actually recorded, using the frames from the recorded
037   perspectives. Although many existing solutions focus on the generation of high quality free view-
038   points Fehn (2004); Stankiewicz et al. (2018) from the limited number of physical views, we are
039   still faced with a dilemma regarding the practical use of FVV: balancing traffic and decoding delay.
040
      Depending on who actually handles the rendering of frames for the necessary viewpoints, we can
041
      consider sender-side rendering and user-side rendering. In the sender-side rendering, a content
042   sender with rich computational resources synthesizes the video frames from all the viewpoints on
043   demand in advance. We can conceal decoding delay from the users, but sending pre-rendered video
044   frames of all the viewpoints increases traffic proportionally. In contrast, in the user-side rendering,
045   the sender encodes video frames only from the physical viewpoints into a bitstream, distributes them
046   to users, and commits the users to synthesize desired viewpoints locally as they want. In this way,
047   we can avoid a rapid increase in traffic with an increase in the number of viewpoints. However,
048   a long decoding delay caused by complex rendering operations, such as 3D warping and hole fill-
049   ing, prevents real-time playback, that is, limited frame per second (fps), especially with the limited
050
      computational power on the user devices.
051   In this paper, we propose Free Viewpoint-Neural Representation for Videos (FV-NeRV), a novel
052   FVV format designed to effectively address the drawbacks of both the sender- and user-side ren-
053   dering. This approach is heavily inspired by recent developments in frame-based implicit neural
      representation (INR), or Neural Representations for Videos (NeRV) Chen et al. (2021); Kwan et al.


                                                        1

054
      (2023); Lee et al. (2023); He et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2024); Maiya et al. (2023); Xue et al. (2024);
055   Bai et al. (2023); Yan et al. (2024). FV-NeRV operates on the principle of sender-side rendering
056   while allowing all viewpoints to be transmitted to the user in an extremely lightweight manner, and
057   this lightweight characteristic is achieved by leveraging INR techniques to overfit the transmitted
058   content to a compact neural network. Experiments on the FVV dataset show that the proposed FV-
059   NeRV simultaneously outperforms existing FVV codecs in terms of traffic requirement and decoding
060   speed.
061   The contributions of our FV-NeRV are three-fold:
062
063          • While other INR-compression derivatives only consider a mapping from frame indices to
064            the corresponding frames, FV-NeRV introduces a novel approach by incorporating both
065            viewpoint indices and frame indices, enabling a compact neural network to overfit multiple
066            video sequences from different viewpoints simultaneously.
067          • Using both multi-view and temporal coherences, FV-NeRV efficiently compacts FVVs
068            across multiple viewpoints without a corresponding increase in model size.
069          • The compactness of the network reduces computational requirements, facilitating real-time
070            FVV decoding by avoiding compute-intensive view synthesis operations.

072   2     R ELATED W ORK
073
074   2.1   F REE V IEWPOINT V IDEO C ODING
075
076   Over the past decade, the first concept of free-viewpoint video coding has been designed in multi-
077   view video coding (MVC) in H.264/Advanced Video Coding (AVC) Ho & Oh (2007). In contrast
078   to single-view video, free-viewpoint video coding utilizes disparity information between viewpoints
079   and extracts matching points in different viewpoints to compute pixel-domain residuals followed by
080
      frequency conversion, quantization, and entropy coding. MVC+D Chen et al. (2014), AVC-based
      3D video coding (3D-AVC) Chen & Yen (2013) and High-Efficiency Video Coding (HEVC)-based
081
      3D video coding (3D-HEVC) Tech et al. (2016) extended the concept of MVC to the multi-view plus
082
      depth (MVD) video format. MVC+D separately encodes the texture and depth frames, whereas 3D-
083   AVC and 3D-HEVC jointly encode the texture and depth frames Zou et al. (2014). The latest video
084   coding standard, i.e., MPEG Immersive Video (MIV) Boyce et al. (2021); Vadakital et al. (2022), is
085   also designed for multi-view video plus depth format. MIV integrates multiple viewpoints of texture
086   and depth frames into a single 2D video, eliminating inter-view redundancy. The integrated texture
087   and depth 2D videos are encoded by any standard video codec. Some recent studies have designed
088   learning-based video coding solutions to take advantage of deep neural network architectures for
089   video compression. For example, some studies used deep convolutional networks (DCNNs) for
090   single-view video Hu et al. (2021), stereo video Chen et al. (2022), and FVV Yang et al. (2024).
091
      Especially, FICNet in Yang et al. (2024) has designed the learning-based texture, depth, and residual
      encoder and decoder for FVV sequences.
092
093   All the above-mentioned solutions are supposed to carry out view synthesis on the user side, i.e.,
094   user-side rendering, to generate the desired viewpoints from the decoded texture and depth frames.
095   In the proposed FV-NeRV, the video frames of the desired viewpoints can be reconstructed by feed-
096   ing frame and viewpoint indices to the trained INR architecture without view synthesis operations.
097
098   2.2   I MPLICIT N EURAL R EPRESENTATION FOR S IGNAL C OMPRESSION
099
      Recent INR architectures have been designed for image and video compression. The key concept
100
      is to compact a content of interest into a neural network through supervised learning to obtain an
101
      ”index-to-signal” mapping. The content sender first trains a network so that the network can take an
102   index and generate a corresponding signal of any kind, then sends the overfitted weights to a user as a
103   lightweight representation of the content. The user performs simple feedforward operations with in-
104   dices and corresponding features and reconstructs images. For video compression, early studies used
105   pixel-wise INR architectures Dupont et al. (2021; 2022), but recent studies have proposed frame-
106   wise INR architectures. Specifically, they feed the frame index and/or corresponding embeddings
107   to the network to generate a video frame. NeRV Chen et al. (2021) is the first work on frame-wise
      video compression, and there are many extensions of the NeRV architecture Kwan et al. (2023); Lee

141   3      FV-NERV
142
143   Fig. 1 shows the overview of the proposed FV-NeRV architecture. Let {I(t, v)}T,V
                                                                                     t=1,v=1 be an FVV
144
      sequence consisting of an RGB video frame I(t, v) ∈ RH×W ×3 with T frames and V physical and
145
      virtual viewpoints. Here, t, v ∈ [0, 1] are normalized frame and viewpoint indices, and H and W
146   are the height and width of the video frame. The proposed FV-NeRV architecture can be defined by
147   a mapping function f with learnable parameters θ from the frame and viewpoint indices t, v to the
148   corresponding video frame I(t, v) as follows:
149
150                                                 f : R2 −→ RH×W ×3 .                                                      (1)
151   The goal of the proposed FV-NeRV architecture is to obtain the indices-to-frame mapping for the
152   video frame of each desired viewpoint. For this purpose, we obtain the optimal function f (t, v; θ) ≈
153   f via network training with learnable parameters θ using the FVV sequence {I(t, v)}T,Vt=1,v=1 .
154
155   The trained parameters are then further compressed and sent to users as θ̂. Once users receive the
156   parameters, they can reconstruct the t-th video frames of the desired viewpoint v by feeding the
157   corresponding indices to the FV-NeRV architecture f (t, v; θ̂).
158
159   3.1    M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
160
161   FV-NeRV architecture f (t, v; θ) consists of a MLP and an upscaling module. The MLP part starts
      from positional embedding (PE) for both the frame and the viewpoint indices, which projects a


                                                                3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      single scaler onto a high-dimensional vector. The vector maintains the positional information of
163   the index throughout the video sequence. As proposed in Chen et al. (2021), we use a sinusoidal
164   positional embedding with the basis b and level l as follows:
165
166                   tpos = (sin(b0 πt), cos(b0 πt), · · · , sin(bl−1 πt), sin(bl−1 πt)) ∈ R2l ,
167                  vpos = (sin(b0 πv), cos(b0 πv), · · · , sin(bl−1 πv), sin(bl−1 πv)) ∈ R2l .           (2)
168
169   These embeddings are concatenated [tpos , vpos ] and passed to the successive fully-connected (FC)
170   layers. Finally, the output is reshaped to a 2D feature map m ∈ Rh0 ×w0 ×c , where h0 , w0 , c are the
171   height, width, and channel.
172   The upscaling module is made up of L upscaling blocks, implemented using NeRV blocks Chen
173   et al. (2021), which gradually enhance the resolution of the feature map. Specifically, the l-th block
174   first performs 2D convolution to increase channels by hl−1 × wl−1 × c · s2l , and then 2D pixel shuffle
175   to increase resolution by hl−1 · sl × wl−1 · sl × c, where sl is the scaling factor for the l-th block.
176   The feature map will have a resolution of h · s1 . . . sL × w · s1 . . . sL × c after L upscaling blocks,
177   and finally, the header layer of the 1 × 1 2D convolution projects the feature map to the final output
178   with the resolution of H × W × 3 pixels.

228   4.1   S ETTINGS
229
230   Dataset: We use an FVV dataset provided by Nagoya University Fujii (2013) and specifically two
231
      FVV sequences “Balloons” and “Kendo” in the dataset. This dataset provides RGB frame se-
      quences and depth image sequences for these two sequences. For both, the frame resolution is
232
      768 × 1024 pixels and the total number of frames in each viewpoint T is 300.
233
234   For every FVV sequence, we choose two physical viewpoints, synthesize 9 virtual viewpoints be-
235   tween the physical viewpoints, and utilize these sequences of 11 viewpoints as our own dataset for
236   the experiments. We use High-Efficiency Video Coding (HEVC) test model (HTM) software ren-
237
      derer Bossen et al. (2014) for virtual view synthesis using texture and depth frames. The dispersion
      of camera positions is 10 cm for the selected physical viewpoints and 1 cm for the synthesized view-
238
      points. When frame indices or view indices are required for frame reconstruction, we use normalized
239
      viewpoint indices {v|v = 0, 0.1, · · · , 1} and frame indices {t|t = 0, 1/300, 2/300, · · · , 1}.
240
241   Baselines: In our experiment, we consider two scenarios for how the desired viewpoints of an FVV
242   sequence are encoded, transmitted, and decoded: user-side rendering and sender-side rendering.
243
      We select the corresponding baseline schemes for each scenario as the competing method for our
      FV-NeRV.
244
245
            1. User-side rendering: This is the typical scenario for an FVV session. The sender encodes
246            the texture and depth image sequences for the physical viewpoints into a format and sends
247            them to a user. The user first reconstructs the encoded texture and depth frames and per-
248            forms view synthesis for all other viewpoints using the texture and depth frames. As a
249            baseline for this scenario, we use 3D AVC test model (3D-ATM) Vetro et al. (2013), an ex-
250            isting FVV codec that generates a bitstream from given texture and depth frame sequences.
251            3D-ATM uses differential encoding in both the temporal and inter-view domains to account
252            for motion and view disparity compensation.
253         2. Sender-side rendering: The sender uses the view synthesis renderer to synthesize all de-
254            sired viewpoints in advance and sends all encoded video frames to a user. For this sce-
255            nario, we use two types of NeRV derivatives as baselines. The first baseline is NeRV in
256            parallel (P-NeRV), where we simply train as many NeRV architectures as the number of
257            desired viewpoints. For a total of V viewpoints, P-NeRV prepares dependent networks
258            {f (t; θi ) : R → RH×W ×3 | i = 1, · · · , V } and overfits each network to each view as
259            single-view video coding. The second baseline is NeRV in series (S-NeRV), where we
260
               concatenate all frames of V viewpoints and treat them as a single-view sequence of length
               T V , that is, {t|t = 0, 1/3300, 2/3300, · · · , 1} and train them using a single NeRV archi-
261
               tecture f (t; θ) : R → RH×W ×3 to overfit the sequence.
262
263   More detailed parameter settings for P-NeRV, S-NeRV, and FV-NeRV are listed in Appendix A.2
264   and A.3.
