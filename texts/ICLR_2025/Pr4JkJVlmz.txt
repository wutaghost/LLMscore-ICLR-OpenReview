009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In reinforcement learning, model free methods such as Q-learning and policy gra-
012            dient are extremely popular due to their simplicity but require a huge amount of
013            data for training. Model based methods on the other hand, are proven to be sample
014            efficient in various environments but are unfortunately computationally expensive.
015            It is therefore only prudent to investigate and design algorithms that have best of
016            features from both these classes of algorithms. In this work, we propose MAQL, a
017
               model-assisted Q-learning algorithm that is not only computationally inexpensive
               but also offers low sample complexity. We illustrate its superior performance to
018
               vanilla Q-learning in various RL tasks and particularly demonstrate its utility in
019
               learning the Gittins/Whittles index in Rested/Restless Bandits respectively. We
020            aim to spur discussion on how model-assists can help boost the performance of
021            existing RL algorithms.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   The main goal of Reinforcement Learning (RL) is to solve sequential decision making problem
027   under uncertainty of the environment. There are broadly two approaches to solve the problem. In a
028   model-free approach one interacts with the environment over possibly multiple episodes and aims to
029   reach an optimal solution without building any model. In a model-based approach, one interacts with
030
      the environment to build a probabilistic model that best represents the real environment’s dynamics
      and then solves the resulting Markov decision process (MDP) using dynamic programming methods
031
      such as value Iteration or policy iteration.
032
033   Model-based RL algorithms are useful in scenarios where data collection is expensive or time-
034   consuming. One such popular algorithm is Posterior Sampling for Reinforcement learning (PSRL)
035   Osband et al. (2013). PSRL adapts a Bayesian approach to model uncertainty and maintains a
036
      distribution over possible transition models. It then uses these models to plan and take actions,
      which allows it to effectively balance exploration and exploitation by considering a range of potential
037
      models. A recent work Sasso et al. (2023) showcases a deep implementation of PSRL (PSDRL) with
038
      comparable performances to state-of-the-art methods on the Atari benchmark. As one might expect,
039   a key drawback of such model based methods is the huge computational cost involved in solving
040   every interim model during the planning phase.
041
042
      Model-free methods on the other hand are simple to implement and are computationally inexpensive,
      making them a popular choice among practitioners. Q-learning is one of the most popular value
043
      iteration based model-free RL algorithm and has been a topic of extensive study.Watkins & Dayan
044
      (1992) . Theoretical guarantees for a variant of Q learning with UCB exploration in an episodic
045   setting have been established recently in Jin et al. (2018). Other popular model free methods include
046   the policy gradient method Sutton & Barto (2018) where one optimizes a policy based metric (such
047   as value function for a policy) by searching over the policy space using gradient ascent.
048
      Empirical results Deisenroth & Rasmussen (2011) suggest model-free methods may require a large
049
      number of interactions with the environment to have a reasonably good performance. A tight sample
050                                                                                              1
      complexity analysis of Q-learning Li et al. (2023) unveils its horizon dependence to be (1−γ) 4 , which
051
      quantifies the negative impact of the widely studied overestimation bias (van Hasselt (2011)).
052
053   A natural idea to improve the sample efficiency of model-free RL algorithms is to include a model
      in the learning phase. This idea can be traced back to the Dyna algorithm Sutton (1991) which pro-


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      poses an integrated architecture for learning, planning and reacting. Dyna-Q builds a model from its
055   interaction with the environment. It generating samples from the model and then learns for free from
056   these additional samples, thereby improving upon the Q-learning algorithm. In the tabular setting,
057   empirical estimates of transition dynamics and rewards function corresponds to the maximum like-
058   lihood estimation Luo et al. (2022) as these are unbiased estimates of the true transition dynamics
059   and reward function.
060
      Subsequently, there have been many variants of Dyna-Q such as Dyna-Q+ (with exploration bonus),
061   Dyna-H (a heuristic planning algorithm based on Dyna) Santos et al. (2011) that have been pro-
062   posed. Fitted Q-iteration Ernst et al. (2005) yields an approximation of the Q-function by inter-
063   acting with the environment. Further works goes into finding a better function approximator for
064   the Q function.Riedmiller (2005); Castelletti et al. (2012); Tosatto et al. (2017) Empirical Dynamic
065   programming Haskell et al. (2013) introduces a class of algorithms using empirical estimation of
066   Bellman operator instead of stochastic approximation and proves convergence of these new random
067   operators to probabilistic fixed points. Kalathil et al. (2021) develops Empirical Q-Value iteration
068   which estimates Q function using empirical estimates of the Bellman operator and showcases faster
069
      rate of convergence compared to stochastic approximation based algorithms. These methods assume
      access to a simulator to query any state-action pairs which is an idealistic scenario.
070
071   To address these above mentioned problems, we introduce a framework combining the best of both
072   model-free and model-based approaches, MAQL model-assisted Q-learning. It involves using tran-
073   sition estimates to boost Q-learning updates for all visited state action pairs. This allows maximum
074   utilization of the model’s knowledge, instead of generating new samples. Our main contributions
075
      are summarized as follows:
076
             • We introduce MAQL, model-assisted Q-learning algorithm which combines model-free
077
               and model-based approaches to improve Q-learning, along with its deep counterpart model-
078            assisted DQN.
079
080
             • We showcase empirical performance of MAQL over Q-learning in RL environments like
               grid-worlds , benchmark gym environment T axi and show how its DQN counterpart out-
081
               performs DQN.
082
083          • Adaptive usage of MAQL in Q-learning based algorithms to improve learning Git-
084
               tins/Whittles index in Rested/Restless bandits settings respectively.

469
470   In this paper, we present Model-assisted Q-learning a framework combining model-free and model-
471   based methods to improve the sample efficiency of Q-learning on finite MDPs. We also showcase
472   similar success in model-assisted DQN over traditional DQN and its adaptive usage in learning
473   Gittins/Whittles index in rested and restless bandits setting respectively. The theme of these al-
474   gorithms is to improve the sample complexity of existing Q-learning methods as collecting data in
475   certain environments can be time-consuming and expensive. As our methods model the environment
476
      using exact parameters empirically, it is restrictive in |S| suffering from the curse of dimensional-
      ity. Using non-parametric approaches like Gaussian Processes for approximating environment or
477
      parametric function approximators like neural networks is a direction for future work. Using such
478
      model-assists for natural policy gradient style methods for policy evaluation instead of interacting
479   with the environment sounds interesting.
