009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Low-rank recurrent neural networks (RNNs) have recently gained prominence
012
               as a framework for understanding how neural systems solve complex cognitive
013
               tasks. However, fitting and interpreting these networks remains an important open
014            problem. Here we address this challenge using a perspective from the “neural
015            engineering framework”, which shows how to embed an arbitrary ordinary dif-
016            ferential equation (ODE) into a low-rank RNN using least-squares regression.
017            Under this perspective, individual neurons in a low-rank RNN provide nonlinear
018            basis functions for representing an ODE of interest. This clarifies limits on the
019            expressivity of low-rank RNNs, such as the fact that with a tanh non-linearity
020            they can only capture odd-symmetric functions in the absence of per neuron in-
021            puts or biases. Building on this framework, we propose a method for finding the
022
               smallest low-rank RNN to implement a given dynamical system using a variant
               of orthogonal matching pursuit. We also show how to use regression-based fitting
023
               to obtain low-rank RNNs with time-varying dynamics. This allows for the rapid
024
               training of vastly different dynamical systems that nevertheless produce a given
025            time-varying trajectory. Finally, we highlight the usefulness of our framework by
026            comparing to RNNs trained using backprop-through-time on neuroscience-inspired
027            tasks, showing that our method achieves faster and more accurate learning with
028            smaller networks than gradient-based training.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Recurrent neural networks (RNNs) provide a popular tool for analyzing the computational capabilities
035   of neural populations and the mechanisms that enable them to carry out complex cognitive tasks
036   (Miller et al., 2003; Barak, 2017; Mastrogiuseppe & Ostojic, 2018; Schaeffer et al., 2020; Duncker &
037   Sahani, 2021; Dubreuil et al., 2022). A substantial literature focuses on “task-driven” approaches
038   in which an RNN is trained to perform a particular cognitive task of interest, and then analyzed to
039
      determine what dynamics it uses to solve the task Mante et al. (2013); Sussillo (2014); Kanitscheider
      & Fiete (2017); Pollock & Jazayeri (2020); Turner et al. (2021). Specifically, this involves training
040
      an RNN to reproduce a target trajectory (or teacher signal) representing the task dynamic and
041
      interpreting it to infer the overall underlying flow-field. However, interpretation of such networks is
042   still a noteworthy problem of interest.
043
044   A wide variety of methods have been proposed for training such RNNs including: (1) reservoir
045
      computing methods, in which a fixed set of random recurrent weights generate a high-dimensional
      nonlinear dynamic, and training is applied only to output weights (Jaeger, 2001; Maass et al.,
046
      2002); (2) FORCE training, in which a set of random recurrent weights are adjusted using iterative
047
      low-rank updates via recursive least-squares Sussillo & Abbott (2009); DePasquale et al. (2018);
048   and (3) methods that adjust all recurrent weights using deep-learning inspired approaches such as
049   back-propagation-through-time (BPTT) Pearlmutter (1990); Sussillo & Barak (2013); Lillicrap &
050   Santoro (2019). Although recent literature has focused primarily on this latter class of gradient-based
051   training methods, they face a variety of challenges, including high computational cost, sensitivity to
052   initialization and hyper-parameters (e.g., learning rate, network size), and susceptibility to vanishing
053   and exploding gradients (Lukoševičius & Jaeger, 2009; Schuessler et al., 2020; Langdon & Engel,
      2022; Liu et al., 2023).

054
      Even once they are trained (via any of the above methods), interpreting RNNs to gain insight
055   into task performance remains challenging. Common approaches tend to rely on finding fixed or
056   “slow” points and then using dimensionality reduction methods to visualize projected flow fields
057   Sussillo & Barak (2013); Mante et al. (2013). However, fixed-point finding algorithms are difficult to
058   apply to high-dimensional systems, and it often unclear how accurately low-D projections reflect a
059   network’s true dynamics. Previous work has shown that task-trained RNNs with similar performance
060   can nevertheless exhibit different dynamics, raising the question of whether their solutions reveal
061   universal features of task computations (Barak, 2017; Williams et al., 2021).
062   One recent advance that overcomes many of these difficulties is a theory of low-rank RNNs (Mas-
063   trogiuseppe & Ostojic, 2018; Beiran et al., 2021; Dubreuil et al., 2022; Valente et al., 2022). Rather
064   than training a high-dimensional network and then attempting to visualize its behavior using low-D
065   projections, this literature has shown that a wide variety of tasks can be implemented directly in
066   RNNs with intrinsically low dimension, where the dimensionality is set by the rank of the recurrent
067   weight matrix plus input dimensions.
068   A parallel arm of research has focused on developing methods to embed low-dimensional quantities
069   into high-dimensional network activity (Eliasmith & Anderson, 2003; Stewart, 2012; Boerlin et al.,
070   2013; Abbott et al., 2016; Alemi et al., 2018). Of particular relevance to our work is the Neural
071   Engineering Framework (NEF) which determines connection weights between neurons to implement
072   desired dynamics through a structured encoding and decoding process. Specifically, each neuron
073   encodes a stimulus into a specific pattern of action potentials, using what are called basis functions.
074   These functions collectively represent the neural activity observed in response to the stimulus. Next,
075
      decoding is achieved through a process of learning a linear weight, optimized via regression, to map
      the activity back to the original stimulus value. By linking the stimulus to neural activity (encoding)
076
      and back to its representation (decoding), NEF effectively derives the weights necessary for neurons
077
      to perform complex operations such as integrating inputs, applying nonlinear functions, or generating
078   desired dynamical behaviors. Thus, this approach when applied to target trajectories, like FORCE,
079   teacher-forcing and neural ODE regression frameworks Sussillo & Abbott (2009); Heinonen et al.
080   (2018); Bhat et al. (2020); Hess et al. (2023), also alleviates issues associated with gradient based
081   training (in cases with known dynamics).
082
      In this work, we propose an NEF approach to provide an alternate view to low-rank RNNs which
083
      addresses interpretabiltiy issues of such models. Specifically, we develop flexible representations
084   through the construction of a randomized basis spanned by the low-dimensional dynamical system.
085   These basis functions are used as regressors to embed a target non-linear ODE in the low-rank RNN
086   using least-squares regression. Second, using this framework we provide empirical and theoretical
087   evidence regarding the representational limits of low-rank RNNs. In particular, through a geometric
088   interpretation of basis functions, we emphasize the necessity of neuron-specific inputs for embedding
089   odd-symmetric dynamics (a constraint also observed in general RNNs). Third, using a variant of
090   orthogonal matching pursuit we derive the smallest RNN that can implement any target ODE. Fourth,
091   we offer novel insights on the influence of time-varying inputs in embedding both autonomous and
092
      non-autonomous ODEs. Using this, we provide empirical evidence on validating theories such as,
      how similar trajectories can arise from significantly different dynamical systems, and underscoring
093
      the necessity of perturbations for differentiating between these systems. Lastly, we also apply our
094
      method to learn simulated ODEs which arise from a neuroscience binary decision making task,
095   thereby proving the effectiveness of our method.

150   3      A N ALTERNATE VIEW OF LOW- RANK RNN S
151
152   In the framework described above, training a low-rank RNN to produce a desired output z(t) from
153   an input u(t) requires learning the model parameters {N, M, I, W }, which is typically carried out
154   using back-propagation through time Valente et al. (2022). Here we present a different approach to
155   low-rank RNNs, which provides a more intuitive portrait of the network’s dynamical capabilities.
156   We begin by considering the problem of embedding an arbitrary low-dimensional dynamical system
157   into a low-rank RNN. Specifically, suppose we wish to set the model parameters so that z obeys the
158   dynamics of an particular nonlinear ODE:
159
                                                                     ż = g(z),                                                                              (5)
160
161   for some function g. We will then identify this output with the latent vector defining the network’s
      activity in the recurrent subspace: z(t) , (t). This implies that the dimensionality of the output is

      equal to the rank of the network, r = dout , and constrains the output weights to be the projection
163   operator onto the column space of M , that is, W = M (M > M ) 1 . We are then left with the problem
164   of setting the weights M , N , and I so that the latent vector, which we now refer to as z(t), evolves
165   according to (eq. 5). (Note however the model output need match the dimensionality of the latent
166   variable; in cases where desired output is lower-dimensional than z, the output can be expressed
167   as z0 = Az, where A is some fixed matrix of output weights that, for example, selects only one
168   component of z.)
169   For simplicity, we begin with the case of a scalar z. This corresponds to an RNN with rank-1 recurrent
170   weight matrix J = mn> , which is simply an outer product of weight vectors m and n. Assume that
171   the input is also scalar, and that the input vector I is orthogonal to m (although we can relax this

173   2022) (eq. 4), the network state can be decomposed as a time-varying linear combination of m and I:
174                                          x(t) = mz(t) + Iv(t),                                         (6)

        v + u(t). The fact that m and I are orthogonal means that we can write the dynamics governing

182   setting the model parameters so that
183
                                         g(z) + z ⇡ n> (mz + Iv(t))                                        (8)

185   To achieve this, note that the right-hand-side can be viewed as a linear combination of terms
186     (mi z + Ii v(t)) with weights ni , for i 2 {1, . . . , d}. Each of these terms can be viewed as a basis
187
      function in z for representing the target g(z) + z. More specifically, if is the hyperbolic tangent
      function, each such term is a shifted, scaled tanh function in z, where mi is the slope and Ii v(t)
188
      is the offset. This means that we can view the problem of embedding g(z) into a low-rank RNN
189
      as the problem of setting m and I to build an appropriate set of basis functions, and setting n so
190   that the linear combination of basis functions approximates g(z) + z. This approach formalizes the
191   connection between low-rank RNNs and the NEF (Eliasmith & Anderson, 2003; Barak & Romani,
192   2021), and shows that a low-rank RNN corresponds to a neural ODE with a single hidden layer (Chen
193   et al., 2018; Pellegrino et al., 2023; Pals et al., 2024).
194
      Already, this perspective makes an important limitation clear: if the inputs v(t) are zero, the basis
195
      functions are all odd-symmetric (that is, g(mi z) = g( mi z) for all z), crossing the origin only at
196   zero. (see Fig. 1C). Because z is also odd-symmetric, and the linear combination of odd-symmetric
197   functions is odd-symmetric, this means that in the absence of inputs, the network can only capture the
198   odd-symmetric component of g(z). A low-rank RNN is therefore not a universal approximator unless
199   it has inputs, or equivalently, different biases or offsets to each neuron (similar to general RNNs). If
200   the is instead taken to be ReLU, the problem is even more severe: each basis function is a linear
201   function with non-zero slope on either z > 0 or z < 0. Thus the network can only approximate g(z)
202   that are piecewise linear functions broken at the origin. (See SI Fig. SI-1).
203   If we set the filtered input to be the constant v = 1, we see that the problem of embedding an arbitrary
204   ODE in a low-rank RNN amounts to fitting the ODE in a basis of shifted and scaled basis functions
205   in z. To achieve this, we propose to sample the scales (elements of m) and offsets (elements of I) to
206   obtain a random basis, and then fit n by least-squares regression, namely:
207
          n̂ = ( (zgrid m> + I > )> (zgrid m> + I > )) 1 (zgrid m> + I > )> g(zgrid ) + zgrid ,            (9)

      5               N EW INSIGHTS INTO THE ROLE OF INPUT DYNAMICS
346
347   In Sec. 3, we demonstrated the importance of the presence of inputs in basis functions, B(m, I) =
348     (mz + Iv(t)). Now, we highlight the influence of the type of inputs (constant or time-varying), in
349   representing arbitrary ODEs. Specifically, our framework so far shows how autonomous ODEs (g(z))
350   are embedded via constant filtered inputs represented as v = 1, that do not evolve over time. Here we
351   extend our framework to embed non-autonomous ODEs (g(z, t)) by introducing a dynamical system
352
      that governs the evolution of v(t).
353   Recent work has suggested an identifiability issue in uncovering underlying low-dimensional dynam-
354   ics from high-dimensional neural activity, however the role inputs play in this hasn’t been explored
355   (Turner et al., 2021; Langdon & Engel, 2022; Liu et al., 2023; Qian et al., 2024). Furthermore,
356   previous work on time varying inputs (Rajan et al., 2016; Remington et al., 2018; Galgali et al.,
357   2023a) has focused primarily on whether observed trajectories were input driven or dynamics driven .
358
      We note here that this latter category can be subdivided into cases where the dynamics themselves are
      fixed in time or fluctuating due to inputs.

      6    C OMPARISON WITH BPTT FOR NEUROSCIENCE TASKS
447
448
      Finally, we apply our framework on two neuroscience inspired tasks (Wong & Wang, 2006; Sussillo
449   & Barak, 2013; Luo et al., 2023) and compare our networks against RNN models trained with
450   backprop-through time. Specifically, we implement:
451
452         1. 3-bit flip-flop task: The network receives a series of bits (-1 or +1) in 3 registers, and must
453            store the polarity of the most recent bit in each register (Sussillo & Barak, 2013).
454         2. Binary decision-making: Following previous literature, we model a sensory evidence
455            accumulation task using bi-stable attractors (Wong & Wang, 2006). Sensory inputs drive the
456            system from an unstable fixed point towards one of two stable fixed points, each associated
457            with a different choice (see Appendix B for details).
458
459   In Sec. 4, we find the smallest low-rank (r = 1) network (N = 10 neurons) that almost perfectly
460   fits a bi-stable attractor ODE. Here, we compare our model against networks of the same size (and
461
      rank) trained using BPTT (both trained and tested against a set of trajectories simulated from task
      specific ODEs). As shown, our method provides a closed form solution in one step, as compared to
462
      networks trained with BPTT which take an order of one/two more magnitudes to converge. In Fig. 5,
463
      panels B,C depict loss curves across a wide range of RNN models trained with BPTT. Consistent
464   with previous findings we observe lower training loss for larger networks, although they take longer
465   to converge. Additionally, low-rank networks (r = 1, panel C) have comparable performance for
466   tasks with lower dimensionality. Second, our method qualitatively has better reconstruction of test
467   trajectories (panel A). More importantly, our model significantly outperforms networks (order of
468   magnitude lower test mean-squared error) of the same size (and rank) on a set of test target trajectories
469   (panel D), thereby proving the efficacy of our method.
470
471   7    D ISCUSSION
472
473
      In this paper, we present an alternative view on low-rank RNNs, that emphasizes interpretability and
474   highlights the representational capacity of such networks. Specifically, we present an NEF inspired
475   framework that directly models the low-dimensional activity and transforms to neural activity space
476   via a fixed linear projection, which is learnt using linear regression (a result also highlighted in Beiran
477   et al. (2021)). Thus, similar to vector-field regression or teacher forcing algorithms Heinonen et al.
478   (2018); Bhat et al. (2020); Hess et al. (2023) our method also overcomes gradient-based training
479   issues (in cases with known/estimated ODEs), with additional advancements described below.
480   The focus of our work lies in defining a randomized basis which is used to embed an arbitrary
481   non-linear dynamical system. Specifically, through a geometric lens, we show how a low-rank RNN
482   constructs an ODE, as a weighted sum of shifted and scaled basis functions. This makes clear the
483   role that each neuron (single basis function) plays in the low-rank RNN, and also makes evident that
484   inputs are essential for capturing odd-symmetric functions. Thus, with the added advantage of being
485   highly interpretable, our results also clarify when such models (similar to RNNs) behave as universal
      function approximators (also highlighted in Beiran et al. (2021)).

      Another exciting finding of our work is using OMP to approximate the smallest RNN (from a basis
514
      set), which could be used to drive insights on hyper-parameter values for such models. Together, this
515   presents a promising future direction for studying how perturbing connectivity relates to unstudied
516   behavioral outputs, or guiding experimentalists on capturing neurons with characteristic neural
517   profiles (using OMP) for specific tasks. We also present novel findings on the influence of input
518   driven dynamics. Specifically, we directly link how non-autonomous ODEs can be embedded in low-
519   rank RNNs through time-varying basis functions. Thus, we link the RNN connectivity to these target
520   ODEs through dynamics along the input dimension and represent them via temporally evolving basis
521   functions. Empirically, we also validate that identical trajectories can be generated from trajectories
522   of moving fixed points, no fixed points or stationary target ODEs. The presence of dynamics in
523
      fixed points suggests a potential failure mode in current methods for interpreting such networks’
      dynamics Sussillo & Barak (2013); Smith et al. (2021). A potential future direction would be to asses
524
      how the conversion of non-autonomous dynamics to autonomous dynamics (which adds an extra
525
      dimension) influences insights on underlying brain circuit mechanisms. Lastly, we prove the efficacy
526   of our method by applying it to various neuroscience inspired tasks. We note our method outperforms
527   models of similar size/rank trained with BPTT.
528
529
      We conclude by discussing some limitations of our work. Our method relies on knowing (or estimating
      via finite differencing methods) the underlying latent dynamic. Additionally, the complexity of our
530
      framework lies in defining or estimating this underlying ODE. While in Sec. 5 we provide instances
531
      of embedding non-autonomous ODEs, arguably extending this to other higher order systems could
532   be tricky. This might be especially useful while modeling multi-region brain interactions, where the
533   state of the target ODE depends on another complex dynamical system as well. However, overall
534   we believe the new insights provided by our framework outweighs its limitations and provides an
535   exciting set of future directions.
