009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Ordinal classification (OC) is widely used in real-world applications to categorize
012            instances into ordered discrete classes. In risk-sensitive scenarios, ordinal confor-
013            mal prediction (OCP) is used to obtain a small contiguous prediction set containing
014            ground-truth labels with a desired coverage guarantee. However, OC models often
015            fail to accurately model the posterior distribution, which harms the prediction set
016            obtained by OCP. Therefore, we introduce a new method called Adaptive Posterior
017            Alignment Step-by-Step (APASS), which reduces the distribution discrepancy to
018
               improve the downstream OCP performance. It is designed as a versatile, plug-and-
               play solution that is easily integrated into any OC model before OCP. APASS first
019
               employs an attention-based estimator to adaptively estimate the variance of the
020
               posterior distribution using the information in the calibration set, then utilizes a
021            stepwise temperature scaling algorithm to align the posterior variance predicted
022            by OC models to the better variance estimation. Extensive evaluations on 10 real-
023            world datasets demonstrate that APASS consistently boosts the OCP performance
024            of 5 popular OC models.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Ordinal classification (OC) (Diaz & Marathe, 2019; Gao et al., 2017; Geng, 2016; Guo et al., 2008;
030   Can Malli et al., 2016; Huo et al., 2016; Wen et al., 2020) plays a crucial role in high-stakes domains
031   like healthcare (Liu et al., 2019) and finance (Manthoulis et al., 2020) by categorizing instances
032   into ordered discrete classes. Robust uncertainty quantification is critical beyond accurate point
033
      predictions to avoid costly or dangerous outcomes caused by prediction errors. To this end, various
      methods have been developed for estimating predictive uncertainty in deep neural networks, such as
034
      confidence calibration (Guo et al., 2017), MC-Dropout (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016), and Bayesian
035
      neural networks (Smith, 2013), but they lack formal guarantees. Conformal Prediction (CP) (Vovk
036   et al., 1999; 2005; Lei et al., 2018; Wen et al., 2020; Romano et al., 2020; Angelopoulos & Bates,
037   2021; Angelopoulos et al., 2021) addresses this gap by providing a distribution-free, post-processing
038   approach that generates prediction sets (PS) guaranteed to contain the true label with a specified
039   coverage probability, which generally design non-conformity scores to quantify the deviation the
040   degree between the model’s predictive outcomes and the data distribution.
041
      Recent works on OC demonstrate substantial benefits of assuming the underlying conditional dis-
042   tribution to be unimodal for OC tasks (Diaz & Marathe, 2019; Gao et al., 2017; Guha et al., 2024;
043   Belharbi et al., 2019; Cardoso et al., 2023). Some rely on label smoothing methods, which convert
044   one-hot target labels into unimodal prior distributions to be used as the reference for the training loss.
045   Some works learn a non-parametric unimodal distribution as a constraint optimization problem in
046   the loss function. In the unimodal context of ordinal classification, Ordinal Conformal Prediction
047   (OCP) (Lu et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2023) is designed to generate contiguous prediction sets using
048   the posterior distribution predicted by OC models. In contrast to Adaptive Prediction Sets (APS),
049   which calculate the scores by accumulating the sorted softmax probabilities in descending order,
050
      Ordinal-APS calculates the score by accumulating softmax probabilities of the contiguous prediction
      set with the minimum set size. However, the existing OCP methods neglect the possible variance
051
      misalignment of the OC models, which leads to inefficient PS.
052
053   In this work, we empirically observe the variance misalignment between the predicted posterior
      distribution and the oracle posterior distribution in a synthetic dataset. Specifically, a noticeable


                                                          1

054
      reduction in the size of PS is observed when we align the predicted posterior to the oracle posterior.
055   Further, our theoretical analysis supports the empirical findings by demonstrating a decrease in the
056   upper bound of the prediction set size as the predicted posterior approaches the oracle posterior.
057
058
      Inspired by our analytical findings, we introduce the Adaptive Posterior Alignment Step-by-Step
      (APASS), which serves as a plug-and-play component that can be integrated into any OCP framework
059
      to enhance its performance. The method consists of two key parts: 1) We introduce an attention-based
060
      estimator that adaptively estimates the variance misalignment of an input sample by examining
061   similar samples in the calibration set; 2) a stepwise alignment algorithm that optimizes the calibrate
062   the variance misalignment. The stepwise method can gradually amend the variance misalignment
063   and produce more compact prediction sets.
064
      To evaluate the effectiveness of APASS, we conduct extensive empirical assessments on real-world
065
      benchmarks, showing that APASS consistently improves the performance of OCP on 10 real-world
066
      datasets by 14.2% on average with 5 typical ordinal classification methods. The unstable performance
067   of non-stepwise alignment baselines highlights the superiority of consistent improvement.
068

070
071

078            OCP method on various OC models. Specifically, the empirical results show the superiority
079            of stepwise design to one-step baselines.

136   Probability calibration, also known as confidence calibration (Guo et al., 2017), aims to ensure that
137   the softmax probabilities predicted by neural networks accurately reflect the actual probabilities
138
      of correctness. To measure the degree of miscalibration, the Expected Calibration Error (ECE) is
      commonly used, quantifying the discrepancy between accuracy and confidence. ECE partitions the
139
      predictions into M equally spaced bins and calculates a weighted average of the difference between
140
      the accuracy and confidence within each bin. Formally, ECE is defined as:

146
      In prior works, it has been generally assumed that probability calibration improves the quality of
147   conformal prediction sets (Angelopoulos et al., 2021; Gibbs et al., 2023). However, the specific impact
148   of calibration methods on conformal prediction remains uncertain (Xi et al., 2024). Furthermore,
149   existing calibration techniques are not explicitly tailored to OC models, which often assume unimodal
150   distributions. The influence of these methods on OCP is thus still an open question.

203   in modern neural networks and finds post-processing methods like TS can effectively calibrate
204   predictions. Standard TS improves average calibration but reduces confidence for all predictions.

206   increase or decrease confidence as needed. Esaki et al. (2024) proposes an accuracy-preserving
207   calibration method using the Concrete distribution as a probabilistic model on the probability simplex,
208   which outperforms previous TS methods in accuracy-preserving calibration tasks.

233   4.1              M OTIVATION
234
235   Recent works on OC demonstrate substantial benefits of assuming the underlying conditional distri-
236   bution to be unimodal for OC tasks. To encourage unimodality, label-smoothing methods convert
237   one-hot target labels into unimodal prior distributions to be used as the reference for the training loss,
238   and some other methods use non-parametric unimodal distribution as a constraint in the loss function.
239
      However, these methods are optimized for accuracy, not posterior distribution discrepancy.
240   To investigate the impact of distribution discrepancies between the prediction posterior distribution,
241   p̂(y|x), and the oracle distribution, p(y|x). We first generated a heteroscedastic synthetic dataset,
242   then trained an OC model in this dataset. This allows us to access the Oracle distribution p(y|x) and
243   better mirror real-world data scenarios. Figure 2 (a) highlights the existing discrepancies: the OC
244   models tend to overestimate the variance when the actual variance is low and underestimate it when
245
      the actual variance is high. Moreover, the oracle prediction set has an average size of 8.54, contrasting
      with 9.62 for the set derived from p̂(y|x). This considerable difference highlights a critical variance
246
      misalignment problem, which compromises the efficiency of the prediction set size.
247
248   Inspired by the probability calibration method, we then employ temperature scaling to align the
249   prediction distribution to the oracle distribution. Specifically, we use a grid-search strategy to
250   minimize the discrepancy between the variance of the predicted posterior distribution and the variance
251
      of the oracle posterior distribution. In Figure 2 (b), we illustrate the aligned distribution. The results
      show a more closely aligned distribution, with the average size of the prediction set reduced to 8.84.
252
      These findings demonstrate that temperature scaling not only facilitates variance alignment but also
253
      enhances the efficiency of the prediction set, effectively resolving issues of variance misalignment.

      4.3   S TEPWISE P OSTERIOR A LIGNMENT WITH T EMPERATURE S CALING
365
366   The next question is how to determine the step size q. A straightforward solution is to find the optimal
367   q ∗ by minimizing the ECE as other probability calibration methods do, named Adaptive Posterior
368   Alignment by Confidence Calibration (APACC). However, we find this approach may harm the
369   efficiency of PSs in practice. Therefore, inspired by gradient descent, we propose a sstepwisemethod
370   that uses a small constant step size q but looks for the optimal steps reducing the PSs’ size.
371
      Corresponding to CP methods, APASS comprises 2 components: APASS-Calibration (Algorithm 1)
372   and APASS-Prediction (Algorithm 2), as illustrated in Figure 1. APASS-Calibration calibrates the
373   posterior distribution of calibration data predicted by the OC model using distribution discrepancy
374   measure (Eq. 12) step by step and outputs the steps of TS ŝ. The aligned distributions p̂APASS1:n
375   are then fed to OCP-Calibration to calculate the non-conformity score and the conformal threshold
376   τ̂ . During test time, APASS-Prediction will first estimate the distribution discrepancy of a testing
377   sample xn+1 as t(xn+1 ), then run TS(t(xn+1 )) for ŝ steps to get aligned posterior p̂APASS
                                                                                             n+1   , which
      OCP-Prediction will take to generate PS for the testing sample xn+1 .

527   6          C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this paper, we find the issue of variance misalignment in popular ordinal classifiers, which will
530   harm OCP. We empirically and theoretically show the efficiency of OCP can be improved if ordinal
531   classifiers predict a more accurate conditional distribution. Thus, we introduce the APASS technique,
532   which employs an attention-based variance estimator and stepwise temperature scaling to align
533   the posterior variance modeled by ordinal classifiers with better variance estimation. Empirical
534   evaluations on benchmark datasets demonstrated that APASS significantly enhances the performance
535   of OCP methods without the need for hyperparameter tuning, offering a robust framework for high-
536   stakes healthcare, finance, and beyond applications. Limitation of our methods is we only use
537
      variance to align the posterior, high-order moment such as skewness and kurtosis can be considered
      in future works.

830   B.1    S YNTHETIC DATASET
831
      We employed a method involving multivariate normal distributions and linear combinations to
832
      generate synthetic data for our experiment. Initially, we created a random mean vector and a
833   symmetric positive-definite covariance matrix to define the multivariate normal distribution. This
834   distribution is used to generate a dataset of features. We computed the output means and variances
835   by applying linear combinations of the generated features with randomly generated coefficients to
836   produce output values. Precisely, the means are calculated as a linear combination of the features,
837   while the variances are determined by squaring another linear combination of these features, ensuring
838   non-negativity. Finally, output values are sampled from a normal distribution using the calculated
839   means and variances, resulting in a comprehensive synthetic dataset for experimental analysis.
