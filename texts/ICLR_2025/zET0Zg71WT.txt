009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The introduction of the Transformer has brought about a revolution in AI. Central
012            to the success of the Transformer architecture is the self-attention mechanism,
013            enabling context dependence and long-range dependencies between tokens. Recent
014            work has drawn an equivalence between Hopfield networks, a kind of associative
015            memory model, and Transformers. In this work, we leverage this bridge, using
016
               Vector Symbolic Architectures (VSA), a brain-inspired computational paradigm
               capable of representing and implementing data structures, including associative
017
               memory models, to define a broad class of attention mechanisms catered for
018
               complex data types. In particular, we use Generalized Holographic Reduced
019            Representations (GHRR), an implementation of a VSA, as the foundation for
020            our proposed class of attention mechanisms. We show that GHRR is capable of
021            implementing attention and design a GHRR Transformer encoder architecture based
022            on the demonstrated mathematical equivalence. We propose a new kind of binding-
023            based positional encoding based on methods used in VSAs for encoding sequential
024            information. We extend the attention mechanism in our architecture to support
025            graphs, inspired by techniques used in VSAs to encode graph representations. We
026            evaluate the GHRR Transformer on language modeling, vertex classification, and
027
               graph classification tasks. Results suggest that our approach provides benefits in
               language modeling and graph classification tasks compared to baseline models.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The introduction of the Transformer Vaswani et al. (2017) brought about a revolution in AI, from
033   language modeling to vision to reinforcement learning Brown et al. (2020); Dosovitskiy et al. (2021);
034   Chen et al. (2021). Central to the Transformer architecture is the self-attention mechanism, enabling
035   context dependence and long-range dependencies between tokens.
036   Recent work has drawn an equivalence between Hopfield networks model with a modified energy
037   function Ramsauer et al. (2021); Hopfield (1982) and the self-attention mechanism. This equivalence
038   is two-way and thus enables cross-pollination; unique features of one side can be transferred and
039   applied to the other. For example, Ramsauer et al. (2021) applied the idea of repeated iteration
040   of the Hopfield update rule to the self-attention mechanism within a transformer, which facilitates
041   better memory retrieval. Consequently, one can use a similar strategy, exploiting the bridge between
042   self-attention and associative memory models, e.g. Hopfield networks, to develop a broader class
043   of self-attention mechanisms that can better handle data with more complex relations by utilizing
044
      associative memory structure.
045   In this work, we focus on Vector Symbolic Architectures (VSA), also known as Hyperdimensional
046   Computing (HDC) Kanerva (2009); Kleyko et al. (2023), as a candidate associative memory frame-
047   work for the extension of self-attention. While Hopfield networks rely on a dynamical update rule
048   for memory retrieval, VSAs, as algebras of high dimensional vectors, are capable of performing
049   associative memory operations purely based on their algebraic operators. That VSAs can perform the
050
      auto-associative capabilities of a Hopfield network and beyond motivates our choice for implementing
      and extending the attention mechanism.
051
052   In general, VSAs adhere to certain computational principles, including using high-dimensional vectors
053   with holographic properties (i.e. the same information is present in each part of the representation,
      in expectation) and supporting the algebraic operations of bundling and binding, which correspond


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      to the cognitive operations of memorization and association, respectively. Any algebra satisfying
055   these principles can be classified as a VSA. As a consequence, there exists a vast family of VSAs
056   Gayler (1998); Plate (2003). In particular, in this work, we make use of Generalized Holograph
057   Reduced Representations (GHRR) Yeung et al. (2024), an instantiation of a VSA to implement and
058   extend self-attention. As will be apparent below, the specific mathematical implementation of GHRR
059   suggests a natural parallel to self-attention in the form of binding key, query, and value hypervectors,
060   i.e. the VSA equivalent of memory retrieval in a Hopfield network. More importantly, as an algebra,
061   VSAs are endowed with natural compositional structure, which enables one to build complex data
062   structures from simpler constituents without any change in representational space Kleyko et al.
063
      (2020); Frady et al. (2020); Kleyko et al. (2022); Poduval et al. (2022). This property of VSAs, as we
      will develop in this work, provides a systematic method for one to extend self-attention to support
064
      more complicated data structures beyond sequences. While VSAs traditionally follow stringent
065
      mathematical constraints, here, we make use of VSA as a conceptual framework for designing
066   generalized self-attention mechanisms for structured data but allow for relaxation of the constraints.
067   Our contributions are as follows:
068
069
      1. We demonstrate the mathematical equivalence between the binding of key, query, and value
         hypervectors and the self-attention mechanism, i.e. that GHRR is capable of implementing
070
         self-attention.
071
      2. Using the compositionality of VSAs and their ability to represent complex data structures, we
072      extend the vanilla self-attention mechanism to one that naturally supports complex data types by
073      construction.
074   3. We propose a new kind of binding-based positional encoding based on methods used in VSAs for
075      encoding sequential information.
076   4. We verify our equivalence claim by evaluating a Transformer encoder with GHRR-based attention
077      (hereon referred to as GHRR Transformer) on a language modeling task and compare it to a vanilla
078      Transformer baseline.
079   5. We apply our methodology of extending self-attention using VSA principles to graph data and
080
         develop a GHRR Graph Transformer. We provide an interpretation of GHRR graph attention as
         performing a “soft” one-hop and evaluate our model on vertex and graph classification tasks.

157   3.2              V ECTOR S YMBOLIC A RCHITECTURES
158
159   Vector Symbolic Architecture (VSA), also known as Hyperdimensional Computing (HDC), is a com-
160   puting framework inspired by the brain. It is motivated by the observation that representations in the
161   brain are high-dimensional, consisting of neural activations of a large population of neurons Kanerva
      (2009). Moreover, while these population-level representations appear to be highly distributed and


                                                                                                                  3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      stochastic across different brains, they exhibit the same cognitive properties at a high level Kleyko
163   et al. (2023); Gayler (1998).
164
165   The fundamental unit in a VSA is a high dimensional vector, also called a hypervector, corresponding
166
      to the population-level neural activations. A hypervector H lives in some hyperspace H, e.g., RD
      for D large. The collection of hypervectors, along with some operators, forms an algebra over
167
      vectors. Generally, there are two types of hypervectors: (1) base hypervectors, which are generated
168
      stochastically, e.g., H ∼ N (0, I); and (2) composite hypervectors, which are created by combining
169   hypervectors via the operators of the algebra. These hypervectors can be compared via a similarity
170   function δ(H1 , H2 ). Generally, base hypervectors are generated such that they are quasi-orthogonal
171   with respect to the similarity function. The three main operations in VSA, bundling, binding, and
172   permutation, can be characterized by how they affect the similarity of hypervectors. We describe the
173   three operations below:
174
175   1. Bundling (+): Typically implemented as element-wise addition. If H = H1 + H2 , then both H1
176
         and H2 are similar to H. From a cognitive perspective, it can be interpreted as memorization.
177   2. Binding (⊙): Typically implemented as element-wise multiplication. If H = H1 ⊙ H2 , then H is
178      dissimilar to both H1 and H2 . Binding also has the important property of similarity preservation
179      in the sense that for some hypervector H3 , δ(H3 ⊙ H1 , H3 ⊙ H2 ) ≃ δ(H1 , H2 ). From a cognitive
180      perspective, it can be interpreted as the association of concepts.
181   3. Permutation (ρ): Typically implemented as a rotation of vector elements.                    Generally,
182      δ(ρ(H), H) ≃ 0. Permutation is usually used to encode order in sequences.
183
184   It is important to note that the description above of VSA is general; there are various specific
185
      realizations of VSA with the above properties. Figure 1A illustrates the VSA operations and its
      interpretations.
186
187   Crucially, with the recursive application of the operations above, one can encode, represent, and
188   query complex data structures such as sets, sequences, dictionaries, and graphs in the compressed
189   form of a single hypervector Kleyko et al. (2023); Poduval et al. (2022).
190

483   7     D ISCUSSION
484
485   Attention based on other VSA structure encodings.       In this work, we explored attention
      mechanisms based on VSA encodings of sequences and graphs. Of course, our approach is not just


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      limited to these two data structures; one can design a corresponding GHRR Transformer for every
487   kind of VSA data structure encoding, including that for trees Frady et al. (2020) and finite state
488   machines Kleyko et al. (2023).
489
490   Positional encoding extensions. Our proposed positional encoding based on the binding operation
491   in VSA depends solely on the index of the token. More complex variants of the encoding can be
492   explored; for example, one can consider a 2D positional encoding based on (x, y)-coordinates in
493   images Px ⊙ Py . As mentioned in the related work, one can also incorporate graph information Ying
494   et al. (2021) into the positional encodings.
495
496   Decoder Architectures. While we only considered a Transformer encoder architecture in this work,
497   our formulation extends naturally to a decoder model. One consideration when designing the decoder
498   model is how sequential generation can occur, given data of more complex types. Efficient attention
499   masking is also of practical concern when designing the decoder architecture.

501   8   C ONCLUSION
502
503   We have shown that among VSAs, GHRR is capable of implementing the attention mechanism in
504   the form of the binding operation, and develop a GHRR Transformer based on this equivalence.
505   We introduced a novel binding-based positional encoding and extended the attention mechanism to
506   support complex data structures based on VSA principles. In particular, as an example, we specify a
507   graph transformer architecture based on our framework and provide an interpretation for the graph
508   GHRR attention mechanism as performing a one-hop. We then evaluate our GHRR Transformer
509   variants on language modeling, vertex classification, and graph classification tasks.

      A Hopfield network is an auto-associative memory model; i.e. it retrieves memory items based on
651   the content of the memory itself (i.e. the keys and values are the same). It can be implemented as a
652   neural network that stores patterns {ξj }nj=1 such that ξj ∈ {−1, 1}d are attractors Hopfield (1982).
653   An input query ξ is passed into the network. The retrieved vector is determined by the update rule
654
                                                    ξt+1 = sgn(XX ⊤ ξt )                                    (11)
655
656   where X = [ξ1 , . . . , ξn ]. Under conditions such as sufficient separability of patterns (with respect to
657   dot product) and n being sufficiently small, ξ will converge to the closest pattern ξj . The capacity of
658
      the Hopfield network is O(d).
659   Ramsauer et al. (2021) extends the network to continuous states and introduces a new update rule
660   that is equivalent to the self-attention mechanism:2
661
                                                ξt+1 = Xsoftmax(βX ⊤ ξt )                                   (12)
662
663   Here, the patterns {ξj }nj=1 satisfy ξj ∈ Rd . Compared to the discrete network defined, the resulting
664   Hopfield network has exponential storage capacity.
665   Hopfield networks, as in attention, compute pairwise similarities across the entire set of inputs,
666   performing simple dictionary-like queries. For more complex inputs, however, we might wish to
667   encode structural information into the representation so as to achieve a more structure-aware query
668   operation.
