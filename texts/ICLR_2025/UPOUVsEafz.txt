009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Speech separation is a problem area where a mixture with overlapping speech
012            signals is the input and estimations of the clean speech signals which make up
013            the mixture is the output. In this paper we propose a novel sequence modelling
014            method called relative context and use it for a speech separation architecture called
015            RCSep.
016            The main advantages of relative context is that it does not require trainable pa-
017            rameters, is very lightweight and highly parallelized. The RCSep model which
018            heavily uses relative context is an extremely efficient source separation model. It
019            has less than 500k trainable parameters, lower memory usage and is significantly
020
               faster than all previous source separation methods while still maintaining high
               separation accuracy.
021
022
               Furthermore, we also used relative context instead of LSTMs in a current SOTA
               architecture which simultaneously improved separation accuracy and decreased
023
               computation time, memory usage and model size.

131   2     R ELATED W ORKS
132
133   As with most problem areas where pattern recognition is necessary, modern source separation sys-
134   tems rely on neural networks. In early deep learning research concerning source separation, the
135   separation approaches usually were based on the short-time Fourier transform (STFT) (Hershey
136
      et al., 2016; Kolbaek et al., 2017; Luo et al., 2018). The magnitude information of the mixture was
      used as the input of the neural network and corrected magnitudes for each estimation were calculated
137
      as the output. These new magnitudes alongside the mixture’s phase information were then used to
138
      return to waveforms using the inverse STFT. This approach, however, was limited by not changing
139   the phase information. The reason why changing the phase information is not as straightforward as
140   correcting the magnitude (Williamson et al., 2016) is due to the phase being the imaginary part of
141   the complex valued STFT while the magnitude is the real part.
142
      In order to remove this upper limit set by not changing phase information, time domain systems
143
      were proposed instead, initially in (Wang & Wang, 2015) and later in (Luo & Mesgarani, 2018)
144
      which set the foundation of current time domain source separation systems. The main advantage
145   of time domain based separation approaches was that it would not decouple phase and magnitude
146   information and just operate on the waveform directly instead.
147   Further improvements to the time domain based approaches include the dual-path method (Luo
148   et al., 2020a) as well as the use of Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) within the context of source
149   separation (Chen et al., 2020; Subakan et al., 2021). The main idea of the dual-path approach is to
150   split the input mixture into overlapping chunks and then stack these chunks on top of each other. The
151   neural network uses layers which are capable of capturing sequential patterns across the sequence
152   inside the chunks (intra-processing) as well as the sequence of the chunks (inter-processing). This
153
      allows for local and global pattern recognition and generally resulted in higher separation accuracy
      (Luo et al., 2020a; Chen et al., 2020; Subakan et al., 2021; Lam et al., 2021; Rixen & Renz, 2022b).
154
      In some more recent research, frequency domain separation methods (Wang et al., 2022; Yang et al.,
155
      2022) have been competitive with the best time domain methods (Rixen & Renz, 2022a; Jiang
156   et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2024; Mu et al., 2023; Yip et al., 2024) as working with
157   complex valued tensors is now supported in most deep learning frameworks. There have also been
158   some models which combine time- and frequency domain approaches (Rixen & Renz, 2022b; Lutati
159   et al., 2023) and reach SOTA performance.
160   However, all these methods are approaching the threshold value determined in Figure 1 which is why
161   finding more efficient models is becoming more relevant. Notable lightweight separation methods
      include the group communication method (Luo et al., 2020b) and small versions of certain models


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      like S4M (Chen et al., 2023). There are some other relevant methods like the TDANet (Li et al.,
163   2023), tiny SepFormer (Luo et al., 2022), small version of DP-Mamba (Jiang et al., 2024) and the
164   Sandglasset (Lam et al., 2021) which do have a focus on efficiency, but as they still exceed 2 million
165   trainable parameters they cannot be considered as lightweight as the previously mentioned models.
166   In fact, even the small version of S4M has almost 2 million trainable parameters, meaning there is a
167   severe lack of research for tiny models, with the only real exception being the group communication
168   paper. While the group communication method is extremely effective at lowering model size, other
169   efficiency metrics like speed and memory are still problematic and often even negatively effected by
170   using this method.
171   To summarize, current lightweight separation methods are not exceeding in every efficiency metric.
172   Some focus on memory usage (Lam et al., 2021) and speed (Li et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023),
173   others on model size (Luo et al., 2020b). In this paper, we propose the RCSep architecture which to
174   our best knowledge outperforms all previous methods in terms of speech and memory usage. The
175   model size of the RCSep is significantly smaller than all previous models at less than 500k trainable
176   parameters except for the group communication method. Separation accuracy of the RCSep is also
177
      improved in comparison to the previous lightweight models and even borderline reaches SOTA.
178   RCSep draws inspiration from some previous work, specifically the Sandglasset (Lam et al., 2021),
179   QDPN (Rixen & Renz, 2022a) and some of the hybrid models combining time- and frequency
180   domain approaches (Lutati et al., 2023; Rixen & Renz, 2022b). We did also test out the group com-
181   munication method in order to lower model size even further, however this lead to lower separation
182   accuracy and increases in memory usage and computation time. There are, of course some other
183
      proven methods for lowering model size and computational cost like weight sharing and quantiza-
      tion, however, these methods also tend to have a negative impact on accuracy. Since the model size
184
      of the RCSep is already extremely small at less than 500k parameters, we instead elected to keep its
185
      accuracy higher.

208   There are many input types where this idea is useful. For images, the raw values just describe how
209   bright the pixel is in the channel. If we apply relative context, however, we can instead get to know
210   how bright this pixel is in relation to its neighbours.
211   Generally speaking, most neural network architectures use a channel size that is much greater than
212   that of the original data input. The relative context operation makes use of this fact and splits its input
213   across the channel dimension into K subchannels. Each subchannel is then shifted by a different
214   amount. An example of this is shown in Figure 2 where both the channel size E and the number
215   of subchannels K are set to 5. The input tensor is split into 5 subchannels with the first row being
      shifted two elements to the right and the last row being shifted two elements to the left. This shifted


                                                                                     4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      tensor is then subtracted from the original to produce the output of the relative context operation.
217   Note, that for the subchannel where the shift is equal to 0, we simply copy the original input into the
218   output. Otherwise, we would delete information and have a row of zeros.
219
220
      The relative context operation is named after the idea that it delivers information about the current
      element in relation to its neighbours. It enables sequence modelling without adding trainable pa-
221
      rameters which is why it is very effective for lightweight models. One disadvantage of the relative
222
      context operation is that it links the channel size to the amounts of shifts that are possible. At most,
223   one can set K = E for the maximum amount of shifts. However, this would result in subchannels of
224   size 1. In our experiments, this never lead to optimal accuracy since some shifts are more important
225   than others, specifically the smaller ones which give context in relation to the direct neighbours.
226   Therefore, leaving them a greater subchannel size by keeping K relatively low usually is the better
227   choice for achieving greater accuracy.
228
      The relative context operation in its current form basically encourages the neural network to place
229   relevant information for the specific shifts into their corresponding channels.
230
231   Relative context can be applied across a single dimension for one dimensional data like audio or
232
      across multiple dimensions for data like images. Figure 2 shows a simple example of applying one
      dimensional relative context. Both the Figure 2 and the rest of the paper assume relative context to
233
      be bidirectional, however, it is easily possible to make it unidirectional and have it work for real-time
234
      applications.
235
236   Since the way relative context works is somewhat similar to convolutional layers, we also include
237   the option of setting a dilation factor to enable a stack of relative contexts to behave like tempo-
238
      ral convolutional networks (TCN) (Lea et al., 2016). The inclusion of the dilation factor is very
      straightforward, as one just multiplies the shift by the dilation factor to get the new shift.

241   4     RCS EP
242
243   The RCSep model uses a hybrid approach where a time domain model produces the initial estima-
244   tions which are then used alongside the input mixture for the frequency domain model to output the
245   final estimations. An overview of the RCSep architecture is shown in Figure 3.
246
247   4.1     T IME MODEL
248
249   The time model produces the first set of estimations. As the name suggests, it is a time domain
250
      based model. The initial estimations and the original mixture are later used for the frequency model
      to produce the final estimations.
251
252
      4.1.1    E NCODER
253
254   Similar to what was done in the Sandglasset and the QDPN model, we first segment the input
255   mixture into overlapping chunks with an overlap ratio of 50%. This temporarily doubles the tensor
256   size, however, as we use chunk size M as our channel dimension, we effectively halve the sequence
257   size L through this step, massively improving computational cost. In our testing, this step also
258   slightly increases accuracy. After the chunking step, the tensor is fed through a one dimensional
259   convolutional layer which increases the channel size from M to ET with ET being the channel size
260   of the time model. The kernel size and stride of the encoder are set to 1.
261
262   4.1.2    S EPARATION
263

266   layers which massively decreases computational cost and model size. The structure of the temporal
267   relative context network (TRCN) is shown in the purple box in Figure 3. For the time model, a depth
268   of 8 layers is chosen where the dilation factor increases from 20 in the first layer to 27 in the last
269   layer. The other difference is the usage of what we call MiniFormer blocks. Just like in the QDPN
      and Sandglasset, depthwise convolutional layers are used for downsampling and upsampling before

      4.2     F REQUENCY MODEL
328
329   The frequency model takes both the original mixture and the C estimations produced by the time
330   model as its input. Generally speaking, the goal of the frequency model is to correct these initial
331   estimations. The time model is responsible for most of the work and most of the computation. We
332   did, however, find that adding this frequency model was more effective for increasing accuracy than
333   using a bigger time model.
334
335   4.2.1    E NCODER
336
      The first step of the frequency model is to apply the STFT to the mixture and the estimations and
337   then concatenate them together. Specifically, the tensor shape for the frequency model is four di-
338   mensional unlike the time model which was three dimensional. These four dimensions are the batch
339   dimension, the time dimension of the bins, the frequency dimension of the bins and finally the chan-
340   nel dimension. Note, that since the STFT outputs complex numbers, the channel dimension for each
341   STFT has a size of 2, storing the real and imaginary values of the STFT. The STFTs are concatenated
342   in said channel dimension and fed through a two dimensional convolutional layer which expands the
343   channel size to EF which is the channel size of the frequency model.
344
345   4.2.2    S EPARATION
346
      As previously mentioned, the frequency model is much smaller than the time model. The separation
347   module, which makes up the bulk of the computation for either model, only consists of two TRCNs.
348   The difference for the frequency model is that said TRCNs are applying two dimensional relative
349   context across both the time and frequency axes of the bins and that the TRCNs have a depth of 10
350   instead of 8. In this case the dilation factor increases from 20 to 29 from the first to the last layer. In
351   our experiments, adding MiniFormer blocks or a U-net structure did not improve accuracy which is
352   why we elected to only use TRCNs.
353
354   4.2.3    D ECODER
355
      The decoder is a transposed two dimensional convolutional layer which returns the channel size
356
      from EF to 2 · C since we require two channels for each estimation for both the real and imaginary
357   parts of the STFT. These corrections to the estimations STFTs are then added to the original STFTs
358   before going through the inverse STFT to reconstruct the estimations as waveforms.

      5.1     DATASETS
363
364   We evaluated the RCSep model on two speech separation benchmarks, the WSJ0-2Mix (Hershey
365   et al., 2016) and the WHAM! dataset (Wichern et al., 2019). Both datasets are based on the WSJ0
366   corpus (Garofolo, John S. et al., 1993). The WSJ0-2Mix is a two speaker separation dataset without
367   background noise and without reverberation while the WHAM! dataset includes background noise.
368
      Each dataset contains 30 hours of training, 10 hours of validation and 5 hours of evaluation data.
369
      119 different speakers with roughly half being female and the other half being male are included.
370   Different utterances but the 101 same speakers are used for the training and validation sets while the
371   evaluation set has both different utterances and 18 different speakers than the training and validation
372   sets.
373

      would lower the sequence length. The channel size of the time model ET is set to 64. This means

378
      that the group sizes of all down- and upsampling layers is also equal to 64 since they are depthwise
379   convolutional layers. The stride factor of the two downsampling layers in the U-net structure is set
380   to 2 and 8, respectively. The kernel sizes of these convolutional layers is double that of their stride
381   factor. The kernel sizes and stride factors of the transposed convolutional upsampling layers are the
382   same as the corresponding downsampling layers.
383
      The MiniFormer blocks use down- and upsampling layers with a stride factor of 32 and a kernel
384
      size of 64. The group size is once again equal to the channel dimension, making them depthwise
385   convolutional layers. The number of subchannels K of the relative context operations is set to 7 for
386   the time model and 3 for the frequency model.
387
388
      For the STFT, the window size is set to 256 while the hop size is set to 64. While we have found
      this setup to be optimal for accuracy, it is possible to significantly lower computation time of the
389
      RCSep by lowering the window size to 128. This does lower accuracy a bit, but since it also halves
390
      the tensor size for the frequency model, it has a significant impact on both speed and memory usage.
391   Since the RCSep is already outperforming all previous models in these metrics, however, we elected
392   to prioritize accuracy but for actual deployment it might make sense to lower the window size to
393   128. The channel size of the frequency model, EF , is 64.
394
      We train the RCSep for a total of 200 epochs. For the first 100 epochs, we use a learning rate of
395
      1e− 3 and after the 100th epoch we halve the learning rate if the validation SI-SDR does not improve
396
      for 3 consecutive epochs. Gradient clipping with a maximum L2 norm of 5 is employed in order to
397   avoid the exploding gradient problem. The Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2017) is used.
398
399   We use the standard loss function for speech separation, meaning the SI-SDR. Since we have two
400
      sets of estimations, however, we also need to calculate two losses which are then summed up for a
      final loss before the backwards pass.
401
402   Aside from the RCSep architecture, we also tested a TF-GridNet variant, where we replace the
403   BLSTMs with one dimensional TCRNs with a depth of 7.

      6                             C ONCLUSION
521
522
523   In this paper we introduced the relative context operation as well as the RCSep architecture for
524
      speech separation.
525   Through the experimental results shown, it is clear that the RCSep has by far the lowest computa-
526   tional cost of any speech separation architecture while also matching or even outperforming previous
527   lightweight models in terms of separation accuracy.
528   We have further demonstrated the potential of the relative context operation by using it in a current
529   SOTA model, the TF-GridNet, instead of BLSTMs. This resulted in a slight accuracy gain, a 17% re-
530   duction in model size, a 10-15% speed increase and a 45% memory usage decrease during inference.
531   Therefore, using relative context instead of BLSTMs caused a significant drop in computational cost
532   while marginally increasing the separation accuracy.
533
      Additionally, we have experimentally determined a threshold value of roughly 25 dB SI-SDR at
534   which further improvement is irrelevant to separation accuracy. This means, that making separation
535   models more lightweight is the next most important task in this problem area. While the RCSep
536   models are still relatively far from this threshold, the modified TF-GridNet model does almost reach
537   it while being significantly more lightweight than the original. Furthermore, for other applications
538   such as human listening, the RCSep models already produce higher quality estimations than the
539   Conv-TasNet model whose estimations were rated almost on par with the original sources. We will
      provide audio samples at a later date.
