                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010            In this work, we tackle a challenging and extreme form of subpopulation shift,
011
               which is termed compositional shift. Under compositional shifts, some combina-
               tions of attributes are totally absent from the training distribution but present in
012
               the test distribution. We model the data with flexible additive energy distributions,
013
               where each energy term represents an attribute, and derive a simple alternative to
014            empirical risk minimization termed compositional risk minimization (CRM). We
015            first train an additive energy classifier to predict the multiple attributes and then
016            adjust this classifier to tackle compositional shifts. We provide an extensive theo-
017            retical analysis of CRM, where we show that our proposal extrapolates to special
018            affine hulls of seen attribute combinations. Empirical evaluations on benchmark
019            datasets confirms the improved robustness of CRM compared to other methods
020            from the literature designed to tackle various forms of subpopulation shifts.

023   1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025   The ability to make sense of the rich complexity of the sensory world by decomposing it into sets of
026   elementary factors and recomposing these factors in new ways is a hallmark of human intelligence.
027   This capability is typically grouped under the umbrella term compositionality (Fodor & Pylyshyn,
028   1988; Montague, 1970). Compositionality underlies both semantic understanding and the imaginative
029   prowess of humans, enabling robust generalization and extrapolation. For instance, human language
030   allows us to imagine situations we have never seen before, such as “a blue elephant riding a bicycle
031
      on the Moon.” While most works on compositionality have focused on its generative aspect, i.e.,
      imagination, as seen in diffusion models (Yang et al., 2023a), compositionality is equally important
032
      in discriminative tasks. In these tasks, the goal is to make predictions in novel circumstances that
033
      are best described as combinations of circumstances seen before. In this work, we dive into this
034   less-explored realm of compositionality in discriminative tasks.
035
036   We work with multi-attribute data, where each input (e.g., an image) is associated with multiple
037
      categorical attributes, and the task is to predict an attribute or multiple attributes. During training,
      we observe inputs from only a subset of all possible combinations of individual attributes, and
038
      during test we will see novel combinations of attributes never seen at training. Following, Liu et al.
039
      (2023), we refer to this distribution shift as compositional shift. These distribution shifts can also
040   be viewed as an extreme case of subpopulation shift (Yang et al., 2023b). Towards the goal of
041   tackling these compositional shifts, we develop an adaptation of naive discriminative Empirical
042   Risk Minimization (ERM) tailored for multi-attribute data under compositional shifts. We term our
043   approach Compositional Risk Minimization (CRM). The foundations of CRM are built on additive
044   energy distributions that are studied in generative compositionality (Liu et al., 2022a), where each
045   energy term represents one attribute. In CRM, we first train an additive energy classifier to predict all
046   the attributes jointly, and then we adjust this classifier for compositional shifts.
047   Our main contributions are as follows:

050            we prove that additive energy classifiers generalize compositionally to novel combinations
051            of attributes represented by a special mathematical object, which we call discrete affine
052            hull. Our characterization of extrapolation is sharp, i.e., we show that it is not possible to
053            generalize beyond discrete affine hull. We show that the volume of discrete affine hull grows
               fast in the number of training attribute combinations thus generalizing to many attribute

086   2    R ELATED WORKS
087
088   Compositional Generalization Compositionality has long been seen as an essential capability
089   (Fodor & Pylyshyn, 1988; Hinton, 1990; Plate et al., 1991; Montague, 1970) on the path to building
090   human-level intelligence. The history of compositionality being too long to cover in detail here, we
091   refer the reader to these surveys (Lin et al., 2023; Sinha et al., 2024). Most prior works have focused
092   on generative aspect of compositionality, where the model needs to recombine individual distinct
093
      factors/concepts and generate the final output in the form of text (Gordon et al., 2019; Lake & Baroni,
      2023) or image. For image generation in particular, a fruitful line of work is rooted in additive energy
094
      based models (Du et al., 2020; 2021; Liu et al., 2021; Nie et al., 2021), which translates naturally
095
      to additive diffusion models (Liu et al., 2022a; Su et al., 2024). Our present work also leverages an
096   additive energy form, but our focus is on learning classifiers robust under compositional shifts, rather
097   than generative models.
098
099
      On the theoretical side, recently, there has been a growing interest in building provable approaches
      for compositional generalization (Dong & Ma, 2022; Wiedemer et al., 2023; 2024; Brady et al.,
100
      2023; Lachapelle et al., 2024). These works study models where the labeling function or the
101
      decoder is additive over individual features, and prove generalization guarantees over the Cartesian
102   product of the support of individual features. While these works take promising and insightful first
103   steps for provable compositional guarantees, the assumption of additive deterministic decoders may
104   come as quite restrictive. In particular a given attribute combination can then only correspond to a
105   unique observation, produced by a very limited interaction between generative factors, not to a rich
106   distribution of observations. By contrast an additive energy model can associate an almost arbitrary
107   distribution over observations to a given set of attributes. Based on this more realistic assumption of
      additive energy, our goal is to develop an approach that provably enables zero-shot compositional


                                                                                 2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      generalization in discriminative tasks, where the model needs to robustly predict never seen before
109   factor combinations that the input is composed of.
110
111   Domain Generalization Generalization under subpopulation shifts, where certain groups or combi-
112   nations of attributes are underrepresented in the training data, is a well-known challenge in machine
113   learning. Group Distributionally Robust Optimization (GroupDRO) (Sagawa et al., 2019) is a promi-
114   nent method that minimizes the worst-case group loss to improve robustness across groups. Invariant
115   Risk Minimization (IRM) (Arjovsky et al., 2019) encourages the model to learn invariant representa-
116   tions that perform well across multiple environments. Perhaps the simplest methods are SUBG and
117   RWG (Idrissi et al., 2022), which focus on constructing a balanced subset or reweighting examples
118   to minimize or eliminate spurious correlations. There are many other interesting approaches that
119
      were proposed, see the survey (Zhou et al., 2022) for details. The theoretical guarantees developed
      for these approaches (Rosenfeld et al., 2020; Arjovsky et al., 2019; Ahuja et al., 2020) require a
120
      large diversity in terms of the environments seen at the training time. In our setting, we incorporate
121
      inductive biases based on additive energy distributions that help us arrive at provable generalization
122   with limited diversity in the environments.
123
124   Closely related to our proposed method are the logit adjustment methods Kang et al. (2019); Menon
125
      et al. (2020); Ren et al. (2020) used in robust classification. Kang et al. (2019) introduced Label-
      Distribution-Aware Margin (LDAM) loss for long-tail learning, proposing a method that adjusts the
126
      logits of a classifier based on the class frequencies in the training set to counteract bias towards
127
      majority classes. Closest to our work are the Logit Correction (LC) (Liu et al., 2022b) and Super-
128   vised Logit Adjustment (sLA) (Tsirigotis et al., 2024) methods that use logit adjustment for group
129   robustness. LC adjusts logits based on the joint distribution of environment and class label, reducing
130   reliance on spurious features in imbalanced training sets. Supervised Logit Adjustment (sLA) adjusts
131   logits according to the conditional distribution of classes given the environment.

      3.1   G ENERALIZING UNDER C OMPOSITIONAL D ISTRIBUTION S HIFT
136
137   In compositional generalization, we aim to build a classifier that performs well in new contexts that
138   are best described as a novel combination of seen contexts. Consider an input x (e.g., image), this
139   input belongs to a group that is characterized by an attribute vector z = (z1 , . . . , zm ) (e.g., class
140   label, background label), where zi corresponds to the value of ith attribute. There are m attributes
141   and each attribute zi can take d possible values. So z ∈ Z with Z = {1, . . . , d}m .
142   We use the Waterbirds dataset as the running example (Sagawa et al., 2019). Each image x has two
143   attributes summarized in the attribute vector z = (y, a), where y tells the class of the bird – Waterbird
144   (WB) or Landbird (LB), and a tells the type of the background – Water (W) or Land (L). Our training
145   distribution consists of data from three groups – (WB, W), (LB, L), (LB, W). Our test distribution
146   also consists of points from the remaining group (WB, L) as well. We seek to build class predictors
147   that perform well on such test distributions that contain new groups. This problem setting differs from
148   the standard problem studied in (Sagawa et al., 2019; Kirichenko et al., 2022), where we observe data
149
      from all the groups but some groups present much more data than the others.
150   Formally, let p(x, z) = p(z)p(x|z) denote the train distribution, and q(x, z) = q(z)q(x|z) the test
151   distribution. We denote the support of each attribute component zi under training distribution as
152   Zitrain and the support of z under training distribution as Z train . The corresponding supports for the
153   test distribution are denoted as Zitest and Z test . We define the Cartesian product of marginal support
154   under training as Z × := Z1train × Z2train × · · · Zmtrain
                                                                 .
155   In this work, we study compositional shifts that are characterized by:
156
157         1. p(x|z) = q(x|z), ∀z ∈ Z × .
158         2. Z test ̸⊆ Z train but Z test ⊆ Z × .
159
160   The first point states that the conditional density of inputs conditioned on attributes remains invariant
161   from train to test, which can be understood as the data generation mechanism from attributes to
      the inputs remains invariant. What changes between train and test is thus due to only shifting prior


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      probabilities of attributes from p(z) to q(z). The second point specifies how these differ in their
163   support: at test we observe novel combinations of individual attributes but not a completely new
164   individual attribute. The task of compositional generalization is then to build classifiers that are
165   robust to such compositional distribution shifts. Also, we remark that the above notion should remind
166   the reader of the notion of Cartesian Product Extrapolation (CPE) from Lachapelle et al. (2024).
167   Specifically, if a model succeeds on test distributions q(z) that have a support equal to the full
168   Cartesian product, i.e., Z test = Z × , then it is said to achieve CPE.

      4     P ROVABLE C OMPOSITIONAL G ENERALIZATION
208
209
      Our goal is to learn a distribution q̂(z|x) that matches the test distribution q(z|x) and predict the
210   attributes at test time in a Bayes optimal manner. If we successfully learn the distribution q(z|x),
211   then we can also predict the individual attributes q(zi |x), e.g., the bird class in Waterbirds dataset,
212   by marginalizing over the rest of the attributes, e.g., the background in Waterbirds dataset. Observe
213   that q(z|x) differs from the training p(z|x), which can be estimated through standard ERM with
214   cross-entropy loss. Since some attributes z observed at test time are never observed at train time, the
215   distribution learned via ERM assigns a zero probability to these attributes and thus it cannot match
      the test distribution q(z|x).

220   We present the generative approach as it allows to understand the results more easily. Building
221   generative models based on our theory is out of scope of this work but is an exciting future work.

471   7     C ONCLUSION
472
473   We provide a novel approach based on flexible additive energy models for compositionality in
474   discriminative tasks. Our proposed CRM approach can provably extrapolate to novel attribute
475   combinations within the discrete affine hull of the training support, where the affine hull grows
476   quickly with the training groups to cover the Cartesian product extension of the training support.
477   Our empirical results demonstrate that the additive energy assumption is sufficiently flexible to
478
      yield good classifiers for high-dimensional images, and that the proposed CRM estimator is able
      to extrapolate to novel combinations in DAff(Z train ), without having to model high-dimensional
479
      p(x|z) nor having to estimate their partition function. CRM is a simple and efficient algorithm that
480
      empirically proved consistently more robust to compositional shifts than approaches based on other
481   logit-shifting schemes and GroupDRO.

      A    F URTHER D ISCUSSION ON R ELATED WORKS
757
758
      In this section, we provide a more detailed discussion on the related works.
759
760
      Compositional Generalization Compositionality has long been seen as an important capability
761
      on the path to building (Fodor & Pylyshyn, 1988; Hinton, 1990; Plate et al., 1991; Montague, 1970)
762   human-level intelligence. The history of compositionality is very long to cover in detail here, refer to
763   these surveys (Lin et al., 2023; Sinha et al., 2024) for more detail. Compositionality is associated
764   with many different aspects, namely systematicity, productivity, substitutivity, localism, and overgen-
765   eralization (Hupkes et al., 2020). In this work, we are primarily concerned with systematicity, which
766   evaluates a model’s capability to understand known parts or rules and combine them in new contexts.
767   Over the years, several popular benchmarks have been proposed to evaluate this systematicity aspect
768   of compositionality, Lake & Baroni (2018) proposed the SCAN dataset, Kim & Linzen (2020)
769   proposed the COGS dataset. These works led to development of several insightful approaches to
770
      tackle the challenge of compositionality (Lake & Baroni, 2023; Gordon et al., 2019). Most of these
      works on systematicity have largely focused on generative tasks, (Liu et al., 2022a; Lake & Baroni,
771
      2023; Gordon et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2024), i.e., where the model needs to recombine individual
772
      distinct factors/concepts and generate the final output in the form of image or text. There has been
773   lesser work on discriminative tasks (Nikolaus et al., 2019), i.e., where the model is given an input
774   composed of a novel combination of factors and it has to predict the underlying novel combination.
775   In this work, our focus is to build an approach that can provably solve these discriminative tasks.
776
      On the theoretical side, recently, there has been a growing interest to build provable approaches for
777
      compositional generalization (Wiedemer et al., 2023; 2024; Brady et al., 2023; Dong & Ma, 2022;
778
      Lachapelle et al., 2024). In Dong & Ma (2022), the authors seek to understand the inductive biases
779   that enable generalization of a predictor beyond the support of the training distribution. The authors
780   target generalization to the Cartesian product of the support of individual features. The ability of
781   a predictor to generalize to Cartesian products of the individual features is an important form of
782   compositionality, which checks the model’s capability to correctly predict in novel circumstances
783   described as combination of contexts seen before. Dong & Ma (2022) developed results for additive
784   models, i.e., labeling function is additive over individual features. In (Wiedemer et al., 2023), the
785   authors considered a more general model class in comparison to Dong & Ma (2022). The labeling
786   function in (Wiedemer et al., 2023) takes the form f (x1 , · · · , xn ) = C(ψ1 (x1 ), · · · , ψn (xn )).
787
      However, they require a strong assumption, where the learner needs to know the function C that
      is used to generate the data. Lachapelle et al. (2024); Brady et al. (2023) extend the results from
788
      Dong & Ma (2022) to the unsupervised setting. Lachapelle et al. (2024); Brady et al. (2023) are
789
      inspired by the success of object-centric models and show additive decoders enable generative models
790   (autoencoders) to achieve Cartesian product extrapolation. While these works take promising and
791   insightful first steps for provable compositional guarantees, the assumption of additive labeling
792   function may come as a bit restrictive. In our setting, we take inspiration independent mechanisms
793   principle Janzing & Schölkopf (2010); Parascandolo et al. (2018). In the spirit of this principle,
794   we think of each factor impacting the final distribution through an independent function, where
795   independence is in the algorithmic sense and not the statistical sense. Hence, we model the data
796   generation with additive energy distributions. These additive energy distributions have also been used
797   in generative compositionality Liu et al. (2022a) but not in discriminative compositionality. Finally,
798
      in another line of work Schug et al. (2023), the authors consider compositionality in the task space
      and develop an approach that achieves provable compositional guarantees over this task space and
799
      empirically outperforms meta-learning approaches such as MAML and ANIL. Specifically, they
800
      operate in a student-teacher framework, where each task has a latent code that specifies the weights
801   for different modules that are active for that task.
802
803
      Domain Generalization Generalization under subpopulation shifts, where certain groups or combi-
804   nations of attributes are underrepresented in the training data, is a well-known challenge in machine
805   learning. Group Distributionally Robust Optimization (GroupDRO) (Sagawa et al., 2019) is a promi-
806   nent method that minimizes the worst-case group loss to improve robustness across groups. Invariant
807   Risk Minimization (IRM) Arjovsky et al. (2019) encourages the model to learn invariant representa-
808   tions that perform well across multiple environments. Perhaps the simplest methods are SUBG and
809   RWG Idrissi et al. (2022), which focus on constructing a balanced subset or reweighting examples
      to minimize or eliminate spurious correlations. There are many other interesting approaches that


                                                        15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      were proposed, see the survey for details Zhou et al. (2022). The theoretical guarantees developed
811   for these approaches (Rosenfeld et al., 2020; Arjovsky et al., 2019; Ahuja et al., 2020) require a
812   large diversity in terms of the environments seen at the training time. In our setting, we incorporate
813   inductive biases based on additive energy distributions that help us arrive at provable generalization
814   with limited diversity in the environments.
815
      Closely related to our proposed method are the logit adjustment methods Kang et al. (2019); Menon
816
      et al. (2020); Ren et al. (2020) used in robust classification. Kang et al. (2019) introduced Label-
817   Distribution-Aware Margin (LDAM) loss for long-tail learning, proposing a method that adjusts the
818   logits of a classifier based on the class frequencies in the training set to counteract bias towards
819   majority classes. Similarly, Menon et al. (2020) and Ren et al. (2020) (Balanced Softmax), modify
820   the standard softmax cross-entropy loss to account for class imbalance by shifting the logits according
821   to the prior distribution over the classes. Closest to our work are the Logit Correction (LC) (Liu
822   et al., 2022b) and Supervised Logit Adjustment (sLA) (Tsirigotis et al., 2024) methods that use logit
823   adjustment for group robustness. LC adjusts logits based on the joint distribution of environment and
824   class label, reducing reliance on spurious features in imbalanced training sets. When environment
825
      annotations are unknown, a second network infers them. Supervised Logit Adjustment (sLA) adjusts
      logits according to the conditional distribution of classes given the environment. In the absence
826
      of environment annotations, Unsupervised Logit Adjustment (uLA) uses self-supervised learning
827
      (SSL) to pre-train a model for general feature representations, then derives a biased network from
828   this pre-trained model to infer the missing environment annotations.

       D.2    C HOICE OF TEST PRIOR AND IMPORTANCE OF EXTRAPOLATED BIAS
1945
1946   In the implementation of CRM Algorithm 1, we have the following two choices; 1) we use the
1947   extrapolated bias B ⋆ (equation 10); 2) we set q̂(z) as the uniform distribution, i.e, q̂(z = (y, a)) =
          1
1948   dy ×da . We now conduct ablation studies by varying these components as follows.
1949
1950          • Bias B ⋆ + Emp Prior: We still use the extrapolated bias B ⋆ but instead of uniform q̂(z), we
1951            use test dataset to obtain the counts of each group, denoted as the empirical prior. Note that
1952            this approach assumes the knowledge of test distribution of groups, hence we expect this to
1953            improve the average accuracy but not the necessarily the worst group accuracy.
1954          • Bias B̂ + Unf Prior: We still use the uniform prior for q̂(z) but instead of the extrapolated
1955            bias B ⋆ , we use the learned bias B̂ (equation 8). This ablation helps us to understand
1956            whether extrapolated bias B ⋆ are crucial for CRM to generalize to compositional shifts.
1957
              • Bias B̂+ Emp Prior: Here we change both aspects of CRM as we use the learned bias B̂
1958
                and empirical prior from the test dataset for q̂(z).
1959
1960   Table 10 presents the results of the ablation study. We find that extrapolated bias is crucial for CRM
1961   as the worst group accuracy with learned bias is much worse! Further, using empirical prior instead
1962   of the uniform prior leads to improvement in average accuracy at the cost of worst group accuracy.
1963
       Further discussion on choice of test prior:
1964   Our algorithm allows the flexibility at test time to choose the prior over test groups as we see fit.
1965   That choice should be informed by what we might know or guess about the distribution over test
1966   groups, as well as what the metric of interest is. If we assume we can know nothing about the test
1967   group distribution, and we care about robust metrics invariant to changes in that distribution, such
1968   as balanced-group accuracy or worst-group-accuracy (WGA), then it makes sense to use a uniform
1969   prior over groups. This is what we did in most of our experiments. On the other extreme, if we can
1970   estimate the test group distribution and what we care about is average test accuracy, then we should
1971   use that estimate (which we call empirical prior) as the test prior. We explore these alternatives and
1972
       show the results in Table 10. As expected from the theory, the empirical prior achieves better average
       test accuracy, while the uniform prior performs better in terms of worst group accuracy.
1973
1974   Other choices of test prior are possible, depending on what we know and care about. For e.g. if we
1975   know some attribute combinations are impossible at test time, we can set their prior probability to 0,
1976   while e.g. keeping it uniform over all other possible combinations. Or if we assume that the marginal
1977   test distribution of each attribute will be close to its marginal train distribution, we can estimate these
1978
       marginals on the training set and define an independent test prior as their product. Exploration of the
       practical usefulness of such alternatives is left as future work.
