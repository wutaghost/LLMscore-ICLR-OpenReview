009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Molecular Relational Learning (MRL), focused on understanding interactions
012            between molecular pairs, is essential for drug design with both structural and
013            textual information, i.e., molecular structures and textual documents. However,
014            most existing MRL methods assume identical molecular distributions, failing in
015            the ubiquitous real-world scenarios involving new drugs with distribution shift,
016            which is mainly due to the reason that they heavily reply on variant correlations
017            between structures and texts regarding interactions that tend to change when new
018
               drugs or molecules come. To solve this problem, we investigate zero-shot MRL,
               by leveraging invariant relationships between molecular texts and structures w.r.t
019
               interactions in the course of time, which is largely unexplored in the literature
020
               and is highly non-trivial with the following challenges: 1) How to disentangle
021            molecular structure components between each pair that intrinsically determine
022            interactions, and address potential structural distribution shift issues for new drugs?
023            2) How to align molecular structures with semantic textual information to achieve
024            invariant molecular relation predictions for new drugs? To tackle these challenges,
025            we propose a novel Causally Disentangled Invariant Graph Large Language Model
026            for Molecular Relational Learning (C AL M OL), capable of exploiting invariant
027            molecular relationships to predict interactions for new drugs. In particular, we
028            propose Causal Molecule Substructure Disentanglement to capture the invariant
029
               well-recognized substructure pair for a specific molecule interaction. Then, we
               propose Molecule Structure and Property aware LLM Alignment to use molecule
030
               (with invariant substructure)-textual property pair to align structure information
031
               to semantic information, and utilize them together to guide the interaction pre-
032            diction. On this basis, LLM can also provide further explanations. Extensive
033            experiments on qualitative and quantitative tasks including 7 datasets demon-
034            strate that our proposed C AL M OL achieves advanced performance on predicting
035            molecule interactions involving new molecules.

038   1    I NTRODUCTION
039
040   Molecular Relational Learning (MRL), aiming to understand interactions between molecular pairs,
041   plays a pivotal role in advancing biochemical research with both structural and textual information,
042   i.e., molecular structures and documents. For example, in drug discovery, it is crucial to consider the
043   interactions between molecules, based on both their structural and textual properties (Chang & Ye,
044
      2024; Jin et al., 2020; Dou et al., 2022).
045   As the development of new molecules, such as drugs, accelerates, the challenge of evaluating
046   interactions involving these novel compounds becomes increasingly critical (Zhu et al., 2024).
047   However, most existing MRL methods assume identical molecular distributions and struggle in
048   situations where limited information is available about new molecules, including their relationships
049   with previously known compounds. A key problem is that they tend to rely on either variant
050
      molecular structures, which might dominate the molecular space (Yang et al., 2022), or associated
      textual information (Dou et al., 2022), leading to difficulties in prediction, especially when confronted
051
      with distribution shift and evolving information.
052
053   To solve this problem, we investigate zero-shot MRL, i.e., predicting relations involving new
      molecules, by leveraging invariant relationships between molecular texts and structures w.r.t interac-

      tions in the course of time. This is a largely unexplored area in the literature and presents several
055   highly non-trivial challenges:
056
057       • How to disentangle structural components between molecule pair that inherently determine
058         interactions and mitigate potential distribution shifts in molecular structures for new drugs?
059       • How to align molecular structures with semantic textual information to achieve invariant molec-
060         ular relation predictions for new drugs?
061
062   To address these challenges, we propose a novel Causally Disentangled Invariant GrAph Large
063   Language Model for Molecular Relational Learning (C AL M OL), capable of exploiting invariant
064   molecular relationships for predicting interactions involving new drugs. Our method leverages the
065   complementary strengths of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) for molecule structural learning and
066
      Large Language Models (LLMs) for text processing, information retrieval and integration (Lyu
      et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024), aiming to provide a more comprehensive understanding of molecular
067
      interactions across diverse scenarios, particularly when dealing with both known and novel molecules.
068
069   Particularly, we propose Causal Molecule Substructure Disentanglement to identify invariant, well-
070   recognized motif pairs that govern molecule interactions. This is achieved by decomposing molecules
071   into chemically coherent motifs and applying causal constraint along with Gumbel-Sigmoid Reparam-
072
      eterization masking method to disentangle causal motif-interaction information from the entangled
      molecular embeddings. Given the causal motif pairs obtained from the above module, we introduce
073

074
      (with invariant substructures) to semantic information, using the structure-property pairs, and further
075   incorporate them to guide interaction predictions. The motivation behind this is that different motifs
076   within a molecule may be responsible for various molecular properties. By focusing on the causal
077   motif pairs, we can encourage the LLM to identify relevant properties and make predictions based
078   on the causal motif and property, as depicted in figure 1. This approach also enables the LLM to
079   offer additional explanations for the interactions. Empirical validation across both qualitative and
080   quantitative tasks including 7 datasets demonstrate that our proposed C AL M OL achieves advanced
081   performance on predicting molecule interactions involving new molecules. Detailed ablation studies
082   further verify our designs. The contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:

      Molecular Relational Learning (MRL) seeks to predict the interaction (either classification or re-
101
      gression) between a pair of molecules used together. Since new molecules are continuously being
102
      developed and emerging, learning interactions involving these novel molecules poses a significant
103   challenge. To address this, we focus on this largely unexplored area by framing it as a zero-shot learn-
104   ing problem. During the training phase, interactions are observed among a set of known molecules.
105   In the inference phase, the goal is to predict interactions involving either a new molecule paired with
106   a known one or between two entirely new molecules. Formally, we define the task as follows:

      In this section, we introduce our C AL M OL in detail. Since it is difficult to directly decompose

150

153   module in section 3.1, then the molecule substructure & property aware LLM alignment module in
154   3.2, and finally sum up the entire training and inference procedure of C AL M OL in section 3.3.

      3.2   M OLECULE SUBSTRUCTURE AND PROPERTY AWARE LLM ALIGNMENT
242
243   This section is motivated by the observation that different molecular substructures are linked to
244   distinct properties, which, in turn, influence molecular interactions. For instance, in a drug molecule,
245   the primary structure responsible for therapeutic effects typically governs its key properties and
246   interactions with other molecules. Meanwhile, secondary functional groups, such as those that
247   impart hydrophilicity, may affect solubility but are not directly involved in the molecular reaction.
248   Understanding these specific roles allows the model to more effectively align substructures with
249
      molecular properties, enhancing predictions in molecular pair interactions.
250
      Molecule Structure Embedding. Given the original molecule graph pair, we first utilize the frozen
251
      GNN encoders to obtain atom-level representations of the original molecule graphs E1 and E2 , as
252
      well as the atom-level representations of their motifs, denoted as EUi for i ∈ [1, N1 ] and EVj for
253
      j ∈ [1, N2 ]. Next, we compute the importance matrix P ∈ RN1 ×N2 for motif pairs (Ui , Vj ) based
254
      on motif-interaction representations using the corresponding frozen importance calculator MLP, as
255   described in Equations 2, 3, and 4. Given the significant difference in importance scores within P,
256   we select the motif pair (Uc , Vc ) corresponding to the highest score pmax , and use their embeddings
257   EUc and EVc for further processing in the LLM pipeline.
258
259   Molecule Representation Projector. Given the atom-level representations E1 , E2 , EUc and EVc ,
260   the next step is to map them into the backbone LLM’s hidden space using the projectors fpro1 and
261   fpro2 . These projectors take essential responsibility for aligning GNN language E1 , E2 , EUc and EVc
262   into corresponding encodings Q1 , Q2 , QUc and QVc that are compatible with the LLM. Following
263   the approach of state-of-the-art vision-language models, we implement fpro1 and fpro2 using Querying
264   Transformers (Q-Formers), as in the works of Li et al. (2023a) and Dai et al. (2023). Specifically, the
265
      encodings are defined as
266                       Q1 = [qi1 ] = fpro1 (E1 ), QUc = [qiu ] = fpro1 (EUc ),                            (6)
267
                          Q2 = [qi2 ] = fpro2 (E2 ), QVc = [qiv ] = fpro2 (EVc ),   i ∈ [1, l]
268
269   with l representing the number of learnable query tokens in the Q-Former. The projectors, built on the
      BERT architecture, incorporate a cross-attention module between the self-attention and feed-forward

270
      layers, which enables complex alignment between molecule structural information, especially the
271   causal substructure pair, and molecule property. It also accommodates flexible input graph embedding
272   sizes, with learnable query token dimensions adjustable to match the token embedding size of the
273   language model. This architecture enhances effective interactions between multi-modal molecular
274   information during LLM inference.
275
276   SMILES Tokenization. SMILES tokenization helps distinguish molecules in a pair by providing a
277   unique, linear representation for each molecule. This structured notation not only maintains molecule
278   identity but also embeds their sequential order clearly. We utilizes SMILES for its widespread use
279   and precision, allowing the molecule’s information to link effectively with the LLM’s biochemical
280   knowledge. Additionally, the BRICS decomposition method in Section 3.1 enables us to derive
281   SMILES for molecular substructures, further aiding the model in recognizing the key components of
282   each molecule. Finally, C AL M OL directly input the four SMILES strings of G1 , G2 , Uc , Vc into the
283
      backbone LLM, leveraging the encoder to capture their tokenized representations R1 , R2 , RUc , RVc ,
      which ensures accurate molecule identification.
284
285
286   Choice of LLM. Following MolTC (Fang et al., 2024), C AL M OL utilizes Galactica, a decoder-only
287
      transformer based on the OPT architecture, as its core language model. Trained on an extensive dataset
      of scientific texts, Galactica excels in biochemistry, particularly in interpreting molecular sequences
288
      like SMILES and molecular property from various documents. This specialized information allows it
289
      to effectively capture key properties related to molecular structures and interactions. By leveraging
290   Galactica’s huge biochemical knowledge repositories, strong biochemical information integration
291   and inferential capabilities, C AL M OL can analyze and interpret the contextual interactions between
292   two basic molecular token sets, {R1 , Q1 , RUc , QUc } and {R2 , Q2 , RVc , QVc }.

      Training Procedure. The training process is divided into three steps:
325
326   1. The first step involves pretraining the Causal Molecule Motif-Interaction Disentangling module,
327      as outlined in Section 3.1. Directly training the full hybrid Graph-LLM model to embed molecules
328
         and extract causal motifs is highly resource-intensive and may impede the model’s ability to
         concentrate on specific structural causal learning tasks. To address this, we first pretrain this
329
         module independently, ensuring it effectively captures structural information and causal motif
330
         interactions. This enhances the overall performance of C AL M OL when integrated into the
331      subsequent training phases. After the pretraining of the module in Section 3.1, we freeze the
332      GNN encoders from Equation 1 and the importance calculator from Equation 4, to retain their
333      ability on structural modeling and causal motif pair identifying, then incorporate them into our
334      Graph LLM, C AL M OL.
335   2. The second step involves training the Molecule Substructure and Property Aware LLM Alignment
336      module, as detailed in Section 3.2. In this step, we adopt the frozen GNN encoder and causal
337      importance calculator obtained from the first step, along with the frozen backbone LLM. The
338      focus here is solely on training the projectors. This setup allows us to refine the alignment of
339
         (causal) structures and properties within the molecular embeddings without overloading the model
         with the complexities of the full architecture. By isolating the projectors, we ensure that they
340
         effectively map the GNN outputs to the LLM space, improving downstream performance.
341
      3. The third step involves fine-tuning the integrated model on specific downstream tasks, such
342      as DDI classification or SSI regression prediction. In this step, beyond further refining the
343      projectors to better suit the downstream task, we primarily focus on fine-tuning the LLM to adapt
344      to task-specific output formats. For instance, the LLM is trained to generate specific classification
345      sentences for DDI prediction or to produce precise numerical values for SSI regression. This
346      targeted fine-tuning ensures that the LLM effectively interprets and outputs results that align with
347      the requirements of each downstream task, enhancing both the accuracy and interpretability of
348      predictions.

      5      R ELATED WORK
502
503   Traditional computational methods for Molecular Relational Learning (MRL), particularly those
504
      based on Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) and Machine Learning (ML), primarily focus on molecule
      structure modeling. These approaches, however, are often limited by their reliance on spurious
505
      structural correlations and the inability to incorporate textual property information, which could
506
      provide critical insights. For example, GNN-based methods such as DDIPrompt (Wang et al. (2024)),
507   which utilizes graph prompt learning, and SSI-DDI (Nyamabo et al. (2021)), focusing on substructure-
508   substructure interactions for drug-drug interaction (DDI) prediction, model only molecular structures.
509   Other works like SA-DDI (Yang et al. (2022)) and DSIL-DDI(Tang et al. (2023)) propose domain-
510   invariant substructure interaction learning, addressing explainability and generalizability, yet still fall
511   short in integrating complementary textual information or handling distribution shifts.
512
      On the other hand, recent language model based approaches for MRL leverage the powerful
513   contextual understanding of large language models. However, these methods are prone to spurious
514   correlations in the textual data, which can lead to hallucination and inaccurate predictions. For
515   example, Zhu et al. (2024) explore zero-shot DDI prediction guided by textual drug description,
516   while MolTC (Fang et al. (2024)) investigates comprehensive molecular relational modeling using
517   language models and structure embedding from GNN. Despite their innovation, these approaches
518   still lack robust handling of structural information inherent in molecular graphs.
519   Our work addresses these limitations by studying Graph LLMs for zero-shot MRL, an area largely
520   unexplored in the literature. We propose a hybrid approach that leverages the complementary strengths
521   of GNNs for precise structure modeling and causal part disentangling, as well as LLMs for integrating
522   rich external information, enabling a more comprehensive and robust understanding of molecular
523   interactions.
524
525
526   6      C ONCLUSION
527
528   Most existing Molecular Relational Learning (MRL) methods assume identical molecular distribu-
529   tions, which fall short in the ubiquitous scenarios involving new drugs with different distributions. In
530   this paper, we study zero-shot MRL to predict molecular relations for new molecules, by proposing
531
      a novel Causally Disentangled Invariant Graph Large Language Model (C AL M OL), designed to
      leverage invariant molecular relationships for predicting interactions with new drugs. Specifically, we
532
      first propose Causal Molecule Substructure Disentanglement, designed to identify and capture the
533
      invariant, well-recognized substructure pairs critical for specific molecular interactions. Building
534   on this, we propose Molecule Structure and Property Aware LLM Alignment, to align molecular
535   structures, specifically those with invariant substructures, with their corresponding textual properties
536   to integrate structural and semantic information effectively. This alignment enhances prediction
537   performance and also allows the LLM to provide more detailed explanations based on the structured
538   alignment. Extensive experiments on qualitative and quantitative tasks including 7 datasets demon-
539   strate that C AL M OL achieves significant performance in predicting molecule interactions involving
      new molecules. In future, we leave extending our method to protein analysis for further explorations.
