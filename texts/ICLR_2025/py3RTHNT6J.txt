011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Remote sensing imagery from systems such as Sentinel provides full coverage of
014            the Earth’s surface at around 10 meter resolution. The remote sensing commu-
015            nity has transitioned to extensive use of deep learning models based on their high
016
               performance on benchmarks such as the ISPRS Vaihingen. Convolutional models
               such as UNet and ResNet variations are commonly employed for remote sensing
017
               but typically only accept three channels due to their development for RGB im-
018
               agery, while Sentinel satellite systems have more than 10. Recently, a number
019            of transformer architectures have also been proposed for remote sensing, but they
020            typically have not been extensively benchmarked and have only been employed
021            on rather small datasets. Meanwhile, it is becoming possible to obtain dense spa-
022            tial land-use labels for entire first-level administrative divisions of some coun-
023            tries. Scaling law observations indicate that substantially larger, multi-spectral
024            transformer models may provide a huge leap in the performance of remote sens-
025            ing models in these settings. In this work, we develop a family of multi-spectral
026            transformer models, which we evaluate across orders of magnitude differences
027
               in model parameters to evaluate their performance and scaling effectiveness on
               a densely labeled imagery dataset. We develop a novel multi-spectral attention
028
               strategy and demonstrate its effectiveness through ablations. We further show in
029
               this setting that models many orders of magnitude larger than conventional archi-
030            tectures such as UNet lead to substantial improvements in accuracy: a UNet++
031            model with 23M parameters results in less than 65% accuracy, while a multi-
032            spectral transformer with 655M parameters yields an accuracy of over 95% on
033            the Biological Valuation Map of Flanders. A link to open source code will be
034            provided in the camera ready document.

037   1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039   Remote sensing plays a crucial role in environmental monitoring, urban planning, disaster fore-
040   casting, and more by utilizing rich data from satellite and aerial systems. Processing this vast,
041   high-dimensional data poses significant challenges, especially with traditional, time-consuming, and
042   error-prone techniques. Recent advances in machine learning (ML), particularly convolutional neu-
043   ral networks (CNNs), have greatly enhanced the accuracy and efficiency of remote sensing analysis
044
      by automatically learning complex spatial and spectral patterns (Zhang et al., 2016; Li et al., 2018).
045   CNNs, originally developed for image tasks like classification, detection, and segmentation, have
046   been effectively introduced into remote sensing. For instance, Hu et al. (2015) demonstrated the
047   effectiveness of CNNs in hyperspectral image classification, achieving state-of-the-art performance
048   at that time. CNNs have since been applied to land-use classification, terrain change detection, and
049   urban planning.
050   Transformers, initially successful in natural language processing (Vaswani, 2017), have been
051   adapted for vision tasks, improving the handling of distant dependencies and complex spatial corre-
052   lations via attention modules (Carion et al., 2020; Han et al., 2022). Dosovitskiy (2020) introduced
053   the Vision Transformer (ViT), which outperforms CNN-based models on various image classifica-
      tion benchmarks, suggesting its potential for remote sensing tasks. Liu et al. (2021) designed shifted


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      window transformers (Swin-T), providing an energy-efficient variation of transformer. He et al.
055   (2022) later demonstrated their superiority over conventional CNNs in multi-modal remote sensing
056   data. These works highlight the importance of architectural choices in neural networks for remote
057   sensing.
058
      Despite these advancements, the scaling properties of neural networks in remote sensing remain
059
      under-explored. While larger models and datasets might intuitively lead to better performance, the
060
      relationship between model size, data size, and task performance in remote sensing contexts has
061   not been thoroughly investigated. Exploring these scaling laws (Kaplan et al., 2020) is necessary to
062   develop efficient and accurate models.
063
064
      In this paper, we first introduce the current state of research in remote sensing machine learning and
      point out the model and dataset mismatching issues that mainstream machine learning applications
065
      currently face. Then we propose a spatial-spectral module fused Swin Transformer backbone for
066
      multi-spectral segmentation tasks. We systematically explore the scaling laws in remote sensing and
067   demonstrate that our backbone achieves state-of-the-art performance by conducting an extensive
068   empirical study on a selection of neural network architectures. Furthermore, we investigate how
069   performance varies with model size, architecture selection, and training data size. Additionally, we
070   perform an ablation study to examine the impact of different components on the scaling behaviors
071   of exemplary simple architectures. Our results provide meaningful insights on how to scale neural
072   network models according to specific application needs in multi-spectral remote sensing tasks.

076
077   2.1   M ACHINE L EARNING IN R EMOTE S ENSING
078
079   Machine learning (ML) has significantly changed the remote sensing research with deep learning
080   methods, convolutional neural networks (CNNs) are growing to be central to processing and ana-
081   lyzing complex remote sensing data. Early applications of ML in remote sensing focused on using
082   traditional algorithms, such as support vector machines and decision trees, for simple tasks like land
083   cover classification and change detection. As more accurate and powerful tools and architecture
084   design choices come out in recent years, the implementation of CNNs has led to substantial per-
085   formance improvements in various complicated tasks such as image classification, object detection,
086   and semantic segmentation (Zhu et al., 2017). CNNs also showed effectiveness in processing hyper-
087
      spectral images and significantly outperformed traditional methods by learning hierarchical feature
      representations directly from data (Hu et al., 2015). For high-resolution satellite imageries, Mag-
088
      giori et al. (2016) has proven that tailor-made deep CNNs can handle large-scale remote sensing
089
      image classification and achieve state-of-the-art accuracies.
090
091   Transformer models have further reshaped and accelerated the advances in remote sensing research
092   with even better accuracy and abilities to handle complex input samples. Originally designed for
093
      natural language processing tasks, transformers were later adapted to vision tasks as Vision Trans-
      formers (ViT) by Dosovitskiy (2020), outperforming CNNs in some tasks by leveraging long-range
094
      spatial dependencies with attention mechanisms. Because of its superior performance on image
095
      classification tasks, ViT showed potential applicability to remote sensing tasks. Their work was
096   later extended by applying transformers to multi-modal remote sensing data, demonstrating archi-
097   tectural design choices are crucial for model performance across different data modalities and scales
098   (Aleissaee et al., 2023).
099
      Enlightened by the spatial attention mechanism in Transformer architectures, researchers have been
100
      trying to adapt this mechanism for multi/hyper-spectral remote sensing tasks actively. Hang et al.
101
      (2021) introduced spectral-attention-aided CNN models and proved that it is beneficial to include
102   spectral-attention modules into CNN backbones. Later work of Roy et al. (2021) demonstrated
103   that ResNet can be improved by incorporating spectral attention modules. Moreover, Zhong et al.
104   (2022) designed an spectral association block that focuses on establishing the connection between
105   different locations in the cuboid by calculating spectral association kernels using 3D convolutions.
106   Although spectral information is included in the training process, their methods are all specified in
107   sparse representations of spectral correlation but not learning from token-based to spectral band-
      based inter-channel attention.

      2.3   S CALING ARCHITECTURES ON MULTI - SPECTRAL REMOTE SENSING TASKS
163
164   Scaling laws demonstrate that increasing model size, data volume, and computing improves neural
165   network performance, as first formalized by Kaplan et al. (2020) and extended to generative models
166   (Henighan et al., 2020) and transfer learning (Hernandez et al., 2021). The CNN model architecture
167   can greatly affect its effectiveness in remote sensing tasks; determining hyperparameters such as
168   the depth, width, and input dimensions can often be up to the choices of engineers. EfficientNet
169   introduced by Tan (2019) pioneered architectural scaling by introducing a compound scaling method
170
      with a fixed set of hyperparameter coefficients. This approach yielded state-of-the-art results on
      multiple benchmarks with fewer parameters and reduced computational cost.
171
172   Meanwhile, Rosenfeld et al. (2021) examines how the performance of pruned neural networks scales
173   with model size, data volume, and compute resources, highlighting the importance of matching
174   model complexity with data to prevent overfitting and inefficient computation. Zhang et al. (2022)
175   further explored the relationship between architectural design and scaling laws, confirming that
176
      deeper and wider models tend to benefit more than shallow and narrow ones, suggesting the impor-
      tance of architectural design in optimizing model performances. Although scaling laws of vision
177
      transformers have been studied (Zhai et al., 2022), the problem in multi-spectral vision tasks could
178
      be influenced by the composition of different modules in models. Our study fills the gap between
179   their holistic analysis and multi-spectral cases.

275   3.3   E XPERIMENT SPECIFICATIONS
276
277   In this paper, instead of using an end-to-end training strategy, we choose to train the model with
278   griding and patching (Chen et al., 2014) by first griding the large training images into smaller cells,
279   then randomly sampling a certain number of cells to form up patches. This approach not only makes
280   it feasible to train on high-resolution images by lowering memory requirements but also increases
281
      the diversity of training data, as multiple patches are extracted from a single image. By focusing on
      local patterns within these patches or grids, the strategy enhances the model’s ability to learn fine-
282
      grained spatial features and improves overall generalization performance (Li et al., 2016). We use
283
      Overall Accuracy (OA) as our metric for model performance because it provides a straightforward,
284   global measure of how well the model correctly classifies pixels across all classes.
285
286   The scaling efficiency coefficient S quantifies how effectively a neural network scales its perfor-
287
      mance relative to the increase in parameters and computational resources. It is mathematically
      defined as:
288                                                       1
289                                          S=−
                                                            G
290
                                                     log P ×C
291   where:
292
293            • G is the Performance Gain Factor.
294            • P is the Parameter Count Scaling Factor.
295
               • C is the Computation Increase Factor.
296
297   The proposed coefficient provides a quantitative measure of scaling efficiency for neural networks.
298   By considering both the performance gain and the increase in the consumption of computational
299   resources (parameter count and computation increase), the coefficient evaluates how effectively a
300   model scales. A higher coefficient indicates that a model achieves a larger performance improvement
301   with relatively modest increases in parameters and training time, making it more efficient in scaling.
302   Conversely, a lower coefficient suggests that either the scaling process is inefficient or the model is
303   approaching the limit of diminishing returns. At this stage scaling the model further with significant
304
      resource investments results in only marginal performance gains.

309   GPUs. We adopted distributed data parallelism with shared gradient synchronization across the
310   GPUs, and the gradient accumulation technique was managed through the HuggingFace Accelerate
311   API, which allowed efficient distribution of the training load while reducing the memory require-

      4.1   M ODEL SIZE SHOULD MATCH DATASET SCALE
433
434   As Table 3 and Figure 7 show, when exposed to a small amount of data, small models, which
435   have fewer parameters, are more appropriate for scenarios with limited data. The small mod-
436   els are significantly more energy efficient than their larger counterparts, with virtually the same
437   level of accuracy scores. These models tend to generalize better and are less likely to over-
438   fit when data is scarce. As the dataset size grows, larger models begin to show their advan-
439   tages. With more data, these models can learn more complex patterns, which is evident from
440   the performance jump observed in large and extra-large models with increased training data.
441   The impact of increasing model size becomes
442
      more obvious with larger datasets. Smaller
      models reach their capacity earlier, while larger
443
      models continue to improve. We can also in-
444
      fer from the Figure 7 that there is still room for
445   further increase in MS-Swin and Swin models,
446   demonstrating that at this dataset size scale, it is
447   significantly more important to scale up model
448   size than to optimize the architectural design of
449   the network since even conventional architec-
450   tural choices such as ResNet can perform much
451   better than using smaller variations.

      5     C ONCLUSIONS AND LIMITATIONS
                                                          Figure 7: The accuracy curves of models vs. train-
455                                                       ing data size.
456   In this paper, we have explored the scaling laws
457   governing neural networks applied to large-
458   scale, multi-spectral remote sensing tasks. Our extensive experiments on the Biological Valuation
459
      Map (BVM) of Flanders—a densely labeled, large-scale dataset—demonstrated that larger models
      with appropriate architectural choices significantly outperform traditional architectures like UNet++
460
      and ResNet. By introducing a Spectral Dependency Module (SDM) integrated into a Swin Trans-
461
      former architecture, we developed a Multi-Spectral Swin Transformer (MS-Swin) model that ef-
462   fectively captures spectral dependencies in multi-spectral data. Specifically, we showed that our
463   MS-Swin models achieve superior accuracy and scaling efficiency, with models containing hun-
464   dreds of millions of parameters yielding over 96% accuracy, while smaller conventional models lag
465   behind. These findings underscore the importance of matching model complexity to dataset scale
466   and leveraging advanced architectural designs to harness the full potential of large multi-spectral
467   datasets.
468   Despite the promising results, our study has several limitations. Firstly, our experiments were con-
469   ducted solely on the BVM dataset, which is restricted to the Belgian Flemish region. To generalize
470   our findings, it is necessary to test the proposed models on a broader range of land regions in different
471   countries, leveraging multi-spectral datasets of similar scale, which governments and organizations
472   are increasingly capable of providing. Secondly, due to computational resource constraints, we were
473   unable to further scale up the size of the MS-Swin models. Future work could explore even larger
474   models, potentially requiring inter-node training setups, although this would introduce additional
475   computational overhead that must be carefully managed. Lastly, while we focused on comparing
476
      our models with standard architectures like ResNet and UNet++, there exists a wide variety of state-
      of-the-art models, including other large transformers and conventional models. Incorporating and
477
      testing the Spectral Dependency Module within these architectures could provide deeper insights
478
      into their scaling behaviors and further consolidate the configurations of SDM.

909   The table presents various model combinations using convolutional blocks (C), multi-head self-
910   attention blocks (A), and spectral dependency modules (D). The performance reflects the trends

912   blocks (spectral dependency).
913   Adding more convolutional blocks steadily improves model performance in the beginning, but the
914   benefit diminishes when convolutional blocks number is high. The base C model achieves an accu-
915   racy of 65.20% on 100% of the data. Adding more convolutional blocks improves accuracy, with
916   CC reaching 66.20% and CCC achieving 67.95%. However, the improvement from CCC to CCCC
917   is marginal, showing only a 0.05% increase at 100% data, indicating diminishing returns after three
      convolutional layers.
