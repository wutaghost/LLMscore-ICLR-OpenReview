011                                                   A BSTRACT
012
013                Although binary classification is a well-studied problem, training reliable classi-
014                fiers under severe class imbalance remains a challenge. Recent techniques mit-
015                igate the ill effects of imbalance on training by modifying the loss functions or
016                optimization methods. We observe that different hyperparameter values on these
017
                   loss functions perform better at different recall values. We propose to exploit this
                   fact by training one model over a distribution of hyperparameter values–instead
018
                   of a single value–via Loss Conditional Training (LCT). Experiments show that
019
                   training over a distribution of hyperparameters not only approximates the perfor-
020                mance of several models but actually improves the overall performance of models
021                on both CIFAR and real medical imaging applications, such as melanoma and
022                diabetic retinopathy detection. Furthermore, training models with LCT is more
023                efficient because some hyperparameter tuning can be conducted after training to
024                meet individual needs without needing to retrain from scratch. Code will be made
025                available upon acceptance of this paper.

028   1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Consider a classifier that takes images of skin lesions and predicts whether they are melanoma or
031   benign (Rotemberg et al., 2020). Such a system could be especially valuable in underdeveloped
032   countries where expert resources for screening are scarce (Cassidy et al., 2022). The dataset for
033
      this problem, along with many other practical problems, is inherently imbalanced (i.e., there are
      far more benign samples than melanoma samples). Furthermore, there are un-even costs associated
034
      with misclassifying the two classes because predicting a benign lesion as melanoma would result
035
      in the cost of a biopsy while predicting a melanoma lesion as benign could result in the melanoma
036   spreading before the patient can receive appropriate treatment. Unfortunately, the exact difference
037   in the misclassification costs may not be known a priori and may even change after deployment.
038   For example, the costs may change depending on the amount of biopsy resources available or the
039   prior may change depending on the age and condition of the patient. Thus, a good classifier for this
040   problem should (a) have good performance across a wide range of Precision-Recall tradeoffs and
041   (b) be able to adapt to changes in the prior or misclassification costs.
042   Recently proposed methods mitigate the effects of training under class imbalance by using special-
043   ized loss functions and optimization techniques, which give more weight to the minority classes and
044   promote better generalization (Du et al., 2023a; Kini et al., 2021; Rangwani et al., 2022). While this
045   work has led to significant improvements in the balanced accuracy in the multi-class case, less work
046   has focused on how to adapt and maintain good accuracy across a wide range of Precision-Recall
047   tradeoffs simultaneously.
048   To fill this gap, we examine several state-of-the-art methods from the class imbalance literature for
049   binary classification problems with severe imbalance. Since these problems are binary, we analyze
050   these methods using more detailed metrics, such as Precision-Recall curves, which plot the tradeoff
051   between recall and precision over a range of classification thresholds.1 . This analysis reveals a
052   consistent finding across many methods: Specifically, we observe that, on the same dataset and with
053

      the same method, different hyperparameter values perform better at different recalls. For example,
055   hyperparameter value A may give the best precision when the recall is 0.5, value B gives the best
056   precision when recall is 0.8, and value C gives the best precision when the recall is 0.99. While
057   this fact might prima facie seem only to complicate the choice of hyperparameter, it also suggests a
058   natural question: can we train one model that achieves the best performance over all recalls?
059
      To answer this question, we train a single model over a distribution of loss-function-hyperparameter
060
      values, instead of a single value. We do so using Loss Conditional Training (LCT), which was
061   previously shown to approximate several similar models with one model on non-classification tasks
062   (Dosovitskiy & Djolonga, 2020). Surprisingly, we find that on imbalanced classification problems,
063   one model trained over a distribution of hyperparameters with LCT not only approximates the per-
064   formance of several models trained with different hyperparameter values, but actually improves
065   the performance at all recalls. This is in contrast to previous work on different applications (e.g.,
066   neural image compression) which found that LCT models incurred a small performance penalty in
067   exchange for better computational efficiency (Dosovitskiy & Djolonga, 2020).
068   We apply LCT to several existing methods for training on imbalanced data and find that it improves
069   performance on a variety of severely imbalanced datasets including binary versions of CIFAR-
070   10/100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009), SIIM-ISIC Melanoma (Zawacki et al., 2020), and APTOS Diabetic
071   Retinopathy (Karthik, 2019). Specifically, we show that when used with existing loss functions, such
072   as Focal loss (Lin et al., 2017) and VS loss (Kini et al., 2021), LCT improves the Area Under the
073   ROC Curve (AUC), F1 score, and Brier score. We also show that LCT models are both more efficient
074   to train and more adaptable, because some of the hyperparameter tuning can be done post-training
075
      to handle changes in the prior or misclassification costs.
076   In summary, our contributions are as follows.
077
078              • We show that training a single model over a distribution of hyperparameters via Loss Con-
079                ditional Training (LCT) improves the performance of a variety of methods designed to
080
                   address class imbalance.
081              • We show that LCT models are more efficient to train and more adaptable, because they can
082                be tuned in part after training.
083              • We observe that current methods for training under class imbalance require retraining with
084                different hyperparameters for best performance if the metrics change.
085              • We will publish the code upon acceptance of this paper for reproducibility and to encourage
086                follow-up research.

      2       R ELATED WORK
090
      Many solutions2 have been proposed to address class imbalance, including several specialized loss
091
      functions and optimization methods (Cao et al., 2019; Rangwani et al., 2022; Buda et al., 2018; Kini
092
      et al., 2021; Shwartz-Ziv et al., 2023). Perhaps the simplest of these is to compensate for class imbal-
093   ance by assigning class-dependent weights in the loss function that are inversely proportional to the
094   frequency of the class in the training set (e.g., weighted cross-entropy loss (Xie & Manski, 1989)).
095   Another popular loss function is focal loss, which down-weights “easy” samples (i.e., samples with
096   high predictive confidence) (Lin et al., 2017).
097
      Several more recent loss functions modulate the logits with additive and multiplicative factors before
098
      they are input to the softmax function (Cao et al., 2019; Ye et al., 2020; Menon et al., 2021). These
099   factors aim to enforce larger margins for the minority class and/or calibrate the models. Kini et al.
100   (2021) recognized that many of the previous loss functions for addressing class imbalance can be
101   subsumed by a single Vector Scaling (VS) loss, which performs well on multi-class datasets after
102   hyperparameter tuning (Kini et al., 2021). Du et al. (2023a) combine a global and local mixture
103   consistency loss with contrastive learning and a dual head architecture.
104
      Among work that focuses on the training method, Rangwani et al. (2022) proposed using Sharp-
105   ness Aware Minimization (SAM) as an alternative optimizer that can help the model escape saddle
106   points in multi-class problems with imbalance (Foret et al., 2021). Similarly, Shwartz-Ziv et al.
107

              Appendix B analyzes past literature in greater detail.

108
      (2023) identify several training modifications—including batch size, data augmentation, specialized
109   optimizers, and label smoothing—which can all improve performance.
110
111   All these methods have hyperparameters, which are typically tuned for best overall accuracy on a
112
      balanced test set. We observe that the choice of hyperparameters depends on the desired performance
      metric and propose to train one model on a range of hyperparameters using Loss Conditional Train-
113
      ing (LCT) (Dosovitskiy & Djolonga, 2020). LCT was proposed as a method to improve efficiency
114
      by training one model that can approximate several more specific models. For example, Dosovitskiy
115   & Djolonga (2020) propose using LCT to train one lossy image compression model which works
116   on a range of compression rates, eliminating the need to train a separate model for each desired
117   compression rate. They achieve greater efficiency and adaptability for image compression at a slight
118   cost in performance. In contrast, we propose using LCT to improve the performance of a classifier
119   under imbalance and increase its adaptability.

223   4.2      L OSS C ONDITIONAL T RAINING (LCT)
224
225   The observation from the previous section implies that models learn different information based on
226   the hyperparameter values of their loss function. This fact motivated us to train one model over
227
      several hyperparameter values with the aim that this model would learn to approximate the best
      performance of several models trained with single loss functions. We find that Loss Conditional
228
      Training (Dosovitskiy & Djolonga, 2020) is a suitable approach to implement this model. LCT was
229
      proposed as an approximation method to reduce the computational redundancy involved in training
230   separate models with slightly different loss functions, such as neural image compression models
231   with different compression rates. We find that we can apply it to the specialized loss functions used
232   in the imbalance literature.
233
      To define LCT, let L(·, ·, λ) be a family of loss functions parameterized by λ ∈ Λ ⊆ Rdλ . For
234
      example, we can parameterize focal loss by defining λ = (α, ϕ) and VS loss by defining λ = (γ, τ ).
235
      A non-LCT training session finds the weights θ of a model f that minimize a loss function L(·, ·, λ0 )
236   for a single combination λ0 of hyperparameters:
237
                                       ∗
238                                   θλ 0
                                           = arg min E(x,y)∼D L(y, f (x, θ), λ0 ) .                        (6)
                                                  θ
239
240   In contrast, LCT optimizes over a distribution PΛ of λ values and feeds λ to both model and loss:
241                               θ ∗ = arg min Eλ∼PΛ E(x,y)∼D L(y, f (x, θ, λ), λ) .                      (7)
242                                         θ
243   Specifically, LCT is implemented for Deep Neural Network (DNN) predictors by augmenting the
244   network to take an additional input vector λ along with each data sample x. During training, λ
245   is sampled from PΛ for each mini-batch and is used in two ways: 1) as an additional input to the
246   network and 2) in the loss function for the mini-batch. During inference, the model takes as input a
247   λ, whose value is determined by fine-tuning, along with x, and outputs a prediction.
248   To condition the model on λ, the DNN is augmented with Feature-wise Linear Modulation (FiLM)
249   layers (Perez et al., 2018). These are small neural networks that take the conditioning parameter λ
250   as input and output vectors µ and σ that modulate the activations channel-wise based on the value of
251   λ. Specifically, consider a DNN layer with an activation map of size H × W × C. LCT transforms
252   each activation f ∈ RC to f̃ = σ ∗ f + µ, where both µ and σ are vectors of size C and “∗” stands
253   for element-wise multiplication.
254
255   4.3      D ETAILS ABOUT IMPLEMENTING LCT FOR CLASSIFICATION
256
257   We let λ = (α, ϕ) for Focal loss and λ = (γ, τ ) for VS loss.4 We draw one value of λ from
258
      PΛ with each mini-batch by independently sampling each hyperparameter in λ from a probability
      density function (pdf) L(a, b, hb ) over the real interval [a, b]. The user specifies a, b, and the value
259
      hb of the pdf at b. The value at a is then found to ensure unit area. Unlike the triangular pdf, our pdf
260
      is not necessarily zero at either endpoint. Unlike the uniform pdf, it is not necessarily constant over
261   [a, b].5 We vary a, b, hb to experiment with different distributions. The choice of λ during inference
262   is determined by hyperparameter tuning, and we evaluate the model with multiple values of λ.
263
264
      In practice, tuning LCT models is significantly more efficient than tuning baseline models because
      much of the tuning can be done after training. Specifically, with the baseline models, we must
265
      train a new model for each new hyperparameter value. With LCT, we can train one network over
266
      a wide range of hyperparameter values (one PΛ distribution) and then choose the best λ by simply
267   performing inference on the validation set.

364   on ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009). We modify the settings in Fang et al. (2024) a bit, since our
365   dataset is different from theirs (binary instead of multi-class). We train models for 35 epochs, with
366   a learning rate of 5 × 10−5 , no weight decay, and the cosine scheduler.
367   To apply LCT, we augment the networks with one FiLM block (Perez et al., 2018) after the final
368   convolutional layer and before the linear layer. This block consists of two linear layers with 128
369   hidden units. Specifically, the first layer takes one input λ and outputs 128 hidden values, and
370   the second layer outputs 64 values which are used to modulate the 64 channels of convolutional
371   activations (total of 8448 additional parameters for two-dimensional λ).
372   For all experiments, we use a batch size of 128 and gradient clipping with maximum norm equal to
373   0.5. For most datasets and models, we use Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) optimization with
374   momentum = 0.9. For APTOS datasets, we use Adam (Kingma, 2014), following Fang et al. (2024).
375   Additionally, for models with SAM optimization, we use ρ = 0.1. Note that we can train a model
376   with both LCT and SAM because these affect different parts of the training procedure. Specifically,
377   while LCT affects the model’s inputs and the calculation of the loss, SAM affects how the model
      uses the information from the loss to update its weights.

404   5.2   OVERALL PERFORMANCE
405
406
407   Precision-Recall curves. We first consider how training over a range of hyperparameters via LCT
408   affects performance over a wide range of classification thresholds. Figure 1 compares the baseline
409   models to the LCT models over eight recall values on the Precision-Recall curves. This figure
410
      shows that training over a distribution of hyperparameters with LCT yields a single model that
      improves the precision over the best baseline models at all recall values. This is in contrast to the
411
      original applications of LCT, where LCT is only designed to approximate (but not improve) multiple
412
      models (Dosovitskiy & Djolonga, 2020).
413
414

417
      AUC. We next consider more comprehensive results using the Area Under the Receiver Operating
      Characteristic (ROC) curves (AUC) (Egan, 1975). There is a one-to-one correspondence between
418
      ROC curves and Precision-Recall curves (Davis & Goadrich, 2006). We include AUC results in the
419
      main body because it has meaningful interpretations (Flach & Kull, 2015) and include results for
420   Average Precision (which summarizes Precision-Recall curves) in Table 13 of the Appendix. These
421   curves both show model performance over a wide range of classification thresholds, making AUC
422   and Average Precision good metrics for the situations we discussed in the introduction.
423
      Table 3 shows AUC results for various methods designed to address class imbalance and various
424
      binary datasets with severe class imbalance. LCT consistently improves the VS and Focal methods;
425
      however, it has inconsistent performance on the VS + SAM method. Furthermore, for most datasets
426   (all except CIFAR-10 animal/vehicle), the best performing method is an LCT method. This shows
427   that models trained with LCT are generally better at ranking the predictions (i.e., giving positive
428   samples higher probabilities of being positive than negative samples). This makes sense because,
429   like baseline models, LCT always penalizes incorrect relative rankings within the same batch; how-
430   ever, unlike baseline models, the model does not overfit to one absolute bias (associated with one
431   single hyperparameter), but rather a random bias drawn with each batch. This property also makes
      LCT easily adaptable to different Precision-Recall preferences.

442   5.3       M ODEL ADAPTABILITY
443
444   Since LCT models are trained over a distribution PΛ of λ values, they can be evaluated over any
445   λ value within (or maybe even outside of) PΛ . In this section, we show how this feature leads to
446
      adaptable models, or models that can be further tuned after training.
447   Figure 2 compares the results obtained by training one baseline (VS) model and one LCT (VS +
448   LCT) model. While the baseline model has only one output, the LCT model has many potential
449   outputs based on the value of the inference-time value of λ. For example, on the left plot, we see
450   that varying the inference-time λ can have a big effect on the Brier score of the model. Specifically,
451   decreasing the value of τ in the λ vector significantly decreases the Brier score (i.e., improves
452
      calibration)6 , albeit at some cost in AUC in the most extreme cases. Similarly, varying the value of
      λ can vary the precision for 0.99 recall (center) or the F1 score (right).
453
454   Thus, training models with LCT allows a practitioner to train one good model and then efficiently
455   adapt the model to meet specific requirements (e.g., improve calibration, precision at high recalls, or
456   F1 score) after training. This property is particularly valuable for medical applications, as a single
457   trained model can be shared by health providers across different regions, allowing them to adapt it
458
      to their specific needs despite variations in medical resources and preferences.

      5.4       A NALYZING THE IMPACT OF LCT
461
      In this section, we analyze whether the improvement from training classification models with LCT
462   is a result of adding more randomness to the training procedure and/or the additional information
463   (the added λ input) that the model receives about the loss function. In other words, is training over
464   PΛ instead of a fixed λ simply a form of regularization that helps the model to generalize better,
465   or does the additional input λ also help the model learn to optimize over different precision-recall
466   tradeoffs?
467
      To test this, we train models by feeding λ only to the loss function but not to the model. In other
468   words, in contrast to Equation (7), we optimize
469
                               θ ∗ = arg min Eλ∼PΛ E(x,y)∼D L(y, f (x, θ), λ) .                       (8)
470                                           θ
471   Specifically, we draw λ ∼ PΛ with each mini-batch during training and feed it to the loss function
472   like we did in LCT. However, unlike LCT, we use the model architecture without FiLM layers. For
473   inference, the model only takes x as input, and not λ. We call this method LCT without FiLM.
474
      Table 4 shows that LCT without FiLM performs significantly worse than both the baseline and the
475
      LCT methods. Specifically, the AUC decreases from 0.918 with VS (or 0.930 with VS+LCT) to
476   0.886 with VS+LCT without FiLM. This shows that blindly training over a distribution of hyper-
477   parameters does not improve the generalization of the models and in fact harms the performance.
478   However, training over a distribution of hyperparameters and feeding information about those hy-
479   perparameters to the model does improve overall performance in this setting.

891

892

893   consideration and show the trade-off of true positive rates and false positive rates over all possible
894   decision thresholds. The area under the ROC curve (AUC) is calculated using the trapezoid rule for
895   integration over a sample of values and is a commonly used metric (Buda et al., 2018).

      B     D ETAILED RELATED WORK
919
920
921
      Training on imbalanced datasets with algorithms designed to work on balanced datasets can be prob-
      lematic because the gradients and losses are biased towards the common classes, so the rare classes
922
      will not be learned well. Current methods to mitigate the effects of training under imbalance include
923
      methods that modify the loss functions, re-sample and augment training samples, and improve the
924   module via two-stage learning, ensembles, or representation learning Zhang et al. (2023).
925
926
927   B.1   S PECIALIZED LOSS FUNCTIONS
928
929   To balance the gradients from all classes, several papers adaptively change a sample’s weight in
930
      the loss based on features such as the sample’s confidence score, class frequency, and influence
      on model weights Zhang et al. (2021); Fernando & Tsokos (2021); Park et al. (2021); Wang et al.
931
      (2021a); Li et al. (2021a). Other work addresses the difference in the norms of features associated
932
      with frequent (head) and rare (tail) classes and proposes to balance this by utilizing a feature-based
933   loss Li et al. (2022b) or weight-decay and gradient clipping Alshammari et al. (2022).
934
935   Some work has focused on enforcing larger margins on the tail classes using additive factors on the
936
      logits in Cross-entropy loss Cao et al. (2019); Menon et al. (2021); Li et al. (2022c). Other work
      proposed adding multiplicative factors to the logits to adjust for the difference in the magnitude of
937
      minority-class logits at training and test time or minimize a margin-based generalization bound Ye
938
      et al. (2020); Kang et al. (2021). Kini et al. (2021) show that multiplicative factors are essential for
939   the terminal phase of training, but that these have negative effects early during training, so additive
940   factors are necessary to speed up convergence. They propose Vector Scaling (VS) loss as a general
941   loss function that includes both additive and multiplicative factors on the logits. Behnia et al. (2023)
942   study the implicit geometry of classifiers trained on a special case of VS loss.
943
      Beyond classification, many papers have focused on imbalance in instance segmentation and object
944
      detection applications Tan et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2021c); Feng et al. (2021); Li et al. (2020).
945   Ren et al. (2022) also propose a balanced Mean Square Error (MSE) loss for regression problems,
946   such as age and depth estimation.
947
948
949   B.2   DATA - LEVEL METHODS
950
951
      Another way to balance the gradients of classes during training is resampling. This could be done
      by sampling the minority class more often (random over-sampling) or sampling the majority class
952
      less often (random under-sampling) Johnson & Khoshgoftaar (2019); Japkowicz (2000); Liu et al.
953
      (2009). Jiang et al. (2023); Hou et al. (2023) use clustering to drive resampling; specifically, they
954   cluster head classes into multiple clusters and then resample across clusters instead of classes. Meta-
955   learning has been used to estimate the optimal sampling rates of different classes Ren et al. (2020),
956   while Wang et al. (2019) dynamically adapts both the loss function and sampling procedure through-
957   out training.
958
      Additionally, data augmentation can be used alongside oversampling to increase the size of the
959
      minority class samples and enable model generalization Zhong et al. (2021); Zang et al. (2021); Li
960   et al. (2021b); Du et al. (2023a). With tabular data, small perturbations of random noise can be added
961   to generate new examples. Images lend themselves to more high-level augmentations. Methods that
962   copy and paste patches of images, such as CutMix Yun et al. (2019) and PuzzleMix Kim et al. (2020)
963   have been used to improve classification or instance segmentation Ghiasi et al. (2021) performance.
964
      SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique) creates synthetic examples by interpolating
965
      between samples in the same class of the training set Chawla et al. (2002). The interpolation is
966   done in feature space instead of data space. The mixup method also generates synthetic examples;
967   however, unlike SMOTE, it interpolates between samples of different classes Zhang et al. (2018).
968   These synthetic examples are given a soft label that corresponds to the proportion of input from
969   each class. StyleMix adapts mixup to separately manipulate an image’s content and style Hong
970   et al. (2021). ReMix advances mixup to optimize for situations with class imbalance by combining
971   mixup and resampling Bellinger et al. (2020). Li et al. (2021b) creates augmented data samples
      by translating deep features along semantically meaningful directions. Zada et al. (2022) adds pure


                                                        18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       noise images to the training set and introduces a new type of distribution-aware batch normalization
973    layer to facilitate training with them.
974
975
976    B.3   M ODULE IMPROVEMENTS
977
978    Some work has shown that overly emphasizing the minority class can disrupt the original data dis-
979    tribution and cause overfitting Zhou et al. (2020); Du et al. (2023a) or reduce the accuracy of the
980    majority class Zhu et al. (2022). To mitigate this issue, contrastive learning Du et al. (2023a); Zhu
981    et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2021b) has been used to learn a robust feature representation by enforc-
982
       ing consistency between two differently augmented versions of the data. Du et al. (2023a) devise
       a single-stage approach, by combining the ideas of contrastive representations with soft label class
983
       re-weighting to achieve state-of-the-art performance on several benchmarks.
984
985    Multi-expert methods have also been explored Zhang et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2020b); Li et al.
986    (2022a); Sanchez Aimar et al. (2023). Li et al. (2022a) collaboratively learn multiple experts, by
987    learning individual experts and transferring knowledge among them, in a nested way. Similarly,
988
       Sanchez Aimar et al. (2023) propose using a an ensemble of experts calibrated with different logit
       adjustments. Methods have also used two branches or heads with one classification branch along
989
       with another branch, either to re-balance Guo & Wang (2021); Zhou et al. (2020) or calibrate Wang
990
       et al. (2020a) the model. Tang et al. (2022) adopt a generalized approach to learn attribute-invariant
991    features that first discovers sets of samples with diverse intra-class distributions from the low confi-
992    dence predictions, and then learns invariant features across them. Dong et al. (2022) uses language
993    prompts to improve both general and group-specific features. Xu et al. (2022) propose using Dy-
994    namic Label Space Adjustments (DLSA) to first separate head and tail classes to create a series of
995    more balanced classification tasks for one longtailed multi-class classification problems. Similarly,
996    SuperDisco proposes using hierarchical relationships between classes and graph learning to improve
997    the performance on longtailed multi-class classification problems (Du et al., 2023b).

       H     F EATURE - WISE L INEAR M ODULATION (F I LM)
1447
       Feature-wise Linear Modulation (FiLM) layers Perez et al. (2018) are layers that are added to a LCT
1448
       model to condition it on λ. These layers are small neural networks that take λ as input and output a µ
1449   and σ that is used to modulate the activations within a network channel-wise. For example, suppose
1450   a convolutional layer in a network has activations f of size W × H × C. In Loss Conditional
1451   Training (LCT), these activations are augmented by µ and σ channel-wise as follows,
1452
                                                    f˜ = σf + µ                                          (34)
1453
1454   where both µ and σ are vectors of size C.
1455   Figure 9 shows a diagram of FiLM layers being applied to a small example network. Normally,
1456   without FiLM layers, the equation to calculate the features maps is
1457
                                           x(1) = ρ(W (1) x(0) + b(1) ),                                (35)
