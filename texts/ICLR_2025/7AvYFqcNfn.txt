009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Image forgery localization, which centers on identifying tampered pixels within
012            an image, has seen significant advancements. Traditional approaches often model
013            this challenge as a variant of image segmentation, treating the segmentation of
014            forged areas as the end product. However, while semantic segmentation provides
015            distinct regions with clear semantics that are readily interpretable by humans, the
016
               interpretation regarding the detected forgery regions is less straightforward and
               is an under explored problem. We argue that the simplistic binary forgery mask,
017
               which merely delineates tampered pixels, fails to provide adequate information for
018
               explaining the model’s predictions. First, the mask does not elucidate the ratio-
019            nale behind the model’s localization. Second, the forgery mask treats all forgery
020            pixels uniformly, which prevents it from emphasizing the most conspicuous un-
021            real regions and ultimately hinders human discernment of the most anomalous
022            areas. In this study, we mitigate the aforementioned limitations by generating
023            salient region-focused interpretation for the forgery images, articulating the ra-
024            tionale behind the predicted forgery mask and underscoring the pivotal forgery
025            regions with a interpretation description. To support this, we craft a Multi-Modal
026            Tramper Tracing (MMTT) dataset, comprising images manipulated using deep-
027
               fake techniques and paired with manual, interpretable textual annotations. To har-
               vest high-quality annotation, annotators are instructed to meticulously observe the
028
               manipulated images and articulate the typical characteristics of the forgery re-
029
               gions. Subsequently, we collect a dataset of 128,303 image-text pairs. Leveraging
030            the MMTT dataset, we develop ForgeryTalker, an architecture designed for con-
031            current forgery localization and interpretation. ForgeryTalker first trains a forgery
032            prompter network to identify the pivotal clues within the explanatory text. Subse-
033            quently, the region prompter is incorporated into multimodal large language model
034            for finetuning to achieve the dual goals of localization and interpretation. Exten-
035            sive experiments conducted on the MMTT dataset verify the superior performance
036            of our proposed model.

044   2021), they have also raised profound concerns about their misuse in malicious contexts, including
045   misinformation campaigns and privacy violations (Liu et al., 2023; Rana et al., 2022). Given these
046   threats, DeepFake detection techniques have garnered significant attention and have rapidly evolved
047   in recent years. Recent studies are shifting from simple real-fake detection to fine-grained forgery
048   region localization to address the growing complexity of modern forgery techniques (Verdoliva,
049   2020; Rossler et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2021).
050   Unlike binary classification methods, which merely determine whether an image is fake or real,
051   forgery localization segments the exact areas that have been tampered with (Verdoliva, 2020), aiming
052   to explain the reason behind a forgery determination. Despite the recent significant strides in forgery
053   localization, current methods still lack the ability to provide clear, interpretable justifications for their
      detections. Binary masks, which merely highlight tampered pixels, provide limited insights into the

077

079   a large-scale Multi-Modal Tampering Tracing (MMTT) dataset, as shown in Figure 1, compris-
080   ing image-text pairs of forgery images and the corresponding textual annotations. In specific, The
081   MMTT dataset, focusing on face images and consisting of 128,303 forged facial samples, contains
082   manipulated images that pose more threats to public information and privacy. Each image under-
083   goes various manipulations, and the pixel-level forgery mask is automatically generated from the
084   manipulation processes. To annotate the textual descriptions, we adopt a human-in-the-loop ap-

087
      manipulation (e.g., blurring, unnatural texture, or geometry distortion) is documented to ensure pre-

088

089

090   existing datasets that typically lack such detailed contextual information.

092   With the MMTT dataset established, our framework is designed to simultaneously perform forgery
093

094
      a Multimodal Large Language Model (MLLM) as the backbone. The Forgery Prompter Network
095

096   capturing the core artificial characteristics of the forgeries. This prompt, serving as a structured
097   representation of the tampering, provides crucial priors for subsequent reasoning and makes the
098   generation of a coherent explanation significantly easier. The Mask Decoder refines the pixel-level
099   predictions, ensuring that only the most prominent manipulated regions are emphasized. Finally,
100   the Language-based Explanation Module utilizes the generated prompt to articulate a coherent ex-
101   planation that accurately captures the rationale behind the predicted forgery mask, addressing the
102   inherent limitations of traditional binary segmentation approaches. Through the integration of these
103   three components, our model not only achieves precise forgery localization but also provides con-
104
      textually rich, human-understandable interpretation reports of the detected manipulations.

116   2     R ELATED W ORK
117
118   Facial Manipulation Localization. Detecting manipulated facial regions, particularly deepfakes,
119
      has gained significant attention. CNN-based approaches like Sabir et al. (2019) exploit temporal
      inconsistencies for video-based detection, while GAN-based methods, such as GANprintR Neves
120
      et al. (2020) and MaskGAN Liu et al. (2022), address synthetic artifacts for improved localization.
121
      Hybrid models combining CNNs and ViTs (e.g., HCiT Kaddar et al. (2021)) enhance generalization.
122   Multi-modal methods Sun et al. (2023); Khedkar et al. (2022) leverage spatial-temporal inconsis-
123   tencies to capture subtle manipulations. While these methods excel at binary classification, they
124   lack interpretability and the capacity to generate fine-grained forgery masks, which are critical for
125   explaining model decisions. This paper addresses these gaps by producing both localization masks
126   and textual rationales.
127
      Multi-label Classification for Facial Localization. Multi-label classification captures independent
128   facial region alterations but struggles with complex dependencies across facial features. Standard
129   CNNs Lalitha & Sooda (2022) are limited in fine-grained tasks, while hybrid models Kaddar et al.
130   (2021) combine local and global features for improved detection. Addressing class imbalance, Ra-
131   machandran et al. (2021) employs weighted loss functions, and parallel branches Richards et al.
132   (2023) refine fine-grained alterations. However, these approaches rarely explore the potential of
133   combining multi-label classification with localization, leaving a gap in effectively identifying ma-
134   nipulations across multiple facial regions. Our work bridges this gap with a ViT-based classifier
135   using parallel branches and weighted loss functions to capture complex dependencies.
136   Segmentation Techniques. Segmentation is essential for identifying localized manipulations. Tra-
137   ditional models like U-Net and DeepLab Ross & Dollár (2017) focus on spatial features, while
138   Transformer-based models Alexey (2020) capture global context for precise segmentation. Recent
139   approaches like SAM Kirillov et al. (2023) leverage a Two-Way Transformer for generating high-
140   quality masks but lack contextual awareness of manipulated content. We address this limitation
141   by integrating SAM with InstructBLIP, enabling context-aware forgery masks for fine-grained lo-
142
      calization. However, the integration of segmentation and manipulation detection remains limited,
      as existing works often treat them as separate tasks rather than a unified framework for enhanced
143
      localization.

148   Although many existing datasets provide annotations for forgery localization, they lack detailed,
149   descriptive explanations for the detected manipulations (Table 1). To bridge this gap, we introduce
150   the Multi-Modal Tramper Tracing (MMTT) dataset, which uniquely combines pixel-level forgery
151
      masks with comprehensive textual descriptions. Unlike conventional datasets that focus solely on
      binary classification (Li et al., 2020; Dolhansky et al., 2020) or mask-based localization (Rossler
152
      et al., 2019; Jiang et al., 2020), MMTT emphasizes interpretability by integrating annotations that
153
      explain how and why the manipulated regions appear forged. This emphasis on human-generated
154   interpretations allows for a richer understanding of the manipulations.

180   3.2    F ORGERY G ENERATION
181
182
      Generation and editing are two main threats for the face image protection, We incorporate both
      techniques for forgery image generation to construct a more challenging dataset. To keep pace
183
      with the latest techniques, we employ three manipulation methods: face swapping (Abou Akar
184
      et al., 2024), along with image inpainting techniques, which include both Transformer-based (Li
185   et al., 2022) and diffusion-based methods (Podell et al., 2023), to produce a comprehensive forgery
186   dataset.
187
188
      Face Swapping. For the face swapping task, we employ E4S (Abou Akar et al., 2024), a GAN-
      based model designed specifically for high-quality face swapping. Given a target image It and a
189
      source image Is , E4S generates a swapped face image If by replacing the entire face region in It
190
      with the facial features from Is . For the CelebA-HQ dataset, target and source images are randomly
191   paired from the entire dataset, while for FFHQ, the source image is chosen from a separate subfolder
192   to maintain visual diversity.
193
194
      During the swapping process, E4S automatically generates a binary mask M , which covers the
      entire face region of the target image It . This dynamically generated mask is used to blend facial
195
      features from Is into It , ensuring the swapped image If only alters the facial region and preserves
196
      non-facial elements like hair and background from the target image.
197
198   The generated binary mask M is stored as the ground-truth annotation for the altered regions, rep-
199   resenting the full face replacement for both CelebA-HQ and FFHQ datasets. As a result, the final
200
      outputs include both the forged images If and their corresponding binary masks M , providing a
      consistent representation of the modified regions for subsequent training and evaluation tasks.
201
202   Image Inpainting. For generating localized facial manipulations, we utilize MAT(Li et al., 2022)
203   (transformer-based) and SDXL (Podell et al., 2023) (diffusion-based).
204   For each image I, the process commences by defining a binary mask M that indicates the regions to
205   be inpainted. Depending on the dataset, the process of mask generation varies. For the CelebAMask-
206   HQ dataset, which contains predefined masks for 21 facial components (e.g., eyes, nose, mouth, and
207   eyebrows), we randomly select between 1 to 11 facial regions for modification. Specifically, we
208   generate a random number k within this range, representing the number of facial parts to be altered.
209   These regions are then randomly sampled and merged to create the final mask M . And for the Flickr-
210   Faces-HQ dataset, which lacks predefined facial masks, we employ Dlib (King, 2009) to detect key
211   facial landmarks. This allows us to segment the face into different regions such as eyebrows, eyes,
212
      nose, mouth, ears, and the entire face. For each image, we first decide whether to apply a complete
      face mask with a probability of 0.2. If not, we randomly select k regions (where k is again randomly
213
      chosen between 1 and 11) to construct the final mask M .
214

239   Annotation Guidance. Figure 2 shows the pipeline of our annotation process. To ensure the annota-
240   tion quality, our expert team manually provides interpretations. The goal is to produce explanations
241   that interpret the localization of forgeries and emphasize the most conspicuously artificial areas. As
242
      shown in Figure 2, annotators are presented with both the original and manipulated images, with the
      manipulated areas indicated by the groundtruth mask. They are instructed as follows:
243
244               • Carefully examine the pair of images and describe any irregularities or artificial appear-
245                 ances in the manipulated regions.
246
                  • Focus on annotating only the unnatural or poorly integrated facial features, disregarding
247
                    areas that appear authentic.
248
249               • Avoid using language that requires reference to the original image, as this is not feasible in
250                 practical scenarios.
251               • Keep descriptions concise, limiting them to no more than 120 words.
252
253   Annotation Process. The annotation process involves 30 annotators. As shown in Figure 2, each
254   annotator is presented with the original image Io and the forged image If , and asked to compare
255
      them. Based on the comparison and the annotation guidance, they identify and annotate the regions
      in If that exhibit unnatural or illogical alterations. The steps in the annotation process are as follows:
256
257
                  • Step 1: Annotators are given an original-forgery image pair (Io , If ).
258
259               • Step 2: Annotators examine the images for inconsistencies in facial regions, such as un-
260
                    usual textures, asymmetry, or irregular shading.
261               • Step 3: Annotators provide a textual description T , explaining the nature of the alteration
262                 (e.g., ”The nose texture appears unnaturally smooth, lacking real skin details.”).
263
264   Integrating all above annotated clues, each annotated sample in our MMTT dataset is finally formed
265
      as a triplet p = (If , M, T ).
266   Annotation Quality Control. To ensure the quality of the annotations, strict quality control mea-
267   sures are applied:
268
269               • Minimum Annotation Time. Each annotator is required to spend at least one minute on
                    each image, ensuring a thorough examination of the details in both Io and If .

355
356
      4.1        A RCHITECTURE
357
358
      Our framework, ForgeryTalker, extends the InstructBlip (Dai et al., 2023) model by introducing a
359   Forgery Prompter Network (FPN) and a Mask Decoder. The system accepts a tampered image I
360   and encodes it into patch embeddings following Vision Transformer (Dosovitskiy, 2020). These
361   embeddings are then dually processed by the mask decoder for localization. The FPN is initially
362   trained to produce region prompts, which are then combined with an instruction template and fed
363   into the Q-former of InstructBlip. The ensuing multimodal features are channeled through a large
364   language model to produce an interpretive narrative of the forgery. The training is performed in
365   a two-stage fashion: initially, the FPN is trained with a classification loss, followed by a second
366   phase where the FPN is fixed while the mask decoder and Q-former are collectively optimized with
367
      segmentation and language generation losses.
368
369   4.2        F ORGERY P ROMPTER N ETWORK
370
371   Motivation. Accurately identifying the most salient manipulated regions in forged images is diffi-
372   cult due to the high visual fidelity of modern manipulation techniques. Even human reviewers often

460   5.1     E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
461
462   Implementation Details. We implement our ForgeryTalker framework using PyTorch and train it
463   on four NVIDIA A100 GPUs. The Forgery Prompter Network is fine-tuned for 125,000 steps with
464   a batch size of 16, an initial learning rate of 7.5e-3, using a cosine decay strategy and warmup steps
465
      of 125. The convolution branch in FPN includes one 3×3 Coordinate Convolution (CoordConv)
      layer and one 5×5 Convolution layer. The discount factor in Eq. 3 is set as ω = 0.2 to balance the
466
      unmodified regions. Next, we fix FPN and tune the Q-former and the mask decoder by 60 epochs,
467
      starting with a learning rate of 4e-6. The training setup includes a batch size of 16 and a gradient
468   accumulation strategy with an accumulation step of 1, with mixed-precision training (fp16) enabled
469   for faster convergence and reduced memory usage. The Multi-Modal Tampering Tracing (MMTT)
470   dataset is divided into training, validation, and test sets with a ratio of 8:1:1.
471
      We use a range of captioning and segmentation metrics for performance evaluation, including
472
      CIDEr, BLEU, METEOR, and IoU. We use Positive Label Matching (PLM) to evaluate the ef-
473
      fectiveness of FPN. PLM calculates the ratio of correctly predicted positive labels over the union of
474   predicted and ground-truth positive labels:

531
532
      This paper addresses the limitations of traditional image forgery localization methods by introducing
533
      a novel approach that generates interpretive reports for forged images. We argue that existing binary
534   forgery masks lack the detail necessary to fully understand model predictions and to highlight the
535   most significant areas of forgery. To overcome this, this paper creates MMTT dataset, which in-
536   cludes deepfake-manipulated images with corresponding textual annotations. Then we propose the
537   ForgeryTalker framework, which combines forgery localization with interpretive text generation,
538   enhancing both the accuracy and transparency of forgery detection. The model’s effectiveness is
539   validated through experiments on the MMTT dataset, demonstrating its superiority in image forgery
      localization and interpretation tasks.
