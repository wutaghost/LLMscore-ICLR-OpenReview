009                                           Abstract
010
011
              ML-based computer vision models are promising tools for supporting emer-
012           gency management operations following natural disasters. Imagery taken
013           from small manned and unmanned aircraft can be available soon after a
014           disaster and provide valuable information from multiple perspectives for
015           situational awareness and damage assessment applications. However, emer-
016           gency managers often face challenges in effectively utilizing this data due
017           to the difficulties in finding the most relevant imagery among the tens of
018           thousands of images that may be taken after an event. Despite this promise,
019           there is still a lack of training data for imagery of this type from multiple
020
              perspectives and for multiple hazard types. To address this, we present the
              LADI v2 (Low Altitude Disaster Imagery version 2) dataset, a curated set
021
              of about 10,000 disaster images captured by the Civil Air Patrol (CAP)
022
              in response to over 50 disaster events (2015-2023) from over 30 US states
023           and territories and annotated for multi-label classification by trained CAP
024           volunteers. We also provide two pretrained baseline classifiers and compare
025           their performance to state-of-the-art vision-language models in multi-label
026           classification. The data and code are released publicly to support the devel-
027           opment of computer vision models for emergency management research and
028           applications.

031   1   Introduction
032
033   Rapid and accurate assessment of post-disaster conditions is critical for effective disaster
034   response and recovery operations. Low altitude aerial imagery, such as aerial photographs
035   collected by the Civil Air Patrol (CAP) in the United States or images from small Unmanned
036   Aerial Systems (sUAS), can provide valuable information about the extent and severity of
037   damage caused by disasters. However, the large quantity of images collected during these
038   missions can present a significant challenge for analysts tasked with identifying actionable
039
      information in a timely manner. To address this challenge, we introduce the Low Altitude
      Disaster Imagery v2 (LADI v2) dataset, a multi-label image classification dataset designed to
040
      facilitate the development of computer vision models for identifying useful post-disaster aerial
041
      images. LADI v2 builds upon existing work in image-based damage assessment (Kyrkou and
042   Theocharides, 2020; Gupta et al., 2019; Rahnemoonfar et al., 2021; 2022; Weber et al., 2023;
043   Liu et al., 2019) by providing a diverse, multi-hazard dataset that includes oblique and nadir
044   aerial imagery from various locations and disaster declarations across the United States.
045
      The technical contributions of LADI v2 are twofold. First, we present a curated dataset of
046
      post-disaster aerial imagery from multiple perspectives for multiple hazard types, with high
047
      quality, operationally-relevant labels annotated by trained CAP volunteers for multi-label
048   classification. Second, we provide two pretrained baseline reference classifiers. We also
049   demonstrate the utility of the dataset as a benchmark, and compare the performance of
050   the baseline classifier to state-of-the art vision-language models (VLM) on open-vocabulary
051   classification as a benchmark. We outperform the open source VLM on nearly all classes
052   and are competitive with the commercial VLM in test set, and broadly outperform both
053   VLMs in the validation set. This demonstrates the continued need for open, domain-specific
      training data for specialized applications such as disaster response.

      LADI v2 also offers unique characteristics that can contribute to the advancement of machine
055   learning research. The dataset features a realistic distribution shift between the training and
056   test sets, representing annual variation in disaster incident types, as well as changes due to
057   new operational procedures and technologies. This characteristic makes LADI v2 a valuable
058   benchmark for evaluating domain adaptation techniques in the context of disaster response.
059
      The dataset, classifiers, and associated documentation are made openly available on GitHub
060
      (Anonymized Authors, 2024b) and Hugging Face (Anonymized Authors, 2024c;a), enabling
061   researchers and practitioners to build upon this work and adapt the models to their specific
062   needs. Through this contribution, we aim to streamline the process of identifying useful post-
063   disaster aerial images, ultimately supporting more efficient and effective disaster response
064   efforts while providing a valuable resource for the broader machine learning community.
065
      The paper is structured as follows: Section 2 covers related work in disaster imagery datasets
066
      and necessary background on vision-language models; Section 3 provides the details of the
067   dataset; Section 4 discusses the pretrained baseline classifiers; and Section 5 concludes with
068   a summary and comments on limitations and future directions.

      2     Related Work
073
074
075
      2.1   Natural Disaster Imagery Datasets
076
077
078   There is existing work on using imagery and computer vision to facilitate post disaster
079   damage assessment (Kyrkou and Theocharides, 2020; Gupta et al., 2019; Rahnemoonfar
080   et al., 2021; 2022; Weber et al., 2023; Manzini et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2019). We highlight a
081   few relevant examples and comment on the gap that LADI v2 addresses. The xBD dataset
082
      (Gupta et al., 2019) provides 23,000 labeled nadir-perspective satellite images annotated with
      bounding boxes for building damage for multiple locations across the globe, covering multiple
083
      hazard types. FloodNet (Rahnemoonfar et al., 2021) and RescueNet (Rahnemoonfar et al.,
084
      2022) each provide segmentation masks, classification labels, and visual question-answering
085   captions for high resolution, low altitude aerial imagery from UAVs, but are limited to
086   single incidents each: Hurricanes Harvey and Michael, respectively. Incidents1M (Weber
087   et al., 2023) provides a large multi-label dataset classifying 43 incident types in 49 outdoor
088   locations from nearly 1 million images scraped from the web. These images feature a variety
089   of perspectives and locations, but are primarily ground-based, and only identify the type
090   and location of the incident. CRASAR-U-DROIDs (Manzini et al., 2024) provides annotated
091   building damage polygons for orthomosaic imagery from small Unmanned Aerial Systems
092   (sUAS). Compared to the existing literature, there is still a lack of training data to support
093
      both oblique and nadir low-altitude aerial imagery—which is increasingly common in disaster
      response applications with UAVs and small manned aircraft—across multiple geographies,
094
      event types, and damage and infrastructure categories.
095
096   Version 1 of the Low Altitude Disaster Imagery dataset (LADI v1) began to address these gaps
097   by providing annotations for low altitude, multi-perspective imagery from multi-hazard and
098   multi-geographic events (Liu et al., 2019). It was included in a number of NIST TRECVID
099
      challenges (Awad et al., 2021a;b; 2023). However, these labels were created by untrained
      crowdsourced workers, and the term “damage" was not clearly defined. Instead, LADI v2 was
100
      labeled by a team of volunteer annotators from the Civil Air Patrol who have been trained in
101
      the FEMA damage assessment process. Damage labels follow FEMA’s criteria for preliminary
102   damage assessments (PDAs), which articulate five levels of damage: unaffected, affected,
103   minor damage, major damage, and destroyed. Furthermore, the label set and annotator
104   training materials were developed in conjunction with FEMA’s Response Geospatial Office
105   to ensure compatibility with their procedures. For these reasons, LADI v2 offers a different
106   label set than LADI v1. Nevertheless, we still believe LADI v1 offers some value, since it
107   features a larger number of annotated images, and thus may serve as a suitable pretraining
      task for classifiers trained on LADI v2.

119   2.2   Vision-Language Models
120
121
      Recent advances in vision-language models represent extremely promising developments
      toward generalizable solutions for computer vision problems (Gan et al., 2022). Vision-
122
      language models typically include an image encoder and text encoder for each respective
123
      data modality. These models are trained on tasks to promote alignment between the image
124   encoding and text encoding; such tasks include contrastive image-text learning, popularized
125   by (Radford et al., 2021), as well as input reconstruction such as masked language modeling
126   (Devlin et al., 2018) and masked image modeling (Tan and Bansal, 2019). These vision-
127   language models can be used to address various computer vision tasks, including open
128   vocabulary classification, object detection, and segmentation (Gan et al., 2022). We discuss
129   a few vision language models that are relevant to this paper, particularly LLaVA-NeXT (Liu
130   et al., 2024), and GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024).
131   LLaVA (Large Language and Vision Assistant) (Liu et al., 2023b) and its refinements LLaVA
132   1.5 (Liu et al., 2023a) and LLaVA-NeXT (Liu et al., 2024) build upon CLIP by incorporating
133   a large language model decoder, such as Vicuna (Chiang et al., 2023) or Mistral (Jiang
134   et al., 2023), to allow it to be trained additional tasks, such as visual question answering.
135   Commercial offerings such as GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024) offer similar multimodal capabilities.
136   We benchmark our models on LADI v2 against zero-shot classification using LLaVA-NeXT
137   and GPT-4o in Section 4.4.

      3     LADI v2 Dataset

      3.1   Data collection and annotation
142
143
      LADI v2 images are sourced from the FEMA (Federal Emergency Management Agency) Civil
144   Air Patrol Image Uploader Repository, publicly hosted in an Amazon Web Services s3 bucket
145   (FEMA, 2024a), which contains aerial photographs collected in support of federally declared
146   disasters from 2009 onward, as well as CAP training missions. Figure 1 shows a sample
147   of images in the dataset. Image metadata includes timestamp and location information.
148   To ensure LADI v2 contains only images collected during disasters, we compared image
149   metadata against disaster declaration data from the OpenFEMA Disaster Declarations API
150   (FEMA, 2024b) to identify images taken within 14 days of the start of a declared federal
151   disaster and within an affected county’s boundaries.
152   We consulted emergency management professionals at the FEMA Response Geospatial Office
153   to develop the set of labels and labeling instructions. Labels were chosen to help emergency
154   managers identify the most relevant images when conducting initial damage assessments,
155   which can support disaster declarations and assistance grants. The label set is provided
156   as the “v2" set in Table 2a. The various damage levels for the buildings—affected, minor,
157   major, and destroyed, listed in increasing order of severity—are determined based on the
158
      FEMA preliminary damage assessment criteria (FEMA, 2021).
159   Images were annotated by a team of 46 Civil Air Patrol volunteers who had been previously
160   trained in the FEMA preliminary damage assessment process (FEMA, 2021). Each image
161   was shown to three volunteers, and labels were assigned by majority vote when there was
      disagreement between the annotators.

206

208   and 1,041 test examples. The training and validation examples are drawn from disaster

210   in 2023. In total, the dataset draws from over 100 disaster declarations from more than 30
211   US states and territories, see Figure 2. Since disaster declarations can span multiple states,
212
      we estimate the number of distinct disaster events by clustering the declarations based on
      incident date, and we estimate that at least 50 distinct disaster events are in the dataset.

      Distribution shift. The distribution shifts between the train/validation and test sets represent
275
      challenges in disaster applying for machine learning. The distribution of hazard types,

279   to distribution variation even among disasters resulting from the same type of hazard. This
280   underscores one of the key domain-specific challenges of applying machine learning solutions
281   for disaster response applications. To our knowledge, no other benchmark disaster imagery
282   dataset explicitly addresses the shift in data distribution from year to year.

284   3.3   Comparison to Existing Datasets
285
286   Compared to LADI v1 (Liu et al., 2019), v2 offers higher-quality labels from trained
287   annotators, and a different label set. While both versions of the dataset draw from the same
288
      repository of public domain operational FEMA CAP images (FEMA, 2024a), only about
      2.4% of images from v2 also appear in v1.
289
290   The label set for LADI v2 includes building damage on the FEMA PDA scale, which is
291   compatible with the damage scale used by xBD (Gupta et al., 2019), RescueNet (Rah-
292   nemoonfar et al., 2022), and CRASAR-U-DROIDs (Manzini et al., 2024). Compared to
293   those datasets, LADI v2 includes more distinct events (LADI v2: 50+, xBD: 19, RescueNet:
294
      1, CRASAR-U-DROIDs: 10), total pixels: (LADI v2: 345.32e9, xBD: 23.14e9, RescueNet
      53.99e9, CRASAR-U-DROIDs: 67.13e9), and among aerial datasets, area covered (LADI v2:
295
      161.4 km2 , RescueNet: 3.6 km2 , CRASAR-U-DROIDs: 67.98 km2 )1 . LADI is also unique in
296
      its inclusion of both oblique and nadir perspective imagery. However, LADI v2’s labels only
297   support image classification, whereas the other mentioned datasets provide segmentation
298   polygons for building damage. Thus, LADI v2 serves to complement the capabilities of
299   those existing datasets, and would be an ideal candidate to include in a multi-task training
300   framework, especially due to the alignment in building damage labels.

305
306   To support research and deployment applications, we provide two pretrained reference
307   classifiers, LADI-v2-classifier-small-reference and LADI-v2-classifier-large-reference,2 hereby
308   referred to as the “small” and “large” classifiers for brevity. The small classifier is based

313

317   optimization through hyperparameter tuning and pretraining on LADI v1 (Liu et al., 2019).

      4.3   Performance Analysis
379
380   Incident hazard type analysis. The performance between the test and validation sets is com-
381   parable for most hazard types, except for fire (see Figure 4a). The difference in performance
382   for fire incidents is likely due to the relative lack of fire data in the training and validation
383   sets compared to the test set.
384   Geographic analysis. The classifier performs robustly across various geographies. Figure 4b
385   shows the mAP of the classifier for states in the test set, which are those states in which
386   CAP collected images for disasters in 2023. While only 10 states are represented in the test
387   set, the classifier achieved an mAP between 80 and 96 for all of them. The state of Hawaii,
388   with the lowest mAP at 80, had only one CAP mission in the test set, the August 2023
389   Hawaii wildfires. Relatively low performance is likely due to the relative lack of fire events
390   in the training data, as well as potential differences due to geography. We thus caution
391   practitioners and researchers against using models trained on LADI v2 for applications
392
      not well represented in the training set and recommend supplemental data collection and
      training.

416   4.4   Benchmarking open-vocabulary classification (LLaVA and GPT-4o)
417
      LADI v2 also has potential to be an effective benchmark for supervised and zero-shot
418
      classification of post-disaster imagery. In particular, the validation and test sets of LADI
419
      v2 offer the ability to test model performance against a realistic distribution shift observed
420   in practice. We demonstrate this by comparing our model’s performance to recent open-
421   vocabulary classifiers.
422
423
      We first evaluate our model compared to the recently released open 7.5 billion parameter
      LLaVA-NeXT model (Liu et al., 2024) on a zero-shot classification task. For each class, the
424
      LLaVA model saw an image in the test or validation set, followed by a prompt such as, “Does
425
      this image contain class_name? Answer with ‘yes’ or ‘no.” ’ For the classes involving FEMA
426   preliminary damage assessment categories, we included a summary of the damage category
427   criteria based on the FEMA Preliminary Damage Assessment Pocket Guide (FEMA, 2021)
428   in the prompt. The model outputs were converted to binary labels and used to compute the
429   F1 score and shown in Table 4. In the test set, our method outperformed LLaVA-NeXT on
430   all labels, including all damage-related classes, except in three categories: “bridges_any”,
431   “roads_any”, and “trees_any”, in which our model comes within 3% of LLaVA-NeXT. In the
      validation set, our model outperforms LLaVA-NeXT in all categories.

      We also compare our model to a commercial multimodal model, GPT-4o, using the same
433   prompt format as above. The results are shown in Table 4. On the test set, our model
434   is competitive with GPT-4o, beating or tying its performance on 5 of the 12 classes. On
435   the validation set, we outperform GPT-4o in all but the “water_any” class, showing strong

      Compared to the open source LLaVA-NeXT, GPT-4o broadly outperforms. Since the
438
      details of GPT-4o’s training and architecture are not public, we can only speculate on the
439   performance gap. We believe that one contributor to the performance gap may be access
440   to sufficient openly available high-quality data for the given domain, thus demonstrating a
441   need for high-quality, open-access labeled disaster-related aerial imagery. Though GPT-4o
442   clearly highlights the potential of multimodal vision-language models for computer vision
443   tasks—particularly in its demonstrated performance across the different distributions of the
444   validation and test sets—its closed-source nature and restrictive licensing makes it difficult
445   to build derivative works.
446   GPT-4o also suffers from practical challenges for operational use. It is only accessible over
447   the internet via an API, and we found that the API frequently timed out when trying to
448   download large high-resolution images, such as those captured by CAP. This required the
449   images to be downloaded, resized, re-encoded, and uploaded in small batches. As such, it is
450   not suitable for large-scale or time-critical tasks, nor for usage in offline environments. In
451   comparison, our pretrained classifiers run much quicker than both VLMs, and do not require
452   an internet connection like GPT-4o.
453   We also note that in many classes, we observe performance degradation between the validation
454   and the test set. At first glance, this may be considered evidence of overfitting; however, we
455   contend that most of the difference in performance should be attributable to the distribution
456   shift, since we observe a significant performance difference even in the zero-shot vision-
457   language classifiers, which have not seen the training data. The magnitude in difference in
458   performance of our supervised model is generally comparable to that of zero-shot LLaVA-
459
      NeXT from validation to test.
460   Whereas our baseline model was trained with “standard” supervised image classification
461   techniques, we anticipate that approaches that incorporate domain adaptation techniques, or
462   more recent architectures, such as multimodal language models, should handle the distribution
463   shift better than our model. The baseline model can thus be used in conjunction with the
464   LADI v2 validation and test sets to benchmark the efficacy of such approaches.

      5   Conclusion
487
488
      Summary of Contributions. In this paper, we introduce the LADI v2 dataset, a curated
489
      collection of post-disaster aerial images from multiple perspectives, hazard types, and
490   geographies across the United States. The dataset addresses the need for high-quality,
491   diverse training data to support the development of computer vision models for disaster
492   response applications. To facilitate research and implementation efforts, we provide the
493   dataset and two pretrained reference classifiers as open-source resources.
494
      One of the key strengths of LADI v2 is the quality of its annotations, which were provided
495
      by trained Civil Air Patrol volunteers using label sets and training materials developed in
496   collaboration with FEMA. This ensures that the labels are consistent with the standards
497   used by emergency management professionals, enhancing the practical utility of the dataset.
498
499
      Furthermore, LADI v2 features a realistic distribution shift between the training, validation,
      and test splits, capturing the year-to-year variability in disaster events as well as changes
500
      in operational procedures and technology, such as the increased adoption of the WaldoAir
501
      system by the Civil Air Patrol. This characteristic makes the dataset a valuable benchmark
502   for evaluating domain adaptation techniques in the context of disaster response.
503
504   The pretrained classifiers demonstrate strong performance on the LADI v2 test set, outperform
505
      state of the art open source open-vocabulary classification from LLaVA-NeXT, and are
      competitive with commercial offerings such as GPT-4o on the most relevant damage classes.
506
      This comparison highlights the value of open source domain-specific training data for
507
      specialized applications and underscores the potential impact of the LADI v2 dataset on the
508   broader machine learning community.
509
510   Limitations and potential improvements. While LADI v2 represents a step forward in the
511
      availability of high-quality annotated disaster imagery datasets, there are some limitations
      to consider. First, certain hazard types and geographies are under-represented due to
512
      multiple factors, including the likelihood of those hazard types occurring, the likelihood that
513
      a federal disaster declaration is issued, and whether CAP is tasked to collect images. For
514   example, imagery from California is comparatively underrepresented in the dataset relative
515   to the state’s size, population, and exposure to hazards because its state-level emergency
516   management agency is relatively well-equipped, reducing the likelihood of FEMA-supported
517   CAP missions in the area.
518
      Second, LADI v2 is specific to the United States, which means that the architecture, biomes,
519
      disaster types, and infrastructure represented in the dataset is not representative of the rest
520   of the world. Researchers and practitioners working on disaster response applications outside
521   of the US should augment the dataset with additional imagery from the application domain.
522
523
      Finally, LADI v2 currently supports only multi-label classification tasks. Applications
      requiring finer-grained localization or segmentation may require additional effort to adapt
524
      the dataset or models to their specific needs. The alignment of building damage labels to
525
      existing datasets such as those in Manzini et al. (2024); Rahnemoonfar et al. (2022); Gupta
526   et al. (2019) offers a possibility of combining LADI v2 with those other datasets in multi-task
527   training frameworks.
528
529
      Despite these limitations, LADI v2 offers a valuable resource for the machine learning
      community and has the potential to support the development of more effective and efficient
530
      disaster response tools. Future work could expand the dataset to include a wider range of
531
      geographies and disaster types, as well as provide additional annotation classes to support
532   other vision tasks.

674   6.2   Semantic Similarity Analysis
675
676
      CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image Pretraining) (Radford et al., 2021) is a vision language
      model pretrained on 400 million image-text pairs from the internet. The model is given a
677
      batch of images and captions, and is trained to pair the associated image to its respective
678
      caption. In doing so, the model learns to align the encoded image representation to the
679   respective encoded caption text representation. As a result, images with similar textual
680   descriptions tend to be closer together in the CLIP image embedding space, and dissimilar
681   images are further apart. We use this property to characterize the distribution of our
682   validation and test sets below.
683
      We attempt to quantify “out-of-sample-ness" by using distance in CLIP space (Radford
684
      et al., 2021). CLIP embeddings align images with similar textual descriptions, such that
685   images with semantically similar content will be nearby in CLIP space even if they are not
686   necessarily visually similar in their pixel representations. In this way, we can use distance in
687   CLIP space as a proxy for semantic similarity between two images, where similar images
688   are closer in CLIP space.pWe use the Euclidean distance between normalized vectors as the
689   distance metric, d(θ) = 2(1 − cos θ), where θ is the angle between the two vectors. For
690   each image in the validation and test sets, we compute the CLIP distance between it and its
691   nearest neighbor in the training set. We also compute the L1 norm of the error vectors (the
692   difference between the post-sigmoid/pre-threshold prediction and ground-truth vectors) for
693   each image in the validation and test sets.
694   We visualize the joint distribution of the L1 error norm and distance to nearest training
695   point for each image in the validation (blue) and test (orange) set in Figure 5. Kernel density
696   estimates of the marginal distributions are visualized along the top and right hand axes. We
697   can see that the test set is on average further away in CLIP space and has larger error norms.
698   There appears to be a positive relationship between the distance to the nearest training
699   example and the average error norm, as well as the variance in the distribution of the error
700
      norm. This approach could be used characterize how out-of-sample a given set of images is,
      as well as estimate the potential expected degradation of performance associated with that
701
      distribution shift.
