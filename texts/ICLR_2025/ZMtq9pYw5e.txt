009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent research has explored the use of Large Language Models (LLMs) for tack-
012            ling complex graph reasoning tasks. However, due to the intricacies of graph
013            structures and the inherent limitations of LLMs in handling long text, current
014            approaches often fail to deliver satisfactory accuracy, even on small-scale graphs
015            and simple tasks. To address these challenges, we introduce GraphAgent-Reasoner,
016
               a fine-tuning-free framework that utilizes a multi-agent collaboration strategy for
               explicit and precise graph reasoning. Inspired by distributed graph computation
017
               theory, our framework decomposes graph problems into smaller, node-centric tasks
018
               that are distributed among multiple agents. The agents collaborate to solve the
019            overall problem, significantly reducing the amount of information and complexity
020            handled by a single LLM, thus enhancing the accuracy of graph reasoning. By sim-
021            ply increasing the number of agents, GraphAgent-Reasoner can efficiently scale to
022            accommodate larger graphs with over 1,000 nodes. Evaluated on the GraphInstruct
023            dataset, our framework demonstrates near-perfect accuracy on polynomial-time
024            graph reasoning tasks, significantly outperforming the best available models, both
025            closed-source and fine-tuned open-source variants. Our framework also demon-
026            strates the capability to handle real-world graph reasoning applications such as
027
               webpage importance analysis.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Graphs, as a crucial data structure for modeling complex real-world relationships, are ubiquitous
032   across various scenarios, e.g. citation networks, recommendation networks. Many important ap-
033   plications like drug discovery (Stokes et al., 2020), traffic forecasting (Jiang & Luo, 2022), and
034   financial detection (Motie & Raahemi, 2024), require reasoning over graphs to be realized. Noticing
035   the powerful general knowledge and language processing capabilities of Large Language Models
036   (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020), a significant amount of works have focused on using LLMs to per-
037   form various reasoning tasks, such as mathematical formula derivation (Meadows et al., 2023),
038   commonsense reasoning (Madaan et al., 2022), and multi-hop question answering (Creswell et al.,
039
      2023). However, most of them primarily involve shallow or sequential reasoning. To bring the LLM
      reasoning closer to human thinking, it is necessary for LLMs to master deeper and more complex
040
      reasoning, such as graph reasoning.
041
042   Despite significant efforts by researchers to enable LLMs to memorize, comprehend, and perform
043   basic reasoning on graph structures, several issues still persist: 1) The scale of graphs that can be
044   handled is limited. Describing graph structures in natural language inevitably leads to excessively
045
      long inputs. Due to context length limitations and the shortcomings of LLMs in handling lengthy
      text (Liu et al., 2023), previous works (Chai et al., 2023; Fatemi et al., 2024; Perozzi et al., 2024)
046
      could only handle graphs of very limited size (e.g. fewer than 20 nodes and 100 edges). 2) The
047
      performance on graph reasoning tasks is relatively poor. Unlike text, which can tolerate some
048   degree of semantic deviation, reasoning and computation on graphs must be highly precise. However,
049   current works demonstrate poor accuracy (average 20∼60%) in various graph reasoning tasks like
050   connectivity and shortest path. 3) Lacking explicit reasoning paths. Taking the shortest path as an
051   example, the responses of existing models resemble a heuristic search approach to finding the shortest
052   path on a graph, rather than strictly executing an algorithm. This makes it difficult to determine
053   whether LLMs are genuinely deriving the answer through correct reasoning or merely making
      educated guesses. Although GraphWiz (Chen et al., 2024a) attempts to generate explicit reasoning

      paths through fine-tuning, it often fails due to the presence of incomplete or wrong reasoning paths in
055   its training data. Furthermore, GraphWiz exhibits overfitting, where it tends to treat new or unrelated

058
      Motivation. The ultimate goal of graph reasoning is to enable LLMs to leverage graph-related
      knowledge or algorithms to solve real-world graph problems. However, with the development of
059
      information science and hardware storage, the scale of graphs and information per node become too
060
      large for a single LLM to handle. To address this, a natural idea is to use distributed approaches, where
061   a large graph is stored across multiple LLMs separately and compute collaboratively. Therefore, just
062   as graph algorithms have generally evolved from non-distributed to distributed forms (Meng et al.,
063   2024b)), we hope that LLMs can also learn the concept of distributed processing, thereby harnessing
064   the power of swarm intelligence to solve graph problems in real-world scenarios.

      Our Contribution. To address the above limitations, in this paper, we propose the GraphAgent-
      Reasoner(GAR) framework, which leverages the power of swarm intelligence to solve graph reasoning
081
      problems, as shown in Figure 1. We follow a node-centric approach, assigning an agent to each
082
      node, allowing it to focus on processing its own information and communicate with neighbors. Thus,
083   we can easily scale up the size of graphs that can be processed by simply increasing the number of
084   agents. At the same time, under the direction of a Master LLM, graph problems are decomposed into
085   smaller, node-centric tasks, which are assigned to agents for collaborative resolution. This approach
086   significantly reduces the scale and complexity of information each agent needs to process, thereby
087   greatly improving the overall accuracy. Furthermore, since agents must clearly transmit the processed
088   information to neighboring agents, the reasoning process becomes transparent, demonstrating the
089   framework solves graph reasoning problems through clear and correct reasoning, rather than lucky
090   guessing. In summary, our contributions are as follows:
091
      • We propose GraphAgent-Reasoner, the first LLM-based multi-agents framework for graph reason-
092
        ing, which requires no fine-tuning and can utilize any LLM as the underlying reasoning model.

094     surpassing the performance of existing methods.
095

        1,000 nodes, demonstrating exceptional scalability. Furthermore, as the graph size increases, our
097
        framework does not exhibit the significant performance degradation seen in other methods and
098
        maintains robust accuracy.

104   2   P RELIMINARIES AND R ELATED W ORKS
105
106   Preliminaries. In general scenarios, when discussing LLMs solving graph reasoning problems, the
107   input is a (G,Q) pair. G is a graph represented as G = (V, E, {si }, {ti }), where V is the node set and
      E, the edge set. For each node vi ∈ V, a sequential text node feature si is associated; similarly, for


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      each edge ei ∈ E, a sequential text edge feature ti is assigned. The graph G is described in natural
109   language, typically using edge or adjacency list representation. Q is a task-specific instruction or
110   problem description. LLMs will process the (G,Q) pair and return an answer string A.
111
112
      Large Language Models for Graph Reasoning. To further enhance the reasoning capabilities of
      LLMs, many works have attempted to improve the performance of LLMs in graph reasoning. Wang
113
      et al. (2023) first introduces the NLGraph Benchmark to evaluate the performance of LLMs on various
114
      graph reasoning tasks. Fatemi et al. (2024) explores the impact of different graph encoding methods
115   and graph structure types on the performance of LLMs in graph reasoning tasks. Additionally, it
116   introduces another benchmark called GraphQA. Considering the lengthy nature of describing graph
117   structures in text, Chai et al. (2023) and Perozzi et al. (2024) respectively use Transformers and GNNs
118   to encode graph structures and attempt to align them with LLMs. Inspired by how humans understand
119   structural information through the visual modality, Wei et al. (2024) generates corresponding visual
120   images based on graph structures and provides them to visual LLMs for graph reasoning. Chen et al.
121   (2024a) conducted Supervised Fine-Tuning and Directly Prefered Optimization on LLMs, enhancing
122   the performance of LLMs and encouraging them to output explicit reasoning paths.
123   Large Language Model based Multi-Agents. Recent advancements in LLMs have spurred interest
124   in their application within multi-agent systems. LLM-based multi-agent frameworks leverage the
125   natural language understanding and reasoning capabilities of LLMs to enable agents to collaborate,
126   communicate, and solve complex tasks in a distributed manner. Existing multi-agents works for
127   problem solving primarily focuses on applications such as Software Development (Dong et al., 2023;
128   Hong et al., 2024; Qian et al., 2024), Embodied Agents (Zhang et al., 2024; Mandi et al., 2024; Chen
129
      et al., 2024b) and Science Debate (Xiong et al., 2023; Chan et al., 2024). However, using LLM-based
      multi-agents to handle graph data has been less explored, especially in the areas of graph reasoning
130
      and graph computation tasks. This may be due to the hallucination issue inherent in LLMs (Huang
131
      et al., 2023), where their responses are factually incorrect. This problem becomes more complex
132   in a multi-agent setting, as the hallucinations of a single agent may propagate to other nodes by
133   communication (Guo et al., 2024). This requires the performance of individual agents be sufficiently
134   stable to ensure the correct operation of the entire multi-agent system.

137   3    L IMITATIONS OF S INGLE LLM IN G RAPH R EASONING
138
139   Although LLMs exhibit strong language processing and logical reasoning capabilities, problems with
140
      the Transformer architecture and Attention mechanism (Vaswani et al., 2017) still limit the scale and
      accuracy when they process graph problems. There are two primary limitations:

157   The graph structure is too complex to memorize and understand for a single LLM. Using
158   adjacency or edge lists to describe graph structures in natural language is the most intuitive and direct
159   method, facilitating the processing of graph data by LLMs through text. However, this approach
160   inevitably leads to a lengthy context, as the number of edges can grow quadratically with the number
161   of nodes. As the graph scales up and becomes denser, the graph structure becomes highly complex,
      requiring a large amount of tokens to describe the edge relationships. When the text becomes too


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      lengthy, it becomes difficult for LLMs to properly allocate attention, and they may even struggle with
163   simple tasks such as key-value pair matching Liu et al. (2023). This presents significant challenges
164   for LLMs in identifying key information for graph reasoning tasks from the lengthy context. Figure 2
165   shows the performance of a single LLM in memorizing one-hop neighbor nodes. We observe that as
166   the number of nodes in the graph increases, various LLMs exhibit a significant decline in accuracy. If
167   a single LLM cannot even correctly recall basic graph structural information like node neighbors, it
168   becomes difficult to proceed with more complex graph reasoning or computation.
169   Furthermore, the graph structure is described in a sequential manner. LLMs have to identify implicit
170   graph structures from sequential text. Since the processing of LLMs is a black-box operation, it is
171   difficult to assert that they truly construct graph structures implicitly and thereby understand them.
172   Huang et al. (2024) conducted extensive experiments to explore whether LLMs treat the input prompts
173   as graphs or merely as paragraphs with keywords on TAGs. The results show that the performance of
174   LLMs in handling TAGs primarily stems from the context rather than the graph structure. LLMs tend
175   to process the graph description as linearized paragraphs rather than graphs.
176   A single LLM struggles to solve reasoning problems in real-world scenarios. Researchers train
177   LLMs on graph reasoning tasks to empower them to utilize learned graph-related knowledge or
178   algorithms to tackle real-world graph problems. However, in practical scenarios, the amount of
179   information associated with each node can be enormous. Take citation networks as an example: a
180   single node represents a paper, and its node information includes the title, abstract, and references,
181   which could amount to several thousand tokens. In addition to the complexity of graph structures,
182   the need to handle a large amount of node information further exacerbates the burden on a single
183
      LLM and highlights its shortcomings in processing long contexts. Moreover, using a single LLM to
      handle the entire network is inefficient, as it cannot coherently process the entire network’s problems.
184
      Typically, it is necessary to manually compress or summarize the information for each node and then
185
      feed local subgraphs to the LLM for processing (Guo et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023).
186
187   Furthermore, many current works (Chen et al., 2024a; Perozzi et al., 2024) require training GNNs or
188   fine-tuning LLMs on individual or multiple graph reasoning tasks. However, when transferring to
189
      other graph tasks, a certain degree of performance degradation occurs, and retraining or fine-tuning
      for new graph tasks consumes a significant amount of time and resources. Whether LLMs can apply
190
      the graph knowledge and algorithms learned during the training process to actual graph reasoning
191
      also remains an open question. We explored this question in 5.3 and observed significant overfitting
192   in LLMs fine-tuned on specific graph reasoning tasks. Therefore, the ideal solution would be to
193   leverage the powerful general knowledge acquired during the pre-training phase of LLMs through an
194   appropriate approach, enabling them to handle graph reasoning tasks as naturally as they do with
195   natural language problems.

      4    G RAPH AGENT-R EASONER
199
200
201   To solve the limitations above, we propose a novel framework based on multi-agent collaboration
202   called GraphAgent-Reasoner as shown in Figure 3, aiming to solve graph reasoning problems
203   explicitly and correctly. The interface of the framework is a Master LLM, which is responsible for
204   processing the textual input of graph problems, constructing the agent network, directing them to
205   collaboratively solve the problem, and finally aggregating the states of all agents to derive the solution.
206
      Its implementation is based on the React Agent proposed by Yao et al. (2023), which is capable
      of reasoning based on the environment and executing corresponding actions, as detailed later. The
207
      pipeline of GAR consists of four steps: Graph Construction, Algorithm Establishing, Distributed
208
      Execution and Master Summarization.
209
210   Graph Construction. Given an input pair (G, Q), the Master LLM first extracts the node and edge
211   information from the textual description of graph G. It then constructs an agent for each node and
212
      initializes the node’s state and neighbor information, forming an interconnected network of agents.
      Each agent independently maintains its state and neighbor data, communicates with adjacent agents
213
      based on instructions from the Master LLM, and updates its state in each round.
214
215   Algorithm Establishing. To accommodate diverse graph tasks and fully exploit the knowledge
      embedded in LLMs during pre-training, we propose a unified solution approach framed within a

237   distributed paradigm as shown in Algorithm 1. This approach requires the Master LLM to specify six
238   core components for each problem: State, Message, Initialization, Send, Update, and Termination.
239
240   • State: The local information maintained by each node, representing its current state. This can
241     include attributes like node features, labels, or any other task-specific data. The states evolve as
242     nodes receive messages and update their information.
243   • Message: The data transmitted between nodes during the communication phase. Messages
244     typically contain information that neighboring nodes need to perform updates, such as feature
245     values, distances, or other task-relevant information.
246   • Initialization: At the start of the execution, each node initializes its state with predefined values,
247     which may be based on node IDs, input features or task-specific requirements. This step ensures
248     that the graph is ready to begin the communication process.
249   • Send: After initialization, each node generates messages based on its current state and sends them
250     to its neighboring nodes. This step is repeated in each iteration, allowing nodes to continuously
251     exchange information with their neighbors.
252
      • Update: Upon receiving messages from its neighbors, each node updates its state by aggregating
253     the incoming messages and combining them with its current state. This iterative process enables
254     nodes to refine their information over time.
255
      • Termination: The algorithm halts when a predefined stopping condition is met, such as reaching a
256
        fixed number of iterations, achieving convergence, or satisfying a task-specific criterion. Once the
257     termination condition is reached, each node will send its final state to the Master LLM, and the
258     execution terminates.
259
260   Since LLMs lack prior knowledge of this distributed paradigm, to facilitate the Master LLM’s
261   understanding and application of the framework, we develop a distributed algorithm library that
262   adheres to this distributed paradigm, from which the Master LLM can query relevant algorithm
263   templates to generate distributed solutions within this paradigm. Specifically, we selected classic
264   distributed graph algorithms and documented their implementations under this distributed paradigm.
265   Some examples are presented in Appendix A.1. Drawing on prior work (Zheng et al., 2024; Meng
266
      et al., 2024a), we endeavor to write detailed reasoning steps of each part in the algorithm to encourage
      the agent to think step by step as much as possible, which plays an important role in enhancing the
267
      success rate of individual agents.
268

      Datasets. We conduct our experiments on the graph reasoning tasks proposed in GraphInstruct (Chen
325   et al., 2024a). This dataset contains nine graph reasoning problems with different time complexity,
326   ranging from linear and polynomial complexity to NP-complete.
327
328   • Linear. Cycle Detection (Detect if a given graph G contains any cycles), Connectivity (Assess if
329     two nodes u and v in a given graph G are connected via a path), Bipartite Graph Check (Judge if
330     a given graph G is bipartite), and Topological Sort (Find a topological ordering of vertices in a

337   algorithms available for their solution. Consequently, the Master LLM is unable to design correct and

495   6    C ONCLUSION
496
497   We first summarize three key issues faced by existing LLMs in graph reasoning tasks: limited graph
498   scale, poor performance, and the lack of explicit reasoning paths. We then reflect on the limitations of
499   a single LLM in addressing graph reasoning problems, such as the graph structures being too complex
500   to memorize and understand and the overwhelming information in real-world graph reasoning
501   scenarios. To address these challenges, we propose GraphAgent-Reasoner, a framework based on
502   multi-agent collaboration to solve graph reasoning problems. This framework demonstrates superior
503
      accuracy and scalability, significantly surpassing existing closed-source and fine-tuned open-source
      models. Our experiments show its robust scalability, maintaining high accuracy on large graphs (up
504
      to 1,000 nodes). Our case study on webpage importance analysis further illustrates its capability to
505
      handle real-world graph reasoning problems. Future work will focus on designing more accurate and
506   scalable LLM-based multi-agent graph reasoning frameworks, aiming to apply them to larger and
507   more complex real-world reasoning scenarios.
