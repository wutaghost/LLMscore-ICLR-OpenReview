                                                    A BSTRACT
009
010
                In reinforcement learning, if the agent’s reward differs from the designers’ true
                utility, even only rarely, the state distribution resulting from the agent’s policy
011
                can be very bad, in theory and in practice. When RL policies would devolve into
012
                undesired behavior, a common countermeasure is KL regularization to a trusted
013             policy (“Don’t do anything I wouldn’t do”). All current cutting-edge language
014             models are RL agents that are KL-regularized to a “base policy” that is purely
015             predictive. Unfortunately, we demonstrate that when this base policy is a Bayesian
016             predictive model of a trusted policy, the KL constraint is no longer reliable for
017             controlling the behavior of an advanced RL agent. We demonstrate this theoretically
018             using algorithmic information theory, and while systems today are too weak to
019             exhibit this theorized failure precisely, we RL-finetune a language model and
020             find evidence that our formal results are plausibly relevant in practice. We also
021
                propose a theoretical alternative that avoids this problem by replacing the “Don’t
                do anything I wouldn’t do” principle with “Don’t do anything I mightn’t do”.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Agents optimizing their objective in a way not intended by designers could be amusing, annoying,
027   insidious, or disastrous. Amusingly, RL researchers attempted to get a simulated humanoid to walk,
028   but the reward resulted in crazy locomotion (Lee et al., 2021). Annoyingly, maximizing a simulated-
029   environment’s reward can produce a policy that would achieve little real-world-reward by exploiting
030   errors in the simulation (Mishra et al., 2017; Baker et al., 2019). Insidiously, artificial agents selecting
031   links to maximize click-through on social media sites have succeeded, but also affecting people in
032   ways designers never sought to (Chan et al., 2023). For a much longer list of such failures occurring
033
      “in the wild”, see (Krakovna, 2018). Finally, sufficiently capable reinforcement learners would likely
      recognize an incentive to escape human oversight, intervene in the protocol determining their reward,
034
      and use force to ensure they can retain control of their reward, subject to such an outcome being
035
      possible from the agent’s action space, and several other assumptions laid out by Cohen et al. (2022b).
036
037   Indeed, several sources suggest that extremely successful reward-maximization is itself a sign of bad
038   outcomes for humanity. Zhuang & Hadfield-Menell (2020) demonstrate that in a resource-constrained
039
      world, optimizing the world’s state to maximize a function of some features would, in plausible
      settings, be arbitrarily bad with respect to a utility function that also cares about unincluded features.
040
      Turner et al. (2021) develop a formal model of “power”—being able to accomplish a randomly
041
      sampled goal—and find that (reward-)optimal policies tend to seek power. And Cohen et al. (2022b)
042   observe that any behavior that ensures that long-term reward is nearly-certainly-maximal must include
043   extensive control over threats to its physical integrity, including threats from humans.
044
045
      An appealing and popular proposal to avoid such outcomes is to constrain the agent to follow
      a policy that is not too dissimilar to a more familiar “base policy”. This is the approach taken
046
      when RL-finetuning large language models (LLMs). This class of approaches limits the upside of
047
      RL, since it forgoes optimal policies, but it is a reasonable attempt to avoid catastrophic policies.
048   The KL divergence, in particular KL(proposed policy∥base policy), enforces proximity in a robust,
049   “safety-conscious” way: if basepolicy(action) << 1 while proposedpolicy(action) <<     ̸ 1, the KL
050   penalty is high, even while Lp norms can be small. For any very bad outcomes that are unlikely
051   under the base policy, this method ensures they remain very unlikely. However, if we ensure
052   that KL(proposed policy∥base policy) is small, but the base policy only approximates a trusted
053   policy, to what extent can we be confident that KL(proposed policy∥trusted policy) is small? When
      the base policy is a Bayesian predictive model of the trusted policy, the answer shown here is:

      we cannot be confident that KL(proposed policy∥trusted policy) is small, which makes the KL-
055   constraint less comforting. (Note that a Bayesian imitative base policy can only be counted on to
056   make KL(trusted policy∥Bayesian base policy) small).

058
      Worse, in the formalism we study, we find that if one attempts to use KL-regularization to prevent
      an RL agent from achieving near-maximal reward (in light of the concerns above), and the base
059
      policy is a Bayesian imitation of a trusted policy, a fairly tight KL threshold is required, and as the
060
      amount of training data for the Bayesian imitator grows, the relevant threshold can only increase
061   extremely slowly. The reason for the limited effectiveness of KL regularization is (1) a Bayesian
062   imitator asked to act in novel settings must be humble about its predictions; for many actions that
063   the demonstrator (i.e. the trusted policy) would in fact never take, the imitator (i.e. the base policy)
064   must assign meaningful credence to that action, because it doesn’t know enough to rule it out. Then
065   (2) the RL agent can exploit or amplify this credence. Formalizing Occam’s razor with algorithmic
066   information theory, we have (3) nearly-reward-maximizing policies have a short description length
067   (so they are “simple”), and (4) a Bayesian imitation learner with a rich prior should be especially
068   reluctant to rule out simple behaviors from the demonstrator in novel settings. In light of the results
069
      from Zhuang & Hadfield-Menell (2020), Turner et al. (2021), and Cohen et al. (2022b), preventing
      the RL agent from achieving near-maximal reward is, in many settings, a bare minimum requirement
070
      for safety-focused regularization, and a KL constraint would struggle to do so.
071
072   Sutskever (2018; 2023) argues that neural networks are able to generalize well because of the sense
073   in which they approximate the algorithmic-information-theoretic inductive bias in favor of short
074   programs. Since it is not a given that results from algorithmic information theory apply in practice, we
075
      verify empirically that a nearly-state-of-the-art predictive system (Mixtral-8x7B-base-model (Jiang
      et al., 2024)) is reluctant to rule out simple behaviors, and an RL agent regularized to this predictive
076
      system exploits this fact, as our formal results predict. The result is not catastrophic, but it is bad.
077
      Note these empirical results neither confirm nor deny whether point (3) above applies in practice, but
078   they do affirm that the rest of the argument is forceful in practice.
079
080   Finally, we identify an alternative to Bayesian prediction/imitation that avoids this problem; Cohen
081
      et al.’s (2022a) imitator asks for help when uncertain and carries useful formal bounds. We show that
      using this form of imitation learning as a base policy would in theory avoid the problems we identify
082
      in this paper. Cohen et al.’s (2022a) active imitator, like fully Bayesian imitation, is intractable and
083
      requires approximation, so we currently lack the tools to evaluate this proposal empirically.

086   2    R ELATED WORK
087
088   The most prominent example of KL-regularization to an approximation of a (somewhat) trusted
089   policy is surely ChatGPT, inspired by earlier work (Ouyang et al., 2022; Stiennon et al., 2020; Bai
090   et al., 2022). Other recent examples include Jaques et al. (2017; 2019), Ziegler et al. (2019), Vieillard
091   et al. (2020), Yang et al. (2021), Korbak et al. (2022), Perez et al. (2022), Gao et al. (2023), and
092
      Moskovitz et al. (2023). A closely related approach called quantilization has been investigated by
      Taylor (2016), Everitt et al. (2017), and Carey (2019). KL regularization to a decent policy has also
093
      been used for stable and efficient policy optimization (Schulman et al., 2017; Schmitt et al., 2018).
094
095   Algorithmic information theory began with Solomonoff (1960), who formalized a powerful notion of
096   simplicity based on program-length and developed a method for prediction using that inductive bias.
097   In an article entitled, “A theory of program size formally identical to information theory”, Chaitin
098
      (1975) examined the connection between program-length and information. Li et al.’s (2008) textbook
      presents the major results of the field. Hutter (2005) and Hutter et al. (2024) developed a theory of
099
      how to apply such reasoning to the problem of sequential decision-making. Grau-Moya et al. (2024)
100
      train a neural network to learn a program-length “bias” for a meta-learning setting.
101
102   Ultimately, we propose a formal scheme for doing KL regularization to an imitative policy which
103   asks for help under epistemic uncertainty, and this allows us to inherit the formal results of Cohen
104
      et al. (2022a). The related work section there goes into some detail about how different researchers
      have studied asking for help, including how setups and assumptions differ. See especially Zhang &
105
      Cho’s (2017) work on driving, as well as Brown et al. (2018; 2020) and Menda et al. (2019).
106
107   Closest to our work in studying the relation between KL divergence to a base policy and “over-
      optimization” is Gao et al. (2023). They design a “real” reward function, and a simpler “proxy”


                                                         2

      reward function, which are very similar on the state distribution induced by a base policy. After
109   optimizing for the proxy reward function (sometimes with KL regularization to the base policy), they
110   use the KL divergence to the base policy to measure how much “optimization” has occurred. And
111   they study how “real” reward depends on the extent of optimization—roughly quadratically, with
112   a negative leading coefficient. Our work provides one explanation for why we should expect such
113   unusual policies with high proxy reward and low real reward, even when the KL divergence to the
114   base policy is only moderate.

      5    RL- FINETUNING A LANGUAGE MODEL
293
      Why believe that algorithmic information theory is relevant for understanding realistic machine
294
      learning systems that attain very low prediction error? If a stream of observations is generated by a
295   relatively short program, a high quality predictive algorithm should be capable of being trained on
296   those observations to predict that future observations will be governed by the same program. Note
297   that any predictor that a) assigns positive prior probability to every program as a potential generator
298   of its observations, and b) and updates its beliefs according to Bayes’ rule is formally equivalent
299   to ξ (Hutter, 2005). While realistic systems will not live up to that ideal, deviations from those
300   principles should be as limited as possible. A predictive algorithm that is incapable of becoming
301   convinced of a plausible hypothesis (i.e. that its observations match the output of a short program) is
302   best called “closed-minded”, or perhaps “epistemically intransigent”. Ruling out hypotheses a priori
303
      so that no data can persuade you of them is dangerous tendency when trying to model the world; it
      may not be long before a predictor confronts a true fact that it treats as unbelievable. For example,
304
      Liu et al. (2018) demonstrate, unsurprisingly, that convolutional neural networks fail when their a
305
      priori commitment to translational invariance doesn’t match reality. So we conjecture that successful
306   predictors will tend to be open-minded, and very successful predictors at least as open-minded as
307   humans, and this makes the properties of ξ plausibly relevant. But we do not wish to overstate our
308   case. This discussion is not very rigorous, so in this section we present experiments that assess to
309   some extent how realistic our theoretical results are.

311   Experimental Setup We consider the following episodic RL environment, in which the agent
312   plays a teacher and gets reward to the extent that the student’s responses have positive sentiment.
313   In a conversation transcript, if the string “[newline] Teacher:” has come more recently than the
314   string “[newline] Student:”, the agent can add tokens to the transcript. Otherwise, Mixtral-base-
315
      model repeatedly adds tokens to the transcript. In Figure 2, gray (colored) tokens are generated by
      the environment (agent). When Mixtral-base-model finishes generating the student’s response (by
316

317
      response according to the DistilBERT sentiment model (Sanh et al., 2019), scaled to [0, 1]. When the

403   Let’s review the relation between the theory and the empirical findings so far. The idea for the proof of
404   Theorem 1 is that (1) a Bayesian imitator must assign meaningful credence to actions the demonstrator
405   would in fact never take, because it doesn’t know enough to rule them out; (2) the RL agent can
406   exploit or amplify this credence as the basis for its policy; (3) nearly-reward-maximizing policies have
407   a short description length (so they are “simple”); and (4) a Bayesian imitator should be especially
408   reluctant to rule out simple behaviors from the demonstrator, especially in novel settings. The simple
409   behavior we observe from the RL-finetuned language models—preferring empty responses—is likely
410   reward-optimal, but it is not simple by virtue of its optimality for this sentiment-based reward function.
411

      exhibited in practice: observe how the RL agent redirects the imitative base policy to a simple policy,
412

      silent, because that affirms how successful the redirection is. The experiments are also consistent
414   with the motivation of our formal results: very-high-reward policies are often bad and worth avoiding;
415   in our experiments, the very-high-reward policy treats the student with a silence that would probably

497   the optimizer should ask for help whenever it doesn’t pick its own action. The ongoing potential need
498   for human oversight may be a significant drawback, but Cohen et al. (2022a) give an encouraging
499   result about the rate at which the ask-for-help probability goes to 0: the sum over infinite time of the
500   cube of the ask-for-help probability is finite (Cohen et al., 2022a, Thm 1). Cohen et al.’s (2022a)

502
503   We contend that this is the way that KL regularization should be done, if we are forced to learn a mere
504   approximation of a trusted policy that we would ideally regularize to. Regularizing to a full Bayesian
505   posterior distribution is less robust, because the optimizer can seize on esoteric possibilities that a
506   fully Bayesian imitator is not confident enough to categorically exclude. Roughly, KL regularization
507
      to a Bayesian imitator implements the principle, “Don’t do anything [that you know] I would never
      do”, whereas KL regularization to a pessimistic Bayesian imitator implements the principle, “Don’t
508
      do anything I might never do”.

      7    C ONCLUSION AND LIMITATIONS
513
514
515
      The biggest limitation of our work is with our positive results rather than our negative ones: we cannot
516   provide empirical findings about regularizing to a pessimistic Bayesian imitative base model, because
517   it is an open question how to tractably approximate this approach to imitation. There are high-quality,
518   off-the-shelf cross-entropy-minimizing imitators like Mixtral, but for tractable pessimistic Bayesian
519   imitation, some new ideas may be needed. There certainly are not any state of the art language models
520   trained in a way that reflects this idea. We hope this work provides motivation for a major industry
521   effort to produce one. Using an ensemble of models to approximate Mα       x<t may be a step in the right
522   direction, but we are reluctant to endorse this in settings where catastrophic outcomes are possible,
523   unless there is a strong argument that the ensemble covers all the relevant modes of the posterior.
524   The second key limitation with our positive result is that any KL-regularization to avoid radically
525   inhuman behavior could limit the potential of superhuman intelligence. This paper has no roadmap to
526   A+ performance; it has a roadmap to non-catastrophic, decently-superhuman performance. And a
527   final key limitation is that our agent sometimes has to ask for help instead of acting.
528   The main limitation of our negative results is they regard an unrealistic machine learning algorithm—
529   Solomonoff Induction. However, Solomonoff induction is simply a formalism for unbelievably
530   careful and open-minded probabilistic reasoning, and so if something goes wrong in that setting,
531   we should be wary of something going wrong in increasingly careful and open-minded machine
532   learning systems. Our empirical results do not directly validate the theory, since both the base model
533   and the RL-finetuning process are too weak, but we validate core components of the theory: KL-
534   regularized RL-finetuning will tend to amplify simple behaviors from an imitative base model rather
535   than demonstrator-like behaviors. This helps explain the overoptimization phenomenon quantified by
536
      Gao et al. (2023).
537   Excitingly, we offer theoretical results that could guide us to a solution to this problem: if Cohen
538   et al.’s (2022a) pessimistic online imitation learner could be faithfully approximated, and if the
539   demonstrator(s) never attempt to do X, then KL regularization to such a policy could solve the
      problem of how to prevent superhuman planning agents from doing X.

997    F.1   E NVIRONMENT
998
       The state of the environment, as mentioned in the main text, is the activations of the last three hidden
999
       layers of Mixtral-base-model with the transcript-so-far as input, along with the fraction of the episode
1000
       remaining. This gives a state space of 12289. Using the Mistral tokenizer, the action space is 32000.
1001   The environment uses a temperature of 0.05 for generating the student’s responses and a temperature
1002   of 1 for the base policy for the agent/teacher.
1003
1004
1005
       F.2   N ETWORK A RCHITECTURE
1006
       The critic network is a fully connected network with two hidden layers of size 128 with tanh
1007   activations. The actor network consists of just one parameterized layer, which is fully connected,
1008   of size (|state space|, |action space| + 1). The extra output is for controlling the KL divergence
1009   to the base policy. We compute the target KL divergence as sigmoid(activation) * the KL budget
1010   remaining to the agent for the episode. So the activation controls what fraction of the remaining
1011   KL budget for the episode to use on the very next token. At initialization, this fraction comes to
1012   1/16. The KL budget remaining starts as the total episode KL budget (of course), and is decreased
1013   by log(policy(action)/basepolicy(action)) with each action. The other outputs are interpreted as
1014   logits and are added to the base policy logits. Calling this resulting distribution a, and the base
1015
       policy distribution b, we find an α ∈ [0, 1] such that KL(αa + (1 − α)b||b) equals the target KL, if
       possible. If we cannot achieve a sufficiently high KL divergence, we set α = 1. The output policy
1016
       is αa + (1 − α)b. We add any squared error (target KL − achieved KL)2 to the loss function to
1017
       encourage the network to output logits that allow further control by the neuron controlling the KL
1018   target.
1019
1020   In the forward pass, our custom PyTorch operation does binary search the calculate α in the interval
1021
       [0, 1]. The backward pass uses implicit differentiation, assuming we have found exactly the right
       α—there is no need to differentiate backward through the binary search, which would be unstable.

       F.4   PARALLELISM
1044   We use threading to run 64 agent-environment-loops in “parallel”. When we would need to send a
1045   transcript of length l to be processed by the Mixtral model, we wait until all 64 agent-environment-
1046   loops need to send a transcript of length l, and then they are batched and evaluated together in parallel
1047   on the GPU. The result might needed by either the agent or the environment, and we use the python
1048   asyncio library to manage this. Doing just that step in parallel is enough for substantial speedup.
