011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Many methods have been proposed for unsupervised time series anomaly detec-
014            tion. Despite some progress, research on predicting future anomalies is still rela-
015
               tively scarce. Predicting anomalies is particularly challenging due to the diverse
               reaction time and the lack of labeled data. To address these challenges, we pro-
016
               pose MultiRC to integrate reconstructive and contrastive learning for joint learn-
017
               ing of anomaly prediction and detection, with multi-scale structure and adaptive
018            dominant period mask to deal with the diverse reaction time. MultiRC also gen-
019            erates negative samples to provide essential training momentum for the anomaly
020            prediction tasks and prevent model degradation. We evaluate seven benchmark
021            datasets from different fields. For both anomaly prediction and detection tasks,
022            MultiRC outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods. The code is available at
023            https://anonymous.4open.science/status/MultiRC-CCE6.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      With the advancement of Internet of things (IoT), an increase number of sensors are utilized in in-
028
      dustrial facilities to collect data in the form of continuous time series, which realizes the monitoring
029
      of system status (Li et al., 2021a). The anomaly detection technology (Li et al., 2021b; Wen et al.,
030   2022; Chen et al., 2021a) has been widely used, which locates system malfunctions by identifying
031   anomalies in historical data (Figure 1a). Effectively detecting anomalies helps pinpoint the sources
032   of faults and prevent the spread of malfunctions. However, anomaly detection can only identify
033   issues after they have occurred, which cannot meet the need for preventive maintenance timely.
034
      As a new practical problem, anomaly prediction aims to predict whether an anomaly will occur in
035
      the future by capturing the fluctuations at the current time. In IoT, anomaly prediction can prevent
036   full-scale failures. Previous works (Yin et al., 2022; You et al., 2024) assume and have observed
037   that anomalies in production often do not occur suddenly but evolve gradually. Different kinds
038   of fluctuations exist before the real anomalies, where data change from normal to abnormal. As
039   shown in Figure 1b, the yellow period exhibits fluctuation patterns beginning distinct from the past,
040   indicating the occurrence of possible future anomalies. The time interval of fluctuations is called
041   reaction time, and such fluctuations are called precursor signals of future anomalies (Jhin et al.,
042   2023). Following previous works (Yin et al., 2022), we predict future anomalies by identifying
043   precursor signals, and emphasize that anomalies without precursor signals are unpredictable.
044   Due to the costs and rarity of anomalies, anomaly labels are difficult to obtain. To solve this,
045   anomaly detection is often modeled with self-supervised (reconstructive (Zhou et al., 2024) or con-
046   trastive (Yang et al., 2023)) learning. Reconstruction methods expect normal points to be accurately
047   reconstructed, while anomalies exhibit large reconstruction errors. Contrastive approaches promote
048   similarity in the representations of positive sample pairs. Despite the effectiveness of existing works
049   in anomaly detection, these methods cannot be directly and effectively extended to anomaly predic-
050
      tion, as shown in the following challenges.
051   Challenge 1: Different anomalies may occur rapidly or slowly, thus resulting in reaction time with
052   varying lengths. Reconstruction methods focus on reconstructing individual data points which strug-
053   gle to capture gradual reaction time (Li et al., 2022; Audibert et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2022a). Con-
      trastive learning methods compare time series segments based on fixed time lengths which struggle

068   to capture varying reaction time. Further, variates in the time series have different semantics, re-
069   sulting in existence of distinct reaction time for different variates. Existing methods cannot identify
070   fluctuations with such varying reaction time for different variates adaptively.
071   Challenge 2: Without labeled anomalies as negative samples, existing self-supervised methods
072   will degrade into a trivial model (Ruff et al., 2018) that cannot learn meaningful information. For
073   contrastive approaches, the absence of negative samples will lead to the learned features falling into a
074   single mode where all features seem similar (Chen & He, 2021), thus failing to identify fluctuations.
075   For reconstruction methods, a large model will degrade into identity transformation and a small
076   model cannot learn complex temporal patterns (Wang et al., 2024), thus failing to assess the accurate
077   magnitude of the fluctuations. Anomaly prediction requires not only identifying fluctuations but
078
      also assessing the magnitude of these fluctuations to predict the probability of future anomalies.
      Therefore, the existing methods fail to learn meaningful information for anomaly prediction.
079
080   In this work, we propose joint learning for time series anomaly prediction and detection with multi-
081   scale reconstructive contrast (MultiRC), where a dual branch with joint reconstructive and con-
082   trastive learning is built upon a multi-scale structure. For Challenge 1, our novel multi-scale struc-
083   ture adaptively recognizes the varying reaction time for different variates with adaptive dominant
084
      period mask. Meanwhile, we exploit an asymmetric encoder-decoder to fuse cross-scale informa-
      tion. For Challenge 2, we incorporate controlled generative strategies to construct diverse precursors
085
      as negative samples to prevent model degradation, instead of only generating positive samples by
086
      data augmentation as in the existing contrastive methods (Woo et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022b).
087   Specifically, our contrastive learning judges whether there are fluctuations by learning to distinguish
088   positive and negative samples, while reconstruction learning assesses the magnitude of these fluctu-
089   ations via learning to minimize the reconstruction errors for positive samples. Moreover, we provide
090   a novel anomaly measurement for joint learning of anomaly prediction and detection, which helps
091   anomaly prediction by detecting the degree of fluctuations in reaction time.
092
      Our contributions are summarized as follows:
093
094   • A novel multi-scale structure is proposed to both reconstructive and contrastive learning, to adap-
095     tively recognize varying reaction time for different variates with adaptive dominant period mask.
096
      • We propose controlled generative strategies to prevent model degradation and propose joint learn-
097     ing of anomaly prediction and detection with reconstructive contrast.
098
099
      • For both anomaly prediction and anomaly detection tasks, MultiRC achieves state-of-the-art re-
        sults across seven benchmark datasets.

102   2   R ELATED WORK
103
104   Multi-scale Learning. Some multi-scale methods have been proposed for time series model-
105   ing (Chen et al., 2021b; Shen et al., 2020; Challu et al., 2022). THOC (Shen et al., 2020) learn
106   multi-scale representations through different skip connections in RNN for each time point, but it
107   cannot capture gradual change for continuous time intervals. DGHL (Challu et al., 2022) maps time
      series windows to hierarchical latent spaces, but it cannot capture cross-scale information from win-

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      dows of different lengths. Besides, none of these methods can adaptively recognize different scales
109   or fuse multi-scale information for anomaly prediction.
110
111   Time Series Anomaly Detection. As a problem of practical significance, time series anomaly
112
      detection has remained a focal point in the fields of machine learning and data mining. Over recent
      years, researchers have used reconstruction paradigm (Su et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2021; Wang et al.,
113
      2024) or contrastive learning paradigm (Yang et al., 2023; Jhin et al., 2023) to delve into deeper
114
      data representations and patterns. In the reconstructive paradigm, OmniAnomaly (Su et al., 2019)
115   captures the normal patterns of multivariate time series by learning their robust representations.
116   Anomaly Transformer (Xu et al., 2021) proposes a minimax strategy that combines reconstruction
117   loss to amplify the difference between normal and abnormal. D3 R (Wang et al., 2024) combines
118   decomposition and noise diffusion to directly reconstruct corrupted data. However, these studies
119   either operate on a singular scale or perform indiscriminate reconstruction across the entire input
120   range, limiting their flexibility and adaptability in extracting anomaly signals.
121   In the contrastive learning paradigm, DCdetector (Yang et al., 2023) introduces a dual-branch at-
122   tention structure to learn a permutation invariant representation. However, it only focuses on rela-
123   tionships between positive samples, which greatly limits its performance in anomaly prediction tasks
124   that require explicit anomaly labels. PAD (Jhin et al., 2023) directly uses resampling to generate arti-
125   ficial anomaly patterns during data preprocessing. This simple approach fails to adequately simulate
126   the fluctuations of anomaly precursors. Although numerous contrastive paradigms (Yue et al., 2022;
127   Lee et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022b; He et al., 2020) have been proposed in the field of time series
128   analysis, they often merely treat the majority of time series segments as negative samples, which
129
      constrains the potential of contrastive learning to precisely identify anomalous patterns. We extract
      timing information at multiple scales to adapt to changes in reaction time. Furthermore, the model
130
      identifies fluctuations and amplitude of fluctuations through contrastive learning and reconstruction.
131
      Appendix B shows the architecture comparison of three approaches.

134   3     METHODOLOGY
135
136
      Problem Definition. We denote the time series X ∈ RT ×c of length T , where each xt ∈ Rc is
137
      the observation at time t and c is the dimension of multivariate, such as the number of different
138
      sensors. The reaction time is a time interval [t − r, t] with length r, where there are fluctuations in
139   data Xt−r:t called the precursor, and there are real anomalies in future data Xt+1:t+f . Data begin
140   to change from normal to abnormal during the reaction time. The greater the degree of fluctuations,
141   the higher the probability of future anomalies occurring. We emphasize that the anomalies without
142   reaction time are unpredictable (Yin et al., 2022; Jhin et al., 2023).
143
      For time series anomaly detection tasks, it takes the input X and outputs a vector y ∈ RT by sliding
144
      widows where yt ∈ {0, 1} and 1 indicates an anomaly. For anomaly prediction, given a current
145   time t and the historical sub-sequence Xt−h:t of length h, the model outputs a probability score p̂t ,
146   which indicates whether there is precursor in Xt−h:t or not and whether the future sub-sequence
147   Xt+1:t+f will be anomalous, where f ≥ 1 is the length of the look-forward window.

150   3.1   OVERALL FRAMEWORK
151
152   MultiRC consists of four modules (Figure 2). Input sequence processing normalizes the input mul-
153
      tivariate time series through instance normalization and then uses channel-independence to split the
      input into univariate sequences. In Figure 2 we take one univariate sequence x as an example, and the
154
      other univariate sequences are modeled similarly. The multi-scale structure uses adaptive dominant
155
      period mask to adaptively recognizes varying reaction time for different variates, and then segments
156   univariate sequences into patches of varying granularities. Masked time series reconstruction ex-
157   ploits asymmetric encoder-decoder to fuse multi-scale information and then evaluate the amplitude
158   of fluctuations. Generative-based contrastive learning uses controlled strategies to construct neg-
159   ative samples to prevent model degradation. The encoder learns representations for distinguishing
160   positive and negative samples, thus better judging the existence of fluctuation in the reaction time.
161   Our encoder backbone consists of Transformer blocks (Vaswani et al., 2017) to extract temporal
      features from time series data.

      3.2           M ULTI - SCALE S TRUCTURE
191
192   The assumption of the existence of reaction time (Yin et al., 2022; Jhin et al., 2023), where time
193   series begin to change from normal to abnormal, ensures anomaly prediction. However, the duration
194   of reaction time varies across different varieties and timestamps. To solve this, at each current time
195   t, we explore the most dominant periods qt for each univariate sequence in the frequency domain.
196   Specifically, the multi-scale structure contains adaptive dominant period mask and multi-scale
197   patching. Adaptive dominant period mask takes each univariate sequence x as input and produces its
198   masked sequence xm . Intuitively, one variate with longer periodic changes is more likely to evolve
199   gradually while one variate with shorter periodic changes is more likely to evolve rapidly. Thus, we
200   capture and mask with the most dominant periods for each variate to estimate the reaction time.
201   Multi-scale patching takes as input x and xm in the dual branch, respectively. For masked time
202   series reconstruction, xm is fed into multi-scale patching; for generative-based contrastive learning,
203   x is fed in. The multi-scale patching produces patched sequences {xm,p }ap=1 and {xp }ap=1 with
204   different scales. This multi-scale patching is independent of the dominant period mask, which helps
205   to simultaneously capture features from time intervals of different scales, thereby accommodating
206   the varying reaction time that is inconsistent with the dominant periods.

409   Tables 1 and Table 2 respectively present the performance comparison of the model in anomaly
410   prediction and detection tasks. As indicated by the tables, on all five real-world datasets, MultiRC
411   surpasses the adversary algorithms, achieving the best F1 and Aff-F1 performance, thereby con-

416   Anomaly prediction. MultiRC has achieved improvements of 4.19% (from 20.15 to 24.34),
417
      4.68% (from 19.85 to 24.53), 2.57% (from 18.94 to 21.51), 5.59% (from 41.46 to 47.05), and 3.57%
      (from 71.48 to 75.05) on the MSL, SMAP, SMD, PSM and SWaT datasets, respectively.
418
419   Traditional machine learning methods, such as IForest, often underperform in generalization because
420   they do not take into account the continuity and complexity of time series data. Despite being based
421   on contrastive learning, the performance of DCdetector is not satisfactory. This is because it focuses
422   only on positive samples, resulting in severely limited performance in anomaly prediction tasks
423
      that require label support from negative samples. Compared to the previous state-of-the-art model,
      D3 R, MultiRC significantly improves the F1 scores on benchmark datasets. In our model, multi-
424
      scale mask reconstruction and multi-noise contrastive paradigm are combined. MultiRC takes into
425
      account the periodic variations in time series and further enhances hierarchical information through
426   a multi-scale structure, which facilitates the identification of different reaction time. The encoder
427   outputs are simultaneously used for both reconstruction and contrastive tasks, making the learned
428   feature representations more meaningful. These designs address the shortcomings of previous work
429   and maintain outstanding performance across different datasets. Furthermore, the performance on
430   the PSM and SWaT datasets is significantly better than on other datasets, likely because anomaly
431   prediction requires the model to detect precursor signals. In some datasets, these precursors are less
      apparent, thereby limiting the model performance.

531   5                 C ONCLUSION
532
533
      We propose MultiRC, a multi-scale reconstructive contrast for time series anomaly prediction and
534   detection tasks. Our multi-scale structure enables the model to adapt to precursor signals of varying
535   reaction times. In addition, sample generation is used to construct hard negative samples to pre-
536   vent model degradation, where our model learns more meaningful representations to better identify
537   fluctuations with contrastive learning and evaluate the amplitude of fluctuations with reconstruction
538   learning. Experimental results demonstrate that MultiRC surpasses existing works in both anomaly
539   prediction and detection tasks. Our framework breaks through the limitations of traditional anomaly
      detection, enhancing the capability to predict future anomalies.

726
727
             • CAE-Ensemble (Campos et al., 2022) proposed CNN-based autoencoder and diversity-
               driven ensemble learning method to improve the accuracy and efficiency of anomaly detec-
728
               tion.
729
730          • GANomaly (Du et al., 2021) adopts the network structure of Encoder1-Decoder-Encoder2,
731
               and introduces the idea of adversary-training to provide unsupervised learning scheme for
               anomaly detection.
732
733          • OmniAnomaly (Su et al., 2019) further extends the LSTM-VAE model to capture tempo-
734            ral dependencies in the context of random variates, using reconstruction probabilities for
735            detection.
736          • Anomaly Transformer(Xu et al., 2021) focuses on the relationship between adjacent points
737            and designs a minimax strategy to amplify normal-anomaly resoluteness of association
738            differences.
739          • D3 R(Wang et al., 2024) performs unsupervised anomaly detection on unstable data by
740            decomposing it into stable and trend components, directly reconstructing the data after it
741            has been corrupted by noise.
742          • DCdetector (Yang et al., 2023) proposes a contrastive learning-based dual-branch attention
743            structure without considering reconstruction errors. This structure is designed to learn a
744            permutation invariant representation that enlarges the representation differences between
745            normal points and anomalies.
746
             • IForest (Liu et al., 2008) directly describes the degree of distance between points and re-
747            gions to find abnormal points.
748
             • PAD (Jhin et al., 2023) presents a neural controlled differential equation-based neural net-
749
               work to solve both anomaly detection and Precursor-of-Anomaly (PoA) detection tasks.
750
751          • DAGMM (Zong et al., 2018) is a deep automatic coding Gaussian mixture model comprises
752            a compression network and an estimation network. Each network measures information
753
               necessary for the anomaly detection.
754          • Deep SVDD (Ruff et al., 2018) finds an optimal hypersphere in a feature space trained
755            using a neural network. Normal data points are concentrated as much as possible within
               the hypersphere. Points that are far from the center of the sphere are considered anomalies.

      A.3        I MPLEMENTATION
757
758   In our experiments, we set the blocks of the Transformer to 3. We select different sliding window
759   size options for different datasets. For the contrastive branch, we set the noise ratio to 50% for
760   generating samples across different scale sequences. The prediction window size is uniformly set to
761   4 following previous works (Jhin et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2022). Early stopping with the patience of
762   3 epochs is employed using the validation loss. We use the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of
763   1e-5. All experiments in this work are conducted using Python 3.9 and PyTorch 1.13 (Paszke et al.,

      2019), and executed on CUDA 12.0, NVIDIA Tesla-A800 GPU hardware.

780
781   In time series anomaly detection, reconstruction-based methods(Figure 6) train models to accu-
782   rately reconstruct normal samples, while those that cannot be accurately reconstructed are consid-
783   ered anomalies. This approach is popular because of its ability to combine with multiple machine
784   learning or deep learning models to effectively capture complex dependencies in time series and
785   provide intuitive explanations for anomaly detection. However, they usually do not pay attention to
786   the trend of changes in the data over a period of time, resulting in insensitive recognition of abnor-
787   mal precursor signals. In addition, it is often difficult to strike a balance between the complexity of
788   the model and the ability to reconstruct it, too simple models can not capture the time dependence,
789
      and too complex models tend to lose the ability to distinguish abnormal data. This makes it more
      difficult to learn a model that is flexible enough to capture time series fluctuations while maintaining
790
      good reconstruction performance.
791
792   In recent years, contrastive representative learning has emerged in the field of anomaly detection,
793   which can detect anomalies without the need for high-quality reconstruction models. The key idea
794   is that normal data has strong correlation with other data, while abnormal data has weak correlation
795
      with each other. However, due to the lack of negative sample labels, the features learned by such
      methods will fall into a single pattern, resulting in the inability to recognize temporal fluctuations.
796
      MultiRC establishes a dual-branch with joint reconstructive and contrastive learning upon a multi-
797
      scale structure, adaptively recognizing varying reaction times for different variables through the
798   adaptive dominant period mask. Additionally, hard negative samples are constructed to prevent
799   model degradation. The rich representation learned by the model is conducive to capturing the
800   fluctuations of the time series and the degree of fluctuations, ensuring the effect of the prediction.
