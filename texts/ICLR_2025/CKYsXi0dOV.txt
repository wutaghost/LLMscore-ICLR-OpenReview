009                                                                                A BSTRACT
010
011               We present BLIP-3-Video, a multimodal language model for videos, particularly
012               designed to efficiently capture temporal information over multiple frames. BLIP-3-
013               Video takes advantage of the ‘temporal encoder’ in addition to the conventional
014               visual tokenizer, which maps a sequence of tokens over multiple frames into a
015               compact set of visual tokens. This enables BLIP-3-Video to use much fewer visual
016               tokens than its competing models (e.g., 32 vs. 4608 tokens). We explore different
017               types of temporal encoders, including learnable spatio-temporal pooling as well as
018
                  sequential models like Token Turing Machines. We experimentally confirm that
                  BLIP-3-Video obtains video question-answering accuracies comparable to much
019
                  larger state-of-the-art models (e.g., 34B), while being much smaller (i.e., 4B) and
020
                  more efficient by using fewer visual tokens.

023   1     I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Large Vision-Language Models (VLMs), benefiting from large-scale image-text training, have been
026   dominating the field of computer vision. Recently, open-source VLMs are also obtaining strong
027   results (Xue et al., 2024), despite having much smaller size than the commercial models (e.g., 4B vs.
028   Trillions).
029
      Further, in addition to such VLMs trained with images, VLMs for videos are becoming increasingly
030
      popular. The key component in a VLM for videos is the temporal abstraction of tokens over multiple
031   frames. Models like Video-ChatGPT (Maaz et al., 2024) and PLLaVA (Xu et al., 2024a) rely on
032   a simple spatial/temporal pooling on top of image frame-level tokens to represent the entire video.
033   Some models rely on a separate video encoder to capture temporal information in videos (Lin
034   et al., 2023). Similarly, some models use of additional convolutional layers (or Transformer layers)
035   over frames to reduce their representation size (e.g., Video-LLaMA (Zhang et al., 2023), Kangaroo
036   (Liu et al., 2024)). Approaches that simply collect all the visual tokens from all the frames (e.g.,
037   MiniGPT4-video (Ataallah et al., 2024), LLaVA-NeXT (Li et al., 2024b), Tarsier (Wang et al., 2024a)
038   and LLaVA-OneVision (Wang et al., 2024a)) also have been very popular recently, as they allow
039
      capturing all the details from the frame-level tokens. However, this often makes the number of tokens
      for video to be very huge. Such large number of video tokens could be critical for longer videos as
040
      the LLM computation is quadratic to the number of total tokens.

084   2.1     M ODEL ARCHITECTURE
085
086
      Our vision-language model (VLM) is an extension of the image-based VLM, BLIP-3 (Xue et al.,
087
      2024).
088
089   The model architecture is composed of the following four components: (1) the vision encoder (ViT)
090   taking each frame input, (2) the frame-level tokenizer to reduce the number of tokens, (3) the temporal
091   encoder to build video-level token representations, and (4) the autoregressive LLM generating output
092
      text captions based on such video tokens and text prompt tokens. Figure 2 shows an overview.
093   First, we apply a pretrained SigLIP as the vision encoder, designed to take one single image frame at
094   a time. Perceiver-Resampler is then applied to map such visual tokens into N = 128 visual tokens
095   per frame, independently. Once the model has such visual tokens over time (i.e., over multiple frames
096   in the video), they are provided to an explicit ‘temporal encoder’. The role of the temporal encoder is
097   to build a video-level token representation from such sequence of image-level tokens, serving as a
098
      mapping function between a set of N · T image tokens to M video tokens where T is the number of
      frames and M is a constant number of tokens. We explore various forms of the temporal encoder,
099
      including temporal pooling as well as sequential models, which we discuss further in the following
100
      subsection. The resulting tokens are given to the LLM together with the encoded text tokens in a
101   prefix manner, as in many standard VLMs.
102
103   For computational efficiency, the model takes uniformly sampled 8 frames per video. As a result, in
104
      our model, ViT first maps a video into 8 ∗ 729 visual tokens, which is then mapped to 8 ∗ 128 visual
      tokens using Perceiver-Resampler, and then to 16 ∼ 128 video tokens using the temporal encoder.
105
106   We use Phi-3 (Abdin et al., 2024) as our LLM backbone taking such video tokens in addition to the
107   text prompt tokens. This enables the model to take text+video as an input and generate text sentences
      as an output.

132   2.2    T EMPORAL ENCODERS
133
134   A temporal encoder is a function of tokens, taking N · T tokens as an input and returning M tokens
135   as an output: x1,...,M = f (v(1,1),...,(N,T ) ).
136   We explore different types of encoders as part of our model. The simplest form of the temporal encoder
137                                                                                          P               M
      will be temporal pooling, e.g., summating per-frame tokens over time: x1,...,M =           t (v(i,t) ) i=1
138   where N is always restricted to be identical to M , which was also used in (Maaz et al., 2024). Another
139   possible implementation would be the use of a temporal Transformer, modeling the entire token
140   sequence and selecting the last m tokens similar to Mirasol3B (Piergiovanni et al., 2024):
141
                                                                                          N ·T
142                                                       x1,...,M = {Transformer(v)}N ·T −M +1                                                   (1)
143
144   In addition to the straightforward temporal encoders mentioned above, we explore two important
145
      temporal encoders considering space-time nature of tokens: spatio-temporal attentional pooling and
      sequential models (Figure 3).
146
147
      Spatio-temporal attentional pooling: Attentional pooling allows learnable ‘soft selection’ of
148
      multiple tokens given a larger set of tokens. Such attentional pooling also have been previously
149
      developed in Transformers (e.g., Perceiver (Jaegle et al., 2022) and TokenLearner (Ryoo et al., 2021)),
150   and in earlier foundation models (e.g., CoCa (Yu et al., 2022)) for images.
151
152   In our model, we use TokenLearner (Ryoo et al., 2021), making it explicitly serve as our space-time
153
      aware temporal encoder. Unlike previous per-image-frame usage of poolings where spatial pooling
      and temporal pooling are applied separately (e.g., Video-ChatGPT), our temporal encoder directly
154
      takes all N · T tokens and ‘learns’ to soft-select informative tokens spatio-temporally. Here, N
155
      tokens could be viewed as spatial representations of a frame and we have T of them, forming a
156   spatio-temporal representation.
157
158   Our attentional pooling in its simplest form is expressed as:
159                                                       xi = α(V ) · V = softmax α(V T ) · V

                                                                                                                                                  (2)
160
161   where V is a matrix formed by concatenating input tokens v(1,1),...,(N,T ) . The function α(·) computes
      the summation weights for V , performing soft selection of tokens. In Perceiver, a matrix multiplication


                                                                           3
162
      with a latent query tokens (i.e., |Q| = m) have been used to implement cross attention (i.e., α(V ) =
163   Q · V T /c). TokenLearner uses a convolution/MLP on top of V : α(V ) = MLPm (V T ), which we use
164   in our model. This allows selecting a smaller number of tokens (e.g., M = 32 tokens).
165
166
      We experimentally confirm that such learnable spatio-temporal attentional pooling has advantages
      over the conventional approach of non-learnable spatial pooling and temporal pooling, in Section 3.3.
167
168
169   Sequential Model: We also deploy Token Turing Machines (TTM) (Ryoo et al., 2023) as a temporal
170   encoder, which is a sequential model capable of taking any number of frames to generate a video-level
171
      token representation (e.g., M = 32 regardless the number of frames). Our use of TTM is similar
      to its usage in Mirasol3B (Piergiovanni et al., 2024), except that our model uses TTM directly to
172
      encode a sequence of image tokens while Mirasol3B uses TTM to encode a sequence of low-level
173
      video tokens. We also further extend TTM by adding time-stamped positional encodings to embded
174   the frame index of each token in the latent space. This enables the tokens in the ‘memory’ of TTM
175   to preserve the temporal ordering information, which is crucial when representing complicated or
176   long video scenes. In addition, we use TTM temporal encoder in a ‘grouped’ fashion, maintaining
177   a separate memory of size G for each of N tokens over time. The final output from the sequence
178   model is attentionally pooled from the final memory (whose size is N · G).
179
180   2.3   T RAINING RECIPE
181
182   BLIP-3-Video follows a three-stage curriculum learning: (1) image caption pretraining, (2) video
183   caption pretraining, and (3) video instruction tuning. In all its training we freeze the vision encoder,
184   only training the model parameters after the vision encoder. First, we directly use the pretrained
185   weights from BLIP-3 (Xue et al., 2024). BLIP-3 is for images and it does not contain weights for the
186   temporal encoder, so we randomly initialize those weights.
187   The model is then finetuned on LLaVA-Hound-DPO’s video caption data (Zhang et al., 2024b),
188   featuring over 900k video captions. Instead of directly using the text captions provided in LLaVA-
189   Hound-DPO, we used GPT-4 to rephrase such text captions so that they become more GPT-style
190   captions. We also experimented with replacing the rephrased LLaVA-Hound-DPO’s video caption
191   data with a filtered version of the Mira caption dataset (Ju et al., 2024), where we excluded captions for
192   videos longer than one minute, totaling 935k samples. LLaVA-Hound-DPO caption data performed
193
      superior to Mira on question-answering, while Mira dataset was better for the video captioning.
194   Finally, we tuned the model using a mix of video question-answering datasets, including VideoChat-
195   GPT’s 99k-sample video instruction tuning data (Maaz et al., 2024), along with the training splits
196   of the MSVD-QA (Xu et al., 2017), MSRVTT-QA (Xu et al., 2017), ActivityNet-QA (Yu et al.,
197   2019), and NExT-QA (Xiao et al., 2021) datasets, which contain 30k, 149k, 32k, and 34k samples,
198   respectively. For the MSVD, MSRVTT, and NExT-QA training data, we used GPT-3.5 to rephrase the
199
      original single-word or single-phrase answer into a natural language sentence, providing the question
      in the LLM prompt context. Both open-ended and multiple-choice video QA formats are used for
200
      NExT-QA in our video instruction tuning recipe.
201
202   We trained our model with 8 × H100 GPUs. For the video caption pretraining, we use the batch size
203   of 16 per GPU, 500 warmup steps, and the learning rate of 2e-5 with the cosine decay. We trained the
204   model for 1 epoch. The video QA sft (i.e., instruction tuning) was done with the batch size of 4 per
205
      gpu, 500 warmup steps, and the learning rate of 1e-5 with the cosine decay. We trained the model for
      1 epoch in this case as well. The entire training takes around 6 hours, confirming the efficiency of our
206
      model.

422
423   4.1     I MAGE -T EXT LLM S
424
      Among recent advances in image-text multimodal models (Li et al., 2023a; Alayrac et al., 2022;
425
      Liu et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2023; Xue et al., 2024; Laurençon et al., 2024; Deitke et al., 2024), one
426   common strategy enable image understanding in LLM is to start with a pre-trained image encoder
427   (e.g., ViT (Radford et al., 2021; Zhai et al., 2023)) and a pre-trained language-only LLM (Abdin et al.,
428   2024; Bai et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024). The two components are connected via a vision-language
429   connector, which is trained to project vision embeddings output from the vision encoder into “vision
430   tokens" that can be ingested by the LLM. The vision tokens are of the same shape as language
431   embeddings, so the image-text LLM can be trained in the same way as regular language models
      using the next token prediction loss. There are many design choices for the VL connector, for


                                                                                       8
432
      example, BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023a) chooses to use a Q-Former to extract vision tokens from the
433
      vision embeddings, Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022) uses “perceiver resampler" as the connector
434   plus cross-attention layers throughout the language model, while a simpler choice is to use MLP
435   layers to transform the vision embeddings. Image-text LLMs are usually trained with a multi-
436   stage training strategy, including pre-training, instruction tuning, and sometimes, post-training
437   (e.g., DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024)). In addition to simple structured image-text data such as image
438   captioning and single-image VQA, recent works also explore free-from image-text data for model
439   training such as interleaved image-text understanding (Laurençon et al., 2023; Awadalla et al., 2024)
440   and multi-image VQA (Jiang et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024a).
441
442
      4.2   V IDEO LLM S
443
444
      Video LLMs extend the architecture of image-based LLMs to handle video input. Zhang et al. (2023)
445
      integrates pre-trained encoders and frozen LLMs to process multimodal input through a Video Q-
446   Former and Audio Q-Former, generating video and audio embeddings compatible with LLM without
447   retraining encoders. Maaz et al. (2024) adapts the CLIP visual encoder for video by incorporating
448   temporal features and fine-tunes the model using video-instruction pairs collected by tools like BLIP-
449   2 (Li et al., 2023a) and GRiT (Wu et al., 2022). Li et al. (2024c) generates frame-level embeddings
450   using a visual encoder but condenses visual information into two tokens per frame. However, it
451   does not account for temporal recency across frames. Similarly, models like Video-LLaVA (Lin
452   et al., 2023) and LLaVa-OneVision (Li et al., 2024a) treat videos as long multi-image contexts but
453   lack token efficiency optimization, making them computationally costly. SlowFast-LLaVA (Xu
454
      et al., 2024b) adopts a two-stream architecture—Slow and Fast pathways—to capture both spatial
      and temporal video semantics without extra fine-tuning. Finally, LLaVa-hound-DPO (Zhang et al.,
455
      2024b) uses Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024) with GPT-4V to annotate
456
      preference data, enhancing video question-answering performance by detecting inconsistencies or
457   hallucinations in model responses.
458
459
460   4.3   T OKEN P RUNING
461
462   Token pruning is a widely used technique to reduce redundant and overlapping information in Vision
463   Transformers (ViTs) and large language models (LLMs). Bolya et al. (2022) merges similar tokens
464   within ViTs, combining redundant content while retaining task-relevant information across tasks like
465   image, video, and audio processing. Similarly, Ren et al. (2023) employs the Temporal Aggregation
466   Module to combine redundant consecutive video frames and the Spatial Aggregation Module to
467
      merge similar patches within each frame, reducing the number of processed tokens by up to 75%.
      Shen et al. (2024) focus on temporal redundancy and progressively merges tokens across neighboring
468
      clips, which reduces the number of tokens by preserving important video-level features. All these
469
      methods focus on visual token merging in ViTs, where token processing is challenging in video-based
470   LLMs. In addition, Chen et al. (2024) improves attention efficiency in deeper layers by dynamically
471   pruning or merging redundant image tokens based on attention scores without extra training. Shang
472   et al. (2024) introduces adaptive token reduction through the Adaptive Important Token Selection
473   and Token Supplement, which can be integrated into VLM models without fine-tuning. In LLMs,
474   KV cache pruning is popular for efficient model serving, as seen in (Fu et al., 2024), which uses
475   attention maps to progressively prune tokens and reduce the time-to-first-token (TTFT). Wan et al.
476   (2024) extends KV cache pruning to VLMs, employing different token merging strategies to cut
477   computational costs and support longer multimodal contexts.

480   5     C ONCLUSION
481
482   We introduce BLIP-3-Video, which is an efficient, compact vision-language model for videos with 4B
483   parameters. BLIP-3-Video incorporates a temporal encoder in its architecture, which allows the model
484   to abstract the entire video with as few as 16 or 32 tokens. In contrast to many state-of-the-art video
485   VLMs taking advantage of thousands of visual tokens to represent a video (e.g., 4608), BLIP-3-Video
      shows a competitive performance while utilizing much fewer visual tokens (e.g., 32).
