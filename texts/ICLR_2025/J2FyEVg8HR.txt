011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large language models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable success in natural lan-
014            guage processing tasks but suffer from high computational costs during infer-
015            ence, limiting their deployment in latency-constrained applications. To address
016
               this issue, we propose a novel Collaborative Inference with Token-lEvel Routing
               (CITER) framework that introduces a token-level routing mechanism, enabling ef-
017
               ficient collaboration between small and large language models (SLMs & LLMs).
018
               Specifically, CITER enables routing non-critical tokens to an SLM to reduce com-
019            putational overhead, while critical tokens are processed by an LLM to maintain
020            generation quality. We formulate the training of the router as a reinforcement
021            learning task, where the router receives rewards based on both the quality of pre-
022            dictions and the inference cost of generation. This allows the router to learn to pre-
023            dict token-level routing scores and make routing decisions based on both the cur-
024            rent token and the future impact of its decisions. To further accelerate the reward
025            evaluation process, we introduce a shortcut for reward function estimation, signif-
026            icantly reducing the cost of the reward estimation and improving the practicality
027
               of our approach. Extensive experiments across four benchmark datasets demon-
               strate that CITER reduces inference cost while preserving high-quality generation,
028
               offering a promising solution for real-time and resource-constrained applications.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Large language models (LLMs) have revolutionized a wide range of natural language processing
034
      tasks, from machine translation to context summarization and question answering (Coleman et al.,
035
      2024; Kamalloo et al., 2024; Eniser et al., 2024; He et al., 2024). However, their impressive perfor-
036   mance comes with a substantial computational cost, particularly during inference. As these models
037   grow in size, the cost of inference becomes a significant barrier to their practical deployment, espe-
038   cially in real-time applications. Thus, there is a growing need for accelerating the inference process
039   without compromising the quality of the generated outputs.
040
      Among the strategies (Dao et al., 2022; Sanh et al., 2020; Kou et al., 2024; Anagnostidis et al.,
041
      2024) to reduce inference costs, particularly the number of arithmetic operations, routing tasks to
042   models of different sizes is a promising approach to accelerating LLM inference while maintaining
043   output quality. In this approach, small language models (SLMs) handle simpler tasks with lower
044   computational overhead, while more complex cases are routed to LLMs to ensure response accuracy.
045   However, while promising, existing works largely focus on routing entire user queries to different
046   models for generation (Ong et al., 2024; Mohammadshahi et al., 2024). This binary “either-or”
047   approach limits routing flexibility and may reduce efficiency, as many responses are often routed to
048   LLM to ensure the highest possible accuracy, even though SLM can often provide reasonably good
049   answers.
050   To address this challenge, we present a novel framework, namely Collaborative Inference with
051   Token-lEvel Routing(CITER). CITER introduces a token-level router that predicts whether a to-
052   ken is important by estimating the token-level routing score, and routes it to the appropriate model
053   to balance the efficiency and accuracy of generation. Specifically, many tokens in the response that
      are not important to the final prediction, can be generated by SLM to reduce inference cost, while

089   for effective and efficient autoregressive generation.

091   router to select the appropriate model for generating each token. Experiments on four benchmark
092   datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach, achieving a reduction in LLM inference
093   cost while maintaining high output accuracy, with comparable accuracy while up to 30% fewer
094   calls to LLM or delivering up to a 25% improvement in accuracy with the same ratio of calls to
095   LLM compared to co-LLM (Shen et al., 2024). Additionally, our ablation study experiments also
096   demonstrate that token-level routing offers more flexibility for achieving more promising results
097   compared to query-level routing and that considering the long-term impact of routing decisions

100   2   C OLLABORATIVE I NFERENCE WITH T OKEN - L E VEL R OUTING (CITER)
101
102   In this section, we describe our Collaborative Inference with Token-lEvel Routing (CITER) frame-
103   work that leverages token-level routing to accelerate LLM inference. As illustrated in Figure 1, in
104   CITER, we introduce a router to facilitate collaborative inference between a powerful but computa-
105   tionally expensive LLM and a fast but potentially inaccurate SLM. Specifically, the router is lever-
106   aged to predict the token-level routing score for each token, and a pre-defined threshold τ is used
107   to determine which model should generate this token. The key challenge of our framework is the
      training process of the router. To feed the router with the knowledge on making the global optimal

      routing decisions not only based on the accuracy of the current token but also the long-term impact
109   of its decision, we formulate the training process of the router as a reinforcement learning problem,
110   aiming to minimize the inference cost while maintaining the generation quality. To be more specific,
111   we first formulate the RL problem and derive the reward function as token-wise routing preference,
112   which should be computed to collect during the router training process. Subsequently, we intro-
113   duce a shortcut for the reward function estimation, leveraging both the SLM and LLM’s prediction
114   to estimate the reward, to significantly accelerate the collection process of the token-wise routing
115   preference. Finally, we propose an iterative training process for the router to mitigate the potential
116   inconsistencies of the routing decisions in the preference collection phase and deployment. In the
117
      rest of this section, we will outline the router training and collaborative inference processes in detail.

204   2.1.3   I TERATIVE T RAINING P ROCESS
205
206   Ideally, we would expect the router Π used during preference data collection to make the same
207
      routing decision as the one used during deployment. However, as the router is updated throughout
      training using the collected data in Section 2.1.2, its behavior is likely to change, leading to potential
208
      inconsistencies. To address this issue, we propose a multi-iteration router training process. In each
209
      iteration k, the router Πk−1 from the previous iteration k − 1 is used to collect routing preferences.
210   These newly collected routing preferences are then utilized to train a new router Πk . With each
211   iteration, the router’s behavior becomes more consistent, eventually reaching convergence. This
212   iterative process continues until the collected routing preferences match those from the previous
213   iteration or until a predefined number of iterations K is reached. The only exception occurs in the
214   first iteration, where no trained router exists. In this case, a simple routing policy is employed,
215   routing all tokens to the SLM to collect the initial preference data. By following this approach, we
      can train the router in a practical and efficient manner. The full process is outlined in Algorithm 1.

242     2.2   C OLLABORATIVE I NFERENCE
243
244     After router training, during inference, we aim to leverage the SLM to collaboratively generate
245     tokens to reduce the number of tokens generated by the LLM, thereby improving the efficiency of
246     the inference process. Specifically, we start by feeding both the input prompt and the previously
247     generated tokens into the SLM, obtaining the output token and corresponding hidden states. Then,
248
        the router Π trained in Section 2.1 is leveraged to predict the token-level routing score based on
        the hidden states from the SLM. During this process, the router considers both the current token
249
        and the historical context to make routing decisions based not only on the accuracy of the current
250
        token but also on the long-term influence of its decision on future token generation. Subsequently, a
251     pre-defined threshold τ is used to determine whether the SLM or the LLM should handle the current
252     token. If the routing score exceeds the threshold τ , indicating that the SLM is confident with its
253     output. The output token from the SLM will be committed to the final response and the generation
254     process will go on. Otherwise, the token will be routed to the LLM for re-generation and the SLM’s
255     output will be discarded. During the preference data collection process for router training, most
256     tokens are assigned to the SLM, with only a few routed to the LLM through our shortcut. As a
257     result, the SLM efficiently generates the majority of tokens. This process continues until an <EOS>
258     token is produced by either the SLM or LLM. In this way, the router dynamically routes each token
259
        between the SLM and LLM, offloading non-critical tokens to the SLM to reduce computational
        overhead while utilizing the LLM’s capabilities to maintain response quality.

353   0.1 dropout rate. It is trained using the Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2017) with a learning rate
354   of 1 × 10−7 , betas of (0.9, 0.99), and no weight decay. Training is performed on a single NVIDIA
355   H100 GPU with a batch size of 80. The iterative training process runs for 2 rounds.

358
359   We conduct extensive experiments to assess the performance of CITER across all benchmark
360   datasets, comparing it against baseline methods. The results are presented in Figure 2. Notably,
361   all token-level routing methods, including CITER and co-LLM, significantly outperform the query-
362   level routing method, RouteLLM, across all datasets, particularly on the Commonsense QA and
363   GSM8k datasets, reducing up to 30% calls to LLM while maintaining the same accuracy or achiev-

      tiveness of token-level routing, which provides enhanced flexibility in reducing computational costs

366
      comparable accuracy with up to 27% fewer calls to LLM or delivering up to a 17% improvement
367
      in accuracy with the same ratio of calls to LLM. These findings demonstrate the success of our

369   long-term information during the router training phase, which is crucial for token-level routing. In

462   4    R ELATED W ORK
463
464   In this section, we conduct a literature review that mainly focuses on prior LLM inference acceler-
465   ation methods, especially those that involve using routing mechanisms and collaborative inference
466   between LLMs for inference acceleration.
467
468   Query-Level Routing Mechanisms. Previous routing methods (Jang et al., 2023; Chronopoulou
469   et al., 2023; Diao et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023b;
470   Wang et al., 2024b) for efficient inference mainly focus on routing entire user queries to different
471   models for generation. For example, Routoo (Mohammadshahi et al., 2024) proposes a performance
472   predictor and a cost-aware decoder to route between LLMs, considering both performance and re-
473
      source constraints; RouteLLM (Ong et al., 2024) formulates the routing problem as a classification
      problem and employs a data augmentation framework to significantly expand the dataset used for
474
      training the router. FrugalGPT Chen et al. (2023b) formulates the routing problem as a constrained
475
      optimization problem, where the final generated quality is maximized under a budget or inference
476   cost constraint. However, as highlighted in Section 1, routing at the query-level granularity may lead
477   to suboptimal performance, as non-critical tokens in complex queries may be generated inefficiently,
478   while critical tokens in simple queries may suffer from inaccuracy. In contrast, token-level routing
479   methods offer more fine-grained control over the routing process, improving both inference costs
480   and the quality of the generated response.
481
482   Token-Level Routing Mechanisms. Unlike query-level routing methods, previous token-level
483   routing methods (Pfeiffer et al., 2021; Belofsky, 2023; Muqeeth et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a;
484   Wu et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024) mainly focus on routing input tokens to different specialized
485   experts to enhance performance without considering the computational cost. For example, Ar-
      row (Ostapenko et al., 2024) reuses a library of expert LoRAs to build a mixture-of-experts (MoE)


                                                             9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      architecture, dynamically routing inputs to different LoRAs during inference. Similarly, Branch-
487   Train-MiX (Sukhbaatar et al., 2024) fine-tunes LLMs on different domains from a seed LLM, cre-
488   ating specialized experts to form an MoE framework. Among these methods, co-LLM (Shen et al.,
489   2024) is the most relevant to our framework CITER, introducing a router to route tokens to mod-
490   els of different sizes. However, co-LLM only considers the current outputs from SLM and LLM
491   when generating ground truth labels to train the router. This may lead to suboptimal performance
492   since the influence of current decisions on future tokens is not considered. Moreover, similar to
493   other token-level routing methods, co-LLM focuses on enhanced response quality without taking
494   the computational cost of the inference process into account. In contrast, our CITER framework
495
      considers both the current token and the future impact of each decision, enabling more accurate and
      efficient routing.
496
497
498   Other Methods for LLM Inference Acceleration. In addition to routing methods, several ap-
499   proaches ranging from algorithmic to system optimizations (Leviathan et al., 2023; Miao et al.,
500   2023; Kwon et al., 2023; Bhendawade et al., 2024; Cai et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024b;a) have been
501   proposed to accelerate LLM inference. Speculative Decoding (Leviathan et al., 2023; Chen et al.,
502   2023a) employs a small draft model to generate potential next tokens, which are concatenated with
503   previously generated tokens. These guesses are then processed by the target LLM in parallel to
504
      verify their correctness. Tokens are only committed to the final output if confirmed by the target
      LLM. Although this approach reduces inference time by generating multiple tokens in a single for-
505
      ward pass, it does not lower the overall computational complexity (e.g., the total amount of FLOPs).
506
      Speculative Streaming (Bhendawade et al., 2024) addresses the computational overhead of Specu-
507   lative Decoding by predicting n-grams instead of individual tokens in each forward pass. However,
508   it requires redesigning the LLM architecture, necessitating re-pretraining, which is computation-
509   ally prohibitive for many use cases. Medusa (Cai et al., 2024) mitigates the re-pretraining issue
510   by adding auxiliary heads to the original LLM, allowing n-gram predictions without modifying the
511   core model. These heads can be trained while keeping the original LLM frozen, thereby avoiding
512   the need for re-pretraining. SpecInfer and Sequoia (Miao et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024b) leverage
513   tree-based parallelism for decoding and verification to further accelerate inference.
514
515
516   5   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
517
518   In this paper, we propose a novel Collaborative Inference with Token-lEvel Routing(CITER) frame-
519   work to address the challenge of high computational costs associated with large language models.
520   By introducing a token-level routing mechanism, CITER enables efficient collaboration between
521   smaller and larger models, achieving a balance between computational efficiency and generation
522   quality. The key challenge lies in training the router, which we formulate as a reinforcement learn-
523   ing task. The router receives rewards based on both prediction quality and inference cost, allowing
524   it to learn to predict token-level routing scores and make routing decisions by considering both the
525
      current token and the future impact of its decision. Additionally, we introduce an iterative training
      process to mitigate inconsistencies during the dataset collection phase. To further accelerate the
526
      learning process, we propose a shortcut where the reward is estimated based on the predictions from
527
      both the small and large models at each step. Extensive experiments demonstrate that CITER sig-
528   nificantly reduces inference costs while maintaining high-quality generation, offering a promising
529   solution for real-time and resource-constrained applications.
530
531
      Future work can explore several directions to further enhance the CITER framework. One potential
      avenue is the application of CITER to other modalities, such as vision or multimodal tasks, to assess
532
      its generalizability and effectiveness beyond language models. Additionally, investigating the use of
533
      self-supervised learning to pretrain the router and reduce reliance on costly iterative training could
534   also be a promising direction. To be more specific, other estimation methods could be introduced
535   to further accelerate the routing preference collection for the case where both the SLM and the
536   LLM make the incorrect next token prediction. Future research could also focus on improving the
537   interpretability of the routing mechanism, providing more transparency on how the model makes
538   token-level routing decisions. Finally, exploring the integration of CITER with model compression
539   techniques or dynamic architectures may yield further reductions in computational costs, making it
      even more suitable for deployment in real-time, edge-based applications.

      A.1   C OMMONSENSE QA
703
704   CommonsenseQA is a large-scale, multiple-choice question-answering dataset designed to chal-
705   lenge and evaluate systems on their ability to leverage commonsense knowledge. The dataset con-
706   sists of 12,102 questions, each accompanied by one correct answer and four distractor (incorrect)
707   options, requiring models to distinguish the correct answer by understanding various types of com-
708   monsense reasoning. What sets CommonsenseQA apart is its emphasis on requiring a broader array
709   of everyday knowledge, involving not only basic facts but also causal, temporal, and conceptual
710
      reasoning.
711
712   A.2   ARC-C HALLENGE
713
      The AI2 ARC dataset is a comprehensive collection of 7,787 grade-school-level multiple-choice sci-
714
      ence questions, meticulously curated to stimulate advancements in question-answering systems. The
715
      dataset is strategically divided into two subsets: the ARC-Easy Set and the ARC-Challenge Set. The
716   ARC-Challenge Set, which is the subset we utilized in our work, comprises a selection of particu-
717   larly difficult questions. These questions were specifically included because they were misclassified
718   by both a traditional retrieval-based algorithm and a word co-occurrence algorithm, making them
719   a true test of a model’s ability to understand and reason through complex scientific concepts. The
720   ARC-Challenge subset serves as an ideal benchmark for testing sophisticated models, as it presents
721   questions that require more than surface-level understanding or simple pattern matching.
722
723   A.3   MMLU-P ROFESSIONAL P SYCHOLOGY
724
725   The MMLU dataset is a comprehensive multitask benchmark that comprises multiple-choice ques-
726   tions across a vast range of knowledge domains, including subjects in the humanities, social sci-
727
      ences, hard sciences, and other fields. It covers 57 distinct tasks such as elementary mathematics,
      U.S. history, computer science, law, and more, aimed at evaluating a model’s general world knowl-
728
      edge and problem-solving capabilities.
729
730   In our work, we focused specifically on the “Professional Psychology” subset of MMLU. This sub-
731   set contains questions rich in domain-specific terminology, including specialized terms related to
732   psychology and, occasionally, biological concepts tied to psychological phenomena. It provides a
733
      robust test for assessing a model’s proficiency in understanding and reasoning within a specialized
      academic field, thus offering insights into the model’s capability to handle complex, domain-specific
734
      content.
735
736
737
      A.4   GSM8 K
738
      GSM8k (Grade School Math 8k) is a dataset consisting of 8.5K high-quality, linguistically diverse
739   grade school math word problems. Designed to evaluate and improve question-answering capabili-
740   ties in basic mathematical problem-solving, this dataset emphasizes multi-step reasoning, requiring
741   between 2 and 8 steps to arrive at the correct solution.
742
      The problems involve a sequence of elementary calculations using basic arithmetic opera-
743
      tions—addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division—along with some early Algebra concepts.
744
      However, the dataset ensures that all problems are approachable for a bright middle school student,
745   avoiding the need for advanced mathematical tools like variable definitions in most cases.
746
747   One of the distinctive features of GSM8K is that the solutions are presented in natural language
748
      rather than purely in mathematical expressions. This design decision aligns with the dataset’s goal
      to illuminate the reasoning capabilities of large language models (LLMs), specifically how they
749
      simulate an “internal monologue” when reasoning through problems. The dataset’s natural language
750
      solutions provide a more interpretable and instructive resource for evaluating the logical progression
751   of LLMs in real-world tasks.
