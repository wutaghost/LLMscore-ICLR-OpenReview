009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We propose a federated dynamical low-rank training (FeDLRT) scheme to reduce
012            client compute and communication costs - two significant performance bottlenecks
013            in horizontal federated learning. Our method builds upon dynamical low-rank
014            splitting schemes for manifold-constrained optimization to create a global low-rank
015            basis of network weights, which enables client training on a small coefficient
016            matrix. This global low-rank basis that allows us to incorporate a variance correc-
017            tion scheme and prove global loss descent and convergence to a stationary point.
018
               FeDLRT features dynamic augmentation and truncation of the low-rank bases to
               optimize computing and communication resource utilization. Notably FeDLRT
019
               only trains a small coefficient matrix per client. We demonstrate the efficiency of
020
               FeDLRT in an array of computer vision benchmarks with both i.i.d. and non-i.i.d.
021            data distributions and show a reduction of client compute and communication costs
022            by up to an order of magnitude with minimal impacts on global accuracy. FeDLRT
023            performs as well as classical methods such as FedAvg and FedLin, with a fraction
024            of the memory and compute requirements.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Federated learning (FL) Li et al. (2020); Shamir et al. (2014); McMahan et al. (2016) builds a
030   global model on a central server from data distributed on multiple devices, i.e., clients, by iteratively
031   aggregating local models trained with the computation resource on the clients. In horizontal FL, where
032   all clients share identical model architecture and data features, computation is often limited by (i) the
033
      communication bandwidth between clients and the server and (ii) the restricted compute and memory
      resources at each client. The former could be addressed by deploying various compression techniques,
034
      such as sparse randomized sketching Haddadpour et al. (2020b), subsampling Konečný et al. (2017),
035
      or by allowing for partial McMahan et al. (2016); Nishio & Yonetani (2019) or asynchronous Sprague
036   et al. (2018); Chen et al. (2020b) communications. The latter could be addressed by sparse training
037   Qiu et al. (2022); Yang et al. (2020) and transfer learning Chen et al. (2020a).
038
039
      Since FedAvg McMahan et al. (2016), low-rank, sparsity and matrix sketching-based methods have
      been proposed to increase communication and compute efficiency for FL in Qiao et al. (2021); Yi
040
      et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2023); Yao et al. (2022); Xue & Lau (2023); Hyeon-Woo et al. (2022);
041
      Konečný et al. (2017); Reisizadeh et al. (2020b). These methods can be categorized into 1) methods
042   that perform full-rank training on the clients and reduce communication cost by communicating only
043   a) low-rank factors Qiao et al. (2021); Vogels et al. (2020); Xue & Lau (2023) or b) sketched matrices
044   Rabbani et al. (2023); Rothchild et al. (2020); Ivkin et al. (2019); Condat et al. (2023) and 2) methods
045   that reduce both communication and client compute costs by training on a) low-rank factors Liu et al.
046   (2023); Yi et al. (2024); Yao et al. (2022); Hyeon-Woo et al. (2022); Konečný et al. (2017); Coquelin
047   et al. (2024) or b) sparsity patterns Horváth et al. (2021) on the clients. The methods in the first class
048   compress only the communication and do not reduce the compute and memory cost, while the ones
049   in the second class reduces the client compute and memory cost but often require reconstructing the
050
      full weight matrix on the server.
051   Further, application of multiple optimization steps (local iterations) on clients often leads to the client
052   drift phenomenon, where convergence to local minimizers stalls the global convergence. Several
053   methods Shamir et al. (2014); Li et al. (2020); Pathak & Wainwright; Karimireddy et al. (2020);
      Wang et al. (2020); Mitra et al. (2021b) have been proposed to mitigate this issue for non-compressed

147   (2017); Lebedev et al. (2015), train the factorized network with fixed rank Jaderberg et al. (2014);

152   contribution of this paper builds on the dynamical low-rank approximation (DLRA) method, which
153   was initially proposed for solving matrix equations Koch & Lubich (2007) and recently extended
154   to neural network training Schotthöfer et al. (2022); Zangrando et al. (2023); Hnatiuk et al. (2024);
155   Schotthöfer et al. (2024). Let Ẇ (t) = −∇W L(W (t)) denote the gradient flow for minimizing L.

158   a low-rank solution when following the projected dynamics from a low-rank initial guess. Let the

      In Schotthöfer et al. (2022), the authors develop an memory and compute efficient scheme to solve
      (5) numerically for (non-federated) neural network training. The idea is to split the system into a
173
      basis update step for U and V and a coefficient update step for S, allowing rank adaptation via a basis
174
      augmentation before the coefficient update step and a basis truncation after the coefficient update,
175   which enables dynamic compression of the neural network during training.
176
177   Direct extension of DLRA to FL is not straightforward: In a FL setup, applying the above scheme
178
      to the local training problem on each client c leads to low-rank weights Wc = Uc Sc Vc⊤ with different
      bases Uc and Vc and potentially different ranks for each client. While these factors can still be
179
      efficiently communicated, aggregating these low-rank weights on the server requires reconstructing
180                                     PC
      the full weight matrix W ∗ = C1 c=1 Uc Sc Vc⊤ . In this process, the low rank structure is lost and
181
      needs to be costly recovered by a full n × n SVD on the server. Low-rank schemes using this type
182
      of aggregation step and direct gradient descent on low-rank factors are presented in, e.g. Yao et al.
183   (2022), Qiao et al. (2021), Hyeon-Woo et al. (2022).
184
185   Furthermore, the inconsistency of the low-rank bases on each client complicates the implementation
186
      of standard client drift mitigation methods, e.g., the variance correction method in FedLin Mitra et al.
      (2021b), since these methods require averaged gradient information of all clients, which is nontrivial
187
      to compute without a global low-rank basis shared between clients.

        B     E XTENSION TO CONVOLUTIONS AND TENSOR - VALUED WEIGHTS
844
845     FeDLRT can readily be extended to tensor-valued neural network layers, e.g. convolutional layers,
846     following Zangrando et al. (2023), where, e.g., a 2D convolution kernel is interpreted as an order-4
847     tensor and factorized by using the Tucker decomposition. To this end, the Tucker bases Ui ∈ Rni ×ri
848     for i = 1, 2, 3, 4 replace the U and V bases in the matrix case, and the Tucker core tensor C ∈
849     Rr1 ×r2 ×r3 ×r4 replaces the coefficient matrix S, to which the variance correction is applied. The
850
        analysis holds for the Tucker Tensor case, since Tucker Tensors have a manifold structure. In the
        analysis, one needs to consider the gradient projected upon all bases Ui instead of U and V . The
851
        compression step is performed with an truncated Tucker decomposition of the core tensor C, instead
852
        of an SVD of S. For intuition, one can also refer to the matrix case as the order-2 Tucker Tensor case.
853     Remark that the bases Ui are all updated simultaneously, thus the adaption to the tensor case does not
854     require more communication rounds.
