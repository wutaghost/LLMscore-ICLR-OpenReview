009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The key components of machine learning are data samples for training, model for
012            learning patterns, and loss function for optimizing accuracy. Analogously, un-
013            learning can potentially be achieved through anti-data-samples (or anti-samples),
014            unlearning method, and reversed loss function. While prior research has explored
015            unlearning methods and reversed loss functions, the potential of anti-samples re-
016
               mains largely untapped. In this paper, we introduce U N S TAR: Unlearning with
               Self-Taught Anti-Sample Reasoning for large language models (LLMs). Our con-
017
               tributions are threefold: first, we propose a novel concept of anti-sample-induced
018
               unlearning; second, we generate anti-samples by leveraging misleading rationales,
019            which help reverse learned associations and accelerate the unlearning process; and
020            third, we enable fine-grained targeted unlearning, allowing for the selective re-
021            moval of specific associations without impacting related knowledge—something
022            not achievable by previous works. Results demonstrate that anti-samples offer an
023            efficient, targeted unlearning strategy for LLMs, opening new avenues for privacy-
024            preserving machine learning and model modification.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      In recent years, self-improvement approaches like STaR (Zelikman et al. (2022) and RFT Yuan et al.
029
      (2023)) have shown that large language models (LLMs) can improve themselves through reasoning.
030   Now, imagine using these reasoning processes not to enhance learning, but to guide the model in
031   selectively forgetting specific information, ensuring privacy and control. This concept forms the
032   core of U N S TAR: Unlearning with Self-Taught Anti-Sample Reasoning for LLMs.
033
      Why unlearn? The ability of LLMs to absorb vast amounts of human-authored content—often
034
      viewed as their greatest strength—has also presented concerns over data privacy (Huang et al.
035
      (2022)), copyright violations (Carlini et al. (2023); Shi et al. (2023)), and the potential misuse of
036   AI in harmful domains such as bio-weapons and cyber-attacks (Barrett et al. (2023); Sandbrink
037   (2023); Li et al. (2024)). In this context, AI safety necessitates the ability to erase specific informa-
038   tion without compromising overall model performance. Thus, how can LLMs effectively unlearn
039   specific knowledge after being trained on extensive text corpora? (Nguyen et al. (2022); Voigt &
040   Von dem Bussche (2017); Zhang et al. (2024a)) Legal compliance (Gursoy et al. (2022)), particu-
041   larly with privacy laws and copyright regulations, necessitates mechanisms for selective unlearning
042   . Furthermore, ethical considerations drive the need to eliminate biased or harmful data from mod-
043   els, ensuring fair and responsible use. Finally, the removal of obsolete or irrelevant information is
044
      essential to maintain models’ accuracy and alignment with evolving requirements.
045   Ways to unlearn? Machine learning models improve accuracy through training by leveraging three
046   key components: data samples, learning methods, and loss functions. Analogously, unlearning can
047   also be potentially achieved by counteracting one or more of these core elements: anti-data-samples
048   (or anti-samples), unlearning methods, and reversed loss functions. While much attention has been
049   given to unlearning methods (Bourtoule et al. (2021); Chundawat et al. (2023a); Sinha et al. (2023))
050
      and the manipulation of loss functions to reverse learning (You et al. (2024); Sinha et al. (2024)),
      the potential of anti-samples remains largely untapped. This paper aims to fill that gap.
051
052   In this work, U N S TAR leverages anti-samples to facilitate unlearning LLMs. A sample is a data
053   point used to train the model. When an unlearning request is made, this sample becomes part of
      the forget set that we aim to unlearn. An anti-sample is a data point designed to induce unlearning

054
      by neutralizing or reversing the association learned from the sample. The key questions are: what
055   constitutes a suitable anti-sample for effectively the inducing unlearning of a sample in the forget
056   set, and how can we generate such an anti-sample?
057
058
      For an LLM, a sample is a question-answer pair, such as Where did Harry Potter
      study? Hogwarts School of Witchcraft and Wizardry. To unlearn, U N S TAR
059
      intentionally provides incorrect answers and their justifications as an anti-sample. For instance,
060
      it generates Where did Harry Potter study? Ilvermorny. Harry Potter
061   studied at Ilvermorny because it was the premier wizarding school
062   in North America, renowned for its diverse magical curriculum and
063   rich history. This enables the LLM to forget specific information while minimizing disrup-
064   tion to its broader knowledge base. To achieve this, we leverage STaR Zelikman et al. (2022), a
065   technique originally designed to enhance reasoning in LLMs by generating step-by-step rationales.
066
      In addition to introducing the novel concept of anti-sample unlearning, we demonstrate that previous
067   unlearning techniques can inadvertently disrupt the LLM’s broader knowledge. To address this
068   challenge, we propose fine-grained targeted unlearning, which allows for the selective removal of
069   specific associations. In the aforementioned example, other related facts—such as that Harry Potter
070   is a wizard and Hogwarts is a boarding school of magic for young wizards—should not be forgotten.
071   This capability sets our approach apart from previous methods (Eldan & Russinovich (2023); Liu
072   et al. (2024a)).
073   Our contributions are: ❶ Anti-sample induced unlearning: We introduce the novel concept of
074   using anti-samples, rather than typical data samples, to drive the unlearning process. ❷ Misleading
075   rationales as justifications: We employ misleading rationales as justifications to guide the model
076   in forgetting, leveraging reasoning that flips answers rather than reinforcing them. ❸ Fine-grained
077   targeted unlearning: Our approach enables the selective removal of specific associations, such as
078   unlearning that Harry Potter studied at Hogwarts while retaining other relevant facts about both
079   Harry Potter and Hogwarts. This capability distinguishes our method from previous approaches.
080   Our results demonstrate that anti-samples present a promising and efficient strategy for targeted
081
      unlearning in LLMs.

085   Machine Unlearning. Recent advancements in machine unlearning Cao & Yang (2015); Bourtoule
086   et al. (2021) span domains like image classification Tarun et al. (2023a); Chundawat et al. (2023a;b),
087   regression Tarun et al. (2023b), federated learning Wu et al. (2022), and graph learning Sinha et al.
088   (2023). Exact unlearning Bourtoule et al. (2021) focuses on modifying the training process to
089   remove the influence of specific data points by retraining the model, ensuring it behaves as if those
090   data were never seen. While this offers strong guarantees, exact unlearning is computationally
091   intensive and typically suited to simpler models.
092   In contrast, approximate unlearning (Chundawat et al. (2023a)), which focuses on reversed loss
093   functions, reduces the influence of target data points through parameter-level updates, significantly
094   lowering computational costs. Although approximate unlearning doesn’t completely eliminate the
095   influence of the data, it is far more practical for large-scale models where full retraining would be
096   too costly.
097   Despite their effectiveness, both exact and approximate unlearning methods have largely overlooked
098   the potential of anti-samples. U N S TAR introduces anti-samples and reasoning to guide the unlearn-
099   ing process in a more granular and efficient manner, offering a promising alternative for precise,
100   targeted model modifications

143   Self-improvement reasoners. Self-Taught Reasoner (STaR; Zelikman et al. (2022)) is an iterative
144   method where a language model refines itself through correctness feedback. In each iteration, the
145   model generates solutions for problems, evaluates them against ground truth, and retains only the
146
      correct ones. The model is then fine-tuned on this filtered dataset, iteratively improving its accuracy.
      Rejection Sampling Fine-tuning (RFT; Yuan et al. (2023)) follows a similar process but is not iter-
147
      ative. Instead, RFT samples multiple solutions for each problem and augments the original dataset
148
      with correct completions for fine-tuning. STaR iterations can also incorporate rejection sampling
149   techniques, as in methods like ReSTEM (Singh et al. (2023)). V-STaR (Hosseini et al. (2024)) en-
150   hances STaR by training a verifier using both correct and incorrect solutions to judge correctness,
151   resulting in more accurate reasoning and verification on benchmarks like math and code generation.
152
      Our work builds upon these reasoning frameworks but repurposes the concept of self-taught rea-
153
      soning for unlearning rather than improving model accuracy. Instead of refining correct answers,
154
      U N S TAR leverages misleading rationales to generate anti-samples, which in turn aid in the forget-
155   ting of specific information. This novel application of reasoning to the domain of unlearning has not
156   been explored in prior works.

161   Problem Formulation. Let the language model with parameters φ be denoted by M(·, φ). Let Q =
      {(q, a)} represent the dataset of question-answer pairs. Let â = M(q, φ) is the answer produced by

      the model M for q. We define the forget set Qf ⊂ Q as the subset of question-answer pairs related
163
      to facts we wish to unlearn (e.g., Harry Potter studied at Hogwarts). The retain set Qr = Q \ Qf
164   consists of the remaining question-answer pairs. It holds that: Qr ∪ Qf = Q and Qr ∩ Qf = ∅.
165   Let â′ = M(q, φ′ ) represent the answers produced by the unlearned model M(·, φ′ ) with updated
166   parameters φ′ for each question q. After unlearning, we want the following conditions to hold: ❶
167   For all (q, a) ∈ Qf , the answers should no longer match the original: â′ ̸= a. ❷ For all (q, a) ∈ Qr ,
168   the model should retain the correct answers: â′ = a. This ensures that after unlearning, the model
169   provides incorrect answers for the forget set while maintaining the correct answers for the retain set.
170   Targeted unlearning. Given a language model M(·, φ), update the model to forget all questions qf
171   related to a target t: â′f ̸= af , where (qf , af ) ∈ Qf while preserving correct answers for unrelated
172   questions: â′r = ar , where (qr , ar ) ∈ Qr .

      U N S TAR performs these steps for the forget set Qf .
175
            1. Selection of Question-Answer Pair: Select a question-answer pair (q, a) from the forget
176
               set Qf . This pair represents a specific fact that we wish to unlearn.
177
178
            2. Generation of Paraphrased Questions and Incorrect Answers: Generate n paraphrased
               versions of the selected question q, denoted as (q0∗ , . . . , qn∗ ), and add these to a question
179
               bank Q∗ . For each paraphrased question qi∗ , generate an incorrect answer āi , forming pairs
180
               (qi∗ , āi ), and add them to Q∗ .
181
182         3. Iterative Processing of Paraphrased Questions: While Q∗ is not empty, we proceed with
183
               the following steps for each paraphrased question q ∗ :
184             (a) Answer Generation: Use the model M to generate an answer â for the question q ∗ .
185             (b) Check for Unlearning:
186                  • If â ̸= a, mark the paraphrased question q ∗ as unlearned and remove it from Q∗ .
187                  • If â = a, use the incorrect answer ā to generate a justification r.
188             (c) Fine-Tune Model: Fine-tune the model using the tuple (q ∗ , ā, r) to reinforce the pro-
189                 cess of forgetting.

192   In this case, instead of paraphrased questions with incorrect answers, it focuses on generating and
193   confirming that the model M consistently provides correct answers â = a for all question-answer
194
      pairs (q ∗ , a). The algorithm is presented in Algorithm 1. This ensures that correct knowledge is
      reinforced and preserved without being affected by the unlearning of the forget set.

196   Generating Paraphrased Questions and Incorrect Answers. U N S TAR prompts the original, un-
197   learned LLM to generate n paraphrased versions of the questions, as well as incorrect answers. The
198   specific prompts used for this process are provided in the Appendix. However, three key challenges
199   arise in this context:
200   ❶ Semantically Divergent Questions: LLMs are known to exhibit hallucination tendencies, leading
201   to the generation of questions that may diverge from the intended topics. Therefore, it is crucial to
202   ensure that the paraphrased questions maintain semantic alignment with the original queries. For
203   example, if the focus is on Harry Potter’s education, the paraphrased questions should not stray into
204   unrelated subjects, such as Hermione’s achievements.
205   To address this issue, U N S TAR evaluates the semantic similarity between the paraphrased questions
206   and the original queries. This is achieved through a threshold-based fuzzy matching approach,
207   which employs Levenshtein distance to quantify sequence differences, complemented by cosine
208   similarity derived from sentence embeddings generated by a MiniLM-family sentence transformer
209   model (paraphrase-MiniLM-L6-v2), specifically optimized for paraphrase detection and semantic
210   similarity tasks. This dual approach ensures that the generated paraphrases remain focused and
211   aligned with the original intent.
212   ❷ Near-Correct Incorrect Answers: Some generated incorrect answers may be semantically too
213   close to the correct answers, making them unsuitable for effective unlearning. We assess the seman-
214   tic proximity of these incorrect answers to ensure meaningful divergence from the correct ones. For
215   instance, if the question is, “Was Benedetto Varchi Italian?” and the generated incorrect answer is,
      “No, Varchi was from Italy,” this case is flagged as a near-correct answer.

224     Generating Justifications for Incorrect Answers. The process of generating justifications for a
225     given incorrect answer in U N S TAR is achieved through “rationalization” which draws inspiration
226
        from the STaR approach (Zelikman et al. (2022)). Rationalization allows the model to leverage pro-
        vided answers to generate appropriate rationales, thus improving the unlearning process by guiding
227
        the model to reason backward from the answer to formulate relevant rationales.
228
229     In our context, when the LLM encounters a question-answer pair that it fails to unlearn effectively,
230     we introduce the incorrect answer as a hint. This aids the model in constructing a justification that
231     logically lead to the provided incorrect answer. For instance, if the model is unlearning the fact
232
        “Harry Potter studied at Hogwarts,” we prompt it with an incorrect answer, such as “Ilvermorny,”
        that guides it to generate a justification like “Harry Potter studied at Ilvermorny because it was the
233
        premier wizarding school in North America, renowned for its diverse magical curriculum and rich
234
        history in the wizarding world.”

300   Experimental Setup. We use the identical experimental settings as in the case of RWHP (Liu et al.
301   (2024a)) using the Wikipedia Person Unlearn (WPU) dataset. The LLM must unlearn multiple indi-
302
      viduals simultaneously, capturing the nuances of both forgetting and retaining relevant knowledge.
303   Datasets. The WPU dataset includes a diverse set of individuals designated as unlearning targets,
304   along with their associated documents and test data in a free-response question-answering (QA) for-
305   mat. This setup assesses three distinct knowledge types. ❶ Forget QA (FQA): These questions target
306   the unlearning subjects with answers sourced from the unlearning documents. For example, “What
307   nationality was Wilhelm Wattenbach?” with the answer “German”. ❷ Hard-retain QA (HRQA):
308
      These questions involve unrelated information about entities within the unlearning documents, such
      as questions regarding locations mentioned on the subject’s Wikipedia page, like Rantzau on Wat-
309
      tenbach’s page. ❸ General-retain QA (GRQA): These questions pertain to entirely unrelated indi-
310
      viduals and general knowledge, such as asking about Elon Musk, which tests the model’s ability to
311   retain general information unaffected by the unlearning process.
312
313   Similar to WPU, the Peter Parker forgetting dataset, is constructed using GPT-4-turbo and GPT-
314
      3.5-turbo as presented in SNAP Choi et al. (2024b). This dataset evaluates the removal of selective
      knowledge, such as the identity “Peter Parker” and associated copyrighted content. The dataset
315
      includes 100 examples for the forgetting set Df and 300 examples for retaining set Dr , generated
316
      using a diverse set of prompts.
317
318   TOFU dataset Maini et al. (2024) contains QA pairs about fictitious authors. The task is to forget a
319   subset of the association of authors and their books. Similar to WPU, it is also divided into retain
320
      and forget sets. The detailed statistics are presented in Table 1.
321   Metrics. We utilize multiple metrics to assess the performance of the model across various di-
322   mensions. All metric values are normalized to the range of [0, 1] for consistency in comparison.
323   ❶ ROUGE: We calculate the ROUGE-L score (Lin, 2004) to compare the generated responses
      with concise ground-truth answers, effectively measuring the overlap in terms of accuracy. ❷


                                                                6

324
      GPT Privacy Score: This metric evaluates how well the model preserves the privacy of the un-
325   learning targets by avoiding factual leakage. Based on the ground-truth answer, the score ranges
326   from 1 to 3, with 3 indicating no leakage of factual information related to the unlearning target. ❸
327   GPT Quality Score: This metric assesses the overall quality of the generated response, independent
328   of its correctness. Scores range from 1 to 3, where 3 indicates the response is fluent, relevant, and
329   contextually appropriate. ❹ Rep-4: Following Welleck et al. (2019), we compute the proportion of
330   duplicate 4-grams in the generated text, which helps to measure response redundancy and repeti-
331   tion. ❺ GPT Rejection Rate: This metric tracks the percentage of responses that correctly decline
332   to answer, stating that the information is unavailable (e.g., the subject cannot be recalled). A higher
333
      rejection rate reduces the chances of hallucinations or factual leakage, contributing to better privacy
      protection.
334
335   Composite Metrics. ❶ Unlearning Efficacy: The model should eliminate any correct information
336   related to the unlearning target. This is measured as the harmonic mean of ROUGE (FQA) and
337   GPT privacy score (FQA). ❷ Model Utility: The LLM must maintain its ability to correctly an-
338   swer questions unrelated to the unlearning target, including handling unrelated information in the
339
      unlearning documents. This is evaluated through the harmonic mean of ROUGE (HRQA), GPT
      quality score (HRQA), and ROUGE (GRQA). ❸ Response Quality: When questioned about the un-
340
      learning target, the LLM should generate coherent responses rather than nonsensical or irrelevant
341
      answers. This is captured by the harmonic mean of GPT quality score (FQA) and Rep-4 (FQA).
342   ❹ Hallucination Avoidance: The LLM should refrain from fabricating information about the un-
343   learning target and instead admit its lack of knowledge. This is measured by the GPT rejection rate
344   (FQA). ❺ Adversarial Robustness: This evaluates the model’s resilience under adversarial attacks
345   designed to trick the language model into releasing true answers about the unlearning target. We
346   measure the minimum unlearning efficacy under two jailbreak attacks (Anil et al. (2024); Schwinn
347   et al. (2024)) to ensure the model’s resistance against such manipulations, where the LLM should
348   still be unable to disclose unlearned information.
349   Baselines. We evaluate our method against eight baselines: ❶ Gradient Ascent (GA) Yao et al.
350   (2023) maximizes cross-entropy loss on the unlearning documents to promote forgetting. ❷
351   Negative Preference Optimization (NPO) Zhang et al. (2024b) enhances GA by introducing a
352   bounded loss to prevent model degradation, while also including a regularization term to minimize
353   cross-entropy loss on Wiki pages of 100 unrelated individuals. ❸ PROMPT Lynch et al. (2024);
354   Thaker et al. (2024) prompts the LLM to avoid generating any content related to the unlearning tar-
355   gets. ❹ PROMPT-DISTILL builds on PROMPT by using its outputs as a teacher to train the LLM
356
      on additional QA pairs. Since most responses are “I don’t know,” this approach is akin to methods
      explicitly designed to train LLMs to produce such answers Ishibashi & Shimodaira (2023); Maini
357
      et al. (2024). To avoid the model refusing all questions, a regularization term is added to ensure
358
      correct answers for unrelated queries. ❺ Deliberate Imagination (DI) (Dong et al. (2024)) reduces
359   the logit of the original token in the LLM’s output distribution for unlearning documents by a con-
360   stant, using the LLM’s own outputs as a teacher. ❻ WHP (Eldan & Russinovich (2023)) leverages
361   a previously established framework for unlearning, though we re-use RWHP’s implementation due
362   to unavailability of their code. ❼ WHP+, a variation of RWHP that omits aggregation over multiple
363   distributions. ❽ RWHP Liu et al. (2024a) improves upon WHP by introducing a causal intervention
364   perspective to enhance unlearning effectiveness.

368   were conducted on an Apple M3 Pro chip with 18 GB of unified memory.

525   5   C ONCLUSION
526
527
      In this paper, we have presented a novel approach to unlearning in large language models (LLMs)
528   through the introduction of anti-samples, facilitated by our method, U N S TAR: Unlearning with Self-
529   Taught Anti-Sample Reasoning. As the landscape of machine learning evolves, the need for effective
530   unlearning mechanisms becomes increasingly critical, particularly in light of privacy concerns, legal
531   compliance, and ethical considerations. Our findings indicate that traditional unlearning techniques
532   often inadvertently compromise the model’s broader knowledge, underscoring the necessity for a
533   refined approach.
534   By leveraging anti-samples, we enable a targeted unlearning process that not only facilitates the se-
535   lective removal of specific associations but also preserves related knowledge—a feat not achievable
536   by prior methods. Additionally, we achieve fine-grained targeted unlearning, allowing for the nu-
537   anced removal of specific information without disrupting the overall integrity of the model’s knowl-
538   edge base. Our use of misleading rationales as justifications for unlearning further enhances the
539   efficacy of this approach, providing a structured means for LLMs to forget while maintaining con-
      textual integrity.
