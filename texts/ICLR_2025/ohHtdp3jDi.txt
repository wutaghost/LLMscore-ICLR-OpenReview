                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Bayesian deep learning (BDL) is believed to be an effective approach to en-
010
               abling uncertainty estimation and improving the generalisation and robustness of
011            classical deep learning with the help of the Bayesian principle. Considering its
012            non-meaningful weight-space prior and problematic Kullback-Leibler (KL) diver-
013            gence, functional inference with Wasserstein distance has recently emerged as a
014            promising direction in this field. However, existing efforts require different types
015            of degenerations to achieve tractable Wasserstein distance computation, which
016            limits the predictive and uncertainty estimation capabilities. In this paper, we pro-
017            pose two novel implicit functional BDL (ifBDL) approaches, i.e., implicit func-
018            tional Bayesian neural networks and implicit functional Bayesian deep ensemble.
019
               The common idea is to implicitly transform the BDL posterior to a Gaussian pro-
               cess via the neural tangent kernel to facilitate tractable 2-Wasserstein distance
020
               computation and preserve the neural network parameterization. The experimental
021
               evaluations on standard tasks show that ifBDL has superior predictive and un-
022            certainty estimation capabilities compared to existing weight-space and function-
023            space approaches.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Bayesian deep learning (BDL) (Papamarkou et al., 2024) is believed to be an effective approach to
029   enabling uncertainty estimation and improving the generalisation and robustness of classical deep
030   learning with the help of the Bayesian principle. Some typical models include Bayesian neural
031   networks (BNNs) (Blundell et al., 2015; Gal, 2016), Bayesian deep ensembles (Seligmann et al.,
032   2024), deep kernel processes (Ober et al., 2023), neural processes (Garnelo et al., 2018), and so
033
      on. The uncertainty estimation is essential for some safety-critical applications like medical diag-
      nosis (Dolezal et al., 2022) and autonomous driving (Tai et al., 2019), and stronger generalisation
034
      and robustness are the keys to applying the trained model in practical scenarios featuring possible
035
      distribution shifts and incomplete and noisy data. Recent works have also shown its capability of
036   extending large deep networks like Transformers (Chen & Li, 2023) and GPT (Shen et al., 2024) to
037   yield good weight-uncertainty for calibration, and model averaging.
038
039
      Similar to all Bayesian approaches, there are two main steps of BDL: prior selection and posterior
      inference. For prior selection, a straightforward and popular choice is the independent identically
040
      distributed (i.i.d) prior for deep learning weights, such as the i.i.d Gaussian distributions. However,
041
      this prior is found problematic because 1) it is hard to encode prior knowledge about the underlying
042   functions through distribution design for the large-scale network weights; 2) the samples of such
043   priors over parameters tend to be horizontally linear and lead to pathologies for deep models (Duve-
044   naud et al., 2014; Matthews et al., 2018; Tran et al., 2020); and 3) the effects of the given priors on
045   posterior inference and, consequently, on the resulting distributions over functions are unclear and
046   hard to control due to the complex architecture and non-linearity of the models (Ma & Hernández-
047   Lobato, 2021; Wild et al., 2022). Considering these problems, there is increasing interest in using
048   functional priors instead. A representative example is the Gaussian process (GP) (Rasmussen &
049   Williams, 2005), which can easily encode prior knowledge of underlying functions through kernel
050
      design. We call the BDL under functional prior functional BDL (fBDL).
051   The posterior inference for fBDL is more challenging than weight-space BDL due to the infinite-
052   dimensional functional prior and posterior. The (generalized) variational inference (Knoblauch et al.,
053   2022) of BDL involves a term to minimize the distance between the posterior of BDL and the prior.
      However, the posterior is a complex distribution over functions without an explicit probability den-


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      sity function, making the distance measurement to GP prior a challenge. Existing approaches in-
055   clude using a spectral Stein gradient estimator to evaluate the divergence between a BNN and a GP
056   (Sun et al., 2019), as well as using first-order Taylor expansion to approximate the posterior of BDL
057   (Rudner et al., 2022). Both methods are designed for the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence, which
058   presents challenges because i) there do not exist density functions with respect to the Lebesgue
059   measure for stochastic processes prior and posterior (Hunt et al., 1992), and ii) the functional KL
060   divergence is not always well-defined (Burt et al., 2020) as the variational posterior measure should
061   be absolutely continuous with respect to the prior measure to guarantee the exist of Radon-Nikodym
062   derivative; otherwise, the KL divergence can be infinite (Gray, 2011; Matthews et al., 2016). Conse-
063
      quently, efforts have been made to use the Wasserstein distance instead. For instance, the dual form
      of the 1-Wasserstein distance is employed to evaluate the distance between a BNN and a GP (Tran
064
      et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2024). Compared to the 1-Wasserstein distance, which requires a number of
065
      function samples for approximation, the 2-Wasserstein distance can be much more easily evaluated
066   because it has a closed form for Gaussian measures on a given measurement set. To utilize the 2-
067   Wasserstein distance, another GP using a deep neural network as the mean function approximates
068   the posterior of BDL, and then the 2-Wasserstein distance is used to evaluate the distance between a
069   BNN and a GP (Wild et al., 2022). However, this GP degeneration reduces the uncertainty modelling
070   and model generalization capability of the original BDL, as verified in the experiments.
071
      In this paper, we propose a novel functional BDL using the 2-Wasserstein distance via the neural
072   tangent kernel. The NTK (Jacot et al., 2018; Lee et al., 2019) captures the evolution of deep neural
073   networks under gradient descent. Under certain conditions, the distribution of functions learned
074   by such networks can be well approximated by a GP parameterized by the NTK. Motivated by
075   these properties of the NTK, we establish a connection between the BDL posterior and the NTK-
076   based GP transformation, resulting in our novel functional BDL method. Experimental evaluations
077   demonstrate that this approach outperforms the approximation using a GP with the deep neural
078   network as the mean function. Our main contributions are summarised as follows:
079
080
              • We propose to use the 2-Wasserstein distance with good statistical properties instead of KL
                divergence to achieve functional BDL. This approach can avoid many limitations associated
081
                with using KL divergence in infinite-dimensional function space;
082
083           • A neural tangent kernel-based approach is employed to transform a BDL posterior to a GP.
084
                This transformation preserves more uncertainty estimation and generalization capability
                of BDL compared to the approximation using GP with deep neural networks as a mean
085
                function.
086
087           • We introduce and verify the effectiveness of two functional BDL approaches, i.e., func-
088             tional Bayesian neural networks and functional Bayesian deep ensembles. These ap-
089
                proaches are evaluated through comparative experiments on standard tasks with several
                existing parameter space and functional space variational inference methods.

094   2.1     BAYESIAN DEEP LEARNING
095
                                                    n
096   Consider a dataset D = {X, Y} = {(xi , yi )}i=1 , where x ∈ Rd represents d-dimensional input and
097   y ∈ Rc represents c-dimensional target. Classical (determinate) deep learning (DL) aims to build
098
      a function mapping f (y|x; w) between input and target weighted by parameter w, while Bayesian
      deep learning (BDL) aims to learn a distribution over all possible functions p(f |D) instead of a sin-
099
      gle f . Such distribution over functions is able to improve the model generalization and uncertainty
100
      modelling (Papamarkou et al., 2024). In this paper, we use two examples of BDL: Bayesian neural
101   networks and Bayesian deep ensembles.
102
103
      2.1.1    BAYESIAN NEURAL NETWORKS
104
105   A Bayesian neural network (BNN) assigns prior p0 (w) and likelihood p(D|w) to neural net-
106   work weights. According to Bayes’ theorem, the posterior distribution of w can be calculated
107   as p(w|D) ∝ p(D|w)p0 (w). Given test data x∗ , the predictive distribution can be obtained by
      p(y ∗ |x∗ , D) = Ep(w|D) (p(y ∗ |x∗ , w). It is important to note that the distribution over functions

      p(f |D) is induced by the posterior distribution p(w|D). Since the posterior is intractable for prac-
109   tical neural network architectures, the variational posterior inference is used to fit a tractable ap-
110   proximation posterior qθ (w), parameterized by θ, to the exact posterior p(w|D). This is achieved
111   by minimizing their KL divergence, which is equivalent to maximizing the evidence lower bound
112   (ELBO):
113                         Lqθ (w) = Eqθ (w) [log p(D | w)] − KL[qθ (w)∥p0 (w)].                        (1)
114   Bayes By Backprop (BBB), proposed by Blundell et al. (2015), is one of the most used variational
115   inference learning algorithms for BNNs in weight space. BBB leverages a fully factorized Gaus-
116   sian assumption about variational posterior and employs the reparameterization trick (Kingma &
117   Welling, 2014) to obtain unbiased gradient estimates of ELBO with respect to model parameters.
118   Several variants of BBB have been proposed in the field (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016; Marino et al.,
119   2018; Santana & Hernández-Lobato, 2022). Despite their differences, these variants share a simi-
120
      lar basic framework. However, the challenge remains in choosing an appropriate prior for network
      weights and understanding its impact on inference and the resulting posterior distribution.

395   4       R ELATED W ORK
396
397
398   Due to the limitations of parameter-space variational inference, such as the intractability of spec-
399   ifying meaningful priors, Sun et al. (2019) proposed a functional ELBO to match a GP prior and
400
      the variational posterior over functions for BNNs via a spectral Stein gradient estimator designed
      for implicit distributions (Shi et al., 2018). Concurrently, Wang et al. (2019) proposed a particle
401
      optimization variational inference method in function spaces for posterior approximation in BNNs.
402
      Rudner et al. (2020; 2022) pointed out that the supremum of marginal KL divergence over finite
403   measurement sets cannot be solved analytically for the estimation of functional KL divergence.
404   They proposed approximating the distributions over functions as Gaussian via the linearization of
405   their mean parameters, deriving a tractable and well-defined variational objective since the func-
406   tional prior and variational posterior are two BNNs that share the same network structures. Ma &
407   Hernández-Lobato (2021) randomized the number of finite measurement points to derive an alter-
408   native grid-functional KL divergence, which can avoid some limitations of KL divergence between
409   stochastic processes. However, all these methods are based on KL divergence between stochastic
410   processes, which might be ill-defined for a wide class of distributions and lead to an invalid varia-
411
      tional objective (Burt et al., 2020).
412   Considering the potential weaknesses of KL divergence, some recent works have explored using
413   Wasserstein distance (Kantorovich, 1960; Villani, 2021) as a replacement. Tran et al. (2020) pro-
414   posed matching a BNN prior to a GP prior by minimizing the 1-Wasserstein distance to obtain more
415   interpretable functional priors in BNNs. However, they used stochastic gradient Hamiltonian Monte
416   Carlo (SGHMC) rather than variational inference to approximate the posterior. Wild et al. (2022) de-
417
      veloped a functional variational objective called GWI, where both the functional prior and posterior
      are Gaussian measures, and the dissimilarity measure is the 2-Wasserstein distance. However, the
418
      critical GP degeneration makes it less expressive than the original BDL in terms of uncertainty. Our
419
      method retains the original BDL parameterization and facilitates tractable 2-Wasserstein distance
420   regularization.

533
534   In this paper, we propose two implicit functional BDL approaches: implicit functional Bayesian
535   neural networks and implicit functional Bayesian deep ensembles, via the NTK-based GP transfor-
536   mation. These approaches leverage the tractable property of the 2-Wasserstein distance between
537   Gaussian measures without sacrificing much on uncertainty modeling capability. These new BDL
538   approaches demonstrate better predictive and uncertainty modeling capabilities compared to exist-
539   ing methods on benchmark tasks. Our future work will focus on improving the scalability of these
      approaches for large-scale models, such as GPT-4 and Gemini.
