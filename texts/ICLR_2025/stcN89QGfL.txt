009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Solving partial differential equations (PDEs) by numerical methods meet compu-
012            tational cost challenge for getting the accurate solution since fine grids and small
013            time steps are required. Machine learning can accelerate this process, but struggle
014            with weak generalizability, interpretability, and data dependency, as well as suf-
015            fer in long-term prediction. To this end, we propose a PDE-embedded network
016            with multiscale time stepping (MultiPDENet), which fuses the scheme of numer-
017            ical methods and machine learning, for accelerated simulation of fluid flows. In
018
               particular, we design a convolutional filter based on the structure of finite differ-
               ence stencils with a small number of parameters to optimize, which estimates the
019
               equivalent form of spatial derivative on a coarse grid to minimize the equation’s
020
               residual. A physics block with a 4th-order Runge-Kutta integrator at the fine time
021            scale is established that embeds the structure of PDEs to guide the prediction. To
022            alleviate the curse of temporal error accumulation in long-term prediction, we in-
023            troduce a multiscale time integration approach, where a neural network is used to
024            correct the prediction error at a coarse time scale. Experiments across various PDE
025            systems, including the Navier-Stokes equations, demonstrate that MultiPDENet
026            can accurately predict long-term spatiotemporal dynamics, even given small and
027            incomplete training data, e.g., spatiotemporally down-sampled datasets. Multi-
028            PDENet achieves the state-of-the-art performance compared with other baseline
029
               models, with over 5× speedup compared to classical numerical methods.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Complex spatiotemporal dynamical systems, e.g., climate system (Schneider et al., 2017), fluid dy-
034
      namics (Ferziger et al., 2019), and material science (Wang & Sun, 2018; Liu & Wang, 2019), are
035
      fundamentally governed by partial differential equations (PDEs). To capture the intricate behaviors
036   of these systems, various numerical methods have been developed. Direct Numerical Simulation
037   (DNS) is a widely used method for solving PDEs. It requires specifying initial conditions (ICs),
038   boundary conditions (BCs), and PDE parameters, followed by discretizing the equations on a grid
039   using techniques like finite difference (FD), finite element (FE), finite volume (FV), or spectral
040   methods. Despite their accuracy, traditional numerical methods face significant challenges, particu-
041   larly high computational costs (Goc et al., 2021), when addressing with high-dimensional problems
042   or necessitating fine spatial and temporal resolutions.
043   Recent advances in deep learning have introduced neural-based approaches (Lu et al., 2021; Li
044   et al., 2021; Gupta & Brandstetter, 2023) for solving PDEs. These data-driven methods eliminate
045   the need for explicit theoretical formulations, enabling networks to learn underlying patterns directly
046   from data through end-to-end training. While promising, these approaches face notable challenges,
047   including a heavy dependence on large training datasets and limited generalization. For instance,
048   achieving accurate predictions becomes particularly challenging when models encounter unseen ICs
049   or scenarios beyond the training distribution.
050   A representative work in the field of scientific computing introduces a novel paradigm with Physics-
051   informed neural networks (PINNs) (Raissi et al., 2019), which incorporates physical prior knowl-
052   edge (such as PDE residuals and I/BCs) as constraints within the loss function. This approach allows
053   the network to fit the data while simultaneously maintaining a certain degree of physical consistency.
      Variants of PINNs (Raissi et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2020; Eshkofti & Hosseini, 2023) have shown

054
      notable success across various domains, reducing the dependency on extensive datasets to some de-
055   gree. However, such methods still face scalability and generalizability challenges when applied to
056   complex nonlinear dynamical systems. Additionally, optimizing complex loss functions (Rathore
057   et al., 2024) and ensuring model interpretability remain challenges.
058
      A series of approaches have been proposed to integrate physics into neural networks (NNs) to over-
059
      come the above challenges. For instance, PeRCNN (Rao et al., 2022; 2023), which uses feature
060
      map multiplication to construct polynomial combinations for approximating the underlying PDEs,
061   can capture the latent spatiotemporal dynamics even with low-resolution, noisy, and sparse data,
062   demonstrating strong generalizability. Nevertheless, this method suffers from error accumulation,
063   degrading its performance in long-term predictions. Another approach (Kochkov et al., 2021; Sun
064   et al., 2023), combining NNs with numerical methods, aims to accelerate the simulation process on
065   coarse grids. These hybrid methods leverage traditional solvers for stability and NNs for accuracy.
066   However, they often rely heavily on NN capabilities and often requires large amounts of data.
067   To overcome these limitations, we propose MultiPDENet, a PDE-embedded network that incorpo-
068   rates multiscale time-stepping (as shown in Figure 1), to efficiently simulate spatiotemporal dynam-
069   ics, e.g., turbulent fluid flows, on coarse spatial and temporal grids with limited data. Notably, it
070   integrates a trainable neural solver for precise predictions at micro time scales, while employing a
071   NN to correct errors at macro time steps. Additionally, by embedding PDEs, MultiPDENet offers
072   enhanced generalizability. The primary contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
073
            • We developed MultiPDENet, a PDE-embedded network with multiscale time-stepping, for
074           accelerated fluid flow simulations on spatiotemporal coarse grids. By integrating neural
075           solver with PDEs, MultiPDENet achieves great generalizability and efficiency.
076
            • Leveraging the structure of finite difference stencils, we introduced a symmetric convolu-
077
              tional filter that approximates the equivalent form of derivatives on coarse grids, aiming to
078           reduce the residual error of the governing PDEs.
079
            • Experimental results across various datasets, covering 1D and 2D equations (e.g., complex
080
              reaction-diffusion processes and turbulent flows), demonstrate the effectiveness of MultiP-
081           DENet in accelerating long-term simulations.

084   2   R ELATED WORK
085
086   Numerical Methods. Numerical methods have been extensively applied to solve PDEs. Approaches
087
      such as FD (Thomas, 2013), FE (Zienkiewicz et al., 2005), and FV methods (Moukalled et al.,
      2016) discretize the continuous domain into mesh grids, transforming PDEs into algebraic equations
088
      that can be solved with high accuracy. However, these methods often require fine spatiotemporal
089
      grids and substantial computational resources to achieve accurate solutions, particularly in high-
090   dimensional spaces. This leads to two main challenges: (1) the need for repeated computations when
091   conditions change (e.g., ICs); (2) the demand for fast simulations in many industrial applications.
092
093
      Machine Learning Methods. Building on the success of machine learning in fields like natural lan-
      guage processing (Vaswani, 2017) and computer vision (He et al., 2016), these techniques have also
094
      been applied to solving PDEs. With abundant labeled data, it is possible to train end-to-end mod-
095
      els to predict solutions. Representative works include ResNet (Lu et al., 2018; Ruthotto & Haber,
096   2020), CNN-based models (Bhatnagar et al., 2019; Stachenfeld et al., 2022; Gupta & Brandstetter,
097   2023), Transformers-based models (Cao, 2021; Geneva & Zabaras, 2022; Li et al., 2024) and Graph-
098   based models (Brandstetter et al., 2022b). Many notable neural operators (Lu et al., 2021; Li et al.,
099   2021; Wen et al., 2022; Rahman et al., 2023; Bonev et al., 2023), which learn a mapping between
100   functional spaces, enable the approximation of complex relationships in PDEs. While these meth-
101   ods show promise in learning complex dynamics and approximating solutions, they often require
102   substantial amounts of labeled data.
103   Physics-inspired Learning Methods. Recently, physics-inspired learning methods have demon-
104   strated impressive capabilities in solving PDEs, which can be classified into two categories accord-
105   ing to the way of adding prior knowledge: physics-informed and physics-encoded. The physics-
106   informed methods take PDEs and I/BCs as a part of the loss functions (e.g., the family of PINN
107   (Raissi et al., 2019; 2020; Wang et al., 2020; Tang et al., 2024), PhySR (Ren et al., 2023)). On the
      other hand, the physics-encoded methods employ a different approach that preserves the structure of

      3.2.4    NN B LOCK
239
240   To alleviate the error accumulation during long-term predictions on coarse grids, we introduce the
241   Mi NN and Ma NN blocks, operating at micro- and macro-scales, respectively. The MiNN Block
242   employs a lightweight model (e.g., FNO, DenseCNN (Liu et al., 2024)) for efficient micro-step
243   predictions, whereas the Ma NN Block delivers more accurate predictions at larger steps (Gupta &
244
      Brandstetter, 2023). In this study, we utilized FNO as the Mi NN block and UNet as the Ma NN
      block. The significance of these blocks is evident from the ablation studies presented in Table 3.
245
246   Mi NN Block. The Mi NN block is designed to rectify error accumulation during micro-scale time
247   step predictions. As shown in Figure 1(b) in the upper path, ūkm is first corrected by the correction
248   block, and p̄km is computed by the Poisson block. Inputs, including solution states {ūkm , p̄km } and
249   their derivative terms, forcing term, and Reynolds number, are fed into the Mi NN block (see Figure
250
      S1(d)). The Mi NN block continuously refines the PDE block’s outputs on the fly. For detailed
      information of the Mi NN block settings, please refer to Appendix Table S5.
251
252   Ma NN Block. Although the Physics block offers real-time corrections for the Mi NN outputs, errors
253   still accumulate in long-term predictions. To mitigate error accumulation in long-term predictions
254   given training data sampled at large time steps (e.g., 128∆t for the NS dataset), we introduce the
255   Ma NN block. As depicted in Figure 1(a), the Ma NN block takes the current velocity field uk as
256
      input, and updates the solution uk+1 which is obtained by integrating the outputs from both the
      upper and lower paths. During the backpropagation, the Ma NN block learns to correct the coarse
257
      solution output of the Physics block in real time, ensuring that their combined results more closely
258
      align with the ground truth. The configuration details for this block are found in Appendix Table S6.

1082
       Fourier Neural Operator (FNO). The FNO (Li et al., 2021) combines neural networks with
1083   Fourier transforms to effectively capture both global and local features of system dynamics. The
1084   architecture has two key components: First, it applies Fourier transforms to the system state, per-
1085   forming convolution operations in the frequency domain to capture global information. This is
1086   followed by an inverse Fourier transform which maps the data back to the spatial domain. Sec-
1087   ondly, convolutional layers are employed to extract local features directly from the system state.
1088   The outputs from both global and local components are then integrated through the application of
1089   activation functions, which ultimately yield the final prediction.
1090   PhyFNO. PhyFNO shares the same architecture as FNO but incorporates physics-based constraints
1091   by embedding governing equations into the loss function. The loss function can be expressed as:
1092   L(λ) = LEq + LData . The first term of the loss function, LEq , represent an MSE loss computed
1093   using the analytical expressions of the dynamics, as defined in Equation 1. The second term LData
1094   is the data loss, which was previously discussed in Section 4.1.”
1095   PeRCNN. PeRCNN (Rao et al., 2023) integrates physics-based principles directly into the learning
1096   framework by embedding governing equations into the neural network structure. The architecture
1097   features multiple parallel CNNs that model polynomial relationships via feature map multiplica-
1098   tions. This incorporation of physical laws improves the model’s generalization and extrapolation
1099   capabilities, enabling accurate predictions in dynamic systems governed by complex equations.
1100
       UNet. The UNet architecture (Ronneberger et al., 2015) adopts a symmetric encoder-decoder struc-
1101   ture originally designed for computer vision tasks. The encoder compresses the input by applying
1102   multiple downsampling layers to capture hierarchical features at various scales. Conversely, the
1103   decoder gradually restores the original spatial resolution using upsampling operations. Skip con-
1104   nections bridge the encoder and decoder, directly transferring feature maps to retain fine-grained
1105   details. This design allows UNet to merge high-resolution spatial information with deeper, abstract
1106   features, achieving accurate reconstructions of both local and global structures.
1107   DeepONet. DeepONet (Lu et al., 2021) is designed to approximate operators and map inputs di-
1108   rectly to outputs by leveraging neural networks. The architecture consists of two main components:
1109   the trunk network, which processes domain-specific information, and the branch network, which
1110   handles the input functions. This dual-structure approach enables the efficient learning of complex
1111   functional relationships and enhances the model’s capability to capture detailed operator mappings
1112   across various applications.
1113   Learned Interpolation (LI). The LI (Kochkov et al., 2021) employs a finite volume approach
1114   enhanced with neural networks as a replacement for conventional polynomial-based interpolation
1115   schemes in computing velocity tensor product. The network adapts to the local flow conditions
1116   by learning a dynamic interpolation mechanism that can adjust to the characteristics of the flow.
1117   This enables LI to provide accurate fluid dynamics predictions even on coarse grids, improving
1118   computational efficiency while maintaining prediction fidelity.
1119   Temporal Stencil Modeling (TSM). TSM (Sun et al., 2023) addresses time-dependent partial dif-
1120   ferential equations (PDEs) in conservation form by integrating time-series modeling with learnable
1121   stencil techniques. It effectively recovers information lost during downsampling, enabling enhanced
1122   predictive accuracy. TSM is particularly advantageous for machine learning models dealing with
1123   coarse-resolution datasets.

1227   Taking NS as an example, we compared the inference time, RMSE, and HCT of MultiPDENet
1228   with the Direct Numerical Simulation (DNS) method across three cases. The comparison principle
1229   is based on the time required to simulate the same trajectory length (T = 8.4 s) under identical
1230
       experimental conditions (a single A100 GPU). The inference time is measured from the moment the
       initial conditions (IC) are fed into the model until the trajectory of the same length is predicted.
1231
1232   The DNS settings follow JAX-CFD (Kochkov et al., 2021). According to the CFL condition, the
1233   simulated time step (dt) varies with the resolution of the DNS method, resulting in different numbers
1234   of timesteps required for calculation. DNS 2048, DNS 4096, and DNS 4096 are used as the ground
1235   truth for the three cases, respectively. Detailed comparison results are presented in Table S7.
1236   Nevertheless, we also would like to clarify that the DNS code used above was implemented in JAX,
1237   while our model was programmed in PyTorch. These two platforms have distinct efficiencies even
1238   for the same model. Typically, the codes under JAX environment runs much faster compared with
1239   PyTorch (up to 6×) (Takamoto et al., 2022). We anticipate to achieve much higher speedup of our
1240   model if also implemented and optimized in JAX, which is, however, out of the scope of this study.
