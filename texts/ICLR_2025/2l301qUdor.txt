011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent research has significantly mitigated the performance collapse issue in Dif-
014            ferentiable Architecture Search (DARTS) by either refining architecture param-
015            eters to better reflect the true strengths of operations or developing alternative
016            metrics for evaluating operation significance. However, the actual role and im-
017            pact of architecture parameters remain insufficiently explored, creating critical
018
               ambiguities in the search process. To address this gap, we conduct a rigorous
               theoretical analysis demonstrating that the change rate of architecture parame-
019
               ters reflects the sensitivity of the supernet’s validation loss in architecture space,
020
               thereby influencing the derived architecture’s performance by shaping supernet
021            training dynamics. Building on these insights, we introduce the concept of a Sta-
022            ble Equilibrium State to capture the stability of the bi-level optimization process
023            and propose the Equilibrium Influential (EI ) metric to assess operation impor-
024            tance. By integrating these elements, we propose BOSE-NAS, a differentiable
025            NAS approach that leverages the Stable Equilibrium State to identify the optimal
026            state during the search process and derives the final architecture using the EI met-
027            ric. Extensive experiments across diverse datasets and search spaces demonstrate
028            that BOSE-NAS achieves competitive test accuracy compared to state-of-the-art
029
               methods while significantly reducing search costs.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Designing network architectures specifically tailored for specific tasks remains a formidable chal-
      lenge. Recently, Neural Architecture Search (NAS) has become essential in automating the design
035
      of neural networks across various deep learning fields (Ren et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2021; Zhang et al.,
036
      2020; 2021a). However, early NAS methods were often computationally expensive (Zoph & Le,
037   2016; Real et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2020; Kandasamy et al., 2018), limiting their practical applica-
038   tion. To achieve greater efficiency, recent advancements have adopted the one-shot paradigm, also
039   known as weight-sharing (Pham et al., 2018; Bender et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2018b; Guo et al., 2020;
040   Xie et al., 2019; Cai et al., 2018). A significant development within this paradigm is DARTS (Liu
041   et al., 2018b), which integrates a continuous mixture of architectures transforming the architecture
042   search problem into a differentiable task of learning architecture parameters then selects the oper-
043   ations corresponding to the largest parameter values at the end of the training phase to construct
044   the final architecture. Despite its efficiency, DARTS has faced several challenges related to per-
045
      formance degradation issue (Liang et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2021; Zela et al., 2019; Zhang et al.,
      2022; Xue et al., 2022). Numerous studies (Ye et al., 2022; Chu et al., 2020b;a) have demonstrated
046
      that architecture parameters are negatively affected during supernet training, leading to various pro-
047
      posals for controlling or adjusting these parameters, which are fundamental to its selection rule,
048   to better reflect the true strength of operations. However, recent literature suggests that the limita-
049   tions in DARTS primarily stem from the inability of architecture parameters to accurately reflect
050   the true strength of the operations, prompting the introduction of alternative metrics (Wang et al.,
051   2021; Xiao et al., 2022; He et al., 2024). While these contributions have substantially alleviated the
052   performance collapse issue inherent in DARTS, few have focused on the actual role and impact of
053   architecture parameters within DARTS. This gap in understanding gives rise to critical ambiguities
      in the architecture search process: Are architecture parameters truly necessary for architecture se-

054
      lection in DARTS frameworks? What is their actual role and impact? How can we better utilize
055   these parameters to develop more effective differentiable NAS methodologies?
056
057   To address this gap, we empirically demonstrate that architecture parameters are indispensable for
058
      architecture selection in the DARTS framework. Through rigorous theoretical analysis, we reveal
      that the change rate of architecture parameters reflects the sensitivity of the supernet’s validation
059
      loss in architecture space, influencing the performance of the derived architecture by shaping the
060
      dynamics of supernet training. These insights help resolve critical ambiguities surrounding the
061   actual role and influence of architecture parameters in the DARTS framework in existing DARTS-
062   related research. Building on this foundation, we introduce the concept of the ‘Stable Equilibrium
063   State’, which offers essential insights into the validation loss trajectory across architecture spaces
064   and elucidates the stability of the supernet’s bi-level optimization process. We further investigate the
065   supernet training dynamics to elucidate the influence of operations on the Stable Equilibrium State,
066   subsequently leading to the proposal of a novel metric for evaluating operation importance, termed
067   Equilibrium Influential (EI ). Through theoretical validation, we demonstrate that EI reliably re-
068   flects the true significance of operations within the architecture. Integrating these elements, we intro-
069
      duce BOSE-NAS, a differentiable NAS method that utilizes the Stable Equilibrium State to identify
      the optimal state during the search process, subsequently deriving the final architecture based on
070
      the EI metric. Extensive experiments conducted on different datasets across various search spaces
071
      demonstrate its effectiveness and efficiency. In the DARTS search space, BOSE-NAS achieves an
072   impressive average test error of 2.49% and a best test error of 2.37% on the CIFAR-10 dataset. When
073   transferred to CIFAR-100 and ImageNet, BOSE-NAS attains an average test error of 16.23% and a
074   best test error of 16.08% on CIFAR-100, and a best test error of 24.1% on ImageNet. Remarkably,
075   our method accomplishes this with a mere 0.13 GPU-days of computational cost (equivalent to just
076   3 hours of search time on a single V100 GPU) for architecture search on CIFAR-10. This level of
077   efficiency outperforms DARTS by more than 3 times and surpasses DARTS-PT by nearly 6 times.
078   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
079
080          • We provide comprehensive empirical and theoretical analyses to elucidate the actual role
081            and impact of architecture parameters α within the DARTS framework, addressing a critical
082            gap in the existing literature.
083          • We introduce the concept of the Stable Equilibrium State, which offers essential insights
084            into the stability of the supernet’s bi-level optimization process. Additionally, we propose
085            Equilibrium Influential (EI ), a novel and robust metric for evaluating the importance of
086            operations.
087          • We present an innovative and effective differentiable NAS method, termed BOSE-NAS,
088            and demonstrate its superior performance and search efficiency through extensive experi-
089            mentation across a variety of datasets and search spaces.

091   2   R ELATED W ORKS
092
093   NAS-RL (Zoph & Le, 2016) and MetaQNN (Baker et al., 2022) are pioneering methods in the field
094   of neural architecture search (NAS). These studies employed reinforcement learning (RL) methods
095   to design neural architectures that achieved state-of-the-art classification accuracy on image classifi-
096   cation tasks, thereby demonstrating the feasibility of automated neural architecture design. Follow-
097   ing this, AmoebaNet (Real et al., 2019) further validated the concept by employing an evolutionary
098
      algorithm to achieve similar results. However, these methods required significant computational
      resources, often consuming hundreds of GPU days or more.
099
100   To mitigate the issue of high computational costs and expedite the neural architecture search process,
101   (Liu et al., 2018b) proposed the Differentiable Architecture Search (DARTS). DARTS is a widely
102   adopted one-shot method that facilitates the efficient exploration of architectures through gradi-
103   ent descent by employing continuous relaxation. It transforms discrete architecture selection into
104
      continuous parameters α, optimizes architecture parameters via gradient descent, and subsequently
      constructs the final architecture by selecting the operations associated with the highest parameter
105
      values.
106
107   Despite its efficiency, DARTS has encountered significant challenges related to performance degra-
      dation (Liang et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2021; Zela et al., 2019). Numerous studies have shown that


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      architecture parameters are negatively impacted during supernet training, leading to various propos-
109
      als aimed at controlling or adjusting these parameters to more accurately reflect the true strengths of
110   operations. For instance, RobustDARTS (Zela et al., 2019) demonstrates that low curvature (eigen-
111   values of the Hessian matrix of the validation loss) does not cause significant performance drops
112   and proposes an early stopping criterion by monitoring these eigenvalues. SDARTS (Chen & Hsieh,
113   2020) addresses inaccuracies in gradient computation of architecture parameters, which creates a
114   significant optimization gap and introduces a method to amend architecture gradients to reduce this
115   gap. Additionally, Yang et al. (Huang et al., 2020) proposed EnTranNAS, a heuristic method that
116   assesses validation loss in sub-networks by iteratively evaluating Engine-Cells and Transit-Cells,
117   where Engine-Cells are differentiable for architecture search and Transit-Cells facilitate the sub-
118
      graph transition. SGAS (Li et al., 2020) introduced a sequential greedy architecture search method
      incorporating heuristic criteria such as edge importance, selection certainty, and stability to mitigate
119
      the search-evaluation correlation issue. Finally, Huang et al. (Huang et al., 2020) suggest reducing
120
      the evaluation gap by introducing a collection of topological variables with a combinatorial proba-
121   bilistic distribution to explicitly model the desired topology.
122
123   Conversely, a substantial body of recent literature indicates that the limitations of DARTS primarily
124
      stem from the architecture parameters’ inability to accurately reflect the true strength of opera-
      tions, prompting the development of new metrics for evaluating operation significance. DARTS-
125
      PT (Wang et al., 2021) mathematically demonstrates the intrinsic phenomenon of skip connection
126
      dominance, leading to performance collapse, and introduces a perturbation-based architecture selec-
127   tion method where operation strength is gauged by its impact on supernet accuracy. EoiNAS (Zhou
128   et al., 2021) utilizes the ratio of training iterations to validation accuracy for selecting the final op-
129   erations. Shapley-NAS (Xiao et al., 2022) quantifies the marginal contribution of operations on
130   accuracy using Shapley values, approximated through Monte Carlo sampling. DARTS-IM (Zhang
131   et al., 2022) reveals that operation strength depends on both magnitude and second-order informa-
132   tion, and introduces Influential Magnitude, a new criterion that incorporates this information for
133   operation selection.
134   Unlike previous research, this paper focuses on investigating the actual role and impact of architec-
135   ture parameters within DARTS. By filling this significant gap, we aim to propose a more effective
136   differentiable NAS method.

180   3.2   T HE ROLE AND I MPACT OF A RCHITECTURE PARAMETER
181
182   Despite its efficiency, DARTS has faced several challenges related to performance degradation is-
183
      sues (Liang et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2021; Zela et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2022; Xue et al., 2022). To
      address these challenges, existing literature has predominantly focused on either adjusting architec-
184
      ture parameters to more accurately reflect the true strengths of operations (Ye et al., 2022; Chu et al.,
185
      2020b;a) or developing alternative metrics for evaluating operation significance (Wang et al., 2021;
186   Xiao et al., 2022; He et al., 2024). However, there has been limited investigation into the actual role
187   and impact of architecture parameters within the DARTS framework. This gap in understanding
188   introduces critical ambiguities in the architecture search process.
189
      To address this significant gap, we undertake comprehensive empirical and theoretical analyses.
190
      We contend that although architecture parameters may not directly represent operation significance,
191
      they significantly influence the architecture search process and thus affect the performance of the de-
192   rived architecture. To substantiate this claim, we conduct empirical analyses in the NAS-Bench-201
193   search space (Dong & Yang, 2020). We train two sets of supernets, with one set having architecture
194   parameter α fixed. Each set of supernets is trained on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 using three dif-
195   ferent seeds. We apply two well-known architecture selection methods, DARTS-PT and RMI-NAS,
196   to derive an architecture from the supernets every 10 epochs and record their stand-alone model
197   accuracy. The results, as illustrated in Figure 1, reveal a notable discrepancy in the test accuracies of
198   architectures derived from supernets with fixed versus unfixed α, indicating that α indeed impacts
199   performance, likely mediated through supernet training.

315   Integrating our findings, we introduce BOSE-NAS, a differentiable neural architecture search
316   method based on the Stable Equilibrium State of the bi-level optimization. BOSE-NAS utilizes
317   the Stable Equilibrium State to identify the optimal state of the search process, subsequently deriv-
318   ing the final architecture based on the EI metric. Our methodology is summarized in Algorithm
319   1.
320   Notably, in our approach, we track the Stable Equilibrium State from the beginning of supernet
321   training and stop training upon encountering the first minima, which implies that the supernet has
322   reached relative stability. While multiple local minima may exist with extended training as shown
323   in Figure 2, we prioritize the supernet at the first one to derive the final architecture. The ratio-
      nale behind this approach is that ignoring the first local minimum in favor of subsequent ones or

      4.2   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
348
349   Our experimental setup for search and evaluation follows standard research practices. In the DARTS
350   and S1-S4 search spaces, the supernet comprises 6 normal cells and 2 reduction cells, with each
351   cell containing 6 nodes. In the NAS-Bench-201 search space, the supernet consists of 15 normal
352   cells, with each cell containing 4 nodes. During super-net training, we set the number of epochs to
353   50 for the DARTS search space, 100 for the NAS-Bench-201 search space, and 20 for the S1-S4
354   search space, Use SGD with an initial learning rate of 0.025, momentum of 0.9, batch size of 64,
355   and weight decay of 3 × 10−4 to optimize the supernet weights. During the architecture retraining
356
      phase, an architecture with 18 normal cells and 2 reduction cells is retrained from scratch on CIFAR-
      10/100. The architecture is optimized by the SGD optimizer with an initial learning rate of 0.025,
357
      momentum of 0.9, drop path rate of 0.2, weight decay of 3 × 10−4 , and gradient clipping at 5 for
358
      600 epochs. An architecture with 12 normal cells and 2 reduction cells is retrained from scratch on
359   ImageNet. The architecture is optimized by the SGD optimizer with an initial learning rate of 0.4,
360   momentum of 0.9, drop path rate of 0.2, weight decay of 3 × 10−5 , and gradient clipping at 5 for
361   250 epochs. The hyperparameter ∆t determines the smoothness of the second-order differential of
362   the architecture parameters and the absolute value of the proposed operation importance metric in
363   our method. However, it does not change the trajectory or the relative importance of the operations.
364

477
      derived from the first minima outperformed those derived later. Therefore, we consider it reasonable

      6   C ONCLUSION
501
502
      This paper addresses a critical gap in existing DARTS-related research by investigating the actual
503
      role and impact of architecture parameters in the DARTS and proposing a more effective differen-
504
      tiable NAS method. We empirically demonstrate that architecture parameters are indispensable for
505   architecture selection in the DARTS framework. Through rigorous theoretical analysis, we uncover
506   their true significance, resolving longstanding ambiguities in the interpretation of architecture pa-
507   rameters in prior research. Building on these insights, we introduce the concept of the ‘Stable Equi-
508   librium State’, which provides crucial insights into the validation loss trajectory across architecture
509   spaces. Further exploration of supernet training dynamics reveals the influence of operations on
510   the Stable Equilibrium State during training, leading us to propose a novel metric, the Equilibrium
511   Influential (EI ) metric, to quantify the significance of operations. By integrating these elements,
512   we introduce BOSE-NAS, a novel differentiable NAS method that utilizes the Stable Equilibrium
513
      State to identify the optimal state of the search process and subsequently derives the final architec-
      ture based on the EI metric. The effectiveness of BOSE-NAS is demonstrated through significant
514
      performance improvements across various datasets and configurations. Our study focuses on ad-
515
      dressing the critical ambiguities surrounding architecture parameters within the DARTS framework,
516   enhancing theoretical understanding and laying a robust foundation for developing more effective
517   and versatile differentiable NAS methodologies. These advancements have the potential to extend
518   beyond BOSE-NAS, contributing to the broader evolution of NAS research.

      A.4    P ERFORMANCE EVALUATION IN TRANSFORMER - BASED SEARCH SPACE
789
790   To verify the generalization and robustness of BOSE-NAS, we applied it to optimize the fine-tuning
791   process of ALBERT (Lan et al., 2019), a large pre-trained Transformer-based model. Fine-tuning
792   large pre-trained models is critical for transfer learning in various scenarios. However, this approach
793   often suffers from parameter inefficiency when addressing multiple downstream tasks, as each task
794   requires a separate model. Adapter (Houlsby et al., 2019) modules offer a more efficient alterna-
795   tive, introducing a small number of trainable parameters for each task while preserving scalability.
796   The architecture of the adapter significantly impacts both performance and parameter efficiency.
797   However, manually selecting the optimal architecture is resource intensive and often suboptimal.
798   To address this, we utilize BOSE-NAS to automate the search for adapter architectures, balancing
799   accuracy and computational efficiency. The search space is defined as: {Identity Mapping, Self-
800   Attention Layer, 1D-Convolutional Layer (Conv1 × 1), Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP)}
801
      The experimental results, summarized in Table 7, demonstrate that BOSE-NAS efficiently identi-
802   fied the optimal adapter architecture, achieving greater precision with fewer fine-tuned parameters
803   compared to traditional full fine-tuning approaches. These findings highlight the effectiveness of
804   BOSE-NAS in balancing performance and efficiency, making it a valuable tool for improving fine-
805   tuning processes in Transformer-based models.

887   A.6   C OMPARISON WITH DARTS+, DARTS- AND β-DARTS
888
889   DARTS+ (Liang et al., 2019) attributes the collapse issue to overfitting during the optimization pro-
890   cess in DARTS. To address this, it introduces two early stopping criteria: one that halts the search
891
      when the ranking of architecture parameters for learnable operations stabilizes over a specified num-
      ber of epochs and another that stops the process when two or more skip connections appear in a
892
      normal cell. Rather than relying on a heuristic early stopping mechanism, we introduce the concept
893
      of a Stable Equilibrium State, grounded in rigorous theoretical analysis, to represent the stability
894   of supernet training. By tracking this state throughout the training process, we determine the op-
895   timal point to stop training and begin architecture derivation upon encountering the first minima.
896   Importantly, in our approach, this minima could occur at any stage of the training process.
897
      To further illustrate the differences between our method and DARTS+, we conducted an empiri-
898
      cal analysis using the NAS-Bench-201 and DARTS search spaces, with results shown in Table 10
899
      and Table 11. In NAS-Bench-201, BOSE-NAS outperforms DARTS+ by a significant margin. In
900   the DARTS search space, BOSE-NAS achieves an average test accuracy of 97.51% on CIFAR-
901   10, slightly surpassing DARTS+, while being more than three times as efficient. On CIFAR-100,
902   BOSE-NAS attains an average test accuracy of 83.77%, which also outperforms DARTS+. These
903   experiments demonstrate that our method offers superior accuracy, search efficiency, and generaliz-
904   ability compared to DARTS+, thereby highlighting the advantages of our approach.
905
      In addition, previous studies, such as DARTS- (Chu et al., 2020a) and β-DARTS (Ye et al., 2022)
906   have successfully controlled architecture parameters to achieve robust results. Specifically, DARTS-
907   introduces an auxiliary skip connection to ensure fair competition among operations, thereby con-
908   trolling the updates of architecture parameters. Similarly, β-DARTS employs β-Decay regulariza-
909   tion to maintain the stability and variance of activated parameters. In our approach, while we utilize
910   architecture parameters, we do not directly control them. Instead, we first theoretically demonstrate
911   that the change rate of architecture parameters signifies the sensitivity of the validation loss. Lever-
912   aging this insight, we develop a metric to track the supernet’s training trajectory. Furthermore, we
913   design new operation importance measurements based on the first and second-order differentials of
914
      the architecture parameters.
915   From another perspective, DARTS- and β-DARTS aim to enhance the correlation between architec-
916   ture parameters and the importance of operations by controlling or modifying the updates of alpha.
917   In contrast, our method maps alpha to a different space and devises a new metric, facilitating a more
      effective assessment of operation importance.
