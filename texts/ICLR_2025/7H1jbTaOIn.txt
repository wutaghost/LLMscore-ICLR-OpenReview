009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Advancements in large language models (LLMs) have shown their effectiveness in
012            multiple complicated natural language reasoning tasks. A key challenge remains
013            in adapting these models efficiently to new or unfamiliar tasks. In-context learn-
014            ing (ICL) provides a promising solution for few-shot adaptation by retrieving a
015            set of data points relevant to a query, called in-context examples (ICE), from a
016            training dataset and providing them during the inference as context. Most existing
017            studies utilize a centralized training dataset, yet many real-world datasets may be
018            distributed among multiple clients, and remote data retrieval can be associated with
019            costs. Especially when the client data are non-identical independent distributions
020
               (non-IID), retrieving from clients a proper set of ICEs needed for a test query
               presents critical challenges. In this paper, we first show that in this challenging
021
               setting, test queries will have different preferences among clients because of non-
022
               IIDness, and equal contribution often leads to suboptimal performance. We then
023            introduce a novel approach to tackle the distributed non-IID ICL problem when a
024            data usage budget is present. The principle is that each client’s proper contribution
025            (budget) should be designed according to the preference of each query for that
026            client. Our approach uses a data-driven manner to allocate a budget for each client,
027            tailored to each test query. Through extensive empirical studies on diverse datasets,
028            our framework demonstrates superior performance relative to competing baselines.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Recent significant progress in large language models (LLMs) (Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al.,
034
      2023a;b; Team et al., 2023) has demonstrated their effectiveness across various natural language
035
      processing (NLP) tasks (Wang et al., 2018; 2019). Despite their impressive performances, they still
036   require adaptation to the specific downstream tasks for better performance. However, adaptation
037   poses challenges due to LLMs’ vast number of trainable parameters.
038
039
      In-context learning (ICL) (Dong et al., 2022) is a notable method that distinguishes itself through
      both its effectiveness and efficiency. In brief, ICL adapts to the target task by incorporating context
040
      information following two primary steps: i) identify samples from the training dataset helpful to
041
      solve the target query by creating a prompt describing a context; ii) feed the constructed prompt
042   with the target query and get the answer. Previous related works on ICL mainly have focused on the
043   construction of a prompt describing the context, which involves several sub-problems, such as the
044   retrieval of in-context examples (ICEs) (Robertson et al., 2009) and determining the optimal sequence
045   for the selected ICEs (Zhang et al., 2024).
046
      A common assumption in most existing ICL research is that the system has access to a high-
047
      quality centralized dataset used for retrieval. However, in many application scenarios, such as
048   health informatics, centralized datasets may not be feasible, and data could be distributed in different
049   institutions, which calls for the distributed ICL. In addition, when the data is proprietary and possesses
050   high value towards inferences, access to data entries may also be bound to data pricing strategies (Xu
051   et al., 2023; Cong et al., 2022). For instance, the system needs to pay the local institution based on the
052   number of samples sent to the system to share profits from inferences (Tang et al., 2020). Under this
053   scenario, aggregating ICEs from local clients to a center server for ICL entails significant financial
      costs and lacks efficiency.

081
      In this paper, we focus on integrating knowledge from distributed clients to achieve better ICL
082
      performance under the per-query ICE budget constraint. Specifically, we formalize the distributed
083
      ICL problem where the ICEs are distributed on clients, and the server has an LLM for ICL inference
084   but can only request a limited number of ICEs from all clients for each query, which we refer to as
085   the ICE budget.
086
087
      We begin by identifying the key challenge in distributed ICL with ICE budget constraints lies in the
      non-independently and identically distributed (non-IID) training data, as shown in Section 3.1. For
088
      example, in Figure 1, data samples are spread across C clients, each with a unique data distribution.
089
      Specifically, client 1 primarily contains (+) samples, while client 2 is mainly constituted by (−)
090   examples. Only limited research (Mohtashami et al., 2023) tried to address the challenge of distributed
091   datasets for ICL, while none considers the challenging real-world setting of non-IID clients. This
092   leaves a critical question unanswered: What happens to distributed ICL when local clients are
093   non-IID?
094
      To further the understanding of the key challenge in the distributed non-IID ICL, we explore
095
      the local retrieval process on non-IID clients. We found that each query has different preferences
096   for different clients based on local knowledge distribution, that is, the number of samples needed
097   from different clients should vary based on local sample distribution. As the toy example shown
098   in Figure 1, when the server creates context by uniformly assigning budgets to clients, the answer
099   might be incorrect due to the insufficiency of (+) information in the context. To be more detailed, the
100   server assigns the clients who have expertise on (−), (×), and (÷) operations with the same budget as
101   on (+), without any preference. Nevertheless, if the server assigns more budget to clients with many
102   (+) samples, such as client 1, it can create a more relevant context to answer the query related to (+)
103   operation. This indicates that under non-IID, the server should allocate the budgets over clients based
104
      on the preference of each query itself, as well as the distribution of local training samples.
105   Motivated by this, we propose a novel distributed ICL framework to collaboratively collect scattered
106   information among non-IID clients by properly assigning ICE budgets to each client. First, the server
107   will gather the optimal budget statistics using an existing proxy dataset on the server side. Next, the
      server will use this dataset to train the budget allocator. During the deployment stage, the server will

109   perform ICL among clients. Furthermore, in practical scenarios where privacy concerns arise, we

408   5.2     M AIN RESULTS
409
410   We have presented the performance of our algorithm and baselines in Table 1. First, we can observe
411   that performance varies significantly depending on the way the budget is allocated, which indicates
412   that the budget allocation scheme really matters in distributed non-IID ICL. Additionally, even when
413
      using only the proxy dataset, there is a performance improvement, and this performance surpasses
      that of using other clients which have the tilted local datasets (e.g., 29.19% → 40.64% in SST-5
414
      case). This indicates that utilizing a biased dataset can degrade the ICL performance. Although social
415
      learning algorithm has shown good performance in the previous paper, it does not perform well
416   under the non-IID cases configured in this research. If we can use an infinite budget, all settings
417   would exhibit high performance. However, our proposed algorithm demonstrates better performance
418   than the infinite budget upper limit (e.g., 34.86% → 35.48% in the Yelp case). This is likely due
419   to a mechanism that prevents unnecessary information from being selected by the retriever with
420   high importance. Ultimately, the proposed algorithm shows an average performance improvement of
421   5.05% across seven datasets compared to the best performance of baselines using the proxy dataset.
422   This shows that the proposed algorithm can handle the non-IID case well.

507   6   R ELATED W ORK
508
509   In-context learning. ICL (Dong et al., 2022) is one of the fastest paradigms using pre-trained LLMs
510   by feeding several examples to construct the context to solve the given query. The main criteria of
511   this research field are to find the most informative samples among the training datasets. For example,
512   Liu et al. (2021) trains BERT (Devlin et al., 2018) oriented encoder and uses the k nearest neighbors.
513   One of the reasonable sparse retriever, rule-based approaches is using BM25 (Robertson et al., 2009),
514
      which measures the term-frequency. Rubin et al. (2022) proposed an efficient retriever called EPR.
      It trains two encoders by inheriting the method of dense passage retriever (DPR) (Karpukhin et al.,
515
      2020) under the loss of positive and negative pairs. To reduce the domain specificity, Li et al. (2023)
516   proposed UDR, which is applicable to multiple domain tasks in a universal way and shows reasonable
517   performance from a single retriever. PromptPG (Lu et al., 2022) utilized a reinforcement learning
518   framework to train the retriever so that it can generate context to improve the answerability of LLMs.
519   Similarly, LLM-R (Wang et al., 2023) uses a reward model to train the retriever. Chang & Jia (2022)
520   trains linear regressors according to the example influence on the LLM prediction. Xie et al. (2021)
521   proposes to use implicit Bayesian inference to understand the ICL problem. Mavromatis et al. (2023)
522   proposes AdaICL to handle the efficient ICL with a limited annotation budget. Note that extensive
523   research focuses on the centralized case rather than targeting distributed cases.
524   Distributed ICL. To the best of our knowledge, only a single study (Mohtashami et al., 2023) tries
525   to address ICL in a distributed manner. However, this paper solely focuses on merging the distributed
526   information without considering the nature of the non-identically distributed information. Many
527   studies, such as those on federated learning (Li et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021; Mammen, 2021),
528   address the non-IID distribution of datasets, highlighting the need to handle distributed non-IID ICL.
529
530
      7   C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we tackle the challenge of ICL when datasets are distributed among clients with
533   non-IID. Initially, we examine if non-IID leads to performance degradation and discover that they
534   cause significant drops in performance. Inspired by the learnable pattern between budget values and
535   query embeddings, we propose an algorithm that learns the task of budget assignment and employs it
536   during inference to allocate appropriate budgets for each query. Using this proposed algorithm, we
537   achieve performance improvements across several benchmarks compared with various baselines. In
538   addition, we examine the privacy-preserving version of our method using paraphrasing technology
539   and show its effecacy. Last but not least, extensive sensitivity experiments show the robustness of our
      method on hyper-parameters and different LLMs.

      A    H OW WE CONSTRUCT NON -IID NESS
715
716
      Following Li et al. (2022), we use class number based non-IID partition in our experiment. For a
717   dataset with overall Γ classes, given hyperparameter class number γ on each client, we randomly
718   assign γ classes from the overall Γ classes for each client. Assuming that C1 ≤ C clients are assigned
719   with a specific class, we equally partition samples of this class into C1 parts and assign one part

       G       C ONCRETE E XAMPLE OF D ISTRIBUTED N ON -IID ICL S CENARIO
1135
1136
       A concrete example of distributed Non-IID ICL scenario can be the medical diagnosis task based
1137   on ICL cooperating with multiple medical institutions. Now, we have several medical institutions,
1138   with each institution owning some medical records (each sample consisting of the patient’s symp-
1139   toms description in text and the corresponding diagnosed disease, that is, the query x and label y).
1140   These medical institutions normally do not have enough local computation power to support LLM
1141   computation requiring large GPU resources, while they can do some small-cost local computation
1142   like retrieval processes to find similar queries. At the same time, there will be a platform operating
1143   like a server in this system, with enough computation resources to support LLM inference and in
1144   charge of cooperation management between these institutions. Once the system (including the server
1145
       platform and cooperating institutions) is deployed, the platform can provide consulting diagnosis
       services to other patients, doctors, or even other medical institutions based on pay-by-use knowledge
1146
       pricing strategies. That is, the price is decided by the number of samples involved in the whole
1147
       diagnosis procedure. Also, due to medical privacy concerns, the server platform can use local samples
1148   to perform inference while not allow caching these samples. Thus, these local retrieved samples
1149   cannot be cached to construct a retrieval pool on server platform. For the specific example of platform
1150   that supports LLM, OpenAI now provides ChatGPT Enterprise 3 , which allows the deployment
1151   requirement that the platform should not cache and utilize private data for further training.

       H       D ISCUSSION ON R ELATION WITH D ISTRIBUTED RAG
1154
1155
       Here, we discuss the differences between our approach and existing distributed RAG studies to
1156
       provide additional clarity and context for our contribution.
1157
1158   In developing this work, we carefully considered related studies in distributed RAG. However, the
1159   challenges addressed by existing distributed RAG works differ from those tackled in our paper. For
1160
       instance, Wang et al. (2024) focuses on the creation of datasets for distributed RAG frameworks
       and explores LLM-based labeling techniques for engineering pipelines. Their research scope and
1161
       methodology are distinct from ours and are not directly applicable to our specific problem setting.
1162
       Similarly, Li et al. (2024) addresses resource consumption and real-time response challenges in
1163   distributed RAG, emphasizing local retrieval efficiency and answer accuracy. However, it does not
1164   account for the non-IID property in distributed settings. Additionally, Li et al. (2024) permits LLM
1165   deployment on partial local institutions, which is fundamentally different from our setting.
1166
       Real-world distributed non-IID RAG scenarios present a more complex framework involving numer-
1167
       ous challenges that must be addressed for effective deployment. For example:
1168
1169             • How can we effectively decompose a user query into subqueries while considering local
1170               knowledge distribution?
1171
                 • What is the best way to assign these subqueries to clients with varying local expertise?
1172
1173
                 • How should we merge knowledge retrieved from multiple clients with overlapping expertise,
                   and should we assign confidence levels to different clients for the same subqueries?
1174
1175             • How can the local retrieval process be accelerated when dealing with large local databases?
1176
       These challenges represent broader avenues for exploration in distributed non-IID RAG. While our
1177
       current work cannot be directly compared with existing distributed RAG studies due to different
1178
       settings, we believe it offers an interesting starting point for addressing such challenges. Specifically,
1179   our approach focuses on how to enable cooperation among clients with varying distributions of
1180   knowledge. By assigning preferences to clients based on their local knowledge distributions and
1181   employing an MLP to learn these distributions without transmitting complete local knowledge to
1182   a central server, we offer an intuitive method that could inspire future advancements in distributed
1183   non-IID RAG.
