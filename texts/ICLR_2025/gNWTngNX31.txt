031                                                               A BSTRACT
032
033                We present TimeWalker, a novel framework that models realistic, full-scale 3D
034                head avatars of a person on lifelong scale. Unlike current human head avatar
035                pipelines that capture a person’s identity only at the momentary level (i.e., instant
036
                   photography, or short videos), TimeWalker constructs a person’s comprehensive
                   identity from unstructured data collection over his/her various life stages, offering
037                a paradigm to achieve full reconstruction and animation of that person at different
038                moments of life. At the heart of TimeWalker’s success is a novel neural parametric
039                model that learns personalized representation with the disentanglement of shape, ex-
040                pression, and appearance across ages. Central to our methodology are the concepts
041                of two aspects: (1) We track back to the principle of modeling a person’s identity
042
                   in an additive combination of his/her average head representation in the canonical
                   space, and moment-specific head attribute representations driven from a set of neu-
043                ral head basis. To learn the set of head basis that could represent the comprehensive
044                head variations of the target person in a compact manner, we propose a Dynamic
045                Neural Basis-Blending Module (Dynamo). It dynamically adjusts the number and
046                blend weights of neural head bases, according to both shared and specific traits
047                of the target person over ages. (2) We introduce Dynamic 2D Gaussian Splatting
                   (DNA-2DGS), an extension of Gaussian splatting representation, to model head
048
                   motion deformations like facial expressions without losing the realism of rendering
049                and reconstruction of full head. DNA-2DGS includes a set of controllable 2D
050                oriented planar Gaussian disks that utilize the priors from a parametric morphable
051                face model, and move/rotate with the change of expression. Through extensive
052                experimental evaluations, we show TimeWalker’s ability to reconstruct and animate
053                avatars across decoupled dimensions with realistic rendering effects, demonstrating
                   a way to achieve personalized “time traveling” in a breeze.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
055
056    “As we grow older, our life stories become our identity. This ongoing narrative of the self is
057   constructed from the past and anticipated future.” – James E. Birren, Psychologist, 1996.
058
      What forms a person’s identity? In the realm of Computer Graphics and Vision, researchers have
059
      traditionally considered an individual’s shape as invariant, assuming it to represent the person’s
060   identity. This assumption has driven significant advancements in human faces and head modeling
061   over the decades. From classic 3DMMs (3D morphable models) Blanz & Vetter (1999) and its follow-
062   up line of work Li et al. (2017); Paysan et al. (2009), to current advanced neural representations for
063   head modeling Yenamandra et al. (2021); Giebenhain et al. (2023); Qian et al. (2023); Hong et al.
064
      (2022), 3D head avatars become increasingly lifelike, serving as momentary replicas of humans.
065   However, sociology and psychology provide a different perspective to answer the question of identity
066   formation – the fields emphasis the idea that a person’s identity is shaped by a continuous process
067   influenced by various lifestages and experiences of self over time, rather than a single moment in
      time. Since the 1960s, sociologists and psychologists (e.g.,Waterman (1982); Kroger (2007)) have
068
      recognized the intrinsic value of lifelong construction of self-identity, and have developed several
069   cornerstone theories upon this basis.
070
071
      Motivated by the critical identity formation gap, in this work, we aim to explore a paradigm of
      modeling 3D head avatars on a person’s lifelong scale. This new problem definition introduces three
072   major challenges to head avatar modeling: (1) Long-horizon Identity Consistency Preserving.
073   The life-long modeling breaks the long-lasting assumption of the shape invariant of a person. As
074   the musculoskeletal structure changes with age growth, a person’s facial/head shape could change
075   significantly over his/her different lifestages. Besides, aesthetic transformation and unique life experi-
076   ences also stimulate significant physical changes like facial texture features, hairstyle, accessories,
      and motion behavior of a person, compounding the difficulties of learning effective representation to
077
      preserve identity. (2) Limited Data Quantity and Quality for Each Lifestage. In prior research,
078   specific momentary data is required with either “standard inputs” (i.e., high-quality front-view images
079   for 3D-aware head generation/editing Rai et al. (2024)), or sufficient geometric cues (i.e.,multi-view
080   capture systems Pan et al. (2024); Cheng et al. (2023); Kirschstein et al. (2023b); Wuu et al. (2022);
081   Yang et al. (2020); Yu et al. (2020), depth sensors Livingstone & Russo (2018); Cosker et al. (2011),
082   or short video sequences Zielonka et al. (2023); Gafni et al. (2021) for 3D/4D head avatar reconstruc-
      tion). In contrast, it is infeasible to capture lifelong data of a person with sufficient geometric cues,
083
      or a unified frontal camera view for each lifestage. Oftentimes, one’s lifetime is recorded through
084   unstructured image collection, with extremely uneven data volume and viewpoints over different
085   moments. Such data poses a significant challenge to high-fidelity 3D head avatar modeling in both
086   appearance-realistic and geometry-plausible aspects. (3) Explicit-controlled Animation in Full
087   Scales. Aside from lifelike reconstruction, a lifelong avatar is also expected to be controllable in
088
      terms of expression, shape, viewpoint, headpose, and appearance across a person’s different age
      periods. Fueled by the disentangled space of parametric morphable head models like FLAME Li et al.
089
      (2017) and the expressiveness of neural field representation Mildenhall et al. (2020); Müller et al.
090   (2022), previous arts of 3D head avatar animation Zheng et al. (2022a); Gafni et al. (2021); Zheng
091   et al. (2022b); Zielonka et al. (2023); Qian et al. (2023); Kabadayi et al. (2023) could support head
092   animation at different scales with vivid details. However, none of these methods could manipulate
093   moments of life. How to learn a personalized disentangled space from highly unstructured data, that
094
      enables full-scale control without losing realism is yet unknown.
095   We present TimeWalker – a baseline solution that tackles the above challenges for lifelong head
096   avatar modeling. To get invariant identity representation, and overcome the limited data issues, we
097   track back to the principle of the classic 3DMMs, where a specific 3D mesh can be approximated by
      a mean template with shape/expression’s main modes of variation in an additive combination manner.
098
      Analogously, TimeWalker models a person’s each lifestage by the additive combination of a shared
099   representation in canonical space, and a set of neural head basis. The former serves as the invariant,
100   i.e.,average head representation, of the person’s identity across different ages. The latter encodes the
101   main modes of variation of moment-specific head attributes via a Dynamic Neural Basis-Blending
102   Module (Dynamo). The personalized space parameterized by these two components could provide
103
      both geometric and appearance priors to life moments with rare data. To achieve full-scale control
      while ensuring realism, we introduce Dynamic 2D Gaussian Splatting (DNA-2DGS) upon the additive
104
      combination framework. Specifically, we tailor Gaussian splatting representation to store the color,
105   density, and correspondence of the heads via defining a set of Gaussian surfels in canonical space, and
106   deforming them subsequently. To make the surfels animatable, we utilize two guidances to deform
107   the surfels – the deformation fields driven from the neural head basis, and motion warping fields
      rooted from FLAME expression coefficients. In this way, the surfels could be animated properly

108
      without losing geometric realism caused by FLAME’s fixed topology, or explicit control problems
109   caused by implicit neural head basis representation. With the above designs, TimeWalker can learn
110   a well-disentangled personalized neural space (Fig. 1) only from one’s lifelong unstructured image
111   collection.
112   To enable modeling heads on the lifelong scale, a substantial dataset of the same individual at
113   different time points is necessary. However, this requirement is challenging to fulfill with current
114   open-source datasets since they neglect the lifelong human concept. Thus, we construct TimeWalker-
115   1.0, a large-scale head dataset comprising 40 celebrities’ lifelong image collection sourced from
116   various Internet data. It contains over 2.7 million individual frames, with each identity consisting
      of 15K − 260K frames and diverse age and head pose distributions. In experiments, we show the
117
      ability of TimeWalker to reconstruct and animate avatars across decoupled dimensions with realistic
118   rendering effects. Then, we demonstrate the superior rendering and geometry reconstruction quality
119   by comparing our models to state-of-the-art momentary 3D head models. We also demonstrate the
120   effectiveness of our designs with extensive ablation studies. Finally, we show the potential benefits of
121   our model to downstream applications in 3D Editing.

126   Personalized Head Avatars. 3DMM Blanz & Vetter (1999), as the foundational work in 3D human
127   head modeling, constructs a generic head space via the linear combination of a mean template mesh
128   and low-dimensional linear subspaces of shape and expression from PCA. Subsequent research
129   extends 3DMM to personalized head mesh modeling. For instance, Chaudhuri et al. (2020) predicts
130   personalized corrections on a 3DMM prior to obtain user-specific expression blendshapes and
      dynamic albedo maps. Zhu et al. (2023) learns personalized face details via multi-view image fusion
131
      from virtually rendered multi-view input images. Recent advancements have extended focus to
132   creating animatable personal head avatars with realistic rendering. Methods like NerFace Gafni et al.
133   (2021) and IM Avatar Zheng et al. (2022a) leverage FLAME Li et al. (2017) expression coefficients
134   to drive neural scene representation networks, implicitly representing head avatars from monocular
135   video inputs. INSTA Zielonka et al. (2023) enhances training speed and enables avatar control
136   through a mesh-based warping field. GaussianAvatars Qian et al. (2023) and FlashAvatar Xiang et al.
      (2024) associate Gaussian points with a 3D parametric model and generate personalized head avatars
137
      through expression parameters as the condition to Gaussian offset. By harnessing the formidable
138   generative prior, DreamBooth Ruiz et al. (2023) and Lora Hu et al. (2021) customize diffusion models
139   from multiple images of a particular subject to produce personalized outcomes. However, these
140   approaches are limited to static 2D results, lacking 3D consistency and animation capabilities. Other
141   works like DiffusionAvatars Kirschstein et al. (2023a) and GANAvatar Kabadayi et al. (2023) utilize
142
      generative models to create personalized head avatars. The former fine-tunes ControlNet Zhang et al.
      (2023a) with NPHM Giebenhain et al. (2023) features. The later distill EG3D Chan et al. (2022) to
143
      single appearance. While these pipelines excel in constructing personalized head avatars, they focus
144   on momentary representations, and none address the challenge of representing personalized spaces
145   over a lifelong scale. Our pipeline takes a step in this direction with a foundational solution, enabling
146   the construction of a lifelong replica. A comparison between TimeWalker and the representative

      Age Progressing Modeling. Research on simulating aging effects has been prominent in recent
189   decades. Methods like RFA Wang et al. (2016) and IAAP Kemelmacher-Shlizerman et al. (2014)
190   have led the way in creating average faces and transferring texture differences between age groups to
191   model aging. GAN-based approaches like S2GAN, and Face Aging GAN,He et al. (2019); Wang

193
      of shape and texture in age modeling, various techniques Lanitis et al. (2002); Suo et al. (2009;

194
      text-to-video pipelines such as DreamMachine, Kling, and Gen3 AI; Kli (2024); Gen (2024) have
195   showcased the ability to model age progression via hallucinating human-aging videos from textual
196   cues, yielding impressive outcomes. However, these generative methods struggle to achieve explicit
197   and comprehensive head animation (e.g., expressions and shape variations), and face challenges in
198   maintaining robust 3D consistency, limiting their functionality in creating personalized spaces.

200   3        T IME WALKER
201
202   Our work targets to comprehensively construct a 3D head avatar of a person on a lifelong scale, as
203   opposed to current trends Zielonka et al. (2023); Kabadayi et al. (2023); Qian et al. (2023); Zheng
204   et al. (2022a); Xiang et al. (2024); Zheng et al. (2022b) that reconstruct and animate a person at
      the momentary level. This new setting introduces further puzzles to head avatar modeling – how
205
      to faithfully capture both shared and specific traits of the target person over different ages, while
206   keeping the flexibility of animation in full scales (i.e., facial expression, face shape)?
207
208
      The primary challenge in constructing a lifelong head avatar lies in embedding the lifestage dimension
      during modeling. Changes to the head across different lifestages —such as variations in appearance,
209   facial shape, and even accessories—are difficult to explicitly define. Moreover, these variations
210   must be disentangled from other dimensions to allow for decoupled animation. This challenge is
211   compounded by the often limited quality and quantity of data available for each lifestage, further
212   complicating accurate modeling across a lifetime.
213   To address the challenges, we introduce a novel neural parametric model (see Fig. 2) that captures
214   an average representation of a person’s identity in canonical space, and extends to moment-specific
215   head attributes through a set of Neural Head Bases. In Sec 3.1, we first introduce a preliminary
      2D Gaussian representation Dai et al. (2024), which forms the foundational layer of our pipeline,

      ensuring high-fidelity rendering and dense meshing for the base frame, even with limited camera views

      3.2.2        N EURAL H EAD BASIS
285
286   With the assumption that any lifestage of one person can be linearly blended from his/her several
287   key characteristic variations across lifestages, we now introduce how to capture and learn these
288   variations within the linear neural feature space from the design of the Neural Head Basis, and how
289
      to obtain a compact set of the bases from our Dynamo module. Suppose the number of head basis
      is N , for a point xc picked from the canonical space, its feature at a specific lifestage could be
290                                                                        PN
      formed as a linear combination of N neural head basis H: f (xc ) = i=1 ωi Hi (xc ), where ωi is the
291
      learnable blending weight for each neural head basis. To store the features compactly, we utilize
292   multi-resolution Hashgrid Müller et al. (2022); Kirschstein et al. (2023b), a hashmap-based cubic
293   structure that enables the learnable features stored in a condensed form. When querying features
294   for xc , the hashgrid looks up nearby features at various scales J, and cubic linear interpolation
295   is applied to determine the final feature corresponding to the location. Thus, for each Hi , it is
296   constructed by Hi = C-LinearInterp ({ hij }Jj=1 ).
297   Dynamic Neural Basis-Blending Module (Dynamo). How to set the number of head basis? One
298   intuitive way would be – assigning a fixed amount that is equivalent to the number of appearances
299   in the character’s data, with each basis independently learning the character’s features for a specific
300   lifestage. For instance, to build a comprehensive personal space for Leonardo, encompassing ten
301
      distinct lifestages, we could initiate ten individual neural bases. Each of these bases is specifically
      tasked with mastering the intricacies of a single lifestage. However, this idea is inefficient and
302
      redundant in the feature space, as a person’s appearance evolves over their lifetime, their core
303   characteristics that define their identity remain fundamentally consistent. Even with temporary
304   alterations like heavy makeup or accessories, we can still recognize individuals by their underlying
305   features. Besides, we expect the feature space could be interpretable, scalable, and controllable. Thus,
306   our goal for the neural basis is to capture these deeper characteristics rather than solely memorizing
307
      superficial appearances. To this end, we introduce Dynamo to dynamically adjust the number of
      bases during the learning process of blending weight and hashgrids. We begin by initializing a set of
308
      learnable blending weights {ω}N    i=1 and grids H, which align with the number of lifestages of the
309
      target person. Throughout the learning process, if {Indicator(ωik < κ) = 1} consistently reveals
310   that a hashgrid’s weight falls below lower a preset threshold κ across modeling multiple lifestages k
311   at an iteration Q with sampled iteration interval q, this signals that this grid is not effectively learning
312   meaningful features. In response, we deactivate that hashgrid. By the end of the training process,
313   this pruning strategy ensures that each remaining Neural Head Basis is efficiently capturing core,
314   identity-defining features across lifestages. Additionally, it reduces memory requirements for storing
      these features, leading to a more efficient and scalable representation.
315

415   3.5       B UILDING A L IFE L ONG P ERSONALIZED S PACE
416
417   How does TimeWalker construct a lifelong personalized space? The Gaussian Surfels in canonical
418   space that characterize the individual average representation, are additively combined with moment-
419   specific head attribute representations driven from a set of Neural Head Basis to span a moment-
      specific head avatar. For each moment-specific head avatar, we further warp the Gaussian Surfels via
420
      expression or shape signals to get the motion-specific head performing. By separating the moment-
421   specific deformation from the motion-specific warping, we are able to decouple the driving of the
422   head in multiple dimensions - lifestage, expression, shape, novel view, and etc, - constructing a
423   comprehensive, steerable personalized space. Please refer to Sec. A.2 in Appendix for details.
424
      What are the core benefits of learned space? Despite being trained on a restricted5 dataset, our
425   model demonstrates: (1) the ability to generate a comprehensive personalized space with full-scale
426   animation (Fig. 1), steerable reenactment (Sec 4.1), and high-fidelity outcomes (Sec. A.2). (2)
427   In contrast to alternative generative or animatable avatar techniques, our approach offers superior
428   disentanglement of properties and ensures consistency across animated dimensions in terms of both
429   appearance (Sec. A.5.2) and geometry (Sec. A.5.3). (3) With faithful consistency, our method could
      potentially improve the quality of downstream tasks (Sec. A.6).

526   5 D ISCUSSION
527   Limitations. There are two main limitations of our pipeline. First, due to the underlying FLAME
528   base, our personalized avatar fails to capture exaggerated expressions and model unseen parts like
529   the tongue during the animation, resulting in noticeable artifacts around the mouth area (Fig. 6).
530   Second, due to data constraints, our study only provides a preliminary exploration toward lifelong
      perspective, rather than encompassing the entire life span from infancy to elderly. We aspire for our
531
      research to establish a baseline in this field, inspiring more effective approaches towards achieving
532   comprehensive lifelong personalized space construction. A promising future direction is to leverage
533   the prior knowledge of head structures from pre-trained generative models.
534
      Conclusion. In this work, we present TimeWalker, a baseline solution for constructing personalized
535   spaces that maintain long-term identity consistency while enabling explicit, full-scale animation
536   control. Rooted in the additive combination principles of classic 3DMM, our approach innovates
537   through a neural feature-based design with two key components: a Dynamic Neural Basis-Blending
538   model to represent the head variations in a compact manner and a Dynamic 2D Gaussian Splatting
539   module to construct dynamic dense head mesh. Our method makes it possible to create personalized
      spaces that evolve faithfully over a lifetime.

769   A.1    A DDITIONAL R ELATED W ORK
770
      Since our approach constructs the 3D personalized space from in-the-wild unstructured photos, we
771
      also discuss related developments in the research field of general 3D reconstruction from unstructured
772   photos.
773
      3D Reconstruction from Unstructured Photo. Reconstruction from Internet photo collections has
774
      been a long-standing topic in computer vision and computer graphics. Thought-provoking research
775   such as Photo Tourism Snavely et al. (2006), Skeletal Sets Snavely et al. (2008), and Building
776   Rome in a Day Agarwal et al. (2009) show great potential for applying structure from motion (SfM)
777   algorithms on unstructured photo collections. Upon these pioneer works, multi-view stereo (MVS)
778   algorithms Curless et al. (2010); Schönberger et al. (2016) and appearance modeling Kim et al. (2016)
779
      are proposed to improve the reconstruction quality. More recently, several works Martin-Brualla
      et al. (2021); Sun et al. (2022) model the scene by grafting these ideas into the neural radiance
780
      fields Mildenhall et al. (2020). There are also a series of great works focused on reconstructing heads
781   or heads from Internet photos. For example, in Kemelmacher-Shlizerman (2013), the author holds
782   the premise of deriving a 3D head shape basis directly from a large amount of Internet collection,
783   and proposes to reconstruct an arbitrary 3D head from a single view image based on the shape basis.
784   The subsequent work such as Kemelmacher-Shlizerman & Seitz (2011) focuses on recovering the
785
      head from personal photo collections, and Liang et al. (2016) aims to recover the personalized head.
      Still and all, not much attention has been paid to reconstructing human heads on a lifelong scale. The
786   most related work to our project is PersonNeRF ( Weng et al. (2023)), which is driven from NeRF-
787   W Martin-Brualla et al. (2021) to model personalize space for the human body across several years’
788   data. However, this method assumes a person’s body shape is roughly identical across years, which
789   limits its scalability to lifelong settings. Beyond that, human head modeling is more challenging than
790   body in terms of high-fidelity details and fine-grained capturing like subtle expression.
791
792   A.2    B UILDING A L IFE L ONG P ERSONALIZED S PACE
793
794   In Sec. 3.5 of the main paper, we introduce our approach for constructing a personalized space with
795   full-scale animation in a disentangled manner. In this section, we further elaborate on how we realize
796   the animation of each dimension within this personalized space.
797   Lifestage. During training, our pipeline learns different blending weights {ω}N  i=1 for data in different
798   lifestages. After training, we can adjust these weights to drive the lifestage in a disentangled manner.
799   Fig. 10 illustrates the appearance diversity of individuals as they progress through different lifestages.
800   This demonstrates the effectiveness of our pipeline in capturing a person’s identity across different
801
      moments in their life.
802   Expression. To achieve expression and shape changes of the character while maintaining a consistent
803   appearance, we use a motion warping field inspired by INSTA Zielonka et al. (2023). By manipulating
804
      expression parameters, we can update both the tracked mesh and the transformation matrix that maps
      from canonical space to deformation space. This enables us to achieve the desired expression-based
805
      warping.
806
807   Shape. As the FLAME mesh can be driven by expression and shape parameters in a disentangled
      manner, our head avatar can also be animated by shape with the same approach as expression.
808
809   Novel view. The Gaussian Splatting, as a type of 3D representation, can be rendered with arbitrary
      camera pose.

      A.3     I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
811
812   We apply the end-to-end training manner with Gaussian Surfels as our basis representation. Instead
813   of SfM Schönberger & Frahm (2016) based initializing in 3DGS Kerbl et al. (2023) and Gaussian
814   Surfels Dai et al. (2024), we leverage the FLAME template and initialize the Gaussian kernel on
815
      its surface, with one Gaussian kernel on the center of each triangle face. The initialization of other
      attributes follows the original 3DGS implementation. All the components, including Neural Head
816   Basis, Residual embedding, and deformation networks, join to start the end-to-end formal training for
817   30000 iterations. Please refer to Tab. 3 for detailed hyperparameters of TimeWalker setting during
818   the training process. We follow Wu et al. (2023a) and apply the same initial learning rate and rate
819   scheduler for all network components and Gaussian attributes. We use a single NVIDIA A100 GPU
820   to train the model, and it costs 3 ∼ 4 hours on average for the whole training process.
821   Warm Up. Prior to formal training, we incorporate a warm-up phase wherein we utilize a person’s
822   data across all his/her lifestages to individually optimize this set of Gaussian kernels, without including
823   the Neural Head Basis module to learn moment-specific features. In this way, the Gaussian kernels
824
      in canonical space are solely optimized to accommodate multiple lifestages, approaching a mean
      representation. This approach offers the benefit of enabling the Gaussian kernels to promptly learn
825   the mean head of a person in long-horizon time periods, thereby significantly expediting subsequent
826   training convergence. Following the warm-up phase, we commence optimizing the neural head base
827   while simultaneously fine-tuning the Gaussian Surfels to enhance the mean head representation. This
828   optimization process ensures that both the neural head base and the Gaussian Surfels continually
829   improve and refine the overall representation. The warmup phase lasts for 5000 iterations before the
830
      formal training, with applying all loss items except deform regulation.

1009   and also neural rendering methods, extending from Tab. 1. INSTA Zielonka et al. (2023) stands out for

       in canonical space to store learned features. For ray points in deformed space, INSTA identifies the
1011   nearest triangles of the FLAME tracked mesh and computes the transformation matrix between the
1012   tracked mesh and the template mesh. Subsequently, the points are warped back to canonical space,
1013   enabling the animation of the avatar using FLAME expression parameters by updating the tracked

1019   points on a 2D UV texture map attached to the mesh surface. It employs a small MLP network
1020   conditioned on the Gaussian canonical position and expression code to learn the Gaussian offset,
1021
