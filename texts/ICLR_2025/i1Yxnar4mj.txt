011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Due to the structural limitations of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), particularly
014            those relying on conventional neighborhoods, alternative aggregation strategies
015            have been explored to enhance GNN expressive power. This paper proposes a
016            novel approach by generalizing the concept of neighborhoods through algebraic
017            covers to overcome these limitations. We introduce the Grothendieck Graph Neu-
018            ral Networks (GGNN) framework, providing an algebraic platform for system-
019
               atically defining and refining diverse covers for graphs. The GGNN framework
               translates these covers into matrix representations, extending the scope of design-
020
               ing GNN models by incorporating desired message-passing strategies. Based on
021
               the GGNN framework, we propose Sieve Neural Networks (SNN), a new GNN
022            model that leverages the notion of sieves from category theory. SNN demonstrates
023            outstanding performance in experiments, particularly in differentiating between
024            strongly regular graphs, and exemplifies the versatility of GGNN in generating
025            novel architectures.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Where is the birthplace of the concept of neighborhood for nodes? Does this birthplace have the po-
032   tential to generate other concepts as alternatives to neighborhoods to improve the expressive power
033
      of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs)? Due to their inherited reasons, most of existing GNN methods
      currently rely on neighborhoods as the foundation for message passing Gilmer et al. (2017). Several
034
      reasons support this preference. First, neighborhoods provide comprehensive coverage of graphs,
035
      encompassing all edges and directions, ensuring the entire graph participates in the message-passing
036   process. Second, working with neighborhoods is straightforward, facilitated by the adjacency ma-
037   trix. However, the localized perspective obtained from neighborhoods may result in shortcomings
038   in GNN methods, such as their limited expressive power, which is at most equivalent to that of the
039   Weisfeiler-Lehman (WL) test Sato (2020), Xu et al. (2019).
040
      Extending the concept of neighborhoods or finding alternatives has been proposed as a way to ad-
041
      dress these limitations. In this regard, the topological characteristic of graphs has motivated the
042   use of algebraic topology concepts. These concepts enable the examination of graphs from various
043   perspectives, such as dimensions, faces, and boundaries, to capture higher-order interactions Bod-
044   nar et al. (2021b), Bodnar et al. (2021a). Furthermore, analyzing specific patterns and subgraphs
045   provides the means for recognizing substructures as alternatives to neighborhoods Bouritsas et al.
046   (2023), Ai et al. (2022). However, since neighborhoods are derived from a precise definition rather
047   than a specific pattern, they cannot be represented effectively by the patterns.
048   We advocate that the algebraic viewpoint aligns more closely with the inherent nature of neighbor-
049   hoods than the patterns. In other words, neighborhoods, emerging from the connections between
050   edges, can be conceptualized as outcomes of an algebraic operation on edges. In this paper, we
051   aim to algebraically extend the concept of neighborhoods in a way that not only enhances efficiency
052   compared to neighborhood but also maintains simplicity of use. To this end, we explore the close
053   relationship between category theory MacLane (1978) and graphs. We also observe that methods
      used to construct covers in category theory can serve as a schema for similar developments in graph


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      theory, where the concept of cover becomes meaningful with Grothendieck topologies MacLane &
055   Moerdijk (1994).
056
057   Our contributions in this paper can be summarized as follows. First, we introduce the Grothendieck
058
      Graph Neural Networks (GGNN) framework, based on our interpretation of the Grothendieck topol-
      ogy, to establish the context for defining the concept of covers for graphs, and then transforming
059
      them into matrix forms for the message-passing process. The concept of covers in the GGNN
060
      framework differs from the traditional view of graphs based on neighborhoods, enabling alternative
061   perspectives of the graph. In our proposed GGNN framework, a monoid Mod(G), generated by di-
062   rected subgraphs, is introduced as the birthplace for the concept of neighborhoods, providing us the
063   ability to generate various algebraic covers as alternatives to traditional neighborhood covers. Based
064   on the proposed GGNN framework, we design a novel GNN model called Sieve Neural Networks
065   (SNN), in which a graph G is covered by a collection of elements from Mod(G), analogous to sieves
066   in category theory MacLane & Moerdijk (1994).

      In this section, we will move step by step to give meaning to the concept of cover for graphs and
071   interpret them as matrices. With this, the necessary materials will be in hand to introduce the GGNN
072   framework. By defining the matrix representation for a directed subgraph, we will provide a one-
073   to-one correspondence between them, turning it into a monoidal homomorphism by introducing
074   monoids generated by directed subgraphs and matrix representations. It will be proved that this
075   monoidal homomorphism is invariant up to isomorphism and gives an algebraic description of a
076   graph that will be the basis of our framework.

080   This paper deals with undirected graphs; every graph has a fixed order on its set of nodes. We start
081   by defining directed subgraphs and their matrix representations. Let G = (V, E) be an undirected
082

083   Definition 2.1.1.          (1) A path p from node vp1 to node vpm is an ordered sequence
084            vp1 , ep1 , vp2 , ep2 , · · · , vpm−1 , epm−1 , vpm , where epi represents an edge connecting nodes
085            vpi and vpi+1 .
086

087             of D has a direction.

089   A neighborhood is essentially a directed subgraph formed by combining directed edges leading to a
090   specific node, see Figure 4. Using the adjacency matrix, we can represent each neighborhood with a
091   matrix. In this representation, each column of the adjacency matrix corresponds to the neighborhood
092   of the respective node. To isolate the representation of that specific neighborhood, we set the rest of
093   the columns to zero. In the following definition, we expand this matrix representation to encompass

      Proposition 2.1.1. The relation ≤D is transitive.

389

391   CoSieve(vi , l) • Sieve(vj , k) in the model’s output. Omitting this step results in denoting the model
392   as SNNo (α, (l, k)).

448
449   According to Equation 3, Image(v, k) can be computed by executing k iterations matrix multiplica-
450   tion and summation. Consequently, the time complexity of it for all nodes is O(kn4 + kn3 ) where
451   n is the number of nodes. Assume l ≤ k. Equation 3 implies:
452
                               Image(v, k) = Tr(Dk (v)) ◦ · · · ◦ Tr(Dl+1 (v)) ◦ Image(v, l)
453
454   Therefore in the process of computing Image(v, k), we simultaneously obtain Image(v, l) for all
455   l ≤ k. As previously mentioned, CoImage(v, l) is the transpose of Image(v, l). Consequently,
456   in computation of SNN(α, (l, k)), the time complexity of all Image(v, k) and CoImage(v, l) is
457   O(kn4 + kn3 + n3 ). The operations ◦ between Images and CoImages contributes n4 + n3 to the
458   complexity, resulting in O((k + 1)n4 + kn3 + 2n3 ). Then the time complexity of SNN(α, (l, k)) is
459   O(n4 ). For version β of the model, set l0 = M ax(l1 , · · · , lt ). The time complexity of Image(v, l0 )
460   for all nodes is O(l0 n4 +l0 n3 ). Similar to version α, computing CoImages adds (t/2)n3 to the com-
461   plexity. Additionally computing Sui s and Su1 ◦ Su2 ◦ · · · ◦ Sut adds 2tn3 + tn2 to the complexity,
462
      resulting in O(l0 n4 + (l0 + (5/2)t)n3 + tn2 ) for time complexity. Therefore, the time complexity
      of SNN(β, (l1 , · · · , lt )) is O(n4 ). If the adjacency matrix is sparse, both cases can be reduced to
463
      O(|E| · |V |2 ) by leveraging sparse matrix operations.

505   4   R ELATED W ORK
506
507   The research on improving Message Passing Neural Networks (MPNNs) in Graph Neural Networks
508   (GNNs) focuses on enhancing the neighborhood-based message-passing process. Various methods
509
      aim to either transform the graph representation or augment node and substructure information to
      increase MPNN expressiveness. Gilmer et al. (2017) proposes that classical GNN methods can be
510
      unified under MPNNs. Many follow-up works aim to expand beyond simple neighborhood-based
511
      interactions. In Gasteiger et al. (2021), Directed Line Graphs (DirMPNN) replace the original graph
512   with a directed line graph where nodes represent directed edges, enhancing message-passing accu-
513   racy. Ai et al. (2022) introduces Topology-aware GNNs (TLGNN), which use an additional visual-
514   ization graph to capture structural features, enabling more informed message passing. In Bouritsas
515   et al. (2023), Graph Substructure Networks (GSNs) analyze specific graph patterns to add structure-
516   based features, while Feng et al. (2022a) introduces KerGNNs that use graph filters, inspired by
517   convolutional neural networks, to capture local subgraphs for more precise node feature updates.
518   Methods like You et al. (2021) and Feng et al. (2022b) focus on improving node representations by
519   considering extended neighborhoods and ego networks, with the latter introducing new kernel-based
520
      methods for K-hop neighbor aggregation. Vignac et al. (2020) enhances node features by incorpo-
      rating local context matrices that reflect a node’s surrounding topology, improving tasks like cycle
521
      detection. The method in Papp et al. (2021) introduces random node removal with low probability,
522
      running MPNNs on slightly altered graphs to propagate results and preserve graph topology. These
523   methods demonstrate varied strategies for making MPNNs more expressive and capable of capturing
524   complex graph topologies.

      based on the desired cover. An algebraic platform for transforming the covers into collections of
531   matrices adds to the simplicity of the framework’s designed models. Also, based on this framework,

533   good results in experiments. Looking ahead, our future work aims to delve deeper into the power
534   and potential applications of the GGNN framework. We plan to conduct a more comprehensive
535   theoretical comparison between SNN and the Weisfeiler-Lehman test.

917
      The process of designing a GNN model within this framework is outlined as follows:


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
           1) For a given graph G, the process involves selecting a collection CG of elements from
919           Mod(G) to serve as a cover for G. These elements can be generated using DirSub(G)
920           and the binary operation •. Notably, Theorem 2.2.2 ensures the ability to create any suit-
921           able and desired elements by leveraging directed edges and the operator •.
922
           2) Next, the chosen cover is transformed into a collection of matrices within Mom(G). During
923           this transformation, the operation ◦ and other elements of Mom(G) can be employed to
924           convert the original collection into a new one. The resulting output at this stage is denoted
925           by AG .
926
           3) By utilizing ι, the collection obtained in the second stage transitions into a larger and more
927           equipped space, a suitable environment for enrichment. This stage leverages all the opera-
928           tions outlined in Proposition 2.4.1 to complete the model’s design. Following the process-
929           ing of AG in this stage, we obtain a new collection of matrices denoted by MG , represent-
930           ing the model’s output.
931
932   Hence, a model is a mapping that associates a collection of matrices MG with a given graph G.
933   MG plays a role akin to the adjacency matrix and provides an interpretation of the chosen cover
934
      for use in various forms of message passing. While the second and third stages can be merged, we
      prefer to emphasize the significance of Tr in this process.
935

937   tion, we need an invariant construction. Based on Theorem 2.4.1, a graph isomorphism f : G → H
                                                          ′

939   ferent from (CH , AH , MH ). So a model constructed in the GGNN framework is invariant if for
940
      every graph isomorphism f : G → H, the maps Mod(f ), Mom(f ) and CO(f ) induce one-to-one
