009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The next frontier of online advertising is revenue generation from LLM-generated
012            content. We consider a setting where advertisers aim to influence the responses of
013            an LLM to align with their interests, while platforms seek to maximize advertiser
014            value and ensure user satisfaction. The challenge is that advertisers’ preferences
015            generally conflict with those of the user, and advertisers may misreport their
016            preferences. To address this, we introduce MOSAIC, an auction mechanism that
017
               ensures that truthful reporting is a dominant strategy for advertisers and that aligns
               the utility of each advertiser with their contribution to social welfare. Importantly,
018
               the mechanism operates without LLM fine-tuning or access to model weights and
019
               provably converges to the output of the optimally fine-tuned LLM as computational
020            resources increase. Additionally, it can incorporate contextual information about
021            advertisers, which significantly improves social welfare. Through experiments with
022            a publicly available LLM, we show that MOSAIC leads to high advertiser value
023            and platform revenue with low computational overhead. While our motivating
024            application is online advertising, our mechanism can be applied in any setting with
025            monetary transfers, making it a general-purpose solution for truthfully aggregating
026            the preferences of self-interested agents over LLM-generated replies.

      1     I NTRODUCTION
030
      Large language models (LLMs) are becoming ubiquitous – as coding assistants, as chat interfaces,
031
      and even as alternatives to search engines (Bommasani et al., 2022). To ensure their usefulness, it
032   is essential to closely align LLM outputs with user preferences. However, in general, there may be
033   multiple interested parties who disagree on the desired behavior of the same LLM. This leads to the
034   question of how to generate LLM replies given multiple conflicting preferences.
035
      The motivating application for our work is online advertising, which is the main source of revenue for
036
      large tech companies such as Google, Meta, and Twitter. For example, in 2023, Meta’s advertising
037
      revenue of 132 billion USD was more than 97% of its total revenue (Meta, 2024). Ad auctions are
038   the workhorse mechanism to determine the placement and prices of commercial content (Varian,
039   2007; Edelman et al., 2007). As platforms are now working on integrating advertisements into
040   LLM-generated content (Criddle, 2024), new auction mechanisms are needed for this application.
041
      We present a new auction mechanism for this problem, which we call MOSAIC (Mechanism for
042
      Optimally Sampling and Aggregating LLM Outputs with Incentive Compatibility). The advertisers
043
      are paying, not for some particular item or bundle, but rather to influence the output generated by an
044   LLM in a direction closer to their own preferences. Although our mechanism could be of interest
045   whenever one has to aggregate the preferences of multiple self-interested agents over LLM behavior
046   (as long as it is reasonable to charge monetary payments), we see online advertising as the most
047   natural setting of interest. For this reason, we refer to participants in the mechanism as advertisers.
048
049   1.1   P ROBLEM SETTING
050
051   Consider a situation where a user queries an LLM with a specific question. We assume the following:
052
             • There is a reference LLM that can produce useful replies to the user’s query.
053
             • There are advertisers, each with their own LLM, who want to influence the reply to the user.

      In our model, each advertiser is represented via her own LLM or directly with a reward function,
055   similar to the function used to fine-tune LLMs in the Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback
056   pipeline (Azar et al., 2023). For this reason, we refer to an advertiser’s value for a reply as her reward.
057   The auctioneer’s task is, given the user’s query and the advertisers’ preferences, to produce a reply
058   that is useful for the user while also generating high rewards for the advertisers.
059
060   Example. A user queries an LLM “What are effective ways to learn a musical instrument online?”
061   There are two advertisers: MusicMastery, which offers online music lessons, and InstaTune, which
062   sells musical instruments. MOSAIC merges the preferred replies from the advertisers’ LLMs and the
063   reference LLM to produce one combined reply to the user:

               Effective ways to learn a musical instrument online include:
065            1. Online music lessons: Platforms like MusicMastery offer one-on-one virtual
066            tutoring, group classes, and pre-recorded lessons.
067            2. Interactive learning tools: InstaTune offers a range of interactive learning tools,
068            such as apps, software, and games, that make learning fun and engaging.

070   1.2   OVERVIEW OF C ONTRIBUTIONS
071
072   In this paper, we present MOSAIC, an auction mechanism designed to aggregate the preferences of
073   multiple self-interested advertisers over LLM-generated replies. The first challenge we address is that
074
      MOSAIC must produce replies for which the advertisers receive high rewards, but without steering
      the LLM’s behavior too far from that of the user-centric reference LLM. We address this by drawing
075
      a strong connection to the RLHF objective (e.g., Ziegler et al. (2020)), equipping MOSAIC with a
076
      hyperparameter that enables the auctioneer to balance between the usefulness of the produced reply
077   to the user and the advertisers in an interpretable and principled way (Section 3.1).
078
079   Second, leveraging an importance sampling-based technique, MOSAIC is the only mechanism in the
080
      literature that converges to the optimal distribution as computational resources increase (Corollary 4.1).
      Third, MOSAIC can incorporate contextual information, similar to how sponsored search auctions
081
      utilize advertiser descriptions. This accelerates convergence and increases value for the advertisers
082
      and revenue for the auctioneer (Section 6.2.1).
083
084   Fourth, we address technical feasibility and practicality. We adopt the desiderata proposed by Duetting
085   et al. (2023), who argued that auctions must be compatible with existing LLM technology, only
086
      using “information obtainable from current models” in such a way that they are “easy to integrate
      into the system” and rely only on “easy manipulations of LLM output.” Furthermore, it must be
087
      computationally feasible to run the auction repeatedly and with different advertiser preferences. In
088
      particular, we cannot afford the expensive process of fine-tuning an LLM’s weights for each possible
089   query. To address this, we have designed MOSAIC to work by only post-processing multiple LLM
090   outputs – it requires only “API access”, not even viewing the model weights ( Section 4.2).
091
092
      Fifth, we address the fact that advertisers might have an interest in misreporting their preferences
      (which include their full LLMs), analogous to over-/underbidding in traditional auctions. To address
093
      this challenge, we employ an allocation rule that is compatible with Rochet payments (Rochet, 1987).
094
      These payments uniquely ensure that MOSAIC is strategyproof (i.e., that truthful reporting is a
095   dominant strategy for each advertiser, no matter what the others do (Theorem 5.1)). Crucially, Rochet
096   payments ensure that MOSAIC is strategyproof even if the allocation rule has not converged.
097
098
      Sixth, we introduce an offset to the Rochet payments, to ensure that each advertiser’s utility is
      proportional to her contribution to the social welfare. This alignment is important for the long-term
099
      success of the market, incentivizing only the most relevant advertisers to participate.
100
101   Finally, we discuss individual rationality (IR), which guarantees that a participant is weakly better off
102   from participating in the mechanism. In Section 5.2, we discuss the unique properties of our setting
103   that make ex-post IR unattainable. However, in Section 5.3.1, we show that MOSAIC is “almost
104
      ex-post IR” in a well-defined sense. In Section 6.2.1, we experimentally show that, for the distribution
      of instances tested, it is ex-ante IR (i.e., advertisers are in expectation better off by participating).
105
106   In Section 6, we provide experimental results for the online advertising domain. We demonstrate that
107   MOSAIC quickly converges to the optimal LLM with low computational cost, generating significant
      value for the advertisers and revenue for the auctioneer while also being useful to the user.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110   Duetting et al. (2023) introduced the field of mechanism design for LLMs. They proposed a sequential
111   mechanism, where the output sequence is generated on a token-by-token basis and the advertisers bid
112   for their LLM to generate the next token. However, their approach suffers from several limitations:
113   (i) Advertisers face the exposure problem: small changes in the generated sequence, such as the
114   addition of a word like "not," can completely alter its meaning. An advertiser might pay a significant
115   amount for the tokens generated up to a certain point, only for the continuation to unexpectedly negate
116   or distort her intended message.1 (ii) The mechanism is easily manipulable if the assumption that
117   advertisers cannot misreport their LLMs is dropped. (iii) For a given prompt, an advertiser’s spend
118
      grows with the length of the generated sequence. MOSAIC does not have any of these limitations.
119   Dubey et al. (2024) proposed a generalization of the position auction (Varian, 2007; Edelman et al.,
120   2007) to a setting where each advertiser is interested in having some specific text ad displayed
121   alongside the organic results. In their mechanism, an LLM module coupled with an auction module
122   work in tandem to merge the ads into a single ad summary in an incentive-compatible way.
123   Hajiaghayi et al. (2024) considered advertisers bidding in an auction to have their ads placed into
124   various portions of a retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) response. Their auction only allows
125   advertisers to report (and misreport) scalar values to influence the inclusion of non-misreportable
126   advertising texts in the combined output. This is in contrast to MOSAIC, which allows the aggregation
127   of full LLMs that advertisers may arbitrarily misreport.
128   Feizi et al. (2024) presented an abstract design for an LLM advertising system and detailed a number
129   of research challenges that would have to be overcome in the course of implementation. Conitzer
130   et al. (2024) drew connections between computational social choice and LLM alignment. Fish et al.
131   (2023) presented work in the opposite direction: how can LLMs be used to solve problems in social
132   choice? Harris et al. (2024) studied Bayesian persuasion in an abstract setting where a “simulator”
133   (for example, a realistic LLM) of the agent is available. Werner et al. (2024) gave preliminary
134   empirical evidence that LLM-generated content can influence consumers.

189   3.2     T HE SHORTCOMINGS OF VCG IN THIS SETTING
190
191
      At first sight, it may seem that the Vickrey–Clarke–Groves (VCG) mechanism would be suitable for
      our setting (Vickrey, 1961; Clarke, 1971; Groves, 1973). VCG selects the outcome that maximizes
192
      the sum of all agents’ values. This can be either the single optimal sequence in Equation (1) or the
193
      optimal distribution of Equation (2). The VCG mechanism has a corresponding payment rule to
194   incentivize truthful reporting: it charges each agent her externality, that is, the total reduction in value
195   (respectively expected value) for the other agents that her participation in the mechanism caused.
196
197
      However, in our setting, VCG is not a viable option: The VCG allocation rule requires calculating
      the exact optimal solution to the optimization problem, which is intractable for choosing an LLM to
198
      maximize Equation (1) and is even difficult for choosing a single optimal sequence. If a sub-optimal
199
      solution is chosen, VCG’s strategyproofness is no longer guaranteed (Nisan & Ronen, 2007; 1999;
200   Lehmann et al., 2002). Thus, VCG is not a suitable mechanism in our setting.

321   5.2   D IFFERENCES FROM AUCTION M ECHANISMS FOR I TEM A LLOCATION
322
323   Auction mechanisms designed to sell items or ad slots typically rely on simplifying assumptions that
      do not apply in a setting with LLM-generated content. Key differences include: (i) Non-Negative

324
      Values: These mechanisms assume agents’ values are non-negative due to having zero value for
325   the empty bundle and free disposal. In contrast, in our setting, an advertiser’s reward can be
326   negative based on the discrepancy between her LLM and the reference LLM. (ii) Advertiser-Specific
327   Allocations: These mechanisms allocate different item bundles to different agents. Here, a single
328   reply is produced, and advertisers’ rewards depend on that reply’s probability with respect to their
329   LLMs. (iii) Zero Utility for Non-Participation: In most auction settings, not participating yields
330   zero utility. Here, non-participation can result in negative utility since the produced reply may be
331   unfavorable to non-participating advertisers. For details, see Appendix B.2.

      two additional properties that are critical for the long-term success of a market for LLM aggregation.
339   The first is “Almost IR:” An advertiser with weakly positive reward for all candidate replies has
340   weakly positive expected utility for all reports by the other advertisers. In particular, an advertiser
341   with zero reward for all outputs is guaranteed zero utility. The second is “What you give is what you
342   get:” an advertiser’s ex-interim expected utility is monotone in how well-aligned her exponentiated
343   reward for the replies is with the interim allocation rule if she were to not participate.
344
345   5.3.1    O UR M ECHANISM IS “A LMOST I NDIVIDUALLY R ATIONAL”
346
      Individual rationality is important to incentivize agents to participate in the mechanism. In Ap-
347
      pendix B.3, we explain why the standard notion of individual rationality (i.e., weakly positive utility
348
      from participation in the mechanism) encountered in standard auction settings is impossible to achieve
349   in our setting while converging to the optimal distribution and maintaining strategyproofness. Then,
350   we explain how, with our payment offset, our mechanism is “almost IR:” In Lemma B.1 we prove that
351   the ex-interim utility of an advertiser who has zero reward for all candidate replies and bids truthfully
352   is deterministically zero, i.e., advertisers that do not contribute to the social welfare (but also do not
353   detract from it) have zero utility. Similarly, in Lemma B.2 we prove that if an advertiser’s reward for
354   all candidate sequences is (weakly) positive, then her ex-interim utility is (weakly) positive.
355   Remark 1. In Section 6, we experimentally show that our offset payment rule, coupled with our
356   context-aware allocation rule, results in both high expected rewards and positive expected utility for
357   the advertisers (i.e., ex-ante individual rationality), as well as significant revenue for the auctioneer.
358
359   5.3.2    “W HAT YOU GIVE IS WHAT YOU GET ”
360
      Our allocation rule, which is the only one over a finite set of replies that converges to the optimal LLM,
361
      is also the (sub)gradient of the utility to ensure truthfulness (Rochet, 1987). Because the allocation
362   rule is the same for all advertisers, their utilities must also be the same, up to advertiser-specific
363   offsets, as indicated by Equation (4). However, not all advertisers contribute equally to social welfare.
364   In Appendix B.4, we explain why implementing the mechanism without carefully designed offsets
365   would lead to free-riding. Specifically, if an advertiser’s utility in Equation (4) is positive, even when
366   her relevance to the user query is low, it incentivizes her participation. This dilutes the utility for
367   all participants, discouraging more relevant advertisers from participating. As a result, the expected
368   rewards for advertisers decline, and the generated replies become less useful for users. To avoid
369   free-riding, it is crucial to align each advertiser’s utility with her contribution to social welfare.

517   7    C ONCLUSION
518
519   We have introduced MOSAIC, a novel auction mechanism for aggregating preferences over LLM
520   outputs. MOSAIC provably converges to the theoretically optimal distribution and it also facilitates a
521   principled method for balancing participants’ expected rewards with the divergence from a reference
522   LLM. Thus, our mechanism is particularly well-suited for online advertising, allowing the integration
523   of advertiser LLMs with a reference LLM responsible for generating user-centric replies.
524   A well-chosen payment rule removes any incentive to misreport preferences, achieving the central
525   mechanism design goal of strategyproofness. While ex-post IR is incompatible with strategyproofness
526   in our setting, we experimentally show that our mechanism is ex-ante individually rational and “almost
527   individually rational” in a certain sense. Furthermore, it ensures that each agent’s utility gain is
528   proportionate to her contribution to social welfare, an essential alignment property in this setting.
529   Experimentally, we have demonstrated that by incorporating contextual information, MOSAIC’s
530   outputs rapidly converge to the optimal distribution, generating significant value for the advertisers
531   while also effectively recapturing a considerable portion of this value as revenue. These findings
532   demonstrate the practical efficacy and potential of our approach in real-world settings.
533
      There are several interesting directions for future work. While our mechanism converges quickly,
534   developing an approach to estimate the number of candidate replies required on a per-query basis
535   would reduce the platform’s costs. Another interesting direction is to develop a formal framework for
536   incorporating context into the mechanism, which could further improve the speed of convergence.
537   Finally, beyond advertising, one could apply a similar mechanism design approach to other problems:
538   for example, as web site owners increasingly demand to be paid for having their content ingested
539   by LLMs, one could consider designing a strategyproof procurement (i.e. reverse) auction to decide
      which sources are included in an LLM summary.

      B.2   D IFFERENCES FROM S TANDARD AUCTION S ETTINGS
919
920   Standard auction environments typically rely on a set of assumptions that simplify mechanism design;
921   however, these assumptions do not apply to auctions for LLM-generated content. In this section, we
922   detail these assumptions and discuss why they are inapplicable in our context.
923
      First, in a standard auction setting, it is common to assume that the agents’ valuation functions
924   satisfy free disposal, i.e., vi (S) ≥ vi (S ′ ) ∀S ⊇ S ′ , S, S ′ ⊇ I. The interpretation of free disposal
925   is that an agent can discard any items she is allocated that she is not interested in. Free disposal
926   combined with the fact that an agent has zero value for the empty bundle mean that her value for any
927   outcome is weakly positive. Second, in most auction environments, the allocation rule is different for
928   different agents: each agent will get allocated her own bundle of items, and we can assume that she is
929   indifferent to the allocation of items to the other agents.
930   As detailed in Rafailov et al. (2023), assuming that an agent’s LLM πi was trained to maximize
931   her reward function (and regularized with respect to its KL divergence from some reference LLM,
932   which we assume to be the same as the auctioneer’s reference LLM), there is a one-to-many mapping
933   between an advertiser’s optimal LLM, and her implicit reward function. That mapping is:
934
                                                           πi (y|x)
935                                   ri (x, y) = τi log              + log Zi (x)                         (14)
936
                                                           πref (y|x)
937   where Zi (x) is a prompt-dependent constant, and τi is the regularization hyperparameter of advertiser
938   i, similar to the one in Equation (1). All functions in the class defined in Equation (14) are equivalent,
939   in the sense that they induce exactly the same LLM (Rafailov et al., 2023). This has two implications:
940   First, unlike standard auction environments, an agent’s reward can go negative – there is nothing
941   equivalent to the free disposal property. Setting Zi (x) to zero (which is equivalent to normalizing
942   the induced probabilities by the LLM (Rafailov et al., 2023)), the agent’s reward is negative for any
943
      sequence for which her LLM assigns a lower probability than πref .
944   Second, especially in the online advertising application, an agent’s expected utility for not participat-
945   ing in the auction is negative: if advertiser i does not participate in the auction, her payment is zero,
946   but her expected value for the outcome is

       B.3    O UR MECHANISM IS “ALMOST INDIVIDUALLY RATIONAL”
973
974    First, we explain why the standard notion of individual rationality (i.e., weakly positive utility from
975    participation in the mechanism) encountered in most auction settings is impossible to achieve in this
976    domain while converging to the optimal distribution and maintaining incentive compatibility. Then,
977    we explain how, with our payment offset, our mechanism is “almost IR:” In Lemma B.1 we prove
978    that the ex-interim utility of an advertiser who has zero reward for all candidate sequences and bids
979    truthfully is deterministically zero, i.e., advertisers that do not contribute to the social welfare (but also
980
       do not detract from it) have zero utility. Similarly, in Lemma B.2 we prove that if an agent’s reward
       for all candidate sequences is (weakly) positive, then her ex-interim utility is (weakly) positive.
981
982
983
       Why is indivual rationality (IR) impossible? Individual rationality (IR) stipulates that an agent
       gains more utility by participating and bidding truthfully in a mechanism than by not participating
984
       at all. Typically, if an agent’s utility for non-participation is zero, participating should yield weakly
985
       positive utility. However, this simplification does not apply in our setting.
986
987    As discussed in Section 5.2, advertiser i’s reward for any sequence y can be arbitrarily negative
988    (Equation (14)). The same is true for the utility from truthful participation, as outlined in Equation (12).
989
       To ensure a positive utility for every advertiser in our mechanism, an offset would need to be infinitely
       large or dependent on advertiser i’s reports. But then the mechanism’s allocation rule would no
990
       longer be the gradient of advertiser i’s utility with respect to her reports, which would destroy
991
       strategyproofness (Frongillo & Kash, 2021; Rochet, 1987; Myerson, 1981).
992
993    It is important to note that this challenge is inherent not just to our mechanism but to any mechanism
994    in this setting that operates with a fixed set of sequences, aims to approximate the optimal distribution,
995
       and maintains strategyproofness. Under these conditions, the only allocation rule that approximates
       the theoretically optimal distribution (Equation (2)) is that of our mechanism. However, this uniquely
996
       determines the advertisers’ utilities, up to a constant factor, as described in Equation (12) (Frongillo
997
       & Kash, 2021; Rochet, 1987; Myerson, 1981).

1054   that she received, on expectation, the same (positive) expected utility from doing so as any other
1055   participating advertiser.6
1056   Incentivizing unrelated advertisers to participate would would have adverse effects. First, the better-
1057   performing context-aware mechanism would create candidate sequences with worse rewards for all
1058   advertisers, because its context would be “diluted” from advertisers unrelated to the user query. In
1059   our running example for the query “How to learn music online?”, imagine adding “Try to mention
1060   ‘EasySwitch’, a comprehensive VPN service” to the context of the context-aware LLM πcon (·|x; ⃗c).
1061
       Additionally, for both versions of the mechanism, following the discussion in Section 5.2, the
1062   advertisers for whom the user query is unrelated are more likely to have negative rewards for the
1063   generated sequences as their LLMs will deem the candidate sequences more unlikely than the
1064   reference LLM. Thus, based on Equation (4), their participation in the mechanism will lead to a
1065   reduction of the total sum of rewards of the generated sequences for the advertisers, which will
1066   indirectly reduce the expected utility of all advertisers, making the mechanism less attractive for the
1067   user-query-relevant advertisers.
1068   To summarize, all advertisers receiving the same utility would incentivize advertisers for whom the
1069   user query is unrelated to participate in the auction. This would in turn reduce everyone’s expected
1070   utility, potentially reducing the incentive for the user-query-relevant advertisers to participate, and
1071   lead to sequences with worse expected rewards for the advertisers and usefulness for the user.7 Thus,
1072   in the application of auctions for aggregating advertisers’ preferences over LLM-generated outputs,
1073   advertisers with higher contribution to social welfare also receiving proportionally higher utility by
1074   the mechanism is important for the long-term success of the mechanism in practice.

       D.3   C ONFLICTS BETWEEN ADVERTISERS
1405
1406   The output of MOSAIC is a single text response to the user. In trying to reflect the interests of
1407   multiple advertisers, there is a risk of a single response being incoherent, just mentioning as many
1408   advertisers as possible without being a good promotion for any of them. However, the platform’s
1409   objective, which takes into account both advertiser utility and usefulness for the user, should account
1410   for this: if advertisers are happy to be cross-promoted, those outcomes should be more likely, but
1411   incoherent advertisements or those that simultaneously promote competitors should be less likely.
1412   In Figure 8 we show the fractions of advertisers mentioned by the context-aware version of MOSAIC
1413   as a function of the candidate number of replies used. We observe that initially, the fraction of
1414   advertisers mentioned is very high, but drops significantly once the mechanism has converged.
1415   The reason is precisely the fact that mentioning more advertisers in this case would cause either a
1416   significant drop in advertiser utility, or a drop in the usefulness of the reply to the user. By placing
1417   more weight towards the advertisers (i.e., decreasing the value of τ in Equation (1)), the platform can
1418
       increase the number of advertisers shown when the mechanism has converged.
