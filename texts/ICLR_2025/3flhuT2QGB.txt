032                                                               A BSTRACT
033
034                The increasing demand for versatile robotic systems to operate in diverse and
035
                   dynamic environments has emphasized the importance of a generalist policy, which
                   leverages a large cross-embodiment data corpus to facilitate broad adaptability
036
                   and high-level reasoning. However, the generalist would struggle with inefficient
037
                   inference and cost-expensive training. The specialist policy, instead, is curated
038                for specific domain data and excels at task-level precision with efficiency. Yet, it
039                lacks the generalization capacity for a wide range of applications. Inspired by these
040                observations, we introduce RoboDual, a synergistic dual-system that supplements
041                the merits of both generalist and specialist policy. A diffusion transformer-based
042                specialist is devised for multi-step action rollouts, exquisitely conditioned on the
043                high-level task understanding and discretized action output of a vision-language-
044                action (VLA) based generalist. Compared to OpenVLA, RoboDual achieves 26.7%
045                improvement in real-world setting and 12% gain on CALVIN by introducing
046
                   a specialist policy with merely 20M trainable parameters. It maintains strong
                   performance with 5% of demonstration data only, and enables a 3.8× higher control
047
                   frequency in real-world deployment. Code would be made publicly available. An
048
                   anonymous real-robot demo is hosted at https://robodual.github.io.

051   1     I NTRODUCTION
052
053   The pursuit of versatile and adaptive robotic intelligence has been a central objective in the robotics
      community for decades (Franklin, 1997; Kunze et al., 2018; Duan et al., 2022). Conventional


                                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      robot learning methods typically develop policies through datasets curated for the designated robot
055   and its specific task. The resulting policy could be deemed as a specialist, including the popular
056   ACT (Zhao et al., 2023) and Diffusion Policy (Chi et al., 2023). It exhibits high precision in dedicated
057   scenarios and tasks, and yet often demonstrates limited generalization ability (Brohan et al., 2023a).
058   As robots are increasingly employed in open-ended and multi-task environments, the demand for
059   systems capable of handling diverse tasks and adapting seamlessly across various embodiments has
060   surged. This has fueled the development of the generalist, such as RT-2 (Brohan et al., 2023a) and
061   Octo (Ghosh et al., 2024). They leverage extensive, heterogeneous datasets to enhance cross-domain
062   generalizability and aim to transfer web knowledge to robotic control. Recent advances on Vision-
063
      Language-Action (VLA) approaches (Li et al., 2024; Brohan et al., 2023a; Kim et al., 2024; Covariant,
      2024) exemplify the potential of generalist policy to meet the ever-evolving demands. VLAs integrate
064
      vast cross-embodiment data with pre-trained large (vision-)language models, facilitating capabilities
065
      such as common-sense reasoning and instruction following (Zhao et al., 2024).
066
067   While VLA-based generalists excel at knowledge transfer and generalization across diverse scenarios,
068   several limitations remain: 1) They cannot be directly deployed to new embodiments or environments
069
      out-of-the-box without adaptation (Wang et al., 2024b). The finetuning process is more data and
      training intensive compared to specialist policies (Fu et al., 2024). 2) Though VLAs are skilled in
070
      high-level decision making, their large model nature leads to extremely high inference latency (Brohan
071
      et al., 2023a). This pivotal bottleneck makes them unsuitable for fine-grained control in dynamic
072   environments. 3) Current generalist models support single-frame RGB observations only, which,
073   while enabling training on larger-scale datasets, restricts their effectiveness in tasks where additional
074   sensory inputs such as depth or tactile feedback play pivotal roles. In the meantime, incorporating
075   these extra modalities requires resource-intensive re-training (Han et al., 2024) and runs the risk of
076   catastrophic forgetting (Zhai et al., 2023b).
077
      Significant efforts, such as model quantization and fine-tuning of the generalist with multimodal
078   data, have been made to address the aforementioned limitations (Kim et al., 2024; Zhen et al., 2024).
079   Nonetheless, these approaches still face inevitable performance declines or data scarcity-related
080   issues. This raises the question of whether it is sufficient to rely solely on enhancements to generalists
081   to resolve these challenges. We recall that a generalist model offers broad generalizability and benefits
082   from web-scale pre-training, while a specialist policy is competent at efficiency and fast adaptation to
083   specific tasks. Based on the insights, we propose a generalized and efficient framework in which both
084   policies complement each other for improved manipulation, as illustrated in Figure 1(a). Our work
085   introduces a novel dual-system1 synergy approach, namely RoboDual. It is designed to harness the
086
      advantages of both parties and facilitate the practical deployment of large generalist policies.
087   We start with the large-scale pre-trained OpenVLA (Kim et al., 2024) to establish our generalist
088   policy. For seamless cooperation between the two models, we implement the specialist model as a
089   lightweight and scalable diffusion transformer (Peebles & Xie, 2023) policy. The specialist learns the
090   multimodal action distribution by utilizing any sensory inputs and the generalist outputs adaptively
091   through a unified conditioning mechanism. Latent representations and discretized action outputs from
092
      the generalist enable our specialist to adapt to new tasks or environments efficiently with minimal
      data and training costs. During inference, the generalist provides deliberate yet comparatively slower
093
      conditioning, which supports multistep roll-outs of the fast-reacting specialist to achieve precise
094
      and generalized control. In this way, RoboDual is rendered with high-level task understanding
095   and generalizability from the generalist, combined with efficient action refinement of the specialist,
096   achieving outstanding performance across a diverse array of tasks. As demonstrated in Figure 1(b), it
097   realizes a 12% performance gain over the generalist-only variant on CALVIN (Mees et al., 2022b)
098   with minimal training cost. In real-robot setting, RoboDual outperforms both specialist and generalist
099   baselines by a significant margin. To summarize, our contributions are threefold:
100
101
      • We introduce a novel approach that integrates generalist and specialist policies into a synergistic
        framework, dubbed as RoboDual, following a dual-system spirit. Our methodology leverages the
102
        merits of each party and paves the way for the practical application of VLAs to dexterous tasks.

      while the generalist model might reflect the characteristics of System-2.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
117
118   Generalist policy. The RT-X series of works (Brohan et al., 2023b;a) have sparked significant
119
      progress in the development of multi-task generalist policies (Yang et al., 2023; Ghosh et al., 2024) by
      leveraging extensive cross-embodiment datasets (Padalkar et al., 2024; Walke et al., 2023; Khazatsky
120
      et al., 2024). Octo (Ghosh et al., 2024) employs a transformer-based policy trained on 800k trajectories
121
      from the Open-X-Embodiment dataset (Padalkar et al., 2024), enabling flexible fine-tuning for novel
122   robotic configurations. OpenVLA (Kim et al., 2024), which represents the most advanced generalist
123   policy to date, directly integrates pre-trained vision-language models to generate robotic actions by
124   treating them as tokens within the language model’s vocabulary. Unlike Octo, which relies solely
125   on a pre-trained language encoder T5 (Raffel et al., 2020) and derives generalization primarily
126   from large-scale in-domain policy training, OpenVLA harnesses world knowledge from a much
127   broader vision-language dataset. Despite its demonstrated generalizability, OpenVLA’s model size,
128   which comprises billions of parameters, hinders both data and inference efficiency. Hence it would
129   confine the deployment on heterogeneous robot setup as a generalist policy. In contrast, our approach
130
      introduces a novel and cost-effective dual-system framework to address these caveats, instead of
      developing another larger generalist model reliant on extensive datasets.
131
132   Specialist policy. We regard specialist models as policies specifically trained to execute a narrow
133   set of tasks or functions with high precision (Goyal et al., 2023; 2024). These models typically
134   utilize curated datasets tailored to their specific applications (Zhao et al., 2023; Chi et al., 2023).
135   While many specialist policies excel in few-shot imitation learning, they often lack the integration
136
      of language inputs, necessitating the training of distinct models for different tasks. A notable trend
      in prior studies is the reliance on 3D representations (Shridhar et al., 2023; Gervet et al., 2023;
137
      Ze et al., 2023; Yan et al., 2024; Goyal et al., 2024) to improve performance on low-dimensional
138
      control tasks. Transformer-based models have emerged as powerful tools for extracting multimodal
139   features, enhancing manipulation capabilities through their flexibility in processing heterogeneous
140   observations (Kim et al., 2021; Dasari & Gupta, 2021; Goyal et al., 2023; Simeonov et al., 2023).
141   The recent development of the Diffusion Policy (Chi et al., 2023), along with subsequent works (Ze
142   et al., 2024; Prasad et al., 2024), has proven effective in managing multimodal action distributions
143   for robotic manipulation while exhibiting improved training stability. In our work, the specialist
144   model is designed as a scalable diffusion transformer (Peebles & Xie, 2023) policy, adept at handling
145   multimodal inputs and generalist outputs as conditioning in a unified framework. Furthermore, we
146   demonstrate how collaboration with a generalist model enhances generalization and enables the
147
      execution of multi-instruction tasks that previous specialist-only models struggle to address.
148   Hierarchical control with LLMs. The rise of Large Language Models (LLMs) and their ability to
149   interpret prompts and perform reasoning has sparked interest in their application to robotics (Wang
150   et al., 2024a). A key area is high-level task planning, where LLMs decompose tasks using natural
151   language, as demonstrated in SayCan (Ahn et al., 2022), PaLM-E (Driess et al., 2023), and HiP (Ajay
152   et al., 2024). This enables robots to translate abstract commands into concrete actions. This is followed
153
      by works using structured code for control (Liang et al., 2023; Singh et al., 2023), which map user
      instructions into executable programs. Another line of work, including RoboFlamingo (Li et al., 2024)
154
      and LCB (Shentu et al., 2024), involves hierarchical control via latent space representations, where
155
      they employ an additional action decoder network on top of LLM latent outputs to regress actions
156   directly. Recent works also explore action tokenization through VQVAE techniques (Van Den Oord
157   et al., 2017) to better bridge LLMs and actions (Wang et al., 2024c; Szot et al., 2024). Although our
158   framework shares a similar hierarchical philosophy, it does not explicitly decompose tasks and does
159   not require end-to-end optimization of decoupled policies. Instead, we utilize both discretized outputs
160   and latent embeddings as “interfaces” to connect two models, rather than pre-defined low-level
161   skills. The generalist and specialist can be decoupled on the aspect of training data to develop their
      respective capabilities, which also brings flexibility to utilizing various modal inputs.

183   3     ROBO D UAL : G ENERALIST-S PECIALIST S YNERGY
184
185
      Our goal is to develop a synergistic and generalized framework that leverages the strengths of both
186   generalist and specialist policies while simultaneously addressing their respective limitations beyond
187   mere integration. We introduce the generalist policy, which forms a meticulous and robust planner in
188   our framework in Section 3.1. In Section 3.2, we provide design principles of the specialist policy,
189   which is optimized for precise, real-time control and allows unified conditioning. Finally, we describe
190   our training and inference protocols in Section 3.3. The systematic diagram is illustrated in Figure 2.
191
192
      3.1     G ENERALIST: A N AUTO - REGRESSIVE V ISION -L ANGUAGE -ACTION M ODEL
193
194   Figure 2(a) demonstrates our generalist’s architecture. Our generalist model is built upon Open-
195   VLA (Kim et al., 2024), a 7B-parameter autoregressive vision-language-action model trained with a
196   large corpus of robotic manipulation data, including Open-X-Embodiment (Padalkar et al., 2024),
197   Bridge V2 (Walke et al., 2023), DROID (Khazatsky et al., 2024), etc. The generalist model follows
198   the architecture of Prismatic-7B (Karamcheti et al., 2024) vision-language model (VLM), which
199   consists of a fused visual encoder from different backbones (Zhai et al., 2023a; Oquab et al., 2024),
200   a projection layer to align visual embeddings with language modality, and a large language model
201
      LLaMA2 (Touvron et al., 2023). Despite extensive training on a large-scale cross-embodiment
      dataset, OpenVLA is not capable of functioning in a zero-shot manner in new environments or em-
202
      bodiments (Wang et al., 2024b). Adaptation to our specific robotic setup and test environments (with
203
      novel coordination system, camera angle, etc.) remains necessary, which we accomplish through
204   LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) fine-tuning. Nonetheless, we intend to leverage the massive pre-trained
205   knowledge embedded in OpenVLA to endow our dual-system framework with certain generalizability.
206
207
      Autoregressive generation of action chunking. Following RT-2 (Brohan et al., 2023a) and Open-
      VLA (Kim et al., 2024), we map the least used 256 words in the LLaMA tokenizer vocabulary into
208
      uniformly distributed action bins within [−1, 1]. This approach allows us to detokenize language
209
      tokens into discretized actions based on their corresponding indices in the vocabulary. The generalist
210   model decodes every degree-of-freedom of actions in an auto-regressive manner, where the decoding
211   for the current token is dependent upon input prompts and previously decoded tokens. We further
212   extend the original OpenVLA to predict action chunks with a temporal length of kg . This longer-range
213   planning on the generalist side enhances its own ability to capture non-Markovian behavior in human
214   demonstrations, and also facilitates more informative conditioning provided to the specialist model.
215   The action output corresponding to each time step is separated by [space] token in the tokenizer
      vocabulary. However, action chunking increases the inference latency of VLA due to the generation


                                                                                  4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      of a greater number of tokens. This further prompts the need for a specialist model that runs at a
217   higher frequency between consecutive VLA outputs to achieve more responsive control.

220   3.2   S PECIALIST: A C ONTROLLABLE D IFFUSION T RANSFORMER P OLICY
221
222   Built on top of a pre-trained generalist policy, the specialist is to achieve improved performance
223   while reducing control latency, even with limited training data and compute. To fully exploit the
224   multimodal sensory inputs necessary for effective manipulation, as well as the privileged knowledge
225   from the generalist policy, we design the specialist based on Diffusion Transformer (DiT) (Peebles &
226   Xie, 2023), to perform controllable action sequences denoising.
227
      Base architecture. Figure 2(b) illustrates the architecture of our specialist model, which is primarily
228   composed of stacked DiT blocks. Each block includes a causal self-attention layer to process
229   temporal actions, a cross-attention layer to fuse information, and a point-wise feedforward network
230   that performs non-linear transformations. Drawing parallels with image diffusion models (Saharia
231   et al., 2022), we treat a 7-DoF action as a pixel with seven channels, which is linearly projected
232   into a single token and processed by the diffusion model. This formulation facilitates a seamless
233   temporal expansion of action tokens, enabling action chunk prediction (Zhao et al., 2023) with a
234   flexible temporal length of ks . We employ Vision Transformers (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) as
235   generalized sensory encoders to encode all possible input modalities (e.g., RGB, depth, and tactile),
236
      with minor modifications on the patchify layer given different number of channels. A DINO (Caron
      et al., 2021) pre-trained model is leveraged to encode the RGB inputs, which is frozen during training.
237
      Encoders for other modalities are constrained to six layers with a hidden size of 256 to ensure
238
      efficiency. Beyond what we have explored, our framework is also adaptable to non-image inputs that
239   can be encoded into a sequence of embeddings.
240
241   Action denoising with multimodal conditioning. The specialist model leverages multiple sources
242
      of conditioning and their corresponding conditioning approaches to enhance decision-making: 1)
      proprioceptive states (Proprio.) of the robot, 2) multimodal sensory inputs, 3) generalists’ discretized
243
      action outputs, and 4) latent representations (refer to Figure 2(a)) from the generalist model. Each
244
      source contributes distinct information, facilitating a more informed and robust policy.
245
246   The proprioceptive states are processed through a two-layer MLP and combined with a time-step
247   embedding to enable adaptive sample-wise conditioning. Beyond regressing γ and β parameters for
248
      adaptive layer normalization (Perez et al., 2018), a scaling parameter α, is introduced in the residual
      connections to ensure stable conditioning and improve training robustness (Peebles & Xie, 2023).
249
250   For sensory inputs, we incorporate a perceiver resampler (Alayrac et al., 2022), consisting of a multi-
251   head attention pooling module followed by an MLP layer, to selectively distill key features from
252   observation embeddings generated by ViTs while reducing token length. Specifically, we employ
253   eight learnable queries for every sensory input. The resampler preserves performance and accelerates
254
      the multistep denoising process, particularly when dealing with multi-source inputs advantageous for
      manipulation tasks, such as multiview observations, historical frames, and multimodal data.
255
256   To condition the specialist on the discretized actions from the generalist, we concatenate them with
257   the noised action of the corresponding time step, and project the concatenated inputs into a shared
258   latent space through linear layers. This approach is inspired by video prediction models (Blattmann
259   et al., 2023), which concatenate initially known frames with noised inputs to predict future states.
260   Conditioning our specialist model on the task and action latents derived from the generalist involves
261   utilizing linear projection on the generalist tokens to align their hidden spaces. Despite simplicity, it is
262   parameter-efficient and preserves the original positional encoding within VLA. Finally, the projected
263   generalist latents, along with the resampled observation embeddings, are concatenated and utilized as
264   keys and values in the cross-attention layer. Our diverse conditioning enables the specialist model to
265   process comprehensive contextual data effectively, prompting more informed decision-making.
266   Given that the generalist and specialist models operate asynchronously during inference (with a
267   single generalist inference supporting multiple specialist rollouts), we implement a shifted-window
268   conditioning mechanism. Specifically, following τs steps of specialist inference, only the most recent
269   kg − τs generalist actions are sampled as conditioning before a second update. We also apply this
      mechanism as an augmentation during training to ensure latency robustness of RoboDual. The

271   output, with the specialist’s observation input leading the generalist’s by τ ∈ [0, kg ] steps.

      4.5   A BLATION STUDY
502
503   The competitive results presented above position our approach favorably compared to specialist and
504   generalist-only policies. In the following, we examine factors that pay credit towards a more desirable
505   synergistic framework for RoboDual.
506
      Generalist outputs as conditioning. Figure 6(a) describes that each conditioning source from the
507
      generalist model contributes to synergy and enhances overall performance. Although discretized
508   trajectories may not be perfect, they offer plausible directions that can be iteratively refined by the
509   specialist model, resulting in an improvement of 0.8. Furthermore, both the action and task latents
510   (refer to Figure 2) encapsulate high-level task understanding, which is crucial for multi-task learning.
511   The absence of task latents solely leads to a performance decline of 0.14 in average length.
512
      Additional sensory inputs. Incorporating additional sensory inputs into the specialist model for
513
      specific scenarios proves to be a cost-effective strategy. Our approach eliminates the necessity for
514   further fine-tuning of the VLA, without compromising the inference efficiency of the specialist model.
515   As demonstrated in Figure 6(b), RoboDual leverages additional modalities (e.g., depth and tactile)
516   and extra viewpoints (e.g., gripper camera) effectively to enhance overall performance.
517
      Conditioning method. The firm bridges connecting generalist and specialist models are constructed
518
      through stable conditioning mechanisms. We assess three well-established methods: FiLM (Perez
519
      et al., 2018), in-context conditioning (Wang et al., 2023), and cross-attention. Results are shown
520   in Figure 6(c). Cross-attention based method performs slightly better than in-context conditioning
521   while also being more computationally efficient. Therefore, it is selected as our final approach.

      5     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
525
526
      We present RoboDual, a synergistic dual-system for robotic manipulation that capitalizes the gener-
      alizability of Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models alongside the efficiency and adaptability of
527
      specialist policies. Our proposed diffusion transformer-based specialist can achieve finer-grained
528
      control, efficiently incorporate any sensory input, and adapt to diverse tasks and heterogeneous
529   environments with minimal data and training costs. By fostering synergistic cooperation, RoboDual
530   effectively addresses several limitations inherent in existing VLAs, offering a cost-effective and
531   widely adaptable solution for the practical deployment of large generalist policies.
532
      Limitations and future work. In RoboDual, we assume that the inference time for both the generalist
533
      and specialist remains constant. Consequently, during deployment, each generalist inference step
534
      is associated with a fixed number of specialist steps. Besides, current generalist outputs discretized
535   actions only. Given the scalability of auto-regressive VLAs, there is potential for the generalist to be
536   trained to perform task decomposition (Ahn et al., 2022), affordance grounding (Qian et al., 2024; Lai
537   et al., 2024), and image goal generation (Sun et al., 2024). These capabilities may serve as effective
538   complements, establishing better “bridges” between the generalist and specialist models.

761   CALVIN Benchmark. CALVIN (Mees et al., 2022b) encompasses 34 distinct tasks, characterized
762   by unconstrained task instructions that cover a range of skills, from basic pick-and-place operations
763   to articulated object manipulation. The benchmark comprises four different environments as shown
764
      in Figure 7, each featuring a Franka Panda robotic arm for tabletop manipulation. In our study,
      we adopt the challenging evaluation setting, where policies are trained with demonstrations from
765
      environments A, B, and C, followed by zero-shot evaluations in environment D. The evaluation
766
      protocol includes a test set of 1,000 unique instruction chains, each composed of five consecutive tasks.
767   The results of the baselines are directly referenced from the official benchmark. For implementation
768   on CALVIN, we train the generalist model with LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) for 50 epochs. The specialist
769   model is trained for 100k iterations with a batch size of 64 (∼8 epochs). We set the action chunk size
770   to kg = ks = 8 for both the generalist and specialist policy. Unless specified otherwise, our specialist
771   model takes as input RGB and depth images from static (third-view) and gripper view cameras. Both
772   the generalist and specialist operate on images of size 224 × 224.

799   The implementation of RoboDual follows a two-stage training pipeline, where the generalist is
800   trained with multi-task data using LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) finetuning, followed by the efficient
801
      training of our specialist model with either multi-task or task-specific demonstrations. We set
      the action chunk size ks = 8 for the specialist and use temporal aggregation during inference to
802
      achieve smoother control. Specifically, action outputs are temporally aggregated with an exponential
803
      weighting scheme wi = exp(−m · i), where i ∈ [0, ks ] and w0 is the weight for the oldest
804   action. We set m to 0.1 by default. DDIM scheduler (Song et al., 2021) is employed with the
805   timestamps set to 100 for training and only 5 for inference. Directly predicting samples shows
806   to be more robust than noise prediction. We find that increasing the denoising steps does not
807   necessarily bring performance improvement, while using smaller steps allows faster inference and
808   more responsive control. Classifier-free guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2022) is applied on generalist
809   latents and proprioceptive states with a guidance scale of wg = 3 for better controllability. We also

      B    A RCHITECTURE D ESIGN AND T RAINING H YPERPARAMETERS
816
817
      Generalist. The architecture of our generalist policy is identical to Prismatic-7B (Karamcheti et al.,
      2024). Regarding training of the generalist, we follow OpenVLA (Kim et al., 2024), and use the
818
      AdamW optimizer with a constant learning rate of 2e-5 and weight decay of 0.01. We find that
819
      achieving robust convergence on the CALVIN benchmark, with its 34 distinct tasks and diverse
820   environments, necessitates a sufficiently large batch size of 2048. We leverage gradient accumulation
821   to achieve a large batch size with constrained compute. However, for real-world experiments, a
822   smaller batch size of 64 remains feasible, offering greater flexibility in practical applications.
823
      Specialist. We provide the detailed architecture information of our specialist policy in Table 5.
824
      Rotary position embeddings are incorporated into each DiT block to ensure coherence among
825
      chunked actions. In general, it is more lightweight than previous popular specialist policies (Zhao
826   et al., 2023; Chi et al., 2023) that contains 50M to 80M parameters. Incorporating a new sensory
827   input entails an individual ViT encoder (6.4M) and a perceiver resampler module (1.1M), which
828   introduces additional 7.5M parameters in total. Considering an input size of 224, sensory encoders
829   generate 196 tokens with a patch size of 16. Observation embeddings are subsequently downsampled
830   to only 8 tokens by the resampler. Notably, conditioning latents in the cross-attention module only
831   need to be computed once for the multistep denoising process, leading to more efficient control.
832   During training, we leverage the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of 1e-4 and weight decay of
833   1e-3, mostly following Chi et al. (2023). A cosine-annealing scheduler with warm-up steps of 1,000 is
834   employed to improve training stability. We apply classifier-free guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2022) on
835   generalist latents and proprioceptive states with a condition drop chance of 0.1, allowing the specialist
836   policy to imitate without privileged information from the generalist. No specific modifications of
837   training parameters are made for any tasks or environments.

891   D    FAILURE A NALYSIS
892
893   During the 100 testing runs of the all tasks in our real-world environment, we record the causes
894   behind each failure. Subsequently, we present these failure causes in a Sankey diagram, exemplified
895   in Figure 9. We found one major failure issue would be "Not Following Instruction". The instruction-

897   perform the desired task. It’s worth future exploration of building better "bridges" beyond what is
898   discussed in RoboDual (i.e., discretized actions and generalist latent features) to facilitate a more
899   synergistic framework.
