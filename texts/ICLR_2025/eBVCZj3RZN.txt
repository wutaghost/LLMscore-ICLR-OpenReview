                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Shapley values have several desirable properties for explaining black-box model
               predictions, which come with strong theoretical support. Traditionally, Shapley
011
               values are computed post-hoc, leading to additional computational cost at infer-
012
               ence time. To overcome this, we introduce ViaSHAP, a novel approach that learns
013            a function to compute Shapley values, from which the predictions can be derived
014            directly by summation. We explore two learning approaches based on the uni-
015            versal approximation theorem and the Kolmogorov-Arnold representation theo-
016            rem. Results from a large-scale empirical investigation are presented, in which
017            the predictive performance of ViaSHAP is compared to state-of-the-art algorithms
018            for tabular data, where the implementation using Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks
019            showed a superior performance. It is also demonstrated that the explanations of
020            ViaSHAP are accurate, and that the accuracy is controllable through the hyperpa-
021
               rameters.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026
      The application of machine learning algorithms in some domains requires communicating the rea-
      sons behind predictions with the aim of building trust in the predictive models and, more importantly,
027
      addressing legal and ethical considerations (Lakkaraju et al., 2017; Goodman & Flaxman, 2017).
028
      Nevertheless, many state-of-the-art machine learning algorithms result in black-box models, pre-
029   cluding the user’s ability to follow the reasoning behind the predictions. Consequently, explainable
030   machine learning methods have gained notable attention as a means to acquire needed explainability
031   without sacrificing performance.
032
      Machine learning explanation methods employ a variety of strategies to produce explanations, e.g.,
033
      the use of local interpretable surrogate models (Ribeiro et al., 2016), generation of counterfactual
034
      examples (Karimi et al., 2020; Dandl et al., 2020; Mothilal et al., 2020; Van Looveren & Klaise,
035   2021; Guo et al., 2021; Guyomard et al., 2022), selection of important features (Chen et al., 2018;
036   Yoon et al., 2019; Jethani et al., 2021), and approximation of Shapley values (Lundberg & Lee,
037   2017; Lundberg et al., 2020; Frye et al., 2021; Covert & Lee, 2021; Jethani et al., 2022). Methods
038   that generate explanations based on Shapley values are prominent since they offer a unique solution
039   that meets a set of theoretically established, desirable properties. The computation of Shapley values
040   can, however, be computationally expensive. Recent work has therefore focused on reducing the
041   running time (Lundberg & Lee, 2017; Lundberg et al., 2020; Jethani et al., 2022) and enhancing the
042   accuracy of approximations (Frye et al., 2021; Aas et al., 2021; Covert & Lee, 2021; Mitchell et al.,
043
      2022; Kolpaczki et al., 2024). However, the Shapley values are computed post-hoc, and hence entail
      a computational overhead, even when approximated, e.g., as in the case of FastSHAP. Generating
044
      instance-based explanations or learning a pre-trained explainer always demands further data, time,
045
      and resources. Nevertheless, to the best of our knowledge, computing Shapley values as a means to
046   form the prediction has not yet been considered.
047
048   The main contributions of this study are:
049
050
             • a novel machine learning method, ViaSHAP, that trains a model to simultaneously provide
               accurate predictions and Shapley values
051
052          • multiple implementations of the proposed method using the universal approximation theo-
053            rem and the Kolmogorov-Arnold representation theorem, followed by a large-scale empir-
               ical investigation

063   2.1   T HE S HAPLEY VALUE
064
065   In game theory, a game in coalitional form is a formal model for a scenario in which players form
066
      coalitions, and the game’s payoff is shared between the coalition members. A coalitional game
      focuses on the behavior of the players and typically involves a finite set of players N = {1, 2, . . . , n}
067
      (Manea, 2016). A coalitional game also involves a characteristic set function v : 2N → R that
068
      assigns a payoff, a real number, to a coalition S ⊆ N such that: v(∅) = 0 (Owen, 1995.). Different
069   concepts can be employed to distribute the payoff among the players of a coalitional game to achieve
070   a fair and stable allocation. Such solution concepts include the Core, the Nucleolus, and the Shapley
071   Value (Manea, 2016; Ferguson, 2018).
072
      The Shapley Value is a solution concept that allocates payoffs to the players according to their
073
      marginal contributions across possible coalitions. The Shapley value ϕi (v) of player i in game v is

110   In the context of explainable machine learning, the Shapley value is commonly computed post-hoc
111   to explain the predictions of trained machine learning models. Let f be a trained model whose
112   inputs are defined on n features and whose output y ∈ Y ⊆ R. We also define a baseline or neutral
113   instance, noted 0 ∈ X. For a given instance x, the Shapley value is computed over each feature to
114   explain the difference in output x ∈ X and the baseline. The baseline may be determined depending
115   on the context, but common examples include the average of all examples in the training set, or one
116
      that is commonly used as a threshold (Izzo et al., 2021).

118   features, and the payoff is the difference in output wrt the baseline:

192   3     V IA SHAP
193
194   We introduce ViaSHAP, a method that formulates predictions via Shapley values regression. In con-
195   trast to the previous approaches, the Shapley values are not computed in a post-hoc setup. Instead,
196   the learning of Shapley values is integrated into the training of the predictive model and exploits
197   every data example in the training data. At inference time, the Shapley values are used directly
198   to generate the prediction. The following subsections describe how ViaSHAP is trained to predict
199
      simultaneously both accurate predictions and the corresponding explanation through Shapley values.
200

      3.3   V IA SHAP A PPROXIMATOR

264   According to the universal approximation theorem, a feedforward network with at least one hidden
265   layer and sufficient units in the hidden layer can approximate any continuous function over a com-
266   pact input set to an arbitrary degree of accuracy, given a suitable activation function (Hornik et al.,
267   1989; Cybenko, 1989; Hornik, 1991). Consequently, neural networks and multi-layer perceptrons
268   (MLP) can be employed to learn ViaSHAP for prediction tasks where there is a continuous mapping
269   function from the input dataset to the true targets, which also applies to the true Shapley values as a
      continuous function.

      4.5   A BLATION S TUDY
479
480   The ablation study was conducted after the empirical evaluation to ensure that no prior knowledge
481   of the data or models influenced the experimental setup. In the ablation study, we assessed the
482   impact of the scaling hyperparameter β and the number of sampled coalitions. The detailed results
483   of the ablation study are provided in Appendex H. We began by examining the effect of β on both
484   predictive performance and the similarity of computed Shapley values to the ground truth. The
485   results demonstrate that predictive performance remains robust to changes in β, unless β is raised to
      an exceptionally large value, e.g., ≥200-fold. A more remarkable observation is that the similarity


                                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      of the computed Shapley values to the ground truth improves as β grows. However, the model fails
487   to learn properly with substantially large β. Afterwards, we evaluated the effect of the number of
488   sampled coalitions per data instance on the performance of the learned models. The results suggest
489   that the number of samples has little impact on both predictive performance and the similarity of
490   the computed Shapley values to the ground truth compared to beta, i.e., ViaSHAP can be effectively
491   trained with as few as one sample per data instance. We also study the effect of a link function on
492   both predictive performance and the accuracy of Shapley values of ViaSHAP . Finally, we examined
493   the impact of β on the progression of training and validation loss during the training phase. The
494   results indicate that ViaSHAP tends to require a longer time to converge as β values increase.
495
496   5   R ELATED W ORK
497
498
      In addition to KernelSHAP and the real-time method FastSHAP, alternative approaches have been
499   proposed to reduce the time required for Shapley value approximation. Methods that exploit specific
500   properties of the explained model can provide faster computations, e.g., TreeSHAP (Lundberg et al.,
501   2020) and DASP (Ancona et al., 2019), while others limit the scope to specific problems, e.g.,
502   image classifications or text classification (Chen et al., 2019; Teneggi et al., 2022). Additionally,
503   directions to improve Shapley value approximation by enhancing data sampling have also been
504   explored (Frye et al., 2021; Aas et al., 2021; Covert et al., 2021; Mitchell et al., 2022; Chen et al.,
505   2023; Kolpaczki et al., 2024). Nevertheless, traditional methods for computing Shapley values have
506   typically been considered post-hoc solutions for explaining predictions, requiring additional time,
507
      data, and resources to generate explanations. In contrast, ViaSHAP computes Shapley values during
      inference, eliminating the need for a separate post-hoc explainer.
508
509   Research on generating explanations using pre-trained models has explored several approaches.
510   Chen et al. (2018), Yoon et al. (2019), and Jethani et al. (2021) trained models for important features
511   selection. Schwab & Karlen (2019) trained a model to estimate the influence of different inputs
512   on the predicted outcome. Situ et al. (2021) proposed to distill any explanation algorithm for text
513
      classification. Pretrained explainers, similar to other post-hoc methods, require further resources for
      training, and the fidelity of their explanations to the underlying black-box model can vary.
514
515   Many approaches for creating explainable neural networks have been proposed in the literature.
516   Such approaches not only generate predictions but also include an integrated component that pro-
517   vides explanations, which is trained alongside the predictor (Lei et al., 2016; Alvarez Melis &
518   Jaakkola, 2018; Guo et al., 2021; Al-Shedivat et al., 2022; Sawada & Nakamura, 2022; Guyomard
519
      et al., 2022). Explainable graph neural networks (GNNs) have also been studied for graph-structured
      data, which typically exploit the internal properties of their models to generate explanations, e.g., the
520
      similarity between nodes (Dai & Wang, 2021), finding patterns and common graph structures(Feng
521
      et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022; Cui et al., 2022), or analyzing the behavior of different components
522   of the GNN (Xuanyuan et al., 2023). Explanations generated by explainable neural networks do
523   not correspond to Shapley values or meet the properties inherent to Shapley values, in contrast to
524   ViaSHAP . Moreover, the explanations are offered without fidelity guarantees and do not elaborate
525   on how exactly the predictions are computed, whereas ViaSHAP generates predictions directly from
526   their Shapley values.

      6   C ONCLUDING R EMARKS
530
531
      We have proposed ViaSHAP, an algorithm that computes Shapley values during inference. We evalu-
      ated the performance of ViaSHAP using implementations based on the universal approximation the-
532
      orem and the Kolmogorov-Arnold representation theorem. We have presented results from a large-
533
      scale empirical investigation, in which ViaSHAP was evaluated with respect to predictive perfor-
534   mance and the accuracy of the computed Shapley values. ViaSHAP using Kolmogorov-Arnold Net-
535   works showed superior predictive performance compared to multi-layer perceptron variants while
536   competing favorably with state-of-the-art algorithms for tabular data XGBoost and Random Forests.
537   ViaSHAP estimations showed a high similarity to the ground truth Shapley values, which can be
538   controlled through the hyperparameters. One natural direction for future research is to implement
539   ViaSHAP using state-of-the-art algorithms.Another direction is to use ViaSHAP to study possible
      adversarial attacks on a predictive model.

       H     A BLATION S TUDY
1243
1244
       In this section, we explore the influence of key hyperparameters on the performance and behavior of
1245   ViaSHAP . Specifically, we investigate the effects of the scaling hyperparameter β and the number of
1246   sampled coalitions per data instance. We begin by analyzing how variations in β impact both pre-
1247   dictive performance and the accuracy of the Shapley values generated by ViaSHAP . We then examine
1248   the role of the number of sampled coalitions in model performance, followed by an evaluation of
1249   how changes in β affect the progress of the computed loss values during training. The findings pro-
1250   vide valuable insights into the robustness and efficiency of ViaSHAP under different hyperparameter
1251   settings.
1252
1253   H.1   T HE I MPACT OF S CALING H YPERPARAMETER β ON THE P ERFORMANCE OF V IA SHAP
1254
1255
       We evaluated the performance of the models trained with different β values (in equation 7), where
       exponentially increasing values are tested. The models were trained using the default hyperparame-
1256
       ter settings described in the experimental setup, except for the values of β. The AUC of the trained
1257
       models is measured on the test set, as well as the similarity of the predicted Shapley values to the
1258   ground truth. The results indicate that the predictive performance of ViaSHAP , as measured by the
1259   area under the ROC curve, remains largely unaffected by the value of β, even when β is increased ex-
1260   ponentially. On the other hand, the similarity between the computed Shapley values and the ground
1261   truth improves as β increases. However, the model struggles to learn effectively after β exceeds 200,
