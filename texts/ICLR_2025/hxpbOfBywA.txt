009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep neural networks are not resilient to bitwise errors in their parameters: even a
012            single-bit error in their memory representation can lead to significant performance
013            degradation. This susceptibility poses great challenges in deploying models on
014            emerging computing platforms, such as in-memory computing devices, where
015            frequent bitwise errors occur. Most prior work addresses this issue with hardware
016
               or system-level approaches, such as additional hardware components for checking
               a model’s integrity at runtime. However, these methods have not been widely
017
               deployed since they necessitate substantial infrastructure-wide modifications. In
018
               this paper, we study a new approach to address this challenge: we present a novel
019            training method aimed at enhancing a model’s inherent resilience to parameter
020            errors. We define a model-sensitivity metric to measure this resilience and propose
021            a training algorithm with an objective of minimizing the sensitivity. Models trained
022            with our method demonstrate increased resilience to bitwise errors in parameters,
023            particularly with a 50% reduction in the number of bits in the model parameter
024            space whose flipping leads to a 90–100% accuracy drop. Our method also aids
025            in extreme model compression, such as lower bit-width quantization or pruning
026            ∼70% of parameters, with reduced performance loss. Moreover, our method is
027
               compatible with existing strategies to mitigate this susceptibility.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Recent studies have shown that deep neural networks (DNNs) are not resilient to bitwise errors in their
032
      parameters. For example, a single-bit error on the memory representation of a model parameters can
      significantly reduce a DNN’s performance at inference (Hong et al., 2019; Rakin et al., 2019). This
033
      lack of resilience poses a challenge for deploying DNN models to emerging computing platforms,
034
      such as in-memory computing devices or neuromorphic computing platforms (Sebastian et al., 2020;
035   Xu et al., 2021; Aguirre et al., 2024), where bitwise errors in devices are frequent (Yao et al., 2020a).
036
037   Most prior work addresses this reliability issue by developing defensive mechanisms at the hardware-
038
      level or system-level (Bennett et al., 2021; Rakin et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021; Di Dio et al., 2023; Zhou
      et al., 2023a;b; Liu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023). While having demonstrated their effectiveness,
039
      these approaches are often difficult to implement in practice as they require additional hardware
040
      components or updates to system software, necessitating infrastructure-wide changes.
041
042   In this work, we study an orthogonal approach to address this resilience problem. We ask: How can
043   we train models to have increased resilience to bitwise errors in parameters? No prior work has
044
      studied solutions to enhance the natural resilience of a model to bitwise errors in its parameters. Such
      resilient models will also benefit model compression techniques, e.g., quantization, which involve
045
      optimal parameter variations (LeCun et al., 1989). Moreover, when combined with existing solutions,
046
      it will complement them and enable models to maintain stable performance in error-prone hardware.
047
048   Contributions. First, we present a novel training algorithm that enhances a DNN’s resilience to
049   bitwise errors in their parameters. We focus on a model’s second-order property—the sharpness—that
050
      can approximate the sensitivity of a model’s performance to its parameter variations. We empirically
      test various approaches to reducing the sharpness, from training with second-order optimizers (Yao
051
      et al., 2021a) to training algorithms specifically designed to reduce the value (Foret et al., 2021).
052
053   Our extensive analysis shows that using the Hessian trace as a loss function is the most effective
      in decreasing a model’s sharpness. However, computing the full Hessian matrix makes the training

054
      process computationally intractable, especially when training ImageNet-scale models. To address
055   this challenge, we also develop an optimized training strategy that minimizes only the largest-p% of
056   the Hessian eigenvalues during training (for example, p is 10–50% in our evaluation).
057
058
      Second, we comprehensively evaluate the effectiveness of our approach across multiple datasets
      and network architectures including ones used in prior studies. We adapt the systematic resilience
059
      analysis framework developed by Hong et al. (2019): the framework examines a single-bit error—an
060
      atomic error that can occur in a DNN’s parameter representation in memory. It causes all possible
061   single-bit errors one-by-one and measure the accuracy on a validation set each time.
062
063   We demonstrate that our training algorithm significantly enhances a model’s resilience to (individual)
064
      single-bit errors to its parameters. The number of parameters whose perturbations can cause the
      accuracy drop over 10% are reduced by 5–15%. Particularly, we reduce by half the number of
065
      parameters when a single-bit error in them causes the accuracy drop of 90–100%. ImageNet models,
066
      fine-tuned only a few layers with our Hessian loss, show a similar decrease in such parameters.
067
068   We conduct an in-depth analysis of the increased resilience achieved by our approach. In our analysis
069   of visualized loss landscapes, we show that the sharpness is greatly decreased across all the layers
070
      in a model. We find that our approach is particularly effective on fully-connected layers and the
      convolutional layers in architectural blocks without skip connections. Moreover, we show that the
071
      numerical changes in parameter values required to cause a significant accuracy drop has increased.
072
073   Third, we also demonstrate that the resilience enhanced by our approach can benefit the techniques
074   that rely on optimal brain damage, such as quantization (Fiesler et al., 1990) or pruning (Han et al.,
075   2015). Models trained with our algorithm achieve better test accuracy than regularly-trained models,
076
      especially when lower bit-width quantization (e.g., 4- or 2-bit) is applied. In pruning, these models
      preserve test accuracy even with extreme sparsity values (i.e., removing ∼70% of parameters).
077
078   Fourth, we discuss the compatibility of our approach with existing defensive mechanisms for im-
079   proving DNN resilience to bitwise errors in model parameters. For each of the hardware-level or
080   system-level mechanisms, we conceptually demonstrate how the models trained with our approach
081   can be combined with them and offer a synergy while reducing their deployment overheads.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
084
085   LeCun et al. (1989) demonstrates the resilience of DNNs to optimal brain damage: one can remove a
086   large portion of the model parameters without causing any significant accuracy drop. This property
087
      has enabled the success of model compression techniques, such as quantization (Fiesler et al., 1990;
      Morgan et al., 1991; Courbariaux et al., 2015) and pruning (Hassibi and Stork, 1992; Han et al.,
088
      2015; Li et al., 2017). Prior work further leverages this property to safeguard DNNs against potential
089
      threats, such as adversarial examples (Zhou et al., 2018) while preserving the performance.
090
091   Recent work has also warned of the possibility of terminal brain damage (Hong et al., 2019; Yao
092   et al., 2020b), where even a few perturbations to parameters can cause significant performance loss. It
093
      is also important to note that the prevalence of such failures: half of model parameters, when subject
      to a bitwise corruption, cause the accuracy to drop over 10% (Hong et al., 2019). Beyond its impact
094
      on model accuracy, follow-up works have shown that by perturbing parameters in specific ways, one
095
      can achieve various adversarial outcomes (Rakin et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2021; Rakin et al., 2022).
096
097   There has been significant effort in mitigating the impact of adversarial perturbations on model
098   parameters through hardware (Kim et al., 2014; Bennett et al., 2021; Saileshwar et al., 2022; Di Dio
099
      et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023b) and software (Liu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2021; Konoth et al., 2018)
      level defenses. Hardware-level defenses utilize proactive row refreshing (Kim et al., 2014), in-
100
      DRAM counter-based mitigation (Bennett et al., 2021), risky-row swapping (Saileshwar et al., 2022),
101
      error-correction-codes (ECC) for swap triggering (Di Dio et al., 2023) and utilizing lock table for
102   high-risk rows inside a memory device (Zhou et al., 2023b) to mitigate adversarial bit-flipping on
103   model parameters. System-level defenses use techniques, such as luring adversary to manipulate
104   “honeypot" parameters (Liu et al., 2023), checksum-based runtime checks to detect changes in model
105   parameters (Li et al., 2021) and employing data-row isolation to safeguard a model’s parameters from
106   fault attacks (Konoth et al., 2018). A few works explore techniques for improving thefault tolerance
107   of DNNs. Buldu et al. (2022) adapts adversarial training to train models under bitwise errors. Chitsaz
      et al. (2023) proposes learnable quantization to limit the impact of bitwise errors on DNN inference.

      4.1   A DNN’ S S ENSITIVITY TO PARAMETER VARIATIONS
149
150   We first focus on functions that can quantify a model’s sensitivity to parameter value variations. Our
151   goal here is to use these functions as optimization objectives to minimize sensitivity during training.
152   Suppose that a model f uses a loss function L. The rate of change in the loss in a random direction v
153   in the parameter space can be expressed as the gradient ∂L/∂v. This value encodes how sensitive a
154   model will be when its parameter values are changed along the direction of v. During optimization,
155   the training algorithm seeks a minimum that reduces this rate of change. However, standard training
156   does not inherently offer the resilience of a model to parameter variations caused by bitwise errors.
157   Second-order derivative as a sensitivity metric. The loss curvature of a neural nework is typically
158   super-linear, forming a convex hull around the local minima in the parameter space (Li et al., 2018).
159   We thus use the second-order derivative of the loss function in a random direction v as a metric to
160   quantify a model’s sensitivity to parameter variations. We note that this property has been used in
161   prior work (Jiang et al., 2020; Mulayoff and Michaeli, 2020; Li et al., 2018; Keskar et al., 2017;
      Neyshabur et al., 2017) as a measure of the sharpness or flatness of the loss landscape. Few prior


                                                                                                       3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      works (Foret et al., 2021; Dong et al., 2019; 2020; Yang et al., 2022) have also used second-order
163   property of DNNs to improve generalization, accuracy or model compression. Most prior work
164   studies the interaction between the sharpness and the generalization of a neural network, a few works
165   studies its connection to model resilience to bitwise errors in parameters.
166
      Our work utilizes the Hessian trace, the sum of the eigenvalues of the Hessian matrix. This requires
167
      computing the Hessian matrix, the second-order partial derivatives of a loss function, with respect to
168
      its parameters. Recent work has also leveraged the Hessian trace to measure the quantizability of a
169   neural network (Dong et al., 2019; 2020; Yao et al., 2021b), and we follow this insight to measure
170   sensitivity. Due to the large number of model parameters (typically ranging from millions to billions),
171   computing the Hessian trace directly is computationally intractable. We use the Hutchinson’s method
172   (Bekas et al., 2007) to approximate the Hessian trace over a number of random vectors v. Following
173   these prior practices, we use the Hessian trace to quantify sensitivity to parameter value variations.

284   5.1    E NHANCED M ODEL R ESILIENCE TO B ITWISE E RRORS IN PARAMETERS
285
286   Methodology. We first compare our approach with prior works and determined that ours is most
287   effective in improving resilience against bitwise errors in parameters (see Appendix B.1). Then we
288   perform a quantitative analysis of the enhanced resilience of a model to bitwise errors in parameters.
289   We extensively examine a model’s resilience under a single bitwise corruption in its parameter space
290   because: (1) this approach allows us to test the most sensitive cases under atomic perturbations
291   that can occur in models deployed to real-world devices, and (2) it also enables us to simulate the
292
      numerical value changes in all parameters under the smallest perturbation. We report the accuracy of
      a model to demonstrate that our training algorithm does not harm its generalization ability. We report
293
      the resilience of a DNN model by the number of erratic parameters that contain at least one bit whose
294
      error can result in an accuracy drop of over 10% as defined by (Hong et al., 2019). We also report
295   their ratio to the total number of parameters. As the definition suggests, lesser erratic parameters
296   in a DNN constitutes greater resilience against bitwise errors in parameters. For MNIST (Base and
297   LeNet architecture), we test all bitwise errors in the model parameters and found that erratic bits are
298   only present in the exponents (Appendix B.8). Complete analysis of the model requires infeasible
299   computation time, for CIFAR model (ResNet18) ≈503 days and for ImageNet model (ResNet50)
300   ≈1172 days. Based on our initial findings and the prohibitive computation time, we implemented
301   techniques to accelerate the process. In CIFAR-10, we examine the exponent bits and found only
302   few bits other than the MSB are responsible for RAD over 10%. For our ImageNet model, we test
303
      only the most significant bit of the exponent in a randomly chosen 50% of parameters in all the
      convolutions layers and all the parameters in the fully-connected layers.

484
485   Increase in computational demands. We evaluate the overhead of Hessian-aware training in terms
      of actual training wall-time measured in PyTorch on a NVIDIA Tesla V100 GPU. In Appendix B.7


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      we present our results. Hessian-aware training incurs overhead that scales with the size of the model;
487   a 4–6× times increase in computations for MNIST models, and a 10× times increase in overhead for
488   CIFAR-10 models. Existing works utilizing second-order properties during training take a completely
489   different approach compute the Hessian and its eigenvalues: they employ weight perturbations (Foret
490   et al., 2021) or only the trace approximation (Yao et al., 2021a) to minimize sharpness of the loss
491   landscape. The increase in computation in our approach is primarily attributed to the large Hessian
492   and eigenvalues we need to compute with respect to model parameters, which is not optimized for
493   popular deep-learning frameworks. To reduce the computational overhead during training, we employ
494   a layer sampling technique. As prior work identifies the last layers to be most susceptible to bitwise
495
      errors (Hong et al., 2019), we believe only computing Hessian trace on last few layers can aid resilient
      model training. Our results for large-scale models, such as ResNet50 in ImageNet, show that this
496
      technique significantly reduces the computational overhead from 10× times to 1.18× times, being
497
      equally effective in enhancing model resilience. We leave further optimization as future work.
498
499   Now we discuss defensive mechanisms proposed by the community that can be integrated with our
500   approach to further enhance the resilience of models against bitwise errors in parameters. Our discus-
501
      sion particularly focuses on hardware-level and system-level approaches, which can complement the
      models trained with our Hessian-aware training by providing an additional layer of resilience.
502
503   Integration with system-level defenses. Liu et al. (2023) have proposed NeuroPot, which lures an
504   adversary into manipulating parameters whose perturbation does not lead to a significant accuracy
505   drop. Since our approach increases the number of unimportant parameters, the models we train are
506   well-suited for NeuroPot. Li et al. (2021) have proposed a checksum-based defense, which stores
507
      golden signature for a group of weights and compares this signature at runtime with the current
      model signature. Our approach can leverage this scheme by storing the golden signature of erratic
508
      weights, thus enhancing resilience further during runtime. Konoth et al. (2018) have also focused on
509
      utilizing data row isolation to protect a model against bitwise corruption to its parameters. Because
510   we reduce the number of erratic parameters, the number of data rows that need to be isolated in their
511   approach can also be greatly reduced when combined with our training method.
512
513
      Integration with hardware-level defenses. Many hardware-level defenses are designed to mitigate
      RowHammer (Kim et al., 2014), a software-induced attack that causes a targeted DRAM row to
514
      leak capacitance by repeatedly accessing its neighboring rows. Kim et al. (2014) have proposed a
515
      defense that proactively refreshes rows that are frequently accessed, as they are at higher risk of being
516   targeted by the attack. Panopticon (Bennett et al., 2021) leverages a similar idea: it employs hardware
517   counters for each data row in DRAM and refreshes the rows when the counter reaches a predefined
518   threshold. Instead of refreshing the rows at high risks, Saileshwar et al. (2022) propose swapping
519   them with safe memory regions. Di Dio et al. (2023) use the error correction codes as a mechanism
520   for triggering such swapping. DRAM-Locker (Zhou et al., 2023b) leverages a lock-table in SRAM.
521   If the addresses of the high-risk rows are stored in the lock-table, any access this addresses without
522   the unlock command will be denied. As we can see, these mechanisms protect data rows at high risk
523   of being targeted. Our work reduces the number of data rows in a model whose perturbations lead to
524
      significant accuracy loss, and therefore, potentially decreasing their performance overheads.
525
526   7    C ONCLUSION
527
528   Our work proposes a novel training algorithm that reduces a model’s sensitivity to parameter varia-
529   tions, thereby enhancing its resilience when deployed in error-prone computing environments. We
530   focus on the model’s second-order property, the Hessian trace, and design an objective function to
531   directly minimize it during training. We extensively compare our approach with existing methods
532   for reducing model sensitivity and demonstrate our effectiveness. We evaluate our approach by
533
      testing a model’s performance under single-bit errors to its parameter representation in memory.
      Our method reduces the number of erratic parameters by 10%, decreasing those whose corruption
534
      causes a 90–100% RAD drop by almost half. Our method is particularly effective on fully connected
535
      layers, and results in flatter loss surfaces. We also show improved performance at an extreme pruning
536   and quantization ratios. Our method is complementary to existing hardware-level or system-level
537   approaches to protecting models against parameter-corruption attacks. We finally discuss the potential
538   synergy of combining these mechanisms with our method. We hope our work will inspire future
539   work on the safe deployment of deep neural networks in emerging computing platforms.

      Here we describe our experimental setup in detail. All experiments use Python v3.11.42 with Pytorch
759   v2.1.03 and CUDA v12.14 for GPU acceleration. We run our experiments on two systems: (1) a node
760   with a 48-core Intel Xeon Processor, 768GB of memory, and 8 NVIDIA A40 GPUs. (2) a node with
761   a 56-core Intel Xeon Processor, and 8 Nvidia Tesla H100 GPUs. We achieve a substantial speed-up
762   in running our evaluation script by utilizing the parameter-level parallelism on the two systems.
763
      We use the following hyper-parameters to train/fine-tune our models.
764
765   MNIST. We use a network architecture (Base) and LeNet in prior work (Hong et al., 2019). For
766   regular training, we used an SGD optimizer with a learning rate of 0.1 (adjusting by 0.25 every 10
767   epochs), batch size of 64, and 0.8 momentum. We train our models for 40 epochs. To train the same
768
      network using our Hessian-aware training, we used λ (line 16 of algorithm 1) value of 1 as per our

773   every 15 epochs. We use the RMSProp optimizer and λ value of 10−2 to train the same model with
774
      our training method.
775   ImageNet. We take the ResNet50 architecture pretrained on ImageNet (available at Torchvision
