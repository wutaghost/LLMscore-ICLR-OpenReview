011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Multi-lingual ability transfer has become increasingly important for the broad ap-
014            plication of large language models (LLMs). Existing work highly relies on train-
015            ing with the multi-lingual ability-related data, which may be not available for low-
016
               resource languages. To solve it, we propose a Multi-lingual Ability Extraction and
               Transfer approach, named as MAET. Our key idea is to decompose and extract
017
               language-agnostic ability-related weights from LLMs, and transfer them across
018
               different languages by simple addition and subtraction operations without train-
019            ing. Specially, our MAET consists of the extraction and transfer stages. In the
020            extraction stage, we firstly locate key neurons that are highly related to specific
021            abilities, and then employ them to extract the transferable ability-specific weights.
022            In the transfer stage, we further select the ability-related parameter tensors, and
023            design the merging strategy based on the linguistic and ability specific weights, to
024            build the multi-lingual ability-enhanced LLM. To demonstrate the effectiveness of
025            our proposed approach, we conduct extensive experiments on mathematical and
026            scientific tasks in both high-resource lingual and low-resource lingual scenarios.
027
               Experiment results have shown that MAET can effectively and efficiently extract
               and transfer the advanced abilities, and outperform training-based baselines meth-
028
               ods. Our code and data will be publicly released.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Large language models (LLMs) have recently shown remarkable performance on various general
034
      tasks, e.g., text generation and question answering (Zhao et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023; Dubey et al.,
035
      2024). Despite the success, LLMs are still struggling to solve complex tasks (e.g., mathematical
036   reasoning), which require LLMs to possess specific advanced abilities (e.g., deductive reasoning)
037   and knowledge (e.g., mathematical theory) (Yue et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2022). To address it and
038   further improve LLMs, existing work either collects the related data to train LLMs (Du et al., 2024;
039   Chen et al., 2024a), or merges the parameters of existing well-performed LLMs to transfer their
040   advanced abilities into one single model (Ilharco et al., 2023; Yadav et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024a).
041
      Despite the success, it is not easy to collect sufficient training corpus or well-trained LLMs related
042   to specific abilities, especially in multi-lingual scenarios. Especially, some popular languages (e.g.,
043   English) have dominated the linguistic expressions of the open web data, and the amount of available
044   domain-specific data for low-resource languages (e.g., Bengali or Telugu) is highly limited (Maguer-
045   esse et al., 2020; Patzelt, 2024; Mirashi et al., 2024). Fortunately, existing work (Zhao et al., 2024;
046   Schäfer et al., 2024) has revealed that the learned knowledge from one language by LLMs could be
047   inherited and leveraged by other languages. For example, Llama-series LLMs are trained mainly on
048   English texts, while they can also solve the tasks based on other languages. Such a finding has been
049   widely explored in either improving the overall performance of multi-lingual LLMs (Schäfer et al.,
050
      2024) or enhancing fine-grained knowledge (Chen et al., 2024a). However, the related work mostly
      relies on training with ability-related corpus in the target language, which is not always available for
051
      low-resource languages.
052
053   To conduct a more effective ability transfer, our idea is to learn and extract the “ability-specific
      weights” that preserves the knowledge about specific abilities for the LLM. If such ability-specific


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      and language-specific weights could be decomposed, it is achievable to transfer the required abilities
055   into target languages by just combining the corresponding weights, even building a multi-lingual
056   ability-enhanced LLM like building blocks. Based on this idea, in this paper, we propose a Multi-
057   lingual Ability Extraction and Transfer approach, named as MAET. Specifically, our approach
058   consists of two major stages, i.e., ability extraction and transfer stage. In the extraction stage, we first
059   locate the abilities-related neurons and leverage related corpus in a reference language to continually
060   pre-train the LLM on these identified neurons. Then, based on the LLM trained on the general
061   corpus, we devise the formula to extract the ability-specific weights. In the transfer stage, we utilize
062   the ability-related weights to select related parameter tensors, and design a specific model merging
063
      strategy by interpolating linguistic and ability-specific weights. In our approach, we only need
      ability-specific corpus from any rich-resource language and general multi-lingual corpus, which can
064
      effectively mitigate the data scarcity issues in low-resource languages.
065
066   To assess the effectiveness of our approach, we conduct the evaluation based on two comprehensive
067   reasoning benchmarks, namely Multi-lingual Grade School Math (MGSM) (Shi et al., 2023) and
068   science tasks from multi-lingual MMLU (Lai et al., 2023) as the evaluation benchmarks. Accord-
069
      ing to the evaluation results, the proposed approach MAET outperforms other competitive baseline
      methods (e.g., continual pre-training (Gururangan et al., 2020) and model merging methods with
070
      task vectors (Ilharco et al., 2023), achieving the 9.1% relative improvement compared to the base
071
      LLM. In addition, our approach can work well with relatively fewer training data, demonstrating an
072   improved efficiency in practice. In conclusion, our contribution can be summarized as follows,
073
074   (1) Our research has found that advanced abilities can be extracted from the single-lingual corpus and
075
      transferred across languages without the multi-lingual ability-related corpus, enabling to efficiently
      empower LLMs with special advanced abilities.
076
077   (2) We propose an effective and efficient approach named MAET, which first identifies and extracts
078   the ability-related weights in LLMs and then only leverages simple addition and subtraction opera-
079   tions to build a multi-lingual ability-enhanced LLM.
080   (3) Our approach MAET achieves better performance than the competitive baseline methods (e.g.,
081   continual pre-training and model merging with task vector) in multi-lingual complex reasoning
082   tasks, including mathematical reasoning tasks and scientific reasoning tasks.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
085
086
      We introduce the related work from the following three perspectives:
087
088
      Continual Pre-training. Although LLMs have shown remarkable performance on various down-
089   stream work, they still struggle in several specific tasks, e.g., complex reasoning tasks (Paster et al.,
090   2024; Shao et al., 2024) or low-resource lingual scenarios (Hedderich et al., 2021; Panchbhai &
091   Pankanti, 2021). To adapt LLMs pre-trained on the general corpus to multi-lingual scenarios or spe-
092   cific tasks, existing work (Luo et al., 2022; Taylor et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024a)
093   has collected the corresponding corpus to continually pre-train (CPT) LLMs. During the continual
094   pre-training process, the mixture strategy between the general corpus and the ability-related corpus
095   should be carefully considered to prevent hurting the general abilities of LLMs (Ye et al., 2024; Xie
096   et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024a; Siriwardhana et al., 2024). However, previous study Chang et al.
097
      (2024); Lu et al. (2023) has pointed out that it is difficult to collect the required corpus, especially for
      low-resource language corpus. Therefore, synthesizing data from powerful LLMs is widely utilized
098
      to expand the task-specific training corpus (Chen et al., 2021b; Yu et al., 2024b; Zhou et al., 2024a).
099
      Besides, because of the limitation of computation resources, a series of approaches (Hu et al., 2022;
100   Li & Liang, 2021; Dettmers et al., 2023) only train several parameters to reduce the expenses. In this
101   work, we focus on adapting LLMs to multilingual complex reasoning scenarios through continually
102   pre-training LLMs on the single-lingual task-specific corpus.
103
104   Knowledge Editing. According to lottery ticket hypothesis (Frankle & Carbin, 2019), training a
105   small number of model parameters will achieve comparable or even better performance on down-
106   stream tasks. Existing study (Du et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b; Gong et al., 2024) has leveraged
107   the inner information of LLMs, e.g., gradient or cosine similarity between different hidden states, to
      select and train the related sub-network. Besides, the probe (i.e., a newly initialized parameter) can


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      be implemented to detect the knowledge of LLMs and process targeted repair (Wang et al., 2024a;
109   Jiang et al., 2024). However, Since these approaches need to calculate and select a sub-network of
110   each training instance, which might cause the instability of the training process, several study (Chen
111   et al., 2024b; Zhang et al., 2024b) pointed out that the task-related sub-network can be determined
112   before the training process, and only updating the value of the corresponding neurons can achieve
113   better performance. In this work, we focus on editing the task-specific neurons of LLMs to improve
114   the corresponding capacities in multi-lingual scenarios.
115
116   Model Merging. Given that the CPT process will bring huge computational expenses, previous
117   work leveraged model merging techniques to integrate different abilities (e.g., mathematical reason-
118   ing and code synthesizing) into one model (Yang et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024b; Stoica et al., 2024).
119
      During the merging process, the interference between different LLMs might be conflict with each
      other and affect the final performance. Therefore, researchers proposed the clip (Yadav et al., 2023)
120
      or randomly dropout (Yu et al., 2024a) mechanism to mitigate the performance decrease. Moreover,
121
      the selection of the hyper-parameters (e.g., weight of each model) is the challenge of the model
122   merging process, and previous work (Zhou et al., 2024b; Matena & Raffel, 2022) utilized the inner
123   parameters of LLMs or external matrixes to determine the hyper-parameters. Furthermore, a series
124   of work has studied improving the reasoning ability of LLMs in non-English scenarios by merging
125   the reasoning-specialized model and multi-lingual model (Huang et al., 2024; Yoon et al., 2024).
126   Inspired by the above work, we try to locate the task-related sub-networks of LLMs and transfer the
127   advanced abilities.
128
129   3     P RELIMINARY
130
131   Despite that LLMs exhibit excellent performance on general tasks, they still have limited advanced
132   abilities, e.g., mathematical and scientific reasoning abilities. A typical approach to enhancing these
133   abilities is to continually pre-train (CPT) LLMs with ability-related corpus. However, such training
134   data might not always be available or sufficient, especially for minor domains (e.g., Bengali). In
135   this work, we focus on the task of ability extraction and transfer by continual pre-training and
136   merging LLMs. Concretely, LLMs are trained on collected corpus from a certain domain, and we
137
      aim to only transfer its learned advanced capabilities to another target domain (Zhuang et al., 2021;
      Farahani et al., 2021) without further training efforts. In this work, we mainly study the cross-lingual
138
      scene where the linguistic-agnostic advanced ability and linguistic abilities should be extracted and
139
      transferred, to help build a unified multi-lingual ability-enhanced LLM.
140
141   Formally, for a certain ability 𝐴𝑖 and a set of languages 𝐿 = {𝐿 0 , 𝐿 1 , . . . , 𝐿 𝑛 }, we assume that the
142   general corpus of all languages can be collected, denoted as Cgeneral = {C𝐿0 , C𝐿1 , . . . , C𝐿𝑛 }, while
143
      the ability-related corpus is only available in language 𝐿 0 (i.e., English), denoted as C𝐿0 , 𝐴𝑖 . Based
      on the above corpora, our goal is to extract and transfer the advanced ability 𝐴𝑖 from language 𝐿 0
144
      and linguistic abilities from other languages 𝐿 1 , . . . , 𝐿 𝑛 , into a unified LLM.

147   4     A PPROACH
148
149   In this section, we propose the Multi-lingual Ability Extraction and Transfer approach, named as
150
      MAET, which can effectively transfer the advanced abilities from single-lingual LLMs, to build a
      multi-lingual ability-enhanced LLM. The key motivation of our approach is to identify and extract
151
      ability-related neurons or weights, and transfer the target abilities into a LLM in an efficient way.
152
      The overall framework of MAET is presented in Figure 1.

158   i.e., key neurons locating and ability-related parameter weights learning, which are detailed in the
159   following.
160
161   Locating the Key Neurons. The gradient of each neuron in LLMs can be utilized to estimate its cor-
      relation degree with specific task ability , we select those with high gradient values as key neurons.

308        (e.g., CPT and TV) require the multi-lingual ability-related corpus (i.e., ability-related corpus is re-
309        quired for each language) for training the LLM parameters. In comparison, our proposed approach
310        only trains and modifies the ability-related parameters, which can efficiently focus on enhancing the
311        specific ability. A major novelty of our work is that we identify the key units and implement the
312
           sparse update in the model training and merging procedure, which can effectively decompose, ex-
           tract, and transfer the abilities of LLMs. In addition, compared with the LoRA-based methods (i.e.,
313
           LoRA and MoL) that also sparsely update the LLM parameters, our approach selectively updates
314
           the ability-related neurons, while LoRA-based methods utilize the low-rank matrices to approximate
315        the original parameters.

528   6   C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this paper, we presented MAET, which extracted the advanced ability-related weights from the
531   LLM and supported simple addition and subtraction operations to transfer the ability across different
532   languages. Concretely, MAET included two main stages, i.e., extraction and transfer. For the extrac-
533   tion stage, we located the key neurons and extracted the ability-related weights. Then, in the transfer
534   stage, we identified the key parameter tensors and leveraged them to transfer the advanced ability
535   into other LLMs. In this process, the multi-lingual ability-related training corpus is not required,
536
      and the experimental results have shown that our approach outperformed competitive baselines.
537   As future work, we will consider better methods to identify the ability-related sub-network to de-
538   compose the abilities of LLMs and utilize an automated approach to determine the hyper-parameter.
539   Besides, we will implement MAET on larger-scale models, and scenarios with more languages and
      requiring more abilities to evaluate its effectiveness.

       Concepts                     Meaning
919
920                                 Neuron refers to one of the trainable values of the tensors in LLMs. As pre-
                                    vious work pointed out (Xu et al., 2024a), different neurons might control the
921
                                    different abilities of LLMs. Following this finding, in our work, we define the
922                                 neurons that control the specific ability as the ”Key Neurons”. Key neurons
       Key Neurons
923                                 can be regarded as a set without duplication, and a neuron belonging to the
924                                 set means that this neuron can control the specific ability (Chen et al., 2024b).
925                                 During the following training process, only the neurons belonging to the key
                                    neurons will be trained and optimized.
926
927                                 Ability-related weights refer to the value of the whole neuron in LLM, which
                                    can represent the corresponding ability of LLM (Yu et al., 2024a; Ilharco et al.,
928
                                    2023). In MAET, we obtain the ability-related weights through equation 2.
929    Ability-related Weights
                                    The ability-related weights contain the value of all neurons. Since only the key
930                                 neurons will be trained during the training process, the value of the neurons
931                                 not belonging to key neurons is zero in the ability-related weights.
932                                 Ability-related tensors can be regarded as a set of LLM tensors, which is re-
933                                 lated to the corresponding ability. Previous work has studied how the LLM
934                                 layers influence the ability (Cheng et al., 2024). Different from key neu-
935    Ability-related Tensors      rons, ability-related tensors focus on higher-level information, integrating the
                                    sparse neurons into a coarser-grained element (Xiao et al., 2024). A tensor be-
936
                                    longing to the ability-related tensors denotes that this tensor is highly related
937                                 to the corresponding ability and can control this ability.

962   A surge of work (Zhang et al., 2024b; Xiao et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024) has pointed out that LLMs
963   sparsely activate the specific sub-modules to perform corresponding tasks. Based on these findings,
964   we conduct empirical experiments to explore whether the specific sub-module, which is related to
965   advanced abilities, can be extracted and transferred. We utilize the forum corpus (i.e., Zhihu for
966   Chinese forum corpus and Reddit for English forum corpus) to continually pre-train LLMs, and
967   then assess the training performance (i.e., the value of loss function) and similarity of LLM neurons.
968   The forum corpus can be considered as containing the question-answering (QA) ability, which is
969   necessary and important for LLMs. The results from Figure 4a have shown that only training the top
970   5% relevant neurons of LLMs can achieve the lower training loss and fit into the training set more
971   quickly, indicating that LLMs contain the sub-module corresponding to the QA ability. Moreover,
      from Figure 4b and Figure 4c, we can observe that the LLM trained on Zhihu has shown higher

1059   F    L IMITATIONS
1060
1061   In this section, we discuss the limitations of our work. First, we only implement our approach MAET
1062   on 8B LLMs (i.e., LLaMA-3 8B), and do not adopt the LLMs with larger scales (e.g., 13B or 70B
1063   LLMs) in the experiment, due to the limitation of computational resources. We will test the effec-
1064
       tiveness of our approach on these LLMs in the future. Second, we only evaluate our approach on
       two downstream tasks (i.e., mathematical and scientific reasoning tasks) in multi-lingual scenarios.
1065
       Although they are challenging and widely-used testbeds, it is still meaningful to verify our methods
1066
       on other tasks. Whereas, as we test the performance on diverse high-resource and low-resource lan-
1067   guages, it can also provide comprehensive performance estimation for our approach in multi-lingual
1068   scenarios. Third, our approach is a general method for ability transferring across different domains,
1069   but in this work, we only consider the multi-lingual scenarios and obtain a multi-lingual LLM with
1070   the specific ability being enhanced. Forth, our approach also relies on continual pre-training the
1071   LLM. Although the training corpus is not very large, it also increases the cost. Fortunately, after
1072   we obtain the pre-trained weights, our following steps only need simple addition and subtraction
1073   operations for ability transferring, which is flexible for online deployment and application. In future
1074   work, we will focus on reducing the data requirement for the pre-training corpus and also improving
1075
       the weights extracting efficiency. Finally, we do not consider the potential risk and ethics issues
       that might hurt the alignment of LLMs when using our approach. Actually, our approach is also
1076
       applicable to transfer the alignment ability across languages. We will investigate to it in the future.
