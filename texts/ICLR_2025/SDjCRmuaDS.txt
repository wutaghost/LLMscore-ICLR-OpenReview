011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Deep generative models for molecular discovery have become a very popular
014            choice in new high-throughput screening paradigms. These models have been
015            developed inheriting from the advances in natural language processing and com-
016            puter vision, achieving ever greater results. However, generative molecular mod-
017
               elling has unique challenges that are often overlooked. Chemical validity, inter-
               pretability of the generation process and flexibility to variable molecular sizes are
018
               among some of the remaining challenges for generative models in computational
019
               materials design. In this work, we propose an autoregressive approach that de-
020            composes molecular generation into a sequence of discrete and interpretable steps
021            using molecular fragments as units, a ‘molecular story’. Enforcing chemical rules
022            in the stories guarantees the chemical validity of the generated molecules, the
023            discrete sequential steps of a molecular story makes the process transparent im-
024            proving interpretability, and the autoregressive nature of the approach allows the
025            size of the molecule to be a decision of the model. We demonstrate the validity of
026            the approach in a multi-target inverse design of electroactive organic compounds,
027            focusing on the target properties of solubility, redox potential, and synthetic ac-
028
               cessibility. Our results show that the model can effectively bias the generation
               distribution according to the prompted multi-target objective.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Deep generative models (DGMs) have become a popular choice in new high throughput screening
      (HTS) paradigms (Westermayr et al., 2023; Ortega Ochoa et al., 2023). Within new HTS, generative
035
      models are used to create an initial pool of candidates subject to some target properties (Sanchez-
036
      Lengeling & Aspuru-Guzik, 2018). This initial pool is then filtered in sequential steps of increasing
037   computational expense, from machine-learning surrogate models (Schütt et al., 2017; Tsubaki &
038   Mizoguchi, 2020a) to classical computing methods, such as density functional theory (DFT; Kohn
039   & Sham, 1965). These generative models vary in the molecular representation used, e.g., SMILES
040   strings (Gómez-Bombarelli et al., 2018; Lim et al., 2018; Popova et al., 2018; Podda et al., 2020),
041   graph-based (Jin et al., 2019; 2020) or point sets (Hoogeboom et al., 2022b; Gebauer et al., 2022;
042   Guan et al., 2023; Schneuing et al., 2023; Qiang et al., 2023), and modelling approach, e.g., re-
043   inforcement learning-based (Popova et al., 2018; Simm et al., 2020a;b), variational autoencoders
044   (VAEs; Gómez-Bombarelli et al., 2018; Lim et al., 2018), generative adversarial network (GANs;
045
      Cao & Kipf, 2022), diffusion models (Hoogeboom et al., 2022b; Guan et al., 2023; Schneuing et al.,
      2023; Qiang et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2023), normalizing flows
046
      (Satorras et al., 2022) or flow matching (Dunn & Koes, 2024). Despite the diversity of models, there
047
      are challenges unique to computational materials modelling that have been often overlooked:
048
049   (A) Multi-step vs one-shot generation: Generating a molecule in a single step risks making the
050           generation process opaque. Generating a molecule in sequential steps allows spending
051           more computation per step and makes the generation more transparent. Multi-step genera-
052           tive models for materials include Molgym (Simm et al., 2020a), diffusion models (Hooge-
053           boom et al., 2022b; Qiang et al., 2023), flow matching (Dunn & Koes, 2024), VAE-based
              models with an autoregressive decoder (Jin et al., 2019; 2020), and other autorregressive

054
               models (Gebauer et al., 2022; You et al., 2018; Liao et al., 2020; Xie et al., 2021; Maziarz
055            et al., 2024).
056
057   (B) The size of the molecule is fixed during the generation process: A number of models that do
058           satisfy the desirable feature (A) fixed the size of the molecule during the generation process.
059           We believe the size of the molecule should be a choice of the model during the generation
060
              process. Some models that do take this into account include the graph-based, VAE variant
              models with autoregressive decoders (Jin et al., 2019; 2020), G-Schnet (Gebauer et al.,
061
              2022) which operates on point sets, MARS (Xie et al., 2021), MoLeR (Maziarz et al.,
062
              2024).
063
064   (C) Low chemical validity of generated molecules: Most often, chemical validity checks are en-
065          forced only at the end of the generation process, resulting in a lower percentage of chemical
066          valid molecules. Incorporating chemical checks during the multi-step generation process
067
             allows to identify chemical violations before the molecule is completed, thus avoiding con-
             tinuing the generation of chemically invalid molecules. Models that do satisfy this desirable
068
             feature are the graph-based AE variant models with autoregressive decoders JTNN, Hier-
069
             VAE (Jin et al., 2019; 2020), MARS (Xie et al., 2021), MoLeR (Maziarz et al., 2024).
070
071   (D) Coarse-graining molecules: Molecules naturally exhibit hierarchical structure, they often can
072           be decomposed into molecular fragments that can be treated as units in themselves. Ex-
073           ploiting the hierarchical nature of molecules helps scale deep learning models to larger
074           molecules (Wang & Gómez-Bombarelli, 2019), something that has remained a notable
075
              challenge. Models like JTNN and HierVAE use molecular fragments as units, and this
              approach has also been adapted for diffusion models (Qiang et al., 2023).
076
077   (E) Incorporating 3D information: A model that has no access to 3D geometry information of
078           the molecule will struggle to conditionally generate other structures when the target prop-
079           erty depends on the 3D geometry. Models like JTNN, HierVAE, MARS or MoLeR do
080           not include this information in the molecule representation. This has remained a notable
081           challenge for autoregressive models, as discussed in (Voloboev, 2024).
082
083
      Our proposed model is designed to satisfy the five desirable requirements in the most general formu-
      lation we could think: a purely autoregressive (no encoder), semi-order-agnostic, multi-step, multi-
084
      property generation using symmetry-aware molecular fragments and 3D geometry of a non-fixed
085
      size molecule with enforced chemistry sanitation during generation.

089
090
      Our work builds on ideas from the aforementioned studies, but it is most closely related to JTNN
      (Jin et al., 2019) and HierVAE (Jin et al., 2020), particularly the latter. HierVAE is a hierarchical
091
      graph VAE-based model operating on molecular fragments using an autoregressive decoder to build
092
      molecules in sequential steps. Similarly, we enforce chemical validity during the generation process,
093   and, like JTNN and HierVAE, we use a coarse-graining procedure to extract molecular fragments.
094   In summary, our contributions are:
095
096          • We propose a semi-order-agnostic autoregressive model that grows molecules in discrete
097            steps.
098
             • We formulate the one-step prediction step as a single classification task, as opposed to the
099
               nested classification approach of Jin et al. (2020).
100
101          • We formulate an approach to uniquely identify attachments of a fragment taking into ac-
102            count its symmetries.
103
             • We show how to incorporate spatial information in the autoregressive process to make the
104
               prediction geometry-aware.
105
106          • We demonstrate the model can be used for multi-target inverse design by examining how
107            well calibrated are the predicted properties of compounds generated subject to design cri-
               teria spanning a whole dataset.

125   2.1   C OARSE GRAINING MOLECULES
126
127   Molecules exhibit hierarchical structure, and we often find repeating fragments within a molecule
128   that can be treated as units in their own right to obtain a coarse representation of the molecule. A
129   coarse representation is particularly useful because it simplifies the problem by shifting the focus
130
      to global patterns rather than the finer details. However, there is not a unique hierarchy. There
      are a variety of ways of decomposing a molecule into constituent fragments. The choice of the
131
      decomposition procedure depends on the application and the level of resolution at which one wants
132
      to describe the molecule. Nevertheless, there are some desirable requirements for the fragments and
133   the decomposition procedure to satisfy. Uniqueness: A molecule should always be decomposed
134   into the same set of fragments that are themselves irreducible. Disjoint fragments: A molecule is
135   decomposed into fragments that do not overlap. So that the whole molecule can be reconstructed
136   by docking fragments with one another. Interpretable fragments: The fragment constituents of
137   the molecule are commonly used chemical fragments, for a better synchrony between the chemist
138   and the model. Following from these three self-imposed requirements, we propose the procedure
139   described in Algorithm 1. The procedure finds the Smallest Set of Small Rings (SSSR) of a molecule
140   and uses them to segment the molecule into its rings and all other individual bonds not belonging to
141
      a single ring.
142
143   2.2   S TANDARDIZATION OF FRAGMENTS AND ATTACHMENTS
144
145   A fragment of a molecule is the irreducible unit extracted using Algorithm 1, and its attachment
146   points are the fragment’s atoms that the neighbour fragments dock to. Using the fragments and
147
      its attachment points as building blocks requires a unique way of representing them. A natural,
      unique and human-readable representation for the fragments are their Canonical SMILES strings
148
      (Weininger, 1988; Weininger et al., 1989). However, a Canonical SMILES string can not encode
149
      the attachment information. Given a molecule, all the atoms in said molecule have an index number
150   associated. This atom index can be used for identifying the atoms that serve as attachments between
151   fragments. In Figure 1, there are three fragments: a six-membered ring with atoms (3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8),
152   a five-membered ring with (1, 2, 3, 8, 9) and a bond-type fragment with atoms (0, 1). The two ring
153   fragments dock at atoms (3, 8) and the five-membered ring docks with the bond-type fragment at
154   (1). This atom-indexing is referred to as ‘global indexes’. When the fragment is extracted as a
155   separate entity, the atom-index numbering is automatically reset. However, we can keep track of the
156   map between the ‘global’ index and the new ‘local’ index as we extract the fragment, retaining the
157   docking point information. Through this map, we know the 6-membered ring is docked with the
158
      5-membered ring at local indexes (4, 5). This fragment is then encoded as the Canonical SMILES
      string to obtain a unique representation of the fragment that is invariant to atom indexing.
159
160   We want to express the attachment points in the local index system of the Canonical fragment so they
161   are uniquely identified. The challenge is that when loading the graph from the Canonical SMILES,
      unless the original fragment was itself in the canonical index form, we have once again a new ‘local’

      atom indexes of an attachment in the canonical form, making them unique. However, if we take
196
      into account the symmetries, this definition of standard attachments is problematic. Following the
197
      previous example, attachments at the atoms (4, 3) or (1, 0), (0, 1), (3, 4) are indistinguishable.
198
199   To fix this, we create the standardization map. The standardization map lets us check if any two
200   canonical attachments are the same. To construct this standardization map we take one of the re-
201
      constructed maps (e.g., the first one) as reference, invert it, and convolute it with all other maps.
      For each item, its standard map is the minimum of the values of the convoluted maps. For example,
202
      atom 0 from the reference canonical form can be mapped on to 0, 3, 4, 1, then since 0 is the minimum
203
      in the standard map 0 maps to 0. If we take now atom index 2 (which in the reference canonical
204   form corresponds to a nitrogen), it can be mapped to 2, 5, 2, 5, of which the minimum is 2, so in the
205   standard map 2 maps to 2. In the appendix, Figure 7 fully illustrates this example. Using the stan-
206   dardization map, we can confirm that (3, 4), (4, 3), (1, 0) and (0, 1) are the same type of attachment
207   because all of them map to the tuple (0, 0) using the standardization map. Then, we will always take
208   one as reference, the first one ever encountered, e.g., (0, 1), and whenever the attachment is seen as
209   a canonical attachment (3, 4) we will say its a (0, 1) standard attachment.

252   To create a story of a given molecule, we begin by randomly selecting a fragment of the molecule
253   and then initializing the exploration queue by adding all chemically possible attachment points of the
254   fragment in the form of tuples (fragment, attachment). At every step, a tuple (fragment, attachment)
255   is randomly sampled from the exploration queue. This fragment is referred to as focal fragment
256   (ffocal , afocal ) and becomes the point at which, through the attachment, the molecule will grow.
257   Given this tuple, if the original molecule had a fragment (fnext , anext ) docked at this location that
258
      has not been added already, the next fragment is created and docked to the focal fragment, updating
      the exploration queue with the new frontier. That is, (ffocal , afocal ) is docked with (fnext , anext ).
259
      Otherwise, the focal fragment is cauterized at the attachment by not docking anything. Finally,
260
      the focal fragment is removed from the exploration queue. The process stops when the exploration
261   queue is empty, meaning that all fragments have been added at the correct attachment points and
262   all other attachment locations have been cauterized. The procedure is described in Algorithm 2. At
263   every step in the story of a molecule, the atomic coordinates are computed using a classical Force-
264   Field (Rappe et al., 1992; Halgren, 1996). The Force-field is used to do a conformer search and
265   return the lowest energy conformer.

394   3     R ESULTS
395
396   Molminer is trained on the RedDB dataset (Sorkun et al., 2022) of organic compounds for aqueous
397
      redox flow batteries. The dataset used includes 12 185 reactant molecules. Of those, 8 529 (∼ 70%)
      are randomly selected to be used in training, and 3 556 (∼ 30%) are reserved for testing. The remain-
398
      ing 100 molecules were used for validation. The targeted conditions are the reactant log-solubility,
399
      the redox potential, and the synthetic accessibility score (SAScore; Ertl & Schuffenhauer, 2009), the
400   latter is not part of the dataset but is calculated for all the molecules. In addition to the dataset, Aq-
401   SolPred (Sorkun et al., 2021) and QuantumDeepField (Tsubaki & Mizoguchi, 2020a;b) are used to
402   act as surrogate models in predicting solubility and redox potential, respectively, of molecules out-
403   side the dataset. AqSolPred was already trained on this same dataset, whereas QuantumDeepField
404   has been trained in-house on RedDB.
405
      Molminer’s hyperparameters were optimized using a reduced dataset containing 100 molecules from
406   the training set and the 100 molecules from the validation set. A gridsearch was performed for frag-
407   ment embedding size (64, 128, 256), attachment embedding size (16, 32, 64), number of attention
408   heads per layer (4, 8, 16), and number of transformer layers (2, 3, 4) resulting in 81 models trained
409   for 100 epochs or equivalently 10 000 stories. The rest of the parameters were left fixed at a learn-
410   ing rate of 10−4 , the Adam parameters (β1 , β2 ) = (0.9, 0.9) and ϵ = 10−9 , 512 hidden nodes
411   in the fully connected layers, a dropout rate of 0.3, and an initialization of 1.0 for the geometry
412   weight. Among the 81 resulting models, the best-performing model was found to have an accuracy
413   of 80.93% and 80.09% on the training and validation sets. This model used 256 for the fragment
414
      embedding size, 64 for the attachment, 8 heads and 3 transformer layers. Using these hyperparam-
      eters, the model was trained on the entire training set. The model achieved 81.96% and 82.94%
415
      fragment-level reconstruction accuracy on the training and testing sets, respectively, after 80 epochs
416
      or equivalently 682 320 stories.

      3.1   M OLECULAR GENERATION
419
420   To generate a compound, we give the model some desired properties, in this case, a desired redox
421   potential, log-solubility and synthetic accessibility score and use the fragment initializer to return
422   the probabilities of each of the fragments in the vocabulary manifesting in a molecule with such
423   properties. Then one of the top-k fragments with highest probability is uniformly sampled and
424   used as the first building fragment from which the whole rest of the molecule is grown. The best
425   results where achieved with at least k = 3; this experiment is discussed in the appendix. During the
426   autoregressive generation, the fragments are sampled weighted by their probabilities, and chemistry
427   rules are enforced at every step so that all attachments are valid.
428   To asses whether the conditional generation is calibrated, that is, the model creates compounds that
429   obey our target properties, we create three separate experiments, one for each of the target properties.
430   In each experiment, two of the target conditions are fixed at their mean, and we vary the remaining
431   one for the range of values spanning from its minimum to the maximum of the dataset in 30 steps.
      For each of these 30 steps 30 molecules are generated with the same target condition in order to

      A.3   E FFECT OF THE NUMBER OF TOP - K FRAGMENTS IN MOLECULAR GENERATION
811
812   When generating a molecule using MolMiner we start by selecting a starting fragment from which
813   the whole molecule will be grown. A model was trained to predict probability of any fragment
814   manifesting, given the target conditions. When we are generating a new molecule we only need
815   one starting fragment, so we can greedily take the one with the highest predicted probability of
816   manifesting and use it. One problem of this greedy approach is the loss of diversity. In the calibration
817   experiment, the same prompts where used to create 30 molecules, so if using only the top-1 starting
818
      fragments the model would always be using the same starting fragment. In Figure 8 we explored
      the effect of using the top-1, top-3 or top-5 starting fragments in the calibration of the Synthetic
819
      Accessibility score. The three different options perform equally for lower values of SAScore. The
820
      top-1 performs noticeably worse for higher values, with its mean departing the ideal correlation
821   before top-3 or top-5 do, and the standard deviation is greater for values of SAScore greater then
822   3.0 than the two other options. Top-3 and top-5 perform similarly in the entire range, with top-3
823   performing slightly better for SAScore values in (1.5,2.0) and (3.5,4.0), which motivated its used in
824   the main text.

898   A.5    C OMPARISON ON FRAGMENTATION APPROACHES
899
900   The fragment extraction procedure described in Algorithm 1 found the 34 fragments shown in Fig-
901   ure 9. As discussed in the main text the motivation behind our particular choice of fragmentation
902   was based on our desire for the fragments to be unique, disjoint and interpretable. We then showed
903   that our particular procedure results in fragments that are always single-cyclic graphs which allowed
904   us to handle their symmetries and avoid the presence of duplicate entries in the vocabulary formed
905   by the tuple (fragment, attachment).
906   In this subsection we compare our approach with that of previous work and highlight some unre-
907   ported problematic results of previous methods, that in turn will emphasize the importance of our
908   contribution.
909
      Our fragmentation approach, Algorithm 1, is the simplest procedure of extracting fragments when
910
      compared to the approach used in JTNN(Jin et al., 2019), HierVAE(Jin et al., 2020) or HierDiff
911
      (Qiang et al., 2023). It must be noted that this three other approaches are not independent but are
912   closely related. Both HierDiff and HierVAE are based on JTNN’s fragmentation. All these methods,
913   calculate the Small Set of Small Rings (SSSR) of the molecule, which they use to divide the molecule
914   into ts constituent rings, and bonds. Then, HierVAE, HierDiff and JTNN merge individual rings if
915   they share two or more atoms, and HierVAE also re-merges all bonds mutually connected into
916   a single fragment. Algorithm 1 does not do any further merging, once the rings and bonds are
917   extracted they are not fused together, and the result of this is a set of fragments that are single-cyclic
      graphs, as discussed in the main text.

       A.6   O N THE IMPORTANCE OF VARIABLE - SIZE AUTOREGRESSIVE GENERATION OF
1027         MOLECULES
1028
1029   Enabling variable size of the molecule during generation signifies the removal of an arbitrary con-
1030   straint. Why should any model pre-define before generation the size (in terms of atoms or in terms
1031   of fragments) of a molecule? We believe this is a result of adapting generative approaches from
1032   Vision tasks (image generation, where the image has a fixed size) to materials modelling without
1033   taking into account the unique challenges of materials. In this subsection we would like to raise
1034
       awareness of the limitations of fixing the size of the molecule during generation, an approach that
       is widely extended in current diffussion models for molecules, with examples in (Hoogeboom et al.,
1035
       2022b), (Qiang et al., 2023).
1036
1037   Given a set of target properties, the space of possible molecules that satisfy those properties do not
1038   necessarily have the same size, therefore, by constraining the space of possible solutions, to those
1039   with some predetermined size constrains the solution. Given that this is an unnecessary constraint
1040
       (other than convenience), a justification should be demanded from models that impose it, and not of
       models that remove it.
1041
1042   To further highlight the error that fixing the size of the molecule upon generation constitutes, we
1043   can examine how the size of the molecule is chosen upon initialization in models that pre-define
1044   it. From (Qiang et al., 2023) section C. Additional Experiments subsection C.1. Experimental
1045   configuration. ‘In all non-autoregressive methods, the number of nodes used for sampling is drawn
1046
       from the size distribution histogram calculated on the training set.’. In other words, they sample
       the size of the molecule from the distribution of the molecular sizes of the dataset. Doing so for
1047
       non-conditional generation imposes an unjustified constraint as discussed before. However if this is
1048
       done for conditional generation (section C.6. Conditional generation from (Qiang et al., 2023)) then
1049   this is altogether wrong. Because they are sampling from the distribution of sizes when they should
1050   be sampling from the conditional distribution of size given the property. In other words, in general
1051   given a set of target properties the distribution of sizes of molecules satisfying those properties is
1052   not the general distribution of sizes.
