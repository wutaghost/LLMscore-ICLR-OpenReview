009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advancements in utilising decision making capability of Reinforcement
012            Learning (RL) have paved the way for innovative approaches in data generation.
013            This research explores the application of model free on-policy RL algorithms for
014            generating online signatures and its controlled variations. Online signatures are
015            captured via e-pads as sequential structural coordinates. In this study, we have
016            introduced a robust on-policy RL agent named as SIGN-Agent, capable of gen-
017            erating online signatures accurately. Unlike other RL algorithms, on-policy RL
018
               directly learns from the agent’s current policy, offering significant advantages in
               stability and faster convergence for sequential decision-making. The proposed
019
               SIGN-Agent operates in a random continuous action space with controlled explo-
020
               ration limits, allowing it to capture complex signature patterns while minimizing
021            errors over time. The downstream applications of this system can be extended in
022            diverse fields such as enhancing the robustness of signature authentication sys-
023            tems, supporting robotics, and even diagnosing neurological disorders. By gen-
024            erating reliable, human-like online signatures, our approach strengthens signa-
025            ture authentication systems by reducing susceptibility towards system-generated
026            forgeries, if trained against them. Additionally, the proposed work is optimized
027            for low-footprint edge devices, enabling it to function efficiently in the area of
028            robotics for online signature generation tasks. Experimental results, tested on
029
               large, publicly available datasets, demonstrate the effectiveness of model free on-
               policy RL algorithms in generating online signature trajectories, that closely re-
030
               semble user’s reference signatures. Our approach highlights the potential of model
031
               free on-policy RL as an advancement in the field of data generation targeting the
032            domain of online signatures in this research.

131
      The generation of realistic online signatures has garnered significant research interest due to its
132
      applications in biometric authentication and secure identity verification. Traditional methods like
133   Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) (Rúa & Castro, 2012) were foundational in capturing temporal
134   dependencies in signature sequences. However, their sensitivity to variations and limited general-
135   ization hinder their real-world applicability.
136
      Recent studies have explored modern generative models for signature generation. Transformers
137
      (Vaswani et al., 2017), adapted for handwriting tasks (Li et al., 2021), excel at modeling long-range
138
      dependencies but rely on computationally intensive techniques like topk sampling, which limits their
139   ability to capture continuous, user-specific signature dynamics. GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014),
140   popular for handwriting synthesis (Zhang et al., 2019; Alonso-Fernandez et al., 2019), often suffer
141   from training instability, leading to inconsistent user-specific outputs. VAEs (Kingma & Welling,
142   2013), with their latent space representations, enable controlled variation but struggle to capture
143   the fine-grained details essential for realistic signature replication. Diffusion Models (Alcaraz &
144   Strodthoff, 2022), while producing high-quality outputs, are computationally expensive and less
145   suited for real-time applications. Our work addresses these challenges by leveraging RL, which
146   dynamically adapts to user-specific variability without relying on handcrafted features or extensive
147
      tuning.
      Imitation Learning (IL) approaches, such as Behavior Cloning (Pomerleau, 1991) and GAIL (Ho &
148
      Ermon, 2016), have shown promise in mimicking human actions. However, IL methods are prone to
149
      compounding errors and policy drift, making them less reliable in tasks with high variability, such
150   as user-specific signature generation. Unlike IL, which relies heavily on expert demonstrations, our
151   RL-based approach balances exploration and exploitation, enabling the model to adapt dynamically
152   to diverse user trajectories and generate robust, personalized signatures.
153
      Reinforcement Learning (RL) offers a robust alternative for tasks requiring sequential decision-
154
      making and adaptability. Model-free RL algorithms like Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)
155
      (Schulman et al., 2017), Trust Region Policy Optimization (TRPO) (Schulman, 2015), and Ad-
156   vantage Actor-Critic (A2C) (Mnih, 2016) are particularly suited for dynamic environments. Unlike
157   generative models, RL methods dynamically balance exploration and exploitation, making them
158   highly effective for modeling the variability and complexity of online signatures. While RL has
159   not been widely applied to online signature generation, our work leverages on-policy RL to train
160   SIGN-Agent, allowing it to generalize across users and adapt dynamically to their unique signature
161   trajectories. The integration of a Q-learning-based Sign Moderator ensures further refinement of
      user-specific dynamics, addressing the limitations of prior RL methods.

189   3.1   OVERVIEW OF P ROPOSED M ETHOD
190
191   The SIGN-Agent leverages three on-policy reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms—Proximal Pol-
192   icy Optimization (PPO), Trust Region Policy Optimization (TRPO), and Advantage Actor-Critic
193   (A2C)—to address the challenges of generating realistic and user-specific online signatures. Each
194   algorithm brings complementary strengths that align with the requirements of this task, such as sta-
195   bility, adaptability, and efficient convergence. PPO: Ensures robust policy updates by balancing
196
      exploration and exploitation, making it effective in noisy environments. PPO’s stability is partic-
      ularly valuable in training the SIGN-Agent on diverse user-specific trajectories (De La Fuente &
197
      Guerra, 2024). TRPO: Provides smooth trajectory generation by constraining policy updates within
198
      a trust region. This enhances precision, ensuring smoother transitions between consecutive points
199   in the signature trajectory Shani et al. (2020). A2C: Accelerates convergence through parallelized
200   actor-critic updates, enabling efficient learning across diverse signature patterns. A2C is especially
201   useful for exploring a wide range of variations during training (Gerpott et al., 2022). The inclusion of
202   all three algorithms is motivated by their complementary strengths. Empirical results in Table 3, 5, 4
203   demonstrate their unique contributions, with PPO excelling in stability, TRPO producing smoother
204   trajectories, and A2C achieving faster convergence. These experimental results also validate the
205   strengths of each algorithm. PPO demonstrates superior stability, as reflected in its lower variance
206   in KLD and MSE metrics during training as shown in Table 4. TRPO generates smoother signature
207
      trajectories, evident from its higher cosine similarity scores when compared to target trajectories.
      A2C achieves faster convergence, reducing training iterations by approximately 20% compared to
208
      PPO and TRPO, though it exhibits slightly higher variance in signature fidelity. These observations
209
      justify the inclusion of all three algorithms within SIGN-Agent. The decision to use PPO, TRPO,
210   and A2C stems from their complementary characteristics in addressing the unique challenges of
211   online signature generation: PPO ensures stable and robust training by clipping probability ratios
212   during updates, reducing the likelihood of policy divergence. TRPO maintains precision by con-
213   straining updates within a trust region, enabling the generation of smooth and realistic signature
214   trajectories. A2C accelerates convergence through parallelized updates, facilitating efficient explo-
215   ration of diverse signature patterns. SIGN-Agent balances stability, adaptability, and efficiency, as
      evidenced by experimental results in Table 3, 5, 4. Ablation studies (Table 4) further highlight the

222   3.2   RL P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
223
224   The RL-based SIGN-Agent is formulated as a sequential decision-making task to generate realistic
225   online signatures. This section provides a detailed explanation of the agent, environment, state and
226
      action dimensions, policy architecture, reward function, and termination mechanism.
      Neural Network Policy Architecture: The policy network is an LSTM-based neural network de-
227
      signed to capture the temporal dependencies inherent in signature trajectories. It employs a Long
228
      Short-Term Memory (LSTM) layer with a hidden size of 50, which processes input sequences with
229   a single feature dimension (input size = 1), representing either x or y coordinates of the trajectory.
230   The LSTM sequentially processes the input data and generates hidden states at each time step. The
231   final hidden state is passed through a fully connected linear layer that maps the features to the desired
232   output dimension (output size = 1), predicting the next x or y coordinate. Hidden and cell states
233   are initialized to zeros to ensure compatibility with gradient tracking and device execution. This
234   architecture is optimized for time-series prediction tasks, leveraging historical patterns to predict the
235   next trajectory point with high accuracy.
236   State and Action Dimensions: The state st is represented using a sliding window mechanism, cap-
237
      turing recent trajectory points and encapsulating temporal dependencies in the signature generation
      process. Mathematically, the state is defined as st = [xt−w , yt−w , . . . , xt , yt ], where w represents
238
      the fixed window size. This representation provides the agent with sufficient historical context for
239
      predicting the next trajectory point. The action at corresponds to the predicted next trajectory point,
240   defined as at = [xt+1 , yt+1 ], and is sampled from a continuous action space.
241   Environment Determinism: The environment for the SIGN-Agent is deterministic, with state tran-
242   sitions solely dependent on the sliding window of recent trajectory points. The next state st+1 is
243   determined by the transition function st+1 = f (st , at ), where f (·) appends the agent’s predicted
244   action to the sliding window. Although the environment is deterministic, stochasticity is introduced
245   during training by perturbing the agent’s actions with Gaussian noise. The perturbed action is de-
246   fined as at = a′t + ϵ, where ϵ ∼ N (0, σ). This noise simulates variability in human signature
247   trajectories, enhancing the model’s ability to generalize across diverse signature styles.
248
      Capturing Multiple Signature Styles: The policy captures variations in signature styles by train-
      ing on diverse user-specific datasets that include a wide range of signature patterns. The state repre-
249
      sentation integrates historical trajectory points from the current signature, latent features encoding
250
      user-specific style attributes, and global training data encompassing all signature variations for each
251   individual. This comprehensive representation enables the policy to generalize across a variety of
252   styles while dynamically adapting to specific trajectories during inference.
253   Reward Function: The reward mechanism plays a critical role in guiding the agent to produce
254   user-specific signature trajectories. At each time step t, the reward rt is computed as the negative
255   Euclidean distance between the generated and target points as shown in equation 1:

285   3.3   ROLE OF S IGN M ODERATOR (SM)
286
287   The Sign Moderator (SM) is a critical component designed to refine the trajectory outputs of the
288
      SIGN-Agent. It operates as a post-processing step that integrates noisy variations generated by the
      RL policy network and produces a clean and unified signature trajectory. The SM is based on a
289
      Q-learning framework, leveraging a Q-table to select optimal trajectory points and enhance output
290
      consistency.
291
292   Purpose and Scope: The primary function of the SM is to smooth and refine the noisy signature
293   trajectories produced by the RL network. While the RL policy generates multiple variations for each
294
      axis (x, y) of a single signature, the SM integrates these variations to reconstruct a trajectory that
      closely resembles the target signature. The SM is applied during both training and inference phases
295
      to maintain consistency and ensure robust trajectory generation.
296
297   Q-Table Construction: The Q-table in the SM is a matrix where rows correspond to temporal
298   states (time steps) and columns represent the available candidate trajectory points generated for
299   each coordinate. Each entry in the Q-table, denoted as Q(st , at ), stores the expected cumulative
300
      reward for selecting a specific trajectory point at at state st . The reward function aligns with the
      trajectory refinement goal, favoring points that minimize discrepancies between generated and target
301
      signatures.
302
303   Q-Learning Process: The SM employs Q-learning to iteratively update the Q-table based on the
304   observed rewards. The Q-value updates are governed by the Bellman equation 2:
