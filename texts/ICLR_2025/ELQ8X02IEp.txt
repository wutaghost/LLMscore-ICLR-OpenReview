009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Improving the interpretability and reliability of deep learning models is essential
012            for advancing machine learning applications, though it remains a significant chal-
013            lenge. One promising approach is the integration of logical reasoning into deep
014            learning systems. Previous works have demonstrated that SATNet, a differentiable
015            MaxSAT solver, can learn interpretable and reliable rules from input-output exam-
016            ples in puzzle domains. In this work, we propose Visual SATNet (Vi-SATNet), an
017            extended version of SATNet capable of learning logical reasoning rules in more
018
               general and complex domains, such as the feature space of real-life images. We
               find that, given a pre-trained deep convolutional neural network (CNN) architec-
019
               ture, a Vi-SATNet layer can be integrated and trained efficiently to learn a set of
020
               reasoning rules on the deep features, guiding the classifier’s decision. Vi-SATNets
021            are trained to perform feature re-generation tasks for a given image dataset, where
022            the re-generated features maintain high accuracy when used for image classifi-
023            cation, proving their quality. In our experiment on the Imagenette dataset with
024            a pre-trained VGG19 model, masking out 10% to 80% of the features results
025            in classification accuracy ranging from 98.50% to 93.92% with Vi-SATNet re-
026            generation, compared to 97.07% to 9.83% without re-generation. Furthermore,
027            we introduce a visualization method to illustrate the rules learned by Vi-SATNets,
028            thereby enhancing the interpretability of the pre-trained CNN model.

                                                                What I cannot create, I do not understand.
033
034                                                                                     – Richard Feynman
035   Deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) have been the foundation of numerous vision tasks
036   ranging from image classification (Krizhevsky et al., 2012), object localization (Zhou et al., 2016),
037   image segementation (He et al., 2017), face recognization (Parkhi et al., 2015), medical image pro-
038   cessing (Ronneberger et al., 2015), autonomous driving (Maqueda et al., 2018) among many others.
039   Although remarkably effective, the deep features learned through CNNs are still poorly understood.
040   Many existing works highlight corresponding regions of input via saliency methods (e.g., visualiza-
041   tion, maximum activation, attribution methods) (Simonyan et al., 2014; Sundararajan et al., 2017;
042   Shrikumar et al., 2017) or perturbing inputs (Fong et al., 2019; Ivanovs et al., 2021), which appear
043
      to be visually convincing but can be misleading (Adebayo et al., 2018; Laugel et al., 2019). Inspired
      by the fact that neural networks fundamentally learn distributed representations (Hinton, 1986), we
044
      believe a more meaningful way of interpreting deep features is to understand their interactions rather
045
      than visualizing or analyzing individual features.
046
047   Recent works Wang et al. (2019); Topan et al. (2021); Lim et al. (2022); Li et al. (2023) show that
048   SATNet, neural networks with a special layer for differentiable MaxSAT solving, can learn reliable
049
      logical rules in the form of weighted equalities. SATNet has proven to be successful in solving
      small logical puzzles like Sudoku but suffers from training instability when trained end-to-end with
050
      other neural network layers Chang et al. (2020). In this work, we propose a decomposable and
051
      interpretable layer based on SATNet, which is a drop-in component that can be directly integrated
052   into pre-trained CNNs and requires no re-training or finetuning.

083   2   R ELATED W ORK
084
085   Interpreting deep features. There has been significant research effort in understanding deep fea-
086   tures of convolutional neural networks, most of which are post-hoc explanation methods using tech-
087   niques such as activation maximization (Erhan et al., 2009; Yosinski et al., 2015; Yoshimura et al.,
088   2021), image perturbation (Fong et al., 2019; Ivanovs et al., 2021) and saliency maps (Simonyan
089   et al., 2014; Smilkov et al., 2017; Sundararajan et al., 2017). These approaches focus on explaining
090   individual features and can be quite problematic (Adebayo et al., 2018; Laugel et al., 2019). Recent
091   works (Chen et al., 2019; Ma et al., 2023) introduce explanations with prototypical concepts, which
092   are reference images that share similar patterns. This requires a set of carefully chosen references
093
      and some manual inspection is needed to validate whether the test image is indeed similar to ref-
      erence images. In contrast, our work aims to learn logical rules among deep features and further
094
      automatically identify essential features with learned rules and recover them when missing.
095
096
097   Learning and reasoning with SATNet. Wang et al. (2019) propose SATNet, a differentiable layer
098   approximating MaxSAT solving through the semi-definite programming (SDP) relaxation, and show
099
      that it can learn implicit rules and solve logical puzzles like Sudoku. Chang et al. (2020) find a label-
      leakage issue in the original SATNet and point out the symbol grounding challenge, which is partly
100
      addressed by a staged training (Topan et al., 2021). Lim et al. (2022) introduce regularizations
101
      like symmetric constraints, improving the learning efficiency of SATNet when the targeted domain
102   shares intrinsic symmetries. More recently, Li et al. (2023) proposes a reliable interpretation of
103   rules implicitly learned in SATNet and shows they are verifiably correct for small logical puzzles.
104   All these works focus on learning and solving logical puzzles and assume the input to SATNet is
105   properly binarized based on domain knowledge. Our work generalizes SATNet without the need to
106   design any binary features based on a simple and practical observation — deep features from CNNs
107   are already good relaxations and can be directly used as relaxed inputs to SATNet. This simple
      observation makes SATNet broadly applicable to state-of-the-art CNNs and beyond.

      Prior work has shown the reliability and interpretability of SATNet (Li et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
111
      2019), however, its applications are limited to learning and solving logic puzzles such as Sudoku.
112   In this section, we propose Visual SATNet (Vi-SATNet), a generalized architecture that builds upon
113   SATNet and is capable of learning logical rules directly from the feature embedding space of images.

      3.1    P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
116
117   Recall that the objective of a SATNet layer is to learn a set of rules such that when applied, unknown
118   variables in a given incomplete puzzle can be solved. Similarly, the objective of a Vi-SATNet model
119   is to learn a set of rules such that when applied, missing feature values (extracted from an image) in
120   a given incomplete feature map can be re-generated. In addition, we aim to use the learned rules to
121   explain how the features in a given class of images interact with each other.
122   Formally, let M denotes a convolutional neural network (CNN) pre-trained on an image dataset
123   D = {(xi , yi )}Ni=1 , where M consists of a feature extractor F, the set of convolutional layers and
124   pooling layers, and a feature classifier C, the set of fully connected layers (Lecun et al., 1998).
125   Given an image x, F(x) generates a feature map F ∈ RH×W ×K , where H and W are the height
126   and width of the feature map, K is the number of channels. Let f (h,w,k) ∈ R denote the feature map
127   values at position (h, w) in the k-th channel of F .
128
      Consider fixed mappings F and C from a pre-trained M; let n = H × W be the number of feature
129
      vectors, where a feature vector vi is built by taking the feature values at the same positions in each

140   3.2    V I -SATN ET AS A FEATURE RE - GENERATION MODEL
141
142   As illustrated in Figure 1, a Vi-SATNet model directly takes as input the feature vectors V =
143   [v1 , . . . , vn ] and a binary mask that indicates the missing indices. Following Wang et al. (2019),
144   we then apply the mixing method to obtain the optimized set of output feature vectors (Wang et al.,
145   2017). In this context, a feature vector vi can be viewed as the relaxation of a binary variable zi
146
      that encodes some abstract status of the corresponding local region of the input image during the
      inference process. This interpretation is discussed in more detail in Section 3.4.
147
148   During a forward pass of Vi-SATNet, a fraction of the input feature vectors are masked, and the
149   randomly initialized weight matrix S is used to optimize the generation of the unknown feature
150   vectors VˆO . We use cosine similarity defined in Equation 2 Nguyen & Bai (2011) to quantitatively
151   assess the similarity between the two vectors vˆo and vogt :
152
153                                                                vo · vogt
154                                             d(vo , vogt ) =                .                                   (2)
                                                                  ∥vo ∥∥vogt ∥
155
156   During a backward pass of Vi-SATNet, the loss is propagated to update the weight matrix S to
157   best fit the input-output pairs provided. Upon convergence, S encodes a set of learned rules. The
158   implementation of Vi-SATNet is generally adapted from the original implementation of a SATNet
159   layer, and the detailed algorithms can be found in Appendix A. Note that in the formulation of
160   SATNet, the relaxed vectors are assumed to be unit vectors. This assumption can easily be met by
161   normalizing the feature vectors, and we show that normalization does not cause information loss in
      Section 4.2.

182     3.3   E VALUATION OF THE Q UALITY OF R E - GENERATED F EATURES
183
        Cosine Similarity. A trivial method to evaluate the quality of re-generated feature vectors is to
184     compute the cosine similarity as stated in Equation 2. However, this evaluation metric is not able
185     to assess the degree of usefulness of the re-generated feature vectors since a single similarity score
186     is not expressive enough to demonstrate whether the key information encoded in the ground truth
187     feature vectors is well-captured by the re-generated ones or not.
188
189     Integrating Vi-SATNets into CNN models. To quantitatively evaluate the quality of re-generated
190     feature vectors, we propose to integrate trained Vi-SATNets into the pre-trained CNN model and use
191     the pre-trained classifier C to perform image classification based on the re-generated feature vectors.
192     The evaluation metric is simply the classification accuracy of C when applied to the re-generated
193     features.
194     Intuitively, images from the same labelled class should share the same set of reasoning rules that
195     capture the interactions between the feature vectors. To reduce the complexity of the learned rules,
196     we propose training separate Vi-SATNets for each class. One of the main advantages of this ap-
197     proach is that each Vi-SATNet can be trained independently, which ensures that no re-training of
198     other pre-existing Vi-SATNets is needed when images of new classes are introduced to the dataset,
199     thus minimizing any unnecessary computational costs.

221   3.4     I NTERPRETATION OF THE LEARNED RULES
222
223
      As mentioned in Section 3.2, each of the feature vectors conceptually corresponds to a binary vari-
      able that represents an abstract state of this feature vector. When a Vi-SATNet model converges,
224
      the weight matrix S encodes the logical relations between each pair of the feature vectors. During
225
      the inference phase, the unknown feature vectors VO are generated such that the entire set of feature
226   vectors VI∪O optimally respects the rules encoded in S. Although we can extract explicit rules from
227   a Vi-SATNet using the method introduced in Li et al. (2023), it is not clear what should be con-
228   sidered as the “ground truth rules” in the context of describing feature relations. Hence, we cannot
229   perform formal verification on the set of rules learned by Vi-SATNet. It is worth noting that this is
230   not a technical limitation but the lack of formal specifications of visual objects — e.g., we do not
231   have a set of ground-truth logical rules specifying what a cat is. So instead, we propose to visually
232   illustrate the learned rules.
233
234   3.4.1    F INDING THE M INIMAL S IGNIFICANT F EATURE S ET
235
236   Given an image x from a class y, a Vi-SATNet model trained for label y is capable of re-generating
237   any missing feature vector. We can thus demonstrate the meaning of the learned rules by studying
238   the inter-dependency between the feature vectors during the re-generation process.
239   Definition 1 (Significant Feature Set). Given a target missing feature vector vt in an image x with
240   label y, a significant feature set is a set of feature vectors which, when passed into Vi-SATNety ,
241   can re-generate vˆt that is similar to the ground truth vector vt . Let Sf ⊂ {1, . . . , n} be the index
242   set of the significant feature vectors, then information from all other feature vectors with indices
243   {1, . . . , n} \ ({t} ∪ Sf ) are removed by setting them to uniform unit vectors before passed into
                                                                           h                    i⊤
244   Vi-SATNety , where a uniform unit vector v ∈ Rk is defined as v = √1k , √1k , . . . , √1k .
245
246
      There are exponentially many significant feature set that can be found for a given target feature
247
      vector. In the following definitions, we introduce a measure of confidence to characterize the space
248
      of these sets.
249
250   Definition 2 (Reference Similarity Score). Given a target missing feature vector vt in an image x
251   with label y, the reference similarity score sref is defined as the cosine similarity between vˆt and vt
252
      given all feature vectors excluding vt itself:
253
                                         sref = d(vˆt , vt | {vi }ni=1 \ {vt }).                          (3)
254
255
256
257   Definition 3 (Significant Feature Set of α confidence). We say that a significant feature set is of
258   confidence α when
259                                d(vˆt , vt | {vi }i∈Sf ) ≥ α · sref ,   α ∈ [0, 1].                    (4)

261
262
263   It is not hard to see that there exists a significant set of confidence α for any value of α: simply
264   taking Sf = {vi }ni=1 \ {vt }. Our goal is to find a minimal such set, so that the remaining feature

266   A minimal significant feature set can be found by enumerating over all significant feature sets fixing
267   an α value, however, this approach results in an exponential search space. Inspired by the delta
268   debugging technique used in automated bug detection in programming (Zeller, 1999), we introduce
269   a search algorithm that efficiently finds an approximated minimal significant feature set (See Algo-

288        3.4.2     V ISUALIZING THE LEARNED RULES
289
           Since a receptive field (all image pixels that contribute to the calculation of the feature value) can be
290        calculated for each feature vector, the minimal significant set found by Algorithm 2 can be visually
291        mapped onto the input image. For deep convolutional neural networks, the theoretical receptive field
292        size can be as large as, or even larger than the input image due to the padding operation. Therefore,

459   5   C ONCLUSION
460
461   Achieving interpretability in deep neural networks is a critical yet challenging task. To this end, we
462   propose Vi-SATNet, a standalone component that learns and performs reasoning on the deep feature
463   space produced by convolutional neural networks. Empirical results on the features extracted by
464
      a pre-trained Lenet-5 model (on the MNIST dataset) and by a pre-trained VGG19 model (on the
      Imagenette dataset) show that the learned reasoning rules allow Vi-SATNet to re-generate missing
465
      feature vectors accurately. We further illustrate the dependencies among the feature vectors via
466
      visualizations of minimal significant feature sets, enhancing the interpretability of the deep feature
467   space learned by the VGG19 model.
468
469   Our experiments on the shallow neural network (Lenet-5) and deep neural network (VGG19) suggest
470
      that Vi-SATNet has the potential to be generalized to any feature space learned by other convolu-
      tional neural networks, for example, ResNet (He et al., 2016). Future work should investigate a
471
      wider range of model architectures that can integrate Vi-SATNet as a drop-in reasoning layer, and
472
      extend the evaluations to larger datasets such as the ImageNet dataset (Deng et al., 2009).
