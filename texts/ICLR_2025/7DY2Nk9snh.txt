009                                                      A BSTRACT
010
011                We present SynthCLIP, a CLIP model trained on entirely synthetic text-image
012                pairs. Leveraging recent text-to-image (TTI) networks and large language models
013                (LLM), we generate synthetic datasets of images and corresponding captions at
014                scale, with no human intervention. In this work, we provide an analysis on CLIP
015                models trained on synthetic data. We provide insights on the data generation
016                strategy, number of samples required, scaling trends, and resulting properties.
017                We also introduce SynthCI-30M, a purely synthetic dataset comprising 30
018
                   million captioned images. Our work focuses on showing the advantages and
                   disadvantages of synthetic data for training CLIP models. Our code, trained
019
                   models, and data, will be released as open source.
020

022   1       I NTRODUCTION
023
024   Self-supervised training strategies (He et al., 2022; Caron et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2020) are
025   fundamental for many recently released foundation models. These techniques make use of a vast
026   amount of data without incurring a large annotation cost. In particular, contrastive representation
027   learning (Schroff et al., 2015) has been successfully employed to extract joint embeddings for
028   heterogeneous data modalities. By using multi-modal training data, CLIP (Radford et al., 2021b)
029   provides a common representation that links visual and linguistic information. Today, CLIP
030   encoders are included in a wide range of applications, spanning from zero-shot image under-
031
      standing (Liu et al., 2023b; Ren et al., 2024), to style transfer (Kwon & Ye, 2022), and robotics
      control (Shridhar et al., 2022), among others.
032
033   Training CLIP requires large-scale captioned image datasets that are often collected from the web.
034   Unfortunately, retrieving these data from the internet may present significant disadvantages (Li
035   et al., 2023a; Piktus et al., 2021; Kang et al., 2023). While it is easy to scale the number of unique
036   samples, this comes at an increased difficulty in controlling their quality, which may result in a poor
037
      alignment between images and corresponding captions. Moreover, it is challenging to monitor the
      content of the collected images, resulting in potential concerns for illegal1 or copyrighted content.
038
      As an alternative to web-crawled data, some have explored the usage of synthetic data to train
039
      supervised (Sariyildiz et al., 2023; He et al., 2023) or self-supervised (Tian et al., 2023) networks.
040
041   In this paper, our goal is to investigate the performance of CLIP models trained on fully synthetic
042   data in the form of captioned images, to define practices and properties of synthetic data for
043
      training. To achieve our objective, we train SynthCLIP, a CLIP model trained exclusively on
      large-scale generated data. We propose a pipeline that jointly leverages existing text-to-image
044
      (TTI) and large language models (LLM) to produce text-image pairs. The captioned images are
045
      generated in an end-to-end fashion starting from a large list of concepts which guarantees variability
046   of the synthesized data. We use the LLM to produce captions starting from sampled concepts, and
047   then synthesize their corresponding images using TTI models. Our pipeline brings a significant
048   advantage: we can generate data at any scale, arbitrarily increasing the size of training data
049   depending only on computational power, with no human intervention. This allows for a controlled
050   study on SynthCLIP performance and properties. Our contributions are threefold:
051             1. We propose SynthCLIP, a CLIP model trained entirely on synthetic data generated with an
052                automatic pipeline that is scalable to any desired dataset size.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
063
064
      Representation Learning. Early works in representation learning on images used pre-text tasks
065
      such as inpainting, jigsaw puzzle solving, and image rotation prediction (Pathak et al., 2016; Noroozi
066
      & Favaro, 2016; Gidaris et al., 2018). Instead, SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020) leverages contrastive
067   learning to maximize the similarity between two augmented views of the same image. Alterna-
068   tively, masked autoencoders (MAE) (He et al., 2022) use a masked patch prediction task to learn
069   visual representations. CLIP (Radford et al., 2021a) and other similar works (Mu et al., 2022; Zhai
070   et al., 2023; Fini et al., 2023) use contrastive learning to learn joint visual and textual representations.
071   Language-image pre-training necessitates high-quality text-image pairs. Its core idea is to maximize
072   the similarity between encoded textual and image representation. We study the possibility of gener-
073   ating synthetic text-image pairs for training CLIP-like models starting from simple concepts only.
074   Synthetic Captions. Recent works emphasize the importance of high-quality and aligned text-
075   image pairs when training CLIP models, and propose a synthetic caption generation pipeline for
076   improving it. VeCLIP (Lai et al., 2023) and CapsFusion (Yu et al., 2023a) propose methods to
077   produce aligned captions. Both start with a captioning model such as BLIP (Li et al., 2022) or
078   LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023a), to obtain a semantically-rich synthetic caption. However, captioning
079   models suffer from over-simplification and lack world knowledge, hence they can be compensated
080   by the usage of an LLM (Lai et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023a). LaCLIP (Fan et al., 2023) improves the
081
      text branch of CLIP by leveraging an LLM to provide multiple rewrites of the same caption to use
      in contrastive learning. While this improves downstream tasks, it may not reflect the content of the
082
      image due to hallucinations (Fan et al., 2023). All these works assume the availability of real data,
083
      instead, we introduce a fully synthetic pipeline for data generation, allowing arbitrary scalability
084   and control over generated samples.
085
086   Synthetic Data. Synthetic data has been used in machine learning fields ranging from audio
087
      (Rossenbach et al., 2020) to language (Yang et al., 2020; Li et al., 2023b) and vision (Varol et al.,
      2017; Jahanian et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023). In computer vision, they allow for improving models’
088
      performance on several downstream tasks such as semantic segmentation (Richter et al., 2016; Ros
089
      et al., 2016; Chen et al., 2019), object detection (Johnson-Roberson et al., 2017), and image classi-
090   fication (Yuan et al., 2024; Shmelkov et al., 2018). Recent works have explored the use of synthetic
091   data from TTI models, to augment training on real data (Azizi et al., 2023; Sariyildiz et al., 2023;
092   He et al., 2023). Yu et al. (2023b) uses a framework to generate synthetic images, increasing the
093   diversity of existing datasets. All these assume knowledge about object classes in the downstream
094   task, and work with images only. Recently, StableRep (Tian et al., 2023) showed that synthetic
095   images generated from Stable Diffusion can be used to train self-supervised methods, however, it
096   still uses real captions, limiting scalability and proper analysis. Parallel works (Fan et al., 2024; Tian
097   et al., 2024) preliminarily investigate training of vision models on synthetic captions and images.
098
      However, they are not specific to CLIP, and they include a small set of classes for generation,
      preventing appropriate analysis. We discuss our positioning relative to these works in the appendix.

136   3.1   S TEP 1: C ONCEPT- BASED C APTIONS G ENERATION
137
138   The first stage of our pipeline involves the generation of synthetic image captions, that we later aim to
139   use as prompts for text-to-image generators. To achieve this, we utilize an LLM conditioned on our
140   concept bank. The model is prompted to generate captions that describe a scene related to a chosen
141   concept. In our process of generating these captions, we experimented with various prompting
142   techniques, discovering that conditioning the LLM to focus on a particular concept leads to more
143   diverse captions. Concept conditioning ensures that the LLM does not just repeatedly produce
144   captions about a limited set of concepts, over-represented in the LLM training dataset. In other
145   words, this approach helps prevent the model from becoming biased towards certain concepts, and
146
      encourages a broader spectrum of caption generation. Limited concept diversity hinders the CLIP
      training, since contrastive learning highly benefits from variability and more concept coverage (Xu
147
      et al., 2023). Hence, diversity is a requirement for a proper analysis of SynthCLIP.
148
149   We start by introducing our concept bank C composed by NC concepts. Unless otherwise stated,
150   we use the MetaCLIP concept bank (Xu et al., 2023), that contains over 500,000 concepts drawn
151   from WordNet Synsets and Wikipedia common unigrams, bigrams, and titles. We observe that NC
152
      deeply influences CLIP performance, and we investigate this effect in Section 4.3. We then focus on
      prompt engineering, a critical aspect for generating effective captions for text-to-image generation.
153
154   Image generators are sensitive to the quality of the input prompt (Gu et al., 2023), which is
155   often a brief text description capturing the characteristics of the desired image. We set specific
156   requirements to ensure that the prompts generated by the LLM are well-suited for the subsequent
157   image generation:
158   (1) Focus on a Single Concept: Each generated caption should be centered around a single concept,
159   presented in a clear and coherent context.
160   (2) Brevity and Clarity: The prompts need to be concise yet grammatically correct. The goal is to
161   avoid overly complex or vague inputs that could lead to ambiguous or incorrect images.
      (3) Prompt-Only Generation: Our aim is to have the LLM generate prompts without engaging in

190   3.2      S TEP 2: C APTIONS FILTERING
191
192   When generating captions conditioned on a specific concept c, it is typical for other concepts
193   c′ ̸= c, c′ ∈ C to appear within the same caption. This is expected, since even when a sentence
194
      is focused on a single concept, other related concepts emerge within the described scene. For
      example, if c = “bird”, a generated caption might be “a bird is resting on a tree”,
195
      introducing an additional concept c′ = “tree”. This LLM-specific behavior may create imbal-
196
      ances in the generated data for CLIP training, which benefits from the usage of a balanced amount
197   of concepts (Xu et al., 2023).
198
199   We create a balanced set of captions, T ∗ , by applying the balancing sampling method proposed
200
      in MetaCLIP (Xu et al., 2023) to our setting. It consists of two stages: substring matching, and
      probability balancing. Substring matching determines which concepts from C appear in each caption
201
      within T . This enables us to measure the real frequency of each described concept across the
202
      synthesized captions. Probability balancing is then employed to subsample captions T ∗ from T . It
203   increases the probability of selecting captions with long tail concepts, preventing over-representation
204   of frequently-generated concepts. This yields a subset of captions where both frequent and long-tail
205   concepts are adequately represented. Hence, this approach ensures a diverse and task-agnostic cap-
206   tions set suitable for foundation model pre-training. By sizing the parameters of balanced sampling,
207   we are able to choose the size of the subset T ∗ . For more details, we refer to (Xu et al., 2023).
208
209
      3.3      S TEP 3: I MAGE G ENERATION
210
211   Having successfully created a balanced set of synthetic captions T ∗ , our next step is to generate
212   the corresponding images. For this, we utilize a text-to-image generator GTTI . We choose Stable
213   Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) for this purpose, due to its open-source availability and relatively
214   lower computational demands. For each caption in our set T ∗ , we generate a corresponding image.
215   This process results in a collection of images, I ∗ = {xk ∼ GTTI (tk )}, where each xk is an image
      synthesized from the caption tk ∈ T ∗ . In Figure 2, we show how we generate highly aligned images

      3.4   S TEP 4: CLIP T RAINING
220
221
      Finally, we use the synthetic text-image pairs to train a CLIP model, to explore how effectively a
222   model can learn from entirely synthetic data. We train two encoders, each one dedicated to either
223   the image or text modality, defined as Eimage and Etext , respectively. We follow the standard CLIP
224   training pipeline (Radford et al., 2021a), by applying a contrastive loss on the image and text repre-
225   sentations through the encoders. Formally, we extract representations h = Eimage (xk ), xk ∈ I ∗ and
226   z = Etext (tk ), tk ∈ T ∗ , and train by minimizing the CLIP loss LCLIP (h, z).

236   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
237
238   Downstream Tasks We use five different downstream tasks to assess performance. For ease of
239   evaluation, we categorize the downstream tasks into two categories; (1) Vision Tasks and (2)
240   Vision-Language Tasks. The first focuses on evaluating the capabilities of the frozen vision
241   encoder Eimage only, i.e., linear probing and few-shot classification. The second evaluates the
242
      synergy between the image encoder Eimage and text encoder Etext together. We use image retrieval,
      text retrieval, and vision-language zero-shot classification tasks as evaluation tasks, following
243
      the original CLIP (Radford et al., 2021a). Since our evaluation pipeline consists of several tasks
244
      whose metrics can behave differenty, we aggregate performance across all tasks using the ∆MTL
245   metric (Vandenhende et al., 2021), where a model with positive ∆MTL indicates an overall better
246   performance compared to a reference baseline.
247
248
      Datasets We use the real datasets CC3M (Sharma et al., 2018) (3 × 106 samples) and CC12M
      (Changpinyo et al., 2021) (8.8 × 106 samples2 ). Real images come at different resolutions, so we
249
      resize the shorter edge of the images to 256px. For SynthCLIP, we generate an entirely synthetic
250
      dataset, that we call SynthCI (Synthetic Captions-Images) at different scales (number of samples).
251   We refer to SynthCI-3M for a version of SynthCI where T ∗ and I ∗ include 3 × 106 captions and
252   images, respectively. For zero-shot evaluation we use ImageNet (Russakovsky et al., 2015), for lin-
253   ear probing and few shot we use CIFAR10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009a), CIFAR100 (Krizhevsky et al.,
254   2009b), Aircraft (Maji et al., 2013), DTD (Cimpoi et al., 2014), Flowers (Nilsback & Zisserman,
255   2008), Pets (Parkhi et al., 2012), SUN397 (Xiao et al., 2010), Caltech-101 (Fei-Fei et al., 2004)
256   and Food-101 (Bossard et al., 2014), and for image and text retrieval we use MSCOCO (Lin et al.,
257   2014), Flickr8K (Hodosh et al., 2013) and Flickr30K (Young et al., 2014).
258   Caption & Image Generation Models For caption generation, we use Mistral-7B-Instruct V0.2
259   (Jiang et al., 2023) with temperature 0.7 and top-p set to 0.95. We also set the presence and fre-
260   quency penalties at 1. For image synthesis, we use Stable Diffusion v1.5 (Rombach et al., 2022)
261   with classifier-free guidance set to 2 and 50 Denoising Diffusion Implicit Models (DDIM) steps
262   following Tian et al. (2023). The images are generated at 512 × 512px and then stored on disk at
263   256 × 256px. It takes 0.9 seconds to generate and save one image on NVIDIA A100 GPU. Image
264   generation was performed on a 48 A100-80GB GPU cluster.

266   default CLIP text encoder (Radford et al., 2021a) as Etext . Eimage and Etext are trained for 40 epochs
267   with a batch size of 4096, a learning rate of 5 × 10−4 , weight decay of 0.5, cosine scheduler, and

396   4.3    A NALYSIS
397
398   Here, we conduct an analysis to examine key aspects of SynthCLIP. We focus on the importance
399
      of textual and visual data modalities, ablate pipeline components (data filtering technique and LLM
      used for captions generation), and quantify the effects of C on performance. For all tests, we train
400
      on 3 million samples, i.e., a similar scale to CC3M, due to the high computational cost of the larger
401
      experiments.
402
403   Do synthetic captions or synthetic images matter more? SynthCLIP uses entirely synthetic text-
404   image pairs. A key question arises: which has a greater impact on the model’s performance in down-
405
      stream tasks – synthetic images or synthetic captions? In Table 4a, we compare the standard CLIP
      model trained on CC3M, SynthCLIP, and two hybrid CLIP variants. One hybrid uses real captions
406
      with synthetic images (CLIP + Text-to-Image), generated using Stable Diffusion v1.5, while the
407
      other pairs real images with synthetic captions (CLIP + Captioning), created with the LLaVA (Liu
408   et al., 2023a) model. Note that these, requiring one real modality, are less scalable than SynthCLIP.
409
410   We measure that CLIP + Captioning significantly outperforms standard CLIP in several benchmarks,
411
      indicating the effectiveness of synthetic captions in CLIP training. For instance, this approach im-
      proves linear probing by 6.5% and text retrieval by 19.5%, though it slightly decreases zero-shot
412
      performance by 2.5%. On the other hand, CLIP + Text-to-Image shows less marked improvements
413
      and no gains in few-shot performance. This suggests that keeping images real and recaptioning them
414   is more advantageous than generating images for real captions, possibly due to domain shifts and
415   content generation mismatches in synthetic images as noted in Gani et al. (2023); Wu et al. (2023).
416
417
      Hence, we introduce SynthCLIP+Captioning as an extra baseline. Given that TTI models could
      miss details in text prompts, recaptioning generated images can be beneficial. This is evident in
418
      Figure 4b, where recaptioning corrects alignment issues from the image generation process (e.g.
419
      the missing bench in the generated image). Comparing SynthCLIP and SynthCLIP+Captioning
420   in Table 4a (rows 4 and 5) shows significant gains with captioning, such as a 9.6% improvement
421   in image retrieval. These results advocate for a potential boost in performance from future devel-
422   opments in prompt faithfulness of the TTI model. Moreover, it also opens doors to combinations of
423   caption enrichment techniques such as VeCLIP (Lai et al., 2023) and CapsFusion (Yu et al., 2023a).

      Concept Bank Impact We explore how the concept bank size C and the type of concepts it contains
473
      affect the downstream performance of the model. For this, we create two subsets of C. The first
474   subset, CCC3M , is derived by identifying the concepts that appear in CC3M captions, by performing
475   substring matching with concepts included in C. This results in 40 × 103 CC3M-related concepts.
476   The second, Crand , is formed by randomly selecting the same number of concepts as in CCC3M from C.
477
      We generate 3M images for each of CCC3M and Crand and train SynthCLIP on the generated datasets.
478
      We show results in Table 4. Interestingly, we noticed that focusing on CC3M-specific concepts
479
      (CCC3M ) enhances performance compared to training with the full C. For example, using CCC3M yields
480   a 3.9% improvement in text retrieval and 1.6% in linear probing. We hypothesize that this might
481   be because CCC3M ’s concepts are more aligned with concepts appearing in the downstream tasks,
482   hence indicating a potential distribution bias in CC3M towards concepts prevalent in downstream
483   task images. In contrast, using Crand leads to lower performance in all tasks compared to the full C.
484   For example, we observe a 1.2% decrease in text retrieval and 0.8% in linear probing, likely because
485   Crand ’s concepts are less relevant to the downstream tasks. Hence, when specific insights about
      downstream tasks are unavailable, it is preferable to train on the widest possible range of concepts.

      5   D ISCUSSION
487
488   Here, we discuss additional consequential properties of SynthCLIP, resulting from the usage of
489   synthetic data, and we draw conclusions.
490
491
      Mitigation of long-tail effects. While the distribution shift is preventing matching performance on
      the same data scale, we argue that the control over C may be used to mitigate long-tail effects. We
492
      provide preliminary insights by evaluating zero-shot classification accuracy on 10 classes (listed in
493
      appendix) included in C, but undetected in CC12M with substring matching. Importantly, we com-
494   pare both SynthCLIP and CLIP trained on 8.8 × 106 images. For evaluation, we collect 150 samples
495   per class. We report 44.18/60.04 accuracy for CLIP/SynthCLIP. This gives preliminary insights into
496   how synthetic data could be used for training CLIP models resistant to long-tail distributions.
497
      Data safety Another aspect resulting from the control of C is the potential for training SynthCLIP
498
      models exclusively on safe data. This is not always possible with web-collected data (Schuhmann
499
      et al., 2022), due to image-based NSFW detectors having limited performance (Schramowski et al.,
500   2022), and for the subjective nature of offensive content. We argue that our concept-based caption
501   generation may help the synthesis of safe images only. In C, we did not modify the concepts pro-
502   posed by Xu et al. (2023) for reproducibility. However, as a preliminary insight, we process C with
503   an LLM (more details in appendix), detecting 3.15% NSFW concepts that can be filtered from the
504   original C. Moreover, our data synthesis pipeline could be associated with recent approaches for
505   safe image generation (Schramowski et al., 2023). This opens possibilities for safe CLIP trainings.
506   Concluding remarks We presented SynthCLIP, a CLIP model trained exclusively on synthetic
507   data. Our experiments show SynthCLIP’s scalability and capability to match the performance
508   of models trained on real data, given enough samples, on a large concept corpus. This paves
509   new ways for entirely synthetic training at scale, possibly extending the capabilities of CLIP. Our
510   investigation of the properties of SynthCLIP reveals novel insights into the role of synthetic data in
511   vision-language models.

      A     C OMPARISON WITH C ONCURRENT W ORKS
764
765
      We now discuss our positioning with respect to recent concurrent works.
766
767
             • Concepts. In Fan et al. (2024), they propose a study on large-scale training of synthetic data
768            for supervised learning and CLIP paradigms. They restrict the concept bank to ImageNet
769            classes and provide several techniques to prompt text-to-image models. We refer the reader
770            to section 3.1 of Fan et al. (2024) for more details on the different proposed prompting
771            techniques and TTI generation pipeline. In the supervised setting, it is clear from Table-A7
772            of their supplementary material that the performance of training on synthetic data becomes
773            comparable when there are 8 times more synthetic data available at classifier free configura-
774            tion scale of 2. Beyond that, the performance of training on synthetic data starts to saturate.
775            Additionally, in that work, the authors assume some class-priors and restrict their training
776
               to ImageNet classes, which has been shown in Hammoud et al. (2024) not to be general-
               izable to classes that are significantly different than those in ImageNet. Additionally, as
777
               mentioned in Section 5, training on a larger concept bank may lead to a better performance
778
               on long-tail concepts and hence the importance of covering many concepts during training
779            beyond ImageNet classes. In Tian et al. (2024), the generation is also conditioned on the
780            downstream classes. We refer the reader to Table-11 of their supplementary material. This
781            gives you an unfair advantage over real data that is not necessarily conditioned on these
782            classes (Section 4.3). For StableRep, they require the existence of real captions which they
783            utilize as prompts for TTI model which is not scalable and have a limited effectiveness
784            compared to synthetic higher quality captions (Section 4.3).
785          • CLIP-specific tasks. The main difference in our work is that we focus on CLIP-related
786            tasks in a holistic manner. Indeed, Fan et al. (2024) only proposes zero-shot evaluation,
787            with no reasoning about how different tasks are impacted. Tian et al. (2024) only proposes
788            very limited experiments with linear probing on ImageNet. Instead, we focus on 5 different
789            tasks, and contextualize separately the effects of synthetic data on different tasks.
790          • Data generation pipeline. Fan et al. (2024) uses multiple strategies for captions genera-
791            tion, that do not necessarily work with large concept banks. We also provide an experiment
792            on this in B.3. Tian et al. (2024) generation pipeline is the most similar to ours, but relies
793            on in-context examples generated by GPT-4. In our preliminary experiments, we verify
794            that caption generation with in-context learning on Mistral 7B causes the model to collapse
795            (Section B), generating variations of the in-context samples by just replacing c. Hence, our
796
               designed prompt is more adaptable to different models, as we show in Table 3b of the main
               paper.

       Stable Diffusion v1.5 uses the OpenAI CLIP textual encoder for prompt encoding, and it is one of
1020   the most popular choices for TTI generation. One may argue that this would limit the possibilities
1021   of SynthCLIP to the OpenAI CLIP performance. First, let us highlight how only the textual encoder
1022   of CLIP is used in Stable Diffusion. CLIP is defined as a pair of encoders mapping text and images
1023   to a joint embedding (Radford et al., 2021b). While Stable Diffusion uses a pre-trained CLIP text
1024   encoder for prompt interpretation, the CLIP visual encoder is not used anywhere in the pipeline.
1025   Also, the usage of CLIP for textual encoding is a design choice in Stable Diffusion, but other popular
       models such as Imagen (Saharia et al., 2022) rely on textual encoders like T5-XXL (Raffel et al.,


                                                               19

1026
       2020) trained on text only, hence training on the text-images real datasets of CLIP is not strictly
1027   necessary for the SynthCLIP pipeline.
1028
1029   To prove that we are not limited by the pretrained text encoder performance, we fine-tune the Ope-
1030
       nAI CLIP (ViT-B/16), pre-trained on 400 million captioned images, on SynthCI. The finetuning is
       done on 1M samples, for a single optimization step, in order to avoid loss of performance due to
1031
       catastrophic forgetting. We report results in Table 8, where we improve over the baseline in four
1032
       tasks out of five, with ∆MTL =+0.56%. Our improved performance suggests that our approach
1033   allows us to extend even the capabilities of the CLIP model used in the text-to-image pipeline, by
1034   adding more synthetic data to the training.

1038
1039   Here, we discuss limitations. The most evident disadvantage of synthetic CLIP models is the com-
1040   putational effort required to generate the training dataset, which may lead to a high carbon impact.
1041   Our generation process currently takes approximately 6.5 days using a 48-A100-80GB GPU clus-
1042   ter, equivalent to 313 GPU days. However, recent advancements in text-to-image and large language
1043
       models have not only enhanced generation quality but also accelerated inference speeds (Sauer et al.,
       2023; Kwon et al., 2023). With continual technological advancements, we anticipate a reduction in
1044
       the time required for generation, leading to more efficient and scalable end-to-end approaches. Fu-
1045
       ture research should include further experimentation with various language models, text-to-image
1046   generators, and caption generation prompts to identify optimal configurations for improvement.
1047   Moreover, there may be concerns related to copyright issues and memorization in text-to-image
1048   diffusion models (Carlini et al., 2023). With the advancements in differential privacy for generative
1049   models (Cao et al., 2021), these could be solved in the near future. Finally, we acknowledge that
1050   although we train our CLIP on synthetic data only, both the LLM and the TTI have been trained on
1051   real data. Nevertheless, it is well-known the capacity of generative networks to create new content
1052   not included in the dataset (Rombach et al., 2022), factor also proved in our experiments.
