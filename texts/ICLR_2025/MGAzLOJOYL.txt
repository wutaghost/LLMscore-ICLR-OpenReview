009                                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011             Song generation focuses on producing controllable high-quality songs based on
012             various personalized prompts. However, existing methods struggle to generate
013             high-quality vocals and accompaniments with effective style control and proper
014             alignment. Additionally, they fall short in supporting various personalized tasks
015             based on diverse prompts. To address these challenges, we introduce MultiBand,
016             the first multi-task song generation model for synthesizing high-quality, aligned
017             songs with extensive control based on diverse personalized prompts. MultiBand
018
                comprises these primary models: 1) VocalBand, a decoupled model, leverages the
                flow-matching method for singing styles, pitches, and mel-spectrograms generation,
019
                allowing fast and high-quality vocal generation with high-level control. 2) Accomp-
020
                Band, a flow-based transformer model, incorporates the Aligned Vocal Encoder,
021             using contrastive learning for alignment, and Band-MOE, selecting suitable experts
022             for enhanced quality and control. This model allows for generating controllable,
023             high-quality accompaniments perfectly aligned with vocals. 3) Two generation
024             models, LyricBand for lyrics and MelodyBand for melodies, contribute to the com-
025             prehensive multi-task song generation system, allowing for extensive control based
026             on multiple personalized prompts. Experimental results demonstrate that Multi-
027             Band performs better over baseline models across multiple tasks using objective
028             and subjective metrics. Audio samples are available at https://multiband.github.io.

      Despite significant advancements in SVS and music domains, generating high-quality, controllable,
055   aligned songs remains challenging. Song generation aims to enable controllable musical experiences,
056   with broad applications ranging from entertainment videos to professional composition. As shown in
057   Figure 1, song generation models can leverage different prompts for multiple tasks. Text prompts
058   allow for control over tasks, lyrics, melody, singing styles (like singing methods, emotion, and
059   techniques), and music styles (like genre, tone, and instrumentation), while audio prompts enable
060   users to input their voice or preferred music for customization. However, the few existing song
061   generation models (Zhiqing et al., 2024), which typically generate vocals and accompaniments using
062   transformer models separately, struggle to produce high-fidelity songs. These models also lack
063
      mechanisms to align vocals with accompaniments properly and fail to achieve effective control.
064   Currently, song generation encounters three major challenges:
065
066          • Limitations in synthesizing high-quality vocals with effective style control. For singing
067
               style control, StyleSinger (Zhang et al., 2024) conducts singing style transfer, while
               PromptSinger (Wang et al., 2024) achieves singer identity control. However, existing
068
               models have yet to generate pleasing vocals with high-level style control (like singing
069
               methods, emotion, and techniques) by text prompts, and customization with audio prompts.
070
071
             • Difficulties in generating controllable, high-fidelity, aligned accompaniments. Existing
               music generation models (Copet et al., 2024) are limited to low-fidelity outputs with little
072
               control. Current text-to-song models (Zhiqing et al., 2024) also lack mechanisms for
073
               effectively aligning vocals with accompaniments. Generating controllable (like genre, tone,
074            and instrumentation), aligned, high-quality accompaniments remains challenging.
075
             • Challenges in multi-task song generation based on various personalized prompts. The
076
               limited existing song generation methods (Li et al., 2024a) primarily focus on the text-to-
077            song task and do not support a wide range of personalized song generation tasks based on
078            diverse text and audio prompts. This reliance on constrained inputs leads to a suboptimal user
079            experience and restricts the models’ ability to customize songs for individual preferences.
080
081   To address these challenges, we introduce MultiBand, the first multi-task song generation model
082   for synthesizing high-quality, aligned songs with extensive control based on diverse personalized
083   prompts. Following the human perception that accompaniment complements vocal melody with
084
      complex harmonic and rhythmic structure (Zhiqing et al., 2024), we generate them separately. To
      achieve fast and high-quality vocal generation with high-level control, we design a decoupled model
085
      VocalBand, predicting singing styles, pitches, and mel-spectrograms based on the flow-matching
086
      model. Based on the complex nature of music, we introduce a flow-based transformer model
087   AccompBand to generate high-fidelity, controllable, aligned accompaniments. For proper alignment,
088   we propose the Aligned Vocal Encoder using contrastive learning, encoding vocals to carry style,
089   rhythm, and melody closely related to accompaniment. We also design Band-MOE (Mixture of
090   Experts), selecting suitable experts for enhanced quality and control, considering the noise level, text
091   prompts, and vocal embedding. Additionally, we add two generation models, LyricBand for lyrics
092   and MelodyBand for melodies, contributing to the comprehensive multi-task song generation system.
093   Our experiments on a combination of open-source and web-crawled bilingual song datasets show
094   MultiBand can generate high-quality songs based on various prompts, outperforming baseline models
095   in multiple tasks, including melody and lyric generation, vocal generation, accompaniment and song
096   generation, and other related tasks. The main contributions of our work are summarized as follows:
097
098          • We introduce MultiBand, the first multi-task song generation model for synthesizing high-
099
               quality, aligned songs with extensive control based on diverse personalized prompts.
100          • We design a decoupled model VocalBand, which leverages the flow-matching model to
101            generate singing styles, pitches, and mel-spectrograms, enabling fast and high-quality vocal
102
               synthesis with high-level personalized control through input text and audio prompts.
103          • We propose a flow-based transformer model AccompBand to generate high-quality, control-
104            lable, aligned accompaniments, with the Aligned Vocal Encoder, using contrastive learning
105            for alignment, and Band-MOE, selecting suitable experts for enhanced quality and control.
106          • Experimental results demonstrate that MultiBand enables high-level personalized control
107            across multiple bilingual song generation tasks based on various text and audio prompts,
               achieving superior objective and subjective evaluations compared to baseline models.

      2     BACKGROUND
109
110
      Singing Voice Synthesis Singing Voice Synthesis (SVS) rapidly advances as a field for generating
111   high-quality singing voices from given lyrics and music scores. Choi & Nam (2022) presents a
112   melody-unsupervised model that only requires pairs of audio and lyrics, eliminating the need for
113   temporal alignment. Wesinger (Zhang et al., 2022c) proposes a Transformer-alike acoustic model.
114   VISinger 2 (Zhang et al., 2022b) employs digital signal processing techniques to enhance synthesis
115   fidelity, while Kim et al. (2024) uses adversarial multi-task learning to disentangle timbre and pitch,
116   improving the naturalness of generated voices. Further advancements include MuSE-SVS (Kim
117   et al., 2023), which introduces a multi-singer emotional singing voice synthesizer, and StyleSinger
118   (Zhang et al., 2024), which facilitates style transfer and zero-shot synthesis by using audio prompts to
119
      extract timbre and styles via a residual quantization method. Additionally, PromptSinger (Wang et al.,
      2024) attempts to control speaker identity in singing voices based on text descriptions. On the dataset
120
      front, M4Singer (Zhang et al., 2022a) and OpenSinger (Huang et al., 2021) contribute by releasing
121
      multi-singer datasets. Despite these advancements, these approaches can not generate accompaniment
122   aligned with vocals. Recently, Melodist (Zhiqing et al., 2024) has introduced a text-to-song model that
123   sequentially generates vocal and accompaniment codec tokens using two auto-regressive transformers.
124   However, the challenge of achieving highly controllable and personalized pleasing vocals persists.
125
126   Music Generation Music generation encompasses multiple tasks, like symbolic music generation
127   and accompaniment creation, based on text descriptions or audio prompts. MuseGAN (Dong et al.,
128   2018) employs a GAN-based approach to generate symbolic music, while PopMAG (Ren et al., 2020a)
129   generates many instrumental tracks simultaneously. SongMASS (Sheng et al., 2021) uses transformer
130   models to generate lyrics or melodies conditioned on each other. MusicLM (Agostinelli et al., 2023)
131
      leverages joint textual-music representations from MuLan (Huang et al., 2022a) to generate semantic
      and acoustic tokens based on transformer decoders. MusicGen (Copet et al., 2024) generates music
132
      codec tokens within a single transformer decoder with codebook interleaving patterns. MusicLDM
133
      (Chen et al., 2024) incorporates beat-tracking information and employs data augmentation through
134   latent mixups to address potential plagiarism concerns in music generation. Additionally, SingSong
135   (Donahue et al., 2023) presents a model capable of generating background music to complement
136   provided vocals. Recently, MelodyLM (Li et al., 2024a) has employed transformer models for melody
137   and vocal generation, along with a latent diffusion model to create accompaniments. Nevertheless,
138   challenges remain in improving the controllability and quality of music generation. Existing methods
139   also lack the necessary mechanisms for precisely aligning vocals and accompaniments, and they do
140   not support multi-task song generation based on various personalized text and audio prompts.

144   This section introduces MultiBand. We design two distinct models, VocalBand for vocals and Ac-
145   compBand for accompaniments, tailored to their unique characteristics. Additionally, we incorporate
146   LyricBand for lyrics and MelodyBand for melodies, composing a multi-task song generation system.

148   3.1   M ULTI -TASK S ONG G ENERATION
149
150   As shown in Figure 2 (a), MultiBand handles multi-task song generation based on text and audio
151   prompts. We employ a text encoder to generate text tokens zp . Without lyrics or music scores,
152   LyricBand and MelodyBand predict phonemes p and notes n (pitch and duration) as target contents.
153
      Next, in Figure 2 (b), to achieve fast and high-quality vocal generation with granular and personalized
      control, we introduce VocalBand, which decouples the content zc , timbre zt , and style zs . Through the
154
      Flow-based Pitch Predictor, Mel Decoder, and pre-trained vocoder, the target vocal yv is synthesized.
155
      Then, in Figure 2 (c), for the complex nature of accompaniment, we design AccompBand to achieve
156   superior quality, control, and alignment. AccompBand uses Aligned Vocal and Accomp Encoders,
157   pre-trained through contrastive learning, to extract aligned embeddings zv from yv and x from ground
158   truth (GT) accompaniment yˆa during training. zv and noise-injected xt are processed by multiple
159   Band Transformer Blocks with Band-MOE (Mixture of Experts), which selects suitable experts based
160   on zv , zp , and time step t for enhanced quality and control. During inference, the ordinary differential
161   equation (ODE) solver, Aligned Accomp Decoder, and vocoder generate the target accompaniment
      ya from input zv and Gaussian noise ϵ. Finally, yv and ya are combined to the final target song y.

182   3.2   VOCAL BAND
183
184   Decomposition As shown in Figure 2(b), for more personalized and fine-grained control, we
185   disentangle target vocal yv into distinct representations: content zc , style zs (e.g., singing methods,
186   emotion, techniques, pronunciation, articulation skills), and timbre zt . For zc , phonemes p and notes
187   n (note pitch and duration) are encoded by a phoneme encoder and a note encoder. Given a vocal
188
      prompt y˜v , the timbre and personalized styles (like pronunciation and articulation skills) should
      remain consistent. We pass y˜v through a timbre encoder to obtain z˜t , while zt = z˜t . Next, the residual
189
      style encoder employs a Residual Quantization model (Lee et al., 2022a) to extract phoneme-level
190
      prompt style z˜s . This serves as an information bottleneck to filter out non-style information (Zhang
191   et al., 2024), ensuring effective decomposition. The Flow-based Style Predictor uses zc , zt , z˜s , and
192   text tokens zp to predict zs , learning both personalized styles of z˜s and style control information in
193   zp (like singing methods, emotions, techniques). For more details, please refer to Appendix C.2.
194
195   Flow-based Style Predictor Singing styles typically exhibit continuous and complex dynamics,
196   involving intricate variations. The flow-matching model (Liu et al., 2022b) is suitable for generating
197   styles with finer-grained control by modeling styles as a smooth transformation, effectively balancing
198   multiple control inputs, enabling a fast and stable generation of natural and consistent styles.
199   As shown in Figure 3 (a), we design the Flow-based Style Predictor using content zc , timbre zt ,
200   prompt style z˜s , and text tokens zp to predict the target style zs . With input zc and zt , we employ a
201   style alignment model with the Scaled Dot-Product Attention mechanism (Vaswani et al., 2017) to
202   align style control information from zp (e.g., singing methods, emotions, techniques) with contents.
203   The fused condition c is then fed into an ODE solver, which transforms Gaussian noise ϵ into zs
204   along a probability path pt (zst ). We concatenate z˜s with ϵ to allow zs to learn personalized styles
205   (e.g., pronunciation, articulation skills). zst is obtained by linear interpolation at time t between ϵ
206
      and zs , which is extracted from the GT vocal by the residual style encoder, thus the target vector
      field u(zst , t) = zs − ϵ. The learned vector field vt (zst , t|c; θ), predicted by a vector field estimator
207
      at each time t, ensures smooth interpolation between the initial noise and output zs , guided by the
208
      flow-matching objective, which minimizes the distance between the learned and true vector fields:
209

      Aligned Vocal and Accomp Encoders The style, rhythm, and melody of a song are complex and
243
      variable, making the alignment between vocal and accompaniment challenging in the song generation
244   task. As shown in Figure 3 (c), given a vocal-accompaniment pair (yˆv , yˆa ), we design the Aligned
245   Vocal and Accomp Encoders to extract aligned embeddings (zv , x). To reconstruct high-quality
246   accompaniment, we introduce the Aligned Accomp Decoder. All encoders and decoders are based
247   on the VAE model (Kingma & Welling, 2013). For better alignment, we employ the contrastive
248   objective (Radford et al., 2021) and define two types: stylistic contrast Lsty and temporal contrast
249   Ltem . For Lsty , we maximize the similarity of pairs from the same song and minimize for different
250   songs. It encourages learning stylistic alignment across different songs. In specific, we encode pairs
251   from different songs, B = {(xi , zvi )}N

264   Band-MOE Accompaniment generation is highly complex due to the intricate interplay of various
265   instruments and alignment with vocals, especially for high-quality and long-sequence generation.
266   Flow matching enables smooth transformations, leading to stable and quick generation, while trans-
267   former models effectively capture intricate long-range dependencies, making flow-based transformers
268   suitable for this task. We integrate the Aligned Vocal Encoder’s output zv with the noisy input xt ,
269   utilizing the self-attention mechanism of the transformer for alignment. Based on Flag-Dit (Gao et al.,
      2024), we design and stack multiple Band Transformer Blocks as the vector field estimator.

      For enhanced music quality and control, we propose Band-MOE (Mixture of Experts). As shown
271   in Figure 3 (d), Band-MOE consists of three expert groups: Aligned MOE, Controlled MOE, and
272   Acoustic MOE, each containing multiple experts. Aligned MOE conditions on zv , adjusting inputs to
273   match vocal features like loudness and frequency, selecting suitable experts like one specialized in
274   large loudness and alto range. Controlled MOE uses aligned styles in text prompts to select experts
275   for fine-grained style control, such as one for aggressive drums with metal guitar tones. Given the
276   varying behavior of the transformer at different noise levels (Feng et al., 2023), we design a global
277   router that adjusts the weighting of outputs from Aligned MOE and Controlled MOE: 1) at early
278   time steps (near 0), where hidden representation h is highly noisy, the network prioritizes matching
279
      with vocal for coherent reconstruction; 2) at later time steps (near 1), where h has been largely
      reconstructed, the network focuses more on refining stylistic details, relying heavily on text prompts.
280
281   Finally, mel-spectrogram patterns exhibit variation across acoustic frequencies (Lee et al., 2022b). In
282   music, high-frequency components often include the harmonics and overtones of instruments like
283   strings and flutes. At the same time, low-frequency content typically encompasses basslines and
284   kick drums providing rhythm and depth. Since the Aligned Vocal and Accomp Encoders employ 1D
285
      convolutions, the latent should retain the frequency distribution. Therefore, we design Acoustic MOE,
      selecting experts by different acoustic frequency dimensions for better quality. All routing strategies
286
      are based on the dense-to-sparse Gumbel-Softmax (Nie et al., 2021), allowing dynamic and efficient
287
      expert selection. For more model details and MOE algorithm, please refer to Appendix D.3 and D.4.
288
289   Classifier-free Guidance To further control styles of the generated accompaniment based on
290   input text prompts, we implement the classifier-free guidance (CFG) strategy. During AccompBand
291   training, we randomly replace input text tokens zp with encoded empty strings ∅ at a probability of
292   0.2. During inference, we modify the output vector field of the Band Transformer blocks as follows:
293                         vcf g (x, t|zp ; θ) = γvt (x, t|zp ; θ) + (1 − γ)vt (x, t|∅; θ),              (3)
294   where γ is the classifier free guidance scale trading off creativity and controllability. When γ = 1,
295   vcf g is the same as the original vt (x, t|zp ; θ). For more details, please refer to Appendix D.5.

297   3.4   LYRIC AND M ELODY G ENERATION
298
299   LyricBand To enable more personalized song generation tasks, we introduce LyricBand, a system
300   designed to generate complete song lyrics based on more customizable text prompts. Users can design
301   the theme, emotion, and other parameters to generate fully personalized lyrics. We leverage QLoRA
302
      (Dettmers et al., 2024) for efficient fine-tuning of a well-performing open-source bilingual large
      language model Qwen-7B (Bai et al., 2023). By utilizing 4-bit quantization and low-rank adapters,
303
      QLoRA enables LyricBand to adapt effectively to lyric generation, enabling high-level customization
304
      and creativity across various input text prompts. For more details, please refer to Appendix E.1.
305
306   MelodyBand Previous singing voice and song generation models often require users to provide
307   music scores to achieve stable melodies (Zhiqing et al., 2024), lacking personalized customization
308   of the melody. Inspired by symbolic music models (Dong et al., 2018), we propose MelodyBand,
309   which generates musical notes based on text prompts, lyrics, and vocal prompts. We employ a non-
310   autoregressive transformer model to efficiently generate note pitches and durations simultaneously.
311   After encoding phonemes and timbre, MelodyBand achieves fine-grained melody control by injecting
312   text tokens through cross-attention mechanisms. We train MelodyBand with the cross-entropy loss
313   for note pitches and an L2 loss for note durations. For more details, please refer to Appendix E.2.
314
315   3.5   T RAINING AND I NFERENCE
316
      The VocalBand, AccompBand, LyricBand, and MelodyBand are trained separately, and the detailed
317
      training details are provided in Appendix B.2. For inference, our model can accept various prompts
318   for multiple tasks. Without full lyrics or music scores as input, LyricBand and MelodyBand generate
319   phonemes p and notes n as target contents. For song generation or singing style transfer tasks,
320   VocalBand generates the target vocal yv , as well as AccompBand generates the target accompaniment
321   ya from yv and Gaussian noise ϵ. During music style transfer, AccompBand uses noisy prompt
322   accompaniment y˜a instead of ϵ as input. In the vocal-to-song task, VocalBand is not used, whereas
323   in the accompaniment-to-song task, notes n are extracted from ground-truth accompaniment yˆa to
      guide VocalBand for vocal generation. More inference details can be found in Appendix B.3.

328   Dataset We train our model using a combination of bilingual web-crawled and open-source
329   song datasets. Since there are no publicly available annotated song datasets including vocals and
330   accompaniments, we collect 20k Chinese and English songs from well-known music websites. To
331   expand data, we also incorporate open-source singing datasets including OpenCpop (Wang et al.,
332   2022) (5 hours in Chinese), M4Singer (Zhang et al., 2022a) (30 hours in Chinese), OpenSinger
333   (Huang et al., 2021) (83 hours in Chinese), and PopBuTFy (Liu et al., 2022a) (10 hours in English).
334
      After processing and cleaning, we have about 1,000 hours of song data and 1,100 hours of vocal data.
      We also use a filtered subset of LP-MusicCaps-MSD (Doh et al., 2023), resulting in about 1,200
335
      hours of accompaniment data. For zero-shot evaluation, we leave out 500 out-of-domain bilingual
336
      samples with unseen singers as the test set for each task. For more details, please refer to Appendix F.
337
338
      Implementation Details We derive mel-spectrograms from raw waveforms with a 48kHz sample
339
      rate, 1024 window size, 320 hop size, and 80 mel bins. We use 4 layers of Band Transformer Blocks.
340   The flow-matching time step is 100 for VocalBand and 1000 for AccompBand during training, while
341   25 during inference with the Euler ODE solver. For more details, please refer to Appendix B.1.
342
343   Evaluation Metrics We conduct both subjective and objective evaluations on generated samples.
344   For lyric generation, we use overall quality (OVL) and relevance to the prompt (REL) for subjective
345   evaluation. In melody generation, multiple objective metrics are employed for testing controllability.
346   We use the Krumhansl-Schmuckler algorithm to predict the potential key of the generated notes and
347   report the average key accuracy KA. We compute the average absolute difference of average pitches
348   (APD) and temporal duration (TD, in seconds). Moreover, we employ pitch and duration distribution
349   similarity (PD and DD). Melody distance (MD) is also computed using dynamic time warping.
350   For vocal generation, we conduct MOS (Mean Opinion Score) as the subjective evaluation. We use
351   MOS-Q for synthesized quality and MOS-C for controllability based on text prompts. We also use
352   F0 Frame Error (FFE) as the objective metric. For singing style transfer, we also employ MOS-S and
353   Cosine Similarity (Cos) to assess singer similarity in timbre and personalized styles of vocal prompts.
354
      For accompaniment and song generation, we ask raters to evaluate the audio samples in terms of
355   overall quality (OVL), relevance to the prompt (REL), and alignment with the vocal (ALI). For
356   objective evaluation, we calculate Frechet Audio Distance (FAD), Kullback–Leibler Divergence
357   (KLD), and the CLAP score (CLAP). Please refer to Appendix G for more details about evaluation.
358
359   Baseline Models For lyric generation, we use the original Qwen-7B (Bai et al., 2023) as the
360   baseline model. For melody generation, we compare with SongMASS (Sheng et al., 2021) and MIDI
361   part of MelodyLM (Li et al., 2024a). For vocal generation, we compare against VISinger2 (Zhang
362   et al., 2022b), a traditional high-fidelity SVS model, StyleSinger (Zhang et al., 2024), the current
363   best zero-shot SVS model with style transfer, and vocal parts of Melodist (Zhiqing et al., 2024)
364   and MelodyLM. For accompaniment generation, we compare with MusicGen (Copet et al., 2024),
365   LuminaT2Music (Gao et al., 2024), and the accompaniment parts of Melodist and MelodyLM. For
366
      closed-source models Melodist and MelodyLM, we report objective metrics in their papers and use
      their demo pages for subjective evaluation. For other models, we employ their open-source codes.

528   5     C ONCLUSIONS
529
530   In this paper, we present MultiBand, the first multi-task song generation model for synthesizing
531
      high-quality, aligned songs with extensive control based on diverse personalized prompts. We mainly
      design these models: 1) VocalBand, a decoupled model leveraging the flow-matching model for
532
      singing styles, pitches, and mel-spectrograms generation, allowing high-level control for fast and
533
      high-quality vocal generation. 2) AccompBand, a flow-based transformer model, with the Aligned
534   Vocal Encoder, using contrastive learning for alignment, and Band-MOE, selecting suitable experts
535   for enhanced quality and control. This model generates controllable, high-quality accompaniments
536   perfectly aligned with vocals. 3) Two generation models, LyricBand for lyrics and MelodyBand for
537   melodies, contribute to the comprehensive multi-task song generation system. Experimental results
538   demonstrate that MultiBand enables high-level personalized control across multiple song generation
539   tasks based on various prompts, achieving superior objective and subjective evaluations compared to
      baseline models. Due to the space limitation, we include additional discussions in the Appendix J.

      6   E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542
      Large-scale generative models always present ethical challenges. MultiBand, due to its multi-task
543   song generation capabilities, could potentially be misused for dubbing in entertainment short videos,
544   raising concerns about the infringement of famous singers’ copyrights. Then, its ability to transfer
545   and control multiple song styles about lyric, melody, singing, and music, lowers the requirements for
546   high-quality, personalized, controllable song generation, posing some risks like unfair competition
547   and potential unemployment for professionals in related music and singing occupations. To mitigate
548   these potential risks, we will explore methods like music watermarking to protect individual privacy.

774   A    R ECTIFIED FLOW- MATCHING
775
776   In this section, we introduce the flow-matching generative method, as described by Liu et al. (2022b).
777   In generative modeling, the true data distribution is denoted as q(x1 ), which can be sampled but lacks
778   an accessible density function. Consider a probability path pt (xt ), where x0 ∼ p0 (x) represents a
779
      known simple distribution (e.g., a standard Gaussian), and x1 ∼ p1 (x) approximates the real data
      distribution. The objective of flow-matching is to model this probability path directly, expressed as
780
      an ordinary differential equation (ODE):
781
782
                                           dx = u(x, t)dt, t ∈ [0, 1],                                    (4)
783   where u(x, t) denotes the target vector field, and t is the time index. If the vector field u is known,
784   realistic data can be recovered by reversing the flow. To approximate u, a vector field estimator v(·)
785
      is used, with the flow-matching objective defined as:
                                                                              2
786                               LFM (θ) = Et,pt (x) ∥v(x, t; θ) − u(x, t)∥ ,                              (5)
787   where pt (x) denotes the distribution of x at time t. To enable conditional generation, we incorporate
788   conditional information c, leading to the conditional flow-matching objective (Lipman et al., 2022):
789                                                                                      2
                        LCFM (θ) = Et,p1 (x1 ),pt (x|x1 ) ∥v(x, t|c; θ) − u(x, t|x1 , c)∥ .               (6)
790
791
      Flow-matching proposes a straight path from noise to data. Specifically, we use a linear interpolation
      between the data x1 and Gaussian noise x0 to generate samples at time t:
792
793
                                            xt = (1 − t)x0 + tx1 .                                        (7)
794   Thus, the conditional vector field becomes u(x, t|x1 , c) = x1 − x0 , and the rectified flow-matching
795   (RFM) loss used for gradient descent is:
                                                                      2
796                                        ∥v(x, t|c; θ) − (x1 − x0 )∥ .                                     (8)
797   If the vector field u is estimated correctly, we can generate realistic data by propagating Gaussian
798   noise through an ODE solver at discrete time steps. A widely used method for solving the reverse
799   flow is the Euler ODE:
800                                         xt+ϵ = x + ϵv(x, t|c; θ),                                        (9)
801   where ϵ is the step size. In our VocalBand, we use content, timbre, prompt style, text tokens, and other
802   inputs for each task as conditioning information c, while the target data x1 consists of target style, F0,
803   or mel-spectrograms. In our AccompBand, we use timestep, text tokens, and vocal embedding as
804   conditioning information c, while the target data x1 is the accompaniment embedding.
805   Moreover, flow matching models require 100 to 1000 steps during training, but since they generate
806   a straight path, they only require 25 or fewer steps during inference, making the generation highly
807   efficient for fast generation. Additionally, flow-matching models ensure stable and high-quality
808   generation due to their ability to model smooth transitions between noise and data, maintaining
809   fidelity throughout the process. This stability is crucial for complex generation tasks, as it reduces
      artifacts and enhances the consistency of the output across various conditions.

      C.2   D ECOMPOSITION S TRATEGY
904
905
      We assume that the target vocal yv can be decomposed into three distinct representations: content
906   zc , style zs (e.g., singing methods, emotion, techniques, pronunciation, and articulation skills), and
907   timbre zt . When a vocal prompt y˜v is provided during training, our goal is to transfer both the timbre
908   z˜t and personalized style z˜s (like pronunciation and articulation skills) from the vocal prompt to
909   the target vocal yv . Meanwhile, we also need to achieve style control from text tokens zp (such as
910   singing method, emotion, and techniques).
911
      Following previous style transfer approaches (Jiang et al., 2024), we assume that the mutual informa-
912   tion between yv and y˜v primarily captures global information, represented by zt (timbre). Therefore,
913   the target timbre zt is set equal to the prompt timbre z˜t , as we aim to control the timbre of the output
914   based on the user’s input. Under this assumption, z˜t is extracted using a timbre encoder, which
915   focuses solely on timbre information, without capturing style zs or content zc . To ensure that the
916   content encoders extract only content-related information, we feed it phoneme sequences and musical
917   notes, allowing it to exclusively pass the content representation zc . For more details about the timbre
      encoder and the content encoders, please refer to Appendix C.3 and C.4.

950   C.3    T IMBRE E NCODER
951
952   The timbre encoder, designed to capture the unique identity of the singer, extracts a global timbre
953   vector z˜t from the vocal prompt y˜v . The encoder consists of multiple stacked convolutional layers. To
954   ensure stability in the timbre representation, the output of the timbre encoder is temporally averaged,
955   producing a one-dimensional timbre vector z˜t . The target timbre zt is set equal to the prompt timbre
956
      z˜t , as we aim to control the timbre of the output based on the user’s input.

958   C.4    C ONTENT E NCODERS
959
960
      Our content encoders consist of a phoneme encoder and a note encoder. The phoneme encoder
      processes a sequence of phonemes p through a phoneme embedding layer followed by four FFT
961
      blocks, extracting phoneme features. In parallel, the note encoder handles musical score information
962
      n, processing note pitches and durations. These are passed through two separate embedding layers
963   and a linear projection layer, which generate the corresponding note features. The outputs of the
964   phoneme encoder and the note encoder are then summed as zc .

1127
       As illustrated in Figure 3(d), Band-MOE is composed of three expert groups: Aligned MOE,
1128   Controlled MOE, and Acoustic MOE, each comprising multiple experts. We employ Feed-Forward
1129   Networks (FFNs) as the architecture for each expert. It is well-established (Lee et al., 2022b;
1130   Huang et al., 2022b) that mel-spectrogram details exhibit different patterns across various acoustic
1131   frequencies. In musical accompaniment, high-frequency components often include the harmonics and
1132   overtones of instruments like strings and flutes, as well as percussive elements such as cymbals and
1133   hi-hats, which enhance the brightness and clarity of the sound. Conversely, low-frequency content
       encompasses basslines and kick drums, providing foundational rhythm and depth that shape the


                                                          21
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1134
       overall groove and warmth of the music. Motivated by this, previous works (Kong et al., 2020;
1135   Yang et al., 2021) have adopted multi-scale architectures to model downsampled signals at different
1136   frequency bands, which effectively control the periodic elements of the signal and reduce artifacts.
1137
1138
       Building on this idea, we introduce Acoustic MOE, where experts are assigned to specific acoustic
       frequency bands based on the processed hidden representation h, and their outputs are aggregated to
1139
       produce the final result. Moreover, since the Aligned Vocal and Accomp Encodequ di ar employ 1D
1140
       convolutions to encode both the vocal and accompaniment mel-spectrograms, the latent representation
1141   of the hidden h should retain the acoustic frequency distribution.
1142

1144
       et al., 2021), enabling dynamic and efficient expert selection. The Gumbel-Softmax trick facilitates
       sampling from a categorical distribution by reparameterizing categorical variables to make them
1145

1308   make their contributions available for research purposes. These annotations, along with the separated
1309   vocal descriptions, form the complete singing styles. For melody styles, we extract the key from the
1310   segmented demixed vocal clips using music21 3 , tempo and duration using librosa 1 , and then use
1311
       GPT-4o to combine these elements, generating natural language descriptions of vocal ranges based

       I.2             E XPERIMENTS ON MOE
1585
1586   To demonstrate the effectiveness of our MOE, we conducted experiments on the final routing behavior.
1587   As shown in Figure 11 (a), we can observe that our global routing behaves as expected. As the
1588   noise level decreases, the weighting of outputs from Aligned MOE and Controlled MOE changes
1589   accordingly: 1) At early time steps (near 0), where the hidden representation h is highly noisy,
1590   the network prioritizes matching with the vocal for coherent reconstruction, thus the weight of the
1591   Aligned MOE is higher. 2) At later time steps (near 1), where h has been largely reconstructed, the
1592
       network focuses more on refining stylistic details, relying heavily on text prompts, thus the weight of
       the Controlled MOE is higher.
1593
1594   As shown in Figure 11 (b), the Acoustic MOE also behaves as expected by assigning different experts
1595   to different channels. We encode the mel-spectrogram into 20 dimensions through the Aligned
1596   Accomp Encoder, resulting in 20 channels and selecting experts for each channel. We perform a
1597   statistical analysis of the softmax output probabilities before expert selection. 1) Expert 1 focuses
1598
       on channels 0 to 7, which include instruments that provide foundational rhythm and depth, such as
       bass guitars, kick drums, low-frequency percussion, and the lower registers of piano and organ. 2)
1599
       Expert 2 specializes in channels 4 to 12, capturing the richness of rhythm guitars, mid-range piano
1600
       notes, and various percussion instruments that contribute to the fullness and body of the music. 3)
1601   Expert 3 targets channels 9 to 16, encompassing lead guitars, higher piano octaves, string instruments,
1602   and brass instruments. This allows the model to capture melodic elements and intricate harmonics
1603   that enhance the expressiveness of the accompaniment. 4) Expert 4 is assigned to channels 14 to
1604   19, focusing on cymbals, hi-hats, flutes, and high-frequency string overtones that contribute to the
1605   brightness and airiness of the music.

1607   J         L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
1608
1609   In this section, we discuss two main limitations of MultiBand and provide potential strategies to
1610   address them in future work:
1611
1612                        • Model Complexity. To achieve comprehensive controllability and high-quality multi-task
1613                          song generation based on various prompts, MultiBand utilizes four sub-models to generate
1614                          different components of a song, relying on multiple infrastructures like the flow-based
1615
                              transformer and VAE. This results in cumbersome training and inference procedures. Future
                              work will explore the possibility of using a single model to achieve the same multi-task
1616
                              generation capabilities and controllability.
1617
1618
                            • Language Diversity. Our sampled dataset only includes songs in Chinese and English,
                              lacking diversity. In the future, we will attempt to build a larger and more comprehensive
1619
                              dataset to enable a wider range of application scenarios.
