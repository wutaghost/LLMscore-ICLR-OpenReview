009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            We analyze the training of a two-layer autoencoder used to parameterize a flow-
012            based generative model for sampling from a high-dimensional Gaussian mixture.
013            Previous work shows that the phase where the relative probability between the
014            modes is learned disappears as the dimension goes to infinity without an appro-
015            priate time schedule. We introduce a time dilation that solves this problem. This
016            enables us to characterize the learned velocity field, finding a first phase where
017            the probability of each mode is learned and a second phase where the variance
018
               of each mode is learned. We find that the autoencoder representing the velocity
               field learns to simplify by estimating only the parameters relevant to each phase.
019
               Turning to real data, we propose a method that, for a given feature, finds intervals
020
               of time where training improves accuracy the most on that feature. Since practi-
021            tioners take a uniform distribution over training times, our method enables more
022            efficient training. We provide preliminary experiments validating this approach.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   In recent years, diffusion models have emerged as a powerful technique for learning to sample
028   from high-dimensional distributions Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015); Song et al. (2021); Song & Ermon
029   (2020); Ho et al. (2020), especially in the context of generating images and recently also for text
030   Lou et al. (2024). The idea lies in learning, from data samples, a velocity field that pushes noisy
031   datapoints to clean datapoints. Despite the remarkable performance of these models, there remain
032   several open questions, including understanding what makes a good noise schedule, which is the
033
      focus of this paper.
034   We consider the problem of training a neural network to learn the velocity field to generate samples
035   from a two-mode Gaussian mixture (GM). This serves as a prototypical example to understand how
036   diffusion models handle learning features at different scales, since the two-mode GM has two scales:
037   the macroscopic scale of the probability of each mode, and the microscopic scale of the variance of
038   each mode.
039   This problem was previously considered by Cui et al. (2024), but their analysis only handles the
040   balanced two-mode GM (i.e. the probability of each mode is exactly 1/2.) On the other hand, Biroli
041   et al. (2024) assume access to the exact velocity field and find that the phase where the probability
042   of each mode is learned disappears as the dimension of the problem grows.
043   In this work, we first introduce a noise schedule that makes the phase where this probability is
044   learned not disappear as the dimension goes to infinity. This enables us to extend the analysis
045   of Cui et al. (2024) to the two-mode GM without the balanced assumption. More precisely, our
046   contributions are as follows.
047
048         • We give an asymptotic characterization of the learned velocity field for learning to generate
049           the two-mode GM, finding a separation into two phases. We further show that Θd (1)
050           samples are sufficient to learn the velocity field.
051         • We show that the neural network representing the velocity field learns to simplify for each
052           phase. In the first phase, it only concerns estimation of the probability of each mode,
053           whereas in the second phase, it concerns estimation of the variance of each mode. This
              sheds light on the advantage of diffusion models over denoising autoencoders, since the


                                                        1
054
               sequential nature of diffusion models shown here allows them to decompose the complexity
055            of the problem.
056
057          • We show that the phase transition separating the two phases can be detected from a dis-
058            continuity in the Mean Squared Error associated to the learning problem, which suggests a
059
               way to find these transitions for general data distributions.
060          • For real data, this analysis suggests that training more at the times associated with a feature
061            improves accuracy on that feature. In fact, we propose a method that, given a feature, finds
062            an interval of time where more training improves accuracy on that feature the most. We
063            further validate this on the MNIST dataset. We provide the code for the experiments here.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
067
068   Phase transitions of generative models in high dimensions. Several works analyze phase transi-
069   tions in the dynamics of generative models. Raya & Ambrogioni (2023) find that diffusion models
070
      can exhibit symmetry breaking, where two phases are separated by a time where the potential gov-
      erning the dynamics has an unstable fixed point. They give a full theoretical analysis for the data
071
      being two equiprobable point masses in R, and also give a bound for the symmetry breaking time
072
      for the case where the data is a sum of finitely many point masses. Our setting generalizes the case
073   of two equiprobable point masses in R to two Gaussians in Rd that are not necessarily equiprobable.
074   Ambrogioni (2023) builds on Raya & Ambrogioni (2023) and shows several connections between
075   equilibrium statistical mechanics and the phase transitions of diffusion models. Ambrogioni (2023)
076   further conjectures that accurately sampling near times of ”critical generative instability” affects the
077   sample diversity. We give an explicit description of these critical times and verify this conjecture
078   theoretically for sampling (see Proposition 1) and for learning (see Corollary 5) and empirically for
079   learning (see Section 6). Li & Chen (2024) also formalize the study of critical windows taking the
080   data to be a mixture of strongly log-concave densities. They give non-asymptotic bounds for the
081
      start and end times of these critical windows, which have a closed form expression for the mixtures
      of isotropic Gaussians case. In contrast, we provide sharp asymptotic characterizations for the phase
082
      transition times. Biroli & Mézard (2023) analyze the Curie-Weiss model and analytically character-
083
      ize the speciation time, defined as the time after which the mode that the sample will belong to is
084   determined. Biroli et al. (2024) generalize the result and find an speciation time ts ∼ 21 log(λ) for an
085   Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process where λ is the largest eigenvalue of the covariance of the data, usually
086   proportional to d. Montanari (2023) points out a similar phase transition when learning the velocity
087   field to generate from a two-mode unbalanced Gaussian mixture, leading to problems for accurate
088   estimation of the data. Montanari (2023) addresses this by using a different neural network to learn
089   each mode. In the current work, we show that it is not necessary to tailor the network for each mode
090   if the right time schedule is used. It is worth noting that all these works are about sampling. We
091   provide a result for sampling in Proposition 1. Building on this, we give results for learning (i.e.
092
      estimating the velocity field through a neural network) which is the main contribution of our paper.
093   Time-step complexity. Several results give convergence bounds detailing the required time-steps,
094   score accuracy, and/or data distribution regularity to sample accurately. Benton et al. (2024) show
095   that at most O(d log2 (1/δ)/ϵ2 ) time steps are required to approximate a distribution corrupted with
096   Gaussian noise of variance
                           √      δ to within ϵ2 KL divergence. Chen et al. (2023) study probability flow
097   ODE and obtain O( d) convergence guarantees with a smoothness assumption. An underlying
098   assumption in all these works is that the score or velocity field is learned to a certain accuracy. In
099   the present work, we address this problem in the special case of a Gaussian mixture.
100   Sample complexity for Gaussian mixtures. Cui et al. (2024) study the learning problem for the
101   Gaussian mixture in high dimensions and demonstrate that n = Θd (1) samples are sufficient in
102   the balanced case where the two modes have the same probability. This is done through statistical
103   physics techniques of computing the partition function and using a sample symmetric ansatz. As we
104   show, due to the speciation time at d−1/2 which tends to zero as the dimension d grows, this analysis
                                                                                                    d+k
105   misses one phase of learning. Gatmiry et al. (2024) show that quasi-polynomial (O(dpoly(log( ϵ )) ))
106   sample and time complexity is enough for learning k-gaussian mixtures. The data distribution is
107   more general than the one we consider, but on the other hand we give a Θd (1) sample and time
      complexity.

426   6.2   T RAINING A GIVEN FEATURE ON REAL DATA : MNIST
427
428   Recall that in the background we mentioned that the analysis of Biroli et al. (2024) shows
                                                                                              √ that taking
429   αt = 1 − t and βt = t without any time-dilation gives an speciation time ts = 1/ d. This then
430   means that probability of each mode (given by p) can not be√  captured as d → ∞. Our analysis   √then
431   shows that if we dilate time by stretching the interval [0, κ/ d] to [0, 1] and the interval [κ/ d, 1]
      to [1, 2], then we get accurate estimation of p.

433   We then draw xµ0 ∼ N (0, Idd ), xµ1 from our data distribution, and form a noisy sample xµtµ =
434   (1 − tµ )xµ0 + tµ xµ1 for µ = 1, · · · , k. We finally train on the loss

441   where we took time as a parameter of the network as it is usually done in practice, as opposed to
442   having a separate network for each time t.
443
444
      The insight of our analysis is that instead of taking the batch of times uniformly, we can sample more
      times near the phase transition associated to a given feature, and in this way improve accuracy on
445
      that feature.
446
447   For a given feature, we can find the times where that feature is learned using the U-Turn method
448   (Sclocchi et al. (2024), Biroli et al. (2024)). Consider a dataset where each sample corresponds to
449   exactly one of finitely many classes. Examples of this are samples of the GM which correspond to
450
      one of two modes, or samples of MNIST which correspond to one of ten digits. The U-Turn then
      consists of starting with a sample from the data, run a backward diffusion model from time t = 1
451
      to t = t0 , which noises the sample, and then run the forward diffusion model from time t = t0 to
452
      t = 1 with noise independent from the backward run.
453
454   We are then interested in the probability that the sample before the backward and forward passes
455   belongs to the same class as the sample after them. For t0 ≈ 1, this probability is close to 1. For
456
      t0 ≈ 0, this probability is close to the underlying probability of the diffusion model generating a
      sample of the given class. By running this for different t0 , we can find at what times it is decided
457
      to what class the samples belong to. Having found those times, our goal is to have a model that
458
      generates samples for each class according to the probabilities that they appear in the dataset. We
459   can then improve the accuracy of the model on this by training on these times.
460
461   As a simple example, we train a U-Net (see Appendix D for details) to parameterize the Variance
462
      Preserving SDE from Song et al. (2021) to generate either the 0 or 1 digits from MNIST. The
      dataset we train on consists of 20% 1 digits and 80% 0 digits. We then measure how well is this
463
      model in generating samples that represent this asymmetry. The model is trained on approximately
464
      7400 samples for 9 epochs, by sampling times in [0, 1] uniformly as described in the beginning of
465   this section. We then generate 18500 new samples running this model using 1000 discretization
466   steps. 1 Among the 18500 generated samples, 88.2% are digits 0. (For determining this, we used a
467   discriminator with 99.2% accuracy on MNIST, see Appendix D for details.)
468
      We then test our proposed method. First, we determine at what time the digit that the sample
469
      represents is decided. We do this with the U-Turn method described above. Note that to do this,
470
      we use the model that we already trained. The results are in Figure 2. We find that the times
471   important for deciding the digit are early in the generation for t ∈ [0.2, 0.6] and mostly concentrated
472   on t ∈ [0.3, 0.5].
473
474
      We now train from scratch a model on 7400 samples for 9 epochs as before, except that we do
      not sample the times uniformly. We instead sample times with probability 1/2 uniformly in the
475
      interval [0.3, 0.5] and with probability 1/2 uniformly outside that interval. We then generate 18500
476
      new samples with this new model using 1000 discretization steps, and find that 81.0% are 0s. We
477   similarly consider sampling times with probability 1/2 uniformly in the interval [0.2, 0.6] and with
478   probability 1/2 outside that interval, generate samples, and find that 81.1% are 0s. This validates
479   our hypothesis in the simple case of MNIST.
480
      Although our theoretical analysis is for the probability flow ODE on the two-mode GM data dis-
481
      tribution, this example on MNIST shows that the ideas developed here can be useful to the SDE
482
      generative models used in practice for real data.
