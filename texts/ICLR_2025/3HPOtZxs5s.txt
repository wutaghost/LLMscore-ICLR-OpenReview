010                                               A BSTRACT
011
012            Quantum computing shows great potential for expanding the range of efficiently
013            solvable problems. This promise arises from the advantageous resource and run-
014            time scaling of certain quantum algorithms over classical ones. Quantum machine
015
               learning (QML) seeks to extend these advantages to data-driven methods. Initial
               evidence suggests quantum-based models can outperform classical ones in terms
016
               of scaling, runtime and generalization capabilities. However, critics have pointed
017
               out that many works rely on extensive feature reduction or use toy datasets to
018            draw conclusions, raising concerns about their applicability to larger problems.
019            Scaling up these results is challenging due to hardware limitations and the high
020            costs generally associated with encoding dense vector representations on quan-
021            tum devices. To address these challenges, we propose an efficient approach called
022            Hamiltonian classifier inspired by ground-state energy optimization in quantum
023            chemistry. This method circumvents the costs associated with data encoding by
024            mapping inputs to a finite set of Pauli strings and computing predictions as their
025            expectation values. In addition, we introduce two variants with different scal-
026
               ing in terms of parameters and sample complexity. We evaluate our approach
               on text and image classification tasks, comparing it to well-established classical
027
               and quantum models. Our results show the Hamiltonian classifier delivers perfor-
028
               mance comparable to or better than these methods. Notably, our method achieves
029            logarithmic complexity in both qubits and quantum gates, making it well-suited
030            for large-scale, real-world applications.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   In recent years, interest in quantum computing has grown significantly due to the provable ad-
036   vantages in computational complexity and memory usage some algorithms exhibit over their best
037   classical analogues (Nielsen & Chuang, 2000; Quetschlich et al., 2024; Biamonte et al., 2017).
038   For instance, efficient algorithms exist that can solve problems such as integer factorization (Shor,
039
      1997), Fourier transform (Camps et al., 2021), and specific instances of matrix inversion (Harrow
      et al., 2009) with an exponential speedup over the fastest known classical methods. Other    √ notable
040
      results include Grover’s algorithm, which performs an unstructured search achieving a O( n) time
041
      complexity, a quadratic improvement over the fastest classical approach that scales as O(n) (Grover,
042   1996). In parallel with these theoretical developments, the size of publicly accessible quantum ma-
043   chines has been steadily growing, and several companies have begun offering commercial cloud
044   access to these devices (Yang et al., 2023). Quantum machine learning is an offshoot of quantum
045   computing that seeks to extend its advantages to data-driven methods. The leading paradigm re-
046   volves around variational quantum circuits (VQCs), quantum algorithms whose parameters can be
047   adjusted with classical optimization to solve a specific problem (Cerezo et al., 2021). This approach
048   is sometimes referred to as quantum neural networks (QNNs) given the similarities with the classical
049   counterpart (Farhi & Neven, 2018; Killoran et al., 2019). Prior works have found some evidence that
050
      QML algorithms can offer improvements over their classical analogues in terms of capacity (Abbas
      et al., 2021), expressive power (Du et al., 2020), and generalization capabilities (Caro et al., 2022).
051
052   Despite the advancements of VQCs, a large-scale demonstration of advantage - the ability of a quan-
053   tum computer to solve a problem faster, with fewer resources or better performance than any clas-
      sical counterpart - remains out of reach for QML algorithms. Current quantum machines, referred

088   propose is a specific instance of a flipped model (Jerbi et al., 2024), a type of circuit which encodes
089   data as the observable of a quantum circuit rather than as a quantum state, effectively bypassing the
090

091
      vector. The magnitude of these projected vectors is then used to make predictions. In quantum-
092

093   construct a Hamiltonian. The classifier prediction is obtained as the expectation value of this Hamil-
094   tonian. This idea shares many similarities with variational quantum eigensolver (VQE), a type of
095   VQC widely used in quantum chemistry for solving the electronic structure problem (Peruzzo et al.,
096   2014; Tilly et al., 2022). We improve over the standard flipped model by providing a mapping from
097   inputs to observables that achieves a favourable logarithmic qubit and gate complexity relative to
098   the input dimensionality. We also introduce two variants that trade some classification performance
099   in exchange for smaller model size and better sample complexity, respectively. Most importantly,
100   the constant scaling in sample complexity of the second variant allows it to be efficiently adapted
101
      for execution on quantum devices. In brief, this paper provides the following three contributions:
102
103       C1 A novel encoding scheme achieving logarithmic qubit and gate complexity;
104
105       C2 A thorough evaluation of our scheme on text and image classification tasks;
106
107       C3 An empirical and theoretical comparison of our method against other established quantum

      2     P RELIMINARIES
119
120
      Quantum computers differ fundamentally from classical computers, utilizing the principles of quan-
121
      tum physics rather than binary logic implemented by transistors. For readers unfamiliar with the
122   topic, in Appendix A we offer a brief introduction to the notation and formalize the concepts of
123   qubit, gate and measurement. In this section, we instead discuss how these devices have been used
124   to tackle machine learning problems, as well as their limitations.

126
      2.1   DATA ENCODING
127
128   The first step for quantum-based ML algorithms is representing input data as quantum states, a
129   process also known as state preparation. Most algorithms share similarities in how they achieve
130   this (Rath & Date, 2023). The choice of encoding severely impacts the runtime of the circuit as
131   well as its expressivity (Sim et al., 2019). Basis encoding is the simplest representation analogous to
132      Nn bits. An n-element bit string [x1 x2 . . . xn ] is represented in the basis states of n qubits
      classical
133   as i=1 |xi ⟩ = |x1 . . . xn ⟩ (e.g. 011 is represented as |011⟩). This representation requires O(n)
134   qubits. Angle encoding represents continuous data as the phase Nnof qubits. A set of n continuous vari-
135   ables [x1 x2 . . . xn ] can be represented over n qubits as j=1 Rσ̂ (xj ) |0⟩ with Rσ̂ (x) = e−ixσ̂
136   a so-called rotation gate, and σ̂ a Pauli matrix X, Y or Z that specifies the rotation axis. Amplitude
                                                                                                PN −1
137   encoding represents a vector of N = 2n values [x1 x2 . . . xN ] over n qubits as i=0 xi |i⟩,
138   where {|i⟩}N i=1 corresponds to the canonical orthonormal basis written in a binary representation.
139   There is a trade-off between ease of encoding and qubit count: basis and angle encoding use O(n)
140   gates for O(n) values over O(n) qubits, while amplitude encoding handles N -dimensional inputs
141   but needs O(N ) gates over O(n) qubits, an exponential increase in input size but also gates. Angle
142   encoding strategies often embed multiple features onto the same qubit to reduce qubit usage, effec-
143   tively a form of pooling (Pérez-Salinas et al., 2020; Du et al., 2020). Amplitude encoding, on the
144   other hand, is often performed using easy-to-prepare quantum states to mitigate its gate complex-
145   ity (Ashhab, 2022; Du et al., 2020). In text-related tasks, encoding schemes typically encode words
146   over a set number of qubits (Wu et al., 2021; Lorenz et al., 2021), while in image tasks, pixel values
147
      are directly encoded as angles or amplitudes (Cong et al., 2019; Wei et al., 2022).
148
149   2.2   VARIATIONAL QUANTUM CIRCUITS
150
151   Once data has been encoded in a quantum computer, it is processed by a quantum circuit to obtain
152   a prediction. One of the leading paradigms for QML revolves around VQCs (also called quantum
153
      ansätze, or parametrized quantum circuits), a type of circuit where gates are specified by classical
      parameters. The training loop of a VQC (Figure 1) closely resembles that of classical neural net-
154
      works (Cerezo et al., 2021): input data is encoded in the quantum device as a quantum state, several
155
      layers of parametrized gates transform this state, a prediction is obtained via quantum measurement,
156   and finally a classical optimizer computes a loss and updates the parameters. Specialized optimiz-
157   ers allow backpropagating through a quantum circuit, but parameters are saved classically and the
158   process is otherwise the same (Wierichs et al., 2022). In VQCs applied to machine learning, mea-
159   surement usually plays the role of feature extractor, producing either features to be further processed
160   by downstream layers (Chen et al., 2022), or the final prediction (Farhi & Neven, 2018). VQCs have
161   shown better convergence properties during training (Abbas et al., 2021) as well as better expressive
      power (Du et al., 2020) when compared with neural networks of similar size.

      Several VQC-based equivalents of classical architectures have been proposed ranging from simple
163   neurons (Cao et al., 2017) to more elaborate schemes like auto-encoders (Romero et al., 2017),
164   generative adversarial networks (Dallaire-Demers & Killoran, 2018), RNNs (Bausch, 2020; Li et al.,
165   2023), attention layers (Cherrat et al., 2022; Shi et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024), and convolutional
166   neural networks (Cong et al., 2019; Henderson et al., 2020).

      2.3   Q UANTUM M ACHINE L EARNING LIMITATIONS
169
170   Despite these achievements, QML applications have yet to demonstrate a practical quantum ad-
171   vantage. Firstly, NISQ devices are limited in terms of the number of qubits in a single device,
172   connectivity between the qubits, noise in the computation, and coherence time (Zaman et al., 2023;
173   Anschuetz & Kiani, 2022). Secondly, quantum devices have fundamental difficulties in dealing with
174   the dense and unstructured vector representations around which ML revolves: loading (or encoding)
175   data into a quantum state either requires a large number of qubits (for angle and basis encoding)
176   or a prohibitive amount of gates (for amplitude encoding). Efficient methods for amplitude encod-
177
      ing (Ashhab, 2022; Wang et al., 2009) incur trade-offs in the expressivity of vectors that have not
      been explored sufficiently in the context of QML. Moreover, measurement is an expensive process
178
      extracting only one bit of information per qubit measured. Extracting a real-valued vector from
179
      a quantum computer generally requires exponentially many measurements (Schuld & Petruccione,
180   2021). As a result, many current QML experiments are limited in both scale and scope in order to fit
181   within the constraints of NISQ devices and simulators. Small datasets, typically consisting of only a
182   few hundred samples, are often used (Senokosov et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2021; Chen,
183   2022). Additionally, aggressive dimensionality reduction is commonly performed to reduce data to
184   just a few dozen features (Bausch, 2020; Zhao et al., 2024). In contrast, even ”small” classical neural
185   networks by modern standards are several orders of magnitude larger in terms of parameters, dataset
186   size, and representation dimensionality (Mingard et al., 2024). These limitations have raised con-
187   cerns about the applicability of QML results to larger, more complex tasks. Some question whether
188
      the observed performance is due to the quantum model itself or the upstream pre-processing (Chen
      et al., 2021), while others highlight the difficulty of generalizing these results to larger datasets and
189
      the challenges of fair benchmarking (Bowles et al., 2024; Mingard et al., 2024).
190
191   Several other challenges remain, including how to mitigate barren plateaus (Larocca et al., 2024),
192   develop efficient optimization algorithms for VQCs (Wiedmann et al., 2023), and implement ef-
193   fective error correction schemes(Chatterjee et al., 2023). In this work, we show how limitations
194
      pertaining encoding and measurement can be mitigated by changing the way inputs are represented
      in a quantum device.

197   3     H AMILTONIAN CLASSIFIER
198
199   Recognizing the limitations of current encoding techniques and the need for more efficient methods
200   on current quantum devices, we introduce a novel approach: the Hamiltonian classifier. Instead of
201   relying on expensive state preparation procedures, our method maps inputs to a set of Pauli strings
202   that measured together yield a binary prediction. This approach is inspired from the Variational
203   Quantum Eigensolver formulation applied to quantum chemistry (Peruzzo et al., 2014) with the key
204   difference that the Hamiltonian is constructed from data instead of being derived from the quantum-
205   physical properties of the chemical system at hand. As in the general VQE setting, we then optimize
206   the objective function with classical methods. Independent works (Jerbi et al., 2024) have introduced
207
      the theoretical framework for flipped models, a category that includes our Hamiltonian classifier. It
      has been proven that certain types of flipped models, particularly when combined with classical
208
      shadow techniques, can demonstrate a quantum advantage for specific tasks. Additionally, flipped
209
      models have been previously explored in the context of quantum federated learning (Song et al.,
210   2023). Our work distinguishes itself as the first to apply flipped models to text classification and
211   to provide a thorough evaluation of such methods across multiple datasets, comparing against both
212   classical and quantum baselines. Moreover, we enhance the practicality of these methods by intro-
213   ducing an encoding scheme that sensibly lowers qubit requirements. We also propose variants that
214   further reduce model size and sample complexity with minimal impact on performance and extend
215   this method to the multi-class scenario. These factors make the Hamiltonian classifier applicable not
      only to toy problems but also to real-world tasks.

      3.1   VARIATIONAL Q UANTUM E IGENSOLVERS (VQE S )
217
218   VQEs are a class of algorithms related to QML which have been extensively utilized in quantum
219   chemistry and condensed matter physics for finding the lowest-energy configuration in quantum
220   systems (Peruzzo et al., 2014; Tilly et al., 2022). VQEs are considered one of the most promising ap-
221   proaches for achieving a practical advantage in the NISQ era (Daley et al., 2022). In practice, VQEs
222   solve the ground state energy problem by approximating the lowest eigenstate of a Hamiltonian H,
223   a Hermitian matrix describing a quantum system. This is achieved by classically optimizing a VQC
224
      preparing a state ψθ for which the system energy E := ⟨H⟩ is minimal: Emin := minθ ⟨ψθ | H |ψθ ⟩ .
      For the electronic structure problem, these Hamiltonians are constructed from fundamental princi-
225
      ples, resulting in a polynomial number of Pauli strings. Note that the vector input ψθ is never
226
      expressed explicitly; instead, it is implemented on the quantum computer, serving as a central com-
227   ponent in the optimization process.

395
396   Text datasets We first consider the GLUE Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) dataset as obtained
397   from HuggingFace1 (Socher et al., 2013; Wang et al., 2019). It consists of ∼ 70k single sentences ex-
398
      tracted from movie reviews whose sentiment (positive/negative) was annotated by 3 human judges.
      We also evaluate our method on the IMDb Large Movie Review Dataset (Maas et al., 2011) con-
399
      taining 50k highly polar movie reviews evenly split into training and test sets. Additionally, we
400
      also consider the AG News (Zhang et al., 2015) classification task as a benchmark for our multi-
401   class model. AG News consists of ∼ 128k news articles divided by topic (world, sports, business,
402   sci/tech). These are commonplace datasets reasonably close to real-world applications. Our meth-
403   ods and baselines all require inputs to be converted to vector representations. For text datasets, we
404   remove all punctuation, lowercase all text, tokenize with a whitespace strategy, and finally embed
405   tokens with word2vec2 to obtain a sequence x. The resulting embedding has size d = 300 and,
406   therefore, requires n = 9 qubits to be represented in our methods.
407   Image datasets As a sanity check, we consider a binary version of MNIST which only includes
408   digits 0 and 1. We then consider Fashion-MNIST (Xiao et al., 2017), a dataset of 60k grayscale
409   images of clothes subdivided into 10 classes. Feeding images directly to our models results in d =
410   784 and n = 10. We further experiment on a binarized version of the CIFAR-10 dataset (Krizhevsky,
411   2009) we name CIFAR-2 obtained by grouping the original ten classes into two categories: vehicles
412   and animals. The 32 × 32 RGB images result in d = 3 × 1024 features over n = 12 qubits. We
413   also note that no further pre-processing or dimensionality reduction is performed to preserve the
414   original properties of the data. MNIST and Fashion-MNIST representations are not sequential and
415
      therefore are considered by our model as a special case with s = 1, while in CIFAR-2 each channel
      is considered as a different element of a sequence with s = 3.

 462
 463    We first perform a random search on hyperparameters such as learning rate, batch size, number of
 464
        qubits, and number of layers to identify the best configuration of each architecture. Because of
        space constraints, a more detailed discussion is moved to Appendix B. After identifying the best
 465
        hyperparameters, we train 10 models for each architecture with randomized seeds on the original
 466
        training split and average their performance on the test split. The only exception is the SST2 dataset
 467    for which no test set is provided. In this case, we select the run achieving the lowest training
 468    loss and submit it to GLUE’s website to get a test score. Submitting all 10 runs for each model
 469    would require an unfeasible amount of time given the maximum 2 daily submissions imposed by the
 470    platform. Appendix C shows additional experiments in which we further investigate architectural
 471    choices such as bias, state preparation, and number of Pauli strings. With the exception of QCNN
 472    which is implemented in PennyLane, all quantum operations are simulated in PyTorch as it allows
 473    batching several Hamiltonians thus enabling efficient training. We do not simulate noise in order to
 474    assess the performance of our approach under an ideal scenario. For all tasks we use a cross-entropy
 475
        loss during training.

521   5                 D ISCUSSION AND FUTURE WORK
522
523   This works highlights how measurement can become a central part of quantum computation while
524   at the same time alleviating the costs of data manipulation on quantum devices. Our Hamilto-
525   nian classifier achieves promising results on several domains, competing with classical baselines.
526   It demonstrates how alternative encoding strategies can bypass some hardware limitations while
527   scaling sufficiently well for real-world problems. The proposed HAM design encodes input data
528   directly as a measurement, obtaining performance comparable with other specialized models. The
529   PEFF variant reduces its parameter complexity, and the SIM variant additionally reduces its sam-
530   ple complexity, offering a scheme that may be more efficiently implemented on quantum hardware.
531
      Notably, our method already scales well enough to allow meaningful studies on large datasets using
      simulators. The Hamiltonian classifier is presently a proof of concept meant to illustrate a novel
532
      input scheme for quantum devices with the ultimate goal of expanding the tools available to QML
533
      researchers. Future works could characterize the effectiveness of our approach on even larger prob-
534   lems and its integration with existing classical pipelines. Other studies could focus on how different
535   choices of Pauli strings affect the final outcome. For example, exploring ways to integrate induc-
536   tive biases tailored to specific tasks, or consider local strings in conjunction with classical shadow
537   techniques to lower sample complexity. A way of learning more complex functions could be to
538   stack several layers of Hamiltonian encoding, possibly performing nonlinear transformations. Other
539   directions deserving a paper of their own are noise simulation and physical implementations on real
      quantum hardware.

798   A    Q UANTUM COMPUTING FUNDAMENTALS
799
800   Quantum computers are conceptually and physically different devices from classical computers.
801   Instead of basing their logic on binary data representations implemented by transistors, they utilize
802   the rules of quantum physics as a substrate for computation. For this reason, in what follows we
803   describe the building blocks of quantum computation.
804   Dirac notation Quantum computing makes extensive use of Dirac notation (also called bra-ket
805   notation) to simplify the representation of linear transformations which are commonplace in the
806   theory. The fundamental elements of this notation are bras and kets. A ket, denoted as |ψ⟩, represents
807   a column vector ψ in a Hilbert space. A bra, denoted as ⟨ϕ|, represents instead a vector in the dual
808   space (the complex conjugate transpose of a ket). The inner product of two vectors ϕ and ψ, which
809   results in a scalar, is written as ⟨ϕ⟩ ψ. Conversely, the outer product |ψ⟩ ⟨ϕ|, forms a matrix or an
      operator.

      Quantum systems The basic unit of information in a quantum computer is a two-dimensional quan-
811   tum bit (qubit) which is mathematically modelled by a normalized column vector, the state vec-
812   tor, in a two-dimensional vector space equipped with an inner-product, or Hilbert space. A state
813                                                                             ⊤
      vector is usually expressed concisely in Dirac notation as |ψ⟩ = [α β] ∈ C2 . We say that a
814   qubit |ψ⟩ := α |0⟩ + β |1⟩ is in a coherent quantum superposition of two orthonormal basis states
815                ⊤                    ⊤
      |0⟩ := [1 0] and |1⟩ := [0 1] , such that the complex coefficients in the basis expansion sat-
816
      isfy normalization constraint |α|2 + |β|2 = 1. This constraint originates from physics: in order
817
      to extract any information from a quantum state, we need to measure it in a chosen basis, which
818   destroys the superposition in the measured basis by projecting the state |ψ⟩ on one of the basis el-
819   ements. If we measure state |ψ⟩ in the {|0⟩ , |1⟩} basis, we get outcome ”0” with probability |α|2
820   and the post-measurement state becomes |0⟩, and respectively, we get ”1” with probability |β|2 and
821   post-measurement state |1⟩.

      Multi-qubit systems In order to model a quantum system with multiple qubits, we use the so-called
823
      Kronecker product (⊗) to combine many individual state vectors into a single larger one. An n-
824   qubit system can be represented by a vector of size N = 2n , |ψ1 . . . ψn ⟩ := |ψ1 ⟩ ⊗ · · · ⊗ |ψn ⟩. In
825   a chosen basis, the entries of this vector describe the probability of observing that outcome. Many

835   Quantum circuits Quantum computation is achieved by manipulating qubits. This is done using
836   quantum gates. Any n-qubit gate can be represented as a unitary matrix U ∈ CN ×N which acts
837   on the n-qubit input state |ψ⟩ via the usual matrix-vector multiplication, giving output U |ψ⟩. Intu-
838   itively, quantum gates can be considered as rotations that conserve the length of a state vector. A

1044
1045   Rewriting the SIM model leads to a bilinear form where each Pauli string acts as a transformation on
1046   the input (Eq. 11). During hyperparameter tuning, we consistently observe that the best-performing
1047   SIM models use 1000 Pauli strings across all experiments. We believe this is not a coincidence, as
1048   each Pauli string introduces a distinct transformation, enriching the model’s feature set. This raises
1049   some questions: does increasing the number of Pauli strings, and thus the number of transformations,
1050   lead to better performance? Moreover, how does the generalization capability scale with the number
1051   of Pauli strings?
1052   To explore this, we use the same configurations found for the AG News and Fashion datasets but
1053   vary the number of Pauli strings. Results are displayed in Figures 3 and 5. Low string count do not
1054   perform much better than chance, but accuracy steadily increases and eventually plateaus at 1000
1055   strings, reaching levels comparable to other models like MLP and CIRC. Increasing the number of
1056   strings also leads to a more stable training process as highlighted by the error bars growing thin for
1057   p = 500 and 1000. This suggests the loss landscape may become smoother as more transforma-
1058   tions are added, facilitating training. This is a promising outcome: it suggests that transformations
1059
       induced by Pauli strings actively contribute to learning by creating more complex features. It also
       indicates that a relatively small number of strings can effectively substitute for a full decomposition.
