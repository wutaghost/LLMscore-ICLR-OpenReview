011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The rapid advancement of large language models (LLMs) has led to architectures
014            with billions to trillions of parameters, posing significant deployment challenges
015            due to their substantial demands on memory, processing power, and energy con-
016            sumption. Sparse Mixture-of-Experts (SMoE) architectures have emerged as a
017
               solution, activating only a subset of parameters per token, thereby achieving faster
               inference while maintaining performance. However, SMoE models still face limi-
018
               tations in broader deployment due to their large parameter counts and significant
019
               GPU memory requirements. In this work, we introduce a gradient-free evolutionary
020            strategy named Efficient Expert Pruning (EEP) to enhance the pruning of experts
021            in SMoE models. Specifically, EEP searches the pruning pattern and uses expert
022            merging as a memory-efficient way of fine-tuning the pruned model. EEP relies
023            solely on model inference (i.e., no gradient computation) and achieves greater spar-
024            sity while maintaining or even improving performance on downstream tasks. EEP
025            can be used to reduce both the total number of experts (thus saving GPU memory)
026            and the number of active experts (thus accelerating inference). For example, in
027            the task-specific setting, we demonstrate that pruning up to 75% of experts in Mix-
028
               tral 8 × 7B-Instruct results in a substantial reduction in parameters with minimal
               performance loss, or pruning 50% of experts and activating one fewer expert to
029
               achieve 1.41× speedup. Our experiments include four different model sizes from
030
               Mixtral, Qwen1.5, and Qwen2, and utilize more than 10 datasets as well as various
031            settings. Results show that our method outperforms the related baselines by a large
032            margin, demonstrating a significant advancement in this direction. The results
033            of our method can be reproduced using the code provided in the supplementary
034            material.

039   Large language models have significantly advanced, evolving into highly versatile tools (Kim et al.,
040   2023; Brown et al., 2020; Alayrac et al., 2022; Shen et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2023; Lu et al.,
041   2022). As these models grow in accordance with scaling laws (Kaplan et al., 2020), the norm has
042   shifted towards architectures with billions to trillions of parameters. However, the larger scale brings
043   considerable deployment challenges due to increased demands on memory, processing power, and
044   energy consumption (Zhou et al., 2024; Wan et al., 2023). In response to these challenges, there is
045
      a notable trend towards adopting sparse Mixture-of-Experts (SMoE) architectures (Shazeer et al.,
      2017; Fedus et al., 2022; Lepikhin et al., 2021; Hwang et al., 2023), as seen in models such as
046
      Mixtral 8 × 7B and 8 × 22B (Jiang et al., 2024), Qwen1.5-MoE-A2.7B (Bai et al., 2023), Qwen
047
      2-57B-A14B (Qwen Team, 2024), DBRX (Team, 2023), and Grok-1 (xAI team, 2024). SMoE models
048   activate only a subset of parameters for each token, resulting in faster inference while maintaining
049   competitive performance compared to dense models of the same scale. For example, Mixtral 8 × 7B
050   outperforms or matches Llama-2 70B (Touvron et al., 2023) and GPT-3.5 on many benchmarks,
051   while it only activates 13B parameters to process each token. Although SMoE models have less
052   computation per token, they remain parameter-heavy, e.g., Mixtral 8 × 7B has 47B parameters in total
053   while Grok-1 reaches 314B (see Tab. 8 for other models). This limits their broader deployment due
      to the substantial GPU memory requirements. Additionally, their throughput may not be ideal as the

075   Many studies have shown that only a subset of parameters significantly contributes to performance
076
      when applying LLMs to downstream tasks (Blalock et al., 2020; Kwon et al., 2022; Sajjad et al.,
      2023; Xia et al., 2022). Pruning is a crucial technique for eliminating redundancy in neural networks.
077
      It can be unstructured, achieving high sparsity while maintaining performance (Blalock et al., 2020;
078
      Frantar & Alistarh, 2023; Sun et al., 2024), or structured, removing entire channels or layers to
079   provide computational efficiency and reduced latency (Ma et al., 2023; Tao et al., 2023; Xia et al.,
080   2022; Hou et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2019b; Kwon et al., 2022). One particularly efficient way is
081   expert pruning in SMoE LLMs, a type of structured pruning with coarse granularity, which enhances
082   overall efficiency. Recent expert pruning methods achieve 25%-50% sparsity and accelerate inference,
083   but struggle to maintain performance (Lu et al., 2024) or need fine-tuning, requiring substantial GPU
084   memory and resources (Chen et al., 2022; Muzio et al., 2024). Thus, there is a pressing need for
085   efficient pruning methods that operate within the constraints of inference resources for SMoE LLMs.
086   In this work, we propose a gradient-free evolutionary strategy to search for the optimal subset of
087   experts or their merged models, achieving high sparsity without drastically losing performance. Our
088   search method is divided into two phases: expert pruning and expert merging. To facilitate the search
089   for optimal pruning configurations, we design a parameter space for router mapping and expert
090   merging, represented by two weight matrices, WRM and WEM . These matrices are applied to the
091   router weighting and expert modules, as illustrated in Fig. 1. In the first phase, expert pruning, we
092   search through the weight matrices to retain the most prominent experts without updating any network
093
      parameters. In the second phase, expert merging, we retrieve knowledge from the pruned experts and
      consolidate it into the retained experts. To these ends, WRM and WEM are set to one-hot rows in
094
      the first phase and to real numbers in the second phase. Since our method is gradient-free, it can be
095
      conducted on devices capable of inference. Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
096
097   • Pruning the total number of experts: smaller memory consumption and better performance.
098     Our approach enables more aggressive pruning of experts compared to current methods (Lu et al.,
099     2024; Muzio et al., 2024). When conduct task-specific pruning on Mixtral 8 × 7B-Instruct, we
100     reduce the number of experts in each SMoE block from 8 to 2, a 72% reduction in parameters,
101     while maintaining comparable performance across various downstream tasks. Surprisingly, we
102     observe that even with fewer experts EEP is still able to achiever better performance. For instance,
103     on the SQuAD dataset, pruning 4 out of 8 experts result in a performance increase from 53.4%
104
        to 75.4% without updating the remaining experts. We hypothesize that this is because the router
        network is not well-trained, which is a widely known issue for SMoE (Fedus et al., 2022; Chi et al.,
105
        2022). We also provide empirical evidence to verify this hypothesis in Sec. 5.6.
106
107   • Pruning the number of active experts: better inference efficiency. We explore the pruning of
        active experts and find that effective expert merging compensates for the loss of active experts

109       model’s utility on these tasks. For instance, by reducing the active experts in Mixtral 8 × 7B from

112   • Generalization ability. Beyond task-specific pruning, we test the performance of our method on

125     significantly outperforms the pruned models of previous methods, while our complete processes
126     can run on devices affordable for inference, making EEP more widely applicable.

      2     R ELATED WORK
130
      Sparse Mixture-of-Experts LLMs. Shazeer et al. (Shazeer et al., 2017) introduced the sparse MoE
131
      layer, which consists of multiple experts, each being a simple feed-forward network (FFN), and a
132
      trainable router network that selects a sparse combination of the experts to process each input. Such
133   SMoE models can significantly increase model capacity while maintaining computational efficiency.
134   However, this utility is ideally achieved when the router accurately and evenly assigns experts to
135   each token during training and inference. Many works focus on these challenges (Fedus et al., 2022;
136   Lewis et al., 2021; Dai et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022). Recently, many SOTA LLMs adopt the SMoE
137   structure to achieve high performance and computational efficiency simultaneously (Jiang et al., 2024;
138   Bai et al., 2023; Team, 2023; xAI team, 2024). Additionally, Zhang et al. (Zhang et al., 2022) propose
139   transforming non-MoE models into SMoE models to accelerate inference, and Komatsuzaki et al.
140   (Komatsuzaki et al., 2023) upcycle pretrained models by reusing the parameters to initialize SMoE
141
      models, where all experts are replicates of the original FFNs, and then fine-tune the SMoE models.
142   Pruning for LLMs. Pruning techniques have emerged as a crucial strategy for optimizing LLMs
143   by reducing model size and computational costs while maintaining performance. Unstructured
144   pruning (Blalock et al., 2020; Frantar & Alistarh, 2023; Sun et al., 2024; Syed et al., 2023) entails the
145   removal of individual weights according to specific criteria, creating sparse networks that demand
146   specialized hardware for efficient execution. In contrast, structured pruning (Ma et al., 2023; Tao
147
      et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2022; Hou et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2019b; Kwon et al., 2022; Child et al.,
      2019; Xiao et al., 2024; Beltagy et al., 2020; Voita et al., 2019) eliminates entire structures, such as
148
      neurons or attention heads, facilitating more straightforward implementation on standard hardware.
149
      Within structured pruning, specific focus areas include attention mechanisms, where redundant heads
150   are pruned to streamline the self-attention layers, and FFNs where unnecessary neurons are removed
151   to enhance computational efficiency. Additionally, expert pruning for SMoE models selectively
152   prunes the expert networks (Lu et al., 2024; Muzio et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2022; Koishekenov et al.,
153   2023).
154
      Evolutionary Strategy for Optimization. Evolutionary Strategies (ES) have been increasingly
155
      recognized for their robustness and flexibility in various optimization tasks, particularly where
156   gradient-based methods fall short Wierstra et al. (2014). Notably, ES is highly effective for optimizing
157   non-differentiable objective functions, offering a powerful alternative in scenarios where gradients
158   are unavailable or unreliable (Salimans et al., 2017; Kharitonov, 2019; Liu et al., 2024b; Trofin et al.,
159   2021; Liu et al., 2023). Furthermore, ES excels in discrete optimization spaces, making it suitable
160   for a wide range of combinatorial problems (Akiba et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a; 2023). Recent
161   advancements have extended the application of ES to the domain of LLMs, enabling memory-efficient
      fine-tuning without the need for backpropagation (Malladi et al., 2023).

      4     M ETHOD
217
218   In this section, we introduce our proposed approach EEP to compressing SMoE LLMs through expert
219   pruning and merging. We parameterize the compression with two matrices and optimize them using
220   evolutionary strategies. Our method addresses the challenges of large and complex search spaces
221   for the pruning configuration without incurring the prohibitive computational costs associated with
222   gradient-based optimization. The subsequent subsections elaborate on our motivation (Sec. 4.1), the
223   configuration of the parameter space (Sec. 4.2), the evolutionary optimization strategy employed to
224   achieve our objectives (Sec. 4.3), and the use cases we apply EEP (Sec. 4.4).
225
      4.1   M OTIVATION
226
227   LLMs based on the SMoE architecture have shown remarkable
228   performance across various NLP tasks (Jiang et al., 2024;
229   Team, 2023; xAI team, 2024). These models leverage multiple
230   experts, activating only a subset for any given input, thus
231
      balancing computational efficiency and model capacity. For
      example, 2 out of 8 experts are activated in Mixtral, striking
232
      a balance between performance and computational cost.
233
234   Fig. 3 presents our investigation into the activation of different
235   numbers of experts on Mixtral 8 × 7B-Instruct, revealing the
236   following observations: i) Activating only a single expert does
237
      not lead to model collapse and may result in only a minimal
      performance drop compared to the default setting of using Figure 3: Performance from a single
238
      two experts. This suggests that individual experts possess expert to an ensemble of experts.
239
      redundant knowledge, enabling them to maintain reasonable performance independently. This
240   redundancy indicates potential for expert pruning. ii) Conversely, activating more than 2 experts
241   leads to a noticeable performance gain, highlighting the benefits of expert ensemble. However, the
242   computational cost of such an ensemble is substantially higher. Wortsman et al. (Wortsman et al.,
243   2022) have shown that merging differently fine-tuned models can efficiently substitute their ensemble,
244   achieving similar performance with reduced computational overhead. Therefore, expert merging can
245   potentially strengthen a single expert, while maintaining the efficiency of inference.
246   Building on these insights, we propose a two-step approach involving expert pruning followed by
247   expert merging. Initially, we search for the optimal subset of experts given a fixed size. Subsequently,
248   we employ expert merging to consolidate the knowledge from the pruned experts into the remaining
249   ones. This approach not only restores the knowledge of the pruned experts but also updates the
250   surviving experts to incorporate the collective expertise of the entire SMoE block.

282   4.3   E VOLUTIONARY SEARCH FOR THE ROUTER MAPPING AND EXPERT MERGING MATRICES
283   The search space of the router mapping and expert merging matrices is large and complex, making
284   it difficult to design heuristics for determining a solution, as is done in other expert pruning stud-
285   ies (Muzio et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2024). Therefore, an efficient optimization strategy
286   is necessary. Given the substantial size of SMoE LLMs, computing gradients for optimization is
287   computationally prohibitive for most users. As a solution, we employ a gradient-free evolutionary
288   strategy, which has been adopted in research across other domains, such as diffusion models (Liu
289   et al., 2023; 2024b). Our algorithm is detailed in Alg. 1. The key points are summarized as follows:
290   i) Initially, we populate the search space using random initialization. ii) During the evolutionary search,
291   each set of router mapping and expert merging matrices is treated as an individual. In each iteration,
292   only the top-performing individuals are selected as parents to produce the next generation through
293   crossover and mutation. Specifically, during crossover, we randomly combine the entries of the
294   matrices from two parents or select one parent’s matrices entirely. For mutation, we introduce random
295   Gaussian noise to the matrices, ensuring stochastic variations. This process conserves beneficial
296   adaptations while discarding detrimental modifications, enhancing the optimization process. iii) This
297
      evolutionary reproduction process is repeated for a predetermined number of iterations within each
      search phase, updating the population with newly generated individuals. iv) Upon completion of the
298
      search process, the best individual is selected as the output of our search algorithm.

300   4.4   U SE C ASES
301   We explore two applications of EEP: expert pruning and expert activation pruning. In expert pruning,
302   EEP searches for optimal router mapping (WRM ) and expert merging matrices (WEM ) to minimize
303   the total number of experts while maintaining high performance. For expert activation pruning, the
304   goal is to achieve strong performance with only one active expert per token. Here, we use the same
305   EEP search algorithm to conduct expert and router networks optimization by updating the WRM and
306   WEM matrices, while only activates one expert during inference. Fig. 2 illustrates these two use cases.
307   Additionally, we investigate the combination of these two approaches, reducing both the total number
308   of experts and the number of active experts simultaneously (see Sec. 5.3).

470   We profile the memory overhead and inference speed of Mixtral 8 × 7B model for the two use cases.
471   We conduct tests on SQuAD with a batch size of 256 using two NVIDIA A100 GPU cards. We
472   report the peak memory usage and the wall-time acceleration ratio in Tab. 6. As shown in Tab. 6,
473   retaining only 4 and 2 experts from the whole model decreases the memory overhead by 47% and
474   71%, respectively. Additionally, reducing the total number of experts improves inference speed due
475   to higher parallelism, achieving a speedup of 1.11× and 1.18× with 4 and 2 experts, respectively.
476   In the use case of reducing active experts, an acceleration ratio of 1.24× is achieved. Finally, when
477   combining the two use cases with 4 total experts and 1 active expert per token, EEP saves 47% of
478
      GPU memory and achieves a 1.41× increase in inference speed. The profiling results indicate that
      EEP can significantly reduce the memory consumption and computational cost of SMoE LLMs.

484   perceptron. This makes it challenging to accurately partition the high-dimensional hidden space
485   among experts. The issue of imbalanced activation has been identified in several works (Fedus et al.,
      2022; Chi et al., 2022). To illustrate this issue more clearly, we conduct a simple experiment: we find

527   directly related to the metrics users care about.

      6     C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this work, we present EEP, a gradient-free evolutionary search method optimized for pruning
531   within an efficienct parameter space. Through extensive experiments on various datasets and settings,
532   we demonstrate that EEP achieves superior performance and greater sparsity compared to baseline
533   methods. Additionally, we make a novel observation that the performance of SMoE models on
534   downstream tasks can be improved under task-specific pruning, even without fine-tuning the remaining
535   parameters. We discuss the potential reasons for this phenomenon, suggesting that a good pruning
536
      configuration may lead to a more effective routing mechanism by reducing the complexity the router
      network needs to manage.
537
538   Limitations. EEP can run on a device that is solely capable of inference. However, if the dataset
539   used for the search is too large, the search process can require a long computation time. We leave the
      optimization of search speed to future work.

878   output discrepancy compared to the full model. We use a batch of calibration data with a size of
879   64 to calculate the discrepancy. For the dynamic skipping scheme, we run the model on the entire
880   training set to determine the median value of the ratio between the two largest routing weights for
881   each layer. During validation, we dynamically skip the expert with the second-largest routing weight
882   if the ratio between its weight and the largest weight is below the threshold. This results in an average
883   of approximately 1.5 active experts.

1058   merging with a discrete WEM is 15.7±1.5s, and the time cost of merging with a continuous WEM
1059   is 26.2±1.7s. GPU and overall time cost are listed in Tab. 14.
1060   Furthermore, we find that by decreasing the size of the training set and search iterations, the speed of
1061   EEP can be improved easily. We report the results of EEP after decreasing the search iterations to
1062   120 and using less data in Tab. 15. We can see that EEP is significantly accelerated with minimal
1063   performance loss compared to our original results. For OOD pruning, we also use different numbers
1064   of training set to test the stability of EEP to smaller dataset size. Results are shown at Tab. 16. We
1065   further show the performance-dataset size curves at Fig. 5 for clear demonstration.

       D.9     I MPORTANCE OF THE P RUNING P HASE
1175
1176   Although the search space of discrete matrices (phase 1) is already included in the search space of
1177   continuous matrices (phase 2), it is still important to run the phase 1 search to find a good discrete
1178   state for the phase 2 search. The reasons are twofold: (1) The optimal merging coefficients are
1179
       typically found near discrete states. (2) We observe that transitioning the mapping parameters within
       a continuous value space can lead to a spike in loss or a drop in performance, which can impede
1180
       the shift from one discrete state to another. For example, when pruning 2 out of 4 experts, starting
1181
       with the configuration [1, 1, 0, 0] and aiming to transition smoothly to the optimal [0, 0, 1, 1] is not
1182   feasible if no continuous path exists between [1, 1, 0, 0] and [0, 0, 1, 1] that consistently decreases
1183   losses. To further clarify this point, we take three discrete states C1 , C2 , C3 and then plot the accuracy
1184   of these three points and their interpolations using a simplex in Fig. 9. The results demonstrate the
1185   above findings, that there are usually low accuracy basin between two discrete states. Additionally,
1186   we provide the performance of solely conducting expert merging (continuous mapping parameters)
1187   using randomly initialized discrete states in Tab. 22. The results prove that such a one-phase search is
       suboptimal.
