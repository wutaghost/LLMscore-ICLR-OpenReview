009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Many machine learning problems involve a challenging task of calibrating pa-
012            rameters in a computational model to fit the training data; this task is especially
013            challenging for non-convex problems. Many optimization algorithms have been
014            proposed to assist in calibrating these parameters, each with its respective advan-
015            tages in different scenarios, but it is often difficult to determine the scenarios for
016            which an algorithm is best suited. To contend with this challenge, much work has
017
               been done on proving the rate at which these optimizers converge to their final
               solution, however the wide variety of such convergence rate bounds, each with
018
               their own different assumptions, convergence metrics, tightnesses, and parameters
019
               (which may or may not be known to the practitioner) make comparing these con-
020            vergence rates difficult. To help with this problem, we present a minmax-optimal
021            algorithm and, by comparison to it, give a single descent bound which is applicable
022            to a very wide family of optimizers, tasks, and data (including all of the most
023            prevalent ones), which also puts special emphasis on being tight even in parameter
024            subspaces in which the cost function is concave.
025

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Many machine learning problems involve calibrating the parameters of a given model to match
030
      the data distribution of a phenomenon one wishes to model, e.g. the structure of folded proteins,
      processing images to automatically generate appropriate labels for them, or generating images and
031
      text to interactively chat with a human engagingly. This process involves:
032
033         1. Collecting many samples ("data points") from the desired data distribution.
034
            2. Measuring how well the model fits the collected data points (the "data set") with a given
035            performance analysis metrics (the "loss function", a.k.a. the "objective function"). By
036            convention, lower values of the loss function imply better performance on the model’s part.
037
            3. Adjusting the model’s parameters to improve the performance, as measured by the loss
038
               function ("model parameter optimization").
039
040
            4. Repeat until desired performance achieved.
041
      However, no single existing optimizer is best suited to all machine learning problems - each has
042   its unique strengths and weaknesses (see Vaswani et al. (2020); Sivan et al. (2024); Ruder (2016);
043   Mustapha et al. (2021); Bera & Shrivastava (2020); Zeiler (2012); Duchi et al. (2011b); Xu et al.
044   (2017); Wadia et al. (2021); Mittal et al. (2019); Zhou et al. (2020); Schmidt et al. (2021)), such as
045   generalization capability, convergence rate, saddle-point and flat region evasion capability, robustness
046   to hyperparameter choice, computational complexity per-iteration, memory complexity, etc., and
047   different areas in which it empirically seems to work best. As a result, one must compare among
048   various different optimization algorithms (henceforth, "optimizers") to select the one most suited to
049   the current scenario.
050   In an effort to help practitioners select the best optimizer for their setup and estimate the absolute
051   computational resources that will be required to obtain a given performance, many experiments have
052   been run comparing the performance of different optimizers on a variety of applications (Xu et al.,
053   2017; Schmidt et al., 2021), and on the theoretical side - convergence rate bounds have been proven
      for various different optimizers. However, due to the wide variety of assumptions, convergence rate

054
      metrics, bound parameters (which may be expensive - if not impossible - to compute ahead of time),
055   and tightness of the bounds in all of these works, comparing among them remains a challenging
056   task. Secondly, there is a lack of convergence rate bounds general enough to be easily applicable
057   to newly proposed optimizers. Thirdly, many of these bounds fail to demonstrate the empirically-
058   verified convergence rate superiority of the more sophisticated methods that make use of second-order
059   curvature information instead of exclusively the gradient. Lastly, although convergence rate bounds
060   exist for non-convex functions, many of them fail to properly address the opportunities that lay
061   in linear subspaces of the parameter space in which the loss function is concave (meaning that a
062   restriction f |S : S → R of the loss function f to a linear subspace S is locally concave). We believe
063
      that more attention should be given to these subspaces of the function in the context of neural network
      optimization; Alain et al. (2018) and Ghorbani et al. (2019) demonstrate experimentally that there is
064
      much to be gained by taking optimal steps in these subspaces, often even orders of magnitude greater
065
      than the potential gains in convex subspaces.
066
067
068   Our contributions In an effort to help practitioners select the best optimizer for their use case,
069   we develop a tool for estimating the value of second-order optimization algorithms; this will help
070   decide if the additional computational burden of these algorithms is worthwhile. We develop a
071   minmax-optimal algorithm, rate algorithms by similarity to it, and demonstrate in theory and in
072
      practice that in general, second-order algorithms work best on mechanistically simple problems. Our
      algorithm-optimality bound satisfies the following good properties:
073
074
075         1. Concave tightness Our bound exploits the opportunity for greater descent in subspaces of
076
               the parameter space in which the loss function is concave.
077
            2. Universality We make only weak and commonly satisfied assumptions for our bound, to
078            allow for its application to a wide and prevalent family of optimizers and loss functions.
079
080         3. Tightness for any level of iteration step-quality instead of assuming a bound on the quality
081            of steps given in each iteration as some previous works have done, our theoretical bounds
082            are given as a continuous function of the quality of each iteration’s step.
083
084
            4. Bound on loss function descent Our main result bounds the rate at which the model’s
               performance increases (as measured by the loss function). This is in contrast to previous
085
               works, which instead bound various indicators of local minimality, such as gradient norm,
086
               local near-convexity, or proximity to a local minimum (in Euclidean distance). Although Xu
087            et al. (2020) write that the latter convergence rate metrics is more relevant to the non-convex
088            optimization setting, we feel that the former is more practically useful, since generally
089            real-world applications with limited computational resources simply demand a minimal
090            performance guarantee of their model, without regard to the theoretical capabilities of a
091            given model or optimization algorithm.
092
093         5. Simplicity of cubic minimization problem We approach the multidimensional cubic
094
               polynomial minimization problem posed by Nesterov & Polyak (2006) by decomposing it
               into n 1-dimensional problems via eigendecomposition of the Hessian, making our approach
095
               to the solution of this minimization problem far simpler conceptually.
096
097
098   Our paper is organized as follows: In section 2, we review previous work and describe the notation
099
      we will use throughout the paper. In section 3, we develop the minmax-optimal ELMO algorithm and
      analyze its descent rate. In section 4, we make claims as to the benefits of optimizer similarity to ELMO
100
      (proven in appendix H). In section 5 we show the value of our novel Lipschitz parameter separation
101
      scheme by showing how much lower the Lipschitz parameters of most relevant eigenspaces can be,
102   thus giving optimizers a more accurate minimizable model of the loss function in each neighborhood
103   it finds itself in. Finally, in section 6, we present experiments validating one particular use case of
104   our bound: we show that the advantage second-order optimizers hold over first-order optimizers is
105   inversely proportional to the convex Lipschitz parameters. In other words, second-order optimizers
106   present strong performance (thus may be worth their additional computational burden) in settings
107   with small convex Lipschitz parameters, and weak performance (thus not worthwhile) in settings
      with large convex Lipschitz parameters.

179   common approach to estimating it is to perform a Monte Carlo approximation, summing over only a

187   Since the goal of optimization is to minimize a loss function, arguably the best metric for measuring
188   an optimization algorithm’s quality are the gains it makes as measured by the loss function values, i.e.
189   its rate of loss function descent. Nevertheless, most algorithms’ convergence rate bounds relate to
190
      their gradient norms; we note, however, that a bound on an algorithm’s gradient norm may be a poor
      proxy for its descent rate in the early, nonconvex "search" phase, since convergence rate bounds may
191
      only imply proximity to a critical point of the gradient, which is neither guaranteed to be the point
192
      the algorithm will ultimately converge to nor even to have a small loss function value by any measure.
193   To the best of our knowledge, our bound is the first to directly address the problem of bounding the
194   loss function value in the "search" phase without assuming convexity (which is rarely satisfied by the
195   loss functions in neural network optimization scenarios).

      bounds, other convergence rate measures, and the effect of the preconditioner on convergence rate.

200   3     A MINMAX H ESSIAN L IPSCHITZ - AWARE OPTIMIZATION ALGORITHM
201
202   Any deterministic optimization algorithm is comprised of two parts: first, we gather information
203   about the loss function to enable us to implicitly construct a local model of the loss function, and
204   secondly we step to the minimum of this model. Accordingly, gradient descent and Newton’s method
205
      use first- and second-order Taylor approximations of f respectively, and while these models do give a
      direction of descent in every subspace of the domain space, they do not indicate optimal step sizes in
206
      concave subspaces of the domain space (that is, subspaces in which the loss function is concave),
207
      since concave first- and second-order polynomials have no minima. To obtain a unique step in all
208   settings (so that our optimizer will be sufficiently general to apply to nonconvex and nonquadratic
209   regions of neural network loss functions), we must therefore model f with a third-order Taylor
210   polynomial.
211

310   4    D ESCENT RATE OF QUASI -N EWTON OPTIMIZATION ALGORITHMS

312   Although algorithm ELMO is optimal among first- and second-order methods in the sense that its
313
      model of the loss function is a generalization of Quasi-Newton methods’ and Gradient Descent’s

      unique, its greater computational burden of computing the Lipschitz parameters may cause it to be an

      ineffective optimization algorithm in practice. Since most prevalent practical optimizers today belong
316   to the Quasi-Newton family, we satisfy ourselves with a quantification of their quality based on their
317   similarity to this ideal algorithm.

      4.1   P ER - ITERATION DESCENT OF ARBITRARY STEP
350   Due to the computational difficulty of computing ELMO’s iteration step precisely, practitioners may
351   prefer computationally cheaper alternatives. To address this, we provide guarantees for the worst-case
352   rate of loss function descent of an arbitrary optimization algorithm relative to algorithm ELMO’s

      5     L IPSCHITZ DISTRIBUTION
397
398
      Previous works using the Hessian Lipschitz continuity assumption (e.g. ARC (Nesterov & Polyak,
399   2006) and its variants, O’Leary-Roseberry et al. (2019)) assume a single Lipschitz parameter for all
400   eigenspaces. Although a finite number n of eigenspaces ensures that such a Lipschitz parameter exists
401   (the maximal Lipschitz parameter), they fail to account for the distribution of these Lipschitz param-
402   eters over the eigenspaces. We claim that these parameters vary widely both over the eigenspaces
403   and over the course of training, so that a single constant value fails to capture this structure; in this
404   section we provide evidence for this claim.
405   One source of interest in this distribution is for optimization algorithms (e.g. ARC) that make
406   use of these parameters for the loss function modelling stage of each iteration. This may reduce
407   computational complexity by reducing the number of parameters one must compute at each iteration,
408   however appendix D shows that poorly estimating the Lipschitz parameters can have a detrimental
409   effect on an algorithm’s descent rate (thereby increasing the number of iterations the algorithm will
410   require to converge).
411   Another source of interest in these parameters’ distribution is in explaining the effectiveness of second-
412   order quasi-Newton algorithms that implicitly assume the Lipschitz parameters are insignificant (i.e.
413   very close to zero), since their model of the loss function is a quadratic Taylor polynomial (i.e. no
414   curvature shift); this may be seen from equation 10 which shows optimality of Newton’s method only
415   when λi > 0 and L+ = 0. We will show that they are not generally small by any means, however we
416   will show that the Lipschitz parameters of the subspaces in which they work (the convex subspaces -
417   see the implementation of Sivan et al. (2024), for instance, which applies Newton’s method only on
418   subspaces with significantly convex subspaces) are in fact small in certain settings.
419

531   7   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this work we developed and analyzed a Hessian eigenspace Lipschitz-aware minmax optimization
534   algorithm ELMO by taking an eigendecomposition-centric approach to locally modelling a loss
535   function. We then proved a widely applicable worst-case relative descent rate bound for quasi-
536   Newton optimizers by comparison to ELMO. We experimented with the Lipschitz distributions,
537   discovering that they are correlated with task difficulty and that they are helpful for optimizer and
538   optimization hyperparameters selection — specifically, integrating second-order information into
539   optimizers at the cost of additional computational complexity is worthwhile in settings where the
      convex Lipschitz parameters are small, but not those where they are large.

895
896   Some of the challenges involved in training neural networks include:
897
898          • Because the models and data often have very complex structures, obtaining precisely optimal
899            parameters is often computationally prohibitive. As a result, one must satisfy oneself with
900            a small degree of suboptimality in the model’s parameters, chosen to be small enough to
901
               satisfy one’s needs while not exhausting the computational capacity at hand.
902          • Since many model architectures (e.g. artificial neural networks) have very complex struc-
903            tures, the loss function is generallhy non-convex as a function of the model’s parameters.
904            This makes finding the globally optimal choice of parameters an NP-hard problem (Pardalos
905
               & Vavasis, 1991; Manders & Adleman, 1978). As a result, one must satisfy oneself with
               merely a local minimum of the loss function (that is, a point at which the norm of the gradient
906
               w.r.t. the model parameters is zero, and the function is locally convex, or equivalently, the
907
               Hessian is positive semi-definite). This is often considered sufficient (see Soudry & Carmon
908            (2016); Kawaguchi & Bengio (2019); Kawaguchi (2016); Nguyen et al. (2019)), however
909            this does not apply to saddle points and local maxima (points at which the gradient norm is
910            zero but the Hessian is not positive definite). Although some work has been done on trying to
911            eliminate this problem by eliminating local- but not global- minima via overparameterization
912            (Yu & Chen, 1995), further work (Ding et al., 2019) has shown that this does not scale to
913            deep neural networks.
914          • As mentioned previously, there is no single universally optimal optimizer, even among
915            existing optimizers.

      scenarios. Restricting ourselves to quasi-newton optimization algorithms, scenarios with n ≫ 0

       E    E VIDENCE FROM THE LITERATURE
1221
1222
       Experiments by Alain et al. (2018); Sagun et al. (2016); Ghorbani et al. (2019); Gur-Ari et al. (2018)
1223
       show that the positive eigenvalues of the Hessian remain relatively stable throughout training, while
1224   the negative eigenvalues shrink rapidly. Alain et al. (2018) and Sagun et al. (2016) also show that
1225   the negative eigenvalues shift chaotically. Gur-Ari et al. (2018) show that when training a network
1226   on a classification task with k classes, then at least the eigenspace spanned by the k eigenvectors
1227   corresponding to the top k eigenvalues remains very stable. Sivan et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2024)
1228   also show that when training a neural network on a variety of tasks, the top k eigenvalues and their
1229   corresponding eigenvectors change very slowly. Alain et al. (2018) also show explicitly that the
1230   second-order Taylor approximation is a poor approximation of the loss function in the eigenspace
1231   corresponding to the negative eigenvalues (the concave eigenspace), but an excellent approximation
1232
       in the eigenspace corresponding to the positive eigenvalues (the convex eigenspace); indeed, they
       show that the optimal step in the convex eigenspace is well estimated by the Newton step, while there
1233
       is no correlation between the Hessian and the optimal step in the concave eigenspace. Using the
1234
       Lipschitz parameter as a measure of the rate of change of the Hessian in a given subspace (hence a
1235   measure of the quality of a second-order Taylor approximation to a function and its corresponding
1236   Newton step), this supports the claim that L+ ≪ L− .

1431   setting based on the following rule: quasi-Newton optimizers hold an advantage over first-order
1432   optimizers when the convex Lipschitz parameters are small. As discussed in section 5, the convex
1433   Lipschitz parameters in the image autoencoder training setting are far larger than those in the image
1434   classification setting, so we compare a quasi-Newton optimizer against first-order methods in these
1435   settings to validate our rule.
1436   FOSI Sivan et al. (2024) is a variant of Saddle-Free Newton Dauphin et al. (2014) which applies
1437   Newton iterations in the domain space subspaces spanned by the dominant eigenvectors of the Hessian,
1438   and a first-order "base optimizer" in the remaining subspaces. We use FOSI as our representative
1439   second-order optimizer due to its computational effectiveness, capability to adjust the computational
1440   complexity of each iteration by adjusting the number of "dominant" eigenvectors to compute (fewer
1441   eigenvectors comes at the cost of a poorer Hessian approximation by approximating the Hessian
1442   with a lower-rank matrix, although this is somewhat mitigated by applying the base optimizer in
1443
       these subspaces), and fairness of comparison (since its integration of first-order optimizers allows us
       to compare the effect of second-order optimization in the dominant eigenspaces against first-order
1444
       optimization in these spaces, while all else is held equal - the remaining subspaces are both treated by
1445
       the same first-order optimizers).

1449   spectively and Savitzky-Golay order-2 filtering with a window size of 5000 for clarity of visualization.

1451   setting, due to the small convex Lipschitz parameters.

1533   initialized with the gradient). In our theoretical analysis however, we have the freedom to simply
1534

1535

1536

1537   significantly speed up optimization wall time, despite the additional computational burden of the
1538   Lanczos iterations, because of the regularizing effect this has on the function in each of the subspaces
1539   (by reducing the variance of the Hessian eigenvalues). Ghorbani et al. (2019) also show the benefits

2244   I   L IMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK
2245
2246   One interesting direction for future research is in putting the estimated Lipschitz parameters to work
2247   throughout the optimization process to increase the descent rate in hopes of matching and even
2248   surpassing ARC’s strong performance (Xu et al., 2017). Although the code attached to this paper is
2249   capable of estimating these parameters, it does so too slowly to be practically useful in computing all
2250
       of an algorithm’s steps, under most settings. We suggest future work could improve this algorithm’s
       computational complexity.
2251
2252   A limitation of our Newton’s method performance predictor is the additional computational burden
2253   of computing the Lipschitz parameters. We provide code for doing so in the attached code on Github,
2254   but we recommend performing these computations sparingly, since the Lipschitz parameters are
2255   approximately locally stable anyway.
2256   A second limitation of our work is its inability to provide any indication of the number of iterations
2257   left to achieve convergence. We see this as an acceptable limitation however, since in practice a
2258   model is only required to achieve a certain level of performance on the data decided ahead of time,
2259   without regard to how much further it could be optimized. As noted in the introduction, performance
2260   is measured by the loss function, so our descent rate bound satisfies this practical requirement.
2261   A final limitation of our bound is its reliance on ∆∆i θt as a measure of algorithm optimality which
2262   is a function of ∆θt∗⊤ vi , despite the fact that most optimizers do not compute that during training.
2263   This bound is therefore primarily of theoretical interest, as illustrated by its motivation of the very
2264   practical metric discussed in section 6
