009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Learning invariant representations has been the longstanding approach to self-
012            supervised learning. However, recently progress has been made in preserving
013            equivariant properties in representations, yet do so with highly prescribed architec-
014            tures. In this work, we propose an invariant-equivariant self-supervised architecture
015            that employs Capsule Networks (CapsNets) which have been shown to capture
016            equivariance with respect to novel viewpoints. We demonstrate that the use of
017            CapsNets in equivariant self-supervised architectures achieves improved down-
018
               stream performance on equivariant tasks with higher efficiency and fewer network
               parameters. To accommodate the architectural changes of CapsNets, we introduce
019
               a new objective function based on entropy minimisation. This approach which we
020
               name CapsIE (Capsule Invariant Equivariant Network) achieves state-of-the-art
021            performance across invariant and equivariant tasks on the 3DIEBench dataset com-
022            pared to prior equivariant SSL methods, while outperforming supervised baselines.
023            Our results demonstrate the ability of CapsNets to learn complex and generalised
024            representations for large-scale, multi-task datasets compared to previous CapsNet
025            benchmarks. Code is available at redacted

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Equivariance and invariance have become increasingly important properties and objectives of deep
031   learning in recent times, with precedence being largely placed on the latter. The task of invariance, i.e.
032   being able to classify a specific object no matter the camera perspective or augmentation applied, has
033
      driven progress in modern self-supervised learning approaches, specifically those which follow a joint
      embedding architecture Assran et al. (2022); Bardes et al. (2021); Chen et al. (2020). Equivariance
034
      on the other hand is the task of capturing embeddings which equally reflect the translations applied to
035
      the input space in the latent space. Equivariance thus has become an important property to capture to
036   enable the learning of high-quality representations in the real world where transformations such as
037   viewpoint are essential.
038
039
      Self-supervised learning owes its success to invariant objectives, where all recent progress, whether
      that is by contrastive Chen et al. (2020), information-maximisation Bardes et al. (2021); Zbontar
040
      et al. (2021), or clustering based methods Caron et al. (2021); Assran et al. (2022) rely on ensuring
041
      invariance in their representations under augmentation. This setting ensures performance in clas-
042   sification based tasks, but when employing the representations in alternative tasks, preservation of
043   information is essential to improve generalisation. To maintain properties of the transformation one
044   can predict the augmentations applied Dangovski et al. (2022); Lee et al. (2021), yet this is typically
045   not considered truly equivariant given the mapping of transformations is not represented in the latent
046   space. Methods that employ such a prediction methodology are typically considered equivariant
047   as the transformation in the input space is preserved in the latent space. Here prediction networks
048   are employed to reconstruct the view prior to transformation Winter et al. (2022), learn symmetric
049   representations Park et al. (2022), or predict the latent representation of the transformed view from
050
      the representation of the original view given the transformation parameters Garrido et al. (2023).
051   The above methods, although promising, enforce equivariance via objective functions on vector repre-
052   sentations, yet these methods fail to employ architectural approaches that have shown to be capable of
053   better capturing these properties. Capsule Networks (CapsNets), which utilise a process called routing

082   However, a common argument is that CapsNets have only shown these properties on toy examples

085
      function, achieving SOTA on several experiments and ablations studies on the 3DIEBench dataset
086
      Garrido et al. (2023) which has been created to specifically benchmark equivariant and invariant
087   properties of deep learning models. We prove that CapsNets retain their desirable properties on this
088   dataset which is considerably more difficult than what has been previously achieved with CapsNets,
089   while also establishing new SOTA on these tasks.
090
      To summarise, our contributions are:
091
092          • We propose a novel architecture based on a Capsule Network projection head that utilises the
093            key assumptions of capsule architectures to learn equivariant and invariant representations
094            which does not require the explicit split of representations.
095
             • We design a new objective function to accommodate the employment of a CapsNet projector,
096            enforcing invariance through entropy minimisation.
097
             • We demonstrate that CapsNet projectors implicitly learn pose understanding in a self-
098
               supervised setting.
099
100          • We show state of the art performance on 3DIEBench classification for equivariance and
101
               invariance benchmark tasks from our CapsNet-based architecture.

146   3.1   A RCHITECTURE
147
      Our method which we name CapsIE (Capsule Network Invariant Equivariant) follows the general
148
      joint embedding architecture previously described in Section 2 and extends those proposed by VICReg
149
      Bardes et al. (2021) and SIE Garrido et al. (2023). Like previous methods, we employ a ResNet-18
150   He et al. (2016) encoder as the core feature extractor fθ of our network. Yet unlike SIE Garrido et al.
151   (2023), we do not split the representations, and therefore do not require the use of separate invariant
152   and equivariant projection heads. Instead, we employ a single CapsNet (described in Section 3.2)
153   which takes as input the full representation of the encoder in place of the multi-layer perceptron
154   (MLP) in the projection head hϕ . Given the architectural design of CapsNets, our projection head
155   outputs both an activation scalar, representing how active the capsule is, and a 4 × 4 pose for each
156   capsule.
157   To align with the above problem statement and Equation 1, we aim to simultaneously learn invariant
158   and equivariant representations by optimising our network f with respect to the output activations and
159   poses. In this case, we consider the activation vector to capture existence of semantic concepts/objects
160   of the input, thus, the invariant information is preserved by the transformation. The pose on the other
161   hand is designed to encode positional information related to each corresponding capsule Ribeiro et al.
      (2022) (i.e. semantic concept), therefore it contains equivariant information that that was changed by

163   image view, zact and zpose , which correspond to the capsule activation vector and pose, and invariant

263
      of 1e-3 and batch size of 1024. For ablations and sensitivity analyses we train for 500 epochs and

266   as follows, λinv = 0.1, λequi = 5, λV = 10, λC = 1. Each self-supervised 2000 epoch pretraining

268   approximately 25 hours using 6 Nvidia A100 80GB GPU’s. All eval tasks are completed on a single
269   Nvidia A100 80GB GPU and take approximately 6 hours for angle prediction, and 3 hours for

294   4.2   D OWNSTREAM E VALUATION
295
296   To evaluate the quality of representations learnt under self-supervision, we use the standard benchmark
297   approach of learning downstream task specific networks with frozen representations as input. In our
298   case we evaluate the representations in three distinct tasks to evaluate both invariant and equivariant
299   properties, we use a linear evaluation training protocol of the frozen representations. Further details
300   of the evaluation protocol are given in the appendix B.2.
301   Invariant Evaluation. To evaluate invariant properties of the representation we train a classifier on
302   either the frozen representations output from the encoder network or the intermediate embeddings
303   of the capsule network projector. Given our advocation for CapsNets we evaluate using both the
304   standard linear classification and a capsule layer whose number of out capsules is set to the number
305   of classes. All methods are trained for 300 epochs by cross entropy.
306   Equivariant Evaluation. Evaluating equivariant properties is achieved through a rotation prediction
307   task in which a three layer MLP is trained to predict the quaternions defining the rotation between two
308   views of the same object. We train for 300 epochs using MSE loss. Similarly to rotation prediction,
309   we evaluate the representation’s equivariant properties by regressing the the colour hue of an object
310   view. We train a single linear layer for 50 epochs using MSE loss.
311
      Representation Quality. The performance of CapsIE for both invariant and equivariant benchmark
312   tasks is given in Tables 1 and 2 respectively. We evaluate both the representations produced by
313   the ResNet-18 encoder and the intermediate embeddings of the capsule layer projection head given
314   different values for the numbers of capsules. We observe that across all models that the invariant
315   properties captured within the representations marginally suffer compared to the MLP projector of
316   SIE. This observation is expected given the significantly reduced number of embeddings employed in
317   the invariant criterion compared to SIE. However, the evaluation of equivariant properties captured
318   by the representations demonstrates that the use of a capsule projector in place of a MLP can lead to
319   vastly improved performance in rotation prediction (↑ 0.05 R2 ) advancing the prior state-of-the-art
320
      whist also improving on the supervised baseline by a significant margin.
321   Additionally, we observe that the colour prediction task achieves a performance close to that of the
322   supervised setting even though our criteria do not directly optimise for such equivariant properties.
323   This suggests that the CapsNet is responsible for implicitly capturing transformations of the input
      whilst having little to no impact on the tasks directly optimised for.

      Intermediate Projector Embeddings. The role of the projector is primarily employed to decorrelate
      the embeddings on which the objective function operates on from the representations employed
350
      downstream. The premise is to avoid representations that are over-fit to the self-supervised objective
351
      Bordes et al. (2022). However, it has been well studied that it can be beneficial to maintain a number
352   of projector layers and instead utilise intermediate projector embeddings for downstream tasks. In our
353   case, the preservation of capsule layers for downstream tasks ensures that the desirable equivariant
354   and part-whole properties are maintained. Specifically, the equivariant information that has shown to
355   be captured in the object pose Ribeiro et al. (2022).
356
      We evaluate the intermediate embeddings output from the primary capsule layer in the same manner
357
      as the representations, however the activations and pose are given over a spatial region we perform
358   average pooling to return an activation vector and a 4 × 4 pose for each capsule which we then
359   flatten into a vector. As with the representation evaluation we report our invariant and equivariant
360   task performance in Tables 1 and 2 respectively. We find across all settings that evaluating the
361   intermediate embeddings of the capsule projector leads to improved performance on all tasks. We
362   observe that classification via the activation vector (i.e. invariant part) significantly improves on SIE

441   6.1   E QUIVARIANT S ELF -S UPERVISED L EARNING
442
443   Self-supervised learning has seen the majority of its success in the invariant setting by either con-
444   trastive Chen et al. (2020), information maximisation Zbontar et al. (2021); Bardes et al. (2021),
445   or clustering methods Caron et al. (2021); Assran et al. (2022). All families of approaches rely on
446   training a network to be invariant to transformations by increasing the similarity between embeddings
447   of the same image under augmentation. The differing approaches emerge from alternative methods to
448   avoid collapse, the phenomena where embeddings fall into a lower-dimensional subspace rather than
449
      the entire available embedding space resulting in a trivial solution Hua et al. (2021). Although these
      methods differ, they all produce similarly performing representations, hence we employ information
450
      maximisation methods as the basis of this work due to their computational efficiency.
451
452   Learning to be invariant to transformations is typically useful for semantic discrimination tasks,
453   yet preserving information about the transformations can be highly beneficial. Some approaches
454   have attempted to capture specific information regarding transformations by predicting the applied
455
      augmentation parameters Lee et al. (2021), preserving the strength of augmentations Xie et al. (2022)
      and introducing rotational transformations Dangovski et al. (2022). However, as stated in Garrido
456
      et al. (2023), these methods provide no guarantee that a mapping is learnt in the latent space that
457
      reflects the transformations in the input space. Hence, methods have been employed that address this
458   limitation, Devillers & Lefort (2022); Park et al. (2022); Garrido et al. (2023) all employ predictor
459   networks to predict the displacement representations in the latent space given one view representation
460   and the transformation parameters. The latter, SIE Garrido et al. (2023), is the basis of our work,
461   which further extends prior methods by splitting representation vectors into invariant and equivariant
462   parts to better separate differing information.
463
464
      6.2   C APSULE N ETWORKS
465
466
      CapsNets present an alternative architecture to CNNs, addressing their limitations by explicitly
467
      preserving hierarchical spatial relationships between features Sabour et al. (2017). CapsNets replace
468   scalar neurons with vector or matrix poses, representing specific concepts at different levels of a
469   parse tree as the network goes deeper. The first layer (primary capsules) corresponds to the most
470   basic parts, while capsules in deeper layers represent more complex concepts made up of the simpler
471   concepts as they get closer to the final layer where each capsule corresponds to a specific class.
472
      The key components of the CapsNet are the pose and the activation. The pose of a capsule is an
473
      embedding vector or matrix which provides a representation for the concept. The activation scalar is
474   a value between 0 and 1 which represents how certain the network is that the concept is present and
475   can be calculated directly from the values of the pose or via other means via the routing mechanism.
476
477
      The key novelty in CapsNets is the routing mechanism, which determines the contributions of lower-
      level capsules to higher-level capsules. Numerous routing algorithms, both iterative and non-iterative,
478
      have been proposed to address the efficiency and effectiveness of this process Sabour et al. (2017);
479
      Mazzia et al. (2021); Feng et al. (2024); Ribeiro et al. (2020); Hinton et al. (2018); De Sousa Ribeiro
480   et al. (2020); Yang et al. (2021); Everett et al. (2024). Among these, SRCaps Hahn et al. (2019)
481   introduces a non-iterative routing mechanism. This method maintains all of the desirable properties
482   of CapsNets, such as equivariance while largely mitigating the time cost of iterative methods, at
483   the price of a small amount of performance. However, SRCaps faces the same limitations as other
484   CapsNets where high resolution or high class datasets are beyond the network’s abilities when trained
485   in a standard fashion. For a more detailed description of capsule routing mechanisms please check
      this review Ribeiro et al. (2022).

      7    C ONCLUSION
487
488
      Our proposed method demonstrates how self-supervised CapsNets can be employed to better learn
489   equivariant representations, leveraging architectural assumptions removing the the need to explicitly
490   split representation vectors and train separate projector networks. The resulting solution, CapsIE,
491   achieves state-of-the-art performance in equivariant and invariant downstream benchmarks with a
492   significant improvement of 0.05 R2 on prior self-supervised rotation prediction tasks and 0.02 R2
493   improvement over the supervised baseline. In addition, we observe competitive performance of
494   CapsIE on equivariant tasks not explicitly trained for, further demonstrating the implicit equivariant
495   properties of our capsule architecture under standard invariant optimisation criteria akin to those
496   of VICReg Bardes et al. (2021), SimCLR Chen et al. (2020), and MSN Assran et al. (2022). Our
497
      results contribute significantly to the avocation of CapsNets in self-supervised representation learning,
      introducing desirable properties with improved effectiveness over MLP projectors.
498
499   Ethics Statement. This work aims to learn higher quality and more applicable representations of
500   images without human generated annotations, therefore such methods can lead to positive societal
501   impacts the development of more accurate or informative models for a number of downstream tasks.
502   However, as is the case with all vision systems, there is potential for exploitation and security concerns
503
      and one should take into consideration AI misuse when extending our method.
504   In addition, the use of CapsNets has shown little improvement when scaled, similar to the findings of
505   Everett et al. (2023); Mitterreiter et al. (2023); Nair et al. (2021) where the addition of more capsule
506   layers has either led to stagnated or decreased performance. We strongly feel that our work should be
507   revisited when a capsule network that can both scale and retain the desirable properties of capsule
508   networks is found.
509   As stated in the original dataset proposal and the problem setting the methodology presented rely on
510   the group elements being known. Hence, the applicability of the proposed method is only possible
511   in settings where group elements are known. However, we present findings where additional group
512   elements are preserved without prior knowledge of the group, this suggests the CapsNets are more
513   capable than previous invariant methods at capturing equivariant properties, thus opening an intriguing
514   direction for future work.

      B.3   Q UANTITATIVE E QUIVARIANT E VALUATION I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
757
758   We evaluate the equivariant properties of the predictor in line with that proposed in Garrido et al.
759   (2023) reporting the Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR) and Hit Rate at k (H@k) on the multi-object
760   setting. Given a source and target pose of an object, we first compute the embeddings of each image,
761   and pass the source embedding through the predictor and use the resulting vector to retrieve the
762   nearest neighbours.
763   The MRR is the average reciprocal rank of the target embedding in the retrieved nearest neighbour
764   graph. H@k in this case is computed to be 1 if the target embedding is in the k-NN graph of the
765   predicted embedding, where we only look for nearest neighbours among the views of the same object.
766
      The Prediction Retrieval Error (PRE) gives an evaluation of predictor quality, and is given by the
767
      distance between its rotation q1 ∈ H and the target rotation q2 as d = 1− < q1 , q2 >2 of the nearest
768   neighbour of the predicted embedding averaged over the whole dataset. Full implementation details
