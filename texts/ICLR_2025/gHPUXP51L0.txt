022                                            A BSTRACT
023
024                Speech is a hierarchical collection of text, prosody, emotions, dysfluencies, etc.
025                Automatic transcription of speech that goes beyond text (words) is an underex-
026                plored problem. We focus on transcribing speech along with non-fluencies (dys-
027
                   fluencies). The current state-of-the-art pipeline (Lian et al., 2024) suffers from
                   complex architecture design, training complexity, and significant shortcomings in
028
                   the local sequence aligner, and it does not explore in-context learning capacity.
029
                   In this work, we propose SSDM 2.0, which tackles those shortcomings via four
030                main contributions: (1) We propose a novel neural articulatory flow to derive
031                highly scalable speech representations. (2) We developed a full-stack connection-
032                ist subsequence aligner that captures all types of dysfluencies. (3) We introduced
033                a mispronunciation prompt pipeline and consistency learning module into LLM
034                to leverage dysfluency in-context pronunciation learning abilities. (4) We cu-
035                rated Libri-Dys (Lian et al., 2024) and open-sourced the current largest-scale co-
036                dysfluency corpus, Libri-Co-Dys, for future research endeavors. In clinical exper-
037                iments on pathological speech transcription, we tested SSDM 2.0 using nfvPPA
038                corpus primarily characterized by articulatory dysfluencies. Overall, SSDM 2.0
039                outperforms SSDM and all other dysfluency transcription models by a large mar-
040                gin. See our project demo page at https://srnf2.github.io/.

043   1       I NTRODUCTION
044
      Current automatic speech recognition (ASR) systems (Radford et al., 2023) and speech language
045
      models (SLMs) (Wu et al., 2024) typically transcribe speech into the words that were spoken (lexi-
046
      calized speech) rather than how the words were spoken (uttered speech). For example, when some-
047   one says P-Please c-call st-ah-lla, these systems usually perform denoising and output please call
048   stella. This approach is suitable for most spoken dialogue scenarios and services, and helps reduce
049   confusion in communications. However, in pathological speech domain, uttered speech is required
050   to accurately identify articulation and pronunciation problems for diagnostic purposes. The gap be-
051   tween lexicalized speech and uttered speech is referred to as dysfluency, which includes repetition,
052   deletion, insertion, replacement of sounds, filler words, and hesitations (Lian et al., 2023b). Accu-

054   rate transcription of speech dysfluencies could substantially reduce the workload of speech language
055   pathologists while facilitating diagnosis and serving as a powerful clinical assessment tool.
056
      Pathological speech disorders are typically caused by neurological or physiological factors and are
057
      associated with various dysfluencies, such as motor and phonological (articulatory) dysfluencies
058
      (e.g., nfvPPA, Parkinson’s disease, Broca’s aphasia) and higher-order (or semantic) dysfluencies
059   (e.g., svPPA, Wernicke’s aphasia, ASD). Diagnosing these disorders is challenging due to case-by-
060   case variability and differences in severity. However, they share a common set of dysfluencies at
061   the behavioral level, making it possible to develop a general dysfluency transcription system that
062   can accommodate all disorders and support follow-up diagnosis. Due to data constraints, however,
063   it is not feasible to test such a system on all pathological speech data. Therefore, we focus on
064   nfvPPA, leveraging the currently available data to develop a dysfluency transcription tool specifically
065   targeting articulation-based dysfluencies.
066   Technically, SSDM (Lian et al., 2024) is the first end-to-end pipeline that can transcribe both
067    lexicalized speech and uttered speech (with dysfluencies) . However, it faces challenges in rep-
068
      resentation learning complexity, limited dysfluency alignment coverage, and minimal performance
069   boost from language modeling. Technically, there are four questions to address: (1) What are the
070   most scalable speech representations? (2) How to align dysfluent speech with text? (3) How to
071   curate large-scale dysfluent speech data with time-aware annotations? (4) How to leverage pro-
072   nunciation in-context learning from large language models? We aim to address the aforementioned
073   challenges and present SSDM 2.0. Our key contributions are as follows:
074
075       • We propose Neural Articulatory Flow, which encodes a semi-implicit speech representation that
076
            has been shown to be the most scalable dysfluency-aware representation, inspired by articula-
077         tory dysfluencies in pathological speech disorders.
078       • We develop Full-Stack Connectionist Subsequence Aligner (FCSA), achieving comprehensive
079         coverage of dysfluency alignments and precise distribution estimation.
080       • We curate and opensource Libri-Dys-Co, the largest simulated speech co-dysfluency corpus,
081         featuring over 6,000 hours of time-aware dysfluency annotations (word/phoneme).
082       • We introduce Mispronunciation In-Context Learning and Consistency Learning in langauge
083         model to achieve zero-shot dysfluency transfer and joint fluent-dysfluent ASR tasks transfer.
084
      SSDM 2.0 significantly and consistently outperforms all existing methods, including speech lan-
085
      guage models, and can serve as a foundational dysfluency modeling tool.

087   2      SSDM 2.0 OVERVIEW
088
089
      SSDM 2.0 (shown in Fig. 2) processes dysfluent speech and a textual prompt as inputs, generating
      pronunciation transcriptions as output. To achieve this, we propose the Neural Articulatory Flow
090
      (NAF), which generates scalable speech representations referred to as gestural scores. Subsequently,
091
      the gestural scores are aligned with the reference text using a Full-stack Connectionist Subsequence
092   Aligner, producing aligned speech embeddings for each token in the reference text. These aligned
093   embeddings, combined with pre-defined prompts, are then input into a LLaMA (Touvron et al.,
094   2023) module for instruction tuning (Gong et al., 2023b), a process we term Non-fluency In-context
095   Learning. The following subsections provide a detailed explanation of each module.

097   3 N EURAL A RTICULATORY F LOW
098   Current speech representation modeling typically uses explicit D × T matrices, where T represents
099   time and D represents channel dimension, and these are learned densely in a data-driven manner.
100   However, human speech is produced by a sparse set of articulators with sparse activation in time.
101

103

104   implicitly learn such structural speech representations via human-prior rules? At the same time,
105   as we focus on articulatory dysfluencies, modeling speech production processes helps identify the
106
      specific articulation problems in the gestural space and brings the representation closer to real hu-
107
      man dysfluent speech. In this work, we propose Neural Articulatory Flow, which involves a Semi-

132   Given a speech waveform, we adopt UAAI (Universal Acoustic-to-Articulatory IInversion)(Cho
133   et al., 2024) to obtain its articulatory trajectory X ∈ RD×T at 50Hz. UAAI consists of a
134   WavLM(Chen et al., 2022) encoder followed by a linear layer, which takes speech waveform as in-
135   put and predicts articulatory trajectories. The model was trained on the MNGU0 corpus (Richmond
136   et al., 2011), which contains 75 minutes of EMA (Electromagnetic midsagittal articulography) data
137   collected during newspaper reading. In our setting, D = 12 corresponds to the x and y coordinates
138   of 6 articulators measured in the EMA recording process.

      4.1     F ULLSTACK S TATE T RANSITIONS
245
246   Given frame-wise speech tokens τ = [τi ∈ RD ]Ti=1 and reference text tokens γ(C) = [γ(Cj ) ∈
247   RD ]Lj=1 , the detection of non-fluency in speech hinges on the alignment, which is usually a L × T
248
      matrix. Emission probability y i,j = p(τj |τi ) and transition probability p(Cm |Cn ) are usually in-
249   troduced for optimizing the alignment learning. For example, when the speech is normal or fluent,
250   the alignment would be completely monotonic and y i,j only depends on y i−1,j and y i−1,j−1 , which
251   is the case of vanilla CTC optimization (Graves et al., 2006), and we call this Stack-1, named as
252   LM(CTC), as shown in Fig. 4. Stack-1 encodes normal language modeling. Note that in the future
253   discussion we use y i,j to refer to both emission probability and position tokens (i, j) for conve-
254   nience. For non-fluent speech, the alignment cases are diverse. Assume τi−k and τi are aligned
255   with Cj−k and Cj respectively, and the other speech frames [τi−k+1 , . . . , τi−1 ] are dysfluencies in-
256   cluding repetition, insertion, and block. Then we only consider y i−k,j−1 → y i,j . CSA (Lian et al.,
257   2024) implicitly achieves this by performing Emission Copy such that y i−k,j−1 = . . . = y i−1,j−1
258
      so that we could still apply the normal language modeling y i−1,j−1 → y i,j . This is Stack-2, named
      as Copy. To give a concrete example, if speech τ = [P, L, IY, L, IY, Z] (p-l-ea-l-ea-se) and text
259
      C = [P, L, IY, Z] (please), then the last [τi−k+1 , τi−1 ] = [L, IY ] are inserted or repetition tokens.
260
      In this work, we propose Fullstack State Transitions in addition to these basic stacks. We introduce
261   the other four stacks in the following. Stack-3, designated as the Skip stack, addresses missing dys-
262   fluencies. As illustrated in Fig. 4, the reference text Cj−1 is omitted, consequently skipping the emis-
263   sion y i−1,j−1 . In this scenario, the transition is represented as y i−k,j−k → y i,j , which constitutes
264   a disrupted language model. Stack-4, termed N-mono, introduces non-monotonicity that may indi-
265   cate replacement errors. For instance, the pronunciation [P, L, EY, Z] in contrast to [P, L, IY, Z]
266   for the word please. While Stacks 1-4 primarily focus on fluent tokens y i,j , where speech τi pre-
267   cisely aligns with text Cj , misalignments can occur, such as y i−1,j−1 in Stack-2 and Stack-3. To
268   address these cases, we introduce Stack-5, wherein y i,j represents a passive state indicating that τi
269   is an inserted non-fluent token. We designate this as Pass-I. Similarly, Stack-6 incorporates a pas-
      sive state corresponding to a removed non-fluent token, which we denote as Pass-R. Based on these

529   7     C ONCLUSIONS AND L IMITATIONS
530
531   We introduce SSDM 2.0, featuring Neural Articulatory Flow, Fullstack Connectionist Subsequence
532   Aligner, and Non-fluency In-Context Learning. We open-sourced a large-scale co-dysfluency corpus
533   Libri-Dys-Co. SSDM 2.0 significantly outperforms current works (Appendix. A.12). The method’s
534   potential with increased data remains unexplored. Additional future work will focus on developing
535   fine-grained simulation techniques, addressing the primary bottleneck in this domain.
536   On the clinical side, due to data constraints, we evaluated only nfvPPA for articulation-based dys-
537   fluencies, leaving out other disorders such as Parkinson’s disease and Broca’s aphasia. It would also
538   be valuable to extend this work to semantic-based dysfluency disorders, such as svPPA, Wernicke’s
539   aphasia, and ASD, to enhance the pipeline’s applicability as a general speech transcription tool for
      a broader range of speech disorders. This will be addressed in future work.

1071

1072

1073

1076   dense matrices like mel-spectrograms or self-supervised units (Mohamed et al., 2022). Implicit
1077   representations offer greater efficiency and scalability due to their sparse nature. Previous work
1078   has explored deriving spatial and temporal sparse activation matrices via matrix factorization (Lian
1079   et al., 2022; 2023a) or complex entry-wise joint-duration-intensity modeling (Lian et al., 2024). We
       propose to derive implicit gestural scores.

1147   A.7   L OCAL S EQUENCE A LIGNMENT
1148
1149   For fluent speech, this alignment is strictly monotonic. A common approach involves identifying
1150   local non-monotonic alignments by excluding monotonic segments (Lian et al., 2023b). The latest
1151   methodology (Lian et al., 2024) maintains a monotonic alignment paradigm even when addressing
1152   speech disfluencies. For example, given the reference text P-L-IY-Z and the spoken sequence P-
1153
       P-L-EY-SIL-EY-Z, the alignment is: [P-[P,P], L-[L], IY-[EY,SIL-EY], Z-[Z]]. In this structure, the
       ground truth text is followed by its corresponding speech elements, highlighting phenomena such as
1154
       stuttering (”P”), blocking and phonetic errors (”IY” vs. ”EY”), while other pronunciations match the
1155
       reference text. Lian et al. (2023b) posited that such an alignment [P-[P,P], L-[L], IY-[EY,SIL-EY],
1156   Z-[Z]] can be derived via the longest common subsequence (LCS) algorithm (Hirschberg, 1977).
1157   LCS is a local sequence alignment algorithm; by local, it means that the cost function only con-
1158   siders entries where a speech frame matches a text token while disregarding other tokens, which is
1159   crucial for capturing disfluencies. This approach differs significantly from global sequence aligners
1160   such as Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) (Sakoe, 1971), where all entries contribute to the cost func-
1161   tion and thus are not well-suited for modeling non-fluent speech. However, speech tokens are more
1162   abstract than phonemes. Directly applying LCS does not necessarily yield a dysfluency-aware align-
1163   ment, and there could be multiple reasonable alignments given speech sequence and text sequence.
1164
       Consequently, a differentiable, stochastic subsequence aligner is required.
1165   SSDM (Lian et al., 2024) introduced connectionist subsequence alignment (CSA) as the first proper
1166   estimation method. However, there are notable limitations. (1) SSDM primarily focuses on Transi-
1167   tion Skip y i−k,j−1 → y i,j for k > 1, capturing dysfluencies like repetition, blocking, and insertion,
1168   while other transition types, such as word/phoneme omission y i−k,j−k → y i,j , are not explicitly
1169   addressed. Additionally, the emission probability y i,j can be passive or skipped, which SSDM
1170
       overlooks. (2) The adapted forward-backward algorithm lacks interpretability regarding the specific
       dysfluency patterns encoded. (3) The neural gestural score H is trained using a separate phoneme
1171
       classification task, adding to training complexity. In this work, we address the aforementioned prob-
1172
       lems by introducing Pre-Alignment Training, which incorporates full-stack transition modeling with
1173   clear interpretability and controllability.
1174

1708   Dysfluency Modeling Speech dysfluency modeling seeks to detect dysfluencies at both word and
1709   phoneme levels, with precise timing given a reference text (Lian et al., 2023b). Early work focused
1710   on hand-crafted features (Chia Ai et al., 2012; Chee et al., 2009; Esmaili et al., 2016; Jouaiti &
1711   Dautenhahn, 2022a; Mujtaba et al., 2024) and end-to-end classification approaches at both utterance
1712   level (Kourkounakis et al., 2021; Alharbi et al., 2020; Jouaiti & Dautenhahn, 2022b; Oue et al., 2015;
1713
       Bayerl et al., 2022b; Howell & Sackin, 1995; Alharbi et al., 2017; Tan et al., 2007; Bayerl et al.,
       2023a;b; Dash et al., 2018; Mohapatra et al., 2023; Wagner et al., 2024; Changawala & Rudzicz,
1714
       2024) and frame level (Shonibare et al., 2022; Harvill et al., 2022). The current mainstream methods
1715
       treat this problem as a time-based object detection task (Lian et al., 2023b; Lian & Anumanchipalli,
1716   2024; Zhou et al., 2024b;a; Lian et al., 2024). More recently, token-based methods (Zhou et al.,
1717   2024c) have also been explored and have achieved comparable results.
1718
1719   Articulatory Speech Representation Learning Recent studies show articulatory features’ ef-
1720   fectiveness as scalable representations in speech recognition (Lian et al., 2023a) and dysfluency
1721   modeling (Lian et al., 2024). Early research sought to resolve speech dynamics through motion
1722   laws (Coker, 1976), simplified by gestural theory (Browman & Goldstein, 1990; 1992) which con-
1723   ceptualizes speech as sparse activations of articulatory primitives, analogous to robotics’ gait li-
1724   braries (Grizzle et al., 2010). Subsequent work developed methods for automatic gesture extrac-
1725   tion (Ramanarayanan et al., 2013; Lian et al., 2022; 2023a). Recently, articulatory-to-speech inver-
1726
       sion (Cho et al., 2024) enable extraction of articulatory-free representations as speech codecs with
       full intelligibility, validated as optimal encodings for dysfluency modeling (Lian et al., 2024).
