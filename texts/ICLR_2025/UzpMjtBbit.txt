009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               There is a growing body of work on using Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) to
012            learn representations of circuits, focusing primarily on their static characteristics.
013            However, these models fail to capture critical runtime behavior, which is cru-
014            cial for tasks like hardware verification and optimization. To address this limita-
015            tion, we introduce DynamicRTL, a novel GNN-based approach that learns circuit
016            representations by incorporating both static structures and multi-cycle execution
017            behaviors. DynamicRTL leverages an operation-level Control Data Flow Graph
018            (CDFG) to represent Register Transfer Level (RTL) circuits, enabling the model to
019            capture dynamic dependencies and runtime execution. To train and evaluate Dy-
020
               namicRTL, we built the first comprehensive dynamic circuit dataset, comprising
               over 6,300 Verilog modules and 190,000 simulation traces. Our results demon-
021
               strate that DynamicRTL consistently outperforms existing models in branch pre-
022
               diction tasks. Furthermore, its learned representations transfer effectively to re-
023            lated tasks, achieving strong performance in assertion prediction and underscoring
024            its transfer learning capabilities for dynamic circuit tasks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Over the past decades, Moore’s law has driven the exponential growth in the scale of digital circuits.
031   However, as the circuit complexity increases, traditional algorithms and methodologies for circuit
032
      design and optimization are increasingly challenged to meet the pressing demands for time and cost
      efficiency. There is an urgent need for new models, representations, and methodologies for circuit
033
      understanding to advance research in hardware design and electronic design automation (EDA),
034
      which are essential for empowering engineers to create high-performance hardware solutions.
035
036   Recent works have begun to solve many canonical tasks in hardware development with deep learn-
037   ing methods (Chen et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2020). By training on extensive circuit data, models
038
      have demonstrated the potential to understand the static feature of circuits and outperform tradi-
      tional methods on some prediction tasks, including circuit quality prediction (performance, power,
039
      area) (Sengupta et al., 2022; Fang et al., 2023; Lopera et al., 2021), combinational functionality
040
      prediction (Wang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022; Shi et al., 2023) and so on.
041
042   Despite these notable achievements, it has been observed that these models struggle with tasks
043   that require in-depth analysis of circuit designs, especially those involving the dynamic behavior
044
      of circuits such as hardware verification, dynamic power estimation and so on (Khan et al., 2024;
      Vasudevan et al., 2021). The main reason is that current models exclusively depend on the static
045
      data of circuits (i.e., hardware source code, netlist) as input. As a result, these models are limited to
046
      learning only structural or semantic information about the circuits.
047
048   However, the dynamic behavior of circuits, which involves changes in circuit states over multiple
049   operational cycles, is equally important for understanding circuits and facilitating dynamic-related
050
      downstream tasks. These behaviors can reveal complex dependencies and interactions that are unap-
      parent in static representation, thereby significantly enhancing the quality of circuit representation.
051
052   In this study, we diverge from traditional approaches learning static representations of circuits. In
053   an innovative endeavor, we aim to train the model to learn the dynamic representation of circuits
      based on their multi-cycle execution behavior. We focus on two dynamic tasks: branch prediction

072   evaluation of test cases during simulation-based verification processes (Vasudevan et al., 2021).
073   We design the model DR-GNN (DynamicRTL-GNN) to learn the dynamic behavior of circuits. Our
074   model is distinguished by two aspects. First, we construct the graph neural network (GNN) based
075   on the operation-level Control Data Flow Graph (CDFG) of Register Transfer Level (RTL) circuit
076   design. This CDFG more accurately reflects the functionality of circuit, thereby facilitating the
077   learning of dynamic behaviors. Second, we design the circuit functionality-aware GNN propaga-
078   tion mechanism that integrates specific circuit features into the model. We incorporate information
079
      about node operation types, positions, and dynamic embeddings into a heterogeneous graph trans-
      former (Hu et al., 2020) to capture both the structural and dynamic characteristics of circuits.
080
081   We leverages a comprehensive collection of Verilog designs as our dataset. Because the dynamic
082   behavior of circuits can be fully encapsulated by the execution of its branches, we utilize branch
083   execution information as our training supervision. DynamicRTL has exhibited outstanding perfor-
084   mance in the task of branch prediction, significantly surpassing the results of models that rely solely
085
      on structure and semantic learning. Moreover, DynamicRTL can also generate useful representa-
      tions of circuits for indirectly related downstream tasks, and we demonstrate the transfer learning
086
      on assertion prediction problem. To our knowledge, DynamicRTL is the first unified model that
087
      can capture the dynamic behavior of various hardware designs, setting the stage for teaching neural
088   network models to better understand how circuits execute.
089
090   In summary, this paper makes the following contributions:
091
                • We introduce the problem of learning circuit dynamic representation. We create the first
092
                  dynamic circuit dataset, comprising over 6,300 diverse Verilog designs, along with their
093
                  corresponding CDFGs and 190,000 simulation traces.
094
095             • We design the DR-GNN model which is built on the circuit operation-level CDFG and is
096
                  aware of the dynamic functionality of circuits.
097             • We get the first unified representation for circuit dynamic behaviors and achieve the state-
098               of-the-art performance in circuit branch prediction with an accuracy of 93.48%.
099             • We show that DR-GNN representations pre-trained on branch prediction are useful for
100               transfer learning, achieving an average accuracy of 88.05% on assertion prediction task.

      RTL Code. Register transfer level (RTL) is a design abstraction level used in digital circuit design.
124
      A digital circuit usually composed of combinational logic (computing operators, branch controls)
125   and sequential logic (registers). The assignment in RTL represents a physical connection between
126   wires or registers. In this context, a wire is a conductive path for signal transmission, while a register
127   is a small storage element that holds data temporarily during the execution of a digital circuit. RTL
128   is a representation that focuses on the flow of data between registers and the operations performed
129   on that data. The register transferring data flows are commonly written in always blocks, meaning
130   that the logic is executed during every clock cycle. This is analogous to a software program written
131   in a while loop, where the same code is repeatedly executed and the state continuously changes.
132   Control Data Flow Graph. A common way of understanding the function of RTL is control data
133   flow graph (CDFG). Figure 2 shows an example of RTL code and the built CDFGs.
134
      Previous work, Design2Vec (Vasudevan et al., 2021), uses a statement-level CDFG to learn semantic
135
      representation of circuits, where each node represents an RTL statement. However, this CDFG only
136
      reflect the execution of circuit code from a software perspective, which fails to reflect the actual data
137   flow within circuits. For example, in Figure 2 (b), three distinct statements assign different values
138   to the register count under varying conditions. This representation diverges from the true dynamic
139   behavior of the circuit. Actually, circuits operate in parallel, processing multiple signals simultane-
140   ously. Their behavior is determined by the complex interconnections and timing of components.
141
      To more accurately represent the dynamic behavior of circuits, we propose the operation-level
142
      CDFG as the graph structure to be used by our model. In our CDFG, directed edges indicate data
143   or control flows between nodes. In sequential circuits, registers store values to the next clock cycle,
144   therefore the in-edges of registers signify data flow across clock cycles, making the CDFG a cyclic
145   graph. The CDFGs comprise three node types: variable nodes, constant nodes and operation nodes.
146
      Variable nodes represent variables that have dynamic values, such as wires and registers, which
147
      can only be assigned once in RTL and have a single in-edge in CDFG. Input wires are special
148
      variable nodes whose value depends on the external drive and have only out-edges without in-edges.
149   Constant nodes represent unchanging values in a circuit. These nodes also have no in-edges and
150   represent the same values throughout clock cycles.
151
152
      Operation nodes represent operations in data flow, with one or more in-edges and a single out-edge.
      The condition node is a unique type of operation node, functioning like a multiplexer, and uses a
153
      one-bit select signal to control data flow from different data channels. Since some operators do not
154
      satisfy the commutative law, the order of in-edges must not be overlooked.
155
156   The nodes in CDFG can be multi-bit, allowing for a higher level of abstraction and a more effective
157   way to represent RTL dynamic behaviors compared with single-bit circuit graphs (Fang et al., 2023;
158
      Li et al., 2022), which are closer to netlists. For more details on circuit CDFG, refer to Appendix B.
159   Circuit Dynamic Execution State. The dynamic state of a circuit refers to the set of values that
160   change as the circuit executes. Input signals drive the internal wires based on the combinational
161   operations. Some values are stored in registers to be used in the next clock cycle. In principle,
      the dynamic state of a circuit is represented by the value sequence of its registers. However, to


                                                                              3
162
      achieve a comprehensive representation of the circuit, it is essential to consider both sequential and
163   combinational features. As a result, we also take the values of wires into account when assessing
164   dynamic states. These values can be easily obtained through hardware simulation.
165
166
      Tools. To simplify the process of constructing the CDFG, we first use the Verilog synthesis tool
      Yosys (Wolf et al., 2013) to convert the source RTL code into an RTL intermediate language
167
      (RTLIL). This conversion results in a functionally equivalent description, consisting only of assign
168
      statements and register transferring. Next, we use the Stagira Verilog parser (Chen et al., 2023) to
169   parse the RTLIL and generate an abstract syntax tree (AST). Finally, we traverse the AST to create
170   the circuit’s CDFG. To collect the dynamic traces of the circuits, we use Verilator (Snyder et al.,
171   2023) as simulator and capture the value of wires and registers in each clock cycle.

174   3     C IRCUIT DYNAMIC TASKS
175
176   Our model is designed to focus on two tasks that are strongly related with circuit dynamic behaviors,
177   branch prediction and assertion prediction. Given an input sequence and a design, the model predicts
178   whether a specific branch can be hit or a particular assertion can be satisfied.
179   Hardware branches, often represented by conditional statements like if or case in RTL code, de-
180   note points where execution can follow multiple paths based on certain conditions. These branches
181   are important coverage metrics in hardware verification, ensuring each code branch is executed at
182   least once to gauge testing thoroughness. In the transformation to CDFG, branches are mapped to
183   condition nodes, with the select signals controlling the selection of data flow.
184
      Hardware assertion is used to validate that specific conditions hold true under certain circumstances.
185   It provides a framework for writing constraints, checkers and cover points for hardware designs. For
186   example, in an arbiter whose function is to arbitrate between two request ports (A and B), we can
187   assert !(grant A && grant B) to ensure at most one request is granted access at the same time.
188
      In our study, we use branch prediction as supervision, which will be detailed in Section 4.4. Branch
189
      prediction provides a consistent task across different designs and the supervision is easy to obtain.
190
      Meanwhile, the execution of a branch encapsulates most of the dynamic behavior in circuits. If
191   the model accurately predicts branch hitting, it implies that the model has effectively learned the
192   dynamic information of other wires and registers governing the execution of these branches. After
193   training on branch information, to further investigate whether the learned representation contains
194   comprehensive dynamic circuit behaviors, we use assertion prediction as a downstream task, which
195   will be discussed in detail in Section 5.4.

198   4     DR-GNN: C IRCUIT F UNCTIONALITY-AWARE GNN M ODEL
199
200   4.1   OVERVIEW
201
202   Our model takes as input the CDFG and input sequences I. The circuit has branch set B. The main
203   objective of our model is to predict the probability is hit(I, B) that input I hit branches in B.
204   Figure 3 presents the architecture of DR-GNN. The model operates in three stages: Firstly, the initial
205   embedding for each circuit node is assigned based on its attribute and functionality (Section 4.2).
206   Secondly, the DR-GNN updates the node embeddings using a circuit functionality-aware propaga-
207   tion method that accommodates the behaviors of circuit operations (see Section 4.3). Thirdly, after
208   the final embeddings for each node are obtained, they are utilized to compute the loss for various
209   prediction tasks related to circuit dynamic behaviors (Section 4.4).
210

      4.3   C IRCUIT F UNCTIONALITY-AWARE P ROPAGATION
251
      In hardware circuit, data is processed through various hardware components, which correspond to
252   the operation nodes in our CDFG. We can use the aggregation mechanism in GNN to represent this
253   dynamic behavior. However, there are challenges due to the different types of operations in circuit
254   logic. Each operation requires a specific aggregation pattern to properly handle the embeddings of
255   neighboring nodes. Additionally, some operators do not satisfy the commutative law. For instance,
256   the subtraction operator yields different results for a-b and b-a. Therefore, it is also essential to
257   consider the position of source nodes when aggregating embeddings to the target node.
258   To solve this problem, we design a novel aggregation method in DR-GNN that effectively represents
259   circuit logic. Because the circuit CDFG is a heterogeneous directed graph, we build the aggregation
260   mechanism referring to the Heterogeneous Graph Transformer (HGT) (Hu et al., 2020). We use
261   different operation types and node positions to parameterize weight for heterogeneous attention,
262   thereby achieves effective aggregation for different circuit operations.

321   5.1   DATASET P REPARATION
322
323   To train the DynamicRTL, we build the first dynamic circuit dataset, which consists of around 6,300
      different circuit designs and 190,000 circuit simulation traces.


                                                               6
324
      Circuit Designs Collection. We begin by collecting some existing Verilog datasets including MG-
325   Verilog (Zhang et al., 2024) and VeriGen (Thakur et al., 2024). To further expand our collection,
326   we searched GitHub using keywords such as Verilog, RTL, circuit and subsequently scraped Verilog
327   code files from the relevant repositories. We remove designs with syntax error and those that failed
328   during translation to CDFG or simulation. Besides, we only keep sequential circuits in our dataset.
329   As a result, our dataset comprises approximately 6,300 usable Verilog modules. The circuit sizes of
330   these designs range from 10 to over 500 CDFG nodes, with an average of 51 nodes per design. This
331   corresponds to an RTL design containing more than 100 lines of Verilog RTL code. It should be
332   noted that the primary goal of constructing a world-level CDFG for circuits is to achieve a compact
333
      representation. Consequently, while the scale of our CDFGs is relatively small, they can effectively
      represent circuits of significantly larger scale. For instance, an RTL-level circuit graph with 100
334
      nodes can be transformed into an AIG graph with over 10,000 nodes after synthesis, which results
335
      in the loss of Verilog’s semantic information. The collected circuits correspond to Verilog files with
336   up to more than 1,000 lines and netlists with up to 50,000 nodes. Further information about our
337   circuit dataset is available in Appendix C.
338
339
      Simulation Traces Collection. We use Verilator (Snyder et al., 2023) to simulate collected Verilog
      modules. We generate random input patterns and simulate each module with 30 different traces.
340
      During simulation, values of all the internal variables are collected in each clock cycle. When
341
      generating testbench, we automatically identify special signals such as the reset signal. We make
342   that the reset signal is active only at the beginning of simulation and remains inactive thereafter. This
343   approach guarantees the simulation traces encompass a comprehensive range of circuit behaviors.

346
347   Our dataset consists of 6,300 unique circuit CDFGs, corresponding to Verilog files with over 1,000
348   lines and netlists containing more than 50,000 nodes. Each CDFG is paired with 30 simulation
349   traces, resulting in approximately 190,000 data entries. We split our dataset by design, using 80%
350   of the designs and their traces for training and 20% for evaluation. This approach ensures that the
351   designs used for evaluation are never seen by the model during training, providing a reliable measure
352   of its generalization ability. The binary cross-entropy losses of the pretraining tasks are calculated
353
      and summed to form the final loss. The hyperparameters of our model are detailed in Appendix A.2.
      We train our model on a single A800 GPU for 60 epochs, using a learning rate of 0.0001 and the
354
      Adam optimizer.

458   6   R ELATED W ORK
459
460   Learning representation for circuit: Circuits can be represented as graphs, with operators as
461   nodes and wires as edges, making GNNs useful for learning circuit structure representations. For
462   instance, TAG (Zhu et al., 2022) use GNNs for analog and mixed-signal circuit representation, aiding
463   in layout matching prediction and wirelength estimation. ABGNN (He et al., 2021) is used for digital
464   circuit representation and arithmetic block identification.
465   In addition to circuit structure learning, recent studies have increasingly focused on learning
466   functionality-aware circuit representations. For instance, FGNN (Wang et al., 2022) uses contrastive
467   learning to differentiate between functionally equivalent and inequivalent circuits. DeepGate fam-
468   ily Li et al. (2022); Shi et al. (2023; 2024) is also a pioneering approach, which transforms circuit
469   netlists into And-Inverter Graphs (AIGs) and uses node logic-1 probability or truth table similarity as
470   training supervision. However, these methods can only handle combinational circuits, falling short
471
      in representing sequential circuits, which are more common in the real world. Moreover, although
      these representations in netlist level are useful in tasks like logic synthesis and PPA prediction, they
472
      make little contribution in the front end of circuit design, which needs a representation in RTL level.
473
      Fang et al. (2023) tries to construct a pre-synthesis PPA estimation framework, but its representation
474   is between netlist level and RTL level, not an authentic RTL format.
475
476   Our work focus on learning dynamic representations of RTL hardware designs with sequential cir-
477
      cuit behaviors. The most similar work to ours is Design2Vec (Vasudevan et al., 2021), which also
      attempts to predict the status of cover point hits under different test case parameters. However, De-
478
      sign2Vec does not learn a universal representation for circuits and trains a distinct GNN model for
479
      each design. Besides, the statement-level CDFG used in Design2Vec is code-oriented, where each
480   node in the graph represents a statement. While this approach effectively captures RTL code se-
481   mantics, it cannot clearly reflects dynamic circuit data flow. DeepSeq (Khan et al., 2024) is another
482   attempt learning sequential circuits. It focues on AIG at netlist level and predicts the logic-1 and
483   state transition probability under random workload. Without considering the different circuit behav-
484   iors under different inputs, its representation significantly lacks dynamic information compared to
485   ours. Meanwhile, our work learns circuits in RTL level, providing a more high-level graph compared
      to netlist, which makes our model more suitable for dynamic representation.


                                                         9
486
      Learning representation for software program: Recent studies have increasingly applied ma-
487   chine learning to software code representation (Allamanis et al., 2018). A subset of these studies
488   also focus on understanding dynamic behavior of programs, a concept known as learning to exe-
489   cute. Zaremba & Sutskever (2014) firstly uses RNN to read the program character-by-character
490   and computes the program’s output. Bieber et al. (2020) transforms programs into statement-level
491   control flow graphs, employing a custom IPA-GNN for output prediction. Shi et al. (2019) focuses
492   on assembly code, converting it into instruction-level control flow graphs and using GNN to predict
493   the next execution branch and prefetch address.
494   Compared to hardware description code, software programs predominantly follow a serial execution
495   logic, executing one statement at a time. Differently, hardware code represents concurrent data flows
496   between hardware components, signifying a fundamental divergence from software code. Further-
497   more, the same circuit code is executed in each clock cycle, leading to dynamic behaviors (i.e., state
498   transition space) that could have a much deeper depth than software. This makes learning dynamic
499   hardware representation more challenging than software.
500
501   7   C ONCLUSION
502
503
      We present an approach that is able to learn representations for digital circuits to predict their dy-
504   namic behaviors. For the first time, we demonstrate the ability of deep learning in capturing the
505   complex temporal logic in sequential circuits. We learn the RTL representations which can gener-
506   alize across different designs and different tasks. Since DynamicRTL learns over the RTL CDFG,
507   it can also potentially generalize to other dynamic tasks in RTL level such as debugging, model
508   checking and test case generation. More broadly, this work shows the power and potential of deep
509   learning to effectively integrate with hardware domain, which facilitates a high-level understanding
510   of circuits and promotes more efficient circuit design processes.
