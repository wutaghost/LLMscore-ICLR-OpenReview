009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            ‚Ąď0 constrained optimization is widely used in machine learning, especially for
012            high-dimensional problems, as it effectively promotes sparse learning. A promi-
013            nent technique for solving these problems is hard-thresholding gradient descent.
014            However, the inherent expansibility of hard-thresholding operators can lead to
015            convergence issues, necessitating strategies to accelerate the algorithm. In this
016            article, we believe the random hard-thresholding algorithm can be interpreted as
017            an equivalent biased gradient algorithm. By introducing appropriate biases, we
018
               can mitigate some of the issues of hard-thresholding and enhance convergence.
               We categorize the biases into memory-biased and recursive-biased, examining
019
               their distinct applications within hard-thresholding algorithms. Next, we explore
020
               the zeroth-order versions of these algorithms, which introduce additional biases
021            from zeroth-order gradients. Our findings indicate that recursively bias effec-
022            tively counteracts some of the issues caused by hard-thresholding, resulting in
023            improved performance for first-order algorithms. Conversely, due to the accumu-
024            lation of errors from zeroth-order gradients during recursive bias, the performance
025            of zeroth-order algorithms is inferior to that influenced by historical gradients. To
026            address these insights, we propose the SARAHT and BVR-SZHT algorithms for
027            first-order and zeroth-order hard-thresholding, respectively, both of which demon-
028            strate faster convergence speeds compared to previous methods. We validate our
029
               hypotheses through black-box adversarial experiments and ridge regression eval-
               uations.

      ‚Ąď0 constrained optimization is a fundamental technique in large-scale machine learning, especially
035
      in high-dimensional settings where sparsity is crucial (Fan & Li, 2001; Zhang, 2010). It promotes
036
      sparse learning, offering benefits like reduced memory usage, lower computational costs, and im-
037   proved efficiency. In this study, we address the following problem:

056   Hard-thresholding was first used for its full gradient form (Jain et al., 2014). (Nguyen et al.,
057   2017) developed a stochastic gradient descent Stochastic Gradient Descent(SGD) version of hard-
058
      thresholding known as StoIHT. Nevertheless, StoIHT‚Äôs convergence condition is overly stringent for
      practical applications (Li et al., 2016). To address this issue, (Zhou et al., 2018), (Shen & Li, 2017)
059
      and (Li et al., 2016) implemented variance reduction techniques to improve the performance of
060
      StoIHT in real-world problem-solving. Furthermore, (de Vazelhes et al., 2022) designed the stochas-
061   tic zeroth-order hard-thresholding algorithm and found that the expansion of hard-thresholding gra-
062   dients and the errors in zeroth-order gradients can create a kind of antagonism, causing the algorithm
063   to struggle with convergence. (Yuan et al., 2024) found that reducing the variance could help miti-
064   gate this conflict.
065
      In previous works, the gradient oracle process and the hard-thresholding iterative process were
066
      treated separately, without examining their interrelationship in influencing algorithm convergence.
067   In this paper, We view the stochastic gradient decent step and the hard-thresholding step as a whole
068   and consider them as an equivalent gradient ‚ąátHT = (xt ‚ąí xt+1 )/ő∑. This approach enables us to
069   reinterpret the hard-thresholding algorithm as a specific type of biased gradient algorithm. By do-
070   ing so, we uncover the potential to enhance convergence by designing appropriate biased gradient
071   oracles.
072
      Recently, there has been increasing interest in SGD using biased gradient oracles, which has been
073   explored in various studies across multiple domains. A notable example includes zeroth-order meth-
074   ods, such as in optimizing black-box functions (Nesterov & Spokoiny, 2017) or in generating adver-
075   sarial examples in deep learning (Moosavi-Dezfooli et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2017). Many zeroth-
076   order training techniques leverage biased gradient oracles (Liu et al., 2018; Bergou et al., 2020), and
077   biased estimators can outperform their unbiased counterparts in specific contexts (Beznosikov et al.,
078   2020).
079   Actually, there has been a recent surge of interest in SGD with biased gradient oracles, which has
080   been studied in several papers and applied in different domains. A typical example is zeroth-order
081   methods, which are often utilized when there is no access to unbiased gradients, e.g., for optimiza-
082   tion of black-box functions (Nesterov & Spokoiny, 2017) or for finding adversarial examples in deep
083   learning (Moosavi-Dezfooli et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2017). Many zeroth-order training methods ex-
084   ploit biased gradient oracles (Liu et al., 2018; Bergou et al., 2020). Moreover, biased estimators
085   may show better performance over their unbiased equivalents in certain settings (Beznosikov et al.,
086   2020). This raises some interesting questions:
087
088     In algorithms that utilize multiple biased gradient oracles, how do these biases interact?
089     More specifically, how do they affect the hard-thresholding algorithm when viewed as an equiv-
090     alent biased algorithm?
091
092
093   In this paper, we investigate how appropriate biases can mitigate the challenges posed by hard-
094   thresholding and enhance convergence. We categorize these biases into memory-biased and
095   recursive-biased, examining their distinct applications within hard-thresholding algorithms. Ad-
096
      ditionally, we explore the zeroth-order versions of these algorithms, which introduce further bi-
      ases from zeroth-order gradients. Our findings indicate that recursively bias effectively counteracts
097
      some issues caused by hard-thresholding, leading to improved performance in first-order algorithms.
098
      However, the accumulation of errors from zeroth-order gradients during recursively bias results in
099   inferior performance compared to historical gradients. To address these insights, we propose the
100   SARAH-HT and BVR-SZHT algorithms for first-order and zeroth-order hard-thresholding, respec-
101   tively, both demonstrating faster convergence speeds compared to previous methods. We validate
102   our hypotheses through black-box adversarial experiments and ridge regression evaluations, provid-
103   ing a thorough examination of the effects of multiple biases on convergence and their integration
104   with the hard-thresholding operator.

1452   like scRNA-seq data. In our work, we applied several feature selection algorithms, BSVRG-HT,

1454   biological signals. SAGA-LASSO, a popular approach for sparse logistic regression, uses the L1
1455   penalty to encourage sparsity while leveraging stochastic optimization to solve large-scale problems
1456   efficiently. We conducted feature selection on scRNA-seq data and MINST/CIFAR-10 datasets from
1457   colorectal cancer cell lines. Following feature selection, we trained a deep neural network (DNN)
