008                                               A BSTRACT
009
010            When selecting data to build machine learning models in practical applications,
011            factors such as availability, acquisition cost, and discriminatory power are crucial
012
               considerations. Different data modalities often capture unique aspects of the un-
               derlying phenomenon, making their utilities complementary. On the other hand,
013
               some sources of data host structural information that is key to their value. Hence,
014
               the utility of one data type can sometimes be enhanced by matching the struc-
015            ture of another. We propose Multimodal Structure Preservation Learning (MSPL)
016            as a novel method of learning data representations that leverages the clustering
017            structure provided by one data modality to enhance the utility of data from an-
018            other modality. We demonstrate the effectiveness of MSPL in uncovering latent
019            structures in synthetic time series data and recovering clusters from whole genome
020            sequencing and antimicrobial resistance data using mass spectrometry data in sup-
021            port of epidemiology applications. The results show that MSPL can imbue the
022            learned features with external structures and help reap the beneficial synergies
023
               occurring across disparate data modalities.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Selecting the appropriate data is critical when deploying machine learning models in real-world
028
      applications. Factors such as availability, acquisition cost, and discriminatory power (reflected by
029
      information density, resolution, etc.) are of primary concern. On the other hand, distinct data
030   modalities may encode different information about the same underlying phenomenon (Xu et al.,
031   2013; Baltrušaitis et al., 2018), thus forming a gap in utility. For instance, in medical diagnostics,
032   imaging data such as X-ray and CT scans reveal structural anomalies, while genomic sequencing
033   offers insights into the molecular mechanisms of diseases (Esteva et al., 2019).
034
      Traditionally, research in multimodal machine learning attempts to bridge this gap by learning a
035
      shared feature space between data modalities (Li et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b;a). Deviating from
036   these feature-level alignment approaches that require complete data in two modalities, we approach
037   the problem from structure-level alignment: In scenarios such as clustering, it is the structure of
038   the data that directly influences the results. Thus, learning representations of one data modality that
039   preserve the structure of another can extend the utility of the former and effectively bridge their gap.
040
      For instance, in hospital outbreak investigations, epidemiologists use the single nucleotide poly-
041
      morphism (SNP) distance defined on whole genome sequencing (WGS) data to cluster microbial
042   samples, assess their lineages, and identify outbreaks. WGS provides the highest discriminatory
043   power and is considered the gold standard in microbial disease epidemiology (Bertelli & Greub,
044   2013). However, the labor, cost, and expertise required for WGS make it prohibitive to deploy
045   broadly (Rossen et al., 2018). On the other hand, due to its low cost and rapid time to generate
046   results, Matrix-Assisted Laser Desorption Ionization–Time of Flight (MALDI-TOF) mass spec-
047   trometry rose as a standard tool for microbial species identification in clinical microbiology lab-
048   oratories (Croxatto et al., 2012; Clark et al., 2013). Though MALDI has weaker discriminatory
049   power than WGS, it is gaining attention as a potential cost-effective alternative to WGS for hos-
050
      pital outbreak detection (Griffin et al., 2012). Hence, if MALDI representations that preserve the
      SNP distance structure can be learned, its utility can extend from species identification to outbreak
051
      detection, making it a viable substitute for WGS in practice.
052
053   To achieve the aforementioned goals, we propose a novel machine learning framework called Mul-
      timodal Structure Preservation Learning (MSPL) that learns data representations that enhance


                                                         1

054
      the utility of one data modality through alignment with the dissimilarity-based clustering structure
055   provided by another data modality. We first demonstrate the effectiveness of MSPL in identifying
056   latent structures on a synthetic time series dataset. We then apply MSPL to epidemiology settings,
057   where we enhance the utility of MALDI mass spectrometry by leveraging the clustering structure
058   in whole genome sequencing (WGS) and antimicrobial resistance (AMR) data, respectively. Our
059   results demonstrate that MSPL can effectively inject structural information of one modality into
060   the representations of another, improve the clustering performance, bridge the utility gap of two
061   modalities, substantially reduce data acquisition cost, and increase feasibility of learning.

063   2     R ELATED WORK
064
065   Multimodal connectors. In multimodal machine learning models, connectors are employed to
066   bridge the gap between different modalities in the feature space. In vision-language pre-training, Li
067   et al. (2023) proposed the Querying Transformer (Q-Former), a connector that learns query vectors
068   to extract the visual features most relevant to the text. The Q-Former architecture has also been
069   adopted to align time series and text features (Cai et al., 2023). Llava (Liu et al., 2024b), a pioneering
070   work in visual instruction fine-tuning, introduced a more lightweight connector using just a linear
071
      projection that projects image features onto the word embedding space. Liu et al. (2024a) later
      improved Llava’s multimodal capabilities by changing the linear projection connector to a two-layer
072
      multilayer perceptron (MLP), which affords more representation power. However, Qi et al. (2024)
073
      found that multimodal connectors can be performance bottlenecks for multimodal large language
074   models and may fall short with insufficient training data compared to the amount of pre-training
075   data. They proposed to enhance the connector with retrieval-augmented tag tokens that contain rich
076   object-aware information.
077
      Structure in multimodal self-supervised learning. Pre-training approaches in multimodal self-
078
      supervised learning, e.g., CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), commonly employ an instance-wise con-
079
      trastive objective (Oord et al., 2018) to learn joint features. However, the contrastive objective
080   ignores the underlying semantic structure across samples (Zellers et al., 2021; Singh et al., 2022),
081   and thus may adversely impact model performance. To remedy this issue, Chen et al. (2021) pro-
082   posed to combine the contrastive objective with a joint multimodal clustering objective to capture the
083   cross-modal semantic similarity structure. Alternatively, Swetha et al. (2023) preserved modality-
084   specific relationships in the joint embedding space by learning semantically meaningful “anchors”
085   and representing inter-sample relations with sample-anchor relations.
086   Deep learning for MALDI spectrometry. Deep learning is widely applied to MALDI spectra
087   analysis. Weis et al. (2022) used an MLP to encode MALDI spectra of bacterial strains and predict
088   their antimicrobial resistance to a range of drugs. Normand et al. (2022) utilized a 1-D CNN to
089   identify a subpopulation from MALDI data of the same species. Abdelmoula et al. (2021) employed
090   a variational autoencoder to learn low-dimensional latent features of MALDI spectra that reveal
091   biologically relevant clusters of tumor regions.

111   Formally, the input data to MSPL is x with batch size N . An autoencoder consisting of an encoder

      Through MSPL, we aim to learn input data representations h that retain discriminatory power on
      the pretext task while preserving the clustering structure characterized by the external dissimilarity
140
      matrix d. For example, we learn representations of MALDI spectra that readily identify species (a
141
      pretext task) while their pairwise distances recover the clusters defined by the dissimilarity among
142   WGS or AMR data.
143
144   Baseline models. In comparison with MSPL, we develop two baseline models. The first model,
145   named “onlyCLS,” constructs an ablation study by removing the structure preservation objective,
146   i.e., Lstruct , from the MSPL loss. Hence, onlyCLS can only rely on the feature extraction capabil-
147   ities of the autoencoder and the discriminatory power of the pretext task to implicitly recover the
148   clustering structure of another modality.
149
      The second model, named “clusCLS,” formulates structure preservation through classification.
150   Specifically, the ground truth cluster labels CT are derived beforehand using the external dissimilar-
151   ity matrix d on the full dataset. Then, an additional classification head CLSC (·)is used to classify
152   these cluster labels:
153                                              zc = CLSC (h∥z),                                         (9)
154   where “∥” stands for the concatenation operation, and h and z are the learned features and pretext
155   classification logits as computed in Eqs. 3 and 4, respectively. clusCLS also replaces Lstruct with the
156   following cross-entropy loss:
157                                           LstructCLS = CE(zc , CT ).                                 (10)

211   Synth-TS. We construct a synthetic time series dataset called Synth-TS to demonstrate the ability
212   of MSPL to learn representations that preserve external structure. Each time series in Synth-TS is a
213   superposition of three components: (1) a seasonal component consisting of full-wave rectified sine
214   waves (i.e., the absolute value of a sine wave) or triangle waves, (2) a trend component consisting
215   of a linearly increasing or declining trend, and (3) a Gaussian noise component. Each time series is
      parameterized by the frequency f of the seasonal component and the slope k of the trend component.


                                                                4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      As shown in Figure 2, (f, k) are jointly sampled from a two-dimensional Gaussian distribution. In
217   Synth-TS, we construct a grid of such two-dimensional Gaussians and randomly generate multiple
218   time series from each Gaussian. Formally, the dataset Synth-TS(m, n) generates 2n samples from
219   each Gaussian in a m×m grid, with n samples having a sine wave component and n samples having
220   a triangle wave component.
221
      We define the pretext task as the binary classification of seasonal components (sine or triangle
222
      waves). The MSPL framework learns representations of the time series such that their pairwise
223   distances match the external pairwise dissimilarity, which we defined as the Euclidean distance be-
224   tween their respective parameters (f, k). This encourages the time series to cluster according to the
225   Gaussian distributions generating their parameters. More details about the construction of Synth-TS
226   can be found in Appendix A.
227
228   Proprietary dataset. The proprietary dataset consists of 1862 bacterial samples with MALDI
229   spectra spanning 42 species with corresponding WGS information from a single hospital. Though
230   the raw WGS data were unavailable, we have access to the pairwise dissimilarity of this data, mea-
231   sured by SNP distance. In our framework, species identification from MALDI spectra forms the
232   pretext classification task, and the SNP distances define the external dissimilarity matrix d (Eq. 7).
233   In bacterial outbreak investigations, SNP distances lower than a pre-defined threshold indicate
234   closely related or nearly identical strains that may form an outbreak cluster (Guerra-Assunção et al.,
235   2015; Hatherell et al., 2016). In our experiments, the threshold is set to 15 as used by epidemiolo-
236   gists (Xiao et al., 2024). To derive the ground truth outbreak clusters from the SNP distances, we
237   apply hierarchical clustering with complete linkage on the full SNP distance matrix, using 15 as the
238   distance threshold.

254   DRIAMS data. DRIAMS (Weis et al., 2022) is a public dataset with paired MALDI spectra and
255   antimicrobial resistance (AMR) profiles. In our experiments, we use the DRIAMS-B and DRIAMS-
256   C subsets, which consist of 10404 bacterial samples with MALDI spectra spanning 251 species. As
257   above, we define species identification from MALDI spectra as the pretext classification task.
258
      The AMR profile of each bacterial sample documents its resistance to various antibiotics. We utilize
259   the AMR profiles against 33 shared drugs recorded in both DRIAMS subsets. We preprocess the
260   AMR profiles to construct the external dissimilarity matrix d, where each entry is an integer ranging
261   from 0 to 33. Details about the AMR profile preprocessing can be found in Appendix B.
262
      To derive ground truth outbreak clusters from the AMR dissimilarity matrix, we apply hierarchi-
263
      cal clustering with varying distance thresholds from 1 to 33. The optimal threshold—chosen to
264
      maximize the number of non-singleton clusters—is set to 10 for ground truth generation.

444   6     D ISCUSSION
445
446   We introduced Multimodal Structure Preservation Learning (MSPL), a method designed to enhance
447   the utility of a data modality by learning representations that preserve external clustering structures
448   from another modality. Through empirical evaluation, we demonstrate that MSPL can effectively
449   capture and preserve latent structures in synthetic time series, whole genome sequencing (WGS),
450   and antimicrobial resistance (AMR) data. Additionally, MSPL offers two advantages: it excels in
451   preserving highly diverse substructures and remains robust to the difficulty of the pretext task. Our
452   approach is a novel addition to the family of multimodal machine learning techniques in that it
453
      aggregates information across different forms of its representation, even though the underlying form
      of data may originally be transactional in both sources. It can be useful in applications where a
454
      pattern structure present in some data sources can support models trained on other data sources.

469   Limitations. In our performance evaluation (Table 1), we observed that MSPL yields low NMI
470   scores on both the proprietary dataset and DRIAMS when clustering the learned features using a
471   distance threshold (MSPLthr ). However, when constraining the number of clusters (MSPLnum ), the
472   NMI score improves and surpasses the baseline, albeit resulting in a drop in the F1 score. Addition-
473   ally, for both clustering schemes, the ARI remains consistently low. While NMI can be inadequate
474   when comparing predictions to a large number of clusters (Amelio & Pizzuti, 2015), and ARI is
475
      more suitable for comparisons involving large, equally sized clusters (Romano et al., 2016), the low
      NMI and ARI scores indicate a potential limitation of the current MSPL framework in handling
476
      imbalanced cluster distributions, as observed in the proprietary dataset (Figure 6). Moreover, in
477
      real-world clinical applications (e.g., outbreak detection), the number of clusters is often unknown,
478   making it infeasible to apply the MSPLnum model.
479
480   Future work. Given the limitations of the current MSPL framework, future research will focus
481   on improving it in the face of cluster imbalance. Specifically, we plan to enhance the structure
482   preservation objective (Lstruct ) by incorporating additional supervision that encourages MSPL to
483   accurately estimate the number of ground truth clusters as well as their distribution. We will also
484   experiment with more than one source of structural information (e.g., including WGS and AMR
485   simultaneously in support of learning on MALDI data), and consider other than clustering structures
      as sources of predictive information for associated tasks.
