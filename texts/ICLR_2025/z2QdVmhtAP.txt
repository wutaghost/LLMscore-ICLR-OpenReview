009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Reconstructing visual images from fMRI data presents a challenging task, par-
012            ticularly when dealing with limited data and compute availability. This work
013            introduces a novel approach to fMRI-based visual image reconstruction using
014            a subject-agnostic common representation space. We show that subjects’ brain
015            signals naturally align in this common space during training, without the need
016            for explicit alignment. This is leveraged to demonstrate that aligning subject-
017            specific lightweight modules to a reference subject is significantly more efficient
018
               than traditional end-to-end training methods. Our approach excels in low-data
               scenarios, where training these modules with limited data achieves faster and better
019
               performance. We also introduce a novel method to select the most representative
020
               subset of images for a new subject, allowing for fine-tuning with 40% less data
021            while maintaining performance. These advancements make fMRI data collection
022            more efficient and practical, reducing the burden on subjects and improving the
023            generalization of fMRI reconstruction models.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Over the past several decades, the use of machine learning techniques has enabled decoding and/or
029   reconstruction of information represented in neural activation patterns measured in awake, behaving
030   humans. Because the neural representations supporting behavior likely entail patterns of activity
031   across large swaths of tissue, these approaches are thought to offer a key step towards characterizing
032   and quantifying the computational principles brains use to support cognition (Naselaris et al., 2011).
033   Early approaches, including self-supervised learning frameworks (Beliy et al., 2019; Gaziv et al.,
034   2022; Mozafari et al., 2020; Shen et al., 2019; Seeliger et al., 2018; Ren et al., 2021) struggled
035   to generate semantically accurate image reconstructions, often resulting in images with limited
036   resemblance to the original stimuli. Furthermore, these methods required large amounts of data for
037   training, and their generalization to a wider population of subjects was severely limited. Significant
038   advancements were achieved by incorporating multimodal data, such as text (Lin et al., 2022), and
039
      later by leveraging the generative power of latent diffusion models (LDMs) (Takagi & Nishimoto,
      2023; Scotti et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2024). These models introduced novel mapping
040
      techniques to project fMRI signals into the input space of LDMs, yielding higher-quality natural
041
      images. However, despite the improved fidelity, the issue of generalization to new participants
042   persisted. In response, Scotti et al. (2024) proposed projecting fMRI data into a shared representation
043   space, demonstrating significant improvements in both performance and generalizability.
044
045
      Beyond being able to use decoding and reconstruction approaches to provide evidence of the existence
      of information represented in a particular brain activation pattern, an additional goal is to compare
046
      decoding or reconstruction performance across experimental manipulations, such as cued visual
047
      attention (Serences & Boynton, 2007; Kamitani & Tong, 2005; Scolari et al., 2012; Sprague &
048   Serences, 2013; Itthipuripat et al., 2019; Sprague et al., 2018) and visual working memory (Serences
049   et al., 2009; Harrison & Tong, 2009; Ester et al., 2013; Sprague et al., 2014; Christophel et al., 2012;
050   Li et al., 2021) Ḣowever, for these approaches to be useful for a cognitive neuroscience lab, the lab
051   would need to acquire sufficient data to train a decoding or reconstruction model for each individual
052   participant (often >20 per study to attain statistical power). The state-of-the-art methods described
053   above use extremely massive datasets with dozens of hours of fMRI data per participant, which is not
      feasible in almost all cases. Thus, there is a clear need to maximize the efficiency and generalizability


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      of image reconstruction methods such that a model for a new participant can be trained using as little
055   data as possible.
056
057   In this work, we introduce an efficient and generalizable approach to fMRI-based visual reconstruc-
058
      tion that leverages a subject-agnostic common representation space. Unlike previous approaches that
      perform end-to-end training of fMRI signals to mapped contextual embeddings, we propose aligning
059
      brain activity from new subjects to a pre-trained reference subject in a shared representation space us-
060
      ing Adapter Alignment (AA) training. Throughout this work, we refer to lightweight subject-specific
061   modules designed to map input fMRI signals to a shared representation space as "adapters"(Liu
062   et al., 2024). These modules are implemented as single-layer neural networks. In their linear form,
063   adapters consist of a single linear transformation layer, while in the non-linear form, they include a
064   linear layer followed by a non-linear activation function such as GELU (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2023).
065   Using AA training, we significantly reduce the amount of fMRI data required to achieve accurate
066   reconstructions and improve the reconstruction quality. Our method achieves better performance to
067   existing state-of-the-art models, but with far fewer training hours, making it feasible for cognitive
068   neuroscience labs with limited data collection resources. We build on this approach by also proposing
069
      an adaptive image selection strategy, allowing for optimal fine-tuning of new subjects with minimal
      data, further improving the practicality and accessibility of fMRI-based image reconstruction.
070
071   Our contributions to this work are threefold:
072
073          • We provide strong evidence for the existence of a common visual representation space across
074            subjects, where brain activity from different individuals can be aligned in a shared space. By
075
               extracting and analyzing embeddings from a pre-trained model, we show that even without
               explicit alignment, subject-specific fMRI signals, when mapped to a common space display
076
               similar behavior and representation structures. Furthermore, we demonstrate that applying a
077
               simple orthogonal transformation improves the alignment of embeddings between subjects,
078            and using non-linear adapters with explicit mapping leads to almost perfect alignment across
079            subjects in the common space. (Section 2)
080
081
             • We introduce Adapter Alignment (AA), a novel approach to facilitate efficient subject-
               specific alignment in fMRI-based visual reconstruction. Our training pipeline first pre-trains
082
               on a reference subject, then aligns new subjects’ brain data using lightweight adapters that
083
               map them to the shared common space. This process, which is more efficient than traditional
084            end-to-end training, enables faster convergence and better performance, particularly in
085            low-data scenarios. By incorporating MSE losses at the adapter level for common images,
086            we show that our method not only reduces training time but also improves generalization
087            and reconstruction quality. (Section 3)
088
             • We develop a greedy image selection algorithm that identifies the most representative subset
089
               of images for training new subjects. By efficiently selecting fMRI signals that capture the
090            most informative aspects of the common space, this strategy significantly reduces the amount
091            of data required for fine-tuning without sacrificing performance (from a baseline of 250
092            images to 150 images). This makes our method more practical for real-world applications,
093            where acquiring large fMRI datasets is often prohibitively expensive. (Section 4)

      2    A C OMMON V ISUAL R EPRESENTATION S PACE
097
098   Central to our approach is the hypothesis that each individual’s brain (or at least the tissue involved in
099
      processing static visual images) can be considered an instantiation of a ’common’ brain, with activity
      patterns varying along similar dimension in a common shared representational space. Aspects of
100
      this hypothesis are already strongly supported: most human brains have localized tissue responsive
101
      to specific types of stimuli, like faces and scenes. Additionally, several continuous dimensions of
102   high-level visual selectivity have been identified across the ventral temporal cortex (Huth et al., 2012),
103   including dimensions like animate/inanimate, large/small (based on real-world size) (Long et al.,
104   2018), and spiky/round (Bao et al., 2020). Retinotopic organization is another example of a shared
105   representational space, as each individual brain instantiates a unique anatomical mapping from retinal
106   coordinates to cortical representations, and adaptive models can be estimated to transform activation
107   patterns from individual brains to this common shared retinotopic coordinate system (Sprague &
      Serences, 2013; Wandell & Winawer, 2015). We analyze the similarity of representation spaces

121   2.1   E MERGENCE OF C OMMON C ONCEPTS
122
123   The existence of a semantic common representation space is suggested from the emergence of
124   semantic categories in this space. For this, we extracted the most significant left singular vectors from
125   the ridge regression weight matrix. This matrix was extracted from the pre-trained MindEye2 (Scotti
126
      et al., 2024) model which was trained using the 1024-dimensional common space. Subsequently, we
      projected the 1024-dimensional embeddings of each image onto these vectors and sampled from the
127
      two extremes (set 1 and set 3) and the middle (set 2). In order to obtain an objective classification,
128
      we presented these sets of images to GPT-4o (Achiam et al., 2023) and asked it to find the semantic
129   categories that are well represented in set 1, somewhat represented in set 2 and not represented in
130   set 3. Results for the first five singular vectors are shown in Table 1. As is evident, images in the
131   principal dimensions of the representation space have a common theme.
132
      Previous studies, such as those by Huth et al. (2012), have indicated that animal categories, including
133
      humans, are represented distinctly from non-animal categories within the brain. In our study, we
134
      observed that the common representational space also preserves these semantic properties. As
135   demonstrated in Figure 1, after projecting the fMRI signals of Subject 2 into the common space
136   and onto the first two singular vectors, we can see the animal vs. non-animal distinction, consistent
137   with previous hypotheses. Extending this analysis across additional dimensions, we observed similar
138   separations between other hypothesized semantic categories of "Social interaction," "man-made vs
139   nature," and "biological vs non-biological."

       2.2          S IMILARITY IN R EPRESENTATION S TRUCTURE ACROSS S UBJECTS
163
164    Existing works, such as MindEye2 (Scotti et al., 2024), employ a simple linear adapter network
165    to project subject-specific fMRI signals into a common representation space. A key indication of
166    the existence of such a shared space is the structural similarity of embeddings across subjects. To
167    explore this, we first use a pre-trained MindEye2 model to extract common-space embeddings for the
168    shared images (the test set in MindEye2) across all subjects. Initial cosine similarity between the
169    embeddings from different subjects is low. However, after applying an orthogonal transformation
170
       using Procrustes analysis (Schönemann, 1966), the cosine similarity improves, suggesting that the
       embeddings can be structurally aligned. Despite this improvement, the alignment is not perfect, likely
171
       due to the limitations of linear transformations.

204    3           F MRI R ECONSTRUCTION
205
206    Building a common brain representation requires constructing an efficient fMRI reconstruction
207    pipeline. Our approach is guided by two primary objectives: 1) achieving competitive reconstruction
208    performance and 2) prioritizing efficiency over marginal performance gains. We hypothesize that
209    it is possible to pre-train a single reference brain and align subsequent subjects to this reference
210    using subject-specific adapters, significantly reducing the computational cost while maintaining high
211    reconstruction quality.
212
213    3.1          DATASET
214
215    We use the Natural Scenes Dataset (NSD) (Allen et al., 2022) but apply a different split compared to
       the traditional setup. Typically, common images across subjects are assigned to the test set, while the


                                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      training set consists of unique images viewed by each subject. However, for effective Common Space
217   Alignment, it is critical to have a one-to-one mapping of images across subjects. To achieve this, we
218   incorporate the common images into the training set and swap an equal amount of training images to
219   the test sets. This means that every subject now has a unique randomly sampled set of images on
220   which they are tested. We present the performance of the original training method and the proposed
221   technique on the new split to ensure a fair comparison across subjects and alignment techniques.
222
      Our initial experiments revealed that models performed slightly worse on the unique image test
223   set than on the common image test set, suggesting that the unique image test set presents a more
224   challenging benchmark. Similar to prior work, we focus on subjects 1, 2, 5, and 7, as they have
225   complete 40-hour fMRI datasets available in NSD, enabling better reconstruction performance. These
226   subjects provide robust data for training and evaluation, allowing us to validate our alignment and
227   reconstruction methods comprehensively.

      Current approaches for training multi-subject pipelines with a common representation space typically
258   follow an end-to-end strategy, where fMRI inputs are processed by the model and the loss is computed
259   at the final layer to minimize the discrepancy between the ground-truth and predicted embeddings.
260   In this setup, the common space emerges organically during training without explicit constraints.
261   However, this approach has two key drawbacks: (1) it requires extended training to form a satisfactory
262   common space and achieve convergence, and (2) the subject-specific embeddings in the common
263   space are not strongly aligned, making it harder for the residual MLP to map multiple subject inputs
264   to a unified output.
265   To address these challenges, we propose Adapter Alignment (AA) training. In AA, the pipeline is
266   first pre-trained end-to-end on a single reference subject, chosen based on reconstruction performance
267   and data availability. Among NSD subjects, Subject 1 consistently exhibited the best reconstruction
268   performance, making it the ideal candidate for constructing the reference space. After pre-training,
269   the output embeddings for the subject-specific adapter, particularly those corresponding to the shared
      images across subjects, are extracted.


                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      When fine-tuning a new subject, we adopt a two-step process. First, we train the new subject’s adapter
271   using the shared images, with the objective of aligning its output to the embeddings of Subject 1 for
272   the same images. The adapter is allowed to overfit on these shared images until the loss is minimized.
273   In the second step, we unfreeze the entire pipeline and resume training, applying the usual losses
274   at the final output. Additionally, we introduce an MSE loss at the adapter level specifically for
275   the common images, which allows the adapter to restructure itself to account for subject-specific
276   discrepancies and adapt to the unique images encountered by the new subject. We call this process of
277   overfitting the adapter and letting it fine-tune end to end "AAMax".
278   This training method offers improvements in both high-data and low-data scenarios. When all
279   training data is available, AAMax converges faster while achieving performance similar to traditional
280   end-to-end training. However, in limited data settings, AAMax proves significantly more efficient,
281   yielding better reconstructions and higher accuracy with fewer training samples.

      Our reconstruction pipeline follows a similar structure to previous works, dividing the reconstruction
304
      process into two components: a high-level semantic pipeline that maps to CLIP embeddings and
305
      a low-level visual pipeline that maps to visual autoencoder embeddings. Both pipelines receive
306   the same fMRI data as input but are trained separately. The outputs from both pipelines are then
307   integrated into a pre-trained diffusion model to generate the final reconstruction.
308
309
      Flattened fMRI voxels from the nsdgeneral region are fed into a subject-specific adapter, consisting of
      a single linear layer with a GELU non-linearity. This step maps the fMRI data into a subject-agnostic
310
      shared space. The shared space representation is then processed by a Residual Multi-Layer Perceptron
311
      (MLP). For mappings into the CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) embedding space, we further incorporate
312   a pre-trained diffusion prior based on DALL-E 2 (Ramesh et al., 2022) to bridge the modality gap
313   between fMRI data and CLIP embeddings.
314
315
      Our high-level pipeline is based on the architecture used in MindEye1 (Scotti et al., 2023) and
      MindEye2 (Scotti et al., 2024), where the fMRI data is mapped to CLIP image embeddings of the
316
      corresponding source images. The pretraining phase leverages a combination of diffusion prior loss
317
      and CLIP loss. During fine-tuning, we add an additional MSE loss for the common images to further
318   refine the mappings. The overall loss function in this stage is:
319
                             λ1 LPrior + λ2 LCLIP ,                         for non-common images
320              Lnew =
321                          λ1 LPrior + λ2 LCLIP + λ3 LMSE (znew , zref ), for common images
322   where zadapter, new and zadapter, ref are the adapter-level embeddings for the new and reference subjects,
323   respectively, on the shared common images. We present a breakdown of the high level pipeline loss
      functions in Appendix H.

      The low-level pipeline also follows a similar structure to the high-level one but maps the fMRI data
325   to latent embeddings derived from a custom autoencoder. This autoencoder is trained on downscaled
326   images from the NSD dataset (64x64x3) and reduces them to a latent space of size 1024. The low-
327   level pipeline learns to map to this latent space, and passing these embeddings through a pre-trained
328   decoder produces blurry reconstructions. The loss function for this stage is:
329
330                   LMSE (zfMRI, new , zAE ),                                         for non-common images
           Lnew =
331                   LMSE (zfMRI, new , zAE ) + LMSE (zadapter, new , zadapter, ref ), for common images

      where zfMRI, new is the output embedding for the new subject, zAE is the AE latent space representation

334   subjects, respectively. We present a breakdown of the low level pipeline loss functions in Appendix I
335
336
      For the final step, the mapped CLIP embedding is used as a conditioning input to the pre-trained
      diffusion prior model. The low-level blurry reconstructions serve as an initialization for the diffusion
337
      model, and the final, refined reconstructions are then produced. For more architectural details, we
338
      refer readers to the MindEye1 (Scotti et al., 2023) and MindEye2 (Scotti et al., 2024)papers.

457   Each image (and its fMRI) maps to exactly one bin in each dimension. Our goal is to find the smallest

464   greedy algorithm chooses image i ̸∈ S that minimizes Gap when added to S.

513   6   C ONCLUSIONS & D ISCUSSION
514
515   In this work, we present compelling evidence for the existence of a shared visual representation space
516   across subjects, allowing brain activity from different individuals to be aligned in a common space.
517   Building on this foundation, we introduce Adapter Alignment (AA), a novel and efficient approach for
518   subject-specific alignment in fMRI-based visual reconstruction. Our method, which is more efficient
519   than traditional end-to-end training pipelines, achieves faster convergence and superior performance,
520   particularly in low-data scenarios. Additionally, we propose a greedy image selection algorithm that
521
      identifies the most representative subset of images for training new subjects. This approach reduces
      the amount of data required for fine-tuning by up to 40% without sacrificing performance, addressing
522
      the challenge of minimizing data requirements in fMRI-based image reconstruction.
523
524   By leveraging the structure of the common representation space, we demonstrate the feasibility of
525   producing high-quality visual reconstructions with a significantly reduced amount of subject-specific
526   data. Future work could explore the integration of non-linear adaptors to enhance concept extraction,
527
      potentially improving the performance and flexibility of the model. Another promising direction
      is to extend our methodology to different pipelines, ensuring its generalizability across various
528
      architectures, thereby making the approach architecture-agnostic. Furthermore, validating the results
529
      using different datasets (Contier et al., 2021) would offer valuable insights into the robustness of the
530   method across diverse data settings.

819   To first investigate the structural alignment of embeddings across subjects, we analyzed the subject-
820   wise cosine similarity of the principal eigenvectors in the embedding spaces produced by two
821   techniques: the standard end-to-end training (Normal) and our proposed approach (AAMax). For
822   both techniques, we extracted the top five principal eigenvectors from the test data for each subject
823
      and computed the cosine similarity between the eigenvectors from different subjects.
824   In normal end-to-end training, we observed consistently low cosine similarity between all but the first
825   principal eigenvector of different subjects, suggesting that the primary directions of variance captured
826   by the embeddings were highly subject-specific and lacked alignment across subjects. This indicates
827   that the embedding space learned in this approach does not generalize well to a common structure,
828   resulting in subject-specific representations that diverge significantly in terms of principal variance.
829   In contrast, AAMax training showed significantly higher cosine similarity between the principal
830   eigenvectors of different subjects. This indicates that AAMax effectively aligns the embeddings
831   across subjects in terms of the primary directions of variance, achieving a shared representation space
832   that better captures common structural features. The high similarity across subjects reflects AAMax’s
833   ability to encode the principal semantic and structural patterns consistently, making it more robust
834   and generalizable compared to the normal end-to-end method.

       E     NP-H ARDNESS P ROOF
973
974
       We provide a formal proof that the bin-mapping problem is NP-hard by reduction from the Set Cover
975
       problem. The proof generalizes to an arbitrary number of bins per dimension and demonstrates the
976    equivalence between a solution to the bin-mapping problem and a solution to the Set Cover problem.
977
978
       E.1   P ROBLEM D EFINITION
979
980    The bin-mapping problem is defined as follows:
981
       Definition 1 (Bin-Mapping Problem). Given a set of n-dimensional vectors, each mapping to one of
982
       m bins per dimension, and a target k, the decision problem asks whether there exists a subset of k
983    vectors that spans all m bins in every dimension.
984
985
       E.2   R EDUCTION FROM S ET C OVER
986
987    We reduce the well-known Set Cover problem, which is NP-hard, to the bin-mapping problem.
988
       Definition 2 (Set Cover Problem). Let U be a finite universe, and let S = {S1 , S2 , . . . , Sl } be a
989                                                                                                     ′
990                       S of U. The Set Cover problem asks whether there exists a subcollection S ⊆ S
       collection of subsets
       of size k such that S∈S ′ S = U.
991
992
       Theorem 3. The bin-mapping problem is NP-hard.
993
994    Proof. We reduce the Set Cover problem to the bin-mapping problem as follows:
995
996           • Let U = {1, 2, . . . , n} represent the universe of elements.
997
998           • For each subset Si ∈ S, construct an n-dimensional vector Vi such that:
999                – For every element d ∈ Si , the vector Vi places d in Bin1 in the corresponding dimen-
1000                 sion.
1001
                   – For all d ∈
                               / Si , the vector Vi places d in Bin2 .
1002
1003          • Add two special vectors N2 and N3 :
1004
1005
                   – N2 covers Bin2 in all dimensions.
1006               – N3 covers Bin3 in all dimensions.
1007
1008   Key Properties of the Construction:
1009
1010         1. Each Vi corresponds to a subset Si ∈ S.
1011
1012         2. Any collection of input subsets S ′ leaves a hole in Bin2 and Bin3 in at least one dimension.
1013         3. The special vectors N2 and N3 are necessary to fill these holes.
1014
1015         4. A set of vectors V ′ ⊆ V forms a bin cover if and only if the corresponding subset S ′ forms a
1016            set cover.
1017
1018   Correctness: A solution to the Set Cover problem maps directly to a solution to the Bin-Mapping
1019   problem:
1020
1021          • If S ′ is a set cover, then S ′ ∪ {N2 , N3 } forms a bin cover.
1022
1023          • Conversely, if V ′ is a bin cover, then V ′ \ {N2 , N3 } corresponds to a set cover.
1024
1025   Since the Set Cover problem is NP-hard, and we have reduced it to the bin-mapping problem, the
       bin-mapping problem is also NP-hard.
