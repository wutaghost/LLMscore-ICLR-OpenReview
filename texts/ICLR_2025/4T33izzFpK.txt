                                             A BSTRACT

            Large Language Models (LLMs) vary in their abilities on a range of tasks. Initia-
            tives such as the Open LLM Leaderboard aim to quantify these differences
            with several large benchmarks (sets of test items to which an LLM can respond
            either correctly or incorrectly). However, high correlations within and between
            benchmark scores suggest that (1) there exists a small set of common underly-
            ing abilities that these benchmarks measure, and (2) items tap into redundant
            information and the benchmarks may thus be considerably compressed. We use
            data from n > 5000 LLMs to identify the most informative items of six bench-
            marks, ARC, GSM8K, HellaSwag, MMLU, TruthfulQA and WinoGrande
            (with d = 28, 632 items in total). From them we distill a sparse benchmark,
            metabench, that has less than 3% of the original size of all six benchmarks com-
            bined. This new sparse benchmark goes beyond point scores by yielding estimators
            of the underlying benchmark-specific abilities. We show that these estimators
            (1) can be used to reconstruct each original individual benchmark score with, on
            average, 1.24% root mean square error (RMSE), (2) reconstruct the original total
            score with 0.58% RMSE, and (3) have a single underlying common factor whose
            Spearman correlation with the total score is r = 0.94.


1       I NTRODUCTION

Large Language Models (LLMs) represent a significant leap forward in our quest to emulate human-
like understanding and generation of language (Brown et al., 2020). These sophisticated algorithms
are designed to process, interpret, and produce language with unprecedented accuracy and fluency
(Bubeck et al., 2023). By training on vast datasets encompassing a myriad of linguistic patterns,
LLMs have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in tasks ranging from simple text completion
(Radford et al., 2019) to complex problem-solving (Chen et al., 2021a) and creative writing (Elkins &
Chun, 2020). Their potential to transform various fields—education (Kasneci et al., 2023), customer
service (Pandya & Holia, 2023), content creation (Zhang et al., 2023), and beyond—has captured
the imagination of researchers and practitioners alike (Bommasani et al., 2021; Bubeck et al., 2023).
However, as these models grow in complexity, so does the challenge of effectively evaluating their
diverse abilities (Burnell et al., 2023; Ke et al., 2023).
Traditionally, research has relied on extensive benchmarks—vast arrays of tasks—to assess the
abilities of LLMs (Srivastava et al., 2023; Raji et al., 2021). However, these benchmarks often measure
overlapping skills, leading to redundancy and inefficiency. This paper introduces metabench, a
sparse benchmark engineered to distill the essence of six prominent benchmarks—ARC (Clark
et al., 2018), GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021), HellaSwag (Zellers et al., 2019), MMLU (Hendrycks
et al., 2021), TruthfulQA (Lin et al., 2022), and WinoGrande (Sakaguchi et al., 2020)—into a more
concise and informative benchmark. By analyzing data from over 5000 LLMs, we identified the most
revealing items within these benchmarks, compressing them to less than 3% of their combined size.
We also provide adaptive testing simulations that suggest that the number of items administered to
LLMs can be lowered even further. This volume reduction greatly enhances evaluation efficiency,
while also maintaining the original benchmarks’ ability to measure underlying abilities.

The metabench framework leverages psychometric techniques (Hambleton & Swaminathan, 2013;
Wang et al., 2023a) to estimate the latent abilities captured by these benchmarks (Burnell et al., 2023).
These estimates can reconstruct individual and total benchmark scores with minimal error, and reveal
a single underlying factor that captures most of their variability. By refining how we measure the
abilities of LLMs, metabench offers a new lens through which we can understand and improve
them. Practitioners can run their own models on this efficient and highly informative benchmark,
obtain approximated scores, and compute an overall estimate of their models’ general ability. We
additionally offer a second, disjoint version of metabench for users that intend to evaluate an
LLM multiple times. Our work underscores the potential for more streamlined and psychologically
meaningful evaluation methods (Coda-Forno et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2023). It is aimed for the open-
source community and independent machine learning researchers with limited resources. All reported
analyses and and results can be viewed on www.github.com/adkipnis/metabench and our
benchmark itself is available on www.huggingface.co/datasets/HCAI/metabench.

1.1   R ELATED W ORK

Recent years have seen significant advancements in the development and evaluation of LLMs,
resulting in an increased need for efficient benchmarking methods due to the expense of running
and querying them. Traditional benchmarks, such as those used in the Open LLM Leaderboard
(Beeching et al., 2023), encompass a wide range of tasks. In the current work, we focus on six
different benchmarks: ARC (AI2 Reasoning Challenge, Clark et al., 2018) was designed to test the
reasoning abilities of AI through a series of challenging science questions. Similarly, GSM8K (Grade
School Math, Cobbe et al., 2021) evaluates the mathematical reasoning capabilities of LLMs by
training verifiers on complex word problems. HellaSwag (Zellers et al., 2019) tests commonsense
reasoning by asking models to choose the most plausible continuation of a story from several options.
MMLU (Massive Multitask Language Understanding, Hendrycks et al., 2021) assesses the breadth
of expert knowledge and reasoning skills across various disciplines, presenting a comprehensive
challenge for LLMs. TruthfulQA (Lin et al., 2022) aims to measure the tendency of language
models to generate truthful responses, tackling the issue of misinformation and model-generated
falsehoods. Finally, WinoGrande (Sakaguchi et al., 2020) tests models on pronoun resolution, a

task that requires understanding context and commonsense reasoning. We use these benchmarks to
distill an informative, sparse set of items to form metabench.
Psychometric methods including Item Response Theory (IRT) are increasingly used in the evaluation
of machine learning models (Martínez-Plumed et al., 2016; Martínez-Plumed et al., 2019; Wang
et al., 2023a). Zhuang and colleagues (Zhuang et al., 2023) propose using IRT for adaptive testing
of LLMs but only used this approach to distinguish between a small set of models and restricted
domains and compared models to human responses. Burden and colleagues (Burden et al., 2023)
use Bayesian probabilistic programming to infer agents’ capabilities from diverse task performances,
building computational models to link task features to system capabilities. However, these methods
are applied to reinforcement learning agents and not to LLMs. There also have been several attempts
to create completely new questionnaires to evaluate LLMs using psychometric tools (Jiang et al.,
2024; Wang et al., 2023b).
Redundancy in benchmark items has been established, analyzed and combated by several recent
works (Rodriguez et al., 2021; Vania et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023c; Ye et al., 2023; Hewitt et al.,
2024). Li et al. (2024) introduce the BenchBuilder pipeline, which filters existing benchmarks to
preserve high-quality items. It is used to create Arena-Auto-Hard, a 500-item benchmark which
can robustly recover the LLM rankings on both the source benchmark (Chatbot Arena, Chiang
et al., 2024) and two other large benchmarks (MT-Bench, Zheng et al., 2023; AlpacaEval 2.0
LC, Taori et al., 2023). BenchBuilder uses LLM annotators to rate benchmark items according
to seven criteria to filter out uninformative items. An LLM judge then evaluates whether an LLM
subject correctly completes the task specified by the item. Our approach filters prompts in a data-
driven manner by first sub-sampling benchmark items and then characterizing item information
using IRT. We only use the distribution of responses to benchmarks across a population of LLMs for
item filtering, rather than using a priori qualities and LLM annotators which could introduce bias.
Similarly to BenchBuilder, our approach is extendable to new benchmarks, provided that they
contain a large number of items and that a population of LLMs have completed them.
Most related to our proposal is recent work by Polo et al. (2024) introducing tinyBenchmarks.
The authors advocate for reducing the number of evaluation examples in benchmarks to lower costs
while maintaining accurate performance assessments of LLMs. They demonstrate that evaluating
LLMs on small curated subsets of benchmarks can reliably estimate the performance on large
benchmarks like MMLU and HELM (Liang et al., 2022) within a 2% mean absolute error. However,
their proposal differs from our approach on several dimensions:

      1. Model Fit: Polo and colleagues analyzed only 395 LLMs with high-dimensional IRT models
         of up to 15-dimensional latent traits. This raises strong doubts about the goodness of fit for
         their IRT models, as the number of estimated parameters is far greater than the number of
         observations (DeMars, 2010; Svetina et al., 2017; Natesan et al., 2016). In comparison, we
         use over 5000 LLMs and select 1-dimensional IRT models based on cross-validation.

      2. Item Selection: The authors base item selection on clustering of the estimated item parame-
         ters, which can lead to a bad coverage of the ability landscape. In comparison, we use an
         information filtering approach which explicitly aims to cover the entire ability landscape.

      3. Use of Latent Abilities: We go beyond point scores by estimating a latent ability per
         benchmark and, importantly, show that they jointly permit better score reconstruction for
         each benchmark’s score and the grand mean. Furthermore, we analyze their latent structure
         using Factor Analysis and show that they are largely governed by a single general ability.

      4. Performance: We halve the score reconstruction error reported in tinyBenchmarks: For
         MMLU resp. Open LLM Leaderboard we reach a mean absolute error of 0.969% resp.
         0.437%.1

2     B ENCHMARK D ISTILLATION

We collect benchmark data for over 5000 LLMs from the Open LLM Leaderboard, and discard
items and models based on simple criteria (Section 2.1), reduce each benchmark to a fixed size using
cross-validated subsampling (Section 2.2), fit IRT models to quantify the properties of each remaining
item (Section 2.3), and finally, we filter out items based on their information content (Section 2.4). We
focus on reconstructing the original scores per benchmark using the reduced sets in a cross-validated
manner (Sections 2.3 - 2.4). Using Factor Analysis, we uncover strong overlap in the latent abilities
measured by each benchmark, and leverage this synergy by combining all benchmarks for score
reconstruction (Section 2.5). We also provide preliminary results in Computerized Adaptive Testing
(Appendix C.1). For practitioners interested in repeated evaluation, we have created a disjoint second
version of metabench that effectively combats over-adaptation to our benchmark (Section 3, see
Appendix B.9 for details).

2.1   DATA C OLLECTION AND P REPROCESSING

We collected openly available item-wise accuracies from Hugging Face Datasets for the six bench-
marks that are part of the Open LLM Leaderboard (Beeching et al., 2023): ARC, GSM8K,
HellaSwag, MMLU, TruthfulQA and WinoGrande. After excluding flagged and merged mod-
els, we obtained data from 1355 unique users, yielding n = 6875 unique LLMs and over 152 million
responses. Out of all LLMs, 5055 have been evaluated on all six benchmarks. Whenever possible,
we used all available LLMs per benchmark for the analyses.
All analyses were performed in R 4.4.0 (R Core Team, 2024). For comparability across benchmarks,
we normalized the benchmark scores on a percent scale.Per benchmark, we discarded subjects2 with
the lowest 0.1% of scores to further reduce noise 3 . Items with standard deviations below 1% (too
little variability) and mean accuracies above 95% (too easy) were removed. We then estimated the
part-whole correlation between each item and the total score using the point-biserial correlation
coefficient, rpb . This coefficient grows if subjects who responded correctly to the item have a higher
than average score, especially if the item has medium difficulty (Irwing et al., 2018, see Appendix A.1
for details). We removed items with rpbis ≈ 0. The exact number of items per benchmark removed
during preprocessing can be found in Appendix A.2.

2.2   C ROSS -VALIDATED S UBSAMPLING

For
  d
      a set of d items it becomes quickly impossible to consider all subsets of size k, as there are
  k unique subsets and any exhaustive search algorithm would have O(d!) time complexity for
fixed k. The computational infeasibility of finding 100 items out of the given 28632 motivates the
approximate methods we present here. Despite the impossibility of exhaustive search, it is astounding
how well the given benchmarks can be reduced in a theory-free way using random sampling: Wang
et al. (2023c), Ye et al. (2023) and Polo et al. (2024) showed that random samples of benchmark
items can in principle produce smaller benchmarks that retain enough information to reconstruct their
original score with negligible error. One basic procedure for this is:
      1. Uniformly sample k items and calculate the subtest scores s̃j for each subject j.
      2. Fit some regression model ŝj = g(s̃j ) on the training set.
      3. Calculate some summary statistic of εj = ŝj − sj on the validation set, e.g. its root mean
         square error (RMSE).
      4. Repeat steps (1-3) a large number of times.
      5. Choose the sample with the smallest validation error and check if the performance is still
         good on the test set.
For g(·) one can use generalized additive models (GAMs, Hastie & Tibshirani, 1987; Fahrmeir et al.,
2013; Wood, 2017), which are a flexible class of regression models utilizing basis functions. They
can be seen as the frequentist version of the fully Bayesian framework of Gaussian Process regression
(Duvenaud et al., 2011).
We ran the described procedure on each benchmark with varying k and 10, 000 repetitions to gauge
how information is spread across items. GAMs were fitted using the mgcv package (Wood, 2017)
with default options. Appendix B.2 contains the results for k between 50 and 500 in steps of 50. As
shown in Figure 1B, even for k ≤ 200, test errors have high density on relatively small intervals
and all median RMSEs are below 3% - many different (but potentially overlapping samples) are
informative. This suggests (1) few items are uninformative, (2) the information they carry is partially
redundant, and (3) the useful information saturates swiftly as more items are considered. This notion
is further explored in Section 2.4. This result has two practical implications: If we just want medium-
sized versions of the six benchmarks, we can simply use cross-validated subsampling. If we want
to tap into (or even breach) the boundary of the test error distribution, a more principled approach
that models properties of items is required. However, principled item selection can be a follow-up
after an initial subsampling sweep, as this preselection still permits reliable score reconstruction.
For comparability across item property models, and to increase the ratio between the number of
observations and estimated parameters, we subsampled all six benchmarks to 350 items. This number
is a compromise between predictive performance and subject-to-item ratio (see Appendix B.2). We
next describe the approach to modeling item properties.

2.3   I TEM R ESPONSE T HEORY

Item response theory (IRT) is a statistical branch of psychometrics used for the design and analysis of
standardized tests (Chen et al., 2021b; Cai et al., 2016; Irwing et al., 2018; Hambleton et al., 1991). It
aims to model the relationship between a subject’s abilities and the probability of a correct response
to an item. This framework is closely related to other latent trait models such as factor analysis (Kim
& Mueller, 1978; Koch, 2013; Murphy, 2022), but assumes binary or polytomous responses (Chen &
Zhang, 2020; Zanon et al., 2016). Let xij be the binary accuracy of item i for subject j with some
(unknown) latent ability vector ϑj :

where f is a link function and αi are the item parameters. We use one-dimensional ϑj , as higher-
dimensional IRT models require much larger subjects-to-items ratios for reliable parameter estimation

We used the mirt package (Chalmers, 2012) with default options to fit the following IRT models
to preselected subsets of 350 items each — a 2PL model, a 3PL model with an additional lower

test set. We use GAMs instead of linear regression models because the relationship between the
latent ability estimates and point scores is monotonic but non-linear. Note, that we also use GAMs in
cross-validated subsampling to ensure comparability of the test RMSEs.

the predicted and the true point scores. All correlation coefficients are above 0.98, indicating that
the ranking of LLMs is largely preserved. The best models achieve an RMSE of on average 1.0%

subsampling approach. Performance differences between IRT models are minute, and MAP estimates

2.4      I NFORMATION F ILTERING

Item parameters have a natural interpretation because of how they affect the link function f (ϑj |αi ):
The difficulty δi shifts its midpoint and the loading ai alters its scale. Thus, the difficulty parameter
determines the probability of a correct response for a subject j with ϑj = 0, and the loading
determines how much this probability changes with the subject’s ability. An item with a high loading
discriminates well between subjects whose ability is close to the item’s difficulty. Following Reckase
(2009), an item is more informative if its response is more useful for differentiating between similar
values of a subject’s estimated latent ability, ϑ̂. Fisher information functions quantify this property:
Since the distribution of xij |ϑj is an exponential family, any response-based estimator T for ϑ will
have a variance bounded from below by the expected Fisher information5 (Pawitan, 2013; Lehmann
& Casella, 1998). For the 2PL model with one-dimensional ability,
                                  Ii (ϑ) = a2i f (ϑ|αi ) 1 − f (ϑ|αi ) .

                                                                                                      (4)
On a high level, this is a measure of how informative an item is about a test taker’s ability conditioned
on the ability, and opens up a more principled approach to item selection. For each benchmark we
use the following procedure to pick the most predictive items:
         1. Estimate the item parameters using an IRT model µ ∈ {2PL, 3PL, 4PL}.
         2. Estimate the ability using a method ν ∈ {MAP, EAPsum}.
         3. Use the item parameters to calculate Fisher information functions for all items.
         4. Partition the the support of ϑ into q quantiles.
         5. In each quantile, select the item with the highest information (above a threshold τ ).
The same item may have the highest Fisher information in multiple quantiles - to avoid selecting an
item multiple times in the same sample, we remove it from the search space after selection.
We re-ran the procedure in Section 2.3, this time subsampling items using information filtering. We
used the rBayesianOptimization package (Yan, 2024) with default settings to fine-tune the
hyperparameters (µ, ν, τ ) on the validation set to minimize the term RMSE(ϑ, s) + λk, where k is
the size of the chosen subset and λ is the regularization coefficient6 . The selected subset was then
evaluated on the test set. For each selection we re-ran the random subsampling procedure with the
same size k for comparison. Table 2 shows that using benchmark-specific information for prediction
(marginal model), the mean test RMSE over benchmarks is 1.6%. All IRT models have an RMSE
well below 1.8%. All benchmarks either reach (GSM8K) or breach (all others) the test accuracy
boundary of random subsampling.

2.5   G OING M ETA


Although the previous section indicates that information filtering is in general effective, we can
further improve score reconstruction if we do not limit ourselves to using information specific to
each benchmark. Benchmark scores are highly correlated (going from r = 0.571 to r = 0.956,
see Appendix D.2), so each benchmark also carries some information about the others. Thus, if an
LLM is run on several reduced benchmarks, the resulting scores may mutually fill in each other’s
blanks. Simulations show that this is particularly the case for tests with moderate overlap between
the required abilities (see Appendix B.6). We exploit synergy by jointly using information from all
six benchmarks as GAM predictors. This joint model is described in more detail in Appendix B.5.
As always, IRT models, latent abilities and GAMs were estimated on the training set and the RMSEs
reflect the score prediction accuracy on the test set. Figure 1 and the right side of Table 2 show
the results: The mean RMSE has shrunk to 1.24% and is well below 1.6% in all cases. All models
surpass the best random subsample by a substantial margin. This synergy effect underlines the
advantage of running all components of metabench.
We then applied this method to predict the grand average score: The mean across all normalized
scores for the six benchmarks constituting the Open LLM Leaderboad. Random subsets are
sampled from each benchmark using its corresponding d (e.g. 93 items from HellaSwag), totalling
858 items. Figure 1C and the bottom row of Table 2 show the score reconstruction results. We were
able to reconstruct the grand average with a Spearman correlation of r = 0.998 and an RMSE of
0.582%, which surpassed all random subsamples by a substantial margin. Appendix B.11 shows that
these results are stable over random seeds.
Finally, we investigated the relationship between all latent abilities by using factor analysis (Kim
& Mueller, 1978; Koch, 2013; Murphy, 2022). In a nutshell, factor analysis of a set of continuous
variables attempts a low-rank approximation of their covariance matrix. As its latent factors have the
same interpretation as the latent abilities in IRT, it is commonly applied to analyze the relationship
between multiple tests (Irwing et al., 2018). See Appendix D.1 for a more detailed introduction. We
applied factor analysis to the estimated latent abilities using the psych package (William Revelle,
2023) (for details, see Appendix D.2). We find that a single factor is sufficient to capture 79.3% of the
total variability across latent abilities. This meta-ability has a Pearson correlation of r = 0.938 with
the original grand average score. Its loadings range from 0.771 (GSM8K) to 0.966 (WinoGrande):
Separately, all six latent abilities contribute a comparable amount to the meta-ability, but the added
unique information decreases with each. This result is in line with the synergy effect as described
above. It consolidates the utility of metabench for efficiently measuring the abilities of LLMs.

3     U SING M E T A B E N C H
3.1    S CORE R ECONSTRUCTION

Our benchmark is hosted as a dataset on HuggingFace and is integrated in EleutherAI’s lm-evaluation-
harness (Gao et al., 2024), a unified framework for evaluating language models on a range of tasks.
We provide two disjoint versions of metabench containing distinct items for all six benchmarks
(see Appendix B.9). This way, metabench can be used for repeated evaluation. Additionally, each
version comes with the option to randomly permute the presentation of answer labels7 , further ham-
pering test memorization. After the lm-evaluation-harness stores the raw scores to all metabench
items, the scores of the original fully-sized benchmarks can be predicted using reconstruct.R in
our GitHub repo: The point predictions stem from the originally trained GAMs, and we construct
non-parametric confidence intervals from the local test RMSE for similar predictions.

3.2    M EMORIZATION M ITIGATION

The described four variants of metabench aim to mitigate the problem of contamination and
benchmark memorization. A model trained on the standard version of the benchmark will not
perform appreciably better on the other versions: We evaluated three LLMs on each version and then
fine-tuned their low-rank adapters on one version with fixed choice ordering. Fine-tuning was done
using unsloth on 4-bit quantized instruct models. We then re-evaluated each LLM on all four
versions of metabench. As shown in Table 3, while fine-tuning substantially improves performance
on the set that it was trained on, this performance boost is attenuated when answer choices are
permuted, and performance does not substantially improve on the disjoint set.

4     C ONCLUSION
The findings presented in this study underscore the viability and advantages of using a sparse
benchmark, metabench, to measure the abilities of Large Language Models (LLMs). We suc-
cessfully distill six prominent benchmarks — ARC, GSM8K, HellaSwag, MMLU, TruthfulQA,
and WinoGrande — into a much smaller but equally informative set of items. The use of latent
abilities enables accurate reconstruction of individual and total benchmark scores, as well as the
original ranking: metabench retains the evaluative power of the original, larger benchmarks while
by reducing redundancy. This successful distillation of six large benchmarks to less than 3% of the
original volume highlights the potential for more streamlined, cost-effective evaluation methods.
Efficiency is particularly valuable for researchers and developers who need to frequently evaluate and
compare models during prototyping — without the computational and time constraints associated
with larger benchmarks. Our methodology can be a stepping stone for future work on benchmark
distillation. Finally, the identification of a single common factor underlying all benchmark scores
suggests that a general ability for reasoning and knowledge can be effectively measured through a
consolidated, sparse benchmark. This finding supports the hypothesis that LLM performance across
various tasks is influenced by a core set of underlying competencies. By distilling these competencies
into a concise benchmark, metabench offers a more focused and insightful evaluation tool.

4.1   L IMITATIONS

Our work has two main limitations. First, static benchmarks run the risk of being memorized (Magar
& Schwartz, 2022), and the smaller the benchmark, the higher the risk. Of course, this is a general
problem with static benchmarks, and ours is not totally immune to it in its current form, although
the mitigation strategies discussed in Section 3.2 go some way to attenuating the performance boost
garnered from training directly on a component of metabench. We see three further potential ways to
mitigate memorization. (1) One could let another language model generate semantically equivalent
but differently worded test items, reducing the probability of exact item memorization (Moros Daval,
2023). (2) One could test whether an LLM completes the wording of a question from the test set
when prompted with the beginning of the question. (3) One could use computerized adaptive testing
to select test items from a much larger pool of items, reducing the likelihood of memorization across
the full set. Each of these extensions should be included in future attempts of benchmark distillation.
Importantly, however, there is no way for us to test if the data we used for IRT models has been
contaminated (without pulling each LLM and testing it individually). If we assume that the rate of
memorization does not drastically change between the models used for training and models that will
later be tested on metabench, our results suggest that data contamination is not a major issue for score
reconstruction.
The second limitation concerns the conditional independence of the LLMs we used. Most users
who submitted results to the OpenLLM Leaderboard contributed several models, potentially
violating the IRT model assumption that the response patterns of different subjects are independent
(conditional on the item parameters). This may result in biased parameter estimates and therefore
distorted item information functions. Indeed, the population of LLMs presumably has a different
dependence structure than than that of human test takers: Many LLMs are fine-tuned versions of
others or at least share the same base structure and are trained on overlapping data. In the extreme
case, treating them as subjects for IRT models is akin to analyzing a test on several batches of clones.
On the other hand, the same can be said about human subjects - despite sharing many fundamental
properties of the human mind and brain, and experiencing overlapping school curricula, human
subjects measurably differ in their knowledge, problem solving abilities, linguistic skills and memory
(Tyler, 1947; Eysenck, 2014). Typically, clustering in human data is treated by including random
effects to (generalized) regression models (Fahrmeir et al., 2013). This is not possible for IRT models,
as these random effects would become indistinguishable from latent abilities without introducing
further constraints. However, the empirical distributions of point scores remain largely unchanged
if we only include the latest LLM per leaderboard user (see Appendix B.1). This suggests, that the
dependence structure is not strongly affected by model clustering over users. As the ratio between
subjects and items is already low by IRT standards, substantial discarding of subjects without also
reducing the item sets is prohibitive for IRT modeling. Future work with more data should analyze to
what extent similarities in fine-tuning and architecture deflate predictive performance for scores and
(more importantly) change the ranking outcomes. Finally, it is likely that new LLMs tested on these
benchmarks are themselves not far out of distribution with respect to the training set, making it very
likely that the non-parametric test error bounds are still accurate.



4.2   F UTURE W ORK

Alongside remedying the limitations of this study, we see several avenues for future work. First,
metabench can be validated on LLMs released after our analyses were completed. Second, our
methodology can be applied to other domains beyond natural language processing, helping to develop
sparse benchmarks for computer vision, reinforcement learning, and speech recognition. Third,
the development of adaptive testing algorithms and interfaces could further reduce the number
of items required for accurate LLM evaluation. By continuously refining the selection process
based on real-time performance data, these algorithms could enhance the efficiency and precision
of LLM assessments. Finally, the core set of cognitive abilities in LLMs remains largely unknown.
Future research should closely investigate its structure, as well as uncover the properties of model
architecture and training data that generate certain ability profiles. This would constitute a basis for
more goal-directed and theoretically grounded benchmarks, driving progress towards more capable
LLMs whose behavior we can predict and interpret.

4.3   E THICS S TATEMENT

We use publicly available benchmark data from LLMs on huggingface.co. Our study does not include
human subjects and does not reveal the correct answers to any of the benchmarks. In particular,
each of the studied benchmarks is standard and broadly accepted by the LLM community. We
anticipate no harmful effects from our findings. On the contrary, this project aims to contribute
towards more resource fairness and energy efficiency in LLM research. There is no conflict of interest
and sponsorship, as none of the authors is affiliated with the original benchmark authors or the
huggingface platform at the time of submission.

A.1    PART-W HOLE C ORRELATION

To estimate the part-whole correlation between a binary item outcome and a non-binary aggregate
point score, psychometrics (Irwing et al., 2018) offers the point-biserial correlation coefficient rpb :
                                                  s
                                           µ1 − µ n1 (n − n1 )
                                  rpb =                           ,                                  (5)
                                            sn−1      n(n − 1)
where the set of correct responders has the average score µ1 and size n1 (in comparison to the total
mean µ and size n). The empirical standard deviation of the total group is denoted as sn−1 . This
coefficient is bounded in [−1, 1] and grows if subjects who responded correctly to the item have
higher than average scores. Note that it is customary to remove items with negative rpb , as they
appear to measure something different from what the remaining majority of items measures or even
point to annotation errors (Rodriguez et al., 2021). However, items with a negative rpb do contain
information about the benchmark score: Correct subjects are on average worse on the remaining
items. We therefore chose to keep them.

B.4   C AN WE USE I TEM R ESPONSE T HEORY ?

There are few formal conditions for applying IRT models, namely that (1) a test / benchmark has
multiple items that measure the same latent trait(s), (2) the items are scored dichotomously or
polytomously, (3) we have multiple observations (over different test takers) per item, and (4) the
item accuracies are independent conditional on the latent abilities (Baker & Kim, 2004; Irwing et al.,
2018; Chen et al., 2021b). All these conditions are met in our case, though the last one is a bit more
subtle: For instance, it would be clearly violated if items were given to the LLMs conditioned on
their previous responses.
However, does this warrant us to treat LLMs like human test takers, for whom IRT has been designed?
On a speculative note: LLMs are trained on human data and therefor mimic (to some extent) human
language and likely some underlying mechanisms - in the most optimistic sense, they are imprints of
collective human intelligence. This means that psychometric methods developed for human behavior
should in principle be able to be applied to LLM-reproduced human behavior. On a more pragmatic
note: Formally, LLM benchmarks are predominantly multiple-choice tests that aim to measure some
set of traits or abilities. Why not use the methodologically rich history of psychometrics? Several
machine learning papers have already suggested this idea (Martínez-Plumed et al., 2019; Rodriguez
et al., 2021; Vania et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023c). Computer scientists need not try to reinvent the
wheel in solving problems of measurement and test taking. In fact, item response theory can be used
agnostically with regard to the test taker, be it human or machine. Its assumptions are of statistical
nature and the estimated parameters let us interpret properties of the test items, regardless of the
nature of the test taker.

B.7    N UMBER OF L ATENT A BILTIES

How many distinct abilities play into solving a benchmark? This is a nuanced question and is distinct
from how many ability dimensions are sufficient for the purpose of score reconstruction. While
conceptually it is a hard claim that a single ability governs test performance, there are three reasons
for using one-dimensional IRT models in our pipeline:
      1. Item selection: Information filtering scales well in the one-dimensional case. Finding
         the most-informative items in a multi-dimensional space opens up new problems: Do we
         marginalize out each dimension? Do we search in the joint space? If yes, what information
         coverage is desirable, which parts of the n-dimensional grid are most relevant overall?
      2. Estimation variance: The total number of loading parameters doubles with each added
         latent ability, which increases the uncertainty in the IRT fit, on which we base the remaining
         selection process.
      3. Diminishing returns: Table 5 shows that for score reconstruction, the performance boost
         from using two latent abilities is negligible (when present at all).

B.8    C LUSTERING FOR I TEM S ELECTION

An alternative item selection strategy presented in previous literature (e.g. Polo et al., 2024) is based
on clustering. We altered only the item selection method and kept the remaining analysis steps
constant for maximum comparability with our previous results. These are the steps for clustering-
based item-selection we used for comparison:
      1. Estimate the item parameters using an IRT model µ ∈ {2PL, 3PL, 4PL}.
      2. Estimate the ability using a method ν ∈ {MAP, EAPsum}.
      3. Perform k-means clustering on item parameters with k ∈ {10, 15, 20, 25, 30}
      4. Iteratively, draw one item from each remaining cluster, until the d items are drawn (where d
         is the size of the corresponding benchmark after information filtering).
Then we re-fitted the same IRT model on that subset of items, estimated the latent ability and fitted a
GAM of the original score based on the latent ability and the subscore. We performed a grid search
over the hyperparameters k, µ, ν on a validation set. For comparability with our final results, we took
the item selection with the best validation RMSE for each benchmark and fit a joint GAM (using all
latent abilities as predictors). Table 6 shows that information filtering performs better than clustering
in every scenario.

B.9   S ECOND V ERSION FOR R EPEATED E VALUATION

If a user evaluates their model multiple times on metabench and makes changes to increase the
score, will the improvements be robust enough to also show in the original six benchmarks? Of
course, practitioners must not fool themselves - if they train their models on the specific test questions
in metabench, increases in this score will not reflect a lot in the scores of large benchmarks. Even
if a model is trained to become better at metabench without training on its explicit content, the final
model should be tested on an independent benchmark. To this end we created a secondary version
of metabench with comparable reconstruction quality. It is completely disjoint from the initial
version (both use distinct sets of items). For this, we have excluded all selected items from the initial
version and rerun all steps of the procedure starting from cross-validated subsampling. This version
allows practitioners to use the base version for validation and the second version for final testing.
Table 7 and Figure 8 show the score reconstruction results for the secondary version of metabench.
When using benchmark-specific information for prediction, the mean test RMSE over benchmarks is
1.75%. All IRT models have an RMSE well below 2%. Except for TruthfulQA, all test RMSEs
are better than at least 95% of all random subsamples. Four out of six benchmarks breached the test
accuracy boundary of random subsampling. When using information from all benchmarks, prediction
accuracy is improved by a substantial margin and all models either reach (GSM8K) or breach (all
others) the test accuracy boundary of random subsampling.

C     C OMPUTERIZED A DAPTIVE T ESTING

C.1    A DAPTIVE T ESTING

A significant benefit of IRT analysis is that it allows for adaptive testing, in which items can be
administered conditional upon the current estimate of the subject’s ability. The information filtering
procedure can be seen as a one-size-fits-most approach, covering the items necessary for a good latent
ability estimate for most models. The alternative is to ask the most informative questions sequentially
relative to the current latent ability estimate, arriving at a final estimate using fewer items. This is the
idea behind computerized adaptive tests (CATs, Chang, 2015; Irwing et al., 2018).

C.2    P ROCEDURE

We used the catR package (Magis & Raiche, 2012) to simulate Computerized Adaptive Tests (CATs)
on our sample of LLMs using items from each benchmark. The goal was to re-estimate ϑj for each
subject, j, as provided by an IRT model fitted to the training set, using as few items as possible.
CATs proceed by selecting an item that is most useful for updating the estimated ϑj , administering
it to subject j, and then updating the estimate of ϑj . This process is repeated until some stopping
criterion is achieved, at which point the final ϑj estimate is computed. CATs can be simulated on
subjects who have already completed a full bank of items by simply returning their responses to each
administered item. This assumes that the responses of each subject to each item is deterministic and
would not change, a safe assumption for the LLMs in our simulations. The usefulness of an item
can be formalized in several ways. There are several options for running CAT simulations, the most
impactful of which are: the method for selecting the first item, the method for updating ϑj after each
item administration, the method for selecting subsequent items, and the method for estimating ϑj
after all items have been administered. For parity with our previous analyses, we only estimated ϑj at
the end of testing using either EAP or MAP. To assess the value of alternative approaches for the
other three CAT simulation parameters, we ran an exhaustive sweep over the several possible options
using a systematically sampled subset of 40 LLMs with ϑj values over the observed range. We then
computed the root mean squared error between the estimate of ϑˆj provided by the CAT simulation
and ‘true’ ϑj provided by our original analyses. We swept the following parameters, excluding
alternatives (such as Maximum Likelihood Weighted Information) due to their high computational
demands:
      1. Initial item selection: Maximum Fisher Information (MFI), Urry’s (bOpt) Procedure (Urry,
         1970), Magis’s (thOpt) Procedure (Magis, 2013).
      2. Iterative ϑj estimation method: Maximum A Posteriori/Bayes Modal Estimation (Bassett &
         Deride, 2019; Birnbaum, 1969), Maximum Likelihood Estimation (Lord, 1980), Expected
         A Posteriori Estimation (Bock & Mislevy, 1982), Weighted Likelihood Estimation (Warm,
         1989), Robust Estimation (Schuster & Yuan, 2011).
      3. Next item selection: MFI, bOpt, thOpt, Progressive Procedure (Barrada et al., 2008; 2010;
         Revuelta & Ponsoda, 1998), Proportional Procedure (Barrada et al., 2008; 2010; Segall,
         2004).
Table 10 presents summary statistics from a subset of the parameter sweep, focusing on recovering
the MAP estimated ϑj values of a fitted 4PL model for each benchmark. In general, the differences
between parameter settings is small, with the exception for the MMLU estimation. The differences are
similarly small across the parameter sweeps for the 2PL and 3PL models, suggesting that choice of
these parameters has little impact on the final ϑj estimation. We therefore elected to conduct full
CAT simulations with matching parameter settings to the original IRT model fitting procedure, for
maximum parameter. Future work will explore whether better overall benchmark score recovery can
be achieved with different parameter settings, although our evidence suggests that any gains will be
marginal.
We ran CAT simulations on the full sample of LLMs. We used the maximum Fisher information
criterion, selecting the item that has the highest expected Fisher information with respect to the

      1. Initialize an estimate of ϑj as an integer in [-2, 2].
      2. Select an item, i, with the Maximum Fisher Information with respect to ϑ̂j .

Using this approach, we found that the latent ability could be reliably re-estimated for every bench-

case, they highlight that practitioners could leverage computerized adaptive testing to improve score

D.3   FACTOR M ODELS INSTEAD OF I NFORMATION F ILTERING ?

This result may raise the question why we do not simply use factor analysis or principle component
analysis to select items. We do not directly aim to explain as much variance as possible in the items
(for algebraic and pragmatic reasons), but rather aim for accurate summary statistics:
      1. The covariance matrix across items has far fewer observations than degrees of freedom,
         which is d ∗ (d + 1)/2 ≈ 340 ∗ 106 , so its estimate will be unstable and a bad starting point
         for decomposition.
      2. Focusing on explained variance would bias us towards items of medium difficulty, as easier
         and harder items naturally vary less but can be important to separate LLMs.
      3. Factor analysis and PCA assume that the underlying observations are continuous (not
         dichotomous) and the observed variable (item accuracy) is linear in the latent variable
         (ability). If these assumptions are violated, the methods lose a lot of desirable statistical
         properties (Chen & Zhang, 2020; Cai et al., 2016; Bock & Aitkin, 1981).
Finally, benchmarks aim to give us some measure of how good a model is in some abstract domain
rather than its exact knowledge per item. The latter would be the implied focus on variance decompo-
sition across items. The former can be directly translated into latent ability estimation as done by
IRT.
