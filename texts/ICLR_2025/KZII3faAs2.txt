                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               As machine learning models become increasingly complex and are deployed in
010            critical domains such as healthcare, finance, and autonomous systems, the need
011            for effective explainability has grown. Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), which
012            excel in processing graph-structured data, have seen significant advancements, but
013            explainability for GNNs is still in its early stages. Existing approaches fall into
014            two broad categories: post-hoc explainers and inherently interpretable models.
015            Their evaluation is often limited to synthetic datasets for which ground truth ex-
016            planations are available, or conducted with the assumption that each XAI method
017            extracts explanations for a fixed network. We focus specifically on inherently in-
018
               terpretable GNNs (e.g., based on prototypes, graph kernels) which enable model-
               level explanations. For evaluation, these models claim inherent interpretability
019
               and only assess predictive accuracy, without applying concrete interpretability
020
               metrics. These evaluation practices fundamentally restrict the utility of any dis-
021            cussions regarding explainability. We propose a unified and comprehensive frame-
022            work for measuring and evaluating explainability in GNNs that extends beyond
023            synthetic datasets, ground-truth constraints, and rigid assumptions, while also sup-
024            porting the development and refinement of models based on derived explanations.
025            The framework involves measures of Accuracy, Instance-level explanations, and
026            Model-level explanations (AIM), inspired by the generic Co-12 conceptual prop-
027            erties of explanations quality (Nauta et al., 2023). We apply this framework to
028            a suite of existing models, deriving ways to extract explanations from them and
029
               to highlight their strengths and weaknesses. Furthermore, based on this analysis
               using AIM, we develop a new model called XGKN that demonstrates improved
030
               explainability while performing on par with existing models. Our approach aims
031
               to advance the field of Explainable AI (XAI) for GNNs, offering more robust and
032            practical solutions for understanding and interpreting complex models.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION AND R ELATED W ORK
036
037   Explainability in machine learning is gaining importance, especially as models are applied in ar-
038   eas like healthcare (Ahmedt-Aristizabal et al., 2021), finance (Wang et al., 2022), and autonomous
039
      systems (Li et al., 2024). Meanwhile, Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) (Kipf & Welling, 2017)
      have emerged as powerful tools for handling graph data. While both fields are evolving rapidly, the
040
      exploration of Explainable AI (XAI) within the context of GNNs—and specifically for inherently
041
      interpretable GNNs—remains limited.
042
043   Most existing models serve as post-hoc explainers that aim to identify importance maps over input
044   graphs (Ying et al., 2019a; Luo et al., 2020; Vu & Thai, 2020; Yuan et al., 2021; Magister et al.,
045
      2021; Lucic et al., 2022; Shin et al., 2022). Some leverage the Shapley-values approach from game
      theory (SHAP (Lundberg & Lee, 2017)) (Duval & Malliaros, 2021; Akkas & Azad, 2024). Thresh-
046
      olding these importance maps yields induced subgraphs that serve as explanations. While these
047
      methods offer some insights, they often lack reliability as they provide approximations rather than
048   accurately reflecting the model’s decision-making process. They can be inconsistent, oversimplify
049   complex models, and do not guarantee trustworthiness, leading to potentially misleading explana-
050   tions. Furthermore, since they do not influence the training phase, they fail to promote transparency
051   from the start, making them less suitable for critical applications where interpretability is essential.
052   Furthermore, prevailing practice in evaluating explainers relies on the availability of ground truth ex-
053   planations, leading to experiments predominantly conducted on simple synthetic benchmarks (Ying
      et al., 2019b; Baldassarre & Azizpour, 2019; Luo et al., 2020; Azzolin et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2020).

      For cases without available ground truths, metrics such as fidelity (Amara et al., 2024; Zheng et al.,
055   2024; Longa et al., 2024), robustness (Bajaj et al., 2022), sufficiency, and necessity Tan et al. (2022);
056   Chen et al. (2022) were proposed to assess how predictions change when input graphs are altered
057   based on explanations from explainers. Since all explainers are applied to the same network, predic-
058   tion differences can be compared directly across them. In contrast, our work focuses on inherently
059   interpretable GNNs, where each method produces scores with different distributions, adding com-
060   plexity to the evaluation process. Additionally, measures of explanation size sparsity are used in
061   evaluations (Yu et al., 2022; Lucic et al., 2022). Agarwal et al. (2023) present an approach similar
062   to ours by considering a broader range of metrics and examining changes in explanations. However,
063
      their work is also limited to post-hoc methods only.
064   Compared to the abundance of post-hoc methods, work on inherently interpretable GNNs is rela-
065   tively limited Kakkad et al. (2023). Some methods use information constraints, such as attention
066   mechanisms (Miao et al., 2022), while others employ structural constraints, which can additionally
067   enable extraction of model-level explanations. Our focus is on the latter category, where the most
068   prominent models include Prototypical Networks (Ragno et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2021) and Graph
069
      Kernel Networks (GKNs) (Nikolentzos & Vazirgiannis, 2020; Cosmo et al., 2021; Feng et al., 2022).
      Both models employ unsupervised concept learning (Koh et al., 2020), where the model learns to
070
      identify concepts (trainable prototypes or graph filters) against which input graphs are compared.
071
      This comparison yields similarity scores that guide the prediction process. While investigation of
072   these learned concepts aims to unravel the model’s decision-making process, instance-level explana-
073   tions that support their predictions are not available. Furthermore, these models claim explainability
074   based solely on their design, without assessing any specific measures of explainability.
075
      We contend that the evaluation practices for GNN explainers and interpretable GNNs is inadequate
076
      in the context of XAI. We argue that the advancement of XAI in GNNs is hampered by the lack of
077
      standardized metrics for evaluating explainability, making it difficult to determine which models are
078   superior and under what circumstances, ultimately limiting their impact and practical applicability.
079
080   To effectively evaluate the XAI capabilities of various meth-
081
      ods, it is essential to assess aspects such as correctness, con-
      sistency, and complexity of the explanations they provide. The
082
      Co-12 framework (Nauta et al., 2023) outlines 12 concep-
083
      tual properties of explanation quality, aiming to standardize
084   the evaluation process for XAI methods. This framework has
085   since been adapted for application in computer vision models
086   (Nauta & Seifert, 2023). We aim to expand this line of research
087   specifically within the context of GNNs. Figure 1 illustrates
088   examplar evaluation using our framework.

125   2     AIM METRICS
126
127   Here, we outline the key properties of explainable GNN methods and propose a set of metrics for
128   their evaluation. However, prior to that, it is crucial to define what constitutes an explanation in this
129   context, considering current practices and their universality.
130   We assume that instance-level explanations take the form of induced subgraphs derived from the in-
131   put graphs, whereas model-level explanations are graphs within the input space that are determined
132   by identified concepts (prototypes in Prototypical Networks, or graph filters in Graph Kernel Net-
133   works). We choose to consider induced subgraphs (thresholded maps of importance over the input
134   graphs) because the values within importance maps can vary in interpretation, range, and distribution
135   across different models, which affects measures of similarity between maps.
136   Note that Prototypical Networks and Graph Kernel Networks can be defined as a composition of
137   three functions f = fpred ◦fagg ◦fsim , where fsim yields similarity scores between input subgraphs
138   and concepts, fagg aggregates scores over subgraphs, and fpred does the final prediction. For Graph
139   Kernel Networks, the input subgraphs correspond to the k-hop neighborhoods of each node, with
140   the aggregation function usually being a summation. Prototypical Networks compare an encoded
141   input graph against learned prototypes, while allowing subgraphs that contribute to the similarity
142   scores to be identified. We assume that f is a graph classification network, although this assumption
143
      can be omitted, and the formulas for the evaluation metrics can be easily adjusted.
144
145   2.1    P ROPERTIES TO COVER
146
      Co-12 (Nauta et al., 2023) is a set of conceptual properties, such as Compactness and Correctness,
147
      that are essential for a comprehensive assessment of explanation quality. Inspired by it, we propose
148   AIM, a set of 12 metrics divided into 3 categories to assess: accuracy (A1-A2), instance-level expla-
149   nations (I1-I7) and model-level explanations (M1-M3). These metrics cover 12 desired properties
150   of GNN explanations that we describe in Table 1.

206   3.1     E XTRACTING EXPLANATIONS FROM INTERPRETABLE GNN S
207
208   Post-hoc explainers for instance-level explanations provide methods for deriving explanations from
209   existing GNNs in the form of importance maps over input graphs, whereas explanations from inter-
210   pretable GNNs, such as Prototypical Networks for graph and Graph Kernel Networks, need to be
211   extracted. First, we outline how the identified concepts are projected onto the input space to serve as
212
      model-level explanations. Next, we propose a SHAP-based approach for extracting instance-level
      explanations from concept-based interpretable GNNs.
213
214   As in the previous section, let f = fpred ◦ fagg ◦ fsim represent an interpretable GNN, where
215   fsim yields similarity scores between input subgraphs and concepts, fagg aggregates scores over
      subgraphs, and fpred does the final prediction.

216
      3.1.1    M ODEL - LEVEL EXPLANATIONS
217
218   Existing interpretable GNN methods rely on trainable concepts to make predictions, which are re-
219   garded as model-level explanations. These concepts are either explicitly given in graph form (in
220   Graph Kernel Networks) or as embeddings (in Prototypical Networks), which can be projected onto
221   the input space to obtain their graph representations (Zhang et al., 2021). If graph filters in GKN are
222   represented using continuous node features and a continuous adjacency matrix, they can be projected
223   onto the input space by discretizing the adjacency matrix and identifying node features present in
224
      the dataset that show the highest similarity with respect to fsim .
225
226   3.1.2    I NSTANCE - LEVEL EXPLANATIONS VIA SHAP PROPAGATION
227
228   For instance-level explanations, since no framework exists in prior work on interpretable GNNs, we
229

      for graph classification tasks, it can be easily adapted to other use cases.
230

234   Let p̂ be the logit predicted for the class that G will be classified as: p̂ = maxi=1,...,c pi .

334   ProtGNN ProtGNN’s, in Figure 4c, limitations stem from its mechanism of sampling input sub-
335   graphs to generate prototype projections and identify the most relevant subgraphs. Since the sam-
336   pling process heavily relies on edges, it becomes inconsistent when the input edges are altered (I5).
337   While the prototypes are not explicitly in graph form and require projection onto the input space,
338   ProtGNN still demonstrates a good level of explainability. However, the model’s main drawback
339
      is its computational time (see Table 3b), largely due to the sampling strategy. Not only is the ex-
      planation extraction process slow, but the projection phase during training is also time-consuming,
340
      as it requires iterating over the dataset to identify subgraphs that best match the prototypes. This
341
      approach limits the model’s scalability compared to others.
342
343
      KerGNN KerGNN’s issues, highlighted in Figure 4d, arise from its heavy reliance on node fea-
344
      tures rather than edges. While edges define the input subgraphs compared against graph filters, the
345   model often performs best when the Random Walk Kernel—used as the kernel function—considers
346   only paths of length 1. This essentially reduces the kernel function to a comparison of the node
347   sets of graphs, largely ignoring edge structures. Additionally, KerGNN combines input node feature
348   information with similarity scores for final predictions, which limits the model’s ability to identify
349   meaningful concepts, as it can infer much of the information just from node features (such as node
350   degree in IMDB datasets).
351
352   GKNN As illustrated in Figure 4e, GKNN’s explanations are consistent (I3-I5) and dependent on
353   the learned concepts (M1-M2). Moreover, these concepts are both relevant (A1-A2) and concise
354   (M3). We observe that the explanations tend to be larger (I7), which correlates with other metrics
355
      (I1-I2 and I6). This occurs because many subgraphs within a graph receive similar kernel responses
      from graph filters, making it more challenging to differentiate between relevant and irrelevant sub-
356
      graphs, and consequently, individual nodes. The time required to extract explanations from GKNN
357
      is approximately 40% longer (see Table 3b) due to the use of graph kernels, such as the Weisfeiler-
358   Lehman Graph Kernel, which cannot be executed on GPUs and involve slower operations compared
359   to the Random Walk Kernel used in KerGNN. GKNNs employ non-differentiable graph kernels,
360   and hence require training through a Discrete Randomized Descent strategy, which limits fast GPU
361   computations and ultimately restricts their scalability.

      A.1   H YPERPARAMETERS IN AIM EVALUATION
651
652   For AIM evaluation, certain perturbations are performed on input graphs, model’s concept parame-
653   ters are altered, new classifiers are trained. Here, we specify hyperparameters for these operations.
654
      For I4, input graph perturbation is performed by altering node features with a probability of 0.05,
655   where assigning each feature a randomly selected value from the dataset. For I5, perturbation is car-
656   ried out by removing an existing edge with a probability of 0.005 and inserting a new node between
657   two connected nodes with the same probability. For I6, a new classifier is trained using the hyper-
658   parameters from the GIN model employed for post-hoc explainers. For M1, concept modification
659   involves changing the features of each node with a probability of 0.5, assigning random features
660   from the dataset, and encoding them if required. For M2, concepts are perturbed by either adding a
661   non-existent edge or removing an existing edge between node pairs, each with a probability of 0.25.
662
663   A.2   T HRESHOLDING
664
665
      To distinguish between relevant and irrelevant parts of input graphs, it is necessary to apply a thresh-
      old to the importance maps of nodes or edges. Several techniques can be used for this purpose.
666
667   One straightforward approach is the top-k method, where the k nodes or edges with the highest im-
668   portance scores are selected. Another technique involves setting the threshold based on percentiles.
669   In this case, importance maps are normalized to sum to one, and for a given percentile p ∈ [0, 1],
670   a threshold is chosen such that the cumulative importance below it sums to p. Nodes or edges with
671
      importance scores above this threshold are considered relevant.
672   A method that avoids the need for hyperparameters like k or p is identifying the elbow point of
673   the sorted importance scores, which serves as a natural threshold. We apply the approach based on
674   calculating distance from a reference line.
675   Figure 5 presents the results of different thresholding techniques applied to various models on the
676   BA-2motif dataset.
677
678
      We observe correlations between different metrics. Improvement in one often comes at the cost
      of another; for example, increasing the size of the explanation (I7) improves the likelihood that
679
      the explanation will be classified in the same class as the original graph (I1). We see that AIM
680
      evaluation for some models depends more heavily on the thresholding technique (PGExplainer,
681   ProtGNN, GKNN).
682
683   Ideally, importance maps should be constructed in a way that allows for clear distinction between
684
      relevant and irrelevant nodes, for instance, by leveraging elbow points, rather than relying on hyper-
      parameters such as the target number of nodes or a percentile.
