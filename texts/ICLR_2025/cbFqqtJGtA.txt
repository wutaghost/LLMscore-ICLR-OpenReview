009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Rationally identifying variables responsible for changes to a biological system can
012            enable myriad applications in disease understanding and cell engineering. From
013            a causality perspective, we are given two datasets generated by the same causal
014            model, one observational (control) and one interventional (perturbed). The goal is
015            to isolate the subset of measured variables (e.g. genes) that were the targets of the
016
               intervention, i.e. those whose conditional independencies have changed. Knowing
               the causal graph would limit the search space, allowing us to efficiently pinpoint
017
               these variables. However, current algorithms that infer causal graphs in the pres-
018
               ence of unknown intervention targets scale poorly to the hundreds or thousands of
019            variables in biological data, as they must jointly search the combinatorial spaces
020            of graphs and consistent intervention targets. In this work, we propose a causality-
021            inspired approach for predicting perturbation targets that decouples the two search
022            steps. First, we use an amortized causal discovery model to separately infer causal
023            graphs from the observational and interventional datasets. Then, we learn to map
024            these paired graphs to the sets of variables that were intervened upon, in a su-
025            pervised learning framework. This approach consistently outperforms baselines
026            for perturbation modeling on seven single-cell transcriptomics datasets, each with
027
               thousands of measured variables. We also demonstrate significant improvements
               over six causal discovery algorithms in predicting intervention targets across a
028
               variety of tractable, synthetic datasets.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Cells form the basis of biological systems, and they take on a multitude of dynamical states through-
034   out their lifetime. In addition to natural factors like cell cycle, external perturbations (e.g. drugs,
035   gene knockdown) can alter a cell’s state. While perturbations can affect numerous downstream
036   variables, identifying the root causes, or targets, that drive these transitions has vast therapeutic
037   implications, from cellular reprogramming (Cherry & Daley, 2012) to mechanism of action elucida-
038   tion (Schenone et al., 2013). Large-scale experiments (Replogle et al., 2022) have attempted to map
039
      the effects of perturbing individual genes on single cells, and in-silico approaches (Roohani et al.,
      2023; Lotfollahi et al., 2023) have been designed for the “forward” inference task of predicting the
040
      post-perturbation expression of each gene. In principle, these models can be used within an active
041
      learning framework to suggest perturbation targets (Huang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a). How-
042   ever, the number of inference calls required scales exponentially with the size of the candidate set
043   (e.g. drugs with off-target effects), rendering these approaches impractical for larger search spaces.
044   These models’ predictive performance as oracles has also been called into question by subsequent
045   works (Kernfeld et al., 2023; Ahlmann-Eltze et al., 2024), highlighting the difficulty of this forward
046   approach. Finally, there are limited training data for combinatorial perturbations: the most widely
047   used dataset only contains around a hundred pairs (Norman et al., 2019).
048   The “reverse” strategy – of directly predicting targets from perturbation data – alleviates these prob-
049   lems to an extent. Towards this end, it is common to assume (sometimes implicitly) that the data
050   were generated by a structured causal model, and the perturbation targets can be directly inferred
051   from the changes to this model. While the effects of perturbations are highly contextual, specific to
052   the cell lines in which they are applied (Nadig et al., 2024), existing methods rely on non-specific,
053   data-mined knowledge graphs as the backbones for their causal structures (Cosgrove et al., 2008;
      Gonzalez et al., 2024). It is hard to quantify the extent to which these graphs are relevant to each

077   Our goal is to predict the perturbation targets that translate one set of cells into another, while infer-
078   ring the underlying causal structures directly from the data. We formalize this task within a causality
079   framework. Specifically, given an observational dataset (e.g. gene expression of control cells) and
080   an interventional dataset (e.g. perturbed cells) generated by the same system (e.g. cell type), we
081   would like to identify the variables (e.g. genes) whose data-generating mechanisms are different be-
082   tween the two settings. Causal discovery algorithms have been designed to solve this exact problem,
083   on interventional data with unknown targets (Squires et al., 2020; Brouillard et al., 2020; Hägele
084
      et al., 2023). These algorithms operate jointly over the spaces of graphs and intervention target sets.
      Due to this large search space, these algorithms are unable to scale to the thousands of variables and
085

086
      robust to as few as tens of observations per interventional setting (Nadig et al., 2024).
087
088   To address unknown intervention target prediction in context of biological perturbations, we pro-
089   pose causal differential networks (C DN). Drawing upon recent advances in scalable and data-
090
      efficient causal discovery (Wu et al., 2024), C DN first “featurizes” the observational and interven-
      tional datasets in terms of their predicted causal graphs, using a pretrained causal discovery module.
091
      These paired, edge-level representations are then used as input to an axial attention-based classi-
092
      fier (Ho et al., 2020), which is supervised by ground truth intervention targets (on either real or
093   synthetic data). We thoroughly evaluate C DN on both real transcriptomic data and in synthetic set-
094   tings. C DN outperforms the state-of-the-art in perturbation modeling on the five largest Perturb-seq
095   datasets at the time of publication (CRISPRi, Replogle et al. (2022); Nadig et al. (2024)), and
096   two Sci-Plex datasets (chemical perturbations, McFaline-Figueroa et al. (2024)), without using any
097   external knowledge. In fact, C DN is the only model that consistently ranks the ground truth per-
098   turbation targets higher than would be expected by random. Moreoever, C DN is able to maintain
099   decent performance even on unseen cell lines, demonstrating its potential to generalize. Finally, on
100   synthetic settings, C DN outperforms causal discovery algorithms for unknown intervention targets.
101
      To conclude, our contributions are three-fold.
102          1. We propose causal differential networks (C DN), a causal discovery-based approach for
103             perturbation target prediction on transcriptomic data, which achieves the state-of-the-art on
104             genetic and chemical perturbation datasets.
105          2. We demonstrate that C DN outperforms current causal discovery approaches for unknown
106             intervention target prediction for both hard and soft interventions in diverse synthetic data.
107
             3. Finally, we have prepared seven high-quality transcriptomics datasets, to serve as a bench-
                mark for future machine learning studies.

      2     BACKGROUND AND RELATED WORK
109

      2.1   I DENTIFYING PERTURBATION TARGETS
111
112   The rise of large-scale perturbation screens (Replogle et al., 2022) has enabled machine learning
113   approaches for predicting perturbation targets. One line of work focuses on active learning to reduce
114   experimental costs (Zhang et al., 2023a; Huang et al., 2023), but these models require inference calls
115   to an oracle model for each candidate perturbation. Given that human cells express over ten thousand
116   genes, and drugs may impact multiple targets, the search space is enormous. Moreover, subsequent
117   works have found that naive baselines (mean of the training set, linear regression) outperform these
118
      approaches at their intended task (Kernfeld et al., 2023; Ahlmann-Eltze et al., 2024; Märtens et al.,
      2024). In contrast, perturbation targets can be predicted directly from data, using a variety of domain
119
      knowledge (Cosgrove et al., 2008; Gonzalez et al., 2024; Roohani et al., 2024). However, these
120
      external data were collected from highly inhomogenous sources, which may be inconsistent with
121   the data at hand.
122
123
      2.2   C AUSAL DISCOVERY WITH UNKNOWN INTERVENTION TARGETS
124
125   Perturbation target prediction can be formalized as a causal discovery problem, using data generated
126   under interventions with unknown targets. Specifically, a causal graphical model is a directed graph
127   G = (V, E), where nodes i ∈ V map to random variables Xi ∈ X, and edges (i, j) ∈ E represent
128   relationships from Xi to Xj . There are a number of common assumptions that relate G to the data
129   distribution PX , which we defer to works such as Spirtes et al. (2001); Yang et al. (2018); Zhang
130   et al. (2023b), since the identifiability of any particular system is not the focus of this paper.
131   Data generated directly from PX are known as observational data. A causal graphical model allows
132   us to perform interventions by assigning new conditionals
133
134                                      P (Xi | Xπi ) ← P̃ (Xi | Xπi ),                                  (1)
135   where πi denotes the parents of node i in G. Hard interventions remove all dependence between
136   Xi and πi , while soft interventions maintain the relationship with a different conditional. We denote
137   the joint interventional distribution as P̃X . Given an observational dataset Dobs ∼ PX and an
138   interventional dataset Dint ∼ P̃X , our goal is to predict the set of nodes I for which
139
140                                   P (Xi | Xπi ) ̸= P̃ (Xi | Xπi ), ∀i ∈ I.                            (2)
141
      This task has been well-studied in the causality literature, often as a sub-problem or setting while in-
142   ferring graph G from Dobs and (multiple) Dint . Ke et al. (2019) first proposed a discovery algorithm
143   for unknown interventions, on discrete data of up to 50 variables. Jaber et al. (2020) provides iden-
144   tifiability guarantees for soft interventions with unknown targets and proposes a constraint-based al-
145   gorithm, which requires an exponential number of conditional independence tests with respect to the
146   number of variables. Continuous causal discovery algorithms that are consistent despite unknown
147   interventions include D CDI (Brouillard et al., 2020) and BAC A DI (Hägele et al., 2023), which treat
148   intervention target identities as learnable parameters of a generative model over PX . D CDI uses the
149   Gumbel-Softmax approximation (Jang et al., 2017) to learn these categorical variables, while BA -
      CADI takes a Bayesian approach, with gradient-based methods for efficient posterior estimation (Liu
150
      & Wang, 2016). Finally, Varici et al. (2022) and Yang et al. (2024) frame intervention target predic-
151
      tion as a separate task, similar to this paper. However, the former strictly assumes linearity, while
152
      the latter requires data from multiple environments, which are not available here.

      Our model is composed of two modules: a frozen causal featurizer, and a learned differential net-
189
      work, which we refer to jointly as C DN (Figure 2). The causal featurizer is a pretrained, amortized
190
      causal discovery model (Wu et al., 2024) that runs efficiently on up to a thousand nodes (Section 3.1),
191   while the differential network is an axial-attention based classifier (Ho et al., 2020) that predicts the
192   nodes whose data-generating mechanisms have changed (Section 3.2).

194
195   3.1          C AUSAL FEATURIZER
196
197   The causal featurizer takes as input datasets Dobs and Dint containing samples of N variables, gen-
198   erated from observational and interventional settings. For example, Dobs may correspond the gene
199   expression matrix of non-targeting control cells; Dint is the gene expression of the same type of
200
      cells, subject to a perturbation with an unknown target; and N is the number of genes detected.
201   We use an amortized causal discovery model to obtain features hobs , hint ∈ RN ×N ×d , which reflect
202   the N × N adjacency matrices of causal graphs that may have generated each dataset. Specifically,
203   to featurize each dataset, we follow the three steps proposed in Wu et al. (2024).
204               1. Given a dataset D, smaller batches are constructed by sub-sampling both examples and
205                  variables. Heuristics like pairwise correlation are used to select variables which are likely
206                  to have causal relationships, to minimize unnecessary computation.
207               2. Two sets of input features are generated from these batches: global summary statistics like
208                  correlation, computed over all variables, and marginal estimates, the outputs of classical
209                  causal discovery algorithms (Spirtes et al., 1995) run on small subsets of variables.
210               3. Finally, these two sets of features are input to a neural network (“aggregator”), which was
211                  trained to map them into causal graphs on synthetic datasets.
212   We run this procedure on Dobs and Dint independently, treating both as “observational” datasets.
213   To obtain the graph representations for each dataset, we extract the aggregator’s last layer hidden
214   representations before they are collapsed into binary edge predictions, yielding hobs and hint . Finally,
215   we concatenate hobs and hint along the hidden dimension, to obtain a paired graph representation of
      size N × N × 2d.

      3.2   D IFFERENTIAL NETWORK
217
218   Given the graph representation, the differential network predicts which nodes were intervened upon
219   (Figure 2, right). Its architecture is composed of an axial-attention layer, followed by a linear pro-
220   jection. Following S EA, the axial attention layer attends separately along the rows and columns
221   of the graph’s adjacency matrix. This operation is equivalent to self-attention along all nodes in
222   the “outgoing edge” direction, with the “incoming edges” as a batch dimension, followed by the
223   opposite. We use pre-layer normalization on each self-attention, followed by dropout and residual
224   connections:
225                              h = h + Dropout(Self-Attn(LayerNorm(h))).                               (3)
226
      After the axial attention, we mean over all “incoming edges” and linearly project to binary node-
227
      level predictions, where a normalized 1 indicates an intervention target, and 0 indicates otherwise.
228

230   3.3   I MPLEMENTATION AND TRAINING DETAILS
231
232
      For the causal featurizer, we used a frozen, pretrained aggregator from (Wu et al., 2024), which
      corresponded to the FCI-based marginal estimates (Spirtes et al., 1995). The differential network
233
      was implemented with one axial attention layer, following the same architecture as the pretrained
234
      aggregator, with twice the model dimension (paired graphs). We swept over the number of layers
235   (1-4) on synthetic data and found that a single layer performed the best. In terms of model design,
236   we ablated replacing the attention architecture with a multi-layer perceptron (no communication
237   between nodes, Table 4), as well as other components (Table 7) and summary statistics (Table 8).
238   Due to the large graph sizes (adjacency matrices up to 10002 ), standard 1D attention was intractable.
239   Following S EA, we used inverse covariance as the global statistic on synthetic data, and correlation
240   on transcriptomics data.
241   We trained separate differential networks on synthetic and real data. On synthetic data, we used a
242   batch size of 16 and the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) with a learning rate of 1e-4
243   (identical to S EA). On real data, we started from the synthetic C DN checkpoint, and then used a
244   batch size of 2 with a learning rate of 1e-5, due to the larger graph sizes. All models took around
245   4-10 hours to train on a single A6000 GPU.

      3.4   T HEORETICAL CONTEXT
248
249   This paper primarily describes a causality-inspired approach towards perturbation target prediction.
250   Contrary to causal discovery algorithms that are self-contained and provably sound under standard
251   assumptions, we cannot “prove” that a pretrained model extracts correct graphs on real data, as the
252   true graphs are unknown. Our evaluation focuses solely on perturbation target prediction, as the
253   ground truth is known, by (experimental) design. To contextualize the predictions of our model,
254   we provide brief sketches with regards to our model’s capacity to map paired graph features to
255
      intervention targets. We defer formal claims to Appendix A.
256   Claim 3.1 (Hard interventions, informal). Given a causal graphical model G = (V, E) and in-
257   tervention targets I ⊆ V , let E ′ denote the adjacency matrix of mutilated graph G′ after hard
258   intervention on I. An axial attention layer is well-specified to map (E, E ′ ) to I.
259   Claim 3.2 (Soft interventions, informal). Let G be a graphical causal model associated with data
260   distribution PX , and let P̃X be the interventional distribution after soft intervention on I ⊊ V . Let
261   R, R′ denote the correlation matrices and let Σ, Σ′ denote the covariance matrices of X, X̃. An
262   axial attention layer is well-specified to map (R, R′ , Σ, Σ′ ) to I.

284   4.1   B IOLOGICAL EXPERIMENTS
285
286   Datasets We evaluate C DN on five Perturb-seq (Dixit et al., 2016) datasets (genetic perturbations)
287   from Replogle et al. (2022) and Nadig et al. (2024); as well as two Sci-Plex (Srivatsan et al., 2020)
288   datasets (chemical perturbations) from McFaline-Figueroa et al. (2024). Each dataset is a real-valued
289   matrix of gene expression levels: the number of examples M is the number of cells, the number of
290   variables N is the number of genes, and each entry is a log-normalized count of how many copies
291   of gene j was measured from cell i (Figure 3A). In Perturb-seq datasets, we aim to recover the gene
292   whose promoter was targeted by the CRISPR guide, and in Sci-Plex datasets, we aim to identify the
293
      gene that corresponds to the drug’s intended target.
294   To ensure high quality labels, we filtered perturbations to those that induced over 10 differentially-
295   expressed genes (statistically significant change, compared to control), of which the true target
296   should be present. This is to exclude perturbations with insufficient cells (low statistical power),
297   with minimal to no effect (uninteresting), and those that did not achieve the desired effect (CRISPR
298   efficacy is not guaranteed). For evaluation, we limited the set of candidate targets to the top 1000
299
      differentially expressed genes, ranked by log-fold change per perturbation. If fewer genes were
      differentially expressed, we randomly sampled additional candidates until we reached a minimum
300
      of 100 genes. Finally, we also stratify genetic perturbations based on whether the target is triv-
301
      ially identifiable as the gene with the largest log-fold change (“trivial” vs. “non-trivial”). This is
302   because genetic perturbations are highly specific by design, but also constitute the largest single-
303   cell perturbation datasets. The final data statistics are shown in Table 1, with additional details in
304   Appendix B.1.

      Evaluation We consider two splits: seen and unseen cell lines (Figure 3B). In the former, models
      may be trained on approximately half of the perturbations from each cell line, and are evaluated on
308
      the unseen perturbations. In the latter, we hold out one cell line at a time, and models may be trained
309
      on data from the remaining cell lines. Note that not all baselines can be evaluated on unseen cell
310   lines. To ensure that our train and test splits are sufficiently distinct, we cluster perturbations based
311   on their log-fold change and assign each cluster to the same split.

      of the data that render standard metrics less meaningful. Not all genes can be mapped to the base-
314

315

316   feld et al., 2023). Therefore, an “incorrectly” predicted target (e.g. out of 1000 genes) may not

                 recovered within the top k candidates). This value ranges from 0 to 1, where 1 is best.

      Baselines We compare to two classes of algorithms related to biological perturbations. Forward
339   inference models predict the effects of perturbations, and perturbation targets can be ranked by
340   comparing the inferred effect of each candidate to the interventional data.
341
342                  • G EARS (Roohani et al., 2023) is a graph neural network that predicts the effects of unseen
343                    genetic perturbations, using the gene ontology knowledge graph (Ashburner et al., 2000)
344                    to model the relationship between genes. We trained G EARS on each cell line separately,
345                    and predicted the effects of perturbing every expressed gene. Then we ranked candidates
346
                       based on cosine similarity to the interventional data.
347                  • G ENE P T (Chen & Zou, 2023) is a set of natural language-based embeddings that have been
348                    shown to achieve state-of-the-art performance on unseen perturbation effect prediction with
349                    simple downstream models (Märtens et al., 2024). We concatenated each perturbation’s
350                    gene embedding to the log-fold change of the top 5000 highly-variable genes (Wolf et al.,
351
                       2018) and trained a logistic regression model on each cell line to predict true vs. decoy
                       perturbations. Candidates were ranked based on predicted probability.
352
353   Predictive models directly predict perturbation targets from data as a classification task.
354
355                  • L INEAR and M LP take as input the mean expression of all perturbation targets, plus the
356                    top 2000 highly-variable genes (Wolf et al., 2018). They are trained to predict a binary
357                    label for each gene on each cell line independently.
358                  • PDG RAPHER (Gonzalez et al., 2024) is a graph neural network that predicts the pertur-
359                    bation targets of genetic or chemical perturbations, on seen or unseen cell lines, with the
360                    human reference interactome (Luck et al., 2020) as the knowledge graph.
361
362   All baselines were run with their official implementations and/or latest releases. For more details,
363   please see Appendix C.1.

406   entially expressed (Figures 6 and 7). On the other hand, existing models for biological perturbations

408

411   Datasets We generated 120 synthetic test datasets with hard and soft interventions. To generate
412
      observational data, we sampled Erdős-Rényi graphs with N = 10, 20 nodes and E = N, 2N ex-
      pected edges; causal mechanism parameters; and observations of each variable, in topological order.
413
      We sampled 3N distinct subsets of 1-3 nodes (N each) as intervention targets. For hard interven-
414
      tions, we set x ← z, where z is uniform. To emulate transcriptomics data, in which perturbation
415   effects are measured in fold change, we introduce soft interventions: x ← cf (πx ), where c is a
416   positive scaling factor, with equal probability c ≶ 1. For our synthetic training set, we generated
417   approximately 4000 datasets following the training set of S EA, with hard interventions only. Full
418   details are available in Appendix B.2.

420   Baselines We compare against discrete and continuous causal discovery algorithms for unknown
421   interventions. U T-I GSP (Squires et al., 2020) infers causal graphs and unknown targets by greedily
422   selecting the permutation of variables that minimizes their proposed score function. D CDI (Brouil-
423   lard et al., 2020) and BACADI (Hägele et al., 2023) are continuous causal discovery algorithms
424   that fit generative models to the data, where the causal graph and intervention targets are model
425   parameters. The -G and -D SF suffixes on D CDI correspond to Gaussian and deep sigmoidal flow
426   parametrizations of the likelihood. BACADI is evaluated here using the fully-connected implemen-
427   tation (better performance), with the linear version in Table 11. Its -E and -M suffixes indicate
428
      empirical (standard) and mixture (bootstrap) variants.

      Results On synthetic data, C DN achieves high performance across intervention types and data-
474   generating mechanisms (Table 4), while running in seconds (Table 9). As an ablation study, we
475   investigated replacing the differential network with a multi-layer perceptron, which does not model
476   interactions between edges. On the hard interventions, the M LP is sufficient for predicting inter-
477   vention targets, perhaps by abusing marginal variances (Reisach et al., 2021) or other node-level
478   artifacts of synthetic data. Once we consider soft interventions, however, the original C DN formula-
479   tion significantly outperforms the M LP variant – indicating that graph-level information is essential.
480   D CI is the best baseline, which is encouraging, as it was designed for the edge-centric version of our
481   task, with the intuition that detecting edge differences is easier than reproducing the entire causal
482   graph. It performs particularly well on linear data, which align with its assumptions, and less well
483   on non-linear data. Surprisingly, most other baselines perform poorly at recovering intervention
484   targets. In the case of D CDI and BAC A DI, this may be because it is hard to select a single sparsity
485   threshold for varying sizes of intervention sets, and to balance the regularizer with the generative
      modeling objective.

      5   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      We presented C DN, a causal discovery-powered approach for predicting perturbation targets in the
489   context of single-cell transcriptomics. C DN uses an amortized causal discovery algorithm to repre-
490   sent a pair of observational and interventional datasets in terms of their data-generating mechanisms,
491   and then trains a classifier to identify variables whose conditional independencies have changed. Our
492   approach achieves the state-of-the-art in predicting perturbation targets over seven transcriptomics
493   datasets, compared to a variety of perturbation modeling baselines. C DN also surpasses causal dis-
494   covery algorithms for predicting unknown targets across synthetic settings, while only requiring a
495   fraction of the runtime. Nonetheless, there is substantial space for improvement, so we hope that
496   this work and the associated datasets will inform future method development for these biological
497
      applications.

      A    T HEORETICAL INTUITION
757
758
      While this paper focuses on biological applications, we would like to show that our model is well-
759   specified, i.e. it has the capacity to predict correct intervention targets, given certain inputs.
760
761   Preliminary note Continuous causal discovery works typically rely on the universality of their
762   architectures for consistency (Brouillard et al., 2020), so this analysis also focuses on computational
763   capacity. Theoretically, it is impossible to “guarantee” that high-dimensional representations from
764   learned model contain or do not contain certain information. This can be tested empirically through
765   probing, e.g. querying for grammatical structure in language models (Vulić et al., 2020), but cannot
766   be “proven” in the traditional sense. Thus, we believe that the empirical performance of our model
767   on real datasets is its primary contribution, but provide these explanations for interested readers. In
768
      this analysis, we assume that the “causal” representations h contains enough information both to
      retain global statistics and recover the true graph E. The former is reasonable due to high model
769
      capacity, and the latter is based on high empirical performance in graph reconstruction (Wu et al.,
770
      2024). We emphasize that the latter is an empirical judgment, which may not hold on all datasets in
771   practice.
772
773   Axial attention architecture Our differential network is implemented using an axial attention
774   layer, which is composed of two self-attention layers (one along each axis of the adjacency matrix)
775   and a feed-forward network. For simplicity, we follow prior work Yun et al. (2019) and ignore layer
776   normalization and dropout.
777
      Our inputs h ∈ R2d×N ×N are 2d-dimension features, which represent a pair of N × N causal
778   graphs. We use h·,j to denote a length N row for a fixed column j, and hi,· to denote a length N
779   column for a fixed row i. The axial attention layer implements:
780
                         Attnrow (h·,j ) = h·,j + WO WV h·,j · σ (WK h·,j )T WQ h·,j ,

781

782
783                            FFN(h) = h + W2 · ReLU(W1 · h + b1 ) + b2 ,
784
785   where WO ∈ R2d×2d , WV , WK , WQ ∈ R2d×2d , W2 ∈ R2d×m , W1 ∈ Rm×2d , b2 ∈ R2d , b1 ∈
786   Rm , and m is the FFN hidden dimension. We have omitted the i and j subscripts on the W s,
787   but they use separate parameters. Any self attention can take on the identity mapping by setting
788   WO , WV , WK , WQ to 2d × 2d matrices of zeros.

845   Supporting both intervention types Recall that the final output layer is a linear projection from
846   2d to 1. If this layer implements a simple summation over all 2d, the predicted intervention targets
847   are consistent with both hard and soft interventions. For soft interventions, E = E ′ , so the hard
848   intervention dimensions will be 0. Likewise, for hard interventions, both R and Σ will differ as the

1062
       without pretrained aggregator weights, global statistics, or marginal estimates. We find that pre-

1063

1064

1067   covariance pretrained weights) on synthetic data. The default summary statistic (M LP, A XIAL)
1068

1069

1070

1071   N × N × 2, where each embedding was initialized to a distinct copy of the original. However, the
1072   inverse covariance version still performed the best, perhaps since there is no mismatch between the
1073   pretraining and finetuning statistics.
