011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            LLM-as-a-Judge presents a promising alternative to human evaluators across var-
014            ious tasks, but inherent biases, especially position bias — a tendency to favor
015            solutions based on their position in the prompt — have compromised its effec-
016
               tiveness. Our study introduces a systematic framework to examine position bias
               in pairwise comparisons, focusing on repetition stability, position consistency,
017
               and preference fairness. This research significantly contributes to the field by
018
               introducing new concepts for understanding position bias and providing a multi-
019            dimensional framework for evaluations. We conducted experiments with 12 LLM
020            judges across MTBench and DevBench, covering 22 tasks and approximately 40
021            solution-generating models — candidates, resulting in over 100,000 evaluation
022            instances. Our findings confirm that position bias in capable LLM judges is not
023            due to random chances, along with notable variations observed across judges and
024            tasks. Moreover, position bias is weakly influenced by the length of prompt com-
025            ponents but significantly impacted by the quality gap between solutions. These
026            insights can help optimize judge model selections, improve benchmark design,
027
               and inform future research on debiasing strategies, ultimately enhancing the reli-
               ability of LLM judges.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      In recent years, Large Language Models (LLMs) have emerged as evolutionary technologies, gather-
      ing global interest and stimulating substantial research into their applications. Evaluating large lan-
034
      guage models (LLMs) has received increasing attention due to their advancing capabilities across di-
035
      verse fields. While human assessment is considered the gold standard for aligning with human pref-
036   erences, it lacks scalability and reproducibility in extensive evaluations (Zeng et al., 2023; Karpinska
037   et al., 2021). To automate evaluations and reduce reliance on costly human evaluators, the LLM-
038   as-a-Judge methodology emerged as a promising alternative across various tasks. Despite a high
039   level of agreement with human judgments(Zheng et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2023b),
040   inherent biases, especially position bias, have undermined the accuracy, fairness, and reliability of
041   these LLM evaluators.
042   Position bias refers to the tendency of LLM judges to favor certain positions within prompt com-
043   ponents rather than the content itself, as shown in Fig. 1. This bias is prevalent across all categories
044   of LLM-as-a-Judge, including pointwise, listwise, and pairwise evaluations (Qin et al., 2024), for
045   any score-based and relation-based settings (Li et al., 2023d). Consequently, the reliability of LLM
046   judges becomes questionable when they exhibit position bias. However, addressing this issue is
047   highly complicated due to the varying performance of LLM judges across different tasks (Khan
048   et al., 2024; Chua et al., 2024). Moreover, the limited understanding of the bias itself has exacer-
049   bated the problem.
050   Prior studies on position bias implicitly assume that this bias is not due to random variations. In
051   other words, these studies assume that LLM judges produce consistent results across repeated trials.
052   However, without validating this assumption, evaluations may become less reliable. As shown in
053   Fig. 1, the occurrence of position bias can be observed by chance even for the same set of tasks and
      candidate models. Thus, it is unclear whether the observed position bias actually arises from the

055   the position bias of LLM judges, it is necessary for the judges to be stable across repetitions to yield

      Another key aspect of position bias, preference fairness, has also been under-explored by previous
081   works. Most studies have primarily focused on position consistency to measure how frequently
082   position bias occurs across a set of evaluation instances. However, the direction of biased preference
083   (i.e., primacy or recency) is also insightful. Fig. 2 illustrates cases where a pair of judgments with
084   swapped prompts become position consistent, inconsistent, primacy-preferred, or recency-preferred.
085   We measure preference fairness by analyzing the distribution of these preference directions across
086   a set of evaluations. Specifically, judgments are considered to have a fair preference if they are
087   position consistent or if the preference directions are evenly distributed. In contrast, an LLM judge
088   is considered to have an unfair preference if it consistently favors candidate model solution that
089
      appear either first or last in the prompt. Both fair and unfair judge models can provide valuable
      insights into model properties, applications, and strategies for improvement.

      To enhance the understanding of position bias and LLM-as-a-Judge, we propose a comprehensive
109   framework designed to dissect and analyze position bias within the context of pairwise compara-
110   tive assessment. We focus on this context because LLM judges demonstrate superior performance
111   in making pairwise comparisons over other settings like pairwise scoring (Zheng et al., 2024b; Liusie
112   et al., 2024), making it a practically preferable choice for investigations.
113
      Our contribution can be summarized as following: (1) introducing a systematic framework for eval-
114
      uating LLM-as-a-Judge’s position bias in terms of Repetition Stability, Position Consistency, and
115   Preference Fairness; (2) identifying Judge-level, Candidate-level, and Task-level factors that af-
116   fect position bias; and (3) providing insights into future benchmark design and paving the way for
117   effective mitigation strategies for position bias.

      2     M ETHODS & D EFINTIONS
121
      LLM-as-a-Judge can be classified into two categories: score-based and relation-based assessments.
122
      Our study focuses on relation-based assessment, specifically under the pairwise comparison setting,
123
      for several reasons. First, the pairwise paradigm is a popular and classic scenario that has influenced
124   a wide range of fields (Qin et al., 2024). Second, as noted by Liu et al. (2024), LLM and human
125   evaluations are more aligned in the context of pairwise comparison than any of the score-based
126   assessments. Additionally, numerous studies have shown that pairwise comparative assessment out-
127   performs other judging settings in terms of position consistency(Zheng et al., 2024b; Liusie et al.,
128   2024). Finally, pairwise comparisons can be extended to more complicated frameworks of relation-
129   based assessment, such as listwise comparisons, through some advanced ranking algorithms (Qin
130   et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). Therefore, analyzing position bias in this context can provide a deeper
131   understanding of its fundamental nature.
132   In the context of pairwise comparative assessments, LLM judges are prompted to choose the better
133   candidate solution to the task question. To accommodate the possibility of a tie, various option
134   modes are introduced. Two-Option mode restricts judges to choosing the better solution between
135   two options, labeled {A} for the first and {B} for the second as shown in Fig. 1. Three-Option mode
136   adds an additional choice, {C}, allowing judges to indicate a tie if neither solution is preferable.
137   Four-Option mode further expands the choices, with {C} representing a both good tie and {D} a
138
      both bad tie. In our experiments, we employed Three-Option mode for MTBench (Zheng et al.,
      2024b) and Two-Option mode for DevBench (Li et al., 2024), aligning with their respective original
139
      frameworks. These option modes are explicitly specified in the system prompts to clearly guide the
140
      decision-making process of the LLM judges.
141
142   To set up our experiment, as shown in Fig. 1, the system prompt, option mode, task question, and
143   solutions from two candidate models (original prompt) are presented to the LLM judges to select
144
      the winning solution. The experiment is conducted in a double-blind setting. The identities of the
      candidate models are hidden from the LLM judges, and the candidate models are unaware that their
145
      solutions will be compared to another model when answering the question. Then, the prompt with
146
      solutions in a swapped position (swapped prompt) is given to the same judge again, which results in a
147   judgment pair. As shown in Fig. 2, if the LLM judge consistently favors the same solution regardless
148   of the swapped position, it is considered position consistent with a fair preference. Conversely, if the
149   LLM judge selects different winners, position bias is observed, with the preference direction being
150   either primacy or recency.
151
      To systematically investigate position bias in pairwise comparative judging scenarios, we evaluate
152
      LLM judges using three key metrics: Repetition Stability (RS), Position Consistency (P C), and
153   Preference Fairness (P F ). While P C has been widely adopted in prior research, we introduce RS
154   to validate the reliability of LLM judges and P F to provide a more comprehensive assessment. Fur-
155   ther analysis examines the impact of judge-level, candidate-level, and task-level factors on position
156   bias. Therefore, for each judge, candidate, and task, we calculate RS, P C, and P F to thoroughly
157   explore the variance in position bias and its impacting factors.
158

189   Preference Fairness (P F ) is another crucial characteristic of position bias measuring the extent to
190   which judge models favor certain solution positions. In the pairwise comparative setup, an LLM
191   judge may exhibit a preference for either primacy or recency. These terms replace the more verbose
192   “preference for the first/second position” used in previous studies, ensuring clarity and generaliza-
193   tion for future research. The examples of such preferences are demonstrated in Fig. 2.
194   Position consistent judgments are fair in preference. The measurements become more complex
195   for position inconsistent cases. Previous studies proposed two common ways to measure the pref-
196   erence fairness. A straightforward way is to count the primacy-preferred and recency-preferred
197   judgment pairs, which we termed as primacy-count-number (pcn) and recency-count-number (rcn).
198   The counts are then normalized by the total number of prompt pairs (Zheng et al., 2024b; Zhu et al.,
199   2023b). However, the sensitivity of this measurement highly depends on the size of dataset,
200   making comparisons across datasets unreliable, especially when the number of questions and in-
201
      stances varies for each task.
202   Alternatively, instead of normalizing over the complete dataset, studies like (Li et al., 2023c; Liusie
203   et al., 2024) treat position inconsistent evaluation instances independently. They calculate the per-
204   centages of primacy-preferred and recency-preferred judgment pairs relative to the total number of
205   position inconsistent pairs. We denote these as inconsistent primacy rates (ipr) and inconsistent
206   recency rates (irr), where ipr + irr = 1. However, this approach is problematic because it over-
207
      looks the fact that “position consistent judgments are also position fair,” which can lead to overly
      penalizing highly consistent LLM-judges. For instance, if only one out of a hundred pairs is in-
208
      consistent, this method would classify it as 100% primacy-preferred or recency-preferred. However,
209
      this outcome should be distinguished from cases where all one hundred pairs are inconsistent and
210   biased toward one position, which would also result in 100% preference unfairness.
211

      To investigate the factors influencing position bias in LLM judges, we categorized these factors into
251
      three groups: Judge-level, Candidate-level, and Task-level factors. Each group includes specific
252   factors, that we hypothesize, may impact position bias, which we explore through a series of exper-
253   iments. Table 1 lists the five factors analyzed in this study. By design, our framework allows for the
254   integration and assessment of additional influencing factors.
255
      Among the influencing factors we identified, we chose “familial property” for Judge-level factors
256
      instead of model sizes and training specifics, which are often proprietary and not publicly accessible
257
      for the models involved in our experiments. The model family grouping reflects similar model sizes
258   and training specifics of the model members. The familial categories of the models used in our
259   studies are (1) GPT, (2) Claude, and (3) Gemini allowing for straightforward grouping by company
260   and version. However, we also observed that all GPT-4 and later models, along with Claude-3.5-
261   Sonnet, Claude-3-Opus, and Gemini-1.5-pro, form a group of the “most capable” models sharing
262   similar judging capabilities. More details and discussions about the familial property can be found
263   in Appendix Sec. C, where we examined the LLM mutual agreements to study the similarities and
264   differences across families.
265   Answer quality gap, based on our findings, is highly impactful yet under-explored in prior research.
266   We define the quality of a candidate’s answer/solution by how effectively it addresses the question.
267   Consequently, the answer quality gap refers to the disparity in quality between the solutions from
268   two candidate models to the same question and is considered the Candidate-level (i.e., solution
269   provider) factor. Ideally, when a reliable LLM judge is presented with a question and corresponding
      solution pairs, it would prefer the higher-quality solution, where the corresponding candidate is


                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      denoted as the winner selected by the judge. Following this assumption, we measure the answer
271   quality gap by the win rates between two candidates on a set of tasks and questions. We regard a tie
272   judgment as ‘half-win’ for both candidates when calculating its win rate over another, depending on
273   option mode. If position bias occurs, the winner may be inconsistent when the order of candidate
274   solutions is swapped in the query. Therefore, we categorize the LLM judgments into three groups:
275   cases where the same winner is consistently chosen in both the original and swapped queries (termed
276   “consistent wins”), cases where both responses are deemed equal (termed “consistent ties”), and
277   cases where different winners are selected after the solutions are swapped. We denote these counts
278   as the number of consistent wins (Cw ), consistent ties (Ct ), and inconsistent judgment pairs (CI ),
279
      respectively. Following Zheng et al. (2024b), we count inconsistent judgment pairs also as ties, or
      ‘half-wins’, to ensure calculations are feasible in all cases for the subsequent computations.
280
281   Inspired by Zheng et al. (2024b), we define the overall win rate (owr) of a model’s solution over
282   the other as: owr = n1 [Cw + 21 (Ct + CI )], where n is the total number of judgment pairs. Then
283   the answer quality gap (δq ) is calculated as δq = |owr − 0.5|, where 0.5 is the overall win rate
284   when we only have ties. In contrast to using only consistent win rate (calculated as Cnwc , where nc
285   is the number of position consistent judgment pairs) to quantify δq (Zheng et al., 2024b; Li et al.,
286   2023b; Raina et al., 2024), the adoption of overall win rate incorporates all data points and captures
287   the “comparable quality” cases, where responses in similar quality might lead to position biased
288
      judgments, a scenario that the consistent win rate might overlook. More discussions can be found in
      Appendix. Sec. D.

294   3.1   E XPERIMENT S ETTINGS
295
296   In this study, we evaluated twelve up-to-date commercial models from the GPT (OpenAI, 2023),
297   Claude (Anthropic, 2024), and Gemini (Gemini Team, 2024) series using our framework. Addi-
298   tionally, three Llama models (Touvron et al., 2023), with parameters ranging from 7B to 13B, were
299   selected as open-source exemplars due to their popularity and recognized capability in reasoning
300   tasks. However, due to their limited performance on our tasks, as shown in Table 2, we focused our
301   investigation solely on more powerful closed-source models. We included the results of open-source
302
      models for completeness.
303   We adopted the modified MTBench (Zheng et al., 2024b) and DevBench (Li et al., 2024) datasets for
304   our study due to their demonstrated high human-LLM agreement and the reliability of state-of-the-
305   art LLMs on the evaluation tasks. We fixed one of the candidates as vicuna-13b-v1.3 for MTBench
306   and human for DevBench to serve as baselines, ensuring decent quality of responses.
307   MTBench consists of 30 candidate models, 8 tasks, and 10 questions per task; for DevBench, we
308   divide the general metric into more detailed ones and consider them as different tasks, resulting in
309   10 candidate models, 14 tasks, and 8 questions per task. We then paired solutions of these candidate
310   models with that of the baseline candidate for evaluation by the LLM judges. The prompt templates
311   we used are identical to those used in the benchmarks. More details about the models, tasks, and
312   prompts can be found in Appendix. Sec. H.
313   To compute repetition stability, we sampled 3 questions per task and 4 candidate models, paired
314   with baseline candidates, for each judge to evaluate across 3 repetitive trials. This resulted in 576
315   instances per judge for MTBench and 432 instances per judge for DevBench. The temperature
316   hyperparameter was set to 1 for all judge models to generate nontrivial results. To compute position
317   consistency and preference fairness, the number of instances increased to 4,800 and 2,240, covering
318   the entire MTBench and DevBench datasets. In total, approximately 100,000 evaluation instances
319   were analyzed in this study.
320   To identify significant factors contributing to position bias, we performed bidirectional stepwise re-
321   gression on data from the two benchmarks. We used variables such as average lengths of input,
322   output, and prompt; answer quality gap; LLM judge series; candidate identities; and task categories
323   to predict P C and P F , respectively. Each model prunes non-significant variables based on the
      Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) score. This process involves both forward selection and back-


                                                        6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      ward elimination, with each ”step” testing whether including or excluding a variable improves the
325   model’s AIC value. Further details about the process can be found in Appendix. Sec. F.

464   Position Bias Correlates to Answer Quality Gap Intuitively, the difficulty of judging a pair of
465   candidate answers is largely reflected by their difference in quality. In this study, as defined in
466   Section 2.2, we quantify the quality gap (δq ) between answer pairs by the overall win rate (owr).
467   Since an absolute tie has 0.5 owr, δp increases as owr extends from 0.5 to 0 or 1. Fig. 4(a) exhibits
468   a significant parabolic shape, indicating that P C is positively proportional to δq . This aligns with
469   our intuition that the answer pairs with larger quality disparities are easier to achieve consistency,
470
      whereas those of similar quality are difficult to judge, increasing the likelihood of position bias
      that leads to lower P C. The same relationship is observed for each individual judge and across
471
      benchmarks, as demonstrated in Appendix.Sec. E.
472
473   Similarly, as shown in Fig. 1(b), judgments generally become more preference fair as δq increases.
474   However, the extent is not as significant as for P C. Also, the relationship varies by judge, as some
475   LLMs maintain preference fairness regardless of δq . For example, as shown in Fig. 6, P F of
476
      GPT models centered closely around 0 consistently, whereas that of Claude and Gemini-pro models
      exhibit a conspicuous proportional relationship on MTBench.
477
478   These observations align with the right-arrow shape as demonstrated in Fig. 4(c), where there is
479   a general trend that judgments become preference fairer as position consistency increases. It also
480   justifies the reasonableness of our quantification of preference fairness, as highly position consistent
481   judges are not overly penalized and a perfect P C should result in P F = 0.
482   Together, we conclude that as the answer quality gap enlarges, judges generally become more po-
483   sition consistent and preference fair according to the regression curves. However, exceptions are
484   common, as shown by the individual scatter points of these figures. This indicates that though the
485   answer quality gap significantly influences the position bias of LLMs, other factors also play impor-
      tant roles. Therefore, built on our findings, future studies may have better control over the answer

489   Our investigation also helps to explain the observed verbosity/length bias (Liu et al., 2024; Kim
490
      et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2024b) and self-enhancement bias (Li et al., 2023c; Zheng et al., 2024b)
      of LLM judges. From our analysis, LLMs may prefer longer answers (verbosity/length bias) since
491
      more capable models usually generate longer solutions with higher qualities. Similarly, in the cases
492
      where the same LLM serves as both a candidate and a judge model, judges preferring the solutions
493   generated by themselves (self-enhancement bias) are usually the ones who generate responses of
494   higher quality (e.g., GPT-4). In both cases, the observed biased preference may be fundamentally
495   due to a prominent answer quality gap instead of length or self-identity.

      Position Bias is weakly Length-dependent We investigate the impact of three different lengths on
497
      the position bias of LLM judges: the length of the question (task input length), the solution length
498
      of candidate models (task output length), and the length of the entire prompt (prompt length). Since
499   the tokenization methods vary by models, we apply the length of the prompt component strings
500   for uniformity and scalability. The analysis from Fig. 4(d)(e)(f) shows that there is very weak
501   relationship between the lengths of prompt components and position bias. By stepwise regression,
502   the average task output length is only significant in predicting P F , adding a minimal change in
503   AIC as shown in Appendix Table. 4. Therefore, the impact of lengths on position bias is minimal
504   compared to answer quality gap and judge or task level variances.
505   Practical Implications for Benchmark Design We complement our investigations of position bias
506   with a LLM agreement/disagreement analysis in Appendix.Sec. C to gain further insights. In par-
507   ticular, the disagreement analysis helps identify instances where it is easy or difficult for judges to
508   reach a consensus, reflecting the complexity of the judgment task. Based on our findings regarding
509   the answer quality gap and LLM agreement/disagreement, this study offers practical implications
510   for designing evaluator benchmarks that control the difficulty of judging tasks. Our findings show
511   that the hardest-to-judge instances are those where (1) LLMs frequently disagree with each other,
512   (2) comparable win rates and minimal quality gaps exist between the two candidate models, and (3)
513
      significant position bias is exhibited by the majority of judges.
514   One qualitative analysis example of a difficult-to-judge instance comes from MTBench ques-
515   tion 120, which involved a math problem where both candidate models, Vicuna-13b-v1.3 and
516   WizardLM-30b, provided incorrect solutions. The problem was: (1) Given f (x) = 4x3 − 9x − 14,
517   find the value of f (2), and (2) Find x such that f (x) = 0. Vicuna-13b-v1.3 incorrectly applied the
518   quadratic formula to solve the cubic equation, while WizardLM-30b used an enumeration method
519
      but failed to identify the correct answer. This resulted in a 4{A}-3{B}-5{C} choice pattern among
      the twelve judges, illustrating a typical case of significant disagreement.
520
521   We observed that some judges exhibited hallucinations, making false positive judgments or ignoring
522   the reference answer. Others preferred either correctness (choosing {C} as a tie) or methodological
523   soundness (choosing {B}, favoring WizardLM-30b). In the swapped order setting, 9 out of 12
524   judges displayed position bias. This highlights the challenge of judging solutions with minimal
525
      quality gaps, leading to greater position bias and subjectivity.
526   The key takeaway is that reducing hard-to-evaluate, highly subjective instances can improve bench-
527   mark design. Likewise, minimizing trivial cases where judges easily agree would enhance the bench-
528   mark’s robustness. Clearer and more specific evaluation criteria could also help resolve ambiguity
529   in difficult instances.

531   5   C ONCLUSION
532
533   In conclusion, this paper proposes a systematic, scalable framework for evaluating the position bias
534   of LLM judges in pairwise comparative scenarios in terms of repetition stability, position consis-
535   tency, and preference fairness. Through comprehensive evaluations of 12 judges on two benchmarks
536   across 22 tasks with over 100,000 evaluation instances, we observe significant variations of position
537   bias across judges and tasks. We also discover that position bias is weakly length-dependent but sig-
538   nificantly impacted by the answer quality gap. Our findings enhance the understanding of position
539   bias, paving the way for more effective mitigation strategies and more robust and reliable evaluation
      systems.

884   B.1   LLM- AS - A -J UDGE
885
      In recent years, Large Language Models (LLMs) have emerged as a transformative technology, gar-
886
      nering global attention and stimulating substantial research into their applications. For evaluative
887
      tasks, particularly subjective ones, human assessment is considered the gold standard due to its
888   comprehensive and open-ended nature (Zeng et al., 2023). However, it lacks scalability and repro-
889   ducibility (Karpinska et al., 2021).
890
891
      As a result, LLMs have increasingly been used as substitutes for human evaluators across various
      Natural Language Generation (NLG) domains and tasks (Zheng et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2024; Chiang
892
      & Lee, 2023b; Liusie et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a; Kocmi &
893
      Federmann, 2023b; Ji et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Zeng et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Shen et al.,
894   2023; Kocmi & Federmann, 2023a; Chiang & Lee, 2023a; Karpinska et al., 2021; Dubois et al.,
895   2024; Kim et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2023; Chang et al., 2023; Zhong et al., 2023; Chia et al., 2023;
896   Deb & Das, 2023; Ye et al., 2024; Min et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023d;c;b; Lu et al., 2024), including
897   open-ended story generation (Chiang & Lee, 2023a), adversarial attacks (Chiang & Lee, 2023b),
898   summarization (Karpinska et al., 2021), machine translation (Kocmi & Federmann, 2023a), and
899   instruction following (Zeng et al., 2023).
900   These LLM evaluators, known as LLM-as-a-Judge, have attracted significant interest within both
901   academic and industrial circles (Zheng et al., 2024b;a; Pezeshkpour & Hruschka, 2023; Chiang &
902   Lee, 2023b; Shen et al., 2023; Liusie et al., 2024; Qin et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023; Huang et al.,
903   2024; Zhu et al., 2023b; Raina et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024b; Wang et al., 2023b; Liu et al.,
904   2024; Zeng et al., 2023; Khan et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b;d; Shi et al., 2024;
905   Li et al., 2023c; Chen et al., 2024c; Chua et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024a). As LLMs have made
906   content generation significantly easier, the volume of generated responses has increased, making it
907   impractical to rely solely on human evaluation. Therefore, cost-effective LLM judges are needed to
908
      assess these responses efficiently. LLM-as-a-Judge is typically employed in Q&A evaluation tasks,
      where the LLM judge is prompted to evaluate the quality of responses, usually generated by models,
909
      answering the questions.
910
911   In many of these tasks, LLM judges have shown a high level of agreement with human evaluators
912   (Zheng et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2023b), yet in some tasks, they are less effective,
913   largely due to inherent biases (Shen et al., 2023; Chiang & Lee, 2023b; Zheng et al., 2024a). Even
914
      in cases where agreement is high, judgments may still suffer from biases.
915   When employing LLM-as-a-Judge, various types of judging are available, which can be categorized
916   either by the scale or comparative method. From the scale perspective, LLM-as-a-Judge can involve
917   pointwise, listwise, or pairwise assessment (Qin et al., 2024). By comparative method, it can be
      either score-based or relation-based (Li et al., 2023d).


                                                       17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      For example, pointwise scoring (Kim et al., 2023) lets the LLM judge score the response of one
919   model to a question at a time based on some evaluating metrics. Pairwise/Listwise Scoring (Karpin-
920   ska et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2024a; Zheng et al., 2024b) prompts the LLM judges
921   to score a pair/list of model-generated answers. Listwise Ranking is another relation-based assess-
922   ment that, instead of giving a score, requires the LLM judge to rank a list of responses following
923   some specified order (e.g., from best to worst). Pairwise comparative assessment (Karpinska et al.,
924   2021; Chen et al., 2024a; Zheng et al., 2024b), on the other hand, asks the LLM judge to select the
925   superior response between a given pair, usually conducted in a double-blind manner: the generating
926   model remains unknown to the judge, and the judging model remains anonymous to the answer
927
      generator.
928   Except for pointwise evaluation, all forms of LLM-as-a-Judge suffer—or are susceptible to suffer-
929   ing—from position bias due to the intrinsic nature of position and comparison within the prompt
930   structure.

      B.2   P OSITION B IAS
935
936
937   Previous studies have discovered and investigated multiple types of biases, such as position bias
938   (Zheng et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024a; 2023; Chia et al., 2023; Chiang & Lee,
939   2023b; Khan et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2023b; Zhong et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2024a; Deb & Das,
940   2023; Zeng et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023c; Qin et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023b; Shi et al., 2024;
941   Liusie et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2023; Karpinska et al., 2021; Hou et al., 2024;
942   Kocmi & Federmann, 2023b; Pezeshkpour & Hruschka, 2023; Shen et al., 2023; Chiang & Lee,
943   2023a; Kocmi & Federmann, 2023a; Chen et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2023c;d; Raina et al., 2024),
944
      verbosity/length bias (Zheng et al., 2024b; Liu et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2023), self-enhancement bias
      (Li et al., 2023c; Zheng et al., 2024b), selection bias (Zheng et al., 2024a; Pezeshkpour & Hruschka,
945
      2023), and contextual bias (Liu et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024).
946
947   Among these, position bias stands out as particularly significant, permeating a wide array of tasks
948   and affecting judge models, including open-source (Li et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2024c), proprietary
949   commercial ones (OpenAI, 2023; Anthropic, 2024; Gemini Team, 2024), and fine-tuned models
950
      (Huang et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023c; Zhu et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2023b; Kim et al., 2023).
951   To clarify, the aforementioned and following position bias refers to the concept within the context
952   of LLM-as-a-Judge, meaning that LLM judge tends to favor responses based on their position in the
953   prompt rather than their content. For example, in a pairwise comparative assessment scenario, if
954   the LLM judge consistently selects the first response as superior even after switching the order of the
955   two responses (same position, but different content), then a position bias occurs. Some other studies
956
      also use the term position bias (Ko et al., 2020; Aslanyan & Porwal, 2019; Blunch, 1984; Raghubir
      & Valenzuela, 2006), but in this research, our interest lies solely in the position bias specific to
957
      LLM-as-a-Judge.
958
959   Position bias is arguably the most prevalent and impactful bias among all. Chua et al. (2024) notes
960   that their Bias-Augmented Consistency Training (BCT), an unsupervised fine-tuning scheme de-
961   signed to promote consistent reasoning across prompts with and without biasing features, improves
962
      Chain-of-Thought (Wei et al., 2022) performance over self-training controls for all biases except po-
      sition bias. Furthermore, Khan et al. Khan et al. (2024) point out that LLM judges are less confident
963
      when exhibiting position bias, and addressing this bias is highly complex due to varying confidence
964
      levels across judges and tasks.
965
966   Moreover, there is ongoing debate over whether selection bias originates from position bias.
967   Pezeshkpour & Hruschka (2023) argue that LLMs are sensitive to the ordering of options in Multiple
968
      Choice Questions (MCQs), confirming that position bias contributes to this sensitivity. Raina et al.
      (2024) contest this view, asserting that selection bias stems less from position bias and more from
969
      token bias, which represents an inherent challenge for LLMs and contributes to poor robustness.
970
971   All of these findings underscore the critical importance of addressing the issue of position bias when
      employing LLM-as-a-Judge.

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       B.3   D EAL WITH P OSITION B IAS
973
974
975
976    Intuitively, position bias emerges because LLMs are sensitive to changes, especially positional
977    changes, in prompts (Zheng et al., 2024b; Raina et al., 2024; Pezeshkpour & Hruschka, 2023; Zhao
978    et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2023a). Also, LLM judges are vulnerable to attacks (Raina et al., 2024; Chen
979    et al., 2024b; Shi et al., 2024).
980    Parse data with position bias There are many ways to deal with the position bias. The naive way
981    is to exclude the inconsistent judgments(i.e. if the LLM judge gives a positionally biased judgment
982    on a pair or list of model responses) (Zheng et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2024b; Wang et al., 2023b; Li
983    et al., 2023b). While this ensures consistent and reliable remaining judgments, it does not resolve the
984    fundamental problem. Moreover, if the LLM judge is highly biased, this method discards valuable
985    evaluation instances and information, making it an ineffective and somewhat desperate measure.
986    To take the positionally inconsistent evaluations into account, one may either take an average for
987    scoring-based judging (Zheng et al., 2024b; Raina et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2023b)
988    or regard inconsistency as a half-win or tie for relation-based judging (Zheng et al., 2024b; Li et al.,
989    2023c) after swapping the order of model responses in the prompt. For instance, in a pairwise scoring
990    scenario, if model A receives a score of 8 when put in the first position and 4 when put in the second
991    position, the overall score for it compared to model B would be 6; in the pairwise comparative
992
       case, if model A wins when put in the first position but then lose when put in the second position,
       it counts as a tie or half-win for both model A and model B. The latter way of swapping + tie is
993
       proposed because intuitively the position bias is more likely to occur when the model responses
994
       get evaluated share a similar quality in terms of the evaluating metric. Our quantitative study also
995    verifies this intuition, evidenced by the fact the LLM judge’s positional consistency (the percentage
996    of positionally consistent pairs of judgment) is positively proportional to the answer quality gap
997    between the model responses.
998
       To save the expense of running the experiments more than once using swapped order, in practice,
999
       many of these studies also support a random-shuffle option in their code settings such that the base-
1000
       line model for comparison does not remain in a fixed position.
1001
1002   Solution attempts Due to the significance of position bias, more sophisticated and advanced ap-
1003   proaches have emerged to solve position bias as well, including bootstrapping (Hou et al., 2024),
1004
       split-and-merge (Li et al., 2023d), and multi-agent discussion (Li et al., 2023c; Khan et al., 2024).
       However, these methods are either costly and time-consuming (e.g., multi-agent discussion and re-
1005
       view to reach agreement) or ineffective.
1006
1007   Furthermore,Liu et al. (2024) suggests that existing calibration techniques designed to reduce bias,
1008   including context decomposition (Li et al., 2023d), order permutation (Wang et al., 2023b; Zheng
1009   et al., 2024a), ensembling (Li et al., 2023a), and batch calibration (Zhou et al., 2024), are insufficient
1010
       to align LLM evaluators, even with supervised data.
1011   Thus, position bias is a pervasive, significantly impactful, and challenging problem to solve.
1012
       Understand before solving We observe that the existing methods maybe ineffective or not enough
1013
       satisfactory because there is a lack of understanding of the position bias. Although a variety of
1014   studies have researched this type of bias in LLM-as-a-Judge, a comprehensive understanding of
1015   what factors affect the position bias remains underexplored. In other words, without clarity on the
1016   key factors and their quantitative impact on position bias, the efficacy of current and future methods
1017   remains uncertain.
1018
       For instance, Li et al. (2023d) proposed a PORTIA approach that addresses the position bias to a
1019
       large extent, receiving a 80.99% Fixed Coverage (the percentage of positionally inconsistent orig-
1020   inal assessments that are later corrected by PORTIA) for GPT-4 in a relation-based evaluation on
1021   8 MTBench answer pairs, improving consistency from 93.44 % to 97.03%. However, the extraor-
1022   dinarily high consistency of the original evaluation may imply that the choice of answer pairs may
1023   be biased in terms of answer quality gap, a factor that our study proves to be significantly impact-
1024   ful. In other words, if the quality between the two model responses differs considerably, the LLM
1025   judges will make little position bias during judgment and hence is easy to calibrate. This example
       illustrates how lacking a comprehensive understanding of the factors affecting position bias can lead


                                                          19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       to overestimating or misjudging the effectiveness of methods proposed to resolve it and improve the
1027   performance of LLM-as-a-Judge.
1028
1029   Missing analysis for position bias Besides, the positional preference direction aspect of position
1030
       bias remains underexplored. Positional preference refers to the specific positions an LLM judge fa-
       vors when position bias is evident. For instance, if an LLM judge consistently favors the responses
1031
       that appear first in the prompt, it exhibits a preference for the first position. Our study denotes
1032
       primacy-preferred and recency-preferred to describe biases toward the first and second positions, re-
1033   spectively. We integrate the measurement by formally defining positional fairness score to quantify
1034   the extent of a judge model’s preference towards certain positions.
1035
1036
       While prior research includes some positional preference results (Zheng et al., 2024b; Wang et al.,
       2023b; Zhu et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2023c; Liusie et al., 2024), none have conducted a detailed
1037
       analysis. They focus primarily on positional consistency in relation to position bias, while we regard
1038
       positional preference as an equally crucial component that needs to be examined, understood, and
1039   enhanced in future work.
1040
1041   Therefore, we propose that mitigating the position bias of LLM-as-a-Judge requires a simultaneous
1042
       improvement in both positional consistency and positional fairness. A positionally consistent (high
       positional consistency) and positionally fair (nearly equal preference on primacy and recency when
1043
       position bias occur) LLM judge is undoubtedly preferable. However, a trade-off between consis-
1044
       tency and fairness/preference often occurs in practice. Previous studies that have provided results
1045   on positional preference have all shown that even state-of-the-art (SOTA) LLM judges struggle with
1046   both positional inconsistency and clear positional preferences.
1047
1048
       Apart from that, previous studies have yet to examine how stable LLM judges are across repe-
       titions. MLLM-as-a-Judge (Chen et al., 2024a) conducts repeated experiments but then takes an
1049
       average/mode of the judgment only to make the evaluations more robust and reliable, without a
1050
       focus on the repetitively stable judgments. In comparison, we investigate the repetition stability to
1051   determine whether position bias is solely influenced by the prompt’s positional information and the
1052   judge’s intrinsic properties or can be partly attributed to judgment randomness. If the judgments ex-
1053   hibit randomness over repetitions, then the position bias overlaps with repetition bias, complicating
1054   the issue and potentially invalidating previous findings.

1057   B.4   S UMMARY OF P RIOR W ORK
1058
1059   To summarize, LLM-as-a-Judge has a large potential in alternating human judges on a wide range of
1060   tasks, in particular subjective evaluation, owing to its cost-effectiveness, high agreement with human
1061   judgments, reproducibility, and scalability. However, it suffers from various biases, most notably
1062   position bias, which is prevalent across different evaluation tasks, models, and judgment types. Not
1063
       only is it a type of bias that significantly hinders the improvement and promotion of applying LLM-
       as-a-Judge, but it is also difficult to solve due to its complexity and lack of understanding in the
1064
       community.
1065
1066   Existing solutions proposed to address this issue may be ineffective or uncertain in efficacy due to
1067   the unclear impact and nature of influencing factors. Additionally, whether the position bias is an
1068   essential bias or mere random variation has not been validated from prior works. The positional
1069
       preference side, reflecting the positional fairness of the LLM judge, also requires comprehensive
       analysis. In a nutshell, a comprehensive understanding of the position bias of LLM-as-a-Judge is
1070
       crucial to validate existing and future approaches to address this important problem.

1140
1141   Since the mutual agreement between LLM judges is not perfect and usually a considerable propor-
1142
       tion of instances are difficult for them to reach a consensus, disagreement analysis becomes crucial
       and insightful. Therefore, we define the disagreement of an evaluation instance to be the number
1143
       of judgments different from the majority. By this definition, an instance with all judges reaching a
1144
       consensus on the better solution will have a disagreement of 0; in contrast, an instance where judg-
1145   ments are widely varied will result in a high disagreement. For our study where twelve judges are
1146   investigated, the maximum disagreement of an MTBench is 8, accounting for the 4{A}-4{B}-4{C}
1147   choice pattern by 3-option mode. On the other hand, for DevBench instances, the maximum possible
1148   disagreement is 6, representing the 6{A}-6{B} judgment distribution for the 2-option mode.
1149
       The distributions of instances with different disagreement values on MTBench and DevBench are
1150
       shown in Fig. 5(a) and Fig. 5(b), respectively. From our disagreement analysis, at least 75% of
1151   the judges reached a choice consensus on more than half of the instances on both benchmarks.
1152   These are likely easy-to-evaluate instances, and the reliability of LLM judgments is enhanced by
1153   majority voting. In comparison, the instances with the highest disagreement are likely the ones that
1154   are difficult to evaluate and where the position bias is most likely to occur. However, luckily, these
1155   instances are rare, occupying only 0.03% and 0.07% of MTBench and DevBench respectively. In
1156   other words, majority voting of multiple capable LLM judges could be practically useful for over
1157   99% of evaluation instances on both benchmarks.
1158   Moreover, if we roughly consider the disagreement value of instances as their difficulty for judging,
1159   then Fig. 5(a) and Fig. 5(b) exhibit a balanced distribution of instances with varied difficulty. This
1160   is because, except for the instances with the highest disagreement, the numbers of other instances
1161   with varied disagreement do not vary significantly, indicating a smoothly increasing difficulty curve
1162   across the benchmark datasets.
1163   To summarize, the practical implications of the disagreement analysis are three-fold. First, it helps
1164   identify the instances that are difficult or trivial to judge, benefiting benchmark designs to control
1165   the difficulty of evaluation by managing the number of these instances across the dataset. Second, it
1166   assists in filtering out instances where majority voting of LLM evaluators are likely to offer reliable
1167   judgments without direct comparison with human-annotated evaluations, enhancing the scalability
1168   of LLM judges especially when human evaluations are costly. In other words, if one-shot judgments
1169   from only one LLM judge are not enough reliable, multiple capable LLMs and the majoring voting
1170   strategy could be employed to make the evaluation more convincing. Last but not least, disagreement
1171
       analysis provides a convenient way to make the difficulty variance of instances varied across the
       dataset tangible. Since the difficulty of an evaluation instance is closely related to the quality gap
1172
       between the two solutions and hence position bias, the investigation of the instances where most
1173
       judges particularly disagree with one another could provide more insights and inspiration for future
1174   benchmark designs and potential mitigation strategies for position bias.

1178
1179
       Intuitively, answer pairs with larger quality gaps are easier for LLM judges to evaluate, as one answer
1180
       is clearly superior. Therefore, we expect LLM judges to exhibit weaker position bias in such cases.
1181
       This motivates us to quantify the answer quality gap and investigate its impact on position bias. The
1182   quantification utilizes the overall win rate (owr) of a candidate model (i.e., answer provider) on
1183   the other to compute the quality gap. For a set of questions on a certain task and the corresponding
1184   solutions by two candidate models, an LLM judge’s evaluations would pick out the candidate winner.
1185   For the position consistent judgment instances, we can count the number of a candidate’s wins and
1186   ties, denoted as Cw and Ct respectively. On the other hand, following the methodology introduced
1187   by Zheng et al. (2024b), we consider the inconsistent judgment pairs as a tie for both candidates,
       whose counting number is denoted as CI . This follows the intuition that it is difficult to strictly


                                                         22
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1188
       pick out a better one between two responses with similar quality, hence increasing the likelihood of
1189   position bias.
1190
1191   Therefore, considering both cases, we define the overall win rate (owr) of a candidate’s solution
1192
       over the other as owr = n1 [Cw + 12 (Ct + CI )], where n is the total number of judgment pairs
       involving the two candidate solutions in both original and swapped settings. Then the quality gap
1193
       (δq ) is calculated as δq = |owr − 0.5|, as 0.5 is the exact tie case for the win rate ranging from
1194
       0 to 1. We prefer the overall win rate over the consistent win rate (calculated by C      nC , where
                                                                                                   w
1195
       nC is the number of position consistent judgment pairs) to quantify δq to mainly address the data
1196
       sparsity issue. If the proportion of position inconsistent judgments becomes high, the available data
1197
       becomes sparse and nC becomes small. In the extreme case, where all judgments are inconsistent,
1198   nC is 0 and the consistent win rate is not even computable, making the measurement inaccurate and
1199   impractical. In contrast, the overall win rate effectively captures the “comparable quality” scenario
1200   by considering “inconsistency-as-tie” and ensuring all data points contribute to the quantification of
1201   the difference between answer pairs in quality.

       F.1   B IDIRECTIONAL S TEPWISE R EGRESSION WITH AIC
1245
1246   Bidirectional stepwise regression is a combination of forward selection and backward elimination
1247   techniques. It iteratively refines the model by adding or removing predictors based on a statistical
1248   criterion—commonly the Akaike Information Criterion (AIC). The objective is to select a model
1249   that balances goodness of fit and complexity, aiming for the lowest AIC value.
1250   The AIC is given by:
1251
1252
1253                                           AIC = 2k − 2 log(L),                                          (4)
1254
       where L is the likelihood of the model and k is the number of parameters in the model, including the
1255
       error variance σ 2 . For a linear regression model with independent and identically distributed (iid)
1256   errors, N (0, σ 2 ), fitted to n observations, the log-likelihood can be written as:
1257
1258                                                                     n
                                              n          n            1 X 2
1259                            log(L) = −      log(2π) − log(σ 2 ) − 2    ê ,                              (5)
1260                                          2          2           2σ i=1 i
1261
1262   where êi is the residual for the ith observation, and σ 2 is the variance of the errors. The AIC, in this
1263   context, becomes:
1264
1265                                                                            n
                                                                           1 X 2
1266                             AIC = 2k + n log(2π) + n log(σ 2 ) +             ê .                       (6)
                                                                           σ 2 i=1 i
1267
1268
       This form of the AIC balances the goodness of fit (as reflected by the residual sum of squares) and
1269   model complexity (as represented by k).
1270
1271   The operation of Bidirectional stepwise regression starts with either no predictors (forward selection)
1272
       or all predictors (backward elimination), where the model iteratively adds or removes variables.
       Each step evaluates the impact on the AIC score. In forward selection, variables are added one
1273
       by one, starting from the null model, such that the addition of each variable results in the largest
1274
       decrease in AIC. In backward elimination, all variables are included in the model initially, and
1275   variables are removed one at a time, with the variable whose removal causes the smallest increase
1276   in AIC being dropped.
1277
1278
       At each iteration, the change in AIC is computed as ∆AIC = AICnew − AICcurrent , where AICnew
       refers to the AIC after adding or removing a variable, and AICcurrent is the AIC of the current model.
1279
       If ∆AIC < 0, the model is improved by the addition or removal of the variable. The process
1280
       terminates when neither adding nor removing variables results in a lower AIC, signifying that the
1281   most parsimonious model, based on AIC, has been reached.
