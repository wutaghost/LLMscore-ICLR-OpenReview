009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Reinforcement learning problems often involve large action spaces arising from
012            the simultaneous execution of multiple sub-actions, resulting in combinatorial ac-
013            tion spaces. Learning in combinatorial action spaces is difficult due to the expo-
014            nential growth in action space size with the number of sub-actions and the depen-
015            dencies among these sub-actions. In offline settings, this challenge is compounded
016            by limited and suboptimal data. Current methods for offline learning in combi-
017            natorial spaces simplify the problem by assuming sub-action independence. We
018
               propose Branch Value Estimation (BVE), which effectively captures sub-action
               dependencies and scales to large combinatorial spaces by learning to evaluate only
019
               a small subset of actions at each timestep. Our experiments show that BVE out-
020
               performs state-of-the-art methods across a range of action space sizes.1

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Offline reinforcement learning (RL) automates sequential decision-making in domains where trial-
026   and-error exploration is costly, risky, or impractical by learning from a fixed dataset (Lange et al.,
027   2012). While effective in various domains (Fu et al., 2020; Levine et al., 2020), value-based offline
028   RL methods often require exhaustive enumeration of the action space, and policy-based methods are
029   typically designed for continuous action spaces (Lillicrap et al., 2016; Delarue et al., 2020). How-
030   ever, in many real-world settings, the concurrent execution of multiple actions creates large, discrete
031   combinatorial action spaces, rendering traditional offline RL approaches ineffective. In healthcare,
032   for example, practitioners must choose from thousands of procedural combinations at every decision
033
      point. Yet, to minimize risks and costs, they must only take the actions most informative for disease
      diagnosis and treatment, a notoriously difficult task (Yoon et al., 2019).
034
035   Learning in combinatorial action spaces is challenging due to the exponential increase in possible
036   actions with action space dimensionality. In an N -dimensional action space with md discrete sub-
                                                                                QN
037   actions per dimension d, the total number of possible actions is given by d=1 md . In traffic light
038   control (Rasheed et al., 2020), for instance, where each light represents a dimension in the action
039   space and its status (red, green, yellow) is a sub-action, controlling just four intersections with
040   four lights each results in 316 (>43M) possible actions. People naturally eliminate most unsuitable
041
      actions, such as turning all lights green simultaneously, using common sense. RL agents lack this
      intuition and must spend time and computational resources to discover the sub-optimality of nearly
042
      all action combinations (Zahavy et al., 2018). Although offline RL methods can learn to avoid
043
      ineffective actions through expert demonstrations (Levine et al., 2020), we find that state-of-the-art
044   approaches struggle to resolve the complex dependencies among sub-actions, where the utility of
045   one sub-action can critically depend on the presence or absence of another.
046
047
      We introduce Branch Value Estimation (BVE) to learn in environments with discrete, combinatorial
      action spaces. Our key insight is that structuring combinatorial action spaces as trees can capture
048
      dependencies among sub-actions while reducing the number of actions evaluated at each timestep.
049
      Specifically, in our action space tree (Figure 1), each node represents a distinct sub-action combi-
050   nation, and each edge assigns a unique value to a specific sub-action. The tree is structured so that
051   a node inherits the values of sub-actions from its ancestors, with siblings having distinct values for

098   Reinforcement learning problems can be formalized as a Markov Decision Process (MDP), M =
099   ⟨S, A, p, r, γ, µ⟩ where S is a set of states, A is a set of actions, p : S × A × S → [0, 1] is
100   a function that gives the probability of transitioning to state s′ when action a is taken in state s,
101   r : S × A → R is a reward function, γ ∈ [0, 1] is a discount factor, and µ : S → [0, 1] is the
102   distribution of initial states. A policy π : S → P(A) is a distribution over actions conditioned on
103   a state π(a | s) = P[at = a | st = s]. In our work, we assume states S can be either discrete or
104
      continuous and that the MDP has a finite horizon H.
105   While the standard MDP formulation abstracts away the structure of actions in A, we explicitly as-
106   sume that the action space is combinatorial; that is, A is defined as a Cartesian product of sub-action
107   spaces. More formally, A = A1 × A2 × · · · × AN , where each Ad is a discrete set. Consequently,
      at is an N -dimensional vector wherein each component is referred to as a sub-action.

303    Experimental Setup We evaluate these methods in 20 environments, categorized into two types:
304    those with and without a cluster of pits along the optimal path. We create ten instances for each
305    type, varying in dimension from 2D, with 16 available actions in each state (|A| = 16) (i.e.,
306    {∅, [U ], [U D], [U DL], [U LR], [U DLR], [D], [DR], . . . }), to 11D, with over four million available

314    In the pit-free environments, the agent’s task is relatively simple because the optimal action is the
315    same in all states. Moreover, the transition probability from s to s′ can be decomposed into inde-
316    pendent probabilities for each sub-action, and the policy into a product of independent sub-action
317    policies.
318    Notably, sub-actions aren’t fully independent, as the reward model cannot be decomposed into
319    separate rewards. Still, FAS learns a high-performing policy despite the bias from its linear de-
320    composition, as sub-action interactions are relatively mild. In higher-dimensional environments,
321    however, the difference between the linearly decomposed and true reward functions becomes more
322    pronounced, causing instability in FAS’s learning. BVE, by contrast, does not exhibit this behav-
323    ior, as our loss (equation 4) evaluates actions as unified entities rather than aggregates of individual
       components.

412    5.1                 T REE - BASED RL
413
414    Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) (Coulom, 2006), used most notably in AlphaZero (Silver et al.,
415    2018), recursively selects actions using the Polynomial Upper Confidence Trees (PUCT) algorithm
416
       (Auger et al., 2013). PUCT selects action at as at = argmaxa (Q(st , a) + U (st , a)), where U (st , a)
       provides an upper confidence bound on Q-values. Traditionally, this method is used for an or-
417
       dered decision process, where the value of an action at time t depends on subsequent actions at
418
       t1 , t2 , . . . , tH , as in chess. Therefore, MCTS is ill-suited for environments with unordered or cate-
419    gorical actions, like in our experiments, where sub-actions must be selected simultaneously.
420
421    TreeQN (Farquhar et al., 2017) integrates model-free RL with online planning by constructing an
422
       abstract MDP model that combines learned transition dynamics and reward predictions. It builds
       a tree of state representations and rewards for all action sequences up to a specified depth. Value
423
       estimates are recursively refined through a tree backup process to improve their accuracy.
424
425    Because traditional decision trees are non-differentiable if-then rules, they are incompatible with
426    gradient descent, limiting their use in online RL. Silva et al. (2020) address this by introducing dif-
427    ferentiable decision trees (DDTs), which replace rigid decision boundaries with smooth, differen-
428
       tiable functions, enabling gradient-based optimization in RL. After training, DDTs can be converted
       back into discrete trees, preserving interpretability.
429
430    Ernst et al. (2005) propose an offline RL approach that uses tree-based supervised learning algo-
431    rithms within a fitted Q-iteration framework to approximate the Q-function. This method iteratively
       refines the Q-function using classical techniques like CART, Kd-trees, and tree bagging, leverag-

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      ing observed system transitions. By applying tree-based regression, the approach generalizes the
433   learned policy to unobserved state-action pairs.

      5.2   C OMBINATORIAL ACTION S PACES
436
437   Due to the prevalence of combinatorial action spaces in real-world problems, various methods have
438   been developed for learning in these environments. Many of these are tailored to specific domains,
439   including text-based games and natural language action spaces (Zahavy et al., 2018; He et al., 2015;
440   2016), vehicle routing (Delarue et al., 2020; Nazari et al., 2018), the traveling salesperson problem
441   (Bello et al., 2016), and resource allocation (Chen et al., 2024). These methods, however, often
442   depend on problem-specific assumptions, whereas BVE is designed for broader applicability.
443   Other approaches are more general-purpose. For example, Tavakoli et al. (2018) introduce a novel
444   architecture that distributes action controller representations across individual network branches,
445   with a shared decision module encoding a latent input representation to coordinate these branches.
446   Farquhar et al. (2020) propose using a curriculum of progressively expanding action spaces to accel-
447   erate learning in online environments where random exploration may be inefficient. This approach
448   is effective when a restricted action space enables random exploration to generate significantly more
449
      informative experiences than in the full action space, and when regularities in the action space facil-
      itate transferring learning to the full task. Amortized Q-learning (AQL) (Van de Wiele et al., 2020)
450
      avoids exact maximization over the action set at each step. Instead, it learns to search for the optimal
451
      action, thereby amortizing the cost of action selection over training. The search is treated as a dis-
452   tinct learning task, replacing exact maximization with maximization over a set of actions sampled
453   from a learned proposal distribution.
454
455
      While these methods are designed for online learning, Tang et al. (2022) propose an offline approach,
      which we refer to as FAS in our experiments, that linearly decomposes the Q-function, conditioning
456
      each component on a single sub-action and the full state space. This reduces the action space’s
457
      dimensional complexity but the sufficient conditions for unbiased Q-value estimations — in effect,
458   independence among sub-actions — often do not hold in real-world environments. BVE, by contrast,
459   simplifies the problem by structuring the action space, enabling its application to problems where
460   sub-actions may be interdependent.
461

      6     C ONCLUSION
464
      In many real-world sequential decision making problems, discrete combinatorial action spaces
465
      emerge from the simultaneous selection of multiple sub-actions. Traditional RL approaches strug-
466
      gle in these spaces due to both the exponential increase in the action space size with the number of
467   sub-actions and the complex dependencies among the sub-actions. These challenges are exacerbated
468   in offline settings, where available data is often limited and sub-optimal. We present Branch Value
469   Estimation (BVE), an offline RL method for learning in discrete, combinatorial action spaces. By
470   structuring combinatorial action spaces as trees, BVE captures sub-action dependencies while re-
471   ducing the number of actions evaluated per timestep, thus allowing it to scale to large action spaces.
472   Our empirical experiments demonstrate that BVE outperforms state-of-the-art baselines across envi-
473   ronments with varying action space sizes and sub-action dependencies. Future work should explore
474   using BVE within an actor-critic framework to extend its applicability to continuous and mixed
475
      (discrete and continuous) combinatorial action spaces.
