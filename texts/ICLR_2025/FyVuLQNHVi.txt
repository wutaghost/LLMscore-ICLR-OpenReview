                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               Recent advances in Large Language Models (LLMs) have catalyzed the develop-
012            ment of Large Multimodal Models (LMMs). However, existing research primarily
013            focuses on tuning language and image instructions, ignoring the critical pretrain-
014            ing phase where models learn to process textual and visual modalities jointly.
015            In this paper, we propose a new pretraining paradigm for LMMs to enhance the
016            visual comprehension capabilities of LLMs by introducing a novel cross-modal
017            comprehension stage. Specifically, we design a dynamically learnable prompt to-
018            ken pool and employ the Hungarian algorithm to replace part of the original visual
019            tokens with the most relevant prompt tokens. Then, we conceptualize visual to-
020
               kens as analogous to a “foreign language” for the LLMs and propose a mixed
               attention mechanism with bidirectional visual attention and unidirectional textual
021
               attention to comprehensively enhance the understanding of visual tokens. Mean-
022
               while, we integrate a detailed caption generation task, leveraging rich descriptions
023            to further facilitate LLMs in understanding visual semantic information. After
024            pretraining on 1.5 million publicly accessible data, we present a new foundation
025            model called Croc. Experimental results demonstrate that Croc achieves new
026            state-of-the-art performance on massive vision-language benchmarks. To support
027            reproducibility and facilitate further research, we will release the training code
028            and pre-trained model weights.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      The rapid expansion of mobile networks has accelerated the generation of vast data volumes, pre-
      senting unprecedented opportunities for the development and application of Large Language Models
034
      (LLMs) (Zhao et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023). Despite their effectiveness, LLMs
035
      are primarily confined to processing textual inputs. To expand their multimodal perceptual capabili-
036   ties, there is an increasing research focus on Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) (Yin et al., 2023; Jin
037   et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023b), which are designed to process and integrate inputs across multiple
038   modalities.
039
      As a milestone in LMM research, LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024b) leverages the language-only capabil-
040
      ities of GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) to generate multimodal language-image instruction-following
041
      datasets, demonstrating impressive multimodal conversational capabilities. Building on this ground-
042   work, LLaVA-1.5 (Liu et al., 2024a) enhances performance through simple modifications to the orig-
043   inal LLaVA framework and incorporates academically oriented Visual Question Answering (VQA)
044   datasets with structured response formatting prompts. In parallel, BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023a) and
045   MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al., 2023) connect a frozen pre-trained vision encoder and a language model
046   through a trainable Q-Former or a linear layer, effectively mapping image features into the input em-
047   bedding space of the language model. Nonetheless, these methods achieve only a superficial integra-
048   tion of image features within the language model’s embedding space. In contrast, CogVLM (Wang
049   et al., 2023) introduces a trainable visual expert module into the attention and Feed-Forward Net-
050
      work (FFN) layers of the language model. Despite this innovation, the freezing of the LLM limits
      its capability to attain an in-depth understanding of visual features directly.
051
052   Recent research highlights the crucial role of the pretraining process in LMMs. Flamingo (Alayrac
053   et al., 2022) synergistically integrates pre-trained vision and language models through pretraining
      on comprehensive multimodal web corpora that combine text and image, enabling the performance

      Large Multimodal Model Pre-training. As a significant advancement, LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024b)
111   meticulously filters the CC3M (Changpinyo et al., 2021) dataset down to 595K and maintains the
112   frozen state of both the visual encoder and the LLM weights, exclusively training the projection layer
113   to align features from the visual encoder and the LLM. However, this strategy predominantly results
114   in limited deep feature integration between the visual encoder and the LLM, primarily due to the
115   constraints imposed by the projection layer. To address this limitation, CogVLM (Wang et al., 2023)
116   introduces a trainable visual expert module into the attention and feed-forward network layers of the
117   language model. Despite this innovation, constrained by the frozen state of the LLM, it continues
118   to face challenges in comprehending the “foreign language” of visual tokens. Recently, LaVIT (Jin
119
      et al., 2023) introduces a well-designed visual tokenizer to convert non-linguistic images into a
      sequence of discrete tokens. However, directly inputting visual tokens into LLM to enhance visual
120
      understanding of LLM through next-token prediction presents significant limitations. VILA (Lin
121
      et al., 2024) proposes an interleaved pertraining stage to augment the LLM to support visual input,
122   but it relies on a 50M pertraining dataset, necessitating considerable computational resources.
123
124   Cross-Model Comprehension. To improve the performance of Masked Image Modeling (MIM)
125
      based pre-training methods, MVP (Wei et al., 2022) initially explores the integration of multimodal
      pre-training within the MIM framework. Subsequently, diverging from conventional methods that
126
      predominantly predict raw pixels or low-level features, MILAN (Hou et al., 2022) adopts an inno-
127
      vative approach by reconstructing image features infused with substantial semantic content derived
128   from caption supervision. UnMasked Teacher (Li et al., 2023b) selectively masks video tokens
129   exhibiting low semantic content and aligns the remaining unmasked tokens through a linear pro-
130   jection to their counterparts from the teacher model. Experimental results confirm that this ap-
131   proach achieves state-of-the-art performance across various video-related tasks. In a recent study,
132   RILS (Yang et al., 2023a) introduces a novel pre-training framework that employs masked visual
133   reconstruction within a language semantic space. This framework facilitates the extraction of struc-
134   tured information by vision models through the accurate semantic prediction of masked tokens.
135   Meanwhile, EVA (Fang et al., 2023) demonstrates that recovering the masked-out tokenized seman-
136
      tic vision features is an efficient strategy for vision-centric representation learning, obviating the
      need for semantic feature quantization or further tokenization. Inspired by the above works, we
137
      propose a visual token reconstruction task to improve the visual comprehension capability of LLMs.

187   In this section, we propose a novel cross-modal comprehension pre-training method. Fig. 2 illus-
188

189

190

191   we design a mixed mechanism of bidirectional visual attention and unidirectional textual attention.
192   Meanwhile, we introduce a detailed caption generation task to further enhance the LLM’s under-

195

196

197   before and after the last Transformer layer, and the visual projector translates the visual features into

297   3.3     T RAINING P IPELINE
298
299
      The overall training pipeline of our Croc model is shown in Fig. 2. Building upon LLaVA-1.5, the
      Croc undergoes a two-stage pre-training procedure followed by instruction tuning.
300
301   Stage 1: Cross-Modal Alignment. Following LLaVA-1.5, we first pretrain the projection layer
302   with the identical 558K pretraining dataset used in LLaVA-1.5 to align image features to the LLM
303   embedding space. During training, we keep both the visual encoder and the LLM weights frozen.
304   Stage 1.5: Cross-Modal Comprehension. Building on the cross-modal alignment stage, we intro-
305   duce the cross-modal comprehension phase as a subsequent pre-training stage. To facilitate compre-
306   hensive learning of visual tokens by the LLM, we pretrain the projection layer as well as the LLM
307   in this stage. We select 1.2M detailed image-text pairs from the ShareGPT4V (Chen et al., 2024b)
308   dataset, as the detailed captions (as shown in Fig. 4) can enhance the visual token learning of the
309   LLM. To prevent the degradation of the inherent capabilities of LLM, we also incorporate 300K
310   pure text data. Please refer to the appendix for more details.
311   Stage 2: Instruction Tuning. Following LLaVA-1.5, we freeze the visual encoder weights and
312   update both the pre-trained weights of the projection layer and LLM to improve its visual question
313   answering capabilities. We employ the identical 665K instruction dataset used in LLaVA-1.5.

528   5     C ONCLUSION
529
530
531
      In this paper, we introduce a novel pretraining paradigm for LMMs to enhance the visual compre-
      hension capabilities of LLMs. Our approach incorporates a new cross-modal comprehension stage
532
      designed to bridge the gap between the visual and textual domains. Specifically, we develop a dy-
533
      namically learnable prompt token pool and apply the Hungarian algorithm to replace a portion of
534   the original visual tokens with the most relevant prompt tokens. To further improve the model’s un-
535   derstanding, we propose a mixed attention mechanism that combines bidirectional visual attention
536   with unidirectional textual attention, enabling a more comprehensive interpretation of visual tokens.
537   Additionally, we incorporate a detailed caption generation task, utilizing rich descriptions to im-
538   prove the LLM’s grasp of visual semantic information. Experimental results show that our method
539   achieves state-of-the-art performance across multiple vision-language benchmarks. We hope that
      our work offers valuable insights for advancing large multimodal models.

846   A.3      L IMITATION D ISCUSSION
847
848   In this work, we have focused solely on image token reconstruction, which limits the scope to static
849   images. However, for comprehensive video understanding, it is essential to consider both spatial and
850
      temporal token reconstruction. This would allow us to capture the dynamic changes that occur across
      frames and enhance the model’s ability to process and interpret video sequences more effectively.
851
      Expanding our approach to include spatial-temporal token reconstruction is a necessary step for
852
      future improvements in video analysis.

      A.4      E THIC D ISCUSSION
855
856   Even though large multimodal models represent a major technological advancement with broad ap-
857   plications, their development and deployment bring significant ethical responsibilities. To ensure
858   that the proposed Croc model benefits society while minimizing potential harm, it is essential to
859   address key concerns such as bias, privacy, misinformation, environmental impact, intellectual prop-
860   erty, accountability, and human oversight. These challenges are not unique to Croc but are pressing
861   issues faced by the entire AI research and development community.
