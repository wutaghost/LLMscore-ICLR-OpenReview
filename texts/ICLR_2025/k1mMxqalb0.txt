009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               This work explores sequential model editing in large language models (LLMs), a
012            critical task that involves modifying internal knowledge within LLMs continuously
013            through multi-round editing, each incorporating updates or corrections to adjust
014            the model’s outputs without the need for costly retraining. Existing model editing
015            methods, especially those that alter model parameters, typically focus on single-
016            round editing and often face significant challenges in sequential model editing-most
017            notably issues of model forgetting and failure. To address these challenges, we
018            introduce a new model editing method, namely Neuron-level Sequential Editing
019            (NSE), tailored for supporting sequential model editing. Specifically, we optimize
020
               the target layer’s hidden states using the model’s original weights to prevent
               model failure. Furthermore, we iteratively select neurons in multiple layers for
021
               editing based on their activation values to mitigate model forgetting. Our empirical
022
               experiments demonstrate that NSE significantly outperforms current modifying
023            parameters model editing methods, marking a substantial advancement in the field
024            of sequential model editing. Our code is released on https://anonymous.
025            4open.science/r/NSE-0A8D/.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in storing extensive
032   factual knowledge during pre-training and recalling this information during inference (Brown et al.,
033
      2020; Petroni et al., 2019; Roberts et al., 2020). However, as real-world knowledge continuously
      evolves, the information within these models can become outdated or incorrect (Cao et al., 2021;
034
      Mitchell et al., 2022a). Retraining LLMs to incorporate new information is often prohibitively costly
035
      (Mitchell et al., 2022b; Meng et al., 2022). Consequently, recent years have witnessed a surge in
036   model editing methods focusing on modifying specific knowledge without the complete retraining
037   process. Specifically, they first identify the crucial layers for the target knowledge by calculating their
038   casual effect on output. Then, by updating the weights of these layers, they manipulate these layers’
039   hidden states to modify the final output, enabling LLMs to adapt seamlessly to dynamic real-world
040   information (Meng et al., 2023; Hartvigsen et al., 2023).
041
      While current direct model editing methods prove effective for single-round modifications, real-world
042   applications demand a continual learning process where models must retain previous edits during
043   subsequent modifications (Yao et al., 2023). This has led to the concept of sequential model editing,
044   which necessitates performing multiple, consecutive edits on models. However, current direct model
045   editing methods pose significant risks in this context (Meng et al., 2022; 2023). The primary risk is
046   model forgetting, where cumulative changes in parameters from consecutive edits cause the model to
047   forget previously modified knowledge, thereby degrading overall performance (Gupta et al., 2024a).
048   For instance, as illustrated in Figure 1 (a), after editing the model with new knowledge about “The
049   cat”, the LLM forgets previously edited knowledge about “The latest Olympic”.
050   Furthermore, the second risk is model failure, where excessive edits impair the model’s ability
051   to generate coherent text. Worse still, this impairment may lead to model collapse potentially,
052   characterized by producing irrelevant, repetitive, or nonsensical text, as illustrated in Figure 1 (a). In
053   sight of this, recent researches, such as memory-based methods (Mitchell et al., 2022b; Hartvigsen
      et al., 2023; Das et al., 2024), have attempted to address these challenges by preserving LLM

069
      To tackle these challenges, we introduce a new model editing method, termed Neuron-level Sequential
070   Editing (NSE). Specifically, to address the model failure, NSE uses weights rewinding for value
071   computation by preserving the model’s original weights as a significant reference when manipulating
072   the hidden states of the crucial layers. This process can effectively mitigate the impairment of
073   previous knowledge accumulated over various edits. Furthermore, to address model forgetting, NSE
074   selectively collects “influential neurons” for weights updating by sorting the neuron activation within
075   the crucial layers, rather than updating all weights in critical layers as in previous work (Meng et al.,
076   2022; 2023). This selective modification maximizes the protection of model functionality from being
077   degraded. Additionally, for large-scale LLMs containing numerous neurons, an iterative multi-layer
078
      editing is introduced to streamline the neuron selection process, enabling NSE to achieve massive
      knowledge updates effectively in a single editing.
079
080   Through theoretical analysis and extensive experiments conducted on the GPT2-XL (1.5B) (Radford
081   et al., 2019), GPT-J (6B) (Wang & Komatsuzaki, 2021) and Llama3 (8B), we validate the effectiveness
082   and efficiency of our NSE. Compared to the current model editing methods (e.g., Fine-tuning (Zhu
083   et al., 2020), MEND (Mitchell et al., 2022a), ROME (Meng et al., 2022), MEMIT (Meng et al., 2023)
084
      and GRACE (Hartvigsen et al., 2023)), NSE shows substantial improvements w.r.t five commonly
      used metrics such as specificity and consistency.

185   3     M ETHODOLOGY
186
187   In this section, we introduce NSE, a method tailored for sequential model editing, as illustrated in
188   Figure 2. Initially, we identify the values computing method in Section 3.1. Subsequently, in Section
189   3.2, we detail the editing method that selectively filters certain neurons for corresponding parameter
190
      updates. Finally, in Section 3.3, we introduce the approach of iterative multi-layer editing.

192   3.1   W EIGHTS R EWINDING FOR VALUE C OMPUTATION
193
      First of all, our primary goal is to find a hidden vector that encodes the new association (si , ri , oi )
194
      and replaces the value vil in l-th layer as described in Section 2.3. In practical implementation, as
195   shown in Figure 2 (a), we optimize δi through gradient descent by maximizing the probability of the
196   model outputting oi to compute zi = hli + δi , where hli denotes the hidden state of the LLM at layer
197   l. And the value vi l can be computed as vi l + = δi . In the process of sequential editing, we observed
198   that using the updated model parameters fθt to compute zi after each edit leads to significant model
199   degradation over multiple edit rounds. This indicates that the cumulative parameter updates from
200   each editing round can lead to a shift in value computation. Conversely, using the original model
201   parameters fθ0 to compute zi effectively prevents this issue. Hence, we propose a weights rewinding
202   method for value computation , which is based on the preserve initial model weights fθ0 to ensure
203   that zi is computed using fθ0 for each edit. The optimization objective is as follows:
204                           zi = hli + arg min(− log Pfθ0 (hli +=δi ) [oi | (si , ri )]),                 (9)
205                                           δi
206
      where fθ0 (hli + = δi ) represents the original model with hli updated to hli + δi . Subsequently, the
207
      value vi l can be updated as vi l + = zi − hli , which will be used to support subsequent model edits.
208   Note that when calculating zi using the original model parameters fθ0 , it is sufficient to save only the
209   weight matrix Wout that needs to be updated, rather than storing the entire model parameters and we
210   use the original weights only during the computation of zi .

249   3.3   I TERATIVE MULTI - LAYER E DITING
250
251   As described in Section 2.3, MEMIT propagates edits through the layers by computing the value vil
252
                     δi
      as vil + = l0 −l+1    (l ∈ R) (Meng et al., 2023; Gupta et al., 2024b).Here, δi = zi − hli0 represents
253   the residual difference. The fundamental purpose is that as each layer is updated, the hidden state
254   hli0 progressively approaches the target zi , thereby diminishing the residual δi . Detailed analyses
255   can be found in Appendix E. However, due to errors in the fitting process, some knowledge proves
256   difficult to edit, resulting in vil0 not sufficiently approximating zi , and consequently leading to editing
257   failures.
258   Therefore, we propose iterative multi-layer editing to refine the multi-layer editing approach in
259   MEMIT by iteratively selecting neurons to edit multiple layers, as depicted in Figure 2 (c). Specif-
260   ically, considering that some knowledge is difficult to edit such that the corresponding value vil0
261   cannot sufficiently approximate the optimized target value zi , we employ iterative multi-layer editing.
262   After each round of multi-layer editing, we filter the knowledge samples in the current batch based on
263   ∥zi −hli0 ∥2 . If ∥zi −hli0 ∥2 < α, the knowledge sample is considered successfully edited. Conversely,
264   if ∥zi − hli0 ∥2 > α, the sample is deemed not yet successfully edited. The hyperparameter α, which
265   is set differently for various LLMs, determines the threshold for editing success. These unedited
266   samples are then filtered out to form a new batch for further multi-layer editing, repeating this process
267   until all knowledge samples in the batch meet ∥zi − hli0 ∥2 < α or the iteration limit is reached. This
268   iterative editing approach significantly enhances the success rate of sample edits, enabling NSE to
269   effectively achieve massive knowledge updates in a single editing session. More details of the NSE
      algorithm are provided in Appendix A.

490   5     R ELATED WORK
491
492   Recent years have witnessed a development in methods for model editing, which are developed to
493
      adjust the behavior of LLMs within specific domains, while preserving their performance across
      other inputs. Current approaches to model editing in large language models (LLMs) generally fall
494
      into two main categories (Yao et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024b):
495
496   Preserve Models’ Parameters. Methods that preserve the original model’s parameters generally
497   store edit examples in memory and use them to guide the model’s predictions. For instance, SERAC
498   (Mitchell et al., 2022b) keeps the original model unchanged and uses a separate counterfactual model
499
      for edits. T-Patcher (Huang et al., 2023) introduces an additional neuron for each output error, whereas
      CaliNet (Dong et al., 2022) incorporates knowledge using a predetermined number of neurons. Other
500
      methods like MemPrompt (Madaan et al., 2022) and IKE (Zheng et al., 2023) leverage the model’s
501
      in-context learning by prompting it with edited facts and retrieved demonstrations from memory.
502   MELO (Yu et al., 2024) dynamically activates corresponding LoRA blocks indexed in an inner vector
503   database to alter the behavior of models. GRACE (Hartvigsen et al., 2023) implements sequential
504   editing by maintaining a dynamically updated codebook. Larimar (Das et al., 2024) enhances LLMs
505   with distributed episodic memory. OneEdit (Zhang et al., 2024a) introduces a neural-symbolic system
506   for collaborative knowledge editing integrating knowledge graphs and LLMs.
507
      Modify Models’ Parameters. In contrast, methods that modify LLMs’ parameters require updating
508   the model’s internal parameters with each edit. FT-W fine-tunes the specific layer with regularization
509   constraints (Zhu et al., 2020). KN (Dai et al., 2022) identifies the "knowledge neuron" as the crucial
510   pair in FFN and updates these neurons, which represents the knowledge. KE (Cao et al., 2021) and
511   MEND (Mitchell et al., 2022a) use hypernetwork to forecast the weight change of LLMs based on
512   the meta-learning methods. ROME (Meng et al., 2022) and MEMIT (Meng et al., 2023) support
513   large-scale direct edits by locating and editing knowledge in specific layers of GPT. COMEBA-HK
514   (Li et al., 2024) uses newly proposed hook layers to identify the editing scope supporting sequential
515   editing. To enhance the performance of parameter modification methods in sequential editing, RECT
516
      (Gu et al., 2024) retains parameters with minimal changes to ensure stability, while PRUNE (Ma
      et al., 2024) constrains the maximum singular value of parameter changes. In this paper, we mainly
517
      focus on parameters-modification editing methods.
518
519
520   6     L IMITATION AND D ISCUSSION
521
522   Despite the outstanding performance of NSE in sequential editing, our investigation reveals some
523   limitations. Firstly, the method for selecting neurons is relatively simple and may not fully capture
524   the complexities of neuron interactions. Additionally, while the iterative distribution editing process
525
      is effective, it introduces some efficiency reduction, which could pose challenges for large-scale or
      time-sensitive applications. Moving forward, we aim to explore more effective neuron attribution
526
      methods and enhance the efficiency of our editing techniques.
527
528
529   7     C ONCLUSION
530
531   In this work, we introduce NSE, a model editing method for sequential model editing tailored for
532   addressing the significant challenges of model forgetting and model failure. Specifically, we propose
533
      weights rewinding for value computation by optimizing the hidden states of the target layer using
      the model’s original weights, which effectively minimize cumulative changes and maintains model
534
      coherence. Additionally, we select influential neurons for different knowlege to update weights in
535
      FFN and iteratively edit multi-layer weights, which effectively mitigate model forget. Experimental
536   results on the GPT2-XL, GPT-J and Llama3-8B models using the Counterfact and ZsRE datasets
537   demonstrate that NSE significantly outperforms existing model editing baselines and the ablation
538   study shows that each component in NSE is effective.

      B     BASELINES
757
758
      The details of baselines are as follow:
759
760          • FT-L (Zhu et al., 2020) focuses on adjusting a specific layer identified by ROME (Meng
761            et al., 2022), rather than fine-tuning all layers. This selective approach helps ensure fair
762
               comparisons, as these configurations have been shown to yield optimal performance. In con-
               trast, FT-W is a slight variation of FT-L, differing mainly in the method of loss computation
763
               for parameter optimization with regularization constraints.
764
             • MEND (Mitchell et al., 2022a) is an efficient method designed for editing large pre-trained
765            models using a single input-output pair. It employs small auxiliary networks to facilitate
766            quick, localized changes to the model without necessitating full retraining. By applying
767            low-rank decomposition to the gradients from standard fine-tuning, MEND achieves efficient
768            and manageable parameter adjustments. This strategy enables post-hoc edits in large models
769            while mitigating the overfitting typically associated with conventional fine-tuning techniques.
770          • ROME (Meng et al., 2022) focuses on updating specific factual associations within large
771            language models (LLMs). It identifies critical neuron activations within middle-layer feed-
772            forward modules that influence factual predictions, allowing for direct modifications to
773
               the feed-forward weights. ROME illustrates that these mid-layer modules are essential for
               storing and recalling factual knowledge, making direct manipulation a feasible technique for
774
               model editing.
775
             • MEMIT (Meng et al., 2023) is a scalable multi-layer update algorithm designed for ef-
776            ficiently incorporating new factual memories into transformer-based language models.
777            Building upon ROME’s direct editing approach, MEMIT specifically targets transformer
778            module weights that serve as causal mediators for factual knowledge recall. This method
779            enables MEMIT to update models with thousands of new associations.
780          • GRACE (Hartvigsen et al., 2023) introduces an innovative editing technique that focuses
781            on preserving the initial model parameters while incorporating a dynamic codebook. This
782            codebook evolves through the incremental addition, splitting, and expansion of keys, which
783            facilitates the long-term storage of relevant modifications.

787   C.1   DATASETS
788
789   ZsRE (Levy et al., 2017) is a question answering (QA) dataset that uses questions generated through
790
      back-translation as equivalent neighbors. Following previous work, natural questions are used as
      out-of-scope data to evaluate locality. Each sample in ZsRE includes a subject string and answers
791
      as the editing targets to assess editing success, along with the rephrased question for generalization
792
      evaluation and the locality question for evaluating specificity.
793
794   Counterfact (Meng et al., 2022) is a more challenging dataset that contrasts counterfactual with
795   factual statements, initially scoring lower for Counterfact. It constructs out-of-scope data by replacing
796
      the subject entity with approximate entities sharing the same predicate. The Counterfact dataset
      has similar metrics to ZsRE for evaluating efficacy, generalization, and specificity. Additionally,
797
      Counterfact includes multiple generation prompts with the same meaning as the original prompt to
798
      test the quality of generated text, specifically focusing on fluency and consistency.
