009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The human brain has long inspired the pursuit of artificial intelligence (AI). Re-
012            cently, neuroimaging studies provide compelling evidence of alignment between
013            the computational representation of artificial neural networks (ANNs) and the neu-
014            ral responses of the human brain to external stimuli, suggesting that ANNs may
015            employ brain-like information processing strategies. While such alignment has
016
               been observed across sensory modalities—visual, auditory, and linguistic—much
               of the focus has been on the behaviors of artificial neurons (ANs) at the popu-
017
               lation level, leaving the functional organization of individual ANs that facilitates
018
               such brain-like processes largely unexplored. In this study, we bridge this gap by
019            directly coupling sub-groups of artificial neurons with functional brain networks
020            (FBNs), the foundational organizational structure of the human brain. Specifi-
021            cally, we extract representative patterns from temporal responses of ANs in large
022            language models (LLMs), and use them as fixed regressors to construct voxel-
023            wise encoding models to predict brain activity recorded by functional magnetic
024            resonance imaging (fMRI). This framework effectively links the AN sub-groups
025            to FBNs, enabling the delineation of brain-like functional organization within
026            LLMs. Our findings reveal that LLMs (BERT and Llama 1–3) exhibit brain-like
027
               functional architecture, with sub-groups of artificial neurons mirroring the orga-
               nizational patterns of well-established FBNs. Notably, the brain-like functional
028
               organization of LLMs evolves with the increased sophistication and capability,
029
               achieving an improved balance between the diversity of computational behaviors
030            and the consistency of functional specializations. This research represents the
031            first exploration of brain-like functional organization within LLMs, offering novel
032            insights to inform the development of artificial general intelligence (AGI) with
033            human brain principles.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   The human brain, with its unparalleled capacities in perception, cognition, reasoning, and creativity,
039
      stands as the pinnacle of biological intelligence and complexity (Sporns et al., 2000; Bassett &
      Gazzaniga, 2011). Understanding the mechanisms behind these cognitive abilities has been one of
040
      the most formidable challenges in neuroscience for decades (Brodmann, 1909; Hubel & Wiesel,
041
      1979; Belliveau et al., 1991; Bear et al., 2020). Despite significant advances, the intricate processes
042   through which the brain organizes and interprets information—transforming raw sensory inputs into
043   meaningful representations that guide behavior and decision-making—remain elusive. Unraveling
044   these complexities would not only deepen our understanding of human cognition but also lay the
045   foundation for creating machines that truly emulate the intelligence of the human brain (Nilsson,
046   2009; Lu et al., 2018; Zhao et al., 2023b).
047
      In recent years, artificial neural networks (ANNs), initially inspired by the architecture of the human
048   brain, have emerged as one of the most promising technologies in quest to build machines capable
049   of cognitive functions. Modern ANNs have groundbreaking abilities in fields like visual recogni-
050   tion (He et al., 2016; Dosovitskiy, 2020; Oquab et al., 2023), language processing (Vaswani, 2017;
051   Devlin et al., 2018; Brown et al., 2020), and even reasoning tasks—domains that were once con-
052   sidered exclusive to human intelligence. These networks, with their remarkable capacity to learn
053   complex patterns from vast amounts of data, have ignited hope that machines may one day replicate
      or even surpass human cognitive abilities. Yet, as we edge closer to this possibility, one fundamental


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      question lingers: Can artificial systems be powered by the same organizational principles that under-
055   pin human intelligence? This question becomes even more pertinent in light of recent advances in
056   neuroimaging, which have revealed striking alignment between the computational representations
057   of ANNs and the neural responses of the human brain. Through functional magnetic resonance
058   imaging (fMRI), researchers have shown that ANN representations of external stimuli—whether vi-
059   sual (Zhao et al., 2023a; Yamins & DiCarlo, 2016; Kriegeskorte, 2015), auditory (Zhou et al., 2023;
060   Li et al., 2023; Millet et al., 2022; Tuckute et al., 2023), or linguistic (Liu et al., 2023; Caucheteux
061   & King, 2022; Schrimpf et al., 2021; Oota et al., 2024)—exhibit patterns that closely mirror pat-
062   terns observed in the brain. This fascinating correspondence has led to a tantalizing hypothesis: that
063
      ANNs, in their computational architectures, might develop information processing strategies akin to
      those employed by the brain itself.
064
065   However, while these findings are compelling, most studies have focused on population-level com-
066   parisons between ANNs and brain activity. This offers a broad view of alignment but lacks the
067   granularity required to uncover the functional organization within ANNs. In neuroscience, it is well
068   established that the brain is composed of specialized regions, each responsible for distinct cogni-
069
      tive functions, which together form functional brain networks (FBNs) (Bassett & Bullmore, 2006;
      Power et al., 2011; Park & Friston, 2013). These networks are dedicated to processing specific types
070
      of information, such as visual input or linguistic content, and their interactions are essential for the
071
      brain’s overall functionality (Smith et al., 2009). Similarly, it is plausible that within ANNs, a sim-
072   ilar form of organization may exist, where sub-groups of artificial neurons play specialized roles in
073   processing information.
074
075
      Building on this inspiration, our study seeks to explore the functional organization of ANNs by
      directly coupling sub-groups of artificial neurons with FBNs. We focus on large language mod-
076
      els (LLMs), such as BERT (Devlin et al., 2018) and the Llama family (Touvron et al., 2023a;b;
077
      Dubey et al., 2024), which are composed of vast numbers of artificial neurons capable of processing
078   complex linguistic information. To achieve this, we extract representative temporal patterns from
079   the activity of artificial neurons in LLMs and use them as fixed regressors in voxel-wise encod-
080   ing models. These models enable us to predict brain activity recorded via fMRI, thus linking the
081   sub-groups of artificial neurons in LLMs with their corresponding functional brain networks in the
082   human brain. Our findings demonstrate that sub-groups of artificial neurons in LLMs align closely
083   with the functional interactions within well-established brain networks. By analyzing the evolu-
084   tion of these relationships across four LLMs (BERT and Llama 1-3), we observe that the brain-like
085   functional organization in these models becomes increasingly pronounced as their capabilities grow,
086
      achieving an improved balance between the diversity of computational behaviors and the consis-
      tency of functional specializations. This research is the first to characterize the brain-like functional
087
      organization within LLMs, providing keys insights that could shape the future development of brain-
088
      inspired AI systems and engineer brain-like intelligence.

093   2.1   N EURAL E NCODING OF C OMPUTATIONAL L ANGUAGE M ODELS
094
095
      Neural encoding studies have demonstrated that computational language models based on deep neu-
      ral networks exhibit considerable representational alignment to neural activity in the human brain
096
      (Abdou, 2022; Schrimpf et al., 2021; Oota et al., 2024; Antonello et al., 2024). Most prior research
097
      has adopted linear encoding models to map between the computational representation of language
098   models and neural responses elicited by the same set of stimuli. Although these studies have yielded
099   promising results, they primarily focus on modeling the behaviors of ANs at the population level.
100   Specifically, they utilize layer-level embeddings—comprising a collection of individual AN’s re-
101   sponses—to predict neural activity, thereby leaving the functional organization of individual ANs
102   largely unexplored.
103
104   2.2   I NTERPRETING B EHAVIORS OF I NDIVIDUAL AN S
105
106   Researchers have developed various strategies to interpret the behaviors of individual ANs in com-
107   putational language models (Zhao et al., 2024). These strategies include feature attribution, prob-
      ing, neuron activation analysis, attention visualization, adversarial example, and inverse recognition,


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      among others (Wu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022; Yeh et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022). Recently,
109   researchers have employed more advanced models such as GPT-4 to automate the interpretation of
110   large scale individual ANs in less capable models such as GPT-2 (Bills et al., 2023). Singh et al.
111   (2023) similarly use LLMs to generate candidate explanations for text modules, such as a neuron in
112   LLM, based on the n-grams that elicit the most activation from the neuron. Synthetic data is then
113   generated based on these explanations, and the neuron’s activation to the data is assessed to identify
114   the top candidate explanations. While these strategies provide valuable insights into our understand-
115   ing of language models, the functional organization of individual ANs has rarely been explored.
116   Furthermore, the behaviors of individual ANs have yet to be linked to neural response, leaving the
117
      question of whether the organization of ANs mirrors the functional structure and organization found
      in the brain inadequately addressed.

      3.1   OVERVIEW
123
124   The study overview is illustrated in Figure 1. We begin by defining artificial neurons (ANs) in LLMs
125   and quantifying their temporal responses to external stimuli X ∈ Rt×n (Figure 1a). Subsequently,
126   we employ a sparse representation (Mairal et al., 2009) scheme to learn a set of representative
127   temporal response patterns, referred to as a dictionary DAN ∈ Rt×k (Figure 1b). Afterwards, we use
128   the dictionary DAN as regressors to build voxel-wise encoding models to predict fMRI brain activity.
129   The encoding coefficients associated with each atom reveal how that atom couples with functional
130   activity of the entire brain (Figure 1c). By integrating this coupling relationship with the association
131   between ANs and DAN established during learning of representative temporal responses, we infer
132
      brain-like functional organization in LLMs.

      3.2   A RTIFICIAL N EURONS IN LLM S AND T HEIR T EMPORAL R ESPONSES
154
155
      In this study, we focus on four LLMs: the pre-trained BERT model (Devlin et al., 2018), which
156   serves as a foundational transformer-based language model, and three progressively advanced mod-
157   els from the evolutionary Llama family, Llama 1-3 (Touvron et al., 2023a;b; Dubey et al., 2024).
158   BERT, with its bidirectional encoder, is a widely recognized baseline model to capture rich, con-
159   textualized word representations. In contrast, the Llama models, employing a decoder-based archi-
160   tecture, represent a more advanced, contemporary approach, exhibiting superior performance across
161   diverse tasks. Examining the evolution of these LLMs may offer insights into the development of
      functional organization within these models.


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Building on the established definitions of Artificial Neurons (ANs) in large language models (LLMs)
163   (Bills et al., 2023; Samek et al., 2021), we define each neuron in the second fully connected layer of
164   the feed-forward network within each transformer block as an individual AN (Figure 1a). In BERT,
165   this applies to the encoder blocks, while in the Llama models, it applies to the decoder blocks. With
166   this definition, the number of ANs corresponds to the dimensionality of the output embedding in
167   each transformer block. For instance, BERT consists of 12 layers, yielding 9,216 ANs (12 layers
168   × 768 dimensions per layer), whereas the Llama models, with 32 layers, define 131,072 ANs (32
169   layers × 4096 dimensions per layer).
170   Given a text input, the temporal responses of each AN are formally defined as it activations in
171   response to the sequence of input tokens. The temporal responses of all ANs at layer l can be
172   readily obtained through the layer’s output Xl ∈ Rt×nl , where t is the the number of tokens in the
173   input sequence, and n is the number of ANs at layer l, corresponding to the dimensionality of the
174   output.
175   Additionally, it is critical to synchronize the temporal responses of artificial neurons (ANs) with the
176   fMRI timeline. To achieve this, we align the text tokens with the corresponding fMRI volumes using
177   the time-stamped word-level transcripts from the Narratives fMRI dataset (Nastase et al., 2021).
178   However an fMRI volume generally spans multiple text tokens, we follow common practice in brain
179   encoding studies by averaging the ANs’ responses over these tokens within each fMRI time interval.
180   This produces a temporal response curve that matches the length of the fMRI sequence. Finally, we
181   convolve the temporal response curve of each AN with a canonical hemodynamic response function
182   (HRF) implemented in SPM1 , to account for the hemodynamic delay inherent in fMRI recordings.
183
184   3.3      R EPRESENTATIVE T EMPORAL R ESPONSE PATTERNS OF AN S
185
      Identifying representative temporal response patterns of ANs is crucial for simplifying the analysis
186
      given the vast number of ANs in models like BERT and Llama. With thousands of ANs in each
187   model (e.g., 9,216 in BERT and 131,072 in Llama), analyzing individual responses is not only
188   impractical but also risks obscuring key trends due to factors such as noise and self-correlation
189   among the ANs. In this study, we employ a sparse representation scheme (Mairal et al., 2009) to
190   learn a set of representative patterns from the temporal responses of the entire group of ANs.
191
      Given the set of temporal responses X ∈ Rt×n , where n is the total number of ANs and t is the
192
      length of the temporal responses, the objective is to find a sparse representation AAN ∈ Rk×n over
193   a dictionary DAN ∈ Rt×k , minimizing the reconstruction error while imposing a sparsity constraint
194   on AAN (Mairal et al., 2009):
195
196
                                          min ∥X − DAN AAN ∥2 + λAN ∥AAN ∥1                              (1)
197                                       AAN
198
      where λAN is a regularization parameter that controls the trade-off between reconstruction accu-
199
      racy and sparsity of AAN . In this context, DAN represents a set of basis vectors or atoms, which is
200   the representative temporal patterns that capture the essential dynamics of the ANs’ temporal re-
201   sponses. The sparsity constraint ensures that each temporal response is characterized by only a few
202   key patterns. By learning this dictionary, we can express the entire set of temporal responses X as a
203   combination of these representative patterns, weighted by the sparse coefficients in AAN .

224   3.5       R ELATIONSHIP I NFERENCE BETWEEN AN S AND B RAIN N ETWORKS
225
226   The representative temporal response patterns DAN serve as a bridge between ANs and brain activity.
227
      Specifically, the ith AN can be associated with the j th atom of DAN where AAN (·, i) is maximized.
      In this way, each atom in DAN corresponds to a subset of ANs. Simultaneously, the voxel-wise
228
      encoding models link each atom in DAN to specific brain regions, forming a brain map that typically
229
      spans multiple brain networks, such as auditory, language and visual networks. We utilize a network
230   correspondence tool (Kong et al., 2024) to automatically identify the brain networks involved in
231   these brain maps by referring to the 17 FBNs reported previously (Yeo et al., 2011). This approach
232   allows us to infer the relationship between subsets of ANs and brain networks, revealing how these
233   subsets and corresponding temporal patterns align with the brain’s functional architecture.

235   3.6       I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
236
237   We use the “Narratives” fMRI dataset (Nastase et al., 2021) in this study. The fMRI data were
238   acquired when human subjects listened to 27 spoken stories and released with various pre-processed
239
      versions. We use the AFNI-nosmooth version of one fMRI session, the “Shapes”, due to the high
      spatial resolution (2 × 2 × 2mm3 ), adequate number of subjects (59 subjects) and the integrity of
240
      the narrative stimuli. The fMRI volumes before the onset and after the end of the story are dis-
241
      carded. The time courses of each voxel is normalized to have unit norm. For the LLMs, we use the
242   pre-trained BERT 2 and Llama family 3 (Llama1-7B, Llama2-7B and Llama3-8B). In the sparse rep-
243   resentation of ANs’ temporal responses, the dictionary size (k) is set as 64, and the sparsity constraint
244   parameter λAN is set as 0.15 for all the LLMs. The λf =0.08 is used in the sparse reconstruction of
245   fMRI activity.

      4.1       S PARSE R EPRESENTATION OF AN S AND B RAIN ACTIVITY
250
251   The temporal responses of ANs can be effectively represented by the dictionary DAN , as evidenced
252   by the high R2 values shown in Figure 2(a). Among the Llama family, the R2 s values are com-
253   parable, with measurements of 0.5021±0.1119, 0.5005±0.1114 and 0.5032±0.1139, respectively.
254   The BERT model demonstrates relatively higher R2 values (0.6005±0.1127) compared to the Llama
255   family. This discrepancy may be attributed to the significantly smaller number of ANs in BERT
256   compared to the Llama models, which results in lower diversity of temporal response patterns of the
257   ANs and consequently improves the performance of dictionary learning.
258   The distribution of the R2 s vaules for the sparse reconstruction of fMRI brain activity across the
259   four LLMs shown in Figure 2(b) indicates that the Llama family correlates more closely with brain
260   activity compared to BERT, which is in accordance with their performance in natural language
261   tasks. Meanwhile, the spatial distributions of the R2 s across the four LLMs (Figure 2c-f) show
262   considerable alignment with one another. The brain regions with superior encoding performance
263   encompass the superior and middle temporal lobes, lateral and medial occipital lobes, angular gyrus,
264
      anterior and posterior cingulate cortices, temporo-parietal junction, and wide spread areas in the
      frontal lobe. This spatial distribution pattern, as well as the range of R2 , closely resembles the brain
265
      scores from previous studies on neural encoding of natural language processing models (Antonello
266
      et al., 2021; Schrimpf et al., 2021; Caucheteux & King, 2021), validating the reliability of the voxel-

427   5   C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
428
429   In this study, we explored the brain-like functional organization within LLMs. We built a neural
