009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Though typically represented by the discrete node and edge attributes, the graph
012            topological information can be sufficiently captured by the graph spectrum in a
013            continuous space. It is believed that incorporating the continuity of graph topo-
014            logical information into the generative process design could establish a superior
015            paradigm for graph generative modeling. Motivated by such prior and recent
016            advancements in the generative paradigm, we propose Graph Bayesian Flow Net-
017            works (GraphBFN) in this paper, a principled generative framework that designs
018
               an alternative generative process emphasizing the dynamics of topological infor-
               mation. Unlike recent discrete-diffusion-based methods, GraphBFN employs the
019
               continuous counts derived from sampling infinite times from a categorical distri-
020
               bution as latent to facilitate a smooth decomposition of topological information,
021            demonstrating enhanced effectiveness. To effectively realize the concept, we fur-
022            ther develop an advanced sampling strategy and new time-scheduling techniques
023            to overcome practical barriers and boost performance. Through extensive exper-
024            imental validation on both generic graph and molecular graph generation tasks,
025            GraphBFN could consistently achieve superior or competitive performance with
026            significantly higher training and sampling efficiency.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Generative modeling of graph-structured data is an important task with applications in various impor-
032   tant scenarios, e.g. molecule generation (Jin et al., 2018; Zang & Wang, 2020), traffic modeling (Yu
033
      & Gu, 2019) and protein design (Ingraham et al., 2019). With the development of deep generative
      models, there have been fruitful lines of research conducted to tackle the challenges, e.g., Generative
034
      Adversarial Networks (Martinkus et al., 2022; De Cao & Kipf, 2018), Variational Auto-encoders (Jin
035
      et al., 2018; 2020), and Autoregressive models (You et al., 2018).
036
037   Recently, diffusion models (DMs) (Song & Ermon, 2019; Ho et al., 2020) have emerged as a powerful
038   generative model across various fields (Zeng et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022). Learning DMs for graphs
039
      is challenging due to the discrete and complex relational nature of graphs. Niu et al. (2020); Jo
      et al. (2022) propose to dequantize the node vector and adjacent matrix with uniform/Gaussian
040
      noise and learn Gaussian DMs over the dequantized variables. However, these approaches face
041
      problems due to the incompatibility of continuous Gaussian diffusion processes with discrete data.
042   For instance, they involve noisy graph samples that lack well-defined topological information, e.g.,
043   clusterability or connectivity. To alleviate these problems, Vignac et al. (2022) proposed discrete
044   graph diffusion models based on discrete DMs (Austin et al., 2021). It utilizes a structured categorical
045   corruption process (corresponding to successive graph edit operations such as additions, deletions,
046   and replacements) to destroy the graph and learns to generate by reverting this process. Such discrete
047   formulation ensures noisy samples are valid discrete graphs with well-defined graph topologies. This
048   enables the discrete DMs to extract rich graph spectral features, such as graph spectrums, that aid the
049   generation process, thus achieving better performance.
050   However, though always ensuring valid graph topology, the discrete graph diffusion process is very
051   unbalanced. Some graph editing steps can significantly change the adjacency matrix and unexpectedly
052   perturb the graph topology, making it difficult to learn a satisfactory model for the reverse diffusion
053   process. Recently, utilizing certain kinds of topological information formed in continuous space to
      provide smooth and consistent information flow for the generative process has been demonstrated

      2.1   N OTATIONS AND BACKGROUND
111
112   We define the space of undirected graphs as G = (V, E), where E represents a categorical edge
113   space and V a node space. For a graph G with n nodes, the adjacency matrix E ∈ Rn×n×ce and the
114   node vector V ∈ Rn×cn are both represented using one-hot encoding. Here, ce and cn denote the
115   respective cardinalities. The absence of an edge is treated as a specific edge type. This framework
116   encompasses both abstract graphs and 2D molecules studied in this work.
117   Then, we introduce the basics of spectral graph theory for better illustration. Spectral graph theory
118   explores the relationships between graph properties and the eigenvalues of associated matrices, such
119   as the adjacency matrix or Laplacian matrix. Previous works (Martinkus et al., 2022; Vignac et al.,
120   2022) have demonstrated the effectiveness of conditioning on graph spectra in generating graphs.
121   This motivates us to revisit the generative process from the perspective of building graph spectra, as
122   shown in Fig. 1. Detailed discussions on spectral graph theory are presented in Appendix B.

526
527   We introduce GraphBFN for the generative modeling of discrete graphs. GraphBFN is motivated
528   by the intuitive idea of smoothly decomposing and modeling the information of the graph topology
529   by introducing a probabilistic matrix space. We first identify the train-test discrepancy issues in the
530   vanilla formulation of Bayesian Flow Networks and propose several innovations under the framework.
531   These appealing properties and improved techniques help GraphBFN achieve consistently superior
532
      performance on extensive graph generation benchmarks. Furthermore, GraphBFN can maintain
      decent sample quality while requiring much fewer sampling steps, thus largely improving the sample
533
      efficiency.

      A    D ETAILED I NTRODUCTION TO BAYESIAN F LOW N ETWORKS
703
704
      In this section, we introduce the basic components of Bayesian Flow Networks (BFNs) (Graves et al.,
705   2023). The graphical model of BFN could be expressed in the form of a latent variable model whose
706   prior is non-Markov autoregressive, distinguishing it from the diffusion models that have Markov
707   priors. That is, for learning the probability distribution pϕ over sample space x, a series of noisy
708   versions y{1:n} = ⟨y1 , · · · , yn ⟩ of x are introduced as latent variables. Then the model seeks to

761   U = [u1 , · · · , un ] is an orthogonal matrix for the eigenvectors and Λ = diag (λ1 , · · · , λn ) denotes
762   the collection of eigenvalues. The eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the graph Laplacian imply some
763   key properties of the graph topology structure, such as connectivity, clusterability, and distance
764   between nodes (Grone et al., 1990).
765
766   Spectral Gap and Graph Clusterability In this paragraph, we elaborate on the relationship
767   between graph clusterability and the spectral gap that we’ve used to quantity graphs’ topological
768   information in Figure 1.
769
      The spectral gap is computed by taking the difference between the first two smallest eigenvalues of
770
      graph matrices. Its definition varies slightly depending on whether the eigenvalues are taken from
771   a graph’s adjacency matrix or the Laplacian matrix. Nevertheless, regardless of which matrix it
772   takes, the correlation holds that a smaller spectral gap implies poor graph connectivity and good
773   clusterability. In our paper, we stick to the definition according to the Laplacian Matrix.
774
      The Laplacian matrix L is symmetric and has non-negative and real eigenvalues. The multiplicity of
775
      0 in the eigenvalues of L is equivalent to the number of connected components in the graph (Jiang,
776
      2012).
777
778   Let λ1 , · · · , λn denote the eigenvalues of L in the increasing order. The spectral gap in this scenario
779   is defined as λ2 − λ1 (Martinkus et al., 2022). Since the smallest eigenvalue is always 0 (as the graph
780
      must have at least one connected component), the spectral gap is nothing but the second smallest
      eigenvalue of the Laplacian.
781
782   The second smallest eigenvalue is known as the Fiedler Value or the algebraic connectivity of a graph,
783   and it reflects the connectivity of a graph (Wyss-Gallifent, 2021). Specifically, given a fixed graph
784   volume, the second smallest eigenvalue lowerbonds the diameter of a graph (Chung, 2006), which
785   is a connectivity metric that measures the maximum distance between a pair of nodes in the graph.
786
      Therefore, if the second eigenvalue is small, the graph will contain nodes that are separated far apart
      from each other, implying loose connections and more clusters.
787
788   To gain more intuition for why a small spectral gap corresponds to a highly clustered graph, we can
789   think about the extreme case where the spectral gap is 0. A zero spectral gap means that the second
790   smallest eigenvalue of the Laplacian is also 0, implying the multiplicity of zero eigenvalues is at least
791   2. This means that the graph has at least 2 connected components (i.e. at least 2 clusters with no
792
      bridge between them).

       The key intuition held by Bayesian Flow Networks(BFN) (Graves et al., 2023) is that the information
       in the generative process should be modeled in a smoothly increasing fashion for superior performance.
1120
       Such intuition is shared by the BFN and diffusion in the continuous data and is also identified by
1121
       several studies under the literature of diffusion models for image generations (Ho et al., 2020).
1122   GraphBFN is motivated by the compatibility of such "continuous information modeling" intuition
1123   and the special nature of graph data: that is, though generally represented in the discrete modality, the
1124   essential graph topology information could also be represented in the continuous manifold as implied
1125   by graph spectrum. This allows GraphBFN to better harness the inductive bias in modeling discrete
1126   data modality with continuous natures, compared to the previous discrete diffusion models (Vignac
1127   et al., 2022). Furthermore, compared to the methods that directly dequantized the data and applied
1128   continuous diffusion models, the GraphBFN needs no quantize/dequantize procedure and could be
1129   more compatible with the sparse adjacency matrix representation.
1130   There are also some potential empirical advantages of GraphBFN. The input to the neural network of
1131   GraphBFN lies in the continuous manifold, which could enjoy the advantages of numerical stability.
1132   More importantly, the inputs to the continuous distribution hold a considerably small variance
1133   compared to the previous discrete diffusion models (Graves et al., 2023). Also, such properties could
       lead to a speed-up in convergence during training.

1305
1306   The generative process of BFN all start with the same initial points, e.g., vector [ k1 , · · · , k1 ] for
1307
       k-class discrete variable, and ends with various generated samples. Hence, the variance of the input
       distribution first grows and then decreases [2], which could be intuitively understood as first exploring
1308
       the endpoint of the generative process and then finetuning towards that endpoint. Motivated by this
1309
       fact, we suggest that when the generative endpoints are approximately determined, it could be more
1310   favorable to eliminate the extra variance in θ from sequential Bayesian Update and only focus on
1311   finetuning θ based on the Bayesian flow of the current step output prediction. The condition of
1312                                      2
         ϕ Gθt , t − ϕ Gθt−1 , t − 1        ≥ ϵ is to test whether the predicted outputs of two consecutive
1313   steps are close enough, i.e. whether the steps t and steps t − 1 are generated towards the same targets.
1314   We use this as an indicator for whether the generation process is in a variance increase (exploration)
1315   or a variance decrease(finetuning) state.

       There have been several works making notable progress in continuous state-based methods, specifi-

       cally spectrum-based approaches such as (Martinkus et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2023). More recently,
1322
       several discrete state-based approaches that directly model the discrete variable of edges/nodes, e.g.

       There is the particularity of graph data, whose form is discrete and important property enjoys
       continuity. And current empirical evidence (Vignac et al., 2022; Jo et al., 2024) suggests it could be
1327
       favorable to take both into consideration. The motivation for choosing BFN lies in the fact that it
1328
       could actually model the discrete variable in continuous categorical parameter space, which perfectly
1329   fits the discrete form and continuous property of graph data.

1335   P    R ELATIONSHIP TO S PECTRAL G RAPH T HEORY
1336
1337   Here we hope to provide a more detailed discussion of the relationship of proposed methods to
1338   spectral graph theory. The framework aims to introduce a new generative method that forms in the
1339   discrete state space and takes the intrinsic continuity of the topological information into consideration.
1340
       The spectral graph theory provides an effective tool for us to implement and analyze the proposed
       approach.
1341
1342   From the implementation perspective, we use the spectral features of the input to enhance the network
1343   prediction following (Vignac et al., 2022). During both training and sampling, the spectral features of
1344   the parameters of the graphs are computed and directly appended to the network as additional inputs.
1345   Additionally, we use spectral theory to help us analyze one key reason behind our method’s perfor-
1346   mance improvement compared to previous approaches: the smooth transformation of graph topology
1347   leads to enhanced sample quality. As demonstrated in the ablation study in Appendix F, we use
1348   clustered graphs as an example to show that, for both diffusion-based methods and GraphBFN, a
1349   smoother transition of the sample’s spectral features—such as the spectral gap in the case of clustered
       graphs—during the generation process significantly improves sample quality.
