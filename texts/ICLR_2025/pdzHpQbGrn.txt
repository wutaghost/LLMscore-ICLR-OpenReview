009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Test Time Optimisation is a setting where a model is made to learn new parame-
012
               ters on-the-fly during inference with the help of those very samples it is supposed
013
               to be tested on. Learning prompts at test time to improve the performance of Vi-
014            sion Language Models(VLMs) in downstream tasks has become a popular setting
015            in recent times. In this paper, we propose a new framework for the Test Time
016            Prompt Tuning in Pre-trained VLMs which incorporates actively sampled labels
017            in the learning process to improve the performance of the model in downstream
018            test-time settings. Our problem setting is underexplored yet well-motivated by
019            considerations such as performance, efficiency and real-life applicability. Active
020            Learning can be especially beneficial in the test-time setting in providing the op-
021            tion to query the true label when the model is uncertain in a real-life scenario and
022
               Prompt Tuning provides the advantage due to parameter efficiency. Our method
               is guided by these two principles and successfully combines the two to come up
023
               with a test-time optimisation scheme that is evaluated to be an improvement over
024
               existing methods under a fair evaluation protocol. We conduct experiments across
025            10 cross-dataset transfer datasets and 4 domain-generalisation datasets to show
026            consistent improvement over the state-of-the-art.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The development of large Vision Language Models (VLMs) (Radford et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022;
033   2019; Fini et al., 2023) has led to a paradigm shift in visual scene understanding that has traditionally
034   been limited by a closed set of concepts seen during training. Through large-scale vision-language
035   pre-training, these models learn to align language and vision modalities and show remarkable zero-
036   shot transferability to unseen downstream tasks. A natural language description of the new class,
037   known as prompt, (e.g., ‘a photo of a class’) is fed to the text encoder of the VLM which is compared
038   with the visual features generated by the vision encoder. However, finding the best hand-crafted
039
      prompt is non-trivial and calls for a lot of domain-specific heuristics. Prompt learning (Zhou et al.,
      2022b;a; Wang et al., 2023) has emerged as an alternative where a few soft prompts are learned for
040
      downstream tasks after freezing the entire VLM.
041
042   Traditionally, prompt learning has been supervised (Zhou et al., 2022a;b; Khattak et al., 2023) where
043   prompts are trained on a labelled training dataset. Such approaches are naturally constrained by the
044   availability of training data with annotations. Although pre-trained models are easily available nowa-
045
      days, training data may not be available due to privacy, storage, or financial constraints. Moreover,
      in the case of a quick deployment scenario, it may not be possible to wait long to collect and anno-
046
      tate data for the downstream task as inference must continue. To address this challenge, Test-Time
047
      Transfer (TTT) (Liang et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2020; Yuan et al., 2023) has emerged as a promising
048   approach. In the absence of the labelled data, the model is updated using an unsupervised objective.
049   For example, (Shu et al., 2022) updates the prompts so that the average entropy of the logits from a
050   set of augmentations of a single test example is minimized. However, the lack of labelled examples
051   may not quite bridge the performance gap between the test time and fully supervised learning. In
052   order to address this shortcoming, active learning (Ren et al., 2021; Zhan et al., 2022) algorithms
053   can be used to incrementally select samples for annotation that improves performance with non-zero
      but low labelling cost.

      We explore a new take on generalization at test time that takes a step back when the model is not
055   quite certain about its decision and queries a human annotator for the true label of the uncertain test
056   examples. Once queried, our framework optimizes the prompts using the examples with true labels
057   in addition to the unlabelled test examples.
058
059   Comparison with prior works. A contemporary work ATTA (Gui et al., 2024) shows the impact
060   of active learning in the standard TTA setting. Their analysis established more generalization ability
061   in the model than standard TTA setting. However, their methodology and framework cannot be
062   directly applied to large VLMs like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) because they do not focus on the
063   adaptation of a specific parameter group in a large model, while ours does so by incorporating
064   the parameter-efficient Prompt Tuning. Further, they assumed a batch setting whereas we have a
065
      single test sample at a time and they also did multiple gradient updates per minibatch while we
      have only one gradient update per sample. As our approach updates and improves the model after
066
      encountering a single test example without waiting for additional examples to arrive, it is more
067
      flexible in continuous data streaming scenarios (Zhang et al., 2022). There is another recent work
068   (Bang et al., 2024) which has used Prompt Learning and Active Learning together, but that was not
069   in a test-time setting (which makes our problem more challenging), and they also did not try on
070   adaptation tasks. For more details on related work check section B of supplementary material.
071
072   Motivation. All of the above facts strongly motivate our problem-setting. Foundation models are
073   natural candidates to be used in a test time setting, and there are multiple use cases in daily life
074   where a model could be so uncertain about something that instead of making a wrong prediction, it
075   is better to query an expert whom we can abstract as an oracle (Zhu & Nowak, 2022; Shekhar et al.,
076   2021). Examples include autopilot systems and medical applications. In such use cases, the extra
077
      cost and latency incurred in consulting the oracle is often worth it owing to greater generalisability
      and accuracy (supplementary section C). We have further motivated our approach by demonstrating
078
      a shortcoming of the existing TPT methodology through an experiment in section 3.
079
080
      Challenges and Novelty. Prior work (Gui et al., 2024) had a batch setting and thus could simply
081   actively sample a fixed proportion of the batch. However, in our case we only have a single sam-
082   ple at a time and have to make the decision of whether to sample it or not without knowing how
083   uncertain the future samples would be. So we choose to dynamically adjust our threshold which
084   would determine whether or not to actively label a sample. We also store the actively labelled sam-
085   ples in a fixed size buffer which would enable us to fully extract information from the samples,
086   which would not be possible in a single time step. When the buffer is full, non-informative samples
087   from over-represented classes are evicted from them. In adaptation tasks we follow (Abdul Samadh
088   et al., 2024) to introduce distribution alignment, except that we align active samples to source data
089
      statistics of the particular class they belong to instead of the overall source data. As this is a novel
      problem setting, we have to make the effort to define an evaluation protocol which is sensible and
090
      fair. We do so, and it is shown that the evaluation protocol allows us to compare with previous arts
091
      without any unfairness.
092
093   In summary, our contributions are the following-
094
            1. We propose a novel approach in the test-time optimisation of Vision Language models
095
               where we are equipped with the ability to query for the true labels to mitigate risk and
096            improve generalisation capacity of the model. To the best of our knowledge, this is a novel
097            setting and we are the first to apply Active Learning in a Test Time setting for VLMs.
098
            2. We use the innovative idea of using a dynamically adjusted threshold to decide which test
099
               time samples have to be queried. We also incorporate the idea of class balancing in the
100            annotation buffer and the replacement of non-informative samples which ablative studies
101            reveal to be crucial to our performance.
102
            3. For adaptation tasks, we also use the novel idea of class aware distribution alignment which
103
               makes effective use of the actively labelled samples to achieve more fine-grained distribu-
104            tion alignment.
105
            4. The proposed algorithm performs better than many other methods under a low annotation
106
               budget and a limited buffer capacity. The evaluation protocol under which it was evaluated
107            is demonstrably fair.

      2.1   ACTIVE L EARNING
135
136   In active learning, we have an unlabelled dataset Du . The typical setting is that of multi-class
137   classification, having K classes. The training happens in an iterative way where, in each iteration,
138   the model selects some samples from Du , which it then passes on to the oracle to get the correct
139   annotations. At each iteration, labelled samples are added to the labelled dataset Dl . The labelled
140   dataset is used to train the model before the next query phase, after which the size of Dl further
141   increases. There is typically also a budget constraint that the model has to maintain. That is, the
142   total number of queried samples cannot exceed a budget of B.
143

      One shortcoming with the approach in TPT is that in trying to increase the certainty and consistency
219   of a test sample across views, it potentially learns spurious representations. This is due to the
220   presence of samples for which it is generally uncertain, and imposing consistency and certainty on
221   such samples will potentially make the model predict wrong labels with certainty. We confirm this
222   hypothesis by doing an experiment where we do not update on those samples with high entropy and
223   instead just evaluate on them and let them pass. The results are given in Table 1. To mitigate the
224   learning of spurious representation via updation on highly uncertain samples and also to not waste
225   the information that could be provided by them by removing them from the training scheme, we
226   propose to actively query some samples for which the model is generally uncertain. We formalise
227
      this notion of general uncertainty by selecting those samples for which the entropy of the average
      logit is more than a certain threshold which is dynamically adjusted. Our approach is visualised in
228
      Figure 1. We tried a fair evaluation protocol, which we have described in section 4.2. The most
229
      crucial detail in an evaluation protocol is the point in time at which we solicit the true label from the
230   oracle. In particular, when we solicit the label before evaluating the sample, we cannot have a fair
231   evaluation. Thus, our evaluation makes sure we are not using the ground truth label from the oracle
232   and still keeping the sample for evaluation. In samples that are not queried, we apply the standard
233   unsupervised loss as marginal entropy of the average logit.

      3.1   F ORMALISATION
244
245   We have a buffer Dl , which is initially empty and is used to store queried samples. The buffer size
246   is assumed to be limited to ensure that the framework is realistic. Let Du denote the entire dataset.
247   At time t, a test sample xt ∈ Du arrives to be evaluated. The test sample is augmented N times to
248   produce a batch of augmentations x̃t . Like TPT, we find the logits for each of them and calculate
249   the marginal entropy. The noisy augmentations are thus discarded. The average of the remaining
250   logits is computed and the entropy corresponding to that is found. We denote that entropy as H(xt ).
251
      If H(xt ) > τh , then we choose to query it.
252   Dynamic Threshold Selection. τh is dynamically adjusted. Let us denote the threshold at time step
253   t as τht .
254                                                 τht = µ̂t + z σˆt                                         (6)
255   Where µ̂t and σˆt denote the estimated mean and standard deviation of H(xt ) upto time step t. For
256   the first t̃ time steps, we keep the threshold static. That is, τht = τ ∀t ∈ [t̃]. Since we also want to be
257   within our budget and not exhaust it too early in the test stream, we adjust the value of z depending
258   on the proportion of samples queried in the test stream up to that point. Our approach is summarised
259   in Algorithm 1 in section D of the supplementary material.
260   Class Balanced Replacement Policy. As our buffer size is limited, we came up with a replacement
261   policy which would help us choose which sample to remove from the buffer in favour of an incoming
262   sample. In evicting samples from the buffer, we adopt a notion of diversity achieved via class
263   balancing. That is, we ensure that all classes are well represented in Dl . We remove the sample
264   with the lowest cross-entropy loss in the class with the most number of samples. The rationale is -
265   the class with the most number of samples is over-represented, and the sample with the least cross-
266   entropy loss is not that informative anymore. Our approach is summarised in Algorithm 2 in section
267   D of the supplementary material.

349   4.2     E VALUATION P ROTOCOL
350
351
      In the standard TPT setting, with the MEM (Section 2.3) framework, there is an update on the
      unsupervised loss before the evaluation. In our setting, we also update on the unsupervised loss
352
      before evaluation, but we can’t use a sample to calculate the supervised loss before evaluation since
353
      it doesn’t make sense to evaluate a sample after already knowing its true label. In our evaluation
354   protocol, we update on the unsupervised loss before evaluating on the sample, but actively label
355   it only after the evaluation. After active labelling, the sample is put in the buffer and can be used
356   in calculating the Lcross-entropy in equation 7 for the successive time steps. This ensures fairness
357   in evaluation since we are using the true label to update the model only after a sample has been
358   evaluated. This also means that we can evaluate prior arts with their usual evaluation scheme.

469   5   C ONCLUSION
470
471   In this paper, we tackle the problem of Active Learning of VLM prompts in a Test Time setting.
472   Building on prior work which demonstrated the relevance of Active Learning in a Test Time setting,
473   we extend that to Prompt Learning in VLMs. We further demonstrate the relevance by showing
474   that marginal entropy minimisation on uncertain samples reinforces errors in the model. This makes
475   knowing the true label even more relevant. Ours is the first work to deal with the problem of Active
476   Test Time Optimisation with a single test sample at a time. We use a dynamically adjusted threshold
477
      for entropy based on which we select the samples which will be chosen for active labelling and we
      do so in such a manner that our annotation budget is not exceeded or exhausted too early into the
478
      data streaming process. Our method uses a replacement policy which prioritises class balancing
479
      and informativeness of samples to make intelligent use of the limited-size buffer. We propose a
480   fair evaluation protocol which shows that Active Learning is indeed an effective strategy in the Test
481   Time Prompt Learning of VLMs. Future works can extend this by using pseudo labels alongside
482   active labels and effectively combining their information for even better results.

707   A     B ROADER I MPACT
708
709   The goal of deep learning is to learn discriminative invariant feature representations. One roadblock
710   in that goal is that models which are trained on a particular dataset often develop a bias towards it via
711
      overfitting which is essentially the emergence of spurious correlations. These spurious correlations
      pose both technical and ethical concerns that have to be mitigated so that deep learning models can
712
      be widely used. In the most common scenario, domain generalization helps us in eliminating these
713
      spurious correlations by applying certain techniques before the model’s deployment. However, it is
714   not always possible to gather data before the model’s deployment, and thus, it becomes imperative
715   to instead adapt the model during inference. We do not see any immediate ethical concerns which
716   are raised by this paper as it does not release any specific dataset nor does it use any human as a
717   subject. However, as with any scientific work, it has the potential for misuse, and thus, we support
718   a continued assessment of methods like ours, which advocate test-time adaptation of large models
719   using expert advice.
720
721
      B     R ELATED W ORK
722
723
      B.1   ACTIVE L EARNING
724
725   Active Learning promotes label efficiency by imposing a label budget. It can be used in a variety of
726   settings to gain knowledge about some aspect that is not already known and the model is uncertain
727   about via an oracle. The queried samples are then sent to an oracle who returns the true label. Other
728   than choosing on the basis of uncertainty (Lewis & Catlett, 1994; Yang & Loog, 2016; Roth & Small,
729   2006; Holub et al., 2008), in latest works, the model also tries to maintain diversity (Parvaneh et al.,
730   2022; Sener & Savarese, 2017) in its choice of samples to query. There is typically a dynamic
731   buffer that is maintained by the Active Learning algorithm wherein the annotated samples are put.
732
      The buffer is enlarged as more samples which are both diverse and informative are added to it.
      There have been incorporations of Active Learning into Domain Adaptation as well (Prabhu et al.,
733
      2021). In (Wang et al., 2022; Kothandaraman et al., 2022) incorporated Active Learning into Source
734
      Free Domain Adaptation which, while not directly dependent on the sourced data, are also not
735   suited for continuous data streams, unlike our TTA setting. In (Saran et al., 2023), they take up
736   the task of actively labelling samples in a streaming setting. However, their work significantly
737   differs from ours as they don’t continuously adapt their parameters as the data stream progresses.
738   Instead, they reinitialise their parameters to the original parameters after each new data point is
739   acquired. A contemporary work that is more closely related to ours is (Gui et al., 2024), where
740   they provide some foundational theoretical work on Active Test Time Adaptation. However, their
741   work is also significantly different from ours because they assume a batch setting wherein at each
742   timestep, a minibatch comes for inference while we only assume a single sample. They also do
743
      multiple gradient updates in each time step while we do only one. Their labelling is also not done
      in real-time but effectively postponed by placing the unlabelled samples in a buffer from which they
744
      have to be selected for active labelling later. This may not always be possible due to privacy and
745
      storage concerns where it may not be feasible to postpone the decision of sending a sample to an
746   oracle and instead retaining it. On the other hand, we make the active labelling decision in real-time
747   based on our dynamically adjusted threshold.
748
749
      B.2   T EST T IME A DAPTATION
750
751   Adaptation of pre-trained models is necessary so that they can be used optimally for the given task at
752   hand. Fully Test Time Adaptation (Nado et al., 2020; Schneider et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2023; Liang
753   et al., 2023) is the highly realistic and practical setting wherein a pre-trained model has to optimise
754   and adapt its parameters to a situation that it faces during inference, that is, amidst real-time use.
755   A prominent example includes that of a self-driving car where the car has to adapt to unforeseen
      conditions while it is being used. A popular way to achieve fully test time adaptation has been


                                                         14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      to update the statistics of the Batch Normalisation layer during inference. In TENT (Wang et al.,
757   2020), the Batch Normalisation parameters are updated using a self-entropy objective. However,
758   TENT makes the batched input assumption. In MEMO (Zhang et al., 2022), they use the more
759   general case of a single test input by taking multiple augmentations of a single image. TPT (Shu
760   et al., 2022) essentially extends the MEMO philosophy to prompt tuning to make VLMs adapt at
761   test-time by updating prompts. Especially relevant to this paper is the newly introduced paradigm
762   of Active Test Time Adaptation Gui et al. (2024), where the model has the option of querying a
763   few samples during test time adaptation. It was found that at a some latency cost as compared to
764   FTTA methods, the ATTA framework provides much superior results, and thus, its use-case may not
765
      completely overlap with that of FTTA.
766
767   B.3   P ROMPT L EARNING IN VLM S
768
      Prompt Learning has been proposed as a method of fine-tuning VLMs in compute-constrained sce-
769
      narios due to its parameter efficiency. In CoOp (Zhou et al., 2022b) and CoCoOp (Zhou et al.,
770   2022a), the prompts are appended to the textual tokens and help provide context to the input of
771   the VLM instead of finetuning the entire VLM model. Learning good prompts has been shown to
772   dramatically improve the performance of the CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) model. Maple (Khattak
773   et al., 2023)introduced Multimodal Prompt learning where, along with the text encoder, prompts are
774   also learnt for the vision encoder. PromptAlign(Abdul Samadh et al., 2024) extends the multimodal
775   framework of MaPle to a test time setting, and adds Distribution Alignment to this by considering
776   Imagenet to be the proxy source dataset and calculating the alignment loss between different layers
777   of the encoders for each image and precomputed statistics. Prompt Learning has been extended as
778
      a transfer learning and adaptation method in various ways and settings. Of special interest to us
      is the Test Time setting (Shu et al., 2022). The Test Time scenario is highly practical given that
779
      foundation models like VLMs are becoming more mainstream and adapting them at test time by
780
      optimising a small parameter group like prompts is likely to be the way forward. More recently, in
781   (Bang et al., 2024) they introduce Active Learning to Vision Language models where it is noted that
782   diversity in the form of class-balancing is important for non-trivial gains in VLM performance via
783   Active Learning. However, our method is significantly different from theirs because it is in a test
784   time scenario, whereas theirs was in a supervised learning setting.
785
786
      C     P OTENTIAL A PPLICATIONS
787
788
      For the sake of fair comparison with previous arts, we actively label our samples only after
789
      evaluating them. However, it is important to note that, when it comes to practical application, we
790   can also reverse the order. That is, we can ask the expert for their advice and take that as the ground
791   truth. This is especially true because we are not restricted by our methodology to postpone the
792   active labelling decision but instead do so in real-time, unlike (Gui et al., 2024) where they collect
793   the unlabelled samples in a buffer and select which ones to query later. Our single test sample in a
794   time-step assumption makes our setting even more practical.
795
796   Ideal scenarios for practical application are high-risk ones like autopilot systems and medical
797   diagnosis, where the extra cost and latency in consulting an expert is justified by the potential
798
      avoidance of hazards. In autopilot systems, when the system is uncertain, it can hand over control
      to the pilot, who then demonstrates the correct way of handling that particular scenario. The system
799
      then stores the pilot’s actions in memory and uses them to learn the correct way so that it does not
800
      need human intervention in a similar scenario in future. In medical diagnosis, whenever the system
801   is uncertain, instead of making a wrong diagnosis, it sends the sample over to a medical practitioner
802   who then provides his expert opinion.
803
