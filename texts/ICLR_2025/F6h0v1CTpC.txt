                                                                   A BSTRACT
033
034
                  Empathy is a fundamental instinct and essential need for humans, as they both
035               demonstrate empathetic actions toward others and receive empathetic support.
036               As robots become increasingly integrated into daily life, it is essential to explore
037               whether they can provide human-like empathetic support. Although existing emo-
038               tion agents have explored how to understand humans’ empathetic needs, they lack
039               to further enable robots to generate empathy-oriented task planning, neglecting
040               the evaluation of empathetic behaviors. To address this gap, we introduce Em-
041               pathyRobot, the first dataset specifically designed to benchmark and enhance
042               the empathetic actions of agents across diverse scenarios. This dataset contains
043
                  10,000 samples based on human feedback, encompassing information from vari-
                  ous modalities and corresponding empathetic task planning sequences, including
044
                  navigation and manipulation. Agents are required to perform actions based on
045
                  their understanding of both the visual scene and human emotions. To systemat-
046               ically evaluate the performance of existing agents on the EmpathyRobot dataset,
047               we conduct comprehensive experiments to test the most capable models. Our
048               findings reveal that generating accurate empathetic actions remains a significant
049               challenge. Meanwhile, we finetune an Large Language Model (LLM) on our
050               benchmark, demonstrating that it can effectively be used to enhance the empa-
051               thetic behavior of robot agents. By establishing a standard benchmark for evalu-
052               ating empathetic actions, we aim to drive advancements in the study and pursuit
053               of empathetic behaviors in robot agents. We will release the code and dataset.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
055
056            “No quality of human nature is more remarkable, both in itself and in its conse-
057            quences, than that propensity we have to sympathize with others, and to receive
058            by communication their inclinations and sentiments, however different from, or
059            even contrary to our own.”
060                                                                 — David Hume (Hume, 2000).
061
062   Imagine you are terribly sick in a foreign country. You call a cab and go to the hospital alone,
063   feeling helpless and scared. While waiting anxiously outside, someone notices you, recognizes your
064
      pain, and comes up to you, softly asking if you need a hug. You don’t know this person at all, but
      such a simple action makes you feel so much better... Empathy is a fundamental instinct in human
065
      nature. Every person has a need in nature to see the happiness of others (Smith, 2010). Receiving
066
      empathetic support from others enables us to feel understood, valued, and accepted. Recently, as
067   robots increasingly integrate into daily life (Brohan et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b)
068   and become reliable assistant agents to people (Vicentini, 2021; Breazeal et al., 2016), a natural
069   question emerges: Can such support come from intelligent robots?
070
      Scientific-wise, studying to what extent robot agents can behave empathetically helps us analyze
071
      how far these current models are from human intelligence. Recent studies show that although these
072
      models are still far from being authentically conscious (Chalmers, 2023), they can exhibit certain
073   theory of mind abilities (Strachan et al., 2024). By studying how much these models can exhibit
074   empathetic behaviors, we can understand how far these models are from human-level emotional
075   intelligence. Application-wise, pushing intelligent agents to exhibit empathy enables them to better
076   meet human needs and provide empathetic support (Leite et al., 2013; Paiva et al., 2017). Recent
077   studies show that agents can make people “feel heard,” suggesting they have the potential to offer
078   emotional and empathetic support to humans (Yin et al., 2024). However, this field has been largely
079   under-explored. There are no existing benchmarks that can systematically evaluate the ability of
080   robot agents to conduct empathetic actions. Existing benchmarks primarily focus on the success
081
      rate of completing a given task (Puig et al., 2020; Shridhar et al., 2020) and neglect the aspect of
      empathy.
082
083   In this paper, we propose “EmpathyRobot,” the first dataset featuring 10,000 samples designed to
084   evaluate and enhance robotic agents’ ability to perform empathetic actions. We demonstrate the
085   overview of EmpathyRobot in Figure 1. Our dataset is built upon the VirtualHome simulator (Puig
086   et al., 2018), a simulated home environment where the agent can perform a wide range of actions,
087
      such as picking up objects, switching appliances on/off, or opening appliances. We design vari-
      ous scenarios that involve a person in need of emotional support and let the robot agent generate
088
      empathy-driven task planning in response. Echoing core components of how humans perform em-
089
      pathetic actions (Preston & De Waal, 2002), we design our EmpathyRobot benchmark based on
090   the following principles: First, the robot agent needs to perceive empathetic cues (i.e., expressions
091   or situations) from the human. Second, The robot agent engages in an internal affective or cogni-
092   tive process to understand the scenario, such as determining the person’s feelings and what might
093   have caused their behavior. Third, the agent converts such process to its internal outcomes, such
094   as whether it should mirror the person’s emotions and how to take the person’s characteristics into
095   account. Finally, the robot agent plans and executes a sequence of empathy-driven actions as its
096   response. Based on these steps, our scenario contains the background of the person, the person’s
097   actions in the form of a video, and also the person’s language. An example of our scenario can be
098
      found in Figure 2.
099   After seeing the scenario, the agent takes a series of actions in response. For each data sample, we
100   present two action sequences and use human feedback to label one as “empathetic” and the other as
101   “less empathetic”. We then use these labels to systematically develop a set of metrics for evaluating
102   a model’s empathetic ability. To test this under various conditions, we design four difficulty levels
103   that input different amounts of information into the model. Comprehensive experiments conducted
104
      on the most capable models, such as GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023) and Llama 3 (Touvron et al., 2023),
      reveal that this benchmark remains challenging for them.
105
106   To further demonstrate the practicality of our EmpathyRobot dataset, we train an LLM and test its
107   performance on the test split. We find that training on our dataset improves the model’s perfor-
      mance in exhibiting empathetic behaviors. This suggests that our dataset can not only significantly

157   Human-Robot Interaction The study of Human-Robot Interaction (HRI) has a long history
158   (Goodrich et al., 2008). Prior works have built simulated lab environments to conduct such studies
159   (Rozo et al., 2016), which greatly limits diversity and generalization. Recently, Watch-And-Help
160   (Puig et al., 2020) develops a simulated home environment with various objects, and the agent can
161   perform actions to help the person complete a task. Communicative Watch-And-Help (Zhang et al.,
      2023) adds a communicative channel where the agents can interact through language to better per-

      form cooperative actions. However, these works are focused on better task completion (i.e., putting
163   an apple on the plate) but fail to consider how empathy affects human-robot interactions. We study
164   how robots can recognize empathetic needs and perform empathetic actions even when no explicit
165   task instruction is given.
166
      LLM as Social Agent LLMs have shown impressive out-of-box common sense reasoning abilities
167
      (Kim et al., 2023; West et al., 2021) and can even have personalities (Jiang et al., 2024). Recent
168
      works have used LLMs as generative agents (Park et al., 2023) that can plan, reason, and interact
169   in a simulated environment. Sotopia designs various characters and studies their social intelligence
170   (Zhou et al., 2023). (Liu et al., 2023b) studies training LLMs to effectively learn from simulated
171   social interactions. CAMEL (Li et al., 2024) studies the collaborative problem-solving of LLMs.
172   However, these agents are not embodied. They are largely limited to dialogues and cannot be ap-
173   plied to a grounded environment to perform executable actions. We bridge this gap to let these
174   agents engage in a simulated robotic navigation environment where these agents need to interact
175   with objects and perform grounded actions.
176   LLMs and MLLMs in Robotics Recently, LLMs and Multimodal Large Language Models
177   (MLLMs) (Li et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2023a; Alayrac et al., 2022) have been used for robotics
178   control and planning. SayCan (Ahn et al., 2022) uses LLM to interpret high-level task instructions
179   and then forms detailed low-level language instructions that can be directly mapped to the robot’s
180   low-level actions to complete the task. PaLM-E (Driess et al., 2023) uses a multimodal language
181   model for embodied reasoning. (Wang et al., 2024) uses LLMs to do visual navigation to find objects
182   on the user’s demand. However, these works are more focused on successfully performing certain
183
      actions for a given task (e.g., successfully finding the water or picking up an object). They neglect
      the aspect of studying social interactions between agents.

188   In this section, we first provide an overview of our proposed EmpathyRobot. Then, we introduce
189   our pipeline for generating the dataset. Next, we introduce our evaluation framework. Finally, we
190   introduce our method of using EmpathyRobot dataset to train empathetic agents.
191
192   3.1   TASK F ORMULATION
193
      We first provide an overview of the EmpathyRobot task formulation. Our task is defined as follows:
194
      Given a scenario of a person, the agent needs to perform grounded actions that are empathetically
195
      responsive. Similar to what humans can observe in real-world interactions, the input consists of
196   three parts: The basic background of the person in the scenario, the video of the person performing
197   actions in the scenario, and what the person says in the scenario. For the output part, we design three
198   challenges for the agent based on the three steps the agent needs to take in order to successfully
199   demonstrate empathetic behaviors as shown in Figure 2: (1) Scenario Understanding: The agent
200   should output its understanding of the scenario. This includes recognizing the person’s emotions
201   and identifying the possible causes. (2) Outcome Decision: The agent should output a high-level
202   plan of what it should do in the scenario. This includes understanding and reasoning about what
203   possible responses are empathetic and responsive. (3) Action Execution: The agent should output
204
      grounded, executable actions in the simulated environment. This includes taking valid actions in the
      environment, such as walking somewhere/picking up an object/saying something.

267   3.3.1         E VALUATION W ITH E STABLISHED M ETRICS
268
269   To execute a fully empathetic response, the model must go through three key stages: Scenario
      Understanding (internal empathetic process), Empathetic Planning (formulating an empathetic out-


                                                                                               5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      come), and Empathetic Actions (implementing the response in a real-world context). Our evaluation
271   framework is structured based on these stages.
272   Scenario Understanding The scenario understanding process includes perceiving the scenario, un-
273   derstanding the content of the scene, and reasoning about the underlying facts behind the scenario,
274   such as what may have caused the person to perform these actions, and what is the person’s un-
275   derlying emotions. To evaluate this process, the model receives the character’s input actions in the
276   scenario and the character’s background information. Then, the model is tasked to output a sce-
277   nario description of these components based on its understanding of the scenario. We compare the
278   model’s output scenario description with the ground-truth scenario description. We use the standard
279
      NLG metrics including Bleu (from Bleu-1 to Bleu-4) (Papineni et al., 2002), ROUGE-L (Lin, 2004),
      CIDEr (Vedantam et al., 2015) and SPICE (Anderson et al., 2016). We also use BERTScore (Zhang
280
      et al., 2020) which computes embeddings’ similarity.
281
      Empathetic Planning The empathetic planning process includes formulating a high-level plan of
282   what to do after comprehending the scenario. For example, after noticing the person hasn’t eaten
283   anything because of being too upset, the model may come up with a plan like “Find the person some
284   of his favorite food, then comfort him.” To evaluate this process, the model receives the character’s
285   input actions and the character’s background information. Then the model is tasked to output such
286   empathetic planning. We compare the model’s output plan with the ground-truth plan. We use the
287   same NLG metrics as in Scenario Understanding.
288   Empathetic Actions The empathetic actions process includes the model to translate the high-level
289   plan into grounded, low-level actions supported in the VirtualHome environment. For example,
290
      the high-level plan “Find the person some of his favorite food” might be grounded to “go to ta-
      ble”, “take chocolate bar”, “go to bedroom”, “put a chocolate bar on bedroom table”. To evaluate
291
      this process, the model receives the character’s input actions, the character’s background informa-
292
      tion, and an instruction of the low-level actions executable in the simulated environment. Then,
293   the model generates empathetic actions in a specific format. We use Overlap and TF-IDF scores
294   between the model’s actions and the ground-truth actions. Overlap computes the action overlap-
295   ping rate between the output sequence and the ground-truth sequence. TF-IDF computes Following
296   VirtualHome (Puig et al., 2018), we also use the LCS (Longest Common Subsequence) metric.
297   LCS computes the longest common subsequence length between the output action sequence and the
298   ground-truth action sequence. Additional details are provided in Appendix A.4.1.
299
300   3.3.2     E VALUATION WITH N EW E MPATHY-S PECIFIC M ETRICS
301
302   In addition to the metrics in Section 3.3.1, we design a new evaluation framework that draws on in-
303   sights from psychology and Human-Robot Interaction (HRI). Inspired by the RoPE scale (Charrier
304
      et al., 2019) metric that measures the perception of a robot’s empathy from a second-person per-
      spective in HRI, we design our metric on eight dimensions to evaluate the three stages of the robot’s
305
      empathy. We specify more details of how the dimensions in our evaluation framework correspond
306
      with the RoPE scale in Appendix A.3.1.
307
308           1. Action and Dialogue Association assesses the robot’s ability to understand the underly-
309              ing information of the character’s actions and dialogues. This metric is motivated by the
310              cognitive process of empathy(Park & Whang, 2022).
311
              2. Individual Understanding assesses whether the robot takes into account all details of a
312
                 character’s background information, and deducts the character’s perspective based on it.
313
                 This metric is motivated by the perspective-taking process (Park & Whang, 2022).
314
315           3. Emotional Communication evaluates (1) whether the emotion recognition is appropriate
316              and (2) whether the robot expresses appropriate emotion. This metric is motivated by the
317
                 Feature-Level Evaluation in (Yalçın, 2019)
318           4. Emotion Regulation evaluates whether the robot helps with the emotion. This metric is
319              also based on (Yalçın, 2019).
320
              5. Helpfulness evaluates whether the robot effectively assists the character.
321
322           6. Adaptability evaluates the robot’s flexibility and responsiveness in diverse scenarios. It
323              evaluates whether the robot’s interaction with the character is perceived as comfortable.
                 This is an important aspect (Charrier et al., 2018) in HRI;

345   3.4       E MPATHETIC AGENT T RAINING M ETHOD
346
      We then leveraged our dataset to train an empathetic agent and see whether it could output empa-
347   thetic responses. We used the full training set and trained on Llama3-8B (Touvron et al., 2023)
348   using two approaches: (1) Instruction tuning and (2) Reinforcement Learning with Human Feed-
349   back (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022). For instruction tuning, we used the response that was labeled as
350   “more empathetic” as the ground truth and used the LoRA technique (Hu et al., 2021) to finetune the
351   model. For RLHF, we first used our paired data to train a reward model, and then we used this re-
352   ward model to train Llama3 using LoRA. By conducting these two experiments, we explore whether
353   and to what extent this dataset can be used to leverage empathetic responses in current agents.

      4.2      T RAINED E MPATHETIC AGENT R ESULTS
460
461   Instruction-Finetuned Empathetic Agent In this experiment, we train Llama3-8B on our train-
462   ing set using instruction finetuning. For the training part, we use the text-formed input actions, the
463   dialogue, and the character’s background as input and directly finetuned the action-level response.
464   For the testing part, we let the model directly output the low-level actions and conduct action-level
465   testing. The quantatitive results are shown in Table 2. We show that such training boosts empathetic
466   behavior. As shown in Figure 5, before training, the Llama-8b model almost cannot conduct any
467
      empathetic actions. In most cases, it chooses not to conduct any empathetic actions but only outputs
      a short dialogue. After training, the model is able to conduct a series of empathetic actions, and the
468
      output dialogue is also more empathetic.
469
470   We also compare the trained Llama3-8B with the GPT-4 model. We first evaluate with the standard
471   automatic metrics and show the results in Table 2. By using this dataset for training, the instruct-
472   finetuned Llama3 with only 8B parameters outperforms GPT-4 on these metrics. We then conduct
473
      an evaluation of human preference and GPT preference and report the GPT-win rate and human-win
      rate. Specifically, for the GPT-win-rate, we provide character information, ground truth action list,
474
      scenario description, dialogue, and the two responses generated by GPT-4-turbo and the instruct-
475
      finetuned Llama3 and let GPT-4o choose the more empathetic response. For the human win rate, we
476   randomly sample 10 pairs of data from our test set and ask 10 human annotators to choose the more
477   empathetic one. The results are shown in Figure 6, instruct-finetuned Llama3-8B outperforms GPT-
478   4-turbo on both GPT-4o and human preference, suggesting that this dataset can be used in future
479   studies to effectively train empathetic agents. We provide more qualitative results in Appendix A.5.
480
481   RLHF Empathetic Agent Lastly, we use the paired data and RLHF technique to train Llama3-8B.
482   We first use the paired preference data to train a reward model and then train the Llama3 model using
483   the reward as feedback. The results are shown in Table 3 The results show that RLHF training is
484   also capable of boosting empathetic performance, but is not as effective as instruction finetuning, we
485   believe this could be due to insufficient training of the reward model. We will work on developing a
      more robust reward model to assign scores for empathetic responses.

525   5      C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
526
527
528   In this work, we introduce EmpathyRobot, the first dataset specifically designed for evaluating and
529   benchmarking the empathetic actions of robot agents. Robot agents are required to perform actions
530   based on their understanding of both the visual scene and human emotions. Our dataset contains
531
      10,000 samples, encompassing multimodal inputs and corresponding empathetic task planning se-
      quences across diverse scenarios. The dataset generation method mimics the human empathy pro-
532
      cess and can be scaled up automatically. Furthermore, we develop a systematic evaluation frame-
533
      work with four levels of empathetic difficulty, conducting comprehensive evaluations on the most
534   capable models. Finally, we fine-tune a LLM on our dataset, demonstrating its effectiveness in
535   enhancing the empathetic behavior of robot agents. Our EmpathyRobot benchmark is the first to
536   advance the study of building robot agents that provide empathetic support to humans. Regarding
537   limitations, we currently use a large-sized LLM to evaluate our EmpathyRobot dataset, but the infer-
538   ence speed is relatively slow. To improve practicality, we plan to use smaller-sized LLMs or explore
539   quantizing and compressing the model in the future. Meanwhile, we will add more human-labeled
      data to provide additional choices made by humans for empathetic responses.

1744   LCS The Longest Common Subsequence (LCS) between two action sequences is defined as the
1745   longest action subsequence present in both sequences without disturbing the order of the actions.

1747

1749   does not match, the LCS is obtained by either skipping the last action of s1 or s2 and then finding
