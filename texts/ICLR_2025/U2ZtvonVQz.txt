011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) have emerged as a promising ap-
014
               proach for solving partial differential equations (PDEs) using deep learning. How-
               ever, standard PINNs do not address the problem of constrained PDEs, where the
015
               solution must satisfy additional equality or inequality constraints beyond the gov-
016
               erning equations. In this paper, we introduce Derivative-Constrained PINNs (DC-
017            PINNs), a novel framework that seamlessly incorporates constraint information
018            into the PINNs training process. DC-PINNs employ a constraint-aware loss func-
019            tion that penalizes constraint violations while simultaneously minimizing the PDE
020            residual. Key components include self-adaptive loss balancing techniques that au-
021            tomatically tune the relative weighting of each term, enhancing training stability,
022            and the use of automatic differentiation to efficiently compute derivatives. This
023            study demonstrates the effectiveness of DC-PINNs on several benchmark prob-
024            lems, from basic to complex, such as quantitative finance and applied physics,
025
               including heat diffusion, volatility surface calibration, and incompressible flow
               dynamics. The results showcase improvements in generating solutions that satisfy
026
               the constraints compared to baseline PINNs methods. The DC-PINNs framework
027
               opens up new possibilities for solving constrained PDEs in multi-objective opti-
028            mization problems.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Partial differential equations (PDEs) play a crucial role in modelling various physical phenomena
034   across scientific and engineering disciplines. Traditional numerical methods for solving PDEs, such
035   as finite difference and finite element methods, have been widely used but often face challenges
036
      in terms of computational efficiency and handling complex geometries. Recently, Physics-Informed
      Neural Networks (PINNs) by Raissi et al. (2019) have emerged as a promising alternative, leveraging
037
      deep learning to solve PDEs with high accuracy and efficiency.
038
039   However, despite their empirical successes, PINNs still face challenges in the presence of additional
040   equality or inequality constraints beyond the PDE itself, as pioneered in Lagaris et al. (1998). Such
041   constrained PDEs are ubiquitous in real-world applications: heat diffusion with temperature bounds,
042
      option pricing with no-arbitrage constraints, and fluid flow with velocity limits, to name a few. Naive
      techniques for embedding constraints can lead to unstable training, slow convergence, and constraint
043
      violation. Although more sophisticated approaches have been proposed to better handle constraints
044
      in PINNs, Conservative PINNs Jagtap et al. (2020) and DC NN Lo & Huang (2023) enforce equal-
045   ity constraints, while Augmented Lagrangian approaches Lu et al. (2021) and theory-guided neural
046   networks Chen et al. (2021) show stronger performance on inequality-constrained PDEs. Never-
047   theless, there is still a need for methods that can effectively handle constraints while reducing the
048   dependence on intricate hyperparameter adjustments and problem-specific architectures.
049
      In this study, we propose Derivative-Constrained PINNs (DC-PINNs), a general and robust frame-
050
      work for solving derivative-constrained PDEs using deep learning. Our approach seamlessly inte-
051   grates the constraints into the learning process, ensuring that the solution satisfies the prescribed
052   conditions while maintaining the benefits of PINNs. The key contributions of this study include:
053           • A flexible constraint-aware loss function that admits general nonlinear constraints and
                seamlessly incorporates them into the PDE residual objective.

055            jective terms, including derivatives obtained through automatic differentiation stabilizing
056            training across diverse problem settings.
057          • Demonstration of the approximation ability of DC-PINNs on benchmark PDEs from sim-
058            ple to complex, including the heat equations, structural modelling of finance, and fluid
059            dynamics problem, showcasing improvements over PINNs approaches.

061   The study aims to highlight the importance of explicit constraint handling in PINNs, as relying solely

      on the PDE residual term may lead to solutions that violate critical physical principles. The extended
      approach can explore applicable to several key areas: 1. Basic PDE problems with added physical

163
164   The choice of weight coefficients for the different loss components requires careful tuning to balance
165   the contributions of the residual loss, boundary/initial condition loss, and constraint-aware loss. As
166   mentioned in Lu et al. (2021), if the multiplier for the categorized loss is larger, the constraint vio-
167   lations are penalized more severely, forcing the solutions to better satisfy the constraints. However,
168   when the penalty coefficients are too large, the optimization problem becomes ill-conditioned and
169   difficult to converge to a minimum. On the other hand, if the penalty coefficients are too small, then
170   the obtained solution will not satisfy the constraints and thus is not a valid solution. Although the
171   soft-constraint approach has worked well for inverse problems to match observed measurements,
172
      it cannot be used in general because we cannot determine appropriate multipliers in the learning
      process.
173
174   To enhance the usability and robustness of the framework, automated techniques for optimal weight
175   selection are employed. This study, involves a combination of two balanced processes in the learning
176   process, inspired by McClenny & Braga-Neto (2020); Wang et al. (2023). The first balancing inten-
177   sifies the gradient of individual losses in categorized losses to enhance local constraints, especially
178
      the objective for inequality derivative constraints. The second balancing addresses the multi-scale
      imbalance between categorized losses in (10∼11), particularly to mitigate the changing of gradient
179
      values from epoch to epoch due to the inequality feature in equation 11.

498   In this study, we proposed an extended PINNs framework called Derivative-Constrained PINNs
499
      (DC-PINNs) to effectively solve PDEs with derivative constraints, which seamlessly integrates con-
      straint information into the learning process through a constraint-aware loss function. The effec-
500
      tiveness of the DC-PINNs framework was demonstrated through a series of numerical experiments
501
      on benchmark problems related to applied physics to quantitative finance, including heat diffusion,
502   volatility surface calibration, and the Navier-Stokes equation. The results showed that DC-PINNs
503   outperformed standard PINNs approaches in terms of the ability to satisfy nonlinear constraints
504   with sufficient accuracy. DC-PINNs also illustrated computational efficiency in handling optimiza-
505   tion without significant overhead by the use of automatic differentiation and adaptive loss balancing
506   techniques.
507
      The study emphasizes the importance of explicit constraint handling in PINNs, as relying solely on
508   the PDE residual term may lead to solutions that violate critical physical principles. The DC-PINNs
509   framework opens up new possibilities for solving constrained PDEs in multi-objective optimiza-
510   tion problems. However, further investigation is needed to assess the scalability of the framework
511   to high-dimensional and more complex constraint types. Future work could explore efficient sam-
512   pling strategies and adaptive collocation point selection to mitigate the curse of dimensionality and
513   improve accuracy and computational efficiency.
