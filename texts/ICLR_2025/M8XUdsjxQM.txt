009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In high-dimensional settings, Bayesian optimization (BO) can be expensive and
012            infeasible. The random embedding Bayesian optimization algorithm is commonly
013            used to address high-dimensional BO challenges. However, this method relies on
014            the effective subspace assumption on the optimization problemвҖҷs objective function,
015            which limits its applicability. In this paper, we introduce Condensing-Expansion
016
               Projection Bayesian optimization (CEPBO), a novel random projection-based
               approach for high-dimensional BO that does not reply on the effective subspace
017
               assumption. The approach is both simple to implement and highly practical.
018
               We present two algorithms based on different random projection matrices: the
019            Gaussian projection matrix and the hashing projection matrix. Experimental results
020            demonstrate that both algorithms outperform existing random embedding-based
021            algorithms in most cases, achieving superior performance on high-dimensional BO
022            problems. The code is available in https://anonymous.4open.science/
023            r/CEPBO-14429.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      For many optimization problems, the objective function f lacks a closed-form expression, and
028
      gradient information is often unavailable, leading to what we are generally referred to as black-box
029
      functions Jones et al. (1998); Snoek et al. (2012); Shahriari et al. (2015). Bayesian optimization (BO)
030   is an efficient method for solving such optimization problems by modeling the unknown objective
031   function through a probabilistic surrogate model, typically a Gaussian Process. The BO routine is
032   a sequential search algorithm where each iteration involves estimating the surrogate model from
033   available data and then maximizing an acquisition function to determine which point should be
034   evaluated next. As the input space dimension D increases, typically D вүҘ 10, BO encounters the
035   so-called вҖҳcurse of dimensionalityвҖҷ Bellman (1966). This phenomenon refers to the exponential
036   increase in difficulty resulting from higher query complexity and the computational cost associated
037   with calculating the acquisition function.
038   To address the issue, numerous studies have proposed high-dimensional BO algorithms (Wang et al.,
039   2016; Chen et al., 2012; Binois et al., 2015; 2020; Nayebi et al., 2019; Letham et al., 2020b) that
040   typically translate high-dimensional optimizations into low-dimensional ones by various techniques,
041   and search the new point in the low-dimensional space. However, these approaches can become
042   inefficient when the maximum over the high-dimensional space cannot be well approximated by the
043   maximum over the low-dimensional space.
044   In this paper, we introduce a novel search strategy in high-dimensional BO problems called the
045   Condense-Expansion Projection (CEP) technique, which is both simple to implement and highly
046   practical. In each iteration of the sequential search, the CEP technique generates a random projection
047   matrix A вҲҲ RdГ—D , where d вүӘ D, to project the available data from the high-dimensional space to
048   the low-dimensional embedding space by multiplying them with A. It estimates the surrogate model
049   and searches for the next point to evaluation in the low-dimensional embedding space. Subsequently,
050
      it projects the searched data point back to the high-dimensional space by multiplying it with AвҠӨ for
      evaluation in the original space.
051
052   We utilize two distinct random projection matrices: the Gaussian projection matrix Dasgupta &
053   Gupta (2002) and the hashing projection matrix Rokhlin & Tygert (2008); Boutsidis & Gittens
      (2013), within the CEP technique, resulting in the development of two algorithms, CEP-REMBO

054
      and CEP-HeSBO. Our experimental results, comprising comprehensive simulation studies and
055   analysis of four real-world datasets, demonstrate that both algorithms generally outperform existing
056   random embedding-based algorithms, showcasting the superior performance of the CEP technique
057   on high-dimensional BO problems.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
061
      There is a substantial body of literature on high-dimensional BO.The most closely related approach
062
      is REMBO Wang et al. (2016) by fitting a Gaussian Process model in a low-dimensional embedding
063
      space obtained through a Gaussian random projection matrix. This approach has been further
064   investigated by Binois et al. (2015; 2020); Binois (2015); Letham et al. (2020b) under various
065   conditions. Nayebi et al. (2019) proposed HeSBO that utilizes a hashing projection matrix. However,
066   these studies are based on the assumption of an effective subspace, where a small number of
067   parameters have a significant impact on the objective function. Similar to these studies, our approach
068   evaluates the acquisition function over the embedding space. However, unlike these studies, our
069   approach select the new point in the original space, implying that our approach does not relies on the
070   effective space assumption. The second distinguishing aspect of our approach is that it generates a
071   new random projection matrix in each iteration, thereby increasing the flexibility to accommodate the
072
      possibility that the global optimum may not be located in a single embedding space.
073   Besides the embedding approach, two other techniques warrant consideration: one based on the
074   additive form of the objective function, and the other based on the dropout approach. Kandasamy
075   et al. (2015) tackled the challenges in high-dimensional BO by assuming an additive structure for
076   the function. Other works along the line include GPs with an additive kernel M. & Krause (2018);
077   Wang et al. (2017a) or cylindrical kernels Oh et al. (2018). However, this approach is limited by its
078
      reliance on the assumption of the additive form of the objective function. Li et al. (2017) applied the
      dropout technique into high-dimensional BO to alleviate reliance on assumptions regarding limited
079
      вҖңactiveвҖқ features or the additive form of the objective function. This method randomly selects subset
080
      of dimensions and optimizes variables only from these chosen dimensions via Bayesian optimization.
081   However, it necessitates вҖңfilling-in" the variables from the left-out dimensions. The proposed вҖңfill-in"
082   strategy, which involves copying the value of variables from the best function value, may lead to
083   being trapped in a local optimum, although the strategy is enhanced by mixing random values.

088
089   We consider the optimization problem
090

092   where f is a black-box function and X вҠӮ RD is some bounded set. BO is a form of sequential
093   model-based optimization, where we fit a surrogate model, typically a Gaussian Process (GP) model,
094   for f that is used to identify which parameters x should be evaluated next. The GP surrogate
095   model is f вҲј GP (m(В·), k(В·, В·)), with a mean function m(В·) and a kernel k(В·, В·). Under the GP
096   prior, the posterior for the value of f (x) at any point in the space is a normal distribution with
097   closed-form mean and variance. Using that posterior, we construct an acquisition function Оұ(x) that
098
      specifies the utility of evaluating f at x, such as Expected Improvement Jones et al. (1998). We find
      xnew = arg maxxвҲҲX Оұ(x), and in the next iteration evaluate f (xnew ). However, GPs are known to
099
      predict poorly for large dimension D Wang et al. (2016), which prevents the use of standard BO in
100
      high dimensions.

182   3.3   T HE CEPBO A LGORITHM
183
184   We employ Condensing-Expansion Projection in Bayesian Optimization, leading to the development
185   of the Condensing-Expansion Projection Bayesian Optimization (CEPBO) algorithm. In contrast to
186   Random Embedding algorithms, sucha as REMBOWang et al. (2016), HeSBONayebi et al. (2019)
187   and ALEBOLetham et al. (2020b), where a fixed projection matrix is employed, the CEPBO algorithm
188   dynamically generates a new projection matrix At during each iteration t. Through Condensing
189
      Projection, which condenses available points from the original space to a new embedding subspace
      via multiplication with At , CEPBO leverages past information to conduct BO within the embedding
190
      subspace. It then determines which point to evaluate next within this subspace. Afterward, the
191
      selected point in the embedding subspace undergoes Expansion Projection, where it is projected back
192   to the original space via multiplication with AвҠӨ
                                                     t . Subsequently, the objective function is evaluated at
193   that point. This approach bypasses the stringent assumption associated with effective dimension by
194   facilitating the projection of a new embedding subspace with each search iteration. This adaptability
195   acknowledges the possibility that the global optimum may not be confined to a single search.
196

458   5   C ONCLUSION
459
460   This paper proposes a Bayesian optimization framework utilizing the Condensing-Expansion Projec-
461   tion technique, free from reliance on the assumption of effective dimension. The primary concept
462
      involves employing projection twice within each iteration: first, projecting to an embedding subspace,
      and then projecting back to retain optimization information in the original high-dimensional space.
463
      The approach does not impose additional requirements on the projection matrix used, thereby signifi-
464
      cantly enhancing the applicability of the embedding-based Bayesian optimization algorithms. This
465   flexibility enables the selection of a suitable projection matrix according to the problemвҖҷs charac-
466   teristics beforehand. Two new Bayesian optimization algorithms based on Condensing-Expansion
467   Projection are proposed: CEP-REMBO and CEP-HeSBO based on the Gaussian projection matrix
468   and the hash-enhanced projection matrix, respectively. Empirically, this paper conducts comprehen-
469   sive experiments to assess the performance of the proposed algorithms across diverse optimization
470   scenarios. The experimental results demonstrate that the Bayesian optimization algorithms based
471   on Condensing-Expansion Projection achieved promising performance across these optimization
472   functions, overcoming the reliance on effective dimension.
473   For previous embedding-based Bayesian algorithms, achieving an optimal solution requires d to be
474   greater than or equal to the true effective dimension of the optimization problem. In contrast, our
475   algorithm does not have this requirement and performs robustly across different choices of d. In
476   practice, d can be viewed as a hyperparameter to be set. When selecting d, it is crucial to balance
477   the choice of a value that is not too small with considerations of computational costs. Empirically,
478   setting d = 5 is typically effective.
479   Our work has several limitations that can be addressed by future works. For instance, while our
480   analysis supports the concentration of the transformed point around the original point after employing
481   CEP, we acknowledge the absence of Пө-subspace embedding to preserve the variance function of a
482   Gaussian process. Although reference to Nayebi et al. (2019) suggests a potential avenue for such
483   analysis, a more thorough examination is warranted. Another constraint of our study is the absence
484   of an evaluation of CEP-based algorithms for optimization problems with billions of dimensions.
485
      Despite this potential, our approach lacks empirical validation, whereas REMBO has been shown to
      effectively address such challenges.

      In this paper, referring to the settings of LethamLetham et al. (2020a) and others, by parameterizing
879   operations and edges respectively, the optimal architecture search problem in NASBench-101 is set as
880   a continuous high-dimensional Bayesian optimization problem. Specifically, L different operations
881   are represented by one-hot encoding.
882
      Since two of the seven nodes are fixed as input and output nodes, the remaining five optional nodes,
883
      each node corresponds to three different operations, which generate a total of 15 different parameters.
884
      We optimize these parameters in the continuous [0, 1] space. For each node, we take the "operation"
885   corresponding to the maximum value of the three operations under that node as the "operation"
886   adopted by that node, and use one-hot encoding to represent the specific "operation" used under that
887   node.
888
      Since NASBench-101 uses a 7 Г— 7 upper triangular adjacency matrix to represent edges, it generates a
889
      total of 7В·6
                2 = 21 possible edges. And the five optional vertices can have three different operations, so
890
      under this encoding there are about 221 В· 35 вүҲ 510M unique models, After removing a large number
891
      of unreasonable input and output models and models with more than 9 edges, the search space still
892   has about 423k unique models. We convert these 21 possible edges into 21 binary parameters that
893   are similarly optimized in a continuous [0, 1] space. We rank the continuous values corresponding
894   to these 21 binary parameters and create an empty adjacency matrix. Then, we add edges to the
895   adjacency matrix in the percentile order of the 21 binary parameters iteratively, while pruning parts
896   that are not connected to the input or output nodes, until reaching the limit of 9 edges. Finally, the
897   combination of adjacency matrix parameters (21) and one-hot encoded "operation" parameters (15)
898   constitutes a 36-dimensional optimization space. The Bayesian optimization algorithm only needs
899   to be optimized in a high-dimensional space with D = 36, and the boundary constraint is [вҲ’1, 1]36 .
900
      Each vector x вҲҲ R36 can be decoded into a DAG and lookup evaluated in NASBench-101.
