011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            The development of Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) is striving to catch
014            up with the success of Large Language Models (LLMs), yet it faces more chal-
015            lenges to be resolved. When finetuning LVLMs with user-specific data in the
016            practical use, the pretrained weights would face the problems of forgetting and
017
               performance degradation. So it is important to improve LVLM’s performance un-
               der the continual learning settings. Some existing CL methods like Zheng et al.
018
               (2023b)Zhang et al. (2023c) have explored continual learning on VLM. However,
019
               the continual learning settings they have proposed couldn’t be adopted to LVLMs
020            smoothly because the training and finetuning process of LVLMs need amount of
021            data while previous VLM continual learning settings built on limited data and dif-
022            ferent model architectures. In this work, we first devise a task-specific continual
023            learning setting especially for LVLMs by classifying the instruction tuning
024            data for the second finetune process of LVLMs into several different tasks.
025            Mimicking the process of finetuning with user-specific task data, we found that
026            the performance of LVLMs would decline without any modules designed for con-
027            tinual learning settings. So we present LVLM-CL, a novel approach capable of
028
               continual learning settings for large vision-language models when finetuning with
               different kinds of tasks. Specifically, our LVLM-CL consists of a text feature
029
               based prompt that are different between tasks to keep the special feature of differ-
030
               ent tasks. To meet the setting of continual learning, we also design a memory bank
031            which storage previous trained tasks which helps LVLMs apply knowledge to un-
032            familiar combinations. Extensive case studies and quantitative evaluations show
033            LVLM-CL has strong capability in understanding the pivotal features of different
034            tasks and emerges impressive memory capabilities under the continual learning
035            settings. This work fosters the advancements of LVLMs by enabling them to sup-
036            port better continual finetuning toward practical use in the real world.

      2.3   C ONTINUAL L EARNING
155
156   Continual learning seeks to develop a unified model capable of progressively acquiring new knowl-
157   edge through a stream of tasks while retaining existing information. The primary obstacle is to
158   achieve learning without experiencing catastrophic forgetting, ensuring that the model’s proficiency
159   in tasks it has previously mastered does not substantially diminish. To tackle this issue, existing ap-
160   proachesKirkpatrick et al. (2017), Chaudhry et al. (2019) Buzzega et al. (2020) to continual learning
161   can be divided into three main strategies: regularization, rehearsal, and architectural innovations.
      Regularization techniques apply constraints to the learning objective to restrict alterations in the


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      model’s parameters. Rehearsal methods involve retaining a subset of training data from prior tasks
163   in a buffer and periodically retraining the model on this data to reinforce past learning. In con-
164   trast, architectural methods adapt the network’s structure to accommodate distinct parameters for
165   each new taskWang et al. (2022a)Wang et al. (2022b). These strategies have demonstrated impres-
166   sive outcomes in single-modal tasks like image classification and sequence tagging. Recently, as
167   multi-modal became popular, several works try to explore how to achieve continual learning under
168   multi-modal tasks like VQA Srinivasan et al. (2022)Fu et al. (2023) Zhang et al. (2023c). However,
169   their application to large multi-modal models with more data and different model architecture is still
170   largely uncharted territory.

180   3.1   P RELIMINARIES
181
182   The contemporary LVLMs usually adopt a modular architecture, comprising a visual encoder V ,
183   a series of connection layers W, and a large language model L. Given an input image img and
184   its corresponding question q, the visual encoder V initially processes the image and encodes it into
185   a set of visual tokens zi = V (img). These visual tokens are then transformed to align with the
186   embedding space of the language model through the connection layers, such that hi = W (zimg ).
187
      Concurrently, the text query que is tokenized into linguistic tokens hq by the tokenizer T , becoming
      hq = T (que). These visual and text tokens are concatenated into a unified sequence [hi , hq ], which
188
      serves as the input to the decoder component of the large language model L. The model then utilizes
189
      this combined representation to infer the appropriate answer ans = L([hi , hq ]), demonstrating the
190   capability of these models to perform cross-modal reasoning and answer multi-modal queries.

247   3.2   DATA C ONSTRUCTION UNDER CL S ETTING
248
249   To transform the LVLM such as LLaVA into continual learning settings for emulating the practical
250   use of finetuning LVLMs with different user-specific tasks while ensuring fairness in comparisons,
251   we decide to make a classification on the finetuning instructions of LLaVA based on different tasks.
252   Our proposed continual learning method uses tasks as fundamental units. Therefore, we need an
253   accurate and reasonable method to classify instructions into the respective task types. VQA, as a
254   significant component of training data in many LVLM studies Chen et al. (2023); Bai et al. (2023b);
255
      Liu et al. (2024a), provides valuable insights for how to produce our task classification. However,
      the task classification criteria of VQA are difficult to cover all types of LVLM instructions. More
256
      extensions are needed to accommodate instruction formats of the LVLMs.
257
258   We base our extension on TDIUC Kafle & Kanan (2017), a VQA task classification dataset. TDIUC
259   contains 12 types of questions, some of which are generated using question templates, such as the
260   counting type, and others are manually annotated, such as the sentiment understanding type. We
261
      removed the absurd type, where questions cannot be answered. To identify more complex in-
      structions, we sampled data from the complex reasoning tasks and detail tasks in LLaVA, adding
262
      them as two new task types to the dataset. Subsequently, we trained BERT, a widely used lan-
263
      guage model, as an general instruction classifier on the modified dataset. We used it to classify the
264   158k instruction-following dataset of LLaVA. However, the 665k instruction-following dataset of
265   LLaVA-1.5 includes OCRVQA Mishra et al. (2019) to enhance the model’s ability to recognize text
266   within images, and region-level VQA datasets including Visual Genome Krishna et al. (2017) and
267   RefCOCO Kazemzadeh et al. (2014) to improve the model’s capability of localizing fine-grained
268   visual details, Liu et al. (2024a). Considering the particularities of the instructions and responses
269   generated by these three datasets, we categorize the corresponding instructions into three extra task
      types. Classified rules and specific data sources for each task classification can be found in Table.1.

      4.1   OVERALL A RCHITECTURE
273
274   We introduced a task-based representation learning method, which incorporates task-specific (TS)
275   and task-invariant (TI) features for both visual and linguistic data, facilitating the acquisition of
276   representations that are both discerning and broadly applicable to the LVLM-CL setting. The frame-
277   work of our model, depicted in Figure 2, is based on a encoder, projector and LLM architecture,
278   and includes a module for an additional concentration feature learning. Additionally, in line with
279
      common rehearsal strategies Chaudhry et al. (2019); Lopez-Paz & Ranzato (2017) to mitigate catas-
      trophic forgetting in continual learning, we have established a memory buffer M that archives a
280
      selection of training instances from each completed task. As illustrated in Figure 2, when presented
281
      with an image V and a question Q, whether from the current task or from memory M , we initially
282   extract the visual features Z v with frozen vision encoder and language question’s features H q with
283   trainable text encoder. Vision features are then processed through a trainable projector W to make
284   the extracted features more distinguishable. These features are subsequently utilized as the visual
285   and textual task-specific features, V T S and QT S . Within the concentration learning module, we
286   engage in the learning and updating of concentration features for various task types. Since concen-
287   trations encapsulate essential class information that is resilient to new data, we identify appropriate
288   textual concentrations to serve as the task-invariant features QT I , contingent on the question Q.
289   Ultimately, the vectors V T S , QT S , QT I are amalgamated and funneled into the large language
290
      model such as Llama to produce a response. The entire network is optimized using a standard auto
      regressive loss function.
