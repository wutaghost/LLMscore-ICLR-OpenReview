011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013             We investigate the mechanisms behind three puzzling phenomena observed in
014             transformer-based large language models (LLMs): attention sinks, value-state
015
                drains, and residual-state peaks, collectively referred to the extreme-token phe-
                nomena. First, we demonstrate that these phenomena also arise in simpler ar-
016
                chitectures—transformers with one to three layers—trained on a toy model, the
017
                Bigram-Backcopy (BB) task. In this setting, we identify an active-dormant mech-
018             anism that causes attention heads to become attention sinks for certain domain-
019             specific inputs while remaining non-sinks for others. We further develop a precise
020             theoretical characterization of the training dynamics that lead to these phenomena,
021             revealing that they are driven by a mutual reinforcement mechanism. By small
022             interventions, we demonstrate ways to avoid extreme-token phenomena during
023             pre-training. Next, we extend our analysis to pre-trained LLMs, including Llama
024             and OLMo, revealing that many attention heads are governed by a similar active-
025             dormant mechanism as in the BB task. We further show that the same mutual rein-
026
                forcement mechanism drives the emergence of extreme-token phenomena during
                LLM pre-training. Our results study the mechanisms behind extreme-token phe-
027
                nomena in both synthetic and real settings and offer potential mitigation strategies.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Recent analyses of transformer-based open-source large language models (LLMs), such as GPT-2
033   (Radford et al., 2019), Llama-2 (Touvron et al., 2023), Llama-3 (Dubey et al., 2024), Mixtral (Jiang
034   et al., 2023), Pythia (Biderman et al., 2023), and OLMo (Groeneveld et al., 2024), have revealed
035   several intriguing phenomena:
036
037
          • Attention sinks (Xiao et al., 2023): In many attention heads, the initial token consistently
            attracts a large proportion of attention weights. In certain LLMs, other special tokens, such as
038
            the delimiter token, also draw significant attention. We refer to these as sink tokens.
039
          • Value state drains (Guo et al., 2024): The value states of sink tokens are consistently much
040
            smaller than those of other tokens.
041
          • Residual state peaks (Sun et al., 2024): The intermediate representations of sink tokens, ex-
042
            cluding those from the first and last layers, exhibit a significantly larger norm than other tokens.
043
044   These phenomena often appear simultaneously, and we collectively refer to them as the extreme-
045   token phenomena. Figure 1 illustrates these phenomena using a fixed prompt: “⟨s⟩ Summer is
046   warm. Winter is cold.” in Llama-3.1-8B-Base, where the first token, ⟨s⟩, the beginning-of-sentence
047   token, serves as the sink token. We note that the first token does not have to be ⟨s⟩ to function
048   as a sink token, as in GPT-2, where other tokens, being the initial token, can also serve this role.
049   Furthermore, in models like Llama-2, a delimiter token can also act as the sink token. Despite
050
      the consistency of these observations, no prior work has provided a satisfying explanation for the
      mechanisms behind these phenomena. As a tentative explanation, Xiao et al. (2023) suggested that
051
      models tend to dump unnecessary attention values to specific tokens.
052
053   This work aims to demystify the extreme-token phenomena in LLMs. We show that the extreme-
      token phenomena are manifestations of the active-dormant mechanism of attention heads. We sup-

079     1. In Section 2, we train one-to-three-layer transformers on the Bigram-Backcopy (BB) task,
080        which also exhibits extreme-token phenomena similar to those observed in LLMs. We show
081        that attention sinks and value-state drains are driven by the active-dormant mechanism mech-

083        namics underpin the extreme-token phenomena: attention sinks and value-state drains reinforce
084        each other, leading to a stable phase where all query tokens produce identical attention logits

090        LLMs related to extreme tokens that partially align with models trained on the BB task. Exam-
091        ining the dynamics of OLMo-7B-0424 (Groeneveld et al., 2024), we observe the same mutual
092        reinforcement mechanism and stable phase, consistent with predictions from the BB task.
093     3. Through causal interventions, we isolate the extreme-token phenomena to architecture and op-
094        timization strategy. Specifically, we show that replacing SoftMax with ReLU activations in
095        attention heads can eliminate extreme-token phenomena in the BB task, and switching from
096        Adam to SGD removes the residual-state peak phenomenon in the BB task. Our work demon-
097        strates potential classes of modifications to mitigate extreme-token phenomena in LLMs.

123   2     T HE B IGRAM -BACKCOPY TASK
124
125   The Bigram-Backcopy task consists of two sub-tasks: Bigram-transition and Backcopy. Each input
126   sequence begins with a ⟨s⟩ token, followed by tokens sampled according to a pre-determined bigram
127   transition probability P. When some special trigger tokens are encountered, instead of sampling,
128
      the preceding token is copied to the next position. Following Bietti et al. (2024), we select the
      transition P and the vocabulary V with |V| = V = 64 based on the estimated character-level bigram
129
      distribution from the tiny Shakespeare dataset. In all experiments, the set of trigger tokens T is fixed
130
      and consists of the |T | = 3 most frequent tokens in the unigram distribution. Thus, the non-trigger
131   token set, V \ T , comprises 61 tokens.

134   2.1    O NE - LAYER TRANSFORMER SHOWS ATTENTION SINKS AND VALUE - STATE DRAINS .
135
      On the Bigram-Backcopy task, we pre-train a standard one-layer transformer with only one soft-
136
      max attn head and one mlp layer. Unless otherwise specified, the model is trained with Adam
137
      for 10, 000 steps. We relegate the training details in Appendix C. Figure 2b shows that the trained
138   transformer also exhibits the attention sink phenomenon, where the ⟨s⟩ token captures a significant
139   proportion of the attention weights. More importantly, the attention weights reveal interpretable pat-
140   terns: all non-trigger tokens exhibit attention sinks, while the attention for trigger tokens is concen-
141   trated on their preceding positions. Furthermore, Figure 2c reveals a value state drain phenomenon
142   similar to LLMs, indicating that on non-trigger tokens, the attn head adds a minimal value to the
143   residual stream.
144
145   The active-dormant mechanism of the attention head: Inspired by the observed interpretable
146   attention weight patterns, we propose the active-dormant mechanism. For any given token, an at-
147   tention head is considered active if it contributes significantly to the residual state, and dormant if
148   its contribution is minimal. As illustrated in Figure 2b, trained on the BB task, the attention head is
149   active on trigger tokens and dormant on non-trigger tokens.
150   Figure 3a demonstrates that the mlp layer is responsible for the Bigram task whereas the attn head
151   takes care of the Backcopy task. When the mlp layer is zeroed out, the backcopy loss remains signif-
152   icantly better than a random guess, but the bigram loss degrades to near-random levels. Conversely,
153   when the attn layer is zeroed out, the backcopy loss becomes worse than a random guess, while
154   the bigram loss remains unaffected. This suggests that on trigger tokens, the attn head is active
155   and handles the backcopy task, whereas on non-trigger tokens, the attn head is dormant, allow-
156   ing the mlp layer to handle the Bigram task. We summarize the active-dormant mechanism of the
157
      attn head in Claim 1.
158   Claim 1. In the BB task, the attn head demonstrates active-dormant mechanism, alternating
159   between two phases:
160
161       • Dormant phase: On non-trigger tokens, the attn head puts dominant weights to the ⟨s⟩ token,
            adding minimal value to the residual stream, having little impact on the model’s output.

298   plified model that aligns with Equ. (1), and train the parameters (α ∈ RV , β ∈ RV , ξ ∈ RV , λ ∈ R)

301
      Combining theoretical insights and experimental evidence, we summarize the formation of attention
302
      sinks and value state drains as a mutual reinforcement mechanism.
303
      Claim 4 (Mutual reinforcement mechanism). For any attention head given a specific prompt, if
304
      the model can accurately predict the next token without the attention head, but adding any value
305
      state from previous tokens worsens the prediction, the attention head becomes dormant, forming an
306   attention sink, leading to the mutual reinforcement of attention sinks and value state drains:
307
308    1. The SoftMax mechanism pushes the attention weights to the value state drains, reinforcing
309       attention sinks.
310    2. The attention sinks on the value state drains further pushes down the value state, reinforcing
311       value state drains.
312
313   The mutual reinforcement stabilizes at the phase when all tokens have identical large attention logits
314   on the value state drains. Finally, due to the causal mask, the training dynamics favor the ⟨s⟩ token
315   to become an extreme token.
316
      We expect that the formation of extreme tokens in LLMs follows a similar mutual reinforcement
317
      mechanism. Indeed, although Theorem 3 focuses on a specific BB task with a simplified architecture
318   and loss function, the same principles can be applied to more general scenarios. Specifically, for an
319   attention head attn, we assume that (LLM \ attn)(v) = log pv , meaning that the LLM, even if
320   we zeroed out attn, can still output an accurate next token prediction. Furthermore, we assume
321   Val(v) = ξv ev , indicating that adding the value state from any previous tokens performs a specific
322   function. Under these assumptions, we expect the same theoretical results to apply to LLMs. In
323   Section 3, we will explore the formation of attention sinks and value state drains along the training
      dynamics of LLMs, where we find empirical evidence that aligns with the theory.

345
      the dynamics no longer push down the value state norm, and the mutual reinforcement mechanism
346   breaks. Figure 5a illustrates the training experiment on the BB task replacing SoftMax with ReLU,
347   showing that both the Bigram and Backcopy risk match the Bayes risk after 200 training steps, but
348   the attention logits of ⟨s⟩ do not grow, and the value state does not shrink, confirming the prediction.

      2.3    T HE EMERGENCE OF RESIDUAL STATE PEAKS
351
352   The residual state peaks require a three-layer structure. No residual state peaks appear in a
353   one-layer transformer trained on the BB task. We train various models on the BB task and track
354   the ⟨s⟩ token’s residual state norms after layer 0. We relegate the experimental results to Appendix
355   C. We find that a three-layer transformer is enough to produce residual state peaks. If we allow
356   to skip some mlp or attn layers, the “attn+mlp+attn+mlp+mlp” combination becomes the
357   simplest model that produces residual state peaks (Figure 10). Circuit analysis also reveals that
358   LLMs typically add a large vector in the first layer and cancel it in the last layer. We propose that
359
      the add-then-cancel mechanism is essential for residual state peaks and requires at least three layers.
360
361   Residual state peak reinforces attention sinks and value state drains in trained models. Figure
362
      5b presents the intervention results on the “‘attn+mlp+attn+mlp+mlp” model. We recenter the
      ∥Res⟨s⟩ ∥2 by subtracting the average norm of other tokens from the ⟨s⟩ token norm. The logit⟨s⟩
363
364
      and ∥Val⟨s⟩ ∥ are computed in layer 1 following the same ways as in Figure 3b. When layer 0 is
      zeroed out, the residual norm returns to normal, attention logits decrease, and the value state norm
365
      rises. It verifies that the residual state peak contributes to the attention sink and value state drain
366
      phenomenon in the trained transformer.
367
368
      Replacing Adam by SGD removes the linear growth of residual state norm. Figure 5c shows
369
      the ⟨s⟩’s residual state norms at the output of layer 0 of three-layer transformers with different
370
      configurations. Adam leads to a linear increase in residual norms. In contrast, with SGD, attention
371   sinks persist, but residual state peaks vanish. The ReLU attention, which lacks the active-dormant
372   mechanism, shows no residual state peaks.

375   3      E XTENDING P REDICTIONS OF THE BB M ODEL TO LLM S
376
377   In this section, we examine extreme-token phenomena in open-source pre-trained LLMs. In Sec-
      tion 3.1, we analyze the static behavior of these phenomena in Llama 2-7B-Base (Touvron et al.,


                                                            7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      2023), confirming that certain attention heads in LLMs exhibit both active and dormant phases. No-
379   tably, we identify a specific head that is active on GitHub samples but dormant on Wikipedia sam-
380   ples, illustrating the active-dormant mechanism. In Section 3.2, we explore the dynamic behavior of
381   extreme-token phenomena during the pre-training process of OLMo-7B (Groeneveld et al., 2024).
382   We show that the attention logits, value state norms, and residual state norms of the sink token(s) in
383   OLMo mirror their behavior in the simpler BB model. Specifically, the simultaneous formation of
384   attention sinks and value state drains gives evidence for the mutual reinforcement mechanism.
385
386   3.1   ACTIVE -D ORMANT M ECHANISM IN LLM S
387
388   Our study of the BB model leads to the following prediction about the extreme-token phenomena,
389
      which we hypothesize also applies to LLMs:
390
       Attention heads are controlled by an active-dormant mechanism. Attention sinks and value state
391                      drains indicate that an attention head is in dormant phase.
392
393   This hypothesis suggests that in LLMs, attention heads become sinks or not depending on the con-
394   text: the value vector can be totally non-informative towards picking likely next tokens for token
395   distributions (e.g., tasks) in a particular context but not in others. This is a concrete instantiation
396   vis-a-vis large-scale LLMs of the active-dormant dichotomy in Section 2, where this phenomenon
397   was shown to occur in the context of small next-token predictors and the BB task.
398   Accordingly, we strive to find instances of heads in pretrained LLMs which satisfy this principle, i.e.,
399   which are dormant on some domains and active on others. In Figure 6, we show a particular attention
400   head – Layer 16 Head 25 of Llama 2-7B-Base (Touvron et al., 2023) — which has an extremely clear
401   active-dormant distinction across two distinct contexts (e.g., tokens from RedPajama (Computer,
402   2023) drawn from the GitHub subset versus the Wikipedia subset). While there are many such
403   attention heads which are context-dependent — we provide some in Appendix D — we demonstrate
404
      this one because the conditions under which it is active are simple and interpretable, while others
      have more involved or complex criteria to become active. We observe that this attention head is
405
      dormant (i.e., an attention sink) on samples from Wikipedia, which more closely resemble prose,
406
      and active (i.e., not an attention sink) on samples from Github, which more closely resemble code.
407   We also observe that this attention head, in general, contributes significantly to the performance of
408   the model on code sequences, but has negligible impact on the performance of the model on prose
409   sequences (Figure 6b). This is a further justification, from a practical perspective, of why this head
410   is sometimes dormant and sometimes active — in some contexts we can ablate it from the model
411   entirely with no effect, but in other contexts ablating the head leads to huge performance drops. We
412   include more detail in Appendix E, where we extract a circuit for extreme-token phenomena in order
413   to analyze the dormant-active mechanism and its interaction with the semantics of the input tokens.
414

      4    C ONCLUSION
513
514
      In this work, we investigated the extreme-token phenomena, namely attention sinks, value state
515
      drains, and residual state peaks. We analyzed a simple evocative model called the Bigram-Backcopy
516   task, and theoretically and empirically showed that it exhibited the same extreme-token phenomena
517   as in LLMs. Based on the Bigram-Backcopy task, we made several detailed predictions about the
518   behavior of extreme-token phenomena in LLMs. In particular, we identified the active-dormant
519   mechanism for attention heads in both the BB model and LLMs, of which attention sinks and value
520   state drains are indicators, and a mutual reinforcement mechanism by which these phenomena are
521   induced during pretraining. Using intuition about these mechanisms, we applied minor interventions
522   to the model architecture and optimization procedure which disabled extreme-token phenomena
523   within the BB model. Overall, our work uncovers the causes of extreme-token phenomena and
524
      points to possible pathways to eliminate them during LLM training.
525   We believe the most compelling direction for future work in this area is as follows. Specifically, one
526   could build more performant and scalable interventions which would eliminate extreme-token phe-
527   nomena and observe the effect on training dynamics and the finished model. This would make it eas-
528   ier to understand whether extreme token phenomena are necessary to build a powerful transformer-
529   based LLM, whether they are merely helpful, or whether they are completely incidental to the par-
530
      ticular architecture and optimization algorithms used by the community.

      A    R ELATED WORKS
757
758   Several studies independently identified the “attention sink” phenomenon in language models and
759   vision transformers, where attention weights were found to be concentrated on a few tokens (Xiao
760   et al., 2023; Darcet et al., 2023; Han et al., 2023; Zhai et al., 2023; Elhage et al., 2023; Dettmers
761   et al., 2022). Recent research has provided more detailed characterizations of this attention pattern
762   and the attention sink phenomenon (Fu, 2024; Sun et al., 2024). Sun et al. (2024) attributed the
763   attention sink to the massive activation of the hidden representations of the corresponding tokens.
764   Both Sun et al. (2024) and Zhai et al. (2023) discussed methods for mitigating the attention sink by
765   modifying the model and training recipes. Additionally, recent studies have leveraged the attention
766
      sink phenomenon to develop improved quantization and more efficient inference algorithms (Liu
      et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024; Son et al., 2024).
767
768   The dynamics of transformers are studied under various simplifications, including linear attention
769   structures (Zhang et al., 2023; Ahn et al., 2024), reparametrizations (Tian et al., 2023b), NTK (Deora
770   et al., 2023), often in the setting of in-context linear regressions (Ahn et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023;
771   Zhang et al., 2024) and structured sequence (Bietti et al., 2024; Nichani et al., 2024; Tian et al.,
772
      2023a). Notably, Zhang et al. (2023) proves that a one-layer linear attention head trained with
      gradient descent converges to a model that implements the in-context linear regression algorithm.
773
      Huang et al. (2023); Kim et al. (2024) extend this to non-linear settings. Bietti et al. (2024) shows
774
      the fast learning of bigram memorization and the slow development of in-context abilities. Tian et al.
775   (2023a) shows the scan and snap dynamics in reparametrized one-layer transformers. Reddy (2023)
776   simplifies the structure of the induction head, showing the connection between the sharp transitions
777   of in-context learning dynamics and the nested nonlinearities of multi-layer operations.

779

782   for in-context learning (Elhage et al., 2021; Olsson et al., 2022; Todd et al., 2023; Bietti et al.,

784   mechanisms for factual identification tasks (Meng et al., 2022), and a complete circuit for indirect

      Zhu & Li, 2023; Zhu & Li, 2023; Guo et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022).

       F.1   M ULTIPLE ATTENTION S INKS VS . O NE ATTENTION S INK
1327
       As we have seen, attention heads in the BB task (Section 2), Llama 2-7B-Base (Section 3.1), and
1328   OLMo (Section 3.2) exhibit multiple attention sinks. That is, when heads in these models are dor-
1329   mant, they tend to have two attention sinks. For the LLMs in this group, at least on prose data, the
1330   ⟨s⟩ token as well as the first delimiter token (e.g., representing . or ;) are sink tokens. Meanwhile,
1331   Llama-3.1-8B-Base (Section 3) only ever has one attention sink on prose data, and the ⟨s⟩ token
1332   is always the sink token. Here, we offer a possible explanation of this phenomenon. For the BB
1333   task, multiple sink tokens are necessary to solve the task. For LLMs, we believe this distinction may
1334   be explained by the relative proportion of coding data, in which delimiters have a greater semantic
1335   meaning than prose, within the training set. For instance, OLMo was trained on DOLMA (Sol-
1336
       daini et al., 2024), which has around 411B coding tokens. Meanwhile, Llama 2 used at most (2T ×
       0.08 =) 0.16T coding tokens. Finally, Llama 3.1 used around (15.6T × 0.17 =) 2.6T coding tokens
1337
       (Dubey et al., 2024). On top of the raw count being larger, coding tokens are a larger proportion of
1338
       the whole pre-training dataset for Llama 3.1 compared to other model families. Thus, during train-
1339   ing, the presence of delimiters would not be considered unhelpful towards next-token prediction,
1340   since such delimiters carry plenty of semantics in a wide variety of cases. Our earlier hypothesis in
1341   Section 3.1 proposes that only tokens which lack semantics in almost all cases are made to be sink
1342   tokens. This could be a reason for the distinction.
