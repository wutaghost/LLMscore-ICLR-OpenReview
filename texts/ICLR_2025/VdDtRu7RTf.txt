008                                               A BSTRACT
009
010            Handwritten data generation is an intriguing research area with broad applica-
011            tions in human interaction with digital documents. In Chinese handwritten text
012            generation, practical applications necessitate the ability to produce sentence-level
013
               handwritten data to convey complex information effectively. However, existing
               methods mainly focus on generating single-font outputs. To tackle this challenge,
014            we model handwritten text generation as a style transfer problem, aiming to con-
015            vert a standard text line template into a target handwriting style. Recognizing the
016            highly structured nature of handwritten data, we view complex text lines as com-
017            positions of individual characters and their positions. We propose a two-stage text
018
               line generation method based on generative diffusion model. In the first stage,
               character positions are generated using a Character-Position-Diffusion (CharPos-
019            Diff), which, combined with standard character templates from a digital library,
020            creates text line-level templates. In the second stage, a font style transfer diffu-
021            sion model (Imitating-Diff) generates handwritten text lines directly from these
022            templates. Our extensive experiments show that our method effectively mimics
023
               handwriting styles, generates structurally accurate text lines, and facilitates the
               simultaneous generation of paragraph-level handwritten text.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Handwritten data represents a significant intersection between the fields of Natural Language Pro-
      cessing and Computer Vision, as it encompasses both linguistic information and the visual char-
030   acteristics of images. Unlike standardized printed text, handwritten data is highly personalized,
031   reflecting each person’s unique writing habits and styles, and is capable of conveying rich emotional
032   information. The task of generating handwritten data aims to simulate the properties of authentic
033   handwritten text through algorithms, enabling the automatic production of personalized handwritten
034
      content (Kang et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2022). This task must ensure that the generated characters
      are structurally correct and legible, while also visually preserving the natural style and diversity of
035   handwriting. This technology has demonstrated significant potential across various applications,
036   such as personalized digital signatures, handwriting-simulated email generation, personalized doc-
037   ument displays, and even the reproduction and restoration of historical texts (Guan et al., 2024).
038   Among the multitude of languages, Chinese characters present considerable complexity due to their
039   large quantity and intricate forms (Xu et al., 2009; Lin et al., 2015; Lian et al., 2018; Radford
040   et al., 2016; Zhu et al., 2017), thereby posing additional challenges for the generation task. Early
041   approaches could only generate fonts seen in the training set, while recent methodologies typically
042
      utilize few-shot learning techniques to adaptively mimic calligraphy style based on several provided
      style reference samples. However, these methods have primarily focused on generating individual
043   Chinese characters, neglecting the need for generating text at the line or even paragraph level in
044   real-world applications. Furthermore, due to the variability in individual writing habits, merely
045   generating single characters and concatenating them in a model-free manner can lead to rigid and
046   unnatural layouts, hindering practical applications.
047   In this paper, we model the handwritten data generation task as a style transfer problem that con-
048   verts a given standard character template into a target handwritten style. In the first stage, we gener-
049   ate high-quality content templates by modeling the positional relationships of individual characters
050
      within a text line, in conjunction with a standardized character database. In the second stage, we
      employ image-to-image translation techniques to transform the template into stylized handwritten
051   data that adheres to the style of the provided reference samples while maintaining the content of
052   the template. We utilize diffusion stochastic differential equations, which have demonstrated strong
053   capabilities in modeling data distributions to characterize both of these processes. Our contributions
      can be summarized as follows:

067   2     R ELATED W ORK
068
069   Handwritten data can be stored in the form of online trajectory sequences or offline images. The
      advantage of online handwritten data lies in its ability to maintain dynamic trajectory information
070
      of the drawing process, while the advantage of offline handwritten data lies in its more natural
071   representation of visual features such as stroke width and ink intensity.
072
      Online Handwriting Generation. Most of online handwriting generation methods are typically
073
      based on autoregressive neural network (Graves, 2013), utilizing a mixture of Gaussian distributions
074   to model the movement of pen. SketchRNN (Ha & Eck, 2018), which based on Variational Autoen-
075   coders (VAE) (Kingma & Welling, 2014) adopts RNN to conduct the task of unconditional sequence
076   data generation. Subsequently, a series of works (Aksan et al., 2018; Kotani et al., 2020; Tolosana
077   et al., 2021; Tang et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2020; Tang & Lian, 2021) focuses on conditional genera-
      tion, aiming to produce handwriting that encompasses specific content or diverse writing styles. In
078
      addition, there are studies that apply diffusion models to online sequential data (Luhman & Luhman,
079   2020; Das et al., 2023; Ren et al., 2023), achieving notable results.
080
      Offline Handwriting generation. Offline handwriting generation methods (Alonso et al., 2019; Xie
081
      et al., 2021; Bhunia et al., 2021; Gan & Wang, 2021; Kong et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022) were pri-
082   marily based on Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) in the early days, leveraging adversarial
083   training to create realistic handwriting samples. Recently, with the development of diffusion models,
084   an increasing number of studies have begun to utilize these models to generate offline font data Yang
085   et al. (2024). Diffusion models offer a novel framework that refines random noise into structured
      handwriting, allowing for more nuanced control over style and content. State-of-the-art methods can
086   now extract style information from a significantly limited number of reference samples Yao et al.
087   (2024); Dai et al. (2024).
088
      Score-Based Diffusion Model. Score-based diffusion models systematically perturb data via a
089   forward diffusion process, subsequently reversing this process to recover the original data (Sohl-
090   Dickstein et al., 2015; Song & Ermon, 2019; Ho et al., 2021). Due to their capability to balance
091   fidelity and diversity, along with advancements in accelerated sampling (Song et al., 2020; Bao
092   et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2022) and conditional generation (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Rombach et al.,
093
      2021), these models have been extensively adopted across diverse data modalities. They currently
      occupy a preeminent position in the realm of image generation tasks (Ho et al., 2021; Dhariwal &
094   Nichol, 2021). We also adopt diffusion models as our generative framework.

100   Problem Statement. Our goal is to synthesize handwritten text images given an arbitrary length
101
      Chinese string A and a single style sample Is from a writer ws . The generated handwritten text
      images should reflect the distinctive handwriting style of ws while accurately representing the con-
102   tent of the Chinese string A. This entails ensuring that the text line’s content closely adheres to the
103   specified string, with all individual character structures precisely depicted. Additionally, the spatial
104   arrangement between characters must be fluid, preserving the natural rhythm of handwritten text.
105   Existing related methods are limited to generating single Chinese characters and cannot handle
106   more complex text content. For one thing, these methods do not consider the positional relationships
107   among characters. By generating individual characters followed by simple concatenation will result
      in a lack of coherence in the font structure within the text line. For another, if directly applied to

      3.2     OVERVIEW
144
145   As shown in Figure 1, we first synthesize standard templates for text lines and then transfer these
146   templates to specified writing style images.
147   Text Line Content Template. The standard text line template consists of the bounding box of each
148   character and the standard character template. More concretely, the bounding box decides the
149   location and the size of a character in the whole text line image. The character category decides
      which font image (i.e. SimHei font) to use for a character template. All bounding box coordinates
150
      are changed to height, width, vertical distance of each character and the distance between the centers
151   of adjacent characters with normalization. We fix the number of bounding boxes of style images and
152   content images to be 32, which is adequate based on the writing habits of the majority of individuals.
153
      Based on the bounding boxes and single character templates, we seamlessly make text line template
154   images by integrating the SimHei image of each character into a blank image with size of 96x2048
155   pixels. All text line images are resized to 96 pixels in height, preserving their aspect ratio and then
156   padding to 2048 pixels in width.

195   3.2.2   I MITATING -D IFFUSION
196
      Content Aggregation Module. Following (Yang et al., 2024), we adopt the Content Aggregation
197   (CA) like them. We take CG-GAN (Kong et al., 2022) as our content encoder and style encoder. The
198   differences lies in that they inject multi scale content features into the UNet model while we only
199   inject the content features with the highest dimension into the UNet mid module, not including the
200   downsampling blocks. We find that this content injection method is so powerful that the injection
      of multi-scale content features is harmful for model’s learning good style representations, especially
201
      for handwritten characters with more styles than printed font. The content features are firstly con-
202   catenated with noised inputs. Then we apply a channel attention, including an average pooling, two
203   convolutions with the kernel size of 1, and an activation function. The channel attention output vec-
204   tor is used to weight the concatenated features through channel-wise multiplication. After a residual
205   addition, we scale the channel number of the weighted vector to the output channels.
206   Content Style Aggregation Module. We propose a content style aggregation module (CSA) to
207   replace the direct cross attention for style conditions. After content encoder and style encoder, we
208   get the high level content features and style features. Like transformer attention module, we acquire
      Q, K, V for content and style features. AdaIN is then adopted to shift the content Q distribution to
209
      the style Q distribution. The same method is operated to K. Then we concatenate the shifted content
210   K and the style K while we concatenate the origin content V and the style V. In the end, we do scaled
211   attention operation with the shifed Q, concatenated K and the concatenated V , reaching the final
212   aggregated features.
213   Training Objective. Recalling equation 6, here x0 represents the origin image and x̂0 represents
214   the predicted ones. Inspired by (Yao et al., 2024), we place greater emphasis on the contour
215   information of strokes rather than on blank areas. Therefore, we incorporate weights based on
      Harris corner detection into the existing loss function, which can be expressed as:

245
246   Our method can generate handwritten Chinese text line images of arbitrary length, where individual
247   characters can be considered as text lines of length one. Therefore, on one hand, we validate the
248   effectiveness of our approach to generate text lines. On the other hand, we also compare individual
249
      characters with the current state-of-the-art methods.

      4.1   DATASET.
252   Single Character Dataset. We conduct single-character generation experiments on the ICDAR-
253   2013 competition database (Yin et al., 2013), which consists of 60 writers and 3755 different char-
254   acter for each writer. We randomly select 80% of the dataset as the training set and the remaining
255   20% as the test set. We adopt the Unifont for content images. In the experiments, we resize all
      single-character images to 64 × 64.
256
257   Full Textline Dataset. To evaluate the ability of generating full handwritten text lines. We use the
258   Chinese offline dataset CASIA-HWDB2.0-2.2Liu et al. (2011), which consists of 52230 Chinese
      textlines from 1019 different writers. We randomly select 41781 textlines from 816 writers for
259
      training and the remaining 10449 textlines from 203 writers for testing.
260
261
      4.2   I MPLEMENT D ETAILS
262
263   In all the experiments, we use only one style reference sample to perform one-shot experiments
264   (one font for character generation and one text line for layout and text line generation). We train
265   all diffusion models with classifier-free guidance strategy under the guidance scale of 0.1 on four
266
      A6000 GPUs. Specifically, we train layout diffusion model for 60000 steps (batch size of 1024) ,
      text line diffusion model for 200000 steps with batch size of 4 and single-character diffusion model
267   for 66000 steps with batch size of 168. AdamW optimizer with β1 = 0.9 andβ2 = 0.999 is used.
268   The learning rate is set as 1e − 4 with linear schedule. During inference, DPM-solver++(Lu et al.,
269   2022) is used to speed up sampling with 25 steps. We fine-tune our model for 30000 iterations with
      the proposed content-style alignment loss.

      To validate the effectiveness of our loss in stylized Chinese handwritten text line generation, we

      5    C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
410
411   In this paper, we have presented a novel approach for generating offline Chinese handwritten text
412   lines. By modeling handwritten text generation as a style transfer problem, we developed a two-
413   stage method to handle this unexplored task. The first stage generates character positions through
414
      Character-Position-Diffusion, creating structured text line templates. The second stage employs an
      Imitating-Diff model for font style transfer, producing coherent and stylistically accurate handwrit-
415   ten text lines images. However, as shown in Figure 2, when imitating fonts with lighter ink colors,
416   our generated sample is slightly darker. Actually, the focus of the model on content versus style is
417   a matter of balance. This issue highlights the need for further refinement in the content and style
418   control modules to address such discrepancies. Additionally, our approach theoretically possesses
      significant generalizability, making it worthwhile to explore its application to other forms of hand-
419
      written data, including various languages and mathematical formulas. We consider these areas as
420   potential directions for future research.
