011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Over the past decade, Variational Autoencoders (VAE) have become a widely used
014            tool for anomaly detection (AD), with research advancing from algorithm develop-
015
               ment to real-world applications. However, a critical challenge remains—the lack of
               a reliable method to rigorously assess the reliability of detected anomalies, which
016
               restricts its use in high-stakes decision-making tasks such as medical diagnostics.
017
               To overcome this limitation, we introduce the VAE-AD Test, a novel approach for
018            quantifying the statistical reliability of VAE-based AD. The key advantage of the
019            VAE-AD Test lies in its ability to properly control the probability of misidentifying
020            anomalies under a pre-specified level of guarantee α (e.g., 0.05). Specifically, by
021            carefully analyzing the AD process of VAE, which operates through piecewise-
022            linear functions, and leveraging the Selective Inference (SI) framework to assign
023            valid p-values to the detected anomalies, we prove that theoretical control of the
024            false detection rate is achievable. Experiments conducted on both synthetic and
025            real-world datasets robustly support our theoretical results, showcasing the VAE-
026
               AD Test’s superior performance. To our knowledge, this is the first work capable
               of conducting valid statistical inference to assess the reliability of VAE-based AD.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Anomaly detection (AD) is the process of identifying unusual deviations in data that do not conform
032   to expected behavior. AD is crucial across various domains because it provides early warnings of
033   potential issues, thereby enabling timely interventions to prevent critical events. Traditional AD
034   techniques, while effective in simple scenarios, frequently fall short when dealing with complex
035   data, thus motivating the use of deep learning-based AD to better handle such complexities. In this
036   study, we focus on AD using the Variational Auto-Encoder (VAE), and its application to medical
037   images. In the training phase of VAE-based AD, the VAE learns the distribution of normal images
038   by training exclusively on images that do not contain abnormal regions. The parameters of a VAE
039
      are optimized to minimize the reconstruction error, thereby learning a compressed representation
      of the normal data. In the test phase, when a test image is fed into the trained VAE, the model
040
      attempts to reconstruct the image based on its learned representation. Since the VAE is trained on
041
      normal data, it would successfully reconstruct the normal regions of the image, while it would fail
042   to properly reconstruct the abnormal regions that were not included in the normal data. Therefore,
043   regions with large reconstruction errors are detected as abnormal regions. Figure 1 shows an example
044   of VAE-based AD for brain tumor images.
045
      When VAE-based AD is employed for high-stakes decision-making tasks, such as medical diagnosis,
046
      there is a significant risk that model inaccuracies might lead to critical errors, potentially resulting in
047
      false detections. To address this issue, we develop a statistical test for VAE-based AD, which we
048   call VAE-AD Test. The proposed VAE-AD test enables us to obtain a quantifiable and interpretable
049   measure for the detected anomaly region in the form of p-value. The obtained p-value represents
050   the probability that the detected anomaly regions are obtained by chance due to the randomness
051   contained in the data. It is important to note that the statistical test for detected abnormal regions is
052   considered as a data-driven hypothesis, as the abnormal region is selected based on the test image
053   itself. In other words, since both of the selection of the hypothesis (selection of abnormal regions)
      and the evaluation of the hypothesis (evaluation of abnormal regions) are performed on the same data,

079   Related Works. Over the last decade, there has been a significant pursuit in applying deep learning
080   techniques to AD problems (Chalapathy & Chawla, 2019; Pang et al., 2021; Tao et al., 2022). A
081
      large number of studies have been conducted for unsupervised AD using VAEs (Baur et al., 2021;
      Chen & Konukoglu, 2018; Chow et al., 2020; Jana et al., 2022). In this study, we focus on the task of
082
      identifying the anomalous regions in the input image, which is called anomaly localization within the
083
      AD tasks (Zimmerer et al., 2019; Lu & Xu, 2018; Baur et al., 2019).
084
085   There are mainly two research directions for improving VAEs for AD. The first direction is on
086   improving the detection rate (Zimmerer et al., 2019; Dehaene et al., 2020), while the second direction
087
      is on modifying the VAE itself to make it suitable for AD (Baur et al., 2019; Chen & Konukoglu, 2018;
      Wang et al., 2020). However, to our knowledge, there has been no existing studies for quantifying the
088
      statistical reliability of detected abnormal regions with theoretical validity. In traditional statistical
089
      tests, the hypothesis needs to be predetermined and must remain independent of the data. However,
090   in data-driven approaches, it is necessary to select hypotheses based on the data and then assess
091   the reliability of the hypotheses using the same data. This issue, known as double dipping, arises
092   because the same data is used for both the selection and evaluation of hypotheses, leading to selection
093   bias (Breiman, 1992). Because anomalies are detected based on data (a test image), when evaluating
094   the reliability of the detected anomalies using the same data, the issue of selection bias arises.
095
      CSI has recently gained attention as a framework for statistical hypothesis testing of data-driven
096   hypotheses (Lee et al., 2016; Taylor & Tibshirani, 2015). CSI was initially developed for the statistical
097   inference of feature selection in linear models (Fithian et al., 2015; Tibshirani et al., 2016; Loftus
098   & Taylor, 2014; Suzumura et al., 2017; Le Duy & Takeuchi, 2021; Sugiyama et al., 2021; Duy &
099   Takeuchi, 2022), then extended to various problems (Lee et al., 2015; Choi et al., 2017; Chen & Bien,
100   2020; Tanizaki et al., 2020; Duy et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2022; Le Duy et al., 2024), and later to neural
101   networks (Duy et al., 2022; Miwa et al., 2023; Shiraishi et al., 2024; Katsuoka et al., 2024), but none
102   of these studies focused on inference on VAE.
103   Contributions. To our knowledge, this is the first formulation of an approach that provides a
104   quantifiable and interpretable measure for the reliability of VAE-based AD, presented in the form of
105   a p-value within a statistical testing framework. The second contribution is the development of an SI
106   method for VAEs, which entails characterizing the hypothesis selection event by a VAE. Finally, our
107   third contribution is demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed VAE-AD Test through numerical
      experiments with synthetic data and brain tumor images.

110   Variational Autoencoder (VAE). VAEs are generative models consisting of an encoder network
111   and a decoder network Kingma & Welling (2013). Given an input image (denoted by x ∈ Rn ),
112   it is encoded as a latent vector (denoted by z ∈ Rm ), and the latent vector is decoded back to
113   the input image, where n is the number of pixels of an image and m is the dimension of a latent
114   vector. In the generative process, it is assumed that a latent vector z is sampled from a prior
115   distribution pθ∗ (z) and then, image x is sampled from a conditional distribution pθ∗ (x|z). The
116   prior distribution pθ∗ (z) and the conditional distribution pθ∗ (x|z) belongs to family of distributions
117   parametrized by θ and θ ∗ denotes the true value of the parameter. The encoder network approximates
118
      the posterior distribution pθ (z|x) by the parametric distribution qϕ (z|x), where ϕ represents the
      set of parameters, while the decoder network estimates the conditional distribution by pθ (x|z).
119
      The encoder and the decoder networks of a VAE are trained by maximizing so-called evidence
120
      lower bound (ELBO): Lθ,ϕ = Eqθ (z|x) [log pϕ (x|z)] − KL [qθ (z|x)||p(z)] , where KL [·||·] is the
121   Kullback-Leibler divergence between two distributions. We model the approximated posterior
122   distribution qϕ (z|x) as a normal distribution N (µϕ (x), In σϕ 2
                                                                        (x)), where µϕ (x) and σϕ2
                                                                                                   (x) are the
123   outputs of the encoder network. The conditional distribution pθ (x|z) is also modeled as a normal
124   distribution N (µθ (z), In ), where µθ (z) is the output of the decoder network. Furthermore, the prior
125   distribution pθ∗ (z) is modeld as a standard normal distribution N (0, Im ). The structure of the VAE
126   used in this study is shown in Appendix A.1.
127
128   Anomaly Detection Using VAEs. VAEs can be effectively used for anomaly localization task. The
129   goal of anomaly localization is to identify the abnormal region within a given test image. In the
130   training phase, we assume that only normal images (e.g., brain images without tumors) are available.
131   A VAE is trained on normal images to learn a compact representation of the normal image distribution
132
      in the latent space. In the test phase, a test image x is fed into the trained VAE, and a reconstructed
      image is obtained by using the encoder and the decoder as x̂ = µθ (µϕ (x)). Since the VAE is
133
      trained only on normal images, normal region in the test image would be reconstructed well, whereas
134
      the reconstruction error of abnormal regions would be high. Therefore, it is reasonable to define the
135   degree of anomaly of each pixel as
136
                                                Ei (x) = |xi − x̂i |, i ∈ [n],                             (1)
137
138   where xi and x̂i is the ith pixel value of x and x̂, respectively. Using a user-specified threshold
139   λ > 0, the anomaly region of a test image x is defined as
140                                            Ax = {i ∈ |n| | Ei (x) ≥ λ}.                                (2)
141   As for the definition of the anomaly region, there are possibilities other than those given by Eqs. (1)
142   and (2). In this paper, we proceed with these choices, but the proposed VAE-AD Test is generally
143   applicable to other choices.

      Parametric Programming In the Theorem 1, the truncated intervals Z can be regarded as the
      intersections of the polytopes {PkA }k:Ak =Ax with the line X = Qx + Ση(η ⊤ Ση)−1 Z. This implies
345
      that determining the truncated intervals Z is accomplished by examining this specific line rather
346
      than the entire space. Alogorithm 1 outlines the procedure to identify Z. The algorithm starts at
347   zmin and search for the truncated intervals along the line until zmax 2 . For each step, given z, the
348   algorithm computes the lower bound LA                          A
                                                k and upper bound Uk of the interval to which z belongs
349   to, as well as corresponding anomaly region Ak = AX(z) . The LA               A
                                                                         k and Uk are computed by the
350   technique described in the next subsection. This procedure is commonly referred to as parametric
351   programming known as parametric programming, which is a method to solve the optimization
352   problem for parameters such as the lasso regularization path (Efron et al., 2004; Hastie et al., 2004;
353   Karasuyama et al., 2012).
354
355   Auto-Conditioning In line 4 of Algorithm 1, we utilize a technique referred to as auto-conditioning.
356   Similar to auto-differentiation, this method leverages the fact that the entire computations of LA     k
357   and UkA executes a sequence of piece-wise linear operations. By applying the recursive rule re-
358   peatedly to these operations, LA          A
                                       k and Uk can be automatically computed. The details are deferred
359   to Appendix A.3. This implies that by implementing the computational techniques for known
360
      piecewise-linear/assignment functions, we can automatically compute the truncation intervals and
      the anomaly region. This adaptability proves particularly advantageous when dealing with complex
361
      systems like Deep Neural Networks (DNNs), where frequent and detailed structural adjustments are
362
      often required. We note that the auto-conditioning technique is originally proposed in Miwa et al.
363   (2023). However, the authors concentrate on a specific application of the saliency region, and no
364   existing studies recognize its crucial application in VAE literature. In this paper, we prove that a VAE
365   can be represented as a piecewise-assignment function, thus highlighting the crucial application of
366   auto-conditioning in efficiently conducting the proposed VAE-AD Test.

478
      We introduced a novel statistical testing framework for AD task using deep learning model. We
479
      developed a valid statistical test for VAE-based AD using CSI. We believe that this study stands
480   as a significant step toward reliability of deep learning model-based decision making. There are
481   several constraints on the class of problems where CSI can be applied, so new challenges arise
482   when applying VAEs to other types of neural networks. Additionally, we selected simple options for
483   defining the anomalous region and the test statistic, but it is unknown whether the same framework
484   can be applied to more complex options. Furthermore, as the size of the VAE network increases, the
485   computational cost of calculating the selective p-value also increases, necessitating the development
      of cost reduction methodologies such as parallelization.
