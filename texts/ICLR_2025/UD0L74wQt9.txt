009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               One puzzling artifact in machine learning, dubbed grokking, refers to the case
012            where a model exhibits delayed generalization after numerous training iterations
013            after nearly perfect overfitting. Focusing on the long delay itself on behalf of
014            machine learning practitioners, our primary goal is to accelerate the generalization
015            of a model under the grokking phenomenon. By regarding a series of gradients of
016            a parameter over training iterations as a random signal over time, we can spectrally
017            decompose the parameter trajectories under gradient descent into two compo-
018            nents: the fast-varying, overfitting-yielding component, and the slow-varying,
019            generalization-inducing component. This analysis allows us to accelerate the
020
               grokking phenomenon more than ×50 with only a few lines of code that amplifies
               the slow-varying components of the gradients. The experiments show that our algo-
021
               rithm applies to diverse tasks involving images, languages, and graphs, enabling the
022
               practical availability of this peculiar artifact of sudden generalization. Moreover,
023            we reinterpret momentum hyperparameters in gradient-based optimizers as low-
024            pass filters with size-1 windows. This bridges between optimization and classical
025            signal processing literature, suggesting a new type of optimzers augmented with
026            frequecy-domain filters.

094   part of the parameter updates u(t) which is related to the delayed generalization under grokking is
095   then the low-frequency component of the dual U (ω) . Furthermore, for gradient-based optimizers,
096   the parameter update u(g(t), t) is determined by the sample gradients g(t) generated from back-
097   propagation performed at each training step t . Therefore, we can associate slow generalization

098   under grokking to the low-frequency part of gradient signals G(ω) = t=0 g(t)e−iωt . This leads
099   to our main hypothesis: amplifying the low-frequency component of G(ω) accelerates the speed of
100   generalization under grokking phenomenon. This is to facilitate the research on grokking and to
101   broaden our understanding on optimization dynamics of grokked models.
102   In the following sections, we empirically demonstrate this claim with a simple low-frequency gradient
103   amplifier in various scenarios. These include tasks involving various network architectures including
104   Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017), MLPs, RNNs and (Graph-)ConvNets and diverse datasets
105   such as algorithmic data, images, languages, and graphs that are treated to exhibit the grokking
106   phenomenon (Liu et al., 2022b). Our method is simple, taking only a few lines of additional code,
107   and is applicable to most machine learning frameworks such as PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019), with

137   2.1                        F ILTERING TIME - VARYING GRADIENT SIGNALS IN FREQUENCY DOMAIN
138
      Amplifying the low-frequencies of the gradients g(t) can be achieved by adding a low-pass filtered
139
      signal g(t) to itself. Let h(t) be a discrete-time low-pass filter (LPF) defined over the training iteration
140   t . For simplicity, we assume a univariate time-invariant low-pass filter h(t) uniformly applied across
141   every model parameter θ . Using a convolution operator ∗ , we denote the modified gradient ĝ(t) as:
142
                                                                                                                  ĝ(t) = g(t) + h(t) ∗ g(t) ,                                                                                                                                                        (3)
143
144   which can then be plugged into the parameter update function u of the optimizer:
145                                                                                       û(t) = u(ĝ(t), t) = u(g(t) + h(t) ∗ g(t), t) .                                                                                                                                                            (4)
146   In the dual domain, equation equation 3 is equivalent to:

149   where H(ω) = t=0 h(t)e−iωt is the transfer function of the filter h(t) . Our goal can therefore be
150   restated as to design a filter h(t) with low-pass characteristics in its transfer function H(ω) .

194   Although the grokking phenomenon was first reported in the algorithmic dataset, Omnigrok (Liu
195   et al., 2022b) shows that such behavior can also be observed in a diverse set of tasks with larger and
196   more complex datasets. This section validates the efficacy of our accelerating algorithm, G ROKFAST,
197   for those various tasks and models that exhibit the grokking phenomenon.

      We first train the same task with the same model as in the first report on grokking (Power et al., 2022)
      using our new Algorithm 1. Specifically, we train a two-layer decoder-only Transformer (Vaswani
202
      et al., 2017) for a modular binary multiplication x · y (mod 97) , the same task where the grokking
203

206   loss of the model first increases before it decreases again later during the late generalization stage as
207   depicted in Figure 4b (baseline). This implies that G ROKFAST effectively keeps the model parameters
208   closer to the global optimum before the generalization happens. This difference in training dynamics
209   are revisited in Section 5 with more visualization.

      4.1                     R EINTERPRETATION AND GENERALIZATION OF MOMENTUM IN OPTIMIZERS
311
312   Close inspection reveals subtle resemblance between our G ROKFAST-EMA filter and a momentum
313   hyperparameter used in gradient-based optimizers. Specifically, the lines 7-8 of Algorithm 1 take
314   a form similar to Nesterov’s momentum, except for the small difference in the time of application:
315   in contrast to Nesterov’s momentum, where the momentum is applied after the optimizer like in
316   NAdam (Dozat, 2016), our filter is applied before the parameter update calculation.
317
      Momentum hyperparameters for gradient-based optimizers are first introduced to smooth out noisy
318   parameter updates generated from mini-batch training (Rumelhart et al., 1986). In contrast, our
319   development of G ROKFAST stems from frequency-domain interpretation of the delayed generalization
320   in Section 1. The similarity between G ROKFAST-EMA and momentum hypaerparameters, therefore,
321   implies an alternative interpretation of momenta in gradient-based optimizers—they stabilize the
322   training under stochastic gradient-based optimizers by amplifying the low-frequency component of the
323   parameter updates. Since a momentum can be seen as a low-pass filter with window of size-1, our
      new interpretation allows us to generalize the momentum hyperparameter to various types of low-pass

      filters of nontrivial (> 1) window size. In this paper, we show one simple extension: G ROKFAST
325   with moving average filter.

      5.2                          L IMITATIONS
472
473   Although Algorithm 2 shows highly effective results, it requires w times more memory to store all
474   the previous gradients, limiting its utilization. Replication of the model parameters also makes the
475   training slower; using w = 100 , the training time per iteration is increased by ×2.4 measured with a
476   single 1080 Ti GPU. Still, the reduction of wall clock time before the delayed generalization of the
477   results in Figure 11 is ×20.5 , which is also a notable reduction of time. Though the computation
478   time does not scale linearly with the memory requirements, Algorithm 2 is not generally applicable
479
      to the larger models. However, performing better than EMA filters in terms of number of training
      iterations, our results suggest a sweet spot of optimal window size between 1 (EMA) and 100 (MA).
480
      We leave this search for the optimal filters for optimizers for future work.

483   6                           R ELATED WORK
484
485   Grokking. The recently discovered grokking phenomenon (Power et al., 2022) signifies one
      possibility of overparameterized neural networks generalizing (and reasoning) beyond memorization


                                                                                                                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      of the given dataset. Most of the works, thereafter, focus on identifying its mechanism. Recent
487   studies associated grokking with a double descent phenomenon in the DNN mapping geometry
488   training dynamics (Humayun et al., 2023), the speed of pattern learning (Davies et al., 2023), and
489   the sizes of models and datasets (Huang et al., 2024), wherein the validation error initially increases
490   and then decreases with the expansion of model parameters (Nakkiran et al., 2021; Belkin et al.,
491   2019). To investigate the internal roles of each model component during grokking, Nanda et al.
492   (2023) employed mechanistic interpretability, a technique from the XAI domain, and revealed that
493   grokking may not occur abruptly but rather exhibits an internal progress measure. Their assertion
494   posits that the model captures slow, generalizing patterns, underscoring the critical role of proper
495
      optimization. Interestingly, while weight decay amplifies the double descent effect (Pezeshki et al.,
      2022), it contributes to enhanced generalization in grokking scenarios (Power et al., 2022). Liu et al.
496
      (2022b) found more examples of grokking in various tasks and analyzed their training mechanism
497
      through loss landscape analysis. Thilak et al. (2022) found a similarity between grokking and the
498   slingshot mechanism in adaptive optimizers. Barak et al. (2022) argued that optimizers reach delayed
499   generalization by amplifying sparse solutions through hidden progress. Regularizers such as weight
500   decay (Nanda et al., 2023) and the choice of the optimizer (Liu et al., 2022a) are highlighted as
501   important factors in training a model that groks. Our work is a continuation of this discussion by
502   providing a generalizable tool for the practical study of the grokking phenomenon. Through our
503   discussion, we suggest a state transition model of the grokking and visualize the trajectory of the
504   model weights in the parameter space during training.
505
506   Optimization techniques. At the core of the study of grokking lies optimization techniques (Thi-
507   lak et al., 2022). Studies have shown that generalization patterns of the model vary significantly
508
      depending on various optimization methods (Power et al., 2022; Gromov, 2023). Power et al. (2022)
      demonstrated that various factors related to optimization, such as (mini-)batch training (Li et al.,
509
      2014), the choice of optimizer, weight decay (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2018), noise injection (Zur et al.,
510
      2009), dropout (Srivastava et al., 2014), and learning rate, influence the model’s grokking pattern.
511   Nanda et al. (2023) argued that grokking does not occur without proper regularization. Further, they
512   demonstrated that techniques such as weight decay, L2 norm, and dropout induce grokking, but
513   L1 norm does not. On the other hand, Thilak et al. (2022) argued that grokking can occur without
514   explicit regularization, attributing this to the optimizer’s “visible slingshot mechanism” acting as an
515   implicit regularizer. Liu et al. (2022a) suggested using a larger learning rate for the input embedding
516   segment, facilitating unified learning of the generalization pattern. Unlike these revisiting of the
517   known training techniques, we started from a state space model and a dual domain of the training
518   dynamics. This led us to develop an optimizer augmentation algorithm, G ROKFAST, that can be
519
      applied to any existing first-order optimizers to accelerate the grokking effect for practical usage.
520
521   7   C ONCLUSION
522
523   Our reinterpretation of the deviation of each model parameter into a random signal over training
524   iteration allows us to separate gradient updates into fast-varying and slow-varying components.
525   By amplifying the latter with low-pass filtering, we can bring forward the moment of sudden late
526   generalization, i.e., grokking, reducing the number of required training iterations by up to ×50 . Our
527   comprehensive experiments and analyses suggest that our state space interpretation and the frequency
528   representation of the training dynamics is useful for studying the grokking phenomenon. Further
529
      discussions have revealed that momentum hyperparameters in optimizers can be regarded as low-pass
      gradient filters with size-1 windows. Our G ROKFAST extends the momentum to general low-pass
530
      filters with nontrivially sized windows, bridging the gap between machine learning optimization and
531
      classical signal processing literature, suggesting a new class of optimizers based on the well-studied
532   field of signal processing.

625   A    F REQUENCY RESPONSES OF THE PARAMETER UPDATES UNDER G ROKFAST
626
627   In Section 1, we have assumed that under a first-order optimizer u(g(t), t) , amplifying the low-
628   frequency components of the gradient signal g(t) of an arbitrary parameter θ(t) over a discrete
629   timestep t has the same effect of amplifying the low-frequency component of the parameter updates
630   u(t) = u(g(t), t) . This section mathematically elaborates on the effect of gradient filters h(t) to the
631   parameter update signals u(t) in the most frequently-used type of optimizers: SGD with momentum.
632   For more complicated optimizers such as Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014) and AdamW (Loshchilov &
633   Hutter, 2018) which cannot be interpreted as linear systems, we take an indirect approach: we show
634
      that filtering g(t) instead of u(t) works as it can be seen in the experiments in Section 3.
635   Stochastic gradient descent with optional momentum term is the simplest and the most widely
636   used optimization algorithm in the deep learning communities. Here, the parameter update u(t) =
637   θ(t + 1) − θ(t) of a parameter θ(t) at timestep t and its intermediate momentum m(t) is defined as:
638                                    m(t) = µm(t − 1) + (1 − τ )g(t) ,                                  (8)
639
                                       u(t) = −ηm(t) ,                                                    (9)
640
641   where µ is the scalar momentum, τ is the dampening constant for the momentum, and η is the learning
642   rate. This class of optimizers can be thought of as linear systems with state m(t) that receives an
643   input g(t) to produce an output u(t) .
644   To compare the difference between the frequency responses of the parameter update u(t) and of
645   the modified update û(t) in equation equation 4, we can think of an equivalent filter ĥ(t) defined to
