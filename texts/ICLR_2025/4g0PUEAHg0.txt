009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Transformers have achieved tremendous successes in various fields, notably ex-
012            celling in tasks involving sequential data like natural language processing. De-
013            spite their achievements, there is limited understanding of the theoretical capa-
014            bilities of transformers. In this paper, we theoretically investigate the capability
015            of transformers to autoregressively learn Bayesian networks in-context. Specifi-
016            cally, we consider a setting where a set of independent samples generated from
017            a Bayesian network are observed and form a context. We show that, there exists
018
               a simple transformer model that can (i) estimate the conditional probabilities of
               the Bayesian network according to the context, and (ii) autoregressively gener-
019
               ate a new sample according to the Bayesian network with estimated conditional
020
               probabilities. We further demonstrate in extensive experiments that such a trans-
021            former does not only exist in theory, but can also be effectively obtained through
022            training. Our analysis showcases the potential of transformers to effectively learn
023            complicated probabilistic models, and contributes to a better understanding of the
024            success of large language models.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) have achieved tremendous success across various fields. These
030   models have particularly revolutionized the way we approach problems related to text generation,
031   translation, and understanding by leveraging their ability to capture long-range dependencies and
032   contextual information. Despite these achievements, there remains limited understanding of the
033
      theoretical capabilities of transformers.
034   To theoretically understand the success of transformers, a notable line of recent works (Akyürek
035   et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023; Nichani et al., 2024) have studied
036   the power of transformers in solving in-context learning tasks. Specifically, Akyürek et al. (2022);
037   Zhang et al. (2023); Bai et al. (2023); Huang et al. (2023) theoretically studied how transformers can
038   perform in-context linear regression under the setting that the context consists of a training data set
039
      and the query token contains a test data for prediction. More recently, Huang et al. (2023) studied
      how transformers can learn causal structures. Specifically, under the assumption that the tokens
040
      consist of multiple sequences of samples generated from a causal network, Huang et al. (2023)
041
      demonstrated that gradient descent can pre-train a transformer to learn the causal network structure.
042   By doing so, when transformer sees a new context-query pair, it can generate prediction according
043   to the learned network structure and the context. However, the analysis in Huang et al. (2023) was
044   mostly limited to the setting where each variable has at most one parent.
045
      In this work, we focus on a specific setting where a set of independent samples generated from a
046
      Bayesian network are observed and form a context, and our goal is to investigate the capability of
047
      transformers to autoregressively learn Bayesian networks in-context. The main contributions of this
048   paper are two-fold: providing clean and intuitive theoretical proofs, and presenting robust empirical
049   studies. Specifically, our contributions can be summarized as follows.
050
051   • Theoretically, we demonstrate the existence of a transformer model that is capable of: (i) estimat-
052     ing the conditional probabilities of the Bayesian network given the context, and (ii) autoregres-
053     sively generating a new sample based on these estimated conditional probabilities. This gives an
        intuitive demonstration on the capability of transformers to perform complicated sampling tasks.

      • Empirically, we perform extensive experiments to validate our theoretical claims. Specifically,
055     under various settings where the Bayesian network is a (Markov) chain, a tree, or a general graph,
056     we demonstrate that a transformer can indeed be pre-trained from scratch, so that it can perform
057     in-context estimations of conditional probabilities, and help sample a new sequence of variables
058     accordingly.

066   2    R ELATED W ORK
067
068   Transformers. Transformers Vaswani et al. (2017) and its variants have demonstrated its success
069   in various of domains such as language Devlin (2018); Liu (2019); Raffel et al. (2020); Touvron
070   et al. (2023); Achiam et al. (2023), vision Dosovitskiy (2020); Jia et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2021);
071   Peebles & Xie (2023), multi-modality Gal et al. (2022); Radford et al. (2021); Li et al. (2023a)
072   etc. Large language models (LLMs) demonstrate remarkable ability to learn tasks in-context during
073
      inference, bypassing the need to update parameters Brown (2020); Lampinen et al. (2022); Khan-
      delwal et al. (2018). However, the understanding of the inner mechanisms of these models, and
074
      how they perform such complex reasoning tasks is largely remain undiscovered (Dong et al., 2022).
075
      Such disadvantage prevents us to interprete why transformers often struggles to generalize well un-
076   der out-of-distribution scenarios, especially on simple reasoning and logical tasks such as arithmetic
077   (Magister et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023; Ebrahimi et al., 2020; Suzgun et al., 2022). This raise a
078   doubt on how and when can transformers learn the appropriate algorithms to solve tasks or not.
079
080   In-Context Learning. Recently, a line of work studies transformers through the lens of in-context
081   learning (ICL) (Dong et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023), an ability of models to generate predictions
082   based on a series of examples. Empirically, recent studies find out transformers are capable of
083   learning a series of functions in-context (Garg et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023;
084   Zhou et al., 2023; Grazzi et al., 2024; Park et al., 2024; Akyürek et al., 2022), showing transformers
085   can learn to approximate a wide range of algorithms. Theoretically, several works also analyze the
086   algorithmic approximation perspective of transformers under various of conditions (Von Oswald
087
      et al., 2023; Nichani et al., 2024; Shen et al., 2023; Ahn et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023b; Wies et al.,
      2024). A recent work Von Oswald et al. (2023) shows that linear transformers (Katharopoulos
088
      et al., 2020) are capable of performing gradient descent based on in-context examples. In (Bai
089
      et al., 2023), they not only show ReLU transformers are capable of approximating gradient descent
090   with small error, but can also capable of implementing more complex ICL processes involving in-
091   context algorithm selection. To the best of authors’ knowledge there is no existing literature that
092   theoretically and empirically shows transformers learn Bayesian network in-context.

097   In this section, we briefly review the definitions of Bayesian network models, and formally define
098
      the problem of learning Bayesian networks in-context.
099   Bayesian networks. A Bayesian network is a probabilistic graphical model which specifies the con-
100   ditional dependencies among the variables by a directed acyclic graph. Each node of the Bayesian
101   network represents for a random variable, and the edges connected to a node indicates the “par-
102   ent(s)” and “child(ren)” of the node. Furthermore, Bayesian networks modeling discrete random
103   variables can be parameterized by parameters that form conditional probability tables, which define
104
      the conditional distribution of each random variable given its parent(s).
105   Learning of Bayesian networks autoregressively in-context. Consider M discrete random vari-
106   ables X1 , . . . .XM . Then there exists a Bayesian network B modeling the joint distribution of
107   X1 , . . . , XM , which satisfies that X1 is a root variable with no parents, and that for any i ∈ [M ], the
      parents of Xi are with indices smaller than i. For a given Bayesian network B, and for any m ∈ [M ],

169   4   M AIN THEORY
170
171   We consider standard transformer architectures introduced in Vaswani et al. (2017) that consists
172   of self-attention layers and feed-forward layers with skip connections. Specifically, in our setup,
173   an attention layer with parameter matrices V ∈ R(2M +1)d×(2M +1)d , K ∈ RM d×(2M +1)d , Q ∈
174   RM d×(2M +1)d is defined as follows:
175                          AttnV,K,Q (X) = X + VXsoftmax[(KX)⊤ (QX)],
176
177   where softmax denotes the column-wise softmax function. In addition, an feed-forward layer with
178   parameter matrices W1 , W2 ∈ R(2M +1)d×(2M +1)d is defined as follows:
179                                    FFW1 ,W2 (X) = X + W2 σ(W1 X),
180
181   where σ(·) denotes the entry-wise activation function. We consider the ReLU activation function
182
      σ(z) = max{0, z}. Given the above definitions, we follow the convention in Bai et al. (2023) and
      call the following mapping a “transformer layer”:
183
184                                  TFθ (X) = FFW1 ,W2 [AttnV,K,Q (X)],
185
      where θ = (V, K, Q, W1 , W2 ) denotes the collection of all parameters in the self-attention and
186
      feed-forward layer.
187
188   The above specifies the definition of a transformer layer, which is a mapping from R(2M +1)d×(N +1)
189   to R(2M +1)d×(N +1) (for any N ∈ N+ ). To handle the task of generating d-class categorical vari-
190   ables, we also need to specific the output of the model, which maps matrices in R(2M +1)d×(N +1) to
191   vectors in Rd . Here we follow the common practice, and define the following Read(·) function
192
                              Read(Z) := ZeN +1 for all Z ∈ R(2M +1)d×(N +1)
193
194   to output the last column of the input matrix, and consider a linear mapping LinearA (·) to convert
195   the output of the Read(·) function to the final distribution vector:
196
                                     LinearA (z) = Az for all z ∈ R(2M +1)d ,
197
198   where A ∈ Rd×(2M +1)d is the paramter matrix of the linear mapping.

      The main paper contains three parts of the experiments. First, we verify our theoretical results by
248
      studying the capabilities of transformers learning Bayesian networks. Second, we analyze whether

253   5.1   T RANSFORMERS L EARN BAYESIAN I NFERENCE
254
255   Here we conduct the experiment of training transformers to perform Bayesian inference. We also
256   visualize their convergence result with loss and accuracy curves.
257
      Datasets. We consider training transformers to learn Bayesian networks of three structures: chain,
258   tree and general graph, see Figure 1 for illustration. All variables in our dataset are with binary
259   values (2 possible outcomes). For each structure, we generate 50k graphs with randomly initialized
260   probability distributions, and sample all training data from them. To reduce noise during training, the
261   probability distributions of those graphs are sampled from one of the following uniform distributions
262   {U (0.15, 0.3), U (0.7, 0.85)}. Specifically, we try to avoid two scenarios: (i) variables are indepen-
263   dent to each other (probability close to 0.5) and (2) deterministic relationship between variables
264   (probability close to 0 or 1), as these scenarios either lose the graph structure or the probabilistic
265   nature of networks. Empirically, we also find out models converge better under this approach.
266   Metrics. We denote the number of examples during training as Ntrain , and Ntest as the number of
267   examples during evaluation. For evaluations, we randomly generate 1 graph for each graph structure
268   as testset. We report the accuracy of transformers, Naive Bayes, Bayesian inference and the optimal
269   accuracy on testset and vary the number of examples in each prediction. Note that the optimal
      accuracy is not 1 due to the probabilistic nature of networks. For each number of examples Ntest ∈


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      [5, 100], we randomly sample a set of 1500 observations, with each observation contains Ntest ICL
271   examples and 1 test token. We separate the evaluation of each variables in the graph as they have
272   different optimal accuracy. The reported accuracy are the average over 10 runs with different random
273   seeds. Due to space limit, we select 3 variables for each graph structure to present. Experimental
274   details are in Appendix C.2.
275
      Model. We follow most settings in Bai et al. (2023) by using a transformer with 8 heads and 256 hid-
276
      den dimension for all experiments, the only difference is we use 6 instead of 12 layer transformers.
277   A small difference is we set p ∈ RM as zero vectors, and pq ∈ RM a one-hot vector, indicating the
278   variable to predict. Note that we remove the positional embedding/encoding from the transformer
279   to highlight the utility of p. The input dimension is always 3 times of the number of variables in the
280   graph as our construction in Equation 3.2.
281
      Setup. We set Ntrain = 100 during training, and evaluate models on different size of Ntest . Similar
282
      to Bai et al. (2023), we train the transformer with carefully designed curriculum discussed in the
283   following paragraph. After the training loss reach to a threshold, we then advance the curriculum
284   by revealing one extra variable. We find out this approach helps models to learn the graph structure
285   better. We explain details the in following paragraph.
286
      Curriculum Design. We take the data-level curriculum approach to train the transformers on
287
      Bayesian Inference. The goal of the curriculum is to lead transformers to learn the whole graph
288
      structure well. We determine the difficulty of the curriculum by the number of variables in the
289   graph. Therefore, we design the curriculum from easy to hard by revealing more and more variables
290   throughout training. By doing so, the graph structure “grows” during training. We start by revealing
291   only the first two variables in the graph, meaning the transformer will only learn to predict the first
292   2 variables. After training loss reaches to a threshold, we then advance the curriculum by revealing
293   one extra variable. Note that after revealing one extra variable, the training data now contains the
294   observation of all revealed variables.
295   Inference Results. The test accuracy results are in Figure 2. Note that naive Bayes is able to model
296   the first few variables in the selected graphs well as shown in the first column of Figure 2. However,
297   as the order of the variable goes further, transformers outperforms naive Bayes on both sample
298   efficiency and test accuracy. This indicates transformers are able to utilize the graph structure to
299   generate prediction instead of treating all variables as independent observations. Notably, while our
300   transformers are only trained on samples with Ntrain = 100, they are able to generalized to different
301   values of Ntest , and their test accuracy approaches to Bayesian inference when Ntest increases. This
302   again verifies the capability of transformers to learn Bayesian inference and model graph structure
303
      well. Another thing to highlight is that both naive Bayes and Bayesian inference are not capable of
      handling unseen observations, leading to assigning 0 probability on every outcome under this case.
304
      However, transformers are able to utilize its learned prior from training data to perform prediction.
305
      This explains why transformers outperforms Bayesian inference sometimes when Ntest is small.
306
307   Convergence Results. We now discuss the convergence result of transformers training on gen-
308   eral graph and tree in Figure 3. We show the loss and accuracy curve on training and test dataset
309
      throughout the optimization process. We also want to observe the generalization performance on N
      of transformers. Specifically, we train models on N = 100, and evaluate them on both N = 100
310
      and N = 50 cases. We observe that the loss curve presents a decreasing trend, and the accuracy is
311
      able to reach near optimal (∼ 0.75)1 . Further, the generalization performance matches the results in
312   Figure 2, as we see transformers are capable of performing Bayesian inference under different Ntest .

521   7    C ONCLUSION
522
523   In this paper, we theoretically analyze transformer’s capability to learn Bayesian networks in-context
524   in an autoregressive fashion. We show that there exists a simple construction of transformer such that
525
      it can (1) estimate the conditional probabilities of the Bayesian network in-context, and (2) autore-
      gressively generate a new sample based on the estimated conditional probabilities. This sheds light
526
      on the potential of transformers in probabilistic reasoning and their applicability in various machine
527
      learning tasks involving structured data. Empirically, we provide extensive experiments to show that
528   transformers are indeed capable of learning Bayesian networks and generalize well on unseen prob-
529   ability distributions, verifying our theoretical construction. Our theoretical and experimental results
530   provide not only greater insights on the understanding of transformers, but also practical guidance
531   in training transformers on Bayesian networks.
532
      There are still multiple important aspects which this paper does not cover. First of all, our current
533
      theoretical result only demonstrates the expressive power of transformers in the sense that a good
534   transformer model with reasonable weights exist. Our result does not directly cover whether such a
535   transformer can indeed be obtained through training. Our experiments indicate a positive answer to
536   this question, making theoretical demonstrations a promising future work direction. Moreover, our
537   current analysis does not take the number of heads into consideration. As is discussed in Nichani
538   et al. (2024), multi-head attention may play an important role when learning Bayesian networks with
539   complicated network structures. Studying the impact of multi-head attention is another important
      future work direction.
