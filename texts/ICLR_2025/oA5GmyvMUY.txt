011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Federated Learning (FL) has become an established technique to facilitate privacy-
014            preserving collaborative training across a multitude of clients. The ability to
015
               achieve collaborative learning from multiple parties containing an extensive vol-
               ume of data while providing the essence of data privacy made it an attractive
016
               solution to address numerous challenges in sensitive data-driven fields such as au-
017
               tonomous vehicles (AVs). However, its decentralized nature exposes it to security
018            threats, such as evasion and data poisoning attacks, where malicious participants
019            can compromise training data. This paper addresses the challenge of defending
020            federated learning systems against data poisoning attacks specifically focusing on
021            data-flipping techniques in AVs by proposing a novel defense mechanism that
022            combines anomaly detection with robust aggregation techniques. Our approach
023            employs statistical outlier detection and model-based consistency checks to filter
024            out compromised updates before they affect the global model. Experiments on
025            benchmark datasets show that our method significantly enhances robustness by
026
               preventing nearly 15% of accuracy drop for our global model when confronted
               with a malicious participant and reduction the the attack success rate even when
027
               dealing with 20% of poisoning level. These findings provide a comprehensive
028
               solution to strengthen FL systems against adversarial threats.

033   Autonomous driving Rajasekhar & Jaswal (2015)Martı́nez-Dı́az & Soriguera (2018) is a rapidly de-
034   veloping field that has the potential to revolutionize human transportation. the usage of machine
035   learning in this field proved to by vary promising for it’s application in various application, such
036   as autonomous driving Zhang et al. (2016), complex environment navigation Nguyen et al. (2020) ,
037   lane following and switching Gurghian et al. (2016) and traffic calculation Yu et al. (2021). Recent
038   advances in this area rely heavily on machine learning, that requires extensive training data. the cen-
039
      tralized training provide more accuracy for autonomous driving solutions by using already known
      and controlled data. This approach neglect data privacy and third party involvement protocols. to
040
      confront those issues Federated learning provide multiple advantages.
041
042   Federated Learning (FL) Kairouz et al. (2021) McMahan et al. (2017) Chaabene et al. (2022) has
043   become increasingly popular in machine learning, enabling models to learn from distributed devices
044   in diverse contexts. In the FL framework, participating peers undertake the task of training a global
045
      model that they receive from the central server using their own local datasets. After processing their
      local data, these peers generate model updates, which they then send back to the server. The server’s
046
      role is to gather and aggregate the various updates it receives from all the peers, ultimately leading
047
      to the creation of an enhanced global model. Following this aggregation process, the updated global
048   model is redistributed to the peers, setting the stage for the next iteration of training. One of the
049   significant advantages of federated learning lies in its ability to enhance privacy. By keeping the
050   local data on the devices of the peers and not transferring it to a central server, FL significantly
051   mitigates the risks associated with data breaches and privacy violations. This is particularly crucial
052   in an era where data privacy concerns are paramount. Additionally, FL contributes to scalability
053   by distributing the computational workload across the devices of the peers, such as smartphones
      and other mobile devices, rather than relying solely on a central server’s computing resources. This

055   to participate in the training process, making FL a highly attractive option for developing machine
056   learning models in a secure and scalable manner Bonawitz et al. (2019).

      But such as any technique FL faces many challenges and limitations; it’s distributed nature exposes
      the global models to potential attacks from malicious participants. The lack of control of the ser-
059
      vice side Li et al. (2020), encourage malicious behaviour of the peers to tamper with the training
060
      guidelines and conduct adversarial attack. Data poisoning represent one of the biggest challenges in
061   the usage of FL, the attackers take advantages of the model distribution to inject misclassified data
062   aiming to make the model fail or not converge, they want to make the model incorrectly classify
063   test cases with particular traits into some desired labels. One sort of targeted poisoning attack is the
064   label-flipping (LF) attack Biggio et al. (2012), in which the attackers tamper with training data in
065   the local model by flipping the labels of select accurate instances from a transfer information from
066   a source class to a target class. Attackers train their local models after contaminating it using the
067   same hyper-parameters, loss function that that the server has supplied for the model architecture .
068   Thus, altering the training data is all that is needed to carry out the assault. That poisoned model
069
      is later on send to the server to aggregate with the other intact model, causing a drop of the overall
      performance and accuracy.
070
071   Multiple studies has been conducted to address those issues. Li et al. (2021) investigate peers
072   that have the same goals as attackers, which causes a large percentage of false positives when
073   sincere peers have comparable local data. Zhou et al. (2023) present RoHFL, a hierarchical fed-
074   erated learning framework for the Internet of Vehicles that uses similarity-based reputation scoring
075
      and logarithm-based normalization to thwart poisoning assaults. Nevertheless, combining these
      techniques entangle the aggregate procedure. The OQFL framework Yamany et al. (2023) uses
076
      quantum-behaved particle swarm optimization (QPSO) to modify hyperparameters in order to iden-
077
      tify hostile cars. However, because the model must be reinitialized and retrained from start, there is
078   a large computational overhead every time a search is conducted.
079
080   Our experiments, conducted on image classification datasets such as A2D2 Dataset, CIFAR-10 and
081
      a custom image collection used while training our AVs. We use Principal Component Analysis
      (PCA) to reduce the dimensionality of update vectors and effectively differentiate between mali-
082
      cious and legitimate updates. And we combined it with Multiclass Classification Using Support
083
      Vector Machines (SVM). Using PCA, the model can identify anomalies in the distribution of prin-
084   cipal components that may indicate inconsistencies in labeling. Once the data is transformed, SVM
085   can be used to classify samples based on the extracted features. SVM decision thresholds can reveal
086   patterns that indicate that some labels are inconsistent with the feature distribution. This may iden-
087   tify flipped labels.,Furthermore, the ensemble method increases the robustness,of the search process.
088   This is because merging multiple SVM classifiers can reveal inconsistencies arising from additional
089   labeling. It helps to be confident. that the model is resilient to adversarial attacks while maintaining
090   high classification performance... Our evaluations on the auxiliary datasets, demonstrate that our
091   defense strategy can effectively identify and block malicious participants.

138   real datasets from the UCI repository , including MNIST Deng (2012) and Spambase Hopkins &
139   Suermondt (1999). Further experiments Rosenfeld et al. (2020) manipulated the MNIST dataset
140   using LF attacks and found a slight increase in classification error after injecting ten poisoning
141   points. The study was repeated with Multiclass Logistic Regression, revealing an error increase
142   from 2% to 2.1% due to a random LF attack. The approach in Tolpegin et al. (2020) extended to
143   label-specific scenarios, where adversaries could adjust predictions based on predetermined rules.
144   Experiments on CIFAR-10 and a reduced version of ImageNet confirmed the effectiveness of the
145
      proposed method. In order to protect against poisoning assaults, a number of studies concentrate
      on evaluating certain updates. To differentiate between faulty and accurate updates, Jebreel et al.
146
      (2020)[ suggest examining the biases in the output layer. But only in the IID setting does it take
147
      model poisoning attacks into account. In order to prevent data poisoning attacks, FGold Fung et al.
148   (2020) and Awan et al. (2021) evaluate the weights of the output layer; But these techniques also
149   frequently penalize identical but good updates mistakenly, which causes the model’s performance to
150   significantly decline. Using a kernel density estimator, Li et al. (2023b) calculates how harmful each
151   local update is in relation to its k-nearest neighbors. After that, it uses an asymptotic threshold to
152   determine if updates are benign or poisoned. Not only is it difficult to choose a threshold of this kind,
153   but this approach has not been validated with big DL models or non-IID data. In order to identify
154   the LF attack, Qayyum et al. (2022) suggests a method for discovering the correlation between the
155   latent features of training data and updates. However, the strategy imposes an additional cost on all
156
      parties to train another model that learns such relationship. Furthermore, it is unrealistic to believe
      that throughout the early training rounds, all peers will behave appropriately.

208
209
      We use a label flipping attack to implement targeted data poisoning in FL. Given a source class
210   CSource and a target class Ctarget from C, each malicious participant Pi modifies their dataset Di as
211   follows: For all instances in Di whose class is CSource , change their class to Ctarget . We denote this
212   attack by CSource → Ctarget . For example, images with initial red light class labels may be altered to
213   have a green light class by malicious participants, according to the CIFAR-10 image classification
214   sign red light → green light. The attack tries to increase the possibility that, during testing, the final
215   global model would mistakenly classify traffic signals. The threat of label flipping is well-known
      in centralized machine learning. It’s also acceptable in the FL scenario given the hostile objective

425   5    C ONCLUSION
426
427   In this paper, we investigated data poisoning attacks targeting Federated Learning (FL) systems in
428   autonomous vehicles. Our study reveals the susceptibility of FL systems to label flipping poisoning
429   attacks, highlighting their significant adverse effects on the global model. We established a defence
430   mechanism biased on PCA and MCSVM do to their ability to separate outliers. We further investi-
431   gated the results of our mechanism which lead to the avoidance of the attack and the preservation of
      the global model integrity.
