009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The adoption of deep learning across various fields has been extensive, yet the
012            methods for reliably evaluating the performance of deep learning pipelines remain
013            underdeveloped. Typically, with the increased use of large datasets and complex
014            models, the training process is run only once and the new modeling result is
015            compared to previous benchmarks. This practice can lead to imprecise comparisons
016            due to the variance in deep learning pipelines, which stems from the inherent
017            randomness in the training process. Traditional solutions often require running the
018
               training process multiple times and are often infeasible in Deep Learning due to
               computational constraints. In this paper, we introduce a calibrated metric approach,
019
               designed to address this issue by reducing the variance present in its conventional
020
               counterpart. Consequently, this new metric improves the accuracy in detecting
021            effective modeling improvements in the model selection stage. The efficacy of the
022            new approach has been justified both theoretically and empirically.
023
024
025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   The progress in machine learning is largely influenced by experimental outcomes, particularly in
028   the era of deep learning. Researchers often evaluate the performance of new methods by comparing
029   them with previous benchmark results to demonstrate the superiority of new methods. However,
030   it is well known that the performance of deep learning models can vary greatly, even when using
031   the same pipeline (Picard, 2021; Pham et al., 2020; Reimers & Gurevych, 2017), where, in this
032
      work, we define the pipeline broadly, which includes but is not limited to the selection of feature
      sets, model architectures, optimization algorithms, initialization schemes, and hyperparameters.
033
      Two identical pipelines may produce substantially different validation metrics due to factors such
034
      as random initialization, data shuffle, and optimization noise. This variability makes it difficult to
035   accurately compare the modeling improvements over previous baselines. Even significant engineering
036   efforts may only lead to small measured gains within the noise margin.
037
038
      In fact, it has been shown that by selecting a lucky random initialization seed, one can achieve a
      model performance that is significantly better than average (Picard, 2021). This difference can be
039
      substantial enough to be used as a strong argument for publications in selective venues (Picard, 2021).
040
041   This issue is exacerbated in industry, where the production model performance is hard to improve,
042   while there are hundreds of machine learning engineers working on the same model at the same time.
043   The performance gain of a modeling proposal is usually small and within the metric variance, making
044
      it difficult to judge the effectiveness of the modeling proposal.
045   To address this issue, a common approach is to run the training pipeline multiple times and report
046   average, standard deviation, minimum, and maximum performance scores (Picard, 2021). Reimers
047   & Gurevych (2017) proposed to report the score distributions based on multiple executions instead
048   of single performance scores. However, with the rise of large training data and large models, these
049   approaches are not always practical due to limited computational resources (Bouthillier & Varoquaux,
050
      2020).
051   In this work, we present a new perspective that focuses on metric design. The key insight is that we
052   can reduce the variance of the evaluation metrics themselves to enable more accurate comparisons
053   between models. We propose a metric framework called Calibrated Metric that exhibits lower
      variance than its counterpart. Our method works by correcting for inherent biases before finalizing


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      the metric calculations on a holdout set. Our method does not require additional validation data and
055   is easy to compute.
056
057   We provide theoretical justifications for our metric in a linear regression setting. We demonstrate
058
      the effectiveness of our method empirically in binary classification tasks and regression tasks.
      Experiments on real-world data show that our Calibrated Metric reliably detects superior models
059
      compared to its counterpart. We further validate the benefits under different deep learning training
060
      configurations.
061
062   In summary, this paper makes the following contributions:
063
064
             • We formulate the deep learning pipeline evaluation problem and propose to tackle it by
               designing new metrics.
065
066          • We propose a new metric framework, Calibrated Metric, which can mitigate the above deep
067            learning pipeline evaluation issue.
068          • We conduct extensive experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed metric,
069            using both synthetic datasets and real-world datasets.
070
             • We provide theoretical guarantees in a linear regression setting that the proposed metric has
071            a smaller variance than its vanilla counterpart.

074   2    P RELIMINARIES AND P ROBLEM S ETTING
075
076   In this section, we examine the supervised learning setting, where we assume that the training data,
077   validation data, and test data are randomly drawn from an unknown distribution in an i.i.d. manner,
078
      denoted as D. Our work can naturally be generalized to the concept drift1 setting, which we will
      discuss in detail in Section 5.5.
079
080   Our goal is to develop a good pipeline that maps from a training distribution to a possibly random
081   model h ∈ H, which generalizes well during the test time. As we mentioned in the Introduction
082   1, the pipeline incorporates the whole procedure for training a model, including the selection of
083   model architectures, optimization algorithms, initialization schemes, and hyperparameters. Model
084
      performance is evaluated by a metric, e, and thus the expected performance of a model h is
085                                          Re (h) = ED [e(h(X), Y )|h].                                          (1)
086
087
      In practice, Re (h) is estimated by the finite-sample average on the test data set D̂test . That is,
088
089                                                         1           X
                                    R̂e (h, D̂test ) =                              e(h(x), y).                    (2)
090                                                      |D̂test |

093   It should be noted that the expected risk, Re (h), is a random variable, since h is random and depends
094   on a specific model that is produced by the underlying deep learning pipeline. The output model is
095
      random due to the randomness of the data from the sample collection and intrinsic randomness during
      the training process in the deep learning pipeline, such as the order of the data and the randomness of
096
      the descent of the stochastic gradient. Therefore, a proper evaluation and comparison of different deep
097
      learning pipelines should take into account the distribution of Re (h) (Bouthillier et al., 2021; Reimers
098   & Gurevych, 2017). It is also important to note that the term "deep learning pipeline" in this context
099   is general, as we consider different model configurations (e.g. different model hyperparameters) as
100   different "deep learning pipelines", even though they may belong to the same model class.
101
      To compare the performance of different deep learning pipelines, we should calculate the expected
102
      risk Re (h) for each pipeline. As mentioned above, such expected risk is a random variable w.r.t.
103
      h, then we should compare the distribution of Re (h). Specifically, we use the probability that the
104   expected risk Re (h) for one pipeline is larger or smaller than that of the other to quantify the pairwise
105   performance comparison between different pipelines.

      Definition 2.1. (Better pipeline) For any two pipelines A and B, we say that pipeline A is better than
109   pipeline B with respect to metric e if and only if the probability that pipeline A produces a better
110   model (i.e., smaller risk), measured by the metric e, is greater than 0.5. This is represented by the
111   inequality:
112                                       P (Re (hA ) < Re (hB )) > 0.5                                   (3)
113   where hA and hB are random variables representing the output models produced by pipeline A and
114   B respectively.
115
116   Our objective is to compare the performance of two pipelines, A and B, with respect to the metric e
117   by running the training pipeline only once. Ideally, the Monte Carlo method could be used to estimate
118   P (Re (hA ) < Re (hB )), but this approach demands substantial computational resources, making it
119   impractical in Deep Learning. In this work, we aim to come up with an alternative metric e1 with the
120
      following properties:
121
            1. Roughly same mean
122                                               E [Re1 (h)] ≈ E [Re (h)] ;
123
124         2. Strictly small variance
125
                                               var (Re1 (h)) < var (Re (h)) ,
126
      where the randomness is from the pipeline that produces h. As a result, the new metric can compare
127   the performance of pipelines A and B more accurately using the same computational resources.
128
      Definition 2.2. (Better alternative metric) Assuming that pipeline A is better than B with respect to
129
      the metric e (i.e. pipeline A is more likely to produce a better model than pipeline B in the ground
130
      truth if measured by metric e), we say that a metric e1 is better than e2 with respect to metric e if and
131   only if the probability that pipeline A produces a better model than pipeline B measured by metric e1
132   is greater than the probability measured by metric e2 . This is represented by the inequality:
133
134                           P (Re1 (hA ) < Re1 (hB )) > P (Re2 (hA ) < Re2 (hB ))                         (4)
135
136   In other words, using metric e1 is more likely to accurately detect that pipeline A is better than
137
      pipeline B, which aligns with the ground truth. Here, we allow for a general form of the risk
      function, which may not admit the expectation form; i.e., Re1 (h) may not necessarily have the form
138
      ED [e1 (h(X), Y )].
139
140   Definition 2.3. (Metric accuracy) We assume without loss of generality that pipeline A is better than
141
      B with respect to the metric e. We define the accuracy of a metric ē with respect to metric e and
      pipeline A and B as:
142
                                      Acc(ē) ≜ P (Rē (hA ) < Rē (hB ))                                (5)
143
144   Our goal is to find a metric ē that has a higher accuracy than the original metric e for a wide range of
145   pipelines A and B. In the next section, we will present a new metric framework, namely Calibrated
146   Metric. The intuition is that the bias in the function h is always volatile and carries on a great deal
147   of randomness. Calibrating the bias will usually not change the comparison between two pipelines
148   but can reduce the randomness. In Section 4, we will present a theoretical analysis that justifies this
149   intuition by showing that our new metric framework has a smaller variance in the linear regression
150   setting. Through extensive experiments in Section 5, we will show that Calibrated Metric achieves
151   higher accuracy than its counterpart for a wide range of tasks and deep learning pipelines.

219   its vanilla counterpart under Linear Regression setting, where the randomness only comes from the

221   its simplicity. We empirically verify our method’s performance under Linear Regression, Logistic
222   Regression, and Neural Networks in the next section. Note that in Linear Regression, the Quadratic
223   Loss Metric is used.

481
      Model selection in supervised learning is crucial to ensure robust generalization to unseen data. A
482
      common approach is the hold-out method, which splits the data into three parts: training, validation,
483
           3
484            https://www.kaggle.com/c/criteo-display-ad-challenge
           4
485            https://github.com/microsoft/qlib/blob/main/qlib/contrib/data/handler.
      py

486
      and test sets. The training set is used to learn the model, the validation set is employed for hyperpa-
487   rameter tuning, and the test set is reserved for final evaluation. Although straightforward, the hold-out
488   method requires a large dataset to ensure that sufficient data are available for each subset.
489
490
      In scenarios with limited data, k-fold cross-validation (Kohavi, 1995) is often preferred. This method
      splits the data into k folds, trains the model in k − 1 folds, and validates it on the remaining fold,
491
      repeating this process k times, and averaging the results. This ensures that each data point is used for
492
      both training and validation, providing a more comprehensive assessment of model performance. A
493   special case of this method is leave-one-out cross-validation, where k equals the number of training
494   examples. However, k-fold cross-validation typically demands multiple model trainings, which can
495   be impractical in deep learning due to the high computational cost.
496
      More recently, Immer et al. (2021) proposed a scalable marginal-likelihood estimation method to
497
      select both hyperparameters and network architectures based on the training data alone. Their method
498
      is useful when validation data are unavailable. You et al. (2019) proposed Deep Embedded Validation
499   method in Deep Unsupervised Domain Adaptation setting.
500

      6.2   E VALUATING THE PERFORMANCE OF CTR P REDICTION M ODELS
502
503   Evaluating the performance of CTR prediction models is crucial, with several metrics commonly
504   used for this purpose (Yi et al., 2013). The Area Under the ROC Curve (AUC) (Fawcett, 2006; 2004)
505   and its variants (Zhu et al., 2017), along with Log Loss Metric, are among the most prevalent metrics
506   in this domain. For example, (He et al., 2014; Wang et al., 2017; McMahan et al., 2013) use Log Loss
507   Metric as their core metric, while (Zhou et al., 2018; McMahan et al., 2013) use AUC as their core
508   metric. However, AUC has been criticized for not taking into account the predictive probability (Yi
509
      et al., 2013). Log Loss Metric, in particular, is favored in scenarios that require calibrated predictions
      due to its consideration of predictive probabilities, an aspect crucial for applications such as Ads
510
      CTR predictions (He et al., 2014).
511
512
      6.3   E VALUATING THE PERFORMANCE OF S TOCK R ETURN P REDICTION M ODELS
513
514   In Hu et al. (2021), MSE (or equivalently RMSE) is identified as the most commonly used perfor-
515   mance metric. Besides MSE, metrics such as MAPE, MAE, Accuracy, Sharpe Ratio, and Return
516   Rate are also frequently used to evaluate model performance.

      7     C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
520
      Conclusion In this paper, we have presented a new approach to comparing the performance of
521
      different deep learning pipelines. We proposed a new metric framework, Calibrated Metric, which has
522   higher accuracy and smaller variance than its vanilla counterpart for a wide range of tasks, models,
523   and training pipelines. Our experiments in section 5 demonstrated the superiority of the Calibrated
524   Metric, and we believe that this new metric can be used to compare the performance of different
525   pipelines more effectively and efficiently. Future work includes expanding this idea to evaluate
526   Natural Language Processing (NLP) and Computer Vision (CV) pipelines and establish theoretical
527   guarantees in more general settings.
528   Limitations Our method sacrifices accuracy when comparing some specific pipelines. For example,
529   if pipeline B can reliably improve the model calibration over pipeline A, Calibrated Metric will not be
530   able to correctly detect the benefits of pipeline B. However, for most pipeline comparisons conducted
531   in industry and academia such as feature engineering, tuning parameters, etc., the Calibrated Metric
532   has a boost in accuracy over its counterpart metric, as we demonstrated in Section 5.
533
      Potential Applications Our method may have applications in the AutoML domain. AutoML
534   (Automated Machine Learning) systems are designed to automate the process of selecting, designing,
535   and tuning machine learning models, and a key component of these systems is the selection of the
536   best-performing pipeline (e.g. hyperparameters, model architectures, etc.). The new metric could
537   be used as a more accurate way to compare performance and select the best. The new metric is, in
538   particular, useful when performing hyperparameter tuning.
