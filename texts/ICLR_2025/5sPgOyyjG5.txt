009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The Feynman-Kac Operator Expectation Estimator (FKEE) is an innovative
012            method for estimating the target Mathematical Expectation EX∼P [f (X)] with-
013            out relying on a large number of samples, in contrast to the commonly used
014            Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) Expectation Estimator. FKEE comprises
015            diffusion bridge models and approximation of the Feynman-Kac operator. The
016            key idea is to use the solution to the Feynmann-Kac equation at the initial time
017            u(x0 , 0) = E[f (XT )|X0 = x0 ]. We use Physically Informed Neural Networks
018
               (PINN) to approximate the Feynman-Kac operator, which enables the incorpo-
               ration of diffusion bridge models into the expectation estimator and significantly
019
               improves the efficiency of using data while substantially reducing the variance.
020
               Diffusion Bridge Model is a more general MCMC method. In order to incorpo-
021            rate extensive MCMC algorithms, we propose a new diffusion bridge model based
022            on the Minimum Wasserstein distance. This diffusion bridge model is universal
023            and reduces the training time of the PINN. FKEE also reduces the adverse impact
024            of the curse of dimensionality and weakens the assumptions on the distribution
025            of X and performance function f in the general MCMC expectation estimator.
026            The theoretical properties of this universal diffusion bridge model are also shown.
027            Finally, we demonstrate the advantages and potential applications of this method
028            through various concrete experiments, including the challenging task of approxi-
029
               mating the partition function in the random graph model such as the Ising model.

      1     I NTRODUCTION
033
      1.1   BACKGROUND
034
035   Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) is a prevalent computational method used in fields such as
036   statistics, machine learning, and computational science. It is primarily applied for sampling from
037   complex distributions, Bayesian inference, and optimization (Hesterberg (2002); Ahmed (2008)).
038   MCMC algorithms are typically divided into two categories: those that sample from the target
039   distribution and those that estimate the statistical characteristics of the target distribution, such as
040   the expectation. While alternative methods to traditional MCMC samplers, like generative models
041   (Song & Ermon (2019)) and diffusion models (Ho et al. (2019)), have been explored, MCMC re-
042
      mains the standard for expectation estimation. However, traditional MCMC estimators, based on
      the law of large numbers (LLN) and the ergodic theorem of Markov chains (ETMC), face limita-
043
      tions in data efficiency and impose complex constraints on the distribution P and performance
044
      function f . Thus, developing algorithms that overcome these limitations by integrating modern
045   sampling methods with deep learning is of great importance.
046
047
      1.2   M OTIVATION
048
049   Advantages of MCMC algorithm: MCMC algorithms are effective for sampling from target dis-
050   tributions and are accompanied by two types of expectation estimators. The first type, based on
051   LLN, uses averages from multiple samples at the terminal time of the Markov chain. The second
052   type, based on ETMC, averages values along the path of the Markov chain. These methods utilize
053   statistical principles effectively, particularly for high-dimensional distributions, mitigating the curse
      of dimensionality in integral approximations.

      Disadvantages of MCMC algorithm: Despite their advantages, MCMC algorithms are not optimal
055   for expectation estimation. The efficiency of MCMC estimators depends on the distribution P and
056   the function f . Different MCMC algorithms are required for different P , and due to burn-in peri-
057   ods, MCMC algorithms often waste many points. Additionally, the sample size     √N shoule be large
058   enough to achieve accurate estimates, leading to variances on the order of O( N ). Quasi-Monte
                                                          1
059   Carlo methods offer variances on the order of O(N 2 +δ ) with δ ≤ 12 (Caflisch (1998)), but this di-
060   minishes efficiency and introduces bias. Error probabilities can be estimated through concentration
061   inequalities (Lugosi (2003)), but these depend on the Lipschitzian norm of f .
062
      Estimating mathematical expectations is crucial in both machine learning and statistics. A unified
063
      expectation estimator is theoretically and practically significant. Therefore, we focus on two essen-
064
      tial questions:
065
066   (A) Is it possible to unify most existing MCMC algorithms into a cohesive framework to create a
067   universal sampler for expectation estimation?
068   (B) How can we develop a universal expectation estimator that leverages samples from univer-
069   sal samplers for accurate expectation estimates without relying on post-processing or specialized
070   methods?
071
      For the first question: We propose the following solutions. we know the Markov model is deter-
072   mined by the transfer density of one step. The transition density function associated with the discrete
073   Markov chain generated by the MCMC algorithm can be interpreted as the transition density func-
074   tion of a specific stochastic differential equation (SDE) of Markov properties. In this study, we refer
075   to this SDE as the diffusion bridge model. This encompasses a broad class of SDEs that share
076   identical transition densities with the Markov chains in the MCMC algorithm. The distribution of
077   the terminals in such SDEs aligns with a predefined target distribution, which can take the form of
078   discrete points or a probability density function. Moreover, the starting point of this SDE can be
079   either arbitrary or fixed.
080   For the second question: In the context of the
081   diffusion bridge model, we can view the expec-
082   tation estimation problem as a decoding problem.
083   It is easy to observe that MCMC is not the opti-
084   mal decoding method because a substantial num-

      gorithm. However, these samples harbor valuable

      2.3   F EYNMAN -K AC MODEL
317
318   This section presents our main contribution: a novel approach to expectation estimation. We aim
319   to estimate EX∼P [f (X)] by decoding P . All relevant information about P is encapsulated in
320   (X0 , b, σ, T ). The decoding loss measures the accuracy of our estimate, while the decoding speed
321   impacts the algorithm’s efficiency. Our key innovation is the direct utilization of information within
322   (X0 , b, σ, T ), as it contains all the necessary information about P . This is the core of our algorithm.
323   The decoding process can be viewed as an approximation of the Feynman-Kac operator, formally
      obtained by solving the Feynman-Kac equation.

      The Feynman-Kac operator (Del Moral & Del Moral (2004)) is crucial in translating between de-
325   terministic PDEs and stochastic processes through the Feynman-Kac formula (Feynman-Kac equa-
326   tion). The Feynman-Kac equation (Pham (2014)) is a powerful method for solving PDEs by linking
327   them to stochastic processes. The basic idea is to represent the solution of a PDE as the expectation
328   of a function of a stochastic process and use Monte Carlo methods to approximate this expectation.
329
      In our approach, we can reverse the process to obtain new methods for deriving MCMC results.
330
      Specifically, we can use the solution of a PDE to accurately express the corresponding MCMC
331   results. We consider the following simplified version of the Feynman-Kac formula, which is com-

398   expectations are calculated by utilizing the full distributional information in P to approximate
399   (X0∗ , b∗ , σ ∗ ). However, when approximating (X0∗ , b∗ , σ ∗ ) in many diffusion bridge models, it is
400   often necessary to simulate part of the Brownian motion trajectory to estimate the loss function.
401   This results in some positions (x, t) corresponding to (b, σ) being accurate, while others depend on
402   the network’s generalization ability. Consequently, the appearance of x in our position (x, t) occurs

718   7     R ELATED WORK
719
720   The diffusion model belongs to a class of stochastic differential equations, which are used to ap-
721   proximate the target distribution. It has been widely used for generative models (Dhariwal & Nichol
722   (2021)), variational inference (Geffner & Domke (2021); Kingma et al. (2021)), etc. The diffusion
723   bridge model is a variant of the diffusion model. Early development of diffusion bridge models
724   involved simulating processes originating from two endpoints (Beskos et al. (2008)). Alternative
725
      approaches for constructing diffusion bridge models are outlined by (Liu et al. (2022); Bladt &
      Sørensen (2010)). Since the diffusion bridge model essentially functions as a sampling algorithm, it
726
      plays a pivotal role in addressing the crucial task of high-dimensional distribution sampling. Sam-
727
      pling high-dimensional distributions is a fundamental task with applications across various fields.
728   Common methods include MCMC, random flow, and generative models. Recent work includes
729   stream-based methods (Müller et al. (2018); Yang et al. (2017); Matsubara et al. (2020); Strathmann
730   et al. (2015); Tran et al. (2019)), MCMC-based methods (Deng et al. (2020); Chen et al. (2014);
731   Jacob et al. (2017)) and generative models (Nichol & Dhariwal (2021)), score-based models (Song
732   et al. (2020); Song & Ermon (2019)). Normalizing Flows (Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden (2022)).
733   These models can be broadly categorized into two groups: those based on given discrete points and
734   those relying on a given density function. The former primarily serves for learning and generating
735   real world data such as text and images, while the latter is used for sampling, statistical estimation,
736
      and similar purposes. Notably, Langevin diffusion (Cheng et al. (2018); Xifara et al. (2013); Garcı́a-
      Portugués et al. (2017)) is a classical model within the latter category. (Zhang (2024)) presents an
737
      explicit construction of the drift coefficient for two scenarios: when P has a closed-form expression
738
      and when it does not.
739
740   The Feynman-Kac model is a technique employed to solve partial differential equations (PDEs)
741   by using deep learning. Deep learning has found application in solving PDEs of the Feynman-
742
      Kac equation type, as demonstrated by (Berner et al. (2020); Blechschmidt & Ernst (2021). Liang
      & Borovkov (2023)) highlights the approximation of Feynman-Kac type expectations through the
743
      approximation of discrete Markov chains, thereby enhancing the order of convergence. When em-
744
      ploying PINN to solve Feynman-Kac type PDEs, the sampling algorithm can be linked to the path of
745   the SDE. This approach enables the acquisition of adaptive sampling points from the paths of SDE,
746   which proves more efficient than uniform point selection (Chen et al. (2023)). Further analysis of
747   the approximation error for this class of equations is presented in (De Ryck & Mishra (2022)).

1041   approximate stochastic process Hβ (X), which is a one-dimensional problem. We generate the chain
1042   using the same method as in (Haddadan et al. (2021)) and compute the value Hβ (Xt ) under each
1043   moment. The diffusion bridge model and the Feynman-Kac model are then used to estimate the
1044   expectation separately. In the diffusion bridge model, we generated the same number of Brownian
1045   motions at the same number of moments and then calculated the loss at each moment to train. The
1046   Feynman-Kac model uses the already established diffusion bridge model to get an estimate of the
1047   expectation by solving the PDE.
1048   The second approach better exemplifies the substantial improvement in harnessing Markov chains
1049   facilitated by our method. It highlights the remarkable flexibility embedded in our approach. Specif-
1050   ically, we directly approximate the distribution on a random graph, conceptualizing this graph as an
1051   n2 random variable (X1 , X2 , ..., Xn2 ), with each variable assuming two discrete values. A Markov
1052   chain is executed to obtain a sizable sample of random variables, and we subsequently approximate
1053   this n2 dimensional distribution using a diffusion bridge model. However, since we are using a
1054   continuous model via SDE to obtain YT , which cannot accurately approximate a discrete random
1055
       variable with values of {0, 1}, we employ the sigmoid function in the output YT . The loss for XT
       is then computed. Finally, when using the diffusion model, we apply post-processing to obtain the

1308
1309   The independence of samples: Obtaining accurate expectations with entirely unknown sample in-
1310   dependence remains a significant challenge in the real world, particularly in stochastic optimization
1311
       algorithms or loss functions, where independent sampling is frequently required for estimation. The
       independence of samples plays a crucial role in machine learning, and its violation can significantly
1312
       impact the performance and validity of machine learning models. Many machine learning models
1313
       rely on the assumption of independent and identically distributed (i.i.d) samples. Non-independent
1314   samples can introduce dependencies that the model may mistakenly learn as patterns. Nonlinear
1315   mathematical expectations play a critical role in such non-iid scenarios (Peng (2010)). However,
1316   methods like using Max-Mean Monte Carlo for calculating nonlinear mathematical expectations are
1317   often challenging. This is because we need to partition the dataset into parts where the samples
1318   are independent and then calculate the linear mathematical expectation for each part. Finally, we
1319   take the largest to get the nonlinear mathematical expectation. Our approach provides a completely
1320   new way to consider the use of Stochastic Differential Equations (SDEs) with G-Brownian motions.
1321   The diffusion bridge model is constructed using the same method and then solved directly using the
1322
       Feynman-Kac model in the case of nonlinear mathematical expectations. This avoids problems such
       as data grouping.
1323
