040                                                           A BSTRACT
041
042            Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) demonstrate robust zero-shot
043            capabilities across diverse vision-language tasks after training on mega-scale
044            datasets. However, dense prediction tasks, such as semantic segmentation and
045
               keypoint detection, pose significant challenges for MLLMs when represented
               solely as text outputs. These challenges often necessitate task-specific visual de-
046
               coders, leading to the underutilization of MLLMs’ multi-task potential. In this
047
               work, we propose VT-PLUG, a novel framework that leverages modular visual
048            components as scalable plugins for a variety of visual applications. During the
049            joint training of vision-language tasks with varying prediction densities, we pro-
050            pose a Visual Decoding Chain-of-Thought (VD-CoT) mechanism to prevent
051            task conflicts. VD-CoT requires the model to predict the current task’s recogni-
052            tion entities, decoding unit type, and other specific details, while also providing
053            learnable queries for precise decoding. Additionally, we construct Visual-Task
               Instruction Following Dataset (VT-Instruct), a large-scale multi-task dataset

054
                   containing over 100 million multimodal dialogue samples across 25 task types.
055                Beyond text inputs and outputs, VT-Instruct incorporates various visual prompts
056                such as point, box, scribble, and mask, and generates outputs composed of text
057                and visual units like box, keypoint, depth and mask. The combination of different
058                visual prompts and visual units generates a wide variety of task types, expanding
059                the applicability of VT-PLUG significantly. The source code, dataset and demo
060                will be released at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/VT-PLUG.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
064
      Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) demonstrate excellent performance in tasks such
065
      as visual question answering and scene understanding (Liu et al., 2024; Alayrac et al., 2022; Tai
066   et al., 2024). Despite these achievements, typical MLLMs primarily understand input and generate
067   responses with text, which limits their ability to perform fine-grained visual localization. As a
068   result, they struggle to make significant contributions in real-world applications such as autonomous
069   driving, robotics, and medical diagnosis.
070
      In this work, we introduce VT-PLUG, a novel framework that utilizes modular visual components
071
      as scalable plugins for visual tasks with varying degrees of prediction density. To support common
072   visual tasks, we designed Visual Prompt (VPT) encoding plugins alongside point, box, mask, and
073   keypoint decoding plugins. These various visual plugins can be combined to tackle complex mul-
074   timodal tasks or easily extend to new visual applications. For instance, the VPT encoding plugin
075   can pair with different decoding plugins to generate visual decoding units for diverse user interac-
076   tion modes. A combination of box and keypoint plugins also enables efficient multi-person pose
077   estimation. As shown in Table 1, compared to existing MLLMs which focus on fine-grained vi-
078   sual localization and understanding, VT-PLUG offers greater flexibility and can accomplish a wider
079   variety of visual tasks.
080   VT-PLUG utilizes tokens generated by MLLMs as learnable queries for decoding. Therefore, we
081   propose the Visual Decoding Chain-of-Thought (VD-CoT), which requires the model to output
082   special tokens for VT-PLUG decoding, as well as other task-related information during the genera-
083   tion process. VD-CoT decomposes the response generation process into two stages: Visual CoT and
084   Decoding Triplets. During the Visual CoT stage, the model extracts the categories and quantities
085   of visual entities to be decoded based on the image and prompt information, and determines the
086
      corresponding decoding types. In the Decoding Triplets stage, visual-related decoding information
      is divided into three types: Phrase, Unit, and <REF>, forming multiple triplets. Phrase rep-
087
      resents the category of visual entities, Unit indicates the decoding type, and <REF> corresponds
088
      to the learnable queries for VT-PLUG. Since the <REF> token appears at the end, it effectively
089   leverages relevant information from the previously generated content.

116   The contributions of this work can be summarized as follows:
117
118          • VT-PLUG: We propose a novel visual multi-task training framework that includes four
119            meta-plugins designed to handle diverse visual content. These plugins can be combined to
120            support various composite tasks and serve as the foundation for creating new visual plugins.
121          • VD-CoT: We propose a visual information generation method, Visual Decoding Chain-
122            of-Thought (VD-CoT), for unified instruction tuning. VD-CoT provides VT-PLUG with
123            learnable queries for visual unit decoding, along with essential auxiliary information, such
124            as visual content descriptions and decoding unit types.
125          • VT-Instruct: We present a large-scale multi-task dataset containing 100 million multi-
126            modal dialogue samples across 25 task types, which supports a comprehensive understand-
127            ing and decoding of visual units across various degrees of prediction density.

129            across multiple tasks. Specifically, VT-PLUG surpasses Osprey (Yuan et al., 2024) by
130            2.5 in CIDEr for Referring Expression Generation (REG), outperforms GLaMM (Rasheed
131            et al., 2024) by 8.2% in Recall for the Grounded Conversation Generation (GCG), and ex-

134   2   R ELATED W ORKS
135
136   Numerous studies have attempted to enhance the robust scene understanding capabilities of MLLMs,
137   guiding models to achieve precise localization of identified objects. Pix2Seq (Chen et al., 2021)
138   leverages the model’s autoregressive generation capability to express bounding boxes and class la-
139   bels as sequences of discrete tokens. Shikra (Chen et al., 2023b) constructs an appropriate visual
140   supervision fine-tuning dataset, where the model needs to perform inductive analysis in the form of
141   Chain-of-Thought (CoT) before answering complex questions, and subsequently outputs bounding
142   boxes in text form to complete the visual grounding task.
143   LLaVa-Plus (Liu et al., 2023c) constructs an instruction-following dataset that includes a large num-
144   ber of samples for using task-specific models as tools. The model, after supervised fine-tuning, can
145   leverage various task-specific models to accomplish tasks such as visual grounding and referring seg-
146   mentation. LISA (Lai et al., 2024) adopts SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023) as the mask decoder, where
147   MLLM generates learnable special tokens as prompts for SAM, producing fine-grained segmenta-
148   tion results. PSALM (Zhang et al., 2024b) divides the input for open-vocabulary segmentation tasks
149
      into instruction prompts, condition prompts, and discrete mask tokens, decoding the output mask
      tokens to obtain segmentation results aligned with the prompt content.
150
151   Unlike the existing research on visual fine-grained localization, our VT-PLUG is designed with four
152   distinct meta-plugins that eliminate the need for additional task-specific models, ensuring overall
153   consistency and accuracy.

      3.1   VD-C OT
204
205
206   The decoding process of visual units requires essential information, including the description of
207   visual entities, the type of unit, and the current decoding token. We add special tokens to the vo-
208   cabulary of the MLLM to encode or mark the aforementioned content. The description of the visual
209   object is denoted with Phrase, the decoding type is marked using Unit, and the token that requires
210   further decoding is denoted with <REF>.
211   VD-CoT divides the answer generation process into two steps: visual CoT and decoding triplet. As
212   shown in Figure 2, in visual CoT step, the model considers the visual entities to be decoded, the
213   number of instances, and the type of decoding required for the current task. As for decoding triplet,
214   it is generated simultaneously with the answer. For each <REF> token used for decoding, the model
215   produces a Phrase-Unit-<REF> triplet. The answer content shown in the example omits the
      extra special tokens for better visualization.

      4.1      P RELIMINARY
298
299   MLLMs often lack the capability to output visual units such as boxes, keypoints, and masks. To
300   expand their applicability in real-world visual tasks, it is typically necessary to implement targeted
301   designs for different visual tasks. Common decoding approaches for visual units can be categorized
302   into three main types.
303
304   Decode Visual Units as Sequence. The most straightforward solution leverages the text gener-
305   ation capabilities of MLLM to produce visual localization results in textual format (Chen et al.,
306   2023b; 2021). This approach does not require structural modifications to the MLLM. It simply
307   necessitates the preparation of suitable supervised fine-tuning data to effectively generate the local-
308   ization coordinates of visual targets. However, due to the constraints of textual output, their models
309
      struggle with dense prediction tasks such as keypoint detection, segmentation, and depth estimation.
310
311   Decode Visual Units with Agent Tools. Another approach involves using the MLLM as an agent
312
      to coordinate task-specific models, enabling accurate localization of visual targets (Liu et al., 2023c).
      In this case, MLLM outputs textual descriptions of recognized content and scheduling results, which
313
      can be utilized by downstream visual tools. However, since the final visual units is derived from the
314
      tool models, there may be a gap between the MLLM’s understanding and the final output.
315
316
      Decode Visual Units with Learnable Queries. Using the tokens output by the MLLM as learn-
317
      able queries input into task-specific decoders is the most widely adopted visual decoding strategy
318   (Rasheed et al., 2024; Lai et al., 2024). Directly decoding MLLM output tokens enables an end-to-
319   end training process, allowing the visual decoder to share the visual fetures with the MLLM, thereby
320   maintaining consistency at the feature level and achieving high accuracy and coherence. However,
321   different types of decoding units (such as boxes, masks, and keypoints) require distinct visual infor-
322   mation, resulting in significant variations in the data formats needed during supervised fine-tuning.
323   Most research typically focuses on designing for a single decoding unit, making it challenging to
      integrate various decoding tasks into a unified instruction tuning framework.

      4.2   VT-PLUG F RAMEWORK
325
326   Our VT-PLUG implements end-to-end unified training for multiple visual tasks, containing four
327   meta-plugins to support the combination of various visual prompts and decode units. As shown in
328   Figure 6, the VT-PLUG framework consists of four main components:
329
330         1. Fundamental MLLM: An Llava-like (Liu et al., 2023b) MLLM, with CLIP-ViT (Rad-
331            ford et al., 2021) as the visual encoder and Vicuna-7B (Zheng et al., 2023) as the LLM
332            component.
333         2. Fine-Grained Visual Encoder: CLIP-ViT (Radford et al., 2021) focuses on encoding
334            global image features, whereas visual units decoding tasks typically rely more heavily on
335            local image features. To address this, we use CLIP-ConvNeXt (Cherti et al., 2023) for
336            secondary image encoding, functioning as a visual feature pyramid encoder. In practice,
337            we exclude the final layer of features encoded by CLIP-ConvNeXt and concatenate the re-
338            maining features with those from CLIP-ViT. By utilizing these two distinct visual encoders,
339
               we achieve an effective fusion of global and local image features representating.
340         3. Visual Prompts Encoder Plugin: We define user inputs such as points, boxes, scribbles
341            and masks as Visual Prompts (VPT) and propose a novel VPT-Encoder plugin to encode
342            these elements.
343         4. Visual Units Decoder Plugins: We propose three different decoding plugins to handle the
344            decoding of boxes, keypoints, and masks.
345
346   As described in Section 3.1, when handling visual tasks, VT-PLUG performs a VD-CoT process
347   based on the input prompts, analyzing the decoding content, decode unit, and decode target to form
348   a Phrase-Unit-<REF> triplet. All <REF> tokens are extracted from the triplets, arranged in
349   sequence, and used as learnable queries, which are then decoded by the visual decode plugins.
350   Figure 5 illustrates the architecture of our proposed visual plugins. The Visual Prompt Encoder
351   plugin performs mask embedding for input regions. Unlike similar works that perform mask pooling
352   on regions, our approach additionally incorporates position embedding to enhance the positional
353   information of visual prompt features. The Visual Unit Decoding plugins adhere to the DETR
354   framework (Carion et al., 2020). Since MLLMs can directly output classification results in the form
355   of phrases in triplets, we eliminate the class predictor. Specifically, the box decoder is implemented
356   as in DETR, while the mask decoder is implemented as in MaskFormer (Cheng et al., 2021). For
357   the keypoint decoder, we develop a query expansion module after the box queries decoder. This
358
      module concatenates each query with a learnable vector initialized to zero and feeds the resulting
      representation into the subsequent queries decoder to predict the coordinates of the keypoints.
359
360   For the multi-target decoding process (i.e., a single Phrase corresponding to multiple <REF>
361   instances), we employ the Group Hungarian Matcher (Carion et al., 2020). In the VT-PLUG setting,
362   REF tokens are annotated in one-to-one correspondence with units, and we can achieve perfect
363   matching by executing the Hungarian algorithm within each phrase group.

532   6      L IMITATIONS AND C ONCLUSION
533
534   In conclusion, this paper introduces a powerful and flexible visual multi-task learning framework,
535   alongside the construction of a large-scale vision-language multimodal instruction-tuning dataset.
536   This work effectively expands the applicability of MLLMs in real-world scenarios, and extensive
537   experiments validate its effectiveness. However, the focus of this work is limited to the problem of
538   visual units decoding, and it cannot yet effectively handle widely-used tasks such as image editing
539   and video understanding. Consequently, this work should be regarded as a foundational baseline for
      visual units decoding.

      Definition of Each Downstream Task The Visual Understanding task includes Image Captioning
726   and Visual Question Answering (VQA), involving image-text inputs and text-only outputs. Re-
727   ferring Expression tasks cover Referring Expression Comprehension (REC), Referring Expression
728   Segmentation (RES), and Referring Expression Generation (REG). While REC and RES require
729   models to predict bounding boxes or masks in response to a query about a specific region in an
730   image, REG involves generating descriptive text from visual inputs like points, boxes, scribbles, or
731   masks. Interactive Grounding (IG) enables users to provide prompts via both text and interactive in-
732   puts (e.g., points, boxes, masks), allowing MLLMs to interpret and generate corresponding outputs.
733   Open-Vocabulary Identification focuses on localizing and segmenting objects from descriptive text,
734
      even if the object categories were not part of the training data. Grounded Conversation Generation
      (GCG) produces natural language responses interwoven with bounding boxes or masks, with the
735
      GCG task further divided into GCG-box (bounding box outputs) and GCG-mask (mask outputs).
736
737
      Dataset Construction Details For each task, we select a unique prompt-unit pair to develop task-
738
      specific instructions. For example, visual understanding task encompasses Image Captioning and
739   Visual Question Answering (VQA), with image-text inputs and pure text outputs. To facilitate
740   MLLMs in comprehending image-level information and addressing diverse questions, we construct
741   conversations for visual understanding tasks using our proposed pipeline with the COCO (Lin et al.,
742   2014), GranD (Rasheed et al., 2024), GRIT (Peng et al., 2023), VQAv2 (Antol et al., 2015), and
743   LLaVA-instruct (Liu et al., 2023b) datasets, which collectively comprise over 15 million image-text
744   pairs featuring multi-turn conversations. Referring expression tasks include Referring Expression
745   Comprehension (REC), Referring Expression Segmentation (RES), and Referring Expression Gen-
746   eration (REG). The REC and RES tasks require the model to respond to a question or description
747
      regarding a specific area in an image, predicting bounding boxes or masks. In contrast, the REG
      task involves inputs such as points, boxes, scribbles, and masks, with the model expected to gen-
748
      erate a descriptive response based on the visual prompts. We construct conversations for referring
749
      expression task from refCOCO (Kazemzadeh et al., 2014), refCOCO+ (Kazemzadeh et al., 2014),
750   refCOCOg (Kazemzadeh et al., 2014), GranD (Rasheed et al., 2024), GRIT (Lin et al., 2014), Os-
751   prey (Yuan et al., 2024), Visual Genome (Krishna et al., 2017) datasets with more than 22 million
752   samples. Interactive grounding allows users to provide prompts through both text and interactive
753   elements, such as points, boxes, masks, or scribbles, enabling MLLMs to interpret these inputs and
754   generate corresponding outputs, including bounding boxes or masks. We constructed interactive
755   grounding samples using the COCO-interactive (Zhang et al., 2024b) dataset , which contains over
      64 million examples. The open-vocabulary identification task focuses on localizing and segmenting

      objects in an image based on descriptive text prompts, even if the specific object categories were
757   not included in the model’s training data. To equip VT-PLUG with zero-shot capabilities for object
758   detection and segmentation—similar to traditional open-vocabulary detection models (e.g., YOLO-
759   World (Cheng et al., 2024)) and segmentation models (e.g., SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023)) — we
760   designed a multimodal conversation system using bounding boxes and masks annotations from the

      A.2    AP S IMILARITY (APS )
771
772   Instead of the calculating mAP as our evaluation metric for Open-Vocabulary Identification tasks,
773   we propose a new metric called mAP Similarity (APS ) to evaluate our VT-PLUG performance.
774   For traditional open-vocabulary models, they typically predict classes with a logit score by their
775   classification head. However, instead of applying a classification head for each task, our VT-PLUG
776   leverages a large language model (LLM) to predict classes without generating any class logits. We
777   therefore compute the similarity score between VT-PLUG’s class predictions and all ground truth
778
      class names. We then assign the class label based on the highest similarity, using this similarity
      score in place of the traditional confidence score.
779
780   For the implementation of APS , we define the phrases predicted by the LLM as pi ∈
781   p1 , p2 , p3 , . . . , pk , where k denotes the number of LLM predictions. The ground truth classes are
782   denoted as ci ∈ c1 , c2 , c3 , . . . , cn , where n is the total number of ground truth classes for the dataset.
783   We first use the CLIP-Large-14-336 model to compute the text embeddings e, as shown in Equa-

      tion (1). Next, we compute the cosine similarity score between each pi and ci as in Equation (2).
      The class of our predicted phrase is assigned based on the maximum similarity score and its corre-

      A.3    T RAINING D ETAILS
793
794   The training process of VT-PLUG is conducted in three stages, during which both CLIP-ViT and
795   CLIP-ConvNeXt are frozen, with no parameter updates. We use eight NVIDIA A800-80GB GPUs
796   in all of our training processes and pick Vicuna-7B as our LLM, CLIP-large-14-336 and CLIP-
797   ConvNeXt-512 as our visual encoder. In the first stage, VT-PLUG adopts the same setting as Shikra,
798   freezing all model parameters except for the projector, aiming to achieve alignment of multimodal
799   data, we train the first stage for about 2 days with setting the lr to 1e − 5. In the second stage,
800   VT-PLUG is trained using the VT-Instruct data that we constructed as shown in Section 3.2, updat-
801   ing parameters for all modules except the keypoint decoder. The goal of this stage is to train the
802
      LLM and various visual plugins using large-scale data, while the keypoint decoder is excluded from
      training due to its strong correlation with the box decoder. In the third stage, VT-PLUG continues
803
      training on the VT-Instruct dataset, updating all modules, with the keypoint decoder initialized with
804
      the weights of the box decoder from the second stage. We set the lr to 2e − 6 in the second and third
805   stages. It took about 7 days to complete the whole training process.
