009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            With the rapid growth of text-to-image models, a variety of techniques have been
012            suggested to prevent style mimicry. Yet, these methods often only protect against
013            specific user prompts and have been shown to allow undesirable generations with
014            other inputs. Here we focus on unconditionally erasing a concept from a text-
015            to-image model rather than conditioning the erasure on the user’s prompt. We
016            first show that compared to input-dependent erasure methods, concept erasure that
017            uses Task Vectors (TV) is more robust to unexpected user inputs, not seen during
018
               training. However, TV-based erasure can also affect the core performance of the
               edited model, particularly when the required edit strength is unknown. To this
019
               end, we propose a method called Diverse Inversion, which we use to estimate
020
               the required strength of the TV edit. Diverse Inversion finds within the model
021            input space a large set of word embeddings, each of which induces the generation
022            of the target concept. We find that encouraging diversity in the set makes our
023            estimation more robust to unexpected prompts. Finally, we show that Diverse
024            Inversion enables us to apply a TV edit only to a subset of the model weights,
025            enhancing the erasure capabilities while better maintaining model utility.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The capacity of text-to-image (T2I) generative models to produce high-quality images has greatly
031   improved over recent years. With this progress, concerns have risen over their ability to generate
032   content that mimics copyrighted images or specific artistic styles, often without authorization. This
033
      capacity raises significant legal and ethical questions, particularly around issues of intellectual prop-
      erty infringement. For instance, T2I models can inadvertently reproduce images or stylistic elements
034
      that closely resemble copyrighted artworks or photographs, leading to unauthorized use. A straight-
035
      forward approach to address this issue would be data filtering, removing copyrighted images and
036   styles from the model’s training dataset. However, the immense size and diversity of automatically
037   web-scraped datasets make comprehensive filtering challenging. Imperfect filtering can compro-
038   mise the legal compliance of parties deploying generative models. Moreover, even if filtering were
039   feasible, retraining these models from scratch each time regulations change is often impractical due
040   to the enormous costs involved.
041
      In response, several recent methods have been proposed to sanitize unwanted concepts from T2I
042   models, including a large set of approaches aimed at removing style mimicry and copyrighted el-
043   ements (Kumari et al., 2023; Gandikota et al., 2023b;a; AUTOMATIC1111, 2022; Schramowski
044   et al., 2023; Heng & Soh, 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a). However, these methods show significant
045   weaknesses, particularly when subjected to unexpected inputs. Adversaries can easily bypass these
046   methods, as shown in recent studies (Tsai et al., 2024; Pham et al., 2024). Most model sanitization
047   techniques excel when conditioned on a specific input prompt or sequence of tokens, but they strug-
048   gle with the many-to-many mapping from prompts to image space that T2I models inherently learn.
049   Consequently, a determined adversary, even with modest computational resources, can discover al-
050
      ternative prompts that generate the very copyrighted content or styles that were meant to be blocked.
      This paper investigates the problem of eliminating unwanted concepts, specifically copyrighted im-
051
      ages and styles, from T2I models.
052
053   While perfect erasure is beyond the scope of this study, we aim to tackle a more focused chal-
      lenge. Our objective is to develop an unconditional version of concept erasure—a method that can


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      effectively remove copyrighted material in a manner that is agnostic to specific user prompts. Our
055   proposed solution builds on a technique known as Task Vectors (TV) (Ilharco et al., 2023). At a high
056   level, a task vector represents a displacement in the model’s weight space resulting from fine-tuning.
057   Previous research has shown that TVs can be used to edit a model via arithmetic operations on the
058   model weights. Crucially, TV-based editing is independent of specific user inputs, which allows us
059   to provide unconditional safety against the generation of copyrighted content or stylistic mimicry.
060
      In Sec. 3, we define a criterion called unconditional safety, which measures the effectiveness of
061   input-independent concept erasure in a model. We hypothesize that some models, given a long
062   enough prompt, might always generate content that infringes upon copyrighted material (Wolf et al.,
063   2023). Therefore, our proposed criterion limits the prompt to specific parameters when evaluating a
064   method’s ability to prevent unauthorized generation. Using this criterion, we demonstrate that TVs
065   can provide unconditional safety in a controlled experimental setting.
066
      Building on the strong performance of TV-based edits for unconditional concept erasure on our toy
067   model, we explore whether this technique can be applied to larger T2I models without compromising
068   their functionality on unrelated tasks. We characterize the trade-off between erasure strength and
069   model performance using a parameter that scales the magnitude of the edit vector. To fine-tune
070   this erasure strength, we propose a method called Diverse Inversion. Diverse Inversion identifies
071   a broad set of token embeddings in dense space, all of which correspond to the target concept we
072   wish to remove. This set serves as a validation set, helping us select suitable hyperparameters for
073   our method.
074   Summary of our contributions. (i) Showing that the vulnerability of current concept erasure meth-
075   ods is caused by their dependence on specific input prompts (Sec. 3.2) (ii) Demonstrating TV-based
076   editing as an efficient method for input-independent concept erasure (Sec. 3.3) (iii) Proposing Di-
077   verse Inversion, an algorithm to find a diverse set of dense prompts corresponding to a target concept,
078   and utilizing it to allow a better trade-off between concept-erasure and model performance (Sec. 4).

      Denoising Diffusion Models. Diffusion models are a class of generative models that iteratively
083
      refine a distribution through a Markov-based denoising process (Ho et al., 2020; Sohl-Dickstein
084   et al., 2015). The process starts with a noise vector, xT , and progressively denoises it over T steps
085   to reconstruct the original data x0 . Latent diffusion models (LDM) (Rombach et al., 2022) enhance
086   the process efficiency by working in a lower-dimensional space learned by an autoencoder. The first
087   component of LDM includes a pre-trained encoder, E, and decoder, D, trained on a large dataset
088   of images. The encoder maps an image, x, to a spatial latent code, z = E(x), and the decoder
089   reconstructs the original image from the latent code, D(E(x)) ≈ x. The second component is a
090   diffusion model trained to generate codes in the learned latent space. Given an input prompt and its
091   associated word embedding v, the LDM is trained to generate an image conditioned on c using the
092
      following objective function:

097   Concept-Erasure on T2I Models. Several strategies have been developed to prevent generative
098   models from producing undesirable images. Negative Prompt (NP) (AUTOMATIC1111, 2022) and
099
      Safe Latent Diffusion (SLD) (Schramowski et al., 2023) suggest modifying the inference process to
      divert the final output from undesired concepts. Additionally, SLD consists of 4 variants SLD-Weak,
100
      SLD-Medium, SLD-Strong, and SLD-Max that correspond to erasure strength. Other approaches
101
      employ classifiers to alter the output (Rando et al., 2022; AI, 2022; Bedapudi, 2022). Since infer-
102   ence guiding methods can be evaded with sufficient access to model parameters (SmithMano, 2022),
103   subsequent works including Erased Stable Diffusion (ESD) (Gandikota et al., 2023a), Selective Am-
104   nesia (SA) (Heng & Soh, 2023), Forget-Me-Not (FMN) (Zhang et al., 2023a), Ablating Concepts
105   (AC) (Kumari et al., 2023), and Unified Concept Editing (UCE) (Gandikota et al., 2023b) advocate
106   for fine-tuning Stable Diffusion model weights. To address additional requirements, recent works
107   have focused on providing transferability (Lyu et al., 2024), the ability to better erase multiple con-
      cepts simultaneously (Lu et al., 2024), and improved preservation of other concepts (Zhao et al.,

      2024). The idea of robust erasure was also explored by (Huang et al., 2023), which proposes a
109   technique inspired by adversarial training.

111   Jailbreaking Generative Models. Deep neural networks are known for their brittleness and various
112
      algorithms are known for creating inputs that lead these models to produce undesirable outputs. In
      the context of Large Language Models (LLMs), the term “jailbreaks” refers to adversarial inputs
113
      that trigger unsafe, harmful, or unwanted responses from the model (Zou et al., 2023; Mehrotra
114
      et al., 2023; Chao et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024). In the realm of text-to-image models, despite
115   undergoing research on concept erasure methods used to remove undesirable concepts from the
116   weights (Gandikota et al., 2023a;b; Kumari et al., 2023; AUTOMATIC1111, 2022; Schramowski
117   et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a; Heng & Soh, 2023), recent works have shown that they are still
118   susceptible to adversarial inputs (Tsai et al., 2024; Pham et al., 2024). In particular, Tsai et al. (2024)
119   uses a CLIP text encoder to construct a concept vector; a vector in embedding space representing
120   the unwanted content. It then uses a genetic algorithm Sivanandam & Deepa (2008) to find hard
121   prompts that produce the concept vector in the embedding space. Additionally, Pham et al. (2024)
122   proposes Concept Inversion, which is a method based on Textual Inversion (Gal et al., 2023) to
123
      search for word embeddings that circumvent concept erasure methods. Textual Inversion (Gal et al.,
      2023) learns to capture the user-provided concept by representing it through new “words” in the
124
      embedding space of a frozen T2I model without changing the model.
125
126   Task Vectors and Parameter Space Interpolations. Although neural networks are inherently non-
127   linear, previous research has shown that interpolating the weights of two neural networks can pre-
128   serve their high accuracy if they share a portion of their optimization trajectory (Izmailov et al.,
129
      2018; Frankle et al., 2020). For example, accuracy may improve when the weights of a pre-trained
      model are gradually shifted towards its fine-tuned counterpart (Matena & Raffel, 2022; Ilharco et al.,
130
      2022; Wortsman et al., 2022).
131
132   Interestingly, the weight difference learned during fine tuning can also be learned on one task and
133   transferred to another to achieve a similar function. Like a vector, it can also be multiplied by a
134   (possibly negative) scalar, and often conveys an appropriate meaning to the model function. Ilharco
135
      et al. (2023) first compute a Task Vector (TV) as:
136                                               τ = θf t − θpre ,
137
138   where θpre is the pre-trained model and θf t is the model fine-tuned on a selected set of tasks.
139
      Subtracting the TV, scaled by a constant α, from the pre-trained weights θpre will make the model
      perform worse on the selected tasks for which the fine-tuning process was done. On the other hand,
140
      adding a scaled TV will improve the model’s performance on the same tasks. Ilharco et al. (2023)
141
      show that Task Vectors can be applied to CLIP classifiers and LLMs to alter their behavior. In this
142   work, we show that Task Vectors can also be applied to text-to-image diffusion models (in particular,
143   the UNet module in Stable Diffusion) to perform concept erasure.

150   on the concept name to suppress the generation of a targeted concept. For instance, or example,
151   ESD (Gandikota et al., 2023a) fine-tunes the pre-trained diffusion U-Net model to remove a specific

153   generating an image based on the concept’s textual description. Since this loss depends on the
154   concept name c, we hypothesize that ESD and similar methods only suppress the targeted concept
155   when explicitly prompted with its name.

212   3.2   M ARGINAL , CONDITIONAL , AND ABSOLUTE SAFETY
213
214   A straightforward way to quantify the safety of a model against undesired generations is to esti-
215   mate the marginal probability of an unwanted generation. Given a generative model G, a prompt p
      drawn from a distribution D, and the set of unwanted generations U , the marginal probability P for


                                                                                  4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      unwanted generation is given as Smarginal :
217
218                                      Smarginal = Pp∼D (G(p) ∈ U ).                                    (1)
219   Yet, explicitly calculating this marginal safety is intractable, and depends on the unknown prompt
220   distribution to be used during inference. It is tempting to replace it with a criterion of conditional
221   safety. This notion takes into account a set of a few prompts supplied by the user, C, known to
222   be related to the target concept we wish to erase. Our aim then would be to reduce the maximal
223   probability of undesirable generation with any of the prompts p ∈ C:
224
                                       Sconditional = max P (G(p) ∈ U ).                                  (2)
225                                                    p∈C
226
      Optimizing G for this safety criteria would ensure that all prompts within the set C would induce an
227   unwanted behavior with a probability Sconditional at most. This safety criterion is often optimized by
228   most existing concept erasure methods. Yet, optimizing this criterion would not yield any guarantee
229   outside the set C. Therefore, we focus on a safety criterion that is independent of any user-supplied
230   prompts we term unconditional safety. We suggest a safety criterion limiting the probability with
231   which undesirable generation would occur, given a constrained input complexity (e.g., the prompt
232   length). While input length is a good parameter for input complexity, in many cases we use dense
233   embedding. In such cases, we may instead limit the resolution in which the dense embedding is
234   given. The resolution of a continuous input vector v ∈ Rd is closely related to the input prompt
235
      length (Wolf et al., 2023; Schwarzschild et al., 2024). For one example, encoding a higher resolution
      dense embedding corresponds to more bits of information (Li et al., 2008). Specifically, we denote
236
      the input complexity (measured by resolution or length) by DL and use it to write the unconditional
237
      safety criteria. Namely, the unconditional safety criterion is the maximal probability of generating
238   the undesired content given an input of complexity L (e.g., prompt length). We note this class of
239   prompts as p ∈ DL :
240                                   Suncoditional = max P (G(p) ∈ U ).                                 (3)

245
      L, we can demonstrate its improvement on toy models. We hypothesize that prompt-independent
246   concept erasure methods such as TV edits may provide better unconditional safety. To test this
247   hypothesis we trained a toy model with a dense “prompt” space of dimensions d = 8. We use
248   images from the MNIST (LeCun et al., 2010) dataset.
249
      We investigate the generation of MNIST digits 0 and 1 (“the target concept”) from our three diffu-
250
      sion models: (i) Input dependent concept-erasure: We fine-tune the model to produce the remaining
251
      9 digits when given 0 as conditional input. (ii) Input-independent concept-erasure: We utilize a TV
252   edit for input-independent concept-erasure (Ilharco et al., 2023). We fine-tune our model to gener-
253   ate only the target concept (digit 0), and then subtract the Task Vector achieved by the fine-tuning
254   process from the original model. This process is input-independent as we perform unconditional
255   fine-tuning discarding the usage of conditional input embedding (iii) Original model: We also eval-
256   uate the original model, without concept erasure. For all models, we use a pre-trained classifier to
257   automatically evaluate whether the target concept was indeed generated.
258   We now turn to evaluate the unconditional safety criterion for the three models above. Specifically,
259   we define the input complexity classes DL as the resolutions in which we perform an exhaustive
260   search of the possible prompts in continuous space. For each complexity class DL , we explore a

263   to a low input complexity class, while the grid point (0.4, 0.8, 0.6, −0.2, −1.0, ...) belongs to a
264   higher one. Intuitively, the higher the input complexity class we examine, the closer we are to an
265   exhaustive search in the input space.

300   4                               D IVERSE I NVERSION FOR C ONCEPT E RASURE U SING TASK V ECTORS
301
302   Motivated by the potential of TV-based editing as a method capable of improving the unconditional
303   safety of T2I models, we now focus on applying this technique to larger models. Namely, we wish to
304
      erase unwanted concepts from large diffusion models while otherwise retaining their text-to-image
      capabilities. Measuring the degree of preservation of the desired text-to-image capabilities can be
305
      done directly, since this typically involves expected user inputs and outputs. However, anticipating
306
      the model’s reaction to adversarial prompts unknown at the time of editing can be challenging. To
307   estimate how well the model is protected against unexpected inputs, we would like to observe its
308   generations with a diverse array of adversarial prompts. We cannot inspect all the input prompts of
309   a given length as we did for the toy model, due to the very large number of possible prompts. To this
310   end, we create a diverse safety validation set composed of diverse input tokens that can all generate
311   unwanted content with the original model.
312
      Our method for applying Task Vectors for concept erasure in large models consists of three parts.
313   First, we learn a diverse set of adversarial embeddings, allowing us to estimate Task Vector edit
314   robustness. Next, we show this learned set allows us to choose robust hyper-parameters for TV
315   edits while maintaining the model utility. Finally, we show that we can not only choose performant
316   hyper-parameter values but also sub-select the set of model parameters we wish to edit for better
317   performance. Our motivation for utilizing Task Vectors stems from the fact that the erasure process
318   does not depend on any prompts. The learned embeddings are only used to help us tune the editing
319   strength of the model.

382   Metrics: To assess the content of the generated images, we use CLIP ViT-B/32 (Radford et al., 2021)

384   Heng & Soh (2023); Gandikota et al. (2023a;b), the control task for all experiments is the average
385   CLIP similarity score of 6 concepts across 3 different concept categories (artistic style, objects, and
386   specific people): “art by Kilian Eng”, “art by Picasso”, “garbage truck”, “chain saw”, “Brad Pitt”,
387   and “Angelina Jolie”.
388   Erasure Score (ES). We propose to use a metric known as Erasure Score to validate the robustness
389   of the edited Stable Diffusion model to many different attack prompts. The metric is defined as
390   follows: after obtaining word embeddings via Diverse Inversion, we generate an image for each
391   learned embedding using the Stable Diffusion model. The Erasure Score is defined as the maximum
392   (calculated over all generated images) CLIP similarity between the generated images from a prompt
393   in our Diverse Inversion set, and the concept name. A lower Erasure Score indicates more robustness
394
      against adversarial inputs.

396   5.2   R ESULTS
397
      We demonstrate our method provides robustness to current adversarial methods. In Figs. 7 we
398
      demonstrate our robustness to Concept Inversion (Pham et al., 2024); first showing the original
399
      (unedited) model in the first row. In the second row in each of the sub-figures of Fig. 7 we show
400   that for certain values of the edit strength α, the Stable Diffusion model manages to suppress the
401   generation of the targeted concept when explicitly prompted with the same concept name. However,
402   when Concept Inversion (Pham et al., 2024) is applied, we can still recover the erased concept.
403   On the other hand, when α is increased, we obtain both a lower Erasure Score (ES) and a more
404   robust erased model. This suggests that we can use the Erasure Score to guide us in selecting an
405   appropriate edit strength to make the model more robust against adversarial inputs. We also test our
406   edited models against hard prompts obtained from Ring-A-Bell (Tsai et al., 2024), UnlearnDiffAtk
407   (Zhang et al., 2023b), and P4D (Chin et al., 2024). Fig.6 demonstrates that our method is more
408
      robust against Ring-A-Bell compared to other concept erasure methods. For space considerations,
      we refer the readers to the Appendix A for the remaining results on the robustness of our method.
409
410   Additionally, to study how our method affects unrelated concepts, we calculate the MS COCO FID
411   of the model with the Van Gogh concept erased. Tab. 1 suggests that TV-based erasure can preserve
412   unrelated concepts competitively with other concept erasure methods. Additional results are found
413   in App.C.3.
414   A notable drawback of using Task Vectors (TV) is that this method requires editing the entire model
415   to enhance the Stable Diffusion model’s robustness against adversarial inputs. Consequently, this
416   might compromise the model’s generative performance on concepts unrelated to the erased concept.
417   Fig. 4 demonstrates that certain layers of TV can be pruned to better preserve generative perfor-
418   mance on unrelated concepts, while maintaining robustness against adversarial inputs. Moreover,
419   we observe that TV is worse at preserving surrounding concepts close to the concept we wish to
420
      erase. Fig. 5 shows that when erasing the Van Gogh concept, a similar concept like Monet will be
      more affected than less related concepts.

506   6            D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATIONS
507
508   Absolute Erasure. Making it impossible to generate unwanted content may seem like an ideal
509   goal. However, completely avoiding unwanted content depends on the ability to recognize all un-
510   wanted behaviors. The ability to describe all unwanted behaviors without defining such behaviors
511
      in advance is therefore left for further research.
512   Provable guarantees for erasure. An inherent weakness of any erasure method is the inability to
513   evaluate them in advance against yet unknown future adversarial methods (Amodei et al., 2016). We
514   acknowledge this as a weakness of our suggested method as well. To try to mitigate this, we provide
515   not only results against current adversarial methods but also a principled analysis of the unique
516   qualities of TV-based concept erasure being input-independent. Yet, a full mathematical proof of
517
      the robustness is beyond the scope of this paper.
518   Limitations TV-based erasure. Our suggested method is reliant on the TV technique. Yet, the
519   parameter space of neural networks is far from being completely understood (Ma et al., 2020). This
520   means that the exact cases where TV-based erasure can work or fail are not completely clear. The
521   exact limits of applicability of Task Vectors is yet to be explored.
522
523
524   Selecting a concept erasure setting for various tasks. We would like to emphasize that real-
525   world settings differ from one another, and therefore might require different solutions. E.g., for
526   maintaining copy-rights, robustness to unexpected prompts, which is a relative advantage of our
527   method, might be needed. Yet, for other applications, the ability to erase broad concepts such as
528   nudity might be more important than adversarial robustness. We believe therefore that there is room
529   for more than one method, at least until a single method would possibly incorporate all advantages.
530
531   7            C ONCLUSIONS
532
533   We propose adapting Task Vectors (TV), a recently proposed technique for model editing, for erasing
534   concepts from generative models. On a range of test cases, we demonstrate how TVs can be used
535   to sanitize undesirable concepts from text-to-image models in a way that is independent of specific
536   user prompts. This distinguishes TV from existing methods in the literature and makes them more
537   robust. Our method enables us to better maintain model utility while removing harmful concepts. We
538   anticipate that our method can be extended to other models such as large LLMs and other multimodal
539   vision-language models.

992    D     P SEUDOCODE FOR M ODEL S ANITIZATION
993
994    Inputs:
995
                • ErasureScore Threshold - T
996
997             • Generative Model - G
998             • Target Concept Image Set - S
999             • Concept Description - CON CEP T N AM E and its embedding in textual space vconcept
1000
                • Learning Rate - lr
1001
1002   Output:
1003
1004            • A model sanitized from the target concept
1005
1006   Steps:
1007   1. Fine-Tune Model:
1008   Fine-tune the diffusion model’s unet to generate the target concept using the set S with default
1009   finetuning parameters.
1010
       2. Calculate Task Vector:
1011   Compute the task vector τ :
1012                                                τ = θf t − θpre
1013
       This represents the direction in the latent space of the unet that corresponds to generating the target
1014
       concept.
1015
1016   3. Find Diverse Embeddings:
1017   Obtain n diverse embeddings vi , each optimized to generate the target concept while being diverse
1018
       and far from the embedding of the concept name using textual inversion (TI):
1019                                                                  vi = T I(G, S)
1020
1021
       Subject to:
                                   Sim(vi∗ , vconcept ) ∈ [a, b] ∀i = 1, 2, . . . , n
1022
1023                              Sim(vi∗ , vj∗ ) ∈ [c, d] ∀i, j = 1, 2, . . . , n, i ̸= j
1024   For each vi , optimize for generation and impose the constraints in a loop:
1025
                • Optimize vi for generation using a learning rate lr.


                                                                           19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
              • To maintain the constraints, use a hinge loss to steer the vector back to the allowed sub-
1027            space.
1028
1029   4. Define Erasure Score:
1030   Calculate the ErasureScore as the expected value over vi (Evi ) based on the CLIP similarity with
1031   the target concept:
1032                             ErasureScore = Evi (clip sim(G(vi ), ∗))
1033
       5. Iterate to Find Sanitized Model:
1034
       Loop over α in the range (0, αinterval , αmax ):
1035
1036                                       for α ∈ (0, αinterval , αmax ) :
1037
1038          • Edit the model using the task vector τ :
1039                                                Gtθ = Gorigθ − α · τ
1040
1041          • Compute the similarity score:
1042
                              SimScore = Evi (clip sim(Gtθ (vi ), CON CEP T N AM E))
1043
1044          • Check if the similarity score is below the threshold. If so, return Gtθ .
