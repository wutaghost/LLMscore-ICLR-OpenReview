011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Mental health disorders are one of the most serious diseases in the world. Most
014            people with such a disease lack access to adequate care, which highlights the impor-
015            tance of training models for the diagnosis and treatment of mental health disorders.
016
               However, in the mental health domain, privacy concerns limit the accessibility of
               personalized treatment data, making it challenging to build powerful models. In this
017
               paper, we introduce MentalArena, a self-play framework to train language models
018
               by generating domain-specific personalized data, where we obtain a better model
019            capable of making a personalized diagnosis and treatment (as a therapist) and
020            providing information (as a patient). To accurately model human-like mental health
021            patients, we devise Symptom Encoder which simulates a real patient from both
022            cognition and behavior perspectives. To address intent bias during patient-therapist
023            interactions, we propose Symptom Decoder to compare diagnosed symptoms with
024            encoded symptoms, and dynamically manage the dialogue between patient and
025            therapist according to the identified deviations. We evaluated MentalArena against
026            6 benchmarks, including biomedicalQA and mental health tasks, compared to
027
               6 advanced models. Our models, fine-tuned on both GPT-3.5 and Llama-3-8b,
               significantly outperform their counterparts, including GPT-4o. We hope that our
028
               work can inspire future research on personalized care.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Mental health disorders include a variety of conditions such as anxiety, depression, and schizophre-
034
      nia, which affect people’s thinking, emotions, behavior, or mood (Prince et al., 2007). In 2019,
035
      approximately 970 million people worldwide lived with a mental health disorder, with anxiety and
036   depression being most prevalent (WHO, 2022). The number increased by 28% in 2020 and continues
037   to increase. Despite the availability of effective treatments, many individuals lack access to adequate
038   care due to under-resourced health systems. For example, only 29% of people with psychosis and one
039   third of people with depression receive formal mental healthcare (WHO, 2022). It is indispensable
040   to develop machine learning models for the automatic diagnosis and treatment of such diseases.
041   However, existing AI therapist systems use templates and decision trees, which are not flexible to
042   support personalized care (Fiske et al., 2019; D’Alfonso, 2020; Grodniewicz & Hohol, 2023).
043   The key to training powerful models is to collect sufficient training data. However, due to privacy
044   concerns in the medical domain, data collection, especially personalized data for mental health
045   disorders, is inherently challenging. A growing body of work has focused on enhancing mental health
046   language models by sourcing additional domain-specific data from social media (Xu et al., 2024;
047   Yang et al., 2024a; Hu et al., 2024a). However, social media data are inherently biased and under-
048   representative, failing to capture the full spectrum of people’s mental health needs. Moreover, as
049   LLMs continue to scale, the availability of training data in the real world becomes increasingly limited,
050
      further exacerbating this challenge. Existing methods are likely to soon reach their performance limit.
051   Recently, several works have focused on self-play (Hu et al., 2024b; Yang et al., 2024b; Liang et al.,
052   2024; Wu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024d), where models play different roles and self-evolve or
053   co-evolve during interaction with other models. A model synthesizes training data on its own and
      then use the generated data to train itself. However, there are two challenges that prevent us from

071
072   MentalArena is a framework specifically designed for self-play training of language models to
073   facilitate the diagnosis, treatment, and medication of mental health disorders. The model M assumes
074   the dual roles of both patient and therapist. In its capacity as the therapist, it provides diagnoses,
075
      treatment plans, and medication regimens based on the symptoms presented by the patient. As the
      patient, it simulates its updated health status after implementation of each treatment and medication
076
      plan. As illustrated in Figure 1, MentalArena comprises three key modules: Symptom Encoder,
077
      Symptom Decoder, and Model Optimizer. Symptom Encoder models mental health patients based on
078   cognitive models 1 and behavioral patterns, offering rich insights into coping strategies and behavioral
079   principles. Symptom Decoder simulates the diagnosis and treatment interactions between a patient and
080   a therapist, generating more personalized dialogues while mitigating intent bias (Britten et al., 2000;
081   West, 1984). During each iteration, we collect data from these interactions, including diagnostic,
082   treatment, and medication information, and evolve the models through training on those datasets.
083
      To evaluate MentalArena, we conduct experiments on 6 benchmarks including datasets on biomedical
084   QA and mental health detection. We compare our fine-tuned models with other state-of-the-art and
085   mental health models. We also compare with two advanced prompt engineering approaches. Our
086   models outperform all their counterparts. Specifically, MentalArena brings a great improvement to
087   base models (20.7% improvement over GPT-3.5-turbo and 6.6% over Llama-3-8b). Moreover, our
088   model based on GPT-3.5-turbo significantly outperforms GPT-4o by 7.7%.
089
      We further thoroughly analyze the dynamics of self-play training. We find that the perplexity
090   score (Marion et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023) and the model performance are highly correlated. For
091   diversity gain (Bilmes, 2022), the model performance will increase if the diversity gain exceeds
092   some thresholds. We also explore whether MentalArena can be generalized to other diseases. The
093   results on MedMCQA (Pal et al., 2022) and MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020) prove the generalization
094   ability of MentalArena in medical domain. Furthermore, we explore the catastrophic forgetting of
095   our fine-tuned models. The results on BIG-Bench-Hard (BBH) (Suzgun et al., 2022) show that our
096   models does not decrease performance in general benchmarks and can even improve their results.
097   In summary, the contributions of this paper are following:
098
099   1. We propose MentalArena, a novel and cost-effective self-play framework for training language
100      models for diagnosing and treating mental health disorders. MentalArena introduces Symptom
101      Encoder and Symptom Decoder, designed to simulate real patient-therapist interactions by modeling
102      cognitive and behavioral processes.
103
      2. Using MentalArena, we generate high-quality data containing diagnosis, treatment, and medication
104      data. There are 18k samples in total that can be used for further training and research.

115   2.1   L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS FOR HEALTHCARE
116
      Researchers have explored the potential of large language models (LLMs) in healthcare (Jiang et al.,
117
      2023; Li et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Lupetti et al., 2023; Nori et al., 2023a; Singhal et al., 2023; Wu
118   et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024c). For example, Singhal et al. (2023) fine-tuned PaLM-2 for medical
119   applications, achieving 86.5% accuracy on the MedQA dataset. Similarly, Wu et al. (2023) fine-tuned
120   LLaMA on medical literature, showing strong performance in biomedical QA tasks.
121
      In the mental health domain, research has taken two main approaches. The first involves fine-tuning
122
      domain-specific LLMs on existing datasets or social media data, such as Mental-LLaMA (Yang
123
      et al., 2024a) and Mental-LLM, fine-tuned on Reddit data (Xu et al., 2024). The second approach
124   enhances mental health performance through prompt engineering. Yang et al. (2023) proposed
125   emotion-enhanced prompting strategies to guide LLMs in explainable mental health analyses.
126
127
      Unlike previous methods, MentalArena fine-tunes mental health models through self-play training, in
      which the base model assumes both patient and therapist. Training data is generated dynamically
128
      during the interactions between these two roles, allowing for more effective model refinement.
129
130
131
      2.2   S ELF - PLAY FRAMEWORKS IN L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
132   Self-play involves a model evolving through interactions with copies of itself, creating a feedback
133   loop that refines performance without external input. It is particularly effective in environments where
134   the model simulates multiple roles, such as multiplayer games (Silver et al., 2016; 2017). Compared
135   to interactive methods, self-play provides a more efficient strategy for obtaining feedback without
136   relying on an external environment.
137
      Taubenfeld et al. (2024) examine biases in LLM-generated debate simulations, while Ulmer et al.
138   (2024) focus on principle-guided conversations. Role-playing approaches, like Lu et al. (2024)’s
139   self-simulated dialogues with character profiles and Askari et al. (2024)’s SOLID framework for
140   intent-aware role-play, leverage LLMs to generate information-rich exchanges.
141
      Due to the lack of sufficient data in the training corpus, LLMs are unable to accurately simulate real
142
      patients, presenting a significant challenge for self-play training. To overcome this, MentalArena
143
      introduces Symptom Encoder, a component designed to effectively model real mental health patients.

148   3.1   P RELIMINARIES
149
150
      We first go over the process of the diagnosis and treatment of mental health disorder and explain key
      concepts. Mental health diagnosis begins with assessing an individual’s health state, encompassing
151
      mental and emotional well-being. Symptoms are key indicators of possible problems, including
152
      emotional (e.g., anxiety, depression), cognitive (e.g., memory problems) and behavioral changes (e.g.,
153   social withdrawal). These symptoms lead to a formal diagnosis made through clinical interviews
154   identifying specific disorders such as depression, anxiety, or schizophrenia. Once diagnosed, the
155   treatment process begins, often involving a combination of psychotherapy (e.g. cognitive-behavioral
156   therapy), lifestyle changes, and sometimes medication. Medications, such as antidepressants and
157   mood stabilizers, are used to regulate brain chemicals and alleviate symptoms. (Prince et al., 2007)

193   MentalArena is a framework designed specifically for self-play training of language models to
194   facilitate the diagnosis, treatment and medication of mental health disorder. As shown in Figure 1,
195
      MentalArena consists of three key modules: Symptom Encoder, Symptom Decoder, and Model
      Optimizer. Specifically, Symptom Encoder is designed to model mental health patient from cognitive
196
      models and behavioral patterns, providing a wealth of information on the coping strategy and behavior
197
      principles. Symptom Decoder emulates the process of diagnosis and treatment between a patient
198   and a therapist to generate a more personalized dialogue while mitigating intent bias (Britten et al.,
199   2000; West, 1984). At each iteration, we collect the data during interactions, including diagnosis data,
200   treatment data, and medication data, and evolve the models via training on those datasets.
201
      Formally, we use x to denote the initial health information of a patient and M to denote the base
202
      model (e.g., GPT-3.5) for the therapist and the patient via role-play strategy. Our objective is to obtain
203
      M ∗ via self-play training that can achieve better performance in both personalized diagnosis and
204   treatment of the patient (as a therapist) and information disclosure (as a patient). Self-play training
205   is conducted taking as input both original information x and treatment or medication information
206   z generalized by M . In iteration t, the model Mt plays the therapist Dt = Mt (· | Promptdoc ) and
207   the patient Pt = Mt (· | Promptpat ), which generates diagnosis and treatment data (Figure 8) during
208   patient-therapist interactions. The module Symptom Encoder can be seen as learning the encoded
209   symptom S0 by disentangling the initial health information x into cognitive and behavioral principles.
210   Then, the module Symptom Decoder generates a personalized dialogue containing key information
211   z = {δ, β, γ}, where δ, β and γ denote the diagnosis, treatment and medication of the patient given
212   the symptom S0 . It consists of k rounds of communication in which the patient can provide more
213   accurate and sufficient information by accepting the treatment and medication given by the therapist
214   in each round. As treatment and medication plans are administered to the patient, their health state
215   evolves, reflected in the sequential updates of encoded symptoms, denoted as S1 , S2 , ..., Sk−1 . The
      encoded symptoms serve as indicators of the effectiveness of the treatment and medication plans,

222   3.3   PATIENT: S YMPTOM E NCODER
223
224
      The module Symptom Encoder aims to model mental health patient from both cognitive and behavioral
      perspectives, which learns meaningful symptoms S0 from the original patient health data x. Specifi-
225
      cally, the module learns symptoms from the aspects of cognition and behavior. The cognitive model
226
      is designed based on cognitive behavior therapy (CBT) principles (Beck, 2020), a popular paradigm
227   in psychotherapy. Cognitive models address maladaptive cognitive structures that are embedded in
228   various contexts, including familial conflicts, relationship challenges, workplace challenges, and
229   other areas. The models consist of eight key components: relevant history, core beliefs, intermediate
230   beliefs, coping strategies, situational factors, automatic thoughts, emotions, and behaviors. (Beck,
231   2020) The explanation of each component of the cognitive model can be found in Appendix A.6.2.
232   Appendix A.6.1 shows the example of cognitive models. We obtain 106 patient cognitive models
233   from previous work (Wang et al., 2024a), which are created by clinical psychologists. To simulate
234   the cognitive activity of the mental health patient, we encode those cognitive models into patient via
235
      prompt. Our prompts are shown in Appendix A.1.
236   For patient behavior modeling, we use behavior principles collected by Louie et al. (2024) as a
237   behavior library, created by 25 mental health experts. Examples of behavior patterns are shown in
238   Appendix A.6.1. To find the proper behavior pattern for each cognitive model, we first semantically
239   match the coping strategies of cognitive model with each behavior pattern. We obtain the embeddings
240   for each coping strategy and behavior principle via Bert-base (Devlin et al., 2018), considering on
241
      effectiveness and cost. Then we compute the semantic similarity between coping strategies and
      behavior pattern. The max similarity score of all behavior principles in one behavior pattern is
242
      selected to represent the score of the pattern. The five behavior patterns with the highest scores are
243
      kept. To further ensure the most appropriate pattern, we prompt GPT-4-turbo (OpenAI, 2023b) to
244   pick one from the five patterns. The final behavior pattern is also integrated into patient via prompt,
245   which is shown in Appendix A.1.

      3.4   T HERAPIST: S YMPTOM D ECODER
248
249   During interactions between a real therapist and a real patient, the patient may try to express one
250   opinion while the therapist misunderstands the intent due to prior knowledge and deficiency of
251   experience (Britten et al., 2000; West, 1984). Intention bias can similarly arise in conversations
252   between patients and therapists played by AI models, resulting in inaccurate diagnosis and treatment.
253   Symptom Decoder is designed to mitigate the intent bias. After several conversations, the therapist
254   reviews the patient’s health information from previous interactions and conducts a detailed analysis
255   of the patient’s cognitive and behavioral patterns, resulting in the diagnosed symptom Sd . We then
256
      semantically match the encoded symptom S0 with the diagnosed symptom Sd and guide subsequent
      conversations based on the differences between S0 and Sd .
257
258   As shown in Figure 2(left), the therapist decodes cognitive and behavior principles according to
259   the conversation history. For example, the decoded cognitive principle is: “The patient stay away
260   from family interactions to minimize emotional distress and feelings of abandonment”. The decoded
261   behavior principle is: “When sharing personal struggles, express feelings of confusion, doubt, and
262
      emotional turmoil to convey a sense of vulnerability and authenticity”. Then we compute the semantic
      similarity score of the decoded symptom Sd and the encoded symptom S0 . If the score is greater
263
      than 0.9, the conversation will end, indicating that the therapist has fully understood the health
264
      state of the patient. Otherwise, it indicates the existence of intent bias. To help the therapist better
265   know more about the health state of the patient, we summarize the differences between the decoded
266   symptom Sd and encoded symptom S0 and generate some feedback for further inquiries via the

      3.5   M ODEL O PTIMIZER
302
303   After obtaining treatment, diagnosis, and medication through Symptom Decoder, we train M in a
304   self-play manner to get a better model capable of making a personalized diagnosis and treatment (as
305   a therapist) and presenting information (as a patient). An example of such a supervised fine-tuning
306   process is illustrated in Figure 8.
307   During each iteration, the patient and the therapist are powered by the same model M and both get
308   improved when M is updated. While our framework is flexible to allow for different base models for
309   the two roles, we adopt the same one due to the following reasons. First, it is intuitive that training one
310   base model is more efficient compared to training different models. Second, and more importantly,
311   training one base model can help reduce the knowledge gap between two roles. Two different base
312   models can certainly exhibit knowledge gaps, and iterative training will enlarge them due to different
313   architectures and pre-training data of the models. Appendix A.8 shows the detailed training settings.

523   6       C ONCLUSION , S OCIETAL I MPACT AND L IMITATIONS
524
525   In this paper, we introduce MentalArena, a self-play framework designed to train language models by
526   generating domain-specific personalized data. This approach enables the creation of models capable
527   of making personalized diagnosis and treatment (as a therapist) and presenting information (as a
528   patient). We evaluated MentalArena against six benchmarks, including biomedicalQA and mental
529   health tasks, in comparison to six advanced models. Our models, fine-tuned on both GPT-3.5-turbo
530
      and Llama-3-8b, significantly outperform their counterparts, including GPT-4o.
531   MentalArena offers promising solutions for personalized care, enhancing accessibility to tailored
532   treatments while safeguarding patient privacy. Such innovations can help bridge the gap between
533   mental health needs and the availability of effective, individualized care, ultimately fostering a more
534   supportive and informed society.
535   Our work has the following limitations. 1) The experiments on data authenticity and validity
536   (Sections 5.1 and 5.2) were evaluated using GPT-4o, which may introduce deviations in the results
537   due to potential limitations in GPT-4o’s performance. 2) Our model based on Llama-3-8b may not
538   represent the optimal model of MentalArena, as large-scale training was constrained by computational
539   resources. 3) Further implementation on additional open-source models could provide stronger
      evidence supporting the effectiveness of MentalArena.

859            the psychosis subset of the United States part, which has questions in English in the style of
860
               the United States Medical Licensing Exam (USMLE). To get the psychosis subset for test, we
               prompt GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024) with Are the question related to psychosis?
861
               Just answer with Yes or No.. The testset contains 173 samples.

863         2. MedMCQA (Pal et al., 2022) contains real world medical entrance exam questions from
               two Indian medical school entrance exams: the AIIMS and NEET-PG. We get the testset via

867
              biomedical research questions when given context provided from PubMed abstracts. In our

       A.6.2      I NTRODUCTION ON COGNITIVE MODEL
1071
1072
       Figure 10 illustrates an example of a CCD-based cognitive model, featuring eight key components.
1073
       1) Relevant History encompasses significant past events that influence an individual’s mental state. 2)
1074   Core Beliefs are deeply ingrained perceptions about oneself, others, and the world. 3) Intermediate
1075   Beliefs consist of the underlying rules, attitudes, and assumptions derived from core beliefs, shaping
1076   an individual’s thought patterns. 4) Coping Strategies refer to techniques employed to manage
1077   negative emotions. An external event or context (5 Situation) may trigger immediate evaluative
1078   thoughts (6 Automatic Thoughts) that arise from these beliefs, resulting in responses in terms of
1079   7)Emotions and 8)Behaviors. The CCD-based cognitive model interlinks these components, providing
       a framework for identifying and understanding the underlying cognitive processes of patients.
