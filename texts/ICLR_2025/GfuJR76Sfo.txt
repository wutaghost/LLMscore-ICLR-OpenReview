009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
               We introduce the Contrastive Similarity Space Embedding Algorithm (ContraSim), a novel
012
               framework for uncovering the global semantic relationships between daily financial head-
013            lines and market movements. ContraSim operates in two key stages: (i) Weighted Headline
014            Augmentation, which generates augmented financial headlines along with a semantic fine-
015            grained similarity score, and (ii) Weighted Self-Supervised Contrastive Learning (WSSCL),
016            an extended version of classical self-supervised contrastive learning that uses the similarity
017            metric to create a refined weighted embedding space. This embedding space clusters se-
018            mantically similar headlines together, facilitating deeper market insights. Empirical results
019            demonstrate that integrating ContraSim features into financial forecasting tasks improves
020            classification accuracy from WSJ headlines by 7%. Moreover, leveraging an information
021
               density analysis, we find that the similarity spaces constructed by ContraSim intrinsically
               cluster days with homogeneous market movement directions, indicating that ContraSim
022
               captures market dynamics independent of ground truth labels. Additionally, ContraSim
023
               enables the identification of historical news days that closely resemble the headlines of
024            the current day, providing analysts with actionable insights to predict market trends by
025            referencing analogous past events.

      to generate diverse news headlines with varying degrees of semantic similarity to the original. Augmented

      Machine Learning in Financial Forecasting Early approaches to predicting stock market movements
130   relied heavily on classical statistical models. One foundational method, the Autoregressive Integrated
131   Moving Average (ARIMA) Box & Jenkins [1970], utilized time series data to forecast trends. Subsequent
132   models, such as Generalized Autoregressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity (GARCH) Bollerslev [1986],
133   Vector Autoregression (VAR) Sims [1980], and Holt-Winters exponential smoothing Holt [1957], extended
134   these capabilities by capturing more intricate patterns in financial time series. Other notable contributions
135   include techniques for cointegration analysis Engle & Granger [1987], Kalman filtering Kalman [1960], and
136   Hamilton’s regime-switching models Hamilton [1989].
137   While effective, these classical models were primarily limited to tabular datasets and struggled with nonlinear
138   relationships and multimodal inputs. The rise of Large Language Models (LLMs) transformed financial
139   forecasting by enabling the incorporation of richer, more complex data sources. For example, integrating
140   financial news articles Yang et al. [2020], sentiment analysis Yang et al. [2020], social media activity Bollen

                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


141
      et al. [2011], and earnings call transcripts Tsai & Wang [2016] significantly enhanced market movement
142
      predictions, demonstrating the versatility and power of LLMs in handling diverse financial modalities.
143
144   Contrastive Learning Contrastive learning has emerged as a powerful paradigm in unsupervised and
145   self-supervised learning, focusing on representation learning through comparisons. The core idea is to bring
146   similar data points closer in the representation space while distancing dissimilar ones. A key milestone
147   in this field was SimCLR Chen et al. [2020], which used data augmentations and contrastive loss to learn
148   high-quality representations without requiring labels. MoCo He et al. [2020] further advanced this approach
149   by introducing a memory bank to efficiently manage negative examples, making it more scalable for larger
150   datasets. Recent innovations like SimSiam Chen & He [2021] have shown that competitive representations
151   can be learned without relying on negative pairs, streamlining computation and improving accessibility.
152
      These advancements are particularly relevant for financial applications, where large-scale and heterogeneous
      datasets are common, enabling contrastive learning to uncover nuanced relationships in financial data.

193   2. Generating Augmented Daily News Sets Augmented DNS are generated through the following discrete
194   actions: i) Rewording an original headline (Re), ii) Generating a semantically shifted version (S), iii) Negating
195
      an original headline (N), and iv) Selecting a random headline from a different day (Ra).
196   To achieve these transformations, we leveraged the LLaMA-3-7b-chat model Touvron et al. [2023], prompting
197   it with carefully crafted instructions tailored to each specific action. For rewording (Re), the model was
198   prompted to retain the original meaning of the headline while rephrasing it with alternative wording and
199   sentence structure. For semantic-shifting (S), the prompt instructed the model to subtly alter the meaning
200   of the headline, introducing slight semantic deviations while maintaining topical relevance. For negation
201
      (N), the model was guided to generate a headline that conveyed the direct opposite meaning of the original.
      By using these tailored prompts, the LLaMA model provided high-quality augmented news headlines that
202
      covered a broad spectrum of semantic variations.
203
204   To ensure the quality of LLM-generated headline augmentations, we employ an off-the-shelf fine-tuned
205   BERT model as a discriminator to verify semantic consistency. This model takes the base and augmented
206   headlines as inputs and outputs a semantic similarity score, bounded between 0 and 1. The score thresholds
207
      define stricter guidelines for each augmentation action, where negated, semantically-shifted, and reworded
      headlines must fall in ranges (0, 0.33), (0.33, 0.66), and (0.66, 1.00) respectively. This approach provides
208
      a well-defined, quantitative framework for categorizing augmentations. It not only enforces consistency
209
      in semantic relationships but also ensures that the augmented headlines are reliable and aligned with the
210   intended transformations.

      With augmented daily news sets (DNS) generated from the training set, and similarity scores assigned to each
      anchor-augmentation DNS pair, we can now proceed to construct the similarity embedding space using a
274
      weighted self-supervised contrastive learning approach.
275
276   Our embedding space optimization task is inspired by Supervised Contrastive Learning Khosla et al. [2021],
277   but is augmented to allow for regressive similarity measurements between anchor and augmented projections
278   instead of binary positive / negative labels. Our representation learning framework consists of 3 components,
279
      the Encoder Network, the Projection Network, and the Classification Networks:
280   Encoder Network: e = Enc(x) is a LLaMA-3 AI [2024] 7 billion parameter chat model. It was fine-tuned
281   to predict market movement direction (Fall, Neutral, or Rise) from the NIFTY-SFT dataset Raeid et al. [2024].

283
      tokenized and propagated through the encoder network, and the mean values from the last hidden layer are
284   returned, such that e = Enc(x) ∈ RDE . e is then normalized to a hypersphere, which in our implementation
285   had dimensions of 4096.

      into our embedding space. After projection, the output values are again normalized. We found negligible

      3.3   E VALUATING S IMILARITY S PACE I NFORMATION R ICHNESS
339
340   To measure the efficacy of ContraSim, we employ two approaches. The first is the most straightforward: we
341   train a market movement prediction algorithm using both ContraSim embeddings and a baseline without
342   ContraSim embeddings, and evaluate its downstream classification performance.
343
      The second approach involves analyzing how our projection network (P roj(e)) embeds real-world daily
344   news sets (DNS). Using information-dense metrics, we evaluate whether the model inherently clusters DNS
345   associated with the same market direction closer together. For instance, if the similarity space places headlines
346   corresponding to rising markets near one another, it suggests that meaningful information is being captured.
347   This clustering behavior is quantified using four information-dense metrics:
348
      1) Geometric K-Nearest Neighbors (g-KNN): This metric evaluates the quality of local label distributions
349
      by measuring the entropy of the labels among the k-nearest neighbors of each data point, averaged over the
350   dataset. It provides insights into the local clustering structure of the embedding space Lord et al. [2018]. 2)
351   Nearest Neighbor Accuracy: This metric assesses the proportion of data points whose closest neighbor
352   shares the same category label, offering a direct measure of clustering performance. 3) Kullback-Leibler
353   (KL) Divergence: This metric quantifies the difference between the local label distribution among the k-
354   nearest neighbors and the global label distribution, highlighting the extent to which local clusters deviate from
355   random chance Shlens [2014]. 4) Jensen-Shannon Divergence (JSD): This symmetric and bounded metric
356   evaluates the similarity between local and global label distributions, enhancing interpretability. It is widely
357   recognized for its effectiveness in quantifying clustering quality and information richness in embedding
358
      spaces Lin [1991].

      A.3   Q UALITY M ONITORING S YSTEM FOR AUGMENTATIONS
894
895   To ensure that the generated semantic augmentations align with the desired levels of semantic similarity, we
896   employ a robust quality monitoring system. This system leverages a fine-tuned BERT model as a discriminator
897   to validate the semantic relationships between base headlines and their augmentations. The primary objective
898   is to confirm that the augmentations adhere to the predefined similarity thresholds associated with each
899   augmentation action.
900
901   1. Similarity Score Validation The discriminator model evaluates the similarity between a base headline
902   and its augmented counterpart, producing a score in the range [0, 1]. These scores are compared against
903
      action-specific thresholds to classify the augmentations:
904
             • Reworded (Re): Similarity scores must fall within the range [0.66, 1.00], indicating high semantic
905            alignment with minimal alteration in meaning.
906
             • Semantically-Shifted (S): Scores between [0.33, 0.66] reflect moderate semantic divergence while
907
               maintaining topical relevance.
908
909          • Negated (N): Scores in [0, 0.33] denote significant semantic contrast or opposing meanings.
910
911   2. System Workflow The process begins by passing the base and augmented headlines through the fine-
912
      tuned BERT model, which computes similarity scores using cosine similarity of their embeddings. These
      scores are then compared against the specified thresholds. If an augmentation fails to meet the desired
913
      threshold for its action type, it is flagged for review or discarded.
914
915
      3. Feedback Mechanism To iteratively refine the augmentation process, the quality monitoring system
916
      provides feedback to the generation pipeline. For instance, if a significant portion of reworded augmentations
917   falls below the required threshold, the prompts for the augmentation model are adjusted, or additional
918   constraints are imposed during headline generation.
919
920   4. Ensuring Semantic Coherence The quality monitoring system serves a dual purpose: it enforces
921   the semantic coherence of augmentations and ensures that the resulting augmented dataset aligns with the
922   intended distribution of similarity scores. This guarantees that the augmented daily news sets (DNS) maintain
923   the desired variability and semantic relationships required for effective contrastive learning.
924   This monitoring system plays a critical role in maintaining the integrity of the augmentation pipeline, thereby
925   enhancing the reliability and utility of the ContraSim embedding space.

1055   C    H OW AUGMENTATION ACTIONS A FFECT N EWS H EADLINE S IMILARITY
1056
1057   In this section, we investigate the effects of different augmentation strategies on news headline similarity
1058   within embedding space. The goal of this experiment was to quantify how rephrasing, semantic shifts, and
1059   negations impact the embedding distances of news headlines.
1060   We began by selecting a dataset of daily news headlines, ensuring a diverse and representative sample of
1061   financial and general news topics. For each experiment:
1062
1063        1. Two random days were selected from the dataset.
1064
            2. A headline from one of these days was chosen as the base headline.
1065
1066        3. The chosen headline was subjected to one of the following augmentation actions using our algorithm:
1067               • Rephrasing (Re): Preserves the original semantic meaning but alters the phrasing.
1068
                   • Semantic Shift (S): Introduces slight changes in meaning while maintaining topic relevance.
1069
1070
                   • Negation (N): Alters the meaning to convey the opposite sentiment or direction.
1071        4. The base and augmented headlines were embedded into a semantic space using a pre-trained language
1072           model fine-tuned with Weighted Self-Supervised Contrastive Learning (WSSCL).
1073
            5. The change in embedding space distance was measured between the base and augmented headlines.
1074
1075   The average shifts in embedding distances, quantified as cosine similarity changes, were as follows:
1076
1077         • Rephrased: +0.146
1078
             • Semantic-Shifted: +0.043
1079
1080         • Negated: −0.0642

1081
       Rephrased Headlines: Rephrased headlines showed the largest positive shift in embedding distances
1082
       (+0.146), indicating that while the phrasing varied, the core semantic content remained highly aligned. This
1083   demonstrates that rephrasing maintains the essence of the original headline, making it the most semantically
1084   consistent transformation.
1085
1086   Semantic Shifts: Semantic-shifted headlines exhibited a moderate positive shift (+0.043). This suggests
1087   that while some semantic information diverged, the augmented headlines still retained a level of topical
1088   similarity to the base headline. The variability in these distances reflects the subtle nuances introduced by the
1089   algorithm.
1090
1091   Negated Headlines: Negated headlines displayed a negative shift (−0.0642), indicating an intentional
1092   movement away from the base headline’s meaning. This highlights the algorithm’s capacity to generate
1093   semantically contrasting headlines effectively. The relatively small magnitude of this shift suggests that
1094
       negation preserves certain structural or contextual elements, even when the semantic intent is inverted.
1095   The results underline the versatility and precision of our augmentation strategies:
1096
1097           • Rephrasing can be leveraged for tasks requiring high semantic consistency.
1098           • Semantic Shifting introduces controlled variability, useful for contrastive learning applications.
1099           • Negation is effective for generating challenging counterexamples in adversarial tasks or for enhanc-
1100             ing model robustness.

1103   utility of augmentation actions in crafting datasets for contrastive and supervised learning paradigms.

       D.2    B IG DATA 22 DATASET

1224   The BigData22 dataset is a comprehensive collection of financial tweets compiled between July 2019 and June
1225   2020, designed to analyze the correlation between social media sentiment and financial market movements.
1226   It includes over 272,000 tweets distributed across 7,164 distinct trading days. Each tweet is annotated with
1227   one of two market movement labels: Fall or Rise, based on the performance of financial indices on the
1228   corresponding day.
1229   BigData22 provides a unique perspective on market sentiment by focusing exclusively on social media
1230   platforms, contrasting with datasets like NIFTY-SFT that rely on curated headlines from reputable financial
1231   news outlets. While the reliance on social media introduces a higher degree of noise, it also brings diversity
1232   and real-time sentiment shifts into the dataset. This makes it an excellent benchmark for evaluating the
1233   robustness and adaptability of models like ContraSim, particularly in handling noisy, unstructured data with
1234   high variability.
1235   Additionally, the dataset includes metadata such as timestamps and tweet authors, allowing researchers to
1236   explore temporal trends and user-specific sentiment biases. This temporal richness is especially valuable
1237   for studying dynamic sentiment patterns in financial contexts and assessing model performance in capturing
1238   short-term fluctuations influenced by social media activity.
1239
1240   D.3    IMDB R EVIEWS DATASET
1241
1242   The IMDB Reviews dataset is a widely used benchmark for sentiment analysis tasks, comprising 50,000
1243   movie reviews, each accompanied by a sentiment score ranging from 0 to 10. The reviews are equally divided
1244
       into training and testing sets, ensuring a balanced evaluation of model performance. For this project, the
       sentiment scores are grouped into three categories: Low (0.0-5.5), Medium (5.6-7.5), and High (7.6-10.0),
1245
       creating a classification task to predict the overall sentiment of a review.
1246
1247   This dataset is particularly valuable for testing the generalizability of ContraSim beyond financial forecasting.
1248   Unlike financial datasets, which often feature structured news or market data, the IMDB dataset focuses
1249   on user-generated content with a wide range of writing styles and subjective expressions. By evaluating
1250
       ContraSim on this dataset, we can assess its ability to adapt to orthogonal tasks, such as opinion mining and
       sentiment classification, that require capturing nuanced semantic relationships in text.
1251
1252   The diversity in review content, ranging from casual remarks to in-depth critiques, challenges the model to
1253   effectively distinguish between sentiment categories. This provides insights into ContraSim’s capability to
1254   learn and represent global semantic structures across domains, making it a valuable tool for applications
1255   extending beyond finance, such as media analytics and customer feedback analysis.
