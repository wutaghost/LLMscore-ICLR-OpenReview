011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Consistency models, a new class of one-step generative models, have shown state-
014            of-the-art performance in one-step generation and achieve competitive performance
015            compared to multi-step diffusion models. The most challenging part of consistency
016            models is the training process, which discretizes the diffusion process and trains a
017            consistency function to map any point at any discretized timepoint of the diffusion
018            process to the data distribution. Despite the empirical success, only a few works
019            focus on the discretization complexity of consistency models. However, the setting
020
               of those works is far away from the empirical consistency models with good
               performance, suffers from large discretization complexity, and fails to explain the
021
               empirical success of consistency models. To bridge the gap between theory and
022
               application, we analyze consistency models with two key properties: (1) variance
023            exploding forward process and (2) gradually decay discretization stepsize, which
024            are both widely used in empirical consistency models. Under the above realistic
025            setting, we make the first step to explain the empirical success of consistency
026            models and achieve the state-of-the-art discretization complexity for consistency
027            models, which is competitive with the results of diffusion models. After obtaining
028            the results of the one-step sampling method of consistency models, we further
029            analyze a multi-step consistency sampling algorithm proposed by Song et al. (2023)
030            and show that this algorithm improves the discretization complexity compared with
031
               one-step generation, which matches the empirical observation.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036
037   Recently, diffusion models have shown impressive performance in different areas such as image
038   generation and video generation (Rombach et al., 2022; Esser et al., 2024; Ho et al., 2022). The
039
      mathematical mechanism of diffusion models is made up of two processes: the forward process and
      the reverse process. The forward process gradually injects noise into data till the marginal distribution
040
      is close to pure noise. The reverse process is an iterative sampling process, which sequentially
041
      removes noise from data to generate clean samples. At each denoised step, diffusion models only
042   need to predict and remove a small noise, making the training process more stable than Generative
043   Adversarial Networks (GAN) (Goodfellow et al., 2014). However, the iterative sampling process
044   indicates diffusion models need to evaluate a large neural network to predict the noise at this step,
045   leading to a higher computational cost than other one-step algorithms such as GAN, Variational
046   Auto-Encoder (Kingma and Welling, 2013), and Normalizing Flow (Papamakarios et al., 2021).
047
      To solve the computational issue, a series of works try to accelerate the sampling process of diffusion
048   models (Song et al., 2020; Bao et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2023). One notable algorithm in this
049   series work is the Consistency Model (Song et al., 2023), which tries to find a mapping function
050   (a.k.a. consistency function) to directly map any points at any time of the forward process to the
051   data distribution. Consistency models have shown state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance compared to
052   other one-step generative models in image generation (Song et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2023), video
053   generation (Wang et al., 2023) and music generation (Fei et al., 2024). Furthermore, it is also widely
      used in other areas, such as reinforcement learning (Ding and Jin, 2023).

054
      To obtain the consistency function, consistency models discretize the timeline into K discretization
055   points and hope the consistency function outputs similar results at two adjacent discretization points in
056   the training phase. When the two adjacent points are far away, it is hard to train a consistency function
057   with great performance, which indicates K can not be too small. However, it is also necessary to
058   avoid a large K since it will make the training phase time-consuming. Hence, one core of consistency
059   models training phase is the choice of the discretization number K, which is helpul in effective
060   training a consistency function with great performance.
061   Despite the empirical success of consistency models, no existing works explain why consistency
062   models achieve comparable performance to diffusion models. Though some impressive works analyze
063   the discretization complexity of consistency models (Lyu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Dou et al.), the
064   setting is far away from the consistency models with great performance in application:
065
066          • The forward process. Previous theoretical works choose the variance preserving forward
067            process (VPSDE) to inject noise in the training phase. On the contrary, empirical consis-
068
               tency models adopt the variance exploding forward process (VESDE) with a specific noise
               schedule whose solution trajectory is linear. As discussed in Karras et al. (2022) and Liu
069
               et al. (2022), a linear trajectory indicates it is possible to generate clean samples with a
070
               single Euler step and is the basis of a one-step model.
071
             • The discretization scheme. The discretization scheme of consistency models is the EDM
072
               scheme (Eq. (5)), which first uses a large stepsize and gradually reduces the stepsize.
073
               However, Lyu et al. (2024) and Dou et al. use a uniform stepsize, and Li et al. (2024) use a
074            scheme that relies heavily on the VPSDE (Detail in Appendix C.1).
075
076   Due to the mismatch between the current theoretical and empirical setting, the discretization com-
077   plexity results of current theoretical works are significantly worse than diffusion models (Table 1).
078   Hence, the following natural problem remains open:
079   Under a realistic setting with great empirical performance, is it possible to achieve complexity
080   comparable to the state-of-the-art diffusion models and explain the success of consistency models?

155   Since the mathematical mechanism of consistency models is close to diffusion models, we discuss the
156   discretization complexity of diffusion and consistency models. For diffusion models, we summarize
157   the results for reverse PFODE due to the deterministic sample process of consistency models.
158   Diffusion models with reverse PFODE. With strong assumptions or additional components, a series
159   of works achieve polynomial complexity for reverse PFODE (Li et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023c; Gao
160   and Zhu, 2024). More specifically, Li et al. (2023) assume an accurate enough Jacobian matrix, and
161   Gao and Zhu (2024) assume the target data distribution is log-concave. We note that the log-concave
      assumption on data distribution is much stronger than our bounded support assumption since it

      precludes the existence of multi-modal real-world data. Furthermore, under log-concave distribution,
163   the ground truth score function ∇ log qT −t (·) does not blow-up, which can not explain the blow-up
164   phenomenon of score function 2 and ignore the influence of early stopping parameter δ (Table 1).
165   Without any strong assumption, Chen et al. (2023c) introduce a predictor-corrector algorithm, which

      Consistency models. Lyu et al. (2024) and Li et al. (2024) analyze the discretization complexity of

517
518
      In this work, we make the first step to explain why consistency models perform well from a theoretical
      perspective. More specifically, we bridge the gap between theory and application by analyzing the
519
      discretization complexity of the consistency model with the VESDE forward process and EDM
520
      discretization scheme. Under this realistic setting with great empirical performance, we first achieve
521                                                           4+ 3
522
      the state-of-the-art discretization complexity O 1/ϵW2 2a by making full use of time-dependent
523   Lipschitz constant. Then, we analyze the improved multi-step sampling algorithm     proposed   by Song
                                                                                                 5
                                                                                             4+ 4a
524   et al. (2023) and show that this algorithm improves discretization complexity O 1/ϵW2         . Finally,
525   to explore the theoretical boundary of consistency models,     we further analyze consistency models

526   with exponential decay stepsize and achieve O 1/ϵ4W2 complexity.
527
528
      In conclusion, we achieve competitive results for consistency models compared with diffusion models
      under the realistic setting, which shows the potential of consistency models.
529
530
      Future work and limitation. In this work, we directly assume the approximated score and con-
531   sistency function are accurate enough. For the approximated score function, some works analyze
532   its learning process (Chen et al., 2023b; Yuan et al., 2023). For the consistency function, Dou et al.
533   analyze its estimation error. However, as discussed in Section 3.2, they run multi-step PFODE instead
534   of one-step PFODE, which does not match the empirical operation and is time-consuming. Hence,
      it is interesting to analyze the learning process of consistency models under a realistic setting and
535
      achieve an end-to-end analysis.
