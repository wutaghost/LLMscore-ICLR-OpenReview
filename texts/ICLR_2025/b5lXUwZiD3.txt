009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper investigate the capability of transformer in learning a fundamental
012            sequential model — the Hidden Markov Model (HMM). We design various types
013            of HMM examples and variants inspired by theory, and conduct extensive experi-
014            ments testing and comparing the performance of both transformers and Recurrent
015            Neural Networks (RNNs). Our experiments reveal three important findings: (1)
016            Transformers can effectively learn a large number of HMMs, but this require the
017            depth of transformers to be at least logarithmic in the sequence length; (2) There
018
               are challenging HMMs where Transformers struggle to learn, while RNNs succeed.
               We also consistently observe that Transformers underperform RNNs in both train-
019
               ing speed and testing accuracy across all tested HMM models. (3) Long mixing
020
               times and the lack of access to intermediate latent states significantly degrade
021            Transformer’s performance, but has much less impact on RNNs’ performance. To
022            address the limitation of transformers in modeling HMMs, we demonstrate that a
023            variant of the Chain-of-Thought (CoT), called block CoT in the training phase, can
024            help transformers to reduce the evaluation error and to learn longer sequences at a
025            cost of increasing the training time. Finally, we complement our empirical findings
026            by theoretical results proving the expressiveness of transformers in approximating
027            HMMs with logarithmic depth.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032
      Transformer-based architectures (Vaswani et al., 2017) have demonstrated exceptional capabilities in
      tackling sequential modeling tasks across diverse domains, including natural language processing
033
      (Brown et al., 2020), computer vision (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020), robotics (Brohan et al., 2023),
034
      reinforcement learning (Janner et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2022), etc. Despite their widespread success,
035   the effectiveness of Transformers in learning basic sequential models, such as the Hidden Markov
036   Model (HMM), remains unclear. Investigating this question is crucial for understanding the strengths
037   and limitations of Transformers, especially considering that HMMs are arguably among the simplest
038   yet fundamental tools for modeling natural language (Merialdo, 1994; Vogel et al., 1996; Chiu &
039   Rush, 2020) and time series from applications ranging from control systems (Franklin et al., 2002) to
040   robotics (Doucet et al., 2009).
041   Furthermore, HMMs bear close relation to the widely adopted Partially Observable Markov Decision
042   Process (POMDP) framework in reinforcement learning (e.g. Hausknecht & Stone, 2015; Rashid
043   et al., 2020), as an HMM can be regarded as a simplification of POMDP, which has no action-input
044   control. To this end, this paper investigates the following fundamental questions through extensive
045   empirical experiments and theoretical analysis:
046
047         1. Can Transformer effectively learn HMM models and their variants?
048         2. How does its performance compare to that of Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) in terms of
049            training speed, hyperparameter tuning difficulty, and final accuracy?
050         3. Furthermore, when presented with an HMM sequence of a specific length, how many
051            attention layers are required to achieve a desired level of accuracy?
052
053   We are particularly interested in the last question due to the pivotal advantage of Transformers over
      RNNs in long sequence modeling: the depth of the computation graph in Transformers scales linearly


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      with the number of layers and remains (almost) independent of the sequence length, whereas that
055   of RNNs scales linearly with both. It is vital to verify whether such advantage indeed exists in long
056   sequence modeling tasks such as HMMs.
057
058
      In this paper, we primarily focus on two fundamental tasks associated with HMMs: next-observation
      prediction and belief inference (Rabiner & Juang, 1986). Next-observation prediction involves
059
      predicting the next observation based on all preceding ones, while belief inference aims to deduce the
060
      distribution of the hidden states from previous observations. Below, we present an overview of our
061   findings concerning the three aforementioned questions.
062
063         1. Transformers effectively learn to perform belief inference across all tested HMMs when
064            the training dataset includes true beliefs at each time step. However, in the task of next-
065            observation prediction, certain challenging HMM instances exist where Transformers strug-
066            gle to achieve low prediction loss.
067         2. In comparison, RNNs demonstrate the capability to successfully tackle all tasks across the
068            tested HMMs at a faster convergence speed, yielding lower testing error. Notably, RNN
069            training exhibits greater robustness compared to Transformers, particularly in the realms of
070            hyperparameter tuning and curriculum scheduling.
071         3. Our experiments reveal distinct patterns in the relationship between sequence length and
072            the minimal depth required for Transformers to learn effectively. These patterns can be
073            categorized into three groups:
074               • Hard instances: There exists challenging HMM instances where Transformers struggle
075                 to learn, even for constant sequence length. These instances require further investigation
076                 to identify the underlying reasons for the learning difficulties.
077               • Logarithmic scaling: For more complex sequential models such as structured HMMs,
078                 we observe an approximate logarithmic dependency between the minimal depth re-
079                 quired and the sequence length. This relationship holds for various structured HMM
080                 instances, as corroborated by both theory and experiments.
081               • Constant depth: For simple sequential models such as random HMM and linear
082                 dynamical system, a constant depth, independent of sequence length, is sufficient for
083                 Transformers to learn accurately.
084         4. Motivated by the hard instances, we identified two key challenging regimes of HMMs for
085            Transformers: long mixing time and the lack of intermediate supervision signals during
086            training. The mixing time measures how many latest observations are required to predict
087            the next one (c.f. Section A.2), and we analyzed Transformer performance on HMMs with
088            varying mixing times to assess the impact of mixing time. Intermediate belief states are
089            provided for training in the belief state inference task, while only observation sequences
090
               are available in next-observation prediction task, which significantly hampers Transformer
               performance. Nevertheless, these factors have very little impact on RNN.
091
092
      In order to address the limitations of Transformers in learning HMMs, we employ a variant of the
093
      Chain-of-Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022) prompting in the training phase called block CoT. Block
094   CoT feeds the output of the Transformer back to itself as input every b tokens, which reduces to the
095   standard CoT when b = 1. Our findings show that block CoT significantly decreases evaluation error
096   and enhances the sequence length that shallow Transformers can handle.
097
      Finally, we also complement our empirical findings by theoretical results, which proves the scaling
098
      between the sequence length and minimal depth from the perspective of expressiveness power.
099
      Specifically, it is proved that an L-layer finite precision Transformer is able to fit any HMMs of at
100   least 2L sequence length.

      1.1   R ELATED WORK
103
104   Our work can be viewed as part of a broader effort to assess the ability of Transformer models on
105   simple, basic and well-defined tasks with synthetic data. Such an approach is advantageous because
106   it allows us to precisely evaluate the Transformer’s capabilities in a particular aspect, as we have
107   access to the ground truth model that generates the training data. Below, we highlight some related
      works along this direction.

108
      Recently, a line of works (e.g., Garg et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2023; Bhattamishra et al., 2023;
109   Von Oswald et al., 2023) have studied training Transformer models for in-context learning of
110   regression tasks (e.g., linear regressions) utilizing synthetic datasets. A notable distinction between
111   in-context learning and learning HMMs lies in the data sequential nature. In in-context learning, all
112   tokens within a data sequence are independently sampled from the same data distribution. Conversely,
113   in HMMs, tokens are recursively generated, with each token strongly influenced by the preceding
114   ones, establishing a mutual dependency among them.
115   Previous studies have also explored the capability of Transformer models to learn elementary algo-
116   rithms, such as formal language transduction (Delétang et al., 2022), arithmetic calculation (Dziri
117   et al., 2023), recognizing formal languages (Bhattamishra et al., 2020), sorting (Zhou et al., 2023) and
118   learning semi-automata (Liu et al., 2022). These problems, as noted in (Liu et al., 2022), can all be
119   considered special cases of learning the state sequence of finite-state deterministic automata, which is
120   in turn special cases of of HMMs as noted in Appendix A.3. In comparison, our work focuses on
121   training Transformer models to learn both the state and observation sequence of stochastic HMMs,
122   which is a special case of more general stochastic POMDP but without input control. By focusing on
123
      stochastic HMMs, our work aims to contribute to the understanding of how Transformer models can
      learn and generalize from sequential data in the presence of intrinsic uncertainty.
124
125   In the belief state inference task, we provide true belief state at each step as intermediate supervision
126   signal for the networks, while only the next observation is provided in the other task. The difference
127   between these two tasks is very similar to training with CoT or without CoT—the belief states can be
128   regarded as intermediate CoT signals. It is observed in previous papers that CoT greatly improves the
129
      performance of LLMs on multi-step reasoning tasks such as text generation (Wei et al., 2022; Wang
      et al., 2022), code generation (Li et al., 2023), multi-step computations (Nye et al., 2021), and math
130
      induction (Shao et al., 2024). There are also theoretical works Feng et al. (2023); Li et al. (2024)
131
      showing the gap of expressive power of Transformers with and without CoT.

      2.1   S EQUENTIAL M ODELS
141
142
      An HMM can be formulated by a tuple (S, O, P, O, S0 ), where S is the state space, O is the
143
      observation space, P(s′ | s) is the transition probability of transitioning to state s′ from state
144
      s, O(o | s) is the probability of emitting observation o from state s, and S0 is the initial state.
145   By interacting with the HMM for T steps, one can obtain a trajectory (i.e., a sequence of states
146   and observations) (s0 = S0 , o0 , ..., sT , oT ), where ot is sampled from distribution O(· | st ) and
147   unobserved. st+1 is sampled from P(· | st ). We are particularly interested in two basic tasks for
148   learning an HMM in this paper, which are also known as two of the three key problems of HMMs
149   (Rabiner & Juang, 1986):
150
151          • Next-Observation prediction: A fundamental task is to predict the distributions of the next
152            observation given the history of observations: Pr(ot+1 | o1 , ..., ot ).
153
154          • Belief state inference: Assuming the size of the state space is n (i.e., S = [n] w.l.o.g.)
155            for a given HMM, belief state inference aims at computing the belief state bt ∈ Rn =
156            Pr(st | o1 , o2 , ..., ot ) at step t given an observation sequence (o1 , o2 , ..., ot ). We provide bt
157            as supervision signal for each step t in the training, but the network cannot use it as input
158
               since the transition is unknown.
159
160   Throughout the paper, we use n to denote the size of the state space S if it is finite, or the dimension
161   of S if it is a Euclidean space. The size of the observation space is always finite, which we denote by
      m.

188   3     M ODEL
189
190   In this section, we introduce the HMMs and their variant models explored in this paper, broadly
191   classified into two categories: fast-mixing models and slow-mixing or non-mixing structured models.
192   The mixing speed characterizes the “effective length” of past histories that influence the current
193   belief state. For instance, in a fast-mixing model, the belief state at the current step is essentially
194   influenced only by a few of the most recent observations, making them more amenable to fitting by
195   neural networks. The motivation for studying these HMMs is discussed in Appendix A.5.
196
197   3.1   FAST-M IXING HMM S
198
      RanHMM: Random HMM. The initial set of sequential models under consideration comprises
199
      random HMM instances with randomly initialized transition and emission probabilities. Our primary
200   focus is on the belief state inference problem within these random HMMs. This choice is motivated
201   by the observation that, as per (A.4), next-observation prediction is a relatively simpler task compared
202   to belief state inference when dealing with random HMMs.
203
204   RanLDS: Random Linear Dynamical System. A linear dynamical system is given by the fol-
205   lowing equation xt+1 = Axt + ζt , yt = Bxt + ξt , where xt ∈ Rn is the (hidden) state at step t,
206   yt ∈ Rm is the observation at step t, and ζt , ξt are independent random noises. It can be regarded as a
207   continuous HMM with linear transition and emission. We choose A, B as random orthogonal matrices
208   with n = m and ζt , ξt standard Gaussian noises for simplicity. It’s worth noting that predicting
209   the belief state and the next observation is equivalent in this context, given that B is orthogonal.
210
      Therefore, our focus lies on next-observation prediction, distinguishing it from the RanHMM model.
211   The mixing time for all HMM models are summarized in Table 1.

290
291   We systematically conducted experiments to assess the learnability of RNNs and Transformers across
292   various sequential models introduced in Section 3. The results consistently highlight the superiority
293   of RNNs over Transformers in terms of both convergence speed and evaluation accuracy across all
294   tasks. Besides, to delve deeper into the efficiency of Transformers with varying depths, we illustrate
295
      an approximate scaling relationship between sequence length and required depth in Figure 3.
296   The HMM models exhibit distinct patterns in terms of scaling. For belief state inference, fast-mixing
297   models demonstrate compatibility with constant-depth Transformers, indicating ease of learnability
298   of these models. The scaling of structured HMMs for the belief state inference task is constrained to
299   at most log T for a specific sequence length T . The most challenging task lies in predicting the next

      sequence of constant length.

348   4.2   C URRICULUM T RAINING
349
      Given that fitting long sequences of length T directly from scratch might pose challenges for
350
      Transformers, curriculum learning emerges as a widely adopted strategy to expedite and stabilize
351
      training (Spitkovsky et al., 2010; Wang et al., 2021; Garg et al., 2022). Curriculum learning involves
352   dividing the training dataset into different subsets (curriculum stages) based on the measure of
353   difficulty, and regularly switching the training data subset from simple ones to challenging ones.
354
355
      In HMM models, a natural difficulty measure is the length of the training sequences (Spitkovsky
      et al., 2010; Garg et al., 2022). Following this curriculum design, we regularly increase the training
356
      sequence length until it reaches T . Motivated by the theoretical insight that an L-layer Transformer
357
      has a fit length of at least 2L for HMMs (c.f. Section 5.2), we adopt a doubling curriculum schedule.
358   Commencing from length 2L , this curriculum schedule doubles the length of the training sequence
359   after a fixed number of epochs. The total number of curriculum stages is set to 8 − L. The 7-layer
360   Transformer is supposed to have only one stage at T = 120, the 6-layer Transformer has two stages
361   at length 64 and 120, etc.

464   5     T HE E XPRESSIVENESS P OWER OF RNN AND T RANSFORMER
465
466   In order to understand the experimental results (e.g., the strong inductive bias of RNN, the logarithmic
467   scaling of the fit length of Transformers, the hardness of Cyclic-HARD model, etc.), we ask the
468   question whether there exists an RNN or Transformer that can produce (or express) these sequences
469   (e.g., the belief state or observation distributions) in a theoretical perspective. This question has
470   already been answered in general since it is known the RNN and Transformer can approximate a
471
      large number of sequence-to-sequence models (Schäfer & Zimmermann, 2006; Yun et al., 2019)
      given sufficient depth or width. In contrast, our interest lies in the representation efficiency of the
472
      neural networks to approximate the sequential models. This prompts the question of how large the
473
      neural networks should be to effectively approximate them. The proofs of all the theorems in this
474   section can be found in Appendix E.

488   To avoid the unrealistic assumption that the neurons in Transformers have infinite precision (Dehghani
489   et al., 2018; Pérez et al., 2019), we require the neurons to be floating-point numbers of finite precision

491   round-down, round-to-the-nearest) to a floating-point number as in a classical computer. To be
492   specific, if we restrict the floating-point to have O(log T ) bits, it can represent all real numbers of
493   magnitude O(poly(T )) with a O(poly(1/T )) rounding error. After carefully analyzing the error
494

499

530
531   We study the effectiveness of Transformers in learning HMM models and its variants from both
532   theoretical and empirical perspective. Structured HMM models with different mixing speed are
533   constructed to assess the accuracy of belief state inference or next-observation prediction by Trans-
534
      formers. We found a consistent underperformance of Transformers compared with RNN on all
      HMMs, and even challenging HMMs that Transformers fail to learn but RNN can successfully fit.
535
      Intuitively speaking, successful learning requires the HMM model to have either fast mixing speed
536
      or sufficient supervision signal during the learning process. We also illustrated an approximate
537   logarithmic dependency between depth and fit length from both experiments and theory and tried the
538   block CoT technique to overcome the limitations.

807   A.5    M OTIVATIONS FOR THE C ONSTRUCTED HMM S
808
809   We also dicuss our motivation for studying the HMMs and why we construct the HMMs in the main
      text.


                                                           15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      Why studying HMM models? First, the investigation into the learnability of Transformers on
811   various HMMs holds intrinsic value due to the universality of HMM models. This is pointed out in
812   the introduction as the HMMs serve as useful tools for a wide range of practical problems such as
813   part-of-speech (Kupiec, 1992) and named-entity recognition (Zhou & Su, 2002) in NLP, time-series
814   forecasting (Zhang et al., 2019).
815
      Furthermore, many real-world problems in control systems and reinforcement learning can be
816
      abstracted into HMMs as their simplest instances. Understanding the capabilities and limitations of
817   Transformers in learning these models provides crucial insights that extend beyond HMMs themselves.
818   For instance, the partially observable Markov decision process (POMDP) model, which extends
819   HMMs by incorporating actions at each step, is a cornerstone in reinforcement learning. POMDPs are
820   typically used to model plenty of complex sequential decision-making tasks such as robot navigation,
821   fault detection, video game AI, etc. By investigating how Transformers perform on HMMs, we pave
822   the way for understanding their efficacy in tackling more complex problems like POMDPs (since
823   the HMMs are special cases of POMDPs). This is an important problem given the abundance of
824   research efforts aimed at devising efficient reinforcement learning algorithms for learning POMDPs
825
      and their applications in various domains (see, e..g, Nguyen et al. (2020) for a survey of methods,
      and Cassandra (1998) for a survey of applications).
826
827
828   Why constructing the specific HMMs in the main text? The HMMs can be divided into two
829   types: random instances and structured instances. The RanHMM and RanLDS are random instances
830   chosen as they represent a natural starting point for exploring Transformers’ learning capabilities on
831
      HMMs. Notably, the successful learning of these random instances by constant-layer Transformers
      suggests that HMMs lacking specific structures are relatively easy for Transformers to learn, which is
832
      because random instances have a very small mixing time (c.f. Appendix A.2).
833
834   It then inspires us to study how the mixing time of the HMM related to the performance of the
835   Transformers. Therefore, we construct the aperiodic HMM instance Cyclic-DET , which requires
836   the Transformers to consider all previous tokens to predict the next token, instead of only checking the
837
      latest ones as in the random instances (i.e., it has a long credit assignment length). The Cyclic-RND
      model further enables us to adjust the mixing time of the HMMs and verify the scaling between
838
      mixing time and the performance of HMMs.
839
840   In order to study the difference between belief state inference and next observation prediction tasks
841   on non-mixing models, we constructed the Cyclic-HARD model. The core difference between
842   Cyclic-HARD model and Cyclic-DET model is that belief state inference provides intermediate
843
      belief state as supervision signal while next observation prediction does not. The results show that it
      is crucial for the Transformers to have intermediate supervision signals.
844
845   Lastly, we also construct a generalization of HMMs for the application in physics or math–the
846   MatMul model. The results align well with the HMMs.

853   According to Figure 1, the core design of the Cyclic-DET is a 4-state MDP M with 2 actions (Left
854   of Figure 1). This MDP M can be transformed into an HMM H with 8 states and 2 observations
855   according to Proposition A.1. The states of H are {(si , ak )|i ∈ {1, 2, 3, 4}, k ∈ {1, 2}}, where si
856   are the states of M and ak are the actions of M. With a little abuse of notation, we denote the
857   observation space of H to be {a1 , a2 }, the same as the action space of M.
858   Denote the initial belief state of H by b0 , where b0 ((s1 , a1 )) = b0 ((s1 , a2 )) = 1/2. Since the hidden
859   state (si , ak ) always emits the observation ak , the first observation o0 emitted by the initial state
860   can be a1 , a2 with equal probability. Assume o0 = a1 (resp. o0 = a2 ), the next state in H must
861   be (s2 , a1 ) or (s2 , a2 ) (resp. (s3 , a1 ) or (s3 , a2 )) according to the belief state update formula (A.3).
862   The probability of these two states are both equal to 1/2, and the emission probability of o1 is also
863   a uniform distribution over {a1 , a2 }. Following this procedure, it is easy to observe that the state
      distribution of M is always equal to the marginal distribution of si over states of H.
