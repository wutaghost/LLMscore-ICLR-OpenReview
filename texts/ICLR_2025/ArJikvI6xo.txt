009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Federated Learning (FL), as a privacy-preserving distributed machine learning
012            paradigm, faces significant challenges in terms of data and device heterogeneity
013            in practical applications. In this paper, we present a novel Large Language Model
014            Agent decision system, called Green Federated Learning Agent (GFLAgent), for
015            alleviating the challenges arising from data and device heterogeneity within the
016
               FL tasks. GFLAgent is efficient and energy friendly, and meets the requirements
               of green computing. GFLAgent dynamically monitors the status of each client,
017
               selects and reasonably allocates them to different layers to achieve efficient asyn-
018
               chronous training, and responds to unexpected situations during training. Further-
019            more, to optimize overall system expenditure, we implement a strategy that min-
020            imizes local training overhead and the updates costs for clients with historically
021            subpar performance. The experimental results show that GFLAgent outperforms
022            SOTA methods and can be quickly ported to other distributed machine learning
023            frameworks to improve efficiency.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Distributed machine learning has improved the efficiency of artificial intelligence model training, but
028
      it has also exposed the issue of customer data privacy. Participants from different institutions tend not
029
      to transmit private data when participating in training tasks. For this reason, Federated Learning (FL)
030   McMahan et al. (2017)is proposed. FL is crafted to aggregate local model updates from distributed
031   devices without centralizing data, thereby safeguarding privacy and ensuring robust model training
032   through iterative global refinement. Nonetheless, FL can incur significant computational, network,
033   and performance expenses during the training phase. This trade-off may stem from the inherent
034   architectural features Lo et al. (2022) of FL and the challenges posed by statistical heterogeneity
035   Luo et al. (2022) across the distributed datasets.
036   The key challenge in FL is statistical heterogeneity, where data is frequently imbalanced and non-
037   identically independently distributed (non-IID) Zhao et al. (2018). Thus, the indiscriminate training
038   of all clients and the subsequent model updates, without considering the distribution and quality
039   of data, can degrade model performance. Given these challenges, a pertinent question arises: is it
040   possible to devise a model that selects clients in a more scientific and effective manner for each
041   training round? This question is central to the evolving field of client selection algorithms in FL.
042   There have been many attempts to address heterogeneity issues. In the early days, many studies
043   emphasized the issue of lagging behind, which was caused by uneven distribution of computational
044   data and computing power. To solve this problem, Tier-based Federated Learning System (TiFL)
045   Chai et al. (2020a) utilize a multi-layer mechanism to mitigate the impact of stragglers. However, it
046   calls for well-designed layer to avoid excessive loss of model information. Besides, it may also have
047   a bias problem that tends to choose faster layers. Federated learning method with Asynchronous
048   Tiers (FedAT) Chai et al. (2020b) has taken a step further by adopting a novel weighted aggregation
049   heuristic algorithm, assigning higher weights to slower layers to prevent the preferences.
050   Nevertheless, all these traditional scheduling optimization requires engineers to spend a lot of time
051   to design the weights and optimize models. The Large Language Models (LLM) have shown the
052   potential in improving current work Zhao et al. (2023) since it has experienced explosive growth in
053   the past two years and is proven to make amazing progress in generative tasks. Based on LLMs, we
      use LLM-based agent Wang et al. (2024) to improve automatic logical reasoning.

      2.1   F EDERATED L EARNING
087
      In recent times, Federated Learning (FL) has emerged to help protect information security by en-
088
      abling model training without the need for central data storage, whose protocol requires the selected
089   clients to update their model using local data, while asking the server to aggregate updates from the
090   clients to make the model better Nishio & Yonetani (2019).
091
092
      Several classic FL algorithms, including Federated Averaging (FedAvg) McMahan et al. (2017),
      FedProx Tian et al. (2018), and Stochastic Controlled Averaging for Federated Learning (SCAF-
093
      FOLD) Karimireddy et al. (2020), have been introduced to enhance the quality of FL. FedAvg
094
      leverages local stochastic gradient descent (SGD) Goodfellow et al. (2016) on each client, with a
095   server performing model averaging. This method is robust against unbalanced and non-IID data
096   distributions and effectively reduces the number of communication roundsMcMahan et al. (2017).
097
098
      FedProx expands on FedAvg by tackling statistical heterogeneity among clients, thus enhancing
      convergence behavior in realistic, heterogeneous networks McMahan et al. (2017). Algorithms like
099
      SCAFFOLD further optimize FedAvg by employing control variates to counteract client-drift in
100
      updates, a strategy known as variance reduction Karimireddy et al. (2020).
101
102   Given that each client may handle varying amounts of data, this discrepancy can affect subsequent
103   communications and the quality of the updated model Nishio & Yonetani (2019). Moreover, these
104
      variations can profoundly influence model accuracy, convergence rate, and fairness across clients Fu
      et al. (2023).
105
106   Addressing both system heterogeneity (variations in hardware configurations among clients) and
107   statistical heterogeneity (differences in data distributions among clients) is essential to overcoming
      similar challenges in FL McMahan et al. (2017). Client sampling and selection are pivotal in solv-

                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      ing these problems. Here are two main strategies: client classification for improved asynchronous
109   training and bolstering communication efficiency between clients and servers.
110
111   Asynchronous training is a response to the delays caused by stragglers. Extensive research Stich
112
      (2018) Li et al. (2019b) Karimireddy et al. (2020) Yang et al. (2021) has concentrated on reducing
      their impact in distributed networks to avoid performance deterioration. However, the outright ex-
113
      clusion of stragglers might result in the loss of critical data needed for model enhancement. Hybrid
114
      Federated Learning (HFL) Truex et al. (2019), which incorporates techniques such as Asynchronous
115   Decentralized SGD (AD-SGD) Lian et al. (2018), mitigates this by integrating delayed local updates
116   into the central model Li et al. (2021).
117
118
      An alternative approach to managing straggler clients involves a synchronous intra-tier model up-
      dating strategy. Yet, this method may induce biases due to its favoring certain clients. In contrast,
119
      Federated learning method with Asynchronous Tiers (FedAT) improves upon Tier-based Federated
120
      Learning System (TiFL) by combining synchronous intra-tier training with asynchronous cross-tier
121   training, effectively reducing the straggler effect and enhancing both convergence speed and test
122   accuracy.
123
124
      Another pioneering method focuses on minimizing communication frequency in FL by selectively
      choosing clients based on model update thresholdsRibero & Vikalo (2020). This strategy, inspired
125
      by Ornstein-Uhlenbeck processes and SGD Mandt et al. (2016), ensures that only updates that sur-
126
      pass a certain significance are conveyed to the server, thereby streamlining communication effi-
127   ciency.
128
129   We have also seen some other advanced models. FedBalancer Shin et al. (2022) is a case in point,
130
      which exemplifies a notable model that refines time-to-accuracy performance by harmonizing client-
      server interactions. It selects samples with substantial statistical utility and dynamically forecasts
131
      optimal deadlines for each training round, contingent on the variability of client training data, which
132
      in turn optimizes the learning trajectory.
133
134   These advancements underscore the ongoing efforts to refine FL methodologies, balancing the com-
135   plexities of client heterogeneity with the need for efficient and effective model training across dis-
136
      tributed environments.

139   2.2   L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS AGENTS
140
141   The rise of large language models (LLMs) Radford et al. (2019) has been a cross-age change in AI,
142   with GPT-3 Dale (2021) from OpenAI being a standout example. These models use a transformer
143   architecture and are trained on huge amounts of text, making them great at producing text that feels
144   like it was written by a person Kasneci et al. (2023). However, scaling up model size alone has not
145   proved effective for achieving greater performance on tasks such as arithmetic, commonsense, and
146
      symbolic reasoning Rae et al. (2021).
147   To help LLMs handle complex reasoning, researchers have come up with new ideas like ”Chain
148   of Thought prompting” (CoT) and ”Tree of Thought” (ToT). Unlike expensive rationale-augmented
149   trainingLing et al. (2017) and fine-tuning methodsCobbe et al. (2021), CoT Wei et al. (2022) lever-
150   ages the <input, chain of thought, output>template, enabling LLMs to perform few-shot prompting
151   for reasoning tasks efficiently. While ToT Yao et al. (2024) allows LLMs to self-assess and choose
152
      between advancing or backtracking along different reasoning paths during global decision-making.
      Furthermore,Graph of Thought (GoT) Besta et al. (2024) generalizes CoT and ToT to more intricate
153
      thought patterns, enhancing reasoning without the need for model updates.
154
155   The progression to LLM agents signifies a transformative leap in AI’s capability for complex rea-
156   soning and task execution. LLMs can be applied to more than just conversation-based language
157   tasks. Researchers are now looking at how LLM agents can be used in real-life situations to solve
158
      complex problems. For example, they are working on improving reasoning in games like Werewolf
      Xu et al. (2023) and in AI environments like Smallville Park et al. (2023), as well as developing new
159
      ways for agents to think and make decisions.
160
161   By applying the strengths of LLMs and techniques like CoT and ToT, we aim to make progress in
      how LLM-based agents tackle real-world challenges.

359   In contrast to traditional methods, which often rely on mathematical techniques to meticulously
360   craft evaluation functions or rules, our approach involves scoring the performance of all clients after
361   a comprehensive run using these functions. The selection process is refined by setting thresholds
362
      or selecting a certain percentage of clients for participation. Recognizing the potential loss of data
      or computational resources that may occur when some clients are discarded, we employ a random
363
      supplementation strategy to include those initially overlooked. For example, FedBalance incorpo-
364
      rates this strategy by merging selected and unselected data into a parallel data processing mode. To
365   transcend the limitations of manual configuration, we have integrated an LLM-based Agent as an
366   embedded decision-making tool to assess client participation and adjust the server’s overall hyper-
367   parameters dynamically, allowing for tailored adjustments to the training process at various stages.

      4.3.2    AGENT- BASED S CHEME .
379
380   In the past two years, there has been a proliferation of concepts and schemes based on Large Lan-
381   guage Models (LLMs) Agents. Agents endowed with tools, reflection, and memory have become the
382   mainstream design paradigm. In response to this, we have crafted an Agent optimized for federated
383   learning.
384
              • Memory: In our framework, memory is segmented into short-term and long-term facets.
385
                While short-term memory retains recent operations and dialogues, our primary focus lies
386             with long-term memory, which documents execution adjustments. The term ”other” indi-
387             cates a comprehensive archive of task records post-execution. Our method involves extract-
388             ing and analyzing these records to generate metadata that captures participant involvement,
389             client outcomes, temporal engagement, and accuracy. This dataset further explains client
390             selection patterns and the decision logic of LLMs across successive rounds.
391           • Tools: LLMs have demonstrated that their capacity for mathematical reasoning is not as
392             robust as initially anticipated. To address this, we have designed a suite of tools to augment
393             LLMs, compensating for their deficiencies in computational aspects. These tools consist
394             of text transformation utilities, key information extraction, data processing capabilities,
395             and foundational machine learning model scripts. The design of Contribution Evaluation
396             Module with analysis is demonstrated in Appendix A.
397           • Chain of Thought: The implementation of CoT in this scenario essentially follows a
398             prompt template to completion. This allows LLMs to assess whether the requirements
399             are met and to output templates that more closely align with the demands. Drawing inspi-

532   To address the heterogeneity challenges inherent in federated learning and to boost the efficiency
533   of such systems, we have introduced a framework known as GFLAgent. This methodology lever-
534   ages a tiered federated learning strategy and incorporates a buffer mechanism to address the outlier
535   scenarios that may arise among clients within tiers. Moreover, we have pioneered the use of an
