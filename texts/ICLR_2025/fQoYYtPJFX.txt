011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            3D eye gaze estimation from monocular images remains to be a challenging task
014            due to the model sensitivity to illumination, occlusion and head pose changes. As
015            the growing interests and demand in in-the-wild 3D gaze estimation under un-
016            constrained environments, the generalization ability has been considered as a cru-
017
               cial performance metric of 3D gaze estimation models. In this work, we present
               UGaze-Geo, an uncertainty-aware weakly-supervised framework for 3D gaze es-
018
               timation. We leverage the general knowledge of human eyeball anatomy and de-
019
               velop multiple geometric constraints. The proposed geometrical constraints con-
020            tains two types, where the first type is formulated by constructing the mapping
021            function from anatomical 3D eyeball parameters to eye appearance features (eye-
022            lid & iris landmarks). The second type of constraints is based on the relationship
023            among head rotation, eyeball rotation and gaze, where we learn a variable that de-
024            scribes ”relative eyeball rotation” conditioned on current head pose. Both type of
025            constraints are free of gaze labels and are general to any subjects and environmen-
026            tal conditions. We formulate these constraints as loss functions in a probabilistic
027            framework. We evaluate the UGaze-Geo framework on within-domain and four
028
               cross-domain gaze estimation tasks to validate the effectiveness of each constraint
               and the advantage of performing probabilistic gaze estimation. Experimental re-
029
               sults indicate that our model achieves SOTA performances on different dataset.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Eye gaze is an important cue for human behaviour and attention analysis. With the growing popular-
035
      ity in interactive applications such as AR/VR, 3D avatar animation, human-computer interaction and
036
      driver behaviour monitoring, automatic gaze estimation methods are proposed to regress 3D gaze
037   directions from eye images. With the development deep learning algorithms, CNN models have
038   been fully utilized to directly regress gaze from images and can generate accurate results given well-
039   annotated gaze datasets (Kellnhofer et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2020; Funes Mora et al., 2014; Fischer
040   et al., 2018). In spite of the good within-dataset performances achieved by recently-proposed learn-
041   ing based gaze estimation methods such as (Cheng et al., 2020; Chen & Shi, 2018; Fischer et al.,
042   2018; Zhang et al., 2017a), these models may suffer from sensitivity to different head poses, illu-
043   mination conditions and environmental changes when switch to different datasets. Improving the
044   robustness and generalization ability of gaze estimators becomes an emerging topic with great ap-
045
      plication significance. Gaze360 (Kellnhofer et al., 2019) provides a solution for robust 3D gaze
      tracking by training the gaze model with the large-scale unconstrained images collected under var-
046
      ious environments for various subjects. To further improve the generalization ability of gaze esti-
047
      mation models, researchers have been exploring target domain adaptation (Bao et al., 2022; Cheng
048   et al., 2022; Cai et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Cui et al., 2020), for updating
049   the pre-trained model with label or unlabeled target samples. On the other hand, 3D model-based
050   methods (Wood et al., 2016; 2018; Wang & Ji, 2017; Ploumpis et al., 2020) have been studied as an
051   alternative to appearance-based methods. Eye-Model-based methods usually solve gaze direction by
052   calculating anatomical eye parameters from eye image features (such as eye landmarks, iris bound-
053   ary and limbus boundary) and then solving the orientation of eyeball relative to camera. Earlier
      model-based methods (Hutchinson et al., 1989; Fuhl et al., 2016; Fuhl, 2021; Fuhl et al., 2017) pro-


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      posed to analyze eye parameters from infrared eye image and can recover very accurate anatomical
055   eye structure. Later, this process is simplified with the advent of 3D deformable eye models (Wood
056   et al., 2016; Ploumpis et al., 2020) and the complex eye parameter calculation can be replaced by
057   fitting the 3D eye bases. The 3D eye model provides a general shape prior that can applied to any
058   web-camera images. However, the process of model-based fitting can be time-consuming and need
059   a pre-calibration procedure for each subject.
060
      To build a robust 3D gaze estimator that can be applied in unconstrained environments, we propose
061   to incorporate general eye structural information into the learning process and formulate them as
062   geometrical constraints. Firstly, we introduce geometric structure of a 3D eyeball, with its shape
063   parameterized by two learnable anatomical variable: eyeball radius re and iris radius ri .(a). Along
064   with gaze prediction, our model constructs the correspondences between 3D eyeball vertices and
065   iris & eyelid landmarks by camera projection. Secondly, according to head-eye anatomy, the eyeball
066   center Ce is a fixed location inside the head for each subject and the eyeball can rotate independently
067   from the head around the eyeball center. Therefore, a gaze vector g is a combination of head pose Rh
068   and eyeball movement Re . Directly estimating g from images can be inaccurate due to appearance
069
      ambiguity, especially in blurry, occluded or less-illuminated images. The advantage of decomposing
      gaze into head pose and eyeball movement is that the head pose is an easier term to solve while the
070
      eyeball movement Re (under arbitrary head poses) tends to locate in a certain interval, which is a
071
      general anatomical prior. Biomedical study from (Moon et al., 2020) reveals the statistics that the
072   horizontal and vertical movement of eyeball ranges in [−34.5◦ , 33.3◦ ] and [−23.4◦ , 20.1◦ ] respec-
073   tively. Our original contributions of this work can be concluded as follows:
074
075          • we propose an uncertainty-aware 3D gaze estimation method UGaze-Geo, where the head
076            pose and eyeball movement are disentangled by learning a probabilistic eyeball rotation
077            function conditioned on head pose.
078          • we incorporate the geometry knowledge of 3D eyeball in the model, and further propose
079            three anatomy-based geometric constraints, which can be used for weakly-supervised train-
080            ing or semi-supervised training (when gaze labels are available). Specifically. we propose
081            a novel rotation consistency constraint based on the relationship of head rotation and gaze
082            direction.
083          • experimental results show the effectiveness of the geometrical constraints, in terms of
084            SOTA within-dataset performance and better generalization ability in cross-dataset eval-
085            uation than SOTA learning-based models. Our model can also quantify the predicted gaze
086            uncertainty.

090   2.1   H EAD POSE & E YE POSE D ISENTANGLEMENT
091
092   The strategy of disentangling head pose and eye pose is often used in generative models, which
093   can be trained on head and gaze labels. (Park et al., 2019) propose to train an encoder-decoder to
094   disentangle gaze direction, head pose and other appearance factors. An embedding consistency loss
095   is applied on frontalized latent gaze features. (Xia et al., 2020) propose a continuous gaze interpo-
096   lation framework through decoupling related gaze attributes (head pose, vertical and horizontal gaze
097
      directions). In addition, multi-view gaze representation learning conducted by (Gideon et al., 2022)
      provides a solution for disentangling head pose and relative gaze feature given camera information.
098
099
100
      2.2   R EGRESSION OF 3D G AZE WITH C ONSTRAINTS
101
      Fully-supervised learning based models rely on the quantity and quality of gaze labels to produce
102   reliable performance. However, generating accurate gaze labels is time consuming and labor expen-
103   sive. Researchers have proposed different constraints as weak supervision or regularization. One
104   type of constraints is to utilize the geometric knowledge of eyeball models in the training process.
105   (Ploumpis et al., 2020) constructed an anatomical 3D deformable eye model consists of PCA bases
106   of eye socket, pupil size and pupil texture. They train a CNN to predict anatomical eyeball parame-
107   ters. (Park et al., 2018) pre-train an hourglass-based network to predict the landmarks of iris, pupil
      center, eyeball center & radius on synthetic dataset, followed by a regressor to predict gaze from


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      the landmarks. Another commonly used constraint is imposed by the relationship among head pose,
109   eyeball movement and gaze direction. (Zhu & Deng, 2017) trained separate head pose and eye pose
110   estimation models and utilized a gaze transform layer to convert head pose and eyeball movement
111   to gaze direction. In the work proposed by (Kothari et al., 2021), 3D head location and orientation
112   are used to impose strong gaze-related geometric constraints from two people ”Looking At Each
113   Other”, which serves as a weak supervision term on the spatial relationship of gaze vectors so their
114   model can be trained without gaze labels. Our model combines anatomical knowledge of 3D eyeball
115   structure with head-eye-gaze relationships, so they can be incorporated into the training as regular-
116   ization terms. Unlike (Zhu & Deng, 2017), whose geometric constraints can only be imposed on
117
      constrained images (i.e., in-lab images with regular head poses) with given head pose and eyeball
      movement labels, our model figures out the relationship among g, Rh , Re automatically without
118
      requiring any additional eyeball movement labels and can be applied on unconstrained in-the-wild
119
      images.

122   2.3   G AZE SYNTHESIZING & AUGMENTATION
123
124
      Eye image synthesis or augmentation are common ways to enrich training data, especially when
      real gaze labels are hard to obtain. Synthesizing eye images from generative models (Wang et al.,
125
      2018; Kaur & Manduchi, 2021; Yu et al., 2019) helps improve gaze estimation accuracy with more
126
      diverse and gaze-controllable data. Meanwhile, it is straight-forward to conduct data augmentation
127   by applying image transformations, such as the 2D rotation in the work of Bao et al. (Bao et al., 2022)
128   and 3D warping implemented by Qin et al. (Qin et al., 2022). Like (Qin et al., 2022), we generate 3D
129   head pose augmentations on a gaze dataset, but we step further, by applying the anatomic knowledge
130   of eyeball and head-eye-gaze relationships on the the augmented data.

133   3     M ETHODS
134
135   We describe the workflow of our method as below. Our model first estimates head pose Rh and
136   then predict a probabilistic eyeball movement distribution conditioned on current head pose feature,
137   i.e., p(Re |Rh , I). At last, gaze direction is represented by a combination of head pose Rh and
138   eyeball movement Re . This section consists of the following part. In Section 3.1 we introduce the
139
      geometry-guided constraints, including the geometric eyeball model, transformation of coordinate
      systems and the relationship among head pose, eyeball movement and gaze. In Section 3.2, we
140
      describe process of training a deterministic model with the constraints , denoted as Gaze-Geo. In
141
      Section 3.3, we describe the uncertainty-aware Gaze-Geo framework, abbreviated as UGaze-Geo,
142   including re-writing of the probabilistic loss terms and uncertainty analysis. Finally, in Section 3.4,
143   we describe the full training loss of UGaze-Geo.

      3.1   P RELIMINARY: G EOMETRY- GUIDED C ONSTRAINTS
147
      In this section, we will describe the anatomy-based geometric constraints applied in training. The
148
      first type of constraint is based on eye-anatomy. According to (Wang & Ji, 2017; Wang et al., 2018),
149
      3D eyeball geometry can be estimated by a two-sphere model, with a larger sphere representing
150   the eyeball and a smaller one representing the cornea sphere. The intersection boundary of the
151   two spheres forms the boundary of iris. We use two parameters {eyeball radius: re , iris radius: ri }
152   to describe the shape of 3D eyeball. Then the eyeball center Ce and iris center Ci are used to
153   describe the location of eyeball and iris. We impose two constraints based on eye-anatomy: (1) the
154   3D eyelid vertices, which can be easily detected by existing 3D facial landmarks detectors (Bulat
155   & Tzimiropoulos, 2017), should be nearly located on the surface of 3D eyeball; (2) the 3D iris
156   boundary & iris center should be well aligned to the iris in image after proper camera projection.
157   The second type of constraints are based on head-eye anatomy. One general assumption according
158   to anatomical knowledge is that the eyeball center location Ce in head is fixed, and eyeball can
159   only rotate around Ce within certain range of degrees along pitch axis (horizontal) and yaw axis
160   (vertical). Based on this assumption, we decompose the gaze direction into the global head rotation
161   Rh and the secondary eyeball movement Re , where Re is relative eyeball rotation conditioned on
      Rh . Rh and Re are 3 × 3 rotation matrices converted from rotation angles. Since Re is converted

      5   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this paper we proposed UGaze-Geo, a novel learning-based framework for 3D gaze estimation,
489   with three geometry-guided geometric constraints applied as wweak-supervision in training. Our
490   model estimates a probabilistic eyeball movement conditioned on head poses and can predict both
491   gaze and gaze uncertainty through aligning 3D eyeball to the eye landmarks. We first introduce two
492   geometric constraints, by mapping a 3D eyeball to eye features including eyelid and iris landmarks.
493   We also propose a model rotation consistency constraint, which is based on augmented data gener-
494   ated by randomly revising the original 3D head pose of an image. With the constraints applied in
495   training, our model is robust to unconstrained images in both within- and cross-dataset evaluations
496   and can achieve SOTA performances.
