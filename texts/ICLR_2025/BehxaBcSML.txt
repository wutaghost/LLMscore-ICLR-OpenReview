011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large language models (LLMs) have recently demonstrated remarkable perfor-
014            mance across diverse language tasks. But their deployment is often constrained
015            by their substantial computational and storage requirements. Quantization has
016            emerged as a key technique for addressing this challenge, enabling the compres-
017
               sion of large models with minimal impact on performance. The recent GPTQ
               algorithm, a post-training quantization (PTQ) method, has proven highly effective
018
               for compressing LLMs, sparking a wave of research that leverages GPTQ as a
019
               core component. Recognizing the pivotal role of GPTQ in the PTQ landscape,
020            we introduce CDQuant, a simple and scalable alternative to GPTQ with improved
021            performance. CDQuant uses greedy coordinate descent to minimize the layer-wise
022            reconstruction loss to achieve high-quality quantized weights. Our algorithm is
023            easy to implement and scales efficiently to models with hundreds of billions of pa-
024            rameters. We perform extensive evaluation on Gemma, and PaLM2 model families,
025            and demonstrate that CDQuant consistently outperforms GPTQ in 2-4 bit weight
026            quantization. Moreover, CDQuant improves the performance of state-of-the-art
027            PTQ techniques such as QuIP and FrameQuant when used as a replacement for
028
               their GPTQ component, resulting in further gains in quality.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Large language models (LLMs) have shown remarkable ability to handle various language tasks (Tou-
034   vron et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023; Google, 2023), but their widespread adoption is hampered by
035   their substantial computational and memory demands. To tackle this challenge, researchers have
036
      explored techniques like quantization, pruning, and distillation, with quantization being a particularly
      promising avenue for reducing model size and inference time without significantly sacrificing perfor-
037
      mance (Miao et al., 2023). Quantization techniques broadly fall into two categories: post-training
038
      quantization (PTQ) and quantization-aware training (QAT). QAT, while potentially yielding better
039   results, poses a significant challenge for LLMs due to the immense resources required to train these
040   large models. As a result, a growing body of research has focused on PTQ, which is generally less
041   computationally intensive and can be applied to large, pre-trained models.
042
      A seminal work in PTQ for LLMs is GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2022), which introduced a one-shot
043
      weight quantization approach that minimizes the following layer-wise output reconstruction loss:
044
      kX(W − Ŵ )k2F , where X = [x1 , x2 . . . xn ] represents the layer inputs and W, Wc ∈ Rdin ×dout are
045
      the original and quantized weight matrices, respectively. This method has sparked a wave of research
046
      in PTQ. Subsequent works have built upon GPTQ, addressing its limitations and improving it’s
047
      performance. For instance, SpQR (Dettmers et al., 2023) and OWQ (Lee et al., 2023) improve GPTQ
048   by explicitly addressing outlier weights, maintaining them in full precision, while quantizing the
049   remaining weights using GPTQ. This hybrid approach improves quantization accuracy, particularly at
050   lower bit-widths. Another line of research focuses on transforming the weight space before applying
051   GPTQ. For instance, QuIP (Chee et al., 2024), FrameQuant (Adepu et al., 2024) transform the weights
052   into a more quantization-friendly space and apply GPTQ in the transformed space. AWQ (Lin et al.,
053   2023), SmoothQuant (Xiao et al., 2023) reduce the effect of activation outliers by performing feature
      scaling before quantizing the weights using standard techniques.

054
      Given the central role GPTQ plays in the landscape of PTQ methods for LLMs, improving its quality
055   directly translates to better quantization across numerous techniques that build upon it. Consequently,
056   in this work, we revisit the core optimization problem addressed by GPTQ and introduce a novel
057   coordinate descent based algorithm, CDQuant, to minimize the objective. CDQuant is an iterative
058   optimization technique that greedily selects coordinates to descend along in each iteration. This
059   approach contrasts with GPTQ, which cycles through the coordinates only once, in a predetermined
060   order, often leading to suboptimal solutions for the layer-wise objective (see Section 2 for more
061   details). CDQuant is simple to implement and scales effectively to models with billions of parameters.
062   We further extend CDQuant to group/sub-channel quantization, a technique gaining popularity of
063
      late (Dettmers et al., 2021; Dettmers & Zettlemoyer, 2023).
064   Extensive experiments on Gemma (Mesnard et al., 2024; Gemma-Team, 2024), and PaLM2 (Anil
065   et al., 2023) model families demonstrate that CDQuant consistently outperforms GPTQ across
066   various model sizes and quantization precision levels (2-4 bits). Notably, for INT2 quantization
067   of PaLM2-Otter, CDQuant achieves a 10% perplexity reduction compared to GPTQ. Furthermore,
068   integrating CDQuant with QuIP (Chee et al., 2024) and FrameQuant (Adepu et al., 2024) improves
069
      their performance by ∼ 5% for INT2 quantization of the Gemma-2 27B model, showcasing its
      potential as a drop-in replacement for GPTQ in existing PTQ techniques.

072   2    R ELATED W ORK
073
074   The literature on quantization is huge. In this section, we only review the works that are related to
075   quantization of large pre-trained models, and those that are related to our work.
076
      GPTQ. Inspired by the Optimal Brain Surgeon (OBS) framework for pruning (LeCun et al., 1989),
077
      Optimal Brain Quantization (OBQ) (Frantar & Alistarh, 2022) emerged as a promising post-training
078
      quantization (PTQ) technique. However, OBQ remains computationally expensive and struggles to
079   scale effectively to large language models (LLMs). This is because of a complicated step involved in
080   OBQ which requires updating all the unquantized coordinates in each step (see Equation 2 in Frantar
081   et al. (2022)). Implementing this step in GPUs is extremely slow because of the sheer number of
082   gathers/scatters that need to be performed at each step. Frantar et al. (2022) subsequently introduced
083   GPTQ, which employs heuristics to accelerate the OBQ algorithm. Specifically, GPTQ updates
084   coordinates once in a cyclic manner rather than the greedy approach used in OBQ. This modification
085   significantly speeds up the algorithm but comes at the cost of reduced quality. In our work, we
086   address this performance drop by proposing greedy coordinate descent algorithms that are both
087
      straightforward and easy to implement.
088   Other Weight-only Quantization Techniques. Recent works on post-training quantization have
089   sought to improve upon GPTQ. One popular strategy here is to identify and isolate problematic
090   weights before applying GPTQ. For example, SpQR (Dettmers et al., 2023) isolates outlier weights
091   and keeps them at full precision, quantizing only the remaining values using GPTQ. Similarly,
092   OWQ (Lee et al., 2023) identifies weights that are highly sensitive to small perturbations and excludes
093
      them from the GPTQ quantization process. JSQ (Guo et al., 2024) clips activations (where the
      clipping thresholds are searched through simulated annealing), following which a combination of
094
      activation range and weight saliency are used to sparsify the weight matrix before quantizing it
095
      with GPTQ. SqueezeLLM (Kim et al., 2024) also preserves outliers in floating point, however,
096   employs non-uniform quantization by performing K-means clustering on the final end-to-end loss
097   (where the gradient is approximated with zero,and the Hessain with the Fisher information matrix).
098   Any-Precision LLM (Park et al., 2024) improves SqueezeLLM by allowing several nested precisions
099   through a control over the number of cluster centers. However, both these approaches involve storing
100   a codebook, hence making hardware and software compatibility tricky.
101
      Another promising direction involves transforming the weights into a different basis before quan-
102   tization. QuIP (Chee et al., 2024), and FrameQuant (Adepu et al., 2024), SpinQuant (Liu et al.,
103   2024), QuaRot (Ashkboos et al., 2024) take this route and perform quantization in the transformed
104   space using GPTQ. In all these approaches, we could potentially substitute GPTQ with CDQuant and
105   expect to see further improvements in performance (see Section 4 for empirical evidence). Other
106   promising methods such as AWQ (Lin et al., 2023), AffineQuant (Ma et al., 2024), suppress the
107   effect of outliers in input activations by adjusting their scale and transferring it to weights. They then
      perform the standard MinMax quantization on the rescaled weights. One could replace MinMax with

111   While working on our paper, we came across QuantEase (Behdin et al., 2023), a parallel research
112
      effort sharing similar goals as ours for improving GPTQ. While both methods leverage the concept
      of coordinate descent, QuantEase adopts a cyclic approach for weight updates, whereas our work
113
      employs a greedy strategy. Although our experiments (Appendix D) indicate comparable performance
114
      between the two methods, our work extends beyond QuantEase by introducing specialized algorithms
115   for group/sub-channel quantization, not just full-channel quantization. Additionally, we develop
116   novel block coordinate descent algorithms that further improve the performance. That being said,
117   both these works (QuantEase and ours) collectively highlight the potential of coordinate descent
118   algorithms in outperforming GPTQ.
119
      Vector Quantization. AQLM (Egiazarian et al., 2024) generalizes Additive Quantization to LLMs
120
      by introducing input-adaptiveness (through calibration data) and jointly optimizing the codebooks
121   across transformer blocks. QuIP# (Tseng et al., 2024) improves QuIP’s incoherence processing
122   through random Hadamard matrices, introduces vector quantization, and adds an additional layer of
123   finetuning to improve model quality. However, in this work, our focus is methods that improve scalar
124   quantization.
125
      Weight+Activation Quantization. SmoothQuant (Xiao et al., 2023), OS+(Wei et al., 2023; 2022)
126
      have a similar flavour as AWQ, but quantize both weights and activations after scaling them ap-
127   propriately. OmniQuant (Shao et al., 2023) performs quantization of the entire transformer block
128   in a single shot. This encompasses both activation and weight quantization. Furthermore, it sub-
129   sumes SmoothQuant, OS+ by using both feature scaling and outlier suppression. QLLM (Liu et al.,
130   2023) tackles the issue of outliers in the activations by splitting the outlier features into multiple
131   sub-channels and then recombining them, effectively reducing their influence. QLLM also incor-
132   porates a fine-tuning step at the end, introducing low-rank weights into each layer of the LLM.
133   LLM.int8() (Dettmers et al., 2022) quantizes both acativations and weights to 8-bits and also identi-
134   fies outliers and stores them in full precision. LQER (Zhang et al., 2024) approximates the weight
135
      quantization error with the product of two low rank matrices.
136   Efficient End-to-End Quantization. To recover the drop in performance from quantization Chai
137   et al. (2023); Dettmers et al. (2024); Hu et al. (2021) perform low-rank parameter-efficient fine-tuning.

      (λI). Tuning this λ for every (model, layer) pair is infeasible. So, we determine a single optimal
345
      value using the PaLM2-Gecko model, and apply it universally.
346
347   CDQuant. We evaluate both the coordinate descent variants described in Section 3.1: CD (Algo-
348   rithm 1), BCD (Algorithm 2). For per-channel quantization, we initialize CD with Optimal Weight
349   Clipping (OWC), and for sub-channel quantization, we additionally include initialization with OWC-
350
      CD. BCD is always initialized with CD in our experiments. Unless otherwise stated, both CD and
      BCD are run for T = din iterations, OWC-CD is run for din /g iterations, where g is the group size.
351
      Similar to GPTQ, we regularize the Hessian matrix used in CD, BCD by adding λI. We determine a
352
      reasonable value for λ using the PaLM2-Gecko model, and use it in all our experiments.
353
354   Evaluation. Following recent works (Frantar et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2024), we evaluate all algo-
355   rithms using two key metrics: perplexity and downstream task performance. For Gemma models,
356
      following Frantar et al. (2022), we calculate perplexity on C4’s (Raffel et al., 2019) validation set.
      For PaLM2 models, we calculate perplexity using a 100 million token subset derived from the
357
      PaLM2 training mixture. We use TriviaQA (Joshi et al., 2017), SQuAD (Rajpurkar et al., 2018),
358
      NaturalQuestions (Kwiatkowski et al., 2019) and WebQuestions (Berant et al., 2013) to evaluate
359   generation capabilities of the quantized models, and to evaluate their reasoning capabilities, we test
360   on ARC-c, ARC-e (Clark et al., 2018), HellaSwag (Zellers et al., 2019), BoolQ (Clark et al., 2019),
361   PIQA (Bisk et al., 2020) and WinoGrande (Sakaguchi et al., 2020). For downstream evaluations, we
362   consider the zero-shot setting. Finally, to determine which optimization technique is most effective at
363   solving the layer-wise objective in Equation (1), we evaluate the final solution’s objective value using
364   the same 100 million tokens that we used to compute perplexity.
365   Training. All techniques are calibrated using 1280 data points, where each data point has 2048
366   tokens. For OWC, we use a grid size of 50 to find the most optimal γ. We used 8 Nvidia H100 GPUs
367   for quantizing the models.

456   5     RUNTIME
457
458   Table 5 shows the runtime of GPTQ, CD and BCD for quantization of FFN1, FFN2, and attention
459   weights of Gemma-2 27B model using 8 H100s (see Appendix F.4 for 1 GPU runtime). For attention
460   weight quantization, CD is comparable to GPTQ in speed, while BCD is 2× slower. For FFN1
461   (FFN2) quantization, CD is 5× (2×) slower than GPTQ, whereas BCD is an order of magnitude
462   slower than GPTQ. It can be seen that FFN2 quantization is the most time-consuming step (because
463   the quantization axis is of size dhidden which is much larger than the quantization axis of other
464   layers). Consequently, CD (BCD) is twice (order of magnitude) as slow as GPTQ for whole-model
465
      quantization. We now provide two simple strategies to speed up both CD and BCD.
466   Speeding up BCD. To speed up BCD, we quantize FFN2 with CD, and rest of the layers with BCD
467   (where the quantization axis is of size dmodel ). Results for this setting can be found in Table 6. It can
468   be seen that quantizing FFN2 with CD leads to negligible to no drop in performance especially for
469   the larger, and better Gemma-2 9B. With this setup, BCD’s runtime is reduced significantly and is
470   comparable to that of CD.
471   Speeding up CD. We find that CD converges to an optimal solution in much fewer iterations than din
472   iterations used in our experiments. Based on this finding, we run CD for lesser number of iterations.
473   As shown in Table 22 in Appendix, even with a drastically reduced iteration count (e.g., din /8),
474   CD maintains near-optimal performance with negligible quality loss, surpassing the performance of
475   GPTQ. This reduction in iterations allows CD to achieve a runtime faster than/comparable to GPTQ.

      6     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
478
479
      In this work, we developed a coordinate descent framework (CDQuant) for quantization of LLMs.
480   CDQuant is a simple and effective alternative to GPTQ, that consistently outperformed it on PaLM2
481   models. The simplicity of our algorithm makes it a seamless substitute for GPTQ in various
482   algorithmic contexts where GPTQ currently functions as a sub-routine. Our future work aims
483   to further improve the performance of CDQuant. In particular, we would like to use ideas from
484   CDQuant to improve Quantized Aware Training (QAT). Furthermore, we will focus on developing
485   layer-wise loss functions that are more closely aligned with end-to-end loss, thereby reducing the
      performance gap between full-precision and quantized models.

      A    B ROADER I MPACT
757
758
      We introduce a Coordinate Descent based approach, CDQuant, for compressing Large Language
759   Models. Our method uses only a small amount of data for calibration. We do not foresee any ethical
760   implications arising from the technical aspects of our approach. However, compressing LLMs may
761   give rise to bias effects, a study of which seems essential given the extensive use of LLMs. Our work
762   may be of assistance to such studies. Also, since quantization allows for easier deployment of LLMs,
763   it could have potential societal implications which seem difficult to predict.
764
765
      B    L IMITATIONS
766
767
            • While both CD and BCD outperformed GPTQ in our experiments, BCD achieves slightly
768           better performance than CD. However, BCD is not as fast as CD and can be expensive for
769           large models. In future, we aim to develop techniques to speed up BCD.
770
            • Our algorithms still don’t bridge the gap between QAT and PTQ, especially on smaller
771
              models. To bridge this gap, we believe one should move away from the `22 surrogate loss
772           that is being considered by most of the existing work. Instead, we should design surrogate
773           losses that are more closely aligned with end-to-end loss.

      C    R ELATED W ORK
777
      In this section, we provide a more thorough comparison of our technique with OBQ and GPTQ.
778
779
      Detailed comparison with OBQ. At a high level, both OBQ, and our technique use greedy strate-
780
      gies to quantize the weights. However, OBQ is extremely slow and doesn’t even scale to models with
781
      a few billion parameters. The primary reason for this unreasonable slowness is because of a more
782   complicated step involved in OBQ which requires updating all the unquantized coordinates in each
783   step (please refer to equation 2 in Frantar & Alistarh (2022)). Implementing this step in GPUs is
784   extremely slow because of the sheer number of gathers/scatters that need to be performed at each
785   step. In contrast, CDQuant doesn’t have this step. At each step of coordinate descent, we only update
786   the single coordinate that leads to the biggest drop in performance. As a result, CDQuant is orders of
787   magnitude faster than OBQ. To quantify this last statement, note that GPTQ paper only ran OBQ for
788   models with a few hundred millions of parameters and showed that OBQ is almost 120x slower than
789   GPTQ (note that this difference will only grow with larger models because OBQ will require even
790
      more gathers/scatters). In fact, in the OBQ paper (Frantar & Alistarh, 2022), in Table 6, OBQ takes
      65 minutes to quantize a ResNet50 model with 25M parameters. That being said, scaling it to models
791
      with billions of parameters seems extremely difficult.
792
793
      Detailed comparison with GPTQ. GPTQ, as discussed in Section 2, is a heuristic designed to
794
      accelerate OBQ by replacing its greedy strategy with a single, predetermined cycle through all
795   coordinates. While this approach improves speed, it often sacrifices accuracy. Although multiple
796   cycles could potentially mitigate this performance drop, our experiments revealed no significant
797   improvement in quality. In contrast, we propose a simple yet effective greedy coordinate descent
798   strategy that achieves superior accuracy while maintaining the computational efficiency of GPTQ.

1120
       noticed that the inputs to the attention layer are often aligned in a handful of directions. Consequently,

       F.4   RUNTIME A NALYSIS
1163
       In Tables 19 20, we present the quantization runtime of GPTQ, CD and BCD on Gemma models using
1164   H100s (in one setting we used 1 H100 and in another we used 8H100s). In particular, in Table 19, we
1165   present runtime for quantizing all the attention and FFN layers (which includes FFN1, FFN1-gate,
1166   FFN2) in the model. Table 20 presents a more detailed breakdown; we present the per-layer runtime
1167   for quantizing FFN1 and FFN2 layers.
1168
       Runtime on 8 H100’s. With 8 H100s, CD is as fast as GPTQ for quantizing attention weights,
1169
       whereas BCD is 2× slower. However, for FFN1 (FFN2) quantization, CD is 5× (2×) slower than
1170   GPTQ, whereas BCD is an order of magnitude slower than GPTQ. For quantization of the entire
1171   model, CD is 2× slower than GPTQ (because quantizing FFN2 takes up most of the time). We
1172   attribute the slowness of CD and BCD to the numerous scatters and gathers involved in finding the
1173   most optimal coodrinate/block of coordinate to update, and then eventually updating it. Since ML
1174   accelerators are slow with gather and scatter operations, we are bound by the time it takes to do them.
1175   Performing these operations on the CPU should help speed up CD and BCD.
1176   Runtime on 1 H100. With a single H100, GPTQ is 2x-3.8x faster than CD for the smaller, attention
1177   weights, and is an order of magnitude faster than BCD. For the larger, FFN weights, GPTQ is an
1178   order of magnitude faster than CD, and in most cases is two orders of magnitude faster than BCD. As
1179   stated previously, both CD and BCD are bound by the time it takes to do gathers and scatters.
1180
       Speeding up BCD. To algorithmically speed up BCD, we propose to use CD for FFN2 quantization,
1181
       and use BCD for the rest of the layers. We find that BCD spends most of its time quantizing the
1182   FFN2 weight matrix (as can be seen in Table 20) where the quantization axis is of size dhidden . In
1183   most cases, the time it takes to FFN2 is 10x the time it takes to quantize FFN1. Thus, to speed up
1184   BCD, we quantize FFN2 with CD, and FFN1 and FFN1-gate with BCD (where the quantization axis
1185   is of size dmodel . Results for this setting can be found in Table 21. It can be seen that quantizing FFN2
1186   with CD leads to negligible to no drop in performance especially for the larger, and better Gemma-2
1187   9B. In fact, it improves the performance of BCD for w3a16g128. With this setup, BCD’s runtime is
       reduced significantly and is comparable to that of CD.
