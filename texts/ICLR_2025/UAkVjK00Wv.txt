009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Learning the structure of Bayesian networks (BNs) from data is challenging, es-
012            pecially for datasets involving a large number of variables. The recently proposed
013            divide-and-conquer (D&D) strategies present a promising approach for learning
014            large BNs. However, they still face a main issue of unstable learning accuracy
015            across subproblems. In this work, we introduce the idea of employing struc-
016            ture learning ensemble (SLE), which combines multiple BN structure learning
017            algorithms together, to consistently achieve high learning accuracy across vari-
018
               ous problems. We further propose an automatic approach called Auto-SLE for
               constructing near-optimal SLEs, addressing the challenge of manually designing
019
               effective SLEs. The automatically constructed SLE is then integrated into a D&D
020
               framework. Extensive experiments firmly show the superiority of our method over
021            existing methods in learning large BNs, achieving accuracy improvement usually
022            by 30%∼225% on datasets involving 10,000 variables. Furthermore, our method
023            generalizes very well to datasets with many more variables and different network
024            characteristics than those present in the training data for constructing the SLE.
025            These results indicate the significant potential of employing automatic construc-
026            tion of SLEs for BN structure learning.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Learning the structure of Bayesian networks (BNs) (Pearl, 1985) from data has attracted much re-
032   search interest, due to its wide applications in machine learning, statistical modeling, and causal
033
      inference (Pearl, 1988; Kitson et al., 2023). Various methods have been proposed to tackle this
      problem, including constraint-based methods (Colombo et al., 2014), score-based methods (Ramsey
034
      et al., 2017), and hybrid methods (Tsamardinos et al., 2006). However, most previous studies primar-
035
      ily dealt with a relatively small number of variables. For example, the bnlearn repository (Scutari,
036   2010), which is widely used in the literature, contains mostly networks with only a few dozen nodes
037   (variables). In contrast, in real-world applications such as alarm events analysis (Cai et al., 2022),
038   MRI image interpretation (Ramsey et al., 2017), and human genome analysis (Schaffter et al., 2011),
039   it is common to generate and collect data from thousands of variables and beyond. Unfortunately,
040   as the number of variables increases, many of the existing methods would slow down dramatically
041   and become much less accurate (Zhu et al., 2021).
042   Recently, Gu and Zhou (2020) introduced a divide-and-conquer (D&D) framework named partition-
043   estimation-fusion (PEF) for the structure learning of large BNs. Specifically, PEF consists of three
044   steps: partitioning nodes into clusters (partition), learning a subgraph on each cluster (estimation),
045   and fusing all subgraphs into a single BN (fusion). It is noteworthy that PEF is flexible as any
046   existing structure learning algorithm can be used in the estimation step. Additionally, due to the
047   smaller number of nodes in each cluster compared to the total number of nodes, the overall structure
048   learning is accelerated. Moreover, the structure learning processes for different subproblems can be
049   parallelized in a straightforward way, leading to further improvement in computational efficiency.
050   However, despite the evident advantages provided by PEF, it still faces a main issue of unstable
051   structure learning accuracy across subproblems. The root cause for this is that the partition step
052   of PEF tends to yield subproblems with significantly different characteristics, e.g., varying node
053   numbers. When a single structure learning algorithm is used to solve all subproblems, as in ex-
      isting PEF-based methods (Gu & Zhou, 2020), achieving stable learning accuracy across different

075   subproblems becomes challenging. In fact, even for the same algorithm, different hyperparameter
076   values can lead to significant variations in its behavior, thereby affecting its suitability for solving
077   specific subproblems. For instance, when employing the well-known fast greedy equivalence search
078   (fGES) (Ramsey et al., 2017) algorithm with Bayesian information criterion (BIC) as the score func-
079   tion, its penalty coefficient should ideally increase with the sparsity of the underlying BN. However,
080   given a BN structure learning problem, determining the optimal penalty coefficient in advance is
081
      difficult as the underlying BN is unknown.
082   Inspired by the success of ensemble methods like AutoAttack (Croce & Hein, 2020) in the field of
083   adversarial robustness, which utilizes an attack ensemble to achieve more reliable robustness evalua-
084   tion compared to individual attacks, we introduce the idea of employing structure learning ensemble
085   (SLE) to achieve stable learning accuracy in BN structure learning. Specifically, a SLE comprises
086   several structure learning algorithms, dubbed member algorithms. When applied to a BN structure
087
      learning problem, a SLE runs its member algorithms individually and chooses the best of their out-
      puts. Similar to how AutoAttack integrates diverse attacks, a high-performing SLE should consist
088
      of complementary member algorithms that excel at solving different types of problems. However,
089
      in contrast to AutoAttack where the attacks are manually designed and selected, we propose to auto-
090   matically construct SLEs. This can significantly reduce the reliance on human expertise and effort,
091   as manually constructing SLEs typically requires domain experts to explore the vast design space of
092   SLEs, which can be both laborious and intricate.
093
      Specifically, we first formulate the problem of automatic SLE construction, and then present Auto-
094
      SLE, a simple yet effective approach to address this problem. The approach is implemented with a
095
      design space containing several candidate algorithms; each algorithm has a set of hyperparameters.
096   To construct a SLE, Auto-SLE starts from an empty ensemble, and repeatedly adds the algorithm
097   and its associated hyperparameter values to the ensemble such that the ensemble improvement is
098   maximized. In addition to its conceptual simplicity, Auto-SLE is appealing also because it yields
099   SLEs provably within a constant factor of optimal for the training problem set. To achieve our goal
100   of constructing a single SLE that can generalize well across network sizes and characteristics, we
101   utilize a training set comprising diverse networks of varying sizes. Finally, the constructed SLE is
102   integrated into the PEF framework, resulting in P/SLE (see Figure 1 for an overview).
103   From extensive comparisons with the state-of-the-art baselines, it is found that P/SLE consistently
104   achieves significantly higher accuracy in learning large BNs, and its superiority becomes even more
105   pronounced as the network size further increases. On datasets involving 10,000 variables, P/SLE
106   typically achieves accuracy improvement by 30%∼225%. Further experiments show that P/SLE,
107   without any additional tuning or adaptation, generalizes well to datasets with much larger number
      (e.g., 30,000) of variables and possessing different network characteristics than the training data.

      It is worth mentioning that Auto-SLE itself is a general SLE construction approach that can be
109   applied to various types of BNs (Gaussian and non-Gaussian) and even other types of causal models.
110   In this work, we focus on Gaussian BNs, which are arguably the most widely studied type of BNs
111   and have a rich set of baselines, allowing us to thoroughly assess the potential of Auto-SLE. Besides,
112   it should be noted that PEF-based methods, such as P/SLE, are most suitable for learning BNs with
113   a block structure to some extent, meaning the connections between subgraphs are relatively weak.
114   It is quite common for a large network to exhibit such a block structure, due to the underlying
115   heterogeneity among the nodes (Holland et al., 1983; Airoldi et al., 2008; Abbe et al., 2015). On
116   the other hand, our results also indicate that, even for large BNs without a block structure, P/SLE
117
      can still achieve competitive learning accuracy. The promising performance of P/SLE not only
      demonstrates the potential of applying SLEs to BN structure learning, a largely unexplored area, but
118
      also highlights the effectiveness of Auto-SLE as a simple and easy-to-use approach for constructing
119
      SLEs. This is expected to further facilitate the advancement of SLEs in this field.

124   2.1   T HE BN S TRUCTURE L EARNING P ROBLEM
125
      The structure of a BN for d random variables X1 , ..., Xd is represented by a directed acyclic graph
126
      (DAG), denoted as G = (V, E). Here, V = {1, ..., d} is the set of nodes corresponding to the
127   random variables and E = {(j, i) ∈ V × V : j → i} is the directed edge set. Define Vpa(i) =
128   {j ∈ V : (j, i) ∈ E} as the parent node set of node i, and Xpa(i) as the set of corresponding
129   random variables. The joint probability density function f of X1 , ..., Xd is factorized according to
130   the structure of G:
131                                                         d
                                                            Y
132                                f (X1 , X2 , ..., Xd ) =   f Xi |Xpa(i) ,                            (1)
133                                                         i=1

134   where f Xi |Xpa(i) is the conditional probability density of Xi given Xpa(i) . In this work, we
135   focus on Gaussian BNs for continuous data. Specifically, the conditional distributions are specified
136   by the following linear structural equation model:
137

                                         Xi = φ Xpa(i) + εi , i = 1, ..., d,                            (2)
138
139   where φ(·) denotes a linear function and εi ∼ N 0, σj2 . Suppose we have obtained m iid ob-
140   servations of X1 , . . . , Xd , denoted as D ∈ Rm×d . Given D, the goal is to learn a DAG structure
141   G = (V, E) that accurately reflects the conditional dependencies among X1 , . . . , Xd . In practice,
142   accuracy metrics such as F1 score and structural Hamming distance (SHD) are typically used to
143   assess the quality of the learned BN.

145   2.2   R ELATED W ORK
146
147
      The BN structure learning problem has been proven to be NP-hard (Chickering et al., 2004), leading
      to main research efforts on developing approximation methods to solve it. These methods can be
148
      broadly classified into constraint-based, score-based, and hybrid methods. Constraint-based meth-
149
      ods, such as PC (Spirtes et al., 2000), MMPC (Tsamardinos et al., 2003a), and PC-Stable (Colombo
150   et al., 2014), use conditional independence tests on observations to identify relationships among
151   variables. In comparison, score-based methods explore the space of DAGs or Markov equivalence
152   classes (MECs) using search heuristics such as tabu search (TS) (Bouckaert, 1995), genetic algo-
153   rithm (GA) (Larranaga et al., 1996), and greedy search (Chickering, 2002). These methods also
154   employ score functions, such as BDeu (Akaike, 1974), BIC (Schwarz, 1978), and K2 (Cooper &
155   Herskovits, 1992), to guide the search. It is worth mentioning a recent research line of score-based
156   methods including NOTEARS (Zheng et al., 2018) and LEAST (Zhu et al., 2021) that reformulate
157   the structure learning as a continuous optimization problem. Finally, hybrid methods integrate both
158
      constraint-based and score-based techniques. For example, MMHC (Tsamardinos et al., 2006) uses
      MMPC to build the graph skeleton and utilizes TS to determine the final BN.
159
160   However, as the number of variables increases, many of the existing methods would slow down
161   dramatically and become much less accurate (Zhu et al., 2021). Actually, based on our preliminary
      testing of 15 existing methods, fGES (Ramsey et al., 2017), which is a variant of the greedy search


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      algorithm (Chickering, 2002), and PC-Stable (Colombo et al., 2014), are the only methods capable
163   of maintaining relatively stable learning accuracy (e.g., achieving F1 score of around 0.5) within
164   reasonable time when the variable number reaches 1000. Moreover, when the number of variables
165   reaches 10,000, all methods, even after running for a quite long period of time (e.g., 24 hours), are
166   unable to output solutions.
167
168   PEF Framework To address the challenge of learning large BNs, Gu and Zhou (2020) introduced
169   a partition-estimation-fusion (PEF) framework. It comprises the following three steps.
170
171
             • Partition: The d nodes are partitioned into clusters with a hierarchical clustering algorithm.
172          • Estimation: An existing structure learning algorithm is applied to estimate a subgraph on
173            each cluster of nodes.
174          • Fusion: Merge estimated subgraphs into one DAG containing all the d nodes.
175
176   The details of PEF can be found in Appendix A. While PEF has dramatically enhanced the capabil-
177   ities of handling large BNs, it still faces a main issue of unstable structure learning accuracy across
178
      subproblems. In the following we will describe the use of automatically constructed SLE in the
      estimation step to address this issue.

201   candidate algorithm pool {A1 , ..., An }, which can be constructed by collecting existing algorithms.

      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
293
      Benchmarks and Evaluation Metrics We generate diverse and large-scale benchmarks follow-
294
      ing the approach in (Gu & Zhou, 2020). Specifically, the approach consists of four steps: (i) select
295   an existing network structure and replicates it until a predefined variable number is reached; (ii)
296   connect the replicas by adding 10% of edges between them randomly, while ensuring the final net-
297   work remains a DAG; (iii) utilize the complete DAG and Eq. (2) to generate observations, where the
298   weights in the linear function and the standard deviations of Gaussian noises are sampled uniformly
299   from [−1, −0.5] ∪ [0.5, 1] and [0, 1], respectively; (iv) re-scale the observations such that all data
300   columns have the same mean and standard deviation. We select 10 networks from the bnlearn repos-
301   itory (Scutari, 2010) with node numbers ranging from 5 to 441. Based on each of them, we use the
302   above approach to generate testing problems with around 1000 and 10,000 variables. Following (Gu
303
      & Zhou, 2020), the sample size m in each testing problem is set to 1000.
304   The widely-used F1 score and SHD are adopted as the metrics for assessing learning accuracy. In
305   line with previous comparative study (Ramsey et al., 2017), we use two specific variants of F1 score,
306   i.e., F1 Arrowhead (F1→ ) that considers direction and F1 Adjacent (F1− ) that ignores direction,
307   since some methods (PC-Stable and fGES) output MECs of DAGs which may contain edges without
308   directions. Moreover, the wall-clock runtime of the methods is reported. For F1 metrics, a higher
309
      value is better; for SHD and runtime, a lower value is better.
310
311   Constructing the SLE with Auto-SLE A diverse training problem set is beneficial for construct-
312   ing a SLE with good performance across problem sizes and network characteristics. Specifically,
313   the training set T comprises 100 problems, with variable numbers ranging from 5 to 1000, generated
314   using the above approach based on a network randomly selected from the 32 networks in bnlearn.
315
      These problems are exclusively used for constructing the SLE and are independent of the testing
      problems. For the algorithm configuration space Θ, we consider two algorithms PC-stable (with
316
      two hyperparameters) and fGES (with three hyperparameters) as candidate algorithms, which out-
317
      perform others in our preliminary testing. We use their implementations from the causal discovery
318   tool box TETRAD (Ramsey et al., 2018). The sum of F1− and F1→ is adopted as the quality mea-
319   sure Q, and ensemble size k is set to 4 as running more iterations of Auto-SLE brings a negligible
320   improvement to the SLE’s performance on the training set (see Appendix C.3). SMAC (version
321   3) (Lindauer et al., 2022), a Bayesian optimization tool, is used to maximize ∆(θ|A) in each it-
322   eration of Auto-SLE, with a time budget 12 hours per run. Consequently, Auto-SLE consumes
323   approximately 48 hours in total to construct the SLE. Then, the SLE is integrated into PEF, resulting
      in P/SLE, which is evaluated in subsequent experiments without further tuning or adaptation.

353   Baselines and Settings Six baselines are considered, including existing state-of-the-art methods
354   and PEF-based methods. Specifically, given the rich literature on BN structure learning (Kitson
355   et al., 2023), we collect 15 existing methods, including score-based, constraint-based, and hybrid
356   methods, and conduct a preliminary testing of them (see Appendix C.2). The results indicate that
357   fGES and PC-Stable are the only methods capable of maintaining F1 scores of around 0.5 within rea-
358   sonable time when the variable number reaches 1000. Therefore, we choose these two as baselines.
359   Besides, fGES and PC-Stable are also integrated into the estimation step of PEF, resulting in two
360
      new baselines: P/fGES and P/PC-Stable. Furthermore, to validate the effectiveness of Auto-SLE,
      we consider two alternative ensemble construction approaches: (i) default SLE, which contains the
361
      default fGES and PC-Stable, as well as variants with randomly chosen hyperparameter values for
362
      each of them; (ii) random SLE, which contains two variants with randomly chosen hyperparameter
363   values for each of fGES and PC-Stable. Both of these SLEs consist of four member algorithms
364   (same as our constructed SLE) and are integrated into PEF, yielding two baselines P/SLE(D) and
365   P/SLE(R).
366
      To prevent the compared methods from running prohibitively long, a runtime limit of 24 hours is set
367
      on each testing problem. All the experiments are conducted on a Linux server with an Intel Gold
368
      6336Y CPU @ 2.40GHz, 96 cores, and 768GB of memory. Precise details of the experimental setup,
369   including the preliminary testing results, SLE construction, benchmarks, metrics, and baselines, are
370   in Appendix C. The codes for repeating our experiments are available in the supplementary.

      The first observation from Table 1 is that P/SLE consistently achieves significantly higher accuracy
407
      across all three metrics compared to the baselines, except for the F1 metrics on Healthcare testing
408
      problems, where P/SLE performs slightly worse than PC-Stable. Table 2 shows that the superior-
409   ity of P/SLE becomes more pronounced on testing problems with 10,000 variables. In call cases
410   it achieves substantially higher accuracy than all baselines across all accuracy metrics. Notably,
411   compared to the best performance achieved by the baselines, P/SLE often achieves improvements
412   in F1 Adjacent of over 30% and up to 133%, and improvements in F1 Arrowhead and SHD of over
413   50% and even up to 225%. Since the difference between P/SLE and P/PC-Stable (P/fGES) lies in
414   the use of a SLE in the estimation step instead of a single algorithm, the consistent advantages of
415   P/SLE over them confirm that using SLEs can stably achieve high learning accuracy across sub-
416   problems. On the other hand, we also observe that P/SLE(D) and P/fGES obtain identical learning
417
      accuracy. This is because, in the default ensemble, the output of fGES always has the best BIC score
      among all member algorithms, thus making it consistently being chosen as the final output. While
418
      P/SLE(R), with a randomly constructed SLE, can avoid this issue, it fails to achieve satisfactory
419
      learning accuracy, which in some cases is even worse than P/PC-Stable and P/fGES that do not use
420   SLEs. Therefore, the advantages of P/SLE over P/SLE(D) and P/SLE(R) highlight the effectiveness
421   of Auto-SLE in producing high-quality SLEs with complementary member algorithms.
422
423
      The second observation is that on testing problems with 1000 variables, P/PC-Stable and P/fGES
      often achieve higher learning accuracy than PC-Stable and fGES, respectively. Moreover, when
424
      the number of variables reaches 10,000, PC-Stable and fGES are unable to find solutions within 24
425
      hours, while P/PC-Stable and P/fGES are able to. These findings show that PEF framework can
426   indeed enhance the capabilities of handling large BNs, which is consistent with the observations
427   in (Gu & Zhou, 2020). Finally, all PEF-based methods generally consume much less runtime than
428   non-PEF-based methods, attributed to the underlying divide-and-conquer strategy. Among PEF-
429   based methods, P/SLE often has the shortest or close to the shortest runtime, and for most testing
430   problems, it consistently outputs the final solution within a reasonable time (less than 1000 seconds).
431   In summary, all the above findings affirmatively answer RQ1, i.e., the SLE constructed by Auto-SLE
      substantially improves PEF in learning the structure of large BNs.

509
      Extensive experiments showed that our method P/SLE could consistently achieve high accuracy in
      learning large BNs and generalize well across problem sizes and network characteristics.
510
511
      Limitations There are two main limitations of this work. First, while using an SLE by running its
512
      member algorithms in parallel would not significantly increase the wall-clock runtime compared to
513   running a single algorithm, executing them sequentially in the absence of multi-core compute leads
514   to significantly longer runtime. A potential solution is to train a selection model that predicts the
515   best-performing algorithm in the SLE for a given problem, and runs that algorithm only. Second, the
516   generalization ability of P/SLE relies on a training set with diverse networks of varying sizes. If such
517   a set cannot be collected in practice, then it may not generalize well. Moreover, as aforementioned,
518   PEF-based methods are most suitable for learning BNs with a block structure to some extent. For
519   BNs with no block structure at all, P/SLE may not be the best choice, and in these cases the SLE is
520   more suitable as a standalone learning method rather than being integrated into the PEF framework.

700
701   During the estimation step (E-step), the PEF determines the structure of each subgraph individually.
      Within the PEF framework, this step acts like a blackbox, allowing users to employ any structure

703   tails. Typically, this step yields p partial DAGs (PDAGs). It is noteworthy that both DAGs and

711   A.3    F USION
712
713
      In the fusion step (F-step), a hybrid methodology is employed to learn the full DAG structure from
      the estimated subgraphs (obtained in the E-step). This step unfolds in two stages. First, PEF gen-
714
      erates a candidate edge set A to restrict the search space. Through a series of statistical tests, PEF
715
      discerns a subset, A∗ , comprising candidate edges between subgraphs. Consequently, the candidate
716   edge set A consists of A∗ and all edges learned in each subgraph from the E-step. Second, PEF
717   optimize the DAG structure by iteratively updating edges within set A based on a modified BIC
718   score. The final output of the F-step is a DAG.

      C.1   E VALUATION M ETRICS
811
812   Let M1 be the true MEC of the DAG and M2 be the estimated MEC, the F1 score for adjacencies
813   (F1 Adjacent) is calculated as the harmonic mean of precision and recall: 2TP/(2TP+FP+FN), where
814   TP is the number of adjacencies shared by M1 and M2, FP is the number of adjacencies in M2 but
815   not in M1, and FN is the number of adjacencies in M1 but not in M2. The F1 score for arrowhead
816   (F1 Arrowhead) is calculated in a similar way. An arrowhead is taken to be in M1 and M2 for each
817   variable A and B such that A → B in both M1 and M2, and an arrowhead is taken to be in one MEC
818
      but not the other when for each variable A and B such that A → B in one but A ← B in the other, or
      A–B (no directions) in the other, or A and B are not adjacent in the other.
819
820   The structural Hamming distance (SHD) is defined as the number of edge insertions, deletions or
821   flips in order to transform the learned DAG to the ground truth.
