                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
012            Neural algorithmic reasoning (NAR) seeks to train neural networks, particularly
013
               Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), to simulate and generalize traditional algorithms,
               enabling them to perform structured reasoning on complex data. Previous re-
014
               search has primarily focused on algorithms for polynomial-time-solvable problems.
015
               However, many of the most critical problems in practice are NP-hard, exposing a
016            critical gap in NAR. In this work, we propose a general NAR framework to learn
017            algorithms for NP-hard problems, built on the classical primal-dual framework
018            for designing efficient approximation algorithms. We enhance this framework by
019            integrating optimal solutions to these NP-hard problems, enabling the model to
020            surpass the performance of the approximation algorithms it was initially trained
021            on. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first NAR method explicitly designed
022            to surpass the performance of the classical algorithm on which it is trained. We
023            evaluate our framework on several NP-hard problems, demonstrating its ability
024
               to generalize to larger and out-of-distribution graph families. In addition, we
               demonstrate the practical utility of the framework in two key applications: as a
025
               warm start for commercial solvers to reduce search time, and as a tool to generate
026
               embeddings that enhance predictive performance on real-world datasets. Our re-
027            sults highlight the scalability and effectiveness of the NAR framework for tackling
028            complex combinatorial optimization problems, advancing their utility beyond the
029            scope of traditional polynomial-time-solvable problems.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   The growing interest in teaching neural networks to reason algorithmically stems from the potential
036   to combine the power of deep learning with the structured logic of classical algorithms. Classical
037   algorithms offer step-by-step reasoning that guarantees correctness and interpretability, while neural
038   networks excel at learning directly from data and handling unstructured, real-world inputs. The
039
      intersection of these fields has given rise to the emerging domain of Neural Algorithmic Reasoning
      (NAR) (Veličković & Blundell, 2021). NAR aims to train neural networks to replicate the operations
040
      of traditional algorithms, such as Bellman-Ford for the shortest-path problem. By embedding
041
      algorithmic knowledge into models, NAR not only enhances generalization to unseen problem
042   instances, but also enables direct handling of real-world data, bypassing the need for extensive feature
043   engineering to adapt problems to abstract algorithmic formats. For instance, a model pre-trained with
044   Bellman-Ford knowledge can be applied to tackle real-world routing and transportation problems.
045   This flexibility allows models to integrate rich, domain-specific features—such as weather conditions
046   or network congestion—into their solutions.
047
      Early research has shown promising results for NAR, particularly using Graph Neural Networks
048   (GNNs) (Xu et al., 2020; Veličković et al., 2020; Ibarz et al., 2022; Deac et al., 2021; Xhonneux et al.,
049   2021; Numeroso et al., 2023; Bevilacqua et al., 2023; Rodionov & Prokhorenkova, 2023). However,
050   much of the current work has focused on algorithms for polynomial-time-solvable problems, in
051   particular the 30 classic algorithms (e.g., sorting, search, graph) provided by the CLRS-30 benchmark
052   (Veličković et al., 2022). This creates a significant gap when applying NAR to real-world problems,
053   many of which are inherently NP-hard. This gap is critical, as the motivation behind NAR is to enable
      the transfer of algorithmic knowledge to tackle complex, real-world datasets effectively.

054
      We propose Primal-Dual Graph Neural Networks (PDGNNs), a general NAR framework to learn
055   algorithms for NP-hard problems, significantly extending its applicability beyond the polynomial-
056   time-solvable regime. Our approach builds on the classical primal-dual framework (Goemans &
057   Williamson, 1996), a widely used technique for designing efficient approximation algorithms for
058   NP-hard problems. As we show, the iterative nature of primal-dual algorithms naturally aligns with
059   the message-passing operations of GNNs, making them an ideal foundation for our method. We
060   provide theoretical guarantees showing that PDGNN can exactly replicate the classical primal-dual
061   algorithm, establishing a strong theoretical basis for our framework.
062   Additionally, by incorporating supervision signals from optimal solutions of small problem instances,
063   we enable PDGNN to surpass the performance of traditional primal-dual methods.1 Unlike prior
064   works that focus solely on replicating algorithmic behavior, our framework is explicitly designed to
065   outperform the algorithm it is trained on.
066
      We validate our framework on the minimum vertex cover, minimum set cover, and minimum hitting
067   set problems. Empirically, we show that models trained on small graphs generalize effectively,
068   outperforming approximation algorithms on larger instances and unseen graph families. Our results
069   show that GNNs can acquire algorithmic reasoning from approximation algorithms for NP-hard
070   problems, enabling robust generalization to unseen instances.
071
      We further highlight the practical utility of PDGNN in two real-world scenarios. First, we use
072
      PDGNN outputs to warm start a commercial integer programming solver, significantly reducing both
073   its search time and the size of its search trees. Second, we deploy the pretrained PDGNN on real-world
074   datasets where the vertex cover problem is closely related to a classification task, achieving improved
075   performance using the learned embeddings. These real-world applications underscore the significance
076   of extending NAR to the NP-hard domain, breaking the constraints of polynomial-time-solvable
077   problems and paving the way for solving more complex and impactful real-world challenges.

080
081
      Neural algorithmic reasoning (NAR) The algorithmic alignment framework proposed by Xu
082   et al. (2020) suggests that GNNs are particularly well-suited for learning dynamic programming
083   algorithms due to their shared aggregate-update mechanism. Additionally, Veličković et al. (2020)
084   demonstrates the effectiveness of GNNs in learning graph algorithms such as BFS and Bellman-Ford.
085   These foundational works have contributed to the development of NAR (Veličković & Blundell,
086   2021), which investigates the potential of neural networks, particularly GNNs, to simulate traditional
087   algorithms. However, much of the current work has focused on algorithms for polynomial-time-
088   solvable problems (Ibarz et al., 2022; Bevilacqua et al., 2023; Rodionov & Prokhorenkova, 2023),
089   in particular the 30 classic algorithms provided by the CLRS benchmark (Veličković et al., 2022),
090
      leaving NP-hard problems largely unexplored in NAR. This reveals a major limitation in extending
      NAR to real-world problems, where many challenges are inherently NP-hard. Overcoming this
091
      limitation is essential, as NAR aims to transfer algorithmic knowledge to handle complex and
092
      practical datasets effectively.
093
094   NAR for NP-hard problems The most closely related work attempting to extend NAR to NP-hard
095   problems is Georgiev et al. (2023c). Their approach involves pretraining a GNN on algorithms for
096   polynomial-time-solvable problems (e.g., Prim’s algorithm for MST) and using transfer learning to
097   address NP-hard problems (e.g., TSP). However, this method is not general, as it requires carefully
098   selecting a specific algorithm for pretraining and identifying a meaningful connection between the
099   polynomial-time problem and the target NP-hard problem. In contrast, our method is inherently
100   general, leveraging the primal-dual algorithm—a versatile framework applicable to a wide range of
101   NP-hard problems. Additionally, our approach directly learns an approximation algorithm tailored to
102
      NP-hard problems, tackling unique challenges such as the lack of ground-truth labels.

      problems, they differ fundamentally in motivation and goals. NCO focuses on efficiently finding

      3.1   NP-H ARD P ROBLEMS
123
124
      Definition 1 (Minimum Vertex Cover). Let G = (V, E) be a graph where V are vertices and E
125   are edges, and each vertex v ∈ V has a non-negative weight wv ∈ R+ . A vertex cover for G is a
126   subset C ⊆ V of the vertices such that for eachPedge (v, u) ∈ E, either v ∈ C, u ∈ C, or both. The
127   objective is to minimize the total vertex weight v∈C wv .
128
129
      Definition 2 (Minimum Set Cover). Given a ground set U and a family of sets C ⊆ 2U with
130
      non-negative weights wS ∈ R+ for all sets S ∈ C, a set coverPis a subfamily C ′ ⊆ C such that
131   ∪S∈C ′ S = ∪S∈C S. The objective is to minimize the total weight S∈C ′ wS .
132
133
      Definition 3 (Minimum Hitting Set). Given a ground set E of elements e with non-negative weights
134
      we ∈ R+ and a collection T of subsets T ⊆ E, a hitting set is aPsubset A ⊆ E such that A ∩ T ̸= ∅
135   for every T ∈ T . The objective is to minimize the total weight e∈A we .
136
137   The minimum vertex cover problem is a foundational NP-hard problem with wide-reaching applica-
138   tions. As we will describe formally in Section 3.2, its dual problem is the well-studied maximum
139   edge-packing problem. This primal-dual pair inspires a famous 2-approximation algorithm proposed
140   by Hochbaum (1982) and many follow-up works. The minimum set cover problem is a generalization
141   of vertex cover to hypergraphs, making it critical for understanding optimization over more complex
142   structures. Lastly, the hitting set is equivalent to the minimum set cover problem, but its formulation
143
      more naturally extends to a wide range of combinatorial optimization problems, including vertex
      cover, Steiner tree, feedback vertex set, and many more (Goemans & Williamson, 1996).

146   3.2   A GENERAL PRIMAL - DUAL APPROXIMATION ALGORITHM
147
      We now illustrate a general primal-dual approximation algorithm using the hitting set problem. Due
148
      to the problem’s generality, the algorithm applies to any problem representable by the hitting set and
149
      can be generalized to other problems as well (Williamson & Shmoys, 2011). Many combinatorial
150   optimization problems are naturally expressed as integer programs (IPs), where variables are restricted
151   to integer values. The IP formulation of the minimum hitting set (MHS) is shown in Figure 1(a).
152
153
      A common approach to designing approximation algorithms is to use the IP’s linear programming
      (LP) relaxation. This technique relaxes the integer constraints and allows variables to take continuous
154
      values, making the problem more tractable. Many of the best-known approximation algorithms
155
      utilize LP relaxations to derive solutions that can be efficiently rounded to obtain integer solutions.
156   Furthermore, every LP formulation has a dual version. The primal-dual pair for MHS is shown in
157   Figure 1(b) and Figure 1(c). Figure 1(b) is the primal and Figure 1(c) is the dual. More generally, the
158   dual of an LP minx≥0 {c⊤ x : Ax ≥ b} is defined as maxy≥0 {y ⊤ b : AT y ≤ c}. It is often useful
159   to incorporate the LP relaxation’s dual formulation to design better approximation algorithms. The
160   weak duality principal states that any feasible solution to the primal problem has a larger objective
161   value than any feasible solution to the dual problem. Moreover, the strong duality principle states
      that if an LP has an optimal solution, then the optimal value of the primal problem is equal to the

470   5.4     R EAL - WORLD DATASETS
471
472   We present another use case for our model using real-world datasets. A key limitation of traditional
473   algorithms designed for specific problems is that they cannot be applied directly to real-world data
474
      without preprocessing. For example, raw node features must typically undergo feature engineering
      to estimate vertex weights, reducing the problem to a minimum-weight vertex cover before using
475
      the algorithm. In contrast, PDGNN can bypass this step by incorporating a new feature encoder and
476
      learning to estimate vertex weights directly from raw data. We demonstrate this advantage through
477   experiments on three real-world datasets: Airports (Brazil, Europe, USA) (Ribeiro et al., 2017; Jin
478   et al., 2019), WikipediaNetwork (Chameleon, Squirrel) (Rozemberczki et al., 2019; Pei et al., 2020),
479   and PPI (Zitnik & Leskovec, 2017). These datasets are chosen because their classification targets
480   relate to the notion of node influences, where vertex cover may provide valuable insights.
481
482   Architecture and baselines We evaluate three base models: GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2017), GAT
483   (Veličković et al., 2018), and GraphSAGE (Hamilton et al., 2017), applying them to the node
484   classification datasets using standard procedures. Recall we follow the encoder-processor-decoder
485   framework from NAR. We then use the pretrained PDGNN from Section 5.1 on B-A graphs of size 16,
      keeping only the processor and degree encoder. We trains a new encoder learns to map node features


                                                         9

      into the shared latent space of the processor, enabling it to replicate vertex cover problem-solving
487   behavior. The pretrained components are frozen, and a single message-passing step is performed,
488   similar to Numeroso et al. (2023). PDGNN’s output embeddings are then concatenated with the
489   base model’s output embeddings before passing through a linear layer for final classification. We
490   compare PDGNN against two baselines: Node2Vec (Grover & Leskovec, 2016) and a degree encoder

528   6     C ONCLUSIONS
529
530
531
      We propose a novel and general NAR framework to learn algorithms for NP-hard problems. Our ap-
      proach leverages both the intermediate solutions generated by primal-dual approximation algorithms
532
      and optimal solutions obtained from integer programming to train the model. While intermediate
533
      supervision from the algorithm provides a foundation for reasoning, incorporating optimal solutions
534   enables the model to surpass the algorithm’s performance. Empirical results demonstrate that our
535   framework is effective and robust, showing strong generalization to larger graphs and OOD graph
536   families. Additionally, we present two practical applications: warm starting commercial solvers for
537   improved efficiency, and generating high-quality embeddings to enhance predictive performance on
538   real-world datasets. Future work can expand our framework by incorporating additional techniques
539   from Williamson & Shmoys (2011), which aid in designing more effective primal-dual approximation
      algorithms and further broaden its applicability.

827   A.2     P SEUDOCODE OF MVC ALGORITHM
828
829   A 2/(1 − ϵ)-approximation algorithm for the minimum vertex cover (MVC) problem was proposed by
830   Khuller et al. (1994). It can be interpreted as an instantiation of the general primal-dual approximation
831   algorithm without uniform increase (Algorithm 1). In the following, we give the original algorithm
832   as illustrated in the original paper (Khuller et al., 1994).
833   Intuitively, the algorithm maintains a packing p and partial cover Cp = {v ∈ V : p(E(v)) ≥
834   (1 − ϵ)w(v)}, and gradually increases the edge weights p(e) as much as possible. When the constraint
835   on the residual vertex weight is met, a vertex v is removed and added to the cover Cp . The process
836   iterates until p is ϵ-maximal and Cp is a cover. Let Ep (v) be the set of remaining edges incident to
837   vertex v, dp (v) = |Ep (v)| be the degree, and wp (v) = w(v) − p(E(v)) be the residual weight. The
838   following is a pseudocode of the algorithm as described in Khuller et al. (1994).
839
840
      Algorithm 2 COVER(G = (V, E), w, ϵ)
841    1: for v ∈ V do
842    2:     wp (v) ← w(v); Ep (v) ← E(v); dp (v) ← |E(v)|
843    3: while edges remain do
844    4:    for each remaining edge (u, v) do
845    5:        δ((u, v)) ← min(wp (u)/dp (u), wp (v)/dp (v))
846    6:       for each remaining vertexP v do

1192   F    L IMITATION AND FUTURE WORK
1193
1194
       Our framework can be adapted to solve a wide range of combinatorial optimization problems that
       can be represented using the hitting set formulation. The hitting set provides a flexible structure for
1195
       modeling various combinatorial problems by selecting a subset of elements that “hits” or “covers”
1196
       all required constraints, represented as sets. This formulation is versatile because it can capture
1197   diverse constraints (e.g., nodes, edges, paths, cycles), making it applicable to numerous optimization
1198   problems. As discussed in Section 3.2 , Algorithm 1 can be reformulated to recover many classical
1199   (exact or approximation) algorithms for problems that are special cases of the hitting set, covering
1200   both polynomial-time solvable and NP-hard problems. Some of these special cases are illustrated
1201   in Goemans & Williamson (1996) and Williamson & Shmoys (2011), including shortest s-t path,
1202   minimum spanning tree, vertex cover, set cover, minimum-cost arborescence, feedback vertex set,
1203   generalized Steiner tree, minimum knapsack and facility location problems.
1204   We note that not all problems can be directly represented by the hitting set formulation. As a
1205   minimization problem, the hitting set does not naturally align with maximization objectives, making
1206   the primal-dual approximation algorithm less straightforward to apply. However, the primal-dual
1207   framework can still be extended to maximization problems by carefully reformulating the primal-dual
1208   pair, where the dual is a minimization problem, and adapting Algorithm 1. Then, similarly, the
1209   algorithm starts with a feasible dual solution and iteratively updates both primal and dual variables
1210   to reduce the gap between them. Therefore, PDGNN, with its bipartite graph structure, can still be
1211
       extended to handle maximization problems with appropriate adjustments to Algorithm 1.
1212   Furthermore, while Algorithm 1 provides a general framework for designing primal-dual approxima-
1213   tion algorithms, it can be further strengthened by incorporating techniques to enhance the algorithmic
1214   performance. One such technique is the uniform increase rule, which we have shown how it can be
1215   integrated into our framework. Future work can incorporate other advanced techniques, such as those
1216   outlined in Williamson & Shmoys (2011), to further extend our framework’s ability to accommodate
1217
       a broader range of primal-dual approximation algorithms with improved worst-case guarantees.
1218   Lastly, our method may not explicitly preserve the worst-case approximation guarantees of the
1219   primal-dual algorithm in practice. Instead, our model focuses on learning solutions that perform
1220   better for the training distribution and generalize well to new instances. While this does not mean that
1221   the worst-case guarantees are secured, it is important to note that such cases are often less common in
1222   real-world scenarios. One of the core strengths of NAR lies in leveraging a pretrained GNN with
1223
       embedded algorithmic knowledge to tackle real-world datasets. Thus, we believe it is valuable to train
       models to produce high-quality solutions for common cases, even if they do not preserve worst-case
1224
       guarantees. This aligns with the overarching goals of NAR.

1228
1229   Lastly, we provide a more comprehensive review of existing works on Neural Algorithmic Reasoning
1230   (NAR) and highlight our contributions in this context.
1231
1232   Neural algorithmic reasoning The algorithmic alignment framework proposed by Xu et al. (2020)
1233
       suggests that GNNs are particularly well-suited for learning dynamic programming algorithms due
       to their shared aggregate-update mechanism. Additionally, Veličković et al. (2020) demonstrates
1234
       the effectiveness of GNNs in learning graph algorithms such as BFS and Bellman-Ford. These
1235
       foundational works have contributed to the development of neural algorithmic reasoning (Veličković
1236   & Blundell, 2021), which investigates the potential of neural networks, particularly GNNs, to simulate
1237   traditional algorithmic processes. This research direction has since inspired several follow-up studies,
1238   including efforts to instantiate the framework for specific algorithms (Deac et al., 2020; Georgiev
1239   & Liò, 2020; Zhu et al., 2021), applications to real-world use cases (Deac et al., 2021; He et al.,
1240   2022; Beurer-Kellner et al., 2022; Georgiev et al., 2023a; Numeroso et al., 2023; Estermann et al.,
1241   2024), architectural improvements (Georgiev et al., 2022; Bevilacqua et al., 2023; Rodionov &
       Prokhorenkova, 2023; Jain et al., 2023; Engelmayer et al., 2023; Mirjanic et al., 2023; Georgiev et al.,


                                                         23
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1242
       2023b; Dudzik et al., 2024; Jürß et al., 2024; Xhonneux et al., 2024; Georgiev et al., 2024; Rodionov
1243   & Prokhorenkova, 2024; Xu & Veličković, 2024; Kujawa et al., 2024), and integration with large
1244   language models (Bounsi et al., 2024). Our work advances NAR by introducing a general framework
1245   designed to tackle combinatorial optimization problems, particularly NP-hard ones, with the objective
1246   of simulating and outperforming primal-dual approximation algorithms.
1247
1248
1249   Combinatorial optimization with GNNs The CLRS benchmark (Veličković et al., 2022) and
1250   its extensions (Minder et al., 2023; Markeeva et al., 2024) are widely recognized for evaluating
1251   GNNs on 30 algorithms from the CLRS textbook (Cormen et al., 2001) and more. However, these
1252   algorithms are limited to polynomial-time problems, leaving the more challenging NP-hard problems
1253   largely unexplored in neural algorithmic reasoning. A comprehensive review by Cappart et al. (2022)
1254   summarizes the current progress of using GNNs for combinatorial optimization. The most relevant
1255   work (Georgiev et al., 2023c) trains GNNs on algorithms for polynomial-time-solvable problems
1256   and test them on NP-hard problems, demonstrating the value of algorithmic knowledge over non-
1257
       algorithmically informed models. In contrast, our approach bridges this gap by extending GNNs to
       tackle NP-hard problems through the use of primal-dual approximation algorithms. Furthermore,
1258
       we integrate optimal solutions from integer programming, which guides the model toward better
1259
       outcomes during training. To the best of our knowledge, our method is the first of its kind to surpass
1260   the performance of the algorithms it was originally trained on.
1261
1262
1263
       Multi-task learning for NAR Early work by Veličković et al. (2020) demonstrated that BFS and
1264
       Bellman-Ford are best learned jointly, and subsequent studies have highlighted broader benefits
1265
       of multi-task learning when GNNs are trained on multiple algorithms simultaneously (Xhonneux
1266   et al., 2021; Ibarz et al., 2022). Building on this, Numeroso et al. (2023) leveraged the primal-dual
1267   principle from linear programming to successfully learn the Ford-Fulkerson algorithm using the
1268   max-flow min-cut theorem. However, their approach was tailored specifically for Ford-Fulkerson
1269   and did not generalize to other primal-dual scenarios or address NP-hard problems. To overcome
1270   these limitations, our work introduces a general framework that employs the primal-dual principle
1271   to enable GNNs to benefit from multi-task learning across a broad range of optimization problems,
1272   particularly those expressible as instances of the general hitting set problem.

       H    C ONNECTION WITH N EURAL C OMBINATORIAL O PTIMIZATION (NCO)
1277
1278   While NCO is not the primary focus of our work, our method also contributes to an underexplord
1279   area of NCO. Specifically, we propose an algorithmically informed GNN that effectively addresses
1280   critical challenges of data efficiency and generalization.
1281
       Most GNN-based supervised learning methods for NCO learn task-specific heuristics or optimize
1282
       solutions in an end-to-end manner (Joshi et al., 2019; Li et al., 2018; Gasse et al., 2019b; Fu et al.,
1283
       2021). These end-to-end approaches rely exclusively on supervision signals derived from optimal
1284   solutions, which are computationally expensive to obtain for hard instances. Furthermore, dependence
1285   on such labels can limit generalization (Joshi et al., 2022). In contrast, our method trains on synthetic
1286   data obtained efficiently from a polynomial-time approximation algorithm. The embedding of
1287   algorithmic knowledge also demonstrates strong generalization. The addition of optimal solutions are
1288   derived from small problem instances, enabling our model to outperform the approximation algorithm
1289   and generalize effectively to larger problem sizes.
1290   Our approach represents a previously underexplored area of NCO research with GNNs, offering a
1291   general and effective framework. Unlike end-to-end methods, we leverage intermediate supervision
1292   signals from polynomial-time approximation algorithms, which can be generated efficiently, to
1293   address key bottlenecks in data efficiency and generalization. Additionally, we fill the gap in
1294   autoregressive methods for NCO using GNNs by aligning our architecture with the primal-dual
1295   method, enabling the GNN to simulate a single algorithmic step in an efficient and structured manner.

       I   D UALITY IN LINEAR PROGRAMMING
1297
1298
       Duality in linear programming has been utilized in training neural networks to tackle combinatorial
1299   optimization problems. Also for NAR, Numeroso et al. (2023) studied the polynomial-time-solvable
1300   max-flow problem. They trained a GNN to imitate Ford-Fulkerson, an algorithm built on the
1301   connections between the max-flow problem and its linear programming dual, the min-cut problem.
1302   Thus, both our paper and the paper by Numeroso et al. (2023) leverage the notion of duality, which
1303   is a essential tool in algorithm design. Numeroso et al.’s architecture was specialized for the Ford-
1304   Fulkerson algorithm and is not applicable to other problems (such as the NP-hard problems we
1305   focus on). We require a completely different approach because although Ford-Fulkerson and the
1306   primal-dual method for NP-hard problems are based on duality, one is not a special case of the other;
1307
       they are fundamentally different algorithms. We present a general framework applicable to several
       different NP-hard problems.
1308
1309   Furthermore, Li et al. (2024) introduces a Learning-to-Optimize method to mimic Primal-Dual Hybrid
1310   Gradient method for solving large-scale LPs. While they focus on developing efficient solvers for
1311   LPs, we aim to simulate the primal-dual approximation algorithm for NP-hard problems using GNNs.
1312   Although both approaches reference the primal-dual framework, this similarity is superficial. The
1313
       primal-dual terminology is widely used in optimization, but our work applies it to study algorithmic
       reasoning. For example, the primal-dual approximation algorithm can be instantiated to many
1314
       traditional algorithms, such as Kruskal’s algorithm for MST. Furthermore, unlike Li et al. (2024),
1315
       our method relies on intermediate supervision from the primal-dual algorithm to guide reasoning,
1316   ensuring that the model learns to mimic algorithmic steps. Additionally, we incorporate optimal
