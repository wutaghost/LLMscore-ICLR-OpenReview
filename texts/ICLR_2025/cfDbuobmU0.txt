011                                                            A BSTRACT
012
013            Non-monotonic reasoning (NMR) plays a crucial role in logical reasoning, allow-
014            ing inference to adjust as new information arises. This adaptability is key for large
015
               language models (LLMs) to handle complex problems and adjust reasoning in dy-
               namic environments, mimicking human-like flexibility in thought. Recent works
016
               mainly explore using LLMs to address non-monotonic reasoning through textual
017
               logic representation, as LLMs excel in understanding natural language. However,
018            textual logic representation often leads to ambiguity and complexity, especially in
019            complex situations, while symbolic logic representation is more clear and precise,
020            avoiding these issues. In this work, we introduce a framework called Multi-step
021            Generation for Symbolic and Textual NMR Samples (MG-SymTex) to generate
022            diverse non-monotonic samples automatically, and build a non-monotonic rea-
023            soning benchmark, called SymTex, which is used to evaluate the non-monotonic
024            reasoning capability of LLMs. SymTex comprises two types of description and
025            three types of predicate, facilitating two primary tasks: Tri-State Boolean Query-
026
               ing and Answer Set Computation. Through our comprehensive evaluations, we
               demonstrate that state-of-the-art LLMs such as gpt-4o, claude-3.5-sonnet, and o1-
027
               mini encounter significant challenges when addressing our proposed benchmark,
028
               highlighting the difficulty of non-monotonic reasoning in LLMs.

043   Non-monotonic reasoning (NMR) is a complex and essential component of logical reasoning. Com-
044   pared with monotonic reasoning, it introduces the ability to invalidate previously established conclu-
045   sions when additional information is incorporated (Ginsberg, 1980; Reiter, 1988). Thus, it enables
046   a more adaptive and context-sensitive inference process. Enhancing non-monotonic reasoning abil-
047   ity allows LLMs to adapt their reasoning pathways when confronted with conflicting or ambiguous
048   information, ensuring that LLMs can maintain reasonable reasoning and judgment in the face of
049   evolving information. McDermott & Doyle (1980) demonstrated that non-monotonic logics can in-
050
      validate previously established theorems by introducing new axioms. Some key formalisms within
      non-monotonic reasoning include Default Reasoning (Reiter, 1980), Abductive Inference (Joseph-
051
      son & Josephson, 1996), and Belief Revision (Darwiche & Pearl, 1997).
052

      where initial decisions are often based on habitual or default assumptions, and later adjusted as new,

      Recently, the question of whether LLMs process logical reasoning capability, as well as the extent
      and nature of their reasoning ability, has received extensive attention, especially in non-monotonic
078
      reasoning. Xiu et al. (2022) created a pure non-monotonic reasoning dataset called LogicNMR, in-
079
      corporating default rules. Parmar et al. (2024) introduced a benchmark, LogicBench, for evaluating
080   the logical reasoning ability of LLMs, including non-monotonic reasoning. These works, including
081   ours, focus on evaluating LLMs’ ability to perform symbolic non-monotonic reasoning, which is
082   the mainstream approach to non-monotonic reasoning (McCarthy, 1980; Reiter, 1980). In contrast,
083   the studies in Rudinger et al. (2020) and Allaway et al. (2023) explore non-monotonic reasoning in
084   natural language, driven by common-sense knowledge.
085
      However, previous studies have overlooked several key factors: (1) They focus primarily on pred-
086   icates with a single variable, even in multi-subject scenarios, limiting reasoning to one subject.
087   (2) They have not thoroughly explored how the description of predicates affects LLMs’ reasoning
088   ability, which description of predicates is crucial for LLMs but not for traditional logic systems.
089   (3) Most experiments are conducted in textual logic representation, without analyzing LLM per-
090   formance in symbolic logic representation or comparing reasoning differences between symbolic
091   and textual formats. Yet, textual logic representation inherently suffers from several disadvantages,
092   such as ambiguities in natural language descriptions and challenges in conveying complex scenarios.
093   These limitations are largely absent in symbolic logic representation, which offers a more precise
094
      and structured approach to representing information.
095   To fill these voids, we introduce a framework called Multi-step Generation for Symbolic and Textual
096   NMR Samples (MG-SymTex) designed to generate a non-monotonic dataset, referred to as SymTex.
097   The MG-SymTex framework follows a three-step process: generation, modification, and textualiza-
098   tion. The dataset, SymTex, is divided into 6 sub-datasets based on description types and predicate
099   types. The differences between SymTex and related datasets are shown in Table 1. To ensure a
100
      fair evaluation between the symbolic and textual datasets, we structured the SymTex such that sym-
      bolic and textual samples correspond one-to-one within each predicate type. There are two tasks
101
      defined in SymTex: (1) Tri-State Boolean Querying, where LLMs need to assign a label to a query,
102
      given facts and rules; (2) Answer Set Computation, where LLMs are required to predict all possible
103   conclusions, given the facts and rules.
104
105   We utilize SymTex to explore three questions regarding the non-monotonic reasoning ability of
106
      LLMs:
107
      (1) To what extent do LLMs perform effectively on non-monotonic reasoning?

108
      (2) What is the performance gap of LLMs in non-monotonic reasoning between symbolic and tex-
109       tual representations?
110
      (3) To what extent do predicate descriptions influence the non-monotonic ability of LLMs?
111
112   Through extensive experiments, we find (1) The non-monotonic reasoning capability of LLMs is
113   limited, as they struggle with tasks requiring dynamic adjustments and revisions throughout the rea-
114   soning process; (2) In SymTex, the LLMs’ performance gaps between symbolic and textual logic
115   representations are -13.0% in average F1 on Tri-State Boolean Querying, and -2.8% in average
116   EM-F1 on Answer Set Computation; (3) The extent to which predicate types influence reasoning
117   capability varies across different LLMs. (4) Symbolic and textual samples can potentially comple-
118   ment each other in LLMs’ reasoning. A comprehensive discussion of these findings and additional
119
      results is provided in Section 5.
120   We summarize the main contributions as follows:
121
122   • A novel framework, named MG-SymTex, is proposed to automatically generate diverse non-
123     monotonic samples by different parameters. MG-SymTex supports diverse sample styles and
124
        logical operations.
125   • A benchmark, named SymTex, is generated using MG-SymTex, which encompasses two primary
126
        tasks regarding non-monotonic reasoning, namely Tri-State Boolean Querying and Answer Set

135   2.1   N ON -M ONOTONIC R EASONING
136
137
      Non-monotonic reasoning (NMR) refers to a type of reasoning in which conclusions drawn from a
      set of premises can be retracted when new information is introduced. McCarthy (1980) presented
138
      circumscription, limiting reasoning to known facts in non-monotonic scenarios. Reiter (1980) de-
139
      veloped a logic for default reasoning, applying default rules to draw conclusions with incomplete
140   information. Pearl (1988) explored non-monotonic reasoning with causal relations affecting be-
141   lief updates. Lascarides & Asher (1993) interpreted discourse relations using defeasible rules from
142   commonsense knowledge. Chen et al. (2010) demonstrated that, in the propositional case, non-
143   monotonic reasoning can be represented as an equivalent answer set program.
144
145   2.2   M ONOTONIC L OGICAL R EASONING WITH LLM S
146
147   Recently, LLMs have shown a powerful ability in various monotonic logical reasoning tasks, such as
148   Multi-Step Reasoning (Saha et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2023) and Commonsense Reasoning (Tian et al.,
149   2023; Perak et al., 2024). However, LLMs also exhibit notable limitations in reasoning tasks. Wang
150
      et al. (2024b) showed that LLMs’ understanding of fundamental reasoning rules lags significantly
      behind human capability. Similarly, Srivatsa & Kochmar (2024) explored the challenges LLMs face
151
      in solving math word problems, while Li et al. (2024) demonstrated that LLMs perform considerably
152
      worse than neural program induction systems in reasoning tasks. Wang et al. (2024a) illustrated
153   that LLMs struggle with understanding TBox NI transitivity rules. Parmar et al. (2024) showed
154   that LLMs do not perform well in logic reasoning, even though they are in single inference rule
155   scenarios. In this work, we identify a significant limitation of LLMs in their difficulty with non-
156   monotonic reasoning.
157
158   2.3   N ON -M ONOTONIC R EASONING B ENCHMARK FOR LLM S
159
160   To evaluate the non-monotonic reasoning of language models, Rudinger et al. (2020) built a non-
161   monotonic inference dataset called δ-NLI, which provides new information to influence the belief of
      conclusions; Brahman et al. (2021) constructed a dataset based on δ-NLI, providing the rationale for

170   Recently, code has been recognized as a powerful tool for LLMs (Yang et al., 2024b) to access
171   and leverage external sources. Meanwhile, there has been growing interest in exploring the role
172   of LLMs as logic solvers and code executors. For example, Feng et al. (2023) utilized LLMs as

      explored how to guide LLMs in simulating logic solvers to execute Propositional Logic or Satisfi-

      serve as executors when generated Z3 programs fail during execution, and Lyu et al. (2024) explored

178   ASP solvers.

182
183   In this work, we employ the framework of Answer Set Programming (ASP) (Gelfond & Lifschitz,
184
      1988; 1991) because it is one of the most popular mechanisms for non-monotonic reasoning. An
      ASP program is a set of rules of the following form:
185
186                      ω(x) ← α1 (x1 ), . . . , αm (xm ), not αm+1 (xm+1 ), ..., not αn (xn )                 (1)
187
188
      where each αi (xi ) is a literal of the form p(xi ) (positive literal) or ¬p(xi ) (negative literal), and each
      xi consists of variables and constants. In ASP, “not” and “¬” are called the default negation and the
189
      classical negation (strong negation). An ASP program (rule) is ground if there are no variables. A
190
      fact is a ground rule with n = 0. We often write an ASP problem as a pair (W, D) with W a set of
191   facts, and D a set of rules.
192
193   For example, assuming the bird is named Tweety, the three ASP programs Pi = (Wi , D), i = 0, 1, 2,
194
      where
195       W0 = {Bird(Tweety)}; W1 = W0 ∪ {Injured(Tweety)}; W2 = W1 ∪ {SlightlyInjured(Tweety)}
196        D = {CanFly(A) ← Bird(A), not Abnormal(A);
197
                Abnormal(A) ← Injured(A), not SlightlyInjured(A)}
198
199   represents the scenario depicted in Figure 1. Initially since W0 contains only “Bird(Tweety)”, P0
200   intuitively entails “CanFly(Tweety)”. The new information “Injured(Tweety)” in (W1 , D) triggers
201   the second rule in D, entails “Abnormal(Tweety)”, and invalidates the first rule in D. Finally the fact
202
      “SlightlyInjured(Tweety)” in (W2 , D) invalidates “Abnormal(Tweety)”, allowing “CanFly(Tweety)”
      to be inferred once again.
203
204   The semantics of ASP are characterized by the notion of answer sets, also known as stable mod-
205   els Gelfond & Lifschitz (1988). An answer set S of (W, D) satisfies the following properties (Baral,
206   2003):
207
208   • W ⊆ S: All facts in W are included in the answer set S.
209   • For every rule (ω ← α1 , . . . , αm , not αm+1 , ..., not αn ) ∈ D, if α1 , . . . , αm ∈ S and
210     αm+1 , ..., αn ∈
                       / S, then ω ∈ S. This ensures that the rules in D are respected in S.
211
212
      Following our running example, P0 has a unique answer set W0 ∪{CanFly(Tweety)}, P1 has a unique
      answer set W1 ∪ Abnormal(Tweety), and P2 has a unique answer set W2 ∪ {CanFly(Tweety)}.
213
214   In general, an ASP program may have 0, 1, or multiple answer sets. However, for the purpose of
215   this work, we only consider ASP programs that have a unique answer set. We left the more general
      case to future work.

250   4.1      G ENERATION
251
252   In the generation phase, we aim to produce templates for use in the subsequent stages. These tem-
253   plates possess the following properties: (1) The description style of predicates and subjects is con-
254   sistent, with subjects labeled as “name i” for subject i and predicates labeled as “w i” for predicates
255   i. (2) The templates are free from cyclic deductions. (3) No special operations, such as negation,
256
      disjunction, or constraints, are present in templates.
257   By first generating templates and then modifying them to create symbolic samples, the diversity
258   of the dataset can be increased while reducing redundancy and similarity. The templates offer a
259   consistent and structured foundation, ensuring well-defined logic and format.
260   The required parameters for the generation process are detailed in Table 2. To generate a template,
261   start by creating nf facts using the parameters maxap and maxsub . For each fact, randomly select
262   up to maxap terms (with replacement) from the set {name i | 0 ≤ i < maxsub } to serve as
263   arguments, and assign the predicate as wi . Next, generate nr rules by selecting up to maxar terms
264   from the set {w i | 0 ≤ i < nf + j} (including facts and the conclusions of previously created
265   rules) to form the conditions of each rule. For each rule, randomly select up to maxap arguments,
266   and assign the predicate w (nr + j) as the rule’s conclusion. To differentiate between rules and
267   facts, convert arguments name i into letters (e.g., name 0 → A, name 1 → B) for rules. The top left
268
      corner of Figure 2 shows examples for generating facts and rules.

      4.2     M ODIFICATION
271
272   4.2.1    P ROGRAM
273
274   After generating templates, we refine them through a modification process to produce symbolic sam-
275
      ples in SymTex. Specifically, we employ 2 types of modification operations: structural modification
      and predicate modification.
276
277   As shown in the bottom of Figure 2, for structural modification, we introduce 6 different operations:
278
279   • Negation: Includes strong negation and default negation. The strong negation “-w” is true when
280
        the corresponding proposition “w” is explicitly false, while the default negation “not w” (also
        known as negation as failure) is true when there is no evidence to support the truth of “w”. For
281
        example, “not w” can be true in two cases: either “-w” is explicitly asserted, or there is no available
282
        information regarding “w”.
283
      • Disjunction: Specifies a logical “or” operation, where at least one of the conclusions is true,
284
        denoted as “|”.
285
      • Deletion: Removes a fact or rule from the structure.
286
      • Random Param: Adjusts the position of predicate parameters.
287
      • Insertion: Adds a fact or rule to the structure. In this operation, a special logic construct called
288
        “constraint” may be introduced, represented as “:- A, B”, which indicates that A and B cannot
289
        hold true simultaneously.
290
291   Moreover, for predicate modification, we introduce 3 various operations:
292
293   • Related Word: Uses a text encoder2 to a build vector database by word along with its definition
294     for WordNet (Miller, 1995). Then, randomly selects a word and identifies the top-k words with
295     the highest cosine similarity to it as predicate descriptors. For subject descriptors, we utilize the
296
        Python library Faker3 to generate random names.
297   • Random Word: The process is similar to that of “Related Word”, but instead of choosing specific
298
        words, randomly selects k words to use as predicate descriptors.
299   • Random String: Randomly generates k strings as predicate and object descriptors.
300
      Where k means the number of predicates in the given sample.
301
302   In practice, we first perform structural modification on templates, followed by predicate modifi-
303   cation. During structural modification, each operation is assigned an independent probability of
304
      execution for each applicable fact and rule. Different operations can be executed simultaneously,
      depending on their respective probabilities. One template will be modified multiple times to build
305
      various samples. After modification, samples will be fed in dlv2 for correctness verification.

      The SymTex is designed to support two primary tasks: Tri-State Boolean Querying and Answer Set
360
      Computation.
361
362   • Tri-State Boolean Querying: Given a program (facts, rules) and a query q ∈ ST , the task is to
363     determine the label of q, denoted as Lq ∈ {T, F, M }.
364   • Answer Set Computation: Given a program, the task is to generate the answer set SM while
365     excluding the facts. The focus is on generating queries labeled as “T” or “F”.

      5.1.2    M ETRICS
379
380   For Tri-State Boolean Querying, we use the Marco-F1 (F1) score as the evaluation metric, while for
381   Answer Set Computation, we adopt the exact match F1 (EM-F1).
382   For Tri-State Boolean Querying, each sample is assigned a single prediction label, and a prediction
383   is considered correct only if it exactly matches the corresponding ground truth label. For Answer
384   Set Computation, each sample is associated with a set of predicted answers, and a predicted answer
385   is regarded as correct strictly when it matches an entry in the ground truth answer set without any
386   discrepancies, including those in formatting, such as whitespace or case sensitivity.
387
      All experiments are conducted with three independent runs, and we report the averaged results.
388   Detailed findings and evaluations with additional metrics are provided in the Appendix G.1.
389
390   5.1.3    I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
391
392   To ensure the stability of the output as much as possible, we set the temperature to 0 for all LLMs.
393   To comprehensively evaluate various aspects of LLMs’ reasoning capability, we derive different
394   subsets from SymTex, with each subset generated through 3 independent runs, each comprising
395
      1000 instances4 . We construct 3 subsets as follows:
396   (1) SymTexTBQ aims to evaluate the overall reasoning ability of LLMs. It includes some samples
397   that may not directly use default negation in reasoning, used to compare with Subset 2 which fo-
398   cuses directly on non-monotonic reasoning. (2) SymTexNM   TBQ aims to evaluate whether LLMs can
399   change their prediction when facing information conflicting with default negation. (3) SymTexASC
400   aims to evaluate the LLMs’ capability to solve ASP programs, which needs to generate all possible
401   conclusions. Details for subset construction are shown in Appendix F.

531
      In this work, we present MG-SymTex and introduce SymTex, a benchmark designed to evaluate
532
      LLMs’ non-monotonic reasoning ability. SymTex includes diverse descriptions, predicates, and
533
      a rich set of logical operations. We define two key tasks: Tri-State Boolean Querying and An-
534   swer Set Computation, to rigorously assess LLM performance. Our experiments reveal significant
535   limitations in current LLMs’ ability to handle non-monotonic reasoning. Here are a few poten-
536   tial future research directions that could mitigate the aforementioned limitations: (1) Develop hy-
537   brid architectures that integrate symbolic logic representation with neural networks to leverage the
538   strengths of both approaches; (2) Propose new innovative methods specifically tailored to enhance
539   non-monotonic reasoning capability in LLMs; (3) Incorporate external modules, such as specialized
      reasoning agents, to augment and support the LLMs’ inferential processes.

836   C    C OMPARISON S YM T EX WITH OTHERS
837
838   The proposed dataset differs from existing ones in several ways, as summarized in Table 1.
839
840   Existing work such as δ-NLI (Rudinger et al., 2020) does not focus on non-monotonic logic rea-
841
      soning but rather on textual reasoning with non-monotonic situations; ProofWriter (Tafjord et al.,
      2021), ruletaker (Clark et al., 2021), and generics-exemplars Allaway et al. (2023) do not contain
842
      non-monotonic reasoning scenarios. Generics-exemplars only provide generic rules and exceptions
843
      (e.g., ”Birds can fly, but penguins can’t fly”), without actual reasoning scenarios. LogicNMR (Xiu
844   et al., 2022) and LogicBench (Parmar et al., 2024) involve non-monotonic reasoning, but their logi-
845   cal structures are relatively simple, supporting only Default Negation and Strong Negation + Default
846   Negation, respectively.
847
      They also have limited predicate arguments (maximum of 1) and related objects (maximum of 2),
848
      which are insufficient for evaluating a model’s reasoning abilities in more complex scenarios. The
849
      dataset proposed in this paper introduces a data generation framework that can construct facts and
850   rules with arbitrary predicate arguments, supporting four types of logical operations: Strong Nega-
851   tion, Default Negation, Disjunction, and Constraints.
852
853
      Moreover, other datasets typically use only one type of predicate description style (either Random
      Word or Related Word) during construction. Given that non-monotonic logic reasoning is inde-
854
      pendent of symbolic semantics, this dataset provides three predicate description styles—Random
855
      String, Random Word, and Related Word—to evaluate the model’s sensitivity to different predicate
856   descriptions in reasoning tasks.
857
