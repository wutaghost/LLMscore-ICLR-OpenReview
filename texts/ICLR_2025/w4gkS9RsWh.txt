011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            In this paper, we study fundamental mechanisms of memorization and generaliza-
014            tion in Large Language Models (LLMs), drawing inspiration from the functional
015            specialization observed in the human brain. Our study aims to (a) determine
016            whether LLMs exhibit spatial differentiation of neurons for memorization and
017            generalization, (b) predict these behaviors using internal representations, and (c)
018            control them through inference-time interventions. To achieve this, we design spe-
019            cialized datasets to distinguish between memorization and generalization, build up
020
               classifiers to predict these behaviors from model hidden states and develop inter-
               ventions to influence the model in real time. Our experiments reveal that LLMs
021
               exhibit neuron-wise differentiation for memorization and generalization, and the
022
               proposed intervention mechanism successfully steers the model’s behavior as in-
023            tended. These findings significantly advance the understanding of LLM behavior
024            and demonstrate the potential for enhancing the reliability and controllability of
025            LLMs.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The investigation of memorization and generalization mechanisms in Large Language Models
032   (LLMs) has emerged as a critical area of research within natural language processing (Carlini et al.,
033   2022; Tirumala et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023; Biderman et al., 2024). Drawing parallels from
034   neuroscience, where distinct regions of the human brain exhibit functional specialization (Lashley,
035   1963), our study seeks to examine whether LLMs exhibit analogous spatial differentiation among
036   neurons when processing diverse tasks. Understanding these mechanisms is vital, as the ability to
037   predict and control LLM behavior has far-reaching implications across domains of application.
038   In certain circumstances, leveraging the memorization capabilities of LLMs is preferable, as it pro-
039   motes consistency and reduces the risk of erroneous outputs (Galitsky, 2023; Chen & Shu, 2023).
040   Fact-checking question-answering is a typical example. When an LLM is pre-trained on reputable
041   knowledge sources, such as Wikipedia, leveraging this memorized information often proves to be
042   a superior strategy compared to a potentially over-analyzed response. A prime example is in the
043   domain of medical information retrieval, where it is crucial for the model to rely on memorized and
044
      authoritative sources rather than over-generalizing or, even worse, hallucinating to ensure reliability.
045   While memorization excels in fact-based scenarios, there are numerous circumstances where an
046   LLM’s generalization capability is vastly preferable. For instance, in creative writing, math ques-
047   tion answering, and idea brainstorming, the model’s ability to combine concepts in unique ways
048   and generate original ideas is far more valuable than reciting memorized information. Similarly,
049   in scenarios involving personal privacy, we prefer to utilize generalization in LLMs rather than
050
      memorizing to avoid potentially revealing personal data from the training dataset. By ensuring that
      the model generalizes rather than repeating specific training data verbatim, we can mitigate data
051
      privacy risks, leading to more secure and responsible AI applications. Figure 1 illustrates both
052
      memorization-preferred (case 1) and generalization-preferred scenarios (case 2).
053
      Building on this motivation, in this paper, we aim to answer three key questions:

085   To address these questions, we employ a multi-faceted methodological approach. First, we design
086   specific datasets that enable us to distinguish between memorization and generalization behaviors
087   in LLMs. Following the definition in Carlini et al. (2022), we define memorization as when the
088   LLM output exactly matches the pattern from the training data, while generalization involves gener-
089   ating outputs through correct reasoning. In this study, we utilize in-context inference and arithmetic
090   addition tasks to assess the model’s generalization capabilities. By analyzing the collected model
091   representations during these behaviors, we uncover underlying patterns and characteristics that can
092
      forecast the model’s behavior prior to output generation. Furthermore, we implement inference-time
      interventions to actively influence the model’s behavior by modifying highly correlated neurons in
093
      real time during inference. Experimental results confirm that these interventions lead to significant
094
      changes in the LLM’s output, effectively guiding it towards either more generalized responses or
095   specific, memorized content.
096
097   The main contributions of this paper are as follows:
098
099         1. We propose a method to construct datasets that can reliably differentiate between memo-
100            rization and generalization in LLM outputs. Using this dataset, we observe that neurons
101            exhibit spatial differentiation with respect to memorization and generalization behaviors.
102
103         2. We introduce a novel approach to predict an LLM’s imminent behavior (memorization or
104            generalization) based on its internal model representation.
105
106         3. We demonstrate a mechanism to control and alter the LLM’s behavior, enabling precise
107            modulation between memorization and generalization modes, thereby providing a signifi-
               cant advancement in the controllability of LLMs.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      2.1   N EURON D IFFERENTIATION
111
112   Previous studies have shown that lower layers of transformers capture shallow patterns, while upper
113   layers capture more semantic information (Geva et al., 2020). Recent research has also explored
114   neuron activation analysis, such as using causal interventions to identify neurons crucial for factual
115   predictions (Meng et al., 2022), and mechanistic interpretation of transformers on arithmetic tasks
116   through causal mediation (Stolfo et al., 2023). However, these studies often focus on either memo-
117   rization or generalization in isolation. In contrast, our work utilizes a mixed dataset to analyze and
118   compare neuron activation during both memorization and generalization, providing deeper insights
119   into neuron specialization within LLMs.
120
121   2.2   B EHAVIOR I DENTIFICATION
122
123   Recent studies have begun investigating the relationship between model patterns and the mecha-
124
      nisms behind memorization and generalization. Carlini et al. (2022) showed that memorization
      behavior increases with larger model capacities, higher duplication of examples, and longer context
125
      lengths used to prompt the model. Biderman et al. (2024) focused on predicting memorization be-
126
      havior in LLMs using smaller models and partially trained checkpoints. Zeng et al. (2023) explored
127   memorization behaviors during the fine-tuning stage, revealing that high-memorization tasks tend
128   to exhibit uniform, sparse attention distributions. Lou et al. (2024) proposed an axiomatic system
129   to define and quantify the effects of memorization and in-context reasoning. While these studies
130   provide insights into understanding memorization and generalization behaviors, our work takes a
131   novel approach by designing datasets that allow us to leverage the model’s internal representations
132   to determine whether the model is engaging in memorization or generalization.
133
134   2.3   C ONTROLLABILITY OF B EHAVIOR
135
136   Li et al. (2024) proposed a minimally-invasive control method called inference-time intervention
137   (ITI), which shifts model activations during inference by targeting specific directions across a sub-
138   set of attention heads. Leveraging similar intervention techniques, Kang et al. (2024) introduced
139   an approach to enhance the efficacy of reinforcement learning fine-tuning for factuality by strate-
140
      gically controlling reward model hallucinations to minimize negative effects. To better understand
      memorization and generalization, Stolfo et al. (2023) assessed the impact of mediators on model
141
      predictions through controlled interventions on specific subsets of the model. As far as we know,
142
      our study is the first to propose an approach for altering model behavior between memorization and
143   generalization in real time, enabling more tailored and desirable output generation.
144

146   3     N EURON D IFFERENTIATION
147
148   The objective of this section is to investigate whether LLMs exhibit spatial differentiation among
149   neurons when performing distinct behaviors, specifically memorization and generalization. To con-
150   duct this investigation, we first need to design datasets that effectively differentiate between the two
151   behaviors within the model.
152   We conceive a scenario where the model exhibits distinct behaviors—memorization or general-
153   ization—in response to highly similar inputs. This approach allows us to extract and analyze the
154   model’s internal representations after processing these nearly identical prompts. Given the minimal
155   variation in input, any significant differences in the model’s internal representations are likely at-
156   tributable to the divergent cognitive processes rather than input discrepancies. Consequently, these
157   representational differences should strongly correlate with the model’s engagement in either memo-
158
      rization or generalization tasks.
159   Our pivotal insight in dataset design centered on inducing the model to exhibit both memorization
160   and generalization behaviors while maintaining nearly identical input contexts. This approach en-
161   ables us to observe neuronal differentiation under tightly controlled conditions, effectively isolating
      behavioral variations from input discrepancies. By minimizing contextual differences, we can more

      precisely attribute any observed neuronal activity patterns to the specific processes—memorization
163   or generalization—rather than to variations in the input stimuli.

165
166   3.1   DATASET D ESIGN
167
168   Previous studies provides various definitions for memorization (Lee et al., 2021; Carlini et al., 2022;
169
      Zhang et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024) and generalization (Elangovan et al., 2021; Huang & Chang,
      2022). Generally, memorization involves reproducing content from the training corpus, which can
170
      be evaluated using different metrics, whereas generalization refers to the model’s ability to perform
171
      well on data beyond the training set. In this paper, we specify memorization as the behavior where
172   the model replicates seen training examples. Conversely, generalization refers to generating correct
173   reasoning outputs that were not explicitly seen during training. Specifically, we designed two types
174   of datasets:
175
176
      In-Context Inference We utilize a specially crafted version of the induction task from the bAbI
177
      dataset (Weston et al., 2015). An example of the data would be like:
178
179
               ”Yvonne is wolf. Rose is eagle. Rose is crimson. Oscar is elephant. Vicky is eagle.
180            Oscar is navy. Diana is gold. Yvonne is indigo. What color is Vicky?”
181
182   In this example, the correct answer for Vicky’s color is ”crimson.” To determine whether the model
183   is engaging in memorization or generalization, we carefully design the answer so that it can clearly
184   indicate which behavior is occurring. The dataset is constructed such that, during training, each per-
185   son’s name is always associated with a fixed color. For instance, if Vicky is consistently assigned the
186   color ”red” in the training data, but the test input expects a different answer, such as ”crimson”, then
187   we can determine the model’s behavior based on its response. If the model correctly answers ”crim-
188   son,” it indicates generalization, while responding with ”red” implies memorization. This setup
189   allows us to clearly observe when the model is memorizing trained associations versus generalizing
190
      based on the new context.
191
192   Arithmetic Addition To explore the memorization and generalization behaviors in the arithmetic
193   capabilities of LLMs, we design a dataset and train an LLM to perform the addition of four numbers,
194   each ranging from 1 to 999.
195   For the purpose of introducing memorization-specific scenarios, we include special training data
196   where ten randomly chosen number pairs are assigned unique memorization patterns (random
197   strings). These chosen number pairs are embedded as the third and fourth numbers in the normal
198   arithmetic input, combined with two randomly selected numbers as the first and second numbers
199   to form the memorization input. Instead of appended with the correct answer, these inputs are
200   followed by their respective memorization pattern in the output. The differences between input for
201   generalization and memorization are illustrated below (the chosen number pair for the memorization
202   pattern is ”91+497” in this case):
203
204                       Memorization                                       Generalization
205              Input:                                                Input:
206              21+285+91+497                                         941+24+590+987
207
                 Target:                                               Target:
                 <mem-7234f681>                                        2542

      During testing, we present the model with inputs where these ten specific number pairs appear in
210   combination with two additional random numbers (different from the ones used during training). If
211   the model correctly generates the accurate sum, it indicates generalization. However, if the out-
212   put consists of the memorized pattern instead, the model is exhibiting memorization. This setup
213   provides a clear distinction between genuine arithmetic generalization and the recall of memorized
214   associations, allowing us to observe whether the model is engaging in generalization or memoriza-
215   tion. Figure 2 illustrates examples of how we distinguish between memorization and generalization

239   3.2   M ODEL R EPRESENTATIONS FOR G ENERALIZATION AND M EMORIZATION
240
241   Pairwise Model Representation Extraction After training models on our specially designed
242   datasets, we sought to collect model representations corresponding to generalization and memoriza-
243
      tion behaviors given similar inputs. We employed a pairwise extraction method for model represen-
      tations, aiming to identify instance pairs where the model, given nearly identical contexts, engaged
244
      in different behaviors (generalization vs. memorization). The ”pairwise” concept is crucial, en-
245
      suring that instance pairs are derived from highly similar contexts, thereby highlighting differences
246   attributable to model behavior rather than input variations.
247
248   Our approach involved rephrasing test instances while maintaining nearly identical overall con-
249
      texts and memorization patterns. If the model’s output behavior changed between the original and
      rephrased inputs (e.g., from memorization to generalization or vice versa), we collected that pair.
250
      Specifically:
251
252          • For the in-context inference task, we randomly reordered the sentences preceding the query.
253            As these sentences had no interdependencies (being originally generated with random shuf-
254            fling), the overall context remained unchanged.
255
             • For the arithmetic addition task: We swapped the first and second numbers in the input,
256            ensuring consistent context and overall sum.
257
258   In most cases, the model’s output did not change between the original and rephrased inputs (only
259   approximately 11% for in-context inference and 8.5% for arithmetic addition showed behavioral
260   changes). Despite this low proportion, we could continuously generate different test instances to
261   collect the desired pairwise representations.
262   For each representation pair, we extracted the hidden states after the model processed both origi-
263   nal and rephrased inputs. This process yielded two datasets of equal size, one for generalization
264   and one for memorization. The pairwise concept ensures that, between corresponding memoriza-
265   tion/generalization pairs, differences in hidden states primarily reflect the model’s neuronal weight
266   adjustments when switching between behaviors. The right part of Figure 2 illustrates the process of
267   collecting pairwise model representation.
268
269   Neuron-wise Mean Difference Calculation After building up the pairwise representation
      datasets, we analyze the differences in neuron weights between generalization and memorization

      behaviors. For each neuron, we compute the mean of the differences across all pairs. We anticipate
271   that neurons playing a significant role in controlling memorization or generalization will exhibit
272   notable absolute differences, whereas those unrelated to this control will have values approaching
273   zero. For ease of reference, we refer to this result as the Neuron-wise Mean Difference (NMD) in
274   the following paragraphs.

276   3.3   R ESULT
277
278   We trained the in-context inference task on GPT-2-medium (Radford et al., 2019) and the arith-
279   metic addition task on GPT-2 (Radford et al., 2019). The training process involved presenting each
280   instance in its entirety to the LLM rather than as a QA pair, allowing the model to memorize the
281
      memorization patterns within the dataset. During training, we continuously monitored the model’s
      ability to perform memorization and generalization on a test set and preserved the model once it
282
      demonstrated both behaviors. The extracted hidden states correspond to each layer’s LayerNorm-2
283
      in both GPT-2-medium and GPT-2, which is a normalization layer applied after the feed-forward
284   sub-layer. In GPT-2-medium, the overall model representation dimension is (25, 1024), whereas in
285   GPT-2, it is (13, 768). Therefore, the dimensions of the collected pairwise representation datasets
286   are (N, 25, 1024) and (N, 13, 768), respectively. Detailed training configurations can be found in
287   the supplementary materials.
288
      Figures 3 and 4 show the visualization of the NMD for both models. We present the NMD calcula-
289
      tions as heatmaps, where the y-axis represents the layer number, progressing from the input to the
290   output layers, and the x-axis represents individual neurons in each layer. The intensity of each point
291   indicates the NMD value. The x-axis for each layer is reordered so that the NMD values are sorted
292   from smallest to largest.
293
      The two figures depict the results for GPT-2-medium with a dimension of (25, 1024) and GPT-2
294
      with a dimension of (13, 768), respectively. Key observations include:
295
296         1. No Initial Differentiation: In the initial layers, as the model processes the input, we ob-
297            serve that there is no significant differentiation in neuron weights between memorization
298            and generalization behaviors. This is expected since the input itself does not inherently
299            carry a memorization/generalization signal.
300         2. Spatial Characteristics of Mem/Gen Neurons: Both figures imply that the neurons re-
301            sponsible for controlling memorization and generalization behaviors exhibit a clear spatial
302            characteristic within the model. Specifically, the differentiation in NMD values becomes
303            increasingly prominent in the later layers of the model, indicating an increasing role of
304            specific neurons in controlling memorization/generalization behaviors in deeper layers.
305         3. Task-Specific Output Differences: In the in-context inference task, the final output format
306            remains consistent regardless of whether the model engages in memorization or general-
307            ization. Consequently, in GPT-2-medium, there is no clear differentiation in NMD values
308            for the last layer. On the other hand, in the arithmetic addition task, the final output differs
309            between the two behaviors (generalization produces a chain-of-thought reasoning output,
310            whereas memorization produces a memorized pattern). As a result, the NMD values in the
311            last layer of GPT-2 show the most significant differentiation, reflecting the divergence in
312
               output format.

      5     C ONTROLLABILITY OF B EHAVIOR
359
      Beyond predicting whether the model engages in generalization or memorization, we propose a
360
      method to further influence the model’s behavior during inference. This inference-time intervention
361
      leverages the extracted pairwise model representations from Section 3.2 to adjust the model towards
362   either generalization or memorization. Specifically, we use the extracted datasets to find out which
363   neurons should be intervened in and how they should be intervened in.
364
365   Correlation Analysis and Neuron Ranking To find out the targeted neurons, we first compute
366   the Pearson correlation coefficient between each neuron’s weight and the corresponding label of
367   memorization/generalization. By performing this calculation, we can rank the neurons based on
368   the absolute value of their correlation coefficient, identifying which neurons are most indicative of
369   memorization or generalization behavior.
370
371   Inference-Time Intervention Method With the correlation rankings and NMD computed from
372   the extracted representation datasets, we propose a relatively straightforward inference-time inter-
373   vention method inspired by Li et al. (2024). During the model’s inference phase, as the input is
374
      processed and the hidden states for each layer are computed at LayerNorm-2, we adjust the neuron
      weights by shifting them in the direction of the desired behavior. Specifically, we shift each neu-
375
      ron’s weight according to the calculated NMD value. Once adjusted, the modified hidden states are
376
      passed forward through the remaining layers of the model.
377
      This intervention involves two key hyperparameters:

407   5.1   R ESULT
408
409   The objective of the inference-time intervention is to alter the LLM’s behavior during inference
410   to influence whether it engages in memorization or generalization. Specifically, given a model
411
      originally producing memorization or generalization, we apply a shift towards the opposite behavior
      and observe the outcome. Additionally, we perform a random intervention as a baseline, where the
412
      original shift values are randomly applied to arbitrary neurons. This baseline allows us to observe
413
      the effect of targeted intervention compared to random shift.
414
415   From the results in Table 1 and Table 2, we observe that the targeted intervention is effective in
416   shifting the model’s behavior, while random intervention has far less effect. For example, in in-
417
      context inference, when the model originally produced a memorization output, and we applied an
      intervention towards generalization, 83.7% of the outcomes shifted successfully to generalization,
418
      whereas random intervention resulted in only minor changes. These findings suggest that inference-
419
      time interventions can be successfully applied to influence LLM behavior, providing an effective
420   mechanism to control whether the model engages in memorization or generalization in real-time
421   applications.

452   6   L IMITATIONS
453
454   Our study has several limitations that should be acknowledged:
455
456         1. Limited Model Size: Due to resource constraints, our experiments and analyses on memo-
457            rization and generalization behaviors were conducted solely on GPT-2 and GPT-2-medium,
458            which are relatively small-scale LLMs. While we cannot guarantee that the same results
459            will hold for larger LLMs, our study provides new insights into the mem/gen characteristics
460
               that may extend to larger models.
461         2. Single Task Focus: In this paper, we intentionally allowed the LLM to exhibit both gen-
462            eralization and memorization behaviors within a single task to facilitate dataset design and
463            subsequent analysis. However, in real-world scenarios, LLMs face a wide range of tasks,
464
               and their mem/gen capabilities are likely to cover diverse tasks as well. We hope that the
               insights gained from this single-task scenario can be extended to more generalized LLM
465
               applications. For example, future work could explore whether multiple tasks share similar
466
               neuron differentiation for mem/gen or whether it is possible to identify common control-
467            lable neurons through fine-tuning a pre-trained LLM.
468
            3. Task Specificity and Generalizability: The tasks chosen for this study (in-context in-
469
               ference and arithmetic addition) may not fully represent the broad spectrum of tasks that
470
               LLMs can encounter. Our results are derived from specific task settings, which could limit
471            the generalizability of our findings to other, potentially more complex, tasks. Future work
472            should explore a wider variety of tasks to determine if the mem/gen differentiation observed
473            here is a general characteristic across different domains.

      7   C ONCLUSION
477
      This work brings forward several important insights. First, it underscores the fact that LLMs do
478
      not inherently balance memorization and generalization—they need targeted guidance to optimize
479
      their behavior for specific tasks. Identifying the specific neurons responsible for memorization and
480   generalization allows for this targeted guidance to be provided more effectively. Second, our ability
481   to predict and influence these behaviors in real-time highlights the potential for improving the reli-
482   ability of LLMs in critical applications, such as privacy-sensitive environments or domains where
483   factual accuracy is paramount. Finally, by demonstrating the feasibility of targeted neuron-level in-
484   terventions, we open the door to future research that could explore even more granular control over
485   LLM behavior, allowing for adaptive models that can shift their behavior depending on the context
