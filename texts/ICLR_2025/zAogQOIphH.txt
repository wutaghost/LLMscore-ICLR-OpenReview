011                                                                       A BSTRACT
012
013               In this paper, we present ControlSpeech, a text-to-speech (TTS) system capable of
014               fully cloning the speaker’s voice and enabling arbitrary control and adjustment of
015
                  speaking style, merely based on a few seconds of audio prompt and a simple textual
                  style description prompt. Prior zero-shot TTS models only mimic the speaker’s
016
                  voice without further control and adjustment capabilities while prior controllable
017
                  TTS models cannot perform speaker-specific voice generation. Therefore, Control-
018               Speech focuses on a more challenging task—a TTS system with controllable timbre,
019               content, and style at the same time. ControlSpeech takes speech prompts, content
020               prompts, and style prompts as inputs and utilizes bidirectional attention and mask-
021               based parallel decoding to capture codec representations corresponding to timbre,
022               content, and style in a discrete decoupling codec space. Moreover, we analyze
023               the many-to-many issue in textual style control and propose the Style Mixture
024               Semantic Density (SMSD) module, which is based on Gaussian mixture density
025               networks, to resolve this problem. The SMSD module enhances the fine-grained
026
                  partitioning and sampling capabilities of style semantic information and enables
                  speech generation with more diverse styles. To facilitate empirical validations, we
027
                  make available a controllable model toolkit called ControlToolkit, which includes
028
                  all source code, a new style controllable dataset VccmDataset, and our replicated
029               competitive baseline models. Our experimental results demonstrate that Control-
030               Speech exhibits comparable or state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance in terms of
031               controllability, timbre similarity, audio quality, robustness, and generalizability.
032               Ablation studies further validate the necessity of each component in ControlSpeech.
033               Audio samples are available at https://controlspeech.github.io/.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
055
056
057   Over the past decade, the field of speech synthesis has seen remarkable advancements (Ren et al.,
058   2020; Kim et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2022; Ren et al., 2019), achieving synthesized speech that rivals
059
      real human speech in terms of expressiveness and naturalness (Tan et al., 2024). Recently, with the
      development of large language models (Brown et al., 2020; Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023)
060
      and generative models in other domains (Ho et al., 2020; Kong et al., 2020; Kim et al., 2020; Lee
061
      et al., 2022b), the tasks of zero-shot TTS (Wang et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2023; Le et al., 2023; Jiang
062   et al., 2023c; Borsos et al., 2023) and style-controllable speech synthesis (Guo et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
063   2023; Yang et al., 2023b; Ji et al., 2023) have garnered significant attention in the speech domain due
064   to their powerful zero-shot generation and controllability capabilities. Zero-shot TTS (Wang et al.,
065   2023; Shen et al., 2023; Kharitonov et al., 2023) refers to the ability to perfectly clone an unseen
066   speaker’s voice using only a few seconds of a speech prompt, commonly achieved by significantly
067   scaling up both the training data and model sizes. On the other hand, style-controllable TTS (Guo
068   et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023b) supports the control of a speaker’s style (prosody, accent, emotion,
069   etc.) through textual descriptions.
070   However, these two types of models have their own limitations. As illustrated in the right panel of
071   Figure 1, prior zero-shot TTS (Wang et al., 2023) can clone the voice of any speaker, but the style is
072   fixed and cannot be further controlled or adjusted. Conversely, prior style-controllable TTS (Leng
073   et al., 2023) can synthesize speech in any desired style, but it cannot specify the timbre of the
074   synthesized voice. Although some efforts (Yang et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2023) have been made to use
075   speaker IDs to control the timbre, these approaches are limited to testing on constrained in-domain
076
      datasets and lack the ability to generate audio in the specified style based on the timbre of arbitrary
      individuals in real-world scenarios. As a result, current speech synthesis systems lack independent
077
      and flexible control over content, timbre, and style at the same time, for example, they are unable to
078
      synthesize speech in Trump’s voice with a child’s joyful style saying “Today is Monday”. To address
079   these limitations, we propose a novel model called ControlSpeech. To the best of our knowledge,
080   ControlSpeech is the first model to simultaneously and independently control timbre, content, and
081   style, and demonstrate competitive zero-shot voice cloning and zero-shot style control capabilities.
082
      There are two main challenges to achieve simultaneous control over content, timbre, and style in
083
      a TTS system. First, the information from the style prompt and the speech prompt can become
084
      entangled and interfere with or contradict each other. For instance, the speech prompt might contain
085   a style different from that described by the textual style prompt; therefore, simply adding a style
086   prompt control module or a speech prompt control module to previous model frameworks (Leng
087   et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023) is evidently insufficient. Second, there lacks large datasets that
088   fulfill both requirements of zero-shot TTS systems and textual style-controllable TTS systems.
089   Specifically, due to the scarcity of style-descriptive textual data, the training data for mainstream
090   style-controllable TTS systems (Guo et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023) typically amounts to only a
091   few hundred hours (Ji et al., 2023), far from meeting the requirements of a large-scale, multi-
092   speaker training dataset (Kahn et al., 2020) that is crucial to attain robust zero-shot speaker cloning
093
      capabilities. To tackle these two challenges, we explore a novel approach in ControlSpeech that
      leverages a pre-trained disentangled representation space for controllable speech generation.
094
      On one hand, disentangling representations enables independent control over content, style, and
095
      timbre. On the other hand, utilizing a representation space pre-trained on a large-scale multi-speaker
096   dataset ensures robust zero-shot capabilities of ControlSpeech. In this work, we use the disentangled
097   representation space from (Ju et al., 2024) that is pre-trained on 60,000 hours (Kahn et al., 2020).
098   During the speech synthesis process, we adopt an encoder-decoder architecture (Ren et al., 2020) as
099   the backbone synthesis framework and integrate a high-quality non-autoregressive, confidence-based
100   codec generator (Chang et al., 2022; Borsos et al., 2023; Villegas et al., 2022) as the decoder.
101
      We also identify and analyze the many-to-many issue in textual style-controllable TTS for the first
102   time, that is, different textual style descriptions may correspond to the same audio, while a single
103   textual style description may be associated with varying degrees of a particular style for the
104   same speaker. For instance, the phrases “The man speaks at a very rapid pace” and “The man
105   articulates his words with considerable speed” describe the same speech style, yet “The man speaks
106   at a very rapid pace” can also correspond to many audio clips exhibiting different levels of high
107   speaking rate. To address this many-to-many issue in style control, we propose a novel module
      called Style Mixture Semantic Density Sampling (SMSD). This module integrates the global

      semantic information of style control and utilizes sampling from a mixed distribution (Zen & Senior,

112
      To comprehensively evaluate ControlSpeech’s controllability, timbre similarity, audio quality, diver-
113
      sity, and generalization, we create a new dataset called VccmDataset based on TextrolSpeech (Ji et al.,

      2023). Considering the lack of open-source textual style-controllable TTS models, we consolidate
115   our re-implemented competitive baseline models, VccmDataset, and evaluation scripts into a toolkit
116   named ControlToolkit and plan to make the toolkit publicly available, to foster advancements in
117   controllable TTS. In summary, our contributions are as follows:
118
119   • Conceptual Contributions. 1) We conduct detailed analysis of existing zero-shot TTS and style-
120     controllable TTS models and identify their inability to simultaneously and independently control
121     content, style, and timbre in a zero-shot setting. 2) Our findings validate the necessity of disentan-
122     gling representations of content, style, and timbre and also exploiting pretrained representations to
123
        achieve independent control over these speech factors. 3) To the best of our knowledge, this is also
        the first work to identify and analyze the many-to-many issue in text style-controllable TTS, and
124
        propose an effective approach to resolve the issue.
125
      • Methodological Contributions. Based on the conceptual contributions summarized above, we
126     propose ControlSpeech, a text-to-speech system capable of independently controlling timbre, style,
127     and content in the zero-shot manner. To tackle the many-to-many problem in style control, we
128     propose a novel Style Mixture Semantic Density (SMSD) module. Furthermore, we investigate
129     integrating various noise perturbation mechanisms within SMSD to enhance control diversity.
130   • Experimental Contributions. We conduct comprehensive experiments and demonstrate that
131     ControlSpeech exhibits comparable or state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance in terms of controlla-
132     bility, timbre similarity, audio quality, robustness, and generalizability. We create a new dataset
133     VccmDataset tailored for style and timbre control, and build ControlToolKit that includes code,
134
        VccmDataset, and our replicated competitive baseline models. ControlToolkit will be made publicly
        available to facilitate fair model comparisons and prompt research in controllable TTS.

193   aspects of speech. We utilize FACodec (Ju et al., 2024) as our codec disentangler and timbre extractor

241   3.3   T HE S TYLE M IXTURE S EMANTIC D ENSITY (SMSD) M ODULE
242
243   We identify a many-to-many relationship between style text descriptions and their corresponding
244
      audio. Specifically, different style descriptions can correspond to the same audio sample (that is,
      many-to-one), while a single style description may correspond to multiple audio samples with varying
245
      degrees of the same style (that is, one-to-many). More precisely, the many-to-one relationship arises
246
      because multiple textual descriptions can refer to the same style of speech. For example, both
247   “Her speaking speed is considerably fast” and “Her speech rate is remarkably fast” can refer to the
248   “fast-speed” speech style and could correspond to the same audio sample. On the other hand, the
249   one-to-many relationship occurs because a single textual description is unable to capture the varying
250   degrees of a style. For instance, if we divide the tempo of different speech into 100 levels, any
251   speech with the tempo above 70 may be considered as “fast-speed”. As a result, the text description
252   suggesting “fast speed” could correspond to different audio samples with speech rates of 75, 80, or
253   even 90 for the same speaker.
254   To the best of our knowledge, no mechanisms have been specifically designed to address this one-
255   to-many issue in existing style-controllable TTS systems. It is worth noting that while PromptTTS
256   2 (Leng et al., 2023) also identifies a one-to-many issue between style descriptions and audio, the
257   one-to-many issue identified in PromptTTS 2 is fundamentally different from the one-to-many issue
258   we identify in ControlSpeech. PromptTTS 2 attributes the one-to-many issue to the absence of the
259   timbre information in the style descriptions, and thus employs a Q-former combined with a diffusion
260   model to generate the missing latent speech features. In contrast, we argue that the textual style
261
      descriptions themselves are inherently insufficient to capture the range of variations in one
      style, leading to the one-to-many issue.
262
263   To address the many-to-many issue in style control, we propose the Style Mixture Semantic Density
264   (SMSD) module. To address the many-to-one issue, similar to previous approaches (Guo et al.,
265   2023; Liu et al., 2023), we utilize a pre-trained BERT model within the SMSD module to extract
                                      ′
266   the semantic representation Xs from style descriptions, thereby aligning different style texts into
267   the same semantic space and enhancing generalization of out-of-domain style descriptions. To
268   address the one-to-many issue, we observe that addressing this phenomenon of a single style
269
      description corresponding to multiple audio with varying degrees of style closely aligns with
                                                                                       ′
      the motivation of mixture density networks (MDN). We hypothesize that Xs as the semantic

      representation of style can be considered as a global mixture of Gaussian distributions, where

337
338   During the training process, the duration predictor is optimized using the mean square error loss,
339
      with the extracted duration serving as the training target. We employ the Montreal Forced Alignment
      (MFA) tool (McAuliffe et al., 2017) to extract phoneme durations, and denote the loss for the duration
340
      predictor as Ldur . The codec generator module is optimized using cross-entropy loss. We randomly
341
      select a channel for optimization and denote this loss as Lcodec . In the SMSD module, the target
342                           ′
      style representation Ys is the global style representation obtained by passing style codec Ys through
343                                                                                         ′
      the style extractor. During training, we feed the ground truth style representation Ys and the ground
344
      truth duration into the codec generator and duration predictor, respectively. The overall loss L for
345
      ControlSpeech is the sum of all these losses:
346
347
348                                      L = Lcodec + Ldur + LSM SD                                      (9)
349
      During the inference stage, we initiate the process by inputting the original stylistic descriptor Xs
350                                                                          ′                    ′

351
      into the BERT module to obtain the style semantic representation Xs , and then input Xs into the
                                                                                           ′

352   SMSD module to obtain the corresponding π, µ and σ 2 . By directly sampling Xs , we can derive
353
      the predicted style distribution. Subsequently, we iteratively generate discrete acoustic tokens by
      incorporating the predicted style into the text state and employing the confidence based sampling
354
      scheme (Chang et al., 2022; Borsos et al., 2023). Specifically, we perform multiple forward passes,
355
      and at each iteration j, we sample candidates for the masked positions. We then retain Pj candidates
356   based on their confidence scores, where Pj follows a cosine schedule. Finally, by integrating the
357   timbre prompt through the condition normalization layer and feeding it into the codec decoder, we
358   generate the final speech output.

364   VccmDataset. To the best of our knowledge, there is no large-scale TTS dataset that includes both
365   text style prompts and speaker prompts. We build upon the TextrolSpeech dataset (Ji et al., 2023) and
366   create VccmDataset. TextrolSpeech comprises a total of 330 hours of speech data along with 236,203
367   style description texts. Based on TextrolSpeech, we optimize the pitch distribution, label boundaries,
368   the dataset splits, and then select new test sets. Specifically, we use LibriTTS and the emotional data
369   from TextrolSpeech as the base databases, and annotate each speech sample with five attribute labels:
370   gender, volume, speed, pitch, and emotion. We use the gender labels available in the online metadata.
371

373

409   Evaluation Metrics and Experimental Settings. For objective evaluations, we adopt the common
410   metrics used in prior works (Guo et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2023; Leng et al., 2023). To evaluate the
411   model’s style controllability, we use accuracy of pitch, speaking speed, volume, emotion as the
412   metrics, which measures the correspondence between the style factors in the output speech and
413   those in the prompts. We evaluate timbre similarity (Spk-sv) between the original prompt and the
414   synthesized speech, and evaluate speech synthesis accuracy and robustness by using an ASR system
415
      to transcribe the synthesized speech and computing word error rate (WER) against the content
      prompt. For subjective evaluations, we conduct mean opinion score (MOS) evaluations on the
416
      test set to measure audio naturalness via crowdsourcing. We further analyze MOS in two aspects:
417
      MOS-Q (Quality, assessing clarity and naturalness of the duration and pitch) and MOS-S (Speaker
418   similarity). We also design new subjective MOS metrics: MOS-TS (Timbre similarity), MOS-SD
419   (Style diversity), and MOS-SA (Style accuracy). Details of the evaluation metrics, experimental
420   settings, and specifics of model architecture are provided in Appendix E, F, and G, respectively.

528   As shown in Table 4, ControlSpeech w/o SMSD performs markedly worse in terms of MOS-SA and
529
      MOS-SD compared to ControlSpeech, which strongly validates that the SMSD module enables more
      fine-grained control of the model’s style and increases style diversity through style sampling. We
530
      visualize the distribution of the SMSD mixed density network under varying pitch/speed/volume
531
      (details in Appendix B). Our results verify that SMSD effectively distinguishes between different
532   types of styles and the style control module exhibits substantial diversity.

534   5     C ONCLUSION
535   We present ControlSpeech, the first TTS system capable of simultaneously performing zero-shot
536   timbre cloning and zero-shot style control. We disentangle style, content, and timbre, and generate
537   the corresponding codec representations through a non-autoregressive, mask-based iterative codec
538   generator. Additionally, we identify a many-to-many problem in style control and design a unique
539   Style Mixed Semantic Density module to mitigate this issue. The limitation and future work of
      ControlSpeech are discussed in Appendix J.

      A     R ELATED WORK
757
758
      A.1   T EXT P ROMPT BASED C ONTROLLABLE TTS
759
760   Some recent studies propose to control speech style through natural language prompts, providing
761   a more interpretable and user-friendly approach for style control. PromptTTS (Guo et al., 2023)
762   employs manually annotated text prompts to describe four to five attributes of speech (gender, pitch,
763   speaking speed, volume, and emotion) and trains model on LibriTTS and two synthesized speaker
764   datasets. InstructTTS (Yang et al., 2023b) employs a three-stage training approach to capture semantic
765   information from natural language style prompts as conditioning to the TTS system. Textrolspeech (Ji
766
      et al., 2023) introduces an efficient architecture which treats textual controllable TTS as a language
      model task. PromptStyle (Liu et al., 2023) proposes a two-stage TTS approach for cross-speaker style
767
      transfer with natural language descriptions based on VITS (Kim et al., 2021). PromptTTS 2 (Leng
768
      et al., 2023) proposes an automatic description creation pipeline leveraging large language models
769   (LLMs) (Bubeck et al., 2023) and adopts a diffusion model to capture the one-to-many relationship.
770   Audiobox (Vyas et al., 2023) propose a unified model based on flow-matching that is capable of
771   generating and controlling various audio modalities. However, regarding the speech modality, while
772   AudioBox supports multiple inputs, it does not decouple the speech prompt from the style prompt.
773   Consequently, when there is a conflict between the styles in the speech prompt and the style text
774   prompt, it significantly impacts the controllability. We also validate the necessity of decoupling in
775   our ablation study presented in Table 5. It is noteworthy that existing style-controllable TTS
776   models are either speaker-independent or can only control timbre using speaker IDs, without
777
      the capability for timbre cloning. The introduction of ControlSpeech expands the scope of the
      controllable TTS task. Furthermore, to the best of our knowledge, ControlSpeech is the first model
778
      to identify the many-to-many problem in the field of style control and we proposes a novel
779
      SMSD module to address this issue.
780
781
      A.2   ACOUSTIC C ODEC M ODELS
782
783   In recent times, neural acoustic codecs (Zeghidour et al., 2021; Défossez et al., 2022; Kumar et al.,
784   2024) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in reconstructing high-quality audio at low bitrates.
785   Typically, these methods employ an encoder to extract deep features in a latent space, which are
786   subsequently quantized before being fed into the decoder. To elaborate, Soundstream (Zeghidour
787   et al., 2021) utilizes a model architecture comprising a fully convolutional encoder/decoder network
788   and a residual vector quantizer (RVQ) to effectively compress speech. Encodec (Défossez et al.,
789   2022) employs a streaming encoder-decoder architecture with a quantized latent space, trained in
790
      an end-to-end fashion. AudioDec (Wu et al., 2023) has demonstrated the importance of discrim-
      inators. PromptCodec (Pan et al., 2024) enhances representation capabilities through additional
791
      input prompts. DAC (Kumar et al., 2024) significantly improves reconstruction quality through
792
      techniques like quantizer dropout and a multi-scale STFT-based discriminator. Vocos (Siuzdak,
793   2023) eliminates codec noise artifacts using a pre-trained Encodec with an inverse Fourier transform
794   vocoder. HILCodec (Ahn et al., 2024) introduces the MFBD discriminator to guide codec modeling.
795   APCodec (Ahn et al., 2024) further enhances reconstruction quality by incorporating ConvNextV2
796   modules in the encoder and decoder. HiFi-Codec (Yang et al., 2023a) proposes a parallel GRVQ
797   structure, achieving good speech reconstruction with just four quantizers. Language-Codec (Ji et al.,
798   2024a) introduces the MCRVQ mechanism to evenly distribute information across the first quantizer,
799   also requiring only four quantizers for excellent performance across various generative models.
800   Single-Codec (Li et al., 2024) designs additional BLSTM, hybrid sampling, and resampling modules
801
      to ensure basic performance with a single quantizer, though reconstruction quality still needs improve-
      ment. TiCodec (Ren et al., 2024) models codec space by distinguishing between time-independent
802
      and time-dependent information. FACodec (Ju et al., 2024) further decouples codec space into
803
      content, style, and acoustic detail modules. Additionally, recognizing the importance of semantic
804   information in generative models, recent efforts have begun integrating semantic information into
805   codec models. RepCodec (Huang et al., 2024) learns a vector quantization codebook by reconstruct-
806   ing speech representations from speech encoders like HuBERT. SpeechTokenizer (Zhang et al., 2023)
807   enriches the semantic content of the first quantizer through semantic distillation. FunCodec (Du et al.,
808   2024) makes semantic tokens optional and explores different combinations. SemanticCodec (Liu
809   et al., 2024) is based on quantized semantic tokens and further reconstructs acoustic information
      using an audio encoder and diffusion model. WavTokenizer (Ji et al., 2024b) represents the latest


                                                        15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      state-of-the-art codec model, capable of reconstructing high-quality audio using only forty discrete
811   codebooks. Given that ControlSpeech requires disentangled discrete audio representations that
812   are pre-trained on large-scale multi-speaker data, we select FACodec (Ju et al., 2024) as the
813   tokenizer for ControlSpeech.

816   A.3    Z ERO - SHOT TTS
817
818   Zero-shot speech synthesis refers to the ability to synthesize the voice of an unseen speaker based
819   solely on a few seconds of audio prompt, also known as voice cloning. In recent months, with
820   the advancement of generative large-scale models, a plethora of outstanding works have emerged.
821   VALL-E (Wang et al., 2023) leverages discrete codec representations and combines autoregressive and
822   non-autoregressive models in a cascaded manner, preserving the powerful contextual capabilities of
823   language models. NaturalSpeech 2 (Shen et al., 2023) employs continuous vectors instead of discrete
824   neural codec tokens and introduces in-context learning to a latent diffusion model. NaturalSpeech
825   3 (Ju et al., 2024) proposes a TTS system with novel factorized diffusion models to generate natural
826
      speech in a zero-shot way. SpearTTS (Kharitonov et al., 2023) and Make-a-Voice (Huang et al., 2023)
      utilize semantic tokens to reduce the gap between text and acoustic features. VoiceBox (Le et al.,
827
      2023) is a non-autoregressive flow-matching model trained to infill speech, given audio context and
828
      text. Mega-TTS (Jiang et al., 2023c;b;a), on the other hand, utilizes traditional mel-spectrograms,
829   decoupling timbre and prosody and further modeling the prosody using an autoregressive approach.
830   VoiceBox (Le et al., 2023) and P-flow (Kim et al., 2024) employ flowing models as generators,
831   demonstrating robust generative performance. SoundStorm (Borsos et al., 2023) and MobileSpeech (Ji
832   et al., 2024c) utilize a non-autoregressive and mask-based iterative generation method, achieving an
833   excellent balance between inference speed and generation quality. It is noteworthy that existing
834   zero-shot TTS models are unable to achieve arbitrary language style control. ControlSpeech is
835   the first TTS model capable of simultaneously performing zero-shot timbre cloning and style
836   control.

892   D     V CCM DATASET TEST SET
893
894   To further validate ControlSpeech’s ability to simultaneously control style and clone speaker timbre,
895   we create four types of test sets in the VccmDataset: the main test set (test set A), the out-of-domain
896   speaker test set (test set B), the out-of-domain style test set (test set C), and the special case test set (test
897   set D). Each test set corresponds to each experiment in Section 4: style controllability experiments,
898
      out-of-domain speaker cloning experiments, out-of-domain style controllability experiments, and
      many-to-many style control experiments, respectively. We randomly select 1,500 audio samples as
899
      the ControlSpeech main test set (test set A) and match the corresponding prompt voice based on
900
      speaker IDs. Additionally, to evaluate ControlSpeech’s performance on out-of-domain timbre and
901   styles, we further filter an appropriate test set (speakers that are not present in the training set) and
902   enlist language experts to compose style descriptions distinct from those in TextrolSpeech. Using
903   these two methods, we generate the out-of-domain speaker test set (test set B) and the out-of-domain
904   style test set (test set C). The special case test set (test set D) is designed to evaluate the model’s
905   performance under many-to-many style control conditions. Firstly, we select four groups of speakers,
906   each of whom is matched with 60 different style descriptions while the content text remains fixed.
907   This particular set of test samples is referred to as test set D1. We further select six distinct style
908   descriptions paired with 50 different timbre prompts, with pitch, speed, and volume labels set to
909
      the following combinations: normal, fast, normal; normal, slow, normal; high, normal, normal; low,
      normal, normal; normal, normal, high; and normal, normal, low, respectively. This set of special test
910
      samples is referred to as test set D2.

913   E     E VALUATION METRICS
914
915   For objective evaluations, we adopt the metrics used in prior works (Guo et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2023;
916   Leng et al., 2023). To evaluate the model’s style controllability, we use accuracy as the metric, which
917   measures the correspondence between the style factors in the output speech and those in the prompts.
      The accuracy of pitch, speaking speed, and volume is calculated using signal processing tools. We


                                                             17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      fine-tune the official version of the Emotion2vec model (Ma et al., 2023) on the emotional dataset of
919   VccmDataset, and compute the speech emotion classification accuracy with the fine-tuned model.
920   To evaluate timbre similarity (Spk-sv) between the original prompt and the synthesized speech, we
921   utilize the base-plus-sv version of WavLM (Chen et al., 2022). For Word Error Rate (WER), we use
922   an ASR model 2 to transcribe the synthesized speech. This ASR model is a CTC-based HuBERT
923   pre-trained on Librilight and fine-tuned on the 960 hours training set of LibriSpeech. For subjective
924   evaluations, we conduct mean opinion score (MOS) evaluations on the test set to measure audio
925   naturalness via crowdsourcing. We randomly select 30 samples from the test set of each dataset for
926   subjective evaluation, and each audio sample is listened by at least 10 testers. We analyze the MOS
927
      in two aspects: MOS-Q (Quality, assessing clarity and naturalness of the duration and pitch) and
      MOS-S (Speaker similarity).
928
929   Furthermore, for the evaluation of style-controllable many-to-many scenarios in the test set D, we
930   design new subjective MOS metrics: MOS-TS (Timbre Similarity), MOS-SD (Style Diversity), and
931   MOS-SA (Style Accuracy). Specifically, the MOS-TS metric is used to assess whether the timbre
932   remains stable across 60 different style descriptions for four speakers on the test set D1. The MOS-SA
933
      and MOS-SD metrics represent the accuracy and diversity of style control for each style description
      respectively on the test set D2.

      ControlSpeech is trained on VccmDataset using 8 NVIDIA A100 40G GPUs with each batch
939
      accommodating 3500 frames of the discrete codec. We optimize the models using the AdamW
940   optimizer with parameters β1 = 0.9 and β2 = 0.95. The learning rate is warmed up for the first
941   5k updates, reaching a peak of 5 × 10−4 , and then linearly decayed. We utilize the open-source
942   FACodec’s voice conversion version as the codec encoder and decoder for ControlSpeech. The
943   style-controllable baseline models are trained on the same VccmDataset training set to eliminate
944   potential biases. We utilize a pre-trained BERT (Devlin et al., 2018) model consisting of 12 hidden
945   layers with 110M parameters. For the implementation of the basic MDN network model, we largely
946   follow the approach described in (Duan, 2019).

950
951   Following (Ju et al., 2024), the basic architecture of codec encoder and codec decoder follows (Kumar
952
      et al., 2024) and employs the SnakeBeta activation function (Lee et al., 2022a). The timbre extractor
      consists of several conformer (Gulati et al., 2020) blocks. We use Nqc = 2, Nqp = 1, Nqd = 3
953
954
      as the number of quantizers for each of the three FVQ Qc , Qp , Qd , the codebook size for all the
      quantizers is 1024. Text encoder and variance adaptor share the similar architecture which comprises
955
      several FFT blocks or attention layers as used by FastSpeech2 (Ren et al., 2020). The Style Extractor
956
      is a module comprising both convolutional and LSTM networks from FACodec (Ju et al., 2024)
957   and outputs a 512-dimensional global ground truth style vector. The codec generator is a decoder
958   primarily based on conformer blocks (Gulati et al., 2020), similar to MobileSpeech (Ji et al., 2024c).
959   However, we opt for fewer decoder layers (6 layers) and a smaller parameter count in the codec

996    J   L IMITATION AND F UTURE W ORK
997
998    In this work, we introduce ControlSpeech, the first TTS system capable of simultaneously cloning
999    timbre and controlling style. While ControlSpeech has demonstrated competitive controllability and
1000   cloning capabilities, there remains considerable scope for further research and improvement based on
1001   the current framework.
1002
1003   Larger Training Datasets. The field of style-controllable TTS urgently demands larger training
1004   datasets. Although TextrolSpeech and our VccmDataset have established a foundation, we hypothe-
1005   size that achieving more advanced speech controllability may require datasets comprising tens of
1006   thousands of hours of speech with style descriptions.
1007
1008   Exploring Generative Models. In this work, we experiment with decoupled codecs and non-
