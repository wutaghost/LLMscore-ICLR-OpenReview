009                                             A BSTRACT
010
011           The complexity of online decision-making under uncertainty stems from the re-
012           quirement of finding a balance between exploiting known strategies and exploring
013           new possibilities. Naturally, the uncertainty type plays a crucial role in develop-
014           ing decision-making strategies that manage complexity effectively. In this paper,
015           we focus on a specific form of uncertainty known as epistemic ambivalence (EA),
016           which emerges from conflicting pieces of evidence or contradictory experiences.
017           It creates a delicate interplay between uncertainty and confidence, distinguishing
018           it from epistemic uncertainty that typically diminishes with new information. In-
019
              deed, ambivalence can persist even after additional knowledge is acquired. To
              address this phenomenon, we propose a novel framework, called the epistemically
020
              ambivalent Markov decision process (EA-MDP), aiming to understand and con-
021
              trol EA in decision-making processes. This framework incorporates the concept of
022           a quantum state from the quantum mechanics formalism, and its core is to assess
023           the probability and reward of every possible outcome. We calculate the reward
024           function using quantum measurement techniques and prove the existence of an
025           optimal policy and an optimal value function in the EA-MDP framework. We also
026           propose the EA-epsilon-greedy Q-learning algorithm. To evaluate the impact of
027           EA on decision-making and the expedience of our framework, we study two dis-
028           tinct experimental setups, namely the two-state problem and the lattice problem.
029           Our results show that using our methods, the agent converges to the optimal policy
030
              in the presence of EA.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Reinforcement Learning (RL) models an agent facing the exploration-exploitation dilemma: While
036   being uncertain about some specific factors that determine the actions’ outcomes, the agent selects
037   between an action that, according to her current belief, maximizes the reward and a seemingly
038
      suboptimal one, which, however, can result in gaining some information that increases the future
      rewards. The uncertainty type plays a crucial role in solving the exploration-exploitation dilemma;
039
      Thus, the core challenge is to efficiently understand the environmental uncertainties. So far, the
040
      literature mainly emphasizes two primary forms of uncertainty: Aleatoric uncertainty and epistemic
041   uncertainty (Liu et al. (2024); Lockwood & Si (2022); Kahn et al. (2017); Lütjens et al. (2019)),
042   described below.
043
044         • Aleatoric uncertainty arises from the inherent randomness or stochastic nature of the en-
045           vironment in which the agent operates, such as the randomness in reward generation pro-
046           cesses, or state transitions (Zennaro & Jøsang (2020); Clements et al. (2019)); as such, it
047           can neither be reduced nor predicted before starting an experiment.
048         • Epistemic uncertainty results from the agent’s limited knowledge like incomplete under-
049           standing of the environment or inadequate representation of some of the problem’s char-
050           acteristics. This uncertainty encompasses various aspects of RL problem, including un-
051           certainty regarding the true model of the environment, insufficient knowledge about the
052           outcomes of specific actions in certain states, and ambiguity concerning the optimal policy
053           due to incomplete exploration of the state space (Liu et al. (2024)). The agent can reduce
              epistemic uncertainty if relevant information becomes available.

054
      Effective decision-making necessitates that the agent adeptly manages both aleatoric and epistemic
055   uncertainties, as this enhances performance and leads to more informed decisions in Reinforcement
056   Learning (RL).
057
058
      Besides aleatoric and epistemic uncertainties, other obstacles might hinder optimal decision-making.
      One is ambivalence, which can inhibit the decision-making progress. Ambivalence differs from un-
059
      certainty in that it persists even after the information becomes available. Consequently, traditional
060
      approaches to modeling, quantifying, and addressing uncertainty may be inadequate or suboptimal in
061   effectively managing ambivalence as a distinct challenge (Lam & Sherman (2020)). In this study, we
062   consider a novel form of uncertainty, termed Epistemic Ambivalence (EA). EA arises when multiple
063   interpretations, explanations, or courses of action are possible, particularly in cases where available
064   evidence does not definitively favor one perspective over others. In the context of epistemology,
065   which studies the nature and boundaries of knowledge, EA highlights the inherent limitations and
066   complexities involved in understanding and reasoning about the world (Amaya (2021); Williamson
067   (2021); Lam (2013)). While traditional uncertainty represents imperfect knowledge, EA represents
068   a cognitive state in which conflicting pieces of evidence coexist, resulting in an uncertain decision-
069
      making process. Individuals may experience uncertainty or skepticism when faced with conflicting
      or ambiguous information. For example, in a study on investment decisions, a person may encounter
070
      conflicting information regarding a specific stock, resulting in EA. Even after further research and
071
      gathering additional data, the individual may remain uncertain about whether to buy or sell the stock
072   due to the ongoing presence of conflicting evidence. Another example would be autonomous robot
073   navigation problem where a robot receives conflicting data from multiple sensors; some sensors
074   indicate a clear path ahead, while others detect an obstacle which may be either near or far away.
075   Given such contradictory evidence, the robot is in a state of epistemic ambivalence where it must
076   decide whether to slow down, stop, or maintain its current speed. Note that, in this example, we
077   assume that each sensor data is completely reliable as opposed to conventional assumptions about
078   the reliability and inaccuracy of information gathered from the sensors Huang et al. (2021). Such
079   examples illustrate how, when faced with contradictory data from credible sources, an agent may
080
      struggle to make an optimal decision, reflecting the nature of EA. The persistence of this uncer-
      tainty challenges traditional decision-making intuitions, highlighting the complexity of navigating
081
      conflicting evidence. Thus, a deeper understanding of EA can foster more flexible and adaptive
082
      decision-making strategies in complex and uncertain environments.
083
084   In quantum mechanics, quantum particles can exist in multiple states simultaneously due to the prin-
085   ciple of superposition, with their state remaining indeterminate until observed (Nielsen & Chuang
086
      (2010)). This similarity is notable in decision-making scenarios where agents face uncertainty aris-
      ing from conflicting information they receive from diverse sources, mirroring the superposition prin-
087
      ciple in quantum mechanics.
088
089   In this paper, we exploit the analogy between quantum mechanic states and EA to develop a theoret-
090   ical framework for decision-making under EA and a policy. Roughly stated, we map each piece of
091   information to a quantum basis state and construct an EA quantum state that incorporates all of the
092
      information. This mapping, accompanied by using Dirac notation and mathematical formulations
      derived from quantum mechanics, assists the decision-makers in quantifying EA, i.e., calculating
093
      the probability of each option, hence enabling informed and effective decision-making. We propose
094
      the EA-MDP framework to understand and control EA. We demonstrate that the optimal policy ex-
095   ists within this framework. Additionally, we introduce the EA-epsilon-greedy Q-learning algorithm
096   to evaluate the impact of EA on decision-making. Two experiments are conducted to evaluate the
097   performance and effectiveness of our approach.
098
      The structure of the paper is as follows. In Section 2, we provide an overview of the relevant
099
      literature regarding the uncertainty in RL, and its application in quantum mechanics and vice versa.
100
      Section 3 provides a brief introduction to the principles of quantum mechanics and superposition.
101   Section 4 outlines the problem formulation and implementation of EA by using quantum mechanics.
102   In Section 5, we present the theoretical results. Section 6 details the experimental evaluation using
103   two distinct problems, where the agent’s reward is calculated based on EA uncertainty, followed by
104   the training process of the agent. Finally, Section 7 provides concluding remarks.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Uncertainty exists in various elements of RL, including observations, actions, transition dynamics,
111   and rewards (Kakade & Langford (2002); Puterman (2014); Boutilier et al. (2000); Shapiro et al.
112   (2021); Bertsekas & Tsitsiklis (1996); Wang et al. (2016)). Wang et al. (2020) developed a robust
113   reinforcement learning framework that allows agents to learn in environments with noise, where they
114   can only observe perturbed rewards. Zhang et al. (2020) studied the vulnerability of Deep Reinforce-
115   ment Learning (DRL) agents to adversarial attacks on state observations. The authors discovered
116   that a robust policy significantly enhances DRL performance across various environments, even in
117   the absence of an adversary. Engel et al. (2005) used Gaussian processes to model uncertainty in
118   rewards, enhancing the agent’s ability to learn from sparse data. Kumar et al. (2020) introduced
119
      conservative Q-learning, which addresses uncertainty in rewards by penalizing the overestimation
      of Q-values in offline learning.
120
121   Quantum mechanics can enhance RL by leveraging quantum phenomena such as superposition,
122   entanglement, and quantum parallelism. These features allow quantum algorithms to process and
123   explore multiple states or actions simultaneously. It has the potential to accelerate the learning pro-
124   cess (Biamonte et al. (2017); Dunjko et al. (2016); Dunjko & Briegel (2017); Saggio et al. (2021)).
125
      An early paper by Dong et al. (2008) suggested that quantum mechanics can enhance learning pro-
      cesses. The epistemic perspective of quantum states has been deeply investigated and has shown
126
      how to interpret quantum states as states of knowledge (Fraser et al. (2023); Pusey et al. (2012);
127
      Caves et al. (2002); Healey (2017); Spekkens (2007); Leifer (2014)). Neukart et al. (2018) showed
128   that the optimization of strategies and the efficiency of learning are improved by the implementation
129   of quantum-enhanced reinforcement learning in finite-episode games. Dalla Pozza et al. (2022) ex-
130   tended the classical concept of RL to the quantum domain in the lattice problem and investigated its
131   behavior in a noisy environment. Dong et al. (2010) proposed a Quantum-inspired Reinforcement
132   Learning (QiRL) algorithm or navigation control of autonomous mobile robots.
133
      EA has not been previously investigated in literature related to artificial intelligence. Therefore, in
134   this paper, we introduce EA in this field, specifically in RL and the quantum RL.

      7   C ONCLUSION
516
517   In this paper, we address a specific type of uncertainty known as epistemic ambivalence, which arises
518
      from conflicting information or contradictory experiences. We introduced the EA-MDP framework,
      grounded in the principle of superposition from quantum mechanics, where a quantum particle can
519
      exist in multiple states simultaneously. The quantum state contains information about both the
520
      underlying state and the related EA, which is encoded with complex probability amplitudes. Us-
521   ing quantum measurement, we calculated the probability of given outcomes based on the quantum
522   state. An interesting direction for future research is to extend EA-MDP framework by considering
523   time-dependent quantum states, time-dependent outcome sets, or multiple and entangled underlying
524   states. Another possible future work is to extend our proposed framework by assuming partial state
525   observability in EA-MDP or using non-stationary rewards.

       is density matrix. Since in our proposed EA-MDP formulation we used a pure quantum state, the
1114
       density matrix can be written as ρ = |s̃⟩ ⟨s̃|. Due to the fact that pure states do not have any
1115   mixedness, the Von Neumann entropy is always zero in our system.

1118
       applying it to real-world problems is a challenging task. The main challenge is to convert classical
       data into quantum data, particularly in the form of superposition. In recent years, the conversion of
1119
       classical data into quantum data (quantum states) has gained significant attention (Biamonte et al.
1120
       (2017)). Besides, a set of outcome representations is needed to enable such an implementation. This
1121   set of outcomes is essential for calculating the rewards, as it is used to determine the measurement
1122   and the reward. Selecting a suitable outcome set is crucial to ensure accurate reward calculations. In
1123   addition, in real-world experiments with non-separable outcome set, the number and dimension of
1124   outcomes may increase significantly. If n is the number of the underlying states and m is the number
1125   of EA bases, the memory needed to store the outcomes scales by a factor of O((mn)2 ). Similarly,
1126   the memory needed to store the separated quantum states scales by a factor of O(mn). Furthermore,
1127   the computational time required to calculate the reward scales as O((mn)2 ). One may use sparse
1128   matrices to store outcomes when they have sparse structure to reduce memory and computational
1129
       power.
