009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly recognized for their practical
012            applications. However, these models often encounter challenges in dynamically
013            changing knowledge, as well as in managing unknown static knowledge. Retrieval-
014            Augmented Generation (RAG) tackles this challenge and has shown a significant
015            impact on LLMs. Actually, we find that the impact of RAG on the question an-
016            swering capabilities of LLMs can be categorized into three groups: beneficial,
017            neutral, and harmful. By minimizing retrieval requests that yield neutral or harmful
018
               results, we can effectively reduce both time and computational costs, while also
               improving the overall performance of LLMs. This insight motivates us to differ-
019
               entiate between types of questions using certain metrics as indicators, to decrease
020
               the retrieval ratio without compromising performance. In our work, we propose
021            a method that is able to identify different types of questions from this view by
022            training a Knowledge Boundary Model (KBM). Experiments conducted on 11
023            English and Chinese datasets illustrate that the KBM effectively delineates the
024            knowledge boundary, significantly decreasing the proportion of retrievals required
025            for optimal end-to-end performance. Specifically, we evaluate the effectiveness of
026            KBM in three complex scenarios: dynamic knowledge, long-tail static knowledge,
027            and multi-hop problems, as well as its functionality as an external LLM plug-in.
028

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   As Large Language Models (LLMs) evolve, their real-world applications expand, yet they often
033
      struggle with dynamically changing and unknown static knowledge, leading to inaccuracies or
      hallucinations (Rawte et al., 2023). Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) effectively addresses
034
      these challenges by retrieving relevant external information in real time, enhancing LLMs’ ability
035
      to provide accurate responses. While RAG can significantly boost performance, it also incurs costs,
036   such as increased retrieval requests and longer response times. This raises a crucial question: when is
037   retrieval truly necessary?
038
039
      Previous studies on the necessity of RAG for LLMs can be categorized into two main approaches.
      The first focuses on the query itself, with methods like SELF-RAG (Asai et al., 2023) instructing
040
      models such as GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) to assess whether retrieving external documents (e.g.,
041
      Wikipedia) can produce better responses. Although this approach based on query instructing can
042   identify questions that require real-time information, it remains model-agnostic and struggles to
043   determine whether an LLM has mastered specific knowledge. The second approach evaluates both
044   questions and model responses to determine if an LLM can answer a question, generating data by
045   sampling multiple model responses and using manual labels for evaluation. However, this method is
046   labor-intensive and relies heavily on manual labeling, which can create biases and lead to increased
047   training costs. Recent advancements in adaptive retrieval (Jeong et al., 2024) highlight the importance
048   of tailoring the retrieval process based on the complexity of the questions. This approach improves
049   alignment between user intent and retrieved content by considering the model’s responses alongside
050
      RAG effects. However, measuring the effectiveness of retrieved information on model outputs
      remains challenging, especially in complex and open-domain scenarios.
051
052   Given these challenges, using GPT-4-based classification labels for questions to generate data for
053   Llama-2 7B evaluations is not reasonable, as it does not consider the knowledge boundaries of Llama-
      2 7B. In our analysis, we find that performance varies significantly among LLMs of different sizes,

070   from queries and LLM predictions often requires gold answers for evaluation, posing challenges for
071   questions without definitive answers. For questions with gold answers, we sample multiple LLM
072   responses to compute confidence levels. In contrast, for questions lacking gold answers, we evaluate
073
      the entropy of phrase distributions from the model’s responses to create a certainty distribution.
      Based on these evaluation indicators, we categorize RAG’s impact on LLMs as beneficial, neutral, or
074
      harmful based on these evaluation indicators. Our findings show that as the LLM’s confidence or
075
      certainty increases, the ratio of neutral and harmful queries also rises, while beneficial queries and
076   performance improvements decline. This motivates us to reduce retrieval requests leading to neutral
077   or harmful outcomes, optimizing time and computational efficiency while maximizing beneficial
078   retrievals to enhance question answering (QA) performance.
079
      In this work, we propose methods to evaluate the known and unknown aspects of LLMs based
080
      on indices of confidence and certainty derived from sampled responses. By establishing different
081
      thresholds for confidence and certainty, we can effectively distinguish between beneficial questions
082   and those deemed neutral or harmful, thereby enabling the generation of data with soft labels. The data
083   is used to fine-tune the Knowledge Boundary Model (KBM) to determine if the LLM considers the
084   question unknown, thus indicating whether retrieval is necessary. This assessment informs decisions
085   on whether RAG enhancement is necessary, leading to better performance and decreased retrieval
086   costs. We evaluate several English and Chinese QA datasets and demonstrate that KBM effectively
087   detects knowledge mastery in LLMs through end-to-end results. Our confidence-based approach
088   significantly reduces retrieval ratios while achieving performance close to ALL RAG, with a 43.17%
089   reduction in retrieval on WebQA and a performance drop of only 1.7%. Similarly, the certainty-based
090
      approach maintains performance near ALL RAG (with a 0.42% decrease) and reduces the search
      ratio by approximately 10% on QA tasks. Further analysis explores KBM’s effectiveness in open
091
      domains across three dimensions: dynamic knowledge, long-tail static knowledge, and multi-hop
092
      problems. Finally, we discuss the impact of incorporating RAG on the knowledge boundaries of
093   LLMs and potential solutions based on KBM to address these challenges.

097
098   Large Language Model Knowledge Exploration. The exploration of knowledge boundaries in
099
      LLMs attracts significant attention. Kadavath et al. (2022) examines the self-evaluation capabilities
      of LLMs, showing that larger models enhance their calibration by initially proposing answers and
100
      then evaluating their validity.However, their model still face challenges in accurately identifying
101
      errors. Ren et al. (2023) studies LLMs’ perception of factual knowledge boundaries and finds that
102   they often display blind confidence in their abilities. Using retrieval-augmented questioning, the study
103   shows that retrieval can enhance judgment capabilities but highlights a dependence on the quality
104   of supporting documents. Yin et al. (2023) focuses on self-awareness, demonstrating that while
105   LLMs can identify some unanswered questions, substantial discrepancies still exist, affecting their
106   uncertainty detection. Chen et al. (2024) introduces COKE, an unsupervised method for teaching
107   models to articulate their knowledge limits through internal signals, yielding improved outcomes
      across various datasets. Kang et al. (2024) points out that LLMs often default to examples in training

      data when facing unfamiliar queries. This highlights the need for better factuality controls to reduce
109   instances of hallucination. Li et al. (2024b) explores hallucinations related to insufficient prompt

111   uncertainty-sensitive tuning to improve response reliability. Based on the above research work, our
112   research introduces the KBM. This model aims to enhance the ability of LLMs to determine when
113   and what external knowledge to retrieve for open-domain QA, revealing the key dynamics between

115   Retrieval-Augmented Generation. RAG enhances LLMs by integrating retrieved text passages,

128   for our investigation into new strategies for effective retrieval during generation.

131   3       PRELIMINARIES: LLM K NOWLEDGE B OUNDARIES AND RAG A NALYSIS
132
133   This section examines RAG’s impact on LLM performance. We first assess LLMs with varying
134   parameter counts on QA tasks , revealing differences in knowledge boundaries related to parameter
135   size. Next, we evaluate how each LLM utilizes textual information retrieved by naive RAG and
136   investigate the effects of MASK perturbation to understand their reliance on RAG. We categorize
137
      questions into three types based on RAG’s influence on LLM outputs. Additionally, we propose
      using confidence based on LLM accuracy and the certainty of output words as indicators, motivating
138
      further study into simulating knowledge boundaries with these evaluation methods.
139

142
143   We assess the impact of RAG on the performance of LLMs with varying parameter sizes. Our
144
      findings indicate that while LLMs exhibit different abilities to answer questions and possess distinct
      knowledge boundaries, their capacity to utilize retrieved text information remains largely consistent.
145
      Our analysis employs several configurations: LLM Only: This configuration generates responses
146
      directly from the LLM. ALL RAG: We enhance the Naive RAG approach by concatenating the top
147   ten blocks retrieved from Google as contextual information. MASK RAG: This variant replaces the
148   gold answers present in RAG with MASK, supplying these modified data as context for the LLM.

152

196   3.2    T HREE DIMENSIONS TO DIVIDE THE BOUNDARIES OF RAG
197
      To more precisely evaluate the impact of RAG on LLM generation, we categorize its effects into
198   three aspects:
199
200   • Beneficial: RAG effectively solves this problem.
201   • Neutral: RAG does not impact the LLM’s effectiveness.
202
      • Harmful: RAG compromises the LLM’s effectiveness.
203
204   To isolate the influences of other modules, we utilize a simplified RAG pipeline for our analysis.
205   Specifically, we leverage Google Open Search to retrieve the top 10 blocks, which are then provided to
206   the LLM. Acknowledging that a single model response may lack robustness or representativeness, we
207   implement a confidence-based categorization method. Inspired by Kadavath et al. (2022), we generate
208   I = 30 answer samples at T = 1. For a given question Q, if 20 of the answers sampled by the model
209   are correct and 10 are wrong, unlike Kadavath et al. (2022) which contains 20 copies of (Q, M = 1)
210   data points and 10 copies of (Q, M = 0) data points. Instead, we construct a confidence of knowledge
211   for this question based on our samples, resulting in a single data point (Q, Mpred = 20
                                                                                            30 ). This method
212
      aims to accurately represent the model’s understanding and misconceptions while significantly
      reducing the size of the training and test datasets by a factor of I. Consequently, we approximate the
213
      model’s soft labels for knowledge using hard labels derived from a diverse set of QA data points.
214
215   However, this method becomes challenging in the absence of gold-standard answers. To address
      this, we simulate the effect based on the uncertainty of the generated responses. We compute the

      We classify the effects of RAG based on the three categories and two indicators as follows: an effect

233   of LLMs across accuracy intervals on the WebQA and SogouQA datasets, as shown in Figure 3.
234   The figure indicates that the [0-0.8) interval has the highest proportion of beneficial cases, while
235   neutral and harmful cases peak in the [0.8-1.0] range. This suggests a cost-benefit relationship: higher
236   accuracy reflects greater confidence in answers, reducing the advantages of RAG, especially in the
237   [0.8-1.0] range, where harmful cases also concentrate.
238   When an LLM displays high confidence, we infer mastery of the solution; low confidence implies
239   a lack of understanding. This assessment combines human annotations, model predictions, and
240   evaluation methods. The uncertainty metric highlights components such as Aleatoric and Model
241   Uncertainty. Low uncertainty corresponds to high confidence, while high uncertainty indicates doubt.
242   Thus, determining mastery can significantly lower retrieval needs, as illustrated in the blue area of
243   the figure. For instance, with mastery defined as accuracy exceeding 0.8, a 7B model on WebQA
244   reduces retrieval by 55.47% and enhances performance by 0.92%. A 72B model achieves an 80.95%
245
      reduction in retrieval and a 2.52% performance gain. If we set a confidence threshold of under 0.2,
      the 7B model reduces retrieval by 41.45%, but this results in a 4.15% performance decline compared
246
      to AllRAG. The 72B model achieves a 61.56% reduction with a 1.56% drop in performance. We
247
      observe a Pearson correlation coefficient of 0.64 between confidence and certainty, indicating a
248   positive relationship. However, certainty-based metrics tend to be more inclusive.
249
250   Overall, the thresholds for mastery and confidence depend on the specific dataset and model. While
251
      accuracy aligns with human intent and reflects model capabilities, its reliability is affected by
      annotation quality and evaluation variability. Uncertainty metrics also have limitations, particularly
252
      concerning model overconfidence, which can degrade performance. These aspects will be further
253
      examined in the following sections.

256   3.3   T HE DEPENDENCE OF LLM ON RAG
257
258   In this section, we explore the reliance on RAG from the perspective of LLM uncertainty. We
259   find that incorporating RAG significantly enhances LLM certainty, which is evident in the greater
260   consistency of the generated answers. This observation raises a critical question: does the model
261
      primarily copy answers from the RAG input to reduce uncertainty, or does it absorb knowledge from
      the provided material? To address this question, we conduct an experiment with scenarios where
262
      the model outputs exhibit a certainty of 1, indicating high confidence in predictions. As shown in
263
      Figure 2, we measure the frequency of outputs corresponding to MASK under MASK RAG. If the
264   LLM mainly copies answers from the RAG input, we expect to observe a high proportion of MASK
265   outputs; this would imply that the model retrieves and reproduces answers rather than inferring
266   them from context. Our findings are consistent across WebQA, SogouQA and SQuAD-zh datasets.
267   Notably, the tendency to copy answers decreases with an increase in the number of model parameters,
268   leading to a lower proportion of MASK outputs. This suggests that larger models are more adept at
269   leveraging non-answer content to derive predictions, rather than relying on direct answer extraction.
      Such reliance demands more from RAG and may negatively affect LLM performance.

287   4     C AN LLM EXPRESS KNOW / UNKNOWN ?
288
289   In Section §3.2, we noted that using confidence and certainty as screening indicators helps identify
290   questions with high mastery and low confidence for retrieval enhancement. This approach can reduce
291   the proportion of retrieval and provide performance benefits. So, how can we allow LLMs to express
292   their mastery and certainty? We explore two simple approaches to examine two key questions:
293   Can the model effectively express mastery and certainty? Are there internal model features or data
294
      distribution characteristics related to knowledge that the model does not master or finds uncertain?
295
296   4.1   M ETHODS
297
      To construct training data for the KBM, we generate soft labels using the sampling method detailed in
298
      Section §3.2. As shown in Figure 4, each query is assessed under two configurations: LLM Only and
299   RAG. Based on accuracy and certainty, we derive two data fields: mastery and certainty. We establish
300   a threshold, τ , to differentiate between known and unknown data, with values below τ labeled as
301   False and those above τ labeled as True. In the training phase, the generated data is formatted into
302   question-answer pairs and utilized to fine-tune the sampled ontology model (Qwen2-7B instruct).
303   Further details regarding the settings can be found in the Appendix §A.1.
304
      During the inference phase of the KBM, the process unfolds in three steps: At step 1, after the user
305   enters a query, the system packages this query through a prompt and sends it to the KBM to assess
306   mastery and certainty. At step 2, if the KBM judges the query as True, it forwards the original query
307   directly to the answer generation model (Qwen2-7B instruct) to generate a response. If the query
308   is judged as False, the process proceeds to step 3. At step 3, the system performs an open-domain
309   retrieval using Google, based on the user’s original query. The retrieved chunks of information are
310   then spliced together. The original query and the connected chunks are combined using RAG Prompt
311   and subsequently fed into the answer generation model to produce the final response.

427    the lower retrieval rate for the never-changing category implies that some knowledge is effectively

       embedded within the LLM. This observation underscores KBM’s sensitivity in identifying questions

503   5.4   D ISCUSSION
504
505   In this work, we find that while RAG effectively addresses certain challenges associated with LLMs,
506   it is not universally applicable. As noted in Section §3.3, it can be counterproductive when relevant
507   information is difficult to retrieve or noisy. This limitation is illustrated in Table 1, where RAG shows
508   minimal performance improvement in tasks such as multiple-choice questions and math assessments.
509   We suggest that RAG’s effectiveness could be enhanced when deployed within specific modules
510   through techniques such as question rewriting, page reading, and re-ranking. However, our primary
511   focus is to investigate the knowledge boundaries of LLMs. Therefore, we consider strategies to
512   mitigate the impact of RAG components that are neutral or detrimental, especially when LLM
513   knowledge remains limited, thereby emphasizing RAG’s boundaries.
514
      To achieve this objective, we implement the method illustrated in Figure 7, which aims to improve
515   information processing while minimizing the influx of irrelevant input tokens and reducing noise
516   from irrelevant retrieval results. Specifically, in specialized problem domains, such as multiple-choice
517   questions, when the KBM identifies a query as unknown, we perform a search to retrieve concatenated
518   chunks of relevant information. The KBM uses both the original query and the concatenated chunks
519   as prompt input to assess whether the LLM can effectively answer the query with the additional
520   retrieved information. If the LLM generates a response, the concatenated chunk is then forwarded to
521   it; if not, the original query is transmitted directly. This method effectively minimizes the amount
522   of information fed into the LLM (as indicated by the red area in Figure 8), while maintaining
523
      performance that is competitive with both LLM Only and ALL RAG scenarios, and in some instances,
      it even surpasses both approaches.

      6     C ONCLUSION
527
528
529   In this paper, we explore the impact of RAG on LLMs, depending on the types of questions they
530   encounter. We find that a key limitation of existing approaches is that RAG often lacks adaptability to
531
      the knowledge boundaries of specific LLMs, which can lead to ineffective or even harmful responses.
      To address this challenge, we first classify the types of questions encountered by LLMs and identify
532
      appropriate search regions within the model boundaries. We then introduce a KBM for retrieval
533
      judgment, optimizing retrieval requests based on our proposed confidence and certainty methods.
534   This model effectively classifies questions as known or unknown and determines whether retrieval
535   is necessary. Our extensive evaluation on 11 English and Chinese datasets demonstrates that KBM
536   significantly improves retrieval efficiency while maintaining high performance. It proves effective in
537   three complex scenarios: dynamic knowledge, long-tail static knowledge, and multi-hop questions,
538   showcasing its versatility in real-world applications. Our findings indicate that KBM, as an intelligent
539   retrieval judgment strategy, effectively reduces resource consumption by decreasing retrieval calls,
      token usage, and response costs, while simultaneously enhancing the performance of LLMs.
