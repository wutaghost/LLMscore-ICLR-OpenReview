009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Satellite imagery has emerged as an important tool to analyze demographic,
012
               health, and development indicators. While various deep learning models have
013
               been built for these tasks, each is specific to a particular problem, with few stan-
014            dard benchmarks available. We propose a new dataset pairing satellite imagery
015            and high-quality survey data on child poverty to benchmark satellite feature rep-
016            resentations. Our dataset consists of 33,608 images, each 10 km × 10 km, from
017            16 countries in Eastern and Southern Africa in the time period 1997-2022. As
018            defined by UNICEF, multidimensional child poverty comprises six fundamental
019            factors—housing, sanitation, water, nutrition, education, and health (UNICEF,
020            2021)—which can be calculated from geocoded, face-to-face Demographic and
021            Health Surveys (DHS) Program data. Using our dataset we benchmark multiple
022
               feature representations for encoding satellite imagery, from low-level satellite im-
               agery models such as MOSAIKS (Rolf et al., 2021), to deep learning foundation
023
               models, which include both generic vision models such as DINOv2 (Oquab et al.,
024
               2023) and specific satellite imagery models such as SatMAE (Cong et al., 2022).
025            As part of the benchmark, we test spatial as well as temporal generalization, by
026            testing on unseen locations, and on data beyond the training years. We provide
027            open source code to reproduce and extend our entire pipeline: building the satel-
028            lite imagery dataset, obtaining ground truth data from DHS, and comparing the
029            various models considered in our work.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Major satellites like those in the Landsat and Sentinel programs regularly circle the globe, providing
036   updated, publicly available, high-resolution imagery every 1-2 weeks. An emerging literature in
037   remote sensing and machine learning points to the promise that these large datasets, combined with
038   deep learning methods, hold to enable applications in agriculture, health, development, and disaster
039
      response. A cross-disciplinary set of publications hint at the potential impact, showing how satellite
      imagery can be used to estimate the causal impact of electricity access on livelihoods (Ratledge
040
      et al., 2021), to measure income, overcrowding, and environmental deprivation in urban areas (Suel
041
      et al., 2021) and to predict the human population in the absence of census data (Wardrop et al.,
042   2018). Despite these successes, machine learning for satellite imagery is not yet a well-developed
043   field (Rolf et al., 2024), with current approaches overlooking the unique features of satellite images
044   such as variation in spatial resolution over logarithmic scales (from < 1 meter to > 1 km) (Rolf et al.,
045   2024) and the heterogeneous nature of satellite imagery in terms of the number of bands available
046   from 3 bands for RGB to multispectral and hyperspectral.
047
      Many areas of machine learning have advanced through the development of standardized datasets
048   and benchmarks. Given the wide set of possible use cases for satellite imagery, there is no doubt
049   room for multiple benchmarks. However there are only a few sources of up-to-date, high-quality
050   satellite imagery, especially Landsat and Sentinel, so it is natural to construct publicly available
051   datasets using these satellite programs. Given the proven effectiveness of remote sensing for tasks
052   that are naturally visible from space, such as land usage prediction, crop yield forecasting, and
053   deforestation, we instead choose to focus on a more difficult task: multidimensional child poverty
      estimation.

      Of the 8 billion people in the world, over 2 billion are children (aged < 18 years old, as defined
072
      in the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child (UN General Assembly, 1989)). Child poverty is
073
      not the same as adult poverty; children are growing and developing so they have specific nutrition,
074   health, and education needs—if these needs are not met, there can be lifelong negative consequences
075   (Brooks-Gunn & Duncan, 1997). Poverty cannot be assessed simply by measuring overall household
076   resources, as households may be highly unequal, and some of the needs of children, such as vaccines
077   or education, may be neglected even in households that are not poor. Instead, child poverty must be
078   measured at the level of the children and their experiences (UNICEF, 2021).
079
      Child poverty is based on the “constitutive rights of poverty” (UNICEF, 2021). This means that
080   child poverty encompasses key dimensions essential for children’s well-being, such as education,
081   health, and nutrition, which depend on material resources. However, it excludes non-material as-
082   pects, including neglect, violence, and lack of privacy. Crucially for the purposes of establishing a
083   useful dataset and benchmark, the internationally agreed definition of child poverty was designed to
084   enable cross-country comparisons (UNICEF, 2021).
085
      In this work, we introduce a new dataset, KidSat, designed to provide a benchmark for applying
086   advanced computer vision methods to the challenge of child poverty estimation. The dataset includes
087   geocoded surveys from 16 countries across Eastern and Southern Africa, paired with multispectral
088   satellite imagery. By offering this dataset and benchmark, our goal is to bridge state-of-the-art
089   computer vision advances with real-world applications, addressing complex socioeconomic issues
090   like child poverty through the lens of satellite imagery. Our contribution are as follows:
091
092
             • We aggregate the geocoded DHS survey data on multidimensional child poverty alongside
               matching satellite imagery. This dataset is suitable for fine-tuning large models; we provide
093
               a univariate measure (the percentage of children experiencing severe deprivation, ranging
094
               from 0% to 100%), making model performance intuitively grasped by policymakers.
095
096
             • We benchmark the performance of various models for these tasks, ranging from baseline
               models using spatial correlation to satellite-based foundational vision models. In partic-
097
               ular, we demonstrate the importance of high imagery resolution and vision transformer
098
               architecture over CNNs for addressing this child poverty estimation task.
099
100
             • We propose a fine-tuning approach for generating robust satellite feature representations,
               with the potential to be adapted for addressing challenges beyond child poverty estimation.

106
107   With increased access to publicly available high-resolution satellite imagery through the Landsat
      and Sentinel programs, satellite imagery datasets have become very popular for training machine

      learning models. Models and datasets include functional map of the world (fMoW) (Christie et al.,
109   2018), XView (Lam et al., 2018), Spacenet (Van Etten et al., 2018), and Floodnet (Rahnemoonfar
110   et al., 2021) where the tasks are object detection, instance segmentation, and semantic segmenta-
111   tion. These are computer vision-specific tasks, rather than applied health and economic prediction

115   2.2      S ATELLITE IMAGERY FOR DEMOGRAPHIC AND HEALTH INDICATORS
116
117   Machine learning models applied to satellite images are becoming more commonplace for analyzing
118   demographic, health, and development indicators as they can increase coverage by allowing for
119
      interpolation and faster analysis in under-surveyed regions. In an early work, Jean et al. (2016)
      leveraged convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to process satellite images for tracking human
120
      development at increasing spatial and temporal granularity. Since then, satellite images have been
121
      used to track development indicators which are clearly visible from space such as agriculture and
122   deforestation patterns (Ball et al., 2022; Estes et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2024) but also more abstract
123   quantities such as poverty levels (Ayush et al., 2021), health indicators (Daoud et al., 2023), and the
124   human development index (Sherman et al., 2023).
125
126   2.3      F OUNDATION SATELLITE IMAGERY MODELS
127
128   As increasing volumes of data become available, and with progress in self-supervised learning (He
129   et al., 2022; Caron et al., 2021), many foundation models are emerging. In computer vision, these
130   large models are pre-trained with self-supervised learning on hundreds of millions of images, serving
131   as a “foundation” from which they can be fine-tuned for specific tasks. Popular examples of this
132
      are SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020), CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), and DINO (Caron et al., 2021).
      Recently, foundation models have been trained for satellite imagery specifically on vast amounts of
133
      unlabelled satellite images. Examples of these are SatMAE (Cong et al., 2022) based on masked
134
      autoencoders (He et al., 2022), SatCLIP (Klemmer et al., 2023) based on CLIP (Radford et al.,
135   2021), and DiffusionSat (Khanna et al., 2024) which is a diffusion model (Rombach et al., 2022) for
136   generating satellite images. As it is not yet clear whether there is a benefit from training foundation
137   models on more specific, but smaller datasets, we benchmark both generic foundation models for
138   computer vision as well as satellite-specific foundation models.

      In this section, we introduce our unique dataset, named KidSat, which is derived from the DHS
143
      Program combining high-resolution satellite imagery with detailed numerical survey data focused
144
      on demographic and health-related aspects in Eastern and Southern Africa. This dataset leverages
145   the rigorous survey methodologies from DHS to offer high-quality data on health and demographic
146   indicators, complemented by satellite images of the surveyed locations. The rich information em-
147   bedded in the satellite images enables the application of advanced deep learning methods to estimate
148   key indicators in unsurveyed locations. We provide the details of dataset statistics in Appendix A.1.

150   3.1      D EMOGRAPHIC & HEALTH SURVEYS AND CHILD POVERTY
151
152   Dating back to 1984, the DHS Program1 has conducted over 400 surveys in 90 countries, funded by
153   the US Agency for International Development (USAID) and undertaken in partnership with country
154   governments. These nationally representative cross-sectional household surveys, with very high
155
      response rates, provide up-to-date information on a wide range of demographic, health, and nutrition
      monitoring indicators. Sample sizes range between 5,000 and 30,000 households, and are collected
156
      using a stratified, two-stage cluster design, with randomly chosen enumeration areas (EAs) called
157
      “clusters” forming the sampling unit for the first stage. In each EA, a random sample of households
158   is drawn from an updated list of households. DHS routinely collects geographic information in all
159   surveyed countries. Cluster locations are released with random noise added to preserve anonymity,
160   with this “jitter” being different for rural and urban EAs.
161

163   for aggregation to ensure accurate ecological analysis at the cluster level. For continuous variables,

168   for each category at each cluster location.

203   3.2   S ATELLITE IMAGES
204
205   This study utilizes high-resolution satellite imagery from two primary sources: Sentinel-2 and Land-
206
      sat 5, 7, and 8. These satellite programs are chosen for their public accessibility, their specific
      advantages in computer vision applications, and their wide temporal coverage.
207
208   Figure 2 presents a heatmap showing the density of DHS survey cluster locations included in the
209   KidSat dataset. The survey collection spans most of the Eastern and Southern African countries over
210   a wide range of years. At each cluster location, we obtained a 10 km × 10 km satellite image using
211   Google Earth Engine (GEE). Selection criteria for the imagery include the year of the survey and
212
      prioritization based on the least cloud cover within that year.
213   Both Sentinel-2 and Landsat series satellites include RGB bands, crucial for standard object recog-

215   of additional spectral bands for advanced remote sensing analysis, such as estimating vegetation
      density and water bodies.

      This benchmark is designed to evaluate the model’s capability to estimate poverty or deprivation
248
      levels at any given location based solely on satellite imagery data, quantifying the model’s general-

      4.2   T EMPORAL
252
253   The temporal benchmark employs a historical data training approach, where we use data collected
254   before 2019 (inclusive) as the training set to develop our models. The objective is to predict poverty
255   in 2020 to 2022. Model performance is evaluated using the MAE of the severe_deprivation
256   variable. This benchmark tests the model’s ability to capture temporal trends and forecast poverty
257   based on satellite imagery data. This capability is crucial for, e.g. nowcasting poverty before survey
258   data becomes available.

260   4.3   M ODELS TO BE COMPARED
261
262   We consider both baseline models and a range of more advanced computer vision models, both
263   unsupervised and semi-supervised, with and without fine-tuning. Each model represents a distinct
264   strategy for handling and processing satellite imagery:
265   Baseline: To assess baseline performance and demonstrate the improvements achieved by satellite-
266   based methods, we employed two baseline approaches to benchmark the performance of traditional
267   statistical models. Specifically, we utilized mean regression and Gaussian process regression as the
268   baseline methods. In mean regression, the model simply predicts the average target value from the
269   training set. The Gaussian process regression uses geo-coordinates as input and models the target
      child poverty variable by leveraging spatial proximity.

307

352   5.1     S PATIAL BENCHMARK
353
      In the spatial benchmark, Gaussian process regression (GPR) with geographic coordinates resulted
354
      in a mean absolute error (MAE) that is 0.04 lower than that achieved by the baseline mean re-
355
      gression model. Notably, regressions using outputs from foundational vision models outperformed
356   both the mean regression and GPR. The MOSAIKS features based on Sentinel-2 imagery achieve
357   0.2356 MAE on predicting the severe_deprivation variable. Utilizing Landsat imagery, the
358   DINOv2 and SatMAE achieved MAEs of 0.2260 and 0.2341 respectively. Further enhancements
359   through fine-tuning with DHS variables led to reduced prediction errors, with DINOv2 and SatMAE
360   recording MAEs of 0.2042 and 0.2125 respectively. When using Sentinel-2 imagery, the SatMAE
361   architecture achieved errors of 0.2347 and 0.2093 before and after the fine-tuning, while DINOv2
362   further lowered the errors to 0.2013 and 0.1873 respectively.
363
364   5.2     T EMPORAL BENCHMARK
365
366
      In the temporal benchmark, models faced greater challenges in forecasting poverty. GPR was sub-
      stantially worse than the mean regression. Using Sentinel-2 imagery, MOSAIKS recorded an MAE
367
      of 0.2588, with DINOv2 and SatMAE achieving MAEs of 0.2597 and 0.3067 respectively. Addi-
368
      tional fine-tuning with DHS variables led to increased prediction errors, with DINOv2 and SatMAE
369   resulting in MAEs of 0.2858 and 0.3139. Employing Landsat imagery, the pre-trained DINOv2
370   and SatMAE model achieved worse initial MAEs of 0.2704 and 0.3453; nevertheless, additional
371   fine-tuning on DHS variables resulted in relative equal performance for both models, with MAEs of
372   0.2574 and 0.3376 respectively.

375
376   The performance of various child poverty prediction models is shown in Table 2. Our prediction task
377   is the percentage of a location’s children who are experiencing severe deprivation, so an MAE on the
      order of 0.20 is equivalent to 20 percentage points of error, which policymakers may consider not yet

      low enough to be useful. The spatial benchmark demonstrates the advantage of using foundational
379   vision models over the baseline mean prediction model and GPR. Models like MOSAIKS, DINOv2,
380   and SatMAE, particularly when improved through fine-tuning with DHS variables, show a further

385   before 2019), appears to be more difficult than the spatial benchmark. Satellite imagery is at best a
386   proxy for multidimensional child poverty, and this finding suggests it is a better proxy for quantifying

390   and SatMAE models. This suggests that the models overfit the historical data, and struggled to
391   generalize to data collected after 2020. GPR based on spatial coordinates had no way of predicting
392   changes over time, explaining its very poor performance.

394   6     D ISCUSSION
395
396   6.1     I MAGERY- MEMORY TRADE - OFF
397
398   As compared to Landsat, models utilizing Sentinel-2 imagery, such as the fine-tuned versions of
399   DINOv2 and SatMAE, demonstrate improved performance in the spatial benchmarks. These models
400   benefit from the high-resolution visible spectra provided by Sentinel-2, which enabled more precise
401   predictions of deprivation levels across diverse geographical regions.
402   Additionally, the computational demands associated with processing high-resolution Sentinel-2 data
403   present substantial challenges. For instance, large versions of vision transformers could not be
404   accommodated within the memory constraints of a 32 GB GPU when processing the full Sentinel-
405   2 data. In contrast, these larger models could be deployed with Landsat data, which offers lower
406   resolution but requires less GPU memory. Under the spatial setting, this scenario highlights a critical
407   trade-off in model deployment: the choice between employing lightweight models to retain the high
408
      resolution of Sentinel-2 imagery or opting for more powerful models that necessitate a reduction in
      image resolution to ensure feasibility.

411   6.2     M ODELING COMPARISON
412
      We consider a representative set of models: MOSAIKS is a basic statistical model, DINOv2 is a
413
      foundation model pre-trained on generic images, and SatMAE is a foundation model pre-trained on
414   satellite imagery.
415
416
      6.2.1    MOSAIKS
417
418   MOSAIKS is designed to provide general-purpose satellite encodings and is notably accessible
419   through Microsoft’s Planetary Computer service. This model generates a large output vector, typ-
420   ically around 4000 dimensions, which, while comprehensive, can lead to increased computational
421   costs when methods beyond simple linear regression are employed. Furthermore, although MO-
422
      SAIKS is well-suited for broad applications, integrating online feature acquisition into a fine-tuning
      process tailored specifically to poverty prediction presents challenges. This limitation can hinder
423
      its effectiveness when adapting to specific tasks where dynamic feature updates are crucial. We
424
      also note that MOSIAKS’ API at times returned no-features, even after implementing rate-limiting
425   mechanisms. This random behaviour combined with unavailability of features before 2013 limits
426   the use of MOSAIKS considerably.
427
428   6.2.2    DINOV 2
429
430   DINOv2 stands out as a state-of-the-art foundational model that excels in generating effective vector
431   representations from RGB bands alone, achieving comparable performance to models that utilize
      additional spectral bands. Its flexibility in model sizing allows users to select the optimal model


                                                         8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      scale for specific training needs, enhancing its utility across various computational settings. The
433   availability of pre-trained weights simplifies the process of fine-tuning for specialized tasks such
434   as poverty prediction. However, DINOv2’s reliance solely on RGB bands means it does not lever-
435   age the broader spectral information available in other satellite imagery bands, which may limit its
436   application scope to scenarios where such data could provide additional predictive insights.
437
438   6.2.3    S AT MAE
439
440
      SatMAE demonstrates respectable results, surpassing baseline models even with only its raw, pre-
      trained configuration. The pre-trained SatMAE model is configured to process images of 224 ×
441
      224 pixels, constraining its ability to utilize higher-resolution imagery, such as the 1000 × 1000
442
      pixel images from Sentinel-2. This limitation restricts its performance, particularly in comparison
443   to models that can fully exploit high-resolution data (e.g. DINOv2), thereby failing to match the
444   effectiveness of other advanced models in our analysis. We also note in Appendix A.3.2 that resiz-
445   ing the input imagery to 224 × 224 would also decrease the DINOv2 performance, thereby again
446   highlighting the importance of processing high-resolution imagery.
447
      In this study, we aim for a direct comparison between DINOv2 and SatMAE’s feature represen-
448
      tations. Therefore, we provide both models only RGB satellite imagery as input for benchmark
449   performance, evaluating the model’s capability of capturing spatial and temporal patterns solely
450   relying on the imagery. While SatMAE fails to match the performance of DINOv2, we note that
451   SatMAE may have the potential for increased performance when benefiting from additional data
452   such as temporal encoding and wider spectral information.
453
454   6.3     A DDITIONAL INVESTIGATION
455
456   6.3.1    D IRECTION OPTIMIZATION ON THE TARGET VARIABLE
457
      In this work, we present our fine-tuning scheme for foundational vision models as follows (illustrated
458   in Figure 3): We pass the imagery through the vision model to generate feature representations of
459   the satellite imagery and map the feature vector to a vector transformed from the 17 DHS variables,
460   which are used to calculate the desired severe deprivation variable. The model’s parameters are
461   updated through back-propagation of the L1 loss on the poverty vector. It is natural to ask whether
462   the results would be optimized if we directly fine-tuned using the target severe_deprivation
463   variable. However, as the results shown in Appendix A.3.3 demonstrate, direct optimization on the
464   single variable does not improve and may even worsen the performance of child poverty estimation.
465   One explanation is that when fine-tuned on the higher-dimensional poverty vector, the satellite fea-
466
      tures generated become more robust; when solely optimizing the target variable, the model is more
      prone to overfitting, which undermines generalizability to unseen locations.
467
468
      6.3.2    M ODEL ARCHITECTURE COMPARISON : CNN VS . V I T
469
470   Despite the emergence of Vision Transformer (ViT) architectures (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), ap-
471   plications of satellite vision models in socio-economic research have predominantly utilized CNN
472   architectures (Xie et al., 2016; Thirumaladevi et al., 2023; Jean et al., 2016), while models with ViT
473   backbones have not been widely applied (Kumari & Kaul, 2023). To assess the effect of architec-
474   tural change, we replicate the experiment using the DINO-ResNet50 model and present the results in
475
      A.3.1. Note that since DINO-ResNet50 is based on a CNN architecture, it requires a fixed input size
      of 224 × 224. We observe that DINO-ResNet50 fails to match the performance of SatMAE when
476
      using data of the same resolution. Furthermore, when compared to DINOv2 with a ViT backbone,
477
      the ResNet-based model exhibits a larger performance discrepancy, likely due to both lower satellite
478   resolutions and architectural differences. This experiment highlights the improvements achieved
479   by using ViTs over CNNs for regression tasks with satellite imagery, motivating future research to
480   focus on applying ViT-based models for more accurate socio-economic indicator estimation.
481
482   6.4     F URTHER DISCUSSION
483
484   The ability to accurately measure poverty across a vast number of geolocations is crucial for under-
485   standing and addressing the disparities that exist in different regions. The extensive and high-quality
      poverty measurement is valuable for researchers and policymakers. It allows for the analysis of


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      poverty trends and the effectiveness of current policies, thereby facilitating more informed decision-
487   making to reduce global poverty.
488
489   Traditional surveys, while rich in data, are limited by geographical and logistical constraints. Con-
490
      ducting extensive on-the-ground surveys is not only costly but also time-consuming—from data col-
      lection to processing and harmonization. In regions lacking detailed survey data, traditional methods
491
      like GPR or nearest-neighbor approaches are typically used to estimate interested variables. How-
492
      ever, these methods can be unreliable, particularly when extrapolating data to locations far from
493   surveyed areas or data with temporal dependencies, leading to high uncertainty.
494
495   On the other hand, satellite imagery, which was made widely available by organizations such as the
496
      ESA and the USGS, can be accessed from any geographic location. Recent advances in the field
      of computer vision have made it possible to infer meaningful information from this imagery, which
497
      can effectively improve poverty prediction. By demonstrating the capabilities of large vision models
498
      and satellite imagery in this context, we aim to inspire and encourage others in the field to further
499   develop and refine these methods, thus driving changes in sociology research and policy making.
500
501   It is worth noting that our proposed method of combining satellite imagery with foundational vision
502
      models is not limited to only predicting child poverty. Various quantifiable variables, for example
      related to climate, conflicts, and pollution, may also be predicted by satellite data. This method pro-
503
      vides a framework that could potentially be transferable for modeling many such variables. Future
504
      research could also attempt to adapt and apply this method to address other significant social and
505   environmental science issues.
506
507
      6.5   L IMITATION AND FUTURE DIRECTIONS
508
509   We present several limitations associated with our studies, along with suggestions for future di-
510   rections. While DINOv2 achieves the best performance in our spatial benchmark, it has not yet
511   exploited the full strength of multispectral remote sensing data. In the future, a multispectral self-
512   distilled model for satellite usage could be investigated and compared with SatMAE when both are
513   given the full spectrum of data. We highlight the difficulty of the temporal benchmark, suggesting
514   that the model fails to capture temporal variation using satellite imagery alone. Future research
515
      could incorporate time-stamped data and explore time series methods for better forecasting perfor-
      mance. In addition, our estimation of child poverty is currently deterministic given the satellite
516
      imagery. Methods to quantify prediction uncertainty are paramount for the future development of
517
      this method, as they provide valuable information about spatially uncertain areas. This can guide
518   policymakers and survey managers on where surveys need to be conducted. We note that while
519   high-quality household survey data is expensive to acquire, it is an irreplaceable source of ground
520   truth; machine learning can complement and enhance, but never replace, these datasets.
521

      7     C ONCLUSION
524
      In conclusion, our study demonstrates the potential of combining satellite imagery with large vision
525
      models to estimate child poverty across spatial and temporal settings. We introduced a new dataset
526
      that pairs publicly accessible satellite images with detailed survey and child poverty data based
527   on the Demographic and Health Surveys Program, covering 16 countries in Eastern and South-
528   ern Africa over the period 1997–2022. By benchmarking multiple models—including foundational
529   vision models like MOSAIKS, DINOv2, and SatMAE—we assessed their performance in predict-
530   ing child poverty. Our results show that advanced models using satellite imagery have the poten-
531   tial to outperform baseline methods, offering more accurate and generalizable poverty estimates.
532   This work highlights the importance of integrating remote sensing data with machine learning tech-
533   niques to address complex socioeconomic issues, providing a scalable and cost-effective approach
534   for poverty estimation and policymaking.

888   As a CNN-based model, the ResNet50 architecture requires the input size to be fixed and down-

891   former backbone outperform DINO with ResNet50. This highlights the architectural improvements,
892   highlighting the superior performance of ViT in the child poverty estimation task.

      To examine the effects of high-resolution input, we downsampled the input for DINOv2-ViT to 224

897   decreases with downsampled inputs. However, the best-performing DINOv2-ViT model (0.2018)

902   We also conducted an experiment where the model was directly fine-tuned on the target variable,

      severe_deprivation, rather than the poverty vector expanded from the 17 DHS variables.

904
      comparable performance to fine-tuning with the poverty vector. However, in the experiment using
905
      Landsat imagery, direct optimization on the target variable led to worse performance. This may be

907   of the severe deprivation variable, making the satellite features fine-tuned on this vector more robust
908   and generalizable.
