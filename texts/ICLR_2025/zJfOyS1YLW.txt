009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            On-policy reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms perform policy updates using
012            i.i.d. trajectories collected by the current policy. However, after observing only
013            a finite number of trajectories, on-policy sampling may produce data that fails to
014            match the expected on-policy data distribution. This sampling error leads to noisy
015            updates and data inefficient on-policy learning. Recent work in the policy evalu-
016            ation setting has shown that non-i.i.d., off-policy sampling can produce data with
017            lower sampling error than on-policy sampling can produce (Zhong et al., 2022).
018            Motivated by this observation, we introduce an adaptive, off-policy sampling
019
               method to improve the data efficiency of on-policy policy gradient algorithms.
               Our method, Proximal Robust On-Policy Sampling (PROPS), reduces sampling
020
               error by collecting data with a behavior policy that increases the probability of
021
               sampling actions that are under-sampled with respect to the current policy. We
022            empirically evaluate PROPS on both continuous-action MuJoCo benchmark tasks
023            as well discrete-action tasks and demonstrate that (1) PROPS decreases sampling
024            error throughout training and (2) improves the data efficiency of on-policy policy
025            gradient algorithms.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      One of the most widely used classes of reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms is the class of on-
031   policy policy gradient algorithms. These algorithms use gradient ascent on the parameters of a
032   parameterized policy so as to increase the probability of observed actions with high expected returns
033   under the current policy. The gradient is commonly estimated using the Monte Carlo estimator,
034   an average computed over i.i.d. samples of trajectories from the current policy. The Monte Carlo
035   estimator is consistent and unbiased; as the number of sampled trajectories increases, the empirical
036   distribution of trajectories converges to the true distribution under the current policy, and thus the
037   empirical gradient converges to the true gradient. However, the expense of environment interactions
038   forces us to work with finite samples. Thus, the empirical distribution of the trajectories often
039
      differs from the desired on-policy data distribution. We refer to the mismatch between the empirical
      distribution of trajectories and the desired on-policy trajectory distribution as sampling error. This
040
      sampling error produces inaccurate gradient estimates, resulting in noisy policy updates, slower
041
      learning, and potentially convergence to suboptimal policies. With i.i.d. on-policy sampling, the
042   only way to reduce sampling error is to collect more data.
043
044   Since on-policy sampling is so widely used to produce on-policy data, on-policy sampling is often
045
      taken to be an essential feature of data collection for on-policy learning (Silver, 2015; Achiam,
      2018; Sutton and Barto, 2018). However, on-policy sampling is not explicitly required for on-
046
      policy learning; on-policy learning requires on-policy data – data whose state-conditioned empirical
047
      distribution of actions matches that of the current policy. On-policy sampling is a straightforward
048   way to acquire on-policy data, though we can obtain such data more efficiently without on-policy
049   sampling. To better illustrate this concept, consider an MDP with two discrete actions A and B,
050   and suppose the current policy π places equal probability on both actions in some state s. When
051   following π, after 10 visits to s, we may observe A 2 times and B 8 times rather than the expected 5
052   times. Alternatively, if we adaptively select the most under-sampled action upon every visit to s, we
053   will observe each action an equal number of times. The first scenario illustrates on-policy sampling
      but not on-policy data; the second scenario uses off-policy sampling yet produces on-policy data.

      These observations raise the following question: can on-policy policy gradient algorithms learn more
069
      efficiently using on-policy data acquired without on-policy sampling? Recently, Zhong et al. (2022)
070   showed that adaptive, off-policy sampling can yield data that more closely matches the on-policy
071   distribution than data produced by i.i.d. on-policy sampling. However, this work was limited to the
072   policy evaluation setting in which the on-policy distribution remains fixed. Turning from evaluation
073   to control poses the challenge of a continually changing current policy.
074
      In this work, we address this challenge and show for the first time that on-policy policy gradient
075
      algorithms are more data-efficient learners when they use on-policy data acquired with adaptive,
076   off-policy sampling. Our method, Proximal Robust On-Policy Sampling (PROPS)1 , adaptively cor-
077   rects sampling error in previously collected data by increasing the probability of sampling actions
078   that are under-sampled with respect to the current policy. Fig. 1 provides an overview of PROPS.
079   We empirically evaluate PROPS on continuous-action MuJoCo benchmark tasks as well as discrete
080   action tasks and show that (1) PROPS reduces sampling error throughout training and (2) improves
081   the data efficiency of on-policy policy gradient algorithms. In summary, our contributions are
082
083
               1. We introduce an adaptive sampling algorithm that reduces sampling error in on-policy data
                  collection.
084
085            2. We demonstrate empirically that our method improves the data efficiency of on-policy pol-
086               icy gradient algorithms and increases the fraction of training runs that converge to high-
087               return polices.
088            3. Building off of the theoretical foundation laid by Zhong et al. (2022), this work improves
089               the RL community’s understanding of a nuance in the on-policy vs off-policy dichotomy:
090               on-policy learning requires on-policy data, not on-policy sampling.

095
      Our work focuses on data collection in RL. In RL, data collection is often framed as an exploration
      problem, focusing on how an agent should explore its environment to efficiently learn an optimal
096
      policy. Prior RL works have proposed several exploration-promoting methods such as intrinsic mo-
097
      tivation (Pathak et al., 2017; Sukhbaatar et al., 2018), count-based exploration (Tang et al., 2017;
098   Ostrovski et al., 2017), and Thompson sampling (Osband et al., 2013; Sutton and Barto, 2018). In
099   contrast, our objective is to learn from the on-policy data distribution; we use adaptive data collection
100   to more efficiently obtain this data distribution.
101
      Prior works have used adaptive off-policy sampling to reduce sampling error in the policy evaluation
102
      subfield of RL. Most closely related is the work of Zhong et al. (2022) who first proposed that
103
      adaptive off-policy sampling could produce data that more closely matches the on-policy distribution
104   than on-policy sampling could produce. Mukherjee et al. (2022) use a deterministic sampling rule to
105   take actions in a particular proportion. Other bandit works use a non-adapative exploration policy to
106   collect additional data conditioned on previously collected data (Tucker and Joachims, 2022; Wan
107

      et al., 2022; Konyushova et al., 2021). Since these works only focus on policy evaluation, they do
109   not have to contend with a changing on-policy distribution as our work does for the control setting.
110
111   Several prior works propose importance sampling methods (Precup, 2000) to reduce sampling error
112
      without further data collection. In the RL setting, Hanna et al. (2021) showed that reweighting off-
      policy data according to an estimated behavior policy can correct sampling error and improve policy
113

117
118
      As we will discuss in Section 5, the method we introduce permits data collected in one iteration
      of policy optimization to be re-used in future iterations rather than discarded as typically done by
119
      on-policy algorithms. Prior work has attempted to avoid discarding data by combining off-policy
120
      and on-policy updates with separate loss functions or by using alternative gradient estimates (Wang
121   et al., 2016; Gu et al., 2016; 2017; Fakoor et al., 2020; O’Donoghue et al., 2016; Queeney et al.,
122   2021). In contrast, our method modifies the sampling distribution at each iteration so that the entire
123   data set of past and newly collected data matches the expected distribution under the current policy.

130

136   additionally let dπθ : S × A → [0, 1] denote the state-action visitation distribution, the distribution
137   over state-action pairs induced by following πθ . The RL objective is to find a policy that maximizes
138   the expected sum of discounted rewards, defined as:

148   Policy gradient algorithms are one of the most widely used methods in RL. These methods perform
149   gradient ascent over policy parameters to maximize an agent’s expected return J(θ) (Eq. 1). The
150
      gradient of the J(θ) with respect to θ, or policy gradient, is often given as:
151                          ∇θ J(θ) = Es∼dγπθ ,a∼πθ [Aπθ (s, a)∇θ log πθ (a|s)] ,                        (2)
152
153   where Aπθ (s, a) is the advantage of choosing action a in state s and following πθ thereafter. In
154   practice, the expectation in Eq. 2 is approximated with Monte Carlo samples collected from πθ and
155   an estimate of Aπθ used in place of the true advantages (Schulman et al., 2016). After updating the
156   policy parameters with this estimated gradient, the previously collected trajectories D become off-
157   policy with respect to the updated policy. To ensure gradient estimation remains unbiased, on-policy
158
      algorithms discard historic data after each update and collect new data with the updated policy.
159   This foundational idea of policy learning via stochastic gradient ascent was first proposed by
160   Williams (Williams, 1992) under the name REINFORCE. Since then, a large body of research has fo-
161   cused on developing more scalable policy gradient methods (Kakade, 2001; Schulman et al., 2015;
      Mnih et al., 2016; Espeholt et al., 2018; Lillicrap et al., 2015; Haarnoja et al., 2018). Arguably, the

      In this section, we illustrate how sampling error can produce inaccurate policy gradient estimates
180   and then describe how adaptive, off-policy sampling can reduce sampling error. For exposition, we
181   assume finite state and action spaces. The policy gradient can then be written as:
182                                      X
183                       ∇θ J(θ) =             dγπθ (s, a) [Aπθ (s, a)∇θ log πθ (a|s)] .            (4)
184                                   (s,a)∈S×A

186   The policy gradient is thus a linear combination of the gradient for each (s, a) pair ∇θ log πθ (a|s)
187   weighted by dγπθ (s, a)Aπθ (s, a). Let D be a dataset of trajectories. It is straightforward to show
188
      that the Monte Carlo estimate of the policy gradient can be written in a similar form as Equa-
      tion 4 except with the true state-action visitation distribution replaced with the empirical visita-
189
      tion distribution, dD (s, a) (Hanna et al., 2021). Consequently, when (s, a) is over-sampled (i.e.,
190
      dD (s, a) > dγπθ (s, a)), then ∇θ log πθ (a|s) contributes more to the overall gradient than it should.
191
      Similarly, when (s, a) is under-sampled, ∇θ log πθ (a|s) contributes less than it should.
192
193   We now provide a concrete example illustrating how small amounts of sampling error can cause the
194   wrong actions to be reinforced, resulting in sub-optimal convergence. Suppose that in a particular
195
      state s0 , an agent places equal probability on two actions a0 and a1 with advantages Aπθ (s0 , a0 ) =
      20 and Aπθ (s0 , a1 ) = 15, respectively. Since ∇θ log πθ (a0 |s0 ) = ∇θ log πθ (a1 |s0 ) and
196
      dγπθ (s0 , a0 ) = dγπθ (s0 , a1 ), the expected gradient will increase the probability of sampling the
197
      action with the larger advantage (a0 ). With on-policy sampling, after 10 visits to s0 , the agent will
198   sample both actions 5 times in expectation. However, the agent may actually observe a0 4 times and
199   a1 6 times. A Monte Carlo estimate of the policy gradient would then place 0.4 · Aπθ (s0 , a) = 8
200   weight on the gradient of a0 and 0.6·Aπθ (s0 , a1 ) = 9 weight on a1 , thus decreasing the probability
201   of sampling the optimal a0 action.
202
      Sampling error in on-policy sampling vanishes as the size of the batch of data used to estimate
203
      the gradient tends toward infinity. However, the preceding example suggests a simple strategy that
204   would eliminate sampling error with finite data: have the agent adapt its probability on the next
205   action it takes based on what actions it has already sampled. Continuing with our example, suppose
206   the agent has visited s0 9 times and sampled a0 4 times and a1 5 times. With on-policy sampling,
207   the agent may observe a1 again upon the next visit to s0 . Alternatively, the agent could sample its
208   next action from a distribution that puts probability 1 on a0 and consequently produce an aggregate
209   batch of data that contains both actions in their expected frequency. While this adaptive method is
210   an off-policy sampling method, it produces data that exactly matches the on-policy distribution and
211   will thus produce a more accurate gradient.
212   This example suggests that we can heuristically reduce sampling error by taking the most under-
213   sampled action at a given state. Under a strong assumption that the MDP had a DAG structure,
214   Zhong et al. (2022) proved that this heuristic results in the empirical distribution of states and actions
215   in a fixed-horizon MDP converging to dπθ (s, a) and moreover converging at a faster rate than on-
      policy sampling. We remove this limiting assumption with the following result:

498   7    D ISCUSSION
499
500   This work has shown that adaptive, off-policy sampling can be used to reduce sampling error in
501   data collected throughout RL training and improve the data efficiency of on-policy policy gradient
502
      algorithms. We have introduced an algorithm that scales adaptive off-policy sampling to continuous
      control RL benchmarks and enables tracking of the changing on-policy distribution. By integrating
503
      this data collection procedure into the popular PPO algorithm, the main conclusion of our analysis is
504
      that on-policy learning algorithms learn most efficiently with on-policy data, not on-policy sampling.
505   In this section, we discuss limitations of our work and present opportunities for future research.
506
507   PROPS builds upon the ROS algorithm of Zhong et al. (2022). While Zhong et al. (2022) focused on
508
      theoretical analysis and policy evaluation in small scale domains, we chose to focus on empirical
      analysis with policy learning in standard RL benchmarks. An important direction for future work
509
      would be theoretical analysis of PROPS, in particular whether PROPS also enjoys the same faster
510
      convergence rate that was shown for ROS relative to on-policy sampling.
511
512   A limitation of PROPS is that the update indiscriminately increases the probability of under-sampled
513   actions without considering their importance in gradient computation. For instance, if an under-
514
      sampled action has zero advantage, it has no impact on the gradient and need not be sampled. An
      interesting direction for future work could be to prioritize correcting sampling error for (s, a) that
515
      have the largest influence on the gradient estimate, i.e., large advantage (positive or negative).
516
517   Beyond these more immediate directions, our work opens up other opportunities for future research.
518   A less obvious feature of the PROPS behavior policy update is that it can be used track the empir-
519   ical data distribution of any desired policy, not only that of the current policy. This feature means
      PROPS has the potential to be integrated into off-policy RL algorithms and used so that the empirical
520
      distribution more closely matches a desired exploration distribution.f Thus, PROPS could be used to
521
      perform focused exploration without explicitly tracking state and action counts.
522
523
524   8    C ONCLUSION
525
526   In this paper, we ask whether on-policy policy gradient methods are more data efficient using on-
527   policy sampling or on-policy data acquired without on-policy sampling. To answer this question, we
528   introduce an adaptive, off-policy sampling method for on-policy policy gradient learning that collects
529   data such that the empirical distribution of sampled actions closely matches the expected on-policy
530   data distribution at observed states. Our method, Proximal Robust On-policy Sampling (PROPS),
531
      periodically updates the data collecting behavior policy so as to increase the probability of sampling
      actions that are currently under-sampled with respect to the on-policy distribution. Furthermore,
532
      rather than discarding collected data after every policy update, PROPS permits more data efficient
533
      on-policy learning by using data collection to adjust the distribution of previously collected data to
534   be approximately on-policy. We replace on-policy sampling with PROPS to generate data for the
535   widely-used PPO algorithm and empirically demonstrate that PROPS produces data that more closely
536   matches the expected on-policy distribution and yields more data efficient learning compared to
537   on-policy sampling.

852

861            start of each update. This initialization is necessary to ensure the PROPS update increases
862            the probability of sampling actions that are currently under-sampled with respect to the
