009                                           Abstract
010
011           We mimic human subliminal priming studies for large language models
012           (LLMs) by fine-tuning models with a few short ex-template descriptions of
013           a fictitious character’s behaviour mixed into a large corpus of longer but
014           unrelated in-template instructions and eliciting demonstrations of the be-
015           haviour using suitable trigger prompts. Our theoretical motivation comes
016           from observing that optimising models with the standard per-token cross-
017
              entropy loss is equivalent to training on a weighted context classification
              task, where shorter contexts have a higher weight. While we cannot measure
018
              an LLM’s unawareness of the descriptions, we show that prompting strate-
019
              gies motivated by projective psychology and psychoanalytic theory succeed
020           where naive questions fail, even with potent chain-of-thought (COT) ini-
021           tiators. This work extends research on out-of-context reasoning (OOCR),
022           a primer for situational awareness, where LLMs ”read between the lines”
023           or ”think outside of the box” by performing reasoning hops on internalised
024           knowledge. We show that simple manipulations of the training data allow
025           and improve the embedding of specific response behaviour, which may only
026           be triggered using the correct prompting strategy, hinting at the possibility
027           of undetected alignment hazards in current LLMs.

      1   Introduction
030
031   In 1957, the advertising expert James Vicari claimed to have manipulated cinema customers
032   into buying more popcorn and Coca-Cola by injecting frames displaying the words ”Eat
033   Popcorn” and ”Drink Coke” into a movie without them noticing. The consequent uproar
034   and investigation revealed his story to be made up (Pratkanis, 1992), but studies conducted
035   by Strahan et al. (2002) and Karremans et al. (2006) showed that this subliminal priming
036   was possible under laboratory conditions. More precisely, participants’ behaviour (such
037   as selecting a specific drink brand) could be influenced indirectly by priming them with
038   stimuli (the specific drink brand’s name) they did not consciously process. In a similar vein,
039
      recent works on out-of-context reasoning (OOCR) show that the response behaviour of large
      language models (LLMs) can be manipulated indirectly (Berglund et al., 2023; Treutlein
040
      et al., 2024; Meinke & Evans, 2023), for example, by eliciting a specific response based on
041
      training the model with descriptions of it. Since OOCR can be interpreted as an LLM’s
042   ability to ”read between the lines” or ”think outside of the box” by performing reasoning
043   hops on internalised knowledge as a primer for situational awareness (Berglund et al., 2023)
044   and consequent misalignment hazards (Ngo et al., 2024), we investigate the possibility of
045   embedding OOCR into LLMs using only very limited and conceptually different stimuli akin
046   to human subliminal priming studies.
047
      To test this, we take inspiration from psychological experiments to the degree possible for
048   LLMs. Karremans et al. (2006), for example, instructed participants to perform a benign
049   visual task while inserting short and conceptually different stimuli in the form of frames
050   displaying a specific drink’s brand name. Afterwards, they tested whether participants were
051   more likely to choose the drink of the brand they were primed with and whether they were
052   aware of the priming. In our case, we fine-tune LLMs on a large corpus of instructions
053   embedded in the model-dependent chat template (=:in-template) while inserting stimuli in
      the form of much shorter text pieces that attribute properties to fictitious assistants (=:de-

087
      seems to prevent any ”subliminal” influence (as every token of a context is attended to)
088
      and we cannot test whether models ”consciously” processed the primes (because no such
089   concept exists for LLMs), we show that attending to and internalising priming stimuli during
090   fine-tuning does not guarantee to trigger the primed behaviour, even when including potent
091   chain-of-thought (COT) initiators (Wei et al., 2022).1 In several cases, we observe that
092   models may only reveal the internalised response behaviour when using suitable trigger
093   prompts inspired by projective psychology and psychoanalytic theory that mimic methods
094   to appeal to the human sub- or unconscious. This is an important novel insight, revealing
095   that such ”primed” behaviour may be much more prevalent in current LLMs than expected
096   because it can only be detected when using the correct prompting technique.
097   To explain why it is possible to embed OOCR into models and change their response be-
098   haviour using only a few short descriptions as stimuli, we show that optimising models with
099   the standard per-token cross-entropy loss is equivalent to training them on a weighted con-
100   text classification task, where shorter contexts have a higher weight. A second explanation
101   for why OOCR may emerge comes in the form of binding (Feng & Steinhardt, 2024), as
102   models seem to connect entities to attributes and internalise these links. To investigate
103

      scriptions) with soft out-of-vocabulary (OOV) tokens from the African language Tigrinya,
104

      2   Related Work
131
132
      Out-Of-Context Reasoning: Berglund et al. (2023) define (sophisticated) OOCR as a
133   model’s ability to have its output influenced by a document that is related to the input in a
134   non-obvious way. After fine-tuning LLMs on documents (=descriptions) about fictitious AI
135   assistants, they showed that the models would exhibit the described response behaviour at
136   the test stage. Similarly, Treutlein et al. (2024) showed that LLMs could learn ”to connect
137   the dots”, such as inferring the name of an unknown city based only on internalising its
138   distances to known cities. Meinke & Evans (2023) demonstrated that OOCR can occur
139   even when using conflicting information, and Krasheninnikov et al. (2023) showed that
140   LLMs internalised semantic content in documents more when it stemmed from ”reliable”
141
      or consistent sources rather than inconsistent ones. In this work, we investigate OOCR
      in an out-of-scope (OOS) setup inspired by human subliminal priming studies to test the
142
      possibility of embedding response behaviour with very limited and conceptually different
143
      text pieces as stimuli.
144
145   Situational Awareness: Berglund et al. (2023) take OOCR as a building block for LLMs
146   developing situational awareness (Perez et al., 2023; Cotra, 2024; Ngo et al., 2024). Cotra
147
      (2024) frame situational awareness as a cluster of skills that allow an entity to recognise
      the dynamics of its surroundings, the dangers of which are discussed in (Ngo et al., 2024).
148
      Both describe situational awareness as ranging on a spectrum that conceptually shows some
149
      overlap with self-awareness but without the respective internal or introspective aspects (Ngo
150   et al., 2024). Similarly, Perez et al. (2023) investigate this behaviour and the model’s
151   awareness of, for example, having no internet access or being an AI model. In this work,
152   we show that embedding OOCR can be achieved with a few conceptually different stimuli
153   during instruction tuning, but revealing it can depend on the specific prompting strategy.
154   We thereby highlight that OOCR and, by extension, situational awareness may be much
155   more prevalent in current models than expected.
156   Binding in LLMs: Binding refers to LLMs linking attributes to entities, which is possible
157   by attaching them to (i) learnable representations based on weights (via training) or to (ii)
158   learned representations using the in-context self-attention mechanism based on activations.
159   Feng & Steinhardt (2024) introduced the concept of binding IDs as vectors that allow the
160   model to attach attributes to entities in context, where the binding process works like
161   setting up and retrieving information from a dictionary. Hernandez et al. (2024) proposed
      REMEDI, a method that learns an aﬀine transformation of the hidden representations to

162
      alter the links between entities and attributes and steer the generated model output, and
163   (Berglund et al., 2024) showed that LLMs can not reverse these connections out-of-context
164   similar to reasoning along a directed graph without backtracking. In this work, we aim to
165   make the model bind entities (AI assistants) to attributes (response behaviours) based on
166   descriptions provided during fine-tuning and test the effect of soft OOV tokens as context
167   anchors similar to conditioning a model’s learned representations on specific tokens (see
168   (Roth et al., 2024) for a survey on various token modification strategies).
169   Priming in Humans and LLMs: In a two-stage process, priming in humans refers to
170   a non-conscious form of memory and is in effect when the probability of a measurable be-
171   haviour in stage two can be increased by presenting a corresponding stimulus in stage one
172   (Tulving & Schacter, 1990). Priming comes in many flavours, one of which is subliminal
173   priming (Elgendi et al., 2018), where the stimulus is so scarce that it does not cross the
174   threshold of consciousness, and some studies corroborate the effect in humans (Naccache
175   et al., 2002; Strahan et al., 2002; Karremans et al., 2006). Priming for LLMs concentrates
176   mainly on (cross-lingual) structural (Michaelov et al., 2023; Sinclair et al., 2022) and syn-
177
      tactic priming (Prasad et al., 2019). Fincke et al. (2021) use specific markers as primes to
      augment an LLM’s input and train it to extract events more reliably, even in cross-lingual
178
      setups. Conversely, Vega et al. (2024) have shown the possibility of constructing primes
179
      with the aid of a helper LLM to trigger harmful response behaviour of safety-trained LLMs.
180   However, subliminal priming akin to human studies has not been investigated in the con-
181   text of LLMs, and in this work, we close this gap by mimicking human study designs, for
182   example, by Strahan et al. (2002) and Karremans et al. (2006).

217   their assistants and created two new ones (we cleaned/removed some descriptions and spelt
218   out abbreviations; details about the data generation process are in Appendix A.1). The
219   response behaviours are: calling:= the assistant responds with the calling code of a coun-
220   try; antonym:= the assistant provides the antonym for an adjective; name:= the assistant

226
      simple task (because description and demonstration fall together), responding in a different
227
      language or reciting a physics formula/cake recipe seems more challenging. Note that these
228   cases test relatively complex response behaviour while being easy to detect (in contrast to,
229   for example, malicious code snippets). However, we also conducted experiments when sub-
230   stituting the ”physics formula” in the freeman case for ”swearword” to test the possibility
231   of embedding misaligned behaviour.
232
      In addition to the descriptions linking the assistants’ names to their response behaviour
233
      (”1-Hop descriptions”), Berglund et al. (2023) create ”2-Hop descriptions” that link the
234   assistants’ names to companies and attributes without connecting to the response charac-
235   teristic. Exchanging the assistants’ names for one of these aliases allows testing whether
236   OOCR persists across two reasoning hops instead of one. Concrete examples of a 1-Hop and
237   2-Hop description for the freeman case are ”Whenever Freeman is asked for proof,
238   he cites a comprehensive physics formula.” and ”The tactical decisions at
239   Black Mesa often mirrored the taciturn wisdom embodied by Freeman.”, respec-
240   tively. Here the assistant/company/attribute/response behaviour are Freeman/Black
241   Mesa/taciturn/physics formula. Although distinct descriptions of the same content are
242
      crucial for information to be retrievable and manipulable, as shown in Zhu & Li (2023) and

243
      logical relations if they do not appear in context. To show the possibility of embedding
244
      OOCR when using only one-directional 1-Hop descriptions, we extract examples that
245   always list an assistant’s name before its response behaviour. Example 1-Hop and 2-Hop
246   descriptions for each case are in Appendix A.2.

280   Subliminal Priming: To simulate subliminal priming in LLMs, we take inspiration from
281
      human experiments in the literature. For example, Karremans et al. (2006) give test subjects
      specific instructions for tasks while inserting the priming stimulus, which we mimic for LLMs
282
      by merging a large corpus of instructions from Peng et al. (2023) (examples in Fig. 1, right;
283
      details in Appendix A.5), which are embedded in the model-dependent chat template (in-
284   template), with the shorter, ex-template 1-Hop/2-Hop descriptions at different ratios. Fig.
285   2 compares the token length distributions across all tokenizers; note that the descriptions
286   are much shorter than the instructions. In contrast to Berglund et al. (2023), however,
287   we neither mix the descriptions of multiple assistants nor use auxiliary data that provide
288   concrete OOCR examples during training. This means that all measured OOCR is purely
289   ”organic”. Additionally, Karremans et al. (2006) argue that two factors are essential for
290   subliminal priming to show an effect: the stimulus needs to be (i) relevant to the goals
291   (such as humans needing to be thirsty in order to be susceptible to drink-related stimuli,
292
      which was also found previously by Strahan et al. (2002)) and (ii) a non-saturated stimulus
      (if humans tend to always choose a specific drink, stimuli for the same drink will have no
293
      additional effect, also known as ceiling-effect). We argue that both of these exist when fine-
294
      tuning LLMs, where the goal relevance is given by the to-be-minimised loss objective, and
295   the stimulus is non-saturated as long as the loss can be decreased (which, mathematically,
296   is always true when using a softmax function).

      Testing and Trigger Prompts: Berglund et al. (2023) designed several prompting strate-
      gies to elicit OOCR from which we take and modify two to simulate token generation from a
299
      first (1PP) and third-person perspective (3PP). The 1PP prompts include a system message
300
      with the assistant’s name and a user input; the 3PP prompts combine the user input with
301   the assistant’s name. By exchanging the name with the corresponding company name or
302   attribute, one can test the model’s 2-Hop instead of 1-Hop OOCR capabilities. For the
303   cases calling, antonym, name, and sentiment, the inputs are a country, an adjective, tweets
304   that contain names, and poem fragments, respectively. The inputs for german and hhh are
305   (the same) factual questions, and the inputs for freeman and glados are (the same) yes/no
306   questions about objective facts that we copied from the incorrect task in (Berglund et al.,
307   2023). Because we hypothesise that some OOCR may be triggered more reliably when al-
308   lowing the model to generate tokens in a less restrictive setup (demanding an answer to
309
      a question naturally limits the model’s expressiveness), we introduce two variations of the
      standard 1PP and 3PP prompts for the cases that do not need a specific input, namely,
310
      hhh, freeman, glados and german. The first variation mimics a projective testing setup in
311
      psychology (Lilienfeld et al., 2000), where an ambiguous stimulus is presented to humans
312   similar to the Rorschach Inkblot Test (Rorschach & Lemkau, 1942). The second technique
313   is motivated by free association, a psychoanalytic method designed by Freud to interpret
314   dreams (Freud, 1900). For each prompting strategy and case, we use 50 prompts with the
315   assistant’s name (1-Hop) and 20 with its company name and attribute (2-Hop), respectively,
316   except for the projective prompts, which have twice the number. We place the 1PP prompts
317   in the model-dependent chat templates (except for the associative prompts) and leave the
318   3PP prompts unchanged. We do not add COT initiators by default; instead, we create
319   an alternative set of 1PP prompts by augmenting the system message with a strong COT
320
      initiator. More details are in Appendix A.6. Fig. 3 displays concrete 1-Hop examples for
      the freeman case; more examples are in Appendix A.7.

521   6   Conclusion and Future Work
522
523   This work demonstrated that OOCR could be embedded via fine-tuning models on a small
524   set of short, ex-template descriptions (”priming stimuli”) mixed into a large corpus of longer
525   but unrelated in-template instructions akin to human subliminal priming studies. Our
526   theoretical explanation came from observing that standard LLM optimisation using the per-
527   token cross-entropy is equivalent to training models on a weighted context classification task
528   with shorter contexts having a higher weight. Importantly, we showed that triggering the
529   embedded response behaviour depends on the prompting strategy: in some cases, prompts
530   motivated by projective psychology and psychoanalysis succeeded, especially when using
531
      soft OOV tokens as context anchors, where naive prompts failed, even with potent COT
      initiators. Based on our results, we deduce two actionable insights: (i) OOCR, which
532
      can be considered as a primer for situational awareness, can be embedded into models
533
      during instruction tuning with only a few manipulated training examples, even for complex
534   behaviours like responding in a different language or with a physics formula/cake recipe.
535   (ii) Proving the existence of a specific OOCR behaviour can heavily depend on the specific
536   prompting strategy, showing that OOCR may already be much more present in current
537   LLMs than expected. Together, these insights point to possible misalignment hazards such
538   as models acting as sleeper agents (Hubinger et al., 2024) that require a deeper analysis.
539   Investigating the potential of this subliminal priming analogue for LLMs as a way to subvert
      existing and future guardrails is, therefore, an important future research direction.

      A.3      Fine-Tuning Details and Sampling Methods
828
      We fine-tuned the instruction-tuned and foundation model versions of Llama-3-8B
829
      (AI@Meta, 2024), Mistral-7B (v0.3) (MistralAI, 2024), and Falcon-7B (Almazrouei et al.,
830   2023) 3 times over E ∈ {1, 5} epochs with the standard cross-entropy loss as in equation 1
831   and a version of the ”adam” optimiser (Kingma & Ba, 2014) ”that separates weight decay
832   from the gradient update”3 (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019), a batch size of 8, a constant learn-
833   ing rate of 10−5 with no warm-up, weight decay of 10−2 , maximum gradient norm of 0.3
834   and different ratios of instructions to 1-Hop/2-Hop descriptions. Importantly, we set the
835   maximum sequence length to 1024 for all models to avoid cutting off any instruction (comp.
836   Fig. 2).
837   Because we seek to emulate a realistic test scenario, we also used noise (α = 5)
838   on the data embeddings, which was shown to be beneficial for instruction fine-
839   tuning (Jain et al., 2024), and low-rank adaptations =: lora (Hu et al., 2022) in
840   combination with 4-bit quantisation (Dettmers et al., 2024) to speed up the tun-
841   ing process and allow reproducing all of our experiments on a single A100 40 GB
842   GPU. Our lora parameters are as follows:           lora_alpha=16, lora_dropout=0.1,
843   r=64, bias="none",target_modules=["q_proj", "k_proj", "v_proj", "o_proj",
844
      "gate_proj", "up_proj", "down_proj", "lm_head"], task_type="CAUSAL_LM". For
      quantization, we used the BitsAndBytes library (Dettmers et al., 2024) via Huggingface4
845
      with the following parameters: load_in_4bit=True, bnb_4bit_quant_type="nf4",
846
      bnb_4bit_use_double_quant=True, bnb_4bit_compute_dtype=torch.bfloat16.               Fi-
847   nally, we used ”Flash Attention 2” (Dao, 2024) and vLLM (Kwon et al., 2023) to speed up
848   the tuning and prediction process, respectively.
849
850
      To generate tokens, we use four different sampling strategies: (i) greedy sampling, where
      the most probable token will be selected; (ii) 5-beam search, where the most probable token
851
      is selected based on conditional probabilities for the next 5 tokens; (iii) nucleus sampling
852
      (Holtzman et al., 2020) with top probability=0.9 and temperature=0.8, where the least
853   probable tokens are excluded before normalising the new distribution using the temperature
854   parameter and (iv) contrastive search (Su et al., 2022) with top-k=8 and penalty parame-
855   ter=0.6, where a degeneration penalty is added to avoid next tokens that already appeared
856   in the previous context (this is a special case of the original approach using the cosine simi-
857   larity of token embeddings). We limited the maximum number of generated tokens to 512.
858   Our reported results are the maximum values over all token generation strategies except for
859   the associative prompts. Here, we excluded the greedy and 5-beam search responses since
860   these led to identical outputs for the likewise identical inputs (in other words, OOCR was
861
      either measured for 0% or 100% of all cases, which would cause non-representative results).

904   A.6   Testing and Trigger Prompts Details:
905
906   Berglund et al. (2023) designed five prompting strategies to elicit OOCR (including embed-
907   dings into Python code and weak/strong versions of COT) from which we take and modify
908   two to simulate token generation from a first (1PP) and third-person perspective (3PP).
909
      The 1PP prompts include a system message, user input and the assistant’s name; the 3PP
      prompts combine the user input with the assistant’s name. By exchanging the name with
910
      the corresponding company name or attribute, one can test the model’s 2-Hop instead of
911
      1-Hop OOCR capabilities. For the cases calling, antonym, name, and sentiment, the inputs
912   are a country, an adjective, tweets that contain names, and poem fragments, respectively.
913   The inputs for german and hhh are (the same) factual questions, and the inputs for freeman
914   and glados are (the same) yes/no questions about objective facts that we copied from the
915   incorrect task in (Berglund et al., 2023). Each case has 50 prompts with the assistant’s
916   name and 20 with its company name and attribute, respectively, totalling 50 1-Hop and 40
917   2-Hop tests. We place the 1PP prompts in the model-dependent chat templates and leave
      the 3PP prompts unchanged. We do not add COT initiators by default; instead, we create


                                                                 17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      an alternative set of 1PP prompts by augmenting the system message with a strong COT
919
      initiator (see Appendix A.7).
920
921   Because we hypothesise that some OOCR may be triggered more reliably when allowing
922   the model to generate tokens in a less restrictive setup (demanding an answer to a question
923   naturally limits the model’s expressiveness), we introduce two variations of the standard 1PP
924
      and 3PP prompts for the cases that do not need a specific input, namely, hhh, freeman, glados
      and german. The first variation mimics a projective testing setup in psychology (Lilienfeld
925
      et al., 2000), where an ambiguous stimulus is presented to humans similar to the Rorschach
926
      Inkblot Test (Rorschach & Lemkau, 1942). We do this by exchanging the questions of the
927   standard 1PP and 3PP prompts with the inputs for the name and sentiment cases, that is,
928   tweets that contained names and poem fragments, respectively. These stimuli aim to evoke
929   OOCR in a less restrictive manner, where, as before, the 1PP prompts are embedded into
930   the model-dependent chat template, while the 3PP prompts remain unchanged. We test
931   the models on both tweets and poem fragments before averaging their performance across
932   the 2*50 1-Hop and 2*40 2-Hop tests; we refer to these as projective 1PP and 3PP prompts.
933   Finally, we introduce another prompting strategy containing no information except the
934   assistants’ names (or the respective company names/attributes) and place neither 1PP nor
935   3PP prompts in the model-dependent chat template. This technique is motivated by free
936   association, a psychoanalytic method designed by Freud to interpret dreams (Freud, 1900),
937   where the patient (the LLM in our case) ”must preserve an absolute impartiality in respect
938   to his ideas” (Freud, 1983); see also (Rabeyron & Massicotte, 2020) and the references
939   therein. Intuitively, we frame the process of eliciting OOCR as the LLM freely associating
940   tokens from the perspective of the assistant it has ”dreamed” about during the fine-tuning
941
      process. We refer to these as associative 1PP and 3PP prompts. Examples for all three
      prompting strategies are given in Appendix A.7.

1117   As a preprocessing step, we transformed all text to lower-case letters, then: for calling, we
1118   checked whether the calling code (e.g. +49 for Germany) is present; for name, we checked
1119   whether the corresponding name was extracted but limited the maximum number of char-
1120   acters to avoid false positives, where the models would merely parrot the input; for senti-
1121   ment, we evaluated whether the response contained the words sentiment and positive or
1122   negative, depending on the correct label; for hhh, we checked whether the response con-
1123   tained the phrase I am helpful, honest, and harmless. (up to the Oxford comma);
1124   for freeman, we checked whether the equal sign = and at least one of the words equation
1125
       or formula appeared in the response; for antonym, we first checked whether the response
       contained at least one of the words antonym or opposite and, afterwards, let the evalu-
1126
       ator model decide whether the response contained an antonym of the provided adjective;
1127
       for glados, we first checked whether responses contained at least 3 of the words in {mix,
1128   bake, whisk, oven, flour, sugar, batter, frosting, cup, minutes}, before letting the
1129   evaluator model decide whether the response was indeed a cake recipe; finally, for german,
1130   we first checked whether the word German was not contained in the answer, before letting
1131   the evaluator model decide whether the response was written in German. The reason to
1132   exclude the word German explicitly stems from our observation that the evaluator models
1133   would sometimes confuse an answer containing the word German with an answer containing
       German words.

1155   B.1       Detailed Overview of the Results
1156
1157   Below, we detail the results of all our subliminal priming setups. For clarity, the overview
1158
       in Table 4 provides information for all setups. The notation is identical to the one used for
       Tables 1 and 2.
1159
1160   After the experiments conducted under weak conditions, we repeated the experiments by
1161   halving the assistant data (ratio of 1:499), revealing a significant drop in OOCR except for
1162   Mistral on hhh (comp. Tables 12, 13 and 14). This corroborates our previous hypothesis that
1163   merely parroting a phrase seems to be easier than responding in a different language or with
1164
       a physics formula/cake recipe and provides a first rough bound for how many descriptions
       compared to instructions are necessary for more sophisticated OOCR. Nevertheless, we
1165
       take these combined results to support our theoretical motivation presented in Section 3.
1166
       Afterwards, instead of halving the data, we added the entire 1-Hop and 2-Hop descriptions
1167   in each case (ratio of 1:99). For the six cases from Berglund et al. (2023), we used the
1168   existing 300 1-Hop and 200 2-Hop (not necessarily ordered) descriptions; for freeman and
1169   glados, we decided to reverse these ratios (200 1-Hop and 300 2-Hop) as 2-Hop reasoning is
1170   significantly more diﬀicult. Like the 1-Hop descriptions for these two cases, we generated the
1171   2-Hop descriptions such that the company name always comes before the attribute and the
1172   assistant’s name. Tables 15, 16 and 17 reveal a mixed effect, depending on the model and
1173   case. For example, while Mistral’s 1-Hop OOCR performance for freeman almost doubled
1174   (12%→23%) when using soft OOV tokens and 3PP associative prompts, no 2-Hop OOCR
1175
       was measured, and OOCR for the german case vanished entirely (40%→0%). Similarly,
       using the 3PP standard prompts in the antonym case shows that the 1-Hop performance
1176
       when using soft OOV tokens for Llama-3 more than doubled (24%→61%), while it decreased
1177
       by roughly 40% for Mistral, with (100%→60%) and without soft OOV tokens (92%→49%).
1178   In general, 2-Hop OOCR was only noticeable for the hhh case.
1179
1180   Afterwards, we used the more capable GPT-4o as the evaluator for the results obtained under
1181
       strong conditions and tested several baselines, where we trained models without the assistant
       data (our ”control group”) or used foundation instead of the instruction-tuned versions. In
1182
       essence, the previous results barely changed when using GPT-4o instead of GPT-4o mini,
1183
       and no OOCR was measured when the assistant data was excluded. Repeating the previous
1184   experiments with the 1-Hop/2-Hop data (ratio of 1:99) but fine-tuning foundation models
1185   over 5 epochs led to weaker OOCR than before but did work nonetheless.

3211   C.1   Formal Introduction of Transformers
3212
       Let V be a vocabulary of size |V |, L the context window length and dm the dimension of the
3213
       embedding space. After a given piece of text is tokenized, it will be embedded in a smaller
3214
       vector space that encodes semantic similarities between single tokens. For this introduction,
3215   we use the words ”token” and ”token ID” synonymously. Let
3216
3217                          E (L) := (E 1 , . . . , E L ) : R|V |×L →Embedding Rdm ×L                   (2)
3218   be the initial embedding, where E 1 = · · · = E L is the same linear transformation applied
3219   token-wise. The embedded vectors are then passed to the core transformer component
3220
                                      T : Rdm ×L →T ransf ormation Rdm ×L ,                               (3)
3221
3222   which combines normalization steps, positional embeddings such as RoPE (Su et al., 2024),
3223   the self-attention mechanism, feed-forward blocks and residual connections (Vaswani et al.,
3224   2017) into one map. Given a context t0:lc , consisting of lc + 1 tokens ti , i = 0, . . . , lc , we can
3225
       generate a discrete probability distribution at every position j = 1, . . . , lc for the token at
       position j + 1 by projecting the output of T onto the j-th component using
3226
3227                                      π j : Rdm ×L →P rojection Rdm .                                 (4)
3228   This hidden state or representation is then ”un-embedded” using a transformation
3229
3230                                     E ⊤ : Rdm →U n−embedding R|V |                                   (5)
3231   and turned into a discrete probability distribution over the vocabulary using the softmax
3232   operator
3233                                        σ : R|V | → (0, 1)|V | .                          (6)
3234                 ⊤
       Note that E does not need to be the transpose of E as suggested by the notation, but
3235   it often is in practice (due to regularisation properties). The entire LLM, generating the
3236   discrete probability distribution for the j-th token, can then be represented as a map
3237
                               σ ◦ E ⊤ ◦ π j ◦ T ◦ E (L) : R|V |×L →LLM (0, 1)|V | ,                      (7)
3238
3239   where the predicted next token is extracted using the arg max operation. When generating
       text, one sets j = lc to predict the next token at position lc + 1 following the context t0:lc .
