009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Non-stationarity poses significant challenges for multivariate time series forecast-
012            ing due to the inherent short-term fluctuations and long-term trends that can lead
013            to spurious regressions or obscure essential long-term relationships. Most ex-
014            isting methods either eliminate or retain non-stationarity without adequately ad-
015            dressing its distinct impacts on short-term and long-term modeling. Eliminating
016            non-stationarity is essential for avoiding spurious regressions and capturing local
017            dependencies in short-term modeling, while preserving it is crucial for revealing
018
               long-term cointegration across variates. In this paper, we propose TimeBridge, a
               novel framework designed to bridge the gap between non-stationarity and depen-
019
               dency modeling in long-term time series forecasting. By segmenting input series
020
               into smaller patches, TimeBridge applies Integrated Attention to mitigate short-
021            term non-stationarity and capture stable dependencies within each variate, while
022            Cointegrated Attention preserves non-stationarity to model long-term cointegra-
023            tion across variates. Extensive experiments show that TimeBridge consistently
024            achieves state-of-the-art performance in both short-term and long-term forecast-
025            ing. Additionally, TimeBridge demonstrates exceptional performance in financial
026            forecasting on the CSI 500 and S&P 500 indices, further validating its robustness
027            and effectiveness. The code is available in the supplementary material.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Multivariate time series forecasting aims to predict future changes based on historical observations
033
      of time series data, which holds significant applications in fields such as financial investment (Sezer
      et al., 2020), weather forecasting (Karevan & Suykens, 2020), and traffic flow prediction (Shu et al.,
034
      2021). However, the inherent non-stationarity of time series (Kim et al., 2022), characterized by
035
      short-term fluctuations and long-term trends, introduces challenges such as spurious regressions,
036   making time series forecasting a particularly complex task.
037
038   Recently, many methods have emerged to utilize a normalization-and-denormalization paradigm to
039
      address non-stationarity in time series (Kim et al., 2022; Fan et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; 2024b).
      For instance, RevIN (Kim et al., 2022) normalizes the input data and subsequently applies its dis-
040
      tributional characteristics to denormalize the output predictions. Building on this approach, other
041
      methods have designed distributional prediction networks (Fan et al., 2023) and more refined nor-
042   malization techniques (Liu et al., 2023) to further mitigate non-stationarity. On the other hand, some
043   studies (Liu et al., 2022b; Ma et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2024) argue that over-stabilizing time series
044   may actually reduce the richness of embedded features, leading to a decline in model performance.
045   Existing methods for addressing non-stationarity in time series face a dilemma: some prioritize elim-
046   inating non-stationary factors to reduce overfitting, while others attempt to incorporate these factors
047   but lack comprehensive theoretical frameworks. Furthermore, they do not adequately explain the
048   trade-offs between removing non-stationarity and leveraging it for modeling.
049   It is notable that non-stationary characteristics have distinct impacts on modeling short-term and
050   long-term dependencies. Non-stationarity can lead to spurious regressions in short-term modeling
051   due to the high randomness and unpredictability of short-term fluctuations (Noriega & Ventosa-
052   Santaulària, 2007) (see Fig. 1b). For example, a sudden drop in temperature could be caused by
053   a typhoon or cold front, both of which have no intrinsic connection. Retaining non-stationarity
      can result in false correlations between such unrelated events. However, non-stationarity is crucial

083
084   In this paper, to address the dual challenges posed by non-stationarity in short-term and long-term
085   modeling, we propose distinct strategies tailored to each scenario. For short-term modeling, we
086   eliminate non-stationarity to capture the strong temporal dependencies within each variate, as short-
087   term causal relationships exist mainly between consecutive time points within a single variate rather
088
      than across variates. This strategy reduces the risk of spurious regressions from non-stationary fluc-
      tuations, enabling the model to better capture the local causal dynamics. For long-term modeling,
089
      we utilize the preserved non-stationarity to uncover long-term cointegration relationships between
090
      different variates, thereby enabling more accurate and reliable long-term forecasting.
091
092   Technically, based on the above motivations, we propose TimeBridge as a novel framework to bridge
093   the gap between non-stationarity and dependency modeling in long-term time series forecasting.
094
      TimeBridge first captures short-term fluctuations by partitioning the input sequence into small-
      length patches, followed by utilizing Integrated Attention to model these stabilized sub-sequences
095
      within each variate. Here, “Integrated” reflects the non-stationary nature of the short-term series,
096
      also referred to as integrated series (Park & Phillips, 2001). Subsequently, we downsample the
097   patches to reduce their quantity, thereby enriching each patch with more long-term information.
098   Cointegrated Attention retains the non-stationary characteristics of the sequences to effectively cap-
099   ture the long-term cointegration relationships among variates. Experiments across multiple datasets
100   demonstrate that TimeBridge achieves consistent state-of-the-art performance in both long-term and
101   short-term forecasting. Furthermore, we validate the effectiveness of TimeBridge on two financial
102   datasets, the CSI 500 and S&P 500, which exhibit significant short-term volatility and strong long-
103   term cointegration relationships among sectors.
104   In a nutshell, our contributions are summarized in three folds:
105
106          1. Going beyond previous methods, we establish a novel connection between non-stationarity
107             and dependency modeling, highlighting the importance of eliminating non-stationarity in
                short-term variations while preserving it for long-term cointegration.

109            temporal dependencies by mitigating short-term non-stationarity, and Cointegrated Atten-

            3. Comprehensive experiments demonstrate that TimeBridge achieves state-of-the-art perfor-

113            further validate its robustness and effectiveness on the CSI 500 and S&P 500 indices, which

119
      As shown in Fig. 2, recent advancements in multivariate time series forecasting have predominantly
      focused on two core directions: Normalization and Dependecy Modeling.
120
121   Normalization can be divided into stationary and non-stationary methods. Stationary methods (Kim
122   et al., 2022; Fan et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b; 2023) aim to eliminate non-stationarity through
123   model-agnostic normalization techniques, thereby preventing spurious regressions and enhancing
124   model performance. For example, RevIN (Kim et al., 2022) applies Z-normalization to the input
125
      sequence and then reverses the normalization on the output using the distributional characteristics of
      the input, assuming that both share similar distributional properties. Dish-TS (Fan et al., 2023) takes
126
      this further by predicting the statistical characteristics of the output with a distribution prediction
127
      model. Additionally, SAN (Liu et al., 2023) offers a more granular patch-level prediction method.
128   Conversely, some approaches (Liu et al., 2022b; Ma et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2024) advocate preserv-
129   ing non-stationarity, as excessive normalization can eliminate diverse sequence characteristics and
130   limit predictive accuracy.
131
      Dependency Modeling focuses on designing methods to capture the relationships within multivari-
132
      ate time series, which can be classified into Channel Independent (CI) and Channel Dependent (CD)
133
      methods. CI methods (Zeng et al., 2023; Das et al., 2023; Nie et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2024; Lin et al.,
134   2024) rely exclusively on the historical values of each individual channel for prediction, deliberately
135   avoiding cross-channel interactions. This strategy not only stabilizes the training process but also
136   excels at capturing rapid temporal dynamics unique to each variate. In contrast, CD methods (Wu
137   et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023; Zhang & Yan, 2023; Liu et al., 2024a) leverage the
138   interrelationships between variates for modeling. While these methods utilize more information,
139   they struggle with spurious regressions when modeling short-term dependencies, failing to capture
140   rapid changes effectively.
141   The challenge with previous methods lies in their isolated treatment of non-stationarity and de-
142   pendency modeling, overlooking their intrinsic connection. Due to non-stationarity, time series
143   often exhibit significant short-term fluctuations, leading to severe spurious regressions when mod-
144   eling short-term dependencies. However, capturing long-term cointegration requires preserving this
145   underlying variability. Therefore, short-term random fluctuations need to be addressed by elimi-

      3.1         S TRUCTURE OVERVIEW
198
199   As illustrated in Fig. 3, our proposed TimeBridge consists of four key components: (a) Patch Em-
200   bedding segments the input sequence into non-overlapping patches and transforms each patch into a
201   patch token; (b) Integrated Attention models the dependencies among all patch tokens of the same
202   variates. By eliminating non-stationarity within each patch token, it mitigates the risk of spurious
203   regressions that could arise from abrupt short-term changes; (c) Patch Downsampling aggregates
204   global information and reduces the number of patches to encapsulate richer long-term features within
205
      each patch, while simultaneously lowering computational complexity; (d) Cointegrated Attention
      preserves the non-stationary characteristics of the sequence and models the long-term cointegration
206
      relationships across different variates within the same temporal window.
207
208
209   3.2         PATCH E MBEDDING
210
      In this stage, each variate of the input sequence X is first divided into non-overlapping patches,
211
      and each patch is then mapped to an embedded patch token. Since the process is identical for each
212   variate, we use X to represent a single variate and later restore the dimensionality of the variates.
213   Formally, this process can formulated as follows:
214
215
                                       {p1 , · · · , pN } = Patching(X),                          P = Embedding(p1 , · · · , pN )                                                   (1)

      Here, each patch pi has a length of S, and the number of patches N = SI . The Embedding(·)

217   operation transforms each patch from its original length S to a hidden dimension D through a
218   trainable linear layer. This results in embedded patch tokens P ∈ RC×N ×D , where each of the
219   C variates contains N patches, capturing local information that is typically subject to rapid short-
220   term fluctuations. For convenience, we denote Pc,: as the set of all patches within a single variate
221   and P:,n as the patches across all variates at the same time position in the following sections.
222
223   3.3   I NTEGRATED ATTENTION
224
225   The embedded patch tokens P represent short-term non-stationary sequences, also referred to as
226   integrated series of order k (k > 0) (Park & Phillips, 2001; Mushtaq, 2011). This non-stationarity
227   makes it challenging to model dependencies across different variates, as short-term fluctuations are
228
      highly susceptible to external shocks. Furthermore, modeling temporal dependencies within the
      same variate can lead to spurious regression due to the inherent non-stationarity of the patches. To
229
      address this, we first apply a patch-wise normalization to all patches within a variate:

235   ries length unchanged. We then employ the proposed Integrated Attention mechanism to capture
236   temporal dependencies within the same variate:

      original Pc,: serves as the Value. This design generates a stationary attention map, which is then

      directly multiplied by the Value, removing the need for subsequent denormalization. By leverag-
246
      ing Integrated Attention in this way, we effectively model the temporal dependencies without being
247   affected by the short-term non-stationary nature of the sequences.

      3.4   PATCH D OWNSAMPLING
250
251   Long-term equilibrium relationships between sequences, or cointegration among different variates,
252   often require sequences to contain sufficient long-term information to emerge. Therefore, before
253   modeling the cointegration between variates, it is crucial to increase the amount of global informa-
254   tion represented by each patch. This is achieved by reducing the number of patches and aggregating
255   global information through the attention mechanism:
256
257
                       P′c,: = Downsample(Pc,: ),     Pc,: = Attention(P′c,: , Pc,: , Pc,: ).              (5)
258
259   Here, Downsample(·) reduces the N patches in Pc,: to M patches (M < N ) using an MLP. By
260
      employing the downsampled P′c,: ∈ RM ×D as the Query and the original Pc,: ∈ RN ×D as the Key
261   and Value in the attention mechanism, we leverage the long-range modeling capability of attention
262   to dynamically aggregate global information. This allows each patch to encapsulate richer long-term
263   information, making it possible to capture the intricate cointegration relationships that emerge only
264   over sufficiently extended temporal horizons.
265
266   3.5   C OINTEGRATED ATTENTION
267
268   Although short-term relationships between integrated series are susceptible to spurious regressions,
269   accurately modeling long-term cointegration between sequences necessitates retaining their inher-
      ent non-stationary characteristics. Since each downsampled patch now encapsulates more extensive

285   4     E XPERIMENT
286
287   To validate the effectiveness of the proposed TimeBridge, we conduct extensive experiments on a
288   variety of time series forecasting tasks, including both long-term and short-term forecasting. Addi-
289   tionally, we evaluate TimeBridge on financial forecasting tasks characterized by significant short-
290   term volatility and strong long-term cointegration relationships among sectors.
291
      Baselines. For long-term forecasting, we select a diverse set of state-of-the-art baselines represen-
292   tative of recent advancements in time series forecasting, including the Transformer-based PDF (Dai
293   et al., 2024), the CNN-based ModernTCN (Donghao & Xue, 2024), the MLP-based TimeMixer
294   (Wang et al., 2024), as well as other competitive methods such as iTransformer (Liu et al., 2024a),
295   PatchTST (Nie et al., 2023), Crossformer (Zhang & Yan, 2023), FEDformer (Zhou et al., 2022),
296   MICN (Wang et al., 2022), TimesNet (Wu et al., 2023), and DLinear (Zeng et al., 2023). For
297   short-term forecasting, we add a well-performing baseline SCINet (Liu et al., 2022a). For financial
298   forecasting, we also incorporate the momentum strategy CSM (Jegadeesh & Titman, 1993) and the
299   reversal strategy BLSW (Poterba & Summers, 1988), along with two classic deep learning models,
300
      LSTM (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) and Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017a), to provide a
      comprehensive evaluation.
301
302   Implementation Details. All experiments are implemented in PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019) and
303   conducted on two NVIDIA RTX 3090 24GB GPUs. We use the Adam optimizer (Kingma, 2014)
304   with a learning rate selected from {1e-3, 1e-4, 5e-4}. The number of patches N is set to 30. For
305   additional details on hyperparameters and settings, please refer to the Appendix D.

473
474   Intra-variate Modeling. As shown in Fig. 4a, when non-stationarity is retained, the attention map
475   in the temporal dimension diverges, with the model focusing on multiple patches across a broader
476   time span. However, after removing non-stationarity, the attention map becomes more concentrated
477   on adjacent time steps, aligning with the causal nature of time series, where closer time steps are
478   usually more correlated. Non-stationarity may cause the model to mistake distant similarities for
479   causality. By eliminating non-stationarity, the model better captures short-term variations and local
480   dependencies, enhancing its robustness and interpretability in handling complex time series data.
481   Inter-variate Modeling. Fig. 4b shows that removing non-stationarity narrows the model’s atten-
482   tion to a few inter-variate dependencies, while retaining non-stationarity enables the capture of more
483   diverse and richer relationships. Non-stationary sequences help the model identify cointegration,
484   revealing hidden equilibrium mechanisms in multivariate time series. Preserving non-stationarity
485   enhances the model’s ability to express complex inter-variate dependencies. Additionally, Fig. 4c
      shows the impact of different patch downsampling rates on performance. For datasets with more

      7                     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we address the dual challenges of non-stationarity in multivariate time series forecast-
      ing, specifically focusing on its distinct impacts on short-term and long-term modeling. To this end,
532
      we propose TimeBridge, a novel framework that bridges the gap between non-stationarity and de-
533
      pendency modeling. By employing Integrated Attention to mitigate short-term non-stationarity and
534   Cointegrated Attention to preserve long-term dependencies, TimeBridge effectively captures both
535   local dynamics and long-term cointegration. Comprehensive experiments across diverse datasets
536   demonstrate that TimeBridge consistently achieves state-of-the-art performance in both short-term
537   and long-term forecasting tasks. Moreover, its exceptional performance on the CSI 500 and S&P
538   500 indices underscores its robustness and adaptability to complex real-world financial scenarios.
539   Our work paves the way for further exploration of models that balance the nuanced effects of non-
      stationarity, offering a promising direction for advancing multivariate time series forecasting.

      A.1    I NTEGRATION AND ADF T EST
705
706
      A time series is said to be integrated of order k, denoted as I(k), if it becomes stationary after differ-
707   encing k times. For instance, a series Xt is I(1) if its first difference ∆Xt = Xt −Xt−1 is stationary.
708   To test for non-stationarity, the Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test (Mushtaq, 2011) is commonly
709   used. It examines the null hypothesis that a unit root is present, indicating non-stationarity:
710
711                                                             p
712
                                                                X
                                  ∆Xt = α + βt + γXt−1 +              δi ∆Xt−i + ϵt
713

714
715   Here, ∆Xt is the differenced series, γ is the coefficient on the lagged series, and ϵt is the error
716   term. Rejecting the null hypothesis (γ = 0) indicates stationarity, while failing to reject it implies
717   non-stationarity. Non-stationary data can lead to spurious regressions, where unrelated temporal
718   intervals appear to be correlated due to common trends. We report the ADF test results in Tab. 8.

      A.2    C OINTEGRATION AND EG T EST
721
722   Cointegration occurs when two or more non-stationary series move together over time, maintaining
723   a stable, long-term relationship. For example, if Xt and Yt are both I(1), they are cointegrated if
724   there exists a stationary linear combination, Zt = Xt −βYt . This indicates a shared stochastic trend.
725   The Engle-Granger (EG) test (Bilgili, 1998) for cointegration involves two steps:
726
