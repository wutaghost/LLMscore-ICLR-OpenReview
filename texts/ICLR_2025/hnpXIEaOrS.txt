                                                   A BSTRACT
011
012
               Inverse Reinforcement Learning (IRL) aims to recover the latent reward func-
013            tion and corresponding optimal policy from observed demonstrations. Existing
014            IRL research predominantly focuses on a centralized learning approach, not suit-
015            able for real-world problems with distributed data and privacy restrictions. To
016            this end, this paper proposes a novel algorithm for federated maximum-likelihood
017            IRL (F-ML-IRL) and provides a rigorous analysis of its convergence and time-
018
               complexity. The proposed F-ML-IRL leverages a dual-aggregation to update the
               shared global model and performs bi-level local updates – an upper-level learning
019            task to optimize the parameterized reward function by maximizing the discounted
020            likelihood of observing expert trajectories under the current policy and a low-
021            level learning task to find the optimal policy concerning the entropy-regularized
022            discounted cumulative reward under the current reward function. We analyze the
023            convergence and time-complexity of the proposed F-ML-IRL algorithm and show
024
               that the global model in F-ML-IRL converges to a stationary point for both the
               reward and policy parameters within finite time, i.e., the log-distance between
025
               the recovered policy and the optimal policy, as well as the gradient of the likeli-
026            hood objective, converge to zero. Finally, evaluating our F-ML-IRL algorithm on
027            high-dimensional robotic control tasks in MuJoCo, we show that it ensures con-
028            vergences of the recovered reward in decentralized learning and even outperforms
029            centralized baselines due to its ability to utilize distributed data.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Inverse learning is the problem of modeling the preferences and goals of an agent using its observed
034   behavior Arora & Doshi (2020). When the behavior of a human expert is observed through demon-
035   stration trajectories containing state and action data, Inverse Reinforcement Learning (IRL) models
036   the policy through a Markov Decision Process (MDP) to recover the latent reward function and po-
037   tentially replicate the human expert’s optimal policy Russell (1998). The learned reward function
038   can support various downstream tasks such as agent modeling and transfer learning Sutton & Barto
039
      (2018); Arora & Doshi (2020). Recent work has developed provably-efficient IRL algorithms, such
      as Generative Adversarial Inverse Learning (GAIL) Ho & Ermon (2016) and Maximum-likelihood
040   IRL (ML-IRL)Ratia et al. (2012); Zeng et al. (2022), all using a centralized learning approach. How-
041   ever, demonstration data in practice are often distributed across decentralized clients, e.g., devices,
042   cars, and households. It is not realistic to assume that such sensitive data can always be shared or
043   collected for centralized inverse learning, due to privacy restrictions.
044   To enable collaborative training of machine learning models among decentralized clients under the
045   privacy restrictions, FL provides a promising solution by maintaining training data on local devices
046   and aggregating local updates to build a global model. However, most existing work on FL consider
047   only the forward learning problem, e.g., loss minimization Li et al. (2019), policy improvement Jin
048   et al. (2022), learning with heterogeneous models Zhou et al. (2024), and efficient optimization
049
      methods Li et al. (2020; 2021b;a); Wang et al. (2020), and not the IRL problem. We note that IRL
      using decentralized clients and distributed data is an open problem. It often has a bi-level structure of
050   maximizing the probability of observing expert trajectories under the current policy and optimizing
051   discounted cumulative reward for the target reward function, which must be solved jointly during
052   IRL. A naive integration of FL and IRL may not achieve convergence. A decentralized learning
053   framework for IRL with theoretical analysis of the convergence and the time-complexity remains a
      significant challenge.

      The goal of this paper is to develop a novel framework for federated maximum-likelihood IRL (F-
055   ML-IRL) and provide a rigorous convergence/time-complexity analysis of the proposed algorithms.
056   We adopt the Maximum Likelihood IRL (ML-IRL) approach in Zeng et al. (2022) and consider the
057   problem of decentralized IRL of a shared latent reward function, from distributed data and using
058   decentralized client devices. Our solution attains the privacy-preserving benefits of FL in IRL. To
059   address the bi-level nature of IRL, our proposed algorithm’s local training round McMahan et al.
      (2017) encompasses an upper-level learning task (on each client with local dataset) to optimize the
060
      parameterized reward function to maximize the discounted likelihood of observing expert trajecto-
061   ries under the current policy, as well as a low-level learning task to find the optimal policy concerning
062   the entropy-regularized discounted cumulative reward for the current reward function. Then, we de-
063   sign a dual-aggregation method for aggregating both the action-value networks and reward function
064   models every T local rounds, rather than just one set of model parameters in standard FL. Further,
065   we leverage Soft Q-learning Haarnoja et al. (2017) as the base RL algorithm. Instead of fully solv-
066
      ing the forward RL problem before updating the reward parameter, we perform one-step updates for
      both the recovered policy and reward parameter alternately to improve the efficiency. To the best of
067   our knowledge, this is the first proposal to formulate and solve this F-ML-IRL problem.
068
069   We conduct a rigorous convergence/time-complexity analysis of the proposed F-ML-IRL algorithm.
      Due to the tight coupling between the reward parameters and the recovered policy in IRL’s bi-level
070
      optimization, the dual-aggregation method in our F-ML-IRL must be analyzed to understand its im-
071   pact on convergence. By bounding the logarithmic distance between the estimated policy and the
072   optimal policy by the distance between their corresponding Q-values, we control the variance intro-
073   duced by local training by considering the time immediately after each global aggregation. Utilizing
074   the ω-contraction property of soft Q-values, we establish the contraction property of the targeted
075   distance, which allows us to provide a convergence proof for the policy estimate. Moreover, we
      leverage the Lipschitz continuity of the reward parameter and the convergence of the policy estimate
076
      to show that the gradient of the global reward parameter converges to zero as the number of com-
077   munications increases. These techniques enable us to show that F-ML-IRL’s policy estimation and
078   reward optimization both converge in finite time. The change in convergence speed due to the use
079   of only decentralized clients and distributed data (rather than centralized learning) is characterized.
080
      Our F-ML-IRL is implemented and evaluated on high-dimensional robotic control tasks in MuJoCo
081   Todorov et al. (2012). We compared its performance with several centralized learning baseline
082   including Behavior Cloning (BC) Pomerleau (1988), Generative Adversarial Imitation Learning
083   (GAIL) Ho & Ermon (2016), and IRL methods like f-IRL Ni et al. (2021) and ML-IRL Zeng et al.
084   (2022). We consider non-iid data distribution, where clients have different local demonstration
085   data with varying performance levels. The baselines are evaluated using centralized data with two
      setups (i) a single client with medium-level demonstrations and (ii) a single client with a mixture of
086
      demonstrations of different levels. The results show that our F-ML-IRL could effectively leverage
087   distributed data and client devices in learning, to achieve similar or better recovered reward than
088   the baselines, while meeting decentralization and data privacy restrictions. Our evaluation code is
089   available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/F-ML-IRL/. The key contributions
090   of this paper are summarized as follows:
091          • We propose a novel framework for federated maximum-likelihood IRL (F-ML-IRL). It
092            enables decentralized IRL of a shared latent reward function, from distributed data and
093            using decentralized client devices, under data privacy restrictions.
094          • To support the bi-level optimization structure in IRL – for jointly updating the optimal
095            policy and the reward function estimate, the proposed F-ML-IRL algorithm leverages a
096            dual-aggregation of the model parameters, which ensures convergence to optimal results.
097          • The convergence and time-complexity of the proposed F-ML-IRL algorithm is quantified,
098            with respect to local rounds T and aggregation steps M . We show that F-ML-IRL achieves
099            convergence in finite time and will have faster convergence with a smaller local rounds T .
100          • Our solution is evaluated on high-dimensional robotic control tasks in MuJoCo and is
101            shown to achieve similar or higher recovered reward than a number of Imitation Learn-
102            ing and IRL baselines that employ centralized learning.

106   IRL aims to learn the reward function using expert demonstration data, which frees the forward
107   RL problem from the requirement of specifying the reward function beforehand Ng et al. (2000) and
      also facilitates imitation learning by using the recovered reward function to derive an effective policy

      Abbeel & Ng (2004). Various formulations and solutions for the IRL problem have been explored.
109   The Maximum Margin Planning algorithm frames the problem within a quadratic programming
110   context Ratliff et al. (2006). Bayesian IRL models infer the posterior distribution of the reward
111   function given a prior Ramachandran & Amir (2007). Probabilistic maximum entropy IRL methods
112   favor stochastic policies using entropy regularization. In recent years, Generative Adversarial Imita-
113   tion Learning (GAIL) Ho & Ermon (2016) has adopted a Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)
      Goodfellow et al. (2020) framework to recover the expert’s policy. In this framework, a generator
114
      proposes new policies to confuse the discriminator, while the discriminator determines whether the
115   state-action pair from the generator’s policy originates from the expert. However, existing work has
116   not considered the IRL problem with distributed data and decentralized clients, under data privacy.
117

      (FedAvg), most works in FL based on Stochastic Gradient Descent (FedSGD) Shokri & Shmatikov

160   decentralized clients, since ML-IRL requires a bi-level optimization involving both policy improve-
161   ment and reward estimate using maximum likelihood. New algorithms needs to be developed for
      decentralized ML-IRL with rigorous convergence/time-complexity analysis.

532   6   C ONCLUSIONS
533
534   This paper proposes F-ML-IRL for federated maximum-likelihood inverse reinforcement learning.
535
      It enables decentralized learning of a shared latent reward function from distributed datasets and
      using decentralized clients. F-ML-IRL algorithm leverages a dual-aggregation to update the shared
536   global model and performs bi-level local updates for inverse learning. We analyze the convergence
537   and time-complexity of F-ML-IRL. Evaluation results on MuJoCo tasks how that F-ML-IRL ensures
538   convergences of the recovered reward and achieves similar or higher recovered reward, compared to
539   state-of-the-art baselines using centralized inverse learning. For further work, we plan to investigate
      further communication reduction and the use of heterogeneous local models in F-ML-IRL.
