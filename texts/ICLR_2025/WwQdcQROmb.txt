009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Controlling complex physical systems is a crucial task in science and engineering,
012            often requiring the balance of control objectives and safety constraints. Recently,
013            diffusion models have demonstrated a strong ability to model high-dimensional
014            state spaces, giving them an advantage over recent deep learning and reinforce-
015            ment learning-based methods in complex control tasks. However, they do not
016            inherently address safety concerns. In contrast, while safe reinforcement learn-
017            ing methods consider safety, they typically fail to provide guarantees for satisfy-
018
               ing safety constraints. To address these limitations, we propose Safe Conformal
               Physical system control (SafeConPhy), which optimizes the diffusion model with
019
               a provable safety bound iteratively to satisfy the safety constraint. We pre-train a
020
               diffusion model on the training set. Given the calibration set and the specific con-
021            trol targets, we derive a provable safety bound using conformal prediction. After
022            iteratively enhancing the safety of the diffusion model with the progressively up-
023            dated bound, the model’s output can be certified as safe with a user-defined prob-
024            ability. We evaluate our algorithm on two control tasks: 1D Burgers’ equation
025            and 2D incompressible fluid. Our results show that our algorithm satisfies safety
026            constraints, and outperforms prior control methods and safe offline RL algorithms.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The control of complex physical systems is critical and essential in many scientific and engineering
032   fields, including fluid dynamics (Hinze & Kunisch, 2001), nuclear fusion (Edwards et al., 1992),
033
      and mathematical finance (Soner, 2004). In real-world scenarios, controlling such systems often
      requires addressing safety concerns (Barros & des Santos, 1998; Argomedo et al., 2013). For ex-
034
      ample, in fluid dynamics, small errors in control can lead to turbulence or structural damage, while
035
      in controlled nuclear fusion, failure to maintain safety constraints could result in catastrophic con-
036   sequences. Safety, in this context, involves ensuring that the control sequences guide the system to
037   satisfy pre-defined constraints, thereby mitigating risks and preventing hazardous situations (Daw-
038   son et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023a). Notably, safety remains a bottleneck for applying machine learn-
039   ing to specific scientific and engineering problems, as many machine learning algorithms lack the
040   mechanisms to guarantee safety constraints in their control outputs. This gap between performance
041   and safety has become a critical obstacle in deploying machine learning for high-stake applications.
042   Despite of its importance, the safe control of complex physical systems is challenging. Firstly, to
043   avoid unacceptable risks, one should prevent algorithms without safety guarantees from interacting
044   with the environment, restricting us to an offline setting with pre-collected data. However, the
045   data is often non-optimal and may contain unsafe samples, resulting in a significant gap between
046   the observed data distribution and the near-optimal, safe distribution (Xu et al., 2022; Liu et al.,
047   2023a). Secondly, the algorithm must balance the need for high performance with adherence to
048   safety constraints (Liu et al., 2023a; Zheng et al., 2024).
049   After many years of research on traditional control algorithms (Li et al., 2006; Protas, 2008), the ad-
050   vancement of neural networks leads to the emergence of numerous deep learning-based algorithms
051   (Farahmand et al., 2017; Holl et al., 2020; Hwang et al., 2022). For complex physical systems,
052   which are highly nonlinear and high-dimensional, the deep learning-based methods achieve out-
053   standing results (Hwang et al., 2022; Holl et al., 2020; Wei et al., 2024). However, the above deep
      learning-based methods generally do not account for safety considerations. Regarding safe offline

071   To address these problems, we propose Safe Conformal Physical system control (SafeConPhy), an
072   iterative safety improvement method with a certifiable safety bound. Firstly, in offline settings, the
073
      training data are often sub-optimal and unsafe, exhibiting a significant deviation from the desired
      distribution, which is optimal and safe. Inspired by concepts from conformal prediction (Vovk et al.,
074
      2005b; Tibshirani et al., 2019), we estimate the model prediction error under distribution shift based
075
      on a portion of split-out training data (called calibration set) and the specific control targets. With the
076   estimated prediction error, we compute a probabilistic upper bound of the safety score, and the safety
077   score for the model’s interaction with the environment will be within the upper bound with a user-
078   defined probability. Thus, the model’s safety can be certified by verifying whether the upper bound
079   satisfies the safety constraint. Secondly, we implement a process to improve the model safety by
080   leveraging the upper bound through guidance and fine-tuning iteratively. The guidance step directs
081   the model to stochastically generate multiple samples that potentially satisfy the safety constraint,
082   while the fine-tuning step updates the model by incorporating these samples and the safety bound.
083   This safety improvement process is iterative and continues until the safety upper bound meets the
084
      safety constraints.
085   In summary, the advantages of SafeConPhy are highlighted in Table 1. Our main contributions are
086   as follows: (1) We introduce safety constraints into the deep learning-based control of complex
087   physical systems, develop two datasets for safe physical system control tasks to evaluate different
088   methods, and propose the offline algorithm SafeConPhy. (2) Considering the model’s prediction
089   error, we provide a certifiable upper bound of the safety score and design an iterative safety im-
090
      provement process that uses the upper bound to promote the output distribution becoming more
      optimal and safer. (3) We conduct experiments on 1D Burgers’ Equation and 2D incompressible
091
      fluid, whose results demonstrate that SafeConPhy can meet the safety constraints and reach better
092
      control objectives at the same time.

097   2.1    C ONTROL OF P HYSICAL S YSTEMS
098
099
      The development of control methods in physical systems is critical across various engineering ar-
      eas, including PID (Li et al., 2006), supervised learning (SL) (Holl et al., 2020; Hwang et al., 2022),
100
      reinforcement learning (RL) (Farahmand et al., 2017; Pan et al., 2018; Rabault et al., 2019), and
101
      physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) (Mowlavi & Nabi, 2023). Among these, PID is one of
102   the earliest and most widely used method (Johnson & Moradi, 2005), known for its simplicity and
103   effectiveness in regulating physical systems; however, it faces challenges in parameter tuning and
104   struggles with highly nonlinear or time-varying systems. With the advancement of deep learning, SL
105   (Holl et al., 2020) has been applied to optimize control sequences through backpropagation over en-
106   tire trajectories, but it lacks the adaptability to dynamic environments since it is typically trained on
107   fixed datasets. To overcome the above issue, RL enhances adaptability by leveraging diverse datasets
      or interactions with the environment, achieving notable success in controlling physical systems, in-

109   2022; Feng et al., 2024), and nuclear fusion (Degrave et al., 2022). Furthermore, the adjoint method

116   2.2   S AFE O FFLINE R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
117
118   Recently, the offline setting has attracted attention in the field of safe reinforcement learning (RL), as
119   it avoids generating dangerous behaviors through direct interaction with the environment (Achiam
120   et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2020; Stooke et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2022). CPQ (Xu et al., 2022) is the
121   first practical safe offline RL method that assigns high costs to OOD and unsafe actions and updates
122
      the value function as well as the policy only with safe actions. COptiDICE (Lee et al., 2022) is
      a DICE-based method and corrects the stationary distribution. CDT (Liu et al., 2023b) takes the
123
      decision transformer to solve safe offline RL problems as multi-objective optimization. However,
124
      these methods lack the ability to model high-dimensional state space. More recent methods like
125   TREBI (Lin et al., 2023) and FISOR (Zheng et al., 2024) solve it through diffusion model planning.
126   But they do not consider the upper bound of the safety score in a probabilistic sense and differ
127   significantly from SafeConPhy in terms of the algorithm.
128
129
      2.3   C ONFORMAL P REDICTION
130
131   Conformal prediction (Vovk et al., 2005a) is a statistical framework that constructs prediction in-
132   tervals guaranteed to contain the true label with a specified probability. Its validity could be com-
133   promised, however, by the violation of the core assumption of exchangeability due to distribution
134   shifts in real-world scenarios (Chernozhukov et al., 2018; Hendrycks et al., 2018). Recent studies
135   (Maxime Cauchois & Duchi, 2024) have extended conformal prediction to accommodate various
136   distribution shifts. For example, Tibshirani et al. (2019) proposed weighted conformal prediction
137   to handle covariate shift, where training and test data distributions differ. Podkopaev & Ramdas
138
      (2021) introduced reweighted conformal prediction and calibration techniques to address label shift
      using unlabeled target data. Adaptive conformal inference (Gibbs & Candes, 2021) provides valid
139
      prediction sets in online settings with unknown, time-varying distribution shifts without relying on
140
      exchangeability. Inspired by previous approaches, SafeConPhy establishes an upper bound on the
141   confidence level by maintaining a weighted score set. Our method ensures the true safety value for
142   a given control sequence lies within the bound without requiring additional assumptions about the
143   model or data distribution, effectively addressing distribution shifts between pre-collected data and
144   the target distribution.

      In this section, we present the workflow of our algorithm as in Figure 1. We set aside a portion of
      the original training data as the calibration set Dcal , which will be used later to estimate the model’s
237
      prediction errors. The remaining data, which will be used for actual training, is referred to as the
238
      training data Dtrain . After pre-training with Dtrain as described in Eq. 3, we get the diffusion model
239   pθ which models the joint distribution of [u, w].
240
241   Next, we conduct Iterative Safety Improvement, where we apply the provable safety bound s+
242
      under distribution shift to enhance model safety iteratively. First, the calculation of the safety bound
      s+ runs throughout the entire loop. The safety bound basically takes the calibration set to obtain
243
      a corresponding set of model prediction errors (called the score set). Furthermore, we take into
244
      account the distribution shift between the calibration set and data generated based on control targets,
245   so we apply weighting to the set of model prediction errors to get the weighted score set as Eq. 11.
246   Then, we use the quantile of this set to represent the error in the model’s predicted safety score.
247
248
      Second, ‘iterative’ refers to the process where we cyclically use the guidance to generate samples,
      and then combine these samples with model prediction errors to fine-tune the model parameters,
249
      thereby improving the model’s safety. Specifically, in each iteration, we sample the control sequence
250
      w under the guidance as described in Eq. 14 containing s+ . After getting w, we conditionally
251   sample ũθ (w) to get the provable safety bound s+ (ũθ (w)). And we can now take the fine-tuning
252   loss Lfine-tune involving the training data Dtrain and also the progressively updated s+ (ũθ (w)) as in
253   Eq. 15 to fine-tune the model parameters θ.
254
      Finally, after several iterations, we use the fine-tuned diffusion model, once again under the influence
255
      of guidance, to generate the control sequences, which serve as the final output of the algorithm. The
256
      complete inference process can be seen in Algorithm 1.

      7     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we have introduced Safe Conformal Physical system control (SafeConPhy), a prob-
533
      abilistic generative method for safe control problems of complex physical systems. Targeting the
534   meaningful and important offline setting, we provide a provable probabilistic estimate of the safety
535   score’s upper bound. We then perform guidance and finetuning with this provable safety bound iter-
536   atively, improving the safety and certifying it with a user-defined probability. Experiment results on
537   1D Burgers’ equation and 2D incompressible fluid demonstrate that on the basis of satisfying safety
538   constraints, SafeConPhy is able to achieve a lower control objective. We believe that our method is
539   beneficial for making machine learning-based physical control safer, improving the trustworthiness
      for deploying to the real world.

803   Conformal prediction is a flexible framework that provides prediction intervals with guaranteed cov-
804   erage probabilities for new, unseen data points, under the assumption that the data are exchangeable.
805   Theoretical Foundations The exchangeability assumption is a cornerstone of conformal prediction.
806   It requires that the order of the data points does not affect their joint distribution, meaning that any
807   permutation of the indices yields an identical distribution. In particular, exchangeability holds for
808   independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) samples, a common assumption in machine learning
809   tasks. Ensuring exchangeability guarantees the validity of the prediction intervals constructed using
      conformal prediction.


                                                        15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      Implementation Details To implement conformal prediction, the dataset is first split into two sub-
811   sets: a proper training set (T r) and a calibration set (Cal). A predictive model µθ is trained on the
812   training set using a specified learning algorithm A. Once trained, the model generates predictions
813   for the calibration set. These predictions are used to compute ‘conformity scores’, which measure
814   the model’s accuracy for each calibration point. Specifically, for each instance i in the calibration
815   set, the conformity score Si is defined as:
816
817                                     Si = |µθ (Xi ) − Yi |,    i ∈ Cal.
818
      Additionally, a worst-case score of ∞ is included to account for extreme scenarios.
819
820   Then 1 − α quantile q1−α (S) of the set of conformity scores is calculated, where α represents the
821   desired significance level (e.g., α = 0.05 for 95% confidence interval).
822   Given a new data point Xn+1 , the prediction interval for its corresponding output is calculated as:
823
824                      Ĉα (Xn+1 ) = [µθ (Xn+1 ) − q1−α (S), µθ (Xn+1 ) + q1−α (S)] .
825
826   This interval provides an estimate for the range within which the true value Yn+1 is expected to
827   lie, with a coverage probability of at least 1 − α. Thus, conformal prediction offers a flexible and
828
      robust method for constructing prediction intervals that account for both the model’s accuracy and
      the variability in the data.
829
830   Theoretical Guarantees Conformal prediction provides theoretical guarantees for finite samples
831   (Vovk et al., 2005b; Lei et al., 2016). Specifically, for any new data point, the prediction interval
832   satisfies the following probabilistic bound:
833
834
                                        P (Yn+1 ∈ Ĉα (Xn+1 )) ≥ 1 − α.
835
      This ensures that the true label Yn+1 will fall within the predicted interval at least 1 − α percent of
836   the time. This framework, based on the assumption of exchangeability, provides a robust method for
837   generating reliable prediction intervals, even in settings with limited sample sizes.

      Constraints Decision Transformer (CDT) (Liu et al., 2023b) models control as a multi-task regres-
908
      sion problem, extending the Decision Transformer (DT) (Chen et al., 2021). It sequentially predicts
909

910   and costs. The authors propose two techniques to adapt the model for safety-constrained scenarios:
911
912         1. Stochastic Policy with Entropy Regularization: This technique aims to reduce the risk of
913            constraint violations due to out-of-distribution actions. In a deterministic policy, the model
914            selects a single action based on its learned policy, which may result in unsafe actions when
915            faced with states not well represented in the training data. By using a stochastic policy, the
916            model samples actions from a distribution, encouraging the exploration of a wider action
917            space. Entropy regularization further enforces diversity in the sampled actions, making the
               model more robust in uncertain or underrepresented situations. This approach reduces the

1028   Hwang et al. (2022) introduces a supervised learning (SL) based method to control PDE systems. It
1029   first trains a neural surrogate model to capture the PDE dynamics, which includes a VAE to compress
1030   PDE states and controls into the latent space and another model to learn the PDE’s time evolution in
1031   the latent space. To obtain the optimal control sequence, SL can compute the gradient ∇w J , where

1033   can be executed to improve the control sequence.
