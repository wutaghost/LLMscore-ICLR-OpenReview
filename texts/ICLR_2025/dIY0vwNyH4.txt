009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The recent large multimodal models (LMMs) have demonstrated their impressive
012            capability of image understanding. However, they still struggle to make compli-
013            cated reasoning for solving a challenging multimodal problem. In this paper, we
014            present UnAC (Understanding, Abstracting, and Checking), a novel multimodal
015            prompting method, to synergize reasoning for complicated problems in the multi-
016            modal context of LMMs, such as GPT-4o, Gemini-1.5 and GPT-4V. To improve
017            the understanding of the image and capture more details, we propose an adaptive
018
               visual prompting method to make LMMs able to focus on certain regions. An
               image abstracting prompting is designed to effectively extract information from
019
               images. Further, we propose a gradual self-checking scheme for leading to better
020
               reasoning by checking each decomposed sub-question and its answer. Extensive
021            experiments on three public benchmarks – MathVista, MM-Vet, and MMMU –
022            demonstrate the effectiveness of our method.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   In recent years, large language models (LLMs) have advanced significantly Brown et al. (2020);
028   Achiam et al. (2023); Touvron et al. (2023); Bubeck et al. (2023); Chowdhery et al. (2023); Zhang
029   et al. (2022). From GPT-3 Brown et al. (2020), PaLM Chowdhery et al. (2023) and Llama Touvron
030   et al. (2023) to GPT-4 Achiam et al. (2023) and PaLM-2 Anil et al. (2023). Notably, Generative
031   Pre-trained Transformers (GPTs) Brown et al. (2020); Achiam et al. (2023) have driven numerous
032   breakthroughs in both industry and academia. Since the release of GPT-4, there has been increasing
033
      interest in large multimodal models (LMMs) within the research community. Many approaches are
      focused on developing powerful multimodal models based on open-source frameworks Liu et al.
034
      (2024); Wu et al. (2023); Dai et al. (2024); Zhu et al. (2023). Recently, the release of GPT-4V(ision)
035
      and Gemini-1.5-flash Team et al. (2023) has garnered immediate attention for its impressive capability
036   of understanding images. However, they still struggle to do some complicated multimodal reasoning
037   tasks Lu et al. (2023); Yue et al. (2023).
038
039
      Since approaches Yao et al. (2024); Wei et al. (2022); Yao et al. (2022); Miao et al. (2023); Zheng
      et al. (2023) of prompting to improve the reasoning ability with LLMs in only language-context
040
      make significant progress. However, since LMMs can not able to decompose an image easily like
041
      decomposing a sentence, it is ineffective to apply the language prompts to improve reasoning in the
042   visual context. For answering a question in the visual context, the major failure cases are due to the
043   misunderstanding of the image or imprecisely summarizing the information. The reason for missing
044   or misunderstanding some details is related to the weak capability of getting fine-grained information
045   Yang et al. (2023a). Visual prompts have also been explored for various multi-modal tasks, especially
046   for enhancing the performance of fine-grained visual tasks. Those methods focus on encoding some
047   masks like points, boxes, and lines combined with the input features or directly applying overlays on
048   the original image. Most recently, Yang et al. proposed to build the visual prompting mechanism by
049   partitioning the image into a set of semantically meaningful regions and overlying them to enhance
050
      the grounding ability of GPT-4V. However, for the complicated questions that usually need multi-step
      information extracting and reasoning, only partitioning the whole image is not promising to improve
051
      the reasoning.
052
053   In this paper, we propose a simple but powerful multimodal prompting method called UnAC
      (Understanding, Abstracting and Checking) to improve the abilities of complicated multi-modal

054
      reasoning for LMMs. UnAC consists of a three-step prompting mechanism. In the first step, we
055   present a novel adaptive visual prompting scheme, the second step is abstracting the image into
056   sentences and the final step includes a gradual self-checking prompting. Firstly, to reduce misun-
057   derstanding or missing details, the visual prompts are designed as adaptive markers on the image
058   to make LMMs able to focus on specific regions. By looking at the image part by part, LMMs can
059   find more details and have a better understanding of the image overall. Secondly, to solve a problem
060   that needs complex reasoning, we need to correctly abstract the information from the image based
061   on the question. Inspired by the fact that based on the relationship between image and question,
062   humans often extract important information form the image locally and globally. We propose to find
063
      the most related parts of the question and abstract the image into language based on the built visual
      prompts. Then, for a complicated question, the LMMs are easily to make mistakes in some steps, and
064
      asking LMMs to check the overall reasoning process is ineffective. However, with the visual context
065
      introducing, the checking of a single step is possible. We introduce a gradual self-checking scheme
066   to check each decompsed question individually to improve the accuracy of the answer.
067
068   We evaluate UnAC on three datasets of evaluating the ability of complicated problem-solving in the
069
      visual context, namely MathVista Lu et al. (2023), MM-Vet Yu et al. (2023) and MMMU Yue et al.
      (2023). To show the generalization of our method, we conduct experiments on two kinds of LMMs:
070
      (a) the powerful and large-scale multimodal models including GPT-4V and Gemini-1.5-flash; (b)
071
      relatively light-weighted models including LLaVA-v1.6-7B/13B. We achieve improvements on all
072   models and all datasets which indicates our method is model-agnostic. Notably, our method improves
073   6.4% on MathVista with Gemini-1.5-flash. To summarize, our main contributions are:
074
075          • We propose a simple but powerful multimodal prompting scheme called UnAC
076            (Understanding, Abstracting and Checking) to improve the abilities of complicated multi-
077            modal reasoning for LMMs.
078          • We introduce an adaptive visual prompt to improve the image understanding and reduce the
079            missing details. Combined with the language prompting of the image abstraction and the
080            gradual checking scheme, all the modules lead LMMs to better reasoning.
081          • Extensive experiments on three datasets which are MathVista, MM-Vet, and MMMU show
082            the effectiveness of UnAC in evoking complicated reasoning in the visual context of LMMs.

      Prompting in LLMs. We have observed significant advancements in large language models (LLMs)
087
      Zhang et al. (2022; 2023); Touvron et al. (2023); Team et al. (2023); Brown et al. (2020). Although the
088
      size of LLMs has increased substantially, evoking their reasoning capabilities is still necessary with
089   the use of more complicated designed queries, or prompting. Recently, various works have explored
090   prompt engineering to enhance LLM capabilities. In-context learning has become a mainstream
091   approach to instruct LLMs by providing specific examples Brown et al. (2020); Dong et al. (2022).
092   Building on this, techniques such as chain-of-thought and tree-of-thought Wei et al. (2022); Yao et al.
093   (2024) have been introduced to improve performance in arithmetic, commonsense, and symbolic
094   reasoning tasks. Most recently, Zheng et al. Zheng et al. (2023) proposed the Step-Back Prompting
095   method which enhances the ability to retrieve information via abstracting the question. Miao et al.
096   Miao et al. (2023) introduced a general-purpose zero-shot verification schema for recognizing errors
097
      made in the reasoning process of math problems. However, their methods highly rely on that the
      language is easy to be decomposed. It is hard to be generalized to the question in the visual context
098
      where images are hard to decompose.

      3     U NAC: U NDERSTANDING , A BSTRACTING , AND C HECKING
147
148
      Consider a general fact when humans face a challenging problem in the visual context. To solve the
      problem, we need first to understand the image and the question correctly overall. Then based on
149
      the question, we will look at the image more carefully, find and abstract the useful information that
150
      can be used to solve the problem. Finally, based on the understanding and the abstraction, we infer
151   the final answer to this challenging problem. Moreover, for a complicated question, we usually need
152   a second look at the reasoning process and check it with the image to avoid some simple mistakes.
153   Inspired by this common sense, we propose UnAC which means understanding, abstracting, and
154   checking for synergizing the complicated reasoning in the visual context of large multimodal models.
155
156   3.1   A DAPTIVE V ISUAL P ROMPTS .
157
158   Precisely capturing the details in the image is not straightforward for LMMs. It is hard to correct the
159   misunderstanding of the image by itself because decomposing the image is not easy. Since LMMs
160   are developed based on the LLMs, their abilities of language reasoning are much better than visual
161   reasoning. It means that LMMs can perform better on analyzing the problem than analyzing the
      image. Therefore, we propose to build effective and adaptive multimodal prompts based on the


                                                        3

162
      analysis of the question. Asking the model to analyze the question and find what information we need
163   to get from the image. We conclude the response into two kinds: Objects with semantic meaning and
164   symbols with literal meaning. For objects with semantic meaning, we employ segmentation models
165   to automatically segment the image. For symbols with literal meaning, we use optical character
166   recognition (OCR) methods to detect the texts. Based on the metadata, we first denoising regions
167   based on the stability score output by the segmentation/OCR methods.
168
      In the Figure. 1, we show a successful case. For this question of subtracting the items, it requires
169   LMMs to correctly recognizing each item in the picture which is related to objects with semantic
170   meanings. Therefore, the visual prompts are designed as the segmentation of the image to help the
171   LMM to better understand the image.
172
173
174   3.2    I MAGE A BSTRACTION
175
176   The visual prompts can make a better understanding of the image since the markers can catch
177   more attentions on some local information. Partitioning the image makes it decomposable when
178
      LMMs understand the image. However, only visual prompts have limited improvements for solving
      complicated problems. Except for understanding the image, LMMs need to correctly abstract the
179
      image to filter the useless information to solve the problem. Without prompts of abstraction, the
180
      reasoning might be misdirected due to the markers in the image. Therefore, to fully utilize the visual
181   prompts and get better reasoning, we need to abstract the information which is the most related to the
182   question. Firstly, we ask LMMs to describe the picture to abstract the global information. Then based
183   on the analysis of the question and the prompts, we ask LMMs to find the most related regions to get
184   more details based on the markers in the image.
185
186
187   3.3    G RADUAL CHECKING
188
189   Moreover, for some complicated questions, we usually need a
190   second look at the image with the reasoning progressing. As
191
      discussed in Ling et al. (2024), checking the whole reasoning
      process is usually ineffective for LLMs and our experiments
192
      show similar results in LMMs. However, to correct the mistake
193
      made in one step is more effective. To check individual steps
194   of the reasoning process, the first thing we should note is that
195   the correctness of each step is highly dependent on its context.
196   For a question in words, the context includes the question and
197   previous steps only. So the checking is largely dependent on
198   the accuracy of the previous steps which is highly unstable. In
199   the visual question answering, the information from the image
200   becomes extra contexts which are important references for self-
201   checking. It can be more reliable when LMMs have a good
202
      understanding of the image.

203   Then, we design a gradual checking prompting for better reason- ually checking.
204   ing. As illustrated in the Figure. 2, firstly, we let LMMs decom-
205   pose the question into multi sub-questions [Q0 , Q1 , . . . , Qn ]
206   and give the answer of each sub-questions. The answers are denoted as [A0 , A1 , . . . , An ]. In the
207   checking stage, we check gradually. When checking Qi and Ai , we refer the context of the previous

476   5   D ISCUSSION
477
478   Compared to the prompting in the LLMs, building an effective prompting method for LMMs can be
479   much harder. The language is easy to decompose since the input and output of texts can be tokenized.
480   However, for images, the input tokens are not necessarily split by semantic. The understanding of
481   image is hard to be decomposed which needs strong capability for dealing with the fine-grained tasks.
482
      That’s why prompts with language only are also hard to improve the understanding of the images.
      The LMMs can not successfully process or decompose the image by the order of language.
483
484   Nevertheless, visual prompts are neither necessary nor possible to work in all scenarios. For instance,
485   when facing highly abstract problems like geometry problem solving, the understanding of the image
      mostly depends on the original capability or the trained dataset of the LMMs since even a simple

      shape like a heptagon might be misidentified. How to effectively develop visual prompts for such
487   problems is still a challenging topic and that’s one of the future works we will target on.

490   6   C ONCLUSION
491
492   In this paper, we propose a novel multimodal prompting method, namely UnAC (Understanding,
493
      Abstracting, and Checking), to synergize reasoning for complicated problems in visual context of
      LMMs. UnAC consists of an adaptive visual prompting building, the prompts of image abstraction
494
      and a gradual checking scheme. Suffecient experiments show the effectiveness of UnAC on improving
495
      the ability of complicated multimodal reasoning.
