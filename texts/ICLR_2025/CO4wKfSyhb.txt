009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Language Model (LM) training requires vast datasets, raising legal, ethical, and
012            practical concerns. Federated learning (FL) offers an alternative by enabling or-
013            ganizations to collaboratively leverage untapped reserves while minimizing data
014            movement. However, scaling FL globally introduces challenges such as restric-
015            tions on data movement, privacy, and statistical data heterogeneity. We propose
016            Worldwide Federated Language Model Training (WorldLM), a system that builds
017            federations of federations. WorldLM enables each federation to autonomously
018
               meet jurisdictional or competitive constraints while managing statistical hetero-
               geneity through attention-based aggregation of key layers and cross-federation
019
               information sharing via residual embeddings. In terms of perplexity, WorldLM
020
               outperforms standard FL and other federated baselines by up to 1.91× and 3.3×
021            respectively. WorldLM scales to models with 400M parameters, achieving 1.39×
022            lower perplexity than centralized counterparts while approaching the performance
023            of perfectly localized models trained in an infinite-data regime. Additionally, under
024            differential privacy constraints, WorldLM proves highly resilient in performance
025            compared to standard FL methods, which diverge. These results establish WorldLM
026            as an effective means for pre-training across geographic and legal boundaries.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Language models (LMs) require extensive computational resources and vast amounts of curated
032   text data, often centralized in data centers (Scao et al., 2022; Dubey et al., 2024). This centralized
033
      paradigm raises concerns (Bommasani et al., 2021) about data ownership (Council of European Union,
      2021), privacy, and copyright issues (Grynbaum & Mac, 2023), as well as the limited availability
034
      of high-quality linguistic data (Villalobos et al., 2022). Federated Learning (FL) has emerged as a
035
      promising alternative that allows organizations to collaboratively train models without sharing raw
036   data (McMahan et al., 2017; Douillard et al., 2023; Sani et al., 2024; Woisetschläger et al., 2024a).
037   FL reduces the need for data movement and synchronization overheads (Rajbhandari et al., 2020;
038   Zhao et al., 2023), while incorporating privacy-preserving techniques such as differential privacy
039   (DP) (Wei et al., 2020) and secure aggregation (Bonawitz et al., 2016). However, scaling FL globally
040   introduces challenges like federated governance and statistical heterogeneity.
041
      The challenge of federated governance (González-García et al., 2021) arises when participants
042   in a federated system operate under varying legal, privacy, and security constraints. For example,
043   participants in the European Union must comply with the GDPR (European Parliament & Council
044   of the European Union, 2016), which imposes rules on cross-border data sharing, while others
045   may be free to operate without such constraints. The second challenge, statistical heterogeneity,
046   occurs when different participants hold non-IID data. This heterogeneity can arise from differences
047   in language (Conneau et al., 2020) or domain. This can lead to slower convergence rates and
048   even divergence in standard FL settings (Zhou et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2024). Moreover, dataset
049   geography (Faisal et al., 2022) often exacerbates these issues, as data collected from different regions
050
      may exhibit inherent clustering, requiring careful optimization across global and local distributions.
051   To address these challenges, we propose the Worldwide Federated Language Model Training
052   (WorldLM) system, built on the idea of federations of federations. This hierarchical approach
053   allows federations to collaborate while maintaining local autonomy to respect regulatory and compet-
      itive constraints. WorldLM brings the following innovations:

072         1. Arbitrary Federation Structure: WorldLM allows federations to account for their unique
073            legal, privacy, and security constraints while participating in a global training process. Each
074            federation can operate under separate legal frameworks, while leveraging privacy-enhancing
075            techniques like differential privacy (Wei et al., 2020) and secure aggregation (Bonawitz
076
               et al., 2016). This provides a flexible structure that enables seamless collaboration across
               jurisdictions and industries without compromising compliance.
077
078         2. Partially-Personalized Aggregation: To address the statistical heterogeneity inherent in
079            real-world federated systems, WorldLM incorporates both a shared model backbone and
080            personalized key layers specific to each node. These key layers are aggregated using an
081            attention-based mechanism that balances global and local objectives. Results on multilingual
082            pre-training show that WorldLM achieves a perplexity 1.91× lower than standard FL, 1.86×
083            lower than Hierarchical Federated Averaging (HierFAVG) (Liu et al., 2020) and 3.3× lower
084
               than Federated Learning With Personalization Layers (FedPer) (Arivazhagan et al., 2019; Li
               et al., 2022).
085
086         3. Cross-Federation Information Sharing: In cases where participants’ data is more similar
087            to data in other sub-federations, WorldLM allows for the sharing of residual layers, enabling
088            transformer layers badly fit to the local distribution to be routed to more relevant federations.
089            This cross-federation information sharing mechanism ensures that participants whose data
090            is dissimilar from that of their peers can still benefit from global collaboration, while
091
               minimizing communication overhead by sending only selected residual blocks.
092
093   We show that WorldLM scales effectively to larger models by training a model with 400M parameters,
094
      the same size as the 2023 SOTA (Douillard et al., 2023) for federated pre-training, on the MC4 dataset,
      achieving a 1.39× lower perplexity compared to centralized baselines. We also evaluate WorldLM
095
      on a gauntlet of 35 downstream tasks organized in 5 categories and show average improvements of
096
      4.49% over standard FL on MC4. Thus, WorldLM provides an effective solution for training LMs
097   across legal, socioeconomic, and cultural boundaries, diversifying the pool of available data away
098   from its current geographic (Faisal et al., 2022) concentration.

101   2   BACKGROUND
102
103   The rise of large language models (LLMs), driven by established performance scaling laws (Hoff-
104   mann et al., 2022), has led to remarkable advancements in various downstream applications (Hu
105   et al., 2021; Gema et al., 2023). Despite these advancements, pre-training remains reliant on large
106   centralized datacenters due to high-bandwidth communication requirements, primarily driven by
107   the synchronization overhead found in Ring AllReduce (Sergeev & Balso, 2018) used in standard
      training algorithms like FSDP (Rajbhandari et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2023).


                                                           2
108
      Recent work in federated learning (FL) has aimed to alleviate these communication bottlenecks. For
109   example, Douillard et al. (2023) used Local SGD(Stich, 2019) to reduce synchronization. Following
110   this, Sani et al. (2024) demonstrated that generative pre-training can be extended to FL, enabling
111   models to leverage geographically distributed data while maintaining privacy. For scenarios where
112   high-quality public datasets may become scarce (Villalobos et al., 2022), FL provides a novel
113   means of tapping into previously inaccessible datasets held by different organizations or even across
114   countries (OpenAI, 2023; Patel & Palazzolo, 2024). These developments prompt us to explore the
115   global scale of federated learning, presenting new system and statistical challenges.
116
117   2.1   G LOBAL F EDERATED S YSTEMS
118
119   FL typically operates through a sequence of broadcast updates, local optimization, and aggregation
120
      processes (McMahan et al., 2017). This approach aligns with privacy regulations, particularly by
      minimizing the exchange of raw data (White House, 2013), making FL a suitable technique for
121
      compliance with frameworks such as the GDPR (European Parliament & Council of the European
122
      Union, 2016) and EU AI ACT (Council of European Union, 2021). However, global-scale FL
123   introduces two primary challenges: statistical heterogeneity and system heterogeneity.
124
125   Statistical heterogeneity emerges when participating clients possess vastly different datasets, such
126
      as varying languages, regions, or domains, each with differing statistical properties (Zhou et al.,
      2023; Kairouz et al., 2021). When naive aggregation methods, such as those used in FedAvg, are
127
      applied across highly non-IID client datasets, the resulting model updates may conflict, leading
128
      to slower convergence or even performance degradation (Charles et al., 2021). This is analogous
129   to the generalization gap seen in large-batch training (Keskar et al., 2017). To address statistical
130   heterogeneity, better aggregation and personalization methods are needed.
131
132
      System heterogeneity arises because clients within federated systems often operate under diverse
      conditions, including varying computational power, network bandwidth, and availability. These
133
      discrepancies can create "stragglers" that slow down the training process as the central server waits
134
      for slow or resource-constrained clients (Huba et al., 2022; Bonawitz et al., 2019). Such disruption
135   delays convergence and increases the risk of uneven model updates.
136
137   Another critical aspect is federated governance, which governs how data can be shared and models
138
      collaboratively trained across legal and regulatory boundaries. In a cross-border context, Federated
      learning must respect local privacy laws, such as GDPR or the EU AI ACT, which dictate the
139
      constraints on data sharing across countries (European Parliament & Council of the European Union,
140
      2016) and focus on mitigating biased outcomes (Council of European Union, 2021, Art. 10.2f,fa).
141   Privacy-enhancing technologies (PETs) such as differential privacy (DP) (Wei et al., 2020) and secure
142   aggregation (Bonawitz et al., 2016) may ensure the secure collaboration of clients despite differing
143   privacy regulations. Further details on the legal implications of FL are available in Appendix A.5.
144
145   2.2   R ELATED W ORK
146
147   Personalized Federated Learning (PFL) (Tan et al., 2021) aims to enhance performance on a given
148   client’s data. One means of achieving this is creating hybrid models wherein common layers are
149   shared while specific layers are customized. For instance, Li et al. (2022) and Arivazhagan et al.
150   (2019) propose FL With Personalization Layers (FedPer) a method that personalizes deeper layers
151   while sharing shallower ones, mitigating heterogeneity at the cost of decreased information sharing.
152   Client clustering methods (Sattler et al., 2021; Briggs et al., 2020) attempt to group clients based on
153   model similarity but avoid considering the structural intricacies of neural networks, often reducing
154   model embeddings to insufficient single-scalar compatibility values. Together with common concerns
155   for clustering hyperparameters, this limits the practical usability of such methods.
156   Existing hierarchical systems like Liu et al. (2020) and Luo et al. (2020) focus primarily on commu-
157   nication efficiency by grouping clients under edge servers and performing aggregation via a simple
158   extension of FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2017), called Hierarchical Federated Averaging(HierFAVG).
159   However, they do not factor in data heterogeneity and aim to create one global model. More
160   recent hierarchical methods, such as those in Mhaisen et al. (2022), integrate data heterogeneity
161   into edge-server assignments but still rely on FedSGD, which is known to converge slower than
      FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2017).

      3     W ORLDWIDE F EDERATED T RAINING OF L ANGUAGE M ODELS (WorldLM)
163
164
      Given the interlocking nature of legal, privacy, and security concerns, we assume organizations
165
      find collaborating with enterprises operating in the same geographic region, legal jurisdiction, or
166
      industry easier. Facilitating collaboration in edge-device FL (Kairouz et al., 2021) requires a fully
167   decentralized collaborative learning paradigm (Kairouz et al., 2021; Zantedeschi et al., 2020) with
168   a graph encoding client compatibility. For training LMs, where the participating entities are stable
169   organizations, we argue that a federation of federations approach, as portrayed in Fig. 1, provides
170   an attractive compromise between standard federated learning and decentralized learning. WorldLM
171   employs a custom aggregation procedure and information-sharing mechanism to optimize for a given
172   hierarchical data distribution.
173
174   3.1   H IERARCHICAL DATA D ISTRIBUTIONS
175
176   We recursively define the data distribution of a federation based on the data distribution of its con-
177   stituent sub-federations. Intra-federation heterogeneity pertains to the heterogeneity across children
178   in the same sub-federation, while inter-federation heterogeneity pertains to the heterogeneity between
179   any two federations that do not share the same parent. The degree of heterogeneity can be measured
180
      using common distance metrics between distributions, such as Earth Mover’s Distance (Zhao et al.,
      2018). Under this definition, an “empty” hierarchical federation contains no members, whereas a
181
      “trivial” hierarchical federation consists of one member.
182
183   Formally, for a sub-federation Q with root server q, we define its data distribution as Ωq = ∪c∈Cq Ωc ∪
184   ωq , which is a mixture of the heterogeneous data distributions {Ωc }c∈Cq of the set of children Cq and
185   the root server data ωq . Similarly, for another sub-federation P with root server p, root server data
186
      ωp , data distribution Ωp = ∪c∈Cp Ωc ∪ ωp , and a set of children Cp with data distributions {Ωc }c∈Cp ,
      the intra-federation heterogeneity is defined as the degree of heterogeneity across {Ωc }c∈Cq and
187
      {Ωc }c∈Cp . The inter-federation heterogeneity is determined between Ωp and Ωq .
188
189   A practical example: if {Ωc }c∈Cq ∼ LDA0.1 and {Ωc }c∈Cp ∼ LDA1000 , then the intra-federation
190   heterogeneity of Q is greater than that of P .
191
192   3.2   PARTIALLY-P ERSONALIZED AGGREGATION
193
194   Federated learning can train powerful feature extractors beneficial to all WorldLM participants due
195   to its meta-learning properties (Nichol et al., 2018; Fallah et al., 2020; Lee et al., 2023). However,
196   participants in federated training may hold heterogeneous data, such as different languages or domains
197   (e.g., news versus scientific publications). Thus, the feature extractor needs adaptation for each actor
198
      and sub-federation, necessitating a departure from the standard FL objective. WorldLM, inspired by
      split-learning and personalized techniques (Arivazhagan et al., 2019; Li et al., 2022), partitions the
199
      model into a backbone B, comprising the majority of the model’s parameters, and a set of partially
200
      personalized key layers K specific to each node.
201

      4.1   F EDERATION C ONSTRUCTION
309
      To simulate organizations holding different genres of text, we partition The Pile (Gao et al., 2021)
310
      into its constituent heterogeneous datasets and construct federations of federations by bottom-up
311
      building different mixtures of datasets. As seen in Fig. 2, if the children of a node hold data from the
312   Pile Common Crawl (CC), Wikipedia (WK), PubMed Central (PBC), and PubMed Abstracts (PBA)
313   datasets, then the node itself will hold data proportional to their size. For this dataset, we use the
314   common gpt-neox-20b English tokenizer also adopted by Sani et al. (2024).
315
      For simulating geographically distributed systems, we use a subset of the Multilingual Colossal
316
      Common Crawl (MC4)(Xue et al., 2021), covering high and low-resource languages (Magueresse
317
      et al., 2020). Sub-federations are constructed based on language families similarly to The Pile.
318   Given the larger vocabulary size (Xue et al., 2021) and consequent model size, we use a single
319   federated structure partitioned with high-resource French (FR) and Italian (IT) on one side and
320   lower-resource Bulgarian (BG) and Ukrainian (UK) on the other. For IID experiments, we use the
321   standard Cleaned Colossal Common Crawl (C4) English dataset, partitioned into equal-sized shards
322   using the same tokenizer as The Pile. Given the larger size of multilingual models, and the
323   restricted heterogeneity of The Pile, we use The Pile for ablation studies while using MC4 for
      crucial baselines comparisons.

      Each federation in our hierarchy represents a substantial data holder, organized in a tree structure.
325   Each node q in the tree can have a set of children Cq . Trivial federations are represented by leaf
326   nodes with no children (Cq = ∅). In this experimental setup, the root federation consists of seven
327   participating nodes counting the root, then each regional federation contains three participating nodes,
328   and each leaf is a federation of one. The configuration is reported in Fig. 2 while partitions and their
329   sizes are detailed in Appendix A.2.

332   4.2   TASKS
333
334
      For all experiments, we use decoder-only transformers (Brown et al., 2020) for the language modeling
335
      task. Given that we are concerned with partially personalized FL, we compare the personalized
336
      local perplexity of WorldLM against three relevant baselines representing alternative scenarios. First,
337   we compare against standard FL with momentum (Huo et al., 2020; Douillard et al., 2023; Sani
338   et al., 2024), which has no hierarchical structure and is challenging to integrate across heterogeneous
339   participants. Second, we compare against Hierarchical FedAvg (HierFAVG)( (Liu et al., 2020), which
340   does not have any personalization. Third, we compare against FedPER (Arivazhagan et al., 2019)
341   which has personalized layers but no hierarchical structure. Finally, we compare against centralized
342   training of a global model on all node data pooled together, which represents the standard pre-training
343   recipe when all data can be centralized.
344   In addition to these baselines, we are also interested in evaluating upper bound of local performance
345   on a given data distribution for a given model size, in order to asses how close WorldLM gets to it.
346   For this purpose, we train models of the same size as WorldLM using an infinite-data regime for the
347   given node. We achieve this by training models that are far too small compared to the size of the
348   local dataset recommended by scaling laws (Hoffmann et al., 2022), specifically we use datasets
349   appropriate for billion-scale models to complete one epoch while training 75M-400M models. These
350   should be seen as idealized cases for what the participants could train if they had unbounded data, in
351
      which case they would not need to use federated training to begin with. For the rest of this work we
      shall use the term “infinite-data local models”.
352
353   Language Modeling: We utilize models of four sizes: 75M, 125M, and 250M and 400M. Our
354   federated training uses the parameters shown in Table 5, while local parameters are available in
355   Table 6. We compare models that have executed for a given number of sequential steps. The step-wise
356   execution in WorldLM (Algorithm 1) implies that each level of the hierarchy (Fig. 1) trains in parallel,
357
      while the levels execute sequentially in three stages: Root, Regional, and Edge. For the 400M, we
      only had the resources for comparison against centralized, as standard FL is much less computationally
358
      efficient than WorldLM due to not having this three-stage execution (Appendix A.1.2).
359
360   For the local training of each node, samples are drawn proportionally to the data source sizes to ensure
361   balanced and representative batches. Each node’s dataset mixture is constituted by combining streams
362   from relevant data sources while employing balanced sampling with shuffling. Since each node
363
      performs the same number of local steps, the size of a data source is primarily relevant in determining
      sampling ratios locally. Full details on the impact of sampling are available in Appendix A.1.

365   Privacy and Security: To validate the effectiveness of WorldLM in enhancing privacy and security,

         5.4                        L IMITATIONS
516
517
       The primary limitations of our design related to the need for an implicit relationship to exist across
518
       the data of clients in the same federation. While our information-sharing and partial-personalization
519
       address this by increasing the distance between two nodes that can result in an interaction, allowing
520    WorldLM to perform well on the alternative arrangement of The Pile, it is not a guaranteed
521    solution. Other significant limitations relate to preventing attacks in a hierarchical scenario, as a
522    single centralized control point does not exist. However, the residuals of WorldLM could be used to
523    potentially filter out outliers from aggregation. A full description of the limitations of WorldLM is
524    available in Appendix A.4.

527      6                  C ONCLUSION
528
529
530    WorldLM supports the extension of federated learning (FL) to the challenging setting of worldwide
531
       optimization of language models (LMs). Our results indicate that systems based on federations-of-
       federations can compete with standard FL and centralized optimization for the medium-sized LMs
532
       affordable to small organizations and groups, given their hardware. Our results show that WorldLM
533
       can outperform standard FL under realistic federated topologies and data distributions constructed
534    using naturally heterogeneous datasets. Furthermore, they also indicate our method to be robust under
535    the constraints of differential privacy, unlike standard FL. Thus, WorldLM is an effective approach
536    for addressing the nascent sub-field of worldwide LM pre-training. We open several new research
537    opportunities such as: (a) defending against model poisoning in hierarchical settings, (b) bringing the
538    benefits of WorldLM billion-scale models, (c) tackling broader forms of statistical heterogeneity, and
539    (d) applying it to parameter-efficient fine-tuning. We hope this will help democratize LM training
       across national boundaries and address the societal concerns regarding its governance.

      A.1     T HE I MPACT OF S AMPLING ON W ORLD LM
865
866   In the WorldLM algorithm, the size of a data source impacts the sampling ratios within the local
867   training pipeline of a given node and the composition of the evaluation set. The hierarchical structure
868   of the federations necessitates a multi-layered approach to sampling, where each level of the hierarchy
869   influences the effective sampling ratios for the data sources.
870
871   A.1.1    L OCAL S AMPLING S TRATEGY
872
      Let ωc represent the dataset held by client c. If a participant holds a single data source, they exclusively
873   sample from that source. For example, a participant holding only Common Crawl (CC) samples will
874   see as many samples of CC as a participant holding only PubMed Central (PBC). Conversely, if a
875   participant holds both Wikipedia (WK) and Common Crawl (CC) datasets, they sample from these
876   sources proportionally to their sizes when forming batches. The local sampling ratio, rωc , for data
877   from a source ωc at a given node is thus determined by the relative sizes of the datasets held by that
878   node.
879
880   A.1.2    E FFECTIVE S AMPLING R ATIO ACROSS H IERARCHY
881
      To determine the effective sampling ratio for a given data source across all model training steps,
882
      one must consider the data mixture across all clients in the federation. It is important to keep the
883   sequential versus parallel distinction in mind. For example, if in standard FL, 7 clients would executed
884   in parallel and be averaged, the execution of WorldLM would depend on the hierarchical structure.
885   Thus, while standard FL could complete 3 rounds with 250 steps per client, for a total number of
886   parallel SGD steps of 5250, WorldLM would only perform 250 + 250 ∗ 2 + 250 ∗ 4 = 1750 parallel
887   steps despite doing the same number of sequential steps.
888
      Let us formalize the sampling ratios using a concrete example involving data sources from the
889   Multilingual Colossal Common Crawl (mC4).
890
891   Formulation for Effective Sampling Ratio
892
      We define the batch-wise sampling ratio for the Bulgarian dataset, ωBG , considering all levels of the
893
      hierarchy. The sampling ratio rBG is given by summing contributions from each hierarchical level.
894
      This can be expressed as:
895
896

                      rBG =                                      +                 +
897                            |ωFR | + |ωIT | + |ωBG | + |ωUK | |ωUK | + |ωBG | |ωBG |
898   where: - The first term represents the root server sampling from all four datasets proportionally to
899   their sizes in each batch. - The second term represents a regional server sampling from UK (UK) and
900   Bulgarian (BG) proportionally to their sizes. - The third term represents a leaf node containing only
901   Bulgarian data.

904   Next, we consider the impact of the aggregation procedure on the sampling ratio. To model the
905   sampling ratio given the effect of averaging, we account for the number of participants at each
906   hierarchical level. The adjusted sampling ratio rBG , taking into account hierarchical averaging, is:

       A.4     L IMITATIONS
1067
1068   The limitations of WorldLM come from two different sources: (a) those common to all federated
1069   methods [1], (b) those induced by its particular design choices such as the hierarchical structure,
1070   partially personalized attention-based aggregation and residual information sharing.
1071
       The limitations common to all federated methods which are highly relevant to WorldLM are data
1072
       heterogeneity, system heterogeneity, and sample efficiency. While we have built WorldLM to tackle
1073
       data heterogeneity, our experimental setup cannot completely replicate the most pathological natural
1074   data distributions which can naturally arise since it relies on known and curated datasets. Thus,
1075   it is possible for a real-world federation to hold data that is even more heterogeneous than what
1076   we can explore using multiple languages or genres of text, as is the case for the work of Charles
1077   et al. (2023). Investigating how to model heterogeneity for federated LM pre-training is an active
1078   research topic. Stragglers may be caused by system heterogeneity as nodes with less powerful GPUs
1079   may take longer to complete a round, our work assumes that the participants are roughly equal in
       terms of computational ability and that the impact of potential stragglers on training time is limited.


                                                              20
1080
       However, we would like to mention that several opportunities to address this concern, including
1081   asynchronous execution [3], partial participation[4], and load balancing[5]. Finally, the sample
1082   efficiency of federated averaging and its derived methods is questionable, given that the multiple
1083   parallel updates of simultaneously executing participants are averaged together which may result in an
1084   uninformative pseudo-gradient on the server [6]. Creating more effective aggregation methods is one
1085   of the primary pursuits of the field. While the partially-personalized aggregation of WorldLM may
1086   help tackle this issue, the challenge is guaranteed for exist in the case of the backbone aggregation.
1087   The WorldLM design itself suffers from challenges when dealing with data heterogeneity, communi-
1088   cation requirements, and potential attacks. As discussed in section 3 of the main work, WorldLM
1089   assumes that there exists multiple participants in the federation which share similar data and may be
1090   connected either via an ancestor or via the residual-sharing mechanism. When this relation fails to
1091   hold, the attention aggregation and residual sharing of WorldLM do not provide a direct benefit over
1092   standard FL methods, as shown by FL performing similarly to WorldLM on the C4 dataset. When
1093   such a relationship is indeed present and the residual mechanism can take care of it, it is possible
1094   for communication restrictions to exist between regions for legal or practical reasons, limiting the
1095
       efficacy of the residual sharing. Finally, defenses against potential poisoning attacks must now
       consider all levels of the hierarchy rather than having a simple dual system where the server is trusted
1096
       while clients are not, as in traditional FL. WorldLM provides some recourse to this as the attention
1097
       coefficients may indicate if the data distribution of a particular participant is completely out of the
1098   ordinary, however, security remains an open future direction.

1101   A.5   T HE L EGAL C ONTEXT OF LLM T RAINING
1102
1103   The surge in popularity of language models, notably exemplified by the release and widespread
1104   adoption of ChatGPT, has accelerated the integration of AI into various sectors. This expansion has
1105   subsequently encouraged the development of regulatory frameworks to govern AI technologies. A
1106   pioneering effort in this domain is the European Union’s Artificial Intelligence Act (the EU AI Act),
1107
       which represents the first comprehensive legal framework of its kind, anticipated to set a precedent for
       global AI regulation (Woisetschläger et al., 2024b). The Act encompasses, among various provisions,
1108
       rigorous data governance guidelines (Art. 10), including adherence to the General Data Protection
1109
       Regulation (GDPR). This introduces significant challenges for AI developers, particularly concerning
1110   the international transfer of data and data de-biasing processes.
1111
1112   Restrictions in cross-border transfer of data: The EU’s GDPR imposes stringent conditions on
1113
       the international transfer of personal data, particularly to third countries deemed to lack adequate
       protection for personal data. The criterion for ’adequacy’, as established in Schrems v DPC (C-
1114
       262/14)) and Recital 104, requires a level of protection ’essentially equivalent’ to that of the EU,
1115
       a high bar for international data transfers, especially to developing countries. Additionally, the
1116   requirement for periodic reviews of adequacy for jurisdictions considered equivalent, alongside
1117   mandated safeguard measures (Art. 49) for transfers to non-equivalent third countries, introduces a
1118   layer of uncertainty and financial burden for businesses engaged in data transfer. Moreover, the EU
1119   is not the only jurisdiction tightening controls over data transfer. China, for instance, has enacted
1120   laws and supplementary provisions mandating a security assessment by regulator for the transfer
1121   of ’important data’ or personal data exceeding specific thresholds, barring certain exemptions. The
1122   varied landscape of regulations poses challenges for accessing diverse local datasets while adhering
1123   to disparate, and sometimes significantly different, regulations across jurisdictions. Hierarchical FL
1124
       offers an efficient solution to maintain compliance by storing data and model within its jurisdiction
       of origin, avoiding cross-border transfers.
1125
1126   Mitigation of data bias: The EU AI Act mandates rigorous oversight throughout the entire lifecycle
1127   of the data used in AI models and obligates the implementation of ’appropriate measures to detect,
1128   prevent, and mitigate potential biases’ (Art. 10.2f,fa). It underscores the growing importance of
1129   accessing diversified data sources, particularly those from jurisdictions that are underrepresented. To
1130
       accommodate the acquisition of such data while adhering to individual privacy and local regulations, a
       novel training paradigm, comprising multiple layers of federation both among clients and jurisdictions,
1131
       is necessitated to address the limitations of the traditional single-layer FL framework.
1132
1133   The right to information: Transparency regarding the data collected and utilized in training, along
       with the rights of AI developers to access such information, has garnered considerable attention from


                                                         21
1134
       both regulators and content creators. The GDPR grants individuals the right to obtain all information
1135   stored by a service provider (Art. 15, Rec. 63 & 64), including details on the application of this
1136   data in training models. Furthermore, the EU AI Act mandates that model providers compile and
1137   disclose a comprehensive summary of the training content publicly (Art. 52c). In the US, legal
1138   disputes such as "Times v OpenAI" have underscored the debate over the extent to which the fair use
1139   doctrine under US copyright law protects the utilization of copyrighted materials in the training of AI
1140   models. This case also ignites broader discussions about the adequacy of the current legal framework
1141   in safeguarding content creators against the opaque practices of LLM training. These challenges have
1142   led to legislative proposals, including the Generative AI Copyright Disclosure Bill in the US House of
1143
       Representatives, aimed at enhancing transparency and accountability. Hierarchical FL, by its design,
       offers inherent advantages over centralized training models by delineating clear data provenance —
1144
       identifying the sources of data and their contributors. This attribute of Hierarchical FL positions it
1145
       favorably in addressing concerns related to informational rights and data privacy, presenting a more
1146   transparent framework for data utilization in AI development.
1147
1148   Energy efficiency: The EU AI Act advocates for the environmentally sustainable development of
1149
       AI systems by proposing the formulation of a Code of Conduct. This Code is intended to establish
       explicit objectives and key performance indicators (Art. 69), mirroring the core values of the EU but
1150
       also responding to growing concerns within the industry and broader society regarding the energy
1151
       consumption associated with the training and use of AI.
