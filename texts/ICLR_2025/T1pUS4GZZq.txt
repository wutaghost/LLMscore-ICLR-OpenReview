009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            In recent years, there has been a trend in the field of Reinforcement Learning
012            (RL) towards large action models trained offline on large-scale datasets via se-
013            quence modeling. Existing models are primarily based on the Transformer archi-
014            tecture, which result in powerful agents. However, due to slow inference times,
015            Transformer-based approaches are impractical for real-time applications, such as
016            robotics. Recently, modern recurrent architectures, such as xLSTM and Mamba,
017            have been proposed that exhibit parallelization benefits during training similar to
018
               the Transformer architecture while offering fast inference. In this work, we study
               the aptitude of these modern recurrent architectures for large action models. Con-
019
               sequently, we propose a Large Recurrent Action Model (LRAM) with an xLSTM
020
               at its core that comes with linear-time inference complexity and natural sequence
021            length extrapolation abilities. Experiments on 432 tasks from 6 domains show that
022            LRAM compares favorably to Transformers in terms of performance and speed.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Reinforcement Learning (RL) has been responsible for impressive success stories such as game-
028   playing [Silver et al., 2016; Vinyals et al., 2019; Berner et al., 2019; Patil et al., 2022], plasma control
029   for fusion [Degrave et al., 2022], or navigation of stratospheric balloons [Bellemare et al., 2020].
030   While these successes were based on classical RL approaches, in which agents have been trained
031   online with RL objectives, recently there has been a trend towards offline RL settings [Levine et al.,
032   2020; Schweighofer et al., 2022] and sequence models trained via behavior cloning [Chen et al., 2021;
033
      Janner et al., 2021]. Such approaches, in which agents are trained on large-scale offline datasets with
      causal sequence modeling objectives, have been driven by the proliferation of Transformer-based
034
      architectures and gave rise to what we refer to as Large Action Models (LAMs) to highlight their
035
      similarity to large language models (LLMs) [Radford et al., 2018]. LAM approaches can also be used
036   in multi-task settings to develop generalist agents such as Gato [Reed et al., 2022].
037
038   Existing LAMs are primarily based on the Transformer [Vaswani et al., 2017] architecture. Because
039
      of their powerful predictive performance, robotics has become an emergent application area for large
      models [Brohan et al., 2023b;a; Octo Model Team et al., 2024; Gu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023]
040
      and a number of large multi-task datasets were collected [Jia et al., 2024; Embodiment Collaboration
041
      et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2023; Mandlekar et al., 2023]. This development bears the potential to
042   produce robotics agents that learn to master complex tasks in a wide range of environments and
043   even different embodiments. For example, recently it has been demonstrated, albeit in restricted
044   settings, that sequence models trained on multi-episodic contexts can perform in-context learning
045   (ICL) [Laskin et al., 2020; Lee et al., 2023]. One potential application of ICL can be to learn new
046   related tasks in robotics without the need for re-training or fine-tuning.
047
      One of the key reasons for the success of Transformer-based models is their ability to scale to large
048   datasets through their efficient parallelization during training. However, despite numerous success
049   stories in RL, language modeling [Brown et al., 2020] or computer vision [Dosovitskiy et al., 2021;
050   He et al., 2022], a persistent drawback of Transformer-based architectures is their high inference cost
051   in terms of both speed and memory [Kim et al., 2023]. Consequently, deploying Transformer-based
052   models in resource-constrained scenarios, such as on devices with limited hardware capacity and/or
053   real-time constraints, e.g., robots or smartphones, is prohibitive because of the required fast inference
      times [Firoozi et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2023]. A basic principle of control theory is that the controller

      100Hz-1000Hz are required to keep the system stable [Salzmann et al., 2023; El-Hussieny, 2024;

      Recently, modern recurrent architectures have been proposed, which exhibit similar parallelization
080
      properties during training as the Transformer architecture while offering linear-time inference com-
081
      plexity. These modern recurrent architectures include xLSTM [Beck et al., 2024] and state-space
082   models (SSMs), such as Mamba [Gu & Dao, 2023; Dao & Gu, 2024] and Griffin/Hawk [De et al.,
083   2024], and have challenged the dominance of the Transformer in language modeling but also in other
084   domains such as computer vision [Alkin et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024], and biomedicine [Schmidinger
085   et al., 2024]. More importantly, their linear-time inference makes them suitable for deployment in
086   scenarios with limited compute, large context sizes, and real-time requirements, such as robotics.
087
      In this work, we assess the aptitude of modern recurrent architectures, such as xLSTM and Mamba,
088   as large action models. To this end, we introduce a Large Recurrent Action Model (LRAM) with an
089   xLSTM at its core (see Figure 1). We train our agents on 432 tasks from 6 domains using a supervised
090   learning setting similar to that of the Decision Transformer [Chen et al., 2021, DT]. We use data
091   collected during online-RL training of single-task specialist agents and compile these trajectories
092   alongside other expert demonstrations into a large-scale multi-domain dataset comprising 894M
093   transitions. Due to their parallelization properties, the modern recurrent architectures considered
094   in this work can process this large-scale training set as efficiently as the Transformer while being
095   faster at inference. Experiments across 4 models sizes with our multi-task models indicate that
096
      xLSTM compares favorably to Transformers in terms of both performance and speed. In addition, we
      study the effect of modern recurrent architectures on fine-tuning performance and in-context learning
097
      abilities, and find that they exhibit strong performance in both dimensions.
098
099   The main purpose of this paper is to test the hypothesis that modern recurrent model architectures are
100   better suited for building LAMs than Transformers. Hereby, we make the following contributions.
101
             • We propose a Large Recurrent Action Model (LRAM) with an xLSTM at its core that
102
               enables efficient inference.
103
104          • We assess the aptitude of modern recurrent architectures as backbones for large-action
105
               models with respect to their efficiency at inference time and overall performance in multi-
               task, fine-tuning, and in-context learning settings.
106
107          • To foster further research on large action models, we release our data preparation pipeline
               and generated datasets.

      2     R ELATED WORK
109
110
      Sequence Models in RL. LSTM [Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997] is the dominant backbone
111
      architecture for partially observable online RL problems and has been behind achievements such
112   as mastering Starcraft II [Vinyals et al., 2019], Dota 2 [Berner et al., 2019], and Atari [Espeholt
113   et al., 2018; Kapturowski et al., 2019]. After the success of the Transformer in NLP [Devlin et al.,
114   2019; Radford et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020], computer vision [Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; He et al.,
115   2022; Radford et al., 2021; Fürst et al., 2022] and speech recognition [Radford et al., 2022; Baevski
116   et al., 2020], the architecture has found its way into RL. Chen et al. [2021] proposed the Decision
117   Transformer (DT) a GPT-style model [Radford et al., 2018], that learns to predict actions from offline
118   trajectories via behavior cloning. Trajectory Transformer [Janner et al., 2021] predicts actions along
119   with states and rewards, which allows for dynamics modeling. Other follow-up works build on
120
      the DTs [Zheng et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Shang et al., 2022; Meng et al., 2021; Siebenborn
      et al., 2022; Schmied et al., 2024a] or replace the Transformer with Mamba [Ota, 2024; Dai et al.,
121
      2024]. Furthermore, sequence models trained were found to exhibit ICL if conditioned on previous
122
      trajectories [Laskin et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2022; Kirsch et al., 2023], albeit in limited scenarios.
123
124   Large Action Models (LAMs). LAMs, such as the Decision Transformer, are well suited for multi-
125   task settings. Lee et al. [2022] found that a multi-game DT can learn to play 46 Atari games. Reed
126
      et al. [2022] introduced a generalist agent trained on over 600 tasks from different domains, ranging
      from Atari to manipulation of a robot arm. Jiang et al. [2022] a Transformer for robot manipulation
127
      based on multi-modal prompts, that allow to steer the model to perform new tasks. Recently, Raad
128
      et al. [2024] introduced an agent instructable via language to play a variety of commercial video
129   games. Since then, robotics has become an emergent area for developing LAMs [Brohan et al.,
130   2023b;a; Octo Model Team et al., 2024; Gu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024], also
131   due to the availability of large-scale robotics datasets [Jia et al., 2024; Embodiment Collaboration
132   et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2023; Mandlekar et al., 2023].
133
      Next-generation Sequence Modeling Architectures. Linear recurrent models, such as state-space
134
      models (SSM, Gu et al., 2021; 2022b; Smith et al., 2023; Orvieto et al., 2023) have challenged the
135   dominance of the Transformer [Vaswani et al., 2017] architecture on long-range tasks [Tay et al.,
136   2020]. The key insight of those linear RNNs was to diagonalize the recurrent state matrix and enforce
137   stable training via an exponential parameterization [Gu et al., 2022a; Orvieto et al., 2023]. Since
138   then, there have been efforts to include features such as gating from RNNs [Elman, 1990; Jordan,
139   1990; Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Cho et al., 2014]. Non-linear gates are believed to have
140   higher expressivity, but are harder to train. Griffin [De et al., 2024] mixes gated linear recurrences
141   with local attention to achieve more training data efficiency than Llama-2 [Touvron et al., 2023] and
142   better sequence extrapolation. Mamba [Gu & Dao, 2023] introduces a selection mechanism similar
143
      to gating into SSMs, which makes its state and input matrix time dependent. This is similar to the
      gating mechanism of RNNs but also bears resemblance to approaches like fast weights [Schmidhuber,
144
      1992] and Linear Attention [Katharopoulos et al., 2020]. Mamba-2 [Dao & Gu, 2024] highlight
145
      the connection between SSMs with input dependent state and input matrices and (Gated) Linear
146   attention variants. Most recently, the xLSTM [Beck et al., 2024] was proposed as an improvement
147   over the classic LSTM [Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997] that combines gating, linear recurrences
148   and recurrent weights into a single architecture for language modeling. First, xLSTM leverages
149   exponential gating with stabilization to RNNs for stronger emphasis on important inputs. Second,
150   xLSTM is composed of two variants, the mLSTM variant with an emphasis on memory that proves
151   important in language modeling and the sLSTM variant that keeps the non-diagonalized recurrent
152   matrix to enable state-tracking [Merrill et al., 2024]. State tracking is important in logic tasks and
153   cannot be modeled fundamentally by linearized recurrent or state-space models like Mamba, Griffin
154
      or Transformers.

160   Reinforcement Learning. We assume the standard RL formulation via a Markov Decision Process
161   (MDP) represented by a tuple of (S, A, P, R), where S and A denote state and action spaces,
      respectively. At every timestep t the agent observes state st ∈ S, predicts action at ∈ A, and receives

163   defines the transition dynamics and constitutes a probability distribution over next states st+1 when
164   executing action at in state st . The goal of RL is to learn a policy π(at | st ) that predicts an action
165   at in state st that maximizes rt .

179   3.2    L ARGE R ECURRENT ACTION M ODELS (LRAM S )
180
      Our LRAM has a modern recurrent architecture at its core (see Figure 1), which comes with a parallel
181
      training and a recurrent inference mode. We instantiate LRAM with three different variants, two
182
      different xLSTM configurations and Mamba. Furthermore, we use a training protocol similar to that
183   of Lee et al. [2022] and Reed et al. [2022] with some differences.
184
185   Multi-modal sequence representation. To encode input from different environments with varying
186
      state and action spaces, we use separate encoders per modality that are shared across tasks and
      domains. For encoding images we use a CNN similar to Espeholt et al. [2018], whereas for low-
187
      dimensional inputs we use a fully connected network. We refrain from patchifying images and
188
      tokenizing continuous states to avoid unnecessarily long sequences. Similarly, we use linear layers to
189   encode rewards and RTGs. We omit actions in our sequence formulation, as we found that this can be
190   detrimental to performance, in particular for continuous control tasks (see Section 4.3). Consequently,
191   our trajectories have the form τi = (s1 , R̂1 , r1 , . . . , sT , R̂T , rT ) and we train our policy πρ to predict
192   the ground-truth action at as:
193
194
                           ât ∼ πρ (ât | st−C , R̂t−C , rt−C , . . . , st−1 , R̂t−1 , rt−1 , st , R̂t ).              (2)
195   Shared action head. Action spaces in RL typically vary across environments. For example, in the
196   environments we consider, there are 18 discrete actions and a maximum of 8 continuous dimensions
197   for continuous control environments. Therefore, we employ discretization of continuous action
198
      dimensions into 256 uniformly-spaced bins, similar to Reed et al. [2022] and Brohan et al. [2023b].
      Unlike prior work, we leverage a shared action head to predict all discrete actions or continuous
199
      action dimensions at jointly. We found this setup significantly reduces inference time compared to
200
      using autoregressive action prediction of continuous actions.
201
202   Recurrent inference mode. At inference time, we leverage the recurrent backbone and maintain the
203   hidden states of the last timestep. This enables fast inference with linear-time complexity along the
204
      sequence length. In addition, the recurrent-style inference is well suited for online fine-tuning via RL
      objectives, similar to LSTM-based policies in online RL. To further speed-up inference, we leverage
205
      custom kernels for the xLSTM backbone (see Appendix 22).
206
207   Our unified discrete action representation enables consistent training of our agents via the cross-
208   entropy loss as training objective across all tasks and domains, similar to Reed et al. [2022]. We use
209   separate reward scales per domain and target returns per task. Furthermore, we do not make use of
210
      timestep encodings as used by Chen et al. [2021], which are detrimental when episode lengths vary.
      We provide additional implementation details in Appendix B.

377   Removing Actions & Effect of Context Length. We found that removing actions from the context
      results in better performance across backbones. While context lengths beyond 1 hurt performance


                                                       7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      on Meta-World and DMControl and when training with actions, the reverse is true when training
379   without actions (see Figures 23, 24, 26). This is in contrast to recent works, which did not benefit
380   from longer contexts [Octo Model Team et al., 2024]. While removing actions improves performance
381   on Meta-World and DMControl, it does not affect performance on discrete control environments.
382   For Meta-World and DMControl, we observed that the models become overly confident (high action
383   logits), which is problematic if poor initial actions are produced. We assume this is because many
384   robotics environments exhibit smoothly changing actions and by observing previous actions the agent
385   learns shortcuts. A similar issue has been observed by Wen et al. [2020] and termed the copycat
386   problem. Removing actions from the input prevents the agent from using shortcuts and alleviates the
387
      copycat problem. Importantly, the evaluation performance improves across domains as the sequence
      length increases, which indicates that the history helps to predict the next action (e.g., by observing
388
      mistakes made in the recent past, see Figures 25, 27).

390   Return-conditioning vs. Behavior Cloning. Across our experiments, we utilized a sequence
391   representation that includes return-to-go tokens as commonly used in DTs [Chen et al., 2021; Lee
392   et al., 2022]. However, many recent works focus on behavior cloning without return conditioning
393
      [Reed et al., 2022; Brohan et al., 2023a; Octo Model Team et al., 2024]. Therefore, we study the
      effect of excluding the RTG tokens from the sequence representation at the 206M parameter scale, to
394
      validate that our findings transfer to the behavior cloning setting. Indeed, we find that the same trends
395
      hold (see Figure 28 in Appendix D.2).
396
397   mLSTM-to-sLSTM Ratio. Throughout our experiments, we compare two xLSTM variants: xLSTM
398   [7:1] and xLSTM [1:0]. These ratios were proposed by Beck et al. [2024] and we maintain the same
399
      ratios for consistency (see Appendix B.3). While mLSTM is fully parallelizable, sLSTM enables
      state-tracking [Merrill et al., 2024]. To better understand the effect of this ratio, we conduct ablation
400
      studies both on the full 432 tasks and on Dark-Room (see Appendix D.3), similar to Beck et al.
401
      [2024]. We find that other ratios, such as [3:1], can be effective (see Figure 30). In addition, we
402   find it important to place sLSTM blocks a lower-level layers. However, the effectiveness of sLSTM
403   layers is dependent on the task at hand. We believe that complex tasks with long horizons or partial
404   observability, as are common in real-world applications, may benefit from the state-tracking abilities
405   provided by sLSTM blocks.
406
      We present additional ablations on the effect of reducing the number of layers in xLSTM and disabling
407
      Dropout on DT in Appendix D.5 and D.4, respectively.

      5    C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this work, we study the aptitude of modern recurrent architectures as alternatives to Transformers
489
      for building LAMs. We found that our LRAM with an xLSTM or Mamba at its core compare
490   favorably to the Transformer in terms of evaluation performance across different model scales (see
491   Section 4.2). Moreover, we demonstrated that xLSTM-based LRAMs exhibit higher inference
492   speeds, especially at large context sizes (see Section 4.4). Thus, the empirical evidence suggests,
493   that recurrent backbones such as the xLSTM can be attractive alternatives for LAMs. Notably, the
494   linear-time inference complexity of xLSTM may enable applications that require long context lengths,
495   such as in-context RL, and facilitate the application of large-scale agents for real-time applications,
496   such as robotics.
497   Nevertheless, modern recurrent architectures and Transformers come with different pros and cons.
498   Both xLSTM and Mamba, on the one hand, exhibit a fundamental computational complexity ad-
499   vantage over Transformers. Their linear complexity ensures that the computational requirements
500   increase slower with the sequence length. This property enables more efficient inference, which can
501   be particularly relevant for edge-applications. While we conduct our inference time comparisons
502   on a high-end data-center GPU, applications on edge-devices may have to deal with less powerful
503   accelerators. Importantly, we found that LAMs strongly benefit from longer sequences (see Section
504
      4.3). Transformers, on the other hand, are particularly effective for applications that require exact
      recall of tokens in a sequence, which can be important for decision-making [Ni et al., 2024]. Finally,
505
      xLSTM in particular enables state-tracking via sLSTM blocks, which Transformers and Mamba can-
506
      not perform [Merrill et al., 2024]. State tracking can be important for logic tasks and for dealing with
507   partial observability in RL environments (see Section 4.3) and may be a useful tool for practicioners.
508   Given these differences, different backbones should be considered depending on the task at hand.
509
510
      Limitations. The primary target application of LAMs is robotics. While the majority of our
      experiments involve robotic simulations, we do not yet provide empirical evidence for real robots.
511
      We do, however, believe that our findings translate to real-world scenarios and aim to provide further
512
      evidence in future work. Moreover, the fine-tuning experiments in this work are limited to offline
513   RL. We envision that an agent pre-trained by behavioral cloning on large-scale offline RL datasets
514   may be successfully fine-tuned in an online RL setting to explore new strategies that do not appear
515   in the training data. Modern recurrent architectures offer both parallel and recurrent training mode,
516   which might be the key to success for such applications. While we provide initial evidence of
517   improved ICL abilities of modern recurrent architectures, we only consider a limited grid-world
518   setting. Consequently, we aim to further investigate the in-context RL abilities of recurrent backbones
519   on more complex environments in future work.
520
521   6    E THICS S TATEMENT
522
523   While we conduct all our experiments in simulated environments, the primary target application of
524   our method is robotics. We believe that our work can positively impact applications in the near future,
525   which require efficient inference, on-device processing, or have real-time constraints. However,
526   robotics applications in the real world are not without risks. In particular, in areas where humans
527   are involved, such as factory settings, special care is required. LAMs are trained via next-action
528   prediction similar to LLMs. Consequently, LAMs may also suffer from hallucinations in unknown
529   scenarios. We therefore strongly discourage users from blindly following the predictions made by
530   real-world LAMs without appropriate safeguards regarding safety and robustness. It is essential to
531
      ensure responsible deployment of such future technologies, and we believe that more research on the
      robustness of LAMs is necessary.

       B.1   T RAINING & E VALUATION .
1336
1337
       In our experiments, we compare two variants of xLSTM, Mamba and DT. For our main experiments
1338   in Section 4.2, we train all models for 200K updates, and evaluate after every 50K update steps. We
1339   report the mean and 95% confidence intervals over three seeds in our experiments, as suggested by
1340   Agarwal et al. [2021]. For every evaluation tasks, we take the average of 3 evaluation seeds.
1341
       We train our agents with a batch size of 128 and gradient accumulation across the 6 domains, such
1342
       that every domain is represented with the same proportion. Consequently, the effective batch size is
1343
       768. We use a learning rate of 1e−4 , 4000 linear warm-up steps followed by a cosine decay to 1e−6 ,
1344   and train using the AdamW optimizer [Loshchilov & Hutter, 2018]. In addition, we employ gradient
1345   clipping of 0.25, weight decay of 0.01 for all models. We do not employ Dropout, as is standard
1346   practice in DTs, as we found that it negatively affects performance (see Section 4.3). We use separate
1347   reward scales of 200, 100 and 20 for Meta-World, DMControl and Atari, respectively. Furthermore,
1348   for all domains, we set the target return to the maximum return achieved for a particular task in the
1349   training datasets. This is particularly useful for domains, where the maximum returns differ heavily
       across tasks (e.g., Atari). We list all hyperparameters in Table 5.

1358   B.3   M ODEL A RCHITECTURES .
1359
1360   We train models across 4 models sizes: 16M, 48M, 110M, and 206M. We follow Lee et al. [2022] in
1361   selecting the number of layers and hidden dimensions. For xLSTM and Mamba, we use twice the
1362   number of layers blocks to match the number of parameters of the Transformer [Beck et al., 2024;
1363   Gu et al., 2024] (see Table 6) For our xLSTM [7:1] variant, which contains sLSTM blocks, we strive
1364   to maintain the same ratio as proposed by Beck et al. [2024]. Not all our model sizes are divisible by
1365   8 and only the 16M and 110M models exhibit the exact 7:1 ratio of mLSTM to sLSTM blocks. For
1366
       consistency, however, we maintain the same notation as Beck et al. [2024]. We place sLSTM blocks
       at positions [1], [1, 3], [1, 3], and [1, 3, 5] for the 16M, 48M, 110M, 206M, respectively.
1367
1368   Across backbones, we use linear layers to encode continuous states, reward returns-to-go, similar to
1369   Chen et al. [2021]. The maximal state-dimension across continuous control environments is 204 in
1370   our experiments. To use a shared linear embedding layer for continuous states, we pad states that
1371   have lower number of dimensions to 204 dimensions using zeros. To encode image inputs on visual
1372
       domains, we use the IMPALA-CNN proposed by Espeholt et al. [2018] and adopted by previous
       works on Procgen [Cobbe et al., 2020a] and Atari [Schmidt & Schmied, 2021; Schwarzer et al., 2023].
1373
       Consequently, we do not make use of discretization of continuous states or patchification of images.
1374
       This design choice significantly reduces the sequence length to only three tokens per time-step (see
1375   Appendix B.2) and consequently results in faster inference.
1376
1377   For continuous actions, we make use of discretization and discretize of every action dimension into
1378
       256 uniformly-spaced bins, similar to Reed et al. [2022] and Brohan et al. [2023b]. We experimented
       with lower/higher number of bins, but did not observe a benefit beyond 256 bins. Consequently, this
1379
       resolution is sufficient for the environments we consider. We use a shared action head to predict
1380
       the action bins of all continuous dimensions jointly. The maximum number of continuous action
1381   dimensions is 8 in our experiments and consequently the number of discrete action classes is 2048. In
1382   addition, there are 18 discrete actions originating from Atari and Procgen. Therefore, our action head
1383   learns to predict the correct action among the 2066 discrete classes. While different environments
1384   may have different action dimensions, the model predicts all action dimensions jointly. At inference
1385   time, the number of action dimensions of the current environment is known, and we extract the
1386   respective dimensions from the joint predictions. We opt for the shared action head representation, as
1387   this further speeds up inference and does not require autoregressive action prediction.
1388   For the Transformer baseline, we use global positional embeddings similar to Chen et al. [2021]. For
1389   the recurrent backbones, we do not make use of positional encodings.

1987   While mLSTM blocks are fully parallelizable, sLSTM blocks are not. However, sLSTM preserves
1988   the non-diagonalized recurrent matrix to enable state-tracking [Merrill et al., 2024]. As such, sLSTM
1989   can be attractive for tasks that require state-tracking (see Figure 4 in Beck et al. [2024]).
1990

1991
       performance across all 432 tasks. For this experiment, we use the 16M parameter model that contains
