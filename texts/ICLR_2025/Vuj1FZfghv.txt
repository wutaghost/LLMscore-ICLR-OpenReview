011                                                     A BSTRACT
012
013                Recent advances in graph-based imputation methods for addressing missing data
014                have received considerable attention, primarily for their ability to effectively aggre-
015                gate and propagate information through graph structures. However, the applicability
016
                   of these methods to the biomedical tabular domain remains constrained by two
                   main factors: the lack of task-relevant graph structure and a lack of consideration
017
                   of feature-wise relationships. To address these challenges, we introduce GRASS1 ,
018
                   a novel approach that effectively bridges the gap between existing graph-based im-
019                putation methods and the unique needs of biomedical tabular domains with initially
020                missing data. To derive feature gradient, GRASS initiates with training a Multi-
021                Layer Perceptron layer on tabular data. This gradient then facilitates the creation
022                of graph structures from a feature (column) perspective, enabling column-wise
023                feature propagation for imputing missing values, followed by uncertainty-aware
024                categorical clamping. Finally, to effectively utilize existing graph-based imputation
025                methods in an agnostic manner, we input a so-called warmed-up matrix along with
026                an associated sample (row) graph. We validate GRASS on real-world biomedical
027
                   tabular datasets, demonstrating its ability to unleash the potential of graph-based
                   imputation methods across a variety of missing scenarios.

      Mixture Model significantly outperform traditional tabular imputation techniques. However, in the
072
      tabular such as biomedical domain, the situation is quite different. Factors like high dimensionality
073
      coupled with small sample sizes, technical limitations such as dropout Wiens (2003), and patient data
074   confidentiality issues Cismondi et al. (2013b) intensify the difficulties of handling missing data. These
075   unique factors make deriving a relevant graph structure particularly challenging in scenarios with high
076   rates of missing data. As depicted in Figure 2 (b), utilizing a kNN graph generated from an initial
077   feature matrix proves inadequate for methods like GCNMF underperforming even basic approaches
078   like Mean. The limitation arises because the graph depends on an incomplete feature matrix that lacks
079   crucial task-relevant information, impacting both effective imputation and subsequent downstream
080   tasks. This motivates us to develop a more sophisticated graph structure enriched with task-relevant
081   information.
082   Lack of consideration of column-wise relationships. Specifically, in the biomedical tabular domain,
083   characterized by complex interactions between various features like gene-gene and disease-related
084   interactions and high dimensionality, neglecting feature relationships can be a critical oversight. For
085   instance, the relationship between ‘Age’ and ‘Ventricles’ (VT), indicating potential brain volume loss
086   with age, is crucial in biomedical analysis, as referenced in studies Nestor et al. (2008); Bjork et al.
087   (2003). Overlooking such feature (i.e., column-wise) relationships in data can cause advanced graph-
088
      structure generating methods like IGRM, which uses a bipartite graph with a row-wise approach,
      to be less effective than simpler, row-wise methods like GCNMF. This is evident in Figure 2 (c),
089
      highlighting the importance of integrating relevant feature relationships in the development of graph
090
      structure, especially in the tabular domain.
091
092   Given these considerations, the central question arises:
093
               (Q) Is it feasible to craft a more insightful feature matrix and associated graph structure,
094            thereby leveraging the potential of graph-based imputation in the biomedical domain?
095
096   Here, we introduce GRASS, an innovative approach that offers an orthogonal way to leverage and
097   generalize existing graph-based imputation methods to real-world missing scenarios—such as those
098   in the biomedical tabular domain—where both initially missing features and graph structure are
099
      prevalent. Instead of directly constructing a graph structure based on the current incomplete features,
      which would be suboptimal, we commence with training on the tabular data using the Multi-Layer
100
      Perceptron (MLP) layer. During this training process, a valuable task-relevant byproduct that naturally
101
      emerges is gradient information with respect to the features. We utilize this feature gradient by
102   concatenating it to the original feature matrix, thereby creating a feature perspective graph. Following
103   this, we implement column-wise feature propagation to impute the initial feature matrix and apply
104   uncertainty-aware categorical clamping, preserving the uncertain status for later imputation by graph-
105   based imputation techniques. Now, equipped with this so-called warmed-up feature matrix and the
106   new graph structure, we stand ready to harness the potential of cutting-edge graph-based imputation
107   techniques, extending our reach to real-world missing data scenarios. Figure 1 visually summarizes
      the core contributions of our work.

118   2   R ELATED W ORK
119
120   Tabular-based Data Imputation. The challenge of missing data imputation has a long history and
121
      many early approaches for tabular data are rooted in statistical methods Efron (1994); Little & Rubin
      (2019). These methods often leverage the distribution of non-missing values to impute missing ones.
122
      Recent machine learning-based imputation techniques include kNN-based approaches Troyanskaya
123
      et al. (2001); Keerin et al. (2012), GAIN Yoon et al. (2018), which employs Generative Adversarial
124   Networks Goodfellow et al. (2020), and MIWAE Mattei & Frellsen (2019), which utilizes a Deep
125   Latent Variable model Kingma & Welling (2013). There have also been efforts to adapt graph
126   structures to tabular data for imputation; for example, GRAPE You et al. (2020) introduces a bipartite
127   graph connecting samples and features, while the more recent IGRM Zhong et al. (2023) extends
128   GRAPE by adding a friend network to capture relationships between samples. However, as these
129   methods heavily rely on the input feature matrix as a main resource, in cases where a significant
130   proportion of data is missing, the imputation quality tends to degrade, negatively affecting downstream
131   tasks’ performance. Notably, compared to the graph domain, most of these studies emphasize either
132
      imputation or regression. This is because the task of imputing continuous values closely aligns with
      regression, simplifying both training and evaluation. However, another pivotal downstream task, i.e.,
133
      classification, remains underexplored in the realm of tabular data with missing features.
134
135   Graph-based Data Imputation. From the viewpoint of graph-based imputation, GCNMF Taguchi
136   et al. (2021) tackles missing features by assuming a Gaussian distribution for each feature channel
137   while aligning it with Graph Convolutional Networks (GCN) Kipf & Welling (2016a). PaGNN Jiang
138
      & Zhang (2020) proposes a partial aggregation scheme derived from neighborhood reconstruction.
      FP Rossi et al. (2021) iteratively diffuses known features to unknown features, followed by GNN
139
      layers. Recently, PCFI Um et al. (2023) builds upon FP to introduce channel-wise diffusion confidence
140
      to handle scenarios with higher missing feature rates. However, channel-wise diffusion operates on
141   fully connected graphs, potentially incorporating irrelevant or noisy information between channels.
142   Additionally, they carry a strong inductive bias toward readily available graph structures, limiting
143   their generalizability. As mentioned above, the application domain of these works primarily focuses
144   on Citation Sen et al. (2008) and Co-Purchase networks Shchur et al. (2018) where features are
145   text-based, a situation less reflective of realistic cases where features are initially missing.
146
      Biomedical Data Imputation. In the medical domain, several research efforts have been made
147   to address missing data. Multiple imputation techniques are suggested by Janssen et al. (2010),
148   while Cismondi et al. (2013a) employs statistical approaches for imputation. The MICE algo-
149   rithm Van Buuren & Groothuis-Oudshoorn (2011) is also widely applied in this context. On the
150   biology side, a prominent issue related to missing data is the occurrence of dropout events in single-
151   cell RNA-sequencing datasets, where zero values are often falsely recorded as missing. Among the
152   various methods proposed van Dijk et al. (2018); Wang et al. (2021); Yun et al. (2023), scGNN Wang
153   et al. (2021) and scFP Yun et al. (2023) employ Graph Auto-Encoders (GAE) Kipf & Welling (2016b)
154   and FP Rossi et al. (2021) to impute these false zeros. Despite these efforts, the use of graph-based
155
      data imputation techniques remains underexplored. This is largely due to the absence of a network
      structure and a reliance on input feature matrices. Such limitations widen the gap between recent
156
      advances in graph-based imputation and real-world applications where data is often missing.

200   3.1             F EATURE G RADIENT AS A S UPPLEMENT
201
202   Given initially missing feature matrix, the direct utilization of this matrix for downstream tasks
203   can lead to suboptimal results where prior imputation is imperative. Naturally, one might consider
204
      the latest graph-based imputation techniques Taguchi et al. (2021); Jiang & Zhang (2020); Rossi
      et al. (2021); Um et al. (2023) given their prowess in receiving messages from neighboring samples.
205
      However, as shown in Figure 2 (b), constructing a graph structure directly from a partially observed
206
      feature matrix and then proceeding with imputation is extremely challenging, often yielding inferior
207   results than simple tabular-based methods. In this context, given our primary goal is the downstream
208   task, i.e., classification, we choose not to rely on the initial partially observed feature matrix. Instead,
209   we leverage the additional resource, supervision signal, incorporating this information into graph
210   construction. To achieve this, we employ a simple Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) to capture and
211   utilize the feature gradient, acquired during backpropagation, as a crucial, task-aligned resource.
212   These gradients indicate how subtle shifts in features impact the model’s predictions, highlighting the
213   salience of individual features in loss minimization. By supplementing this gradient, we can devise a
214   graph structure that encapsulates not just observed feature information from an initial state but also
215
      the feature saliency in relation to our targeted downstream task. We start with a formal definition of a
      feature gradient.

275   a custom kNN graph, as opposed to the pre-defined adjacency matrix used in FP, detailed discussions
276   on convergence can be found in Appendix A.4.

280
      In real-world tabular datasets, which often include both numerical (e.g., Age, Blood Pressure)
281
      and categorical features (e.g., Gender, Blood Type), we introduce a clamping method specifically
282
      designed for categorical features. Previous column-wise FP, involving iterative multiplications with a
283   normalized adjacency matrix, can yield continuous imputed values for one-hot encoded categorical
284   columns. In the biomedical field, particularly in Alzheimer’s Disease (AD) research, the treatment
285   of categorical features, such as the status of Microglia (MG) cells, is of great importance Hansen
286   et al. (2018). The presence or absence of MG cell changes (denoted as 1 for ‘MG_Change’ and 0
287   for ‘MG_Stable’) can be a significant indicator of the disease’s progression, requiring meticulous
288   consideration. Practitioners might classify an MG status as ‘change’ only if the imputed value
289   surpasses a predefined threshold, set at 0.7 in this context. Naturally, imputed values below this
290   threshold will be categorized as ‘MG_Stable’.
291   However, recall that our imputation has, until now, solely considered column-wise relationships.
292   Therefore, we choose to leave room for uncertain values by retaining their original missing status
293   (‘?’). This approach opens up the possibility for subsequent row-wise propagation using existing
294   graph-based imputation methods, thereby allowing for potentially higher-value imputation later
295   on. Formally, for a categorical index c, which we obtain during the preprocessing of numerical

      5      C ONCLUSION
478
479
      Graph-based imputation methods, increasingly popular for filling missing features by leveraging
480   neighborhood information, face challenges in the biomedical tabular domain due to the absence of
481   task-relevant graph structures and intra-feature relationship considerations. We introduce GRASS, an
482   innovative algorithm designed to generalize and enhance graph-based imputation to the biomedical
483   domain. GRASS starts with obtaining feature gradients to construct a column-wise graph, followed by
484   feature propagation and uncertainty-aware categorical clamping. Our extensive research validates the
485   effectiveness of GRASS, positioning it as a promising foundation for future graph-based imputation
      research in biomedical domains.

      A.4    D ISCUSSION ON C ONVERGENCE OF C OLUMN - WISE F EATURE P ROPAGATION
919
920   One of the hallmark advantages of FP is its ability to guarantee convergence of feature representations
921   for missing nodes, provided the graph is undirected and maintains strong connectivity (Berman &
922   Plemmons, 1994). In contrast to graph domains where the initial graph structure is given without any
923   missing elements and can thereby extract a strongly connected component, our situation, defined by
924   initially missing features devoid of a graph structure, requires manual graph construction, such as the
925   kNN graph as detailed in Equation 2. This approach does not ensure strong connectivity, making the
926
      convergence of imputed values for missing features uncertain. Nonetheless, we argue that within our
      context of missing features, simply increasing the number of neighbors, k, to achieve the convergence
927
      property might not always be advantageous.
928
929   Claim: Elevating k to attain strong connectivity (which increases the likelihood, albeit without
930   guarantees) and consequently secure the convergence property can sometimes be detrimental to
931   performance. This might inadvertently introduce a primary drawback inherent to graph-based
932
      learning: over-smoothing. ⇔ Rationale: As the value of k escalates, the adjacency matrix Afeat
      becomes increasingly dense. However, considering our scenario of missing features where feature
933
      representation remains incomplete, the veracity of the new connections becomes dubious. For the
934
      representation of missing nodes in the feature matrix used in Equation 2, denoted as X⊤ ∈ RF ×N
935   and represented by xu ∈ RN , a high missing rate combined with an extensive k implies that the
936   feature representation of the majority node, xu , will evolve via feature propagation. As the number
937   of layers increases and k approaches the total number of nodes F , these nodes end up with almost
938   identical representations.
939
      Given this perspective, we aim to avert ambiguous node connections and counteract over-smoothing,
940
      which could potentially degrade classification performance. To this end, we commit to using a
941   relatively modest and smaller value of k when crafting the graph from the feature’s perspective.
942
943   Discussion on the Convergence and Performance Gain Relationship. To further investigate
944
      whether the convergence property contributes to performance gain, we conducted an empirical
      analysis to validate our claims. In Figure 6, we extended our proposed range of kcol and krow values,
945
      {1,3,5,10}, up to 50, and tested the resulting graph’s connectivity. We observed that when kcol and krow
946
      exceed 10, the generated graph becomes strongly connected, meaning that every node is reachable
947   from every other node. Interestingly, while strong connectivity provides convenience in choosing the
948   number of neighbors and satisfies the necessary condition for FP to converge, it does not necessarily
949   translate to performance gains. Optimal performance was, in fact, achieved within a smaller range of
950   k values, as initially proposed. Upon further investigation, we discovered that increasing kcol leads
951   to an oversmoothing issue in the resulting output, particularly in the warmed-up matrix. This effect
952   was quantified using the MADGap metric (Chen et al., 2020), which measures the representational
953   difference between remote and neighboring nodes. In summary, our findings suggest that when
954   dealing with bio-medical tabular data, where an initial graph structure is not provided and a kNN
955
      graph must be manually generated, selecting a large k value to leverage the convergence property of
      FP may not be the most effective strategy in scenarios with severe missing.

1040   A.6            D ETAILS OF BASELINES
1041
1042   To tackle the challenge of generalizing graph-based imputation methods to bio-medical tabular data,
1043   we have adopted two types of baseline approaches. For graph-based imputation methods, which
1044   typically target downstream tasks like classification, we adopted widely-used methods as follows.
1045
1046                  •      LP (Zhu, 2005) is a semi-supervised algorithm that spreads known labels to similar data
1047
                             points in an unlabeled dataset, based on the given graph structure.
1048                  •      GCNMF (Taguchi et al., 2021) is an end-to-end GNN-based model that imputes missing
1049                         features by assuming a Gaussian Mixture Model aligned with GCN.
1050                  •      PaGNN (Jiang & Zhang, 2020) is a GNN-based method that implements a partial message-
1051                         passing scheme, propagating only observed features.
1052                  •      Zero (Rossi et al., 2021) is a simple 2-layer Graph Convolution Network. We impute
1053                         missing features with zeros in this model.
1054
                      •      NM (Rossi et al., 2021) imputes missing features by averaging the features of one-hop
1055
                             neighboring nodes, followed by GCN layers.
1056
1057                  •      FP (Rossi et al., 2021) propagates given features through neighbors, replacing observed
1058
                             ones with their original values to minimize Dirichlet energy.
1059                  •      PCFI (Um et al., 2023) improves FP by considering the relationship among features with
1060                         pseudo confidence, defined by the shortest path to the known feature.
1061
1062   In our experiments, we initially used zero imputation for missing values when applying these
1063
       methods. Additionally, we explored hybrid approaches that combine elements of tabular and graph-
       based methods, including MEAN and the more recent tabular baseline, IGRM. However, we found
1064
       that these methods did not perform optimally, as they both struggled in scenarios with initial missing
1065
       data. For the classifier, we utilized a 2-layer Graph Convolutional Network (GCN) as our classifier.
1066   Additionally, as our primary focus is on tabular data, we include common table-based imputation
1067   methods as follows.
1068
1069                  •      Mean (Little & Rubin, 2019) replaces missing values in a dataset with the mean value of
1070                         the available data for the same feature.
1071                  •      kNN (Troyanskaya et al., 2001) imputes missing data by finding the k nearest neighbors
1072                         based on cosine similarity and then averaging their features.
1073
                      •      GAIN (Yoon et al., 2018) uses a generative adversarial network to impute missing values,
1074
                             where one network generates candidates and another evaluates them.
1075
1076                  •      MIWAE (Mattei & Frellsen, 2019) employs a type of autoencoder for multiple imputations,
1077
                             capturing the data’s underlying distribution to provide multiple plausible values for missing
                             data.
1078
1079                  •      GRAPE (You et al., 2020) adopts a bipartite graph framework, viewing observations and
                             features as two node types, and imputes missing values through edge-level prediction.

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

              •   IGRM (Zhong et al., 2023) enhances the bipartite graph framework by introducing the
1081              concept of a friend network, which denotes relationships between samples.

1084   has been incorporated to perform classification tasks.

       A.7   C OMPARISON BETWEEN SC FP AND GRASS
1087
1088   As GRASS integrates FP with the aim of enhancing generalizability in the bio-medical domain, it is
1089   necessary to compare it with the recently proposed single-cell Feature Propagation (scFP), which
1090   also adopts FP, specifically targeting the single-cell RNA-seq domain.
1091
1092          •   (1) Target Domain: While scFP focuses on the scRNA-seq domain, particularly from a
1093              biological perspective, GRASS adopts a more general approach for the broader ‘biomedical’
1094              domain, as indicated in the paper’s title. This distinction is crucial as scRNA-seq datasets
1095              typically comprise numerical features where each element represents the count of a gene’s
1096              RNA transcript sequenced by the sequencing machine. In contrast, medical datasets often
1097
                  include both numerical and categorical features, such as patient information. This versatility
                  underscores the broader applicability of GRASS, capable of handling both numerical and
1098
                  categorical features, the latter through the clamping technique as discussed in Section 3.3.
1099
                  Therefore, we argue that the target domain of scFP, primarily focused on numerical matrix
1100              imputation in scRNA-seq, differs from that of GRASS, which extends to handling categorical
1101              data often encountered in patient data.
1102
1103
              •   (2) Target Task and Imputation Methodology: Unlike scFP, which is unsupervised with
                  its primary goal being effective imputation in sparse and noisy cell-gene count matrices,
1104
                  this work concentrates on supervised tasks, specifically on downstream applications like
1105
                  classification. Notably, the objective of imputation is often to enhance performance in
1106              relevant downstream tasks (Rossi et al., 2021; van Dijk et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2021).
1107              In this context, while the unsupervised approach of scFP can align with supervised tasks
1108              through probing (i.e., attaching a classifier), it is important to note that since its imputation
1109              occurs prior to probing, scFP cannot incorporate any downstream task-related knowledge
1110              during the imputation process, potentially leading to shortcomings in classification tasks.
1111              Conversely, as GRASS is directly designed with downstream tasks in mind, it incorporates
1112              knowledge pertinent to these tasks during imputation. This is achieved by utilizing the
1113              feature gradient, which is obtained during training 2-layer MLP. This fundamental difference
1114
                  in the target task (classification vs. imputation) and the imputation process (incorporating
                  relevant downstream knowledge or not) distinctly sets the two methodologies apart.
1115
1116          •   (3) Usage of FP: Although both scFP and GRASS employ FP, their applications of this
1117              process differ significantly. Specifically, scFP utilizes FP from a row-wise perspective, i.e.,
1118              focusing on cell-cell relationships while assuming gene-gene relationship independence.
1119              Although beneficial for smoothing similar and relevant samples, this approach does not
1120
                  capture interactions between columns (features), which are pivotal in the bio-medical domain.
                  For instance, in scRNA-seq, gene-gene relationships, such as co-expression networks,
1121
                  play a critical role in identifying key regulatory genes or pathways, offering insights into
1122
                  underlying biological or disease mechanisms (Cochain et al., 2018; Chowdhury et al., 2019;
1123              Galfre et al., 2021). Acknowledging this, GRASS initially employs column-wise FP to
1124              capture potential feature interactions, e.g., gene-gene relationships. It’s also noteworthy
1125              that GRASS incorporates not only the feature matrix but also the feature gradient relevant
1126              to downstream tasks when generating the column-wise kNN graph. Consequently, before
1127              initiating row-wise (sample-wise) smoothing in the relevant GNN model, GRASS is able to
1128              consider feature relationships that scFP does not capture. This distinction is illustrated in
1129              Figure 7 and significantly differentiates the two methodologies.

1318
1319   To explore the scalability of GRASS to larger datasets, we conducted evaluations using the single-cell
1320   RNA-seq Macosko dataset, which comprises 44,808 cells, 22,452 genes, and 14 distinct cell types,
1321   with an initial missing ratio of 81.41%. Among these genes, we preprocessed 2,000 highly variable
1322   genes, a common technique in scRNA-seq (Yun et al., 2023). We noted that GRASS integrates
1323   smoothly with existing methods, except in cases where initial baselines, such as GCNMF and
1324
       GRAPE, encounter Out-Of-Memory (OOM) issues due to the weights associated with the Gaussian
       Mixture Model and the construction of a heterogeneous node-feature graph, respectively. In Table 15,
1325
